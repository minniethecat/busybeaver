[072-001]"But Jael did not have a friend to help her in that way," said Miss

[072-002]Van Siever.

[072-003]At the end of an hour and a half the two ladies retired, and Jael

[072-004]disrobed herself, and Miss Van Siever put on her customary raiment.

[072-005]It was agreed among them that they had commenced their work

[072-006]auspiciously, and that they would meet again on the following Monday.

[072-007]The artist begged to be allowed an hour to go on with his work in

[072-008]Mrs. Broughton's room, and the hour was conceded to him. It was

[072-009]understood that he could not take the canvas backwards and forwards

[072-010]with him to his own house, and he pointed out that no progress

[072-011]whatever could be made, unless he were occasionally allowed some

[072-012]such grace as this. Mrs. Broughton doubted and hesitated, made

[072-013]difficulties, and lifted up her hands in despair. "It is easy for

[072-014]you to say, Why not? but I know very well why not." But at last she

[072-015]gave way. "Honi soit qui mal y pense," she said; "that must be my

[072-016]protection." So she followed Miss Van Siever downstairs, leaving Mr.

[072-017]Dalrymple in possession of her boudoir. "I shall give you just one

[072-018]hour," she said, "and then I shall come and turn you out." So she

[072-019]went down, and, as Miss Van Siever would not stay to lunch with her,

[072-020]she ate her lunch by herself, sending a glass of sherry and a biscuit

[072-021]up to the poor painter at his work.

[072-022]Exactly at the end of the hour she returned to him. "Now, Conway, you

[072-023]must go," she said.

[072-024]"But why in such a hurry?"

[072-025]"Because I say that it must be so. When I say so, pray let that be

[072-026]sufficient." But still Dalrymple went on working. "Conway," she said,

[072-027]"how can you treat me with so much disdain?"

[072-028]"Disdain, Mrs. Broughton!"

[072-029]"Yes, disdain. Have I not begged you to understand that I cannot

[072-030]allow you to remain here, and yet you pay no attention to my wishes."

[072-031]"I have done now;" and he began to put his brushes and paints

[072-032]together. "I suppose all these things may remain here?"

[072-033]"Yes; they may remain. They must do so, of course. There; if you will

[072-034]put the easel in the corner, with the canvas behind it, they will not

[072-035]be seen if he should chance to come into the room."

[072-036]"He would not be angry, I suppose, if he saw them?"

[072-037]"There is no knowing. Men are so unreasonable. All men are, I think.

[072-038]All those are whom I have had the fortune to know. Women generally

[072-039]say that men are selfish. I do not complain so much that they are

[072-040]selfish as that they are thoughtless. They are headstrong and do not

[072-041]look forward to results. Now you,--I do not think you would willingly

[072-042]do me an injury?"

[072-043]"I do not think I would."

[072-044]"I am sure you would not;--but yet you would forget to save me from

[072-045]one."

[072-046]"What injury?"

[072-047]"Oh, never mind. I am not thinking of anything in particular. From

[072-048]myself, for instance. But we will not talk about that. That way

[072-049]madness lies. Tell me, Conway;--what do you think of Clara Van

[072-050]Siever?"

[072-051]"She is very handsome, certainly."

[072-052]"And clever?"

[072-053]"Decidedly clever. I should think she has a temper of her own."

[072-054]"What woman is there worth a straw that has not? If Clara Van Siever

[072-055]were ill-used, she would resent it. I do not doubt that for a moment.

[072-056]I should not like to be the man who would do it."

[072-057]"Nor I, either," said Conway.

[072-058]"But there is plenty of feminine softness in that character, if she

[072-059]were treated with love and kindness. Conway, if you will take my

[072-060]advice you will ask Clara Van Siever to be your wife. But perhaps you

[072-061]have already."

[072-062]"Who; I?"

[072-063]"Yes; you."

[072-064]"I have not done it yet, certainly, Mrs. Broughton."

[072-065]"And why should you not do it?"

[072-066]"There are two or three reasons;--but perhaps none of any great

[072-067]importance. Do you know of none, Mrs. Broughton?"

[072-068]"I know of none," said Mrs. Broughton in a very serious,--in almost a

[072-069]tragic tone;--"of none that should weigh for a moment. As far as I am

[072-070]concerned, nothing would give me more pleasure."

[072-071]"That is so kind of you!"

[072-072]"I mean to be kind. I do, indeed, Conway. I know it will be better

[072-073]for you that you should be settled,--very much better. And it will be

[072-074]better for me. I do not mind admitting that;--though in saying so I

[072-075]trust greatly to your generosity to interpret my words properly."

[072-076]"I shall not flatter myself, if you mean that."

[072-077]"There is no question of flattery, Conway. The question is simply of

[072-078]truth and prudence. Do you not know that it would be better that you

[072-079]should be married?"

[072-080]"Not unless a certain gentleman were to die first," said Conway

[072-081]Dalrymple, as he deposited the last of his painting paraphernalia in

[072-082]the recess which had been prepared for them by Mrs. Broughton.

[072-083]"Conway, how can you speak in that wicked, wicked way!"

[072-084]"I can assure you I do not wish the gentleman in question the

[072-085]slightest harm in the world. If his welfare depended on me, he should

[072-086]be as safe as the Bank of England."

[072-087]"And you will not take my advice?"

[072-088]"What advice?"

[072-089]"[[About]] Clara?"

[072-090]"Mrs. Broughton, matrimony is a very important thing."

[072-091]"Indeed, it is;--oh, who can say how important! There was a time,

[072-092]Conway, when I thought you had given your heart to Madalina

[072-093]Demolines."

[072-094]"Heaven forbid!"

[072-095]"And I grieved, because I thought that she was not worthy of you."

[072-096]"There was never anything in that, Mrs. Broughton."

[072-097]"She thought that there was. At any rate, she said so. I know that

[072-098]for certain. She told me so herself. But let that pass. Clara Van

[072-099]Siever is in every respect very different from Madalina. Clara, I

[072-100]think, is worthy of you. And Conway,--of course it is not for me

[072-101]to dictate to you; but this I must tell you--" Then she paused, as

[072-102]though she did not know how to finish her sentence.

[072-103]"What must you tell me?"

[072-104]"I will tell you nothing more. If you cannot understand what I have

[072-105]said, you must be more dull of comprehension than I believe you to

[072-106]be. Now go. Why are you not gone this half-hour?"

[072-107]"How could I go while you were giving me all this good advice?"

[072-108]"I have not asked you to stay. Go now, at any rate. And, remember,

[072-109]Conway, if this picture is to go on, I will not have you remaining

[072-110]here after the work is done. Will you remember that?" And she held

[072-111]him by the hand while he declared that he would remember it.

[072-112]Mrs. Dobbs Broughton was no more in love with Conway Dalrymple than

[072-113]she was in love with King Charles on horseback at Charing Cross.

[072-114]And, over and beyond the protection which came to her in the course

[072-115]of nature from unimpassioned feelings in this special phase of her

[072-116]life,--and indeed, I may say, in every phase of her life,--it must be

[072-117]acknowledged on her behalf that she did enjoy that protection which

[072-118]comes from what we call principle,--though the principle was not

[072-119]perhaps very high of its kind. Madalina Demolines had been right when

[072-120]she talked of her friend Maria's principles. Dobbs Broughton had been

[072-121]so far lucky in that jump in the dark which he had made in taking a

[072-122]wife to himself, that he had not fallen upon a really vicious woman,

[072-123]or upon a woman of strong feeling. If it had come to be the lot of

[072-124]Mrs. Dobbs Broughton to have six hours' work to do every day of her

[072-125]life, I think that the work would have been done badly, but that it

[072-126]would have kept her free from all danger. As it was she had nothing

[072-127]to do. She had no child. She was not given to much reading. She could

[072-128]not sit with a needle in her hand all day. She had no aptitude for

[072-129]May meetings, or the excitement of charitable good works. Life with

[072-130]her was very dull, and she found no amusement within her reach so

[072-131]easy and so pleasant as the amusement of pretending to be in love. If

[072-132]all that she did and all that she said could only have been taken for

[072-133]its worth and for nothing more, by the different persons concerned,

[072-134]there was very little in it to flatter Mr. Dalrymple or to give cause

[072-135]for tribulation to Mr. Broughton. She probably cared but little for

[072-136]either of them. She was one of those women to whom it is not given by

[072-137]nature to care very much for anybody. But, of the two, she certainly

[072-138]cared the most for Mr. Dobbs Broughton,--because Mr. Dobbs Broughton

[072-139]belonged to her. As to leaving Mr. Dobbs Broughton's house, and

[072-140]putting herself into the hands of another man,--no Imogen of a wife

[072-141]was ever less likely to take a step so wicked, so dangerous, and so

[072-142]generally disagreeable to all the parties concerned.

[072-143]But Conway Dalrymple,--though now and again he had got a side glance

[072-144]at her true character with clear-seeing eyes,--did allow himself to

[072-145]be flattered and deceived. He knew that she was foolish and ignorant,

[072-146]and that she often talked wonderful nonsense. He knew also that she

[072-147]was continually contradicting herself,--as when she would strenuously

[072-148]beg him to leave her, while she would continue to talk to him

[072-149]in a strain that prevented the possibility of his going. But,

[072-150]nevertheless, he was flattered, and he did believe that she loved

[072-151]him. As to his love for her,--he knew very well that it amounted to

[072-152]nothing. Now and again, perhaps twice a week, if he saw her as often,

[072-153]he would say something which would imply a declaration of affection.

[072-154]He felt that as much as that was expected from him, and that he

[072-155]ought not to hope to get off cheaper. And now that this little

[072-156]play was going on about Miss Van Siever, he did think that Mrs.

[072-157]Dobbs Broughton was doing her very best to overcome an unfortunate

[072-158]attachment. It is so gratifying to a young man's feelings to suppose

[072-159]that another man's wife has conceived an unfortunate attachment for

[072-160]him! Conway Dalrymple ought not to have been fooled by such a woman;

[072-161]but I fear that he was fooled by her.

[072-162]As he returned home to-day from Mrs. Broughton's house to his own

[072-163]lodgings he rambled out for a while into Kensington Gardens, and

[072-164]thought of his position seriously. "I don't see why I should not

[072-165]marry her," he said to himself, thinking of course of Miss Van

[072-166]Siever. "If Maria is not in earnest it is not my fault. And it would

[072-167]be my wish that she should be in earnest. If I suppose her to be so,

[072-168]and take her at her word, she can have no right to quarrel with me.

[072-169]Poor Maria! at any rate it will be better for her, for no good can

[072-170]come of this kind of thing. And, by heavens, with a woman like that,

[072-171]of strong feelings, one never knows what may happen." And then he

[072-172]thought of the condition he would be in, if he were to find her some

[072-173]fine day in his own rooms, and if she were to tell him that she could

[072-174]not go home again, and that she meant to remain with him!

[072-175]In the meantime Mrs. Dobbs Broughton had gone down into her own

[072-176]drawing-room, had tucked herself up on the sofa, and had fallen fast

[072-177]asleep.

[072-178]CHAPTER XXXIX.

[072-179]A NEW FLIRTATION.

[072-180][Illustration]

[072-181]John Eames sat at his office on the day after his return to London,

[072-182]and answered the various letters which he had found waiting for him

[072-183]at his lodgings on the previous evening. To Miss Demolines he had

[072-184]already written from his club,--a single line, which he considered to

[072-185]be appropriate to the mysterious necessities of the occasion. "I will

[072-186]be with you at a quarter to six to-morrow.--J. E. Just returned."

[072-187]There was not another word; and as he scrawled it at one of the club

[072-188]tables while two or three men were talking to him, he felt rather

[072-189]proud of his correspondence. "It was capital fun," he said; "and

[072-190]after all,"--the "all" on this occasion being Lily Dale, and the

[072-191]sadness of his disappointment at Allington,--"after all, let a fellow

[072-192]be ever so down in the mouth, a little amusement should do him good."

[072-193]And he reflected further that the more a fellow be "down in the

[072-194]mouth," the more good the amusement would do him. He sent off his

[072-195]note, therefore, with some little inward rejoicing,--and a word or

[072-196]two also of spoken rejoicing. "What fun women are sometimes," he

[072-197]said to one of his friends,--a friend with whom he was very intimate,

[072-198]calling him always Fred, and slapping his back, but whom he never by

[072-199]any chance saw out of his club.
