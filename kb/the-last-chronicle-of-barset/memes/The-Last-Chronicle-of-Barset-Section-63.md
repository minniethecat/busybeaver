[063-001]Such a one was Mr. John Chadwick, and as it was a portion of his

[063-002]duties to accompany the bishop to consecrations and ordinations, he

[063-003]knew Dr. Proudie very well. Having been brought up, as it were, under

[063-004]the very wing of Bishop Grantly, it could not well be that he should

[063-005]love Bishop Grantly's successor. The old bishop and the new bishop

[063-006]had been so different that no man could like, or even esteem, them

[063-007]both. But Mr. Chadwick was a prudent man, who knew well the source

[063-008]from which he earned his bread, and he had never quarrelled with

[063-009]Bishop Proudie. He knew Mrs. Proudie also,--of necessity,--and when I

[063-010]say of him that he had hitherto avoided any open quarrel with her, it

[063-011]will I think be allowed that he was a man of prudence and sagacity.

[063-012]But he had sometimes been sorely tried, and he felt when he got

[063-013]her note that he was now about to encounter a very sore trial. He

[063-014]muttered something which might have been taken for an oath, were it

[063-015]not that the outward signs of the man gave warranty that no oath

[063-016]could proceed from such a one. Then he wrote a short note presenting

[063-017]his compliments to Mrs. Proudie, and saying that he would call at the

[063-018]palace at eleven o'clock on the following morning.

[063-019]But, in the meantime, Mrs. Proudie, who could not be silent on

[063-020]the subject for a moment, did learn something of the truth from

[063-021]her husband. The information did not come to her in the way of

[063-022]instruction, but was teased out of the unfortunate man. "I know that

[063-023]you can proceed against him in the Court of Arches, under the 'Church

[063-024]Discipline Act,'" she said.

[063-025]"No, my dear; no;" said the bishop, shaking his head in his misery.

[063-026]"Or in the Consistorial Court. It's all the same thing."

[063-027]"There must be an inquiry first,--by his brother clergy. There must

[063-028]indeed. It's the only way of proceeding."

[063-029]"But there has been an inquiry, and he has been committed."

[063-030]"That doesn't signify, my dear. That's the Civil Law."

[063-031]"And if the Civil Law condemns him, and locks him up in prison;--as

[063-032]it most certainly will do?"

[063-033]"But it hasn't done so yet, my dear. I really think that as it has

[063-034]gone so far, it will be best to leave it as it is till he has taken

[063-035]his trial."

[063-036]"What; leave him there after what occurred this morning in this

[063-037]palace?" The palace with Mrs. Proudie was always a palace, and never

[063-038]a house. "No; no; ten thousand times, no. Are you not aware that he

[063-039]insulted you, and grossly, most grossly insulted me? I was never

[063-040]treated with such insolence by any clergyman before, since I first

[063-041]came to this palace;--never, never. And we know the man to be a

[063-042]thief;--we absolutely know it. Think, my lord, of the souls of his

[063-043]people!"

[063-044]"Oh, dear; oh, dear; oh, dear," said the bishop.

[063-045]"Why do you fret yourself in that way?"

[063-046]"Because you will get me into trouble. I tell you the only thing to

[063-047]be done is to issue a commission with the rural dean at the head of

[063-048]it."

[063-049]"Then issue a commission."

[063-050]"And they will take three months."

[063-051]"Why should they take three months? Why should they take more than

[063-052]three days,--or three hours? It is all plain sailing."

[063-053]"These things are never plain sailing, my dear. When a bishop has

[063-054]to oppose any of his clergy, it is always made as difficult as

[063-055]possible."

[063-056]"More shame for them who make it so."

[063-057]"But it is so. If I were to take legal proceedings against him, it

[063-058]would cost,--oh, dear,--more than a thousand pounds, I should say."

[063-059]"If it costs two, you must do it." Mrs. Proudie's anger was still

[063-060]very hot, or she would not have spoken of an unremunerative outlay of

[063-061]money in such language as that.

[063-062]In this manner she did come to understand, before the arrival of

[063-063]Mr. Chadwick, that her husband could take no legal steps towards

[063-064]silencing Mr. Crawley until a commission of clergymen had been

[063-065]appointed to inquire into the matter, and that that commission

[063-066]should be headed by the rural dean within the limits of whose rural

[063-067]deanery the parish of Hogglestock was situated, or by some beneficed

[063-068]parochial clergyman of repute in the neighbourhood. Now the rural

[063-069]dean was Dr. Tempest of Silverbridge,--who had held that position

[063-070]before the coming of Dr. Proudie to the diocese; and there had grown

[063-071]up in the bosom of Mrs. Proudie a strong feeling that undue mercy

[063-072]had been shown to Mr. Crawley by the magistrates of Silverbridge, of

[063-073]whom Dr. Tempest had been one. "These magistrates had taken bail for

[063-074]his appearance at the assizes, instead of committing him to prison

[063-075]at once,--as they were bound to do, when such an offence as that

[063-076]had been committed by a clergyman. But, no;--even though there was

[063-077]a clergyman among them, they had thought nothing of the souls of

[063-078]the poor people!" In such language Mrs. Proudie had spoken of the

[063-079]affair at Silverbridge, and having once committed herself to such an

[063-080]opinion, of course she thought that Dr. Tempest would go through fire

[063-081]and water,--would omit no stretch of what little judicial power might

[063-082]be committed to his hands,--with the view of opposing his bishop and

[063-083]maintaining the culprit in his position. "In such a case as this, can

[063-084]not you name an acting rural dean yourself? Dr. Tempest, you know, is

[063-085]very old." "No, my dear; no; I cannot." "You can ask Mr. Chadwick,

[063-086]at any rate, and then you could name Mr. Thumble." "But Mr. Thumble

[063-087]doesn't even hold a living in the diocese. Oh, dear; oh, dear; oh,

[063-088]dear!" And so the matter rested until Mr. Chadwick came.

[063-089]Mrs. Proudie had no doubt intended to have Mr. Chadwick all to

[063-090]herself,--at any rate so to encounter him in the first instance. But

[063-091]having been at length convinced that the inquiry by the rural dean

[063-092]was really necessary as a preliminary, and having also slept upon the

[063-093]question of expenditure, she gave directions that the lawyer should

[063-094]be shown into the bishop's study, and she took care to be absent

[063-095]at the moment of his arrival. Of course she did not intend that Mr.

[063-096]Chadwick should leave the palace without having heard what she had

[063-097]to say, but she thought that it would be well that he should be made

[063-098]to conceive that though the summons had been written by her, it

[063-099]had really been intended on the part of the bishop. "Mr. Chadwick

[063-100]will be with you at eleven, bishop," she said, as she got up from

[063-101]the breakfast-table, at which she left his lordship with two of

[063-102]his daughters and with a married son-in-law, a clergyman who was

[063-103]staying in the house. "Very well, my dear," said the bishop, with a

[063-104]smile,--for he was anxious not to betray any vexation at his wife's

[063-105]interference before his daughters or the Rev. Mr. Tickler. But he

[063-106]understood it all. Mr. Chadwick had been sent for with reference to

[063-107]Mr. Crawley, and he was driven,--absolutely driven, to propose to his

[063-108]lawyer that this commission of inquiry should be issued.

[063-109]Punctually at eleven Mr. Chadwick came, wearing a very long face

[063-110]as he entered the palace door,--for he felt that he would in all

[063-111]probability be now compelled to quarrel with Mrs. Proudie. Much he

[063-112]could bear, but there was a limit to his endurance. She had never

[063-113]absolutely sent for him before, though she had often interfered

[063-114]with him. "I shall have to tell her a bit of my mind," he said, as

[063-115]he stepped across the Close, habited in his best suit of black,

[063-116]with most exact white cravat, and yet looking not quite like a

[063-117]clergyman,--with some touch of the undertaker in his gait. When he

[063-118]found that he was shown into the bishop's room, and that the bishop

[063-119]was there,--and the bishop only,--his mind was relieved. It would

[063-120]have been better that the bishop should have written himself, or that

[063-121]the chaplain should have written in his lordship's name; that,

[063-122]however, was a trifle.

[063-123]But the bishop did not know what to say to him. If he intended to

[063-124]direct an inquiry to be made by the rural dean, it would be by no

[063-125]means becoming that he should consult Mr. Chadwick as to doing so.

[063-126]It might be well, or if not well at any rate not improper, that he

[063-127]should make the application to Dr. Tempest through Mr. Chadwick; but

[063-128]in that case he must give the order at once, and he still wished to

[063-129]avoid it if it were possible. Since he had been in the diocese no

[063-130]case so grave as this had been pushed upon him. The intervention

[063-131]of the rural dean in an ordinary way he had used,--had been made

[063-132]to use,--more than once, by his wife. A vicar had been absent a

[063-133]little too long from one parish, and there had been rumours about

[063-134]brandy-and-water in another. Once he had been very nearly in deep

[063-135]water because Mrs. Proudie had taken it in dudgeon that a certain

[063-136]young rector, who had been left a widower, had a very pretty

[063-137]governess for his children; and there had been that case, sadly

[063-138]notorious in the diocese at the time, of our excellent friend Mr.

[063-139]Robarts of Framley, when the bailiffs were in his house because he

[063-140]couldn't pay his debts,--or rather, the debts of his friend for whom

[063-141]he had signed bills. But in all these cases some good fortune had

[063-142]intervened, and he had been saved from the terrible necessity of any

[063-143]ulterior process. But now,--now he was being driven beyond himself,

[063-144]and all to no purpose. If Mrs. Proudie would only wait three months

[063-145]the civil law would do it all for him. But here was Mr. Chadwick in

[063-146]the room, and he knew that it would be useless for him to attempt to

[063-147]talk to Mr. Chadwick about other matters, and so dismiss him. The

[063-148]wife of his bosom would be down upon them before Chadwick could be

[063-149]out of the room.

[063-150]"H--m--ha. How d'ye do, Mr. Chadwick--won't you sit down?" Mr.

[063-151]Chadwick thanked his lordship, and sat down. "It's very cold, isn't

[063-152]it, Mr. Chadwick?"

[063-153]"A hard frost, my lord, but a beautiful day."

[063-154]"Won't you come near the fire?" The bishop knew that Mrs. Proudie was

[063-155]on the road, and had an eye to the proper strategical position of

[063-156]his forces. Mrs. Proudie would certainly take up her position in a

[063-157]certain chair from whence the light enabled her to rake her husband

[063-158]thoroughly. What advantage she might have from this he could not

[063-159]prevent;--but he could so place Mr. Chadwick, that the lawyer should

[063-160]be more within the reach of his eye than that of his wife. So the

[063-161]bishop pointed to an arm-chair opposite to himself and near the fire,

[063-162]and Mr. Chadwick seated himself accordingly.

[063-163]"This is a very sad affair about Mr. Crawley," said the bishop.

[063-164]"Very sad indeed," said the lawyer. "I never pitied a man so much in

[063-165]my life, my lord."

[063-166]This was not exactly the line which the bishop was desirous of

[063-167]taking. "Of course he is to be pitied;--of course he is. But from all

[063-168]I hear, Mr. Chadwick, I am afraid,--I am afraid we must not acquit

[063-169]him."

[063-170]"As to that, my lord, he has to stand his trial, of course."

[063-171]"But, you see, Mr. Chadwick, regarding him as a beneficed

[063-172]clergyman,--with a cure of souls,--the question is whether I should

[063-173]be justified in leaving him where he is till his trial shall come

[063-174]on."

[063-175]"Of course your lordship knows best about that, but--"

[063-176]"I know there is a difficulty. I know that. But I am inclined to

[063-177]think that in the interests of the parish I am bound to issue a

[063-178]commission of inquiry."
