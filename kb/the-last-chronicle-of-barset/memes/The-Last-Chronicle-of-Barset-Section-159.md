[159-001]"Of course, my dear, I will see him."

[159-002]"And Posy shall come back when he has gone," said Mrs. Arabin.

[159-003]"Posy will do me more good than Dr. Filgrave I am quite sure;--but

[159-004]Posy shall go now." So Posy scrambled off the bed, and the doctor was

[159-005]ushered into the room.

[159-006]"A day or two will see the end of it, Mr. Archdeacon;--I should say a

[159-007]day or two," said the doctor, as he met Dr. Grantly in the hall. "I

[159-008]should say that a day or two would see the end of it. Indeed I will

[159-009]not undertake that twenty-four hours may not see the close of his

[159-010]earthly troubles. He has no suffering, no pain, no disturbing cause.

[159-011]Nature simply retires to rest." Dr. Filgrave, as he said this, made a

[159-012]slow falling motion with his hands, which alone on various occasions

[159-013]had been thought to be worth all the money paid for his attendance.

[159-014]"Perhaps you would wish that I should step in in the evening, Mr.

[159-015]Dean? As it happens, I shall be at liberty." The dean of course said

[159-016]that he would take it as an additional favour. Neither the dean nor

[159-017]the archdeacon had the slightest belief in Dr. Filgrave, and yet they

[159-018]would hardly have been contented that their father-in-law should have

[159-019]departed without him.

[159-020]"Look at that man, now," said the archdeacon, when the doctor had

[159-021]gone, "who talks so glibly about nature going to rest. I've known

[159-022]him all my life. He's an older man by some months than our dear

[159-023]old friend upstairs. And he looks as if he were going to attend

[159-024]death-beds in Barchester for ever."

[159-025]"I suppose he is right in what he tells us now?" said the dean.

[159-026]"No doubt he is; but my belief doesn't come from his saying it." Then

[159-027]there was a pause as the two church dignitaries sat together, doing

[159-028]nothing, feeling that the solemnity of the moment was such that it

[159-029]would be hardly becoming that they should even attempt to read. "His

[159-030]going will make an old man of me," said the archdeacon. "It will be

[159-031]different with you."

[159-032]"It will make an old woman of Eleanor, I fear."

[159-033]"I seem to have known him all my life," said the archdeacon. "I have

[159-034]known him ever since I left college; and I have known him as one

[159-035]man seldom knows another. There is nothing that he has done,--as I

[159-036]believe, nothing that he has thought,--with which I have not been

[159-037]cognizant. I feel sure that he never had an impure fancy in his mind,

[159-038]or a faulty wish in his heart. His tenderness has surpassed the

[159-039]tenderness of woman; and yet, when an occasion came for showing it,

[159-040]he had all the spirit of a hero. I shall never forget his resignation

[159-041]of the hospital, and all that I did and said to make him keep it."

[159-042]"But he was right?"

[159-043]"As Septimus Harding he was, I think, right; but it would have been

[159-044]wrong in any other man. And he was right, too, about the deanery."

[159-045]For promotion had once come in Mr. Harding's way, and he, too, might

[159-046]have been Dean of Barchester. "The fact is, he never was wrong.

[159-047]He couldn't go wrong. He lacked guile, and he feared God,--and a

[159-048]man who does both will never go far astray. I don't think he ever

[159-049]coveted aught in his life,--except a new case for his violoncello and

[159-050]somebody to listen to him when he played it." Then the archdeacon got

[159-051]up, and walked about the room in his enthusiasm; and, perhaps, as

[159-052]he walked some thoughts as to the sterner ambition of his own life

[159-053]passed through his mind. What things had he coveted? Had he lacked

[159-054]guile? He told himself that he had feared God,--but he was not sure

[159-055]that he was telling himself true even in that.

[159-056]During the whole of the morning Mrs. Arabin and Mrs. Grantly were

[159-057]with their father, and during the greater part of the day there was

[159-058]absolute silence in the room. He seemed to sleep; and they, though

[159-059]they knew that in truth he was not sleeping, feared to disturb him

[159-060]by a word. [[About]] two Mrs. Baxter brought him his dinner, and he did

[159-061]rouse himself, and swallowed a spoonful or two of soup and half

[159-062]a glass of wine. At this time Posy came to him, and stood at the

[159-063]bedside, looking at him with her great wide eyes. She seemed to be

[159-064]aware that life had now gone so far with her dear old friend that she

[159-065]must not be allowed to sit upon his bed again. But he put his hand

[159-066]out to her, and she held it, standing quite still and silent. When

[159-067]Mrs. Baxter came to take away the tray, Posy's mother got up, and

[159-068]whispered a word to the child. Then Posy went away, and her eyes

[159-069]never beheld the old man again. That was a day which Posy will

[159-070]never forget,--not though she should live to be much older than her

[159-071]grandfather was when she thus left him.

[159-072]"It is so sweet to have you both here," he said, when he had been

[159-073]lying silent for nearly an hour after the child had gone. Then they

[159-074]got up, and came and stood close to him. "There is nothing left for

[159-075]me to wish, my dears;--nothing." Not long after that he expressed a

[159-076]desire that the two husbands,--his two sons-in-law,--should come to

[159-077]him; and Mrs. Arabin went to them, and brought them to the room. As

[159-078]he took their hands he merely repeated the same words again. "There

[159-079]is nothing left for me to wish, my dears;--nothing." He never spoke

[159-080]again above his breath; but ever and anon his daughters, who watched

[159-081]him, could see that he was praying. The two men did not stay with him

[159-082]long, but returned to the gloom of the library. The gloom had almost

[159-083]become the darkness of night, and they were still sitting there

[159-084]without any light, when Mrs. Baxter entered the room. "The dear

[159-085]gentleman is no more," said Mrs. Baxter; and it seemed to the

[159-086]archdeacon that the very moment of his father's death had repeated

[159-087]itself. When Dr. Filgrave called he was told that his services could

[159-088]be of no further use. "Dear, dear!" said the doctor. "We are all

[159-089]dust, Mrs. Baxter; are we not?" There were people in Barchester who

[159-090]pretended to know how often the doctor had repeated this little

[159-091]formula during the last thirty years.

[159-092]There was no violence of sorrow in the house that night; but there

[159-093]were aching hearts, and one heart so sore that it seemed that no cure

[159-094]for its anguish could ever reach it. "He has always been with me,"

[159-095]Mrs. Arabin said to her husband, as he strove to console her. "It was

[159-096]not that I loved him better than Susan, but I have felt so much more

[159-097]of his loving tenderness. The sweetness of his voice has been in my

[159-098]ears almost daily since I was born."

[159-099]They buried him in the cathedral which he had loved so well, and

[159-100]in which nearly all the work of his life had been done; and all

[159-101]Barchester was there to see him laid in his grave within the

[159-102]cloisters. There was no procession of coaches, no hearse, nor was

[159-103]there any attempt at funereal pomp. From the dean's side door, across

[159-104]the vaulted passage, and into the transept,--over the little step

[159-105]upon which he had so nearly fallen when last he made his way out of

[159-106]the building,--the coffin was carried on men's shoulders. It was

[159-107]but a short journey from his bedroom to his grave. But the bell had

[159-108]been tolling sadly all the morning, and the nave and the aisles and

[159-109]the transepts, close up to the door leading from the transept into

[159-110]the cloister, were crowded with those who had known the name and

[159-111]the figure and the voice of Mr. Harding as long as they had known

[159-112]anything. Up to this day no one would have said specially that Mr.

[159-113]Harding was a favourite in the town. He had never been forward enough

[159-114]in anything to become the acknowledged possessor of popularity. But,

[159-115]now that he was gone, men and women told each other how good he had

[159-116]been. They remembered the sweetness of his smile, and talked of

[159-117]loving little words which he had spoken to them,--either years ago

[159-118]or the other day, for his words had always been loving. The dean and

[159-119]the archdeacon came first, shoulder to shoulder, and after them came

[159-120]their wives. I do not know that it was the proper order for mourning,

[159-121]but it was a touching sight to be seen, and was long remembered in

[159-122]Barchester. Painful as it was for them, the two women would be there,

[159-123]and the two sisters would walk together;--nor would they go before

[159-124]their husbands. Then there were the archdeacon's two sons,--for the

[159-125]Rev. Charles Grantly had come to Plumstead on the occasion. And in

[159-126]the vaulted passage which runs between the deanery and the end of

[159-127]the transept all the chapter, with the choir, the prebendaries, with

[159-128]the fat old chancellor, the precentor, and the minor canons down

[159-129]to the little choristers,--they all were there, and followed in at

[159-130]the transept door, two by two. And in the transept they were joined

[159-131]by another clergyman whom no one had expected to see that day. The

[159-132]bishop was there, looking old and worn,--almost as though he were

[159-133]unconscious of what he was doing. Since his wife's death no one had

[159-134]seen him out of the palace or of the palace grounds till that day.

[159-135]But there he was,--and they made way for him into the procession

[159-136]behind the two ladies,--and the archdeacon, when he saw it, resolved

[159-137]that there should be peace in his heart, if peace might be possible.

[159-138]They made their way into the cloisters where the grave had been

[159-139]dug,--as many as might be allowed to follow. The place indeed was

[159-140]open to all who chose to come; but they who had only slightly known

[159-141]the man, refrained from pressing upon those who had a right to stand

[159-142]around his coffin. But there was one other there whom the faithful

[159-143]chronicler of Barchester should mention. Before any other one had

[159-144]reached the spot, the sexton and the verger between them had led in

[159-145]between them, among the graves beneath the cloisters, a blind man,

[159-146]very old, with a wondrous stoop, but who must have owned a grand

[159-147]stature before extreme old age had bent him, and they placed him

[159-148]sitting on a stone in the corner of the archway. But as soon as the

[159-149]shuffling of steps reached his ears, he raised himself with the

[159-150]aid of his stick, and stood during the service leaning against the

[159-151]pillar. The blind man was so old that he might almost have been

[159-152]Mr. Harding's father. This was John Bunce, a bedesman from Hiram's

[159-153]Hospital,--and none perhaps there had known Mr. Harding better than

[159-154]he had known him. When the earth had been thrown on to the coffin,

[159-155]and the service was over, and they were about to disperse, Mrs.

[159-156]Arabin went up to the old man, and taking his hand between hers

[159-157]whispered a word into his ear. "Oh, Miss Eleanor," he said. "Oh, Miss

[159-158]Eleanor!" Within a fortnight he also was lying within the cathedral

[159-159]precincts.

[159-160]And so they buried Mr. Septimus Harding, formerly Warden of Hiram's

[159-161]Hospital in the city of Barchester, of whom the chronicler may say

[159-162]that that city never knew a sweeter gentleman or a better Christian.

[159-163]CHAPTER LXXXII.

[159-164]THE LAST SCENE AT HOGGLESTOCK.

[159-165][Illustration]

[159-166]The fortnight following Mr. Harding's death was passed very quietly

[159-167]at Hogglestock, for during that time no visitor made an appearance in

[159-168]the parish except Mr. Snapper on the Sundays. Mr. Snapper, when he

[159-169]had completed the service on the first of these Sundays, intimated to

[159-170]Mr. Crawley his opinion that probably that gentleman might himself

[159-171]wish to resume the duties on the following Sabbath. Mr. Crawley,

[159-172]however, courteously declined to do anything of the kind. He said

[159-173]that it was quite out of the question that he should do so without a

[159-174]direct communication made to him from the bishop, or by the bishop's

[159-175]order. The assizes had, of course, gone by, and all question of the

[159-176]trial was over. Nevertheless,--as Mr. Snapper said,--the bishop had

[159-177]not, as yet, given any order. Mr. Snapper was of opinion that the

[159-178]bishop in these days was not quite himself. He had spoken to the

[159-179]bishop about it and the bishop had told him peevishly--"I must say

[159-180]quite peevishly," Mr. Snapper had said,--that nothing was to be done

[159-181]at present. Mr. Snapper was not the less clearly of opinion that Mr.

[159-182]Crawley might resume his duties. To this, however, Mr. Crawley would

[159-183]not assent.
