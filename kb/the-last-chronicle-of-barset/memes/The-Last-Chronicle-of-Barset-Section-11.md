[011-002]"I must go home," said Grace.

[011-003]"Of course you shall, if you think it right at last; but let us

[011-004]talk of it. No one in this house, you know, has the slightest

[011-005]suspicion that your father has done anything that is in the least

[011-006]dishonourable."

[011-007]"I know that you have not."

[011-008]"No, nor has Anne." Miss Prettyman said this as though no one in that

[011-009]house beyond herself and her sister had a right to have any opinion

[011-010]on any subject.

[011-011]"I know that," said Grace.

[011-012]"Well, my dear. If we think so--"

[011-013]"But the servants, Miss Prettyman?"

[011-014]"If any servant in this house says a word to offend you,

[011-015]I'll--I'll--"

[011-016]"They don't say anything, Miss Prettyman, but they look. Indeed I'd

[011-017]better go home. Indeed I had!"

[011-018]"Do not you think your mother has cares enough upon her, and burden

[011-019]enough, without having another mouth to feed, and another head to

[011-020]shelter? You haven't thought of that, Grace!"

[011-021]"Yes, I have."

[011-022]"And as for the work, whilst you are not quite well you shall not be

[011-023]troubled with teaching. I have some old papers that want copying and

[011-024]settling, and you shall sit here and do that just for an employment.

[011-025]Anne knows that I've long wanted to have it done, and I'll tell her

[011-026]that you've kindly promised to do it for me."

[011-027]"No; no; no," said Grace; "I must go home." She was still kneeling at

[011-028]Miss Prettyman's knee, and still holding Miss Prettyman's hand. And

[011-029]then, at that moment, there came a tap at the door, gentle but yet

[011-030]not humble, a tap which acknowledged, on the part of the tapper, the

[011-031]supremacy in that room of the lady who was sitting there, but which

[011-032]still claimed admittance almost as a right. The tap was well known by

[011-033]both of them to be the tap of Miss Anne. Grace immediately jumped up,

[011-034]and Miss Prettyman settled herself in her chair with a motion which

[011-035]almost seemed to indicate some feeling of shame as to her late

[011-036]position.

[011-037]"I suppose I may come in?" said Miss Anne, opening the door and

[011-038]inserting her head.

[011-039]"Yes, you may come in,--if you have anything to say," said Miss

[011-040]Prettyman, with an air which seemed to be intended to assert her

[011-041]supremacy. But, in truth, she was simply collecting the wisdom and

[011-042]dignity which had been somewhat dissipated by her tenderness.

[011-043]"I did not know that Grace Crawley was here," said Miss Anne.

[011-044]"Grace Crawley is here," said Miss Prettyman.

[011-045]"What is the matter, Grace?" said Miss Anne, seeing the tears.

[011-046]"Never mind now," said Miss Prettyman.

[011-047]"Poor dear, I'm sure I'm sorry as though she were my own sister,"

[011-048]said Anne. "But, Annabella, I want to speak to you especially."

[011-049]"To me, in private?"

[011-050]"Yes, to you; in private, if Grace won't mind?"

[011-051]Then Grace prepared to go. But as she was going, Miss Anne, upon

[011-052]whose brow a heavy burden of thought was lying, stopped her suddenly.

[011-053]"Grace, my dear," she said, "go upstairs into your room, will

[011-054]you?--not across the hall to the school."

[011-055]"And why shouldn't she go to the school?" said Miss Prettyman.

[011-056]Miss Anne paused a moment, and then answered,--unwillingly, as though

[011-057]driven to make a reply which she knew to be indiscreet. "Because

[011-058]there is somebody in the hall."

[011-059]"Go to your room, dear," said Miss Prettyman. And Grace went to her

[011-060]room, never turning an eye down towards the hall. "Who is it?" said

[011-061]Miss Prettyman.

[011-062]"Major Grantly is here, asking to see you," said Miss Anne.

[011-063]CHAPTER VII.

[011-064]MISS PRETTYMAN'S PRIVATE ROOM.

[011-065][Illustration]

[011-066]Major Grantly, when threatened by his father with pecuniary

[011-067]punishment, should he demean himself by such a marriage as that he

[011-068]had proposed to himself, had declared that he would offer his hand

[011-069]to Miss Crawley on the next morning. This, however, he had not done.

[011-070]He had not done it, partly because he did not quite believe his

[011-071]father's threat, and partly because he felt that that threat was

[011-072]almost justified,--for the present moment,--by the circumstances

[011-073]in which Grace Crawley's father had placed himself. Henry Grantly

[011-074]acknowledged, as he drove himself home on the morning after his

[011-075]dinner at the rectory, that in this matter of his marriage he did owe

[011-076]much to his family. Should he marry at all, he owed it to them to

[011-077]marry a lady. And Grace Crawley,--so he told himself,--was a lady.

[011-078]And he owed it to them to bring among them as his wife a woman who

[011-079]should not disgrace him or them by her education, manners, or even by

[011-080]her personal appearance. In all these respects Grace Crawley was, in

[011-081]his judgment, quite as good as they had a right to expect her to be,

[011-082]and in some respects a great deal superior to that type of womanhood

[011-083]with which they had been most generally conversant. "If everybody had

[011-084]her due, my sister isn't fit to hold a candle to her," he said to

[011-085]himself. It must be acknowledged, therefore, that he was really in

[011-086]love with Grace Crawley; and he declared to himself, over and over

[011-087]again, that his family had no right to demand that he should marry

[011-088]a woman with money. The archdeacon's son by no means despised money.

[011-089]How could he, having come forth as a bird fledged from such a nest

[011-090]as the rectory at Plumstead Episcopi? Before he had been brought by

[011-091]his better nature and true judgment to see that Grace Crawley was

[011-092]the greater woman of the two, he had nearly submitted himself to the

[011-093]twenty thousand pounds of Miss Emily Dunstable,--to that, and her

[011-094]good-humour and rosy freshness combined. But he regarded himself as

[011-095]the well-to-do son of a very rich father. His only child was amply

[011-096]provided for; and he felt that, as regarded money, he had a right

[011-097]to do as he pleased. He felt this with double strength after his

[011-098]father's threat.

[011-099]But he had no right to make a marriage by which his family would be

[011-100]disgraced. Whether he was right or wrong in supposing that he would

[011-101]disgrace his family were he to marry the daughter of a convicted

[011-102]thief, it is hardly necessary to discuss here. He told himself that

[011-103]it would be so,--telling himself also that, by the stern laws of

[011-104]the world, the son and the daughter must pay for the offence of the

[011-105]father and the mother. Even among the poor, who would willingly marry

[011-106]the child of a man who had been hanged? But he carried the argument

[011-107]beyond this, thinking much of the matter, and endeavouring to think

[011-108]of it not only justly, but generously. If the accusation against

[011-109]Crawley were false,--if the man were being injured by an unjust

[011-110]charge,--even if he, Grantly, could make himself think that the

[011-111]girl's father had not stolen the money, then he would dare everything

[011-112]and go on. I do not know that his argument was good, or that his

[011-113]mind was logical in the matter. He ought to have felt that his own

[011-114]judgment as to the man's guilt was less likely to be correct than

[011-115]that of those whose duty it was and would be to form and to express

[011-116]a judgment on the matter; and as to Grace herself, she was equally

[011-117]innocent whether her father were guilty or not guilty. If he were

[011-118]to be debarred from asking her for her hand by his feelings for her

[011-119]father and mother, he should hardly have trusted to his own skill in

[011-120]ascertaining the real truth as to the alleged theft. But he was not

[011-121]logical, and thus, meaning to be generous, he became unjust.

[011-122]He found that among those in Silverbridge whom he presumed to be

[011-123]best informed on such matters, there was a growing opinion that Mr.

[011-124]Crawley had stolen the money. He was intimate with all the Walkers,

[011-125]and was able to find out that Mrs. Walker knew that her husband

[011-126]believed in the clergyman's guilt. He was by no means alone in his

[011-127]willingness to accept Mr. Walker's opinion as the true opinion.

[011-128]Silverbridge, generally, was endeavouring to dress itself in Mr.

[011-129]Walker's glass, and to believe as Mr. Walker believed. The ladies

[011-130]of Silverbridge, including the Miss Prettymans, were aware that Mr.

[011-131]Walker had been very kind both to Mr. and Mrs. Crawley, and argued

[011-132]from this that Mr. Walker must think the man to be innocent. But

[011-133]Henry Grantly, who did not dare to ask a direct question of the

[011-134]solicitor, went cunningly to work, and closeted himself with Mrs.

[011-135]Walker,--with Mrs. Walker, who knew well of the good fortune which

[011-136]was hovering over Grace's head and was so nearly settling itself upon

[011-137]her shoulders. She would have given a finger to be able to whitewash

[011-138]Mr. Crawley in the major's estimation. Nor must it be supposed that

[011-139]she told the major in plain words that her husband had convinced

[011-140]himself of the man's guilt. In plain words no question was asked

[011-141]between them, and in plain words no opinion was expressed. But there

[011-142]was the look of sorrow in the woman's eye, there was the absence of

[011-143]reference to her husband's assurance that the man was innocent, there

[011-144]was the air of settled grief which told of her own conviction; and

[011-145]the major left her, convinced that Mrs. Walker believed Mr. Crawley

[011-146]to be guilty.

[011-147]Then he went to Barchester; not open-mouthed with inquiry, but rather

[011-148]with open ears, and it seemed to him that all men in Barchester were

[011-149]of one mind. There was a county-club in Barchester, and at this

[011-150]county-club nine men out of every ten were talking about Mr. Crawley.

[011-151]It was by no means necessary that a man should ask questions on

[011-152]the subject. Opinion was expressed so freely that no such asking

[011-153]was required; and opinion in Barchester,--at any rate in the

[011-154]county-club,--seemed now to be all of one mind. There had been every

[011-155]disposition at first to believe Mr. Crawley to be innocent. He had

[011-156]been believed to be innocent, even after he had said wrongly that the

[011-157]cheque had been paid to him by Mr. Soames; but he had since stated

[011-158]that he had received it from Dean Arabin, and that statement was also

[011-159]shown to be false. A man who has a cheque changed on his own behalf

[011-160]is bound at least to show where he got the cheque. Mr. Crawley had

[011-161]not only failed to do this, but had given two false excuses. Henry

[011-162]Grantly, as he drove home to Silverbridge on the Sunday afternoon,

[011-163]summed up all the evidence in his own mind, and brought in a verdict

[011-164]of Guilty against the father of the girl whom he loved.

[011-165]On the following morning he walked into Silverbridge and called at

[011-166]Miss Prettyman's house. As he went along his heart was warmer towards

[011-167]Grace than it had ever been before. He had told himself that he was

[011-168]now bound to abstain, for his father's sake, from doing that which

[011-169]he had told his father that he would certainly do. But he knew also,

[011-170]that he had said that which, though it did not bind him to Miss

[011-171]Crawley, gave her a right to expect that he would so bind himself.

[011-172]And Miss Prettyman could not but be aware of what his intention had

[011-173]been, and could not but expect that he should now be explicit. Had

[011-174]he been a wise man altogether, he would probably have abstained from

[011-175]saying anything at the present moment,--a wise man, that is, in the

[011-176]ways and feelings of the world in such matters. But, as there are men

[011-177]who will allow themselves all imaginable latitude in their treatment

[011-178]of women, believing that the world will condone any amount of fault

[011-179]of that nature, so are there other men, and a class of men which on

[011-180]the whole is the more numerous of the two, who are tremblingly alive

[011-181]to the danger of censure on this head,--and to the danger of censure

[011-182]not only from others, but from themselves also. Major Grantly had

[011-183]done that which made him think it imperative upon him to do something

[011-184]further, and to do that something at once.
