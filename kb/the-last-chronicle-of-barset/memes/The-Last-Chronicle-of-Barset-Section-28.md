[028-001]"I don't believe she'll say anything of the kind, Cradell. If I

[028-002]remember anything of her, she has a stouter heart than that." Cradell

[028-003]admitted that his wife had a stouter heart than himself, and then

[028-004]made his way back to his own part of the office.

[028-005]This little interruption to the current of Mr. Eames's thoughts was,

[028-006]I think, for the good of the service, as, immediately on his friend's

[028-007]departure, he went to his work; whereas, had not he been thus called

[028-008]away from his reflections about Miss Dale, he would have sat thinking

[028-009]about her affairs probably for the rest of the morning. As it was, he

[028-010]really did write a dozen notes in answer to as many private letters

[028-011]addressed to his chief, Sir Raffle Buffle, in all of which he made

[028-012]excellently-worded false excuses for the non-performance of various

[028-013]requests made to Sir Raffle by the writers. "He's about the best

[028-014]hand at it that I know," said Sir Raffle, one day, to the secretary;

[028-015]"otherwise you may be sure I shouldn't keep him there." "I will allow

[028-016]that he is clever," said the secretary. "It isn't cleverness, so much

[028-017]as tact. It's what I call tact. I hadn't been long in the service

[028-018]before I mastered it myself; and now that I've been at the trouble to

[028-019]teach him I don't want to have the trouble to teach another. But upon

[028-020]my word he must mind his _p_'s and _q_'s; upon my word he must; and

[028-021]you had better tell him so." "The fact is, Mr. Kissing," said the

[028-022]private secretary the next day to the secretary,--Mr. Kissing was at

[028-023]that time secretary to the board of commissioners for the receipt

[028-024]of income tax--"The fact is, Mr. Kissing, Sir Raffle should never

[028-025]attempt to write a letter himself. He doesn't know how to do it. He

[028-026]always says twice too much, and yet not half enough. I wish you'd

[028-027]tell him so. He won't believe me." From which it will be seen Mr.

[028-028]Eames was proud of his special accomplishment, but did not feel any

[028-029]gratitude to the master who assumed to himself the glory of having

[028-030]taught him. On the present occasion John Eames wrote all his letters

[028-031]before he thought again of Lily Dale, and was able to write them

[028-032]without interruption, as the chairman was absent for the day at the

[028-033]Treasury,--or perhaps at his club. Then, when he had finished, he

[028-034]rang his bell, and ordered some sherry and soda-water, and stretched

[028-035]himself before the fire,--as though his exertions in the public

[028-036]service had been very great,--and seated himself comfortably in his

[028-037]arm-chair, and lit a cigar, and again took out Lady Julia's letter.

[028-038]As regarded the cigar, it may be said that both Sir Raffle and Mr.

[028-039]Kissing had given orders that on no account should cigars be lit

[028-040]within the precincts of the Income-tax Office. Mr. Eames had taken

[028-041]upon himself to understand that such orders did not apply to a

[028-042]private secretary, and was well aware that Sir Raffle knew his

[028-043]habit. To Mr. Kissing, I regret to say, he put himself in opposition

[028-044]whenever and wherever opposition was possible; so that men in the

[028-045]office said that one of the two must go at last. "But Johnny can

[028-046]do anything, you know, because he has got money." That was too

[028-047]frequently the opinion finally expressed among the men.

[028-048]So John Eames sat down, and drank his soda-water, and smoked his

[028-049]cigar, and read his letter; or rather, simply that paragraph of the

[028-050]letter which referred to Miss Dale. "The tidings of her death have

[028-051]disturbed her, and set her thinking again of things that were fading

[028-052]from her mind." He understood it all. And yet how could it possibly

[028-053]be so? How could it be that she should not despise a man,--despise

[028-054]him if she did not hate him,--who had behaved as this man had behaved

[028-055]to her? It was now four years since this Crosbie had been engaged to

[028-056]Miss Dale, and had jilted her so heartlessly as to incur the disgust

[028-057]of every man in London who had heard the story. He had married an

[028-058]earl's daughter, who had left him within a few months of their

[028-059]marriage, and now Mr. Crosbie's noble wife was dead. The wife was

[028-060]dead, and simply because the man was free again, he, John Eames,

[028-061]was to be told that Miss Dale's mind was "disturbed," and that her

[028-062]thoughts were going back to things which had faded from her memory,

[028-063]and which should have been long since banished altogether from such

[028-064]holy ground.

[028-065]If Lily Dale were now to marry Mr. Crosbie, anything so perversely

[028-066]cruel as the fate of John Eames would never yet have been told in

[028-067]romance. That was his own idea on the matter as he sat smoking his

[028-068]cigar. I have said that he was proud of his constancy, and yet, in

[028-069]some sort, he was also ashamed of it. He acknowledged the fact of his

[028-070]love, and believed himself to have out-Jacobed Jacob; but he felt

[028-071]that it was hard for a man who had risen in the world as he had done

[028-072]to be made a plaything of by a foolish passion. It was now four years

[028-073]ago,--that affair of Crosbie,--and Miss Dale should have accepted him

[028-074]long since. Half-a-dozen times he had made up his mind to be very

[028-075]stern to her; and he had written somewhat sternly,--but the first

[028-076]moment that he saw her he was conquered again. "And now that brute

[028-077]will reappear, and everything will be wrong again," he said to

[028-078]himself. If the brute did reappear, something should happen of which

[028-079]the world should hear the tidings. So he lit another cigar, and began

[028-080]to think what that something should be.

[028-081]As he did so he heard a loud noise, as of harsh, rattling winds in

[028-082]the next room, and he knew that Sir Raffle had come back from the

[028-083]Treasury. There was a creaking of boots, and a knocking of chairs,

[028-084]and a ringing of bells, and then a loud angry voice,--a voice that

[028-085]was very harsh, and on this occasion very angry. Why had not his

[028-086]twelve-o'clock letters been sent up to him to the West End? Why not?

[028-087]Mr. Eames knew all about it. Why did Mr. Eames know all about it? Why

[028-088]had not Mr. Eames sent them up? Where was Mr. Eames? Let Mr. Eames

[028-089]be sent to him. All which Mr. Eames heard standing with the cigar in

[028-090]his mouth and his back to the fire. "Somebody has been bullying old

[028-091]Buffle, I suppose. After all he has been at the Treasury to-day,"

[028-092]said Eames to himself. But he did not stir till the messenger had

[028-093]been to him, nor even then, at once. "All right, Rafferty," he said;

[028-094]"I'll go in just now." Then he took half-a-dozen more whiffs from the

[028-095]cigar, threw the remainder into the fire, and opened the door which

[028-096]communicated between his room and Sir Raffle's.

[028-097]The great man was standing with two unopened epistles in his hand.

[028-098]"Eames," said he, "here are letters--" Then he stopped himself, and

[028-099]began upon another subject. "Did I not give express orders that I

[028-100]would have no smoking in the office?"

[028-101]"I think Mr. Kissing said something about it, sir."

[028-102]"Mr. Kissing! It was not Mr. Kissing at all. It was I. I gave the

[028-103]order myself."

[028-104]"You'll find it began with Mr. Kissing."

[028-105]"It did not begin with Mr. Kissing; it began and ended with me. What

[028-106]are you going to do, sir?" John Eames had stepped towards the bell,

[028-107]and his hand was already on the bell-pull.

[028-108]"I was going to ring for the papers, sir."

[028-109]"And who told you to ring for the papers? I don't want the papers.

[028-110]The papers won't show anything. I suppose my word may be taken

[028-111]without the papers. Since you're so fond of Mr. Kissing--"

[028-112]"I'm not fond of Mr. Kissing at all."

[028-113]"You'll have to go back to him, and let somebody come here who will

[028-114]not be too independent to obey my orders. Here are two most important

[028-115]letters have been lying here all day, instead of being sent up to me

[028-116]at the Treasury."

[028-117]"Of course they have been lying there. I thought you were at the

[028-118]club."

[028-119]"I told you I should go to the Treasury. I have been there all the

[028-120]morning with the chancellor,"--when Sir Raffle spoke officially of

[028-121]the chancellor he was not supposed to mean the Lord Chancellor--"and

[028-122]here I find letters which I particularly wanted lying upon my desk

[028-123]now. I must put an end to this kind of thing. I must, indeed. If you

[028-124]like the outer office better say so at once, and you can go."

[028-125]"I'll think about it, Sir Raffle."

[028-126]"Think about it! What do you mean by thinking about it? But I can't

[028-127]talk about that now. I'm very busy, and shall be here till past

[028-128]seven. I suppose you can stay?"

[028-129]"All night, if you wish it, sir."

[028-130]"Very well. That will do for the present.--I wouldn't have had these

[028-131]letters delayed for twenty pounds."

[028-132]"I don't suppose it would have mattered one straw if both of them

[028-133]remained unopened till next week." This last little speech, however,

[028-134]was not made aloud to Sir Raffle, but by Johnny to himself in the

[028-135]solitude of his own room.

[028-136]Very soon after that he went away, Sir Raffle having discovered that

[028-137]one of the letters in question required his immediate return to the

[028-138]West End. "I've changed my mind about staying. I shan't stay now. I

[028-139]should have done if these letters had reached me as they ought."

[028-140]"Then I suppose I can go?"

[028-141]"You can do as you like about that," said Sir Raffle.

[028-142]Eames did do as he liked, and went home, or to his club; and as

[028-143]he went he resolved that he would put an end, and at once, to the

[028-144]present trouble of his life. Lily Dale should accept him or reject

[028-145]him; and, taking either the one or the other alternative, she should

[028-146]hear a bit of his mind plainly spoken.

[028-147]CHAPTER XVI.

[028-148]DOWN AT ALLINGTON.

[028-149]It was Christmas-time down at Allington, and at three o'clock on

[028-150]Christmas Eve, just as the darkness of the early winter evening was

[028-151]coming on, Lily Dale and Grace Crawley were seated together, one

[028-152]above the other, on the steps leading up to the pulpit in Allington

[028-153]Church. They had been working all day at the decorations of the

[028-154]church, and they were now looking round them at the result of their

[028-155]handiwork. To an eye unused to the gloom the place would have been

[028-156]nearly dark; but they could see every corner turned by the ivy

[028-157]sprigs, and every line on which the holly-leaves were shining.

[028-158]And the greeneries of the winter had not been stuck up in the

[028-159]old-fashioned, idle way, a bough just fastened up here and a twig

[028-160]inserted there; but everything had been done with some meaning, with

[028-161]some thought towards the original architecture of the building. The

[028-162]Gothic lines had been followed, and all the lower arches which it had

[028-163]been possible to reach with an ordinary ladder had been turned as

[028-164]truly with the laurel cuttings as they had been turned originally

[028-165]with the stone.

[028-166]"I wouldn't tie another twig," said the elder girl, "for all the

[028-167]Christmas pudding that was ever boiled."

[028-168]"It's lucky then that there isn't another twig to tie."

[028-169]"I don't know about that. I see a score of places where the work has

[028-170]been scamped. This is the sixth time I have done the church, and I

[028-171]don't think I'll ever do it again. When we first began it, Bell and

[028-172]I, you know,--before Bell was married,--Mrs. Boyce, and the Boycian

[028-173]establishment generally, used to come and help. Or rather we used to

[028-174]help her. Now she hardly ever looks after it at all."
