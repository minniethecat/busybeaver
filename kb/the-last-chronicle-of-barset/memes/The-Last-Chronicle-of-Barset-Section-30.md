[030-001]"Nobody will. There never is anybody at Hogglestock to stick up

[030-002]wreaths, or to do anything for the prettinesses of life. And

[030-003]now there will be less done than ever. How can mamma look after

[030-004]holly-leaves in her present state? And yet she will miss them, too.

[030-005]Poor mamma sees very little that is pretty; but she has not forgotten

[030-006]how pleasant pretty things are."

[030-007]"I wish I knew your mother, Grace."

[030-008]"I think it would be impossible for any one to know mamma now,--for

[030-009]any one who had not known her before. She never makes even a new

[030-010]acquaintance. She seems to think that there is nothing left for her

[030-011]in the world but to try and keep papa out of misery. And she does not

[030-012]succeed in that. Poor papa!"

[030-013]"Is he very unhappy about this wicked accusation?"

[030-014]"Yes; he is very unhappy. But, Lily, I don't know about its being

[030-015]wicked."

[030-016]"But you know that it is untrue."

[030-017]"Of course I know that papa did not mean to take anything that was

[030-018]not his own. But, you see, nobody knows where it came from; and

[030-019]nobody except mamma and Jane and I understand how very absent papa

[030-020]can be. I'm sure he doesn't know the least in the world how he came

[030-021]by it himself, or he would tell mamma. Do you know, Lily, I think I

[030-022]have been wrong to come away."

[030-023]"Don't say that, dear. Remember how anxious Mrs. Crawley was that you

[030-024]should come."

[030-025]"But I cannot bear to be comfortable here while they are so wretched

[030-026]at home. It seems such a mockery. Every time I find myself smiling at

[030-027]what you say to me, I think I must be the most heartless creature in

[030-028]the world."

[030-029]"Is it so very bad with them, Grace?"

[030-030]"Indeed it is bad. I don't think you can imagine what mamma has to

[030-031]go through. She has to cook all that is eaten in the house, and then,

[030-032]very often, there is no money in the house to buy anything. If you

[030-033]were to see the clothes she wears, even that would make your heart

[030-034]bleed. I who have been used to being poor all my life,--even I, when

[030-035]I am at home, am dismayed by what she has to endure."

[030-036]"What can we do for her, Grace?"

[030-037]"You can do nothing, Lily. But when things are like that at home you

[030-038]can understand what I feel in being here."

[030-039]Mrs. Giles and Gregory had now completed their task, or had so nearly

[030-040]done so as to make Miss Dale think that she might safely leave the

[030-041]church. "We will go in now," she said; "for it is dark and cold, and

[030-042]what I call creepy. Do you ever fancy that perhaps you will see a

[030-043]ghost some day?"

[030-044]"I don't think I shall ever see a ghost; but all the same I should be

[030-045]half afraid to be here alone in the dark."

[030-046]"I am often here alone in the dark, but I am beginning to think I

[030-047]shall never see a ghost now. I am losing all my romance, and getting

[030-048]to be an old woman. Do you know, Grace, I do so hate myself for being

[030-049]such an old maid."

[030-050]"But who says you're an old maid, Lily?"

[030-051]"I see it in people's eyes, and hear it in their voices. And they

[030-052]all talk to me as if I were very steady, and altogether removed from

[030-053]anything like fun and frolic. It seems to be admitted that if a girl

[030-054]does not want to fall in love, she ought not to care for any other

[030-055]fun in the world. If anybody made out a list of the old ladies in

[030-056]these parts, they'd put down Lady Julia, and mamma, and Mrs. Boyce,

[030-057]and me, and old Mrs. Hearne. The very children have an awful respect

[030-058]for me, and give over playing directly they see me. Well, mamma,

[030-059]we've done at last, and I have had such a scolding from Mrs. Boyce."

[030-060]"I daresay you deserved it, my dear."

[030-061]"No, I did not, mamma. Ask Grace if I did."

[030-062]"Was she not saucy to Mrs. Boyce, Miss Crawley?"

[030-063]"She said that Mr. Boyce scratches his nose in church," said Grace.

[030-064]"So he does; and goes to sleep, too."

[030-065]"If you told Mrs. Boyce that, Lily, I think she was quite right to

[030-066]scold you."

[030-067]Such was Miss Lily Dale, with whom Grace Crawley was staying;--Lily

[030-068]Dale with whom Mr. John Eames, of the Income-tax Office, had been

[030-069]so long and so steadily in love, that he was regarded among his

[030-070]fellow-clerks as a miracle of constancy,--who had, herself, in former

[030-071]days been so unfortunate in love as to have been regarded among her

[030-072]friends in the country as the most ill-used of women. As John Eames

[030-073]had been able to be comfortable in life,--that is to say, not utterly

[030-074]a wretch,--in spite of his love, so had she managed to hold up her

[030-075]head, and live as other young women live, in spite of her misfortune.

[030-076]But as it may be said also that his constancy was true constancy,

[030-077]although he knew how to enjoy the good things of the world, so also

[030-078]had her misfortune been a true misfortune, although she had been able

[030-079]to bear it without much outer show of shipwreck. For a few days,--for

[030-080]a week or two, when the blow first struck her, she had been knocked

[030-081]down, and the friends who were nearest to her had thought that she

[030-082]would never again stand erect upon her feet. But she had been very

[030-083]strong, stout at heart, of a fixed purpose, and capable of resistance

[030-084]against oppression. Even her own mother had been astonished, and

[030-085]sometimes almost dismayed, by the strength of her will. Her mother

[030-086]knew well how it was with her now; but they who saw her frequently,

[030-087]and who did not know her as her mother knew her,--the Mrs. Boyces of

[030-088]her acquaintance,--whispered among themselves that Lily Dale was not

[030-089]so soft of heart as people used to think.

[030-090]On the next day, Christmas Day, as the reader will remember, Grace

[030-091]Crawley was taken up to dine at the big house with the old squire.

[030-092]Mrs. Dale's eldest daughter, with her husband, Dr. Crofts, was to be

[030-093]there; and also Lily's old friend, who was also especially the old

[030-094]friend of Johnny Eames, Lady Julia De Guest. Grace had endeavoured to

[030-095]be excused from the party, pleading many pleas. But the upshot of all

[030-096]her pleas was this,--that while her father's position was so painful

[030-097]she ought not to go out anywhere. In answer to this, Lily Dale,

[030-098]corroborated by her mother, assured her that for her father's sake

[030-099]she ought not to exhibit any such feeling; that in doing so, she

[030-100]would seem to express a doubt as to her father's innocence. Then she

[030-101]allowed herself to be persuaded, telling her friend, however, that

[030-102]she knew the day would be very miserable to her. "It will be very

[030-103]humdrum, if you please," said Lily. "Nothing can be more humdrum than

[030-104]Christmas at the Great House. Nevertheless, you must go."

[030-105]Coming out of church, Grace was introduced to the old squire. He

[030-106]was a thin, old man, with grey hair, and the smallest possible grey

[030-107]whiskers, with a dry, solemn face; not carrying in his outward gait

[030-108]much of the customary jollity of Christmas. He took his hat off to

[030-109]Grace, and said some word to her as to hoping to have the pleasure

[030-110]of seeing her at dinner. It sounded very cold to her, and she became

[030-111]at once afraid of him. "I wish I was not going," she said to Lily,

[030-112]again. "I know he thinks I ought not to go. I shall be so thankful if

[030-113]you will but let me stay."

[Illustration: Grace Crawley is introduced to Squire Dale.]

[030-114]"Don't be foolish, Grace. It all comes from your not knowing him, or

[030-115]understanding him. And how should you understand him? I give you my

[030-116]word that I would tell you if I did not know that he wishes you to

[030-117]go."

[030-118]She had to go. "Of course I haven't a dress fit. How should I?" she

[030-119]said to Lily. "How wrong it is of me to put myself up to such a thing

[030-120]as this."

[030-121]"Your dress is beautiful, child. We are none of us going in evening

[030-122]dresses. Pray believe that I will not make you do wrong. If you won't

[030-123]trust me, can't you trust mamma?"

[030-124]Of course she went. When the three ladies entered the drawing-room of

[030-125]the Great House they found that Lady Julia had arrived just before

[030-126]them. Lady Julia immediately took hold of Lily, and led her apart,

[030-127]having a word or two to say about the clerk in the Income-tax Office.

[030-128]I am not sure but what the dear old woman sometimes said a few more

[030-129]words than were expedient, with a view to the object which she had so

[030-130]closely at heart. "John is to be with us the first week in February,"

[030-131]she said. "I suppose you'll see him before that, as he'll probably be

[030-132]with his mother a few days before he comes to me."

[030-133]"I daresay we shall see him quite in time, Lady Julia," said Lily.

[030-134]"Now, Lily, don't be ill-natured."

[030-135]"I'm the most good-natured young woman alive, Lady Julia, and as for

[030-136]Johnny, he is always made as welcome at the Small House as violets

[030-137]in March. Mamma purrs about him when he comes, asking all manner of

[030-138]flattering questions as though he were a cabinet minister at least,

[030-139]and I always admire some little knicknack that he has got, a new

[030-140]ring, or a stud, or a button. There isn't another man in all the

[030-141]world whose buttons I'd look at."

[030-142]"It isn't his buttons, Lily."

[030-143]"Ah, that's just it. I can go as far as his buttons. But come, Lady

[030-144]Julia, this is Christmas-time, and Christmas should be a holiday."

[030-145]In the meantime Mrs. Dale was occupied with her married daughter and

[030-146]her son-in-law, and the squire had attached himself to poor Grace.

[030-147]"You have never been in this part of the country before, Miss

[030-148]Crawley," he said.

[030-149]"No, sir."

[030-150]"It is rather pretty just about here, and Guestwick Manor is a fine

[030-151]place in its way, but we have not so much natural beauty as you have

[030-152]in Barsetshire. Chaldicote Chase is, I think, as pretty as anything

[030-153]in England."

[030-154]"I never saw Chaldicote Chase, sir. It isn't pretty at all at

[030-155]Hogglestock, where we live."

[030-156]"Ah, I forgot. No; it is not very pretty at Hogglestock. That's where

[030-157]the bricks come from."

[030-158]"Papa is clergyman at Hogglestock."

[030-159]"Yes, yes; I remember. Your father is a great scholar. I have often

[030-160]heard of him. I am so sorry he should be distressed by this charge

[030-161]they have made. But it will all come right at the assizes. They

[030-162]always get at the truth there. I used to be intimate with a clergyman

[030-163]in Barsetshire of the name of Grantly;"--Grace felt that her ears

[030-164]were tingling, and that her face was red;--"Archdeacon Grantly. His

[030-165]father was bishop of the diocese."

[030-166]"Yes, sir. Archdeacon Grantly lives at Plumstead."

[030-167]"I was staying once with an old friend of mine, Mr. Thorne of

[030-168]Ullathorne, who lives close to Plumstead, and saw a good deal of

[030-169]them. I remember thinking Henry Grantly was a very nice lad. He

[030-170]married afterwards."

[030-171]"Yes, sir; but his wife is dead now, and he has got a little

[030-172]girl,--Edith Grantly."

[030-173]"Is there no other child?"

[030-174]"No, sir; only Edith."

[030-175]"You know him, then?"

[030-176]"Yes, sir; I know Major Grantly,--and Edith. I never saw Archdeacon

[030-177]Grantly."

[030-178]"Then, my dear, you never saw a very famous pillar of the church.

[030-179]I remember when people used to talk a great deal about Archdeacon

[030-180]Grantly; but when his time came to be made a bishop, he was not

[030-181]sufficiently new-fangled; and so he got passed by. He is much better

[030-182]off as he is, I should say. Bishops have to work very hard, my dear."
