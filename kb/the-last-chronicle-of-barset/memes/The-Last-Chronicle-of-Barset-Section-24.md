[024-001]"It is illegal," said Mr. Crawley, speaking somewhat louder than

[024-002]before, "and will be absolutely futile. As you pleaded to me that you

[024-003]yourself and your own personal convenience were concerned in this

[024-004]matter, I have made known my intentions to you, which otherwise I

[024-005]should have made known only to the bishop. If you please, we will

[024-006]discuss the subject no further."

[024-007]"Am I to understand, Mr. Crawley, that you refuse to obey the

[024-008]bishop?"

[024-009]"The bishop has written to me, sir; and I will make known my

[024-010]intention to the bishop by a written answer. As you have been the

[024-011]bearer of the bishop's letter to me, I am bound to ask you whether

[024-012]I shall be indebted to you for carrying back my reply, or whether

[024-013]I shall send it by course of post?" Mr. Thumble considered for a

[024-014]moment, and then made up his mind that he had better wait, and carry

[024-015]back the epistle. This was Friday, and the letter could not be

[024-016]delivered by post till the Saturday morning. Mrs. Proudie might be

[024-017]angry with him if he should be the cause of loss of time. He did

[024-018]not, however, at all like waiting, having perceived that Mr. Crawley,

[024-019]though with language courteously worded, had spoken of him as a mere

[024-020]messenger.

[024-021]"I think," he said, "that I may, perhaps, best further the object

[024-022]which we must all have in view, that namely of providing properly

[024-023]for the Sunday services of the church of Hogglestock, by taking your

[024-024]reply personally to the bishop."

[024-025]"That provision is my care and need trouble no one else," said

[024-026]Mr. Crawley, in a loud voice. Then, before seating himself at his

[024-027]old desk, he stood awhile, pondering, with his back turned to his

[024-028]visitor. "I have to ask your pardon, sir," said he, looking round

[024-029]for a moment, "because, by reason of the extreme poverty of this

[024-030]house, my wife is unable to offer to you that hospitality which is

[024-031]especially due from one clergyman to another."

[024-032]"Oh, don't mention it," said Mr. Thumble.

[024-033]"If you will allow me, sir, I would prefer that it should be

[024-034]mentioned." Then he seated himself at his desk, and commenced his

[024-035]letter.

[024-036]Mr. Thumble felt himself to be awkwardly placed. Had there been no

[024-037]third person in the room he could have sat down in Mr. Crawley's

[024-038]arm-chair, and waited patiently till the letter should be finished.

[024-039]But Mrs. Crawley was there, and of course he was bound to speak to

[024-040]her. In what strain could he do so? Even he, little as he was given

[024-041]to indulge in sentiment, had been touched by the man's appeal to his

[024-042]own poverty, and he felt, moreover, that Mrs. Crawley must have been

[024-043]deeply moved by her husband's position with reference to the bishop's

[024-044]order. It was quite out of the question that he should speak of that,

[024-045]as Mr. Crawley would, he was well aware, immediately turn upon him.

[024-046]At last he thought of a subject, and spoke with a voice intended to

[024-047]be pleasant. "That was the school-house I passed, probably, just as I

[024-048]came here?" Mrs. Crawley told him that it was the school-house. "Ah,

[024-049]yes, I thought so. Have you a certified teacher here?" Mrs. Crawley

[024-050]explained that no Government aid had ever reached Hogglestock.

[024-051]Besides themselves, they had only a young woman whom they themselves

[024-052]had instructed. "Ah, that is a pity," said Mr. Thumble.

[024-053]"I,--I am the certified teacher," said Mr. Crawley, turning round

[024-054]upon him from his chair.

[024-055]"Oh, ah, yes," said Mr. Thumble; and after that Mr. Thumble asked

[024-056]no more questions about the Hogglestock school. Soon afterwards Mrs.

[024-057]Crawley left the room, seeing the difficulty under which Mr. Thumble

[024-058]was labouring, and feeling sure that her presence would not now be

[024-059]necessary. Mr. Crawley's letter was written quickly, though every now

[024-060]and then he would sit for a moment with his pen poised in the air,

[024-061]searching his memory for a word. But the words came to him easily,

[024-062]and before an hour was over he had handed his letter to Mr. Thumble.

[024-063]The letter was as follows:--

[024-064]The Parsonage, Hogglestock, Dec. 186--.

[024-065]RIGHT REVEREND LORD,

[024-066]I have received the letter of yesterday's date which your

[024-067]lordship has done me the honour of sending to me by the

[024-068]hands of the Reverend Mr. Thumble, and I avail myself of

[024-069]that gentleman's kindness to return to you an answer by

[024-070]the same means, moved thus to use his patience chiefly

[024-071]by the consideration that in this way my reply to your

[024-072]lordship's injunctions may be in your hands with less

[024-073]delay than would attend the regular course of the

[024-074]mail-post.

[024-075]It is with deep regret that I feel myself constrained to

[024-076]inform your lordship that I cannot obey the command which

[024-077]you have laid upon me with reference to the services of my

[024-078]church in this parish. I cannot permit Mr. Thumble, or any

[024-079]other delegate from your lordship, to usurp my place in my

[024-080]pulpit. I would not have you to think, if I can possibly

[024-081]dispel such thoughts from your mind, that I disregard your

[024-082]high office, or that I am deficient in that respectful

[024-083]obedience to the bishop set over me, which is due to the

[024-084]authority of the Crown as the head of the church in these

[024-085]realms; but in this, as in all questions of obedience, he

[024-086]who is required to obey must examine the extent of the

[024-087]authority exercised by him who demands obedience. Your

[024-088]lordship might possibly call upon me, using your voice as

[024-089]bishop of the diocese, to abandon altogether the freehold

[024-090]rights which are now mine in this perpetual curacy. The

[024-091]judge of assize, before whom I shall soon stand for my

[024-092]trial, might command me to retire to prison without a

[024-093]verdict given by the jury. The magistrates who committed

[024-094]me so lately as yesterday, upon whose decision in that

[024-095]respect your lordship has taken action against me so

[024-096]quickly, might have equally strained their authority. But

[024-097]in no case, in this land, is he that is subject bound

[024-098]to obey, further than where the law gives authority and

[024-099]exacts obedience. It is not in the power of the Crown

[024-100]itself to inhibit me from the performance of my ordinary

[024-101]duties in this parish by any such missive as that sent to

[024-102]me by your lordship. If your lordship think it right to

[024-103]stop my mouth as a clergyman in your diocese, you must

[024-104]proceed to do so in an ecclesiastical court in accordance

[024-105]with the laws, and will succeed in your object, or fail,

[024-106]in accordance with the evidences as to ministerial fitness

[024-107]or unfitness, which may be produced respecting me before

[024-108]the proper tribunal.

[024-109]I will allow that much attention is due from a clergyman

[024-110]to pastoral advice given to him by his bishop. On that

[024-111]head I must first express to your lordship my full

[024-112]understanding that your letter has not been intended to

[024-113]convey advice, but an order;--an inhibition, as your

[024-114]messenger, the Reverend Mr. Thumble, has expressed it.

[024-115]There might be a case certainly in which I should submit

[024-116]myself to counsel, though I should resist command. No

[024-117]counsel, however, has been given,--except indeed that I

[024-118]should receive your messenger in a proper spirit, which

[024-119]I hope I have done. No other advice has been given me,

[024-120]and therefore there is now no such case as that I have

[024-121]imagined. But in this matter, my lord, I could not have

[024-122]accepted advice from living man, no, not though the

[024-123]hands of the apostles themselves had made him bishop

[024-124]who tendered it to me, and had set him over me for my

[024-125]guidance. I am in a terrible strait. Trouble, and sorrow,

[024-126]and danger are upon me and mine. It may well be, as your

[024-127]lordship says, that the bitter waters of the present

[024-128]hour may pass over my head and destroy me. I thank

[024-129]your lordship for telling me whither I am to look for

[024-130]assistance. Truly I know not whether there is any to be

[024-131]found for me on earth. But the deeper my troubles, the

[024-132]greater my sorrow, the more pressing my danger, the

[024-133]stronger is my need that I should carry myself in these

[024-134]days with that outward respect of self which will teach

[024-135]those around me to know that, let who will condemn me, I

[024-136]have not condemned myself. Were I to abandon my pulpit,

[024-137]unless forced to do so by legal means, I should in doing

[024-138]so be putting a plea of guilty against myself upon the

[024-139]record. This, my lord, I will not do.

[024-140]I have the honour to be, my lord,

[024-141]Your lordship's most obedient servant,

[024-142]JOSIAH CRAWLEY.

[024-143]When he had finished writing his letter he read it over slowly, and

[024-144]then handed it to Mr. Thumble. The act of writing, and the current of

[024-145]the thoughts through his brain, and the feeling that in every word

[024-146]written he was getting the better of the bishop,--all this joined to

[024-147]a certain manly delight in warfare against authority, lighted up the

[024-148]man's face and gave to his eyes an expression which had been long

[024-149]wanting to them. His wife at that moment came into the room and he

[024-150]looked at her with an air of triumph as he handed the letter to Mr.

[024-151]Thumble. "If you will give that to his lordship with an assurance

[024-152]of my duty to his lordship in all things proper, I will thank you

[024-153]kindly, craving your pardon for the great delay to which you have

[024-154]been subjected."

[024-155]"As to the delay, that is nothing," said Mr. Thumble.

[024-156]"It has been much; but you as a clergyman will feel that it has been

[024-157]incumbent on me to speak my mind fully."

[024-158]"Oh, yes; of course." Mr. Crawley was standing up, as also was Mrs.

[024-159]Crawley. It was evident to Mr. Thumble that they both expected that

[024-160]he should go. But he had been specially enjoined to be firm, and he

[024-161]doubted whether hitherto he had been firm enough. As far as this

[024-162]morning's work had as yet gone, it seemed to him that Mr. Crawley had

[024-163]had the play all to himself, and that he, Mr. Thumble, had not had

[024-164]his innings. He, from the palace, had been, as it were, cowed by this

[024-165]man, who had been forced to plead his own poverty. It was certainly

[024-166]incumbent upon him, before he went, to speak up, not only for the

[024-167]bishop, but for himself also. "Mr. Crawley," he said, "hitherto I

[024-168]have listened to you patiently."

[024-169]"Nay," said Mr. Crawley, smiling, "you have indeed been patient, and

[024-170]I thank you; but my words have been written, not spoken."

[024-171]"You have told me that you intend to disobey the bishop's

[024-172]inhibition."

[024-173]"I have told the bishop so certainly."

[024-174]"May I ask you now to listen to me for a few minutes?"

[024-175]Mr. Crawley, still smiling, still having in his eyes the unwonted

[024-176]triumph which had lighted them up, paused a moment, and then answered

[024-177]him. "Reverend sir, you must excuse me if I say no,--not on this

[024-178]subject."

[024-179]"You will not let me speak?"

[024-180]"No; not on this matter, which is very private to me. What should

[024-181]you think if I went into your house and inquired of you as to those

[024-182]things which were particularly near to you?"

[024-183]"But the bishop sent me."

[024-184]"Though ten bishops had sent me,--a council of archbishops if you

[024-185]will!" Mr. Thumble started back, appalled at the energy of the words

[024-186]used to him. "Shall a man have nothing of his own;--no sorrow in his

[024-187]heart, no care in his family, no thought in his breast so private and

[024-188]special to him, but that, if he happen to be a clergyman, the bishop

[024-189]may touch it with his thumb?"

[024-190]"I am not the bishop's thumb," said Mr. Thumble, drawing himself up.

[024-191]"I intended not to hint anything personally objectionable to

[024-192]yourself. I will regard you as one of the angels of the church." Mr.

[024-193]Thumble, when he heard this, began to be sure that Mr. Crawley was

[024-194]mad; he knew of no angels that could ride about the Barsetshire lanes

[024-195]on grey ponies. "And as such I will respect you; but I cannot discuss

[024-196]with you the matter of the bishop's message."
