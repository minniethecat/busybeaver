[078-001]"He must know that best himself, Grace;--but what did he say more to

[078-002]you?"

[078-003]"I don't think he said anything more."

[078-004]"He just left you then?"

[078-005]"He said one thing more."

[078-006]"And what was that?"

[078-007]"He said;--but he had no right to say it."

[078-008]"What was it, dear?"

[078-009]"That he knew I loved him, and that therefore-- But, mamma, do not

[078-010]think of that. I will never be his wife,--never, in opposition to his

[078-011]family."

[078-012]"But he did not take your answer?"

[078-013]"He must take it, mamma. He shall take it. If he can be stubborn, so

[078-014]can I. If he knows how to think of me more than himself, I can think

[078-015]of him and Edith more than of myself. That is not quite all, mamma.

[078-016]Then he wrote to me. There is his letter."

[078-017]Mrs. Crawley read the letter. "I suppose you answered it?"

[078-018]"Yes, I answered it. It was very bad, my letter. I should think after

[078-019]that he will never want to have anything more to say to me. I tried

[078-020]for two days, but I could not write a nice letter."

[078-021]"But what did you say?"

[078-022]"I don't in the least remember. It does not in the least signify now,

[078-023]but it was such a bad letter."

[078-024]"I daresay it was very nice."

[078-025]"It was terribly stiff, and all about a gentleman."

[078-026]"All about a gentleman! What do you mean, my dear?"

[078-027]"Gentleman is such a frightful word to have to use to a gentleman;

[078-028]but I did not know what else to say. Mamma, if you please, we won't

[078-029]talk about it;--not about the letter I mean. As for him, I'll

[078-030]talk about him for ever if you like it. I don't mean to be a bit

[078-031]broken-hearted."

[078-032]"It seems to me that he is a gentleman."

[078-033]"Yes, mamma, that he is; and it is that which makes me so proud.

[078-034]When I think of it, I can hardly hold myself. But now I've told you

[078-035]everything, and I'll go away, and go to bed."

[078-036]CHAPTER XLII.

[078-037]MR. TOOGOOD TRAVELS PROFESSIONALLY.

[078-038][Illustration]

[078-039]Mr. Toogood paid another visit to Barsetshire, in order that he might

[078-040]get a little further information which he thought would be necessary

[078-041]before despatching his nephew upon the traces of Dean Arabin and

[078-042]his wife. He went down to Barchester after his work was over by

[078-043]an evening train, and put himself up at "The Dragon of Wantly,"

[078-044]intending to have the whole of the next day for his work. Mr. Walker

[078-045]had asked him to come and take a return pot-luck dinner with Mrs.

[078-046]Walker at Silverbridge; and this he had said that he would do. After

[078-047]having "rummaged about for tidings" in Barchester, as he called it,

[078-048]he would take the train for Silverbridge, and would get back to

[078-049]town in time for business on the third day. "One day won't be much,

[078-050]you know," he said to his partner, as he made half an apology for

[078-051]absenting himself on business which was not to be in any degree

[078-052]remunerative. "That sort of thing is very well when one does it

[078-053]without any expense," said Crump. "So it is," said Toogood; "and the

[078-054]expense won't make it any worse." He had made up his mind, and it was

[078-055]not probable that anything Mr. Crump might say would deter him.

[078-056]He saw John Eames before he started. "You'll be ready this day week,

[078-057]will you?" John Eames promised that he would. "It will cost you some

[078-058]forty pounds, I should say. By George,--if you have to go on to

[078-059]Jerusalem, it will cost you more." In answer to this, Johnny pleaded

[078-060]that it would be as good as any other tour to him. He would see the

[078-061]world. "I'll tell you what," said Toogood; "I'll pay half. Only you

[078-062]mustn't tell Crump. And it will be quite as well not to tell Maria."

[078-063]But Johnny would hear nothing of this scheme. He would pay the entire

[078-064]cost of his own journey. He had lots of money, he said, and would

[078-065]like nothing better. "Then I'll run down," said Toogood, "and rummage

[078-066]up what tidings I can. As for writing to the dean, what's the good of

[078-067]writing to a man when you don't know where he is? Business letters

[078-068]always lie at hotels for two months, and then come back with double

[078-069]postage. From all I can hear, you'll stumble on her before you find

[078-070]him. If we do nothing else but bring him back, it will be a great

[078-071]thing to have the support of such a friend in the court. A Barchester

[078-072]jury won't like to find a man guilty who is hand-and-glove with the

[078-073]dean."

[078-074]Mr. Toogood reached the "Dragon" about eleven o'clock, and allowed

[078-075]the boots to give him a pair of slippers and a candlestick. But

[078-076]he would not go to bed just at that moment. He would go into the

[078-077]coffee-room first, and have a glass of hot brandy-and-water. So the

[078-078]hot brandy-and-water was brought to him, and a cigar, and as he

[078-079]smoked and drank he conversed with the waiter. The man was a waiter

[078-080]of the ancient class, a gray-haired waiter, with seedy clothes, and

[078-081]a dirty towel under his arm; not a dapper waiter, with black shiny

[078-082]hair, and dressed like a guest for a dinner-party. There are two

[078-083]distinct classes of waiters, and as far as I have been able to

[078-084]perceive, the special status of the waiter in question cannot be

[078-085]decided by observation of the class of waiter to which he belongs. In

[078-086]such a town as Barchester you may find the old waiter with the dirty

[078-087]towel in the head inn, or in the second-class inn, and so you may

[078-088]the dapper waiter. Or you may find both in each, and not know which

[078-089]is senior waiter and which junior waiter. But for service I always

[078-090]prefer the old waiter with the dirty towel, and I find it more easy

[078-091]to satisfy him in the matter of sixpences when my relations with the

[078-092]inn come to an end.

[078-093]"Have you been here long, John?" said Mr. Toogood.

[078-094]"A goodish many years, sir."

[078-095]"So I thought, by the look of you. One can see that you belong in a

[078-096]way to the place. You do a good deal of business here, I suppose, at

[078-097]this time of the year?"

[078-098]"Well, sir, pretty fair. The house ain't what it used to be, sir."

[078-099]"Times are bad at Barchester,--are they?"

[078-100]"I don't know much about the times. It's the people is worse than the

[078-101]times, I think. They used to like to have a little bit of dinner now

[078-102]and again at a hotel;--and a drop of something to drink after it."

[078-103]"And don't they like it now?"

[078-104]"I think they like it well enough, but they don't do it. I suppose

[078-105]it's their wives as don't let 'em come out and enjoy theirselves.

[078-106]There used to be the Goose and Glee club;--that was once a month.

[078-107]They've gone and clean done away with themselves,--that club has.

[078-108]There's old Bumpter in the High Street,--he's the last of the old

[078-109]Geese. They died off, you see, and when Mr. Biddle died they wouldn't

[078-110]choose another president. A club for having dinner, sir, ain't

[078-111]nothing without a president."

[078-112]"I suppose not."

[078-113]"And there's the Freemasons. They must meet, you know, sir, in

[078-114]course, because of the dooties. But if you'll believe me, sir, they

[078-115]don't so much as wet their whistles. They don't indeed. It always

[078-116]used to be a supper, and that was once a month. Now they pays a

[078-117]rent for the use of the room! Who is to get a living out of that,

[078-118]sir?--not in the way of a waiter, that is."

[078-119]"If that's the way things are going on I suppose the servants leave

[078-120]their places pretty often?"

[078-121]"I don't know about that, sir. A man may do a deal worse than 'The

[078-122]Dragon of Wantly.' Them as goes away to better themselves, often

[078-123]worses themselves, as I call it. I've seen a good deal of that."

[078-124]"And you stick to the old shop?"

[078-125]"Yes, sir; I've been here fifteen year, I think it is. There's a many

[078-126]goes away, as doesn't go out of their own heads, you know, sir."

[078-127]"They get the sack, you mean?"

[078-128]"There's words between them and master,--or more likely, missus.

[078-129]That's where it is. Servants is so foolish. I often tell 'em how

[078-130]wrong folks are to say that soft words butter no parsnips, and hard

[078-131]words break no bones."

[078-132]"I think you've lost some of the old hands here since this time last

[078-133]year, John?"

[078-134]"You knows the house then, sir?"

[078-135]"Well;--I've been here before."

[078-136]"There was four of them went, I think it's just about twelve months

[078-137]back, sir."

[078-138]"There was a man in the yard I used to know, and last time I was down

[078-139]here, I found that he was gone."

[078-140]"There was one of 'em out of the yard, and two out of the house.

[078-141]Master and them had got to very high words. There was poor Scuttle,

[078-142]who had been post-boy at 'The Compasses' before he came here."

[078-143]"He went away to New Zealand, didn't he?"

[078-144]"B'leve he did, sir; or to some foreign parts. And Anne, as was

[078-145]under-chambermaid here; she went with him, fool as she was. They got

[078-146]theirselves married and went off, and he was well nigh as old as me.

[078-147]But seems he'd saved a little money, and that goes a long way with

[078-148]any girl."

[078-149]"Was he the man who drove Mr. Soames that day the cheque was lost?"

[078-150]Mr. Toogood asked this question perhaps a little too abruptly. At any

[078-151]rate he obtained no answer to it. The waiter said he knew nothing

[078-152]about Mr. Soames, or the cheque, and the lawyer suspecting that the

[078-153]waiter was suspecting him, finished his brandy-and-water and went to

[078-154]bed.

[Illustration: Mr. Toogood and the old Waiter.]

[078-155]Early on the following morning he observed that he was specially

[078-156]regarded by a shabby-looking man, dressed in black, but in a black

[078-157]suit that was very old, with a red nose, whom he had seen in the

[078-158]hotel on the preceding day; and he learned that this man was a cousin

[078-159]of the landlord,--one Dan Stringer,--who acted as a clerk in the

[078-160]hotel bar. He took an opportunity also of saying a word to Mr.

[078-161]Stringer the landlord,--whom he found to be a somewhat forlorn and

[078-162]gouty individual, seated on cushions in a little parlour behind the

[078-163]bar. After breakfast he went out, and having twice walked round the

[078-164]Cathedral close and inspected the front of the palace and looked up

[078-165]at the windows of the prebendaries' houses, he knocked at the door of

[078-166]the deanery. The dean and Mrs. Arabin were on the Continent, he was

[078-167]told. Then he asked for Mr. Harding, having learned that Mr. Harding

[078-168]was Mrs. Arabin's father, and that he lived at the deanery. Mr.

[078-169]Harding was at home, but was not very well, the servant said. Mr.

[078-170]Toogood, however, persevered, sending up his card, and saying that he

[078-171]wished to have a few minutes' conversation with Mr. Harding on very

[078-172]particular business. He wrote a word upon his card before giving it

[078-173]to the servant,--"about Mr. Crawley." In a few minutes he was shown

[078-174]into the library, and had hardly time, while looking at the shelves,

[078-175]to remember what Mr. Crawley had said of his anger at the beautiful

[078-176]bindings, before an old man, very thin and very pale, shuffled into

[078-177]the room. He stooped a good deal, and his black clothes were very

[078-178]loose about his shrunken limbs. He was not decrepit, nor did he seem

[078-179]to be one who had advanced to extreme old age; but yet he shuffled

[078-180]rather than walked, hardly raising his feet from the ground. Mr.

[078-181]Toogood, as he came forward to meet him, thought that he had never

[078-182]seen a sweeter face. There was very much of melancholy in it, of that

[078-183]soft sadness of age which seems to acknowledge, and in some sort to

[078-184]regret, the waning oil of life; but the regret to be read in such

[078-185]faces has in it nothing of the bitterness of grief; there is no

[078-186]repining that the end has come, but simply a touch of sorrow that so

[078-187]much that is dear must be left behind. Mr. Harding shook hands with

[078-188]his visitor, and invited him to sit down, and then seated himself,

[078-189]folding his hands together over his knees, and he said a few words in

[078-190]a very low voice as to the absence of his daughter and of the dean.
