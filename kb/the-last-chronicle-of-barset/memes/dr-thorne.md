## Character

Dr. Thorne of Chaldicotes is a figure of quiet authority in Barsetshire society — a country doctor who has risen to the status of gentleman through character and competence rather than birth. He appears in *The Last Chronicle of Barset* chiefly as one of the magistrates at the preliminary hearing of Josiah Crawley's case, where he sits alongside Dr. Tempest and others. "Dr. Thorne was the husband of Mrs. Thorne" (005-046), and his marriage into the gentry reflects his social ascent. He is a man whose medical training has given him a practical, humane outlook — he judges facts rather than reputations, and his presence on the bench represents the kind of fair-minded local justice that Trollope admired.

## Biography

Dr. Thorne is the hero of his own novel (*Doctor Thorne*, the third in the Barsetshire series), and by the time of *The Last Chronicle of Barset* he has long been established as a respected member of the county community. Living at Chaldicotes with his wife, he moves easily between the professional and gentry classes. As "Dr. Thorne, of Chaldicotes, a gentleman" (015-057), he is described at the magistrates' hearing, where he sits at the professional boundary between the clerical magistrates (Dr. Tempest) and the lay gentry. His role in the Crawley hearing is limited but significant — his presence signals that the case is being judged by men of sense and experience, not by those swayed by rumour or episcopal pressure.

## Significance

Dr. Thorne's presence in *The Last Chronicle of Barset* is one of many threads that bind this final novel to its predecessors. As the hero of an earlier book now appearing in a supporting role, he embodies the continuity of the Barsetshire world. His position as a magistrate who is neither clergyman nor lawyer — a doctor, trained to observe and diagnose — makes him a symbol of the rational, humane judgment that Trollope sets against the rigid legalism of the cheque case and the political manoeuvring of the ecclesiastical inquiry. He says little in the novel, but his presence on the bench is itself a statement: justice in Barsetshire is administered by men who know the community and its people.
