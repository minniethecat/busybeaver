[073-001]"What's up now, Johnny? Some good fortune?"

[073-002]"Good fortune; no. I never have good fortunes of that kind. But I've

[073-003]got hold of a young woman,--or rather a young woman has got hold of

[073-004]me, who insists on having a mystery with me. In the mystery itself

[073-005]there is not the slightest interest. But the mysteriousness of

[073-006]it is charming. I have just written to her three words to settle

[073-007]an appointment for to-morrow. We don't sign our names lest the

[073-008]Postmaster-General should find out all about it."

[073-009]"Is she pretty?"

[073-010]"Well;--she isn't ugly. She has just enough of good looks to make the

[073-011]sort of thing pass off pleasantly. A mystery with a downright ugly

[073-012]young woman would be unpleasant."

[073-013]After this fashion the note from Miss Demolines had been received,

[073-014]and answered at once, but the other letters remained in his pocket

[073-015]till he reached his office on the following morning. Sir Raffle had

[073-016]begged him to be there at half-past nine. This he had sworn he would

[073-017]not do; but he did seat himself in his room at ten minutes before

[073-018]ten, finding of course the whole building untenanted at that early

[073-019]hour,--that unearthly hour, as Johnny called it himself. "I shouldn't

[073-020]wonder if he really is here this morning," Johnny said, as he entered

[073-021]the building, "just that he may have an opportunity of jumping on

[073-022]me." But Sir Raffle was not there, and then Johnny began to abuse Sir

[073-023]Raffle. "If ever I come here early to meet him again, because he says

[073-024]he means to be here himself, I hope I may be ---- blessed." On that

[073-025]especial morning it was twelve before Sir Raffle made his appearance,

[073-026]and Johnny avenged himself,--I regret to have to tell it,--by a fib.

[073-027]That Sir Raffle fibbed first, was no valid excuse whatever for Eames.

[073-028]"I've been at it ever since six o'clock," said Sir Raffle.

[073-029]"At what?" said Johnny.

[073-030]"Work, to be sure;--and very hard work too. I believe the Chancellor

[073-031]of the Exchequer thinks that he can call upon me to any extent that

[073-032]he pleases;--just any extent that he pleases. He doesn't give me

[073-033]credit for a desire to have a single hour to myself."

[073-034]"What would he do, Sir Raffle, if you were to get ill, or wear

[073-035]yourself out?"

[073-036]"He knows I'm not one of the wearing-out sort. You got my note last

[073-037]night?"

[073-038]"Yes; I got your note."

[073-039]"I'm sorry that I troubled you; but I couldn't help it. I didn't

[073-040]expect to get a box full of papers at eleven o'clock last night."

[073-041]"You didn't put me out, Sir Raffle; I happened to have business of my

[073-042]own which prevented the possibility of my being here early."

[073-043]This was the way in which John Eames avenged himself. Sir Raffle

[073-044]turned his face upon his private secretary, and his face was very

[073-045]black. Johnny bore the gaze without dropping an eyelid. "I'm not

[073-046]going to stand it, and he may as well know that at once," Johnny said

[073-047]to one of his friends in the office afterwards. "If he ever wants any

[073-048]thing really done, I'll do it;--though it should take me twelve hours

[073-049]at a stretch. But I'm not going to pretend to believe all the lies

[073-050]he tells me about the Chancellor of the Exchequer. If that is to be

[073-051]part of the private secretary's business, he had better get somebody

[073-052]else." But now Sir Raffle was very angry, and his countenance was

[073-053]full of wrath as he looked down upon his subordinate minister. "If

[073-054]I had come here, Mr. Eames, and had found you absent, I should

[073-055]have been very much annoyed, very much annoyed indeed, after having

[073-056]written as I did."

[073-057]"You would have found me absent at the hour you named. As I wasn't

[073-058]here then, I think it's only fair to say so."

[073-059]"I'm afraid you begrudge your time to the service, Mr. Eames."

[073-060]"I do begrudge it when the service doesn't want it."

[073-061]"At your age, Mr. Eames, that's not for you to judge. If I had acted

[073-062]in that way when I was young I should never have filled the position

[073-063]I now hold. I always remembered in those days that as I was the hand

[073-064]and not the head, I was bound to hold myself in readiness whether

[073-065]work might be required from me or not."

[073-066]"If I'm wanted as hand now, Sir Raffle, I'm ready."

[073-067]"That's all very well;--but why were you not here at the hour I

[073-068]named?"

[073-069]"Well, Sir Raffle, I cannot say that the Chancellor of the Exchequer

[073-070]detained me;--but there was business. As I've been here for the last

[073-071]two hours, I am happy to think that in this instance the public

[073-072]service will not have suffered from my disobedience."

[073-073]Sir Raffle was still standing with his hat on, and with his back to

[073-074]the fire, and his countenance was full of wrath. It was on his tongue

[073-075]to tell Johnny that he had better return to his former work in the

[073-076]outer office. He greatly wanted the comfort of a private secretary

[073-077]who would believe in him--or at least pretend to believe in him.

[073-078]There are men who, though they have not sense enough to be true,

[073-079]have nevertheless sense enough to know that they cannot expect to be

[073-080]really believed in by those who are near enough to them to know them.

[073-081]Sir Raffle Buffle was such a one. He would have greatly delighted

[073-082]in the services of some one who would trust him implicitly,--of

[073-083]some young man who would really believe all that he said of himself

[073-084]and of the Chancellor of the Exchequer; but he was wise enough to

[073-085]perceive that no such young man was to be had; or that any such young

[073-086]man,--could such a one be found,--would be absolutely useless for any

[073-087]purposes of work. He knew himself to be a liar whom nobody trusted.

[073-088]And he knew himself also to be a bully,--though he could not think so

[073-089]low of himself as to believe that he was a bully whom nobody feared.

[073-090]A private secretary was at the least bound to pretend to believe in

[073-091]him. There is a decency in such things, and that decency John Eames

[073-092]did not observe. He thought that he must get rid of John Eames, in

[073-093]spite of certain attractions which belonged to Johnny's appearance

[073-094]and general manners, and social standing, and reputed wealth. But

[073-095]it would not be wise to punish a man on the spot for breaking an

[073-096]appointment which he himself had not kept, and therefore he would

[073-097]wait for another opportunity. "You had better go to your own room

[073-098]now," he said. "I am engaged on a matter connected with the Treasury,

[073-099]in which I will not ask for your assistance." He knew that Eames

[073-100]would not believe a word as to what he said about the Treasury,--not

[073-101]even some very trifling base of truth which did exist; but the boast

[073-102]gave him an opportunity of putting an end to the interview after his

[073-103]own fashion. Then John Eames went to his own room and answered the

[073-104]letters which he had in his pocket.

[073-105]To the club dinner he would not go. "What's the use of paying two

[073-106]guineas for a dinner with fellows you see every day of your life?"

[073-107]he said. To Lady Glencora's he would go, and he wrote a line to his

[073-108]friend Dalrymple proposing that they should go together. And he would

[073-109]dine with his cousin Toogood in Tavistock Square. "One meets the

[073-110]queerest people in the world there," he said; "but Tommy Toogood is

[073-111]such a good fellow himself!" After that he had his lunch. Then he

[073-112]read the paper, and before he went away he wrote a dozen or two of

[073-113]private notes, presenting Sir Raffle's compliments right and left,

[073-114]and giving in no one note a single word of information that could be

[073-115]of any use to any person. Having thus earned his salary by half-past

[073-116]four o'clock he got into a hansom cab and had himself driven to

[073-117]Porchester Terrace. Miss Demolines was at home, of course, and he

[073-118]soon found himself closeted with that interesting young woman.

[073-119]"I thought you never would have come." These were the first words she

[073-120]spoke.

[073-121]"My dear Miss Demolines, you must not forget that I have my bread to

[073-122]earn."

[073-123]"Fiddlestick--bread! As if I didn't know that you can get away from

[073-124]your office when you choose."

[073-125]"But, indeed, I cannot."

[073-126]"What is there to prevent you, Mr. Eames?"

[073-127]"I'm not tied up like a dog, certainly; but who do you suppose will

[073-128]do my work if I do not do it myself? It is a fact, though the world

[073-129]does not believe it, that men in public offices have got something to

[073-130]do."

[073-131]"Now you are laughing at me, I know; but you are welcome, if you like

[073-132]it. It's the way of the world just at present that ladies should

[073-133]submit to that sort of thing from gentlemen."

[073-134]"What sort of thing, Miss Demolines?"

[073-135]"Chaff,--as you call it. Courtesy is out of fashion, and gallantry

[073-136]has come to signify quite a different kind of thing from what it used

[073-137]to do."

[073-138]"The Sir Charles Grandison business is done and gone. That's what you

[073-139]mean, I suppose? Don't you think we should find it very heavy if we

[073-140]tried to get it back again?"

[073-141]"I'm not going to ask you to be a Sir Charles Grandison, Mr.

[073-142]Eames. But never mind all that now. Do you know that that girl has

[073-143]absolutely had her first sitting for the picture?"

[073-144]"Has she, indeed?"

[073-145]"She has. You may take my word for it. I know it as a fact. What a

[073-146]fool that young man is!"

[073-147]"Which young man?"

[073-148]"Which young man! Conway Dalrymple to be sure. Artists are always

[073-149]weak. Of all men in the world they are the most subject to flattery

[073-150]from women; and we all know that Conway Dalrymple is very vain."

[073-151]"Upon my word I didn't know it," said Johnny.

[073-152]"Yes, you do. You must know it. When a man goes about in a purple

[073-153]velvet coat of course he is vain."

[073-154]"I certainly cannot defend a purple velvet coat."

[073-155]"That is what he wore when this girl sat to him this morning."

[073-156]"This morning was it?"

[073-157]"Yes; this morning. They little think that they can do nothing

[073-158]without my knowing it. He was there for nearly four hours, and she

[073-159]was dressed up in a white robe as Jael, with a turban on her head.

[073-160]Jael, indeed! I call it very improper, and I am quite astonished that

[073-161]Maria Clutterbuck should have lent herself to such a piece of work.

[073-162]That Maria was never very wise, of course we all know; but I thought

[073-163]that she had principle enough to have kept her from this kind of

[073-164]thing."

[073-165]"It's her fevered existence," said Johnny.

[073-166]"That is just it. She must have excitement. It is like dram-drinking.

[073-167]And then, you know, they are always living in the crater of a

[073-168]volcano."

[073-169]"Who are living in the crater of a volcano?"

[073-170]"The Dobbs Broughtons are. Of course they are. There is no saying

[073-171]what day a smash may come. These City people get so used to it that

[073-172]they enjoy it. The risk is every thing to them."

[073-173]"They like to have a little certainty behind the risk, I fancy."

[073-174]"I'm afraid there is very little that's certain with Dobbs Broughton.

[073-175]But about this picture, Mr. Eames. I look to you to assist me there.

[073-176]It must be put a stop to. As to that I am determined. It must be--put

[073-177]a--stop to." And as Miss Demolines repeated these last words with

[073-178]tremendous emphasis she leant with both her elbows on a little table

[073-179]that stood between her and her visitor, and looked with all her eyes

[073-180]into his face. "I do hope that you agree with me in that," said she.
