## Character

Mr. John Toogood is a London solicitor practising at Gray's Inn and a cousin to Mary Crawley. Though he plays no part in the early volumes of the Barsetshire chronicles, in *The Last Chronicle of Barset* he emerges as one of the novel's most decisive agents of justice: the professional man who, against the inertia of provincial suspicion, methodically unravels the case against Josiah Crawley. He is introduced through a family connection — "cousin, Mr. John Toogood, of Gray's Inn" (057-119) — and is described as a man whose livelihood depends upon the trust of his clients: "The Mr. Toogood of those days, who had been Mr. Toogood's father, had been a solicitor of the old school" (057-129).

## Biography

Toogood belongs to the firm of Toogood and Crump — "Crump and Toogood" as it is sometimes styled (057-141) — a solid London legal practice. His professional life is far removed from the ecclesiastical politics of Barsetshire, yet family obligation draws him into the Crawley affair. When the case becomes desperate, the advice given is simple and direct: "If it seems good to you, go to Mr. Toogood. I think that he will be able to do something for you" (058-011). Toogood responds with characteristic professional warmth, writing back immediately to offer his services (058-064). He travels to Barchester and Silverbridge to conduct his own investigation, interviewing witnesses and visiting the scenes connected to the disputed cheque. His appearance is memorably ordinary: "Poor Mr. Toogood, who on ordinary days was a man not much given to hurry" (058-128), yet his professional acumen and persistence prove decisive.

## Professional Character

Toogood represents the ideal of the Victorian professional man — diligent, unpretentious, and morally serious. Unlike the ecclesiastical and aristocratic figures who dominate Barsetshire society, Toogood earns his standing through competence rather than inheritance or preferment. His interview at the palace reveals both his social awkwardness and his professional integrity: when challenged, he responds with the straightforward declaration, "I am Mr. Toogood, the solicitor, and I hope I may not be intruding" (058-141). At moments of tension he can be blunt: "Oh, bother!" said Mr. Toogood, jumping up out of his chair (059-054), revealing a man more comfortable with honest action than with diplomatic restraint.

## Key Actions

1. **Receives the appeal** — Responds to the Crawley family's request for legal assistance, writing back immediately to offer his services (058-064)
2. **Travels to Silverbridge** — Conducts an on-site investigation, interviewing local witnesses including the Silverbridge attorney Mr. Walker and visiting the butcher's shop (001-038–001-039)
3. **Confronts the Bishop's palace** — Attends a meeting at the palace where he defends Crawley's interests against ecclesiastical pressure (058-139–058-141)
4. **Clears Crawley's name** — His professional investigation ultimately establishes that Crawley took the cheque innocently, having received it from Mrs. Arabin without knowledge of its origin

## Significance

Toogood serves a structural function in the novel that exceeds his relatively brief page-time. As a London professional entering provincial Barsetshire, he bridges two social worlds — the metropolitan world of law and commerce, and the ecclesiastical world of the diocese. His successful defence of Crawley demonstrates Trollope's recurring theme that institutional justice, however imperfect, can be made to work when competent professionals apply themselves honestly to a case. He also embodies the Victorian ideal of the self-made professional man, whose authority derives from expertise rather than birth. In a novel deeply concerned with honour and reputation, Toogood represents the possibility of restoring a man's good name through patient, unglamorous work.
