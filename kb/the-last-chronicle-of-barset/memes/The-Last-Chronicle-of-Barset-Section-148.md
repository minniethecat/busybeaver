[148-001]What was Johnny to do, when he left Tavistock Square? His club was

[148-002]open to him. Should he go to his club, play a game of billiards, and

[148-003]have some supper? When he asked himself the question he knew that he

[148-004]would not go to his club, and yet he pretended to doubt about it, as

[148-005]he made his way to a cabstand in Tottenham Court Road. It would be

[148-006]slow, he told himself, to go to his club. He would have gone to see

[148-007]Lily Dale, only that his intimacy with Mrs. Thorne was not sufficient

[148-008]to justify his calling at her house between nine and ten o'clock at

[148-009]night. But, as he must go somewhere,--and as his intimacy with Lady

[148-010]Demolines was, he thought, sufficient to justify almost anything,--he

[148-011]would go to Bayswater. I regret to say that he had written a

[148-012]mysterious note from Paris to Madalina Demolines, saying that he

[148-013]should be in London on this very night, and that it was just on the

[148-014]cards that he might make his way up to Porchester Terrace before

[148-015]he went to bed. The note was mysterious, because it had neither

[148-016]beginning nor ending. It did not contain even initials. It was

[148-017]written like a telegraph message, and was about as long. It was the

[148-018]kind of thing Miss Demolines liked, Johnny thought; and there could

[148-019]be no reason why he should not gratify her. It was her favourite

[148-020]game. Some people like whist, some like croquet, and some like

[148-021]intrigue. Madalina would probably have called it romance,--because by

[148-022]nature she was romantic. John, who was made of sterner stuff, laughed

[148-023]at this. He knew that there was no romance in it. He knew that he

[148-024]was only amusing himself, and gratifying her at the same time, by

[148-025]a little innocent pretence. He told himself that it was his nature

[148-026]to prefer the society of women to that of men. He would have liked

[148-027]the society of Lily Dale, no doubt, much better than that of Miss

[148-028]Demolines; but as the society of Lily Dale was not to be had at that

[148-029]moment, the society of Miss Demolines was the best substitute within

[148-030]his reach. So he got into a cab and had himself driven to Porchester

[148-031]Terrace. "Is Lady Demolines at home?" he said to the servant. He

[148-032]always asked for Lady Demolines. But the page who was accustomed to

[148-033]open the door for him was less false, being young, and would now

[148-034]tell him, without any further fiction, that Miss Madalina was in

[148-035]the drawing-room. Such was the answer he got from the page on this

[148-036]evening. What Madalina did with her mother on these occasions he had

[148-037]never yet discovered. There used to be some little excuses given

[148-038]about Lady Demolines' state of health, but latterly Madalina had

[148-039]discontinued her references to her mother's headaches. She was

[148-040]standing in the centre of the drawing-room when he entered it, with

[148-041]both her hands raised, and an almost terrible expression of mystery

[148-042]in her face. Her hair, however, had been very carefully arranged

[148-043]so as to fall with copious carelessness down her shoulders, and

[148-044]altogether she was looking her best. "Oh, John," she said. She called

[148-045]him John by accident in the tumult of the moment. "Have you heard

[148-046]what has happened? But of course you have heard it."

[148-047]"Heard what? I have heard nothing," said Johnny, arrested almost

[148-048]in the doorway by the nature of the question,--and partly also, no

[148-049]doubt, by the tumult of the moment. He had no idea how terrible

[148-050]a tragedy was in truth in store for him; but he perceived that

[148-051]the moment was to be tumultuous, and that he must carry himself

[148-052]accordingly.

[148-053]"Come in, and close the door," she said. He came in and closed the

[148-054]door. "Do you mean to say that you haven't heard what has happened in

[148-055]Hook Court?"

[148-056]"No;--what has happened in Hook Court?" Miss Demolines threw herself

[148-057]back into an arm-chair, closed her eyes, and clasped both her hands

[148-058]upon her forehead. "What has happened in Hook Court?" said Johnny,

[148-059]walking up to her.

[148-060]"I do not think I can bring myself to tell you," she answered.

[148-061]Then he took one of her hands down from her forehead and held it in

[148-062]his,--which she allowed passively. She was thinking, no doubt, of

[148-063]something far different from that.

[148-064]"I never saw you looking better in my life," said Johnny.

[148-065]"Don't," said she. "How can you talk in that way, when my heart

[148-066]is bleeding,--bleeding." Then she pulled away her hand, and again

[148-067]clasped it with the other upon her forehead.

[148-068]"But why is your heart bleeding? What has happened in Hook Court?"

[148-069]Still she answered nothing, but she sobbed violently and the heaving

[148-070]of her bosom showed how tumultuous was the tumult within it. "You

[148-071]don't mean to say that Dobbs Broughton has come to grief;--that he's

[148-072]to be sold out?"

[148-073]"Man," said Madalina, jumping from her chair, standing at her full

[148-074]height, and stretching out both her arms, "he has destroyed himself!"

[148-075]The revelation was at last made with so much tragic propriety, in

[148-076]so excellent a tone, and with such an absence of all the customary

[148-077]redundances of commonplace relation, that I think that she must have

[148-078]rehearsed the scene,--either with her mother or with the page. Then

[148-079]there was a minute's silence, during which she did not move even an

[148-080]eyelid. She held her outstretched hands without dropping a finger

[148-081]half an inch. Her face was thrust forward, her chin projecting, with

[148-082]tragic horror; but there was no vacillation even in her chin. She did

[148-083]not wink an eye, or alter to the breadth of a hair the aperture of

[148-084]her lips. Surely she was a great genius if she did it all without

[148-085]previous rehearsal. Then, before he had thought of words in which to

[148-086]answer her, she let her hands fall by her side, she closed her eyes,

[148-087]and shook her head, and fell back again into her chair. "It is too

[148-088]horrible to be spoken of,--to be thought about," she said. "I could

[148-089]not have brought myself to tell the tale to a living being,--except

[148-090]to you."

[148-091]This would naturally have been flattering to Johnny had it not been

[148-092]that he was in truth absorbed by the story which he had heard.

[148-093]"Do you mean to tell me," he said, "that Broughton has--committed

[148-094]suicide?" She could not speak of it again, but nodded her head at him

[148-095]thrice, while her eyes were still closed. "And how was the manner of

[148-096]it?" said he, asking the question in a low voice. He could not even

[148-097]as yet quite bring himself to believe it. Madalina was so fond of a

[148-098]little playful intrigue, that even this story might have something in

[148-099]it of the nature of fiction. He was not quite sure of the facts, and

[148-100]yet he was shocked by what he had heard.

[148-101]"Would you have me repeat to you all the bloody details of that

[148-102]terrible scene?" she said. "It is impossible. Go to your friend

[148-103]Dalrymple. He will tell you. He knows it all. He has been with Maria

[148-104]all through. I wish,--I wish it had not been so." But nevertheless

[148-105]she did bring herself to narrate all the details with something more

[148-106]of circumstance than Eames desired. She soon succeeded in making him

[148-107]understand that the tragedy of Hook Court was a reality, and that

[148-108]poor Dobbs Broughton had brought his career to an untimely end. She

[148-109]had heard everything,--having indeed gone to Musselboro in the City,

[148-110]and having penetrated even to the sanctum of Mr. Bangles. To Mr.

[148-111]Bangles she had explained that she was bosom-friend of the widow

[148-112]of the unfortunate man, and that it was her miserable duty to make

[148-113]herself the mistress of all the circumstances. Mr. Bangles,--the

[148-114]reader may remember him, Burton and Bangles, who kept the stores

[148-115]for Himalaya wines at 22_s._ 6_d._ the dozen, in Hook Court,--was a

[148-116]bachelor, and rather liked the visit, and told Miss Demolines very

[148-117]freely all he had seen. And when she suggested that it might be

[148-118]expedient for the sake of the family that she should come back to

[148-119]Mr. Bangles for further information at a subsequent period, he very

[148-120]politely assured her that she would "do him proud," whenever she

[148-121]might please to call in Hook Court. And then he saw her into Lombard

[148-122]Street, and put her into an omnibus. She was therefore well qualified

[148-123]to tell Johnny all the particulars of the tragedy,--and she did

[148-124]so far overcome her horror as to tell them all. She told her tale

[148-125]somewhat after the manner of Æneas, not forgetting the "quorum pars

[148-126]magna fui." "I feel that it almost makes an old woman of me," said

[148-127]she, when she had finished.

[148-128]"No," said Johnny, remonstrating;--"not that."

[148-129]"But it does. To have been concerned in so terrible a tragedy takes

[148-130]more of life out of one than years of tranquil existence." As she

[148-131]had told him nothing of her intercourse with Bangles,--with Bangles

[148-132]who had literally picked the poor wretch up,--he did not see how

[148-133]she herself had been concerned in the matter; but he said nothing

[148-134]about that, knowing the character of his Madalina. "I shall

[148-135]see--that--body, floating before my eyes while I live," she said,

[148-136]"and the gory wound, and,--and--" "Don't," said Johnny, recoiling in

[148-137]truth from the picture, by which he was revolted. "Never again," she

[148-138]said; "never again! But you forced it from me, and now I shall not

[148-139]close my eyes for a week."

[148-140]She then became very comfortably confidential, and discussed

[148-141]the affairs of poor Mrs. Dobbs Broughton with a great deal of

[148-142]satisfaction. "I went to see her, of course, but she sent me down

[148-143]word to say that the shock would be too much for her. I do not wonder

[148-144]that she should not see me. Poor Maria! She came to me for advice,

[148-145]you know, when Dobbs Broughton first proposed to her; and I was

[148-146]obliged to tell her what I really thought. I knew her character so

[148-147]well! 'Dear Maria,' I said, 'if you think that you can love him, take

[148-148]him!' 'I think I can,' she replied. 'But,' said I, 'make yourself

[148-149]quite sure about the business.' And how has it turned out? She

[148-150]never loved him. What heart she has she has given to that wretched

[148-151]Dalrymple."

[148-152]"I don't see that he is particularly wretched," said Johnny, pleading

[148-153]for his friend.

[148-154]"He is wretched, and so you'll find. She gave him her heart after

[148-155]giving her hand to poor Dobbs; and as for the business, there isn't

[148-156]as much left as will pay for her mourning. I don't wonder that she

[148-157]could not bring herself to see me."

[148-158]"And what has become of the business?"

[148-159]"It belongs to Mrs. Van Siever,--to her and Musselboro. Poor

[148-160]Broughton had some little money, and it has gone among them.

[148-161]Musselboro, who never had a penny, will be a rich man. Of course you

[148-162]know that he is going to marry Clara?"

[148-163]"Nonsense!"

[148-164]"I always told you that it would be so. And now you may perhaps

[148-165]acknowledge that Conway Dalrymple's prospects are not very brilliant.

[148-166]I hope he likes being cut out by Mr. Musselboro! Of course he will

[148-167]have to marry Maria. I do not see how he can escape. Indeed, she is

[148-168]too good for him;--only after such a marriage as that, there would be

[148-169]an end to all his prospects as an artist. The best thing for them

[148-170]would be to go to New Zealand."

[148-171]John Eames certainly liked these evenings with Miss Demolines. He sat

[148-172]at his ease in a comfortable chair, and amused himself by watching

[148-173]her different little plots. And then she had bright eyes, and she

[148-174]flattered him, and allowed him to scold her occasionally. And now

[148-175]and again there might be some more potent attraction, when she would

[148-176]admit him to take her hand,--or the like. It was better than to sit

[148-177]smoking with men at the club. But he could not sit all night even

[148-178]with Madalina Demolines, and at eleven he got up to take his leave.

[148-179]"When shall you see Miss Dale?" she asked him suddenly.
