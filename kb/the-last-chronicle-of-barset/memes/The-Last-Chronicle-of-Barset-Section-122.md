[122-001]We are, however, clearly of opinion that should the jury

[122-002]find themselves able to acquit you, no further action

[122-003]whatsoever should be taken. In such case we think that the

[122-004]bishop may regard your innocence to be fully established,

[122-005]and in such case we shall recommend his lordship to look

[122-006]upon the matter as altogether at an end. I can assure you

[122-007]that in such case I shall so regard it myself.

[122-008]You will perceive that, as a consequence of this

[122-009]resolution, to which we have already come, we are

[122-010]not minded to make any inquiries ourselves into the

[122-011]circumstances of your alleged guilt, till the verdict of

[122-012]the jury shall be given. If you are acquitted, our course

[122-013]will be clear. But should you be convicted, we must in

[122-014]that case advise the bishop to take the proceedings to

[122-015]which I have alluded, or to abstain from taking them. We

[122-016]wish to ask you whether, now that our opinion has been

[122-017]conveyed to you, you will be willing to submit to the

[122-018]bishop's decision, in the event of an adverse verdict

[122-019]being given by the jury; and we think that it will be

[122-020]better for us all that you should meet us here at the hour

[122-021]I have named on Monday next, the 15th instant. It is not

[122-022]our intention to make any report to the bishop until the

[122-023]trial shall be over.

[122-024]I have the honour to be,

[122-025]My dear sir,

[122-026]Your very obedient servant,

[122-027]MORTIMER TEMPEST.

[122-028]The Rev. Josiah Crawley,

[122-029]Hogglestock.

[122-030]In the same envelope Dr. Tempest sent a short private note, in which

[122-031]he said that he should be very happy to see Mr. Crawley at half-past

[122-032]one on the Monday named, that luncheon would be ready at that hour,

[122-033]and that, as Mr. Crawley's attendance was required on public grounds,

[122-034]he would take care that a carriage was provided for the day.

[122-035]Mr. Crawley received this letter in his wife's presence, and read

[122-036]it in silence. Mrs. Crawley saw that he paid close attention to it,

[122-037]and was sure,--she felt that she was sure,--that it referred in some

[122-038]way to the terrible subject of the cheque for twenty pounds. Indeed,

[122-039]everything that came into the house, almost every word spoken there,

[122-040]and every thought that came into the breasts of any of the family,

[122-041]had more or less reference to the coming trial. How could it be

[122-042]otherwise? There was ruin coming on them all,--ruin and complete

[122-043]disgrace coming on father, mother, and children! To have been accused

[122-044]itself was very bad; but now it seemed to be the opinion of every one

[122-045]that the verdict must be against the man. Mrs. Crawley herself, who

[122-046]was perfectly sure of her husband's innocence before God, believed

[122-047]that the jury would find him guilty,--and believed also that he had

[122-048]become possessed of the money in some manner that would have been

[122-049]dishonest, had he not been so different from other people as to be

[122-050]entitled to be considered innocent where another man would have been

[122-051]plainly guilty. She was full of the cheque for twenty pounds, and of

[122-052]its results. When, therefore, he had read the letter through a second

[122-053]time, and even then had spoken no word about it, of course she could

[122-054]not refrain from questioning him. "My love," she said, "what is the

[122-055]letter?"

[122-056]"It is on business," he answered.

[122-057]She was silent for a moment before she spoke again. "May I not know

[122-058]the business?"

[122-059]"No," said he; "not at present."

[122-060]"Is it from the bishop?"

[122-061]"Have I not answered you? Have I not given you to understand that,

[122-062]for a while at least, I would prefer to keep the contents of

[122-063]this epistle to myself?" Then he looked at her very sternly, and

[122-064]afterwards turned his eyes upon the fireplace and gazed at the fire,

[122-065]as though he were striving to read there something of his future

[122-066]fate. She did not much regard the severity of his speech. That, too,

[122-067]like the taking of the cheque itself, was to be forgiven him, because

[122-068]he was different from other men. His black mood had come upon him,

[122-069]and everything was to be forgiven him now. He was as a child when

[122-070]cutting his teeth. Let the poor wayward sufferer be ever so petulant,

[122-071]the mother simply pities and loves him, and is never angry. "I beg

[122-072]your pardon, Josiah," she said, "but I thought it would comfort you

[122-073]to speak to me about it."

[122-074]"It will not comfort me," he said. "Nothing comforts me. Nothing can

[122-075]comfort me. Jane, give me my hat and my stick." His daughter brought

[122-076]to him his hat and stick, and without another word he went out and

[122-077]left them.

[122-078]As a matter of course he turned his steps towards Hoggle End. When he

[122-079]desired to be long absent from the house, he always went among the

[122-080]brickmakers. His wife, as she stood at the window and watched the

[122-081]direction in which he went, knew that he might be away for hours. The

[122-082]only friends out of his own family with whom he ever spoke freely

[122-083]were some of these rough parishioners. But he was not thinking of the

[122-084]brickmakers when he started. He was simply desirous of again reading

[122-085]Dr. Tempest's letter, and of considering it, in some spot where

[122-086]no eye could see him. He walked away with long steps, regarding

[122-087]nothing,--neither the ruts in the dirty lane, nor the young primroses

[122-088]which were fast showing themselves on the banks, nor the gathering

[122-089]clouds which might have told him of the coming rain. He went on for

[122-090]a couple of miles, till he had nearly reached the outskirts of the

[122-091]colony of Hoggle End, and then he sat himself down upon a gate. He

[122-092]had not been there a minute before a few slow large drops began to

[122-093]fall, but he was altogether too much wrapped up in his thoughts to

[122-094]regard the rain. What answer should he make to this letter from the

[122-095]man at Silverbridge?

[122-096]The position of his own mind in reference to his own guilt or his

[122-097]own innocence was very singular. It was simply the truth that he did

[122-098]not know how the cheque had come to him. He did know that he had

[122-099]blundered about it most egregiously, especially when he had averred

[122-100]that this cheque for twenty pounds had been identical with a cheque

[122-101]for another sum which had been given to him by Mr. Soames. He had

[122-102]blundered since, in saying that the dean had given it to him. There

[122-103]could be no doubt as to this, for the dean had denied that he had

[122-104]done so. And he had come to think it very possible that he had indeed

[122-105]picked the cheque up, and had afterwards used it, having deposited

[122-106]it by some strange accident,--not knowing then what he was doing, or

[122-107]what was the nature of the bit of paper in his hand,--with the notes

[122-108]which he had accepted from the dean with so much reluctance, with

[122-109]such an agony of spirit. In all these thoughts of his own about his

[122-110]own doings, and his own position, he almost admitted to himself his

[122-111]own insanity, his inability to manage his own affairs with that

[122-112]degree of rational sequence which is taken for granted as belonging

[122-113]to a man when he is made subject to criminal laws. As he puzzled

[122-114]his brain in his efforts to create a memory as to the cheque, and

[122-115]succeeded in bringing to his mind a recollection that he had once

[122-116]known something about the cheque,--that the cheque had at one time

[122-117]been the subject of a thought and of a resolution,--he admitted to

[122-118]himself that in accordance with all law and all reason he must be

[122-119]regarded as a thief. He had taken and used and spent that which he

[122-120]ought to have known was not his own;--which he would have known

[122-121]not to be his own but for some terrible incapacity with which God

[122-122]had afflicted him. What then must be the result? His mind was

[122-123]clear enough about this. If the jury could see everything and know

[122-124]everything,--as he would wish that they should do; and if this

[122-125]bishop's commission, and the bishop himself, and the Court of

[122-126]Arches with its judge, could see and know everything; and if so

[122-127]seeing and so knowing they could act with clear honesty and perfect

[122-128]wisdom,--what would they do? They would declare of him that he was

[122-129]not a thief, only because he was so muddy-minded, so addle-pated as

[122-130]not to know the difference between meum and tuum! There could be no

[122-131]other end to it, let all the lawyers and all the clergymen in England

[122-132]put their wits to it. Though he knew himself to be muddy-minded and

[122-133]addle-pated, he could see that. And could any one say of such a man

[122-134]that he was fit to be the acting clergyman of a parish,--to have a

[122-135]freehold possession in a parish as curer of men's souls! The bishop

[122-136]was in the right of it, let him be ten times as mean a fellow as he

[122-137]was.

[122-138]And yet as he sat there on the gate, while the rain came down heavily

[122-139]upon him, even when admitting the justice of the bishop, and the

[122-140]truth of the verdict which the jury would no doubt give, and the

[122-141]propriety of the action which that cold, reasonable, prosperous man

[122-142]at Silverbridge would take, he pitied himself with a tenderness of

[122-143]commiseration which knew no bounds. As for those belonging to him,

[122-144]his wife and children, his pity for them was of a different kind. He

[122-145]would have suffered any increase of suffering, could he by such agony

[122-146]have released them. Dearly as he loved them, he would have severed

[122-147]himself from them, had it been possible. Terrible thoughts as to

[122-148]their fate had come into his mind in the worst moments of his

[122-149]moodiness,--thoughts which he had had sufficient strength and

[122-150]manliness to put away from him with a strong hand, lest they should

[122-151]drive him to crime indeed; and these had come from the great pity

[122-152]which he had felt for them. But the commiseration which he had felt

[122-153]for himself had been different from this, and had mostly visited him

[122-154]at times when that other pity was for the moment in abeyance. What

[122-155]though he had taken the cheque, and spent the money though it was not

[122-156]his? He might be guilty before the law, but he was not guilty before

[122-157]God. There had never been a thought of theft in his mind, or a desire

[122-158]to steal in his heart. He knew that well enough. No jury could make

[122-159]him guilty of theft before God. And what though this mixture of guilt

[122-160]and innocence had come from madness,--from madness which these courts

[122-161]must recognize if they chose to find him innocent of the crime? In

[122-162]spite of his aberrations of intellect, if there were any such, his

[122-163]ministrations in his parish were good. Had he not preached fervently

[122-164]and well,--preaching the true gospel? Had he not been very diligent

[122-165]among his people, striving with all his might to lessen the ignorance

[122-166]of the ignorant, and to gild with godliness the learning of the

[122-167]instructed? Had he not been patient, enduring, instant, and in all

[122-168]things amenable to the laws and regulations laid down by the Church

[122-169]for his guidance in his duties as a parish clergyman? Who could point

[122-170]out in what he had been astray, or where he had gone amiss? But for

[122-171]the work which he had done with so much zeal the Church which he

[122-172]served had paid him so miserable a pittance that, though life and

[122-173]soul had been kept together, the reason, or a fragment of the reason,

[122-174]had at moments escaped from his keeping in the scramble. Hence it was

[122-175]that this terrible calamity had fallen upon him! Who had been tried

[122-176]as he had been tried, and had gone through such fire with less loss

[122-177]of intellectual power than he had done? He was still a scholar,

[122-178]though no brother scholar ever came near him, and would make

[122-179]Greek iambics as he walked along the lanes. His memory was stored

[122-180]with poetry, though no book ever came to his hands, except those

[122-181]shorn and tattered volumes which lay upon his table. Old problems

[122-182]in trigonometry were the pleasing relaxations of his mind, and

[122-183]complications of figures were a delight to him. There was not one of

[122-184]those prosperous clergymen around him, and who scorned him, whom he

[122-185]could not have instructed in Hebrew. It was always a gratification to

[122-186]him to remember that his old friend the dean was weak in his Hebrew.

[122-187]He, with these acquirements, with these fitnesses, had been thrust

[122-188]down to the ground,--to the very granite,--and because in that harsh

[122-189]heartless thrusting his intellect had for moments wavered as to

[122-190]common things, cleaving still to all its grander, nobler possessions,

[122-191]he was now to be rent in pieces and scattered to the winds, as being

[122-192]altogether vile, worthless, and worse than worthless. It was thus

[122-193]that he thought of himself, pitying himself, as he sat upon the gate,

[122-194]while the rain fell ruthlessly on his shoulders.
