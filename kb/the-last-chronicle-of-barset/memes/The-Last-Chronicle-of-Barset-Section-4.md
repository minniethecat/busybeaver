[004-001]A daughter of the archdeacon had made a splendid matrimonial

[004-002]alliance,--so splendid that its history was at the time known to all

[004-003]the aristocracy of the county, and had not been altogether forgotten

[004-004]by any of those who keep themselves well instructed in the details of

[004-005]the peerage. Griselda Grantly had married Lord Dumbello, the eldest

[004-006]son of the Marquis of Hartletop,--than whom no English nobleman was

[004-007]more puissant, if broad acres, many castles, high title, and stars

[004-008]and ribbons are any signs of puissance,--and she was now, herself,

[004-009]Marchioness of Hartletop, with a little Lord Dumbello of her own. The

[004-010]daughter's visits to the parsonage of her father were of necessity

[004-011]rare, such necessity having come from her own altered sphere of life.

[004-012]A Marchioness of Hartletop has special duties which will hardly

[004-013]permit her to devote herself frequently to the humdrum society of a

[004-014]clerical father and mother. That it would be so, father and mother

[004-015]had understood when they sent the fortunate girl forth to a higher

[004-016]world. But, now and again, since her August marriage, she had laid

[004-017]her coroneted head upon one of the old rectory pillows for a night

[004-018]or so, and on such occasions all the Plumsteadians had been loud in

[004-019]praise of her condescension. Now it happened that when this second

[004-020]and more aggravated blast of the evil wind reached the rectory,--the

[004-021]renewed waft of the tidings as to Major Grantly's infatuation

[004-022]regarding Miss Grace Crawley, which, on its renewal, seemed to bring

[004-023]with it something of confirmation,--it chanced, I say, that at that

[004-024]moment Griselda, Marchioness of Hartletop, was gracing the paternal

[004-025]mansion. It need hardly be said that the father was not slow to

[004-026]invoke such a daughter's counsel, and such a sister's aid.

[004-027]I am not quite sure that the mother would have been equally quick to

[004-028]ask her daughter's advice, had she been left in the matter entirely

[004-029]to her own propensities. Mrs. Grantly had ever loved her daughter

[004-030]dearly, and had been very proud of that great success in life which

[004-031]Griselda had achieved; but in late years, the child had become,

[004-032]as a woman, separate from the mother, and there had arisen, not

[004-033]unnaturally, a break of that close confidence which in early years

[004-034]had existed between them. Griselda, Marchioness of Hartletop,

[004-035]was more than ever a daughter to the archdeacon, even though he

[004-036]might never see her. Nothing could rob him of the honour of such a

[004-037]progeny,--nothing, even though there had been actual estrangement

[004-038]between them. But it was not so with Mrs. Grantly. Griselda had

[004-039]done very well, and Mrs. Grantly had rejoiced; but she had lost her

[004-040]child. Now the major, who had done well also, though in a much lesser

[004-041]degree, was still her child, moving in the same sphere of life with

[004-042]her, still dependent in a great degree upon his father's bounty, a

[004-043]neighbour in the county, a frequent visitor at the parsonage, and

[004-044]a visitor who could be received without any of that trouble which

[004-045]attended the unfrequent comings of Griselda, the marchioness, to the

[004-046]home of her youth. And for this reason Mrs. Grantly, terribly put out

[004-047]as she was at the idea of a marriage between her son and one standing

[004-048]so poorly in the world's esteem as Grace Crawley, would not have

[004-049]brought forward the matter before her daughter, had she been left

[004-050]to her own desires. A marchioness in one's family is a tower of

[004-051]strength, no doubt; but there are counsellors so strong that we

[004-052]do not wish to trust them, lest in the trusting we ourselves be

[004-053]overwhelmed by their strength. Now Mrs. Grantly was by no means

[004-054]willing to throw her influence into the hands of her titled daughter.

[004-055]But the titled daughter was consulted and gave her advice. On the

[004-056]occasion of the present visit to Plumstead she had consented to lay

[004-057]her head for two nights on the parsonage pillows, and on the second

[004-058]evening her brother the major was to come over from Cosby Lodge

[004-059]to meet her. Before his coming the affair of Grace Crawley was

[004-060]discussed.

[004-061]"It would break my heart, Griselda," said the archdeacon,

[004-062]piteously--"and your mother's."

[004-063]"There is nothing against the girl's character," said Mrs. Grantly,

[004-064]"and the father and mother are gentlefolks by birth; but such a

[004-065]marriage for Henry would be very unseemly."

[004-066]"To make it worse, there is this terrible story about him," said the

[004-067]archdeacon.

[004-068]"I don't suppose there is much in that," said Mrs. Grantly.

[004-069]"I can't say. There is no knowing. They told me to-day in Barchester

[004-070]that Soames is pressing the case against him."

[004-071]"Who is Soames, papa?" asked the marchioness.

[004-072]"He is Lord Lufton's man of business, my dear."

[004-073]"Oh, Lord Lufton's man of business!" There was something of a sneer

[004-074]in the tone of the lady's voice as she mentioned Lord Lufton's name.

[004-075]"I am told," continued the archdeacon, "that Soames declares the

[004-076]cheque was taken from a pocket-book which he left by accident in

[004-077]Crawley's house."

[004-078]"You don't mean to say, archdeacon, that you think that Mr.

[004-079]Crawley--a clergyman--stole it!" said Mrs. Grantly.

[004-080]"I don't say anything of the kind, my dear. But supposing Mr. Crawley

[004-081]to be as honest as the sun, you wouldn't wish Henry to marry his

[004-082]daughter."

[004-083]"Certainly not," said the mother. "It would be an unfitting marriage.

[004-084]The poor girl has had no advantages."

[004-085]"He is not able even to pay his baker's bill. I always thought Arabin

[004-086]was very wrong to place such a man in such a parish as Hogglestock.

[004-087]Of course the family could not live there." The Arabin here spoken of

[004-088]was Dr. Arabin, dean of Barchester. The dean and the archdeacon had

[004-089]married sisters, and there was much intimacy between the families.

[004-090]"After all it is only a rumour as yet," said Mrs. Grantly.

[004-091]"Fothergill told me only yesterday, that he sees her almost every

[004-092]day," said the father. "What are we to do, Griselda? You know how

[004-093]headstrong Henry is." The marchioness sat quite still, looking at the

[004-094]fire, and made no immediate answer to this address.

[004-095]"There is nothing for it, but that you should tell him what you

[004-096]think," said the mother.

[004-097]"If his sister were to speak to him, it might do much," said the

[004-098]archdeacon. To this Mrs. Grantly said nothing; but Mrs. Grantly's

[004-099]daughter understood very well that her mother's confidence in her was

[004-100]not equal to her father's. Lady Hartletop said nothing, but still

[004-101]sat, with impassive face, and eyes fixed upon the fire. "I think

[004-102]that if you were to speak to him, Griselda, and tell him that he

[004-103]would disgrace his family, he would be ashamed to go on with such a

[004-104]marriage," said the father. "He would feel, connected as he is with

[004-105]Lord Hartletop--"

[004-106]"I don't think he would feel anything about that," said Mrs. Grantly.

[004-107]"I dare say not," said Lady Hartletop.

[004-108]"I am sure he ought to feel it," said the father. They were all

[004-109]silent, and sat looking at the fire.

[004-110]"I suppose, papa, you allow Henry an income," said Lady Hartletop,

[004-111]after a while.

[004-112]"Indeed I do,--eight hundred a year."

[004-113]"Then I think I should tell him that that must depend upon his

[004-114]conduct. Mamma, if you won't mind ringing the bell, I will send

[004-115]for Cecile, and go upstairs and dress." Then the marchioness went

[004-116]upstairs to dress, and in about an hour the major arrived in his

[004-117]dog-cart. He also was allowed to go upstairs to dress before anything

[004-118]was said to him about his great offence.

[004-119]"Griselda is right," said the archdeacon, speaking to his wife out of

[004-120]his dressing-room. "She always was right. I never knew a young woman

[004-121]with more sense than Griselda."

[004-122]"But you do not mean to say that in any event you would stop Henry's

[004-123]income?" Mrs. Grantly also was dressing, and made reply out of her

[004-124]bedroom.

[004-125]"Upon my word, I don't know. As a father I would do anything to

[004-126]prevent such a marriage as that."

[004-127]"But if he did marry her in spite of the threat? And he would if he

[004-128]had once said so."

[004-129]"Is a father's word, then, to go for nothing; and a father who allows

[004-130]his son eight hundred a year? If he told the girl that he would be

[004-131]ruined she couldn't hold him to it."

[004-132]"My dear, they'd know as well as I do, that you would give way after

[004-133]three months."

[004-134]"But why should I give way? Good heavens--!"

[004-135]"Of course you'd give way, and of course we should have the young

[004-136]woman here, and of course we should make the best of it."

[004-137]The idea of having Grace Crawley as a daughter at the Plumstead

[004-138]Rectory was too much for the archdeacon, and he resented it by

[004-139]additional vehemence in the tone of his voice, and a nearer personal

[004-140]approach to the wife of his bosom. All unaccoutred as he was, he

[004-141]stood in the doorway between the two rooms, and thence fulminated

[004-142]at his wife his assurances that he would never allow himself to be

[004-143]immersed in such a depth of humility as that she had suggested. "I

[004-144]can tell you this, then, that if ever she comes here, I shall take

[004-145]care to be away. I will never receive her here. You can do as you

[004-146]please."

[004-147]"That is just what I cannot do. If I could do as I pleased, I would

[004-148]put a stop to it at once."

[004-149]"It seems to me that you want to encourage him. A child about sixteen

[004-150]years of age!"

[004-151]"I am told she is nineteen."

[004-152]"What does it matter if she was fifty-nine? Think of what her

[004-153]bringing up has been. Think what it would be to have all the Crawleys

[004-154]in our house for ever, and all their debts, and all their disgrace!"

[004-155]"I do not know that they have ever been disgraced."

[004-156]"You'll see. The whole county has heard of the affair of this twenty

[004-157]pounds. Look at that dear girl upstairs, who has been such a comfort

[004-158]to us. Do you think it would be fit that she and her husband should

[004-159]meet such a one as Grace Crawley at our table?"

[004-160]"I don't think it would do them a bit of harm," said Mrs. Grantly.

[004-161]"But there would be no chance of that, seeing that Griselda's husband

[004-162]never comes to us."

[004-163]"He was here the year before last."

[004-164]"And I never was so tired of a man in all my life."

[004-165]"Then you prefer the Crawleys, I suppose. This is what you get from

[004-166]Eleanor's teaching." Eleanor was the dean's wife, and Mrs. Grantly's

[004-167]younger sister. "It has always been a sorrow to me that I ever

[004-168]brought Arabin into the diocese."

[004-169]"I never asked you to bring him, archdeacon. But nobody was so glad

[004-170]as you when he proposed to Eleanor."

[004-171]"Well, the long and the short of it is this, I shall tell Henry

[004-172]to-night that if he makes a fool of himself with this girl, he must

[004-173]not look to me any longer for an income. He has about six hundred

[004-174]a year of his own, and if he chooses to throw himself away, he had

[004-175]better go and live in the south of France, or in Canada, or where he

[004-176]pleases. He shan't come here."

[004-177]"I hope he won't marry the girl, with all my heart," said Mrs.

[004-178]Grantly.

[004-179]"He had better not. By heavens, he had better not!"

[004-180]"But if he does, you'll be the first to forgive him."

[004-181]On hearing this the archdeacon slammed the door, and retired to his

[004-182]washing apparatus. At the present moment he was very angry with his

[004-183]wife, but then he was so accustomed to such anger, and was so well

[004-184]aware that it in truth meant nothing, that it did not make him

[004-185]unhappy. The archdeacon and Mrs. Grantly had now been man and

[004-186]wife for more than a quarter of a century, and had never in truth

[004-187]quarrelled. He had the most profound respect for her judgment, and

[004-188]the most implicit reliance on her conduct. She had never yet offended

[004-189]him, or caused him to repent the hour in which he had made her Mrs.

[004-190]Grantly. But she had come to understand that she might use a woman's

[004-191]privilege with her tongue; and she used it,--not altogether to his

[004-192]comfort. On the present occasion he was the more annoyed because he

[004-193]felt that she might be right. "It would be a positive disgrace, and

[004-194]I never would see him again," he said to himself. And yet as he said

[004-195]it, he knew that he would not have the strength of character to carry

[004-196]him through a prolonged quarrel with his son. "I never would see

[004-197]her,--never, never!" he said to himself. "And then such an opening as

[004-198]he might have at his sister's house."
