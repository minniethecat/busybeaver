[134-001]She was preparing to go up to her chamber, with her hand on the

[134-002]banisters and with her foot on the stairs, when she saw the servant

[134-003]who had answered the bishop's bell. "John," she said, "when Mr.

[134-004]Thumble comes to the palace, let me see him before he goes to my

[134-005]lord."

[134-006]"Yes, ma'am," said John, who well understood the nature of these

[134-007]quarrels between his master and his mistress. But the commands of the

[134-008]mistress were still paramount among the servants, and John proceeded

[134-009]on his mission with the view of accomplishing Mrs. Proudie's behests.

[134-010]Then Mrs. Proudie went upstairs to her chamber, and locked her door.

[134-011]Mr. Thumble returned to Barchester that day, leading the broken-down

[134-012]cob; and a dreadful walk he had. He was not good at walking, and

[134-013]before he came near Barchester had come to entertain a violent hatred

[134-014]for the beast he was leading. The leading of a horse that is tired,

[134-015]or in pain, or lame, or even stiff in his limbs, is not pleasant

[134-016]work. The brute will not accommodate his paces to the man, and will

[134-017]contrive to make his head very heavy on the bridle. And he will not

[134-018]walk on the part of the road which the man intends for him, but

[134-019]will lean against the man, and will make himself altogether very

[134-020]disagreeable. It may be understood, therefore, that Mr. Thumble was

[134-021]not in a good humour when he entered the palace yard. Nor was he

[134-022]altogether quiet in his mind as to the injury which he had done to

[134-023]the animal. "It was the brute's fault," said Mr. Thumble. "It comes

[134-024]generally of not knowing how to ride 'em," said the groom. For Mr.

[134-025]Thumble, though he often had a horse out of the episcopal stables,

[134-026]was not ready with his shillings to the man who waited upon him with

[134-027]the steed.

[134-028]He had not, however, come to any satisfactory understanding

[134-029]respecting the broken knees when the footman from the palace told

[134-030]him he was wanted. It was in vain that Mr. Thumble pleaded that

[134-031]he was nearly dead with fatigue, that he had walked all the way

[134-032]from Hogglestock and must go home to change his clothes. John was

[134-033]peremptory with him, insisting that he must wait first upon Mrs.

[134-034]Proudie and then upon the bishop. Mr. Thumble might perhaps have

[134-035]turned a deaf ear to the latter command, but the former was one which

[134-036]he felt himself bound to obey. So he entered the palace, rather

[134-037]cross, very much soiled as to his outer man; and in this condition

[134-038]went up a certain small staircase which was familiar to him, to a

[134-039]small parlour which adjoined Mrs. Proudie's room, and there awaited

[134-040]the arrival of the lady. That he should be required to wait some

[134-041]quarter of an hour was not surprising to him; but when half an hour

[134-042]was gone, and he remembered himself of his own wife at home, and of

[134-043]the dinner which he had not yet eaten, he ventured to ring the bell.

[134-044]Mrs. Proudie's own maid, Mrs. Draper by name, came to him and said

[134-045]that she had knocked twice at Mrs. Proudie's door and would knock

[134-046]again. Two minutes after that she returned, running into the room

[134-047]with her arms extended, and exclaiming, "Oh, heavens, sir; mistress

[134-048]is dead!" Mr. Thumble, hardly knowing what he was about, followed the

[134-049]woman into the bedroom, and there he found himself standing awestruck

[134-050]before the corpse of her who had so lately been the presiding spirit

[134-051]of the palace.

[134-052]The body was still resting on its legs, leaning against the end of

[134-053]the side of the bed, while one of the arms was close clasped round

[134-054]the bed-post. The mouth was rigidly closed, but the eyes were open as

[134-055]though staring at him. Nevertheless there could be no doubt from the

[134-056]first glance that the woman was dead. He went up close to it, but did

[134-057]not dare to touch it. There was no one as yet there but he and Mrs.

[134-058]Draper;--no one else knew what had happened.

[134-059]"It's her heart," said Mrs. Draper.

[134-060]"Did she suffer from heart complaint?" he asked.

[134-061]"We suspected it, sir, though nobody knew it. She was very shy of

[134-062]talking about herself."

[134-063]"We must send for the doctor at once," said Mr. Thumble. "We had

[134-064]better touch nothing till he is here." Then they retreated and the

[134-065]door was locked.

[134-066]In ten minutes everybody in the house knew it except the bishop; and

[134-067]in twenty minutes the nearest apothecary with his assistant were in

[134-068]the room, and the body had been properly laid upon the bed. Even then

[134-069]the husband had not been told,--did not know either his relief or his

[134-070]loss. It was now past seven, which was the usual hour for dinner at

[134-071]the palace, and it was probable that he would come out of his room

[134-072]among the servants, if he were not summoned. When it was proposed to

[134-073]Mr. Thumble that he should go in to him and tell him, he positively

[134-074]declined, saying that the sight which he had just seen and the

[134-075]exertions of the day together, had so unnerved him, that he had not

[134-076]physical strength for the task. The apothecary, who had been summoned

[134-077]in a hurry, had escaped, probably being equally unwilling to be

[134-078]the bearer of such a communication. The duty therefore fell to Mrs.

[134-079]Draper, and under the pressing instance of the other servants she

[134-080]descended to her master's room. Had it not been that the hour of

[134-081]dinner had come, so that the bishop could not have been left much

[134-082]longer to himself, the evil time would have been still postponed.

[134-083]She went very slowly along the passage, and was just going to pause

[134-084]ere she reached the room, when the door was opened and the bishop

[134-085]stood close before her. It was easy to be seen that he was cross. His

[134-086]hands and face were unwashed and his face was haggard. In these days

[134-087]he would not even go through the ceremony of dressing himself before

[134-088]dinner. "Mrs. Draper," he said, "why don't they tell me that dinner

[134-089]is ready? Are they going to give me any dinner?" She stood a moment

[134-090]without answering him, while the tears streamed down her face. "What

[134-091]is the matter?" said he. "Has your mistress sent you here?"

[134-092]"Oh, laws!" said Mrs. Draper,--and she put out her hands to support

[134-093]him if such support should be necessary.

[134-094]"What is the matter?" he demanded angrily.

[134-095]"Oh, my lord;--bear it like a Christian. Mistress isn't no more." He

[134-096]leaned back against the door-post, and she took hold of him by the

[134-097]arm. "It was the heart, my lord. Dr. Filgrave hisself has not been

[134-098]yet; but that's what it was." The bishop did not say a word, but

[134-099]walked back to his chair before the fire.

[134-100]CHAPTER LXVII.

[134-101]IN MEMORIAM.

[134-102][Illustration]

[134-103]The bishop when he had heard the tidings of his wife's death walked

[134-104]back to his seat over the fire, and Mrs. Draper, the housekeeper,

[134-105]came and stood over him without speaking. Thus she stood for ten

[134-106]minutes looking down at him and listening. But there was no sound;

[134-107]not a word, nor a moan, nor a sob. It was as though he also were

[134-108]dead, but that a slight irregular movement of his fingers on the top

[134-109]of his bald head, told her that his mind and body were still active.

[134-110]"My lord," she said at last, "would you wish to see the doctor when

[134-111]he comes?" She spoke very low and he did not answer her. Then, after

[134-112]another minute of silence, she asked the same question again.

[134-113]"What doctor?" he said.

[134-114]"Dr. Filgrave. We sent for him. Perhaps he is here now. Shall I go

[134-115]and see, my lord?" Mrs. Draper found that her position there was

[134-116]weary and she wished to escape. Anything on his behalf requiring

[134-117]trouble or work she would have done willingly; but she could not

[134-118]stand there for ever watching the motion of his fingers.

[134-119]"I suppose I must see him," said the bishop. Mrs. Draper took this

[134-120]as an order for her departure and crept silently out of the room,

[134-121]closing the door behind her with the long protracted elaborate click

[134-122]which is always produced by an attempt at silence on such occasions.

[134-123]He did not care for noise or for silence. Had she slammed the door

[134-124]he would not have regarded it. A wonderful silence had come upon

[134-125]him which for the time almost crushed him. He would never hear that

[134-126]well-known voice again!

[134-127]He was free now. Even in his misery,--for he was very miserable,--he

[134-128]could not refrain from telling himself that. No one could now press

[134-129]uncalled-for into his study, contradict him in the presence of those

[134-130]before whom he was bound to be authoritative, and rob him of all his

[134-131]dignity. There was no one else of whom he was afraid. She had at

[134-132]least kept him out of the hands of other tyrants. He was now his own

[134-133]master, and there was a feeling,--I may not call it of relief, for as

[134-134]yet there was more of pain in it than of satisfaction,--a feeling as

[134-135]though he had escaped from an old trouble at a terrible cost of which

[134-136]he could not as yet calculate the amount. He knew that he might now

[134-137]give up all idea of writing to the archbishop.

[134-138]She had in some ways, and at certain periods of his life, been very

[134-139]good to him. She had kept his money for him and made things go

[134-140]straight, when they had been poor. His interests had always been

[134-141]her interests. Without her he would never have been a bishop. So,

[134-142]at least, he told himself now, and so told himself probably with

[134-143]truth. She had been very careful of his children. She had never been

[134-144]idle. She had never been fond of pleasure. She had neglected no

[134-145]acknowledged duty. He did not doubt that she was now on her way to

[134-146]heaven. He took his hands down from his head, and clasping them

[134-147]together, said a little prayer. It may be doubted whether he quite

[134-148]knew for what he was praying. The idea of praying for her soul, now

[134-149]that she was dead, would have scandalized him. He certainly was not

[134-150]praying for his own soul. I think he was praying that God might save

[134-151]him from being glad that his wife was dead.

[134-152]But she was dead;--and, as it were, in a moment! He had not stirred

[134-153]out of that room since she had been there with him. Then there had

[134-154]been angry words between them,--perhaps more determined enmity on his

[134-155]part than ever had before existed; and they had parted for the last

[134-156]time with bitter animosity. But he told himself that he had certainly

[134-157]been right in what he had done then. He thought he had been right

[134-158]then. And so his mind went back to the Crawley and Thumble question,

[134-159]and he tried to alleviate the misery which that last interview with

[134-160]his wife now created by assuring himself that he at least had been

[134-161]justified in what he had done.

[134-162]But yet his thoughts were very tender to her. Nothing reopens the

[134-163]springs of love so fully as absence, and no absence so thoroughly as

[134-164]that which must needs be endless. We want that which we have not; and

[134-165]especially that which we can never have. She had told him in the very

[134-166]last moments of her presence with him that he was wishing that she

[134-167]were dead, and he had made her no reply. At the moment he had felt,

[134-168]with savage anger, that such was his wish. Her words had now come to

[134-169]pass, and he was a widower,--and he assured himself that he would

[134-170]give all that he possessed in the world to bring her back again.
