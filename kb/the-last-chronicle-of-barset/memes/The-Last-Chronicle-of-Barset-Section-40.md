[040-001]But he must go to Plumstead before he could go to Allington. He

[040-002]was engaged to spend his Christmas there, and must go now at once.

[040-003]There was not time for the journey to Allington before he was due

[040-004]at Plumstead. And, moreover, though he could not bring himself

[040-005]to resolve that he would tell his father what he was going to

[040-006]do;--"It would seem as though I were asking his leave!" he said to

[040-007]himself;--he thought that he would make a clean breast of it to his

[040-008]mother. It made him sad to think that he should cut the rope which

[040-009]fastened his own boat among the other boats in the home harbour

[040-010]at Plumstead, and that he should go out all alone into strange

[040-011]waters,--turned adrift altogether, as it were, from the Grantly

[040-012]fleet. If he could only get the promise of his mother's sympathy

[040-013]for Grace it would be something. He understood,--no one better than

[040-014]he,--the tendency of all his family to an uprising in the world,

[040-015]which tendency was almost as strong in his mother as in his father.

[040-016]And he had been by no means without a similar ambition himself,

[040-017]though with him the ambition had been only fitful, not enduring.

[040-018]He had a brother, a clergyman, a busy, stirring, eloquent London

[040-019]preacher, who got churches built, and was heard of far and wide as a

[040-020]rising man, who had married a certain Lady Anne, the daughter of an

[040-021]earl, and who was already mentioned as a candidate for high places.

[040-022]How his sister was the wife of a marquis, and a leader in the

[040-023]fashionable world, the reader already knows. The archdeacon himself

[040-024]was a rich man, so powerful that he could afford to look down upon a

[040-025]bishop; and Mrs. Grantly, though there was left about her something

[040-026]of an old softness of nature, a touch of the former life which had

[040-027]been hers before the stream of her days had run gold, yet she, too,

[040-028]had taken kindly to wealth and high standing, and was by no means one

[040-029]of those who construe literally that passage of scripture which tells

[040-030]us of the camel and the needle's eye. Our Henry Grantly, our major,

[040-031]knew himself to be his mother's favourite child,--knew himself to

[040-032]have become so since something of coolness had grown up between her

[040-033]and her august daughter. The augustness of the daughter had done much

[040-034]to reproduce the old freshness of which I have spoken in the mother's

[040-035]heart, and had specially endeared to her the son who, of all her

[040-036]children, was the least subject to the family failing. The clergyman,

[040-037]Charles Grantly,--he who had married the Lady Anne,--was his father's

[040-038]darling in these days. The old archdeacon would go up to London and

[040-039]be quite happy in his son's house. He met there the men whom he loved

[040-040]to meet, and heard the talk which he loved to hear. It was very fine,

[040-041]having the Marquis of Hartletop for his son-in-law, but he had never

[040-042]cared to be much at Lady Hartletop's house. Indeed, the archdeacon

[040-043]cared to be in no house in which those around him were supposed to

[040-044]be bigger than himself. Such was the little family fleet from out of

[040-045]which Henry Grantly was now proposing to sail alone with his little

[040-046]boat,--taking Grace Crawley with him at the helm. "My father is a

[040-047]just man at the bottom," he said to himself, "and though he may not

[040-048]forgive me, he will not punish Edith."

[040-049]But there was still left one of the family,--not a Grantly, indeed,

[040-050]but one so nearly allied to them as to have his boat moored in

[040-051]the same harbour,--who, as the major well knew, would thoroughly

[040-052]sympathize with him. This was old Mr. Harding, his mother's

[040-053]father,--the father of his mother and of his aunt Mrs. Arabin,--whose

[040-054]home was now at the deanery. He was also to be at Plumstead during

[040-055]this Christmas, and he at any rate would give a ready assent to such

[040-056]a marriage as that which the major was proposing for himself. But

[040-057]then poor old Mr. Harding had been thoroughly deficient in that

[040-058]ambition which had served to aggrandize the family into which his

[040-059]daughter had married. He was a poor old man who, in spite of good

[040-060]friends,--for the late bishop of the diocese had been his dearest

[040-061]friend,--had never risen high in his profession, and had fallen even

[040-062]from the moderate altitude which he had attained. But he was a man

[040-063]whom all loved who knew him; and it was much to the credit of his

[040-064]son-in-law, the archdeacon, that, with all his tendencies to love

[040-065]rising suns, he had ever been true to Mr. Harding.

[040-066]Major Grantly took his daughter with him, and on his arrival at

[040-067]Plumstead she of course was the first object of attention. Mrs.

[040-068]Grantly declared that she had grown immensely. The archdeacon

[040-069]complimented her red cheeks, and said that Cosby Lodge was as

[040-070]healthy a place as any in the county, while Mr. Harding, Edith's

[040-071]great-grandfather, drew slowly from his pocket sundry treasures

[040-072]with which he had come prepared for the delight of the little girl.

[040-073]Charles Grantly and Lady Anne had no children, and the heir of all

[040-074]the Hartletops was too august to have been trusted to the embraces of

[040-075]her mother's grandfather. Edith, therefore, was all that he had in

[040-076]that generation, and of Edith he was prepared to be as indulgent as

[040-077]he had been, in their time, of his grandchildren the Grantlys, and

[040-078]still was of his grandchildren the Arabins, and had been before that

[040-079]of his own daughters. "She's more like Eleanor than any one else,"

[040-080]said the old man in a plaintive tone. Now Eleanor was Mrs. Arabin,

[040-081]the dean's wife, and was at this time,--if I were to say over forty

[040-082]I do not think I should be uncharitable. No one else saw the special

[040-083]likeness, but no one else remembered, as Mr. Harding did, what

[040-084]Eleanor had been when she was three years old.

[Illustration: "She's more like Eleanor than any one else."]

[040-085]"Aunt Nelly is in France," said the child.

[040-086]"Yes, my darling, aunt Nelly is in France, and I wish she were at

[040-087]home. Aunt Nelly has been away a long time."

[040-088]"I suppose she'll stay till the dean picks her up on his way home?"

[040-089]said Mrs. Grantly.

[040-090]"So she says in her letters. I heard from her yesterday, and I

[040-091]brought the letter, as I thought you'd like to see it." Mrs. Grantly

[040-092]took the letter and read it, while her father still played with the

[040-093]child. The archdeacon and the major were standing together on the rug

[040-094]discussing the shooting at Chaldicotes, as to which the archdeacon

[040-095]had a strong opinion. "I'm quite sure that a man with a place like

[040-096]that does more good by preserving than by leaving it alone. The

[040-097]better head of game he has the richer the county will be generally.

[040-098]It is just the same with pheasants as it is with sheep and bullocks.

[040-099]A pheasant doesn't cost more than he's worth any more than a

[040-100]barn-door fowl. Besides, a man who preserves is always respected by

[040-101]the poachers, and the man who doesn't is not."

[040-102]"There's something in that, sir, certainly," said the major.

[040-103]"More than you think for, perhaps. Look at poor Sowerby, who went on

[040-104]there for years without a shilling. How he was respected, because he

[040-105]lived as the people around him expected a gentleman to live. Thorne

[040-106]will have a bad time of it, if he tries to change things."

[040-107]"Only think," exclaimed Mrs. Grantly, "when Eleanor wrote she had not

[040-108]heard of that affair of poor Mr. Crawley's."

[040-109]"Does she say anything about him?" asked the major.

[040-110]"I'll read what she says. 'I see in Galignani that a clergyman in

[040-111]Barsetshire has been committed for theft. Pray tell me who it is. Not

[040-112]the bishop, I hope, for the credit of the diocese?'"

[040-113]"I wish it were," said the archdeacon.

[040-114]"For shame, my dear," said his wife.

[040-115]"No shame at all. If we are to have a thief among us, I'd sooner find

[040-116]him in a bad man than a good one. Besides we should have a change at

[040-117]the palace, which would be a great thing."

[040-118]"But is it not odd that Eleanor should have heard nothing of it?"

[040-119]said Mrs. Grantly.

[040-120]"It's odd that you should not have mentioned it yourself."

[040-121]"I did not, certainly; nor you, papa, I suppose?"

[040-122]Mr. Harding acknowledged that he had not spoken of it, and then they

[040-123]calculated that perhaps she might not have received any letter from

[040-124]her husband written since the news had reached him. "Besides, why

[040-125]should he have mentioned it?" said the major. "He only knows as yet

[040-126]of the inquiry about the cheque, and can have heard nothing of what

[040-127]was done by the magistrates."

[040-128]"Still it seems so odd that Eleanor should not have known of it,

[040-129]seeing that we have been talking of nothing else for the last week,"

[040-130]said Mrs. Grantly.

[040-131]For two days the major said not a word of Grace Crawley to any one.

[040-132]Nothing could be more courteous and complaisant than was his father's

[040-133]conduct to him. Anything that he wanted for Edith was to be done. For

[040-134]himself there was no trouble which would not be taken. His hunting,

[040-135]and his shooting, and his fishing seemed to have become matters of

[040-136]paramount consideration to his father. And then the archdeacon became

[040-137]very confidential about money matters,--not offering anything to his

[040-138]son, which, as he well knew, would have been seen through as palpable

[040-139]bribery and corruption,--but telling him of this little scheme and of

[040-140]that, of one investment and of another;--how he contemplated buying a

[040-141]small property here, and spending a few thousands on building there.

[040-142]"Of course it is all for you and your brother," said the archdeacon,

[040-143]with that benevolent sadness which is used habitually by fathers on

[040-144]such occasions; "and I like you to know what it is that I am doing.

[040-145]I told Charles about the London property the last time I was up,"

[040-146]said the archdeacon, "and there shall be no difference between

[040-147]him and you, if all goes well." This was very good-natured on the

[040-148]archdeacon's part, and was not strictly necessary, as Charles was the

[040-149]eldest son; but the major understood it perfectly. "There shall be an

[040-150]elysium opened to you, if only you will not do that terrible thing of

[040-151]which you spoke when last here." The archdeacon uttered no such words

[040-152]as these, and did not even allude to Grace Crawley; but the words

[040-153]were as good as spoken, and had they been spoken ever so plainly the

[040-154]major could not have understood them more clearly. He was quite awake

[040-155]to the loveliness of the elysium opened before him. He had had his

[040-156]moment of anxiety, whether his father would or would not make an

[040-157]elder son of his brother Charles. The whole thing was now put before

[040-158]him plainly. Give up Grace Crawley, and you shall share alike with

[040-159]your brother. Disgrace yourself by marrying her, and your brother

[040-160]shall have everything. There was the choice, and it was still open

[040-161]to him to take which side he pleased. Were he never to go near Grace

[040-162]Crawley again no one would blame him, unless it were Miss Prettyman

[040-163]or Mrs. Thorne. "Fill your glass, Henry," said the archdeacon. "You'd

[040-164]better, I tell you, for there is no more of it left." Then the major

[040-165]filled his glass and sipped the wine, and swore to himself that

[040-166]he would go down to Allington at once. What! Did his father think

[040-167]to bribe him by giving him '20 port? He would certainly go down

[040-168]to Allington, and he would tell his mother to-morrow morning,

[040-169]or certainly on the next day, what he was going to do. "Pity it

[040-170]should be all gone; isn't it, sir?" said the archdeacon to his

[040-171]father-in-law. "It has lasted my time," said Mr. Harding, "and I'm

[040-172]very much obliged to it. Dear, dear; how well I remember your father

[040-173]giving the order for it! There were two pipes, and somebody said it

[040-174]was a heady wine. 'If the prebendaries and rectors can't drink it,'

[040-175]said your father, 'the curates will.'"
