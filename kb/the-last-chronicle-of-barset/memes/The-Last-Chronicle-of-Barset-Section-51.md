[051-001]"Allington!" said Johnny.

[051-002]"Yes, Allington. Is there not a village called Allington?"

[051-003]"There is a village called Allington, certainly. It lies over there."

[051-004]And Johnny pointed with his finger through the window. "As you do not

[051-005]know the country you can see nothing, but I can see the Allington

[051-006]trees at this moment."

[051-007]"I suppose there is no inn at Allington?"

[051-008]"There's a public-house, with a very nice clean bedroom. It is

[051-009]called the 'Red Lion.' Mrs. Forrard keeps it. I would quite as soon

[051-010]stay there as at 'The Magpie.' Only if they don't expect you, they

[051-011]wouldn't have much for dinner."

[051-012]"Then you know the village of Allington?"

[051-013]"Yes, I know the village of Allington very well. I have friends

[051-014]living there. Indeed, I may say I know everybody in Allington."

[051-015]"Do you know Mrs. Dale?"

[051-016]"Mrs. Dale?" said Johnny. "Yes, I know Mrs. Dale. I have known Mrs.

[051-017]Dale pretty nearly all my life." Who could this man be who was going

[051-018]down to see Mrs. Dale,--Mrs. Dale, and consequently, Lily Dale?

[051-019]He thought that he knew Mrs. Dale so well, that she could have no

[051-020]visitor of whom he would not be entitled to have some knowledge. But

[051-021]Major Grantly had nothing more to say at the moment about Mrs. Dale.

[051-022]He had never seen Mrs. Dale in his life, and was now going to her

[051-023]house, not to see her, but a friend of hers. He found that he could

[051-024]not very well explain this to a stranger, and therefore at the moment

[051-025]he said nothing further. But Johnny would not allow the subject to be

[051-026]dropped. "Have you known Mrs. Dale long?" he asked.

[051-027]"I have not the pleasure of knowing her at all," said the major.

[051-028]"I thought, perhaps, by your asking after her--"

[051-029]"I intend to call upon her, that is all. I suppose they will have

[051-030]an omnibus here from 'The Magpie?'" Eames said that there no doubt

[051-031]would be an omnibus from "The Magpie," and then they were at their

[051-032]journey's end.

[051-033]For the present we will follow John Eames, who went at once to his

[051-034]mother's house. It was his intention to remain there for two or three

[051-035]days, and then go over to the house, or rather to the cottage, of

[051-036]his great ally Lady Julia, which lay just beyond Guestwick Manor,

[051-037]and somewhat nearer to Allington than to the town of Guestwick. He

[051-038]had made up his mind that he would not himself go over to Allington

[051-039]till he could do so from Guestwick Cottage, as it was called,

[051-040]feeling that, under certain untoward circumstances,--should untoward

[051-041]circumstances arise,--Lady Julia's sympathy might be more endurable

[051-042]than that of his mother. But he would take care that it should be

[051-043]known at Allington that he was in the neighbourhood. He understood

[051-044]the necessary strategy of his campaign too well to suppose that he

[051-045]could startle Lily into acquiescence.

[051-046]With his own mother and sister, John Eames was in these days quite a

[051-047]hero. He was a hero with them now, because in his early boyish days

[051-048]there had been so little about him that was heroic. Then there had

[051-049]been a doubt whether he would ever earn his daily bread, and he had

[051-050]been a very heavy burden on the slight family resources in the matter

[051-051]of jackets and trousers. The pride taken in our Johnny had not been

[051-052]great, though the love felt for him had been warm. But gradually

[051-053]things had changed, and John Eames had become heroic in his mother's

[051-054]eyes. A chance circumstance had endeared him to Earl De Guest, and

[051-055]from that moment things had gone well with him. The earl had given

[051-056]him a watch and had left him a fortune, and Sir Raffle Buffle had

[051-057]made him a private secretary. In the old days, when Johnny's love

[051-058]for Lily Dale was first discussed by his mother and sister, they had

[051-059]thought it impossible that Lily should ever bring herself to regard

[051-060]with affection so humble a suitor;--for the Dales have ever held

[051-061]their heads up in the world. But now there is no misgiving on that

[051-062]score with Mrs. Eames and her daughter. Their wonder is that Lily

[051-063]Dale should be such a fool as to decline the love of such a man. So

[051-064]Johnny was received with the respect due to a hero, as well as with

[051-065]the affection belonging to a son;--by which I mean it to be inferred

[051-066]that Mrs. Eames had got a little bit of fish for dinner as well as a

[051-067]leg of mutton.

[051-068]"A man came down in the train with me who says he is going over to

[051-069]Allington," said Johnny. "I wonder who he can be. He is staying at

[051-070]'The Magpie.'"

[051-071]"A friend of Captain Dale's, probably," said Mary. Captain Dale was

[051-072]the squire's nephew and his heir.

[051-073]"But this man was not going to the squire's. He was going to the

[051-074]Small House."

[051-075]"Is he going to stay there?"

[051-076]"I suppose not, as he asked about the inn." Then Johnny reflected

[051-077]that the man might probably be a friend of Crosbie's, and became

[051-078]melancholy in consequence. Crosbie might have thought it expedient

[051-079]to send an ambassador down to prepare the ground for him before he

[051-080]should venture again upon the scene himself. If it were so, would it

[051-081]not be well that he, John Eames, should get over to Lily as soon as

[051-082]possible, and not wait till he should be staying with Lady Julia?

[051-083]It was at any rate incumbent upon him to call upon Lady Julia the

[051-084]next morning, because of his commission. The Berlin wool might remain

[051-085]in his portmanteau till his portmanteau should go with him to the

[051-086]cottage; but he would take the spectacles at once, and he must

[051-087]explain to Lady Julia what the lawyers had told him about the income.

[051-088]So he hired a saddle-horse from "The Magpie" and started after

[051-089]breakfast on the morning after his arrival. In his unheroic days he

[051-090]would have walked,--as he had done, scores of times, over the whole

[051-091]distance from Guestwick to Allington. But now, in these grander days,

[051-092]he thought about his boots and the mud, and the formal appearance of

[051-093]the thing. "Ah dear," he said, to himself, as the nag walked slowly

[051-094]out of the town, "it used to be better with me in the old days. I

[051-095]hardly hoped that she would ever accept me, but at least she had

[051-096]never refused me. And then that brute had not as yet made his way

[051-097]down to Allington!"

[051-098]He did not go very fast. After leaving the town he trotted on for a

[051-099]mile or so. But when he got to the palings of Guestwick Manor he let

[051-100]the animal walk again, and his mind ran back over the incidents of

[051-101]his life which were connected with the place. He remembered a certain

[051-102]long ramble which he had taken in those woods after Lily had refused

[051-103]him. That had been subsequent to the Crosbie episode in his life, and

[051-104]Johnny had been led to hope by certain of his friends,--especially

[051-105]by Lord De Guest and his sister,--that he might then be successful.

[051-106]But he had been unsuccessful, and had passed the bitterest hour of

[051-107]his life wandering about in those woods. Since that he had been

[051-108]unsuccessful again and again; but the bitterness of failure had not

[051-109]been so strong with him as on that first occasion. He would try

[051-110]again now, and if he failed, he would fail for the last time. As he

[051-111]was thinking of all this, a gig overtook him on the road, and on

[051-112]looking round he saw that the occupant of the gig was the man who had

[051-113]travelled with him on the previous day in the train. Major Grantly

[051-114]was alone in the gig, and as he recognized John Eames he stopped his

[051-115]horse. "Are you also going to Allington?" he asked. John Eames, with

[051-116]something of scorn in his voice, replied that he had no intention of

[051-117]going to Allington on that day. He still thought that this man might

[051-118]be an emissary from Crosbie, and therefore resolved that but scant

[051-119]courtesy was due to him. "I am on my way there now," said Grantly,

[051-120]"and am going to the house of your friend. May I tell her that I

[051-121]travelled with you yesterday?"

[051-122]"Yes, sir," said Johnny. "You may tell her that you came down with

[051-123]John Eames."

[051-124]"And are you John Eames?" asked the major.

[051-125]"If you have no objection," said Johnny. "But I can hardly suppose

[051-126]you have ever heard my name before?"

[051-127]"It is familiar to me, because I have the pleasure of knowing a

[051-128]cousin of yours, Miss Grace Crawley."

[051-129]"My cousin is at present staying at Allington with Mrs. Dale," said

[051-130]Johnny.

[051-131]"Just so," said the major, who now began to reflect that he had been

[051-132]indiscreet in mentioning Grace Crawley's name. No doubt every one

[051-133]connected with the family, all the Crawleys, all the Dales, and all

[051-134]the Eameses, would soon know the business which had brought him down

[051-135]to Allington; but he need not have taken the trouble of beginning the

[051-136]story against himself. John Eames, in truth, had never even heard

[051-137]Major Grantly's name, and was quite unaware of the fortune which

[051-138]awaited his cousin. Even after what he had now been told, he still

[051-139]suspected the stranger of being an emissary from his enemy; but the

[051-140]major, not giving him credit for his ignorance, was annoyed with

[051-141]himself for having told so much of his own history. "I will tell the

[051-142]ladies that I had the pleasure of meeting you," he said; "that is, if

[051-143]I am lucky enough to see them." And then he drove on.

[051-144]"I know I should hate that fellow if I were to meet him anywhere

[051-145]again," said Johnny to himself as he rode on. "When I take an

[051-146]aversion to a fellow at first sight, I always stick to it. It's

[051-147]instinct, I suppose." And he was still giving himself credit for

[051-148]the strength of his instincts when he reached Lady Julia's cottage.

[051-149]He rode at once into the stable-yard, with the privilege of an

[051-150]accustomed friend of the house, and having given up his horse,

[051-151]entered the cottage by the back door. "Is my lady at home, Jemima?"

[051-152]he said to the maid.

[051-153]"Yes, Mr. John; she is in the drawing-room, and friends of yours are

[051-154]with her." Then he was announced, and found himself in the presence

[051-155]of Lady Julia, Lily Dale, and Grace Crawley.

[051-156]He was very warmly received. Lady Julia really loved him dearly, and

[051-157]would have done anything in her power to bring about a match between

[051-158]him and Lily. Grace was his cousin, and though she had not seen him

[051-159]often, she was prepared to love him dearly as Lily's lover. And

[051-160]Lily,--Lily loved him dearly too,--if only she could have brought

[051-161]herself to love him as he wished to be loved! To all of them Johnny

[051-162]Eames was something of a hero. At any rate in the eyes of all of them

[051-163]he possessed those virtues which seemed to them to justify them in

[051-164]petting him and making much of him.

[051-165]"I am so glad you've come,--that is, if you've brought my

[051-166]spectacles," said Lady Julia.

[051-167]"My pockets are crammed with spectacles," said Johnny.

[051-168]"And when are you coming to me?"

[051-169]"I was thinking of Tuesday."

[051-170]"No; don't come till Wednesday. But I mean Monday. No; Monday won't

[051-171]do. Come on Tuesday,--early, and drive me out. And now tell us the

[051-172]news."

[051-173]Johnny swore that there was no news. He made a brave attempt to

[051-174]be gay and easy before Lily; but he failed, and he knew that he

[051-175]failed,--and he knew that she knew that he failed. "Mamma will be so

[051-176]glad to see you," said Lily. "I suppose you haven't seen Bell yet?"
