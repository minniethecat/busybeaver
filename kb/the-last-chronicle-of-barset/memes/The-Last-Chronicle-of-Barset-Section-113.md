[113-001]"Perhaps you had not expected to see me?" he said.

[113-002]"No, indeed, sir."

[113-003]"Nor had I intended when I came over here to call on my old friend,

[113-004]Lady Lufton, to come up to this house. But as I knew that you were

[113-005]here, Miss Crawley, I thought that upon the whole it would be better

[113-006]that I should see you." Then he paused as though he expected that

[113-007]Grace would say something; but Grace had nothing to say. "Of course

[113-008]you must understand, Miss Crawley, that I should not venture to speak

[113-009]to you on this subject unless I myself were very closely interested

[113-010]in it." He had not yet said what was the subject, and it was not

[113-011]probable that Grace should give him any assistance by affecting to

[113-012]understand this without direct explanation from him. She sat quite

[113-013]motionless, and did not even aid him by showing by her altered colour

[113-014]that she understood his purpose. "My son has told me," said he, "that

[113-015]he has professed an attachment for you, Miss Crawley."

[113-016]Then there was another pause, and Grace felt that she was compelled

[113-017]to say something. "Major Grantly has been very good to me," she said,

[113-018]and then she hated herself for having uttered words which were so

[113-019]tame and unwomanly in their spirit. Of course her lover's father

[113-020]would despise her for having so spoken. After all it did not much

[113-021]signify. If he would only despise her and go away, it would perhaps

[113-022]be for the best.

[113-023]"I do not know about being good," said the archdeacon. "I think he is

[113-024]good. I think he means to be good."

[113-025]"I am sure he is good," said Grace, warmly.

[113-026]"You know he has a daughter, Miss Crawley?"

[113-027]"Oh, yes; I know Edith well."

[113-028]"Of course his first duty is to her. Is it not? And he owes much to

[113-029]his family. Do you not feel that?"

[113-030]"Of course I feel it, sir." The poor girl had always heard Dr.

[113-031]Grantly spoken of as the archdeacon, but she did not in the least

[113-032]know what she ought to call him.

[113-033]"Now, Miss Crawley, pray listen to me; I will speak to you very

[113-034]openly. I must speak to you openly, because it is my duty on my son's

[113-035]behalf--but I will endeavour to speak to you kindly also. Of yourself

[113-036]I have heard nothing but what is favourable, and there is no reason

[113-037]as yet why I should not respect and esteem you." Grace told herself

[113-038]that she would do nothing which ought to forfeit his respect and

[113-039]esteem, but that she did not care two straws whether his respect and

[113-040]esteem were bestowed on her or not. She was striving after something

[113-041]very different from that. "If my son were to marry you, he would

[113-042]greatly injure himself, and would very greatly injure his child."

[113-043]Again he paused. He had told her to listen, and she was resolved that

[113-044]she would listen,--unless he should say something which might make a

[113-045]word from her necessary at the moment. "I do not know whether there

[113-046]does at present exist any engagement between you?"

[113-047]"There is no engagement, sir."

[113-048]"I am glad of that,--very glad of it. I do not know whether you are

[113-049]aware that my son is dependent upon me for the greater part of his

[113-050]income. It is so, and as I am so circumstanced with my son, of course

[113-051]I feel the closest possible concern in his future prospects." The

[113-052]archdeacon did not know how to explain clearly why the fact of his

[113-053]making a son an annual allowance should give him a warmer interest

[113-054]in his son's affairs than he might have had had the major been

[113-055]altogether independent of him; but he trusted that Grace would

[113-056]understand this by her own natural lights. "Now, Miss Crawley, of

[113-057]course I cannot wish to say a word that shall hurt your feelings. But

[113-058]there are reasons--"

[113-059]"I know," said she, interrupting him. "Papa is accused of stealing

[113-060]money. He did not steal it, but people think he did. And then we are

[113-061]so very poor."

[113-062]"You do understand me then,--and I feel grateful; I do indeed."

[113-063]"I don't think our being poor ought to signify a bit," said Grace.

[113-064]"Papa is a gentleman and a clergyman, and mamma is a lady."

[113-065]"But, my dear--"

[113-066]"I know I ought not to be your son's wife as long as people think

[113-067]that papa stole the money. If he had stolen it, I ought never to be

[113-068]Major Grantly's wife,--or anybody's wife. I know that very well. And

[113-069]as for Edith,--I would sooner die than do anything that would be bad

[113-070]to her."

[113-071]The archdeacon had now left the rug, and advanced till he was

[113-072]almost close to the chair on which Grace was sitting. "My dear," he

[113-073]said, "what you say does you very much honour,--very much honour

[113-074]indeed." Now that he was close to her, he could look into her

[113-075]eyes, and he could see the exact form of her features, and could

[113-076]understand,--could not help understanding,--the character of her

[113-077]countenance. It was a noble face, having in it nothing that was poor,

[113-078]nothing that was mean, nothing that was shapeless. It was a face that

[113-079]promised infinite beauty, with a promise that was on the very verge

[113-080]of fulfilment. There was a play about her mouth as she spoke, and a

[113-081]curl in her nostril as the eager words came from her, which almost

[113-082]made the selfish father give way. Why had they not told him that

[113-083]she was such a one as this? Why had not Henry himself spoken of the

[113-084]speciality of her beauty? No man in England knew better than the

[113-085]archdeacon the difference between beauty of one kind and beauty

[113-086]of another kind in a woman's face,--the one beauty, which comes

[113-087]from health and youth and animal spirits, and which belongs to the

[113-088]miller's daughter, and the other beauty, which shows itself in fine

[113-089]lines and a noble spirit,--the beauty which comes from breeding.

[113-090]"What you say does you very much honour indeed," said the archdeacon.

[113-091]"I should not mind at all about being poor," said Grace.

[113-092]"No; no; no," said the archdeacon.

[113-093]"Poor as we are,--and no clergyman, I think, ever was so poor,--I

[113-094]should have done as your son asked me at once, if it had been only

[113-095]that,--because I love him."

[113-096]"If you love him you will not wish to injure him."

[113-097]"I will not injure him. Sir, there is my promise." And now as she

[113-098]spoke she rose from her chair, and standing close to the archdeacon,

[113-099]laid her hand very lightly on the sleeve of his coat. "There is my

[113-100]promise. As long as people say that papa stole the money, I will

[113-101]never marry your son. There."

[113-102]The archdeacon was still looking down at her, and feeling the slight

[113-103]touch of her fingers, raised his arm a little as though to welcome

[113-104]the pressure. He looked into her eyes, which were turned eagerly

[113-105]towards his, and when doing so was quite sure that the promise would

[113-106]be kept. It would have been sacrilege,--he felt that it would have

[113-107]been sacrilege,--to doubt such a promise. He almost relented. His

[113-108]soft heart, which was never very well under his own control, gave

[113-109]way so far that he was nearly moved to tell her that, on his son's

[113-110]behalf, he acquitted her of the promise. What could any man's son do

[113-111]better than have such a woman for his wife? It would have been of no

[113-112]avail had he made her such offer. The pledge she had given had not

[113-113]been wrung from her by his influence, nor could his influence have

[113-114]availed aught with her towards the alteration of her purpose. It was

[113-115]not the archdeacon who had taught her that it would not be her duty

[113-116]to take disgrace into the house of the man she loved. As he looked

[113-117]down upon her face two tears formed themselves in his eyes, and

[113-118]gradually trickled down his old nose. "My dear," he said, "if this

[113-119]cloud passes away from you, you shall come to us and be my daughter."

[113-120]And thus he also pledged himself. There was a dash of generosity

[113-121]about the man, in spite of his selfishness, which always made him

[113-122]desirous of giving largely to those who gave largely to him. He would

[113-123]fain that his gifts should be the bigger, if it were possible. He

[113-124]longed at this moment to tell her that the dirty cheque should go for

[113-125]nothing. He would have done it, I think, but that it was impossible

[113-126]for him so to speak in her presence of that which moved her so

[113-127]greatly.

[113-128]He had contrived that her hand should fall from his arm into his

[113-129]grasp, and now for a moment he held it. "You are a good girl," he

[113-130]said--"a dear, dear, good girl. When this cloud has passed away, you

[113-131]shall come to us and be our daughter."

[113-132]"But it will never pass away," said Grace.

[Illustration: "But it will never pass away," said Grace.]

[113-133]"Let us hope that it may. Let us hope that it may." Then he stooped

[113-134]over her and kissed her, and leaving the room, got out into the hall

[113-135]and thence into the garden, and so away, without saying a word of

[113-136]adieu to Mrs. Robarts.

[113-137]As he walked across to the Court, whither he was obliged to go,

[113-138]because of his chaise, he was lost in surprise at what had occurred.

[113-139]He had gone to the parsonage, hating the girl, and despising his son.

[113-140]Now, as he retraced his steps, his feelings were altogether changed.

[113-141]He admired the girl,--and as for his son, even his anger was for the

[113-142]moment altogether gone. He would write to his son at once and implore

[113-143]him to stop the sale. He would tell his son all that had occurred, or

[113-144]rather would make Mrs. Grantly do so. In respect to his son he was

[113-145]quite safe. He thought at that moment that he was safe. There would

[113-146]be no use in hurling further threats at him. If Crawley were found

[113-147]guilty of stealing the money, there was the girl's promise. If he

[113-148]were acquitted, there was his own pledge. He remembered perfectly

[113-149]well that the girl had said more than this,--that she had not

[113-150]confined her assurance to the verdict of a jury, that she had

[113-151]protested that she would not accept Major Grantly's hand as long

[113-152]as people thought that her father had stolen the cheque; but the

[113-153]archdeacon felt that it would be ignoble to hold her closely to her

[113-154]words. The event, according to his ideas of the compact, was to

[113-155]depend upon the verdict of the jury. If the jury should find Mr.

[113-156]Crawley not guilty, all objection on his part to the marriage was to

[113-157]be withdrawn. And he would keep his word! In such case it should be

[113-158]withdrawn.

[113-159]When he came to the rags of the auctioneer's bill, which he had

[113-160]before torn down with his umbrella, he stopped a moment to consider

[113-161]how he would act at once. In the first place he would tell his son

[113-162]that his threats were withdrawn, and would ask him to remain at Cosby

[113-163]Lodge. He would write the letter as he passed through Barchester,

[113-164]on his way home, so that his son might receive it on the following

[113-165]morning; and he would refer the major to his mother for a full

[113-166]explanation of the circumstances. Those odious bills must be removed

[113-167]from every barn-door and wall in the county. At the present moment

[113-168]his anger against his son was chiefly directed against his ill-judged

[113-169]haste in having put up those ill-omened posters. Then he paused to

[113-170]consider what must be his wish as to the verdict of the jury. He had

[113-171]pledged himself to abide by the verdict, and he could not but have a

[113-172]wish on the subject. Could he desire in his heart that Mr. Crawley

[113-173]should be found guilty? He stood still for a moment thinking of this,

[113-174]and then he walked on, shaking his head. If it might be possible he

[113-175]would have no wish on the subject whatsoever.
