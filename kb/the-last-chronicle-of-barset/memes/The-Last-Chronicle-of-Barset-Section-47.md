[047-001]"And that, you think, is a bad sign?"

[047-002]"I don't believe you really care about her. I think you are aware

[047-003]that you have got a love-affair on hand, and that you hang on to it

[047-004]rather persistently, having in some way come to a resolution that

[047-005]you would be persistent. But there isn't much heart in it. I daresay

[047-006]there was once."

[047-007]"And that is your opinion?"

[047-008]"You are just like some of those men who for years past have been

[047-009]going to write a book on some new subject. The intention has been

[047-010]sincere at first, and it never altogether dies away. But the would-be

[047-011]author, though he still talks of his work, knows that it will never

[047-012]be executed, and is very patient under the disappointment. All

[047-013]enthusiasm about the thing is gone, but he is still known as the man

[047-014]who is going to do it some day. You are the man who means to marry

[047-015]Miss Dale in five, ten, or twenty years' time."

[047-016]"Now, Conway, all that is thoroughly unfair. The would-be author

[047-017]talks of his would-be book to everybody. I have never talked of Miss

[047-018]Dale to any one but you, and one or two very old family friends. And

[047-019]from year to year, and from month to month, I have done all that has

[047-020]been in my power to win her. I don't think I shall ever succeed, and

[047-021]yet I am as determined about it as I was when I first began it,--or

[047-022]rather much more so. If I do not marry Lily, I shall never marry at

[047-023]all, and if anybody were to tell me to-morrow that she had made up

[047-024]her mind to have me, I should well nigh go mad for joy. But I am not

[047-025]going to give up all my life for love. Indeed the less I can bring

[047-026]myself to give up for it, the better I shall think of myself. Now

[047-027]I'll go away and call on old lady Demolines.

[047-028]"And flirt with her daughter."

[047-029]"Yes;--flirt with her daughter, if I get the opportunity. Why

[047-030]shouldn't I flirt with her daughter?"

[047-031]"Why not, if you like it?"

[047-032]"I don't like it,--not particularly, that is; because the young lady

[047-033]is not very pretty, nor yet very graceful, nor yet very wise."

[047-034]"She is pretty after a fashion," said the artist, "and if not wise,

[047-035]she is at any rate clever."

[047-036]"Nevertheless, I do not like her," said John Eames.

[047-037]"Then why do you go there?"

[047-038]"One has to be civil to people though they are neither pretty nor

[047-039]wise. I don't mean to insinuate that Miss Demolines is particularly

[047-040]bad, or indeed that she is worse than young ladies in general. I only

[047-041]abused her because there was an insinuation in what you said, that I

[047-042]was going to amuse myself with Miss Demolines in the absence of Miss

[047-043]Dale. The one thing has nothing to do with the other thing. Nothing

[047-044]that I shall say to Miss Demolines will at all militate against my

[047-045]loyalty to Lily."

[047-046]"All right, old fellow;--I didn't mean to put you on your purgation.

[047-047]I want you to look at that sketch. Do you know for whom it is

[047-048]intended?" Johnny took up a scrap of paper, and having scrutinized it

[047-049]for a minute or two declared that he had not the slightest idea who

[047-050]was represented. "You know the subject,--the story that is intended

[047-051]to be told?" said Dalrymple.

[047-052]"Upon my word I don't. There's some old fellow seems to be catching

[047-053]it over the head; but it's all so confused I can't make much of it.

[047-054]The woman seems to be uncommon angry."

[047-055]"Do you ever read your Bible?"

[047-056]"Ah, dear! not as often as I ought to do. Ah, I see; it's Sisera. I

[047-057]never could quite believe that story. Jael might have killed Captain

[047-058]Sisera in his sleep,--for which, by-the-by, she ought to have been

[047-059]hung, and she might possibly have done it with a hammer and a nail.

[047-060]But she could not have driven it through, and staked him to the

[047-061]ground."

[047-062]"I've warrant enough for putting it into a picture, at any rate. My

[047-063]Jael there is intended for Miss Van Siever."

[047-064]"Miss Van Siever! Well, it is like her. Has she sat for it?"

[047-065]"O dear, no; not yet. I mean to get her to do so. There's a strength

[047-066]about her, which would make her sit the part admirably. And I fancy

[047-067]she would like to be driving a nail into a fellow's head. I think I

[047-068]shall take Musselboro for a Sisera."

[047-069]"You're not in earnest?"

[047-070]"He would just do for it. But of course I shan't ask him to sit, as

[047-071]my Jael would not like it. She would not consent to operate on so

[047-072]base a subject. So you really are going down to Guestwick?"

[047-073]"Yes; I start to-morrow. Good-by, old fellow. I'll come and sit for

[047-074]Sisera if you'll let me;--only Miss Van Jael shall have a blunted

[047-075]nail, if you please."

[047-076]Then Johnny left the artist's room and walked across from Kensington

[047-077]to Lady Demolines' house. As he went he partly accused himself, and

[047-078]partly excused himself in that matter of his love for Lily Dale.

[047-079]There were moments of his life in which he felt that he would

[047-080]willingly die for her,--that life was not worth having without

[047-081]her,--in which he went about inwardly reproaching fortune for having

[047-082]treated him so cruelly. Why should she not be his? He half believed

[047-083]that she loved him. She had almost told him so. She could not

[047-084]surely still love that other man who had treated her with such vile

[047-085]falsehood? As he considered the question in all its bearings he

[047-086]assured himself over and over again that there would be now no fear

[047-087]of that rival;--and yet he had such fears, and hated Crosbie almost

[047-088]as much as ever. It was a thousand pities, certainly, that the man

[047-089]should have been made free by the death of his wife. But it could

[047-090]hardly be that he should seek Lily again, or that Lily, if so sought,

[047-091]should even listen to him. But yet there he was, free once more,--an

[047-092]odious being, whom Johnny was determined to sacrifice to his

[047-093]vengeance, if cause for such sacrifice should occur. And thus

[047-094]thinking of the real truth of his love, he endeavoured to excuse

[047-095]himself to himself from that charge of vagueness and laxness which

[047-096]his friend Conway Dalrymple had brought against him. And then again

[047-097]he accused himself of the same sin. If he had been positively in

[047-098]earnest, with downright manly earnestness, would he have allowed the

[047-099]thing to drag itself on with a weak uncertain life, as it had done

[047-100]for the last two or three years? Lily Dale had been a dream to him in

[047-101]his boyhood; and he had made a reality of his dream as soon as he had

[047-102]become a man. But before he had been able, as a man, to tell his love

[047-103]to the girl whom he had loved as a child, another man had intervened,

[047-104]and his prize had been taken from him. Then the wretched victor

[047-105]had thrown his treasure away, and he, John Eames, had been content

[047-106]to stoop to pick it up,--was content to do so now. But there was

[047-107]something which he felt to be unmanly in the constant stooping.

[047-108]Dalrymple had told him that he was like a man who is ever writing

[047-109]a book and yet never writes it. He would make another attempt to

[047-110]get his book written,--an attempt into which he would throw all his

[047-111]strength and all his heart. He would do his very best to make Lily

[047-112]his own. But if he failed now, he would have done with it. It seemed

[047-113]to him to be below his dignity as a man to be always coveting a thing

[047-114]which he could not obtain.

[047-115]Johnny was informed by the boy in buttons, who opened the door for

[047-116]him at Lady Demolines', that the ladies were at home, and he was

[047-117]shown up into the drawing-room. Here he was allowed full ten minutes

[047-118]to explore the knicknacks on the table, and open the photograph book,

[047-119]and examine the furniture, before Miss Demolines made her appearance.

[047-120]When she did come, her hair was tangled more marvellously even than

[047-121]when he saw her at the dinner-party, and her eyes were darker, and

[047-122]her cheeks thinner. "I'm afraid mamma won't be able to come down,"

[047-123]said Miss Demolines. "She will be so sorry; but she is not quite well

[047-124]to-day. The wind is in the east, she says, and when she says the wind

[047-125]is in the east she always refuses to be well."

[047-126]"Then I should tell her it was in the west."

[047-127]"But it is in the east."

[047-128]"Ah, there I can't help you, Miss Demolines. I never know which is

[047-129]east, and which west; and if I did, I shouldn't know from which point

[047-130]the wind blew."

[047-131]"At any rate mamma can't come downstairs, and you must excuse her.

[047-132]What a very nice woman Mrs. Dobbs Broughton is." Johnny acknowledged

[047-133]that Mrs. Dobbs Broughton was charming. "And Mr. Broughton is so

[047-134]good-natured!" Johnny again assented. "I like him of all things,"

[047-135]said Miss Demolines. "So do I," said Johnny;--"I never liked anybody

[047-136]so much in my life. I suppose one is bound to say that kind of

[047-137]thing." "Oh, you ill-natured man," said Miss Demolines. "I suppose

[047-138]you think that poor Mr. Broughton is a little--just a little,--you

[047-139]know what I mean."

[047-140]"Not exactly," said Johnny.

[047-141]"Yes, you do; you know very well what I mean. And of course he is.

[047-142]How can he help it?"

[047-143]"Poor fellow,--no. I don't suppose he can help it, or he

[047-144]would;--wouldn't he?"

[047-145]"Of course Mr. Broughton had not the advantage of birth or much early

[047-146]education. All his friends know that, and make allowance accordingly.

[047-147]When she married him, she was aware of his deficiency, and made up

[047-148]her mind to put up with it."

[047-149]"It was very kind of her; don't you think so?"

[047-150]"I knew Maria Clutterbuck for years before she was married. Of course

[047-151]she was very much my senior, but, nevertheless, we were friends.

[047-152]I think I was hardly more than twelve years old when I first began

[047-153]to correspond with Maria. She was then past twenty. So you see, Mr.

[047-154]Eames, I make no secret of my age."

[047-155]"Why should you?"

[047-156]"But never mind that. Everybody knows that Maria Clutterbuck was

[047-157]very much admired. Of course I'm not going to tell you or any other

[047-158]gentleman all her history."

[047-159]"I was in hopes you were."

[047-160]"Then certainly your hopes will be frustrated, Mr. Eames. But

[047-161]undoubtedly when she told us that she was going to take Dobbs

[047-162]Broughton, we were a little disappointed. Maria Clutterbuck had

[047-163]been used to a better kind of life. You understand what I mean, Mr.

[047-164]Eames?"

[047-165]"Oh, exactly;--and yet it's not a bad kind of life, either."

[047-166]"No, no; that is true. It has its attractions. She keeps her

[047-167]carriage, sees a good deal of company, has an excellent house, and

[047-168]goes abroad for six weeks every year. But you know, Mr. Eames, there

[047-169]is, perhaps, a little uncertainty about it."

[047-170]"Life is always uncertain, Miss Demolines."

[047-171]"You're quizzing now, I know. But don't you feel now, really, that

[047-172]City money is always very chancy? It comes and goes so quick."

[047-173]"As regards the going, I think that's the same with all money," said

[047-174]Johnny.

[047-175]"Not with land, or the funds. Mamma has every shilling laid out in

[047-176]a first-class mortgage on land at four per cent. That does make one

[047-177]feel so secure! The land can't run away."

[047-178]"But you think poor Broughton's money may?"

[047-179]"It's all speculation, you know. I don't believe she minds it; I

[047-180]don't, indeed. She lives that kind of fevered life now that she likes

[047-181]excitement. Of course we all know that Mr. Dobbs Broughton is not

[047-182]what we can call an educated gentleman. His manners are against him,

[047-183]and he is very ignorant. Even dear Maria would admit that."
