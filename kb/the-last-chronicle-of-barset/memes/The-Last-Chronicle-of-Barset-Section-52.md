[052-001]"I only got to Guestwick yesterday afternoon," said he.

[052-002]"And it will be so nice our having Grace at the Small House;--won't

[052-003]it? Uncle Christopher has quite taken a passion for Grace,--so that I

[052-004]am hardly anybody now in the Allington world."

[052-005]"By-the-by," said Johnny, "I came down here with a friend of yours,

[052-006]Grace."

[052-007]"A friend of mine?" said Grace.

[052-008]"So he says, and he is at Allington at this moment. He passed me in a

[052-009]gig going there."

[052-010]"And what was his name?" Lily asked.

[052-011]"I have not the remotest idea," said Johnny. "He is a man about my

[052-012]own age, very good-looking, and apparently very well able to take

[052-013]care of himself. He is short-sighted, and holds a glass in one eye

[052-014]when he looks out of a carriage-window. That's all that I know about

[052-015]him."

[052-016]Grace Crawley's face had become suffused with blushes at the first

[052-017]mention of the friend and the gig; but then Grace blushed very

[052-018]easily. Lily knew all about it at once;--at once divined who must

[052-019]be the friend in the gig, and was almost beside herself with joy.

[052-020]Lady Julia, who had heard no more of the major than had Johnny, was

[052-021]still clever enough to perceive that the friend must be a particular

[052-022]friend,--for she had noticed Miss Crawley's blushes. And Grace

[052-023]herself had no doubt as to the man. The picture of her lover, with

[052-024]the glass in his eye as he looked out of the window, had been too

[052-025]perfect to admit of a doubt. In her distress she put out her hand and

[052-026]took hold of Lily's dress.

[052-027]"And you say he is at Allington now?" said Lily.

[052-028]"I have no doubt he is at the Small House at this moment," said

[052-029]Johnny.

[052-030]CHAPTER XXVIII.

[052-031]SHOWING HOW MAJOR GRANTLY TOOK A WALK.

[052-032][Illustration]

[052-033]Major Grantly drove his gig into the yard of the "Red Lion" at

[052-034]Allington, and from thence walked away at once to Mrs. Dale's house.

[052-035]When he reached the village he had hardly made up his mind as to the

[052-036]way in which he would begin his attack; but now, as he went down

[052-037]the street, he resolved that he would first ask for Mrs. Dale. Most

[052-038]probably he would find himself in the presence of Mrs. Dale and her

[052-039]daughter, and of Grace also, at his first entrance; and if so, his

[052-040]position would be awkward enough. He almost regretted now that he had

[052-041]not written to Mrs. Dale, and asked for an interview. His task would

[052-042]be very difficult if he should find all the ladies together. But he

[052-043]was strong in the feeling that when his purpose was told it would

[052-044]meet the approval at any rate of Mrs. Dale; and he walked boldly

[052-045]on, and bravely knocked at the door of the Small House, as he had

[052-046]already learned that Mrs. Dale's residence was called by all the

[052-047]neighbourhood. Nobody was at home, the servant said; and then, when

[052-048]the visitor began to make further inquiry, the girl explained that

[052-049]the two young ladies had walked as far as Guestwick Cottage, and that

[052-050]Mrs. Dale was at this moment at the Great House with the squire. She

[052-051]had gone across soon after the young ladies had started. The maid,

[052-052]however, was interrupted before she had finished telling all this to

[052-053]the major, by finding her mistress behind her in the passage. Mrs.

[052-054]Dale had returned, and had entered the house from the lawn.

[052-055]"I am here now, Jane," said Mrs. Dale, "if the gentleman wishes to

[052-056]see me."

[052-057]Then the major announced himself. "My name is Major Grantly," said

[052-058]he; and he was blundering on with some words about his own intrusion,

[052-059]when Mrs. Dale begged him to follow her into the drawing-room. He had

[052-060]muttered something to the effect that Mrs. Dale would not know who

[052-061]he was; but Mrs. Dale knew all about him, and had heard the whole

[052-062]of Grace's story from Lily. She and Lily had often discussed the

[052-063]question whether, under existing circumstances, Major Grantly should

[052-064]feel himself bound to offer his hand to Grace, and the mother and

[052-065]daughter had differed somewhat on the matter. Mrs. Dale had held that

[052-066]he was not so bound, urging that the unfortunate position in which

[052-067]Mr. Crawley was placed was so calamitous to all connected with him,

[052-068]as to justify any man, not absolutely engaged, in abandoning the

[052-069]thoughts of such a marriage. Mrs. Dale had spoken of Major Grantly's

[052-070]father and mother and brother and sister, and had declared her

[052-071]opinion that they were entitled to consideration. But Lily had

[052-072]opposed this idea very stoutly, asserting that in an affair of love

[052-073]a man should think neither of father or brother or mother or sister.

[052-074]"If he is worth anything," Lily had said, "he will come to her

[052-075]now,--now in her trouble; and will tell her that she at least has got

[052-076]a friend who will be true to her. If he does that, then I shall think

[052-077]that there is something of the poetry and nobleness of love left."

[052-078]In answer to this Mrs. Dale had replied that women had no right to

[052-079]expect from men such self-denying nobility as that. "I don't expect

[052-080]it, mamma," said Lily. "And I am sure that Grace does not. Indeed I

[052-081]am quite sure that Grace does not expect even to see him ever again.

[052-082]She never says so, but I know that she has made up her mind about

[052-083]it. Still I think he ought to come." "It can hardly be that a man is

[052-084]bound to do a thing, the doing of which, as you confess, would be

[052-085]almost more than noble," said Mrs. Dale. And so the matter had been

[052-086]discussed between them. But now, as it seemed to Mrs. Dale, the man

[052-087]had come to do this noble thing. At any rate he was there in her

[052-088]drawing-room, and before either of them had sat down he had contrived

[052-089]to mention Grace. "You may not probably have heard my name," he said,

[052-090]"but I am acquainted with your friend, Miss Crawley."

[052-091]"I know your name very well, Major Grantly. My brother-in-law who

[052-092]lives over yonder, Mr. Dale, knows your father very well,--or he did

[052-093]some years ago. And I have heard him say that he remembers you."

[052-094]"I recollect. He used to be staying at Ullathorne. But that is a long

[052-095]time ago. Is he at home now?"

[052-096]"Mr. Dale is almost always at home. He very rarely goes away, and I

[052-097]am sure would be glad to see you."

[052-098]Then there was a little pause in the conversation. They had managed

[052-099]to seat themselves, and Mrs. Dale had said enough to put her visitor

[052-100]fairly at his ease. If he had anything special to say to her, he must

[052-101]say it,--any request or proposition to make as to Grace Crawley, he

[052-102]must make it. And he did make it at once. "My object in coming to

[052-103]Allington," he said, "was to see Miss Crawley."

[052-104]"She and my daughter have taken a long walk to call on a friend, and

[052-105]I am afraid they will stay for lunch; but they will certainly be home

[052-106]between three and four, if that is not too long for you to remain at

[052-107]Allington."

[052-108]"O dear, no," said he. "It will not hurt me to wait."

[052-109]"It certainly will not hurt me, Major Grantly. Perhaps you will lunch

[052-110]with me?"

[052-111]"I'll tell you what, Mrs. Dale; if you'll permit me, I'll explain

[052-112]to you why I have come here. Indeed, I have intended to do so all

[052-113]through, and I can only ask you to keep my secret, if after all it

[052-114]should require to be kept."

[052-115]"I will certainly keep any secret that you may ask me to keep," said

[052-116]Mrs. Dale, taking off her bonnet.

[052-117]"I hope there may be no need of one," said Major Grantly. "The truth

[052-118]is, Mrs. Dale, that I have known Miss Crawley for some time,--nearly

[052-119]for two years now, and--I may as well speak it out at once,--I have

[052-120]made up my mind to ask her to be my wife. That is why I am here."

[052-121]Considering the nature of the statement, which must have been

[052-122]embarrassing, I think that it was made with fluency and simplicity.

[052-123]"Of course, Major Grantly, you know that I have no authority with our

[052-124]young friend," said Mrs. Dale. "I mean that she is not connected with

[052-125]us by family ties. She has a father and mother, living, as I believe,

[052-126]in the same county with yourself."

[052-127]"I know that, Mrs. Dale."

[052-128]"And you may, perhaps, understand that, as Miss Crawley is now

[052-129]staying with me, I owe it in a measure to her friends to ask you

[052-130]whether they are aware of your intention."

[052-131]"They are not aware of it."

[052-132]"I know that at the present moment they are in great trouble."

[052-133]Mrs. Dale was going on, but she was interrupted by Major Grantly.

[052-134]"That is just it," he said. "There are circumstances at present which

[052-135]make it almost impossible that I should go to Mr. Crawley and ask his

[052-136]permission to address his daughter. I do not know whether you have

[052-137]heard the whole story?"

[052-138]"As much, I believe, as Grace could tell me."

[052-139]"He is, I believe, in such a state of mental distress as to be hardly

[052-140]capable of giving me a considerate answer. And I should not know how

[052-141]to speak to him, or how not to speak to him, about this unfortunate

[052-142]affair. But, Mrs. Dale, you will, I think, perceive that the same

[052-143]circumstances make it imperative upon me to be explicit to Miss

[052-144]Crawley. I think I am the last man to boast of a woman's regard, but

[052-145]I had learned to think that I was not indifferent to Grace. If that

[052-146]be so, what must she think of me if I stay away from her now?"

[052-147]"She understands too well the weight of the misfortune which has

[052-148]fallen upon her father, to suppose that any one not connected with

[052-149]her can be bound to share it."

[052-150]"That is just it. She will think that I am silent for that reason. I

[052-151]have determined that that shall not keep me silent, and, therefore,

[052-152]I have come here. I may, perhaps, be able to bring comfort to her in

[052-153]her trouble. As regards my worldly position,--though, indeed, it will

[052-154]not be very good,--as hers is not good either, you will not think

[052-155]yourself bound to forbid me to see her on that head."

[052-156]"Certainly not. I need hardly say that I fully understand that,

[052-157]as regards money, you are offering everything where you can get

[052-158]nothing."

[052-159]"And you understand my feeling?"

[052-160]"Indeed, I do,--and appreciate the great nobility of your love for

[052-161]Grace. You shall see her here, if you wish it,--and to-day, if you

[052-162]choose to wait." Major Grantly said that he would wait and would see

[052-163]Grace on that afternoon. Mrs. Dale again suggested that he should

[052-164]lunch with her, but this he declined. She then proposed that he

[052-165]should go across and call upon the squire, and thus consume his time.

[052-166]But to this he also objected. He was not exactly in the humour, he

[052-167]said, to renew so old and so slight an acquaintance at that time. Mr.

[052-168]Dale would probably have forgotten him, and would be sure to ask what

[052-169]had brought him to Allington. He would go and take a walk, he said,

[052-170]and come again exactly at half-past three. Mrs. Dale again expressed

[052-171]her certainty that the young ladies would be back by that time, and

[052-172]Major Grantly left the house.

[052-173]Mrs. Dale when she was left alone could not but compare the good

[052-174]fortune which was awaiting Grace, with the evil fortune which had

[052-175]fallen on her own child. Here was a man who was at all points a

[052-176]gentleman. Such, at least, was the character which Mrs. Dale at once

[052-177]conceded to him. And Grace had chanced to come across this man, and

[052-178]to please his eye, and satisfy his taste, and be loved by him. And

[052-179]the result of that chance would be that Grace would have everything

[052-180]given to her that the world has to give worth acceptance. She would

[052-181]have a companion for her life whom she could trust, admire, love, and

[052-182]of whom she could be infinitely proud. Mrs. Dale was not at all aware

[052-183]whether Major Grantly might have five hundred a year to spend, or

[052-184]five thousand,--or what sum intermediate between the two,--nor did

[052-185]she give much of her thoughts at the moment to that side of the

[052-186]subject. She knew without thinking of it,--or fancied that she knew,

[052-187]that there were means sufficient for comfortable living. It was

[052-188]solely the nature and character of the man that was in her mind, and

[052-189]the sufficiency that was to be found in them for a wife's happiness.

[052-190]But her daughter, her Lily, had come across a man who was a

[052-191]scoundrel, and, as the consequence of that meeting, all her life was

[052-192]marred! Could any credit be given to Grace for her success, or any

[052-193]blame attached to Lily for her failure? Surely not the latter! How

[052-194]was her girl to have guarded herself from a love so unfortunate, or

[052-195]have avoided the rock on which her vessel had been shipwrecked? Then

[052-196]many bitter thoughts passed through Mrs. Dale's mind, and she almost

[052-197]envied Grace Crawley her lover. Lily was contented to remain as she

[052-198]was, but Lily's mother could not bring herself to be satisfied that

[052-199]her child should fill a lower place in the world than other girls. It

[052-200]had ever been her idea,--an idea probably never absolutely uttered

[052-201]even to herself, but not the less practically conceived,--that it is

[052-202]the business of a woman to be married. That her Lily should have been

[052-203]won and not worn, had been, and would be, a trouble to her for ever.
