[027-001]That he was still in love, and in love with the same lady, was known

[027-002]to every one in the office. When it was declared of him that in the

[027-003]way of amatory expressions he had never in his life opened his mouth

[027-004]to another woman, there were those in the office who knew that this

[027-005]was an exaggeration. Mr. Cradell, for instance, who in his early

[027-006]years had been very intimate with John Eames, and who still kept up

[027-007]the old friendship,--although, being a domestic man, with a wife and

[027-008]six young children, and living on a small income, he did not go much

[027-009]out among his friends,--could have told a very different story; for

[027-010]Mrs. Cradell herself had, in days before Cradell had made good his

[027-011]claim upon her, been not unadmired by Cradell's fellow-clerk. But the

[027-012]constancy of Mr. Eames's present love was doubted by none who knew

[027-013]him. It was not that he went about with his stockings ungartered, or

[027-014]any of the old acknowledged signs of unrequited affection. In his

[027-015]manner he was rather jovial than otherwise, and seemed to live a

[027-016]happy, somewhat luxurious life, well contented with himself and the

[027-017]world around him. But still he had this passion within his bosom,

[027-018]and I am inclined to think that he was a little proud of his own

[027-019]constancy.

[027-020]It might be presumed that when Miss Dale wrote to her friend Grace

[027-021]Crawley about going beyond friendship, pleading that there were so

[027-022]many "barriers," she had probably seen her way over most of them. But

[027-023]this was not so; nor did John Eames himself at all believe that the

[027-024]barriers were in a way to be overcome. I will not say that he had

[027-025]given the whole thing up as a bad job, because it was the law of

[027-026]his life that the thing never should be abandoned as long as hope

[027-027]was possible. Unless Miss Dale should become the wife of somebody

[027-028]else, he would always regard himself as affianced to her. He had so

[027-029]declared to Miss Dale herself and to Miss Dale's mother, and to all

[027-030]the Dale people who had ever been interested in the matter. And there

[027-031]was an old lady living in Miss Dale's neighbourhood, the sister of

[027-032]the lord who had left Johnny Eames the bank shares, who always fought

[027-033]his battles for him, and kept a close look-out, fully resolved that

[027-034]John Eames should be rewarded at last. This old lady was connected

[027-035]with the Dales by family ties, and therefore had means of close

[027-036]observation. She was in constant correspondence with John Eames, and

[027-037]never failed to acquaint him when any of the barriers were, in her

[027-038]judgment, giving way. The nature of some of the barriers may possibly

[027-039]be made intelligible to my readers by the following letter from Lady

[027-040]Julia De Guest to her young friend.

[027-041]Guestwick Cottage, -- December, 186--.

[027-042]MY DEAR JOHN,--

[027-043]I am much obliged to you for going to Jones's. I send

[027-044]stamps for two shillings and fourpence, which is what I

[027-045]owe you. It used only to be two shillings and twopence,

[027-046]but they say everything has got to be dearer now, and

[027-047]I suppose pills as well as other things. Only think

[027-048]of Pritchard coming to me, and saying she wanted her

[027-049]wages raised, after living with me for twenty years! I

[027-050]was _very_ angry, and scolded her roundly; but as she

[027-051]acknowledged she had been wrong, and cried and begged my

[027-052]pardon, I did give her two guineas a year more.

[027-053]I saw dear Lily just for a moment on Sunday, and upon my

[027-054]word I think she grows prettier every year. She had a

[027-055]young friend with her,--a Miss Crawley,--who, I believe,

[027-056]is the cousin I have heard you speak of. What is this sad

[027-057]story about her father, the clergyman? Mind you tell me

[027-058]all about it.

[027-059]It is quite true what I told you about the De Courcys. Old

[027-060]Lady De Courcy is in London, and Mr. Crosbie is going to

[027-061]law with her about his wife's money. He has been at it

[027-062]in one way or the other ever since poor Lady Alexandrina

[027-063]died. I wish she had lived, with all my heart. For though

[027-064]I feel sure that our Lily will never willingly see him

[027-065]again, yet the tidings of her death disturbed her, and set

[027-066]her thinking of things that were fading from her mind. I

[027-067]rated her soundly, not mentioning your name, however; but

[027-068]she only kissed me, and told me in her quiet drolling way

[027-069]that I didn't mean a word of what I said.

[027-070]You can come here whenever you please after the tenth of

[027-071]January. But if you come early in January you must go to

[027-072]your mother first, and come to me for the last week of

[027-073]your holiday. Go to Blackie's in Regent Street, and bring

[027-074]me down all the colours in wool that I ordered. I said you

[027-075]would call. And tell them at Dolland's the last spectacles

[027-076]don't suit at all, and I won't keep them. They had better

[027-077]send me down, by you, one or two more pairs to try. And

[027-078]you had better see Smithers and Smith, in Lincoln's Inn

[027-079]Fields, No. 57--but you have been there before,--and beg

[027-080]them to let me know how my poor dear brother's matters are

[027-081]to be settled at last. As far as I can see I shall be dead

[027-082]before I shall know what income I have got to spend. As

[027-083]to my cousins at the manor, I never see them; and as to

[027-084]talking to them about business, I should not dream of it.

[027-085]She hasn't come to me since she first called, and she may

[027-086]be _quite sure_ I shan't go to her till she does. Indeed

[027-087]I think we shall like each other apart quite as much as

[027-088]we should together. So let me know when you're coming,

[027-089]and _pray_ don't forget to call at Blackie's; nor yet at

[027-090]Dolland's, which is much more important than the wool,

[027-091]because of my eyes getting so weak. But what I want you

[027-092]specially to remember is about Smithers and Smith. How is

[027-093]a woman to live if she doesn't know how much she has got

[027-094]to spend?

[027-095]Believe me to be, my dear John,

[027-096]Your most sincere friend,

[027-097]JULIA DE GUEST.

[027-098]Lady Julia always directed her letters for her young friend to his

[027-099]office, and there he received the one now given to the reader. When

[027-100]he had read it he made a memorandum as to the commissions, and

[027-101]then threw himself back in his arm-chair to think over the tidings

[027-102]communicated to him. All the facts stated he had known before; that

[027-103]Lady De Courcy was in London, and that her son-in-law, Mr. Crosbie,

[027-104]whose wife,--Lady Alexandrina,--had died some twelve months since

[027-105]at Baden Baden, was at variance with her respecting money which he

[027-106]supposed to be due to him. But there was that in Lady Julia's letter

[027-107]which was wormwood to him. Lily Dale was again thinking of this

[027-108]man, whom she had loved in old days, and who had treated her with

[027-109]monstrous perfidy! It was all very well for Lady Julia to be sure

[027-110]that Lily Dale would never desire to see Mr. Crosbie again; but

[027-111]John Eames was by no means equally certain that it would be so.

[027-112]"The tidings of her death disturbed her!" said Johnny, repeating to

[027-113]himself certain words out of the old lady's letter. "I know they

[027-114]disturbed me. I wish she could have lived for ever. If he ever

[027-115]ventures to show himself within ten miles of Allington, I'll see if I

[027-116]cannot do better than I did the last time I met him!" Then there came

[027-117]a knock at the door, and the private secretary, finding himself to

[027-118]be somewhat annoyed by the disturbance at such a moment, bade the

[027-119]intruder enter in angry voice. "Oh, it's you, Cradell, is it? What

[027-120]can I do for you?" Mr. Cradell, who now entered, and who, as before

[027-121]said, was an old ally of John Eames, was a clerk of longer standing

[027-122]in the department than his friend. In age he looked to be much older,

[027-123]and he had left with him none of that appearance of the gloss of

[027-124]youth which will stick for many years to men who are fortunate in

[027-125]their worldly affairs. Indeed it may be said that Mr. Cradell was

[027-126]almost shabby in his outward appearance, and his brow seemed to be

[027-127]laden with care, and his eyes were dull and heavy.

[027-128]"I thought I'd just come in and ask you how you are," said Cradell.

[027-129]"I'm pretty well, thank you; and how are you?"

[027-130]"Oh, I'm pretty well,--in health, that is. You see one has so many

[027-131]things to think of when one has a large family. Upon my word, Johnny,

[027-132]I think you've been lucky to keep out of it."

[027-133]"I have kept out of it, at any rate; haven't I?"

[027-134]"Of course; living with you as much as I used to do, I know the whole

[027-135]story of what has kept you single."

[027-136]"Don't mind about that, Cradell; what is it you want?"

[027-137]"I mustn't let you suppose, Johnny, that I'm grumbling about my lot.

[027-138]Nobody knows better than you what a trump I got in my wife."

[027-139]"Of course you did;--an excellent woman."

[027-140]"And if I cut you out a little there, I'm sure you never felt malice

[027-141]against me for that."

[027-142]"Never for a moment, old fellow."

[027-143]"We all have our luck, you know."

[027-144]"Your luck has been a wife and family. My luck has been to be a

[027-145]bachelor."

[027-146]"You may say a family," said Cradell. "I'm sure that Amelia does the

[027-147]best she can; but we are desperately pushed some times,--desperately

[027-148]pushed. I never was so bad, Johnny, as I am now."

[027-149]"So you said the last time."

[027-150]"Did I? I don't remember it. I didn't think I was so bad then.

[027-151]But, Johnny, if you can let me have one more fiver now I have made

[027-152]arrangements with Amelia how I'm to pay you off by thirty shillings a

[027-153]month,--as I get my salary. Indeed I have. Ask her else."

[027-154]"I'll be shot if I do."

[027-155]"Don't say that, Johnny."

[027-156]"It's no good your Johnnying me, for I won't be Johnnyed out of

[027-157]another shilling. It comes too often, and there's no reason why I

[027-158]should do it. And what's more, I can't afford it. I've people of my

[027-159]own to help."

[027-160]"But oh, Johnny, we all know how comfortable you are. And I'm sure no

[027-161]one rejoiced as I did when the money was left to you. If it had been

[027-162]myself I could hardly have thought more of it. Upon my solemn word

[027-163]and honour if you'll let me have it this time, it shall be the last."

[027-164]"Upon my word and honour then, I won't. There must be an end to

[027-165]everything."

[027-166]Although Mr. Cradell would probably, if pressed, have admitted the

[027-167]truth of this last assertion, he did not seem to think that the end

[027-168]had as yet come to his friend's benevolence. It certainly had not

[027-169]come to his own importunity. "Don't say that, Johnny; pray don't."

[027-170]"But I do say it."

[027-171]"When I told Amelia yesterday evening that I didn't like to go to you

[027-172]again, because of course a man has feelings, she told me to mention

[027-173]her name. 'I'm sure he'd do it for my sake,' she said."

[027-174]"I don't believe she said anything of the kind."

[027-175]"Upon my word she did. You ask her."

[027-176]"And if she did, she oughtn't to have said it."

[027-177]"Oh, Johnny, don't speak in that way of her. She's my wife, and you

[027-178]know what your own feelings were once. But look here,--we are in that

[027-179]state at home at this moment, that I must get money somewhere before

[027-180]I go home. I must, indeed. If you'll let me have three pounds this

[027-181]once, I'll never ask you again. I'll give you a written promise if

[027-182]you like, and I'll pledge myself to pay it back by thirty shillings

[027-183]a time out of the two next months' salary. I will, indeed." And

[027-184]then Mr. Cradell began to cry. But when Johnny at last took out his

[027-185]cheque-book and wrote a cheque for three pounds, Mr. Cradell's eyes

[027-186]glistened with joy. "Upon my word I am so much obliged to you! You

[027-187]are the best fellow that ever lived. And Amelia will say the same

[027-188]when she hears of it."
