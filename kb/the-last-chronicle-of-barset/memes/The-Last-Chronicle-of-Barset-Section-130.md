[130-001]"He is not here."

[130-002]"He is in the house, for I heard him. Why have you come back?"

[130-003]Dalrymple's eye fell on the tattered canvas, and he thought of the

[130-004]doings of the past month. He thought of the picture of three Graces,

[130-005]which was hanging in the room below, and he thoroughly wished that he

[130-006]had never been introduced to the Broughton establishment. How was he

[130-007]to get through his present difficulty? "No," said he, "Broughton did

[130-008]not come. It was Mr. Musselboro whose steps you heard below."

[130-009]"What is he here for? What is he doing here? Where is Dobbs? Conway,

[130-010]there is something the matter. He has gone off!"

[130-011]"Yes;--he has gone off."

[130-012]"The coward!"

[130-013]"No; he was not a coward;--not in that way."

[130-014]The use of the past tense, unintentional as it had been, told the

[130-015]story to the woman at once. "He is dead," she said. Then he took both

[130-016]her hands in his and looked into her face without speaking a word.

[130-017]And she gazed at him with fixed eyes, and rigid mouth, while the

[130-018]quick coming breath just moved the curl of her nostrils. It occurred

[130-019]to him at the moment that he had never before seen her so wholly

[130-020]unaffected, and had never before observed that she was so totally

[130-021]deficient in all the elements of real beauty. She was the first to

[130-022]speak again. "Conway," she said, "tell it me all. Why do you not

[130-023]speak to me?"

[130-024]"There is nothing further to tell," said he.

[130-025]Then she dropped his hands and walked away from him to the

[130-026]window,--and stood there looking out upon the stuccoed turret of

[130-027]a huge house that stood opposite. As she did so she was employing

[130-028]herself in counting the windows. Her mind was paralysed by the blow,

[130-029]and she knew not how to make any exertion with it for any purpose.

[130-030]Everything was changed with her,--and was changed in such a way

[130-031]that she could make no guess as to her future mode of life. She was

[130-032]suddenly a widow, a pauper, and utterly desolate,--while the only

[130-033]person in the whole world that she really liked was standing close to

[130-034]her. But in the midst of it all she counted the windows of the house

[130-035]opposite. Had it been possible for her she would have put her mind

[130-036]altogether to sleep.

[130-037]He let her stand for a few minutes and then joined her at the window.

[130-038]"My friend," he said, "what shall I do for you?"

[130-039]"Do?" she said. "What do you mean by--doing?"

[130-040]"Come and sit down and let me talk to you," he replied. Then he led

[130-041]her to the sofa, and as she seated herself I doubt whether she had

[130-042]not almost forgotten that her husband was dead.

[130-043]"What a pity it was to cut it up," she said, pointing to the rags of

[130-044]Jael and Sisera.

[130-045]"Never mind the picture now. Dreadful as it is, you must allow

[130-046]yourself to think of him for a few minutes."

[130-047]"Think of what! O God! yes. Conway, you must tell me what to do. Was

[130-048]everything gone? It isn't about myself. I don't mind about myself. I

[130-049]wish it was me instead of him. I do. I do."

[130-050]"No wishing is of any avail."

[130-051]"But, Conway, how did it happen? Do you think it is true? That man

[130-052]would say anything to gain his object. Is he here now?"

[130-053]"I believe he is here still."

[130-054]"I won't see him. Remember that. Nothing on earth shall make me see

[130-055]him."

[130-056]"It may be necessary, but I do not think it will be;--at any rate not

[130-057]yet."

[130-058]"I will never see him. I believe that he has murdered my husband. I

[130-059]do. I feel sure of it. Now I think of it I am quite sure of it. And

[130-060]he will murder you too;--about that girl. He will. I tell you I know

[130-061]the man." Dalrymple simply shook his head, smiling sadly. "Very well!

[130-062]you will see. But, Conway, how do you know that it is true? Do you

[130-063]believe it yourself?"

[130-064]"I do believe it."

[130-065]"And how did it happen?"

[130-066]"He could not bear the ruin that he had brought upon himself and

[130-067]you."

[130-068]"Then;--then--" She went no further in her speech; but Dalrymple

[130-069]assented by a slight motion of his head, and she had been informed

[130-070]sufficiently that her husband had perished by his own hand. "What am

[130-071]I to do?" she said. "Oh, Conway;--you must tell me. Was there ever so

[130-072]miserable a woman! Was it--poison?"

[130-073]He got up and walked quickly across the room and back again to the

[130-074]place where she was sitting. "Never mind about that now. You shall

[130-075]know all that in time. Do not ask any questions about that. If I were

[130-076]you I think I would go to bed. You will be better there than up, and

[130-077]this shock will make you sleep."

[130-078]"No," she said. "I will not go to bed. How should I know that that

[130-079]man would not come to me and kill me? I believe he murdered Dobbs;--I

[130-080]do. You are not going to leave me, Conway?"

[130-081]"I think I had better, for a while. There are things which should be

[130-082]done. Shall I send one of the women to you?"

[130-083]"There is not one of them that cares for me in the least. Oh, Conway,

[130-084]do not go; not yet. I will not be left alone in the house with him.

[130-085]You will be very cruel if you go and leave me now,--when you have so

[130-086]often said that you,--that you,--that you were my friend." And now,

[130-087]at last, she began to weep.

[130-088]"I think it will be best," he said, "that I should go to Mrs. Van

[130-089]Siever. If I can manage it I will get Clara to come to you."

[130-090]"I do not want her," said Mrs. Broughton. "She is a heartless cold

[130-091]creature, and I do not want to have her near me. My poor husband was

[130-092]ruined among them;--yes, ruined among them. It has all been done that

[130-093]she may marry that horrid man and live here in this house. I have

[130-094]known ever so long that he has not been safe among them."

[130-095]"You need fear nothing from Clara," said Dalrymple, with some touch

[130-096]of anger in his voice.

[130-097]"Of course you will say so. I can understand that very well. And it

[130-098]is natural that you should wish to be with her. Pray go."

[130-099]Then he sat beside her, and took her hand, and endeavoured to speak

[130-100]to her so seriously, that she herself might become serious, and if

[130-101]it might be possible, in some degree contemplative. He told her how

[130-102]necessary it was that she should have some woman near her in her

[130-103]trouble, and explained to her that as far as he knew her female

[130-104]friends, there would be no one who would be so considerate with her

[130-105]as Clara Van Siever. She at one time mentioned the name of Miss

[130-106]Demolines; but Dalrymple altogether opposed the notion of sending for

[130-107]that lady,--expressing his opinion that the amiable Madalina had done

[130-108]all in her power to create quarrels both between Mrs. Broughton and

[130-109]her husband and between Dobbs Broughton and Mrs. Van Siever. And he

[130-110]spoke his opinion very fully about Miss Demolines. "And yet you liked

[130-111]her once," said Mrs. Broughton. "I never liked her," said Dalrymple

[130-112]with energy. "But all that matters nothing now. Of course you can

[130-113]send for her if you please; but I do not think her trustworthy, and

[130-114]I will not willingly come in contact with her." Then Mrs. Broughton

[130-115]gave him to understand that of course she must give way, but that in

[130-116]giving way she felt herself to be submitting to that ill-usage which

[130-117]is the ordinary lot of women, and to which she, among women, had been

[130-118]specially subjected. She did not exactly say as much, fearing that if

[130-119]she did he would leave her altogether; but that was the gist of her

[130-120]plaints and wails, and final acquiescence.

[130-121]"And you are going?" she said, catching hold of his arm.

[130-122]"I will employ myself altogether and only about your affairs, till I

[130-123]see you again."

[130-124]"But I want you to stay."

[130-125]"It would be madness. Look here;--lie down till Clara comes or till I

[130-126]return. Do not go beyond this room and your own. If she cannot come

[130-127]this evening I will return. Good-by now. I will see the servants as I

[130-128]go out, and tell them what ought to be told."

[130-129]"Oh, Conway," she said, clutching hold of him again, "I know that you

[130-130]despise me."

[130-131]"I do not despise you, and I will be as good a friend to you as I

[130-132]can. God bless you." Then he went, and as he descended the stairs he

[130-133]could not refrain from telling himself that he did in truth despise

[130-134]her.

[130-135]His first object was to find Musselboro, and to dismiss that

[130-136]gentleman from the house. For though he himself did not attribute

[130-137]to Mrs. Van Siever's favourite any of those terrible crimes and

[130-138]potentialities for crime, with which Mrs. Dobbs Broughton had

[130-139]invested him, still he thought it reasonable that the poor woman

[130-140]upstairs should not be subjected to the necessity of either seeing

[130-141]him or hearing him. But Musselboro had gone, and Dalrymple could not

[130-142]learn from the head woman-servant whom he saw, whether before going

[130-143]he had told to any one in the house the tale of the catastrophe which

[130-144]had happened in the City. Servants are wonderful actors, looking

[130-145]often as though they knew nothing when they know everything,--as

[130-146]though they understood nothing, when they understand all. Dalrymple

[130-147]made known all that was necessary, and the discreet upper servant

[130-148]listened to the tale with a proper amount of awe and horror and

[130-149]commiseration. "Shot hisself in the City;--laws! You'll excuse me,

[130-150]sir, but we all know'd as master was coming to no good." But she

[130-151]promised to do her best with her mistress,--and kept her promise. It

[130-152]is seldom that servants are not good in such straits as that.

[130-153]From Mrs. Broughton's house Dalrymple went directly to Mrs. Van

[130-154]Siever's, and learned that Musselboro had been there about half an

[130-155]hour before, and had then gone off in a cab with Mrs. Van Siever. It

[130-156]was now nearly four o'clock in the afternoon, and no one in the house

[130-157]knew when Mrs. Van Siever would be back. Miss Van Siever was out,

[130-158]and had been out when Mr. Musselboro had called, but was expected

[130-159]in every minute. Conway therefore said that he would call again, and

[130-160]on returning found Clara alone. She had not then heard a word of

[130-161]the fate of Dobbs Broughton. Of course she would go at once to Mrs.

[130-162]Broughton, and if necessary stay with her during the night. She wrote

[130-163]a line at once to her mother, saying where she was, and went across

[130-164]to Mrs. Broughton leaning on Dalrymple's arm. "Be good to her," said

[130-165]Conway, as he left her at the door. "I will," said Clara. "I will be

[130-166]as kind as my nature will allow me." "And remember," said Conway,

[130-167]whispering into her ear as he pressed her hand at leaving her, "that

[130-168]you are all the world to me." It was perhaps not a proper time for an

[130-169]expression of love, but Clara Van Siever forgave the impropriety.

[130-170]CHAPTER LXV.

[130-171]MISS VAN SIEVER MAKES HER CHOICE.

[130-172]Clara Van Siever did stay all that night with Mrs. Broughton. In the

[130-173]course of the evening she received a note from her mother, in which

[130-174]she was told to come home to breakfast. "You can go back to her

[130-175]afterwards," said Mrs. Van Siever; "and I will see her myself in the

[130-176]course of the day, if she will let me." The note was written on a

[130-177]scrap of paper, and had neither beginning nor end; but this was after

[130-178]the manner of Mrs. Van Siever, and Clara was not in the least hurt

[130-179]or surprised. "My mother will come to see you after breakfast," said

[130-180]Clara, as she was taking her leave.
