# The Handbag

## Definition

The handbag in C.S. Forester's 1926 crime novel *Payment Deferred* functions as the physical container for the money William Marble steals after murdering his nephew James Medland. Passed between the female members of the Marble household — Annie and Winnie — it becomes a tangible locus of the novel's themes of greed, fear, and moral complicity.

## In the Novel

The handbag first appears in the possession of Annie Marble, who "had been clutching her handbag all the morning in terror" [007-051] after receiving the first instalment of Medland's money. The gesture of clutching — a physical manifestation of both possession and anxiety — recurs throughout its appearances. After Annie's death, the handbag passes to Winnie, who receives a hundred pounds and "put the money in her handbag as if she was accustomed to handling large sums" [012-039], [012-045].

In Chapter 14, the handbag contains "a monstrous sum — an amount undreamed of by her a few months ago" [014-021], marking the apex of the Marble family's financial transformation before its collapse.

## Thematic Role

The handbag functions as a silent witness to the women's entanglement in Marble's crime. Neither Annie nor Winnie knows the money's true origin, yet their physical possession of it — fingers curled around the clasp, notes tucked inside — enmeshes them in the moral consequences. The handbag's repeated appearances trace an arc from terror (Annie clutching) through normalisation (Winnie's casual acceptance) to hubris ("monstrous sum"), paralleling the novel's broader trajectory.
