# Poison in Payment Deferred

Potassium cyanide is the murder weapon in both of William Marble's killings. Kept in a locked glass-fronted bathroom cupboard among "manifold bottles" of chemicals (002-023), it is both a domestic commonplace — part of Marble's amateur chemistry hobby — and the instrument of death that drives the novel's plot.

## Context

William Marble keeps a collection of chemicals in the bathroom at 53 Malcolm Road, "neatly arranged" on shelves behind a locked, glass-fronted cupboard door (002-023). His wife Annie has peered into this cupboard "hundreds of times before," looking at the labels without comprehension. The chemicals are part of the furniture of suburban domesticity — unremarkable, mundane, safe.

This ordinariness is precisely what makes cyanide so effective as a murder weapon. It belongs to the household. Its presence raises no suspicion. Marble needs no special access, no suspicious purchase — he simply reaches into his own bathroom cupboard.

## The First Poisoning

Marble administers cyanide to James Medland in whisky. The murder is hastily conceived, executed in panic when Medland demands repayment of a loan. The following morning, Annie notices the cyanide bottle "a little out of its place on the shelf. (As it might be if a man had put it back there in the dark.)" (002-023). The detail lodges in her memory without comprehension — the "queer name stuck in her mind — potassium cyanide" (002-023) — but she turns away "without further thought."

## The Second Poisoning

When Marble decides to kill Annie, cyanide is again his weapon of choice. The method is the same but the context has changed: this is no longer panic but calculation. Marble's medical books, his amateur knowledge of chemistry, and his reading about forensic detection all inform his second attempt. Yet he is caught anyway.

> They proved that she had died of cyanide poisoning, and they proved that Marble had cyanide in his possession. (016-015)

The prosecution's case is straightforward: the poison was in the house, Marble had exclusive access to it, and he had prevented anyone else from nursing Annie. The very domesticity of the weapon — its place in the bathroom cupboard, its ordinariness — becomes the evidence that condemns him.

## Thematic Significance

Poison is an intimate form of murder. Unlike a gun or a knife, it is administered in food or drink, often by someone close to the victim. It turns the routines of domestic life — a shared drink, a meal, a bedside cup of water — into instruments of death. This intimacy makes it the perfect weapon for a novel concerned with the darkness concealed within suburban ordinariness.

Marble's cyanide also functions as a Chekhov's gun: introduced in the early chapters as a detail of household inventory, it becomes the instrument of two deaths and the evidence that sends Marble to the gallows. The locked cupboard, the "mysterious brown" bottles, the neat arrangement — all acquire retrospective menace once the reader understands what they contain.
