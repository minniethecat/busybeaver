# The Armchair

The armchair in the Marble family's dining room at 53 Malcolm Road is William Marble's most cherished object of domestic comfort—a recurring motif that registers his yearning for respite from financial anxiety and, later, from the psychological torment of murder. The armchair appears across the novel in two contrasting forms: the worn but beloved armchair by the Marble fireside, and the various uncomfortable armchairs Marble encounters in the world beyond his home, each measuring his progressive alienation from the domestic sanctuary he has destroyed.

## The Dining-Room Armchair

The Marble household possesses two armchairs, differentiated by their coverings. "Of the two armchairs one was covered with plush, the other with a chintz that blended unhappily with the wallpaper" (001-064). This detailed description, part of the narrator's unsparing inventory of the Marble dining room's hideousness, nonetheless identifies the armchair as the room's most significant furnishing—the seat of the master of the house.

From the novel's opening chapter, the armchair represents Marble's evening ritual of escape. His fantasy, rehearsed nightly, is precise: "When those blessed kids had been packed off to bed he would drag the armchair up to the fire and put his feet up on the coalbox and read the paper and be comfortable for a bit" (001-006). The verb "drag" conveys the armchair's weight and Marble's determination; the ritual of pulling it close to the fire is itself part of the comfort. This fantasy of the armchair, the fire, the whisky, and the newspaper constitutes Marble's entire conception of happiness—a modest, domestic, and ultimately doomed vision.

## Medland in the Armchair

When James Medland arrives unexpectedly, Mrs. Marble instinctively offers him the best seat in the house: the armchair Marble has coveted all evening. Medland "flung himself down in an armchair with a sigh" (002-025), occupying the seat of domestic authority with the casual entitlement of wealth. The young Australian's occupation of the armchair is a symbolic displacement—the rich nephew installed in the poor uncle's throne, a visual prefiguration of the financial transaction Marble hopes to secure and the murderous one he will eventually commit.

After Medland departs, Marble reclaims his territory. "He dragged his armchair up to the fire, and put the sheaf of banknotes in his pocket" (002-057). The armchair ritual resumes, but now it is charged with the presence of Medland's money—the Treasury notes that have already begun to corrupt the domestic sanctuary.

## Uncomfortable Armchairs

The novel contrasts the Marble fireside armchair with its opposites: the armchairs of the wider world that offer no comfort. In Saunders's office, Marble sits in "an uncomfortable late Victorian armchair, facing the window" (006-068), the adjective "uncomfortable" marking his displacement from domestic ease. In the Forward Operation office, he endures "the discomfort of the Empire armchair in which he sat" (008-026), the Empire furniture—bought with a signed cheque—providing the form but not the substance of respectability.

After the murder, Marble "threw himself down in the armchair beside the empty grate" (006-005). The fire that once complemented the armchair is now dead; the comfort the chair once provided is hollowed out by guilt. Yet later, after the scheme's apparent success, Marble sits "comfortably seated in his armchair by the sitting-room window" (011-008), the adverb "comfortably" suggesting a temporary restoration of domestic peace—one that Madame Collins's blackmail will soon shatter.

## Symbolic Significance

The armchair traces Marble's trajectory from domestic anxiety through criminal ambition to hollow comfort. In the early chapters, it is an object of pure desire—the reward that makes the miseries of debt and children and a disappointing marriage bearable. During Medland's visit, it becomes contested territory. After the murder, it is the seat from which Marble "faintly visualiz[es] the sequence of events" (006-108) that his crime has set in motion, no longer a refuge but a vantage point onto his own ruin. The armchair's steady presence across the narrative makes it one of the novel's most understated but persistent symbols of the domestic world that Marble both clings to and destroys.
