# Henderson

Henderson is the executive chief of the [[the-foreign-exchange-department|Foreign Exchange department]] at the [[the-national-county-bank-org|National County Bank]] — [[william-marble|William Marble]]'s immediate superior and the man whose authority [[william-marble|Marble]] must navigate, exploit, and ultimately circumvent to execute his [[franc-speculation|franc speculation]]. He occupies "the partitioned cubbyhole that housed the superior majesty of Henderson" (004-005), a physical arrangement that literalizes his position: always present, always watching, but ultimately unable to see what his subordinate is doing.

## Character

Henderson is barely characterized — he is a **function** rather than a person. He represents institutional authority: the chain of command that [[william-marble|Marble]] must respect in form while violating in substance. His acquiescence in the "first expansion of the deal" provides [[william-marble|Marble]] with "a specious plea of justification for having done all this on his own responsibility" (004-016).

## Role in the Novel

Henderson's role is **structural**: he embodies the institutional framework that [[william-marble|Marble]]'s actions both depend on and subvert. The Bank's systems — its credit ratings, its "[[franc-speculation|forward operation]]" mechanisms, its hierarchies — are the infrastructure of [[william-marble|Marble]]'s scheme. Henderson is the human face of this infrastructure: well-meaning, professionally competent, and entirely deceived.

## Key Passages

> (004-005) Mr. Marble was at his desk farthest from the entrance to the room, and close beside the partitioned cubbyhole that housed the superior majesty of Henderson, chief of the department.

## The Indulgent Superior

Henderson's attitude toward Marble is described with Forester's characteristic economy. The head of the department "noticed it frequently" that Marble had been drinking and looked worried, "but he took no drastic action. For one thing, he was glad to have an inefficient second, as that enabled him to keep the work more closely under his own hand, thereby making his retention of his job more probable, and for another he had a queer sort of liking for 'poor old Marble,' with his worried expression, and his worried eyes, and his worried moustache" (004-011). Henderson's tolerance is not kindness but self-interest: Marble's deterioration makes Henderson look more essential by comparison. The "queer sort of liking" is genuine enough — it's the affection of the secure for the harmless — but it blinds Henderson to the "keen mind — razor keen still, despite too much whisky of late" that exists beneath Marble's surface incompetence (004-011).

Forester adds a devastating coda: "Mr. Henderson, of course, knew nothing of a piece of work that Marble had performed marvellously efficiently some months ago" (004-011). The "piece of work" is, of course, the murder — the one task at which Marble's "razor keen" mind and "fount of potential fierce energy" have been applied with complete success. Henderson's professional assessment of Marble is simultaneously accurate and catastrophically wrong: Marble is indeed a poor second-in-command, but the reason Henderson cannot see is not incompetence but guilt.

## The Cubbyhole and the Tape Machine

The spatial organization of the Foreign Exchange department encodes its power structure. Marble sits at the desk "farthest from the entrance" but "close beside the partitioned cubbyhole that housed the superior majesty of Henderson" (004-005) — near enough to be watched, far enough to operate unseen. On the morning of the franc speculation's climax, Marble arrives early and goes "straight to Mr. Henderson's room... that private sanctum which only he and Henderson had the right to enter" to check the tape machine (004-015). The violation of Henderson's private space is symbolic: the authority Marble defers to in form, he usurps in practice.

Later, when Marble must speak to Saunders on the telephone about the speculation, "he had to pick his words, for Henderson was within earshot, and it would never do for him to know that he was acting in collusion with one of the Bank's customers" (004-016). The entire scheme plays out within Henderson's hearing — a performance of normalcy conducted inches from the man whose ignorance is its enabling condition.
