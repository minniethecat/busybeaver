# Panic and Terror in Payment Deferred

The acute psychological states of blind panic and sustained terror that grip William Marble after his crime, forming the novel's dominant emotional register.

## Definition

In *Payment Deferred*, panic is the sudden, overwhelming fear that periodically seizes William Marble — the "blind panic" that threatens to betray him. Terror is the underlying, persistent dread that governs his every waking moment: the Damocles' sword of discovery hanging perpetually over his head.

## In the Novel

Marble's psychological arc moves from the "blind panic of the earlier months" after the murder (003-017) to a colder, more permanent state of terror. Yet panic never fully recedes: it returns in crises, as when "blind panic began to overmaster him" during a moment of suspicion (006-100), forcing him to grip a chair for steadiness until "his panic passed" (006-103).

The terror is existential. Marble lives in "terror of the Damocles' sword of discovery" (006-055), and the narrator observes that his family endures a "panic-stricken existence" (013-001). This terror is not merely internal — it expresses itself physically: Marble starts "out of his chair in a panic" at unexpected sounds (008-040), and his wife Annie experiences moments of "blind terror that made her knees tremble" (007-053).

By the novel's later chapters, the terror has become Marble's defining trait: he "spends every waking minute in terror of being hanged" (012-046), transforming the murderer from active agent to passive victim of his own fear.

## Thematic Role

Panic and terror function as the novel's psychological realism. They demonstrate that the true punishment for murder is not the gallows but the years of fear preceding it. Marble's terror is the mechanism by which the novel argues that crime contains its own punishment — the murderer is never free, never at peace, never safe.

## Key Passages
