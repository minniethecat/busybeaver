# The Soda Siphon

## Definition

The soda siphon is a household appliance used by William Marble to dispense carbonated water into whisky tumblers on the night he murders his nephew James Medland. It appears in the extended murder scene of Chapter 1, functioning as an accessory to the novel's central crime — its hiss of soda water providing an aural detail to the otherwise silent, methodical preparation of the poisoned drink.

## In the Novel

The siphon appears exclusively in the murder scene, where it plays a crucial practical role: Marble uses it to add soda water to both tumblers, diluting the whisky and, more importantly, masking any unusual taste from the potassium cyanide dissolved in Medland's glass. "He lifted the siphon, and it hissed into the tumblers" [001-153] — the sibilant "hissed" is one of the few sounds Forester records in this otherwise eerily quiet scene.

The siphon's role is ancillary but indispensable: without soda, Medland might have detected the cyanide's bitterness and the murder would have failed at its first step. The siphon thus enables the crime through its ordinary function, a domestic object pressed into lethal service.

## Thematic Role

The soda siphon belongs to the same thematic register as the tumbler and the decanter: objects of suburban domesticity that become instruments of murder without changing their essential nature. Its hiss — the sound of a perfectly ordinary drink being prepared — is the aural signature of the novel's central irony: that murder can wear the face of hospitality.
