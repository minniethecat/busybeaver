# The Purse

## Definition

The purse belonging to Annie Marble in C.S. Forester's 1926 crime novel *Payment Deferred* is the intimate personal container into which she places the first fifty-pound note from James Medland's estate. Smaller and more personal than the handbag, it represents the moment of initial receipt — the physical act of accepting stolen money.

## In the Novel

The purse appears in two early chapters, both centred on Annie's receipt of money from her husband. In Chapter 7, William gives Annie a fifty-pound note: "With fifty pounds in her purse she would be able to go down to the shops" [007-008], and she "thrust it into her purse" [007-010]. The verb "thrust" conveys an urgency and possessiveness that belies her usual timid demeanour. Later, in Chapter 10, "when she had it safely in her purse, she would melt with gratitude" [010-008], the purse serving as a token of her bought loyalty.

The purse's appearances are concentrated in the novel's middle section, when the Marbles are first adjusting to their sudden wealth. It does not appear in the later chapters, replaced by the larger handbag that holds progressively greater sums.

## Thematic Role

Where the handbag represents public display and the transmission of the money between family members, the purse is private — it is Annie's alone. The act of thrusting the note into her purse is the novel's most concentrated image of moral compromise through small, physical gestures. The purse absorbs the money without questioning its origin, just as Annie absorbs her husband's story without probing its implausibility.
