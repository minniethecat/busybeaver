# Bond Street

**Bond Street** is a shopping street in London's West End, appearing in *Payment Deferred* as a destination for the Marbles' extravagant spending after William Marble's sudden acquisition of wealth through currency speculation.

## The Shopping Expedition

After Marble speculated successfully on the French franc and came into a fortune, he took his wife Annie on a Saturday shopping expedition. Following a morning of purchasing Empire furniture at Tottenham Court Road (007-035), Marble hailed a taxicab and directed the driver: "Bond Street" (007-040). The couple alighted at Bond Street tube station and walked slowly down the street, gazing at shop windows (007-042).

## Mrs. Marble's Experience

For Annie Marble, Bond Street represented an intimidating world of wealth and sophistication far beyond her experience. When her husband instructed her to enter a women's clothing shop, she clung to his arm, protesting "Oh, I can't, Will, I can't. I—I don't like to" (007-045). Marble dismissed her hesitation, announcing "his contempt for Selfridge's to all Bond Street" (007-049) and insisting she spend the fifty pounds he had given her.

Inside the shop, Mrs. Marble experienced acute class anxiety. She became painfully aware that her clothes were "dowdy" and that her hat was "not, in the eyes of those aristocratic assistants, a hat at all" (007-054). She realised "in a blinding flash that these people mentally divided the world into the clothed and the unclothed, and to them she was on no higher plane than a naked savage" (007-054).

## Later Associations

Mrs. Marble later associated Bond Street with her extravagant but ill-matched wardrobe. Among her possessions were "pastel shade silk underclothes bought in Bond Street" mixed indiscriminately with "wool and cotton mixture garments" from her poorer days (009-039). She never returned to Bond Street, finding even High Street, Kensington "a little above her head" (014-006), and instead did her shopping in Rye Lane, Peckham.

## Significance

Bond Street serves in the novel as a symbol of the Marbles' uneasy relationship with wealth. Mr. Marble's insistence on shopping there reflects his desire to display his newfound prosperity, while Mrs. Marble's discomfort exposes the class barriers that money alone cannot bridge. The episode illustrates the gulf between the Marbles' suburban life and the fashionable London they attempt to enter.
