Conscience and [[guilt-and-conscience|remorse]] in Payment Deferred are notable primarily for their absence in the novel's protagonist. [[william-marble|William Marble]] experiences [[fear-in-payment-deferred|anxiety]], [[fear-in-payment-deferred|fear]], and suspicion, but never [[guilt-and-conscience|remorse]] in any recognizable form. The novel's treatment of [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]] is thus more interesting for what it withholds than for what it depicts, making it a study in the limits of moral psychology.

## Definition

Conscience, traditionally understood as the internal faculty that distinguishes right from wrong and produces [[guilt-and-conscience|guilt]] when wrong is committed, operates unevenly in the novel. [[william-marble|Marble]] has "somewhere inside his [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]] there was a nasty feeling" [001-005] — but this feeling attaches to his financial failures, not to his [[murder-method|murder]]. [[annie-marble|Annie]] is "occasionally [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]]-stricken" [010-036] about her neglect of her family, suggesting that [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]] survives in her, but in a diminished and ineffectual form. The novel's psychological realism lies in this uneven distribution of moral awareness.

## In the Novel

[[william-marble|Marble]]'s relationship to [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]] is defined by displacement — he feels [[guilt-and-conscience|guilt]] about the wrong things. Before the [[murder-method|murder]], his [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]] troubles him about his [[financial-desperation|debt]]s and his failures as a provider [001-005], but never about his scheme to exploit his nephew. After the [[murder-method|murder]], he feels no [[guilt-and-conscience|remorse]] for killing [[medland|Medland]]; his psychological energy is entirely consumed by the [[fear-in-payment-deferred|fear]] of being caught. The question "Is your [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]] troubling you?" [014-010] — which no character can bring themselves to ask directly — hangs unspoken over [[53-malcolm-road|the Marble house]]hold. The novel's conclusion offers no moment of moral reckoning: [[william-marble|Marble]] goes to his execution without having experienced genuine [[guilt-and-conscience|remorse]], making him one of literature's more chillingly plausible criminals.

## Thematic Role

The absence of [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]] is the novel's **darkest thematic claim** — that ordinary people can commit terrible acts without experiencing the moral emotions that convention ascribes to them. [[william-marble|Marble]] is not a monster but a bank clerk, and his lack of [[guilt-and-conscience|remorse]] is not psychopathy but a kind of moral compartmentalization: he has taught himself to see the [[murder-method|murder]] as a business decision rather than a moral act. The novel thus raises uncomfortable questions about the relationship between action and [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]] that it deliberately declines to answer.
