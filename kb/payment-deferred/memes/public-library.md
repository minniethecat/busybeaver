# Public Library

The **Public Library** is a local lending library appearing in *Payment Deferred*, where William Marble borrows books on crime and criminal investigation following his murder of James Medland.

## Marble's First Visit

Shortly after the murder, Marble visited the local Public Library and obtained a reader's ticket. The librarian noted him as "a little man in a seedy blue suit, with a ragged, red moustache and weak blue eyes" who went through the formalities and then "demanded a selection of books on crime" (003-008). The librarian dated the ticket and handed it over unwillingly, harbouring a particular dislike for the "omnivorous browser who read all sorts of things," suspecting such readers of a "prurient desire to lay hold of those books which even a Public Library has to a small extent" (003-013).

## Criminal Research

Marble used the library to study the methods and fates of criminals. Through his reading he discovered that "two out of every three of the criminals mentioned came to grief through inability to dispose of the body," which caused the hair to bristle on his head and filled him with "a horrible sensation of despair" (003-016). He read accounts of Crippen's life in London with a body buried in his cellar and made a "mental note that he must get another book from the library in which he could look that up" regarding identification of remains (003-016).

## Expanding the Collection

As Marble's wealth grew, he sought to acquire his own library. After purchasing Empire furniture for 53 Malcolm Road, he ordered "a crate of books he had ordered that morning—crime books, mystery books, all the books he had seen advertised at the back of the limited selection available at the Public Library, and had coveted during his poverty" (008-026). His personal library became "rapidly-expanding and catholic" in its coverage of criminal matters (009-011).

## Significance

The Public Library serves as Marble's gateway to criminal knowledge, reflecting his desperate attempt to understand and avoid the fate of other murderers. His progression from borrowing books at the Public Library to building his own extensive collection of crime literature mirrors his growing wealth and his deepening entanglement in guilt and fear.
