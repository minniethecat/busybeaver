The body in the cellar at [[53-malcolm-road|53 Malcolm Road]] is the physical evidence of [[william-marble|William Marble]]'s [[murder-method|murder]] of his nephew James [[medland|Medland]]. The novel's plot turns on the disposal and [[secrecy|concealment]] of this body, but its true narrative function is psychological: the buried body becomes a constant, invisible presence that haunts [[william-marble|Marble]]'s every waking moment.

## Role in the Novel

The [[the-burial-in-the-garden|burial]] of [[medland|Medland]]'s body in the cellar is the practical problem that occupies [[william-marble|Marble]] immediately after the [[murder-method|murder]]. The novel's early chapters detail his careful excavation of the cellar floor, his calculations about soil displacement, and his constant [[fear-in-payment-deferred|anxiety]] about discovery [003-015]. The body transforms [[53-malcolm-road|53 Malcolm Road]] from a home into a crime scene, and [[william-marble|Marble]]'s relationship to his own house becomes permanently altered — he lives above the evidence of his [[guilt-and-conscience|guilt]], and the physical proximity of the corpse to his daily life creates an unbearable psychological pressure.
