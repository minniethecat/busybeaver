# Dream and Nightmare in Payment Deferred

The blurring of dream and reality that characterizes William Marble's psychological experience after his crime, where waking life becomes indistinguishable from nightmare.

## Definition

In *Payment Deferred*, dreams and nightmares serve as a narrative device for exploring Marble's fractured psyche. The boundary between sleep and waking dissolves: Marble repeatedly questions whether the murder was real or "a wild dream" [003-003], and his daily existence takes on the quality of a "nightmare world" [012-003] from which he cannot awaken.

## In the Novel

The motif appears in the novel's opening pages, when Annie Marble hears her husband muttering in his sleep and thinks she "must have been dreaming" [002-003]. Marble himself, upon waking, insists "I wasn't dreaming" [002-011] — a denial that implicitly admits how dreamlike his reality has become.

Marble experiences his life as "too like a fantastic nightmare for anything really to have happened" [003-003]. The murder, the buried body, the constant fear — all feel unreal to him, as though they belong to someone else's story. On the verge of sleep, "he would realize that it was all real" [003-003], and the recognition brings fresh horror.

The banker Saunders, learning the truth, behaves "as though in a dream" [004-074], suggesting that the nightmare is contagious — those who come into contact with Marble's secret enter the same unreal space. By the novel's end, the "nightmare world" [012-003] is not merely Marble's internal state but the objective condition of the household.

## Annie's Dream: The Night of the Murder

The dream motif enters the novel not through Marble but through his wife. On the morning after Medland's murder, Annie awakens with a confused memory of the night: "She must have been dreaming that someone had called out loud, and had awakened still dreaming it" [002-003]. Her muddled recollection includes "a hazy, muddled recollection of hearing a strange noise downstairs, as though something was being dragged along the linoleum of the passage downstairs, and one or two sharp taps as though some things were dropping sharply from one step to another on the little dark staircase just outside the kitchen door" [002-003].

The passage is laden with dramatic irony. Annie dismisses as a dream precisely what happened: a body being dragged along the linoleum, footsteps on the cellar stairs. Her rationalising mind — "what a silly thing to dream!" [002-003] — protects her from knowledge that would destroy her world. Forester's irony is that Annie's dream is the truth, and her waking life, in which her husband is merely a "nice little baby" [002-004], is the real fantasy.

## Night Terrors: Marble's Sleeping and Waking

After the murder, sleep becomes for Marble both a refuge and a torture chamber. "He would wake sweating in the night too, with the fevered blood running hot under his skin, as some fantastic possibility developed in his sleeping mind" [003-002]. The phrase "fantastic possibility" captures the mechanism: Marble's sleeping mind generates scenarios — detection, exposure, arrest — that are entirely realistic but feel surreal because of their intensity.

The worst dreams involve not the future but the past. "And sometimes, when he had been sleeping very badly, one or two horrible memories returned to him: memories of a pair of staring eyes, and of a boyish face, the mouth smeared with foam" [003-002]. The foam on Medland's lips — a forensic detail from Marble's reading about cyanide poisoning — has been internalised and returns in nightmare, blurring the boundary between what Marble has done and what he has read.

By day, Marble craves company precisely because it makes the murder seem unreal. "Drugged by Annie's talk, or by John's or Winnie's chatter about school, Mr. Marble not infrequently succeeded in persuading himself that it was indeed a dream" [003-003]. The verb "drugged" is significant: domestic chatter functions as an anaesthetic, temporarily suspending Marble's awareness of his own guilt. But the relief is temporary: "when night came, Mr. Marble paid for his relaxation. Then, on the verge of sleep, he would realize that it was not a dream" [003-003].

## The Contagious Nightmare

The dream state extends beyond Marble to those who encounter his secret. When Saunders accepts Marble's financial scheme, he moves "as though in a dream" [004-074] — a trance-like compliance that suggests Marble's unreality is communicable. Saunders, the cautious banker, allows himself to be led into illegal speculation because the encounter with Marble has temporarily suspended his ordinary judgment.

The contagion reaches its fullest expression in the Marble household after the second murder. "Back into this nightmare world came Winnie, flushed with triumphs at school" [012-003]. The adjective "flushed" — suggesting life, health, the normal world of school triumphs — collides with "nightmare world," creating a dissonance that defines the novel's closing movement. Winnie's vitality cannot penetrate the nightmare; if anything, her presence makes the nightmare more visible by contrast.

## "Half Dream, Half Nightmare": Marguerite and the Evenings of Escape

The novel's most explicit fusion of dream and waking occurs during Marble's relationship with Marguerite Collins. "They were strange evenings, half dream, half nightmare" [010-018]. The formulation is precise: the evenings with Marguerite are not pure nightmare (they offer genuine escape) but neither are they pure dream (the secret in the garden remains). Marble "could lose himself within the nest of her warm, white arms more completely than had ever been possible to him before" [010-018] — a temporary obliteration of consciousness that is the closest the novel offers to genuine respite.

Yet the morning brings a different quality of waking: "Even the awakening next morning, blear-eyed and foul-mouthed, was not as wretched as might have been thought. For at least he had grateful solitude" [010-019]. The solitude is "grateful" because no one looks at him with knowledge. Annie's "anxious look" [010-019] is the gaze he most wishes to avoid, because her eyes, even uncomprehending, register his deterioration.

## The Phantom Wife

The novel's most disturbing deployment of the dream motif concerns Annie's transformation from wife to phantom. "His wife was a nobody now, a phantom less real than the squint-eyed hangman that so often came tapping him on the shoulder; she was a soft-footed ghost that wandered about the house" [011-001]. The hangman — a creature of pure imagination — is more real to Marble than the woman he lives with. Annie's reduction to phantom status is the novel's darkest comment on the marriage: she has become unreal to him precisely because her reality (her knowledge, her suffering, her continued existence) is intolerable.

## Winnie's Return and the Persistence of Nightmare

Winnie's return from school in the novel's closing chapters provides the decisive test of the nightmare motif. She arrives with all the trappings of normal life — "flushed with triumphs" [012-003], dressed "to perfection," her beauty having "won over to her one party at school" [012-003]. But the house she enters is not the home she left. The narrative deliberately withholds the reunion scene, cutting instead to the nightmare persistence: the household has become a space where the normal rhythms of family life have been replaced by "the solitude of the suburbs" [012-001], a silence maintained by shared knowledge that cannot be spoken.

## Thematic Role

The dream/nightmare motif externalizes Marble's guilt and denial. If the murder was a dream, he can wake from it; but the recurring realization that "it was all real" is the novel's mechanism of moral reckoning. The nightmare is truth insisting on itself against the psyche's wish to escape into fantasy.
