Fate and chance in Payment Deferred operate as the novel's hidden antagonists — the unpredictable forces that no amount of criminal planning can anticipate or neutralize. [[william-marble|Marble]] believes he can control events through careful preparation, but the novel systematically demonstrates that chance favours detection, arranging coincidences that transform a perfect crime into an inevitable catastrophe.

## Definition

Fate, in the novel's implicit philosophy, is not supernatural predestination but the statistical tendency of hidden truths to surface. Chance is the specific mechanism — the unexpected knock, the overheard conversation, the casual observation — through which fate operates. Together they form a kind of narrative physics: the pressure of the concealed truth increases over time until something, somewhere, gives way.

## In the Novel

The novel's pivotal chance events are numerous: [[william-marble|Marble]] "had gambled with fate and he had won" [004-079] after his Franc speculation succeeds, but this early success is the novel's cruel joke — fate allows the small gamble to succeed so that the larger one can fail more spectacularly. The [[murder-method|murder]] itself is partly chance: [[medland|Medland]]'s unexpected visit, the convenient presence of the [[poison|cyanide]]. After the [[murder-method|murder]], chance events accumulate: [[annie-marble|Annie]]'s chance encounter with the dressmaker [010-001], [[the-marble-children|the children]]'s innocent questions, the neighbour's chance observation of [[the-garden-at-53-malcolm-road|the garden]] excavations. Each is trivial in isolation; together they form a chain that leads inexorably to the police knock. [[william-marble|Marble]]'s "edge of desperation against the fate that would inevitably close upon him" [009-047] captures his growing awareness that chance is not on his side.

## Thematic Role

Chance functions as the novel's **moral randomizer** — it introduces the element of unpredictability that makes crime untenable as a rational strategy. [[william-marble|Marble]]'s tragedy is not that he is evil but that he is a rationalist who believes the world can be controlled through planning, and the novel's counter-argument is that the world is too complex, too full of other people and their unpredictable actions, for any plan to hold. The thematic implication is conservative in the deepest sense: the existing order — social, moral, legal — has a resilience that individual defiance cannot overcome.
