# Alcohol and Escapism

Whisky is [[william-marble|William Marble]]'s "only friend" in C. S. Forester's "Payment Deferred" — a nightly ritual of self-medication that both enables his crimes and accelerates his destruction. The novel's treatment of alcohol is clinically precise: it shows drinking not as melodramatic dissolution but as the quiet, desperate habit of a man who cannot bear his own thoughts. From the opening pages, "the custom had died out months before, when Marble had begun turning to the decanter in the sideboard for distraction from his troubles" (001-101), and the decanter becomes the novel's most constant presence — more reliable than family, more demanding than conscience.

## Definition

[[william-marble|Marble]]'s relationship with alcohol is defined by its **dual function**: as anesthetic (numbing the [[fear-in-payment-deferred|fear]] and [[guilt-and-conscience|guilt]] that threaten to overwhelm him) and as catalyst (lowering the inhibitions that might otherwise prevent his most dangerous decisions). The whisky bottle in the [[the-sideboard|sideboard]] is both comfort and trap — the one source of relief that ultimately deepens his isolation. "All he ever attempted was to reach the happy stage — after the grim preliminary period when his imagination had been stimulated — when he was unable to think connectedly, so that he could not work out the long trains of thought that led inevitably to the picturing of detection and the scaffold" (011-008).

## In the Novel

The novel establishes [[william-marble|Marble]]'s drinking pattern in its opening pages. After [[the-marble-children|the children]] are sent to bed, his thoughts turn obsessively to "the [[the-whisky-decanter|decanter]] in the [[the-sideboard|sideboard]]": "In the decanter in the sideboard there was a little drop of whisky left. Not much of course; three drinks perhaps, or maybe four. Mr. Marble hoped it was four. With a drink and a paper and the fire he could forget his troubles for a little" (001-006). The precision of this accounting — counting drinks before they are poured — reveals both the centrality of alcohol to his evening and the scarcity that governs his life. Notably, Marble has "an objection to drinking whisky in front of his children" (001-007), a residual shred of propriety that demonstrates how even his addiction bends to the demands of middle-class performance. "The whisky would taste all the better if he could come to it fresh from a parental triumph and an autocratic exertion of authority" (001-007).

Alcohol serves as [[william-marble|Marble]]'s primary coping mechanism throughout. After the [[murder-method|murder]], [[the-whisky-decanter|the whisky]] becomes more necessary and less effective. "He was not even happy sitting alone in the dining-room at Malcolm Road, with a full bottle of his only friend beside him... The whisky only made him think the more at first. It took a long time before he could stop his mind from thinking about what might happen" (003-003). The alcohol that once provided escape now only postpones — defers — the reckoning, mirroring the novel's central metaphor. In the same passage, Forester describes the worst moments: "At times the prospect so frightened him that he flinched from his preliminary torture, and would go through the evening without drinking at all, although every fibre of his body seemed to be shrieking for it" (003-003).

Drinking also shapes the murder itself. The night Medland visits, Marble pours whisky with "great deliberation" (001-120), carefully managing the amount so that "there should be enough for two drinks left in the decanter" — part of his half-formed plan to drug his guest. The decanter chatters against the glass because his hand shakes. The day after the murder, Marble eyes the bottle and reflects: "He felt he needed some, despite the fact that he had already had three or four — or was it five or six? — whiskies that day. He was very tired, very, very tired... How his arms ached with that digging!" (002-049). Drink and murder are braided together from the start.

In a crucial [[irony-in-payment-deferred|irony]], [[william-marble|Marble]]'s drinking also facilitates his most dangerous social interactions. It is over whisky at [[the-public-house|the public house]] round the corner from the Bank that he recruits [[saunders|Saunders]] for the [[franc-speculation|franc speculation]] (004-012). "The girl at the bar knew him, and the double Scotch was ready for him before even he was within reach of it" — his drinking is public, habitual, part of his identity (004-012). The same substance that isolates him in private enables his boldest public performance.

As the novel progresses, drinking becomes Marble's full-time occupation. "His father was drunk five nights out of seven" (009-004), John observes. Marble's drinking follows a precise economy: "He could reach this stage comfortably quite early in the morning before the effects of the previous day's drinking had worn off, and then he was able to keep like it for the rest of the day by the simple mechanical process of drinking every time he found his thoughts taking an unpleasant line" (011-008). His wife is reduced to a "convenient person to send out, green string bag in hand, to the grocer's to buy more whisky when his reserve dropped below the amount he had fixed upon — two untouched bottles" (011-008).

Even at the Bank, his drinking is noticed: "He always looked worried, and clearly often he had been drinking. The head of the department... noticed it frequently, but he took no drastic action" (004-001). His superior Henderson is "heartily glad that Marble had apparently struggled out of the financial difficulties" and fails to see that "somewhere in Marble there was a keen mind — razor keen still, despite too much whisky of late" (004-001). The tragedy is that Marble's intelligence remains sharp enough to bring off the franc speculation and sharp enough to know he is destroying himself.

## Thematic Role

Alcohol functions as the novel's **chemical metaphor for [[deferred-payment|deferral]]**: it postpones pain without removing its source, and the relief it provides becomes progressively less effective, requiring larger doses for diminishing returns. This mirrors the novel's larger argument about moral evasion — that all forms of [[deferred-payment|deferral]] (financial, psychological, chemical) share the same structure of diminishing returns and accumulating costs. The whisky that once brought relief becomes another debt — a deferred reckoning that Marble can never pay.

The novel is notably unsentimental about alcoholism. It does not moralize and it does not romanticize. It simply records — with the same precision Marble uses to count his drinks — the mechanics of a man drinking himself into a corner from which there is no exit. "A man who has five hundred a year for which he can find no use does not worry about odd hundreds, especially when he spends every waking minute in terror of being hanged" (012-046). The wealth that should liberate him cannot compete with the terror that alcohol can only temporarily muffle.
