# Cyanide

The bottle of [[poison|potassium cyanide]] kept by [[william-marble|William Marble]] in the locked bathroom cupboard — the instrument of [[james-medland|James Medland]]'s [[murder-method|murder]] and, ultimately, the means of [[annie-marble|Annie Marble]]'s suicide when she discovers her husband's crime.

## Description

The [[poison|cyanide]] exists in the novel as a physical object with precise spatial coordinates: it occupies a specific shelf in a locked, glass-fronted cupboard in the [[william-marble|Marble]]s' bathroom at [[53-malcolm-road|53 Malcolm Road]]. [[annie-marble|Annie]] observes it during her morning rounds: "The locked, glass-fronted cupboard in which Will kept his chemicals hung on the wall beside her. She peered in, as she had done hundreds of times before" (002-023). The bottle is one among many — "some of the bottles were of a mysterious brown, and some were white" — distinguished only by the "queer name" on its label that "stuck in her mind — [[poison|potassium cyanide]]" (002-023).

The cupboard is locked, and [[william-marble|Marble]] keeps the keys. This physical barrier — glass door, metal key — becomes a critical forensic detail at trial, where [[dr-atkinson|Dr. Atkinson]] testifies that [[annie-marble|Annie]] "would be quite incapable of going into the bathroom to get it for herself" (016-015), disproving [[william-marble|Marble]]'s suggestion that she died by her own hand. Yet in the novel's final [[irony-in-payment-deferred|irony]], [[annie-marble|Annie]] does reach the bottle — crawling, swaying, fumbling with keys — and uses it to end her own life after discovering [[william-marble|Marble]]'s [[guilt-and-conscience|guilt]].

## In the Novel

The [[poison|cyanide]] appears at four critical junctures, each marking a stage in the novel's fatal progression:

**Discovery (Chapter II)**. [[annie-marble|Annie]] notices the bottle during her [[domestic-life-at-53-malcolm-road|household]] routine, the morning after [[william-marble|Marble]] has [[murder-method|murder]]ed [[james-medland|Medland]]. The name "[[poison|potassium cyanide]]" registers in her memory without comprehension — a casual detail observed and forgotten: "She turned away from the cupboard without further thought" (002-023). Forester plants the detail with the unobtrusiveness of Chekhov's gun. [[annie-marble|Annie]] had earlier heard [[william-marble|Marble]]'s keys rattle as "he unlocked, she supposed, his photographic cupboard" (002-002) on the night of the murder.

**Research (Chapter V)**. [[william-marble|Marble]] reads a *[[the-handbook-to-medical-jurisprudence|Handbook to Medical Jurisprudence]]* from the [[the-free-library-institution|Free Library]], finding the [[poison|cyanide]] section particularly compelling: "Death is practically instantaneous. The patient utters a loud cry and falls heavily. There may be some foam at the lips, and after death the body often retains the appearance of life" (005-007). He already knows this — he has already used [[poison|cyanide]] on [[james-medland|Medland]]. The reading is retrospective self-education, the [[murder-method|murder]]er studying his own method. He stops before reaching the section on treatment: "it was easy to see that there would rarely be an opportunity of treating a sufferer from [[poison|cyanide]] [[murder-method|poisoning]]" (005-009).

**Discovery Repeated (Chapter XI)**. [[annie-marble|Annie]], ill and suspicious, retrieves [[william-marble|Marble]]'s crime book. Her thumb rests "between the pages, at the point where there was that interesting description of the effects of potassium cyanide" (011-014). When [[william-marble|Marble]] enters, the book "fell open at the passage on the [[poison|cyanide]]s" (011-015). "He saw the look on her face, too. He stopped aghast. There was no need for words. In that brief space of time he realized that his wife knew. That she *knew*" (011-016). Neither could speak: "They eyed each other in poses curiously alike, she with her hand to her bosom as she peered at him, fluttering, tearful, and he with his hand, too, over his heart" (011-017). [[annie-marble|Annie]] "gave a little, inarticulate cry. The book fell to the ground from her hand, and then sobbing, she fled from the room" (011-018). The [[poison|cyanide]] has become knowledge — shared, fatal knowledge.

**Suicide (Chapter XV)**. Dying already, poisoned slowly by [[william-marble|Marble]]'s neglect and deliberately by the tainted sustenance he brings her, [[annie-marble|Annie]] crawls to the bathroom. She retrieves the keys from [[william-marble|Marble]]'s dressing-table, opens the locked cupboard, takes down the bottle: "She took it in her hand, fondled it, she almost smiled as she looked at it" (015-054). She fills a medicine glass, replaces the bottle tidily, and "tried to bow to it; she tried to say 'thank you' to it" (015-055). She crawls back to her bed, "pushing the medicine glass in front of her, the keys trailing" (015-056), settles herself neatly with "her boudoir cap straight and the lace at her throat" (015-057), and drinks. But the [[poison|cyanide]], "kept in solution for over a year, slowly reacting with itself and with the atmosphere" (015-058), does not grant her a quick death.

**Trial (Chapter XVI)**. The [[poison|cyanide]] becomes forensic evidence. The prosecution proves [[william-marble|Marble]] "had [[poison|cyanide]] in his possession" and that [[annie-marble|Annie]] died of [[poison|cyanide]] [[murder-method|poisoning]]. The "much thumbed and dirty" page on [[poison|cyanide]] [[murder-method|poisoning]] in his crime book, worn "through constant study" (016-015), clinches the case against him.

## Symbolism

The [[poison|cyanide]] functions as the novel's central symbolic object, embodying several interlocking themes:

**Knowledge and its consequences**. The [[poison|cyanide]] is knowledge materialized. [[william-marble|Marble]]'s technical understanding of the poison (from his chemical hobby and crime books) enables [[murder-method|murder]]; [[annie-marble|Annie]]'s gradual comprehension of what the bottle signifies (from the same books) seals both their fates. The chain of causation runs: chemical knowledge to [[murder-method|murder]], to forensic knowledge, to detection, to suicide, to execution. The [[poison|cyanide]] is the constant threading through every link.

**Domestic poison**. The [[poison|cyanide]] lives in the family bathroom, among other [[domestic-life-at-53-malcolm-road|household]] chemicals, behind a locked glass door. Its domestic location mirrors the novel's insistence that [[murder-method|murder]] is not exotic but domestic — committed by a suburban bank clerk in a semi-detached villa, using materials at hand. The horror of *Payment Deferred* is not that [[william-marble|Marble]] is a monster but that he is ordinary, and the ordinary [[domestic-life-at-53-malcolm-road|household]] contains everything needed for [[murder-method|murder]].

**The locked cupboard**. The glass-fronted cupboard — visible but inaccessible — mirrors the novel's structure of hidden knowledge. [[annie-marble|Annie]] sees the bottle daily without understanding what it means; [[william-marble|Marble]] sees his wife's knowing look and understands everything in an instant. The glass door separates observation from comprehension, a barrier that collapses in a single moment of recognition (011-016).

**Decay and justice**. The degraded [[poison|cyanide]] — "slowly reacting with itself and with the atmosphere" over a year (015-058) — gives [[annie-marble|Annie]] "not an easy death, not a quick death." The poison's deterioration mirrors the moral decay of [[william-marble|Marble]] himself, and the slow, painful death it inflicts on [[annie-marble|Annie]] prefigures the delayed but inexorable justice that awaits him (016-015).

## Key Passages

> The locked, glass-fronted cupboard in which Will kept his chemicals hung on the wall beside her. She peered in, as she had done hundreds of times before. The manifold bottles meant nothing to her; but she liked to look at the labels and wonder what they were all about. Some of the bottles were of a mysterious brown, and some were white. They were all very neatly arranged. Except one, and that was a little out of its place on the shelf. (As it might be if a man had put it back there in the dark.) Annie glanced casually at the label. It conveyed nothing to her at all, but it happened that the queer name stuck in her mind — potassium cyanide. She turned away from the cupboard without further thought. (002-023)

> Mr. Marble spent the evening sitting in the drawing-room of 53 Malcolm Road; on his knee was the last of the Free Library books on crime. It was very interesting — a Handbook to Medical Jurisprudence... The comments on the cyanides were particularly interesting: "Death is practically instantaneous. The patient utters a loud cry and falls heavily. There may be some foam at the lips, and after death the body often retains the appearance of life, the cheeks being red and the expression unaltered." (005-006, 005-007)

Her thumb was between the pages, at the point where there was that interesting description of the effects of potassium cyanide... the book fell open at the passage on the cyanides. Mr. Marble saw this. He saw the look on her face, too. He stopped aghast. There was no need for words. In that brief space of time he realized that his wife knew. That she knew. (011-014, 011-015, 011-016)

Then she thrust the letter back into the drawer and closed it. Somehow she was still able to think clearly. The next thing she wanted was the key. All Will's keys were on a ring on the dressing-table. She had to crawl there to get them. Then she crawled — oh, so slowly — out through the door to the bathroom. (015-053, 015-054)
