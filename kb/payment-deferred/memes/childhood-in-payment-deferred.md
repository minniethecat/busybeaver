Childhood in Payment Deferred is portrayed through the experiences of [[winnie-marble|Winnie]] and [[john-marble|John Marble]], who occupy a peripheral yet structurally significant position in the novel. The children function as silent observers of the [[william-marble|Marble]] household's descent from [[financial-desperation|financial desperation]] into crime, their presence adding a dimension of moral consequence to the adult tragedy unfolding around them. Both children attend secondary schools on scholarships, a detail that underscores the family's precarious lower-middle-class position [005-004].

## The Children as Narrative Witnesses

[[winnie-marble|Winnie]] and [[john-marble|John]] are introduced in the novel's opening scene, where [[annie-marble|Mrs. Marble]] immediately silences them: "Be quiet, children," she says, "Can't you see that father's busy?" [001-001]. Throughout the early chapters, they are repeatedly positioned as bystanders — present in the room, absorbing tension, yet excluded from adult affairs. When [[william-marble|Mr. Marble]] sends them to bed early in a fit of irritation, it is not discipline but displacement of his own [[financial-desperation|financial anxiety]] onto the nearest available targets [001-023].

The domestic routine of the children is established through their evening activities. John does homework for school while Winnie sews — "John was deep in homework he had to prepare for school, and Winnie sewed a button on her glove in the interval of eating porridge" [002-016]. The quiet of these scenes is punctuated only by "the scratching of Winnie's pen and the whispering of the two children to themselves as they toiled over their homework" [002-038], creating a portrait of ordinary domesticity against which the novel's violent events stand in stark relief.

## Parental Indifference and Instrumentalisation

[[william-marble|Marble]]'s attitude toward his children is characterised by irritation and expediency. He thinks of them as obstacles to his comfort, resenting that "the children must be disposed of first" before he can enjoy his whisky [001-007]. His oft-repeated "threat of sending the children to bed without any supper" [001-006] is less about discipline than about clearing the domestic stage for his own consolation. Marble harboured "an objection to drinking whisky in front of his children" [001-007], a scruple that reveals less about his moral sensibility than about his need to preserve a private space for his solitary consolation.

After [[medland|Jim Medland]]'s arrival, the children are briefly animated — "both children writhed in delight" [001-091] at the Australian's tales — but this moment of family warmth is exceptional and quickly extinguished. The children's relationship with their mother is marked by [[annie-marble|Annie]]'s distracted affection: "Both John and Winnie kissed their father awkwardly, and their mother casually" [001-101], a detail that captures the emotional texture of the Marble household — duty without warmth, proximity without intimacy.

## The Children After the Murder

Following the [[murder-method|murder]] of [[annie-marble|Annie]], the children become an unspoken complication. [[william-marble|Marble]]'s concern at night — "listening to hear if either of his children was frightened" [001-150] — is telling: he fears not for their welfare but for what their fear might reveal. The children's continued presence in the house, their ordinary routines of tea and homework [002-025], create an ironic counterpoint to the concealed body of their mother. They represent the domestic normality that [[william-marble|Marble]] is attempting to maintain as a facade, even as the foundations of that normality have been destroyed.

The murder transforms the children's relationship with each other and their father. "All John knew was that there was trouble in the family, terrible trouble, too, and he guessed, childlike, at his father's hatred for him and resented it with a hatred equally bitter" [009-005]. This passage is significant: John intuits the emotional reality of the household without understanding its material cause. His hatred mirrors his father's — an emotional inheritance that darkens the novel's closing chapters.

## John Marble as Protector and Adversary

John Marble's role intensifies in the novel's later sections as he becomes aware of the violence within the household. "Only twice had John to interfere actively between his mother and his father, fearing that he would do her an injury, but John knew in his soul that there was a far worse trouble than drunkenness in his home" [009-004]. This passage marks John's transition from passive witness to active participant in the family's crisis. His awareness is partial — he does not know of the murder — but his emotional comprehension outstrips his factual knowledge.

The father-son relationship reaches its nadir in a scene of mutual recognition: "At the end of it John knew that he hated his father; and his father knew that he hated his son" [006-104]. This is a moment of terrible clarity. Forester presents the hatred as fully reciprocal, neither party innocent. Marble's resentment of John stems partly from the boy's resemblance to his mother and partly from the threat John represents — a witness who cannot be silenced.

## Winnie Marble and the Flight to School

[[winnie-marble|Winnie]]'s response to the family crisis differs from her brother's. "Winnie had gone back to school, glad to escape from her mother's helpless unhappiness and her father's brooding gloom" [011-001]. Her departure constitutes a form of self-preservation that John, as the younger child, cannot emulate. The school functions as a refuge, a world outside the claustrophobic confines of [[domestic-life-at-53-malcolm-road|53 Malcolm Road]] where the normal rhythms of life continue.

Winnie's escape is enabled by the same educational aspiration that defines the Marble family's social position. Marble "sent his children to secondary schools — on scholarships, it is true, and after some years at public elementary schools" [005-004]. The scholarships are a marker of the family's precarious gentility: respectable enough to value education, but too poor to pay for it. The children's schooling thus serves a double function in the novel — it places them within the social aspirations of the lower middle class while also providing Winnie with the physical escape that John lacks.

## Childhood and Moral Consequence

The children's presence makes the Marble tragedy not merely a crime story but a moral one. Each act of violence — the [[murder-method|murder]] of Annie, the financial deception, the drinking — reverberates through the children's experience. Marble contemplates the newspaper reports of family tragedies with a kind of premonitory dread: "He supposed it would end like the cases one read about in the paper, with his children's throats cut and himself and his wife dead of gas-poisoning" [001-006]. The irony is that he himself, not some external agent, is the threat to his children's welfare.

In one of the novel's most charged scenes, "The children looked anxiously at their father" [006-046] as the family's fortune appears to change. Their anxiety is revealing: they have learned to read their father's moods, to calibrate their own emotional states against the barometer of his temper. This is the education that no scholarship can fund — the education of fear.
