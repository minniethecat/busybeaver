# Marguerite Collins

Marguerite Collins is the French wife of [[collins|Collins]] — a peripheral figure who appears only in the novel's final chapters, when the [[collins|Collins]] [[domestic-life-at-53-malcolm-road|household]] provides a brief counterpoint to the [[william-marble|Marble]] family's disintegration. Her [[marriage-in-payment-deferred|marriage]] to [[collins|Collins]], contracted during "a violent interval in [[rouen|France]] during the last year of the war" (010-001), represents a form of ordinary domesticity that the [[william-marble|Marble]]s never achieved.

## Character

Marguerite is barely sketched — a function of her husband's story rather than an independent presence. She is French, married impulsively during wartime, and now lives in a drab English suburb where her husband tunes pianos. Her dissatisfaction with "drab" English life is mentioned in passing, a quiet echo of the larger discontent that pervades the novel.

## Role in the Novel

Marguerite's role is **contrastive**: she and [[collins|Collins]] represent an alternative domestic arrangement — imperfect, disappointed in its own ways, but not [[murder-method|murder]]ous. Their brief appearance near the novel's end suggests that the horrors of [[53-malcolm-road|53 Malcolm Road]] are not inevitable products of suburban life but specific outcomes of specific choices.

## Key Passages

> (010-001) Collins was a dull and a tragic figure. He was cursed with an intense sensitiveness for music combined with complete absence of creative talent. All his life, with the exception of a violent interval in France during the last year of the war culminating in his marriage to Marguerite, he had earned his living tuning pianos.
