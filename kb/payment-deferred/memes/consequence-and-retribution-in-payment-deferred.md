Consequence and retribution in Payment Deferred take forms both formal and informal. The novel traces the arc from [[murder-method|crime]] to punishment, but the punishment that matters most is not the legal sentence at the end but the psychological [[fear-in-payment-deferred|torment]] that precedes it. [[william-marble|Marble]] is punished long before the law catches up with him — by his own [[imagination-and-reality-in-payment-deferred|imagination]], by [[guilt-and-conscience|anxiety]], and by the inexorable logic of cause and effect.

## Definition

Consequence, in the novel, is the inevitable result of action — the chain of causation that [[william-marble|Marble]] sets in motion with his [[murder-method|crime]] and cannot control. Retribution is the punishment that follows: partly legal (arrest and execution), partly psychological ([[fear-in-payment-deferred|fear]] and [[guilt-and-conscience|remorse]]), and partly ironic (the novel's structure of "poetic justice" [010-012]). The novel distinguishes between the consequences [[william-marble|Marble]] anticipates and those he cannot foresee — the second category proving far more destructive than the first.

## In the Novel

After the [[murder-method|murder]], "the consequences of the evening's work were presented to him in every possible variation" [006-108]. [[william-marble|Marble]]'s [[imagination-and-reality-in-payment-deferred|imagination]] constructs an exhaustive catalogue of possible punishments: arrest, trial, condemnation, execution. But these anticipated consequences are not the ones that destroy him. The unforeseen consequence is the psychological toll — the inability to sleep, the [[fear-in-payment-deferred|paranoia]], the progressive isolation from his family. "The more he had to lose the more anxious he was not to lose it, and the more difficult it became in consequence" [009-011] — this is the novel's formulation of the ironic law of consequence: the attempt to secure gain makes loss more likely.

The novel invokes the concept of "poetic justice" explicitly: "It would be poetic justice if it were so — newspaper poetic justice" [010-012]. This is [[william-marble|Marble]] reflecting on the possibility that his secret might be discovered while he is away from home, distracted by [[madame-collins|Madame Collins]]. The qualifier "newspaper" is significant — it suggests that poetic justice is a literary convention, not a reliable feature of reality. The novel is interested in the gap between the retribution that would satisfy a reader's sense of justice and the retribution that actually occurs.

[[annie-marble|Annie]]'s perspective on consequence is simpler: "money — so said her peasant soul to herself — was sweeter than revenge at any time" [013-016]. For her, practical gain trumps moral satisfaction — a value system that implicates her, however passively, in her husband's crime.

## Thematic Role

Consequence and retribution are the novel's mechanisms for asserting moral order — but the order they assert is ambiguous. [[william-marble|Marble]] is caught and executed, satisfying the demands of formal justice. But the novel devotes far more attention to the informal punishments — [[fear-in-payment-deferred|fear]], [[guilt-and-conscience|anxiety]], [[dream-and-nightmare|sleeplessness]] — that precede and arguably exceed the legal penalty. The novel's position is that retribution is real but unreliable: it may come through the law, through psychology, or through chance, but it cannot be escaped through planning or cleverness.
