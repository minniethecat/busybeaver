# The Tiepin

## Definition

The diamond tiepin is a piece of jewellery worn by William Marble, the protagonist of C.S. Forester's 1926 crime novel *Payment Deferred*. It is one of three personal accessories — alongside his gold cigarette case and wristwatch — that collectively project an image of prosperity and success, masking the financial strain and moral corruption beneath.

## In the Novel

The tiepin makes two notable appearances, both in contexts that foreground Marble's concern with public presentation. During his interview with the police, "the diamond in Mr. Marble's tiepin winked and glittered as he shook with fright" [008-048] — the jewel's ostentatious sparkle creating an ironic counterpoint to his terror. Later, the tiepin is listed among the accessories that define his public image: "Mr. Marble himself, tiepin, wristwatch, cigarette case, well-cut clothes" [010-002].

The tiepin's appearances are concentrated in the novel's middle chapters, when Marble's financial position — funded by Medland's money — is at its peak. It vanishes from the narrative as his circumstances deteriorate in the later chapters, implicitly pawned or abandoned along with his other pretensions.

## Thematic Role

The tiepin belongs to a constellation of objects that construct Marble's outward identity. It is the smallest and most intimate of these signifiers — worn at the throat, closest to the voice with which he lies. Its diamond's glitter during the police interview visually enacts the gap between surface (wealth, respectability) and substance (guilt, terror) that defines the novel's dramatic irony.
