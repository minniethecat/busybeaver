# Poison (Cyanide)

[[cyanide|Cyanide]] [[murder-method|poisoning]] is the method by which [[william-marble|William Marble]] [[murder-method|murder]]s his wife [[annie-marble|Annie]] in C. S. Forester's "Payment Deferred" — a choice driven by its accessibility, rapid lethality, and the protagonist's meticulous research into [[forensic-medicine-in-payment-deferred|forensic medicine]].

## Definition

[[cyanide|Cyanide]], in the form of potassium cyanide or hydrocyanic (prussic) acid, is a rapidly acting poison that causes death within seconds to minutes. In the novel, it appears as a bottle of potassium cyanide kept in [[william-marble|William Marble]]'s locked, glass-fronted chemical cupboard in the bathroom at [[53-malcolm-road|53 Malcolm Road]] (002-023). [[william-marble|Marble]] studies its effects through a *[[the-handbook-to-medical-jurisprudence|Handbook to Medical Jurisprudence]]* borrowed from the [[the-free-library-institution|Free Library]], finding the section on cyanides "particularly interesting" because of their "infinitely superior deadliness" (005-006). The Handbook describes death as "practically instantaneous" — the patient "utters a loud cry and falls heavily," with foam at the lips and the body retaining "the appearance of life" (005-007). [[william-marble|Marble]] stops reading before the section on treatment, reasoning that "there would rarely be an opportunity of treating a sufferer from [[poison|cyanide]] [[murder-method|poisoning]]" (005-009).

## In the Novel

[[william-marble|William Marble]]'s use of cyanide begins before he opens the Handbook. The novel implies that his first victim is [[james-medland|James Medland]], his wife's young nephew, whom [[william-marble|Marble]] murders with cyanide from the bathroom cupboard on the night of [[james-medland|Medland]]'s visit — [[annie-marble|Annie]] later recalls hearing [[william-marble|Marble]] go to the bathroom where his chemicals were kept, and then hearing "that loud cry" (011-012), matching exactly the Handbook's description of cyanide death (005-007).

After a financial windfall creates both opportunity and motive, [[william-marble|Marble]] visits the [[the-free-library-institution|Free Library]] to study poisons retrospectively, focusing on cyanides for their rapid action and accessibility (005-006). The *[[the-handbook-to-medical-jurisprudence|Handbook]]* becomes his morbid self-education — the murderer studying his own method. He reads "all about inquests, and the methods for discovering whether a dead body found in the water had been put there after death or not" before reaching the toxicology section (005-006).

[[william-marble|Marble]]'s research extends beyond method to forensics. He learns that "nine [[murder-method|murder]]ers out of ten were only discovered through some silly mistake" (005-010), and that careful preparation — acquiring the poison inconspicuously, ensuring no witnesses, planning the disposal — could theoretically allow a [[murder-method|murder]]er to escape detection. The cyanide itself comes from his own bathroom: [[annie-marble|Annie]] had noticed the bottle of potassium cyanide among [[william-marble|Marble]]'s chemicals, its label "a little out of its place on the shelf. (As it might be if a man had put it back there in the dark)" (002-023).

The [[poison|cyanide]] becomes the novel's fatal nexus. [[annie-marble|Annie]], ill and suspicious, retrieves [[william-marble|Marble]]'s crime book from the sitting-room shelf. She reads the passage on cyanides, her thumb "between the pages, at the point where there was that interesting description of the effects of potassium cyanide" (011-014). When [[william-marble|Marble]] enters, the book "fell open at the passage on the cyanides" (011-015). "He saw the look on her face, too. He stopped aghast. There was no need for words. In that brief space of time he realized that his wife knew. That she *knew*" (011-016). Neither could speak; they "eyed each other in poses curiously alike, she with her hand to her bosom as she peered at him, fluttering, tearful, and he with his hand, too, over his heart" (011-017). [[annie-marble|Annie]] fled the room sobbing (011-018).

[[william-marble|Marble]] subsequently poisons [[annie-marble|Annie]] with cyanide — a slow administration over days, mixed into food and drink during her illness. In her final act, dying already, [[annie-marble|Annie]] crawls to the bathroom, retrieves the keys, opens the locked cupboard, and takes down the bottle: "She took it in her hand, fondled it, she almost smiled as she looked at it" (015-054). But the cyanide, "kept in solution for over a year, slowly reacting with itself and with the atmosphere" (015-058), does not grant her a quick death.

At trial, the prosecution proves [[annie-marble|Annie]] died of [[poison|cyanide]] [[murder-method|poisoning]] and that [[william-marble|Marble]] "had cyanide in his possession" (016-015). [[dr-atkinson|Dr. Atkinson]] testifies that [[annie-marble|Annie]] "would be quite incapable of going into the bathroom to get it for herself" (016-015) — though the reader knows she did, in her final desperate crawl. The "much thumbed and dirty" page on cyanide poisoning in [[william-marble|Marble]]'s crime book, worn "through constant study" (016-015), clinches the case.

## Thematic Role

[[cyanide|Cyanide]] [[murder-method|poisoning]] serves multiple thematic functions in the novel. **As a plot device**, it is the irreversible act that transforms [[william-marble|Marble]] from a desperate [[financial-desperation|debt]]or into a [[murder-method|murder]]er — he was already a murderer when he walked to Saunders' office, and the hurrying crowds "had likewise paid no heed to him when he was only a murderer" (005-046). **As a character study**, the methodical research [[william-marble|Marble]] undertakes reveals a chilling rationality beneath his desperation — he approaches [[murder-method|murder]] with the same diligence he might apply to a bank transaction, his mind "racing like a steamship propeller in a rough sea" as he calculates both the franc and his chances of escaping detection (005-010, 005-011). **As social commentary**, the presence of cyanide in an ordinary Edwardian bathroom — among other household chemicals, behind a locked glass door — underscores the thin line between domesticity and violence, echoing the novel's broader critique of [[domestic-life-at-53-malcolm-road|suburban respectability]].

The poison also embodies the novel's central [[irony-in-payment-deferred|irony]]: [[william-marble|Marble]]'s "payment" is deferred not once but twice — first from his creditors, then from justice — and the very substance that seemed to offer escape becomes the instrument of his eventual destruction (016-015). The aged, degraded cyanide that gives [[annie-marble|Annie]] "not an easy death, not a quick death" (015-058) mirrors the novel's moral logic: the instrument of murder, kept too long, corrupts and turns against its keeper.

## Key Passages

> (002-023) The locked, glass-fronted cupboard in which Will kept his chemicals hung on the wall beside her. She peered in, as she had done hundreds of times before... they were all very neatly arranged. Except one, and that was a little out of its place on the shelf. (As it might be if a man had put it back there in the dark.) Annie glanced casually at the label... the queer name stuck in her mind — potassium cyanide. She turned away from the cupboard without further thought.

> (005-006, 005-007) Mr. Marble spent the evening... on his knee was the last of the Free Library books on crime. It was very interesting — a *Handbook to Medical Jurisprudence*... The comments on the cyanides were particularly interesting: "Death is practically instantaneous. The patient utters a loud cry and falls heavily. There may be some foam at the lips, and after death the body often retains the appearance of life, the cheeks being red and the expression unaltered."

> (005-010) He knew that nine murderers out of ten were only discovered through some silly mistake. Even if they were very careful about planning the deed, and carried it out successfully, they still made some ridiculous blunder that betrayed them.

> (011-014, 011-015, 011-016) Her husband entered the sitting-room while she still held the book open in her hand. Her thumb was between the pages, at the point where there was that interesting description of the effects of potassium cyanide... the book fell open at the passage on the cyanides. Mr. Marble saw this. He saw the look on her face, too. He stopped aghast. There was no need for words. In that brief space of time he realized that his wife knew. That she *knew*.

> (015-054) She took it in her hand, fondled it, she almost smiled as she looked at it.

> (015-058) But even now things went hardly for her. That cyanide had been kept in solution for over a year, slowly reacting with itself and with the atmosphere. It was not an easy death, not a quick death.
