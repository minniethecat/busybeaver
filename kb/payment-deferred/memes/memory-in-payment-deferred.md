Memory in Payment Deferred is neither neutral nor reliable. It functions as a storehouse for the [[guilt-and-conscience|guilt]] that [[william-marble|William Marble]] cannot consciously acknowledge, returning to him in the form of "horrible memories" [003-002] during his sleepless nights. The novel traces how the past survives into the present through the imperfect medium of individual and collective recollection.

## Definition

Memory, in the novel's psychological framework, refers to the persistence of the past in the minds of characters — sometimes as explicit recollection, sometimes as half-buried association. For [[william-marble|Marble]], memory is primarily punitive: "when he had been sleeping very badly, one or two horrible memories returned to him: memories of a pair of staring eyes, and of a boyish face" [003-002]. These are memories of his [[murder-method|crime]], and they surface against his will. For [[annie-marble|Annie]], memory is tangled and unreliable — "half buried in her tangled memory there were one or two recollections of times when her husband had cruelly taken advantage of her dimness of thought" [006-048]. Memory in the novel is thus partisan: it serves the needs of the present rather than the truth of the past.

## In the Novel

[[william-marble|Marble]]'s relationship with memory is one of active suppression. He seeks air "to flood his brain and blot away that memory of beastly, drunken nudity" [010-053] — the memory of his encounter with [[medland|Medland]]'s body. Yet memory resists suppression. "Half-remembered fragments" [010-012] of Oscar Wilde's "The Ballad of Reading Gaol" intrude on his thoughts, connecting his situation to the literary archetype of the condemned man. His good spirits after the [[murder-method|murder]] surprise his children, who find him "more jovial and friendly than the children could remember his being for years" [006-044] — a grim irony in which the absence of [[financial-desperation|financial worry]], not the absence of [[guilt-and-conscience|guilt]], is what memory registers.

For [[annie-marble|Annie]], memory is tied to loss. "Sometimes Mrs. Marble wept at the memory of her dead son" [011-001], and "she remembered with a slight shudder the time when she had flung herself between her son and her husband" [010-034]. Her memories are of maternal protection and its failure. The novel's earliest pages establish memory as a framing device: "Do you remember him when he was a little boy?" [001-047] asks a character of [[william-marble|Marble]]'s past, before his descent into crime.

## Thematic Role

Memory is the novel's mechanism for showing that the past is never truly past. [[william-marble|Marble]] can dispose of a body but cannot dispose of his memories. The return of the repressed — in the form of sleepless nights, intrusive images, and half-remembered literary fragments — is the novel's substitute for [[guilt-and-conscience|conscience]]. Memory punishes where [[guilt-and-conscience|morality]] cannot. The novel also suggests an intergenerational dimension to memory: [[john-marble|John]] and [[winnie-marble|Winnie]] remember their father as he was before the [[murder-method|murder]], creating a dramatic irony in which the reader knows what the children's memories cannot contain.
