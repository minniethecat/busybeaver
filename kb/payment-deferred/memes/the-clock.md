# The Clock

The clock on the mantelpiece in the Marble dining room at 53 Malcolm Road is a recurring domestic object in C.S. Forester's *Payment Deferred* (1926). Audible throughout the house and visible from William Marble's chair, it serves as both a literal timepiece and a narrative device that registers the protagonist's deteriorating relationship with time — his anxious watching, his desperate attempts to control the evening, and the inexorable approach of consequences that his crimes have set in motion.

## Description and Location

The clock stands on the mantelpiece above the "spurious marble" fireplace surround in the Marble dining room, the symbolic hearth of the household [001-064]. It is a conventional domestic timepiece of the early twentieth-century suburban home, positioned prominently in the room where the family gathers and where the pivotal encounter with James Medland takes place.

## Narrative Role

The clock punctuates the novel's first half at critical moments, each appearance keyed to Marble's shifting emotional state.

### Chapter I: Impatience and Desperation

In the novel's opening domestic scene, Marble waits impatiently for his children's bedtime. "A glance at the clock disappointed him a little. It was only half-past seven" [001-007]. The clock thwarts his desire for solitary comfort, compelling him to endure the domestic chaos until the children are asleep and the whisky decanter becomes accessible.

During Medland's unexpected visit later the same evening, the clock's behaviour inverts. "It seemed to Marble that every time he looked at the clock on the mantelpiece another half-hour had fled" [001-115]. Marble desperately prolongs the conversation, talking "nonsense" to prevent Medland from leaving before he can ask for a loan. The clock, previously too slow, now races — its subjective speed governed by Marble's psychological need.

### Chapter II: Disorientation

Marble wakes in a drugged stupor. He hears a striking sound and Mrs. Marble reassures him: "It's just the clock, dear... half-past seven" [002-008]. Disoriented, he repeats, "The clock? I thought—I was dreaming. Just the clock?" [002-009]. The clock's familiar chime becomes an anchor to reality after the disorienting events of the previous night — the murder, the drugging, the concealment of Medland's body.

### Chapter III: Whisky and Time

The clock becomes a gauge of Marble's drinking. He measures "the two most important things of the moment — the height of the whisky in the bottle and the distance the hands of the clock had moved round" [003-006]. The parallel measurement of spirit and time prefigures the novel's fatal convergence of drink, debt, and desperation.

### Chapter IV: Urgency

Preparing to execute his scheme with Saunders, Marble "with a glance at the clock" declares, "We'd better get a move on" [004-073], using the timepiece to impose urgency on his co-conspirator. This is the clock's final significant appearance — after Chapter IV, the novel's focus shifts from domestic time to public exposure.

## Symbolic Significance

The mantelpiece clock embodies several thematic currents that run through the novel.

**Domestic Order and Its Destruction.** The clock represents the regular, respectable suburban existence that Marble is about to destroy through murder and fraud. Its steady striking of the hours marks a rhythm of normal life that his actions will permanently disrupt. Positioned at the symbolic hearth of the home, the clock presides over the domestic world that the novel systematically dismantles.

**Subjective Time and Guilty Conscience.** The clock's variable pace — too slow when Marble craves solitude, too fast when he needs to stall Medland — registers the subjective experience of time under psychological pressure. This elasticity of temporal perception anticipates the longer temporal distortions of the later chapters, where days and weeks blur under the weight of Marble's guilty conscience.

**Measurement and Control.** Marble's habit of measuring the whisky against the clock's advance reveals his desperate need for control in a life spinning beyond it. The clock, the decanter, and the cheque-book form a trio of domestic instruments by which Marble attempts — and fails — to manage his existence.
