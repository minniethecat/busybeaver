# Popular Corner House

The **Popular Corner House** is a large, gilded restaurant in London appearing in *Payment Deferred*, where William Marble dines alone in the aftermath of his crime, his guilty conscience rendering every meal an ordeal.

## Furtive Dining

After the murder of James Medland, Marble could occasionally be seen at "the big, gilded, popular Corner Houses" — "a little round-faced man sitting alone" (003-005). He would order an expensive dinner, "as expensive as the limited menu allowed," eat hurriedly and furtively, "with never a glance from his pale-blue eyes at the people at the other tables," and then call for his bill and hurry out (003-005).

## The Five-Pound Note

Marble paid for his meals with five-pound notes, thrusting the change into his pocket and rushing away "as though pursued" (003-005). He was indeed pursued — by an "impossible fear of one of the frock-coated floorwalkers stopping him and asking him whence came the note" and by "the haunting fear lest there might be someone casually finding out something in a desolate back garden in a dreary street in a southern suburb" (003-005).

## The Cost of Guilt

Later, when Marble faced the threat of eviction from 53 Malcolm Road and desperately needed to buy the freehold, he reflected grimly on his excessive spending. He told himself he was "living beyond his income as it was, with a grim recollection of five-pound notes changed in Popular Corner Houses" (003-017). The money he had gained through crime was slipping away in furtive indulgences that brought him no pleasure.

## Significance

The Popular Corner House represents the hollow nature of Marble's ill-gotten wealth. Despite his fortune, he cannot enjoy a meal in peace — every public appearance is shadowed by the fear of discovery. The restaurant scenes dramatise the psychological cost of his crime: wealth without security, luxury without enjoyment.
