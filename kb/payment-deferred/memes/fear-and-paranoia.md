Fear is the dominant emotional register of *Payment Deferred*. From the opening pages, where William Marble wakes with a headache and "the grim feeling of blank despair" [001-020], to the final scenes of exposure and execution, the novel maps the progressive encroachment of fear on a mind already compromised by guilt, alcohol, and isolation.

## Fear as Constant Companion

Marble's life after the murder is defined by fear's relentless presence. The novel repeatedly returns to the physical manifestations of terror: his "heart gave a convulsive throb" and he "suddenly felt sick with fear, fear of the future" [001-508]. These are not momentary panics but a sustained condition. Even in moments of apparent calm, "tired to death, weary with fear, worn out by the thumping of his heart," Marble "crept brokenly to his desk and buried his face in his hands" [001-642]. Fear is not an emotion he experiences; it is the medium in which he now lives.

## The Paranoia of the Guilty

Marble's fear gradually crystallizes into systematic paranoia. He studies books on crime not out of intellectual curiosity but from the desperate need to anticipate every threat: "He knew that nine murderers out of ten were only discovered through some silly mistake" [001-665]. This knowledge, far from reassuring him, only multiplies the sources of potential danger. Every neighbour becomes a potential witness, every casual remark a possible accusation. The novel traces the psychology of the guilty mind with clinical precision — how it magnifies trivial incidents into mortal threats and transforms ordinary social interaction into a minefield.

## Domestic Terror

One of the novel's most disturbing achievements is its portrayal of fear within the domestic sphere. Annie Marble experiences her own species of terror: "clutching her handbag all the morning in terror lest she should lose it" [001-1112], and later "in the grip of a blind terror that made her knees tremble" [001-1116]. Her fear is not of murder or discovery but of her husband — his moods, his drinking, his increasingly erratic behaviour. The home at 53 Malcolm Road, far from being a refuge, becomes a pressure chamber in which two people live in mutual dread: he afraid of exposure, she afraid of him.

## Fear and Alcohol

The relationship between fear and drinking in the novel is circular and self-reinforcing. Marble drinks to escape fear, but intoxication only sharpens his paranoia in the long run. The "happy stage" he seeks — "after the grim preliminary period" [001-1466] — is always temporary, always followed by the return of terror with renewed force. Alcohol, which Marble calls his "only friend" [001-448], is in fact fear's closest ally, weakening the very faculties he needs to maintain his composure and his secret.

## The Contagion of Fear

Fear in *Payment Deferred* is contagious. It spreads from Marble to his wife, and from both parents to their children. John senses something wrong without understanding it; Winnie escapes to school "glad to be away from the atmosphere of the house" [001-1452]. Even the neighbours, though ignorant of the crime, pick up on something amiss — the "queer moods" that set the Marble household apart. The novel suggests that fear, like the poison Marble eventually uses, has a way of seeping into everything it touches.
