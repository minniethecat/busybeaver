# The Carpet Slippers

## Definition

The carpet slippers are a pair of worn, shapeless household slippers belonging to William Marble, the protagonist of C.S. Forester's 1926 crime novel *Payment Deferred*. They appear as a recurring physical detail across multiple chapters, serving as a visual shorthand for Marble's domestic stagnation, financial decline, and moral disintegration.

## In the Novel

The slippers are first described in their "shapeless" state [001-064], establishing Marble's shabby domesticity early in the narrative. When disturbed by a noise in the middle of the night, Marble hurriedly puts them on before investigating [002-014], anchoring the slippers to the novel's pivotal moment — the discovery of James Medland's visit and the subsequent murder.

By Chapter 11, following his wife Annie's death, the slippers have vanished along with the domestic order they represented: "the restraint imposed by carpet slippers, and then they were gone" [011-009]. In the novel's closing chapter, they reappear as "wrecks of carpet slippers" [012-010], now physically mirroring Marble's complete moral and psychological ruin.

## Thematic Role

The carpet slippers function as a physical index of Marble's trajectory through the novel. Their deterioration — from "shapeless" to "wrecks" — parallels his descent from mild domestic dissatisfaction to murder, paranoia, and suicide. They are the antithesis of his gold cigarette case and other outward status symbols: where the cigarette case projects an aspirational public self, the slippers reveal the private squalor he cannot escape.

The slippers also serve a structural function in the novel's domestic spaces. They tether Marble to 53 Malcolm Road, the house that becomes both his prison and his stage for self-destruction. When they disappear after Annie's death, the domestic order they anchored collapses with them.

## Key Passages
