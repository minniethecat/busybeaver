# Collins

Collins is a minor but pivotal character in C. S. Forester's "Payment Deferred" — a former subordinate of [[william-marble|William Marble]] at the [[the-national-county-bank-org|National County Bank]] who has been posted to the [[the-paris-branch-bank|Paris branch]]. His "voluble" personality and habit of inserting unnecessary personal remarks into official correspondence inadvertently provides [[william-marble|Marble]] with the intelligence that enables the [[franc-speculation|franc speculation]].

## Character

Collins is defined by his **volubility** — he is a "chatty" fellow whose garrulousness is "noticeable even in this official correspondence" (004-005). [[william-marble|Marble]] describes him as pleasant but undisciplined: the kind of man who cannot resist adding a personal touch to a business letter. It is precisely this indiscipline that proves invaluable — his offhand remark about possible [[the-french-government|French Government]] intervention is the signal [[william-marble|Marble]]'s trained mind needs.

## Role in the Novel

Collins appears only through his letter — he never enters the novel's physical space — but his role is decisive. The "paragraph that was quite unnecessary by all rules of routine" (004-005) provides the market intelligence that [[william-marble|Marble]] converts into a fortune. Collins himself never knows what his words set in motion; he is an unwitting agent of both [[william-marble|Marble]]'s rise and his destruction.

## Key Passages

> He was a voluble fellow — "chatty," Mr. Marble described him to himself — and his volubility was noticeable even in this official correspondence, for at the end of the letter he had inserted a paragraph that was quite unnecessary by all rules of routine. (004-005)

## The Biweekly Letter

The mechanism of Collins's intervention is meticulously described. His letter arrives as part of the routine morning post — "the biweekly letter from the Paris branch confirming the actions reported already by cable and telephone" (004-005). Marble reads it only because "the man who had written it was known to him personally" — Collins had "worked under him in that very department for a couple of years before being sent out to Paris" (004-005). Without this personal connection, Marble might have scanned the letter as mechanically as he did the others. The entire plot turns on a former colleague's chatty style.

Collins's inserted paragraph is "quite brief for Collins" — the phrase suggesting that Collins's letters usually ran to even greater length — and says "that more steadiness might be noticed in the French franc in future, as it was rumoured that the Government were taking the matter in hand, and might even resort to 'pegging' on the London exchange" (004-005). The information is a rumor, not a fact; Collins reports it casually, almost as an afterthought. But Marble reads it with the intensity of a man whose life depends on finding an edge.

## The Unwitting Catalyst

Collins's role exemplifies the novel's preoccupation with chains of unintended consequence. A garrulous clerk in Paris writes an indiscreet paragraph; a desperate clerk in London reads it and gambles everything; a French government nationalizes foreign credits; and a fortune worth fifty-one thousand pounds materializes from the interplay (004-016). Collins never learns of any of this. He remains in Paris, writing his chatty letters, while Marble's life is transformed — and eventually destroyed — by the trajectory his paragraph initiated.

The novel's irony operates at Collins's expense in a quieter register as well. His name echoes the French diminutive "Collin" (a variant of Nicolas), a detail that aligns him with the Parisian world he now inhabits. But "Collins" in English also suggests the ordinary, the common — Collins the bank clerk is a nobody, and the fact that a nobody's idle chatter can determine the fate of a murderer is Forester's final comment on the randomness of the forces that shape human affairs.
