Ismarus is a coastal city in Thrace, the land of the [[the-cicones|Cicones]], who were allies of [[troy|Troy]] during the Trojan War. It marks the first destination in [[odysseus|Odysseus's]] great narrative of his wanderings, recounted in Book IX. The sack of Ismarus sets the tone for the journey ahead — success quickly followed by disaster when Odysseus's men refuse to heed his counsel and depart promptly.

## In the Odyssey

### The Sack of the City (Book IX)

After departing from Troy, the Greek fleet is driven by winds to the Thracian coast:

> "The winds from Ilion to the Cicons' shore, / Beneath cold Ismarus our vessels bore. / We boldly landed on the hostile place, / And sack'd the city, and destroy'd the race" [009-NA]

The epithet "cold" distinguishes Ismarus from the warmer Mediterranean lands and locates it in the northern Aegean. The sack is thorough: the Greeks take captives, divide spoils, and every soldier receives an equal share — a model of Homeric warfare and distribution.

### Odysseus's Warning

Despite the successful sack, Odysseus counsels immediate departure. His men, however, choose to celebrate:

> "I then advised to fly; not so the rest, / Who stay'd to revel, and prolong the feast: / The fatted sheep and sable bulls they slay, / And bowls flow round, and riot wastes the day." [009-NA]

This refusal to heed prudent counsel becomes a recurring pattern throughout the wanderings. The crew's indulgence — slaughtering sheep and bulls, drinking freely — provides the Cicones time to regroup.

### The Ciconian Counterattack

The surviving Cicones rally inland allies and return at dawn with overwhelming force:

> "Meantime the Cicons, to their holds retired, / Call on the Cicons, with new fury fired; / With early morn the gather'd country swarms, / And all the continent is bright with arms" [009-NA]

The simile of leaves and flowers — "Thick as the budding leaves or rising flowers / O'erspread the land" — conveys the vast numbers of the Ciconian reinforcements. The battle rages all day until evening, when the Cicones finally prevail:

> "But when his evening wheels o'erhung the main, / Then conquest crown'd the fierce Ciconian train. / Six brave companions from each ship we lost, / The rest escape in haste, and quit the coast" [009-NA]

The loss of six men from each ship — the first casualties since leaving Troy — establishes the cost of disobedience that will echo throughout the epic.

## Significance

Ismarus serves as a microcosm of the entire Odyssey: military success, crew insubordination, divine consequence, and tragic loss. The episode establishes Odysseus's role as the voice of prudence whose counsels are too often ignored. It also introduces the theme of *nostos* (homecoming) as a journey marked by repeated setbacks.

The Ismarus episode also has martial significance: it demonstrates that Odysseus's men are still in a "war mode" of raiding and plundering, having not yet transitioned to the mindset of peaceful return. The Cicones, as Trojan allies, are legitimate targets by Homeric convention, but the crew's inability to control their appetites transforms a tactical victory into a costly defeat.

The wine taken from Ismarus — specifically from [[maron|Maron]], the priest of Apollo whom Odysseus spared — later proves crucial in the [[the-cyclops-episode|Cyclops episode]], where Odysseus uses it to intoxicate [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]]. This narrative thread connects the first wanderings adventure to one of its most famous episodes.

## Geography

Ismarus is identified with a site near modern Maroneia in Greek Thrace, on the northern coast of the Aegean Sea. The region was historically known for its wine production — fittingly, since the famous Maronian wine that Odysseus uses against the Cyclops originates here. The epithet "cold Ismarus" reflects the northern Aegean's cooler climate compared to the Greek heartland.
