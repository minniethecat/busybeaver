# Irus

Irus is the resident beggar of Ithaca's palace, whose challenge to Odysseus (disguised as a beggar) provides one of the epic's most vivid comic episodes — and, simultaneously, a miniature of the entire suitor plot. His given name is Arnaeus, but the suitors nickname him Irus (after Iris, the messenger goddess) because he runs errands for them.

## Identity & Names

The name Irus (Greek: Ιρος, *Iros*) derives from Iris, the rainbow goddess and divine messenger:

> "Irus, a name expressive of the employ
> By which he ran on errands for the boy" [018-018]

His birth name was Arnaeus, but the suitors' nickname defines him — he is what he serves. Pope describes him as a figure known to all for his gluttony and cowardice.

## Role & Significance

Irus is a mortal beggar of Ithaca, aligned with the suitors. His narrative role is to function as a **foil for Odysseus**:

1. **Pretender vs. true king**: Irus is a false beggar — arrogant, bullying, and cowardly — who contrasts with the true king concealed in the same disguise.
2. **Comic miniature of the suitor plot**: His challenge, defeat, and humiliation mirror the suitors' larger challenge (the bow contest), defeat (the slaughter), and humiliation (the dishonored death).
3. **Warning unheeded**: His brutal beating by the disguised Odysseus [018-108, 018-115] is a portent of the suitors' fate — which they, blind to omens, find merely entertaining.

## Appearances

### Book 18 — The Beggar's Fight

Irus encounters Odysseus, disguised as a beggar, at the palace gate and immediately tries to drive him away [018-016]. He insults the stranger with threats:

> "Irus! alas! shall Irus be no more?" [018-089]

The suitors, amused by the prospect of a fight between beggars, arrange a contest with a goat's-paunch as the prize. Antinous threatens Irus with mutilation and enslavement to King Echetus (the "maimer") if he loses [018-094].

Odysseus, restraining his full strength, strikes Irus with a single blow:

> "In every joint the trembling Irus shook." [018-108]

> "That instant Irus his huge arm extends,
> Full on the Trojan's front the blow descends" [018-115]

Irus is dragged out bleeding, and the suitors mock him:

> "From Irus, and the guilty Irus bleeds." [018-283]

The scene ends with a warning the suitors fail to take:

> "Perhaps these outrages from Irus flow,
> Prompted by thee?" [018-441]

## Relationships

### Allies

- **The Suitors**: Particularly Antinous, who arranges the fight and threatens Irus if he loses [018-094]. Irus runs errands for them, earning his nickname [018-018].

### Enemies

- **Odysseus**: The disguised king who humiliates him in combat. Odysseus fights with restraint — killing Irus outright would have exposed his identity; instead he breaks his jaw.
- **Amphinomus**: It is to Amphinomus that Odysseus addresses the veiled warning that follows the fight [018-441].

## Analysis

### The Mock-Heroic Beggar

Irus is the epic's most sustained exercise in mock-heroic comedy. His challenge, the suitors' formal arrangement of the contest, the prize (a goat's stomach), the fight itself, and Irus's abject defeat — all parody the conventions of heroic combat. The scene's humor depends on the gap between Irus's pretension and his reality: he swaggers like a champion but crumples at a single blow.

### Narrative Miniature

The Irus episode is a scaled-down version of the entire Odyssey. A pretender occupies a place that rightfully belongs to another; the rightful owner, disguised, returns and defeats the usurper; the usurper is punished and humiliated. That the suitors watch this drama unfold and laugh — never recognizing it as their own story — is one of the epic's most elegant instances of dramatic irony.

### Odysseus's Restraint

The fight with Irus demonstrates Odysseus's self-control. A full-strength blow would have killed the beggar and exposed Odysseus's identity; instead, he hits just hard enough to break Irus's jaw [018-108]. This calculated violence is the hallmark of the Odysseus who will later methodically slaughter the suitors — controlled, proportional, and devastating.
