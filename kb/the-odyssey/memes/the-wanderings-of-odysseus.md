# The Wanderings of Odysseus

## Summary

The Wanderings of Odysseus (also known as the *Apologoi*) are the first-person narrative recounted by [[odysseus|Odysseus]] to the [[scheria|Phaeacia]]n court in Books IX–XII of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*. They span ten years of post-Trojan War voyaging — from the raid on the [[the-cicones|Cicones]] to the loss of his last ship and crew at [[thrinacia|Thrinacia]] — and constitute the most famous sequence of adventures in classical literature. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] introduces his tale as one of "wanderings" and "woes" [004-054], a thread that recurs throughout the poem: "While I, so many wanderings past, and woes" [015-541]. The narrative serves both as entertainment for his [[scheria|Phaeacia]]n hosts and as a meditation on the cost of *nostos* (homecoming).

## Context & Causes

The wanderings are the direct consequence of the Trojan War's aftermath. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] and his twelve ships departed [[troy|Troy]] but were immediately blown off course. His first misadventure — the raid on the [[the-cicones|Cicones]] at [[ismarus|Ismarus]] — was a self-inflicted wound: his men refused to leave after the sack, "by whom they were repulsed" when [[the-cicones|Cicones]] reinforcements arrived [009-006]. This pattern of divine hostility and human folly repeats throughout: [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s encounter with [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] incurs [[poseidon|Poseidon]]'s wrath ("For injured Cyclops, and his eyeball lost" [011-145]), ensuring that every attempt to reach [[ithaca|Ithaca]] is thwarted. The wanderings are thus framed as a double punishment — divine retribution for hubris and the natural consequence of mortal recklessness.

## The Journey

### The Cicones

[[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s first stop after [[troy|Troy]] is [[ismarus|Ismarus]], land of the [[the-cicones|Cicones]]. He sacks the city, but his crew refuses to sail away: "The winds from [[troy|Ilion]] to the Cicons' shore" [009-052] brought them, and the men feast while the surviving [[the-cicones|Cicones]] rally inland reinforcements: "Meantime the Cicons, to their holds retired, / Call on the Cicons, with new fury fired" [009-062–063]. Seventy-two of [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s men are killed in the counterattack before they escape — a grim lesson in discipline that his crew never fully learns.

### The Lotus-Eaters

Driven south by a storm, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] reaches the land of the [[moira|Lot]]us-Eaters — "The land of [[moira|Lot]]us and the flowery coast" [009-107]. The inhabitants offer his scouts the lotus fruit, which induces forgetfulness of home. "[[moira|Lot]]us the name: divine, nectareous" [009-117], those who eat it lose all desire to return. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] forcibly drags his men back to the ships and binds them beneath the benches, departing immediately — one of the few episodes where his leadership prevents disaster rather than courting it.

### The Cyclops

The most consequential episode. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] and twelve men enter the cave of [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]], the one-eyed [[polyphemus|son of Poseidon]]: "The land of Cyclops first, a savage kind" [009-130]. [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] devours six of his men before [[odysseus|Odysseus]] intoxicates him with wine and blinds him with a sharpened stake. As they escape, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] reveals his true name — a fatal act of pride. The Cyclops prays to his father [[poseidon|Poseidon]] for vengeance, and "Mindful of Cyclops and his human food" [010-241], the sea god becomes [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s implacable enemy, ensuring the wanderings will last another decade.

### Aeolus and the Laestrygonians

[[aeolus|Aeolus]], keeper of the winds, gives [[odysseus|Odysseus]] a bag containing all adverse winds, leaving only the west wind to carry him home. Within sight of [[ithaca|Ithaca]], his suspicious crew opens the bag, releasing a storm that drives them back to [[aeolus|Aeolus]]'s island — where the wind god now refuses aid, recognizing that [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is cursed by the gods.

[[the-laestrygonians|The Laestrygonians]] represent the nadir of the journey. These giant cannibals destroy eleven of [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s twelve ships, spearing his men like fish. Only [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s own vessel escapes, and the survivors — a fraction of the original fleet — limp onward toward [[circe|Circe]]'s island.

### Circe

[[circe|Circe]], the enchantress "the daughter of the Day" [010-171], transforms half of [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s remaining crew into swine. With [[hermes|Hermes]]'s aid (the herb moly), [[odysseus|Odysseus]] resists her magic and compels her to restore his men. He stays on her island for a year — "E'en [[circe|Circe]] wept, her adamantine heart" [010-486] moved when he finally asks to leave. Before his departure, [[circe|Circe]] instructs him to consult the shade of [[tiresias|Tiresias]] in the Underworld — the *[[the-underworld|Nekyia]]* — which becomes the journey's spiritual center.

### The Underworld (Nekyia)

In Book XI, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] sails to the land of the Cimmerians and summons the dead. [[tiresias|Tiresias]] prophesies the remainder of his journey and his eventual death "from the sea." He encounters the shades of his mother [[anticlea|Anticlea]], [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] (who warns him of women's treachery), [[achilles|Achilles]], [[ajax|Ajax]], and a parade of mythological heroines and heroes. It is the most serious and theological episode of the wanderings, and it cements the poem's meditation on mortality and fame.

### The Sirens, Scylla, and Charybdis

[[nostos|Return]]ing from the Underworld, [[circe|Circe]] warns [[odysseus|Odysseus]] of the remaining perils: "Next, where the Sirens dwell, you plough the seas" [012-061]. [[the-sirens|The Sirens]] — "In flowery meads the sportive Sirens play" [012-204] — lure sailors to their death with irresistible song. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] has his crew plug their ears with wax while he, bound to the mast, becomes the only mortal to hear their song and live.

Next comes the strait between two monsters. On one side, [[scylla|Scylla]] — "Dire [[scylla|Scylla]] there a scene of horror forms" [012-290] — the six-headed creature who seizes six of his men: "When lo! fierce [[scylla|Scylla]] stoop'd to seize her prey" [012-304]. On the other, [[charybdis|Charybdis]], [[charybdis|the whirlpool]] that swallows and disgorges the sea three times daily. [[circe|Circe]] has counseled him to hug [[scylla|Scylla]]'s cliff and lose six men rather than the whole ship: "Ah, shun the horrid gulf! by [[scylla|Scylla]] fly" [012-147].

### The Cattle of the Sun

The final catastrophe. Despite explicit warnings from [[tiresias|Tiresias]] and [[circe|Circe]], and [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s own command, his starving crew slaughters the sacred cattle of [[helios|Helios]] on [[thrinacia|Thrinacia]] while [[odysseus|Odysseus]] sleeps. [[zeus|Zeus]] destroys the ship with a thunderbolt. Only [[odysseus|Odysseus]] survives, clinging to wreckage, drifting for nine days until he washes ashore on [[calypso|Calypso]]'s island — where the wanderings end and a new, seven-year captivity begins.

## Aftermath & Significance

The wanderings end with [[odysseus|Odysseus]] washed up on [[ogygia|Ogygia]], [[calypso|Calypso]]'s island, alone — "So may I find, when all my wanderings cease" [013-070] became a prayer answered only after seven more years of captivity. Every crew member he left [[troy|Troy]] with is dead. The wanderings thus form a sustained study in the cost of survival: [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s cunning (*metis*) preserves him while his crew's recklessness destroys them, yet his own pride — especially the revelation of his name to [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] — is the deepest cause of his suffering.

The *Apologoi* are also the poem's internal meditation on storytelling itself. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] narrates his own adventures to the [[the-phaeacians|Phaeacians]], controlling how he is perceived, and the wanderings become the source of his fame (*kleos*) even as they are the cause of his misery. The phrase "narrating his wanderings, and telling of the cities he saw" [P01-270] from the proem frames the entire epic as a tale within a tale — the hero's voice enfolded within the poet's.
