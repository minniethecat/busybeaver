# Eumaeus

## Identity & Names

**Eumaeus** (Greek: Εὔμαιος, *Eumaios*) is the swineherd of Odysseus's household in Ithaca. Though now a slave, he was born a prince: the son of Ctesius, king of Syrie (probably an island in the Aegean), kidnapped as a child by Phoenician traders and sold to Laertes. Homer consistently calls him "divine Eumaeus" (δῖος ὑφορβός) — an epithet normally reserved for heroes and gods, applied to a swineherd as a mark of the poet's esteem. His name means "good seeker" or "good destiny" — befitting the man who proved his worth not through birth but through character.

## Role & Significance

Eumaeus is the moral exemplar of the *Odyssey*'s second half. When the disguised Odysseus arrives at his humble hut — a simple stone enclosure in the woods — Eumaeus offers everything he has: food, shelter, his own cloak, to a complete stranger. His credo, spoken while dressing a pig for his guest, is the poem's most concise statement of the hospitality ethic:

> "The stranger and the poor are sent by Jove;
| And what we give, though small, is given with love."

This generosity, offered from poverty rather than abundance, makes Eumaeus a more impressive host than the wealthy Nestor or Menelaus. His poverty makes his xenia more, not less, impressive — a rebuke to the suitors who consume the household's wealth without giving anything in return.

Eumaeus's faithfulness (*pistis*) to his absent master, sustained over twenty years, is the ethical counterweight to the suitors' betrayal. He loves the household even when it cannot reward him, maintains Odysseus's herds even when the suitors consume them, and speaks of the vanished king with the grief of a son:

> "I call him still my lord, though far from home,
> And in his name each stranger I receive."

When Odysseus tests him with a false story claiming the king will return, Eumaeus refuses to hope — he has been disappointed too many times by lying travelers seeking reward. This refusal to be deceived makes his eventual joy at the genuine recognition all the more powerful.

## Appearances

### Book 14: The Hut

The entire book takes place at Eumaeus's swineherd's hut, making it unique in the epic — no other character receives an entire book of sustained attention outside the hero's immediate family. Odysseus, disguised as a beggar, arrives at the hut and is greeted by the barking dogs, which Eumaeus drives off with stones. Eumaeus welcomes him, seats him on a pile of brushwood covered with a wild goat's skin, and kills two fattened hogs for the meal.

Their long conversation establishes Eumaeus's character through his own words: his sorrow for the absent Odysseus, his contempt for the suitors who "devour his substance without shame", and his story of his own origins — the kidnapped prince who became a slave yet never lost his dignity. The book is a masterclass in characterization through dialogue.

### Book 15: Telemachus Returns

Telemachus, returning from Sparta, comes first to Eumaeus's hut (as Athena has instructed) rather than to the palace. Eumaeus greets him with the emotion of a father: "As a fond father, when his son returns / From some far country, all his bosom burns". The scene establishes Eumaeus as a surrogate parent to the young prince, filling the void left by Odysseus's absence.

### Books 16–17: The Conspiracy

Eumaeus is present when Odysseus reveals himself to Telemachus — one of only three people (with Telemachus and later Euryclea) who know the king's identity before the massacre. He becomes an active conspirator: carrying messages, guarding the hut, and helping to plan the suitors' destruction.

### Books 21–22: The Battle

During the bow contest, Eumaeus is entrusted with the crucial task: when Odysseus nods to him, he tells Euryclea to lock the hall doors and instructs the women to remain inside whatever they hear. In the battle itself, he fights alongside Odysseus, Telemachus, and Philoetius — the only four men against more than a hundred. His participation in the slaughter is the poem's democratic assertion that virtue, not birth, qualifies a man for martial honor.

## Relationships

- **Odysseus**: His beloved master, for whom he has grieved for twenty years. Their relationship is the epic's ideal of master-servant loyalty — a bond not of compulsion but of mutual respect and affection. When Odysseus reveals himself, Eumaeus weeps and embraces him.

- **Telemachus**: He treats the young prince with protective, fatherly affection, calling him "dear child" and rejoicing at his safe return. Along with Euryclea, he represents the continuity of care that sustained the household in Odysseus's absence.

- **Philoetius**: The cowherd, Eumaeus's counterpart and fellow loyal servant. Together they form a pair — swineherd and cowherd — representing the faithful stewardship that contrasts with the suitors' consumption.

- **The Suitors**: He despises them with a righteous anger that is entirely impersonal — he hates what they do, not who they are. His moral clarity about their violation of xenia and household order makes him the poem's most reliable ethical voice.

- **Melanthius**: The goatherd, Eumaeus's foil — a servant who has betrayed his master and sided with the suitors. Their contrast demonstrates that the poem's moral vision is not class-based: a swineherd is virtuous, a goatherd is villainous, and nobility is found in character rather than station.

## Analysis

### The Democratic Swineherd

Eumaeus embodies the democratic potential within the *Odyssey*'s aristocratic world. Though a slave, he is presented as morally superior to the noble suitors. Homer's insistent use of the epithet "divine" for a swineherd is a theological provocation — it claims that virtue, not birth, is what the gods honor. The suitors, for all their noble lineage, are never called divine.

### Xenia from Poverty

Eumaeus's hospitality is the poem's most radical demonstration of xenia because it costs him something. Nestor and Menelaus entertain lavishly from abundance; Eumaeus gives from scarcity. His offering — "though small, is given with love" — enacts the principle that the value of a gift is measured by the giver's sacrifice, not the recipient's benefit. The suitors, who take without giving from another's abundance, are condemned by the contrast.

### Theoxeny Enacted

Eumaeus's treatment of the disguised Odysseus is a living demonstration of theoxeny — the religious principle that a stranger may be a god in disguise. Without knowing who his guest is, Eumaeus treats him as though he were worthy of honor. He is the only character in the poem who perfectly fulfills the obligation of xenia without any expectation of reward — and he is the one who receives the greatest reward: reunion with the master he thought dead.

### The Slave's Autobiography

Eumaeus's account of his own origins is unique in the epic — no other minor character is given a complete backstory. The tale of the kidnapped Phoenician prince is a narrative within the narrative, and it humanizes a character who might otherwise remain a stock "faithful servant" type. Eumaeus is not faithful because he was born a slave; he was born a prince and reduced to slavery. His virtue is not servile; it is royal.
