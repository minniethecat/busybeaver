# The Ithacan Assembly

## Summary

The Ithacan Assembly (Book 2) is the first public event of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* and the opening act of the Telemachy. Dawn breaks on the second day of the poem's action, and [[telemachus|Telemachus]] — "majestic as a god" — summons the [[ithaca|Ithaca]]n people to assembly for the first time since [[odysseus|Odysseus]] departed for [[troy|Troy]] twenty years before. "Soon as in solemn form the assembly sate" (002-027), the aged Aegyptius opens with wonder: "Why here once more in solemn council sit?" (002-052). [[telemachus|Telemachus]] rises, scepter in hand, and delivers an impassioned indictment of the suitors' consumption of his household. [[antinous|Antinous]] deflects, blaming [[penelope|Penelope]]'s delay. The augur [[halitherses|Halitherses]] interprets a portent of two eagles as a sign of [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s imminent return, but [[eurymachus|Eurymachus]] dismisses him. When the suitor-dominated assembly refuses to act, [[telemachus|Telemachus]] shifts tactics and demands a ship to seek news of his father — a request that [[mentor|Mentor]] publicly denounces the [[ithaca|Ithaca]]ns for failing to grant. The assembly dissolves, but [[athena|Athena]] (as [[mentor|Mentor]]) privately assures [[telemachus|Telemachus]] and arranges the ship herself.

## Context & Causes

The assembly is the direct outcome of [[athena|Athena]]'s visit to [[telemachus|Telemachus]] in Book 1. [[dolos|Disguise]]d as [[mentor|Mentes]], she had urged the youth to "convoke the peerage, and the gods attest" (001-372) and challenge the suitors before the people. The [[ithaca|Ithaca]]n assembly had not convened since [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s departure — a sign of the political vacuum the absent king left. [[telemachus|Telemachus]]'s decision to call one is his first autonomous political act, and it transforms him from a passive sufferer into an agent. The assembly also serves the poem's larger design: it introduces the suitors' political power, the public dimension of the household's crisis, and the generational tension between [[telemachus|Telemachus]] and the older men who dominate [[ithaca|Ithaca]].

## Key Moments

1. **The Summons**: At dawn, [[telemachus|Telemachus]] sends heralds through [[ithaca|Ithaca]] to call the assembly. The people gather at the public meeting ground, and the youth takes his father's throne — a symbolic act of claiming inheritance that the watching elders acknowledge.

2. **Aegyptius's Opening**: The aged Aegyptius, who lost a son to [[polyphemus|the Cyclops]], opens the floor with the question on everyone's mind: who dares to call an assembly after two decades of silence? His question frames [[telemachus|Telemachus]]'s act as both bold and unprecedented.

3. **[[telemachus|Telemachus]]'s Indictment**: Scepter in hand, [[telemachus|Telemachus]] delivers his public case against the suitors. He frames their feasting as both personal injustice and public disgrace — a violation of the house that shames all [[ithaca|Ithaca]]. His speech concludes with a dramatic gesture: "He spoke. [[telemachus|Telemachus]] with transport glows" (002-059), and he hurls the scepter to the ground, weeping.

4. **[[antinous|Antinous]]'s Counter-Attack**: The lead suitor deflects blame onto [[penelope|Penelope]], describing her ruse of the shroud — weaving by day, unraveling by night. [[antinous|Antinous]] proposes that [[telemachus|Telemachus]] send his mother to her father [[icarius|Icarius]] and force her to remarry. [[telemachus|Telemachus]] refuses.

5. **The Omen and Dismissal**: Two eagles appear in the sky, tearing at each other. The augur [[halitherses|Halitherses]] interprets this as a sign of [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s imminent return and destruction of the suitors. [[eurymachus|Eurymachus]] dismisses the prophecy with contempt: the eagles are just birds, and seers are always hungry for pay. The suitor-dominated assembly votes with [[eurymachus|Eurymachus]].

6. **[[mentor|Mentor]]'s Rebuke**: As the assembly dissolves — "with a rushing sound the assembly bend" (002-307) — [[mentor|Mentor]] ([[athena|Athena]] in disguise) rises to rebuke the [[ithaca|Ithaca]]ns for their cowardice. No one responds. The people return home, and [[telemachus|Telemachus]] withdraws to the shore to pray.

## Aftermath & Consequences

The assembly is, in practical terms, a failure: [[telemachus|Telemachus]] does not expel the suitors, the people do not rise in his support, and his public appeal is dismissed. But it is a pivotal personal victory. For the first time, [[telemachus|Telemachus]] has acted as his father's son before the entire community. The suitors, for all their mockery, recognize the threat — [[antinous|Antinous]] privately remarks that [[telemachus|Telemachus]] has become dangerous. More importantly, the assembly's failure drives [[telemachus|Telemachus]] to the second half of his plan: the secret journey to [[pylos|Pylos]] and [[sparta|Sparta]]. [[athena|Athena]], as [[mentor|Mentor]], provides the ship that the assembly refused. [[telemachuss-journey|The Telemachy]] — the education of the hero's son — begins with this public failure, which teaches [[telemachus|Telemachus]] that he must build his own authority rather than inherit it.
