# Clytemnestra

## Identity & Names

Clytemnestra (Greek: Κλυταιμνήστρα, _Klytaimnestra_) is the queen of [[mycenae|Mycenae]], daughter of Tyndareus (or [[zeus|Zeus]] in some traditions) and Leda, sister of [[helen|Helen]], wife of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], and mother of [[orestes|Orestes]]. The etymology of her name is contested — possibly from κλυτός (_klytos_, "famous") + μνήστρα (_mnestra_, "wooer" or "courter"), or from μήδομαι (_medomai_, "to contrive"), suggesting "famous contriver" — a name that would encode her defining trait.

In Pope's translation she is named explicitly only once, in the Underworld, when Agamemnon's shade delivers the poem's most bitter condemnation:

> "And should posterity one virtuous find, / Name Clytemnestra, they will curse the kind."
> [011-554]

Her name in the _Odyssey_ has become synonymous with female treachery — the absolute antithesis of the faithful [[penelope|Penelope]]. She is never a character who acts within the poem's present; she exists entirely in reported speech, a fixed moral reference point whose story frames every question the epic asks about marriage, homecoming, and trust.

## Role & Significance

Clytemnestra functions as the _Odyssey_'s most powerful negative paradigm. The poem draws the contrast between her and Penelope explicitly and repeatedly, constructing a binary that structures the entire homecoming narrative:

| Aspect | Clytemnestra | Penelope |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Husband's return | Greeted with murder | Greeted with testing and recognition |
| Lover | [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] (invited in openly) | None (suitors are invaders, not lovers) |
| Son's role | [[orestes|Orestes]] avenges father by killing mother | [[telemachus|Telemachus]] aids father against suitors |
| Legacy | "They will curse the kind" | "Her fame will never die" (κλέος ἄφθιτον) |
| Agency | Active conspirator in murder | Active strategist of delay and testing |
| Divine attitude | [[zeus|Zeus]] cites her as example of human folly | [[athena|Athena]] protects her household |

Zeus opens the poem with the story of Aegisthus — and by extension Clytemnestra — as the paradigmatic case of mortals who "through their own reckless folly take sorrows beyond their portion":

> "Behold Aegisthus! he, the common fate / Of mortal kind, foresaw but shunn'd too late."
> [001-055]

Agamemnon's shade in the Underworld makes the contrast with Penelope painfully explicit, declaring that Odysseus is fortunate in his wife's virtue — a virtue rendered all the more precious and precarious by Clytemnestra's counter-example. Indeed, the shade warns Odysseus to approach Ithaca in secret precisely because "there is no trusting women any more" — a judgment that Clytemnestra's crime has made universal.

## The Crime

### Before the _Odyssey_

While [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was at Troy for ten years, Clytemnestra initially resisted [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]]'s advances. The poem notes that Aegisthus had to abduct the palace bard — the man charged with guarding the queen's virtue — and leave him to die on a deserted island before Clytemnestra "willingly went with him to his house." This detail, recounted by Nestor in Book 3, is significant: it partially exculpates Clytemnestra by suggesting she was guarded and protected before her fall.

### The Murder

When Agamemnon returned to [[mycenae|Mycenae]] — bringing with him the Trojan prophetess [[cassandra|Cassandra]] as his war-prize and concubine — Aegisthus welcomed him with a feast of treachery:

> "As men kill an ox at the manger, so Aegisthus killed Agamemnon"
> (Book 4, Nestor's account)

The simile is brutal in its domesticity: the king is slaughtered like livestock at its own feeding trough, in his own hall, at a banquet meant to celebrate his return. Twenty of Agamemnon's men died with him. [[cassandra|Cassandra]], who had foreseen the murder but was cursed never to be believed, was killed alongside him — cut down by Clytemnestra herself, according to Agamemnon's shade in Book 11:

> "The most pitiful cry I ever heard was Cassandra's, / as she was struck down by treacherous Clytemnestra / over my body."

### Aftermath

Clytemnestra and Aegisthus ruled [[mycenae|Mycenae]] for seven years. In the eighth, [[orestes|Orestes]] — who had been smuggled away as a child to [[phocis|Phocis]] — returned and killed them both, avenging his father. Nestor tells this story to [[telemachus|Telemachus]] in Book 3 as an explicit model for how a son should act: "And you, my friend — for I see you are handsome and tall — be brave, so that men yet to be born may speak well of you."

## Appearances in the _Odyssey_

Clytemnestra never appears as a living character in the poem. She exists entirely in reported speech — a story told and retold, each retelling adjusting the moral calculus:

1. **Zeus's Opening (Book 1)**: Her crime with [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] is the paradigm of human folly that opens the poem [001-055]. Zeus names Aegisthus explicitly; Clytemnestra is the unspoken co-conspirator, her guilt assumed.
2. **Nestor's Warning (Book 3)**: Nestor recounts the full story to [[telemachus|Telemachus]] as a cautionary tale, emphasizing Orestes's vengeance as the model of filial duty [003-340].
3. **Agamemnon's Lament (Book 11)**: The most vivid and misogynistic account — Agamemnon's shade in the Underworld delivers a bitter tirade against "the destroyer of men, the contriver of evil" [011-554]:
   > "And should posterity one virtuous find, / Name Clytemnestra, they will curse the kind."
   > [011-554]
4. **Agamemnon's Contrast (Book 24)**: In the second _Nekyia_, the shade of Agamemnon contrasts his fate with the blessed homecoming of Odysseus, comparing the "foul murder" at his return with the joyous reunion at Ithaca [024-200].

The cumulative effect of these retellings is to build Clytemnestra into the poem's fixed star of female treachery — a gravitational force that pulls every reference to marriage, fidelity, and homecoming into its moral orbit.

## Relationships

- **Husband**: [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] — murdered at his homecoming banquet
- **Lover and co-conspirator**: [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] — with whom she ruled Mycenae for seven years
- **Son and killer**: [[orestes|Orestes]] — who returned from exile to avenge his father by killing his mother; his story is the _Odyssey_'s recurring model of filial duty
- **Sister**: [[helen|Helen]] — the two daughters of Leda embody complementary forms of female transgression: Helen, the adulteress whose beauty caused war; Clytemnestra, the murderess who destroyed a homecoming
- **Daughter**: Iphigenia — sacrificed by Agamemnon at Aulis to secure favorable winds for the Trojan fleet; the _Odyssey_ does not mention her, but her death is Clytemnestra's unspoken grievance, the wound behind the wound
- **Children by Agamemnon**: Electra and Chrysothemis — daughters who survived the slaughter
- **Foil**: [[penelope|Penelope]] — the faithful wife whose story, the poem insists, redeems what Clytemnestra's has damned

## Narrative Function

The _Odyssey_ needs Clytemnestra. Without her story, [[penelope|Penelope]]'s fidelity would be remarkable but not structurally essential. With Clytemnestra as the ever-present counter-example, every test of Penelope's loyalty — the weaving and unweaving of Laertes's shroud, the demand that the beggar prove his knowledge of Odysseus, the bed-test — becomes freighted with existential stakes.

The question "will this Ithaca be Mycenae?" is asked explicitly by [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s shade in Book 11, but it is also asked implicitly by the narrative at every turn. When Odysseus returns in disguise and tests his household, he is not merely being cautious — he is conducting an investigation whose terms were set by Clytemnestra's crime. Every loyal servant ([[eumaeus|Eumaeus]], [[philoetius|Philoetius]], [[euryclea|Euryclea]]) and every disloyal one ([[melanthius|Melanthius]], [[melantho|Melantho]]) is weighed on a scale calibrated at Mycenae.

The poem's resolution answers the question definitively: Ithaca is not Mycenae. [[penelope|Penelope]] is not Clytemnestra. But the answer is only meaningful because the question was real, and the poem does not let the reader forget it.
