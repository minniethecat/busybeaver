# Kleos (Glory)

## Definition

**Kleos** (Greek: κλέος, *kléos*) is the ancient Greek concept of heroic glory — the immortal renown a warrior earns through extraordinary deeds, which survives him in poetic song. The word literally means "that which is heard" or "rumor," deriving from the verb *kluein* ("to hear"), reflecting an oral culture where a hero's fame spread through bardic recitation and a name was only as durable as the stories told about it.

In Homer, kleos is the ultimate mortal aspiration. Since men must die — "as leaves on trees, the race of man is found" — the only true immortality lies in being remembered and sung about by future generations. The poet (*aoidos*) and the hero are symbiotically linked: the hero provides the deeds, the poet provides the permanence. Without the song, the greatest deeds vanish; without the deeds, the song has nothing to commemorate.

## Greek Context

### Kleos and the Heroic Code

In the warrior society of Archaic Greece, kleos was inseparable from the heroic code. A man's worth (*time*) was measured not by wealth or piety but by the stories told of him after death. Battle was the primary arena for winning kleos — each victory added to a hero's renown, while death in combat, especially in defense of one's people, secured eternal fame.

This logic reaches its apotheosis in the *[[iliad|Iliad]]*, where Achilles chooses a short life with immortal kleos over a long but obscure one:

> "My mother Thetis, goddess of the sea,
> Told me two fates conduct me to my end:
> If here I stay and fight round Priam's town,
> My safe return is lost, but endless fame;
> If I return to my dear native land,
| My fame is lost, but long will be my life." [Iliad 9.410]

Achilles chose kleos over nostos — and died for it. The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* is, in part, a response to this choice.

### Kleos in an Oral Culture

Kleos is inseparable from the technology of its transmission. In a pre-literate society, a hero's name survived only through memorized poetic song, passed from bard to bard across generations. The formulaic language of epic — the "winged words," the repeated epithets, the type scenes — was a technology for preserving kleos. Each performance of the *Odyssey* was an act of re-inscribing Odysseus's fame in the living memory of the audience. The poem is a kleos-machine.

## In the Odyssey

The *Odyssey* presents a more complex and revisionary vision of kleos than the *Iliad*. Where Achilles won glory through martial prowess and early death, Odysseus pursues a different path — his kleos comes through cunning (*metis*), endurance (*tlasmos*), and survival.

### The Wanderings as Kleos

Odysseus's wanderings themselves generate kleos. The tales of [[polyphemus|the Cyclops]], [[circe|Circe]], [[scylla|Scylla]], and [[hades|the Underworld]] spread his name across the Mediterranean. When he arrives among [[the-phaeacians|the Phaeacians]], he is already a figure of song — Demodocus the blind bard sings of the quarrel between Odysseus and Achilles at [[troy|Troy]], and Odysseus weeps, hearing his own kleos being made. This moment is one of the most self-reflexive in ancient literature: the hero of the epic hears an epic about himself.

After revealing his identity, Odysseus supplies the Phaeacians with the material for a new song — his own storytelling (Books 9–12) is an act of kleos-production, ensuring that his deeds will be remembered. The Phaeacians are so moved that [[alcinous|Alcinous]] declares:

> "If such thy sufferings, and such thy fame,
> What god could hate thee, or what man defame?"

### Kleos Through Homecoming

The *Odyssey*'s most radical revision of kleos is its claim that glory can be won through homecoming, not only through battle. Odysseus's refusal of [[calypso|Calypso]]'s offer of immortality is a choice for mortal kleos over divine obscurity:

> "I long for home, and for the sight of home.
| If any god should wreck me on the main,
| I'll bear it; for I have a patient mind."

This is a complete inversion of Achilles's choice. Achilles chose death-with-glory over life-without-fame; Odysseus chooses life-with-return over immortality-without-identity. The *Odyssey* argues that nostos is itself a form of kleos — perhaps the highest form, because it requires not one moment of heroic self-sacrifice but twenty years of sustained endurance.

### Telemachus's Quest for Kleos

[[telemachus|Telemachus]] begins the epic as a youth without kleos, overshadowed by his absent father. His name means "far from battle" — he has won no glory and inherited only his father's absence. [[athena|Athena]] urges him to [[telemachuss-journey|journey to [[pylos|Pylos]] and Sparta]] not only to seek news but to win his own renown:

> "Go, seek the venerable sage in Pylos;
> There learn, if aught the ancient king can tell;
> Then to atrides [Menelaus] turn, and break the spell."

His journey to [[nestor|Nestor]] and [[menelaus|Menelaus]] is a rite of passage — a miniature nostos that earns him the beginning of his own kleos. By the poem's end, he stands armed beside his father and grandfather, having crossed the threshold from nameless youth to named warrior.

### Penelope's Kleos

[[penelope|Penelope]] wins a form of kleos unique in Greek epic — not through battle or travel, but through fidelity and intelligence. Her fame is explicitly linked to her husband's:

> "The gods, who gave thee all the charms of life,
> Have given thee fame, a husband, and a wife."

The symmetry is striking: Homer gives the wife a kleos that matches the husband's, a recognition of female virtue almost unique in archaic poetry. Her cunning (the weaving ruse) parallels Odysseus's cunning; her endurance (twenty years of waiting) parallels his; her kleos is the feminine counterpart of his.

### Kleos and the Suitors

[[the-suitors|The suitors]] pursue a hollow imitation of kleos through consumption rather than deeds. Their feasting is a parody of the heroic feast (where warriors earn their share through valor), and their deaths bring no poetic immortality — only infamy as violators of xenia. The *Odyssey* is explicit: the suitors' names will be remembered, but only as examples of what not to be.

### Kleos and the Poet's Self-Awareness

The *Odyssey* is intensely self-conscious about its own function as a vehicle of kleos. When Odysseus praises Demodocus — "the muse / Teaches the bard, and gives him all his fire" — he is acknowledging that his own immortality depends on poets like this one. The *Odyssey* itself is the fulfillment of that acknowledgment: it is the song that has given Odysseus nearly three thousand years of kleos.

## Significance

### Redefining the Heroic

The *Odyssey*'s most important contribution to the concept of kleos is its redefinition of what counts as heroic. Battle is one path to immortal fame; endurance, cunning, fidelity, and homecoming are others. By the poem's end, Penelope's kleos rivals Odysseus's — a claim that would be unthinkable in the *Iliad*'s martial world.

### The Economy of Memory

Kleos operates in an economy of scarcity: the poet can only sing so many songs, and only the greatest deeds earn a place. The *Odyssey* is acutely aware of this economy — it includes catalogues of heroines and heroes precisely to preserve names that might otherwise be lost. The poem is not only about kleos; it is a technology for manufacturing and distributing it.

### Kleos and Modernity

The *Odyssey*'s vision of kleos anticipates modern concerns about legacy and memory in a secular age. If there is no afterlife, what survives of a person? The answer, for Homer, is clear: the story. In a world without monuments, the song is the only monument — and it is more durable than stone.
