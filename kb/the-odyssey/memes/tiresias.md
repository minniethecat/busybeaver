# Tiresias

## Identity & Names

**Tiresias** (Greek: Τειρεσίας, *Teiresias*) is the blind prophet of Thebes, the most famous seer in Greek mythology. His biography is extraordinary even by mythic standards: he lived seven years as a woman after striking two mating snakes with his staff; called to settle a dispute between Zeus and Hera about which sex experiences greater pleasure, he answered "woman enjoys it nine times more" and was struck blind by the angry Hera; Zeus, unable to reverse another god's curse, compensated him with the gift of prophecy and a life spanning seven generations. After his death, Persephone granted him the unique privilege of retaining his mind and prophetic power in the Underworld while all other shades became witless wraiths.

In the *Odyssey*, Circe directs Odysseus to consult him as the only being in the realm of the dead capable of providing guidance. Homer describes the encounter with solemnity: Tiresias approaches "bearing a golden scepter" — the symbol of authority that distinguishes him from the gibbering crowd of the dead.

## Role & Significance

Tiresias's encounter with Odysseus in Book 11 is the structural and theological center of the epic. In a poem concerned with return (*nostos*), Tiresias is the only figure who can see through time and reveal whether return is possible — and under what conditions. His prophecy has three movements:

1. **The Obstacle**: Poseidon's wrath pursues Odysseus because of Polyphemus's blinding. Yet even this wrath has limits: "Still hopes to reach his native shore, / Though stern Neptune his course oppose".

2. **The Warning**: Odysseus must not harm the Cattle of the Sun on the island of Thrinacia. If he restrains himself and his crew, he will reach Ithaca; if not, he will arrive alone, "late and in evil plight, upon a foreign ship".

3. **The Propitiation**: Even after reclaiming Ithaca, Odysseus's journey is not complete. He must travel inland carrying an oar until he meets a people who mistake it for a winnowing fan. There he must plant the oar and sacrifice a ram, a bull, and a boar to Poseidon.

> "Then to the god a sacred altar raise,
> And sacrifice a ram, a bull, a boar;
> So shall the god his former grace restore."

This third movement transforms the *Odyssey* from a story of homecoming into a story of cosmic reconciliation. Odysseus must make peace not only with his household but with the god whose son he blinded — and the ritual for doing so is stranger than any of the monsters he has faced.

## Appearances

### Book 10: The Directive

Though Tiresias does not appear in person, Circe tells Odysseus that he must seek him out. Her instructions are precise: sail to the boundary of Oceanus, dig a pit, pour libations of honey, milk, wine, water, and barley, and sacrifice a black ram and ewe so that the shades may drink the blood and speak. Only Tiresias is specifically named as worth consulting — the other shades are incidental.

### Book 11: The Nekyia

Odysseus performs the ritual at the entrance to the Underworld. The shades crowd around the blood-filled pit — "brides and youths, and old men worn with toil, / And tender virgins, whom the grave had claimed" — but Odysseus holds them off with his sword until Tiresias arrives.

The prophet approaches with his golden scepter and addresses Odysseus directly:

> "'Why, wretched man! forsaking the fair light,
> Com'st thou to view the realms of endless night?'"

He drinks the blood and delivers his prophecy. The speech is brief — fewer than forty lines — but its content determines the remaining half of the epic. Tiresias withdraws immediately after speaking, his function complete. The other shades (Anticlea, Agamemnon, Achilles, and the parade of heroines and heroes) follow, but Tiresias alone speaks with prophetic authority.

### Book 23: The Recounting

After the massacre of the suitors, Odysseus recounts Tiresias's prophecy to Penelope as part of his proof of identity. The recounting confirms both that Odysseus is who he claims to be and that their story does not end with the reclaiming of the household — a final journey remains.

## Relationships

- **Odysseus**: The seeker who summons him. Their relationship is formal and transactional — Tiresias provides information, Odysseus receives it — but the information is the most consequential in the epic. Without Tiresias's prophecy, Odysseus would not know about the Cattle of the Sun, would not know how to propitiate Poseidon, and would not know that his nostos is ultimately guaranteed by fate.

- **Circe**: The goddess who directs Odysseus to Tiresias. Her acknowledgment that her own knowledge is insufficient — that only the dead prophet can provide what Odysseus needs — is a rare moment of divine humility in the epic.

- **Persephone**: The queen of the Underworld, who granted Tiresias his unique privilege of retaining consciousness after death. The *Odyssey* does not explain why Persephone did this; the reason belongs to Tiresias's Theban mythology rather than the epic's Ithacan concerns.

- **Anticlea**: Odysseus's mother, whose shade appears after Tiresias departs. The sequence is emotionally powerful: prophecy first (the cosmic framework), then personal grief (the mother who died waiting). Tiresias and Anticlea represent the two dimensions of the Nekyia — revelation and mourning.

## Analysis

### The Only Functional Shade

Tiresias is unique in the Homeric Underworld. All other shades are described as "strengthless heads of the dead" — gibbering wraiths who cannot speak until they drink the sacrificial blood, and who lose coherence the moment they finish. Tiresias alone retains his *phrenes* (mind, consciousness) independently of the blood offering. This singularity makes him the one fully reliable source of truth in a poem otherwise saturated with disguise, deception, and dramatic irony. When Tiresias speaks, the poem's habitual ambiguity lifts: this is what will happen.

### The Prophecy's Three Movements

The structure of Tiresias's prophecy maps onto the structure of the *Odyssey* itself: past (Poseidon's wrath, already incurred), present (the choice at Thrinacia, immediately ahead), and future (the inland journey, beyond the poem's own ending). This temporal comprehensiveness makes Tiresias more than a plot device — he is the poem's mechanism for locating its own action within the largest possible frame of past causes and future consequences.

### Prophecy and Free Will

Tiresias's prophecy about the Cattle of the Sun is conditional: "If thou restrain thy hand, and spare the herd, / Thou may'st return". This conditionality distinguishes Homeric prophecy from the fixed, unalterable fate of Greek tragedy. Odysseus does have a choice — and his crew, by slaughtering the cattle, makes the wrong one. The prophecy is a warning, not a sentence.

### Beyond the Poem's Ending

The strangest element of Tiresias's prophecy — the inland journey with the oar — extends the *Odyssey*'s concerns beyond its own final book. Odysseus's nostos is not complete when he embraces Penelope or even when he stands armed with Laertes and Telemachus. True homecoming requires reconciliation with the god he has offended, and that reconciliation requires a journey to a place so far from the sea that an oar is unrecognizable. This final prophecy gestures toward a life beyond the poem — an Odysseus who continues to journey, but now in service of peace rather than survival.
