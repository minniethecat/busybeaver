The Planctae (the "Wandering Rocks" or "Clashing Rocks") are among the most fearsome maritime hazards in Greek mythology. Located in a narrow strait, these rocks clash together with such force that they crush any vessel attempting passage. Only the [[argo|Argo]], guided by [[hera|Juno's]] favor, has ever successfully traversed them. In Book XII of the [[the-odyssey|Odyssey]], [[circe|Circe]] warns [[odysseus|Odysseus]] to avoid the Planctae entirely, directing him instead toward the alternative perils of [[scylla-and-charybdis|Scylla and Charybdis]].

## In the Odyssey

### Circe's Warning (Book XII)

When Circe advises Odysseus on the routes home, she presents him with a choice: the Planctae or the strait of Scylla and Charybdis. Her description of the Planctae is vivid with destructive imagery:

The rocks are described as towering pinnacles against which the sea hurls itself with such violence that not even birds — the doves that carry ambrosia to [[zeus|Jove]] — can pass unscathed. The passage is explicitly identified as the route taken only by the Argo, and even that vessel required divine intervention.

Circe counsels Odysseus to choose Scylla and Charybdis instead — a calculated choice between certain destruction (the Planctae would destroy all twelve ships) and probable loss (Scylla would take only six men, and the ship might survive). This strategic calculus — the lesser of two lethal options — is one of the most consequential decisions of the journey.

### Odysseus's Choice

Odysseus accepts Circe's counsel and steers clear of the Planctae, heading instead for [[scylla-and-charybdis|Scylla and Charybdis]]. The Planctae thus serve as a narrative device — the absolute worst-case scenario that makes the merely terrible alternative seem acceptable. Without the threat of the Planctae, Odysseus's decision to face Scylla (and lose six men) would appear reckless; with it, it becomes the prudent choice.

### Later Reference (Book XXIII)

In the final books, Odysseus recounts his adventures to [[penelope|Penelope]], and the Planctae are referenced among the perils he navigated — a reminder of the choices and losses that shaped his return.

## Significance

The Planctae represent the ultimate maritime barrier in Greek mythology — a passage so dangerous that it constitutes a boundary between the known and unknown worlds. The fact that only the Argo (Juno's favored ship) has passed through elevates the Planctae to a quasi-divine status: they are not merely a geological hazard but a cosmic threshold.

The narrative function is equally important: by presenting an even worse alternative, the Planctae legitimize Odysseus's decision to sacrifice six men to Scylla. The hero is not choosing between safety and danger, but between certain destruction and calculated loss. This is the moral calculus of command in extremis, and it recurs throughout the wanderings.

The Argo reference serves as a mythological touchstone — the Planctae are a known hazard from the earlier generation of heroes (Jason's quest), placing Odysseus's journey in continuity with prior heroic voyages while emphasizing its unique difficulties.

## Geography

The Planctae are traditionally associated with the Cyanean Rocks (Symplegades) at the entrance to the Black Sea — the Bosporus. In Apollonius's Argonautica, the Symplegades are the "Clashing Rocks" that Jason must navigate. However, Homer locates the Planctae in the western Mediterranean (near Scylla and Charybdis), creating a geographic inconsistency that has fueled centuries of scholarly debate. Some traditions identify them with the Lipari Islands or other volcanic formations in the Tyrrhenian Sea.
