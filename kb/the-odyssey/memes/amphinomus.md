# Amphinomus

Amphinomus, son of Nisus of Dulichium, is the most sympathetic of Penelope's suitors — a man of conscience trapped among the ruthless. His kindness to the disguised Odysseus and the personal warning he receives from the king make his death at Telemachus's hand one of the epic's most morally complex moments.

## Identity & Names

The name Amphinomus (Greek: Αμφινομος, *Amphinomos*) may derive from *amphi* (on both sides) and *nomos* (custom, law) — a name suggestive of balanced judgment, fitting for the suitor who hovers between virtue and complicity. He is consistently identified as the son of Nisus of Dulichium [016-375], and Pope presents him as a figure of natural nobility degraded by bad company.

## Role & Significance

Amphinomus is a mortal suitor from Dulichium. His role in the narrative is to demonstrate that even among the suitors, gradations of guilt exist:

1. **Conscience among the conscienceless**: He is the only suitor who questions the plot to murder Telemachus [016-375], [016-417].
2. **Recipient of Odysseus's warning**: Odysseus, in disguise, singles him out for a veiled but urgent warning to flee the palace [018-466].
3. **Tragic failure**: Despite his better nature, Amphinomus cannot break free of the suitors' company, and his hesitation costs him his life [022-116].

## Appearances

### Book 16 — The Conscience

When the suitors plot to ambush and kill Telemachus on his return from Pylos, Amphinomus alone dissents:

> "Amphinomus survey'd the associate band" [016-375]

He persuades the suitors to consult the gods before acting:

> "Till from his throne Amphinomus ascends" [016-417]

His intervention saves Telemachus's life — temporarily.

### Book 18 — The Warning

Odysseus, disguised as a beggar, observes Amphinomus's character and offers him a veiled warning. He tells him that Odysseus will return soon, and that the suitors should leave while they can:

> "Till thus Amphinomus the silence broke" [018-466]

Amphinomus is troubled by the warning but does not act on it. The wine that "laughs in gold" [018-153] — the luxury of the suitors' feast — holds him in its grip.

### Book 20 — The Omen

At the final feast, Amphinomus sees an omen — perhaps the darkening of the hall — and presages doom:

> "Amphinomus, who thus presaging cried" [020-315]

Yet still he does not leave.

### Book 22 — The Death

In the slaughter, Amphinomus charges Telemachus:

> "Next bold Amphinomus his arm extends" [022-116]

Telemachus strikes him down. The man who saved Telemachus's life in Book 16 is killed by him in Book 22. It is a death that fulfills the tragic logic of the epic: complicity, however reluctant, carries the same penalty as willing participation.

## Relationships

### Allies

- **Odysseus**: The disguised king recognizes Amphinomus's virtue and tries to save him. The warning [018-466] is an act of genuine — if conditional — mercy.
- **Penelope**: Amphinomus is said to be her favorite among the suitors for his courtesy and good sense.

### Enemies

- **Telemachus**: The prince whom Amphinomus once saved becomes his killer [022-116].

### Fellow Suitors

- **Antinous**: The ringleader whose brutality Amphinomus opposes but cannot escape.
- **Eurymachus**: The smooth-tongued schemer whose plans Amphinomus half-heartedly follows.

## Analysis

### The Tragedy of the Half-Good Man

Amphinomus is the most instructive failure in the Odyssey. He is not evil — he has a conscience, he shows kindness, he dissents from murder. But these virtues are not enough. His tragedy is that he knows what is right but lacks the courage to act on that knowledge. The epic's judgment is clear: virtue without decision is not virtue at all; it is merely a disposition that, when tested, proves worthless.

### The Wasted Warning

Odysseus's warning to Amphinomus [018-466] is a test as much as a mercy. The disguised king gives Amphinomus the chance that none of the other suitors receive — a direct, personal warning backed by prophetic knowledge. Amphinomus's failure to heed it makes his death not merely just but earned. The epic invites us to imagine what might have been: if Amphinomus had walked out that night, he would have lived.

### The Moral Spectrum of the Suitors

Amphinomus exists to demonstrate that the suitors are not a monolithic evil. Antinous is cruel, Eurymachus is duplicitous, Ctesippus is crude — but Amphinomus is merely weak. His presence complicates the moral clarity of the slaughter and forces the audience to confront the uncomfortable truth that not everyone who dies in a just cause deserves their fate equally.
