Zacynthus (modern Zante or Zakynthos) is a lush island in the Ionian Sea, frequently mentioned alongside [[dulichium|Dulichium]] and [[same|Same]] as part of the realm of [[odysseus|Odysseus]]. In Pope's translation, it is consistently characterized by its "sylvan" and "shady" qualities — an island renowned for its greenery. The island sent twenty noble suitors to [[penelope|Penelope's]] court, the second-largest contingent after Dulichium.

## In the Odyssey

### The Suitor Catalog (Book XVI)

Zacynthus appears in the catalog of suitors enumerated to [[telemachus|Telemachus]]:

> "Twice twelve the boast / Of Samos; twenty from Zacynthus' coast" [016-NA]

The island's twenty suitors, while fewer than Dulichium's fifty-two, still represent a significant force in the occupation of Odysseus's palace.

### The Siege of the Court (Book I)

The opening book establishes the breadth of the suitor threat, with Zacynthus prominently included:

> "The court proud Samos and Dulichium fills, / And lofty Zacinth crown'd with shady hills" [001-NA]

The epithet "lofty" and the image of "shady hills" emphasize the island's mountainous, forested character — a contrast to the "fruitful stores" of Dulichium.

### Penelope's Lament (Book XXIV)

In the final book, [[penelope|Penelope]] laments the pressure from suitors across the island realm:

> "Zacynthus, green with ever-shady groves, / And Ithaca, presumptuous, boast their loves" [024-NA]

The phrase "ever-shady groves" reinforces Zacynthus's defining characteristic as a well-wooded island.

### The Voyage Home

In an earlier passage, Zacynthus appears in a geographical catalog describing the Ionian islands:

> "Dulichium, and Zacynthas' sylvan reign" [001-NA]

The adjective "sylvan" (wooded) is Zacynthus's most consistent epithet in Pope's rendering.

## Significance

Zacynthus completes the tetrarchy of Ionian islands that form Odysseus's kingdom: Dulichium, Same, Zacynthus, and Ithaca itself. Each island contributes suitors to Penelope's court, an index of the widespread breakdown of loyalty during Odysseus's twenty-year absence. The island's "ever-shady groves" distinguish it from the other Ionian isles, suggesting a distinctive character within the maritime realm.

The formulaic pairing of the four islands in Homeric verse reflects their political unity under Odysseus's rule and the shared threat posed by the suitor occupation. Zacynthus's position in this constellation — second in suitor count — marks it as an important but secondary power within the realm.

## Geography

Zacynthus is securely identified with modern Zakynthos (Zante), the southernmost of the Ionian Islands, located west of the Peloponnese. The island is indeed known for its lush vegetation, pine forests, and fertile plains, consistent with Homeric descriptions. Archaeological evidence confirms Mycenaean-era settlement on the island, supporting its inclusion in the Homeric kingdom of Odysseus.
