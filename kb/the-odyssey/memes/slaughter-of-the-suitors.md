# Slaughter of the Suitors

## Summary

The Slaughter of the Suitors (*Mnesterophonia*, "murder of the suitors") is the climactic event of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*, occurring in Book 22. Having won the bow contest, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] reveals his identity — "Big with revenge, the mighty warrior flies" (002-385) — and, with [[telemachus|Telemachus]], [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]], and the cowherd Philoetius at his side, systematically kills all 108 suitors. [[athena|Athena]], in the form of [[mentor|Mentor]], fights alongside them, and the battle ends with the unfaithful maidservants hanged and the traitor [[melanthius|Melanthius]] mutilated.

## Context & Causes

The slaughter is the culmination of forces accumulated over twenty years. The suitors have violated xenia for years, consuming [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s wealth, abusing his servants, and plotting his son's murder. Their hubris has called down [[zeus|Zeus]]'s judgment, and their *ate* (divine blindness) prevents them from recognizing [[odysseus|Odysseus]] until it is too late. The bow contest in Book 21 serves as the immediate trigger — [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s victory demonstrates his unique identity, and the arrow through [[antinous|Antinous]]'s throat signals the beginning of the *nemesis* (retribution) that hubris always invites.

## Key Moments

1. **[[antinous|Antinous]]'s Death**: [[odysseus|Odysseus]] shoots [[antinous|Antinous]] through the throat as he raises a wine cup — the symbolically perfect moment of the consumer consumed.
2. **[[eurymachus|Eurymachus]]'s Failed Negotiation**: The suitor attempts to blame everything on [[antinous|Antinous]] and offer restitution; [[odysseus|Odysseus]] refuses.
3. **The Battle**: Armed with spears, swords, and [[athena|Athena]]'s aegis, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] and his allies fight the suitors in the great hall. [[athena|Athena]] deflects the suitors' spears.
4. **The Maids' Execution**: [[eurycleia|Eurycleia]] identifies twelve unfaithful maidservants who are forced to clean the hall and then hanged.
5. **[[melanthius|Melanthius]]'s Punishment**: The treacherous goatherd is mutilated — nose, ears, hands, and feet cut off — for his betrayal.

## Aftermath & Consequences

The slaughter restores dike to the household but provokes a crisis: the suitors' families, led by Eupeithes ([[antinous|Antinous]]'s father), march on [[odysseus|Odysseus]] seeking revenge. This would begin an endless blood-feud — the cycle of poine that the *Oresteia* dramatizes — but [[athena|Athena]] intervenes. "Big with revenge" (002-385), the families are halted by divine command and [[zeus|Zeus]]'s thunderbolt. The cycle of vengeance is broken, and peace is imposed from above. The event thus marks both the climax of personal justice and the transition from private vengeance to divinely administered order.
