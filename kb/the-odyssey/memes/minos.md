# Minos

**Minos** is a figure of Greek mythology who appears in the _Odyssey_ as the judge of the dead in the Underworld. A son of Zeus, he was the legendary king of Crete whose name became synonymous with law and justice.

## Role in the Odyssey

Odysseus sees Minos in the Underworld, in one of the most striking images of Book 11: "Stern Minos waves a mace of burnish'd gold" (011-712). Seated as judge, he presides over the shades — "the gathering ghosts of the dead" — who crowd around him for judgment. Homer presents Minos as the ultimate arbiter of the afterlife, dispensing justice with an authority that extends beyond the grave.

Minos is also invoked in the false tales Odysseus tells to conceal his identity. In Book 17, Odysseus claims to have been "his father's guest, for Minos' birth renown'd" (017-622), linking himself to the Cretan royal house. In Book 19, he tells Penelope that he was born in Crete, where "sceptred Minos with impartial hand" (019-218) once ruled — a kingdom of ninety cities. These Cretan cover stories exploit Minos's reputation for justice and legitimacy.

## Mythological Background

Minos was the son of Zeus and Europa, born on Crete. In life, he was a lawgiver so renowned that after death he became a judge of the Underworld alongside Rhadamanthus and Aeacus. He was the father of Ariadne and the builder of the Labyrinth that housed the Minotaur. In the _Odyssey_, Homer omits the Minotaur story, emphasizing only the king's role as the just ruler and infernal judge.

> "Stern Minos waves a mace of burnish'd gold, / And deals, unquestion'd, to his ghostly crowd" (011-712)
