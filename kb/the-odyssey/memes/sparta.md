# Sparta

## Description

Sparta is the kingdom of [[menelaus|Menelaus]] and [[helen|Helen]], located in the fertile valley of the Eurotas River in the southern Peloponnese. In the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*, it is the second destination of [[telemachuss-journey|Telemachus's journey]]. The palace is described in terms of overwhelming opulence — bronze, gold, silver, and electrum gleam from every surface.

> "Above, beneath, around the palace shines / The sunless treasure of exhausted mines; / The spoils of elephants the roofs inlay, / And studded amber darts the golden ray"

The interior is "resplendent as the blaze of summer noon, / Or the pale radiance of the midnight moon". Telemachus whispers to [[pisistratus|Pisistratus]] that such magnificence could belong only to the palace of Olympian [[zeus|Jove]]. Yet this wealth, Menelaus confesses, is "possess'd, but not enjoy'd" — accumulated through suffering no opulence can efface.

> "With all my affluence, when my woes are weigh'd, / Envy will own the purchase dearly paid"

The kingdom is at peace, but an uneasy peace: the restored king and returned queen preside over a household haunted by the war they caused and the dead they left behind. Helen moves through the palace like a figure from another world — beautiful, unsettling, morally ambiguous — giving the Sparta episode its distinctive atmosphere of brilliance shadowed by guilt.

## In the Odyssey

### Book 4 — Arrival at Sparta

Telemachus and Pisistratus arrive after a two-day chariot journey from [[pylos|Pylos]]. Pope captures the moment with ceremonial grandeur:

> "And now proud Sparta with their wheels resounds, / Sparta whose walls a range of hills surrounds"

They arrive during a double wedding: Menelaus's daughter Hermione marries [[achilles|Achilles]]'s son Neoptolemus, and his son Megapenthes marries a Spartan noblewoman. The timing is significant — Menelaus secures his dynasty at the very moment Odysseus's heir arrives seeking his own patrimony. The steward Eteoneus questions whether to receive the strangers; Menelaus rebukes him, recalling his own dependence on [[xenia|hospitality]] during his wanderings.

### Helen's Recognition

The episode pivots when Helen enters. Pope describes her with the language of divine epiphany:

> "In this suspense bright Helen graced the room; / Before her breathed a gale of rich perfume. / So moves, adorn'd with each attractive grace, / The silver shafted goddess of the chase!"

She studies the strangers and, without introduction, identifies Telemachus:

> "With wonder wrapp'd on yonder check I trace / The feature of the Ulyssean race"

Menelaus confirms: Telemachus had wept at his father's name, his "purple garment veil'd the falling tear". Pisistratus formally introduces the travelers and explains their mission.

### The Feast of Stories

The recognition triggers collective grief — all four weep until Pisistratus, displaying the wisdom of his father [[nestor|Nestor]], suggests deferring mourning. Helen then doses the wine with nepenthe, a drug from Egypt that suspends grief without erasing memory — a fitting metaphor for Sparta itself:

> "Bright Helen mix'd a mirth-inspiring bowl; / Temper'd with drugs of sovereign use, to assuage / The boiling bosom of tumultuous rage"

Helen and Menelaus tell complementary stories of [[odysseus|Odysseus]] at [[troy|Troy]]. Helen recounts how Odysseus entered Troy disguised as a self-wounded beggar; she alone recognized him and helped him gather intelligence. Menelaus follows with the Trojan Horse: when Helen circled the Horse calling in the voices of the warriors' wives, only Odysseus's iron restraint prevented the hidden men from answering. The juxtaposition is characteristic — husband and wife each testify to the heroism of the man whose return they confirm, while their own uneasy restoration frames every word.

### The Proteus Revelation

The next morning, Menelaus tells the longest inset narrative in the poem: his encounter with [[proteus|Proteus]] on Pharos. Stranded and starving, Menelaus was taught by Proteus's daughter Eidothea to ambush the god. Disguised in seal skins, he and three men seized Proteus, who transformed into lion, serpent, leopard, boar, water, and tree before surrendering. Proteus revealed the fates of the returning Greeks — [[ajax|Ajax]] destroyed by [[poseidon|Poseidon]], [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] murdered by [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] — and, most critically for Telemachus, that Odysseus lives on [[calypso|Calypso]]'s island:

> "Him in Calypso's cave of late I view'd, / When streaming grief his faded cheek bedow'd"

This first verified news transforms Telemachus's journey from a quest for information into a quest for return.

### Book 15 — Departure from Sparta

[[athena|Minerva]] visits Telemachus as he lies sleepless under Menelaus's portico, warning that [[the-suitors|the suitors]] plot an ambush and that [[penelope|Penelope]] is pressured to marry [[eurymachus|Eurymachus]]. At daybreak, Telemachus takes leave. Menelaus grants it with one of the poem's definitive statements on hospitality:

> "True friendship's laws are by this rule express'd, / Welcome the coming, speed the parting guest"

Menelaus insists on parting gifts: a silver mixing-bowl by Sidonian craftsmen, a silver beaker, and — from Helen — the finest of her embroidered robes, chosen after examining "her various textures of unnumber'd dyes". It lay "divinely bright, / Like radiant Hesper o'er the gems of night".

As Telemachus departs, an eagle appears bearing a goose. Helen interprets the omen:

> "So shall thy godlike father, toss'd in vain / Through all the dangers of the boundless main, / Arrive... / From slaughter'd gluttons to release the dome"

This prophecy — Helen's final act in the poem — foreshadows the [[slaughter-of-the-suitors|slaughter of the suitors]] and sends Telemachus on his way carrying heroic gifts.

## Significance

Sparta is the *Odyssey*'s most complex representation of post-war restoration. Menelaus has recovered his kingdom and queen, but the kingdom is built on wealth accumulated through suffering, and the queen's presence is a constant reminder of the war's origin. Helen drugs the wine to banish sorrow; her story of helping Odysseus is followed by Menelaus's story of her near-betrayal. Sparta is both the model of what Odysseus hopes to achieve — restoration of kingdom and marriage — and a warning of how incomplete that restoration can be.

The episode functions as a mirror to [[ithaca|Ithaca]]. Both kingdoms lack their rightful king; both have queens besieged by circumstances. But Menelaus has returned while Odysseus is absent. Telemachus witnesses a completed [[nostos|nostos]] — and the lesson is ambiguous. Restoration is possible, but the scars remain.

The episode also provides the first verified intelligence of Odysseus's survival. Proteus's prophecy is divinely guaranteed. When Telemachus leaves Sparta, he knows his father is alive — knowledge that transforms his character in the poem's second half.

Helen's role is particularly significant: she is the only mortal in the poem who interprets omens, controls the emotional atmosphere through drugs, and tells her own war story. Her ambiguous moral status makes Sparta the poem's most psychologically dense setting.

## Geography

Sparta corresponds to the real ancient city in Laconia, on the Eurotas River. The Mycenaean-era palace at Therapne (the Menelaion) has been excavated, revealing a substantial Bronze Age settlement with occupation levels from the 15th–13th centuries BCE — contemporaneous with the traditional date of the Trojan War.

The Menelaion site, located on a ridge east of the Eurotas about 5 km southeast of classical Sparta, was excavated by the British School at Athens beginning in 1909. The dig revealed a mansion complex with fine painted plaster, imported pottery, and evidence of feasting consistent with Homeric descriptions. A later shrine to Helen and Menelaus attests to a hero cult centered on the royal pair from the 8th century BCE.

The "broad streets" and "hollow Lacedaemon" of Homeric formula reflect the Eurotas valley's topography — a wide, fertile plain bounded by Taygetus mountains to the west and Parnon to the east. In the *Odyssey*'s geography, Sparta is a two-day chariot journey from [[pylos|Pylos]], a route of roughly 90 kilometers that remains among the most geographically coherent elements in the poem.
