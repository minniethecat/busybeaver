# Patroclus

Patroclus is the beloved companion of Achilles, whose death before the walls of Troy is recounted in the *Iliad* but whose memory reverberates through the *Odyssey* in three distinct registers: as a marker of Nestor's grief for his own son, as a shade alongside Achilles in the underworld, and as the companion whose remains share an urn with the greatest of the Greeks. He never speaks in the *Odyssey*, yet his presence — always paired with Achilles — defines how heroic friendship transcends death.

## Identity and Names

Patroclus (Greek: Πάτροκλος, *Pátroklos*) is the son of Menoetius. In Pope's rendering he is "wise Patroclus" [003-150], an epithet that emphasises his counsel and loyalty over martial prowess. The *Odyssey* inherits his character from the *Iliad*, where his death at Hector's hands drives Achilles back into battle; here, he appears only in retrospective and infernal contexts, always in Achilles's shadow.

The name Patroclus — from πατήρ ("father") and κλέος ("glory") — means "glory of the father." The etymology is resonant: Patroclus's death is what brings Achilles (his surrogate father in affection, if not in age) his greatest glory — the slaying of Hector — yet Patroclus himself receives only the reflected radiance of that glory. He is the instrument of another's *kleos*, never its primary recipient.

## Patroclus in the Iliad

To understand Patroclus's role in the *Odyssey*, one must recall his *Iliad* trajectory. In the earlier epic, Patroclus is Achilles's closest companion, sent into battle wearing Achilles's armour after Achilles withdraws from the fighting. He drives the Trojans back from the ships, kills Sarpedon (Zeus's own son), and presses his attack to the very walls of Troy — where Apollo strikes him, Euphorbus wounds him, and Hector delivers the killing blow. His death is the turning point of the *Iliad*: it brings Achilles back into the war, sets in motion the death of Hector, and seals Achilles's own fate, since Thetis has prophesied that Achilles will die soon after Hector.

The *Odyssey* never recounts these events. It assumes the audience knows them and instead focuses on the *consequence* of Patroclus's death — the grief it leaves, the funeral it occasions, the underworld companionship it eternalises. This is characteristic of the *Odyssey*'s relationship to the *Iliad*: it is not a sequel but a meditation on aftermath.

## Appearances in the Odyssey

Patroclus is mentioned four times across three books, each deepening the thematic resonance of his loss.

### Book 3 — Nestor's Lament

When Nestor recounts the Trojan War dead to Telemachus, he names Patroclus in the company of the greatest fallen Greeks, using the toll as a prelude to mourning his own son Antilochus:

> "There wise Patroclus, fill an early grave: / There, too, my son — ah, once my best delight / Once swift of foot, and terrible in fight" [003-150]

Nestor's pairing of Patroclus with Antilochus establishes a parallel between the grief of Achilles for his companion and Nestor's grief for his son — both are "early graves" that define the survivors' sorrow.

### Book 11 — The Underworld

In the catalogue of shades Odysseus encounters in the underworld, Patroclus appears beside Achilles as a unified presence:

> "Till side by side along the dreary coast / Advanced Achilles' and Patroclus' ghost, / A friendly pair!" [011-590]

The phrase "a friendly pair" captures the inseparable bond that persists even in death — Patroclus and Achilles walk together in Hades as they fought together at Troy. This is the *Odyssey*'s most direct tribute to the central relationship of the *Iliad*.

### Book 24 — The Second Nekyia

In the underworld scene that closes the poem, Patroclus is mentioned twice. First, Agamemnon's shade describes Achilles's funeral procession:

> "And loved Patroclus still attends his shade" [024-039]

Then, addressing Achilles directly, Agamemnon describes the burial urn:

> "With dear Patroclus, thy departed friend: / In the same urn a separate space contains / Thy next beloved, Antilochus' remains" [024-109]

The urn's architecture — Patroclus and Antilochus sharing the vessel with their bones separated — literalises the theme of companionship in death that runs through all four mentions. Patroclus is never alone: he is always "loved," "dear," "wise," "beloved."

## The Question of Eros

The nature of Achilles and Patroclus's relationship was debated in antiquity as it is today. Homer never uses explicitly erotic language for their bond, but the intensity of Achilles's grief — his refusal to bury Patroclus's body until the ghost itself demands release, his slaughter of twelve Trojan captives on the pyre — exceeds what the poem's conventions of friendship prescribe. The *Odyssey*'s choice to keep Patroclus "side by side" with Achilles in Hades [011-590] and in the burial urn [024-109] perpetuates the ambiguity: are they companions in death because they were companions in life, or because their bond transcends the categories available to epic — a third thing, neither friendship nor eros but more consuming than either?

The poem does not answer this question. It does not need to. By keeping Patroclus always in Achilles's company — "a friendly pair" [011-590] — the *Odyssey* honours the relationship without defining it, leaving the reader (as the *Iliad* left its audience) to supply the emotional register.

## Thematic Significance

Patroclus in the *Odyssey* functions as the archetype of the *beloved companion* — a relationship category that the poem explores through multiple pairings (Odysseus and Eumaeus, Telemachus and Peiraeus, Achilles and Patroclus). His absence from the action of the *Odyssey* proper, appearing only in memory and in the underworld, heightens the sense that the heroic age of the *Iliad* has passed. The living Odysseus will return home; Patroclus, like Achilles, belongs irrevocably to the world of the dead.

There is also a structural irony in Patroclus's *Odyssey* role. In the *Iliad*, Patroclus is the substitute — he wears Achilles's armour, he fights in Achilles's place, he dies a death that in some sense belongs to Achilles. In the *Odyssey*, he is similarly substitutive: his name appears where Achilles's might be, his ghost walks where Achilles walks. He is, in both epics, the figure through whom another's story is told. His tragedy — and his dignity — is that he is never the protagonist of his own narrative.
