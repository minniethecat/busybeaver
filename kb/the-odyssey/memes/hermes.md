# Hermes

## Identity & Names

**Hermes** (Greek: Ἑρμῆς, Latin: *Mercurius* / Mercury) is the Olympian god of messengers, travelers, boundaries, commerce, and the guide of souls to the Underworld. In Pope's translation of the *Odyssey*, he appears as "Hermes" — the swift messenger executing the will of [[zeus|Zeus]], bearing the golden wand (*caduceus*) that marks his divine office:

> Immortal Hermes with the golden rod

His epithet *Argeiphontes* ("slayer of Argus") references his mythological role as the cunning liberator of Io, but in the *Odyssey* his identity is defined almost entirely by his function as divine messenger and guide. He is "the messenger of Jove", the one who bridges the gap between Olympus and the mortal world.

### Mythological Background

Born in a cave on Mount Cyllene, Hermes was the son of [[zeus|Zeus]] and the Pleiad Maia. The *Homeric Hymn to Hermes* records his precocious infancy: on his first day he invented the lyre from a tortoise shell and stole Apollo's cattle. When caught, he charmed Apollo with music until the elder god granted him heraldship, herds, and trade in exchange for the instrument.

The epithet *Argeiphontes* commemorates his most famous exploit: lulling the hundred-eyed giant Argus Panoptes to sleep through storytelling, then beheading him. His golden wand — the *caduceus* — appears throughout the *Odyssey*:

> He grasps the wand that causes sleep to fly,
> Or in soft slumber seals the wakeful eye

As *Psychopompus*, Hermes escorts souls to the Underworld — a function realized in Book 24, where he leads the suitors' shades past the streams of Ocean to their eternal rest.

## Role & Significance

Hermes serves three crucial functions in the *Odyssey*. First, he delivers Zeus's decree that [[calypso|Calypso]] must release [[odysseus|Odysseus]] — the event that sets the hero's return in motion after seven years of stasis. Second, he gives Odysseus the herb *moly* to resist [[circe|Circe]]'s enchantment. Third, as psychopomp he escorts the dead suitors' souls to the Underworld in Book 24.

These interventions create a divine scaffolding. His dispatch in Book 1 (fulfilled in Book 5) begins the *nostos*; his moly in Book 10 enables survival at a supernatural threshold; his escort in Book 24 closes the poem. Between Hermes's first journey to Ogygia and his last to Hades, the entire epic unfolds.

In Book 5, Hermes's flight to Calypso's island is one of the poem's most beautiful descriptive passages:

> He grasps the wand that causes sleep to fly,
> Or in soft slumber seals the wakeful eye;
> Then shoots from heaven to high Pieria's steep,
> And skims the surface of the boundless deep.

> With wings expanded o'er the foaming flood,
> A cormorant he skimm'd the briny sea.

He "o'er the world of waters flew", arriving at the remote cave where Odysseus weeps for home. His message is direct:

> "Hermes, thou chosen messenger of heaven,
> Go, to the nymph the sovereign mandate bear:
> The wise Ulysses to his home restore."

In Book 10, Hermes appears as a protective guide:

> This Hermes gave, then, gliding off the plain

He is "powerful with the wand of gold", a description echoing his Book 5 epiphany. Hermes is also the patron deity of [[autolycus|Autolycus]], Odysseus's grandfather: "Hermes, his patron god, those gifts bestow'd". The cunning intelligence that defines Odysseus — his *[[metis]]* — has its genealogical root in Hermes's favor toward his grandfather's line.

### Book 1: The Council's Decree

Hermes does not appear in person, but his mission is ordained. Zeus declares: "Hermes I sent"; Athena repeats: "Let Hermes to the Atlantic isle repair". The double dispatch — Hermes to Calypso, Athena to Telemachus — establishes the epic's dual plot.

### Book 5: The Flight to Ogygia

Book 5 contains the *Odyssey*'s most celebrated description of divine motion. Hermes binds on his sandals, takes up his wand, shoots from Olympus to Pieria, then skims the waves like a cormorant until "the isle of Ogygia rose to view". The garden he finds — vine, violets, four fountains — is so beautiful "a god might gaze, and wander with delight".

Calypso welcomes him hospitably, but when she learns his errand, her warmth turns to protest — a famous complaint against divine double standards. Hermes insists that "so destiny commands", and she obeys.

### Book 8: The Song of Demodocus

Demodocus sings of Ares and Aphrodite ensnared. Apollo asks Hermes whether he would endure such chains for Aphrodite; Hermes replies he would gladly suffer triple bonds — the god's lighter, comic aspect.

### Book 10: The Gift of Moly

Hermes meets Odysseus disguised as a young man and provides the counter-charm: *moly*, "black at the root, but milky white the flower". He instructs Odysseus to make Circe swear a solemn oath. When her potion fails, Circe names Hermes as the source:

> "Thou art Ulysses! that sagacious man,
> Whose wandering course the wise Mercurius' plan
> Foretold should lead him to this magic coast."

### Book 12: The Recalled Warning

Odysseus recalls that "Hermes convey'd these counsels of the gods" — the warning about the Cattle of the Sun.

### Book 19: The Patron of Autolycus

The scar-recognition scene invokes Hermes's favor: "Hermes, his patron god, those gifts bestow'd". The gifts — skill in oaths and transformation — pass by inheritance to Odysseus.

### Book 24: The Psychopomp

In the final book, Hermes assumes his most ancient function:

> Thus they: while Hermes o'er the dreary plain
> Led by the hand the suitor-ghostly train.

He guides the suitors' shades past Ocean's streams and the White Rock to the asphodel meadow, where they encounter [[achilles|Achilles]] and [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]. The messenger who opened the action by flying to Ogygia now closes it by descending to Hades.

> Thus o'er the world of waters Hermes flew,
> Till from the deep the isle of Ogygia rose to view.

> Hermes the hospitable rite partook,
> Divine refection of the gods.

> By Hermes, powerful with the wand of gold,
> The vital herb I cropp'd.

> "God of the golden wand! on what behest
> Arriv'st thou here?" the nymph divine address'd.

## Relationships

- **[[zeus|Zeus]]** — Hermes's father and sovereign. He acts as Zeus's direct agent: "Hermes, thou chosen messenger of heaven". Their relationship is one of absolute obedience — the *Odyssey*'s Hermes is purely instrumental, Zeus's voice made mobile.

- **[[calypso|Calypso]]** — The nymph who holds Odysseus captive. She greets Hermes as "God of the golden wand!" but her hospitality turns to protest at the double standard that forbids goddesses mortal lovers. Hermes simply insists on obedience.

- **[[circe|Circe]]** — The enchantress whose magic Hermes's *moly* neutralizes: "This Hermes gave". Circe acknowledges her defeat as Hermes's doing.

- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]** — The mortal beneficiary of Hermes's interventions — freed by the decree to Calypso, protected by the moly gift. His grandfather's divine patron suggests a hereditary link.

- **[[autolycus|Autolycus]]** — Odysseus's grandfather, favored by Hermes above all mortals. Hermes bestowed on him "various gifts" — oaths, transformation, cunning — that pass to Odysseus.

- **[[athena|Athena]]** — The two gods complement each other. Athena guides by inspiration and disguise; Hermes enables by transmission and passage. Together: wisdom and motion, insight and crossing.

## Analysis

### The Perfect Messenger

Hermes in the *Odyssey* is defined by function rather than personality. Unlike the trickster infant of the *Homeric Hymn*, the Odyssey's Hermes is a conduit for Zeus's will. This is structurally necessary: in a poem about the journey home, the god of travel and boundaries enables passage across every threshold — Ogygia, Circe's island, the boundary between life and death — that defines the hero's progress.

### Hermes and Mortal Intelligence

Hermes's gift of *moly* — "black at the root, but milky white the flower" — represents divine knowledge enabling mortal action. Odysseus cannot defeat Circe by strength; he needs Hermes's foreknowledge. This pattern — divine intelligence enabling mortal [[metis]] — mirrors Athena's broader patronage and makes Hermes a crucial link in the chain that makes *nostos* possible.

The herb's dual nature mirrors Hermes himself: a god who moves between Olympus and Hades, blessing thieves and merchants, guiding the living traveler and the dead soul. In giving Odysseus this gift, Hermes imparts a fragment of his own liminal power.

### The Structural Hermes

Hermes's three appearances create the poem's divine architecture. Book 5 releases Odysseus from stasis, beginning the *nostos*. Book 10 enables survival at a supernatural threshold. Book 24 closes the epic by conducting the suitors to their destination, mirroring the Nekyia of Book 11.

This tripartite structure maps onto the poem's deepest themes. Hermes releases Odysseus from Calypso as he releases the suitors from life. He protects from Circe as divine knowledge protects from mortal peril. In each case, he stands at a boundary — captivity/freedom, danger/safety, life/death — and enables passage. He is the god who makes the *Odyssey* possible.

### The God of Boundaries

The *Odyssey* is organized around boundaries — sea/land, human/divine, life/death, home/exile. Hermes, who presides over all crossings, is the poem's structural deity. His caduceus opens paths; his sandals traverse worlds; his psychopomp role makes him the only Olympian who crosses the boundary between living and dead. In a poem whose hero must cross every threshold to reach home, Hermes is not merely an assistant — he is the divine principle of passage itself.
