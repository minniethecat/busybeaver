# Antiphus

Antiphus is an Ithacan elder of Odysseus's generation who remained loyal to the absent king's household during the suitors' occupation. Together with Halitherses and Mentor, he represents the old guard — the remnant of Odysseus's contemporaries who remember his just rule and await his return.

## Identity & Names

The name Antiphus (Greek: Αντιφος, *Antiphos*) is a common Homeric name borne by several figures across the epic cycle. This Antiphus is distinguished by his Ithacan context and his association with Halitherses. He is mourned as a lost companion: "Yet still his Antiphus he loves, he mourns" (002-047) — a line that suggests the depth of Odysseus's bond with his loyal followers.

The assembly scene in Book 2 provides Antiphus's context. After Telemachus calls the Ithacan assembly — the first in twenty years — the elder Aegyptius rises to speak. Aegyptius is identified as the father of four sons: one, Antiphus, who went to Troy with Odysseus and was killed by the Cyclops on the return journey; another, Eurynomus, who joined the suitors. This divided family — one son loyal and dead, the other disloyal and alive — crystallizes the moral fragmentation the suitors' occupation has produced in Ithaca.

## The Divided House of Aegyptius

Antiphus's father Aegyptius is one of the few named elders who speak in the Ithacan assembly (Book 2). His family embodies the civic fracture at the heart of the poem: one son (Antiphus) died serving Odysseus; another son (Eurynomus) survives by serving the suitors. Aegyptius himself occupies a position of anguished neutrality — an elder of the old order who has lost a son to loyalty and watches another son degrade himself through disloyalty.

This family drama, sketched in a few lines, illuminates the _Odyssey_'s political dimension. The suitors are not merely individual villains; they represent a generational rupture within Ithaca itself, dividing families and corrupting the bonds that hold the community together. Antiphus's absence — his death at the hands of the Cyclops — is a loss that can never be avenged, a sacrifice that cannot be repaid.

## Role in the Odyssey

Antiphus appears briefly but significantly. In Book 2, he is alluded to among those Odysseus longs for (002-047). In Book 17, he appears alongside Halitherses as a sage of the old generation:

> "With Antiphus, and Halitherses sage / (His father's counsellors, revered for age)" (017-087)

His pairing with Halitherses — the augur who publicly opposed the suitors — marks him as belonging to the loyal faction. Unlike Halitherses, Antiphus does not speak publicly; his loyalty is quiet, a presence rather than a performance. He represents the many Ithacans whose fidelity to Odysseus was expressed not in dramatic confrontation but in patient endurance.

The epithet structure of 017-087 is telling: Antiphus is named first, Halitherses second and qualified as "sage." Antiphus requires no epithet — his name alone carries the weight of association. This suggests a figure whose identity was sufficiently established in the Ithacan oral tradition that the poet could invoke him without elaboration.

## The Other Antiphus

Homer's world contains at least three distinct figures named Antiphus. The Trojan Antiphus, son of Priam, appears in the _Iliad_ and is killed by Agamemnon. A second Antiphus, son of Talaemenes, leads the Maeonian contingent at Troy. The Ithacan Antiphus — the loyal elder — is distinguished by his domestic context and his association with the old order. The name's recurrence across the epic cycle reflects a common Homeric pattern: the same name applied to figures in different narrative spheres (Trojan, allied, Ithacan), each iteration reinforcing the name's heroic connotations while the specific context differentiates the character.
