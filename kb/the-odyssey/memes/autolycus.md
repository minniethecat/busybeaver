# Autolycus

Autolycus is the maternal grandfather of Odysseus, a figure of ancestral cunning whose reputation for craft and oath-breaking prefigures the hero's own celebrated resourcefulness. Though he never appears in person in the *Odyssey*, his name is invoked at four critical moments: at Odysseus's naming and the boar hunt that leaves the identifying scar (Book 19), during the scar's revelation to Euryclea (Book 21), and in Odysseus's reunion with Laertes (Book 24).

## Identity and Names

Autolycus (Greek: Αὐτόλυκος, *Autólykos*) means "the wolf itself" or "the very wolf" — a name suggesting predation and cunning. Pope calls him "Autolycus the bold" and describes him as "a mighty name / For spotless faith and deeds of martial fame" [019-479]. The epithet "spotless faith" is ironic: in broader Greek tradition, Autolycus was notorious as a cattle-thief who could change the appearance of stolen beasts and swear false oaths without detection.

His gifts, Pope notes, came from divine patronage: "Hermes, his patron god, those gifts bestow'd" [019-481]. The association with Hermes — god of thieves, travelers, and boundary-crossers — marks Autolycus as a liminal figure whose talents run toward deception. This inheritance passes to his grandson: Odysseus's signature epithet *polymetis* (of many wiles) has its genealogical root in Autolycus.

## Role in the Odyssey

Autolycus's importance in the poem is concentrated in Book 19, where the story of Odysseus's naming, youth, and the boar-hunt scar is recounted at length.

### The Naming of Odysseus (Book 19)

When Autolycus visits Ithaca shortly after his daughter Anticlea gives birth, he is asked to name the child. His choice is deliberate and prophetic:

> "Autolycus the bold (a mighty name / For spotless faith and deeds of martial fame: / Hermes, his patron god, those gifts bestow'd) ..." [019-479]

He names the infant *Odysseus* — a name that Pope glosses as connected to the Greek verb meaning "to be angry" or "to hate," reflecting Autolycus's own experience of having made enemies throughout the world. The naming scene establishes that Odysseus's identity — his very name — is a legacy from his maternal grandfather.

### The Boar Hunt and the Scar (Book 19)

After Odysseus comes of age, he visits Autolycus on Mount Parnassus, where a boar hunt is organized. The young Odysseus kills the boar but receives a deep wound on his thigh:

> "Autolycus, enamoured with his fame, / Confirm'd the cure; and from the Delphic dome / With added gifts return'd him glorious home" [019-552]

The scar — and Autolycus's role in healing it and sending Odysseus back to Ithaca with honors — becomes the very mark by which Euryclea recognizes the returned hero in Book 19. The scar thus functions as both a physical legacy of the grandfather's world and the narrative key to Odysseus's recognition.

### The Scar's Revelation (Book 21)

In Book 21, when Odysseus reveals his identity to the herdsmen, he invokes the scar again: "When with Autolycus' sons, of yore, / On Parnass' top I chased the tusky boar" [021-241]. The shared memory of Autolycus's household on Parnassus becomes proof of Odysseus's identity.

### The Final Reunion (Book 24)

In the poem's closing book, Odysseus reminds Laertes of their shared past, which includes the journey to Autolycus's realm:

> "To old Autolycus' realms I went" [024-398]

Here Autolycus is "old" — the epithet conveying both age and ancestral authority, anchoring the poem's final recognition scene in genealogical memory.

## Thematic Significance

Autolycus embodies the principle of *inherited identity* that runs through the *Odyssey*. Odysseus is not self-made; his cunning, his name, his very scar are gifts and marks received through his maternal line. The grandfather who "enjoy'd the theft" and was favored by Hermes prefigures the grandson who will deceive Polyphemus, outwit Circe, and engineer the destruction of the suitors.

This genealogical transmission of character traits is a recurring Homeric theme: Telemachus inherits his father's potential for action, just as Odysseus inherits Autolycus's cunning. The scar on Odysseus's thigh is the literal inscription of this inheritance — a wound received in the grandfather's domain that identifies the hero to those who know him best.
