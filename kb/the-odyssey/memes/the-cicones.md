# The Cicones

## Identity

The Cicones (Greek: Κίκονες, *Kikones*) are a Thracian people living on the southern coast of Thrace, near the city of Ismarus. In the *Iliad*, they fought as allies of Troy under their leader Euphemus. When [[odysseus|Odysseus]] departs from Troy with his twelve ships, the Cicones are his first target — and the raid on their city, followed by their counterattack, establishes the pattern of suffering through recklessness that characterizes the entire *Odyssey*.

The Cicones are the first human adversaries Odysseus faces after the Trojan War. Unlike the monsters and gods of later episodes, they are a real people — Thracians, known to history as well as myth — and their resistance to the Greek raiders is militarily organized and devastatingly effective.

## In the Odyssey

### The Raid (Book 9)

Fresh from the sack of Troy, Odysseus and his crew sail to Ismarus, the city of the Cicones. What follows is the poem's first cautionary tale about the consequences of unchecked impulse:

1. **Sack and plunder**: Odysseus and his men sack the city, kill the men, and take the women and treasure as spoils. This is standard Homeric warfare — the same pattern they followed at Troy.

2. **The fatal delay**: Odysseus urges immediate departure. His men, drunk on victory and wine, refuse. They slaughter sheep and cattle on the beach and feast late into the night.

3. **Counterattack at dawn**: The surviving Cicones, who had fled inland, rally their neighbors — "Cicones from the mountains, skilled at fighting from chariots and on foot" — and attack the Greeks at dawn. The battle rages through the morning; by the time Odysseus orders retreat, six men from each ship have been killed — a total of 72 dead.

> The Cicones routed us; disaster fell on our doomed companions through their own recklessness.

The episode encapsulates the pattern that will repeat throughout the wanderings: victory through force, followed by disaster through indiscipline. The crew's refusal to obey Odysseus's command directly causes their first casualties after Troy.

### Maron's Gift

Among the spoils, Odysseus spares Maron, a priest of Apollo, and his family. In gratitude, Maron gives him twelve jars of a remarkable wine — so strong it must be diluted twenty-to-one with water, "a drink for the gods." This is the wine Odysseus later uses to intoxicate [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] in the [[the-cyclops-episode|Cyclops episode]]. The gift that saves Odysseus in Book 9 originates in the raid on the Cicones — a thread that connects the first adventure to the most famous one.

## Characteristics

| Trait | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| Military capability | Mounted and infantry forces; rally neighbors; inflict heavy casualties |
| Regional alliance network | Call on "Cicones from the mountains" for reinforcement |
| Agricultural wealth | City of Ismarus; flocks of sheep and cattle; vineyards |
| Trojan alliance | Fought alongside Troy in the *Iliad* |
| Divine connection | Maron is a priest of Apollo; Apollo's wine features in the episode |

The Cicones are notable for being entirely human antagonists. Unlike the Cyclops (monster), Circe (goddess), or the Laestrygonians (giants), they are a real people who respond to a raid with organized military resistance. Their counterattack is the first hard lesson of the wanderings: not every enemy can be overcome by force alone, and discipline matters more than courage.

## Significance

The Cicones episode occupies a pivotal structural position — it is the very first adventure after Troy, and it foreshadows the central themes of the *Odyssey*:

- **The cost of hubris**: Odysseus's crew sacks Ismarus successfully but pays for their overconfidence with 72 lives. The pattern of victory-through-intelligence undone by loss-through-recklessness is established here and repeated through Book 12.
- **The limits of leadership**: Odysseus gives the correct order (sail immediately) but cannot enforce it. This tension between strategic wisdom and command authority recurs throughout — most fatally with the Cattle of the Sun.
- **Causal threads**: Maron's wine, spared in this episode, becomes the instrument of escape from Polyphemus. The *Odyssey* weaves its episodes through such connected objects and debts.
- **Allies of Troy**: The Cicones fought for Troy, making the raid a continuation of the war by other means — and demonstrating that the war's violence does not end with the city's fall.
