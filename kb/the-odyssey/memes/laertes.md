# Laertes

## Identity & Names

**Laertes** (Greek: Λαέρτης, *Laertēs*) is the son of Arcesius, father of Odysseus, and grandfather of Telemachus — the patriarch of Ithaca's royal line across three generations. Once a vigorous king who expanded Ithaca's territory through conquest (he captured the coastal town of Nericus [024-448]), he has retreated from public life in grief, living in rags on a remote farm, tending his orchard. His name (from *laos* "people" + *eretēs* "rower" or "stirrer") suggests one who rouses or leads the people — a role he has tragically abdicated.

He appears only in Book 24, but his absence haunts the entire poem. The *Odyssey*'s argument describes the reunion: "Ulysses in the country goes to the retirement of his father, Laertes; he finds him busied in his garden all alone; the manner of his discovery to him is beautifully described" [024-004–006].

## Role & Significance

Laertes represents the final link in the chain of restoration that structures the *Odyssey*. Odysseus has reclaimed his wife (Penelope), his son (Telemachus), his household, and his kingdom — but Book 24 reveals that his father remains unrecovered. Laertes lives in self-imposed exile on a remote farm, a king reduced to the condition of a laborer:

> But all alone the hoary king he found;
> His habit course, but warmly wrapp'd around;
> His head, that bow'd with many a pensive care,
> Fenced with a double cap of goatskin hair:
> His buskins old, in former service torn,
> But swell repair'd; and gloves against the thorn.
> In this array the kingly gardener stood,
> And clear'd a plant, encumber'd with its wood. [024-272–279]

The reunion of father and son in Book 24 is the poem's culminating recognition scene. Odysseus first tests him with a false story — "More gentle methods on weak age employs: / And moves the sorrows to enhance the joys" [024-292] — but when he sees his father's grief, he can no longer continue the deception:

> Deep from his soul he sigh'd, and sorrowing spread
> A cloud of ashes on his hoary head.
> Trembling with agonies of strong delight
> Stood the great son, heart-wounded with the sight:
> He ran, he seized him with a strict embrace,
> With thousand kisses wander'd o'er his face:
> "I, I am he; O father, rise! behold
> Thy son, with twenty winters now grown old;
> Thy son, so long desired, so long detain'd,
> Restored, and breathing in his native land." [024-380–390]

Laertes demands proof, and Odysseus provides two tokens: the scar on his thigh and the orchard trees his father gave him in childhood — "Twelve pear-trees, bowing with their pendent load, / And ten, that red with blushing apples glow'd; / Full fifty purple figs" [024-406–408]. The specificity of the orchard — each tree counted, each variety named — makes it the most intimate recognition token in the poem.

Laertes's subsequent transformation is one of the epic's most dramatic reversals. Athena restores his vigor:

> Pallas attending gives his frame to shine
> With awful port, and majesty divine;
> His gazing son admires the godlike grace,
> And air celestial dawning o'er his face. [024-438–441]

The old man who began the book digging in the dirt ends it as a warrior. He kills Eupeithes, the father of Antinous, with a single spear-cast: "Full at Eupithes drove the deathful spear: / The brass-cheek'd helmet opens to the wound; / He falls, earth thunders, and his arms resound" [024-616–618]. Three generations of Ithaca's royal house stand together in arms:

> "And bless'd! thrice bless'd this happy day! (he cries,)
> The day that shows me, ere I close my eyes,
> A son and grandson of the Arcesian name
> Strive for fair virtue, and contest for fame!" [024-605–608]

## Appearances

### Book 24: The Reunion

The entire book belongs, in its emotional center, to Laertes. Odysseus finds his father alone in the orchard: "Beneath a neighbouring tree, the chief divine / Gazed o'er his sire, retracing every line, / The ruins of himself, now worn away / With age, yet still majestic in decay!" [024-280–283].

Odysseus's approach is characteristically circuitous — he praises the old man's gardening before revealing his identity: "Great is thy skill, O father! great thy toil, / Thy careful hand is stamp'd on all the soil" [024-298–299]. He asks whether this is Ithaca, and whether Laertes still lives [024-315–328]. When Laertes, believing his son dead, mourns anew [024-335–358], Odysseus reveals himself.

The recognition reverses Book 19's pattern: where Euryclea recognized Odysseus by his scar before he was ready, here Odysseus chooses the moment of revelation. The trees are a more intimate token than the scar — they are the gift of a father to a child, returned as the sign of a child to a father: "Whate'er my childish fancy ask'd, bestow'd" [024-405].

> Smit with the signs which all his doubts explain,
> His heart within him melts; his knees sustain
> Their feeble weight no more: his arms alone
> Support him, round the loved Ulysses thrown;
> He faints, he sinks, with mighty joys oppress'd:
> Ulysses clasps him to his eager breast. [024-412–417]

After the recognition, Athena restores Laertes's youth and the old man bathes and emerges transformed. His first act as a restored warrior is to kill Eupeithes — the father of Antinous, whose son was the first suitor Odysseus killed. The symmetry is exact: a father avenges a son, and a father dies for a son.

## Relationships

- **Odysseus**: His son, whose twenty-year absence has broken him. The reunion in Book 24 restores both men — Laertes regains his son and his identity. Their bond is expressed through the orchard: the trees Laertes planted for his child become the token by which the child reclaims the father.

- **Telemachus**: His grandson, the third generation. Laertes's joy at seeing both son and grandson armed together is the poem's final image of familial wholeness [024-605–608].

- **Anticlea**: His wife, who died of grief for Odysseus [011-202]. Her absence makes the reunion bittersweet — the mother who died of longing cannot be brought back.

- **Eumaeus**: The swineherd remembers Laertes's kindness: he treated the kidnapped boy generously, and that generosity is repaid decades later in the slave's loyalty to the king's son [015-352].

- **Penelope**: His daughter-in-law, whose fidelity makes the household's restoration possible.

## Analysis

### The Orchard and Rootedness

The orchard where Odysseus finds Laertes is the poem's deepest image of rootedness. Unlike the sea that has tormented Odysseus, or the palace that the suitors have defiled, the orchard is a place of steady cultivation — trees planted, tended, and harvested across decades. When Odysseus enumerates the trees — twelve pear, ten apple, fifty fig [024-406–408] — he names the specific coordinates of home. The orchard is the agricultural counterpart of the olive-tree marriage bed: both are symbols of a life rooted in particular soil and particular care.

### Three-Generation Restoration

The *Odyssey* insists that restoration is not complete until Laertes stands armed beside his son and grandson. The household (*oikos*) is a dynastic entity, and its health requires continuity across time. The suitors threatened the entire lineage of Arcesius — Laertes's restoration guarantees the line will continue. Athena's final words to Laertes — "Son of Arcesius, reverend warrior, hear!" [024-610] — invoke the ancestral name in the moment of its renewal.

### The King as Laborer

Laertes's condition — a king dressed in goatskin, sleeping on the ground, digging in the dirt — is the most extreme image of the disorder Odysseus's absence has created: "In this array the kingly gardener stood, / And clear'd a plant, encumber'd with its wood" [024-278–279]. It inverts the social order more radically than the suitors' feasting: a kingdom whose king has become a peasant is a kingdom that has lost its center.

### Recognition and the Limits of Deception

Odysseus's decision to test Laertes with a false story before revealing himself is morally ambiguous. The poet notes that Odysseus chose "More gentle methods on weak age" [024-292] — but when Laertes spreads ashes on his head in grief, Odysseus instantly abandons the test. The hero who has spent twenty years deceiving everyone finally learns that not every deception is justified.
