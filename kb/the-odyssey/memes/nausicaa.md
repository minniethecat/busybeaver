# Nausicaa

## Identity & Names

**Nausicaa** (Greek: Ναυσικάα, *Nausikaa*) is the daughter of King Alcinous and Queen Arete of the Phaeacians, the legendary seafaring people who dwell on the island of Scheria. Her name combines *naus* (ship) and *kaio* (to burn), suggesting a connection to the sea and perhaps to the fiery beauty that catches Odysseus's attention. She appears only in Books 6–8, but her role is pivotal: without her aid, Odysseus would never reach home.

## Role & Significance

Nausicaa represents youthful courage and instinctive hospitality. When her maids flee from the naked, sea-encrusted stranger on the beach, Nausicaa stands her ground. "If in that bright disguise" (006-187) a god stands before her, she will honor him; if a mortal, she will offer aid. Her reasoning is theoxeny in its purest form: treat strangers well regardless of their identity, because identity is never certain.

Her interaction with Odysseus is charged with subtle erotic possibility — she confesses to her maids that she would like a husband like him — but Homer handles this with delicacy. Nausicaa remains a model of maidenly propriety, and Odysseus treats her with formal courtesy. Their relationship is the poem's most wholesome male-female encounter, free of the coercive dynamics that characterize his relationships with Calypso and Circe.

## Appearances

- **Book 6**: Inspired by Athena in a dream to go wash clothes by the river, she discovers Odysseus, provides him clothing and directions, and escorts him toward the city.
- **Book 8**: Briefly appears as Odysseus prepares to depart; he thanks her, promising to remember her as a savior.

## Relationships

- **Alcinous and Arete**: Her parents, who receive Odysseus based on her recommendation.
- **Odysseus**: The stranger she aids. Their brief encounter contains the poem's most idealized vision of hospitality between strangers.
- **Athena**: Inspires her through the dream that leads to the meeting.

## Analysis

Nausicaa represents what the *Odyssey* values most: the instinct to help a stranger without calculation. She asks nothing in return, expects nothing, and is content to be remembered. Her brief appearance is essential to the epic's moral vision — she proves that xenia is not merely a social obligation but a natural human impulse toward compassion.
