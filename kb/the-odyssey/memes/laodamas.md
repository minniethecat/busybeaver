# Laodamas

Laodamas is a prince of Phaeacia, the son of King Alcinous and Queen Arete, and — as Pope notes — his father's "best-beloved" [007-239]. A champion athlete of grace and courtesy, he serves as the bridge between the Phaeacian court and the disguised Odysseus, extending the invitation that precipitates one of the epic's most memorable episodes: Odysseus's discus throw and self-revelation in strength.

## Identity and Names

Laodamas (Greek: Λαοδάμας, *Laodámas*, "tamer of the people") bears a name that encodes the qualities of leadership — the compound of *laos* (people) and *damazo* (to tame, subdue, or master) suggests the ruler who governs not through force alone but through the cultivated authority that commands voluntary obedience. It is a name befitting a prince destined for kingship.

He is introduced in Book 7 as the prince who yields his seat to Odysseus at Alcinous's command, an act of deference to both his father's authority and the stranger's dignity:

> "And from his seat Laodamas removed / (The monarch's offspring, and his best-beloved)" [007-239]

His identity is defined by his filial bond to Alcinous and his athletic prowess; he appears alongside his brothers — "three brave sons, from great Alcinous sprung" [008-135] — at the games. Among them, he is preeminent: "Or equall'd by Laodamas alone" [008-133].

## Role in the Odyssey

Laodamas's primary function in the narrative is to serve as the courteous inviter — the Phaeacian whose polite hospitality stands in deliberate contrast to Euryalus's blunt insult. At the games in Book 8, he rises to address the still-unnamed stranger:

> "In act to speak, Laodamas ascends. / 'O friends,' he cries, 'the stranger seems well skill'd / To try the illustrious labours of the field'" [008-153]

His invitation is genuine and warm — he sees in Odysseus a man who could compete, and he wishes to include him. The phrasing is notable: Laodamas does not demand or challenge; he *observes* ("seems well skill'd") and *suggests*. This is the language of hospitality, not competition.

When Odysseus declines, citing grief and weariness, it is [[euryalus|Euryalus]] who escalates with contempt. But Laodamas had set the right tone; his invitation was never an insult. The contrast between the two young Phaeacians is deliberate and instructive: Laodamas embodies the *xenia* principle of active inclusion, while Euryalus stumbles into its violation. Together they form a diptych — the model host and the corrected offender — that teaches the poem's audience the spectrum of guest-friendship behaviour.

Odysseus himself later names Laodamas as a peer in prowess — a striking endorsement from the hero who has just demonstrated his superiority over every other Phaeacian athlete:

> "In such heroic games I yield to none, / Or yield to brave Laodamas alone" [008-249]

But he adds a crucial qualification: "A friend is sacred, and I style him friend" [008-251] — meaning he would never compete against a host. This moment is rich with dramatic irony: the audience knows that Odysseus will soon be the guest who receives the Phaeacians' conveyance home, and his declaration of friendship with Laodamas seals the bond between guest and host that will enable that journey. The rhetorical gesture — "I style him friend" — transforms athletic rivalry into the language of *philia*, the bond of reciprocal obligation that structures Greek guest-friendship.

## Characterisation

Laodamas is the model young aristocrat of the Phaeacian court — athletic, courteous, and loyal to his father. His willingness to yield his seat to a stranger [007-239] demonstrates the humility that his fellow prince Euryalus must learn through apology. Pope's description of him as Alcinous's "best-beloved" [007-239] suggests a particular warmth between father and son, and his conduct throughout Book 8 bears this out: he does nothing that would shame his father's house or violate the guest-right.

His role in the narrative is brief but structurally essential. Without Laodamas's invitation, there is no occasion for Euryalus's insult; without the insult, there is no discus throw; without the discus throw, Odysseus's identity as a man of heroic stature remains undisclosed to his Phaeacian hosts. Laodamas is thus the narrative catalyst — the courteous hand that sets in motion the chain of events leading to Odysseus's self-revelation and, ultimately, to his conveyance home to Ithaca.
