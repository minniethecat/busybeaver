# Peiraeus

Peiraeus is an Ithacan of proven loyalty who serves as Telemachus's trusted retainer during the prince's return from Sparta. His two appearances in the *Odyssey* — in Books 15 and 17 — frame Telemachus's journey home and arrival at the assembly, demonstrating the network of faithful servants that sustains Odysseus's household during his absence.

## Identity and Names

Peiraeus (Greek: Πειραιεύς, *Peiraieus*) appears in Pope's translation as *Peiraeus* — the same spelling used for the Athenian port in later Greek history, though there is no mythological or etymological connection between the two. Pope characterizes him with the epithet "thou whom time has proved / A faithful servant, by thy prince beloved" [015-592], establishing his role entirely through his relationship to Telemachus.

## Role in the Odyssey

Peiraeus serves a narrow but significant function as Telemachus's designated guardian of persons and property. In Book 15, when Telemachus disembarks at Ithaca and must proceed to the swineherd's hut in disguise, he cannot bring the gifts he received from Menelaus or the stranger Theoclymenus with him. He turns to his trusted companion:

> "Thou whom time has proved / A faithful servant, by thy prince beloved! / Till we returning shall our guest demand, / Accept this charge with honour, at our hand." [015-592]

Peiraeus responds without hesitation: "Joyful I obey, / Well pleased the hospitable rites to pay. / The presence of thy guest shall best reward / (If long thy stay) the absence of my lord" [015-596]. His reply encapsulates the Ithacan ideal of xenia — hospitality extended on behalf of the absent master.

In Book 17, Telemachus arrives at the Ithacan assembly and the elders question him about his journey. Peiraeus appears trailing Theoclymenus, the prophet-guest entrusted to him:

> "Much ask'd the seniors; till Peiraeus came. / The stranger-guest pursued him close behind" [017-090]

Peiraeus then asks Telemachus for "slaves to bring / The gifts and treasures of the Spartan king" [017-093] from his ship to a secure location, completing his custodial duty. This moment demonstrates his practical competence — he has kept both the guest and the treasures safe throughout Telemachus's absence, fulfilling every aspect of his charge.

## Character Analysis

Peiraeus's role is emblematic of the loyal retainer type in the *Odyssey* — a figure whose narrative significance derives not from heroic deeds but from the faithful discharge of a trust. Like Eumaeus (the loyal swineherd) and Philoetius (the faithful cowherd), Peiraeus represents the principle that household loyalty is the moral foundation upon which Odysseus's eventual restoration rests.

His immediate and cheerful obedience to Telemachus's command contrasts with the suitors' defiance of proper authority, making Peiraeus a moral counterweight to the disorder in Ithaca. Though he appears in only two books, his reliability — time-proven and prince-approved — mirrors the broader thematic concern with faithfulness as the measure of character.
