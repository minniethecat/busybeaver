# Menelaus

## Identity & Names

**Menelaus** (Greek: Μενέλαος) is the king of Sparta, son of Atreus, and husband of [[helen|Helen]] — the woman whose abduction sparked the Trojan War. In Pope's *Odyssey*, he is most often referred to as "Atrides" (son of Atreus), a patronymic he shares with his brother [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]. He rules from Sparta, "Sparta whose walls a range of hills surrounds" [004-014], and is described as "the martial Menelaus" [003-334] and a king "favour'd of high Jove" [015-082].

Pope deploys shifting epithets for Menelaus: "great Atrides" for regal authority, "the warlike brother" for kinship with Agamemnon, and — in Proteus's climactic prophecy — "the son of Jove, and beauteous Helen's lord" [004-787]. At moments of deep emotion, the epithets fall away: "Deep from his inmost soul Atrides sigh'd" [004-457].

Nestor describes Menelaus as wise and resourceful:

> And sure he will; for Menelaus is wise. [003-436]

Pisistratus echoes: "To crowd thy various gifts, the sage assign'd / The glory of a firm capacious mind" [004-273].

His palace is the scene of the poem's most elaborate display of heroic hospitality (*xenia*), a double wedding feast at which Telemachus and Pisistratus arrive unannounced and are immediately welcomed with food, wine, and baths — the model of proper guest-friendship against which the suitors' abuse in Ithaca is implicitly measured.

## Role & Significance

Menelaus serves two functions in the *Odyssey*: he is the source of the most important intelligence about Odysseus's whereabouts, and his court is the poem's showcase of proper heroic hospitality. His narrative embodies the successful *nostos* (homecoming) that Odysseus has not yet achieved — the fortunate return of a Greek hero who, despite years of wandering, now reigns in prosperity.

### The Successful Nostos

Menelaus's own return from Troy was arduous: "There wander'd Menelaus through foreign shores" [003-400], driven as far as Egypt and the island of Pharos, where he wrestled the shape-shifting sea-god Proteus to learn his fate. But unlike Odysseus, Menelaus's wanderings ended:

> And Menelaus moves to cross the main;
> And Menelaus came, but came the last. [003-190, 003-218]

That he "came the last" of the Greek leaders to reach home — yet *did* reach home — makes him the perfect informant for Telemachus. He has experienced protracted wandering and, crucially, survived it.

His wanderings spanned eight years across the eastern Mediterranean: "From Cypress to the far Phoenician coast / (Sidon the capital), I stretch'd my toil / Through regions fatten'd with the flows of Nile" [004-109]. Stranded on Pharos for twenty days without provisions, he was met by the sea-goddess Eidothea — "a bright cerulean form" [004-505], daughter of [[proteus|Proteus]] — who revealed the means to ambush her shape-shifting father.

Hiding beneath seal skins whose "unsavoury stench of oil, and brackish ooze" [004-608] Eidothea sweetened with nectar, Menelaus and three men seized Proteus at noon. The god transformed in rapid succession — "A lion now, he curls a surgy mane; / Sudden our hands a spotted pard restrain" [004-626], then a boar, a dragon, a stream, a tree — but Menelaus held fast and compelled him to speak.

Proteus revealed the fates of the Greek leaders. Ajax perished for defying the gods [004-683 to 004-693]. Agamemnon was murdered by Aegisthus. And of Odysseus the god declared:

> Him in Calypso's cave of late I view'd,
> When streaming grief his faded cheek bedew'd. [004-766]

For Menelaus himself, the prophecy held a different end: as husband of Helen he would pass living to [[elysium|Elysium]], "the blissful plains / Of utmost earth, where [[rhadamanthus|Rhadamanthus]] reigns" [004-776].

His account of Proteus's prophecy about Odysseus also appears:

> There, in Calypso's ever-fragrant bowers,
> The much-enduring man consumes the hours. [012-543]

### The Exemplar of Xenia

The hospitality of Menelaus's court in Book 4 is one of the poem's most sustained set-pieces. Telemachus and Pisistratus arrive during a double wedding feast; when the seneschal Eteoneus suggests turning them away, Menelaus rebukes him:

> "Insensate! (with a sigh the king replies,)
> Too long, misjudging, have I thought thee wise
> But sure relentless folly steels thy breast,
> Obdurate to reject the stranger-guest;
> To those dear hospitable rites a foe,
> Which in my wanderings oft relieved my woe." [004-049]

The palace overwhelms. Telemachus whispers to Pisistratus:

> "View'st thou unmoved, O ever-honour'd most!
> These prodigies of art, and wondrous cost!
> Above, beneath, around the palace shines
> The sunless treasure of exhausted mines;
> The spoils of elephants the roofs inlay,
> And studded amber darts the golden ray." [004-096]

The feast is served on "a massy vase of silver mould" with "the burnish'd laver flames with solid gold" and "in solid gold the purple vintage flows" [004-077]. Guests are bathed by a "bright damsel train" who attend them "with liquid odours, and embroider'd vests" [004-072].

> "Accept this welcome to the Spartan court:
> The feast is ready; share the friendly sport." [004-082]

This lavish welcome — gifts of silver bowls, embroidered robes, a mixing-bowl "of Sidon's wealthy king the labour'd pride" — establishes Menelaus as the epic's practitioner of *xenia* par excellence. His philosophy of hospitality is balanced:

> Alike he thwarts the hospitable end,
> Who drives the free, or stays the hasty friend. [015-092]

His generosity stands in pointed contrast to the suitors in Ithaca, who consume another man's household without offering anything in return.

### Book 4: The Conference

Menelaus's narrative in Book 4 contains the most detailed account of the returns (*nostoi*) of the Greek heroes after Troy. He tells Telemachus of:
- His own eight-year wandering, including the encounter with Proteus on the island of Pharos
- The fate of Ajax, who perished at sea after defying the gods: "Impious he roar'd defiance to the gods" [004-685]
- The murder of his brother Agamemnon by Aegisthus and Clytemnestra
- The knowledge — extracted from Proteus — that Odysseus is alive, held by Calypso

Proteus speaks as one "whose certain eye foresees / The fix'd event of fate's remote decrees" [004-638]. When Menelaus presses for news of the Greek leaders, the god first warns — "Press not too far" [004-672] — then reveals that Odysseus endures on Calypso's island:

> That chief his race derives
> From Ithaca, and wondrous woes survives;
> Laertes' son: girt with circumfluous tides,
> He still calamitous constraint abides. [004-762]

When Helen enters and immediately identifies Telemachus by his resemblance to Odysseus, the emotional register shifts. Menelaus, Helen, Telemachus, and Pisistratus all weep openly — a shared grief that binds the generations.

> Fast streams a tide from beauteous Helen's eyes;
> Atrides' cheeks the tears unbidden greet;
> Pisistratus, a tender youth, the same
> In form, in mind, in spotless virtue, came. [004-262]

### The Gift Exchange

Menelaus's parting gifts to Telemachus are the concrete manifestation of heroic *xenia*: a silver mixing-bowl of Sidonian craftsmanship and, from Helen, a richly embroidered robe for his future bride.

> Atrides in his chariot shall attend;
> His friendly care he to the port shall lend. [015-104]

## Relationships

- **[[helen|Helen]]** — His wife, restored to him after the Trojan War. Their relationship in Book 4 is complex: Helen speaks of her past errors with self-awareness ("shameless me!"), and Menelaus treats her not as a captive but as a queen in full possession of her dignity. "To Helen's bed the gods alone assign / A daughter, to complete the race divine" [004-029].
- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** — His brother, murdered on his return from Troy by Aegisthus and Clytemnestra. Menelaus's grief over Agamemnon's death pervades his narrative in Book 4 and serves as a dark counterpoint to his own successful homecoming.
- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]** — Fellow Greek commander at Troy, whose fate Menelaus both mourns and, through Proteus's prophecy, illuminates. Menelaus describes Odysseus's endurance with admiration.
- **[[telemachus|Telemachus]]** — The young prince whom Menelaus hosts with full heroic hospitality. Their encounter is one of the poem's key moments of generational transmission: the older hero passes wisdom and intelligence to the son of his absent comrade.
- **[[nestor|Nestor]]** — Fellow veteran of Troy who directs Telemachus to Menelaus. Their relationship exemplifies the network of *xenia* relationships that binds the Greek heroic aristocracy.

## Analysis (optional — `standard`+ quality)

### The Successful Nostos as Foil

Menelaus's story functions as a structural foil to Odysseus's. Both heroes wandered for years after Troy; both faced supernatural obstacles; both were detained by a divine figure (Calypso for Odysseus, Proteus for Menelaus). But Menelaus's return was, ultimately, a return to prosperity — a kingdom intact, a wife restored, a palace of "orient gold and silver shining bright" [004-072].

The contrast is instructive: Menelaus's wealth and stability are what Odysseus has lost and struggles to regain. Telemachus's visit to Sparta shows him a vision of what his own household could be — and what the suitors are destroying. The political implication is clear: Menelaus reigns because he is present; Odysseus's absence is the vacuum that the suitors exploit.

### The Weeping of Menelaus

The tears in Book 4 are remarkable. Menelaus, the triumphant king, weeps openly for his dead brother and his lost friend. These are not tears of weakness but of *[[kleos|kleos]]* — the grief that comes from having known and lost great men. When Helen doses the wine with an Egyptian drug to ease their sorrow, it is not an escape from grief but an acknowledgment that even heroes must sometimes set grief aside to continue living.

### Wealth and Guilt

Menelaus's palace is the richest in the *Odyssey*, yet he articulates its moral cost:

> With all my affluence, when my woes are weigh'd,
> Envy will own the purchase dearly paid. [004-107]

The source of his wealth — the spoils of Troy — is the source of his grief: "These riches are possess'd, but not enjoy'd!" [004-129], and "My heart bleeds fresh with agonizing pain" [004-145] for Odysseus. Menelaus has achieved what the epic celebrates — successful nostos, prosperous kingship, a restored marriage — yet confesses the cost was almost unbearable. He has everything Odysseus wants, but his lament reveals that material prosperity cannot heal the wounds of war. In this sense, his Sparta stands as a foil not only to Odysseus's Ithaca but also to the suitors' rapacious consumption: Menelaus knows what wealth costs, and his hospitality is informed by loss.
