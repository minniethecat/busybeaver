# Achilles

**Achilles** is the greatest of the Greek heroes who fought at Troy, whose wrath and death before the city's fall form the backdrop against which the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* repeatedly measures both glory and loss. Though Achilles himself does not appear as a living character in the *Odyssey* — having died before the events of the poem — his memory haunts the epic's reflections on heroism, mortality, and the cost of war.

## Identity & Names

Achilles (Greek: Ἀχιλλεύς, *Achilleus*) is the son of Peleus, king of the Myrmidons in Phthia, and the sea-nymph Thetis, daughter of Nereus. His dual parentage — mortal father, immortal mother — defines his tragic arc: born for greatness, doomed to die young.

In Pope's translation, he is "great Achilles", "Achilles the brave", and in [[hades|the underworld]] "Pelides": "None but Pelides brighter shone in arms". His tomb makes him "the mighty ghost". The *Odyssey* inherits him from the [[iliad|Iliad]] as a completed legend — his wrath (*[[menis|menis]]*), the death of [[patroclus|Patroclus]], and his own fall at Troy all precede this epic's timeline.

## Role & Significance

Achilles embodies the *Iliad*'s heroic ideal — immortal [[kleos|kleos]] won through martial excellence and early death. The *Odyssey* complicates this, holding him as both paradigm and caution. His function as a foil to [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is the poem's most sustained contrast: Achilles conquers through force (*bie*), Odysseus through [[metis|metis]] (cunning); Achilles chose a short, glorious life, Odysseus endures twenty years for [[nostos|nostos]] (homecoming).

Demodocus' song crystallizes the opposition: "From that fierce wrath the noble song arose, / That made Ulysses and Achilles foes". Achilles rules as "the sceptred monarch of the dead" — yet would trade it all for mortal breath.

Achilles' quarrel with Agamemnon and his subsequent withdrawal from battle are the subject of the *Iliad*. The *Odyssey*'s prefatory material references "the quarrel of Achilles and Agamemnon" as a defining episode of the war (P01-722). His secession from the Greek army is noted alongside other leaders — "Elphenor, chief of the Euboeans; Tlepolemus, of Rhodes" (P01-617) — emphasizing his centrality to the Greek cause.

Achilles led the Greeks: "Dauntless we roved; Achilles led the way". His name is paired with Ajax: "There Ajax great, Achilles there the brave".

## Appearances

Though dead, Achilles is invoked in six distinct contexts across the poem.

### Book 3: Nestor's Recollections

Nestor names Achilles first among the fallen:

> "Dauntless we roved; Achilles led the way; / There Ajax great, Achilles there the brave, / There wise Patroclus, fill an early grave"

He notes [[telemachus|Telemachus]] seated "Beneath Achilles' warlike son's command".

### Book 4 & Book 5

Hermione is married "to great Achilles' son resign'd". [[calypso|Calypso]] invokes his funeral: "From conquering Troy around Achilles dead".

### Book 8: Demodocus' Song

The blind bard sings of the quarrel "That made Ulysses and Achilles foes". Odysseus weeps — hearing his own legend made while the dead hero's name resounds in verse.

### Book 11: The Underworld

Achilles' most significant appearance. He approaches with Patroclus:

> "Till side by side along the dreary coast / Advanced Achilles' and Patroclus' ghost, / A friendly pair!"

Odysseus recognises him "through the thick gloom" and offers tribute — blessed in life, a king among shades. Achilles' reply is among the most famous passages in ancient literature:

> "Rather I'd choose laboriously to bear / A weight of woes, and breathe the vital air, / A slave to some poor hind that toils for bread, / Than reign the sceptred monarch of the dead."

The speech overturns the *Iliad*'s value system. The hero who chose death-with-glory now declares that choice a catastrophe. Achilles then asks after his son and father; Odysseus's account of Neoptolemus's valour brings the shade joy before he strides away across the asphodel.

### Book 24: The Second Nekyia

[[the-suitors|The suitors]]' shades find Achilles among the illustrious dead:

> "Ajax and great Achilles are no more! / Yet still a master ghost, the rest he awed"

Agamemnon calls him "thrice happy" for dying in battle, then narrates his funeral: Thetis leading sea-nymphs — "To mourn Achilles leads her azure train" — the Muses' dirge, the golden urn blending his bones with Patroclus, a separate space for [[antilochus|Antilochus]], and the tomb: "May point Achilles' tomb, and hail the mighty ghost".

## Relationships

**Patroclus**: The beloved companion whose death returned Achilles to battle. Their bond persists beyond death — "a friendly pair" — sharing the [[same|same]] urn. Nestor names him: "There wise Patroclus, fill an early grave".

**Odysseus**: The rivalry between force and cunning is the *Odyssey*'s generative tension. Their quarrel asked whether Troy falls by strength or guile. Guile won, and the poem vindicates that victory — but Achilles haunts it, his shadow across every triumph of the living hero.

**Agamemnon**: The quarrel over Briseis precipitated the *Iliad*'s catastrophe. In Hades it is settled. Achilles laments Agamemnon's murder; Agamemnon contrasts it with Achilles' glorious death, making the funeral a standard against which all deaths are measured.

**Neoptolemus**: Achilles' son carries his legacy after death. Telemachus is placed "Beneath Achilles' warlike son's command"; Hermione is married "to great Achilles' son resign'd". In the Underworld, Odysseus describes Neoptolemus as "third in wisdom, where they all were wise" — the only news that brings Achilles joy. Neoptolemus serves as a foil to Telemachus, who must grow into his own father's legacy.

**Thetis**: The sea-nymph mother appears through Agamemnon's funeral narrative. Rising with her Nereids, "To mourn Achilles leads her azure train", while the Muses' dirge makes "iron-hearted heroes melt in tears".

## Analysis

### The Underworld Encounter

The [[the-underworld|Book 11]] encounter is the *Odyssey*'s most radical revision of the *Iliad*'s values. Odysseus deploys conventional praise and Achilles demolishes it:

> "Rather I'd choose laboriously to bear / A weight of woes, and breathe the vital air, / A slave to some poor hind that toils for bread, / Than reign the sceptred monarch of the dead."

This rejects the premise on which Achilles lived and died — that *kleos aphthiton* is worth mortal life — and validates Odysseus's project of survival. [[nostos|Nostos]] over *kleos* is vindicated by the hero who chose the opposite.

Yet this is not simple repudiation. Achilles immediately asks after his son and father; news of Neoptolemus fills him with pride. Even in death, after renouncing the heroic code, Achilles is bound to the world through lineage. The *Odyssey* redefines *kleos* as alive in survivors, not monuments alone.

### Glory vs. Life

The *Odyssey* is the *Iliad*'s answer — testing the war-epic's code against the demands of home. Achilles chose glory over long life; the underworld declares that choice wrong. Odysseus faces a parallel choice — immortality with Calypso or mortality with [[penelope|Penelope]] — and chooses home. Life, however hard, exceeds death, however glorious. Achilles' greatness is undiminished ("None but Pelides brighter shone"); his regret makes that greatness human.

### Legacy Through His Son

Neoptolemus is Achilles' answer to death. Odysseus describes him eloquent in council, ferocious in battle, safe home from Troy — the son reborn. This generational theme extends through Telemachus, who sits "Beneath Achilles' warlike son's command". Neoptolemus has proven himself; Telemachus is only beginning. The contrast drives Books 1-4: a seating arrangement doubling as a curriculum.

## Death and Its Aftermath

Achilles died before Troy fell, slain by an arrow to his heel, his only vulnerable point. The *Odyssey* captures the grief surrounding his death: "From conquering Troy around Achilles dead" describes the funeral rites and mourning that attended his passing. His relics and tomb become focal points of remembrance: "There, we thy relics, great Achilles! blend / With dear Patroclus" and "May point Achilles' tomb, and hail the mighty ghost".
