## Overview

Elpenor is the youngest member of [[odysseus|Odysseus's]] crew. Unlike the other companions celebrated for strength or wit, Elpenor is characterized as a figure of ordinary weakness — "not much for sense, nor much for courage famed" [^1] — yet his death and the rites that follow it form one of the epic's most poignant arcs spanning three books.

## Death at Circe's Palace

After Odysseus and his crew spend a year feasting at [[circe|Circe's]] palace on [[aeaea|Aeaea]], preparations are made to depart for the underworld. Elpenor, described as "the youngest of our band, a vulgar soul, / Born but to banquet, and to drain the bowl," drinks too much wine and climbs to the roof of Circe's palace to sleep [^1]. In the morning, roused by the noise of the crew preparing to leave, he forgets his elevated position, falls from the roof, and breaks his neck. His death goes unnoticed by the crew in their haste to depart.

## Encounter in the Underworld

When Odysseus summons the shades of the dead at the shore of the Cimmerians in Book XI, the first shade to appear is Elpenor's, "new to the realms of death" [^2]. His shade has arrived before Odysseus because his body remains unburied. Elpenor's ghost implores Odysseus:

> "O say what angry power Elpenor led
> To glide in shades, and wander with the dead?
> How fell my strength by undetermined ill?" [^3]

He begs Odysseus not to leave his corpse "unwept, unburied" on Circe's shore, lest he become a cause for the gods' wrath against the living.

## Burial Rites

Odysseus fulfills his promise in Book XII. Upon returning to Aeaea after the underworld journey, the crew immediately attends to Elpenor's body: "Soon as the morn restored the day, we paid / Sepulchral honours to Elpenor's shade" [^4]. They cremate his body with his armor and raise a burial mound. Odysseus fulfills Elpenor's final request by planting his oar atop the tomb, "to show posterity Elpenor was" [^5] — a lasting memorial on Circe's shore.

## Significance

Elpenor serves as a narrative bridge between the worlds of the living and the dead. His unburied state introduces the Greek cultural imperative of proper burial rites, and his brief exchange with Odysseus in the underworld emphasizes the hero's responsibility toward his men — even those who die through their own folly.
