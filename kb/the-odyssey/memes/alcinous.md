# Alcinous

## Identity & Names

**Alcinous** (Greek: Ἀλκίνοος, "strong mind") is the king of the Phaeacians, ruler of the island of Scheria, and one of the *Odyssey*'s most important mortal figures. In Pope's translation, he is consistently described as "great Alcinous," "wise Alcinous," and "good Alcinous" — epithets that underscore his virtue as a ruler and host:

> And wise Alcinous held the legal sway. [006-028]

His palace — with its bronze walls, golden doors, and immortal guardians — is described in Book 7 as a marvel of the heroic world. Yet Alcinous himself is distinguished not by wealth but by his wisdom, generosity, and perfect observance of [[xenia]]:

> On good Alcinous, honor'd as a god. [007-027]

## Role & Significance

Alcinous and his court represent the ideal of civilized hospitality against which other episodes in the *Odyssey* are measured. Where [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] consumes his guests and the suitors abuse their host's hospitality, Alcinous receives the shipwrecked stranger (Odysseus, still disguised) with perfect [[xenia]]: he offers food before asking questions, provides entertainment (the Phaeacian games, Demodocus's songs), and ultimately grants Odysseus the ships and gifts that carry him home.

His role is both narrative and thematic. Narratively, Alcinous is the audience for Odysseus's great tale — the *Apologoi* (Books 9–12) are told at his court, making him the frame within which the hero's adventures are recounted. Thematically, his generous provision of passage — even knowing that Poseidon will punish the Phaeacians for aiding Odysseus — demonstrates the cost of true hospitality:

> Wide o'er the world Alcinous' glory shine! [007-438]

## Relationships

- **[[arete|Arete]]** (Queen Arete) — His wife, to whom Nausicaa advises Odysseus to appeal first. The Phaeacian court is unusual in giving the queen significant authority; Alcinous's respect for Arete's counsel marks him as a wise ruler.
- **[[nausicaa|Nausicaa]]** — His daughter, whose encounter with Odysseus on the beach sets the Phaeacian episode in motion. Alcinous's gentle response to Nausicaa's request for laundry (he understands her unspoken desire for marriage without embarrassing her) reveals his perceptiveness as a father.
- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]** — The stranger whom Alcinous receives, hosts, and ultimately sends home. Their relationship exemplifies the guest-host bond: Alcinous offers everything without knowing the stranger's identity, and honours him once it is revealed.
- **[[poseidon|Poseidon]]** — The god whose wrath falls on the Phaeacians for aiding Odysseus. Alcinous's willingness to incur this cost demonstrates the sacrificial dimension of true hospitality.

## Analysis

### The Model Host

Alcinous embodies the ideal of the Homeric king as host. His court is a microcosm of civilized life: music (Demodocus), athletics (the Phaeacian games), feasting, storytelling, and mutual respect. The contrast with Odysseus's own household — overrun by suitors who violate every principle of [[xenia]] — is deliberate: Alcinous shows what Odysseus has lost and must restore.

### The Listener-King

Alcinous's most important function is as audience. For four books, he listens to Odysseus's tale without interruption, asking questions that prompt the hero to deeper self-revelation. His perceptive observation — noticing Odysseus's tears at Demodocus's song of Troy — is the catalyst that transforms the stranger into the hero. In this sense, Alcinous enables the *Odyssey*'s own narration: without his hospitality, the tale would remain untold.
