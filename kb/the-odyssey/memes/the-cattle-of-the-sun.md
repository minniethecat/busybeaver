# The Cattle of the Sun

## Summary

The Cattle of the Sun episode is the tragic culmination of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*'s wanderings. After passing [[scylla-and-charybdis|Scylla and Charybdis]], [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s ship approaches [[thrinacia|Thrinacia]], the island where [[helios|Helios]] pastures his immortal cattle. Both [[circe|Circe]] and [[tiresias|Tiresias]] had warned [[odysseus|Odysseus]]: do not land there, and if you must, do not touch the cattle. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] urges his crew to sail past, but [[eurylochus|Eurylochus]], speaking for the exhausted men, demands they rest ashore. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] extracts a solemn oath that none will harm the sacred herds. For a month, favorable winds trap them on the island. When provisions run out, [[eurylochus|Eurylochus]] persuades the crew while [[odysseus|Odysseus]] sleeps: better to die quickly by the god's wrath than slowly by starvation. They slaughter the finest cattle. Lampetia, the nymph who tends the herds, reports to [[helios|Helios]], who appeals to [[zeus|Zeus]]: punish the transgressors or he will take the sun down to [[hades|Hades]]. [[zeus|Zeus]] agrees. When the ship finally departs, a storm destroys it with a thunderbolt. All perish except [[odysseus|Odysseus]], who clings to wreckage and drifts to [[calypso|Calypso]]'s island.

## Context & Causes

This episode is the only peril in the wanderings that is entirely the crew's doing — [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is asleep when the fatal decision is made. It represents the culmination of a conflict between [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s leadership and his crew's autonomy that runs throughout the poem: the opening of [[aeolus|Aeolus]]'s bag, the refusal to flee [[polyphemus|the Cyclops]]'s island promptly, and now the slaughter of the cattle. Each time, the crew's action (or inaction) costs them. Here, [[eurylochus|Eurylochus]] articulates a grim philosophy — a quick death at divine hands is preferable to slow starvation — that inverts the heroic code's preference for death in battle. The episode is also the poem's starkest illustration of divine justice: [[helios|Helios]]'s cattle are immortal, and their slaughter is a cosmic violation, not merely a pragmatic error.

## Key Moments

1. **The Warning**: [[odysseus|Odysseus]] recounts [[circe|Circe]]'s and [[tiresias|Tiresias]]'s explicit prohibitions against landing on [[thrinacia|Thrinacia]]. He urges the crew to sail on, but [[eurylochus|Eurylochus]] speaks for the exhausted men and prevails.

2. **The [[horkos|Oath]]**: [[odysseus|Odysseus]] extracts a binding oath from every crew member that they will not touch the sacred herds, however hungry they become. The crew swears.

3. **The Month of Waiting**: Contrary south winds trap the ship for a full month. The men consume all provisions. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] withdraws to pray, and while he sleeps — the second fateful sleep in the poem (after the sleep that preceded the opening of [[aeolus|Aeolus]]'s bag) — [[eurylochus|Eurylochus]] makes his case to the crew.

4. **The Transgression**: The crew slaughters the choicest cattle while [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is absent. They offer sacrifices to the gods with oak leaves instead of barley (all the barley is gone) and water instead of wine. The roasted meat moos on the spits — an unmistakable omen they ignore.

5. **Divine [[poine|Vengeance]]**: Lampetia reports to [[helios|Helios]], who threatens to desert the sky and shine among the dead. [[zeus|Zeus]] promises retribution. When the ship departs after six days of feasting, [[zeus|Zeus]] strikes it with a thunderbolt — "[[zeus|Jove]] in a moment snatch'd the ship away" — and all the crew are drowned.

6. **[[odysseus|Odysseus]] Alone**: Clinging to the keel and mast, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] drifts for nine days before washing ashore on [[ogygia|Ogygia]]. He is now, for the first time in the wanderings, utterly alone.

## Aftermath & Consequences

The destruction of the ship represents the absolute end of the heroic expedition that left [[troy|Troy]]. Not one of the 600+ men who sailed with [[odysseus|Odysseus]] survives. The crew's death is, in Homeric terms, both just (they violated a divine prohibition they had sworn to respect) and tragic (they were starving, and [[eurylochus|Eurylochus]]'s argument has a terrible logic). [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s survival, clinging to wreckage, marks the final transition: he ceases to be a commander and becomes the solitary sufferer who will tell his own story to the [[the-phaeacians|Phaeacians]]. The episode also completes the sequence of "no-return" prophecies: [[tiresias|Tiresias]] had warned that if the cattle were harmed, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] would return "late and alone, on a stranger's ship, to find trouble in his house." Every word will prove true.
