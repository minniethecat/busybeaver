# Metis (Cunning)

## Definition

**Metis** (Greek: μῆτις, *mêtis*) is a form of intelligence distinct from abstract wisdom (*sophia*): it is practical, adaptive, situational intelligence — the ability to read a situation instantly, devise a strategy, and execute it through skill, trickery, or disguise. In Homer, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is the embodiment of metis: "the man for wisdom's various arts renown'd" (001-018). His epithet *[[odysseus|polymetis]]* ("many-counseled" or "of many wiles") follows him through the epic as a title of honor, not reproach.

## Greek Context

Metis was highly valued in a world where survival depended on out-thinking stronger opponents. The goddess Metis was [[zeus|Zeus]]'s first wife, swallowed by him so that her cunning would become part of his own — [[athena|Athena]], born from [[zeus|Zeus]]'s head, inherited her mother's quality and became the divine patron of metis. In the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*, [[athena|Athena]]'s affection for [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is explicitly based on their shared nature: both are masters of disguise, indirect approach, and strategic patience. Metis is the virtue of the weaker but smarter — the quality that allows humans to survive in a world of hostile gods and monsters.

## In the Odyssey

The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* is a sustained celebration of metis in action:

### The Trojan Horse and the Cyclops
[[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s defining achievements are acts of metis. The Trojan Horse — his invention — won the war that ten years of combat could not. In [[polyphemus|the Cyclops]]'s cave, his self-naming as "Nobody" (Outis) and his escape beneath the sheep are metis at its purest: he cannot overpower [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]], so he out-thinks him. "The dire restraint his wisdom will defeat" (001-282) applies to every crisis he faces.

### Disguise and Recognition
The second half of the epic — [[odysseus|Odysseus]] in [[ithaca|Ithaca]] — is a masterclass in metis. He enters his own kingdom disguised as a beggar, testing everyone he meets before revealing himself. Each recognition scene ([[eurycleia|Eurycleia]], the dog [[argo|Argo]]s, [[penelope|Penelope]]) is a carefully controlled release of identity. [[penelope|Penelope]] herself demonstrates metis equal to her husband's: the shroud-weaving trick delays the suitors for three years, and her final test of the bed is an act of cunning recognition.

### Athena as Partner
[[athena|Athena]]'s relationship with [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is the divine sanction of metis. She delights in his lies, aids his disguises, and applauds his caution. Their collaboration — "And more than woman with deep wisdom crown'd" (002-152) — shows metis as a quality shared across the mortal-divine boundary.

## Significance

Metis redefines heroism in the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*:

- **Against the heroic code**: Where the *[[iliad|Iliad]]* celebrates *biē* (force, might), the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* elevates metis. [[achilles|Achilles]] wins with the sword; [[odysseus|Odysseus]] wins with the mind. This contrast was recognized in antiquity — the two epics together define the full range of Greek excellence.
- **Survival strategy**: In a world of monsters, gods, and hostile strangers, metis is the only reliable weapon. Brute strength fails [[odysseus|Odysseus]] repeatedly; cunning never does.
- **Modern resonance**: Metis anticipates a recurrent figure in Western literature — the trickster-hero, the survivor, the one who talks his way out. From [[odysseus|Odysseus]] to Huckleberry Finn, metis is the virtue of the marginalized who cannot afford to fight fair.
