# Circe

## Identity & Names

**Circe** (Greek: Κίρκη, *Kirkē*) is a goddess-enchantress, daughter of the sun-god Helios and the Oceanid Perse, dwelling on the wooded island of Aeaea. Her name is linked to *kirkos* (hawk or circle), suggesting both predatory swooping and the cyclical transformations she effects. Homer describes her as "the fair-tressed Circe, goddess skilled in song" and emphasizes her weaving — a domestic art that masks supernatural danger. She appears primarily in Books 10–12, and her island serves as the threshold between the world of ordinary adventure and the Underworld.

## Role & Significance

Circe is the most complex of Odysseus's supernatural hosts. Unlike Calypso (who simply detains him) or the Sirens (who would destroy him), Circe moves through a complete narrative arc: from threat to lover to ally to guide. Her transformation of Odysseus's crew into swine literalizes what the epic repeatedly suggests — that those who abandon human restraint become beasts. Odysseus's immunity to her magic (protected by the herb *moly*, given by Hermes) marks him as exceptional among mortals.

After Odysseus masters her, Circe becomes his most knowledgeable ally. It is she who tells him he must visit the Underworld to consult Tiresias, she who maps the route past the Sirens, Scylla, and Charybdis, and she who warns him against harming the Cattle of the Sun. Her island is the pivot between adventure and revelation — and Odysseus and his crew spend an entire year there, a period of recuperation and forgetting that mirrors Calypso's seven-year detention in miniature.

## Appearances

### Book 10: From Threat to Ally

Odysseus's crew, exploring Aeaea, discover Circe's palace where "mountain wolves and lions round her gate appeared, by magic tamed". She welcomes them with a drugged potion and transforms them into swine with a stroke of her wand — though their minds remain human. Eurylochus, who had refused to enter, reports the disaster to Odysseus.

Odysseus sets out alone to rescue his men. On the way, Hermes appears in the form of a young man and provides the counter-charm: the herb *moly*, "black at the root, but milky white the flower", which renders Circe's magic powerless. Hermes also instructs Odysseus to make Circe swear a "solemn oath" that she will do him no further harm.

When Circe's potion fails, she recognizes Odysseus:

> "Thou art Ulysses! that sagacious man,
> Whose wandering course the wise Mercurius' plan
> Foretold should lead him to this magic coast."

Odysseus compels her to restore his men to human form — "and from their bristly hides the enchantment freed" — and she becomes his lover and hostess. After a year of feasting, his crew urges him to remember Ithaca.

### Book 11: The Underworld Gateway

When Odysseus asks to leave, Circe tells him he must first journey to the Underworld to consult the shade of Tiresias. She provides precise sacrificial instructions: dig a pit, pour libations of honey, wine, water, and barley, and sacrifice a black ram and ewe so the shades may drink the blood and speak.

### Book 12: The Navigator

Upon Odysseus's return from the Underworld, Circe provides the most detailed navigational advice in the epic. She maps the two possible routes home: past the Wandering Rocks (which only the Argo has survived) or through the strait between Scylla and Charybdis. She describes each danger in detail:

> "No ship yet boasts to 'scape without a wound;
> Her six long necks, extending o'er the flood,
| Catch six swift sailors from the deck for food."

She warns him about the Sirens, the Cattle of the Sun, and the necessity of restraint at every turn. Her parting words make clear that survival depends on self-mastery, not strength.

## Relationships

- **Odysseus**: From adversary to lover to ally to navigator. Their relationship is the epic's most complete arc of transformation — mutual recognition converting enmity into collaboration. She is arguably the most helpful single figure Odysseus encounters.

- **Hermes**: The god who arms Odysseus against her. Their relationship is intriguingly neutral — Circe shows no resentment toward Hermes for foiling her magic, and Hermes treats the encounter as part of the cosmic order rather than a personal betrayal.

- **Helios**: Her father, the sun-god. Her warning about the Cattle of the Sun is both practical advice and filial loyalty — protecting her father's sacred herd from violation.

- **Eurylochus**: Odysseus's second-in-command, whose refusal to trust Circe after the transformation (he calls her "a murderous monster") represents the cautious, non-heroic perspective. Odysseus overrules him.

- **Calypso**: The other goddess who hosts Odysseus. Where Calypso offers immortality in exchange for permanent stay, Circe offers knowledge in exchange for temporary companionship — a more equitable transaction.

## Analysis

### The Enchantress as Threshold Guardian

Circe occupies a liminal position in the *Odyssey*'s symbolic geography. Her island is the last stop before the Underworld — the boundary between the living world and the realm of the dead. Her magic is transformative (men into beasts, beasts back into men) in a way that mirrors the Underworld's transformative power (the living into the dead, the dead into prophetic voices). She is, in Joseph Campbell's terms, the "threshold guardian" who must be mastered before the hero can descend.

### Metis and Counter-Magic

The Circe episode is a contest of *metis* — cunning intelligence — rather than strength. Odysseus cannot overpower a goddess; he can only outmaneuver her with Hermes's help. The *moly* herb is a botanical embodiment of metis: a natural counter-force to supernatural power. The episode thus reinforces the poem's central claim that intelligence, properly guided by divine aid, can master even the most dangerous forces.

### Gender and Power

Circe's transformation of men into swine is a nightmare of emasculation — reducing warriors to grunting beasts penned in a sty. Yet her subsequent submission to Odysseus (at sword-point, after the oath) reverses this power dynamic completely. Feminist readings have noted that Circe is "punished" for autonomous female power by being forced into a sexual relationship with the male hero — but the text also grants her continued agency as guide and advisor. Her power is redirected rather than destroyed.

### The Year on Aeaea

The year spent feasting on Circe's island is a miniature of Calypso's seven years — a period of pleasurable forgetting from which the hero must be roused by his companions. The crew's reminder ("wake, and think of Ithaca") parallels Hermes's later intervention on Ogygia. Both episodes suggest that *nostos* requires active remembrance; the journey home is a struggle against the seduction of comfortable oblivion.
