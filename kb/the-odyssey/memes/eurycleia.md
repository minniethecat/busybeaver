# Eurycleia

## Identity & Names

**Eurycleia** (Greek: Εὐρύκλεια, *Eurykleia*, "wide-famed") is the aged nurse of the Ithacan royal household. Purchased by Laertes in her youth for the price of twenty oxen, she nursed Odysseus as an infant and later Telemachus. She is the oldest and most trusted servant in the household, and her recognition of Odysseus by his scar in Book 19 is the epic's first full recognition scene between the returned king and a member of his household.

## Role & Significance

Eurycleia's recognition of Odysseus — when she washes his feet as the disguised beggar and feels the familiar scar on his thigh — is one of the most suspenseful moments in the *Odyssey*. Odysseus must physically restrain her from crying out, gripping her throat and threatening death if she reveals his identity. Her obedience in the face of this threat demonstrates the absolute loyalty that the poem values above all else in servants.

After the slaughter, Eurycleia identifies the twelve maids who betrayed the household by sleeping with the suitors, enabling Odysseus to order their execution. Her knowledge of the household's intimate history — who was faithful and who was not — makes her an essential ally in the restoration of order.

## Appearances

- **Book 1**: Lights Telemachus to his chamber.
- **Book 19**: The recognition scene — washes Odysseus's feet, feels the scar, is silenced by her master.
- **Book 22**: Reports to Odysseus which maids were unfaithful.
- **Book 23**: Wakes Penelope with the news that Odysseus has returned — a report Penelope initially refuses to believe.

## Relationships

- **Odysseus**: Nursed him as a child; is the first in the household to recognize him. Her loyalty spans three generations.
- **Telemachus**: Raised him as she raised his father.
- **Penelope**: Serves as confidante and messenger between the queen and the returned king.

## Analysis

Eurycleia represents the continuity of the household across generations. She is a living archive of the family's history — she knows the scar, knows the maids, knows every intimate detail of the house. Her recognition scene, coming before Penelope's, prepares the way for the full restoration: the body first, then the household, then the marriage.
