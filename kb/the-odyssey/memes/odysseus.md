# Odysseus

## Identity & Names

**Odysseus** (Greek: Ὀδυσσεύς, Latin: *Ulixes* / Ulysses) is the king of Ithaca and the protagonist of Homer's *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*. In Pope's translation, he appears primarily as "Ulysses" — "the man for wisdom's various arts renown'd" [001-018] — a hero whose primary weapon is intelligence rather than brute strength. His defining epithet, *polymetis* ("of many counsels"), is matched by *polytropos* ("of many turnings"), describing both his wanderings and his adaptable character. Other epithets include *polytlas* ("much-enduring"), *ptoliporthos* ("sacker of cities"), and the patronymic Laertiades.

## Role & Significance

Odysseus is unique among Greek heroes: where [[achilles|Achilles]] embodies martial prowess (*biē*), Odysseus embodies cunning intelligence ([[metis]]). He is the inventor of the Trojan Horse, the blinder of the Cyclops, the man who navigates [[scylla|Scylla]] and [[Charybdis]], and the beggar-king who reclaims his household through strategic patience. "Sole of all the victor train" to remain exiled from home, he is "by fate oppress'd" [001-035, 001-079] — yet his suffering is, in part, self-inflicted through the act of [[hubris]] that earned [[poseidon|Poseidon]]'s wrath.

The *Odyssey* follows his journey from [[troy|Troy]] to [[ithaca|Ithaca]], a voyage that should have taken weeks but consumed ten years. His character arc moves from the proud warrior who taunts [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] to the patient strategist who endures [[the-suitors|the suitors]]' insults before striking — a transformation from *bie*-like recklessness to mature [[metis]].

As king, husband, father, and son, Odysseus embodies the Homeric ideal of the complete man — his [[time]] (honour) measured not in birth alone but in deeds witnessed and acknowledged:

> The great Ulysses reach'd his native shore [002-405]

> For brave Ulysses, still by fate oppress'd [001-079]

His refusal of [[calypso|Calypso]]'s offer of immortality — choosing mortal homecoming over ageless obscurity — is the poem's central moral choice, defining what [[nostos]] truly means.

## Relationships

Odysseus's relationships define the epic's moral architecture. His network of allies and antagonists spans the mortal and divine realms:

- **[[penelope|Penelope]]** — His wife and counterpart in [[metis]]. Each has spent twenty years outwitting those who would destroy their household. Their recognition scene (Book 23), hinging on the secret of the olive-tree marriage bed, is the poem's climactic reunion.
- **[[telemachus|Telemachus]]** — His son, who grows from passive youth to active ally. Their reunion in Book 16 — "born, the Ulysses of thy age to rise" [002-324] — marks the turning point where the household begins to reassert itself.
- **[[athena|Athena]]** — His divine patron, who recognizes in him a kindred spirit of cunning. She disguises, guides, and ultimately restores him, intervening at every critical juncture.
- **[[poseidon|Poseidon]]** — His divine antagonist, whose wrath drives the plot. "Whose visual orb Ulysses robb'd of light" [001-107] — the blinding of Poseidon's son [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]], in the [[the-cyclops-episode|Cyclops episode]], is the original sin that dooms Odysseus to wander.
- **[[laertes|Laertes]]** — His aged father, living in grief on the margins of the kingdom, whose recognition of his son in Book 24 closes the generational arc.
- **[[eumaeus|Eumaeus]]** — The faithful swineherd who receives the disguised king with perfect [[xenia]], proving that virtue is measured by action, not status.

## Key Themes

### Metis vs. Biē

Odysseus represents a new kind of heroism — one defined not by physical strength but by intelligence. In the [[the-cyclops-episode|Cyclops episode]], his "Nobody" trick (Book 9) is the paradigmatic act of [[metis]]: linguistic cunning that turns the monster's own words against him. Yet the episode also reveals metis's fatal edge — his boastful self-naming transforms a clean escape into a divine curse.

### The Man of Sorrows

Odysseus weeps more than any other Homeric hero. On [[ogygia|Calypso's island]], on [[scheria|Phaeacia]] hearing the bard's song, upon reunion — his tears mark him as a man who feels loss acutely. This vulnerability coexists with his ruthlessness: the [[slaughter-of-the-suitors|slaughter of the suitors]] is methodical, the hanging of the maidservants is brutal, and the mutilation of [[melanthius|Melanthius]] is deliberately cruel. Homer does not resolve this tension; he presents it as the nature of the man.

### Homecoming as Identity

> To hail Ulysses' safe return I came [001-269]

The return is not merely geographic but ontological. Odysseus must shed layer after layer — the lost warrior, the nymph's lover, the shipwrecked castaway, the beggar — before he can reclaim the identity of king, husband, father, son. Each recognition scene (Telemachus, Argos, [[eurycleia|Eurycleia]], Penelope, Laertes) restores one piece of this shattered self.
