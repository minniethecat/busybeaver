# Halitherses

Halitherses, son of Mastor, is the aged seer of Ithaca whose interpretation of omens provides divine validation for Telemachus's cause and prophetic warning to the suitors. He is one of the few Ithacans who publicly opposes the suitors' abuse of Odysseus's household, and he reappears in the final book to counsel peace after the slaughter.

## Identity & Names

The name Halitherses (Greek: Αλιθερσης, *Halitherses*) likely derives from elements meaning "sea" (*hali-*) and "courage" or "boldness" (*-thersos*). He is consistently identified as the son of Mastor and is described as "the prince of augurs" [002-202] and "sage" [017-087], marking him as the foremost interpreter of bird-signs in Ithaca.

## Role & Significance

Halitherses is a mortal augur of Ithaca, distinguished by his age, wisdom, and skill in divination. His role spans the entire epic, providing a prophetic frame:

1. **Divine endorsement of Telemachus**: By interpreting the eagle omen favorably for Telemachus's planned journey [002-202], Halitherses legitimizes the young prince's assertion of authority.
2. **Warning to the suitors**: He publicly foretells Odysseus's return and the suitors' destruction [002-303], establishing the moral arc that the narrative will fulfill.
3. **Voice of reconciliation**: In Book 24, he counsels the Ithacans against avenging the suitors' deaths, arguing that the gods have judged justly [024-528].

## Appearances

### Book 2 — The Assembly

At Telemachus's called assembly, after the young prince has made his complaint against the suitors, Zeus sends two eagles that fight overhead. Halitherses rises to interpret:

> "The prince of augurs, Halitherses, rose:
> Prescient he view'd the aerial tracks, and drew
> The sure presage from every wing that flew." [002-202]

He foretells that Odysseus is near and will return to bring destruction upon the suitors. Eurymachus mocks him, but Halitherses stands firm [002-303].

### Book 17 — Odysseus's Return

Halitherses is mentioned among the loyal Ithacans, alongside Antiphus, as a "sage" elder [017-087] — part of the small remnant of Odysseus's generation who have not collaborated with the suitors.

### Book 24 — The Reconciliation

After the slaughter of the suitors, when Eupeithes rallies the Ithacans to avenge their sons, Halitherses rises to speak:

> "When slowly rising, Halitherses spoke
> (His words the attention of the multitude awoke)..." [024-528]

He argues that the suitors brought their fate upon themselves through their own wickedness, and that further bloodshed would defy the will of the gods. His wisdom helps pave the way for Athena's final intervention and the restoration of peace.

## Relationships

### Allies

- **Telemachus**: Halitherses's favorable omen-reading gives Telemachus the divine endorsement he needs to proceed with his journey.
- **Mentor**: Another loyal elder of Ithaca, to whom Odysseus entrusted his household. Halitherses and Mentor represent the old guard who remember Odysseus's just rule.
- **Odysseus**: Though they share no direct scene before Book 24, Halitherses's loyalty to Odysseus's house is unwavering throughout.

### Antagonists

- **The Suitors**: Particularly Eurymachus, who dismisses Halitherses's prophecy with contempt [002-303]. This dismissal is itself a narrative marker of the suitors' impiety.

## Analysis

### The Prophet's Function

Halitherses exemplifies the Homeric type of the mantis (seer), whose function is to interpret divine signs for the community. Unlike Tiresias (who prophesies for Odysseus personally) or Theoclymenus (whose visions are private and dramatic), Halitherses operates in the public, political sphere — his prophecies address the polis of Ithaca and carry civic weight.

### Loyalty and Moral Clarity

In a political landscape dominated by the suitors' faction, Halitherses's willingness to speak truth publicly is an act of courage. His loyalty to Odysseus's house is not merely personal but principled: he recognizes the suitors' behavior as violating both xenia and divine law, and he is willing to say so in the assembly.

### Narrative Framing

Halitherses's prophecy in Book 2 and his counsel in Book 24 create a prophetic frame around the entire epic. What he foretells in Book 2 comes to pass exactly, establishing the reliability of divine signs and the inevitability of the suitors' punishment. His reappearance at the end confirms the moral order he has always represented.
