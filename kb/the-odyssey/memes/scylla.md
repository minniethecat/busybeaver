# Scylla

## Identity & Names

**Scylla** (Greek: Σκύλλα, *Skylla*) is a monstrous creature with six long-necked heads and twelve feet, who dwells in a cave high on a cliff opposite the whirlpool Charybdis. In later myth she was said to have been a beautiful nymph transformed by Circe's jealousy, but Homer presents her simply as an immortal evil — one that cannot be fought, only endured and passed. Her mother is Crataeis, a sea-goddess whose name means "mighty force."

## Role & Significance

Scylla represents the limit of heroic agency. Unlike Polyphemus (who can be outwitted) or the Sirens (who can be resisted), Scylla cannot be fought, tricked, or negotiated with. Circe's advice to Odysseus is stark: do not arm yourself, do not try to fight — just row as fast as you can and accept that six men will die. This is the *Odyssey*'s most honest moment about the nature of survival: sometimes the only choice is between losing six men and losing all.

The Scylla episode has given Western literature one of its fundamental metaphors: "between Scylla and Charybdis" means choosing the lesser of two unavoidable evils. Odysseus's decision to sail closer to Scylla (losing six men) rather than Charybdis (losing the entire ship) is the paradigm case of tragic choice.

## Appearances

- **Book 12**: Circe describes Scylla and the route past her. Odysseus, despite his intention to fight, follows Circe's advice. As the ship passes, Scylla snatches six men from the deck — the most horrifying moment in the epic, described with unflinching vividness.

## Relationships

- **Charybdis**: Her counterpart, the whirlpool on the opposite cliff. Together they form an inescapable dilemma.
- **Crataeis**: Her mother, invoked as the only being who might restrain her.
- **Odysseus**: The captain who must sacrifice his men to pass her.

## Analysis

Scylla is the *Odyssey*'s most radical statement about the limits of metis. For all his cunning, Odysseus cannot outthink or outfight Scylla — he can only endure. This acknowledgment is essential to the poem's moral realism: intelligence can solve many problems, but not all. Some evils are simply there, and the best that human skill can do is minimize the damage. The image of Scylla fishing for men with her six heads is the epic's darkest vision of the cosmos as a place of irreducible danger.
