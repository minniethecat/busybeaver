Ithaca's Harbor, also called the Harbor of Phorcys, is the principal port of [[ithaca|Ithaca]], sacred to Phorcys, a sea-god of the abyss. Nestled between two craggy headlands that shelter it from storm winds, the harbor is described in rich detail in Book XIII when the [[the-phaeacians|Phaeacians]] deposit the sleeping [[odysseus|Odysseus]] on his native shore with his treasure.

## In the Odyssey

### The Return of Odysseus (Book XIII)

After the Phaeacian ship approaches Ithaca by night, the crew bears Odysseus ashore at the harbor:

> "Far from the town a spacious port appears, / Sacred to Phorcys' power, whose name it bears; / Two craggy rocks projecting to the main, / The roaring wind's tempestuous rage restrain; / Within the waves in softer murmurs glide, / And ships secure without their halsers ride." [013-NA]

The harbor's natural defenses — the twin rocky headlands — make it an ideal anchorage. The epithet "spacious" conveys that it is not a cramped inlet but a proper harbor capable of sheltering multiple vessels. The "softer murmurs" of the waves within create a sense of peace that contrasts with the dangerous open sea beyond.

### The Olive Tree

A distinctive landmark crowns the harbor's headland:

> "High at the head a branching olive grows, / And crowns the pointed cliffs with shady boughs." [013-NA]

This olive tree serves a practical function in the narrative: the Phaeacians deposit Odysseus's treasure — gifts from [[alcinous|Alcinous]] and the Phaeacian nobles — in its shade, concealing it from passersby while Odysseus sleeps on the rocky shore.

### Athene Reveals Ithaca

When Athene later reveals Ithaca to the newly-awakened Odysseus, the harbor is the centerpiece of her description:

> "Bebold the port of Phorcys! fenced around / With rocky mountains, and with olives crown'd." [013-NA]

The goddess's exclamation frames the harbor as the definitive landmark of Ithaca — the first thing an Ithacan would recognize upon returning home. The repetition of "olives crown'd" links the harbor's description to the earlier Phaeacian landing scene.

## Significance

The Harbor of Phorcys represents the threshold between Odysseus's wanderings and his homecoming. It is the liminal space where the hero passes from the fantastical world of his adventures — represented by Phaeacia, the last and most hospitable stop — back into the harsh reality of Ithaca under siege by the suitors.

The harbor's dedication to Phorcys, a primordial sea deity associated with hidden dangers of the deep, subtly underscores the dangers still ahead: Odysseus has reached Ithaca, but the struggle to reclaim his household has only just begun. Athene conceals the harbor's identity from him at first with a mist, requiring her divine intervention before he can recognize his homeland — a symbolic blindness that mirrors his long absence.

The olive tree at the harbor's head links the scene to Athene's broader patronage of Odysseus: the olive is her sacred tree, and its presence here signals her watchful protection over this pivotal moment of return.

## Geography

The Harbor of Phorcys is described with enough topographical specificity — twin headlands, an olive-crowned cliff, a cave beneath — that scholars have attempted to identify it with actual harbors on modern Ithaca (Ithaki). The most commonly proposed candidate is the Bay of Dexa on Ithaca's eastern coast, which features a distinctive olive grove and matches the epic's description of a sheltered anchorage accessible by sea.
