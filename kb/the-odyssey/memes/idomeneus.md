# Idomeneus

Idomeneus is the king of Crete and a distinguished Achaean commander at Troy. Though he never appears directly in the Odyssey, his name and reputation loom over the narrative — Odysseus uses him as the basis for one of his Cretan disguise-stories, and his safe return from Troy serves as a point of contrast with Odysseus's prolonged wandering.

## Identity & Names

Idomeneus (Greek: Ιδομενευς, *Idomeneus*) is the son of Deucalion and grandson of Minos, placing him in Crete's royal lineage — descended from Zeus himself through Minos. He led the Cretan contingent at Troy with eighty ships (as recorded in the _Iliad_) and, unlike many Achaean leaders, returned home safely.

Pope refers to him in contexts of martial prowess: "From fierce Idomeneus' revenge I flew" (013-327) and "Idomeneus whom Ilion fields attest / Of matchless deeds" (019-224). These epithets — "fierce" and "matchless" — convey Idomeneus's reputation as a formidable warrior.

## Genealogy and Lineage

Idomeneus's descent from Minos connects him to Crete's mythological deep history. Minos, son of Zeus and Europa, was the legendary king who ruled Crete, built the Labyrinth, and became a judge of the dead in the Underworld (where Odysseus encounters him in Book 11). Deucalion, Idomeneus's father, was Minos's son by Pasiphaë. This genealogy places Idomeneus in a line stretching from Zeus through the great Cretan kings to the generation of the Trojan War — a pedigree Odysseus exploits in his lies by associating himself with a figure of unimpeachable heroic ancestry.

Idomeneus had a son, Orsilochus, whom Odysseus (in his false tale) claims to have killed: "Whose son, the swift Orsilochus, I slew" (013-328). The invention is carefully crafted — Odysseus invents a plausible crime (theft of Trojan spoils) that would force him to flee Crete, explaining his presence abroad.

## Role in the Odyssey

Idomeneus functions in the Odyssey primarily through **absence and allusion**. In Book 13, Odysseus (disguised by Athena) tells a false tale in which he claims to have killed a son of Idomeneus and fled Crete (013-327). In Book 19, Odysseus (still disguised) tells Penelope a "Cretan tale" in which he claims to have entertained Idomeneus on his way to Troy: "'Twas then to Crete the great Ulysses came" (019-228). Both lies derive their plausibility from Idomeneus's historical reality — he is a known figure whose biography provides credible scaffolding for Odysseus's fictions.

The Cretan tales form a recurring motif in the poem. Odysseus tells no fewer than five false stories about his identity, and three of them involve Crete. Idomeneus's kingdom serves as a narrative resource — a distant, plausible origin for a wandering stranger — precisely because Crete was a real and powerful place in the Homeric world.

His safe return — unlike Agamemnon's murder, Ajax's suicide, or Odysseus's wandering — represents the ideal nostos that Odysseus has been denied. Idomeneus is the successful homecomer, the counter-example whose fate Odysseus yearns to share.

## Crete in the Odyssey

Crete occupies a distinctive place in Homeric geography. It is described as a land of ninety cities, whose inhabitants speak multiple languages — a cosmopolitan island at the crossroads of the Aegean. The _Odyssey_ mentions Crete more frequently than any other location except Ithaca itself, making it the poem's most significant "offstage" setting. Odysseus's repeated use of Cretan identities suggests Crete's reputation as a place where a stranger's story could plausibly begin — far enough from Ithaca to be unverifiable, distinguished enough to command respect.
