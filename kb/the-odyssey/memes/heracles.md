# Heracles

**Heracles** (Roman: Hercules) is the greatest hero of Greek mythology, a son of Zeus famed for his Twelve Labours. In the _Odyssey_, Odysseus encounters him in the Underworld — or rather, his phantom (eidolon), for the real Heracles has been deified and dwells on Olympus.

## Role in the Odyssey

Odysseus sees Heracles among the shades in Book 11. Homer uses the word "Hercules" (the Roman name) in Pope's translation: "Now I the strength of Hercules behold, / A towering spectre of gigantic mould" (011-755). The shade of Heracles approaches Odysseus, recognizes him, and speaks: he laments the hardships he endured in life serving a lesser man (Eurystheus), including his descent to Hades to capture Cerberus.

Homer makes an important distinction: the image Odysseus sees is Heracles's phantom, while the hero himself "amidst the immortal gods, / In ease and honour, shares the blest abodes" (011-761), married to Hebe, the goddess of youth.

The bow of Heracles also appears in the narrative: "For journeying on to Hercules, at length" (021-042), Odysseus once traveled to recover the poisoned arrows that Philoctetes later used at Troy — the same bow that the suitors' challenge in Book 21 echoes.

> "Now I the strength of Hercules behold, / A towering spectre of gigantic mould" (011-755)

## Mythological Background

Heracles was the son of Zeus and the mortal Alcmene. Hera's jealousy drove him to madness and set him on the path of the Twelve Labours: slaying the Nemean Lion, the Lernaean Hydra, capturing Cerberus, and other seemingly impossible tasks. After his death (by poison), Zeus granted him apotheosis and marriage to Hebe. His shade in the Underworld represents the mortal part left behind.
