# Melanthius

Melanthius is the goatherd of Odysseus's household whose betrayal of his absent master makes him one of the most contemptible figures in the epic. Son of the loyal servant Dolius and brother of the equally treacherous Melantho, Melanthius actively aids the suitors, insults Odysseus in his beggar disguise, and arms the suitors during the final battle — a sequence of betrayals for which he suffers one of the most gruesome deaths in Homer.

## Identity & Names

The name Melanthius (Greek: Μελανθιος, *Melanthios*) derives from *melas* (black, dark), fitting his role as the dark counterpoint to the loyal servants Eumaeus and Philoetius. Pope describes him as having grown "pamper'd" in luxury under the suitors [017-254]. He is consistently identified as "son of Dolius" [022-186], linking him to the household he betrays.

## Role & Significance

Melanthius is a mortal goatherd, "master of the bearded fold" [020-228]. His narrative function is as the **anti-Eumaeus**: where the swineherd remains loyal to his absent master, Melanthius embraces the suitors' regime. His betrayals escalate across three phases:

1. **Verbal abuse**: He insults Odysseus in his beggar disguise with "sharp" rejoinders [017-301] and bold replies [017-452].
2. **Material support**: He supplies the suitors from Odysseus's own stores, providing wine from "an ample jar" [020-329].
3. **Armed treachery**: During the final battle, he brings arms from the storeroom for the suitors [021-195], [021-294], [022-007].

## Appearances

### Book 17 — First Encounter

Melanthius first appears driving goats to the palace, where he encounters Odysseus (disguised as a beggar) and Eumaeus. He insults the disguised king:

> "What mutters he?" Melanthius sharp rejoins [017-301]

### Books 20–21 — Arming the Suitors

On the day of the contest, Melanthius supplies the suitors with wine [020-329]. When the bow contest begins, he is ordered to prepare a fire [021-195] and bring "the choicest offerings" for the suitors' feast [021-294]. Most critically, he brings arms from the storeroom during the battle [022-007].

### Book 22 — Capture and Execution

After his treachery is discovered — he attempts to fetch more arms through "that outlet to the gate" [022-163] — Melanthius is caught. The loyal servants bind him and hoist him to the rafters. After the battle:

> "Then forth they led Melanthius, and began
> Their bloody work..." [022-520]

He is mutilated — nose, ears, hands, and feet cut off — and left to die, a punishment that marks the severity of a servant's betrayal in Homeric ethics.

### Book 24 — Epilogue

In the Underworld, the slain suitors' shades refer to Agamemnon as "Son of Melanthius" in a context of vengeance [024-140], a passing reference that echoes the name's association with treachery.

## Relationships

### Family

- **Father**: Dolius, the aged and loyal servant of Laertes. That Melanthius is the son of a faithful man makes his betrayal more damning.
- **Sister**: Melantho, mistress of Eurymachus, who likewise insults Odysseus in disguise.

### Allies

- **The Suitors**: Melanthius aligns himself entirely with the suitors' faction, enjoying the luxury of their patronage [017-254].

### Enemies

- **Odysseus**: The master Melanthius betrays, whom he fails to recognize in disguise.
- **Eumaeus and Philoetius**: The loyal servants who capture and punish him.
- **Telemachus**: The heir whose household Melanthius helps plunder.

## Analysis

### The Traitor Servant

Melanthius represents the ultimate violation of the master-servant bond that structures the Homeric household (*oikos*). His treachery is worse than the suitors' because he owes Odysseus direct loyalty as a member of the household. The brutality of his execution [022-520] is proportionate, in Homeric terms, to the depth of his betrayal — a servant who arms his master's enemies has forfeited all claim to mercy.

### The Dark Double

Melanthius functions as Eumaeus's dark double. Both are herdsmen of Odysseus; both encounter the disguised king. Where Eumaeus shows generosity to the stranger, Melanthius offers only contempt. This contrast sharpens the moral stakes of the recognition plot — the same disguise that reveals Eumaeus's loyalty exposes Melanthius's corruption.

### Familial Irony

That Melanthius is Dolius's son adds tragic irony. Dolius remains loyal to Odysseus's house to the end [024-457]. The household that produces both loyal Dolius and treacherous Melanthius embodies the divided state of Ithaca itself during Odysseus's absence.
