# Ate (Blind Ruin)

## Definition

**Ate** (Greek: ἄτη, *átē*) is a state of blind infatuation or delusion sent by the gods — a temporary madness in which the victim cannot perceive the consequences of their actions. In the Homeric moral sequence (*koros* → *hubris* → *ate* → *nemesis*), ate is the penultimate stage: after satiety breeds arrogance, the gods send blindness, and blindness rushes the sinner toward divine retribution. It is not mere mistake but divinely induced self-destruction — the gods harden the heart they have decided to destroy.

## Greek Context

Ate was personified as a goddess in Greek myth — in the *[[iliad|Iliad]]*, [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] excuses his insult to [[achilles|Achilles]] by saying "Ate seized me," and [[zeus|Zeus]] himself was once deceived by Ate. The concept served a crucial theological function: it explained how otherwise intelligent people could make catastrophically foolish decisions. When a person acted against their own obvious interest, ate was the explanation — the gods had taken their wits. This was not comfort but terror: ate meant you were marked for destruction.

## In the Odyssey

### The Suitors' Blindness
The suitors are the epic's clearest case of collective ate. Despite repeated warnings — from [[telemachus|Telemachus]] in the assembly, from [[halitherses|Halitherses]] the seer interpreting omens, from the disguised [[odysseus|Odysseus]] himself — they persist in their abuse. They laugh at prophecy, mock the beggar, and go to their deaths unable to see what the audience sees clearly. This is not ordinary foolishness but divinely sent blindness: [[athena|Athena]] deliberately prevents them from recognizing [[odysseus|Odysseus]] or heeding the signs.

### The Crew's Fatal Folly
[[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s crew also suffers ate. Despite explicit warning, they slaughter and eat the Cattle of [[helios|the Sun]] — an act of such obvious self-destruction that only divine delusion can explain it. Their ate condemns them to death, leaving [[odysseus|Odysseus]] alone to continue his journey.

### Odysseus's Narrow Escape
[[odysseus|Odysseus]] himself flirts with ate when he taunts [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] after escaping the cave. His pride nearly brings [[polyphemus|the Cyclops]]'s rocks down on his ship, and his self-naming gives [[poseidon|Poseidon]] a target for his wrath. [[athena|Athena]]'s protection may be what saves him from the full blindness that consumes his crew.

## Significance

- **Moral theology**: Ate solves a theological problem in Homer: why do the gods punish people for actions they themselves caused? The answer is that divine causation and human responsibility coexist — the gods send the blindness, but the victim's prior hubris earned it.
- **Dramatic irony**: Ate creates the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*'s characteristic dramatic irony. The audience watches the suitors march toward doom, seeing what they cannot see. The poem's pleasure is partly in watching ate work.
- **Psychological insight**: Despite its theological framing, ate describes a real human phenomenon — the capacity for self-destructive blindness, the refusal to see what threatens us, the rush toward the thing that will destroy us.
