# Polyphemus

## Identity & Names

**Polyphemus** (Greek: Πολύφημος, *Polyphēmos*, "much-spoken-of" or "famous") is a Cyclops — a member of a race of one-eyed giant shepherds dwelling on an island identified in antiquity with Sicily. He is the son of Poseidon and the sea-nymph Thoosa. His encounter with Odysseus in Book 9 is the most famous single episode of the *Odyssey* and the origin of the divine wrath that drives the entire plot.

The Cyclopes are described as a lawless, pre-agricultural people: they have "no assemblies for debate, no laws, no public rule" and dwell in isolated mountain caves, each man ruling his own family without regard for his neighbors. Polyphemus is their most formidable representative — a being of immense physical power and complete moral isolation.

## Role & Significance

Polyphemus is the anti-host — the absolute negation of *xenia*. When Odysseus and his men enter his cave seeking hospitality according to the sacred custom, he responds by seizing two of them, dashing their brains out, and devouring them raw. His mockery of the obligation to Zeus Xenios marks him as outside civilization entirely:

> "'Fool,' he replied, 'thou dost not know the race
> Of us Cyclopean sons: we own no law,
> Nor fear the god who bears the sceptred awe;
> For we are stronger far, and scorn to save.'"

Odysseus's escape — getting Polyphemus drunk on undiluted wine, naming himself "Nobody" (Οὖτις), and blinding the giant with a sharpened olive stake heated in the fire — is the supreme demonstration of *metis* triumphing over brute force. The stratagem is layered: the wine exploits appetite, the pseudonym exploits language, and the stake (prepared in advance) exploits the Cyclops's only vulnerability — his single eye.

But the escape is followed by an act of fatal hubris. Safely aboard his ship, Odysseus cannot resist revealing his true identity:

> "Cyclop! if any, as thy dreadful flame,
> Shall ask the reason of thine eye's sad maim,
> Say that Ulysses, who in arts excels,
> Laertes' son, who dwells in Ithaca's fair fields,
> Deprived thee of thy sight."

This self-naming transforms a successful escape into a catastrophe. Polyphemus now has a name to curse, and he directs his prayer to his father Poseidon that Odysseus either never reach home, or arrive "late, in evil plight, with loss of all his crew, upon a foreign ship". Every subsequent misfortune in the epic — every storm, every shipwreck, every dead crewman — is the unfolding of this curse.

## Appearances

### Book 1: The Shadow

Polyphemus does not appear in person, but his blinding is invoked by Zeus as the origin of Poseidon's persecution of Odysseus. The entire epic's plot is set in motion by an event the audience has not yet witnessed.

### Book 9: The Cave

The full episode is narrated by Odysseus himself to the Phaeacian court — a tale within a tale that occupies the center of the poem's longest flashback. The sequence:

1. **The Cave**: Odysseus and twelve men enter Polyphemus's cave, finding "crates of cheese and crowded pens of lambs". His men urge him to steal and flee; he refuses, wanting to meet the owner and claim a guest-gift — a fatal application of civilized norms to an uncivilized being.

2. **The First Meal**: Polyphemus returns with his flocks, rolls a boulder across the cave entrance ("two-and-twenty four-wheeled wains could scarce have moved"), and discovers the intruders. He seizes two men, dashes them against the ground, and devours them — "limb by limb he tore them, and on the reeking maw, / With entrails, flesh, and marrow, filled his jaw".

3. **The Wine**: Odysseus offers Polyphemus the strong Maronean wine, which the Cyclops — accustomed only to crude grape-juice — finds irresistible. He drinks three bowls and asks Odysseus's name, promising a guest-gift in return. Odysseus replies: "My name is Noman".

4. **The Blinding**: While Polyphemus sleeps in a drunken stupor, Odysseus and his men heat the sharpened olive stake in the fire and drive it into the giant's single eye: "the hissing ball, in streaming torrents drowned, / Smoked, as the bubbling root of iron reddens round".

5. **The Escape**: The blinded Polyphemus rolls away the boulder to let his sheep out, feeling their backs for riders. Odysseus has tied his men beneath the rams — "three sheep together tied, the mid one bore a man" — and himself clings to the belly of the mightiest ram.

6. **The Boast**: Once at sea, Odysseus taunts the blinded giant. Despite his crew's pleas to stay silent, he reveals his name, lineage, and homeland — and Polyphemus hurls a cliff-top boulder that nearly destroys the ship.

### Book 11: The Prophecy Fulfilled

Tiresias in the Underworld confirms that Poseidon's anger, originating in the blinding, will pursue Odysseus to the shores of Ithaca and beyond.

## Relationships

- **Poseidon**: His father and avenger. The relationship transforms a local monster-slaying episode into a cosmic conflict, as Poseidon's divine power extends Polyphemus's reach across the entire Mediterranean.

- **Odysseus**: Blinder and victim. Their encounter is a study in opposed values: brute force versus intelligence, lawless nature versus civilization, boastful pride versus cunning restraint. Both characters are undone by their own excess — Polyphemus by his greed for wine, Odysseus by his need to be known.

- **The Other Cyclopes**: Polyphemus's neighbors, who come running when they hear his screams. When he tells them "Noman is killing me," they reasonably conclude that no one is harming him and depart — a comic moment that demonstrates both the cleverness of Odysseus's ruse and the Cyclopes' lack of social solidarity.

## Analysis

### The Anti-Host and the Obligation of Xenia

Polyphemus is the *Odyssey*'s most thorough negation of civilized values. He lives alone, acknowledges no gods (except, when convenient, his father), and treats strangers as food rather than guests. His encounter with Odysseus is structured as a dark parody of the hospitality sequence: the host inquires about the guest's identity, offers food (the guest becomes the food), and gives a guest-gift (a promise to eat Odysseus last).

### Metis as the Civilized Man's Weapon

The Polyphemus episode is the epic's clearest argument for the superiority of intelligence over strength. Odysseus cannot fight a creature who can hurl boulders the size of hills. But he can outthink him. Each element of the escape — the wine, the pseudonym, the stake, the sheep — is a product of planning, not power. The episode validates the Greek preference for *metis* over *bie* (brute force) as the proper way for a civilized man to confront chaos.

### Hubris and Its Cost

The most painful moment in the episode is not the blinding but the boast. Odysseus has won — and then he cannot resist claiming his victory publicly. His men beg him: "O cruel man! why wilt thou yet provoke / The giant's rage?". But Odysseus needs to be known. This need — the hero's hunger for *kleos* — is what converts a triumph into a catastrophe. The remaining twelve books of the poem are the price of that moment of self-assertion.

### The Cyclops in Comparative Mythology

The Polyphemus story belongs to a widespread folktale type (ATU 1137, "The Ogre Blinded") found from Celtic to Siberian traditions. Homer's genius was to graft this folktale onto an epic narrative about divine wrath — so that a monster-blinding that might otherwise be a self-contained adventure becomes the engine of a twenty-four-book poem.
