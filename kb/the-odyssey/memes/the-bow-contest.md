# The Bow Contest

## Summary

The Bow Contest (Book 21) is [[penelope|Penelope]]'s masterstroke — a challenge that simultaneously delays the suitors, tests their worth, and creates the mechanism for their destruction. She announces that she will marry whoever can string [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s great bow and shoot an arrow through twelve axe-heads lined in a row. One by one, the suitors try and fail — none can even string the bow. The disguised [[odysseus|Odysseus]], revealed to [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]] and Philoetius, asks to try. Despite the suitors' protests, [[telemachus|Telemachus]] (now asserting authority) grants him the bow. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] strings it effortlessly, and with the first arrow shot through the axes, the contest becomes a massacre.

## Key Moments

- [[penelope|Penelope]]'s announcement and the suitors' eager acceptance
- [[telemachus|Telemachus]]'s near-success and [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s silent signal to stop
- Each suitor's failure — [[antinous|Antinous]] suggests greasing the bow, but none can bend it
- [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s revelation to [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]] and Philoetius: the scar and the plan
- The stringing of the bow: the moment of truth
- The first arrow through the axes: contest becomes execution

## Aftermath

The bow contest leads directly into the [[slaughter-of-the-suitors|Slaughter of the Suitors]] (Book 22). But its deeper significance is thematic: the bow is a test of identity — only [[odysseus|Odysseus]] can string it, and his doing so proves who he is before he speaks his name. [[penelope|Penelope]], who set the contest, is not present for its execution — she sleeps through the slaughter, another instance of divine orchestration ([[athena|Athena]] ensures she rests).

## The Bow's History

The great bow is not an ordinary weapon. Its history is carefully narrated (Book 21, 11–41): it was a gift from Iphitus of Messene, whom Odysseus met as a young man at the house of Ortilochus while on a diplomatic mission to recover stolen sheep. Iphitus, himself searching for twelve lost mares, gave the bow to Odysseus as a token of friendship — a bond of *xenia* that took on tragic irony when Iphitus was murdered by Heracles shortly afterward. Odysseus never used the bow in war; he kept it as a memorial of his dead friend and used it only in Ithaca, for hunting.

This backstory does far more than explain where the bow came from. It establishes three crucial themes. First, the bow is an object of *xenia* — a gift given in friendship that will be turned against those who violated the hospitality bond. Second, it connects Odysseus to the heroic generation before the Trojan War (Iphitus's father Eurytus was the greatest archer of the age, taught by Apollo himself). Third, it explains why Odysseus left it behind when he sailed for Troy: it was not a weapon of war but a domestic possession, a piece of Ithaca itself. The bow thus embodies Odysseus's identity as Ithacan king, host, and inheritor of a heroic past that transcends the Achaean expedition.

## The Symbolism of the Twelve Axes

The twelve axe-heads through which Odysseus must shoot are not a random target. The number twelve carries cosmological significance in Homeric epic — the twelve Olympians, the twelve months of the year, the twelve ships of Odysseus's fleet — and the axes themselves are double-headed (*πέλεκυς*), a shape that suggests the doorways of the palace. The arrow's path through the aligned socket-holes of twelve axe-heads mimics Odysseus's own trajectory: a single, unerring line through a sequence of thresholds, each one carrying him closer to the restoration of his house.

Scholars have debated the exact arrangement of the axes — whether they were planted in the ground or set in a frame — since antiquity. The Homeric text says Telemachus "dug a trench" and "set the axes in a straight line," suggesting they were embedded in the earth with their iron heads aligned. In either case, the visual image is powerful: the bow that once killed stags on the slopes of Parnassus now defeats the men who have devoured Odysseus's herds.

## Penelope's Agency

The bow contest is Penelope's most decisive act of agency in the entire epic. Until Book 21, she has been the object of others' decisions — the suitors' demands, Telemachus's commands, Athena's manipulations. By setting the contest, she seizes the initiative: she determines the terms, the prize (herself), and the criterion (stringing Odysseus's bow). The contest is a test that only one man can pass, and Penelope knows it — whether consciously or through what the poem presents as divine inspiration.

Her absence from the contest's execution is often read as Homer's refusal to grant her full narrative agency. But it can equally be read as Athena's protection: the goddess ensures Penelope sleeps through the slaughter so that she will not witness the bloodbath in her own hall. Penelope's sleep, like Odysseus's sleep before the landing on Ithaca, is the sleep of transition — the passage from one world to another that the gods accomplish while mortals rest.
