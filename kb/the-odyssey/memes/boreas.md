# Boreas

**Boreas** is the Greek god of the north wind, one of the four directional Anemoi (wind gods). In the _Odyssey_, he appears not as a character but as a personified natural force — a violent wind that drives storms across the sea and threatens sailors.

## Role in the Odyssey

Boreas is invoked three times in Pope's translation, each time as a destructive wind. After Odysseus leaves Calypso's island, Poseidon stirs a storm against him, and Boreas rages alongside other winds (Book 5, 005-480). In a simile, Homer compares the rush of warriors to "the drier blasts alone of Boreas" sweeping a newly shorn field (005-502). Later, during the storm that destroys Odysseus's raft, Boreas "raged along the inclement sky" (005-637), piling waves upon the hero.

## Mythological Background

Boreas is the son of Astraeus (the starry sky) and Eos (the dawn). His brothers are Zephyrus (west wind), Notus (south wind), and Eurus (east wind). In Greek myth, Boreas was associated with winter, cold, and violent storms. His home was said to be in Thrace, a region north of Greece.

## Name and Etymology

The Greek name Βορέας (*Boréas*) derives from the Proto-Indo-European root *\*gʷorʰ-* ("mountain"), cognate with Sanskrit *giri-* ("mountain") and Slavic *gora* ("mountain, forest"). The semantic development from "mountain" to "north wind" is characteristic of Greek — the north wind was conceived as the wind that descends from the mountains of Thrace. An alternative etymology links the name to βορός (*borós*, "devouring"), emphasizing the wind's destructive power, a quality Homer exploits in his storm similes.

In Latin, Boreas became *Aquilo* (the "eagle wind") and was occasionally identified with *Septentrio*, though the Greek name remained standard in poetic usage. Ovid and Virgil both preserve the Homeric characterization of Boreas as violent and cold, the bringer of winter storms.

## Boreas and the Anemoi System

Homer's wind-god system differs from later classical systematization. In the *Odyssey*, the winds are not fully individualized gods but personified forces under [[aeolus|Aeolus]]'s command. Boreas, however, already possesses a distinct personality — he is the most violent of the winds, associated exclusively with destruction at sea. This differs from his later representation (e.g., in the *Iliad*'s agricultural similes and in Hesiod), where he also serves a fertilizing function, driving away clouds and drying the land.

The relationship between Boreas and the other winds in the *Odyssey* reflects Homeric cosmology: all winds ultimately answer to Zeus, who delegates authority to Aeolus. When Poseidon stirs the storm against Odysseus, he does not command Boreas directly but stirs all winds together — a chaotic convergence that represents divine hostility made manifest as weather.

## Comparative Context

The association of wind gods with cardinal directions is not unique to Greek religion. Near Eastern storm-gods (such as the Mesopotamian *Enlil* and the Canaanite *Baal Hadad*) combine wind, storm, and kingship functions that Greek religion distributes across multiple deities (Zeus as sky-king, Poseidon as sea-storm, Boreas and his brothers as directional winds). The Homeric separation of these functions — Zeus rules, Poseidon stirs, Boreas executes — reflects the Greek tendency toward divine specialization.

In later Greek tradition, Boreas was credited with the destruction of the Persian fleet at Artemisium (480 BCE), and the Athenians built him a sanctuary by the Ilissus river in gratitude. Herodotus (7.189) records that the Athenians prayed to Boreas as their "son-in-law" — a reference to the myth in which Boreas abducted the Athenian princess Orithyia and made her his wife. This civic cult of Boreas appears to have been a genuine element of fifth-century Athenian religion, transforming the Homeric storm-demon into a tutelary deity of the city.

## Literary Legacy

Boreas's Homeric characterization — violent, cold, implacable — shaped his reception in later European literature. In Dante's *Purgatorio*, the north wind appears as the agent of divine punishment in the circle of the lustful. Shakespeare uses "the bitter sky" and "winter's wind" as a figure for harsh fortune (*As You Like It*, 2.7). The Romantic poets, particularly Shelley in his "Ode to the West Wind," inverted the Homeric hierarchy: Zephyrus, not Boreas, becomes the poet's alter ego, the wind of inspiration and revolution rather than of mere destruction.

> "So the rude Boreas, o'er the field new-shorn, / Sweeps with his winged force the scattered corn" (005-480)
