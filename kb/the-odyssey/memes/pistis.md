# Pistis (Faithfulness)

## Definition

**Pistis** (Greek: πίστις, *pístis*) is the bond of trust and faithfulness that holds relationships together — between husband and wife, master and servant, host and guest, mortal and god. In Homer, pistis is not an abstract virtue but a concrete social reality: it is demonstrated through action, tested through suffering, and rewarded with loyalty returned. The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* measures every character by their pistis: [[penelope|Penelope]]'s fidelity, [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]]'s loyalty, the suitors' faithlessness, and the servants' betrayal.

## Greek Context

In a society without written contracts, pistis was the foundation of all durable relationships. A promise, an oath, or a bond of hospitality created obligations that were binding not legally but morally — and the gods enforced them. "His praise, eternal on the faithful stone" (001-324) captures the permanence of true pistis: it outlasts death. The faithless person destroyed not just one relationship but the fabric of trust on which the community depended.

## In the Odyssey

### Penelope's Fidelity
[[penelope|Penelope]] is the epic's supreme exemplar of pistis. For twenty years she maintains faith with an absent husband, using her wits to delay the suitors. Her final test of [[odysseus|Odysseus]] — asking about their marriage bed — is an act of pistis in action: she trusts no appearance, only the truth that fidelity discerns.

### The Servants' Test
The disguised [[odysseus|Odysseus]] tests the pistis of every member of his household. [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]] the swineherd passes perfectly — "faithful to his trust" (002-278) — offering his poor hospitality to a stranger. The goatherd [[melanthius|Melanthius]] fails, siding with the suitors. The maids who slept with the suitors are executed. The poem judges each by a single standard: were you faithful to the absent master?

### Divine Faithfulness
[[athena|Athena]]'s protection of [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is a form of divine pistis. She does not abandon him even when [[poseidon|Poseidon]] rages, because he has earned her trust through shared qualities of mind. "[[mentor|Mentor]] arose, [[odysseus|Ulysses]]' faithful friend" (002-270) — [[athena|Athena]] in disguise — shows that even gods honor the claims of loyalty.

## Significance

- **Moral compass**: Pistis is the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*'s primary criterion for judgment. Characters are not evaluated by birth, wealth, or strength but by their faithfulness — or lack of it.
- **Narrative suspense**: The recognition scenes (anagnorisis) that structure the poem's second half are tests of pistis. Each reunion asks: will this person prove faithful?
- **Contrast with the [[iliad|Iliad]]**: Where the *[[iliad|Iliad]]* values prowess in battle, the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* values loyalty in absence — a quiet, domestic virtue elevated to heroic status.
