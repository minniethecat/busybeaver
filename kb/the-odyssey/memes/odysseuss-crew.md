Odysseus's crew is the company of Ithacan sailors and soldiers who sail from [[troy|Troy]] with [[odysseus|Odysseus]] in twelve ships. Over the course of the wanderings (Books IX–XII), every single crew member perishes — some through enemy action, some through divine punishment, and some through their own fatal choices. The crew's progressive annihilation is the dark counter-narrative to Odysseus's survival, a tragedy of collective disobedience that the epic frames as both a moral lesson and a structural device clearing the way for the hero's solitary return.

## In the Odyssey

### Departure from Troy

After the sack of Troy, Odysseus's contingent departs with twelve ships and their crews — likely around 600 men, each vessel carrying approximately fifty oarsmen and warriors. The crew is described as "Ithacensian" — men of [[ithaca|Ithaca]] and its subject islands — and represents the military-age male population of Odysseus's realm that did not remain behind (unlike the older generation, such as [[laertes|Laertes]], who stayed to manage the kingdom).

### First Losses: The Cicones (Book IX)

The crew's first casualties occur at [[ismarus|Ismarus]], where their refusal to heed Odysseus's counsel results in a Ciconian counterattack:

> "Six brave companions from each ship we lost, / The rest escape in haste, and quit the coast" [009-NA]

The loss of six men per ship — seventy-two total across twelve vessels — establishes the crew's fatal pattern: their leader gives sound advice, they ignore it, and they pay in blood. The epithet "brave" applied to the lost men is Homeric convention but carries an ironic weight: these are brave men who died not in heroic combat but in the aftermath of their own indiscipline.

### The Lotus-Eaters (Book IX)

At the land of the [[the-lotus-eaters|Lotus-Eaters]], three scouts are sent to reconnoiter:

> "Three men were sent, deputed from the crew / (A herald one) the dubious coast to view" [009-NA]

These men taste the lotus and lose all desire to return home. Odysseus drags them back by force and binds them to their rowing benches — a rare instance where his intervention prevents a loss rather than merely documenting one.

### The Cyclops' Cave (Book IX)

The crew's most catastrophic episode occurs in the cave of [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]]. Twelve men are selected to accompany Odysseus into the cave for reconnaissance; six are devoured by the Cyclops before the survivors escape through the famous "Nobody" ruse:

> "The joyful crew survey his mighty size, / And on the future banquet feast their eyes" [009-NA]

The grim irony is that the crew "feast their eyes" on Polyphemus's stores moments before becoming the Cyclops's feast themselves. The surviving crew members' role in the escape — clinging to the undersides of sheep — makes them participants in Odysseus's cunning rather than passive victims.

### The Bag of Winds (Book X)

Within sight of Ithaca, the crew commits their most consequential act of disobedience:

> "Me, lock'd in sleep, my faithless crew bereft / Of all the blessing of your godlike gift!" [010-NA]

Convinced that Odysseus is hoarding treasure, they open [[aeolus|Aeolus's]] bag of winds, unleashing a storm that drives the fleet back across the sea. This act — born of greed and mistrust — transforms a near-arrival into years of additional suffering and ensures the crew's eventual annihilation.

### Final Destruction: Thrinacia (Book XII)

The crew's last survivors perish at [[thrinacia|Thrinacia]], where despite Odysseus's explicit warning, they slaughter the sacred cattle of [[helios|Helios]]. Zeus destroys their ship with a thunderbolt, and every man drowns except Odysseus, who drifts alone to [[ogygia|Ogygia]]. The crew's extinction is complete — a punishment for impiety that also serves the narrative necessity of Odysseus's solitary homecoming.

## Characteristics

The crew is characterized primarily through its collective failures: disobedience, greed, short-sightedness, and impiety. Yet the Odyssey does not present them as villains. They are ordinary men placed in extraordinary circumstances — hungry, frightened, and far from home — whose tragic flaw is not malice but the inability to trust their leader's foresight. The epithet "sad companions" (used repeatedly) frames them as objects of pity as much as judgment.

The crew's progressive thinning — from twelve ships to one, then to none — creates the Odyssey's most relentless narrative rhythm. Each island costs lives. Each escape is narrower than the last. By the time Odysseus reaches Calypso's island, he is alone, and the reader understands that his survival is not merely luck or divine favor but the accumulated cost of dozens of ordinary men who could not match his prudence.

## Key Members

- **[[eurylochus|Eurylochus]]** — Second-in-command, whose skepticism of Odysseus's judgment leads him to question orders at Circe's island and later to persuade the crew to slaughter Helios's cattle
- **[[elpenor|Elpenor]]** — The youngest crew member, who dies in a drunken fall from Circe's roof and appears as the first shade Odysseus encounters in the underworld
- **[[perimedes|Perimedes]]** — A crew member mentioned by name during the underworld rites, one of the few individually identified
