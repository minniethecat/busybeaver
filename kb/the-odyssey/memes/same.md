Same (called Samos in Pope's translation, though distinct from the larger Aegean Samos) is one of the Ionian islands that form part of [[odysseus|Odysseus's]] realm, alongside [[ithaca|Ithaca]], [[dulichium|Dulichium]], and [[zacynthus|Zacynthus]]. It is a mountainous island whose name appears across multiple books as both a geographical marker and a political entity contributing suitors to the court at Ithaca.

## In the Odyssey

### Odysseus's Domain (Book IX)

When Odysseus declares his identity to [[alcinous|Alcinous]] and the Phaeacian court, he names Same as part of his kingdom:

> "Dulichium, Same and Zaccynthus crown'd / With shady mountains spread their isles around." [009-NA]

The grouping of three islands — Dulichium, Same, Zacynthus — alongside Ithaca forms the canonical quartet of Odysseus's realm. The epithet "crown'd with shady mountains" characterizes Same as forested and elevated, consistent with its identification as a substantial island rather than a minor rock.

### The Suitors' Numbers (Book XVI)

In Book XVI, a precise tally of the suitors reveals Same's contribution to the siege of Penelope's hall:

Twenty-four suitors hail from Same, a number that places it second only to Dulichium (fifty-two suitors) and ahead of Zacynthus (twenty). This distribution suggests Same was a significant population center within the realm — more populous than Zacynthus but less than Dulichium, the largest of the subject islands.

### Political Geography

Same's relationship to Ithaca is that of a subject territory within a broader island kingdom. The suitors from Same are counted among the "peers" — noblemen of standing — who have gathered at Odysseus's court, indicating that Same maintained its own aristocratic class that participated in the political life of the realm.

## Significance

Same's role in the Odyssey is primarily demographic and political rather than narrative. Unlike Ithaca (the stage of the main action) or Dulichium (home of the leading suitor Amphinomus), Same contributes to the suitor problem without producing a named major character. This anonymity makes Same representative of the broader realm — the unnamed many whose collective weight threatens to overwhelm Odysseus's household.

The island also contributes to the epic's geography of return: Same is one of the places Odysseus must re-establish authority over after reclaiming Ithaca. The twenty-four dead suitors from Same represent twenty-four households on that island whose lords will not return — a political aftershock that the Odyssey acknowledges but does not narrate, leaving it to the final book's divine intervention (Athene's imposed peace) to resolve.

## Geography

Same is most commonly identified with modern Kefalonia (Cephallenia), the largest of the Ionian Islands, located west of Ithaca (Ithaki). Strabo and other ancient geographers support this identification, noting that Same/Kefalonia is indeed mountainous ("shady mountains") and substantial in size. However, some scholars have proposed alternative identifications, including the possibility that Homeric Same corresponds to a part of modern Ithaki itself, reflecting changes in island names and boundaries since antiquity.
