# Iliad

## Identity

The *Iliad* (Greek: Ἰλιάς, "The Song of Ilium") is the epic poem of the Trojan War — specifically, of the wrath of Achilles and its catastrophic consequences for Greeks and Trojans alike. Traditionally attributed to Homer and composed in the late 8th century BCE, it precedes the *Odyssey* both in narrative chronology and in the Western canon: the *Iliad* is the poem of war, honor, and death; the *Odyssey* is the poem of its aftermath.

The *Iliad* covers approximately fifty days in the tenth year of the Trojan War. Its opening word — *mēnin* ("wrath") — announces its theme: the rage of Achilles at Agamemnon's seizure of his prize of honor, Briseis. From this personal slight unfolds a cosmic drama: Achilles withdraws from battle, the Greeks are driven to their ships, Patroclus is killed wearing Achilles's armor, and Achilles returns to slaughter Hector, whose body he drags behind his chariot around the walls of [[troy|Troy]]. The poem ends not with the fall of the city (that is the story of the Trojan Horse, recounted in the *Odyssey*) but with the ransoming of Hector's body by his father Priam — a moment of shared grief that transcends enmity.

The *Iliad*'s world is one of mortal glory (*kleos*) won at the cost of early death. Achilles's choice — a short life with immortal fame or a long life in obscurity — is the poem's central moral calculus, and its reverberations echo throughout the *Odyssey*. When [[odysseus|Odysseus]] meets Achilles's shade in the [[the-underworld|Underworld]] (Book 11), the dead hero's lament — "I would rather be a slave on earth to another man than rule over all the dead" — is a devastating revision of the *Iliad*'s heroic code.

## Relationship to the Odyssey

The *Iliad* is the absent prequel to the *Odyssey* — the story that precedes and shapes everything that follows:

- **Shared characters**: Odysseus, Nestor, Menelaus, Agamemnon, Achilles, Ajax, Helen, and many others appear in both epics. The *Odyssey* assumes the reader knows their stories from the *Iliad*.
- **The Trojan Horse**: The most famous episode of the war — Odysseus's stratagem of the wooden horse — is not narrated in the *Iliad* (which ends before the fall) but is recounted in the *Odyssey* by Menelaus (Book 4) and Demodocus (Book 8).
- **The returns (*nostoi*)**: The *Odyssey* is one of many "return stories" (*nostoi*) that followed the *Iliad*. Where the *Iliad* asks "how does a man win glory?", the *Odyssey* asks "how does a man survive glory and find his way home?"
- **Moral contrast**: The *Iliad*'s world is governed by *biē* (force) and *kleos* (glory); the *Odyssey*'s world substitutes *metis* (cunning) and *nostos* (homecoming). Odysseus is the bridge figure — the warrior of the *Iliad* who becomes the survivor of the *Odyssey*.

## Themes

| Theme | In the Iliad | In the Odyssey |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| [[kleos]] (glory) | The supreme value — Achilles chooses immortal fame over long life | Questioned — Achilles's shade rejects the heroic bargain |
| [[time]] (honour) | The cause of conflict — Agamemnon's seizure of Briseis | The goal of recovery — Odysseus reclaiming his status |
| [[metis]] (cunning) | Marginal — Odysseus is the exception among warriors | Central — the defining heroic quality |
| Wrath (*mēnis*) | The poem's subject — Achilles's cosmic rage | Transformed — Poseidon's wrath as obstacle, not subject |
| Mortality | The tragic core — even the greatest heroes die | The defining condition — Odysseus chooses mortality |

## Significance

The *Iliad* is the foundational text of Western literature — the first epic, the first tragedy, the first extended meditation on war and mortality. Its influence on the *Odyssey* is pervasive: the later poem is not a sequel but a response, a rethinking of what heroism means when the war is over and the hero must find his way home.

The two epics form a diptych: war and peace, wrath and cunning, death and survival, the field of battle and the domestic hearth. To read the *Odyssey* without the *Iliad* is to hear the answering voice without the call — possible, but incomplete.
