## Identity and Names

Icarius (Greek: Ἰκάριος, *Ikários*) is distinguished from the Athenian Icarius (father of Erigone, associated with the introduction of wine) by his Spartan domicile and his role as Penelope's father. His daughter Iphthima — "Icarius' daughter she, whose blooming charms / Allured Eumelus to her virgin arms" [004-1059] — lives in Pherae, while Penelope was married to Odysseus of Ithaca. Pope consistently prefixes his name with the epithet "great" [001-378][002-240], marking Icarius's stature as an elder patriarch.

The name Icarius may derive from the Greek Ἴκαρος, suggesting a connection to the root meaning "to come" or "to arrive" — a fitting etymology for a figure whose significance lies entirely in what will happen when Penelope *arrives* back at his house. The epithet "great" (δῖος, *dîos*, more accurately "brilliant, noble") places Icarius among the class of elder aristocrats whose authority is a given of the Homeric social order.


## Family Connections

Through Penelope, Icarius is Odysseus's father-in-law and Telemachus's grandfather. His other daughter, Iphthima, is married to Eumelus of Pherae [004-1059]. When Penelope grieves for Telemachus, Athena sends a dream-vision in the form of Iphthima to comfort her — a narrative thread that connects Icarius's two daughters across the distance.

At the poem's close, the shade of Agamemnon praises Penelope as "Icarius' daughter, glory of the past" [024-233], putting her father's name in the climactic position. The epithet elevates Icarius from a mere plot device to a figure whose lineage defines the virtuous queen — her Spartan blood, through him, is part of what makes her "the model to the future age" [024-234].

Icarius also belongs to a broader pattern of absent fathers in the *Odyssey*. Like Laertes (absent through grief), like Odysseus himself (absent through wandering), Icarius is a father defined by the space he leaves. His absence from Ithaca is what makes him powerful: because he is not there to claim Penelope, the suitors can invoke him without his consent, turning his paternal authority into a rhetorical weapon.


## Role in the Odyssey

Icarius's significance in the *Odyssey* is primarily structural. As Penelope's father, he possesses the legal and customary right to receive his daughter back and arrange a new marriage for her. This provides the suitors with their strongest argument: if Penelope will not choose among them, she should return to Icarius's house so he can select a husband on her behalf.

In Book 1, Antinous invokes this paternal authority: "Dismiss'd with honour, let her hence repair / To great Icarius, whose paternal care / Will guide her passion, and reward her choice / With wealthy dower, and bridal gifts of price" [001-378]. The same formula is repeated by Eurymachus in Book 2 [002-240], establishing it as a stock argument among the suitors.

Telemachus himself, in his private lament, wrestles with the dilemma: "How to Icarius in the bridal hour / Shall I, by waste undone, refund the dower?" [002-169]. Returning Penelope to Icarius would trigger a financial obligation — Telemachus would have to repay the bride-price Icarius gave at the wedding. This economic dimension makes Icarius's paternal claim not merely a matter of sentiment but of household economy.

### The Dower Economics

The bride-price (ἕδνα, *hédna*) that Icarius gave at Penelope's wedding is not a one-time ceremonial gift but a continuing financial instrument. Under Homeric custom, if a marriage is dissolved and the wife returns to her father's house, the bride-price must be refunded. For Telemachus, whose estate is being consumed by the suitors, this represents an impossible financial burden: "by waste undone" [002-169], he cannot pay.

Icarius is thus not merely a paternal figure but an economic pressure point. Every invocation of his name in the suitors' speeches carries a double meaning: on the surface, it appeals to filial piety and custom; beneath, it threatens Telemachus with financial ruin. The suitors' argument that Penelope should "hence repair / To great Icarius" [001-378] is, in effect, an argument for the destruction of Odysseus's household — which is exactly what their continued presence in the palace is accomplishing.


## Icarius and Spartan Identity

That Penelope is "Icarius' daughter" locates her origins in Sparta, a kingdom associated in Homeric epic with wealth, horses, and female beauty. Helen, the face that launched a thousand ships, is also Spartan — and the parallel between the two daughters of Spartan fathers (Helen daughter of Tyndareus, Penelope daughter of Icarius) is thematically significant. Both are pursued by suitors; both are the objects of contest. But where Helen's departure from Sparta triggers war, Penelope's potential return to Sparta (through Icarius) would signal domestic collapse. The *Odyssey* uses Icarius to invert the *Iliad*'s central motif: the woman who stays is as narratively powerful as the woman who left.
