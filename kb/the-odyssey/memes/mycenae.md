# Mycenae

## Description

Mycenae is Agamemnon's kingdom, the wealthiest and most powerful of the Achaean cities, and the dark mirror of Ithaca throughout the *Odyssey*. Unlike Ithaca, which is a medium-sized island kingdom of modest wealth, Mycenae is "rich in gold" — the seat of the most powerful king in Greece, the commander of the entire Trojan expedition. But where Ithaca's absent king has a faithful wife, Mycenae's returning king had a wife who conspired with her lover Aegisthus to murder him in his bath. And where Ithaca's son Telemachus is slowly coming of age, Mycenae's son Orestes has already taken vengeance on his father's killers. Mycenae is the story of what happens when a hero's return goes terribly wrong, and Orestes's example — praised by Athena, Nestor, and the shade of Agamemnon himself — is held up to Telemachus throughout the poem as both model and warning.

## In the Odyssey

**The Paradigm of Orestes**: Mycenae enters the *Odyssey* in the very first divine council: Zeus, meditating on human folly, recalls how Aegisthus ignored Hermes's warning not to kill Agamemnon and was killed in turn by Orestes. Athena immediately cites Orestes as a model for Telemachus: "Or have you not heard what glory Orestes won among all mankind when he killed the murderer of his father?" (Book 1). Nestor tells the same story at Pylos (Book 3), and the shade of Agamemnon himself recounts his murder to Odysseus in the Underworld (Book 11). In Book 24, in Hades, Agamemnon's shade greets the slaughtered suitors and contrasts his own wretched homecoming with Odysseus's triumphant one.

**Agamemnon's Story (Book 11)**: In the Nekyia, Agamemnon tells Odysseus the full story: how he returned to Mycenae after ten years at Troy, how Clytemnestra welcomed him with a feast, and how she and Aegisthus murdered him in his bath — "as one kills an ox at the manger" — while his concubine Cassandra screamed in the courtyard. The story is the anti-nostos, the inverse of the return Odysseus seeks.

**The Contrast**: Throughout the poem, the *Odyssey* invites the reader to compare Mycenae and Ithaca. Both are kingdoms whose kings went to Troy. Both have sons who must deal with their father's absence. Both have wives besieged by unwanted men. But Clytemnestra succumbed; Penelope did not. Aegisthus was invited in; the suitors forced their way in. Agamemnon was murdered in his moment of triumph; Odysseus will reclaim his household in his.

## Significance

Mycenae is the *Odyssey*'s negative example — the story of what Odysseus and Penelope and Telemachus could have become. Every time the poem praises Penelope's fidelity, Clytemnestra's infidelity is the unspoken foil. Every time Telemachus is praised for taking action, Orestes's more decisive action is invoked as precedent. The *Odyssey* is, in part, a sustained argument that Ithaca is different from Mycenae — that this return will not be that return, that this wife is not that wife, and that this son will not need to commit matricide to reclaim his patrimony. The return to Ithaca is a repudiation of the return to Mycenae.

## Geography

Mycenae is one of the most important archaeological sites in Greece, located in the northeastern Peloponnese, about 90 kilometers southwest of Athens. The citadel, with its famous Lion Gate and shaft graves, was the center of a powerful Bronze Age kingdom (c. 1600–1100 BCE) that gave its name to the entire Mycenaean civilization. The site's wealth — gold masks, inlaid daggers, carved ivories — matches Homer's description of "rich-in-gold Mycenae."
