# Poseidon

## Identity & Names

**Poseidon** (Greek: Ποσειδῶν, Latin: *Neptune*) is the god of the sea, earthquakes, and horses — one of the three great sons of Cronus who divided the cosmos, receiving the oceans as his domain. In Pope's translation he is called Neptune throughout. His epithets include *Ennosigaeus* ("Earth-Shaker") and *Gaieochos* ("Earth-Holder"), reflecting his dual power over sea and land. Homer addresses him as "the monarch of [[the-aegean-sea|the deep]]" and "the god who shakes the shores".

## Role & Significance

Poseidon is the divine antagonist of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* — the force whose wrath converts a weeks-long voyage into a ten-year ordeal. His anger was incurred when Odysseus blinded his son, the Cyclops Polyphemus, and then proudly revealed his true name, allowing Polyphemus to direct his father's curse against a specific target. Poseidon cannot kill Odysseus (fate decrees his return), but he can — and does — make every mile of the journey a torment.

The poem resolves Poseidon's wrath only at its very end, when Odysseus performs the prescribed propitiation: traveling inland until he meets people who do not recognize an oar, then planting it and sacrificing to the sea-god. This resolution transforms the *Odyssey* from a story of divine persecution into one of cosmic reconciliation.

## Appearances

Poseidon appears directly in four books, but his influence pervades the entire poem:

### Book 1: Strategic Absence

Poseidon's absence from the divine assembly on [[olympus|Olympus]] — he is visiting the Ethiopians, "the most remote of nations" — allows Athena to persuade Zeus to decree Odysseus's return. This narrative detail establishes both Poseidon's implacable opposition (he would have vetoed any relief for Odysseus) and the limits of his power (Zeus's decree binds even him).

### Book 5: The Storm

Returning from Ethiopia, Poseidon sees Odysseus sailing on his handmade raft and erupts in fury:

> "Oh, heavens! no doubt the gods, in my long stay,
> Some new decree have fixed, to bless his way;
> For lo! he near the Phaeacian coast appears,
> Where fate decrees to end his wandering years."

He summons a catastrophic storm: "He spoke, and high the forky trident hurled", shattering the raft and casting Odysseus into the waves. Only the intervention of the sea-nymph Leucothea and Athena's calming of the winds saves Odysseus from drowning.

### Book 9: The Origin of Wrath

Though Poseidon does not appear in person, Book 9 contains the narrative origin of his anger. Odysseus recounts how he and his men blinded the Cyclops Polyphemus, and how — after escaping — Odysseus could not resist revealing his identity:

> "Cyclop! if any, as thy dreadful flame,
> Shall ask the reason of thine eye's sad maim,
> Say that Ulysses, who in arts excels,
> Laertes' son, who dwells in Ithaca's fair fields,
> Deprived thee of thy sight."

Polyphemus then prays to his father Poseidon, asking that Odysseus never reach home, or if fate decrees otherwise, that he arrive "late, in evil plight, with loss of all his crew, upon a foreign ship". Poseidon hears and grants the curse.

### Book 11: Tiresias's Prophecy

In [[hades|the Underworld]], the shade of [[tiresias|Tiresias]] reveals to Odysseus the full scope of Poseidon's enmity. The seer warns that Poseidon "pursues with unrelenting hate" and prescribes the only path to reconciliation: Odysseus must travel inland carrying an oar until he meets a people who mistake it for a winnowing fan, then plant the oar and sacrifice a ram, a bull, and a boar to Poseidon.

### Book 13: The Phaeacian Ship

After [[the-phaeacians|the Phaeacians]] deliver Odysseus safely to [[ithaca|Ithaca]], Poseidon demands recompense from Zeus for this defiance of his will. Zeus grants him permission to punish the Phaeacians, and Poseidon turns their returning ship — and its crew — to stone in the harbor:

> "He fixed the ship, a monument of stone,
> Deep in the bay, and rooted to the ground."

The petrified ship becomes a permanent warning of the sea-god's power and a boundary marker between the Phaeacians' sheltered world and the mortal realm.

## Relationships

### Divine Family

Poseidon is the son of Cronus and Rhea, brother to Zeus and Hades. After the Titanomachy, the three brothers divided the cosmos by lot: Zeus received the sky, Hades the underworld, and Poseidon the sea — though the earth and Olympus remained common ground. He is married to the sea-goddess Amphitrite, and their son Triton serves as his herald.

### Polyphemus

The Cyclops Polyphemus is Poseidon's son by the nymph Thoosa. The [[the-cyclops-episode|blinding of Polyphemus]] is the precipitating event for the entire *Odyssey*'s plot. The relationship is dynastic — Poseidon's defense of his son's honor is presented not as tender paternal love but as a matter of divine prerogative: to injure a god's offspring is to challenge the god himself.

### Odysseus

The central mortal relationship of the poem. Poseidon's hatred of Odysseus is personal (his son was blinded), principled (Odysseus's hubris in boasting demanded punishment), and persistent (it lasts twenty years). Yet it is never absolute — Poseidon accepts the limits imposed by fate and Zeus's authority. The relationship evolves from active persecution (Books 5, 9, 13) to a state of negotiated truce after Odysseus performs the propitiation rites.

### Athena

Athena is Poseidon's niece and counter-force in the divine economy. Where Poseidon blocks, Athena opens; where he destroys, she builds. Their opposition is not personal — they never confront each other directly — but structural: Athena represents the civilized intelligence that can overcome brute natural force. The *Odyssey*'s resolution depends on Athena's diplomacy (persuading Zeus) outmaneuvering Poseidon's wrath.

### Zeus

Zeus is both Poseidon's brother and his superior. Poseidon chafes at Zeus's authority — he reminds Zeus that "the lots assigned to each his realm defines" — but ultimately submits to it. Zeus's decree that Odysseus shall return limits Poseidon's destructive capacity, creating the dramatic tension that defines the poem: Poseidon can torment but not kill.

## Analysis

### Divine Wrath as Moral Transaction

Poseidon's role in the *Odyssey* is theologically sophisticated: he is not evil but offended. His wrath is not arbitrary but a response to a specific injury — the blinding of his son compounded by Odysseus's hubristic self-naming. The poem thus presents divine persecution as intelligible within a moral framework: the gods punish specific transgressions, and the sufferer can understand (if not easily endure) the reason for his suffering.

This makes Poseidon fundamentally different from the arbitrary gods of Mesopotamian epic. His anger has a reason; therefore, it has a remedy. The *Odyssey* ends not in despair but in propitiation — the restoration of right relationship between mortal and god.

### Poseidon and the Epic's Geography

Poseidon governs the physical medium of Odysseus's suffering — the sea. Every island, every storm, every shipwreck occurs within his domain. This makes him more than a personal antagonist; he is the embodiment of the chaos that separates the hero from home. Athena's patronage and Poseidon's persecution map onto the poem's central tension between civilization (*nostos*) and dissolution (*ate*).

### Comparative Context

Poseidon's anger bears comparison to the wrath of [[achilles|Achilles]] in the *[[iliad|Iliad]]* — both are forms of [[menis]], the cosmic anger that drives epic narrative. But where Achilles's menis is destructive to his own community, Poseidon's menis is directed outward against a single mortal who offended him. The *Odyssey* thus inverts the *Iliad*'s pattern: divine wrath, rather than human, becomes the obstacle that the hero must patiently endure and finally transcend.

### Scholarly Reception

Classicists have long noted the theological paradox Poseidon embodies: why should an Olympian god be unable to kill a single mortal? The standard answer — that fate (*[[moira]]*) binds even the gods — is complicated by Zeus's statement in Book 1 that mortals "by their own follies fall", suggesting that divine persecution serves a moral rather than merely fated purpose. The Austrian philologist Albin Lesky argued that Poseidon represents the "dark double" of Odysseus — both are cunning, both are wrathful, and both are ultimately forced to accept limits on their power.
