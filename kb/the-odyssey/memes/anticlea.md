# Anticlea

Anticlea is the mother of Odysseus and wife of Laertes. She dies of grief during Odysseus's long absence, and her shade is among the first spirits Odysseus encounters in the Underworld. Her reunion with her son is one of the most poignant moments in the epic — a meeting that reveals the human cost of Odysseus's protracted nostos.

## Identity & Names

The name Anticlea (Greek: Αντικλεια, *Antikleia*) means "against fame" or "opposed to glory." She is the daughter of Autolycus, the notorious trickster and thief, making her lineage one of cunning on both sides. Pope describes her shade as "pale Anticlea" [023-362], emphasizing her spectral, diminished state.

## Role & Significance

Anticlea is a mortal woman of Ithaca who dies before Odysseus returns home. As a shade in the Underworld, she serves three crucial narrative functions:

1. **Bearer of domestic news**: She tells Odysseus that Penelope remains faithful and Telemachus holds the estate, offering the first reliable intelligence from Ithaca [011-008].
2. **Witness to grief's cost**: Her own death from longing for her son [011-121] measures the human toll of the Trojan War and its aftermath on those left behind.
3. **Emotional anchor**: The three failed attempts to embrace her shade [011-189], [011-201] dramatize the unbridgeable gulf between the living and the dead, and between Odysseus and the home he left decades ago.

## Appearances

### Book 11 — The Nekyia (Underworld)

Odysseus encounters his mother's shade among the first spirits drawn to the blood offering. He is shocked to find her dead, having left her alive when he sailed for Troy:

> "Now a thin form is all Anticlea was!" [011-121]

She explains that she died not of disease but of grief:

> "Unmindful of her son, Anticlea stands?" [011-189]

When Odysseus tries three times to embrace her, she slips through his arms like a shadow or a dream. She drinks the blood and speaks [011-201], telling him of Penelope's constancy and Telemachus's administration of the estate [011-008].

### Book 23 — Recognition

After the slaughter of the suitors, Odysseus tells Penelope of his journey, including his encounter with "his royal mother, pale Anticlea's shade" [023-362].

### Book 24 — The Second Nekyia

In the final book, the suitors' shades descend to Hades, where they find Anticlea among the illustrious dead. She is mentioned as having sent her son forth [024-397], a testament to her lasting place in the epic's memorial architecture.

## Relationships

### Family

- **Father**: Autolycus, the master thief and trickster, from whom Odysseus inherits his cunning.
- **Husband**: Laertes, king of Ithaca before Odysseus, now withdrawn to his orchard in grief.
- **Son**: [[odysseus]], her only child, whose absence kills her and whose return comes too late.

### Emotional Bonds

Anticlea's grief for Odysseus is the purest expression of maternal love in the epic. Unlike Penelope's constancy (sustained by hope) or Laertes's withdrawal (sustained by despair), Anticlea's response is total: she simply cannot survive her son's absence.

## Analysis

### Grief as Narrative Force

Anticlea's death from longing is not merely a biographical detail but a thematic statement. The Odyssey repeatedly shows that the Trojan War's cost extended far beyond the battlefield — to mothers, wives, and children who died of broken hearts years after the fighting ended. Anticlea is the epic's most direct embodiment of this collateral grief.

### The Failed Embrace

The three attempts to embrace Anticlea's shade [011-201] is one of the most imitated scenes in Western literature (echoed by Virgil in Aeneas's encounter with Anchises, by Dante with various shades, and by countless later poets). The image captures the fundamental theme of nostos: homecoming is always partial, always belated, always shadowed by what has been irretrievably lost.

### Matrilineal Cunning

As daughter of Autolycus, Anticlea is the conduit through which Odysseus inherits his trademark cunning. The epic does not explore this genetic transmission explicitly, but the genealogy suggests that Odysseus's mētis has a matrilineal source — an inheritance from his mother's father rather than from Laertes, who is portrayed as a straightforward, honorable king.
