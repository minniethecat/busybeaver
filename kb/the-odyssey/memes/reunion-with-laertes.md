# Reunion with Laertes

The reunion of [[odysseus|Odysseus]] with his father [[laertes|Laertes]] in Book 24 is the final recognition scene of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* — the last of the nested revelations that structure the poem's second half. Unlike the dramatic recognitions by Euryclea (the scar), [[telemachus|Telemachus]] (the transformation), and [[penelope|Penelope]] (the bed), this reunion unfolds in the quiet of [[laertes|Laertes]]'s orchard, where the aged king tends his garden in mourning. It is a scene of paternal love restored, and it immediately precedes the final battle that closes the poem.

## Context

After the slaughter of the suitors in Book 22 and the reunion with [[penelope|Penelope]] in Book 23, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] departs the palace for the countryside. He seeks his father, whom he has not seen for twenty years. [[laertes|Laertes]], believing his son dead, has retreated from the palace to live in rustic solitude, cultivating his orchard in a state of perpetual grief:

> "Not old Laertes, broken with despair" [014-206]

The reunion thus carries the weight not only of recognition but of restoration — the father must be brought back from the living death of mourning. [[laertes|Laertes]] had once been a king and warrior; now, as the swineherd [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]] reports, "Laertes lives, the miserable sire" [015-386], a man who "still lives, but lives to weep and pray" — his only occupation the tending of his orchard and the nursing of his grief.

The gathering of three generations — Laertes, Odysseus, Telemachus — in the orchard, surrounded by loyal servants, creates a microcosm of the restored Ithacan household before the final battle.

## The Recognition Scene

[[odysseus|Odysseus]] approaches his father in disguise, testing [[laertes|Laertes]]'s heart before revealing himself. The orchard setting is freighted with memory:

> "Pass'd to Laertes' cultivated land. / The ground himself had purchased with his pain, / And labour made the rugged soil a plain" [024-246]

The setting is not heroic but domestic — a garden worked by an old man's hands. It is here, among the pear and olive trees, that [[odysseus|Odysseus]] must prove his identity.

When [[odysseus|Odysseus]] first addresses his father, he does not reveal himself but instead tests Laertes with a fabricated tale — claiming to have hosted Odysseus five years prior. Laertes's grief-stricken response, weeping and pouring dust upon his grey head, moves Odysseus so deeply that he can no longer sustain the disguise.

When persuasion fails, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] produces the scar — the same mark that identified him to Euryclea — and, more intimately, recalls the trees [[laertes|Laertes]] gave him as a child:

> "Lo here the wound (he cries) received of yore, / The scar indented by the tusky boar, / When, by thyself, and by Anticlea sent, / To old Autolycus' realms I went" [024-395]

Then he names the trees: "the several trees you gave me long ago" [024-399] — thirteen pear trees, ten apple trees, forty fig trees, and fifty rows of vines. This catalogue of gifts is the ultimate proof: only the son who walked this orchard as a boy could enumerate its planting.

> "Amazed, Laertes: 'Give some certain sign / (If such thou art) to manifest thee mine'" [024-393]

The father's stunned question — "manifest thee mine" — encapsulates the reunion's emotional core. [[laertes|Laertes]] needs not merely proof of identity but proof of *belonging*: is this man truly his son?

## Aftermath

Once convinced, [[laertes|Laertes]]'s transformation is immediate. He who had been "busied in his garden all alone" [024-005] now arms himself for battle alongside his son and grandson:

> "And, still more old, in arms Laertes shone. / Trembling with warmth, the hoary heroes stand" [024-586]

[[athena|Athena]] herself intervenes, breathing renewed vigor into the old king: "Then thus [[athena|Minerva]] in [[laertes|Laertes]]' ear: / '[[laertes|Son of Arcesius]], reverend warrior, hear!'" [024-609]. [[laertes|Laertes]] hurls his spear and kills Eupeithes, father of [[antinous|Antinous]], in the battle that follows — an act of martial closure that confirms his restoration from grieving father to warrior-king.

The spear-cast is laden with symbolic weight: the father who had withdrawn from the world of men now strikes the killing blow against the father of the man who led the assault on his son's house. The symmetry of justice — son kills usurpers, father kills usurper's father — seals the house's restoration with a final act of blood-vengeance that the poem frames as righteous.

## Thematic Significance

The reunion with [[laertes|Laertes]] completes the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*'s pattern of recognitions (*anagnorisis*), ascending from servants (Euryclea, [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]]) through son ([[telemachus|Telemachus]]) and wife ([[penelope|Penelope]]) to father ([[laertes|Laertes]]). This ordering is deliberate: the father is the last to know, because the father's grief is the deepest — twenty years of believing his son dead, with no [[penelope|Penelope]] to share the vigil.

The scene also resolves the poem's concern with *patrilineal continuity*. At the opening of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*, [[telemachus|Telemachus]] is a fatherless son; at its close, [[laertes|Laertes]] has a son and grandson fighting beside him. The three generations — [[laertes|Laertes]], [[odysseus|Odysseus]], [[telemachus|Telemachus]] — stand together in arms, a living emblem of the house restored.

The scar itself acquires new meaning in this final recognition. When Euryclea discovered it in Book 19, it proved identity; when Odysseus shows it to his father, it proves *belonging* and *inheritance*. The scar links Odysseus not only to the boar hunt of his youth but to the orchard his father planted, the trees enumerated, and the line of Arcesius — a chain of fathers and sons that the reunion makes whole again.
