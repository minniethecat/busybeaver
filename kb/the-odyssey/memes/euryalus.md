# Euryalus

Euryalus is a young Phaeacian nobleman, renowned as a wrestler, whose insult to Odysseus and subsequent repentance form one of the most pointed demonstrations of the guest-friendship code in the *Odyssey*. His character arc — from boastful challenger to contrite gift-giver — is compressed into a single book but carries thematic weight: it dramatises the correction of a breach in *xenia* and the education of a young man in the obligations of hospitality.

## Identity and Names

Euryalus (Greek: Εὐρύαλος, *Eurýalos*, "broad sea") shares his name with several Homeric figures, including a Greek warrior in the *Iliad* (the companion of Diomedes who accompanies him on the night raid in Book 10). In the *Odyssey*, he is exclusively a Phaeacian, introduced among the athletes competing at Alcinous's games. Pope's similes place him in the heroic register: "Euryalus, like Mars terrific, rose, / When clad in wrath he withers hosts of foes" [008-130].

The name's etymology — "broad sea" (*eurys* + *hals*) — links him to the maritime culture of Phaeacia, an island people defined by their relationship to the sea. His wrestling prowess and physical vitality embody the Phaeacian ideal of *arete* (excellence) in its athletic form.

## Role in the Odyssey

Euryalus first appears as a competitor in the Phaeacian games, where he wins the wrestling contest. His physical prowess is established before his character is tested:

> "The conquest, great Euryalus, is thine" [008-145]

When [[laodamas|Laodamas]] invites the still-anonymous Odysseus to compete, and Odysseus declines, Euryalus delivers the provocation that drives the narrative turn. He dismisses Odysseus as no athlete but "a wandering merchant" who "bears no claim / Among the great" [008-188]. This insult — delivered to a guest who has not yet revealed his identity — is more than a competitive taunt; it is a violation of the sacred obligation to honour strangers, the fundamental law of *xenia*.

The insult reveals a character flaw not in malice but in youthful arrogance. Euryalus has never known hardship; his world is Phaeacia's insulated prosperity. He cannot imagine that a weather-beaten stranger might be someone of consequence — a failure of imagination that is itself a failure of hospitality.

Odysseus's response is electrifying. He seizes a discus heavier than any the Phaeacians have used and hurls it far beyond their marks, then challenges any Phaeacian to contest him in any sport. The rebuke silences the crowd, and Alcinous moves quickly to de-escalate, offering music and dancing instead.

[[alcinous|Alcinous]] then instructs Euryalus to make amends with a formula that articulates the *xenia* ethic directly:

> "And thou, Euryalus, redeem thy wrong; / A generous heart repairs a slanderous tongue" [008-442]

Euryalus obeys instantly, offering Odysseus a silver-studded sword and a formal apology. His response is notably complete — he does not merely hand over a gift but accompanies it with explicit verbal acknowledgment of his error:

> "Then thus Euryalus: 'O prince, whose sway / Rules this bless'd realm, repentant I obey; / Be his this sword'" [008-446]

Odysseus accepts the gift graciously, and the breach in hospitality is healed. The sword itself is narratively significant — an all-silver sword with a silver-studded hilt, a gift of high material value that matches the seriousness of the offence.

## Thematic Significance

Euryalus functions as a foil to the suitors. Like [[antinous|Antinous]], he insults a stranger; unlike Antinous, he repents and makes restitution. His story-within-a-story demonstrates the correct outcome of a *xenia* violation — acknowledgement, apology, and gift — precisely the outcome that the suitors, in their obstinacy, refuse to achieve.

The contrast is structural: the Phaeacian episode in Books 6–8 is positioned immediately before Odysseus's narrative of his wanderings (Books 9–12), and the Euryalus episode within it models the restoration of proper guest-relations that the suitors' behaviour in Ithaca will systematically violate. When the poem returns to Ithaca in Book 13, the reader has already been shown — through Euryalus — what correction looks like.

The episode also foreshadows Odysseus's revelation of his identity: Euryalus's insult and the subsequent discus throw prepare the Phaeacians — and the reader — for the disclosure that the stranger is a man of enormous capability. The pattern of "disguise → insult → demonstration → recognition" here mirrors, in miniature, the larger arc of Odysseus's return to Ithaca.
