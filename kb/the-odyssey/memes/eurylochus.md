# Eurylochus

## Identity & Names

**Eurylochus** (Greek: Εὐρύλοχος) is a prominent member of Odysseus's crew during the return voyage from Troy. In Pope's translation, he appears as a cautious, sometimes defiant figure — the only crew member who resists [[circe|Circe]]'s enchantment through prudence and the voice of dissent on the island of the Sun. He is kin to Odysseus by marriage ("in wedlock with my sister join'd"), which grants him a unique status among the crew and explains both his frankness and his ultimate survival until the Helios episode.

## Role & Significance

Eurylochus occupies a structurally important role in the *Odyssey*: he is the foil to Odysseus, representing caution — and, at times, mutinous doubt — that counterpoints the hero's confidence. His two defining moments reflect the crew's tragic trajectory from survival to doom.

In Book 10, he alone escapes Circe's transformation by refusing to enter her palace. While his companions are turned to swine, "Eurylochus alone of all the band" [010-279] returns to warn Odysseus:

> Eurylochus alone of all the band,
> Escaped the terrors of the direful land. [010-279]

His warning is prudent rather than cowardly: he has witnessed something terrible and survived through instinct.

But in Book 12, this same caution curdles into defiance. On the island of [[helios|Helios]], when Odysseus forbids the crew from touching the sacred cattle, Eurylochus speaks for the starving men:

> Till wrathful thus Eurylochus began. [012-342]

His speech — that it is better to die quickly at sea than slowly of starvation — persuades the crew to slaughter the cattle of the Sun, the act that dooms them all. This is Eurylochus's tragedy: the same survival instinct that saved him from Circe destroys him at Thrinacia.

## Relationships

- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]** — His commander and kinsman by marriage. Their relationship is complex: Odysseus trusts him ("in whom my soul could most confide" [010-248]) but is also frustrated by his caution and, ultimately, his mutiny.
- **[[circe|Circe]]** — The enchantress whose magic Eurylochus alone survives through cautious refusal. His report to Odysseus makes him the first to articulate the danger Circe represents.
- **The Crew** — As Odysseus's second-in-command, Eurylochus speaks for the crew's interests. His mutiny in Book 12 represents the crew's collective desperation, not mere insubordination.

## Analysis

### Prudence and Its Limits

Eurylochus embodies the ambiguity of caution. In the Circe episode, his refusal to enter the palace is vindicated — he is the sole survivor among his party. But the same instinct, applied to starvation on Thrinacia, becomes the catalyst for the crew's destruction. The *Odyssey* does not present Eurylochus as a villain; his argument — that a quick death by divine wrath is preferable to slow starvation — has a grim logic. The tragedy is structural: the crew is caught between two impossible choices, and Eurylochus's voice of survival is also the voice of doom.
