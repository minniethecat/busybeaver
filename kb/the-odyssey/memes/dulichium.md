Dulichium is an island kingdom in the Ionian Sea, frequently mentioned in the [[the-odyssey|Odyssey]] as part of the realm associated with [[odysseus|Odysseus]] and as a source of [[penelope|Penelope's]] suitors. In Pope's translation, Dulichium is described as "crown'd with fruitful stores," indicating its agricultural wealth. The island sent the largest single group of suitors — fifty-two young nobles — to the court at [[ithaca|Ithaca]].

## In the Odyssey

### The Suitors of Dulichium (Book XVI)

In Book XVI, [[telemachus|Telemachus]] learns the full extent of the suitor threat when he asks the swineherd [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]] about their numbers. Dulichium contributes the largest contingent:

> "Hear then their numbers; from Dulichium came / Twice twenty-six, all peers of mighty name." [016-NA]

The passage underscores Dulichium's prominence among the Ionian islands — its fifty-two noble suitors far outnumber those from [[same|Same]] (twenty-four), [[zacynthus|Zacynthus]] (twenty), and Ithaca itself (twelve).

### The Suitors' Siege (Book I)

Early in the epic, the Dulichian suitors are among those who have overrun Odysseus's palace:

> "The court proud Samos and Dulichium fills, / And lofty Zacinth crown'd with shady hills." [001-NA]

### Odysseus's Feigned Story (Book XXIV)

In the final book, Odysseus (in disguise) fabricates a tale claiming Dulichian origin to test his father [[laertes|Laertes]]:

> "I for Dulichium urge the watery way." [024-NA]

## Significance

Dulichium represents the fracturing of Odysseus's kingdom during his twenty-year absence. As the largest contributor of suitors, it embodies the collective threat to Ithaca's sovereignty. The island's prominence in the suitor catalog also reflects the geopolitical reality of the Ionian archipelago — Dulichium was historically one of the Echinades islands under Odysseus's rule, alongside Same, Zacynthus, and Ithaca.

The name "Dulichium" derives from the Greek *dolichos* ("long"), possibly referring to the island's elongated shape. Its frequent pairing with Same and Zacynthus in Homeric formulas establishes a tetrarchy of islands that form Odysseus's domain.

## Geography

Modern scholarship identifies Dulichium with the island of Makri or one of the Echinades islands off the coast of Acarnania in western Greece. The identification remains debated, with some scholars suggesting it corresponds to Cephallenia (modern Kefalonia). The island is consistently described as fertile and productive, "crown'd with fruitful stores" in Pope's rendering.
