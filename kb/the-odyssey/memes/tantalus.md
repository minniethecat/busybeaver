# Tantalus

**Tantalus** is a figure of Greek mythology whose eternal punishment in Tartarus is witnessed by Odysseus during his journey to the Underworld in Book 11 of the _Odyssey_. His name is the origin of the English word "tantalize."

## Role in the Odyssey

In Book 11, Odysseus describes seeing Tantalus among the great sinners of the Underworld: "There Tantalus along the Stygian bounds / Pours out deep groans" (011-733). He stands in a pool of water that recedes whenever he bends to drink, beneath fruit-laden branches that the wind lifts away whenever he reaches for them. Hunger and thirst torment him endlessly — punishment for crimes against the gods.

Homer describes Tantalus's torment with elaborate sensory detail. The water surrounds him — even rising to his chin — yet "when to the water he his lip applies, / Back from his lip the treacherous water flies" (011-737). Above his head, trees of every kind bear fruit: "There figs, sky-dyed, a purple hue disclose, / Green looks the olive, the pomegranate glows" (011-741). Yet whenever he reaches upward, blasts of wind "toss it on high, and whirl it to the skies" (011-745). The punishment is designed for maximum frustration — abundance perpetually visible, perpetually withdrawn.

Tantalus is introduced alongside his fellow sufferers: "He then beholds Tityus, Tantalus, Sisyphus" (011-012). This triad — Tantalus, Tityus, and Sisyphus — forms the canonical group of great sinners in Homer's Underworld, each enduring a distinct eternal torment that later artists and writers would elaborate across centuries.

## Mythological Background

Tantalus was a king (of Lydia or Phrygia) and a son of Zeus. His crimes vary by tradition: serving his son Pelops as a feast to the gods, stealing nectar and ambrosia, or revealing divine secrets. For his offenses, he was condemned to Tartarus. His descendants — Pelops, Atreus, Agamemnon, and Orestes — form the cursed House of Atreus.

The crime most relevant to the _Odyssey_ is Tantalus's violation of xenia, the sacred bond of hospitality. By serving his own son's flesh to the gods at a banquet, he inverted the most fundamental obligations of host and guest. This makes his punishment — eternal hunger and thirst at a feast he can never touch — a form of poetic justice: the perverter of hospitality is himself denied its sustenance.

## The House of Atreus

Tantalus's curse extends through his lineage. His son Pelops (restored to life by the gods) fathered Atreus and Thyestes, whose rivalry produced a chain of betrayals and murders. Atreus's son Agamemnon appears prominently in the _Odyssey_ as a cautionary example of the disastrous homecoming — murdered by his wife Clytemnestra and her lover Aegisthus. His son Orestes, in turn, avenges him — a story repeatedly invoked in the poem as a parallel to Telemachus's situation. Through this chain, Tantalus's primordial crime echoes across the entire epic.

> "He then beholds Tityus, Tantalus, Sisyphus, / And each the hateful price of torment pays" (011-012)
