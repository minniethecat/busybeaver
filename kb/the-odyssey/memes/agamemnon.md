# Agamemnon

## Identity & Names

**Agamemnon** (Greek: Ἀγαμέμνων) is the king of Mycenae, commander-in-chief of the Greek expedition to [[troy|Troy]], and the central figure of Aeschylus's *Oresteia*. In the *Odyssey*, however, he never appears alive — he is a ghost, a cautionary tale, and the dark mirror against which [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s homecoming is measured. In Pope's translation, he is "the mighty Agamemnon," "Atrides," and "the king of men":

> How he, the mighty Agamemnon, fell. [003-325]

His story — triumphant return from war followed by betrayal and murder at the hands of his wife [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and her lover [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] — is the *Odyssey*'s recurring counter-narrative, the tragedy that might have been Odysseus's fate.

Pope invokes him most often as **Atrides** ("son of Atreus"), a patronymic that alone carries the weight of his cursed lineage. He is also **"the king of men"** and **"king of a hundred kings"** [024-048] — titles won at Troy but rendered ironic by his death at his own hearth. No other figure in the poem embodies both the zenith of [[kleos|kleos]] and the nadir of [[nostos|nostos]] in a single life.

> "King of a hundred kings! to whom resign'd / The strongest, bravest, greatest of mankind" [024-048–049]

## Role & Significance

Agamemnon's role in the *Odyssey* is entirely as a foil and exemplar. Where Odysseus's *nostos* ends in reunion and restoration, Agamemnon's ends in murder — "touch'd his Argive coast" only to die at his own hearth [003-250]. The contrast is established in the poem's opening lines, where [[zeus|Zeus]] cites Aegisthus's crime as evidence that mortals bring suffering on themselves:

> How Agamemnon touch'd his Argive coast,
> And how his faithless wife received her lord. [003-250]

The Zeus-frame is essential: the first story told in the *Odyssey*, after the proem, is not Odysseus's but Agamemnon's [001-064–069]. Zeus declares mortals blame gods for self-inflicted woes, and the proof is Aegisthus — who ignored [[hermes|Hermes]]'s warning. This elevates Agamemnon's fate from backstory to theological thesis.

His shade appears in [[the-underworld|Book 11]], when Odysseus travels to [[hades|the Underworld]]. There, Agamemnon recounts his murder in detail — "he, the mighty Agamemnon, fell" [003-325] — and warns Odysseus to return to [[ithaca|Ithaca]] in secret, never trusting even his own wife. This warning, delivered from beyond death, haunts the second half of the poem and shapes Odysseus's cautious approach to his homecoming.

The generational parallel deepens the foil: Orestes avenges his father and is held up as the model for [[telemachus|Telemachus]]. But Orestes must kill his own mother; Telemachus fights alongside his living father against external usurpers. The *Odyssey* offers a resolution — patriarchal restoration without matricide — that the Oresteia cannot.

> "Warn'd by my ills beware, (the shade replies,) / Nor trust the sex that is so rarely wise" [011-559–560]

In Book 24, Agamemnon's shade appears again in the Underworld, this time greeting the slaughtered suitors. His praise of Penelope — contrasting her fidelity with Clytemnestra's treachery — provides the poem's final judgment on the ideal of marital loyalty:

> Thus Agamemnon's kingly shade rejoin'd [024-063]

## Relationships

- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]** — His wife and murderer. While Agamemnon was at Troy, she took [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] as her lover and conspired in her husband's death. Her betrayal is the *Odyssey*'s ultimate example of violated *philia* (household loyalty). At first she resisted, guarded by a bard left by Atrides [003-350–351]; once the gods removed that safeguard, she fell. Agamemnon's shade declares her name will "blacken all the race" of women [011-552].

- **[[orestes|Orestes]]** — His son and avenger. Orestes's killing of Aegisthus and Clytemnestra is held up repeatedly — by [[athena|Athena]], Nestor, and Zeus — as the model that [[telemachus|Telemachus]] should emulate. His fame "through all the Grecian land" echoes as a call to action. Yet Agamemnon's shade reveals the cost: "my Orestes never met these eyes, / Without one look the murder'd father dies" [011-573–574].

- **[[aegisthus|Aegisthus]]** — His murderer and usurper. Aegisthus seduced Clytemnestra and killed Agamemnon at a banquet, seizing the throne of Mycenae. Zeus's opening speech frames this as paradigmatic human folly: warned by Hermes, yet "impotent of mind, and uncontroll'd, / He plunged into the gulf which Heaven foretold" [001-070–071].

- **[[menelaus|Menelaus]]** — His brother, king of [[sparta|Sparta]], who fought alongside him at Troy. Menelaus's grief for Agamemnon colours his reception of Telemachus in Book 4. The asymmetry is poignant: Menelaus returned with wealth restored; his brother returned only to die. "Deep from his inmost soul Atrides sigh'd" [004-457].

- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]** — His fellow commander at Troy, whose homecoming is the poem's central narrative. Agamemnon's shade warns Odysseus: return in secret, test your household, trust no one — a warning that shapes Odysseus's entire strategy in Ithaca. The two kings are bound by shared war, opposite fates, and counsel from beyond the grave.

- **[[penelope|Penelope]]** — The wife he never met but whose virtue he extols from Hades. His praise in Book 24 — "Not more thy wisdom than her virtue shined" [024-231] — is the poem's definitive contrast between the faithful wife and the treacherous one [024-233–240].

- **[[achilles|Achilles]]** — Fellow commander at Troy. In Book 24, Agamemnon describes Achilles's funeral — seventeen days of mourning — contrasting that heroic death with his own ignoble end: "But what to me avail my honours gone, / Successful toils, and battles bravely won?" [024-130–131].

## Analysis

### The Anti-Odysseus

Agamemnon functions in the *Odyssey* as a structural opposite to its hero. Both are kings returning from Troy. Both have wives and sons waiting at home. But where Penelope is faithful, Clytemnestra is treacherous; where Telemachus grows into manhood and joins his father, Orestes must avenge a dead one; where Odysseus approaches Ithaca with [[metis|metis]]-driven caution, Agamemnon walked openly into his palace — and his death.

This contrast is not merely thematic; it is the engine of suspense that drives the second half of the poem. When Odysseus lands on Ithaca and assumes his beggar disguise, the reader knows that Agamemnon's fate is the alternative. Every test of loyalty, every moment of restraint, is charged with the awareness of what happened at Mycenae.

### Bloodguilt and Justice

Agamemnon's death sets in motion the chain of revenge that culminates in Orestes's matricide — a cycle that will, in later Greek tragedy, require Athena's intervention to break. The *Odyssey* alludes to this chain but focuses on a different resolution: Odysseus's revenge is legitimate because it targets those who have violated [[xenia|xenia]] and usurped his household, not kin. The poem's ending, in which Athena halts the cycle of vengeance, suggests that the justice Odysseus achieves is of a different, more stable kind than the blood-vengeance of [[mycenae|the house of Atreus]].

The Atreid curse stretches back generations. Atreus fed his brother Thyestes his own children; Aegisthus, Thyestes's surviving son, murders Agamemnon in part as family vengeance, perpetuating the cycle. The *Odyssey* acknowledges this dark inheritance but offers an alternative: legitimate authority restored by divine sanction, rather than endless intra-familial bloodshed.

### The Oresteia in the Odyssey

The *Odyssey* uses Agamemnon's story as a structuring device on multiple levels. Theologically, Zeus's opening speech establishes the Agamemnon-Aegisthus-Orestes triangle as proof of mortal responsibility [001-064–069]. Narratively, five retellings — by Zeus, Nestor, Menelaus, Agamemnon's shade, and Amphimedon's shade — create a refrain that punctuates the poem's arc from crisis to resolution.

Each retelling adds a layer. Zeus offers the divine perspective: the crime was forewarned and inexcusable. Nestor adds political detail: the guardian bard and Clytemnestra's slow corruption [003-350–360]. Agamemnon's shade supplies visceral horror: the feast turned abattoir, the futile reach for a sword [011-519–544]. In Book 24, [[the-suitors|the suitors]]' narrative closes the circle — those who would have re-enacted the crime in Ithaca are themselves destroyed.

The *Odyssey* thus contains a compressed Oresteia, but uses it as foil rather than tragedy. Where Aeschylus moves from blood-vengeance to civic law, the *Odyssey* moves from threatened chaos to restored household order. Agamemnon's story is the negative space that defines the positive shape of Odysseus's triumph.

### The Shadow of Troy

Agamemnon bridges the *Odyssey* to the [[iliad|Iliad]]. He is the only major Iliadic commander whose post-war fate is fully narrated. He who "high above the rest Atrides press'd the plain" [011-498] at Troy is reduced to "an empty shade" [011-504]. The contrast with Achilles sharpens the point: Achilles died in battle and received a god's funeral; Agamemnon died at a banquet, slaughtered like "the stately ox" that "bleeds within the stalls" [011-523–524]. His question — "what to me avail my honours gone?" [024-130] — interrogates the heroic code itself.
