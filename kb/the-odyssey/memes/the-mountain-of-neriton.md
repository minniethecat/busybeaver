The Mountain of Neriton (also called Neritus) is the dominant topographical feature of [[ithaca|Ithaca]], a forested peak whose summit "divides the clouds." It serves as the island's most recognizable landmark across multiple books of the Odyssey, anchoring Ithaca's identity in the epic landscape.

## In the Odyssey

### Ithaca's Defining Peak (Book IX)

When [[odysseus|Odysseus]] first declares his identity to Alcinous and the Phaeacian court, Neriton is the first feature he names:

> "My native soil is Ithaca the fair, / Where high Neritus waves his woods in air" [009-NA]

The mountain is presented as the emblem of Ithaca itself — "the fair" island's most distinctive natural feature. The phrase "waves his woods" personifies the mountain and emphasizes its forested flanks, a detail that recurs in later descriptions. The poet positions Neriton at the head of Odysseus's self-introduction, before any mention of the island's political geography or its people.

### Athene's Revelation (Book XIII)

When Athene dispels the mist and reveals Ithaca to the returned Odysseus, Neriton is among the landmarks she points to:

> "Behold! where Neritus the clouds divides, / And shakes the waving forests on his sides." [013-NA]

The image of the mountain "dividing the clouds" conveys its height relative to the surrounding landscape. The "waving forests" that shake on its flanks suggest the winds that sweep the exposed summit — a detail that would resonate with any Ithacan accustomed to the island's weather patterns. The goddess's exclamation frames Neriton as the vertical axis of Ithaca, the visual anchor around which the island's geography is organized.

### A Royal Name (Book XVII)

In Book XVII, the personal name Neritus appears among the sons of Ithacus, suggesting the mountain's name predates the epic and carries dynastic significance:

> "Neritus, Ithacus, Polyctor, there" [017-NA]

The appearance of "Neritus" as a personal name confirms the mountain's cultural embeddedness: it is not merely a geographical feature but a totemic name woven into the island's founding mythology. The mountain gives its name to one of Ithaca's ancestral figures, linking the natural landscape to the human lineage that rules it.

## Significance

Neriton functions as Ithaca's axis mundi — the vertical center around which the island's identity is organized. Unlike the Harbor of Phorcys (a site of arrival) or the palace (a site of contest), the mountain is a constant, visible from both sea and land, unchanged by the twenty years of Odysseus's absence.

The mountain's three appearances span three distinct registers of the poem: Odysseus's self-presentation to strangers (Book IX), divine revelation of home (Book XIII), and the deep history of Ithaca's ancestral names (Book XVII). This distribution reinforces Neriton as the most stable and enduring feature of Ithaca — the one thing about the island that requires no explanation, no recovery, no disguise.

## Geography

Neriton is traditionally identified with Mount Neritos (modern Anogi) on the Greek island of Ithaki, rising to approximately 808 meters. The mountain's summit is indeed often wreathed in cloud, consistent with the Homeric epithet. Its forested slopes were noted in antiquity; deforestation has since reduced the tree cover, but the mountain remains the highest point on Ithaki and a dominant visual landmark for approaching vessels.
