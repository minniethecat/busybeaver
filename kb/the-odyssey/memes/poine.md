# Poine (Revenge)

## Definition

**Poine** (Greek: ποινή, *poinḗ*) is the ancient Greek concept of retribution — literally a "blood-price" or penalty paid for wrongdoing, and by extension the vengeance that restores moral equilibrium. In Homeric society, revenge was not optional but obligatory: a son must avenge his father, a king must punish those who violate his household. The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* is structured around two parallel acts of poine: [[orestes|Orestes]]'s revenge against Aegisthus for murdering [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], and [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s revenge against the suitors for violating his household.

## Greek Context

In pre-legal Greek society, poine was the primary mechanism of justice. Without police or courts, kin-obligation enforced order: the victim's family must exact retribution, or the dead would find no rest and the community would be polluted (*miasma*). This system was gradually displaced by the law courts of the polis, but in Homer's world it remains the foundation of moral order. The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* itself is a document of this transition — the epic culminates not in endless blood-feud but in divine intervention that halts the cycle of revenge.

## In the Odyssey

### The Orestes Paradigm
The poem opens by establishing [[orestes|Orestes]] as the model of righteous revenge. [[zeus|Zeus]] himself recounts how [[orestes|Orestes]] killed Aegisthus to avenge his father [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] — "the revenge of [[orestes|Orestes]]" (003-009) is the standard against which [[telemachus|Telemachus]] and [[odysseus|Odysseus]] are measured. [[athena|Athena]] urges [[telemachus|Telemachus]]: "Then let revenge your daring mind employ" (001-401), explicitly linking his coming-of-age to the duty of vengeance.

### The Suitors' Slaughter
[[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s revenge on the suitors (Book 22) is the epic's central act of poine. The suitors have violated every sacred bond — xenia, loyalty to the absent king, respect for the household. Their deaths are presented as both personal retribution and divine justice. The poet asks: "With great revenge, immortal praise acquired?" (001-405) — framing revenge as a path to kleos.

### The Cycle Broken
Unlike the *Oresteia*, where revenge generates endless counter-revenge, the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* ends with the cycle broken. After the slaughter, the suitors' families march on [[odysseus|Odysseus]], but [[athena|Athena]] intervenes: "the mighty warrior flies, big with revenge" (002-385) is halted by divine command. [[zeus|Zeus]]'s thunderbolt ends the feud, marking the transition from private vengeance to divinely administered justice.

## Significance

- **Moral engine**: Poine drives the plot of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* from beginning to end. Without the obligation of revenge, there is no narrative — only passive suffering.
- **Character definition**: [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s capacity for calculated revenge distinguishes him from both the impulsive suitors and the passive [[telemachus|Telemachus]] of Book 1. His revenge is not hot-blooded rage but patient, strategic justice.
- **Theological resolution**: The poem's ending — divine intervention halting the blood-feud — offers a vision of justice beyond endless retribution. The gods enforce a limit on poine that human society cannot.
