# Aegisthus

## Identity & Names

Aegisthus (Greek: Αἴγισθος, _Aigisthos_) is the son of Thyestes and the lover of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], wife of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]. Together they conspire to murder Agamemnon upon his return from Troy — an act that establishes the *Odyssey*'s central moral paradigm. He never appears directly in the poem but is invoked repeatedly as the archetype of the usurper whom divine warning cannot save.

The name Aegisthus is inextricable from the poem's opening ethical argument, where [[zeus|Zeus]] cites his fate as proof that mortals bring destruction upon themselves: "Aegisthus' fate revolving in his breast" [001-055].

## Role & Significance

Aegisthus serves as the *Odyssey*'s primary negative exemplar — the figure whose story defines what [[odysseus|Odysseus]] must avoid. His role operates on three intertwined levels:

1. **As moral parable**: Zeus's opening meditation on Aegisthus establishes the poem's foundational claim — "they miscall'd the crimes of fate" [001-061] — that human suffering arises from human choice, not divine caprice. Aegisthus was warned by [[hermes|Hermes]] not to kill Agamemnon and court Clytemnestra, yet he persisted, and so perished.

2. **As narrative foil**: Where Odysseus's _nostos_ (homecoming) ends in reunion and the restoration of order, Agamemnon's — destroyed by Aegisthus — ends in bath-murder. The *Odyssey* plays these two homecomings against each other throughout, with Aegisthus's crime as the hinge.

3. **As paradigm of violated _dike_ (justice)**: The kill chain — Aegisthus murders Agamemnon, [[orestes|Orestes]] kills Aegisthus — is the poem's model of retributive justice, cited repeatedly (Books 1, 3, 4, 11, 24) as the template for Telemachus's own destiny.

The [[moira|Moira]] concept page frames Aegisthus as the epic's case study in moral agency: fate sets boundaries, but within those boundaries humans choose and are held accountable.

## Genealogy and the Curse of Atreus

Aegisthus was born into the most cursed house in Greek mythology. His father Thyestes was the brother of Atreus, and the enmity between them — involving adultery, cannibalism (Atreus fed Thyestes his own children), and exile — set the stage for the entire Trojan War cycle. Aegisthus himself was conceived as an instrument of vengeance: an oracle prophesied that a son born of Thyestes's incestuous union with his own daughter Pelopia would avenge the house. That child was Aegisthus.

In the Homeric telling, the backstory is compressed to its essentials — Aegisthus is simply "Thyestes' son" — but the weight of the house of Atreus hangs over every mention of his name. His murder of Agamemnon is not merely personal ambition but the culmination of a multi-generational curse that the poem treats as the dark counterpoint to Odysseus's blessed homecoming.

## The Crime

### The Warning

[[hermes|Hermes]] delivered [[zeus|Zeus]]'s explicit warning: do not kill Agamemnon, do not court Clytemnestra, for "young [[orestes|Orestes]], grown / To manly prime, should one day claim his own" [001-056, 001-068]. Aegisthus ignored the warning. The poem's emphasis on this foreknowledge is crucial — it removes any possibility of exculpation through ignorance. Aegisthus knew the cost and chose anyway.

### The Murder

Aegisthus and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] murdered Agamemnon at a homecoming banquet — an act of supreme violation of *xenia*, murdering a guest on his own hearth. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s shade describes the killing in the Underworld: "as one kills an ox at the manger" (Book 11). The simile reduces the king of men to slaughtered livestock, emphasizing both the brutality and the perversion of domestic space — the feast becomes a murder scene, the wife becomes a killer.

With Agamemnon dead, Aegisthus seized the throne of [[mycenae|Mycenae]] and reigned seven years. His usurpation establishes the pattern that [[the-suitors|the Suitors]] will later replicate in [[ithaca|Ithaca]].

### The Reckoning

In the eighth year, [[orestes|Orestes]] returned and killed Aegisthus. His death is memorialized as both justice and fame: [[athena|Athena]] holds him up to [[telemachus|Telemachus]] as the model of filial action in Book 1, and [[nestor|Nestor]] retells the story at length in Book 3. The timing — seven years of rule, destroyed in the eighth — mirrors the *Odyssey*'s own temporal structure, where Odysseus's seven years with Calypso end with his departure in the eighth.

## Relationships

- **Father**: Thyestes, of the cursed house of Atreus — the generational curse flows through this lineage
- **Lover**: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], Agamemnon's wife — their adulterous union drives the tragedy
- **Victim**: [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], "king of men," murdered at his homecoming
- **Avenger**: [[orestes|Orestes]], Agamemnon's son, who kills Aegisthus after seven years — held up as the model for [[telemachus|Telemachus]]
- **Narrative witness**: [[zeus|Zeus]], [[athena|Athena]], [[nestor|Nestor]], [[menelaus|Menelaus]], and the shade of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] himself all recount his crime
- **Parallel figure**: [[the-suitors|The Suitors]], whose usurpation of Odysseus's household mirrors Aegisthus's seizure of Mycenae — both groups ignore divine warnings and pay with their lives

## The Thematic Architecture

The Aegisthus story supplies the *Odyssey* with its master narrative structure:

| Element | Aegisthus-Agamemnon | Suitors-Odysseus |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Usurper | Aegisthus | [[the-suitors|The Suitors]] |
| Faithful wife? | No — Clytemnestra conspires | Yes — [[penelope|Penelope]] resists |
| Returning king | Agamemnon (murdered) | [[odysseus|Odysseus]] (triumphant) |
| Avenging son | [[orestes|Orestes]] (acts decisively) | [[telemachus|Telemachus]] (comes of age) |
| Divine warning | Ignored by Aegisthus | Ignored by suitors |
| Outcome | Tragedy | Restoration |

This parallel structure is the poem's most elaborate narrative device — every mention of Agamemnon's fate is an implicit question: will Ithaca follow Mycenae? The answer, established at the poem's opening and confirmed at its close, is no: [[penelope|Penelope]] is not Clytemnestra, [[telemachus|Telemachus]] is Orestes-in-training, and [[odysseus|Odysseus]] will not be another Agamemnon.
