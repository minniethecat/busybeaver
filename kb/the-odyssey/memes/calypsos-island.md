# Calypso's Island

## Summary

The detention on [[ogygia|Ogygia]] is the longest and most emotionally complex episode of [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s wanderings. After losing all his men and ships, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is held for seven years by the nymph [[calypso|Calypso]] — "[[calypso|Calypso]] in her caves constrain'd his stay" (001-038) — who loves him and offers immortality if he remains. The episode opens with [[athena|Athena]]'s complaint to [[zeus|Zeus]], who dispatches [[hermes|Hermes]] to command [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s release. [[calypso|Calypso]], though unwilling — "And press'd unwilling in [[calypso|Calypso]]'s arms" (005-034) — obeys divine will. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] builds a raft of pine, departs after a final night with the goddess, and is nearly destroyed by [[poseidon|Poseidon]]'s storm before reaching [[scheria|Scheria]].

## Context & Causes

The episode takes place against the backdrop of the divine council that opens the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*. [[athena|Athena]], in Book 1, complains to [[zeus|Zeus]] that [[odysseus|Odysseus]] languishes on [[ogygia|Ogygia]] while the suitors devour his estate. [[zeus|Zeus]] acknowledges her claim and, in Book 5, sends [[hermes|Hermes]] — "Him, entering in the cave, [[calypso|Calypso]] knew" (005-109) — to command the nymph's compliance. The detention itself has no narrative cause beyond [[calypso|Calypso]]'s desire: she rescued the shipwrecked [[odysseus|Odysseus]] and, "with all her charms" (009-044), sought to make him immortal and her eternal consort.

## Key Moments

1. **The [[the-council-of-the-gods|Divine Council]]**: [[athena|Athena]]'s complaint to [[zeus|Zeus]] sets the rescue in motion. Her rhetorical strategy — "Let kings no more with gentle mercy sway" — frames [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s suffering as a cosmic injustice demanding divine correction.

2. **[[hermes|Hermes]]'s Journey**: The messenger god flies to [[ogygia|Ogygia]], a remote island of lush beauty: groves, meadows, a vine-covered cave, and four fountains. [[calypso|Calypso]] receives him with divine hospitality before hearing his unwelcome command.

3. **[[calypso|Calypso]]'s Complaint**: The goddess bitterly protests the double standard of the gods — goddesses may not keep mortal lovers, but [[zeus|Zeus]] and the other gods do so freely. Yet she submits, acknowledging [[zeus|Zeus]]'s supremacy.

4. **The Raft-Building**: [[odysseus|Odysseus]], distrusting [[calypso|Calypso]]'s promise of release, extracts an oath from her. Over four days, he builds a raft from twenty pine trees — "a vessel" built "with his own hands" — provisioned by the goddess.

5. **Departure and Storm**: On the eighteenth day at sea, [[poseidon|Poseidon]] spots the raft and unleashes a storm that destroys it. Leucothea's veil and [[athena|Athena]]'s guidance save [[odysseus|Odysseus]] from drowning, and after two days swimming, he reaches the shores of [[scheria|Scheria]].

## Aftermath & Consequences

The [[calypso|Calypso]] episode is the pivot of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*'s narrative structure. [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s seven years on [[ogygia|Ogygia]] constitute the longest single period in the poem's chronology — longer than the Trojan War itself. [[calypso|Calypso]]'s offer of immortality represents the ultimate temptation: the complete erasure of mortal identity and the nostos that defines it. [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s refusal — he weeps daily on the shore, longing for [[ithaca|Ithaca]] — defines his character more sharply than any combat. The raft he builds is the first craft he has commanded since losing his last ship; it marks the transition from passive sufferer to active agent in his own return.
