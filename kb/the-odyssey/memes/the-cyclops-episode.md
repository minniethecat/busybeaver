# The Cyclops Episode

## Summary

The Cyclops episode (Book 9) is the most famous single adventure in the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* — and the one that determines the entire poem's trajectory. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] and twelve of his crew enter the cave of [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]], a one-eyed giant son of [[poseidon|Poseidon]], seeking [[xenia]]. Instead, [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] traps them, eats six men over two days, and imprisons the rest.

> Whose visual orb Ulysses robb'd of light [001-107]

[[odysseus|Odysseus]] executes a masterful escape plan: he gets [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] drunk on strong wine, tells him his name is "Nobody" (Οὖτις), and when the giant sleeps, drives a sharpened olive stake into his single eye. The crew escapes by clinging to the undersides of [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]]'s sheep. Safely at sea, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] commits his fatal error — boasting of his deed and revealing his true name, enabling [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] to curse him by name to [[poseidon|Poseidon]]. The ten years of wandering follow from this single act of [[hubris]].

## Context & Causes

The episode follows [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s departure from [[troy|Troy]] and the failed raid on the [[the-cicones|Cicones]]. The crew, seeking provisions, enters the cave on [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s initiative — his characteristic curiosity overriding caution. The Cyclops's violation of [[xenia]] — eating guests rather than feeding them — is the episode's moral catalyst, making his blinding a legitimate act of retribution within the epic's value system. The Cyclopes are depicted as a pre-social people: they have no assemblies, no laws, no agriculture, and notably no practice of xenia. [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] is not merely a monster but a cultural antithesis — the anti-host, the anti-citizen.

## Key Moments

### 1. The Trap

[[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] rolls a massive stone across the cave entrance — "a rock so huge that two-and-twenty four-wheeled wagons could not move it" — and immediately eats two men, dashing their brains out and devouring them "like a mountain lion." [[odysseus|Odysseus]] cannot kill him in his sleep because no one could move the stone. He must find a way to make [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] open the door himself.

### 2. The "Nobody" Ruse

This is the paradigmatic act of [[metis]] in Western literature. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] gives his name as Οὖτις (Outis / Nobody), a linguistic trick that weaponizes language itself. When the other Cyclopes hear [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]]'s screams and ask who is hurting him, his answer — "Nobody is killing me by craft, not force" — causes them to leave, assuming divine affliction. The pun works in Greek: *mē tis* ("no one") echoes *mētis* ("cunning intelligence"), folding the hero's defining quality into the very word that saves him.

### 3. The Blinding

The sharpened olive stake, heated in the fire until glowing, driven into the giant's single eye. The simile Homer uses — comparing the hissing sound to a blacksmith plunging hot iron into water — emphasizes the violence while linking it to craft and civilization: the same fire that cooks food and forges tools becomes a weapon.

### 4. The Escape

The crew clings to the bellies of [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]]'s sheep as he feels only their backs, letting them pass. This is metis in its purest form: using the enemy's own routine (the daily release of the flock) as the vehicle of escape. Where brute force would fail against the stone, intelligence finds the gap.

### 5. The Boast

From the safety of his ship, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] shouts his true name and lineage:

> If any mortal man inquire how you were blinded, say Odysseus, sacker of cities, son of Laertes, who makes his home in Ithaca

This is the turning point of the entire epic. Until this moment, the escape is perfect metis. The boast transforms a clean getaway into a cosmic catastrophe. [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] recalls an old prophecy — that he would be blinded by [[odysseus|Odysseus]] — and prays to [[poseidon|Poseidon]]: grant that [[odysseus|Odysseus]] never reach home, or if he must, let him arrive late, alone, on a stranger's ship, and find trouble in his house. [[poseidon|Poseidon]] grants this prayer, and every subsequent adventure follows from this single act of pride.

## Aftermath & Consequences

The boast is the fulcrum on which the entire *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* turns. The sequence exemplifies the Homeric chain of causation:

```
metis (cunning escape) → hubris (the boast) → menis (divine wrath) → nostos deferred
```

Specifically:
- **Metis**: The "Nobody" trick, the wine, the stake, the sheep-belly escape — all perfect intelligence.
- **Hubris**: The boast reveals his name, his deed, his lineage, and his home — giving [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]] everything needed for an effective curse.
- **Menis**: [[poseidon|Poseidon]]'s wrath, "unappeasable," pursues [[odysseus|Odysseus]] across the seas for ten years.
- **Nostos deferred**: Every subsequent adventure — [[aeolus|Aeolus]], the [[telepylus|Laestrygonia]]ns, [[circe|Circe]], the Underworld, the Sirens, [[scylla-and-charybdis|Scylla and Charybdis]], the Cattle of [[helios|the Sun]] — follows from this moment.

Without the boast, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] might have sailed home within weeks. The Cyclops episode is therefore not just one adventure among many — it is the narrative origin of the entire poem.
