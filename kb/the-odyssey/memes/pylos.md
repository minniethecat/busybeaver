# Pylos

## Description

Pylos is the kingdom of Nestor, the aged king of the Pylians, located on the southwestern coast of the Peloponnese. In the *Odyssey*, it is the first destination of Telemachus's journey and the poem's primary model of a well-ordered kingdom ruled by a pious and hospitable king. The city is described as "sandy Pylos," with a long beach where the Pylians offer a massive sacrifice of ninety black bulls to Poseidon — the scene that greets Telemachus and Athena (as Mentor) upon their arrival. Nestor's palace is a place of dignified abundance, where hospitality is offered before questions are asked, in perfect accordance with the laws of xenia. "The youth of Pylos, some on pointed wood" (003-059) captures the industrious peace of a kingdom that returned safely from Troy.

## In the Odyssey

**Book 3 — Telemachus's Arrival**: Telemachus and Athena (disguised as Mentor) arrive at Pylos at dawn to find the entire population on the beach, sacrificing black bulls to Poseidon. Nestor and his sons welcome the strangers before asking their names, demonstrating the ideal of xenia. Nestor recounts the returns of the Achaeans from Troy — the quarrel between Agamemnon and Menelaus, the separate departures, and the fates of various heroes — but can offer no news of Odysseus. He sends his youngest son Pisistratus to accompany Telemachus overland to Sparta.

**Book 15 — Telemachus's Return**: On his return from Sparta, Telemachus stops briefly at Pylos. He declines Nestor's insistence on extended hospitality — a sign of his new decisiveness — and sails directly for Ithaca, asking only that Nestor's son Pisistratus be allowed to accompany him to the ship. This brief scene marks Telemachus's transformation: he now controls the pace of his own journey.

## Significance

Pylos represents the ideal of post-Trojan-War kingship. Nestor returned safely from Troy, rules a peaceful and prosperous kingdom, and has raised sons who emulate his piety. His hospitality to Telemachus is flawless, and his stories of the war provide the youth with his first direct knowledge of his father's world. Pylos also embodies the poem's generational theme: the aged Nestor, who can only tell stories of the past, passes the torch to the young Pisistratus, who accompanies Telemachus into the future. The kingdom's peace and order form a pointed contrast with the chaos of suitor-dominated Ithaca.

## Geography

Pylos is identifiable with the real archaeological site at Ano Englianos in Messenia, southwestern Greece, where a large Mycenaean palace (the so-called "Palace of Nestor") was excavated by Carl Blegen in 1939. The site includes a throne room with a central hearth, storerooms with thousands of clay tablets in Linear B, and evidence of a wealthy and well-organized kingdom. The "sandy" beach described in the epic corresponds to the long shoreline of Navarino Bay.
