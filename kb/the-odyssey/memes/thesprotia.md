> "In rich Thesprotia, and the nearer bound / Of Thesprotian tribes, a duteous race, obey." [019-322]

## Geography and Historical Identity

Thesprotia is a coastal region of Epirus, located on the northwestern mainland of Greece, facing the Ionian Sea and the island of Corcyra (modern Corfu). In the historical period, the Thesprotians were one of the three principal Epirote tribes (alongside the Molossians and Chaonians), known to Herodotus and Thucydides. The region's strategic position — between the Adriatic and the Greek heartland — made it a natural waypoint for seaborne travel between the western islands and the mainland.

In the *Odyssey*, it is portrayed as a wealthy kingdom ruled by King Pheidon, rich in treasure and hospitable to travellers:

> "Thesprotian tribes, a duteous race, obey." [019-343]

The region is positioned as a waypoint between the western islands and the Greek mainland, making it a plausible destination for seafaring wanderers. Its coastal geography and proximity to Ithaca (roughly 80 miles across the Ionian Sea) give the false tales a veneer of geographical credibility that an entirely fictional destination would lack.

## In the Odyssey

Thesprotia appears exclusively within the false tales that [[odysseus|Odysseus]] tells during his disguised return to Ithaca. Like [[crete|Crete]], it serves as a setting for the elaborate stories Odysseus constructs to test the loyalty of his household. But whereas Crete functions as his claimed *homeland* in the tales, Thesprotia functions as a *transit point* — a place where Odysseus (in the fabrication) passed through, leaving behind news of himself that the disguised beggar now relays.

### Book 14 — The First Tale to Eumaeus

In his narrative to the swineherd [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]], Odysseus (as the beggar) claims that after his ship was driven off course, he landed in Thesprotia after ten days at sea:

> "The tenth soft wafts me to Thesprotia's shore." [014-358]

The beggar claims that the Thesprotian king received him with hospitality and gave him news of Odysseus himself — that Odysseus had recently passed through the region, having gathered rich treasure, and was now consulting the oracle at Dodona. This detail is carefully chosen: Dodona was the oldest and most venerable oracle of [[zeus|Zeus]] in the Greek world, located in Epirus near Thesprotia, and a man consulting Zeus about his homecoming would be entirely plausible.

### Book 16 — Telemachus's Retelling

[[telemachus|Telemachus]] briefly refers to Thesprotia when relaying what he learned from the disguised Odysseus to [[penelope|Penelope]], mentioning the Taphian pirates operating near its shores:

> "The Taphian pirates on Thesprotia's shores." [016-453]

### Book 17 — The Second Tale

Odysseus retells the story in the palace, again emphasizing the wealth gathered in Thesprotia:

> "With boundless treasure, from Thesprotia's shore." [017-624]

### Book 19 — The Tale to Penelope

In his most detailed account to [[penelope|Penelope]], Odysseus locates Thesprotia as the place where he last heard reliable news of her husband. He claims Odysseus had gone to Dodona and that the Thesprotians had given him lavish gifts. The detail of *treasure* recurs in every telling — a strategic choice, since the beggar's familiarity with wealth and gift-exchange signals to his audience that he is a man of substance, not a mere vagrant.

## Narrative Function

Thesprotia, like [[crete|Crete]], functions as a narrative device in Odysseus's disguise plot. However, the two locations serve distinct functions in the fabrication architecture:

| Location | Function in the Tales | Emotional Weight |
|----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **[[crete|Crete]]** | Odysseus's claimed *homeland* in the false tales | Establishes a false identity and personal history; allows Odysseus to claim warrior status |
| **Thesprotia** | A *transit point* where Odysseus was last seen | Provides a plausible recent sighting; bridges the gap between Troy and Ithaca |

The choice of Thesprotia is narratively strategic on multiple levels. As a real region of Epirus adjacent to Ithaca's broader geography, it lends credibility to the tale — the audience in Ithaca would know of Thesprotia as a real place, not a mythical realm. Its proximity to the oracle at Dodona also explains why Odysseus (in the fabrication) would have gone there — to consult Zeus about his homecoming, a motive entirely in character for the piety-conscious hero.

The recurring motif of "treasure" associated with Thesprotia in these tales subtly reinforces the beggar's claim to recognize wealth and quality, preparing the household for the eventual revelation of Odysseus's own treasure — and, by extension, his true identity.

## Significance

Thesprotia exemplifies the *Odyssey*'s sophisticated use of real geography within fictional narrative. Unlike the fantastical locations of the wanderings (the Cyclops's cave, Circe's island, the Underworld), Thesprotia belongs to the known world — it can be found on a map. By embedding the false tales in real, verifiable geography, Odysseus makes the incredible credible. The listener thinks: "If the Thesprotian king exists, and Dodona exists, then perhaps this beggar's story is true."
