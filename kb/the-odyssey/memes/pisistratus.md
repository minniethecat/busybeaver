# Pisistratus

Pisistratus is the youngest son of Nestor, the aged king of Pylos, and a loyal companion to Telemachus during the Telemachy. His presence in the narrative embodies the Homeric virtue of *xenia* — the sacred bond of hospitality and guest-friendship — as he serves as guide, host, and friend to Odysseus's son.

## Identity & Names

The name Pisistratus (Greek: Πεισιστρατος, *Peisistratos*) means "persuader of the army" or "he who persuades the host." In Pope's translation, he is rendered consistently as "Pisistratus." He is distinguished from the later Athenian tyrant of the same name; here he is purely a Homeric figure, defined by his filial piety and his role as Telemachus's travelling companion.

## Role & Significance

Pisistratus is a mortal prince of Pylos, the youngest of Nestor's sons [003-015]. His primary narrative function is threefold:

1. **Host and guide**: He receives Telemachus at Pylos, extending the hospitality for which Nestor's house is renowned. He is the first to grasp Telemachus's hand in welcome [003-063].
2. **Travelling companion**: He accompanies Telemachus on the journey to Sparta to visit Menelaus and Helen, driving the chariot and sharing the road [003-528], [004-004].
3. **Diplomatic intermediary**: He speaks on Telemachus's behalf at the Spartan court, interpreting his companion's grief and facilitating the exchange of information about Odysseus [004-221].

Throughout the journey, Pisistratus demonstrates the tact, piety, and courtesy befitting a prince raised in Nestor's household. He sleeps beside Telemachus [003-528], rises last from rest [003-544], and gently wakes Telemachus when it is time to depart [015-064].

## Appearances

### Book 3 — Arrival at Pylos

Pisistratus first appears when Telemachus arrives at Pylos, where Nestor and his sons are offering sacrifice to Poseidon. He is the first to extend hospitality:

> "Pisistratus was first to grasp their hand..." [003-063]

That night, he sleeps beside Telemachus [003-528] and is the last to rise the following morning [003-544]. He serves as Telemachus's faithful guide [003-627].

### Book 4 — Journey to Sparta

Pisistratus accompanies Telemachus to Sparta, where they are received by Menelaus and Helen [004-004]. At the Spartan court, he speaks eloquently on Telemachus's behalf:

> "See there confess'd," Pisistratus replies,
> "The gen'rous youth demands my native strain..." [004-221]

Menelaus observes the tears on Pisistratus's cheeks as he hears of Odysseus's fate [004-265].

### Book 15 — Return to Pylos

Pisistratus and Telemachus take leave of Menelaus and return toward Pylos [015-005]. He gently wakes Telemachus at dawn, urging their departure [015-064]. When Telemachus requests to sail directly to Ithaca rather than return to Pylos, Pisistratus helps persuade Nestor to agree to this change of plan [015-231].

## Relationships

### Family

- **Father**: Nestor, the aged and wise king of Pylos [003-007]. Pisistratus is the youngest of Nestor's sons [003-015].
- **Brothers**: Thrasymedes and others among Nestor's royal line [003-087]. Pisistratus is described as Nestor's youngest, who stops the vents of age [003-595].

### Allies

- **Telemachus**: The strongest bond in the narrative. Pisistratus serves as guide, host, and friend to Odysseus's son throughout the Telemachy. Their companionship models ideal guest-friendship.
- **Nestor**: Revered by his son; Pisistratus fulfills the filial duty expected of a Homeric prince.

### Divine Connections

As a mortal prince of Pylos, Pisistratus participates in the sacrifices to Poseidon that frame his first appearance [003-007], but he has no direct divine patronage or antagonism in the narrative.

## Analysis

### Character Arc

Pisistratus has no developmental arc of his own; he functions as a **foil and facilitator** for Telemachus. His ease in noble company, his practiced piety, and his instinct for hospitality highlight Telemachus's own growth from hesitant youth to confident prince. Where Telemachus initially hesitates, Pisistratus acts — a contrast that dramatizes Telemachus's transformation over the course of the journey.

### Thematic Significance

Pisistratus embodies **xenia** (guest-friendship), the sacred code of hospitality that structures the Odyssey's moral universe. His immediate welcome of Telemachus [003-063], his willingness to travel alongside a stranger, and his tactful speech at the Spartan court all exemplify the ideal host and companion. His character serves as a positive counterpoint to the suitors' violation of xenia in Ithaca.

He also represents **filial piety** — the loyalty of a son to his father — which resonates with Telemachus's own quest to learn of Odysseus. Pisistratus is what Telemachus might have been had his father never left: secure in his patrimony, confident in noble society, at ease with the rituals of hospitality and sacrifice.

### Placement in the Epic

Pisistratus appears exclusively in Books 3, 4, and 15 — the Telemachy. His disappearance from the narrative after Book 15 is unremarked but fitting: once Telemachus returns to Ithaca and the main Odysseus plot resumes, the guide figure of the journey narrative is no longer needed.
