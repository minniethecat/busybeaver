# Orion

**Orion** is a giant hunter of Greek mythology, famous for his size, beauty, and tragic death. In the _Odyssey_, he appears twice: once in a simile about the dawn, and once among the great shades Odysseus sees in the Underworld.

## Role in the Odyssey

Orion first appears in a dawn simile in Book 5: "So when Aurora sought Orion's love" (005-165), referencing the myth that Eos (Dawn) fell in love with the handsome hunter. Later, in Book 11, Odysseus describes the constellation: "And great Orion's more refulgent beam" (005-358), a star by which sailors navigate.

In the Underworld (Book 11), Odysseus sees Orion among the shades: "None but Orion e'er surpassed their height" (011-394). Homer depicts him as a colossal figure still pursuing his earthly occupation — hunting beasts across the asphodel meadow with his bronze club.

## Mythological Background

Orion was a Boeotian hunter of extraordinary size and beauty. Various myths explain his death: some say Artemis killed him, others that he was stung by a scorpion sent by Gaia. After death, he was placed among the stars as the constellation Orion, the brightest in the night sky.

> "And great Orion's more refulgent beam, / To the pale moon, a silver light supply" (005-358)
