# Ajax

The name Ajax in the Odyssey refers to two distinct Achaean heroes of the Trojan War: **Ajax the Great** (Telamonian Ajax), the towering warrior second only to Achilles in strength, and **Ajax the Lesser** (Oilean Ajax), whose impious boast after the fall of Troy led to his death at sea. Both appear as shades in Odysseus's Underworld journey, each embodying different aspects of the Achaeans' tragic homecomings.

## Identity & Names

The name Ajax (Greek: Αιας, *Aias*) is borne by two heroes in the Homeric corpus:

- **Ajax the Great**, son of Telamon, is described as "Ajax great" [003-149] and "towering Ajax, an illustrious shade" [011-592]. He was the bulwark of the Achaean army, renowned for his massive shield and unyielding defense.
- **Ajax the Lesser**, son of Oileus, is the "Oilean Ajax" who met his death on the Gyraean rocks [004-683] after boasting that he had escaped the sea against the gods' will.

Pope distinguishes them consistently by their patronymics and epithets.

## Role & Significance

### Ajax the Great

The greater Ajax is a tragic figure in the Odyssey. His death — by suicide after losing the contest for Achilles's armor to Odysseus — is never narrated directly, but his shade's behavior in the Underworld speaks volumes. When Odysseus encounters him among the dead, Ajax keeps "at a sullen distance" [011-011], refusing to speak to the man who won the armor he believed rightfully his:

> "And towering Ajax, an illustrious shade!" [011-592]

> "A gloomy shade the sullen Ajax stood" [011-680]

His silent anger is one of the most powerful moments in the Nekyia — a wordless rebuke that Odysseus's cunning, which saved the Achaeans so often, also destroyed one of their greatest warriors.

### Ajax the Lesser

The lesser Ajax appears in Nestor's and Menelaus's accounts of the returns. He survived the war only to die at sea for his impiety:

> "On Gyrae, safe Oilean Ajax sate" [004-683]

Poseidon saved him from the rocks, but Ajax boasted that he had escaped "against the gods' will" — a blasphemy for which Poseidon split the rock with his trident, drowning him. His fate exemplifies the theme of divinely punished hubris.

## Appearances

### Book 3 — Nestor's Account

Nestor mentions Ajax among the Achaean heroes who fell at Troy, pairing him with Achilles: "There Ajax great, Achilles there the brave" [003-149].

### Book 4 — Menelaus's Account

Menelaus recounts the death of Oilean Ajax on the Gyraean rocks [004-683].

### Book 11 — The Underworld

Odysseus encounters both:
- Ajax the Great's shade at a sullen distance [011-011], [011-592], [011-680]
- He attempts to speak with him, but Ajax refuses — the only shade in the entire Nekyia to reject Odysseus's approach.

### Book 24 — The Second Nekyia

The suitors' shades find Ajax and Achilles among the illustrious dead [024-035].

## Relationships

### Ajax the Great

- **Achilles**: His closest peer in martial prowess; their shades appear together [003-149], [024-035].
- **Odysseus**: The rival who won Achilles's armor, provoking Ajax's suicide. Their Underworld encounter is defined by Ajax's silent, implacable anger.
- **Telamon**: His father, king of Salamis.

### Ajax the Lesser

- **Poseidon**: The god who first saves, then destroys him for his blasphemy [004-683].
- **Athena**: Whose anger at the Achaeans' sacrilege during the sack of Troy extends to Oilean Ajax.

## Analysis

### The Silent Shade

Ajax the Great's refusal to speak in the Underworld [011-680] is one of the most psychologically acute moments in Homer. Odysseus's charm — the eloquence that has persuaded kings, goddesses, and monsters — fails before Ajax's silence. The moment acknowledges that some wounds cannot be healed by words, some wrongs cannot be righted by apology. It is a rare admission, within the Odyssey's triumphal narrative, of the irreparable damage Odysseus's cunning has caused.

### Two Deaths, One Lesson

The deaths of the two Ajaxes represent complementary forms of tragic excess. Ajax the Great dies of wounded honor — too much pride. Ajax the Lesser dies of impious boast — too little reverence. Together they map the boundaries of acceptable heroic conduct: courage must be tempered by piety, and honor must not become self-destruction.

### The Armor of Achilles

The unspoken center of Ajax the Great's tragedy — the contest for Achilles's armor — is never narrated in the Odyssey, only alluded to. Homer trusts his audience to know the story, and by keeping it in the background, he makes Ajax's silence more powerful. What happened at Troy is over; what remains is the permanent, silent estrangement between two men who once fought side by side.
