# Melantho

Melantho is a serving-woman in Penelope's household who, like her brother Melanthius, betrays her absent master. Raised by Penelope as a favorite, she repays the queen's kindness with ingratitude — becoming the mistress of Eurymachus and insulting Odysseus when he appears in beggar's disguise.

## Identity & Names

The name Melantho (Greek: Μελανθω, *Melantho*) shares its root (*melas*, black/dark) with her brother Melanthius, marking them both as morally "dark" figures in the household. She is "fair and young" [018-374], and Pope emphasizes her beauty as a counterpoint to her corruption:

> "Melantho, fair and young,
> (Melantho, from the loins of Dolius sprung)" [018-374-375]

Her lineage from Dolius — the loyal servant — makes her betrayal particularly damning.

## Role & Significance

Melantho is a mortal serving-woman of Ithaca. Her role in the narrative is to demonstrate that the corruption of the suitors has penetrated even the intimate spaces of Penelope's household:

1. **Betrayer of the queen's trust**: Raised by Penelope "like a child" [018-374], she repays maternal care with treachery.
2. **Agent of the suitors' corruption**: As Eurymachus's mistress, she embodies the suitors' desecration of the household — not merely consuming its wealth but corrupting its servants.
3. **Female counterpart to Melanthius**: Together, brother and sister represent the complete failure of Dolius's line in two of his eight children.

## Appearances

### Book 18 — Insulting the Beggar

Melantho first appears when Odysseus, in his beggar disguise, encounters her in the palace:

> "Scornful they heard: Melantho, fair and young" [018-374]

She insults him with "imperious mien" [019-091], mocking his appearance and ordering him out of the hall. Odysseus responds with a veiled threat — the master will return, and those who have betrayed his house will pay.

### Book 19 — Second Confrontation

Melantho insults Odysseus again [019-091], and again he warns her. Her repeated insolence marks her as incorrigible — unlike Amphinomus, who at least hears and is troubled by Odysseus's warning, Melantho dismisses it entirely.

## Relationships

### Family

- **Father**: Dolius, the loyal aged servant, whose faithfulness Melantho dishonors [018-375].
- **Brother**: Melanthius, the treacherous goatherd, who shares her fate.
- **Six loyal brothers**: The other sons of Dolius who remain faithful, making Melantho and Melanthius the dark exceptions in an otherwise loyal family [024-584].

### Allies

- **Eurymachus**: Her lover, the smooth-tongued suitor. Their relationship is a microcosm of the suitors' corruption — the master's servant becomes the usurper's mistress.
- **The Suitors**: She aligns herself with the faction that destroys her master's house.

### Enemies

- **Odysseus**: The master she insults and betrays, failing to recognize.
- **Penelope**: The queen who raised her "like a child" and whom she repays with disloyalty [018-374].

## Analysis

### The Corrupted Ward

Melantho's betrayal is uniquely painful because it violates the foster-bond. Penelope raised her, gave her "trinkets and sweetmeats," treated her as a daughter. Melantho's response — to become the mistress of a man who consumes Penelope's household — is not mere disloyalty but a kind of familial treason. In the Homeric moral universe, ingratitude to a benefactor is among the gravest sins.

### The Female Traitor

As Melanthius's female counterpart, Melantho demonstrates that the corruption of Ithaca was not gendered. Just as some men (Eumaeus, Philoetius) remained loyal while others (Melanthius) betrayed, so among the women — Eurycleia remained faithful while Melantho and her fellow serving-women became the suitors' accomplices. The epic insists on individual moral responsibility regardless of status or sex.

### Unfinished Arc

Melantho's fate after the slaughter is not individually narrated. She is presumably among the unfaithful serving-women whom Odysseus forces to clean the gore-spattered hall before hanging them [Book 22]. Her story's abrupt conclusion — she disappears from the text after Book 19 — mirrors the suddenness with which the vengeance falls: one moment she is insulting a beggar, the next, the beggar is king and she is dead.
