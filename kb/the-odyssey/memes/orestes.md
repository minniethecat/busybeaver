# Orestes

**Orestes**, son of Agamemnon and Clytemnestra, is the *Odyssey*'s central paradigm of filial action. After his father was murdered by Aegisthus upon returning from Troy, Orestes avenged the crime — a deed that earns him immortal fame throughout the epic. His story is recounted multiple times as an explicit model for Telemachus, who must decide whether to passively endure the suitors' depredations or take decisive action to reclaim his household.

## The Crime

Agamemnon's murder at the hands of Aegisthus and Clytemnestra is described early in the poem. The gods' council in Book 1 establishes the parallel: as Aegisthus was warned by Hermes not to kill Agamemnon nor court his wife, "for young Orestes, grown / To manly prime, should one day claim his own" (001-056, 001-068), so too the suitors are warned through Telemachus' potential for action.

The geography of the crime — Agamemnon slain at his homecoming feast — is grim: "Whom young Orestes to the dreary coast / Of ruthless Pluto sent" (001-056), establishing Orestes' act as a descent into the underworld's justice.

## The Model of Action

Athena herself invokes Orestes to inspire Telemachus: "Hast thou not heard how young Orestes, fired / With great revenge, the murderous pair expired?" (001-404). The goddess frames Orestes' deed as a source of *kleos* (glory) that reached all nations, explicitly urging Telemachus to emulate this model.

Nestor reinforces the message in Book 3, telling Telemachus "And be what brave Orestes was before!" (003-261). The old king presents Orestes as proof that a son, even one young and initially powerless, can restore order through determined action.

The avenging act itself is recalled with vivid finality: "Orestes brandish'd the avenging sword" (003-407), a single line that encapsulates the transition from suffering to justice.

## In the Underworld

When Odysseus encounters Agamemnon's shade in Book 11, the murdered king laments that he died without seeing his son: "But my Orestes never met these eyes" (011-573). Agamemnon's grief at missing Orestes' maturation and vengeance adds poignancy to the father-son reunion that Odysseus will eventually achieve with Telemachus.

## Contrast with Telemachus

| Dimension | Orestes | Telemachus |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| Father's fate | Murdered at homecoming | Lost at sea, fate unknown |
| Mother's role | Complicit in murder | Faithful, besieged by suitors |
| Initial position | Exiled, powerless | Young, untested, in his own home |
| Action taken | Kills usurpers | Must be urged toward action |
| Outcome | Avenger, celebrated | Hero-in-training |

The *Odyssey* uses Orestes not to diminish Telemachus but to provide a roadmap: filial action is possible, glorious, and divinely sanctioned. Telemachus' journey is, in part, the story of his growth into an Orestes-like capacity for decisive action.

## Significance

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Paradigmatic role | The exemplary avenging son, a narrative foil for Telemachus |
| Moral framework | Demonstrates that divine justice operates through human agents |
| Narrative structure | Repeated invocation (Books 1, 3, 4, 11) creates a thematic refrain |
