The Spring of Arethusa is a natural freshwater source on [[ithaca|Ithaca]], referenced in the Odyssey as a recognizable landmark near Eumaeus's territory. Its "sable water" — dark, clear, and cool — makes it a gathering place for flocks and a site of rustic life on the island.

## In the Odyssey

### A Landmark of Ithaca (Book XIII)

Athene, when revealing the landmarks of Ithaca to [[odysseus|Odysseus]], points out the spring among the island's distinctive features:

> "Where Arethusa's sable water glides" [013-NA]

The epithet "sable" (dark, black) characterizes the spring's water as deep and shadowed — perhaps shaded by overhanging foliage or darkened by depth and minerals. In the epic's geography, the spring is positioned in the vicinity of Neritus (the island's central mountain) and the Harbor of Phorcys, forming part of the recognizable topography of Ithaca's eastern coast.

### Eumaeus's Territory (Book XVII)

In Book XVII, the spring is situated within the pastoral landscape where [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]] the swineherd tends his flocks. The spring serves as a watering place for livestock and a natural meeting point for herdsmen. Its association with Eumaeus places it in Ithaca's rural interior, some distance from the town but within the swineherd's daily range.

### The Fountain of the Nymphs

The spring is also associated with the broader water-sacred landscape of Ithaca. [[ithacas-harbor|Ithaca's Harbor]] and [[the-grotto-of-the-nymphs|the Grotto of the Nymphs]] both feature flowing water as a divine element — the grotto's "perpetual waters" and the spring's gliding stream together establish Ithaca as an island blessed with fresh water despite its rocky character. This abundance of springs and fountains belies the island's modest size and rugged terrain.

## Significance

Arethusa's spring embodies the pastoral, inland dimension of Ithaca, in contrast to the coastal landmarks (harbor, grotto) that dominate the return narrative. The spring is not a site of heroic action but of daily life: shepherds watering flocks, travelers pausing to drink, the quiet routines of the countryside that Odysseus must reclaim along with his palace.

The name "Arethusa" carries mythic resonance beyond the Odyssey. In Greek mythology, Arethusa was a nymph who fled from the river-god Alpheus and was transformed into a spring by Artemis — a story set in Sicily but adopted to Ithaca's landscape by Homer. The presence of a mythologically-named spring on Ithaca elevates the island beyond mere geography into a storied landscape where every feature is inhabited by narrative memory.

## Geography

The Spring of Arethusa on Ithaca has been identified by some scholars with a spring near modern Perachori in the island's southern interior, an area of karst limestone where springs emerge from the rock. The water is notably cool and clear, consistent with the Homeric epithet "sable" (suggesting depth and shade rather than turbidity). However, as with many Homeric landmarks, scholarly consensus on precise identification remains elusive.
