The Argo is the ship built for [[jason|Jason]] and his Argonauts for their quest to retrieve the Golden Fleece from Colchis. In the Odyssey, the Argo is referenced in a single but highly significant passage in Book XII: it is the only vessel before Odysseus's to have passed through the [[the-planctae|Planctae]] (the Wandering or Clashing Rocks), a feat accomplished only through the direct intervention of [[hera|Juno]] (Hera). The Argo thus serves as the sole mythological precedent for Odysseus's own survival of a passage that destroys all other ships.

## In the Odyssey

### Circe's Warning (Book XII)

When [[circe|Circe]] instructs [[odysseus|Odysseus]] on the dangers ahead, she describes the Planctae — rocks surrounded by fire and towering waves that crush any ship that attempts passage:

> "Scarce the famed Argo pass'd these raging floods, / The sacred Argo, fill'd with demigods! / E'en she had sunk, but Jove's imperial bride / Wing'd her fleet sail, and push'd her o'er the tide." [012-NA]

The passage carries several layers of meaning. First, the Argo is described as "sacred" and "fill'd with demigods" — its crew included Heracles, Orpheus, Castor and Pollux, and other heroes of the generation before the Trojan War. If even this legendary company required divine rescue to survive the Planctae, the implication is clear: Odysseus should not even attempt the passage.

Second, the mention of "Jove's imperial bride" (Juno/Hera) identifies the specific divine patronage that saved the Argo. Juno's favor toward Jason and the Argonauts is well-attested in Greek myth, and her intervention here is presented as exceptional — a one-time divine rescue that cannot be counted upon.

### Odysseus's Choice

Unlike the Argo, Odysseus does not attempt the Planctae. Heeding Circe's warning, he chooses instead the route past [[scylla|Scylla]] and [[charybdis|Charybdis]] — a path that costs six men but preserves the ship. The Argo's precedent thus serves a practical narrative function: by establishing that the Planctae are barely survivable even for demigods, it validates Odysseus's decision to take the lesser of two terrors.

## In Greek Myth

The Argo was built by Argus (hence its name) under the guidance of [[athena|Athene]], using timber from Mount Pelion. It was the largest ship of its era and possessed the gift of speech from a beam of Dodonian oak, enabling it to counsel its crew. Its voyage to Colchis — the first long-distance sea expedition in Greek myth — was both a heroic quest and a pioneering feat of navigation.

After the quest, the Argo was dedicated to [[poseidon|Poseidon]] at the Isthmus of Corinth. In some traditions, its prow was later placed in the heavens as the constellation Argo Navis, visible in the southern sky — a celestial memorial to the ship that first opened the seas to Greek exploration.

## Significance in the Odyssey

The Argo reference is the only direct mention of an earlier generation's heroic quest within the Odyssey's wanderings narrative. It places Odysseus's voyage in the context of a broader heroic tradition — one in which Odysseus is not the pioneer but the successor, navigating waters that even an earlier generation of demigods barely survived.

The contrast between the Argo's crew and Odysseus's is instructive. The Argo carried demigods and enjoyed Juno's explicit protection; Odysseus commands a crew of ordinary Ithacans and relies on his own cunning. That Odysseus survives where the Argo nearly sank — not by force but by choosing a different route — reinforces the Odyssey's central theme: survival through intelligence and prudence, not through heroic might.
