# Arete

## Identity & Names

**Arete** (Greek: Ἀρήτη) is the queen of the Phaeacians, wife of [[alcinous|Alcinous]], and mother of [[nausicaa|Nausicaa]]. Her name means "virtue" or "excellence" (ἀρετή) — a name that is itself a statement about her character and standing. In Pope's *Odyssey*, she is "divine Arete" [007-326], a queen "with honours yet to womankind unknown" [007-099], whose authority in the Phaeacian court rivals and in some respects exceeds that of the king.

Arete is one of the most remarkable female figures in the *Odyssey*: a mortal woman whose people gaze upon her "as on a goddess" [007-106], whose judgment is the key to Odysseus's safe passage home, and whose influence is described in terms that have no parallel in the rest of Homeric epic.

## Role & Significance

Arete's role is concentrated in Odysseus's stay among the Phaeacians, but her importance to the poem's structure is immense. She is the gatekeeper of Odysseus's return: without her favour, there is no ship to Ithaca.

Athena herself instructs Odysseus to approach Arete first — not Alcinous:

> First to the queen prefer a suppliant's claim,
> Alcinous' queen, Arete is her name. [007-082]

> With honours yet to womankind unknown.
> This queen he graces, and divides the throne;
> In all the female virtues unconfin'd,
> She charms the people, and she rules the mind;
> What praise she asks, the ready train bestows,
> And all look up, and all forget their woes. [007-099 to 007-106]

This is extraordinary: a woman described as ruling *minds*, whose people grant her whatever she asks, and whose "wisdom all our common cares disposes" [paraphrase]. The Phaeacian court is, effectively, a diarchy — a dual monarchy in which the queen's authority is equal to or greater than the king's.

### The Genealogy of Power

Arete's authority is hereditary, not merely marital. She is the daughter of Rhexenor, Alcinous's brother:

> From him Rhexenor and Alcinous came.
> No son survived; Arete heir'd his state,
> And her, Alcinous chose his royal mate. [007-094]

The marriage of Arete and Alcinous thus united two lines of the royal house: Alcinous, the king by descent from Nausithous, and Arete, the heiress of Rhexenor. Her authority derives from her own lineage, not merely from her husband.

### The Supplication

When Odysseus enters the Phaeacian palace, he follows Athena's instructions precisely: he bypasses Alcinous and falls at Arete's feet:

> "Daughter of great Rhexenor! (thus began,
> Low at her knees, the much-enduring man)
> ... A suppliant bends: oh pity human woe!" [007-207]

The gesture is calculated and correct. Arete's response determines everything that follows. It is she who questions him about his garments (recognising the handiwork of her daughter Nausicaa), she who presides over the feast, and she who, at the end of the evening, commands that a bed be prepared for the stranger:

> Meantime Arete, for the hour of rest,
> Ordains the fleecy couch. [007-440]

### The Silence and the Speech

Arete's most distinctive moment comes in Book 11, after Odysseus's long narrative of his journey to the Underworld. The entire Phaeacian court sits in stunned silence — and it is Arete who breaks it:

> Till, rising up, Arete silence broke,
> Struck with the manly port, and thus she spoke. [011-430]

She commands the court to generosity, ensuring that Odysseus receives the gifts that will constitute his wealth when he arrives in Ithaca. Her speech is the practical, decisive act that transforms admiration into tangible aid — the difference between a story well told and a hero actually helped.

## Relationships

- **[[alcinous|Alcinous]]** — Her husband and co-sovereign. Their marriage is presented as a model of shared rule: Alcinous holds the formal kingship, but his authority is incomplete without Arete's consent. "This queen he graces, and divides the throne" [007-100].
- **[[nausicaa|Nausicaa]]** — Her daughter, who first encounters Odysseus on the beach and directs him to the palace. Nausicaa's wisdom in advising Odysseus to approach her mother first reflects Arete's own astuteness.
- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]** — The suppliant who falls at her feet. Their interaction is governed by the formal protocols of *hiketeia* (supplication), but also by mutual recognition: Arete sees in Odysseus a man of quality and suffering, and he sees in her the authority that can grant his heart's desire — passage home.
- **[[athena|Athena]]** — The goddess who advises Odysseus to approach Arete first. Athena's strategic recognition of Arete's power is a remarkable acknowledgment of female authority in a patriarchal world.

## Analysis (optional — `standard`+ quality)

### A Matriarchal Exception?

Arete's position has attracted considerable scholarly attention because it seems to reflect a more archaic, matriarchal model of queenship. Unlike [[penelope|Penelope]], whose authority is exercised through indirection and cunning within a male-dominated household, or [[helen|Helen]], whose power derives from her beauty and her husband's status, Arete rules openly and directly.

Some scholars have interpreted the Phaeacian diarchy as a survival of pre-Hellenic social structures in which women held genuinely co-equal political power. The Phaeacians, with their magical ships and their isolation from the world of war, represent a kind of utopian alternative to the heroic society of the Greek mainland — and Arete's queenship is part of that utopian vision.

### The Strategic Supplication

Odysseus's decision to supplicate Arete rather than Alcinous is one of the poem's most politically astute moments. It reflects the intelligence that defines Odysseus: he listens to Athena's instructions, assesses the power dynamics of an unfamiliar court, and acts precisely as the situation demands. The supplication of Arete is, in microcosm, the strategy of the entire *Odyssey*: know who holds power, and know how to approach them.
