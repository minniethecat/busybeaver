# The Underworld (Nekyia)

## Summary

The Nekyia (Greek: [[entrance-to-the-underworld|νέκυια]], "rite of the dead") is [[the-wanderings-of-odysseus|Odysseus's journey]] to the entrance of the Underworld in Book 11, undertaken at [[circe|Circe]]'s command to consult the shade of the Theban seer [[tiresias|Tiresias]]. Sacrificing a black ram and ewe, [[odysseus|Odysseus]] summons the dead with an offering of blood. [[tiresias|Tiresias]] drinks and delivers his prophecy: [[odysseus|Odysseus]] will reach home, but only after great suffering; he must not harm the Cattle of [[helios|the Sun]]; and after reclaiming his household, he must journey inland to propitiate [[poseidon|Poseidon]]. Following [[tiresias|Tiresias]], a procession of shades approaches — his mother [[anticlea|Anticlea]] (who died of grief waiting for him), the great heroes and heroines of myth, and his fallen comrades from [[troy|Troy]]: [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], [[achilles|Achilles]], and [[ajax|Ajax]].

## Key Moments

- [[tiresias|Tiresias]]'s three-part prophecy: return, the Cattle warning, and the final journey
- [[anticlea|Anticlea]]'s shade: the most personal encounter — [[odysseus|Odysseus]] weeps but cannot embrace her
- [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s warning about faithless wives contrasted with [[penelope|Penelope]]'s fidelity
- [[achilles|Achilles]]'s famous lament: he would rather be a living slave than king of all the dead
- [[ajax|Ajax]]'s silent refusal to speak — still wounded by his loss to [[odysseus|Odysseus]] in the contest for [[achilles|Achilles]]'s armor

## Aftermath

The Nekyia establishes the poem's cosmic horizon. [[tiresias|Tiresias]]'s final-journey prophecy extends the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* beyond its own ending — nostos is not complete with the recovery of [[ithaca|Ithaca]] but requires reconciliation with [[poseidon|Poseidon]]. The parade of the dead situates [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s suffering within the larger tragedy of the Trojan War generation.
