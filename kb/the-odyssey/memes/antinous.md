# Antinous

## Identity & Names

**Antinous** (Greek: Ἀντίνοος, *Antinoös*, "opposite mind" or "hostile thought") is the son of Eupeithes and the recognized leader of the 108 suitors who occupy Odysseus's palace, consuming his wealth and pursuing his wife. His name — opposing *noos* (mind, intelligence) — is thematically precise: he represents everything opposed to Odysseus's defining quality of *metis*. He is the first suitor to die, shot through the throat by Odysseus's arrow as he raises a golden wine cup to his lips.

Homer introduces him as the most physically impressive of the suitors — "the bold Antinous, / In pride of youth, superior to the rest" [004-638] — but his beauty is the deceptive surface over moral rot.

## Role & Significance

Antinous is the embodiment of hubris given narrative form. Unlike some suitors who are merely weak (Leocritus), foolish (Ctesippus), or morally compromised but redeemable (Amphinomus), Antinous is actively and consistently malicious. His villainy escalates across the poem:

1. **Public disrespect**: He dismisses Telemachus's complaints in the Ithacan assembly, blaming Penelope's weaving ruse rather than the suitors' conduct [002-085].
2. **Attempted murder**: He plots to ambush and kill Telemachus on his return voyage, stationing a ship in the strait between Ithaca and Samos [004-660].
3. **Guest abuse**: He strikes the disguised Odysseus with a footstool — the most explicit act of violence against a suppliant in the epic [017-462].
4. **Beggars' contest**: He taunts the disguised Odysseus and tries to prevent him from participating in the bow contest [021-287].

His death is the epic's most symbolically charged moment. As he raises a golden cup to drink — consuming another man's wine, in another man's house, pursuing another man's wife — Odysseus's arrow pierces his throat:

> Full through his throat Ulysses' weapon pass'd,
> And pierced his neck. He falls, and breathes his last. [022-030–031]

The wine cup spills and mingles with his blood. The image is one of absolute pollution — the feaster becomes the feast's corruption, and the wine of hospitality becomes a libation of death.

## Appearances

### Book 1: The Suitor's Challenge

Antinous first appears among the suitors feasting in Odysseus's hall. When Telemachus protests their consumption of his inheritance, Antinous does not bother to respond — it falls to another suitor, Eurymachus, to make the false-polite reply [001-398]. His silence communicates contempt more effectively than words.

### Book 2: The Assembly Speech

In the Ithacan assembly — the first called since Odysseus's departure twenty years ago — Antinous delivers the suitors' counter-argument. He blames Penelope: she promised to choose a husband after finishing Laertes's shroud, but for three years she unraveled at night what she wove by day [002-093]. His argument has the form of reason — Penelope did deceive them — but the substance of bad faith: the suitors' presence in the palace is itself an act of coercion, and Penelope's ruse is self-defense, not fraud.

### Book 4: The Ambush

Antinous learns from Noemon that Telemachus has sailed to Pylos. His response is immediate and violent: he proposes stationing a ship with twenty men in the strait between Ithaca and Samos to kill Telemachus on his return [004-660]. The ambush fails only because Athena guides Telemachus home by a different route [015-295]. This plot transforms Antinous from a parasite into an attempted murderer, making his eventual death a matter of justice rather than vengeance.

### Book 17: The Stool

The disguised Odysseus, begging in the hall, approaches Antinous. The suitor's response is a direct assault on the sacred obligation of xenia:

> "'Hence, dotard! lest I make thee quit the gate,
> And drag thee forth, and on thy carcase wait.'
> He said, and seized a stool." [018-021]

He hurls the stool and strikes Odysseus on the shoulder. The other suitors are shocked — even they recognize that to strike a stranger, who might be a god in disguise, is to risk divine punishment [017-482]. Antinous alone is beyond restraint, and his violence against the guest prefigures the violence that will be visited upon him.

### Book 21: The Bow

When Odysseus (still disguised) asks to try the bow, Antinous vehemently objects. His fear is not that the beggar might succeed but that his success would shame the suitors — a fear that proves prophetic [021-287].

### Book 22: The Death

Odysseus's first arrow strikes Antinous as he lifts a wine cup: "High in his hands he rear'd the golden bowl!" [022-025]. The arrow passes through his throat:

> Full through his throat Ulysses' weapon pass'd,
> And pierced his neck. He falls, and breathes his last. [022-030–031]

He falls, kicking over the table, and the suitors believe at first that the shot was an accident. Odysseus's revelation — "One venturous game this hand hath won to-day, / Another, princes! yet remains to play" [022-018–019] — makes clear that it was execution, not accident.

## Relationships

- **Odysseus**: His nemesis, whom he unknowingly abuses in disguise. The dramatic irony of their encounters — Antinous striking the man he has spent years trying to supplant — gives their conflict a mythic quality: the returned king judges before he reveals himself.

- **Penelope**: The object of his ambition. He seeks not her love but the kingship she represents. His pursuit is political rather than romantic — Penelope is the mechanism through which he would claim Odysseus's throne.

- **Telemachus**: The rightful heir he schemes to murder. Antinous's plot against Telemachus is the most damning evidence of his character: he is willing to kill a young man to secure his own ambition.

- **Eurymachus**: His co-leader among the suitors — smoother, more diplomatic, but equally ruthless. After Antinous's death, Eurymachus tries to negotiate, offering recompense. His attempt fails, and he dies second.

- **Amphinomus**: The "good suitor" — Antinous's moral foil. Where Antinous is cruel, Amphinomus is reluctant; where Antinous plots murder, Amphinomus counsels restraint. But Amphinomus's decency is passive — he lacks the courage to oppose Antinous openly — and he dies alongside the rest.

- **Eupeithes**: His father, who after the massacre leads the suitors' families in an attempted counter-revenge. Athena stops the battle, but Eupeithes is killed by Laertes [024-523] — the father of the victim avenging the father of the perpetrator, completing the cycle.

## Analysis

### The Argument for the Death Penalty

Antinous serves as the poem's moral argument for capital punishment. His characterization is designed to make his killing satisfying rather than troubling — he is given every opportunity to show decency and refuses each one. When Penelope extracts gifts from the suitors, the others comply; Antinous alone refuses [018-284]. When Odysseus begs, the others give; Antinous throws a stool [017-462]. By making Antinous so thoroughly irredeemable, Homer ensures that the slaughter in Book 22 reads as justice rather than massacre.

### Hubris Personified

Antinous is not merely arrogant — he is a systematic violation of every value the poem affirms. He violates xenia (striking a guest), violates dike (consuming another's property without recompense), violates aidos (showing no shame), and violates themis (plotting the murder of the rightful heir). His name — "opposite mind" — marks him as the antithesis of the poem's central virtue: the intelligence that recognizes limits and obligations.

### The Wine Cup and the Arrow

The image of Antinous dying with a wine cup at his lips [022-015] is one of the most carefully crafted death scenes in ancient epic. The wine cup is the symbol of xenia — the shared drink that seals the bond between host and guest. By dying as he drinks, Antinous is condemned by the very ritual he has violated. The arrow through the throat is also symbolically apt: the organ of speech and consumption, through which Antinous has done his greatest harm (speaking lies, consuming another's wealth), is the organ that receives the punishment.

### Antinous and the Problem of Evil

Classicists have debated whether Antinous is a fully realized character or a narrative function — the designated villain whose death licenses the slaughter that follows. The text supports both readings. Antinous has no backstory, no moment of doubt, no redeeming quality. He is as flat as a morality-play Vice. Yet this flatness may be the point: evil in the *Odyssey* is not psychologically complex. It is the simple refusal of obligation, and its remedy is similarly simple — removal.
