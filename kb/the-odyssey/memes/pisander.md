> "With these, Pisander, great Polyctor's son." (022-277)

## Identity

Pisander, son of Polyctor, is one of the suitors occupying the palace of [[odysseus]] in Ithaca. His father Polyctor is named among the founding nobles of Ithaca — alongside Neritus and Ithacus — marking Pisander as a man of distinguished local lineage whose presence among the suitors represents a betrayal of his family's heritage.

The name Pisander (Greek: Πεισανδρος, *Peisandros*) combines *peitho* (persuasion) with *aner* (man), yielding "persuader of men" — an ironic name for a suitor who fails to persuade Penelope and whose persuasive arts are reduced to gift-giving.

## In the Odyssey

### Book 18 — The Gift of the Necklace

During the suitors' gift-giving to [[penelope]] — prompted by her ruse of accepting bridal presents — Pisander offers a precious necklace:

> "Pisander bears a necklace wrought with art." (018-354)

The necklace is described as elaborately crafted ("wrought with art"), suggesting Pisander's wealth among the suitor company. This scene occurs in Book 18, when Penelope appears before the suitors, inspired by [[athena]], to extract gifts while maintaining her deception.

The gift-giving episode reveals the suitors' collective psychology. Penelope has just rebuked them for their parasitism — consuming Odysseus's wealth without contributing anything in return — and their response is immediate and competitive: each suitor rushes to offer the finest gift, trying to outdo the others in a display of generosity that paradoxically confirms Penelope's accusation. Pisander's necklace is one of the most elaborate gifts mentioned, alongside Antinous's embroidered robe and Eurymachus's golden chain.

### Book 22 — The Battle in the Hall

When [[odysseus]] reveals himself and the slaughter begins, Pisander stands among the suitor vanguard. He is listed among the leaders who rally behind [[agelaus]] to mount a coordinated counterattack:

> "Amphimedon, Demoptolemus, and he / That well-instructed in the fair degree / Of polities and fights, the wise, the great / Eurydamas; with these, Pisander's fate / Unites his own." (022-276)

This passage, naming six suitor leaders including Pisander, marks the moment when the suitors transition from panicked individuals to an organized fighting force. Agelaus, the most capable of the suitor commanders, rallies this group for a coordinated assault — the poem's only moment where the suitors pose a genuine military threat to Odysseus.

### Death

Pisander dies in the general slaughter, struck down as one of the suitor ringleaders. His death is recorded as part of the suitor casualties in Book 22:

> "The goatherd's quiver'd in Pisander's heart." (022-306)

The line is ambiguous — "the goatherd" could refer to Melanthius (whose weapons are turned against the suitors) or to a combatant wielding a goatherd's equipment. In either reading, Pisander's death by a weapon associated with the pastoral world — the world of Eumaeus and Philoetius — symbolically inverts the suitors' consumption of Odysseus's herds: the herdsmen's tools become instruments of justice.

## Local Lineage and Betrayal

Pisander's father, Polyctor, is named in Book 17 alongside Neritus and Ithacus as one of Ithaca's founding figures. This genealogy makes Pisander's participation in the suitors' occupation particularly egregious: he is not a foreign adventurer or a man from the margins seeking advancement, but a scion of Ithaca's founding families. His betrayal is a betrayal of his own heritage — a man whose ancestors built the kingdom now helping to consume it.

The necklace he offers Penelope acquires additional meaning in this context. A necklace is an heirloom object, a piece of family wealth passed through generations. By offering it to Penelope, Pisander is literally trading his family's past for a claim on Odysseus's future — a transaction that the slaughter in Book 22 renders void.

## Characteristics

Pisander appears only briefly in the poem, but his two scenes define him: he is a wealthy suitor (the precious necklace) who joins the doomed resistance in the final battle. His father's connection to Ithaca's founding gives him a local prestige that makes his participation in the suitors' occupation particularly egregious — he is not a foreigner but a man of Ithacan heritage betraying his own king. Among the suitor company, he represents the tragedy of wasted nobility: a man whose lineage should have made him a pillar of the community instead becomes a cautionary example of corrupted privilege.
