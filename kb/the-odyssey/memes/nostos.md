# Nostos (Homecoming)

## Definition

**Nostos** (Greek: νόστος, *nóstos*) means "return" or "homecoming" — specifically the journey back from war or long wandering to one's homeland and household. The word gives the *Odyssey* its thematic core: the epic is the story of Odysseus's nostos after the Trojan War, a return that should have taken weeks but consumed ten years. Unlike the *Iliad*'s centripetal movement toward Troy, the *Odyssey* is centrifugal — every force pulls the hero away from home, and the entire poem measures the distance between where he is and where he belongs.

The word itself implies more than physical travel. *Nostos* derives from the Indo-European root *nes-* ("to return safely home"), related to Germanic *nesan* ("to be saved") and Sanskrit *nasate* ("he approaches"). It encodes both the journey and its successful completion — a nostos that ends in death (like Agamemnon's) is a nostos betrayed.

## Greek Context

In the post-Trojan War myth cycle, the *Nostoi* ("Returns") was a separate epic tradition (now lost, attributed to Agias of Troezen) recounting the homecomings of various Greek heroes. The spectrum of outcomes defined the concept's range:

| Hero | Nostos Outcome | Significance |
|------|---------------|-------------|
| Nestor | Swift, peaceful return to Pylos | The ideal — honoring the gods ensures safe passage |
| Menelaus | Delayed return via Egypt, eventually restored | Suffering followed by restoration |
| Odysseus | Ten years of wandering, eventual return | The prototypical difficult nostos |
| Agamemnon | Swift return → murdered by Clytemnestra | The anti-nostos — reaching home means death |
| Ajax the Lesser | Shipwrecked and drowned by Poseidon | Nostos denied entirely |

This spectrum establishes that homecoming was not merely geographical but social and moral — to return meant to reassume one's place as husband, father, king, and master of a household (*oikos*). Failure to return, or return to a corrupted home, was the ultimate catastrophe.

## In the Odyssey

The *Odyssey* is the nostos narrative par excellence, exploring the concept through multiple registers and across twenty-four books.

### The Protracted Return

Odysseus's nostos is constantly deferred. Each temptation encountered in the wanderings is a variation on the same threat: the abandonment of homecoming.

> "Calypso, the most beautiful of goddesses,
> Detains the hero in her vaulted cave,
> And with deluding arts his soul enslaves."

Calypso offers immortality if he stays; Circe offers a year of pleasure; the Lotus-Eaters offer oblivion; the Sirens offer fatal knowledge. His refusal of Calypso — choosing mortal return over ageless obscurity — defines nostos as an existential commitment, not merely a journey:

> "Full well I know, that, matched with heavenly charms,
> My Penelope is poor in form and face;
> Yet, day by day, I long to see the place,
| The hour of my return."

### Negative Nostos: Agamemnon

The fate of Agamemnon looms as the anti-nostos throughout the poem. He returned swiftly to Argos only to be murdered by Clytemnestra and Aegisthus. His shade's warning to Odysseus in the Underworld is explicit:

> "Be not, like me, to thy returning wife
> Too open; for the sex is frail, and life
> Has given examples."

Agamemnon's fate transforms nostos from a geographical problem into a hermeneutic one: Odysseus must learn to read his household before revealing himself to it. The poem's second half — the testing of Penelope, Euryclea, Eumaeus, and the suitors — is the working-out of this lesson.

### The Nostos of Telemachus

Telemachus's journey to Pylos and Sparta (Books 1–4) is a micro-nostos — the son seeking news of the father, but also coming of age through travel. Athena sends him "to win a noble name", framing his journey as a nostos of identity: he must leave Ithaca to learn who he is, and return to it as a man. His safe return, evading the suitors' ambush, prefigures his father's more dangerous homecoming.

### Ithaca as Destination

The *Odyssey* makes homecoming a sacred goal. Ithaca is described not as a great kingdom (it is "rocky" and "barren") but as the place of identity: only in Ithaca is Odysseus fully himself. His nostos is completed not when he reaches the shore but through a sequence of recognitions — by Telemachus, by Argos, by Euryclea, by Eumaeus, by Penelope, and finally by Laertes. Each recognition restores a piece of his relational identity. Homecoming is not a moment but a process of reconnection.

### The Unfinished Nostos

Tiresias's prophecy reveals that Odysseus's nostos is not complete even after reclaiming Ithaca. He must undertake a final journey inland with an oar until he meets people who do not recognize it, then sacrifice to Poseidon. This extends nostos beyond the poem's ending: true homecoming requires reconciliation not only with household and kingdom but with the divine order itself.

## Significance

### Narrative Architecture

Nostos structures the *Odyssey* at every level. Every episode of the wanderings is measured by its distance from Ithaca — not cartographic distance (the poem's geography is mythical) but moral distance. To be far from Ithaca is to be far from right order. The poem's movement from Ogygia (the farthest point, where Odysseus weeps daily on the shore) to the olive-tree marriage bed (the innermost point of home) maps the restoration of *kosmos* (order) from *chaos*.

### Return as Transformation

Odysseus's nostos involves symbolic death and rebirth. He enters Ithaca disguised as a beggar — the lowest social station — and must re-win his identity step by step. The scar, the bow, the bed, the orchard trees: each token restores a piece of who he is, but the man who reclaims them is not identical to the man who left. Nostos is not circular but spiral — the return is to a home that has changed and to a self transformed by suffering.

### Universal Pattern

Nostos became the template for countless later narratives — from Virgil's *Aeneid* to Joyce's *Ulysses* to Tolkien's *Lord of the Rings* — because it embodies the most fundamental human journey: the attempt to go home and find it, and oneself, changed. The pattern is so deep that it transcends its Homeric origins: in psychological terms, nostos is the journey of individuation; in religious terms, it is the soul's return to its source; in narrative terms, it is comedy's fundamental shape (descent into chaos, ascent into restored order).
