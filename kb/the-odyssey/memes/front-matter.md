[P01-065] INTRODUCTION.

[P01-066] THE ODYSSEY OF HOMER

[P01-067] INTRODUCTION.

[P01-068] Scepticism is as much the result of knowledge, as knowledge is of

[P01-069] scepticism. To be content with what we at present know, is, for the

[P01-070] most part, to shut our ears against conviction; since, from the very

[P01-071] gradual character of our education, we must continually forget, and

[P01-072] emancipate ourselves from, knowledge previously acquired; we must set

[P01-073] aside old notions and embrace fresh ones; and, as we learn, we must be

[P01-074] daily unlearning something which it has cost us no small labour and

[P01-075] anxiety to acquire.

[P01-076] And this difficulty attaches itself more closely to an age in which

[P01-077] progress has gained a strong ascendency over prejudice, and in which

[P01-078] persons and things are, day by day, finding their real level, in lieu

[P01-079] of their conventional value. The same principles which have swept away

[P01-080] traditional abuses, and which are making rapid havoc among the revenues

[P01-081] of sinecurists, and stripping the thin, tawdry veil from attractive

[P01-082] superstitions, are working as actively in literature as in society. The

[P01-083] credulity of one writer, or the partiality of another, finds as

[P01-084] powerful a touchstone and as wholesome a chastisement in the healthy

[P01-085] scepticism of a temperate class of antagonists, as the dreams of

[P01-086] conservatism, or the impostures of pluralist sinecures in the Church.

[P01-087] History and tradition, whether of ancient or comparatively recent

[P01-088] times, are subjected to very different handling from that which the

[P01-089] indulgence or credulity of former ages could allow. Mere statements are

[P01-090] jealously watched, and the motives of the writer form as important an

[P01-091] ingredient in the analysis of his history, as the facts he records.

[P01-092] Probability is a powerful and troublesome test; and it is by this

[P01-093] troublesome standard that a large portion of historical evidence is

[P01-094] sifted. Consistency is no less pertinacious and exacting in its

[P01-095] demands. In brief, to write a history, we must know more than mere

[P01-096] facts. Human nature, viewed under an introduction of extended

[P01-097] experience, is the best help to the criticism of human history.

[P01-098] Historical characters can only be estimated by the standard which human

[P01-099] experience, whether actual or traditionary, has furnished. To form

[P01-100] correct views of individuals we must regard them as forming parts of a

[P01-101] great whole—we must measure them by their relation to the mass of

[P01-102] beings by whom they are surrounded; and, in contemplating the incidents

[P01-103] in their lives or condition which tradition has handed down to us, we

[P01-104] must rather consider the general bearing of the whole narrative, than

[P01-105] the respective probability of its details.

[P01-106] It is unfortunate for us, that, of some of the greatest men, we know

[P01-107] least, and talk most. Homer, Socrates, and Shakespere have, perhaps,

[P01-108] contributed more to the intellectual enlightenment of mankind than any

[P01-109] other three writers who could be named, and yet the history of all

[P01-110] three has given rise to a boundless ocean of discussion, which has left

[P01-111] us little save the option of choosing which theory or theories we will

[P01-112] follow. The personality of Shakespere is, perhaps, the only thing in

[P01-113] which critics will allow us to believe without controversy; but upon

[P01-114] everything else, even down to the authorship of plays, there is more or

[P01-115] less of doubt and uncertainty. Of Socrates we know as little as the

[P01-116] contradictions of Plato and Xenophon will allow us to know. He was one

[P01-117] of the _dramatis personæ_ in two dramas as unlike in principles as in

[P01-118] style. He appears as the enunciator of opinions as different in their

[P01-119] tone as those of the writers who have handed them down. When we have

[P01-120] read Plato _or_ Xenophon, we think we know something of Socrates; when

[P01-121] we have fairly read and examined both, we feel convinced that we are

[P01-122] something worse than ignorant.

[P01-123] It has been an easy, and a popular expedient of late years, to deny the

[P01-124] personal or real existence of men and things whose life and condition

[P01-125] were too much for our belief. This system—which has often comforted the

[P01-126] religious sceptic, and substituted the consolations of Strauss for

[P01-127] those of the New Testament—has been of incalculable value to the

[P01-128] historical theorists of the last and present centuries. To question the

[P01-129] existence of Alexander the Great, would be a more excusable act, than

[P01-130] to believe in that of Romulus. To deny a fact related in Herodotus,

[P01-131] because it is inconsistent with a theory developed from an Assyrian

[P01-132] inscription which no two scholars read in the same way, is more

[P01-133] pardonable, than to believe in the good-natured old king whom the

[P01-134] elegant pen of Florian has idealized—_Numa Pompilius_.

[P01-135] Scepticism has attained its culminating point with respect to Homer,

[P01-136] and the state of our Homeric knowledge may be described as a free

[P01-137] permission to believe any theory, provided we throw overboard all

[P01-138] written tradition, concerning the author or authors of the [[iliad|Iliad]] and

[P01-139] Odyssey. What few authorities exist on the subject, are summarily

[P01-140] dismissed, although the arguments appear to run in a circle. “This

[P01-141] cannot be true, because it is not true; and that is not true, because

[P01-142] it cannot be true.” Such seems to be the style, in which testimony upon

[P01-143] testimony, statement upon statement, is consigned to denial and

[P01-144] oblivion.

[P01-145] It is, however, unfortunate that the professed biographies of Homer are

[P01-146] partly forgeries, partly freaks of ingenuity and imagination, in which

[P01-147] truth is the requisite most wanting. Before taking a brief review of

[P01-148] the Homeric theory in its present conditions, some notice must be taken

[P01-149] of the treatise on the Life of Homer which has been attributed to

[P01-150] Herodotus.

[P01-151] According to this document, the city of Cumae in AEolia was, at an

[P01-152] early period, the seat of frequent immigrations from various parts of

[P01-153] Greece. Among the immigrants was Menapolus, the son of Ithagenes.

[P01-154] Although poor, he married, and the result of the union was a girl named

[P01-155] Critheis. The girl was left an orphan at an early age, under the

[P01-156] guardianship of Cleanax, of Argos. It is to the indiscretion of this

[P01-157] maiden that we “are indebted for so much happiness.” Homer was the

[P01-158] first fruit of her juvenile frailty, and received the name of

[P01-159] Melesigenes from having been born near the river Meles in Bœotia,

[P01-160] whither Critheis had been transported in order to save her reputation.

[P01-161] “At this time,” continues our narrative, “there lived at Smyrna a man

[P01-162] named [[phemius|Phemius]], a teacher of literature and music, who, not being

[P01-163] married, engaged Critheis to manage his household, and spin the flax he

[P01-164] received as the price of his scholastic labours. So satisfactory was

[P01-165] her performance of this task, and so modest her conduct, that he made

[P01-166] proposals of marriage, declaring himself, as a further inducement,

[P01-167] willing to adopt her son, who, he asserted, would become a clever man,

[P01-168] if he were carefully brought up.”

[P01-169] They were married; careful cultivation ripened the talents which nature

[P01-170] had bestowed, and Melesigenes soon surpassed his schoolfellows in every

[P01-171] attainment, and, when older, rivalled his preceptor in wisdom. Phemius

[P01-172] died, leaving him sole heir to his property, and his mother soon

[P01-173] followed. Melesigenes carried on his adopted father’s school with great

[P01-174] success, exciting the admiration not only of the inhabitants of Smyrna,

[P01-175] but also of the strangers whom the trade carried on there, especially

[P01-176] in the exportation of corn, attracted to that city. Among these

[P01-177] visitors, one [[mentor|Mentes]], from Leucadia, the modern Santa Maura, who

[P01-178] evinced a knowledge and intelligence rarely found in those times,

[P01-179] persuaded Melesigenes to close his school, and accompany him on his

[P01-180] travels. He promised not only to pay his expenses, but to furnish him

[P01-181] with a further stipend, urging, that, “While he was yet young, it was

[P01-182] fitting that he should see with his own eyes the countries and cities

[P01-183] which might hereafter be the subjects of his discourses.” Melesigenes

[P01-184] consented, and set out with his patron, “examining all the curiosities

[P01-185] of the countries they visited, and informing himself of everything by

[P01-186] interrogating those whom he met.” We may also suppose, that he wrote

[P01-187] memoirs of all that he deemed worthy of preservation. Having set sail

[P01-188] from Tyrrhenia and Iberia, they reached [[ithaca|Ithaca]]. Here Melesigenes, who

[P01-189] had already suffered in his eyes, became much worse; and Mentes, who

[P01-190] was about to leave for Leucadia, left him to the medical

[P01-191] superintendence of a friend of his, named Mentor, the son of Alcinor.

[P01-192] Under his hospitable and intelligent host, Melesigenes rapidly became

[P01-193] acquainted with the legends respecting [[odysseus|Ulysses]], which afterwards formed

[P01-194] the subject of the Odyssey. The inhabitants of Ithaca assert, that it

[P01-195] was here that Melesigenes became blind, but the Colophonians make their

[P01-196] city the seat of that misfortune. He then returned to Smyrna, where he

[P01-197] applied himself to the study of poetry.

[P01-198] But poverty soon drove him to Cumae. Having passed over the Hermaean

[P01-199] plain, he arrived at Neon Teichos, the New Wall, a colony of Cumae.

[P01-200] Here his misfortunes and poetical talent gained him the friendship of

[P01-201] one Tychias, an armourer. “And up to my time,” continues the author,

[P01-202] “the inhabitants showed the place where he used to sit when giving a

[P01-203] recitation of his verses; and they greatly honoured the spot. Here also

[P01-204] a poplar grew, which they said had sprung up ever since Melesigenes

[P01-205] arrived.”

[P01-206] But poverty still drove him on, and he went by way of Larissa, as being

[P01-207] the most convenient road. Here, the Cumans say, he composed an epitaph

[P01-208] on Gordius, king of Phrygia, which has however, and with greater

[P01-209] probability, been attributed to Cleobulus of Lindus.

[P01-210] Arrived at Cumae, he frequented the _conversaziones_ of the old men,

[P01-211] and delighted all by the charms of his poetry. Encouraged by this

[P01-212] favourable reception, he declared that, if they would allow him a

[P01-213] public maintenance, he would render their city most gloriously

[P01-214] renowned. They avowed their willingness to support him in the measure

[P01-215] he proposed, and procured him an audience in the council. Having made

[P01-216] the speech, with the purport of which our author has forgotten to

[P01-217] acquaint us, he retired, and left them to debate respecting the answer

[P01-218] to be given to his proposal.

[P01-219] The greater part of the assembly seemed favourable to the poet’s

[P01-220] demand, but one man “observed that if they were to feed _Homers_, they

[P01-221] would be encumbered with a multitude of useless people.” “From this

[P01-222] circumstance,” says the writer, “Melesigenes acquired the name of

[P01-223] Homer, for the Cumans call blind men _Homers_.” With a love of economy,

[P01-224] which shows how similar the world has always been in its treatment of

[P01-225] literary men, the pension was denied, and the poet vented his

[P01-226] disappointment in a wish that Cumae might never produce a poet capable

[P01-227] of giving it renown and glory.

[P01-228] At Phocaea Homer was destined to experience another literary distress.

[P01-229] One Thestorides, who aimed at the reputation of poetical genius, kept

[P01-230] Homer in his own house, and allowed him a pittance, on condition of the

[P01-231] verses of the poet passing in his name. Having collected sufficient

[P01-232] poetry to be profitable, Thestorides, like some would-be literary

[P01-233] publishers, neglected the man whose brains he had sucked, and left him.

[P01-234] At his departure, Homer is said to have observed: “O Thestorides, of

[P01-235] the many things hidden from the knowledge of man, nothing is more

[P01-236] unintelligible than the human heart.”

[P01-237] Homer continued his career of difficulty and distress, until some Chian

[P01-238] merchants, struck by the similarity of the verses they heard him

[P01-239] recite, acquainted him with the fact that Thestorides was pursuing a

[P01-240] profitable livelihood by the recital of the very same poems. This at

[P01-241] once determined him to set out for Chios. No vessel happened then to be

[P01-242] setting sail thither, but he found one ready to start for Erythrae, a

[P01-243] town of Ionia, which faces that island, and he prevailed upon the

[P01-244] seamen to allow him to accompany them. Having embarked, he invoked a

[P01-245] favourable wind, and prayed that he might be able to expose the

[P01-246] imposture of Thestorides, who, by his breach of hospitality, had drawn

[P01-247] down the wrath of [[zeus|Jove]] the Hospitable.

## Section 2

[P01-248] At Erythrae, Homer fortunately met with a person who had known him in

[P01-249] Phocaea, by whose assistance he at length, after some difficulty,

[P01-250] reached the little hamlet of Pithys. Here he met with an adventure,

[P01-251] which we will continue in the words of our author. “Having set out from

[P01-252] Pithys, Homer went on, attracted by the cries of some goats that were

[P01-253] pasturing. The dogs barked on his approach, and he cried out. Glaucus

[P01-254] (for that was the name of the goat-herd) heard his voice, ran up

[P01-255] quickly, called off his dogs, and drove them away from Homer. For some

[P01-256] time he stood wondering how a blind man should have reached such a

[P01-257] place alone, and what could be his design in coming. He then went up to

[P01-258] him and inquired who he was, and how he had come to desolate places and

[P01-259] untrodden spots, and of what he stood in need. Homer, by recounting to

[P01-260] him the whole history of his misfortunes, moved him with compassion;

[P01-261] and he took him and led him to his cot, and, having lit a fire, bade

[P01-262] him sup.

[P01-263] “The dogs, instead of eating, kept barking at the stranger, according

[P01-264] to their usual habit. Whereupon Homer addressed Glaucus thus: O

[P01-265] Glaucus, my friend, prythee attend to my behest. First give the dogs

[P01-266] their supper at the doors of the hut: for so it is better, since,

[P01-267] whilst they watch, nor thief nor wild beast will approach the fold.

[P01-268] “Glaucus was pleased with the advice and marvelled at its author.

[P01-269] Having finished supper, they banqueted afresh on conversation, Homer

[P01-270] narrating his wanderings, and telling of the cities he had visited.

[P01-271] “At length they retired to rest; but on the following morning, Glaucus

[P01-272] resolved to go to his master, and acquaint him with his meeting with

[P01-273] Homer. Having left the goats in charge of a fellow-servant, he left

[P01-274] Homer at home, promising to return quickly. Having arrived at Bolissus,

[P01-275] a place near the farm, and finding his mate, he told him the whole

[P01-276] story respecting Homer and his journey. He paid little attention to

[P01-277] what he said, and blamed Glaucus for his stupidity in taking in and

[P01-278] feeding maimed and enfeebled persons. However, he bade him bring the

[P01-279] stranger to him.

[P01-280] “Glaucus told Homer what had taken place, and bade him follow him,

[P01-281] assuring him that good fortune would be the result. Conversation soon

[P01-282] showed that the stranger was a man of much cleverness and general

[P01-283] knowledge, and the Chian persuaded him to remain, and to undertake the

[P01-284] charge of his children.”

[P01-285] Besides the satisfaction of driving the impostor Thestorides from the

[P01-286] island, Homer enjoyed considerable success as a teacher. In the town of

[P01-287] Chios he established a school, where he taught the precepts of poetry.

[P01-288] “To this day,” says Chandler, “the most curious remain is that which

[P01-289] has been named, without reason, the School of Homer. It is on the

[P01-290] coast, at some distance from the city, northward, and appears to have

[P01-291] been an open temple of Cybele, formed on the top of a rock. The shape

[P01-292] is oval, and in the centre is the image of the goddess, the head and an

[P01-293] arm wanting. She is represented, as usual, sitting. The chair has a

[P01-294] lion carved on each side, and on the back. The area is bounded by a low

[P01-295] rim, or seat, and about five yards over. The whole is hewn out of the

[P01-296] mountain, is rude, indistinct, and probably of the most remote

[P01-297] antiquity.”

[P01-298] So successful was this school, that Homer realised a considerable

[P01-299] fortune. He married, and had two daughters, one of whom died single,

[P01-300] the other married a Chian.

[P01-301] The following passage betrays the same tendency to connect the

[P01-302] personages of the poems with the history of the poet, which has already

[P01-303] been mentioned:—

[P01-304] “In his poetical compositions Homer displays great gratitude towards

[P01-305] Mentor of Ithaca, in the Odyssey, whose name he has inserted in his

[P01-306] poem as the companion of Ulysses, in return for the care taken of him

[P01-307] when afflicted with blindness. He also testifies his gratitude to

[P01-308] Phemius, who had given him both sustenance and instruction.”

[P01-309] His celebrity continued to increase, and many persons advised him to

[P01-310] visit Greece whither his reputation had now extended. Having, it is

[P01-311] said, made some additions to his poems calculated to please the vanity

[P01-312] of the Athenians, of whose city he had hitherto made no mention, he set

[P01-313] out for [[same|Samos]]. Here, being recognized by a Samian, who had met with him

[P01-314] in Chios, he was handsomely received, and invited to join in

[P01-315] celebrating the Apaturian festival. He recited some verses, which gave

[P01-316] great satisfaction, and by singing the Eiresione at the New Moon

[P01-317] festivals, he earned a subsistence, visiting the houses of the rich,

[P01-318] with whose children he was very popular.

[P01-319] In the spring he sailed for Athens, and arrived at the island of Ios,

[P01-320] now Ino, where he fell extremely ill, and died. It is said that his

[P01-321] death arose from vexation, at not having been able to unravel an enigma

[P01-322] proposed by some fishermen’s children.

[P01-323] Such is, in brief, the substance of the earliest life of Homer we

[P01-324] possess, and so broad are the evidences of its historical

[P01-325] worthlessness, that it is scarcely necessary to point them out in

[P01-326] detail. Let us now consider some of the opinions to which a

[P01-327] persevering, patient, and learned—but by no means consistent—series of

[P01-328] investigations has led. In doing so, I profess to bring forward

[P01-329] statements, not to vouch for their reasonableness or probability.

[P01-330] “Homer appeared. The history of this poet and his works is lost in

[P01-331] doubtful obscurity, as is the history of many of the first minds who

[P01-332] have done honour to humanity, because they rose amidst darkness. The

[P01-333] majestic stream of his song, blessing and fertilizing, flows like the

[P01-334] Nile, through many lands and nations; and, like the sources of the

[P01-335] Nile, its fountains will ever remain concealed.”

[P01-336] Such are the words in which one of the most judicious German critics

[P01-337] has eloquently described the uncertainty in which the whole of the

[P01-338] Homeric question is involved. With no less truth and feeling he

[P01-339] proceeds:—

[P01-340] “It seems here of chief importance to expect no more than the nature of

[P01-341] things makes possible. If the period of tradition in history is the

[P01-342] region of twilight, we should not expect in it perfect light. The

[P01-343] creations of genius always seem like miracles, because they are, for

[P01-344] the most part, created far out of the reach of observation. If we were

[P01-345] in possession of all the historical testimonies, we never could wholly

[P01-346] explain the origin of the Iliad and the Odyssey; for their origin, in

[P01-347] all essential points, must have remained the secret of the poet.”

[P01-348] From this criticism, which shows as much insight into the depths of

[P01-349] human nature as into the minute wire-drawings of scholastic

[P01-350] investigation, let us pass on to the main question at issue. Was Homer

[P01-351] an individual? or were the Iliad and Odyssey the result of an ingenious

[P01-352] arrangement of fragments by earlier poets?

[P01-353] Well has Landor remarked: “Some tell us there were twenty Homers; some

[P01-354] deny that there was ever one. It were idle and foolish to shake the

[P01-355] contents of a vase, in order to let them settle at last. We are

[P01-356] perpetually labouring to destroy our delights, our composure, our

[P01-357] devotion to superior power. Of all the animals on earth we least know

[P01-358] what is good for us. My opinion is, that what is best for us is our

[P01-359] admiration of good. No man living venerates Homer more than I do.”

[P01-360] But, greatly as we admire the generous enthusiasm which rests contented

[P01-361] with the poetry on which its best impulses had been nurtured and

[P01-362] fostered, without seeking to destroy the vividness of first impressions

[P01-363] by minute analysis, our editorial office compels us to give some

[P01-364] attention to the doubts and difficulties with which the Homeric

[P01-365] question is beset, and to entreat our reader, for a brief period, to

[P01-366] prefer his judgment to his imagination, and to condescend to dry

[P01-367] details. Before, however, entering into particulars respecting the

[P01-368] question of this unity of the Homeric poems, (at least of the Iliad,) I

[P01-369] must express my sympathy with the sentiments expressed in the following

[P01-370] remarks:—

[P01-371] “We cannot but think the universal admiration of its unity by the

[P01-372] better, the poetic age of Greece, almost conclusive testimony to its

[P01-373] original composition. It was not till the age of the grammarians that

[P01-374] its primitive integrity was called in question; nor is it injustice to

[P01-375] assert, that the minute and analytical spirit of a grammarian is not

[P01-376] the best qualification for the profound feeling, the comprehensive

[P01-377] conception of an harmonious whole. The most exquisite anatomist may be

[P01-378] no judge of the symmetry of the human frame; and we would take the

[P01-379] opinion of Chantrey or Westmacott on the proportions and general beauty

[P01-380] of a form, rather than that of Mr. Brodie or Sir Astley Cooper.

[P01-381] “There is some truth, though some malicious exaggeration, in the lines

[P01-382] of Pope:—

[P01-383] “‘The critic eye—that microscope of wit—

[P01-384] Sees hairs and pores, examines bit by bit;

[P01-385] How parts relate to parts, or they to whole.

[P01-386] The body’s harmony, the beaming soul,

[P01-387] Are things which Kuster, Burmann, Wasse, shall see,

[P01-388] When man’s whole frame is obvious to a flea.’”

[P01-389] Long was the time which elapsed before any one dreamt of questioning

[P01-390] the unity of the authorship of the Homeric poems. The grave and

[P01-391] cautious Thucydides quoted without hesitation the Hymn to [[apollo|Apollo]], the

[P01-747] great works to a plurality of authors, the most powerful internal

[P01-748] evidence, and that which springs from the deepest and most immediate

[P01-749] impulse of the soul, also speaks eloquently to the contrary.

[P01-750] The minutiae of verbal criticism I am far from seeking to despise.

[P01-751] Indeed, considering the character of some of my own books, such an

[P01-752] attempt would be gross inconsistency. But, while I appreciate its

[P01-753] importance in a philological view, I am inclined to set little store on

[P01-754] its aesthetic value, especially in poetry. Three parts of the

[P01-755] emendations made upon poets are mere alterations, some of which, had

[P01-756] they been suggested to the author by his Maecenas or Africanus, he

[P01-757] would probably have adopted. Moreover, those who are most exact in

[P01-758] laying down rules of verbal criticism and interpretation, are often

[P01-759] least competent to carry out their own precepts. Grammarians are not

[P01-760] poets by profession, but may be so _per accidens_. I do not at this

[P01-761] moment remember two emendations on Homer, calculated to substantially

[P01-762] improve the poetry of a passage, although a mass of remarks, from

[P01-763] Herodotus down to Loewe, have given us the history of a thousand minute

[P01-764] points, without which our Greek knowledge would be gloomy and jejune.

[P01-765] But it is not on words only that grammarians, mere grammarians, will

[P01-766] exercise their elaborate and often tiresome ingenuity. Binding down an

[P01-767] heroic or dramatic poet to the block upon which they have previously

[P01-768] dissected his words and sentences, they proceed to use the axe and the

[P01-769] pruning knife by wholesale; and, inconsistent in everything but their

[P01-770] wish to make out a case of unlawful affiliation, they cut out book

[P01-771] after book, passage after passage, till the author is reduced to a

[P01-772] collection of fragments, or till those who fancied they possessed the

[P01-773] works of some great man, find that they have been put off with a vile

[P01-774] counterfeit got up at second hand. If we compare the theories of

[P01-775] Knight, Wolf, Lachmann; and others, we shall feel better satisfied of

[P01-776] the utter uncertainty of criticism than of the apocryphal position of

[P01-777] Homer. One rejects what another considers the turning-point of his

[P01-778] theory. One cuts a supposed knot by expunging what another would

[P01-779] explain by omitting something else.

[P01-780] Nor is this morbid species of sagacity by any means to be looked upon

[P01-781] as a literary novelty. Justus Lipsius, a scholar of no ordinary skill,

[P01-782] seems to revel in the imaginary discovery, that the tragedies

[P01-783] attributed to Seneca are by _four_ different authors. Now, I will

[P01-784] venture to assert, that these tragedies are so uniform, not only in

[P01-785] their borrowed phraseology—a phraseology with which writers like

[P01-786] Boethius and Saxo Grammaticus were more charmed than ourselves—in their

[P01-787] freedom from real poetry, and last, but not least, in an ultra-refined

[P01-788] and consistent abandonment of good taste, that few writers of the

[P01-789] present day would question the capabilities of the same gentleman, be

[P01-790] he Seneca or not, to produce not only these, but a great many more

[P01-791] equally bad. With equal sagacity, Father Hardouin astonished the world

[P01-792] with the startling announcement that the AEneid of Virgil, and the

[P01-793] satires of Horace, were literary deceptions. Now, without wishing to

[P01-794] say one word of disrespect against the industry and learning—nay, the

[P01-795] refined acuteness—which scholars like Wolf have bestowed upon this

[P01-796] subject, I must express my fears, that many of our modern Homeric

[P01-797] theories will become matter for the surprise and entertainment, rather

[P01-798] than the instruction, of posterity. Nor can I help thinking that the

[P01-799] literary history of more recent times will account for many points of

[P01-800] difficulty in the transmission of the Iliad and Odyssey to a period so

[P01-801] remote from that of their first creation.

[P01-802] I have already expressed my belief that the labours of Peisistratus

[P01-803] were of a purely editorial character; and there seems no more reason

[P01-804] why corrupt and imperfect editions of Homer may not have been abroad in

[P01-805] his day, than that the poems of Valerius Flaccus and Tibullus should

[P01-806] have given so much trouble to Poggio, Scaliger, and others. But, after

[P01-807] all, the main fault in all the Homeric theories is, that they demand

[P01-808] too great a sacrifice of those feelings to which poetry most powerfully

[P01-809] appeals, and which are its most fitting judges. The ingenuity which has

[P01-810] sought to rob us of the name and existence of Homer, does too much

[P01-811] violence to that inward emotion, which makes our whole soul yearn with

[P01-812] love and admiration for the blind bard of Chios. To believe the author

[P01-813] of the Iliad a mere compiler, is to degrade the powers of human

[P01-814] invention; to elevate analytical judgment at the expense of the most

[P01-815] ennobling impulses of the soul; and to forget the ocean in the

[P01-816] contemplation of a polypus. There is a catholicity, so to speak, in the

[P01-817] very name of Homer. Our faith in the author of the Iliad may be a

[P01-818] mistaken one, but as yet nobody has taught us a better.

## Section 5

[P01-819] While, however, I look upon the belief in Homer as one that has nature

[P01-820] herself for its mainspring; while I can join with old Ennius in

[P01-821] believing in Homer as the ghost, who, like some patron saint, hovers

[P01-822] round the bed of the poet, and even bestows rare gifts from that wealth

[P01-823] of imagination which a host of imitators could not exhaust,—still I am

[P01-824] far from wishing to deny that the author of these great poems found a

[P01-825] rich fund of tradition, a well-stocked mythical storehouse, from whence

[P01-826] he might derive both subject and embellishment. But it is one thing to

[P01-827] _use_ existing romances in the embellishment of a poem, another to

[P01-828] patch up the poem itself from such materials. What consistency of style

[P01-829] and execution can be hoped for from such an attempt? or, rather, what

[P01-830] bad taste and tedium will not be the infallible result?

[P01-831] A blending of popular legends, and a free use of the songs of other

[P01-832] bards, are features perfectly consistent with poetical originality. In

[P01-833] fact, the most original writer is still drawing upon outward

[P01-834] impressions—nay, even his own thoughts are a kind of secondary agents

[P01-835] which support and feed the impulses of imagination. But unless there be

[P01-836] some grand pervading principle—some invisible, yet most distinctly

[P01-837] stamped archetypus of the great whole, a poem like the Iliad can never

[P01-838] come to the birth. Traditions the most picturesque, episodes the most

[P01-839] pathetic, local associations teeming with the thoughts of gods and

[P01-840] great men, may crowd in one mighty vision, or reveal themselves in more

[P01-841] substantial forms to the mind of the poet; but, except the power to

[P01-842] create a grand whole, to which these shall be but as details and

[P01-843] embellishments, be present, we shall have nought but a scrap-book, a

[P01-844] parterre filled with flowers and weeds strangling each other in their

[P01-845] wild redundancy; we shall have a cento of rags and tatters, which will

[P01-846] require little acuteness to detect.

[P01-847] Sensible as I am of the difficulty of disproving a negative, and aware

[P01-848] as I must be of the weighty grounds there are for opposing my belief,

[P01-849] it still seems to me that the Homeric question is one that is reserved

[P01-850] for a higher criticism than it has often obtained. We are not by nature

[P01-851] intended to know all things; still less, to compass the powers by which

[P01-852] the greatest blessings of life have been placed at our disposal. Were

[P01-853] faith no virtue, then we might indeed wonder why God willed our

[P01-854] ignorance on any matter. But we are too well taught the contrary

[P01-855] lesson; and it seems as though our faith should be especially tried,

[P01-856] touching the men and the events which have wrought most influence upon

[P01-857] the condition of humanity. And there is a kind of sacredness attached

[P01-858] to the memory of the great and the good, which seems to bid us repulse

[P01-859] the scepticism which would allegorize their existence into a pleasing

[P01-860] apologue, and measure the giants of intellect by an homaeopathic

[P01-861] dynameter.

[P01-862] Long and habitual reading of Homer appears to familiarize our thoughts

[P01-863] even to his incongruities; or rather, if we read in a right spirit and

[P01-864] with a heartfelt appreciation, we are too much dazzled, too deeply

[P01-865] wrapped in admiration of the whole, to dwell upon the minute spots

[P01-866] which mere analysis can discover. In reading an heroic poem, we must

[P01-867] transform ourselves into heroes of the time being, we in imagination

[P01-868] must fight over the same battles, woo the same loves, burn with the

[P01-869] same sense of injury, as an Achilles or a Hector. And if we can but

[P01-870] attain this degree of enthusiasm (and less enthusiasm will scarcely

[P01-871] suffice for the reading of Homer), we shall feel that the poems of

[P01-872] Homer are not only the work of one writer, but of the greatest writer

[P01-873] that ever touched the hearts of men by the power of song.

[P01-874] And it was this supposed unity of authorship which gave these poems

[P01-875] their powerful influence over the minds of the men of old. Heeren, who

[P01-876] is evidently little disposed in favour of modern theories, finely

[P01-877] observes:—

[P01-878] “It was Homer who formed the character of the Greek nation. No poet has

[P01-879] ever, as a poet, exercised a similar influence over his countrymen.

[P01-880] Prophets, lawgivers, and sages have formed the character of other

[P01-881] nations; it was reserved to a poet to form that of the Greeks. This is

[P01-882] a feature in their character which was not wholly erased even in the

[P01-883] period of their degeneracy. When lawgivers and sages appeared in

[P01-884] Greece, the work of the poet had already been accomplished; and they

[P01-885] paid homage to his superior genius. He held up before his nation the

[P01-886] mirror in which they were to behold the world of gods and heroes, no

[P01-887] less than of feeble mortals, and to behold them reflected with purity

[P01-888] and truth. His poems are founded on the first feeling of human nature;

[P01-889] on the love of children, wife, and country; on that passion which

[P01-890] outweighs all others, the love of glory. His songs were poured forth

[P01-891] from a breast which sympathized with all the feelings of man; and

[P01-892] therefore they enter, and will continue to enter, every breast which

[P01-893] cherishes the same sympathies. If it is granted to his immortal spirit,

[P01-894] from another heaven than any of which he dreamed on earth, to look down

[P01-895] on his race, to see the nations from the fields of Asia, to the forests

[P01-896] of Hercynia, performing pilgrimages to the fountain which his magic

[P01-897] wand caused to flow; if it is permitted to him to view the vast

[P01-898] assemblage of grand, of elevated, of glorious productions, which had

[P01-899] been called into being by means of his songs; wherever his immortal

[P01-900] spirit may reside, this alone would suffice to complete his happiness.”

[P01-901] Can we contemplate that ancient monument, on which the “Apotheosis of

[P01-902] Homer” is depictured, and not feel how much of pleasing association,

[P01-903] how much that appeals most forcibly and most distinctly to our minds,

[P01-904] is lost by the admittance of any theory but our old tradition? The more

[P01-905] we read, and the more we think—think as becomes the readers of

[P01-906] Homer,—the more rooted becomes the conviction that the Father of Poetry

[P01-907] gave us this rich inheritance, whole and entire. Whatever were the

[P01-908] means of its preservation, let us rather be thankful for the treasury

[P01-909] of taste and eloquence thus laid open to our use, than seek to make it

[P01-910] a mere centre around which to drive a series of theories, whose

[P01-911] wildness is only equalled by their inconsistency with each other.

[P01-912] As the hymns, and some other poems usually ascribed to Homer, are not

[P01-913] included in Pope’s translation, I will content myself with a brief

[P01-914] account of the Battle of the Frogs and Mice, from the pen of a writer

[P01-915] who has done it full justice:—

[P01-916] “This poem,” says Coleridge, “is a short mock-heroic of ancient date.

[P01-917] The text varies in different editions, and is obviously disturbed and

[P01-918] corrupt to a great degree; it is commonly said to have been a juvenile

[P01-919] essay of Homer’s genius; others have attributed it to the same Pigrees

[P01-920] mentioned above, and whose reputation for humour seems to have invited

[P01-921] the appropriation of any piece of ancient wit, the author of which was

[P01-922] uncertain; so little did the Greeks, before the age of the Ptolemies,

[P01-923] know or care about that department of criticism employed in determining

[P01-924] the genuineness of ancient writings. As to this little poem being a

[P01-925] youthful prolusion of Homer, it seems sufficient to say that from the

[P01-926] beginning to the end, it is a plain and palpable parody, not only of

[P01-927] the general spirit, but of numerous passages of the Iliad itself; and,

[P01-928] even if no such intention to parody were discernible in it, the

[P01-929] objection would still remain, that to suppose a work of mere burlesque

[P01-930] to be the primary effort of poetry in a simple age, seems to reverse

[P01-931] that order in the development of national taste, which the history of

[P01-932] every other people in Europe, and of many in Asia, has almost

[P01-933] ascertained to be a law of the human mind; it is in a state of society

[P01-934] much more refined and permanent than that described in the Iliad, that

[P01-935] any popularity would attend such a ridicule of war and the gods as is

[P01-936] contained in this poem; and the fact of there having existed three

[P01-937] other poems of the same kind attributed, for aught we can see, with as

[P01-938] much reason to Homer, is a strong inducement to believe that none of

[P01-939] them were of the Homeric age. Knight infers from the usage of the word

[P01-940] δὲλτος, ‘writing tablet,’ instead of διφθέρα, ‘skin,’ which, according

[P01-941] to Herod 5, 58, was the material employed by the Asiatic Greeks for

[P01-942] that purpose, that this poem was another offspring of Attic ingenuity;

[P01-943] and generally that the familiar mention of the cock (v. 191) is a

[P01-944] strong argument against so ancient a date for its composition.”

[P01-945] Having thus given a brief account of the poems comprised in Pope’s

[P01-946] design, I will now proceed to make a few remarks on his translation,

[P01-947] and on my own purpose in the present edition.

[P01-948] Pope was not a Grecian. His whole education had been irregular, and his

[P01-949] earliest acquaintance with the poet was through the version of Ogilby.

[P01-950] It is not too much to say that his whole work bears the impress of a

[P01-951] disposition to be satisfied with the general sense, rather than to dive

[P01-952] deeply into the minute and delicate features of language. Hence his

[P01-953] whole work is to be looked upon rather as an elegant paraphrase than a

[P01-954] translation. There are, to be sure, certain conventional anecdotes,

[P01-955] which prove that Pope consulted various friends, whose classical

[P01-956] attainments were sounder than his own, during the undertaking; but it

[P01-957] is probable that these examinations were the result rather of the

[P01-958] contradictory versions already existing, than of a desire to make a

[P01-959] perfect transcript of the original. And in those days, what is called

[P01-960] literal translation was less cultivated than at present. If something

[P01-961] like the general sense could be decorated with the easy gracefulness of

[P01-962] a practised poet; if the charms of metrical cadence and a pleasing

[P01-963] fluency could be made consistent with a fair interpretation of the

[P01-964] poet’s meaning, his words were less jealously sought for, and those who

[P01-965] could read so good a poem as Pope’s Iliad had fair reason to be

[P01-966] satisfied.

[P01-967] It would be absurd, therefore, to test Pope’s translation by our own

[P01-968] advancing knowledge of the original text. We must be content to look at

[P01-969] it as a most delightful work in itself,—a work which is as much a part

[P01-970] of English literature as Homer himself is of Greek. We must not be torn

[P01-971] from our kindly associations with the old Iliad, that once was our most

[P01-972] cherished companion, or our most looked-for prize, merely because

[P01-973] Buttmann, Loewe, and Liddell have made us so much more accurate as to

[P01-974] ἀμφικύπελλον being an adjective, and not a substantive. Far be it from

[P01-975] us to defend the faults of Pope, especially when we think of Chapman’s

[P01-976] fine, bold, rough old English;—far be it from us to hold up his

[P01-977] translation as what a translation of Homer _might_ be. But we can still

[P01-978] dismiss Pope’s Iliad to the hands of our readers, with the

[P01-979] consciousness that they must have read a very great number of books

[P01-980] before they have read its fellow.
