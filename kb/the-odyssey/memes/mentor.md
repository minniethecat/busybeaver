# Mentor

## Identity & Names

**Mentor** (Greek: Μέντωρ) is an aged Ithacan nobleman, "the son of Alcinor" [P01-191], and one of Odysseus's most trusted friends. In Pope's *Odyssey*, Mentor is consistently described as "faithful Mentor" [002-270, 017-086] and "sage Mentor" [002-322], a figure whose wisdom and loyalty make him the natural vessel for Athena's guidance.

Mentor's historical identity in the poem is inseparable from Athena's use of his form. The goddess repeatedly assumes "Mentor's friendly form" [022-234] and "sage Mentor's form" [002-322] to intervene in mortal affairs. So complete is this identification that when Pallas Athena aids Telemachus or Odysseus in Mentor's shape, the text often simply names her "Mentor" — blurring the line between the mortal friend and the divine patron.

Mentor's name has passed into the English language as the common noun for a trusted guide and teacher — a testament to the enduring power of this character's role in the epic.

## Role & Significance

Mentor's role in the *Odyssey* is unique: he is simultaneously a mortal character — a real Ithacan noble who knew Odysseus — and the preferred disguise of Athena. This duality makes him the poem's embodiment of wise counsel and divine guidance.

As a mortal, Mentor was entrusted by Odysseus with the care of his household during the Trojan War:

> Mentor arose, Ulysses' faithful friend:
> "My friend (he cried), my palace be thy care." [002-270]

Odysseus, departing for Troy, committed his household to Mentor's oversight — an arrangement that testifies to Mentor's standing in Ithaca and the depth of trust between the two men. When Telemachus calls the assembly in Book 2, the real Mentor rises to rebuke the suitors and the passive Ithacans who tolerate their abuse. His speech is one of the poem's clearest moral indictments:

> True, while my friend is grieved, his griefs I share;
> Yet now the sons of pride my counsel dare. [002-283]

But the "Mentor" who actually accompanies Telemachus to Pylos and guides his transformation is Athena in Mentor's shape. This divine adoption of Mentor's identity elevates the mortal counsellor into something more: a bridge between human wisdom and divine will.

### Mentor and the *Telemachy*

Athena-as-Mentor is the driving force of Telemachus's coming-of-age journey. She appears as Mentor to:
- Encourage Telemachus to call the assembly [002-012]
- Procure a ship and crew for the voyage [002-302]
- Accompany him to Nestor's court at Pylos: "Oh tell me, Mentor! tell me, faithful guide" [003-043]
- Counsel him through the delicate diplomacy with kings

In each instance, "Mentor" provides the courage, practical assistance, and moral authority that the young Telemachus lacks. The device is poignant: Telemachus is being guided by his father's oldest friend — or rather, by the goddess wearing that friend's face — creating a chain of loyalty that links Odysseus → Mentor → Telemachus → Athena.

## Relationships

- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]** — His dearest friend, who entrusted the care of his household to Mentor before sailing to Troy. The bond between them is described as one of unwavering fidelity: "Ulysses' faithful friend" [002-270].
- **[[telemachus|Telemachus]]** — The young prince whom Mentor counsels and protects. Their relationship, mediated by Athena's use of Mentor's form, is the tutorial that transforms Telemachus from a passive youth into a man capable of standing beside his father.
- **[[athena|Athena]]** — The goddess who repeatedly assumes Mentor's form. The choice is not arbitrary: Mentor's established authority and his known loyalty to Odysseus make his appearance the perfect vehicle for divine intervention. "Sage Mentor's form, and thus like Mentor spoke" [002-322].
- **[[halitherses|Halitherses]]** — Fellow Ithacan elder and ally, mentioned alongside Mentor in Telemachus's appeal for assistance: "To arm the vessel, Mentor! be thy care, / And Halitherses! thine" [002-302].
- **The Suitors** — The antagonists who mock Mentor (both real and divine) but ultimately fall before Athena's power in the battle that Mentor's form witnesses and aids.

## Analysis (optional — `standard`+ quality)

### The Disguise as Narrative Device

Athena's use of Mentor's form is one of the poem's most sophisticated narrative devices. By "borrowing" a mortal identity, Athena achieves three things at once: she bestows divine authority on mortal counsel; she preserves the epic's decorum (gods seldom walk openly among men in the *Odyssey*); and she creates dramatic irony — the audience knows that the "Mentor" speaking is Athena, but the mortal characters do not.

The device also has emotional resonance. Telemachus never knew his father; Mentor is the closest thing to paternal wisdom available to him. When "Mentor" counsels him, the audience feels both the comfort of paternal guidance and the pathos that it takes a goddess's intervention to provide what Odysseus's absence has denied his son.

### The Withdrawal of Mentor in Book 22

Athena's most enigmatic "Mentor" moment occurs during the battle in the hall. After initially appearing at Odysseus's side, she deliberately withdraws to test his mettle. The suitor Agelaus interprets this as cowardice and mockery:

> Lo! Mentor leaves him with an empty boast. [022-285]

This withdrawal is crucial: it demonstrates that while divine aid is available to the hero, it is not unconditional. Odysseus must prove himself worthy. The "Mentor" who departs leaves the hero to fight his own battle — a final test before the recognition and reunion that follow.
