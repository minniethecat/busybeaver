# Euryclea

## Identity & Names

Euryclea (Greek: Εὐρύκλεια, _Eurykleia_) is the aged nurse of the house of [[odysseus|Odysseus]] in Ithaca. Her name means "wide-famed" or "far-renowned" (εὐρύ + κλέος), an ironic resonance for a woman whose fame rests entirely on her intimate service within a single household. In Pope's translation she appears as "Euryclea," described as "the sage" and "the wise."

> "The lighted torch, the sage Euryclea bears / (Daughter of Ops, the just Pisenor's son)"
> [001-557]

She is the daughter of Ops, son of Pisenor, and has served the household since [[laertes|Laertes]] purchased her in her youth for the price of twenty oxen — a substantial sum that indicates her value. Laertes "honoured her even as his own wife, but never lay with her, for he feared his wife's anger" — a detail that establishes both her status and the household's moral order.

## Role & Significance

Euryclea serves as the keeper of the household's domestic order and the guardian of its secrets. Her role is threefold:

1. **Nurse to Odysseus and Telemachus**: She raised Odysseus as an infant — "I never found any other woman to love as I loved you, and you the child I nursed" — and later raised [[telemachus|Telemachus]], establishing a bond deeper than mere servitude. She alone calls Odysseus by the intimate name "my child" (τέκνον ἐμόν).
2. **Gatekeeper of the household**: She guards the doors of the women's quarters and controls access to the treasure chamber:
   > "Fast by the door the wise Euryclea waits; / Euryclea, who great Ops! thy lineage shared"
   > [002-407]
3. **Witness to the recognition**: She is the first mortal in Ithaca to recognize Odysseus upon his return, discovering the scar on his thigh while washing his feet — the pivotal recognition scene that occupies most of Book 19.

Her loyalty stands in stark contrast to the twelve disloyal maidservants — including [[melantho|Melantho]] — who consort with [[the-suitors|the Suitors]] and are eventually hanged. This binary of female fidelity structures the domestic drama of the second half of the poem.

## Narrative Function

Euryclea is the poem's primary instrument of domestic recognition. Where [[argos|Argos]] the dog recognizes Odysseus by scent (Book 17) and [[penelope|Penelope]] demands proof through the secret of the marriage bed (Book 23), Euryclea's recognition is tactile and involuntary — she touches the scar and knows. The scene's power lies in its physicality: "the old woman, holding his leg in the flat of her hands, recognized the scar as she handled it."

Her recognition is also the poem's greatest threat to Odysseus's disguise. When she nearly cries out, Odysseus seizes her throat and threatens to kill her — "Nurse, would you destroy me? You yourself suckled me at your breast" — a moment that conflates violence and intimacy with shocking directness.

## Appearances

### Book 1: First Introduction

Euryclea attends Telemachus, lighting his way to bed with torches. The epithets "sage" and "wise" are immediately established:

> "The lighted torch, the sage Euryclea bears"
> [001-557]

### Book 2: Guardian of the Departure

As Telemachus prepares his secret journey to [[pylos|Pylos]] and [[sparta|Sparta]], Euryclea alone is entrusted with the secret. She fetches provisions from the storehouse and swears a solemn oath not to inform [[penelope|Penelope]] until twelve days have passed — a vow that requires her to lie to her mistress and risk her wrath:

> "Fast by the door the wise Euryclea waits; / And watch'd all night, all day, a faithful guard"
> [002-407]

### Book 4: Counsel to Penelope

When [[penelope|Penelope]] learns of Telemachus's departure and falls into despair, Euryclea confesses her role and counsels endurance:

> "Then Euryclea thus: 'My dearest dread; / Though to the sword I bow this hoary head'"
> [004-990]

Her confession transforms Penelope's fear into grief tempered by information — she now knows Telemachus left voluntarily, with provisions, rather than being abducted.

### Book 19: The Recognition (central scene)

Odysseus, disguised as a beggar, allows his feet to be washed — a test of the household's hospitality and a calculated risk. Euryclea performs the office:

> "Rise, Euryclea! with officious care / For the poor friend the cleansing bath prepare"
> [019-427]

As she washes, her hand finds the scar — the mark left by a boar's tusk during Odysseus's youth on [[parnassus|Parnassus]], when he visited his grandfather [[autolycus|Autolycus]]. The narrative then breaks into a long digression (the "Scar Episode") recounting how Odysseus received the wound and how Autolycus named him. When Euryclea's hand touches the scar:

> "When Euryclea found, the ablution ceased"
> [019-427]

She looks up at Odysseus's face, and her eyes go to Penelope — but Odysseus seizes her throat and whispers his threat. She swears silence, and the recognition becomes a shared secret between master and nurse, a conspiracy of the loyal that will erupt in Book 22.

### Books 20–22: Aiding the Reckoning

On the day of the slaughter, Euryclea is ordered to lock the women in their quarters and keep them there regardless of what they hear. After the suitors are killed, Odysseus summons her to identify which of the fifty maidservants were disloyal:

- She names twelve who "dishonoured me and my mother" — the women who slept with the suitors.
- These twelve are forced to clean the hall of blood and corpses before being hanged together.
- Euryclea then fetches sulphur and fire to purify the hall — an act that bridges the slaughter and the restoration of domestic order.

### Book 23: Bearing the News

Euryclea wakes [[penelope|Penelope]] with the news of Odysseus's return and the suitors' death. Penelope's refusal to believe her — "the gods have driven you mad" — sets up the final recognition scene between husband and wife, the poem's most psychologically complex moment. Euryclea's role shifts from guardian of secrets to herald of joy, though her news is initially rejected.

## Relationships

- **Father**: Ops, son of Pisenor — a lineage recorded because her purchase by Laertes was a transaction between noble houses [001-557].
- **Masters across generations**: [[laertes|Laertes]] (who purchased her for twenty oxen), [[odysseus|Odysseus]] (whom she nursed), [[telemachus|Telemachus]] (whom she also raised) — she has served three generations of the same house.
- **Household peers**: [[eurynome|Eurynome]] — the housekeeper who shares domestic authority with Euryclea but lacks her intimate connection to the family.
- **Narrative foils**: [[melantho|Melantho]] (the disloyal maidservant who sleeps with [[eurymachus|Eurymachus]]), [[melanthius|Melanthius]] (the goatherd who sides with the suitors) — together they define the binary of loyalty and betrayal in the household.
- **Mistress**: [[penelope|Penelope]] — Euryclea serves her faithfully but keeps secrets from her on two occasions (Telemachus's departure and Odysseus's identity), a tension that tests the boundary between servant loyalty and family loyalty.

## The Scar Episode

The recognition scar is one of the _Odyssey_'s most famous narrative devices. The scar on Odysseus's thigh was given by a boar during a hunt on Mount [[parnassus|Parnassus]], when young Odysseus visited his maternal grandfather [[autolycus|Autolycus]] — the man who named him. The scar functions as:

1. **A mark of identity**: It is the physical proof that the beggar is Odysseus, recognized by the one person who has touched his body since infancy.
2. **A narrative hinge**: The digression into the scar's origin (the so-called "Scar Episode," some seventy lines) delays the recognition at the moment of maximum tension, creating what Erich Auerbach famously analyzed as the Homeric technique of "foregrounding" — every detail, however digressive, is brought into full light.
3. **A generational link**: Through Autolycus, the scar connects Odysseus's identity to his maternal lineage, his naming, and his coming-of-age as a hunter.
