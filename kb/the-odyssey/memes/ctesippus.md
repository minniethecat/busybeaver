# Ctesippus

Ctesippus is the suitor from Same whose act of violence against the disguised Odysseus — hurling an ox-hoof at a suppliant — marks the moral nadir of the suitors' behavior and provides one of the epic's most direct justifications for their slaughter.

## Identity & Names

The name Ctesippus (Greek: Κτησιππος, *Ktesippos*) derives from *ktesis* (possession, acquisition) and *hippos* (horse), suggesting a man of wealth and status. Pope identifies him by name when he enters the narrative:

> "(Ctesippus named); this lord Ulysses eyed" [020-367]

He is from Same (modern Cephalonia), one of the islands of Odysseus's kingdom.

## Role & Significance

Ctesippus is a mortal suitor from Same. Among the suitors, he represents the **lowest rung of moral degradation** — the man whose crudeness and violence strip away any pretense of nobility:

1. **Violator of xenia**: He throws an ox-hoof at a suppliant [020-367] — an act that violates the most fundamental law of hospitality.
2. **Object-lesson in impiety**: His behavior is so egregious that even the other suitors rebuke him, demonstrating that his crime exceeds even their degraded standards.
3. **Symbolic target**: In the slaughter, his is one of the most personally satisfying deaths — killed by Philoetius, the loyal cowherd, with a spear through the chest [022-319], [022-327].

## Appearances

### Book 20 — The Ox-Hoof

At the final feast, Ctesippus, emboldened by wine and the suitors' license, throws an ox-hoof at the disguised Odysseus. The narrator identifies him:

> "(Ctesippus named); this lord Ulysses eyed" [020-367]

Odysseus dodges the projectile and responds with a silent, grim smile — a moment of terrifying restraint that prefigures the slaughter to come. Even the suitors are shocked by Ctesippus's breach of decorum, and Telemachus rebukes them all.

### Book 22 — The Death

In the slaughter, Ctesippus throws a spear at Odysseus but misses. Philoetius, the loyal cowherd, answers:

> "And from Ctesippus' arm the spear elanced" [022-319]

> "...through the breast the rude Ctesippus bled" [022-327]

The cowherd's taunt — calling him "rude Ctesippus" — echoes the ox-hoof insult and completes the poetic justice of his death. The man who threw an ox-hoof is killed by the man who tends oxen.

## Relationships

### Allies

- **The Suitors**: Even among them, Ctesippus is a marginal figure — tolerated for his wealth but despised for his manners.

### Enemies

- **Odysseus**: The disguised king whom Ctesippus assaults. The ox-hoof is the final provocation before the slaughter.
- **Telemachus**: The prince who rebukes Ctesippus after the ox-hoof incident.
- **Philoetius**: The loyal cowherd who kills Ctesippus in the battle [022-327].

## Analysis

### The Final Provocation

Ctesippus's ox-hoof [020-367] is the last in a long series of suitor outrages, and it functions as a dramatic trigger. Until this moment, Odysseus's restraint — however terrifying — has held. The physical assault on his person, in his own hall, while he sits as a suppliant, exhausts the last possibility of mercy. When Odysseus smiles his grim smile after dodging the hoof, the audience knows that the time for warnings is over.

### Poetic Justice

Ctesippus's death at Philoetius's hand [022-327] is one of the epic's most elegant instances of cosmic retribution. The suitor who mocked the disguised king by throwing an animal part is killed by the servant who cares for the king's animals. The symmetry is perfect — the insult returns upon the insulter, transformed into the instrument of his death.

### The Hierarchy of Evil

Among the suitors, Ctesippus occupies the lowest moral tier. Antinous is cruel but formidable; Eurymachus is duplicitous but intelligent; Amphinomus is weak but decent. Ctesippus is simply brutish — a man whose vices have no redeeming qualities and whose death provokes no complexity of response. He exists so that the audience can enjoy his destruction without ambivalence.
