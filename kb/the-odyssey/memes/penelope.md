# Penelope

## Identity & Names

**Penelope** (Greek: Πηνελόπεια, *Penelopeia*) is the queen of [[ithaca|Ithaca]], daughter of [[icarius|Icarius]], wife of [[odysseus|Odysseus]], and mother of [[telemachus|Telemachus]]. Her name may derive from *pēnē* (thread, weft) — fitting for the woman whose weaving defined her strategy of resistance. In Pope's translation she is Penelope throughout. Her defining epithets include "faithful Penelope," "wise Penelope," and "daughter of Icarius". Unlike [[helen|Helen]] or [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], the other great wives of the Trojan War cycle, Penelope's story is one of constancy under siege.

> "Severely chaste Penelope remains; / But, lost to every joy, she wastes the day / In tedious cares, and weeps the night away."

## Role & Significance

Penelope is the quiet center of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* — the fixed point toward which the entire narrative moves. While Odysseus wanders the Mediterranean, Penelope holds Ithaca together through intelligence, emotional endurance, and the most famous deception in Greek literature: for three years, she weaves a burial shroud for [[laertes|Laertes]] by day and secretly unweaves it by night, promising [[the-suitors|the suitors]] she will choose one of them when the shroud is complete. This act of *[[metis]]* — matching her husband's cunning — sustains the kingdom until Odysseus returns.

Homer presents Penelope as Odysseus's equal, not his subordinate. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s shade in [[hades|the Underworld]], contrasting her with his own murderous wife Clytemnestra, declares that Penelope's fame will be immortal. Her intelligence is practical rather than philosophical, domestic rather than martial — but within the *Odyssey*'s value system, this makes her no less heroic.

> "O why, Penelope, this causeless fear, / To render sleep's soft blessing unsincere? / Alike devote to sorrow's dire extreme / The day-reflection, and the midnight-dream! / Thy son the gods propitious will restore."

## Appearances

- **Book 1**: First appears descending the stairs to silence the bard [[phemius|Phemius]]'s song about the Trojan returns, which pains her. Telemachus's rebuke ("go to your chamber") marks the beginning of his assertion of authority.
- **Book 2**: The shroud-weaving ruse is revealed by one of her maids; the suitors demand she choose.
- **Book 4**: Receives news of Telemachus's departure with characteristic anxiety, calmed by Athena in a dream.
- **Book 18**: Appears before the suitors, extracting gifts from each — a tactical display of beauty in service of household economy.
- **Book 19**: The great interview with the disguised Odysseus — a scene of sustained dramatic irony where husband and wife speak without recognition, testing each other.
- **Book 21**: Sets the bow contest, the mechanism of the suitors' destruction.

> "With her own art Penelope inspires / Who now can bend Ulysses' bow, and wing / The well-aim'd arrow through the distant ring, / Shall end the strife, and win the imperial dame"

- **Book 23**: The recognition scene — her final test of the marriage bed proves both her fidelity and her intelligence. "More than woman with deep wisdom crown'd".

## Relationships

- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]**: Her husband and equal. Their marriage is built on shared [[metis]] — each can outwit anyone, including each other. The marriage bed, rooted in the living olive tree, symbolizes the permanence of their bond.
- **[[telemachus|Telemachus]]**: Her son, whom she has raised alone for twenty years. His growth from passive boy to active ally transforms their relationship.
- **The Suitors**: Her antagonists — [[antinous|Antinous]], [[eurymachus|Eurymachus]], and the rest. She pursues a strategy of delay and extraction, turning their own greed against them.
- **[[athena|Athena]]**: Her divine protector, who grants her the gifts of beauty, skill in weaving, and intelligence.
- **[[euryclea|Euryclea]]**: Her loyal nurse, who recognizes Odysseus by his scar and keeps the secret at Penelope's unintentional direction.

## Analysis

Penelope represents a radical redefinition of female virtue in epic. She is neither the passive prize (Helen) nor the treacherous wife (Clytemnestra) but an active agent whose intelligence shapes the plot. Her shroud-weaving is a perfect symbolic act: creation by day, destruction by night, sustaining the household through artful delay. Her final test of Odysseus — the bed that cannot be moved — is an act of epistemological rigor: she trusts no appearance, only verifiable truth.

The Penelope-Odysseus marriage models a particular ideal of partnership: not based on physical presence (they have been apart for twenty years) but on *homophrosyne* — like-mindedness. Both are survivors by wit, both are suspicious of easy answers, both test before trusting. Their reunion is delayed not by external obstacles but by mutual testing — each must prove to the other that twenty years of separation have not changed who they essentially are.

Penelope's grief is as central to her characterization as her cunning. She weeps daily, wastes away, and describes herself as consumed by longing for her absent husband. This grief is not weakness — it is what makes her endurance morally significant. Unlike the suitors who consume Odysseus's household thoughtlessly, Penelope's sorrow is the cost of loyalty, and Homer insists we see it.
