# The Cave of the Nymphs

## Description

The Cave of the Nymphs is a sacred grotto on the shore of Ithaca, described in luminous detail in Book 13. It has two entrances: one facing north, for mortals to enter; the other facing south, for the gods, through which no mortal passes. Inside are stone looms where the Naiad nymphs weave sea-purple cloth, stone jars where bees store honey, and a perpetual spring of fresh water. The cave is dedicated to the nymphs called Naiads, and it has served as a place of worship for Ithacan sailors since before Odysseus's time. It is here that the Phaeacian sailors deposit the sleeping Odysseus along with the gifts of gold, bronze, and woven cloth that Alcinous and the Phaeacian nobles gave him.

## In the Odyssey

**The Deposition (Book 13)**: The Phaeacian ship, traveling at supernatural speed, reaches Ithaca in a single night. The sailors carry the sleeping Odysseus ashore and lay him — still wrapped in the linen sheet — on the sand at the mouth of the cave, piling his treasures nearby. They then depart. When Odysseus wakes, Athena has shrouded the island in mist, and he does not recognize his homeland. The cave is the first place he stands on Ithacan soil after twenty years.

**Athena's Revelation**: Athena, disguised as a young shepherd, approaches Odysseus. After he tells her a false story about his identity, she reveals herself — laughing, golden, and full of affection — and disperses the mist. She shows him that he is home, pointing out the cave's features and the harbor of Phorcys. Together, they hide the Phaeacian treasure in the cave's recesses. The cave thus serves as both the threshold of return and the site of the poem's most intimate divine-human encounter.

**The Planning**: Inside the cave's shelter, Athena and Odysseus sit together and plan the destruction of the suitors. The goddess outlines her strategy — Odysseus must enter his palace disguised as a beggar, test the household, and reveal himself only when the time is right. The sacred cave, with its two doors (mortal and divine), is the perfect setting for this collaboration between man and goddess.

## Significance

The Cave of the Nymphs is the *Odyssey*'s symbolic threshold. Its two entrances — mortal and divine — encode the poem's central relationship: human action guided by divine will. The looms where the nymphs weave sea-purple cloth echo Penelope's loom in the palace above, linking the subterranean sacred space to the domestic space of the household. The hidden treasure represents the wealth Odysseus has accumulated through suffering and storytelling, now stored in a sacred place before he reclaims his house. The cave is the last stop before the domestic world — the place where the hero who crossed the sea becomes the beggar who will enter his own palace.

## Geography

The cave's location on Ithaca is described by Athena with topographic precision: it is near the harbor of Phorcys (the Old Man of the Sea), at the head of a bay, with an olive tree at its top. Despite centuries of attempts to identify the real cave, no archaeological consensus exists. The cave's mythic function — as the interface between worlds — is more significant than its cartographic location.
