# Tiresias

## Identity & Names

Tiresias (Greek: Τειρεσίας, _Teiresias_) is the legendary blind prophet of Thebes, whose shade dwells in the Underworld. He is the son of Everes, a descendant of the Spartoi (the sown-men of Thebes), and the nymph Chariclo. In Pope's translation he appears as "Tiresias," the seer "for wisdom's various arts renown'd." Odysseus travels to the realm of Hades specifically to consult him, as instructed by [[circe|Circe]]:

> "To seek Tiresias' awful shade below, / And thence our fortunes and our fates to know"
> [010-690]

The name _Teiresias_ may derive from the Greek τέρας (_teras_, "sign" or "portent"), reflecting his role as interpreter of divine signs. His blindness — traditionally explained by his having seen [[athena|Athena]] bathing or having been asked to judge a dispute between [[zeus|Zeus]] and [[hera|Hera]] — is paradoxically the source of his insight: he who cannot see the visible world perceives the invisible with perfect clarity.

## Genealogy & Mythological Background

Tiresias belongs to the Theban cycle of myths, a figure whose life spans seven generations, from the founding of Thebes by Cadmus to its destruction by the Epigoni. His parentage — Everes (a Spartos, born from the dragon's teeth sown by Cadmus) and Chariclo (a nymph beloved of Athena) — places him at the intersection of the human and divine, the autochthonous and the Olympian.

Before the events of the _Odyssey_, Tiresias had already lived through the entire tragic history of Thebes: he witnessed the reign and fall of Oedipus, the war of the Seven Against Thebes, and the eventual destruction of the city. His death — traditionally located in the flight from Thebes after its sack — places his shade in the Underworld at the time of Odysseus's visit.

## Role & Significance

Tiresias is unique among the shades Odysseus encounters in the Underworld: he alone retains full prophetic faculties and consciousness, while other shades must drink sacrificial blood before they can speak. His role in the _Odyssey_ is that of the ultimate revealer — he delivers the most complete prophecy of Odysseus's remaining journey and the only vision that extends beyond the hero's return to Ithaca.

Circe's instruction establishes the prophet's singular authority:

> "To seek Tiresias in the nether sky, / To learn my doom"
> [011-215]

Unlike other prophetic figures in the epic ([[halitherses|Halitherses]], [[theoclymenus|Theoclymenus]]), Tiresias's vision extends beyond mortal events to the terms of reconciliation with [[poseidon|Poseidon]]. His prophecy operates on three temporal planes simultaneously:

| Temporal Plane | Content of Prophecy |
|---------------|-------------------|
| **Immediate future** | The danger of the Cattle of the Sun on [[thrinacia|Thrinacia]] |
| **Return to Ithaca** | The suitors in Odysseus's palace and their impending doom |
| **Beyond the epic** | Odysseus's final journey inland, carrying an oar, and his peaceful death "from the sea" |

This tripartite structure makes Tiresias's prophecy the most comprehensive in the poem: it bridges the wandering (Books 9–12), the return (Books 13–24), and the world beyond the narrative frame.

## Appearances

### Book 10: Circe's Directive

Circe informs Odysseus that he must travel to the Underworld to consult Tiresias, making the prophet the stated goal of the entire _Nekyia_ (Book 11). The language she uses — "awful shade," "nether sky" — marks Tiresias as a being of singular authority among the dead [010-690].

### Book 11: The Underworld (primary appearance)

Odysseus performs the prescribed ritual — digging a trench and offering blood — to summon the shades:

> "So shall a ram, the largest of the breed, / Black as these regions, to Tiresias bleed."
> [011-056]

Tiresias rises from the darkness, and the poet marks the moment with solemn brevity:

> "Till awful from the shades Tiresias rose."
> [011-078]

The prophet drinks the sacrificial blood and delivers his revelation. He foretells the perils that remain on Thrinacia, warns against harming the Cattle of the Sun, describes the suitors in Ithaca, and — most remarkably — prophesies Odysseus's final journey inland to appease Poseidon, carrying an oar until he reaches a people who know nothing of the sea. There, he will plant the oar and make sacrifice, and only then will death come to him "from the sea" — gentle, in prosperous old age.

This final prophecy is unique in Homeric epic in extending the hero's biography beyond the poem's narrative frame into a peaceful death — a striking contrast to the violent ends of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and [[achilles|Achilles]].

### Referenced Elsewhere

Tiresias is invoked throughout the epic as the reason for Odysseus's katabasis (descent to the Underworld):

- [[book-10|Book 10]]: Circe's instructions to seek him [010-690]
- [[book-23|Book 23]]: Odysseus recounts the journey to Penelope:
  > "For to Tiresias through the eternal gates / Of hell I trode, to learn my future fates"
  > [023-281]
- The prophet's method — requiring blood before speech — marks the Underworld's fundamental condition: the dead are mute without the living's offering. [[proteus|Proteus]]'s shape-shifting prophecy shares structural similarities with this ritual encounter [023-360].

## The Prophecy in Detail

Tiresias's speech to Odysseus follows a careful rhetorical structure, moving from the general to the specific and from the near to the far:

1. **The Warning**: Poseidon's anger pursues Odysseus for the blinding of [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]], and the Cattle of the Sun on Thrinacia are a mortal peril — harm them, and the hero will lose all his men.
2. **The Return**: Despite these dangers, Odysseus will reach Ithaca, though "late and in evil case, upon a foreign ship, having lost all comrades."
3. **The Reckoning**: He will find the suitors consuming his household, and he must exact justice upon them.
4. **The Final Journey**: After restoring order in Ithaca, Odysseus must journey inland carrying an oar, until he reaches a landlocked people who mistake it for a winnowing fan. There he plants the oar, sacrifices to Poseidon, and receives a death "from the sea" — peaceful and in old age.

This prophecy is the structural backbone of the poem's second half, and its very existence raises the question of fate versus free will in the _Odyssey_ — a tension the poem never fully resolves.

## Relationships

- **Parentage**: Son of Everes (a Spartos) and the nymph Chariclo, placing him in the Theban aristocratic and chthonic lineage.
- **Consulted by**: [[odysseus|Odysseus]], who travels to the edge of the world and descends into [[the-underworld|the Underworld]] at Circe's command.
- **Mentioned alongside**: [[anticlea|Anticlea]] (Odysseus's mother, whose shade appears after Tiresias drinks the blood), [[elpenor|Elpenor]] (whose unburied shade speaks before Tiresias rises).
- **Divine connection**: Retains prophetic power after death by decree of Persephone, who granted him alone among the dead "consciousness and voice" (νοῦν καὶ φρένας, _nous kai phrenas_).
- **Contrast with**: [[halitherses|Halitherses]] and [[theoclymenus|Theoclymenus]] — living prophets whose visions are partial compared to Tiresias's comprehensive knowledge.

## Thematic Significance

Tiresias embodies the _Odyssey_'s most profound paradox: that true sight often requires blindness, and that knowledge of the future is inseparable from confrontation with death. The journey to the Underworld — the _Nekyia_ — is the poem's ontological center, and Tiresias is its gatekeeper. Without his prophecy, Odysseus's homecoming would lack its metaphysical architecture: the promise that suffering, however prolonged, has a terminus, and that beyond the violence of return lies the peace of reconciliation with the divine.
