## Overview

Aeolus (also called Hippotades) is the divine keeper of the winds, appointed by [[zeus|Jove]] himself: "For him the mighty sire of gods assigned / The tempest's lord, the tyrant of the wind" [^1]. His "word alone the listening storms obey, / To smooth the deep, or swell the foamy sea" [^1]. He rules the floating island of [[aeolia|Aeolia]].

## Aeolia and Family

Aeolia is described as a "floating isle, high-raised by toil divine, / Strong walls of brass the rocky coast confine" [^2]. Aeolus lives there with his twelve children — six sons and six daughters, who are married to each other — in a state of perpetual festivity: "All day they feast, all day the bowls flow round, / And joy and music through the isle resound" [^2]. This idyllic domestic arrangement reinforces Aeolus's position as a benevolent, orderly divine ruler.

## The Gift of the Winds

When [[odysseus|Odysseus]] and his crew arrive at Aeolia, they are received with generous hospitality for an entire month. Aeolus "full oft the monarch urged me to relate / The fall of Ilion, and the Grecian fate" [^3], eager to hear tales of the Trojan War. When Odysseus finally asks for departure aid, Aeolus grants him a remarkable gift: "The adverse winds in leathern bags he braced, / Compressed their force, and locked each struggling blast" [^4]. Only [[zephyrus|Zephyrus]], the favorable west wind, is left free to guide the ships toward Ithaca. The bag is "securely fettered by a silver thong" [^4].

## The Crew's Folly

Nine days of smooth sailing bring Ithaca within sight. But while Odysseus sleeps, his crew — suspecting the bag contains gold and treasure — open it. All the adverse winds burst forth, driving the fleet back to Aeolia in a single stroke. When Odysseus returns to beg for help a second time, Aeolus refuses, declaring that a man so clearly hated by the gods cannot be aided: "From friendly Aeolus with prosperous gales / Yet fate withstands!" [^5].

## Significance

Aeolus embodies the boundary between divine favor and human limitation. His initial generosity demonstrates the gods' capacity for kindness, but his refusal to help a second time underscores a recurring Odyssean theme: that fortune, once squandered through folly, cannot be reclaimed by asking twice.
