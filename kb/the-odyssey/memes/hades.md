# Hades

## Description

Hades is the realm of the dead — not a place of punishment (that is Tartarus, deeper still) but the common destination of all mortal souls, where the psychai (shades) dwell in a shadowy half-existence, bereft of strength, memory, and identity unless revived by the offering of blood. Its entrance lies at the western edge of the world, beyond the stream of Oceanus, in a land of perpetual darkness where the Cimmerians dwell. To reach it, Odysseus sails beyond the known world, following Circe's directions: beach the ship at the shore of Oceanus, dig a pit, pour libations of milk, honey, wine, and water, sprinkle barley, and sacrifice a black ram and a black ewe so that their blood pools in the pit. The shades, drawn by the blood, swarm up from Erebus — "And all the dire assembly shriek'd around" (011-068) — and Odysseus must hold them at sword-point until Tiresias drinks first.

## In the Odyssey

**The Nekyia (Book 11)**: Odysseus's journey to the entrance of Hades is the center of the epic. Tiresias, the blind Theban seer, drinks the blood and delivers his three-part prophecy: Odysseus will reach home, but only after suffering; he must not harm the Cattle of the Sun on Thrinacia; and after reclaiming his household, he must journey inland carrying an oar until he meets a people who do not know the sea, there to make sacrifice to Poseidon. Following Tiresias, a procession of shades approaches: Anticlea (Odysseus's mother, who died of grief waiting for him), the great heroines of myth, and his fallen comrades from Troy — Agamemnon (murdered by Clytemnestra), Achilles (who would rather be a living slave than king of the dead), and Ajax (who refuses to speak, still wounded by his loss to Odysseus in the contest for Achilles's arms).

**Hermes's Escort (Book 24)**: In the final book, Hermes Psychopompus (the guide of souls) leads the shades of the slaughtered suitors down to Hades. They encounter the shades of Achilles and Agamemnon, and the dead suitor Amphimedon tells Agamemnon the story of Odysseus's return and their slaughter — a mirror of the Nekyia that bookends the epic.

## Significance

Hades is the *Odyssey*'s structural and theological center. The Nekyia (Book 11) marks the poem's midpoint, and the encounter with the dead transforms Odysseus's understanding of his journey. Tiresias's prophecy extends the *Odyssey* beyond its own ending — nostos is not complete with the recovery of Ithaca but requires reconciliation with Poseidon, a journey that no mortal has undertaken. The parade of the dead situates Odysseus's suffering within the larger tragedy of the Trojan War generation. And Achilles's famous words — that he would rather be a living slave than king of all the dead — are the poem's most powerful statement of the value of life and the horror of the unremembered dead.

## Geography

Hades has no geographical location in the ordinary sense. It lies beyond the stream of Oceanus, at the edge of the world, in the land of the Cimmerians who live in perpetual darkness. Ancient commentators placed it variously at the western edge of the known world or beneath the earth. Later tradition distinguished Hades (the realm) from Tartarus (the prison of the Titans), but in Homer, Hades is the common destination of all mortal souls.
