## Identity and Names

Antilochus (Greek: Ἀντίλοχος, *Antílochos*) is the son of Nestor, king of Pylos, and in some traditions Eurydice. He appears briefly in the *Iliad* as a swift runner and loyal companion — it is Antilochus who brings Achilles the news of Patroclus's death. His own death at the hands of Memnon is recounted in the lost epic *Aethiopis*; the *Odyssey* assumes this knowledge and dwells instead on the grief his loss leaves behind.

The etymology of his name — "instead of ambush" (ἀντί + λόχος) — hints at the martial character praised by the poem: Antilochus is the warrior who meets the enemy in open combat rather than from concealment. This transparency of character aligns with Nestor's description of "a faultless body and a blameless mind" [003-153].


## Antilochus in the Iliad

The *Odyssey*'s portrait of Antilochus gains depth when read against his *Iliad* appearances. In the earlier epic, Antilochus is a messenger of catastrophe: it is he who brings Achilles the news of Patroclus's death (*Iliad* 18), running "swift of foot" to deliver the worst possible tidings. The epithet "swift of foot" [003-152] that Nestor gives him in lament thus carries a bitter resonance — his son's speed was used not for glory but to carry grief.

In *Iliad* 23, at Patroclus's funeral games, Antilochus competes in the chariot race against Menelaus and wins through a reckless manoeuvre that Menelaus declares unfair. The dispute is resolved when Antilochus yields the prize with grace — an episode that prefigures his characterisation in the *Odyssey* as the youth in whom "stern courage with soft virtue join'd" [003-153]. He is competitive but not ruthless; bold but capable of yielding.


## The Death of Antilochus

The *Aethiopis*, a lost epic of the Trojan cycle attributed to Arctinus of Miletus, narrates Antilochus's death: he falls defending his father Nestor from Memnon, the Ethiopian king and son of Eos (Dawn). Memnon kills Antilochus, and Achilles in turn kills Memnon to avenge his friend. This chain of vengeance — Memnon kills Antilochus, Achilles kills Memnon, Paris (with Apollo's aid) kills Achilles — links Antilochus's death to the deaths of the two greatest heroes of the Trojan War.

The *Odyssey* suppresses the mechanics of Antilochus's death, mentioning only that he died at Troy. The suppression is artful: by focusing entirely on the grief his death leaves behind, the poem makes Antilochus's loss feel more immediate, less mediated by heroic narrative. He is not a warrior who fell in combat but a son who never came home.


## Appearances in the Odyssey

### Book 3 — Nestor's Lament

In recounting the Trojan War dead to Telemachus, Nestor's catalogue of heroes culminates in his own son:

> "There, too, my son — ah, once my best delight / Once swift of foot, and terrible in fight; / In whom stern courage with soft virtue join'd / A faultless body and a blameless mind; / Antilochus — What more can I relate?" [003-151]

Nestor's voice breaks on his son's name: "What more can I relate?" The question is not rhetorical but a genuine failure of speech — the father cannot continue past the name of his dead child. The epithets "faultless body" and "blameless mind" make Antilochus the most comprehensively praised character in the *Odyssey*: no flaw is recorded, only perfection interrupted.

### Book 4 — Menelaus's Pledge

When Menelaus learns of his brother Agamemnon's murder, he proposes a joint mourning — and names Antilochus alongside his own brother as the dead who deserve honour:

> "And mourn the brave Antilochus, a name / Not unrecorded in the rolls of fame; / With strength and speed superior form'd, in fight / To face the foe, or intercept his flight; / Too early snatch'd by fate ere known to me!" [004-287]

Menelaus's grief triangulates: Agamemnon is his blood, Antilochus is the friend he "never knew" but mourns nonetheless. The phrase "too early snatch'd by fate" echoes Nestor's lament, establishing premature death as Antilochus's defining attribute.

### Book 24 — The Funeral Urn

In the second *Nekyia*, Agamemnon's shade describes the burial of Achilles to the newly arrived suitors:

> "There, we thy relics, great Achilles! blend / With dear Patroclus, thy departed friend: / In the same urn a separate space contains / Thy next beloved, Antilochus' remains." [024-108]

The architecture of the urn is precise: Achilles and Patroclus share one space, Antilochus occupies another — "next beloved" but separate. He is ranked third in Achilles's affections, after Patroclus, yet his inclusion in the same great urn at all marks him as among the most honoured of the Greek dead.


## Thematic Significance

Antilochus embodies the *Odyssey*'s meditation on fathers and sons. Nestor's lament for Antilochus is the poem's most direct expression of parental grief, and it resonates throughout Telemachus's journey. When Telemachus visits Pylos, he meets a king whose own son is dead — a living reminder of what Odysseus risks by remaining absent. The parallel is not stated but felt: Nestor lost Antilochus; Telemachus may yet lose Odysseus.

Antilochus also completes the triad of the beloved dead in the *Odyssey*'s underworld: Patroclus (Achilles's companion), Antilochus (Nestor's son), and Achilles himself. Each represents a different bond — erotic friendship, filial love, and heroic glory — and each is remembered as irreplaceable. That Antilochus stands alongside Patroclus and Achilles as the three who share one urn [024-108] is the poem's final judgment: in the economy of grief, a father's love for his son is equal to the greatest heroic friendships.
