# Dike (Justice)

## Definition

**Dike** (Greek: δίκη, *díkē*) is the ancient Greek concept of justice — not merely legal judgment but the proper order of things, the way the world should be arranged. Where modern justice is primarily procedural (fair trials, equal treatment), dike is ontological: it describes a state of right relationship between gods and mortals, kings and subjects, hosts and guests, parents and children. The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* begins in a world where dike has collapsed — the suitors consume another man's household — and ends when dike is restored through [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s return and revenge.

## Greek Context

In Hesiod's *Works and Days*, Dike is a goddess who sits beside [[zeus|Zeus]] and reports human injustices. This personification reflects the Greek conviction that justice is not a human invention but a cosmic reality that the gods uphold. A king who ruled with dike brought prosperity to his land; one who violated it brought famine and ruin. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] himself is described as having ruled "by justice, truth, and probity of mind" (007-291) — a model of the just king whose return the community desperately needs.

## In the Odyssey

### The Collapse of Order
The epic opens with dike in ruins. The suitors have occupied [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s household for years, and [[telemachus|Telemachus]] complains of the "injustice done him by the suitors" (002-005). [[ithaca|Ithaca]] has no king, no assembly with authority, and no mechanism to enforce right order. The community is paralyzed.

### The Restoration
[[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s return is the restoration of dike on every level: social (the king reclaims his throne), domestic (the husband reunites with his wife), economic (the household's resources are no longer looted), and cosmic ([[zeus|Zeus]]'s justice is enacted against the violators of xenia). The slaughter in Book 22 is not revenge in the modern sense but the violent reassertion of order — dike enforced by the returning king.

### Divine Sanction
The poem repeatedly emphasizes that the gods underwrite dike. [[penelope|Penelope]] prays: "Judge and revenge my right, impartial [[zeus|Jove]]!" (001-500). The suitors' destruction is depicted not as [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s personal vengeance but as [[zeus|Zeus]]'s judgment executed through a human agent. [[athena|Athena]]'s presence throughout the final battle confirms divine approval.

## Significance

- **Narrative arc**: The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* is the story of dike restored. Every episode measures the distance from right order, and the entire plot drives toward its reestablishment.
- **Theological foundation**: The poem insists that the cosmos is moral — injustice is punished, if not immediately then inevitably. "Nor is it injustice" (P01-374) to say that the gods enforce dike.
- **Political philosophy**: The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* offers an implicit theory of kingship: the good king maintains dike, and without him the community descends into chaos. This would influence Greek political thought for centuries.
