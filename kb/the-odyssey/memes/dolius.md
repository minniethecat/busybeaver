# Dolius

Dolius is the aged servant of Laertes whose loyalty to Odysseus's house endures despite the treachery of his own children, Melanthius and Melantho. A figure of quiet fidelity, he plays a crucial role in the final book — recognizing the returned Odysseus and, at over eighty years of age, donning his rusted armor to fight for his king.

## Identity & Names

The name Dolius (Greek: Δολιος, *Dolios*) derives from *dolos* (trickery, cunning) — an ironic etymology given his straightforward loyalty, though fitting for the father of the treacherous Melanthius. He is consistently described as "aged" and "venerable" [024-457], marking him as among the oldest members of the household.

## Role & Significance

Dolius is a mortal servant of Laertes's household. His role demonstrates that loyalty can survive even the deepest familial betrayal:

1. **Faithful servant**: Despite his children's treachery, Dolius never wavers in his service to Laertes and Odysseus.
2. **Recognition witness**: He is among the first to acknowledge Odysseus upon his return to Laertes's farm [024-457].
3. **Warrior elder**: At an age when most men have laid down their arms, Dolius takes up his "rusted arms" [024-585] to fight alongside Odysseus against the suitors' avenging families.

## Appearances

### Book 4 — A Mention

Dolius is first mentioned when Penelope, learning of the suitors' plot against Telemachus, calls for a loyal servant:

> "Bid Dolius quick attend, the faithful slave" [004-982]

The epithet "faithful" establishes his character before he ever appears on stage.

### Book 18 — Family Connection

His daughter Melantho is identified by her lineage:

> "Melantho, from the loins of Dolius sprung" [018-375]

The juxtaposition of Dolius's loyalty with his children's betrayal becomes a recurring narrative irony.

### Book 22 — Treachery Discovered

When Melanthius betrays the household by fetching arms for the suitors, Odysseus suspects:

> "Or (as my thought misgives) by Dolius' son" [022-186]

That Melanthius is Dolius's son is stated as an aggravating factor — the son of the loyal servant has become a traitor.

### Book 24 — Recognition and Battle

Dolius appears in person in the final book, working on Laertes's farm. When Odysseus reveals himself, Dolius is the first to embrace him:

> "Dolius, the first, the venerable man..." [024-457]

He spreads his hands in love [024-468]. When the suitors' kinsmen march for revenge, a son of Dolius carries the message [024-578]. Despite his age, Dolius arms himself:

> "And six were all the sons of Dolius' race:
> Old Dolius too his rusted arms put on" [024-584-585]

He fights in the final battle before Athena's intervention restores peace.

## Relationships

### Family

- **Children**: Melanthius (the treacherous goatherd), Melantho (the disloyal servant), and six other sons [024-584] who, unlike their siblings, remain loyal.
- **Master**: Laertes, whom Dolius serves on the farm.

### Allies

- **Odysseus**: The king Dolius recognizes and fights for.
- **Laertes**: Dolius's aged master and companion.
- **His six loyal sons**: The branch of his family that inherits his virtue.

### The Divided Household

Dolius's family embodies the moral division of Ithaca under the suitors. Two of his eight children choose treachery and are punished; six choose loyalty and are rewarded. Dolius himself — the father who outlives his traitor children and fights alongside his loyal ones — represents the triumph of principle over blood.

## Analysis

### The Unshaken Foundation

Dolius represents the bedrock of loyalty upon which Odysseus's kingdom is rebuilt. Unlike Eumaeus (whose loyalty is heroic and vocal) or Philoetius (whose loyalty is passionate), Dolius's fidelity is quiet, patient, and enduring. He has served Laertes for decades; he has waited for Odysseus's return for twenty years; he has watched his own children betray the house he serves — and he has never wavered.

### The Tragedy of the Good Father

Dolius is one of Homer's most poignant minor characters because his goodness does not save his children. Melanthius and Melantho, raised in the same household as their six loyal brothers, choose betrayal. The epic offers no explanation for this division — it simply records it as a fact of human nature. Dolius's tragedy is to be the father of traitors; his triumph is to remain himself faithful.

### Geriatric Heroism

The image of "old Dolius" putting on his "rusted arms" [024-585] — weapons long unused, at an age when his fighting days should be over — is a moving testament to loyalty that transcends physical capacity. His willingness to die for Odysseus completes the arc from Book 4's "faithful slave" [004-982] to Book 24's warrior.
