# Leucothea

**Leucothea** ("White Goddess") is a sea goddess in Greek mythology who takes pity on Odysseus during Poseidon's storm and grants him the magical veil that saves his life. She was originally the mortal Ino, transformed into a divinity after her death.

## Role in the Odyssey

Leucothea appears at a critical moment in Book 5. After Poseidon shatters Odysseus's raft with a terrible storm, Leucothea sees him struggling in the waves: "Leucothea saw, and pity touched her breast" (005-435). Rising from the sea like a diving bird, she gives him her immortal scarf, telling him to wrap it around his chest, swim to the Phaeacian shore, and then throw it back into the sea.

Odysseus follows her instructions: when he reaches land, "The mindful chief Leucothea's scarf unbound" and cast it into the waves (005-599). The goddess retrieves it, and Odysseus survives.

> "Leucothea saw, and pity touch'd her breast. / (A sea-nymph once, by mortal woes opprest)" (005-435)

## Mythological Background

Leucothea was born as Ino, daughter of Cadmus and Harmonia, and sister to Semele (mother of Dionysus). After her death — driven mad by Hera and leaping into the sea with her son Melicertes — she was transformed into the sea goddess Leucothea. Her son became the sea-god Palaemon. In Homer, she acts as a compassionate divine figure, counterbalancing Poseidon's wrath.
