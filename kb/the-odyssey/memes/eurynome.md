# Eurynome

Eurynome is the housekeeper of Odysseus's palace in Ithaca, serving alongside Eurycleia in the domestic management of the royal household. She emerges in the latter books of the *Odyssey* as a figure of loyal service — dutiful, chaste, and trusted with the most intimate domestic tasks of the royal couple. Where Eurycleia is the aged nurse whose recognition of Odysseus's scar triggers the revelation, Eurynome is the quiet attendant whose care ensures the household's dignity is restored after the slaughter.

## Identity and Names

Eurynome (Greek: Εὐρυνόμη, *Eurynómē*, "wide-ranging") bears a name shared by several figures in Greek myth, including an Oceanid. In the *Odyssey*, she is distinguished by her domestic role within Odysseus's household. Pope attaches the epithet "the chaste" [020-015] to her name, and later calls her "sage Eurynome" [023-322], marking both her virtue and her practical wisdom.

The name's etymology — from εὐρύς ("wide") and νόμος ("law, custom, pasture") — suggests a figure whose authority or influence extends broadly. For a housekeeper, this "wide-ranging" quality translates to comprehensive domestic oversight: Eurynome's domain is the entire household, from the chambers of the queen to the bath of the master.

## Role in the Odyssey

Eurynome appears in the final books of the epic, after Odysseus's return to Ithaca. Her role is predominantly domestic but carries symbolic weight: she represents the restoration of orderly household management after the suitors' disruption.

In Book 18, she carries Penelope's mandate to the maids: "Then while Eurynome the mandate bears" [018-226]. She serves as the conduit between Penelope — who has resolved to appear before the suitors — and the household. Her reliability as a messenger signals that she belongs to the loyal faction within the palace.

In Book 20, after Odysseus has been mocked and assaulted by the suitors, Eurynome tends to him with practical care: "Then o'er the chief, Eurynome the chaste / With duteous care a downy carpet cast" [020-015]. At this moment Odysseus is still disguised as a beggar; Eurynome's act of covering him with a carpet is an instinctive gesture of hospitality and decency, administered without regard to his apparent low status.

In Book 23, after the slaughter, Eurynome attends Odysseus's bath: "With faithful cares Eurynome attends, / O'er every limb a shower of fragrance sheds" [023-162]. She then partners with Eurycleia to prepare the marriage bed: "And sage Eurynome, the couch prepare" [023-322]. Finally, she escorts Penelope to Odysseus: "And to the queen Eurynome descends; / A torch she bears, to light with guiding fires / The royal pair" [023-326]. As torchbearer, she illuminates the climactic reunion — a domestic servant elevated to symbolic guide.

## Characterisation

Unlike Eurycleia, who recognises Odysseus by his scar and is party to the dramatic revelation, Eurynome serves without intimate knowledge of the hero's identity. Her loyalty is impersonal — she serves the household, not a specific master she recognises. This makes her fidelity arguably more virtuous: she does the right thing because it is her nature, not because she knows a secret. Pope's choice of the epithet "the chaste" [020-015] aligns her with the poem's moral axis, contrasting her with the disloyal maids who consort with the suitors.

## The Servant Typology of the Odyssey

Eurynome belongs to a carefully constructed taxonomy of servants in the *Odyssey*, each representing a different mode of loyalty. Eurycleia is the *knowing* servant — she recognises Odysseus and must be silenced until the right moment. Eumaeus is the *hospitable* servant — he receives the disguised Odysseus with generosity despite his own poverty. Dolius is the *patient* servant — he tends Laertes's orchard in obscurity, awaiting the return of order. Melanthius and Melantho are the *treacherous* servants — they side with the suitors and are punished with hideous deaths.

Eurynome occupies a distinctive position in this taxonomy: she is the *unconscious loyalist* — faithful without special knowledge, virtuous without recognition. She is not tested as Eumaeus is tested; she does not face the temptation of betrayal. Her virtue is untroubled because it is unexamined — she simply does what is right, from the carpet she casts over the beggar to the torch she bears before the royal pair.

This typology serves the poem's broader moral argument. The *Odyssey* insists that character is revealed not in exceptional moments but in ordinary ones — in how a housekeeper spreads a carpet, in how a swineherd shares his bread. Eurynome's small acts of care are the domestic equivalent of Odysseus's heroic endurance: both are expressions of an ordered soul in a disordered world.

## Eurynome and the Restoration of Order

The image of Eurynome bearing a torch before Odysseus and Penelope [023-326] is the final visual of her in the poem — and it is freighted with symbolism. After the bloodshed of Book 22 and the reunion of Book 23, the household must be restored not only to safety but to *dignity*. Eurynome's torch illuminates more than a corridor; it lights the way from chaos back to order, from the suitors' defilement of the house to the royal couple's reclamation of it. A servant carrying a light before her masters is, in the *Odyssey*'s moral vocabulary, an image of the cosmos set right.
