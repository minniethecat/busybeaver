## Identity

Agelaus, son of Damastor, is one of the prominent suitors of [[penelope]] occupying Odysseus's palace in Ithaca. He is consistently identified by his patronymic "Damastor's son" and "Damastorides," marking him as a man of notable lineage among the suitor band. Unlike the more aggressive [[antinous]] or duplicitous [[eurymachus]], Agelaus presents himself as the voice of moderation — though his counsel serves self-interest rather than justice.

## In the Odyssey

### Book 20 — Counsel of Moderation

As the suitors' feast descends into unnatural, mocking laughter — a divine madness sent by [[athena]] — Agelaus speaks to calm the company. He is the one who advocates delaying the contest of the bow until the next day, a suggestion that, ironically, hastens their doom:

> "By gentler Agelaus thus renew'd." [020-398]

### Book 22 — The Battle in the Hall

When [[odysseus]] reveals himself and begins the slaughter, Agelaus emerges as the suitors' battlefield commander. After the initial panic, he is the first to organize resistance, calling on his companions to fight rather than flee:

> "When Agelaus thus: 'Has none the sense / To plan defense?'" [022-159]

He rallies the suitors to throw spears in coordinated volleys, reasoning that if they can wound Odysseus, the others will lose heart:

> "And first, loud-threatening, Agelaus cried." [022-245]

Agelaus leads a charge of six against Odysseus and his allies:

> "Damastor's son, bold Agelaus, leads." [022-275]

Encouraging the remaining suitors, he insists the victory is still within reach:

> "Thus Agelaus roused the lagging band." [022-282]

### Death

Agelaus's death is swift. In the exchange of spears, he is struck down:

> "Which Agelaus' dying hand forsook." [022-375]

## Characteristics

Among the suitors, Agelaus represents a distinct type: the pragmatic leader who attempts rational coordination when brute force fails. Where [[antinous]] relies on intimidation and [[eurymachus]] on deception, Agelaus relies on tactical thinking — urging his comrades to fight as a unit. His approach, though ultimately futile against [[odysseus]] and [[athena]]'s divine aid, marks him as the most competent warrior among the suitors.
