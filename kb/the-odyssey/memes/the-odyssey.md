# Odyssey (Work)

## Identity

The *Odyssey* (Greek: Ὀδύσσεια) is the epic poem of homecoming (*[[nostos]]*) — the story of [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s ten-year journey from the fallen city of [[troy|Troy]] to his island kingdom of [[ithaca|Ithaca]], and of his wife [[penelope|Penelope]] and son [[telemachus|Telemachus]] who wait and resist in his absence [P01-061]. Traditionally attributed to Homer and composed shortly after the *Iliad* (c. 725–675 BCE), it is the second-oldest surviving work of Western literature and, in many ways, the first "modern" narrative — a story not of war but of survival, not of glory but of identity, not of the battlefield but of the household [P01-139].

The poem comprises 24 books (conventionally divided in the Hellenistic period) and approximately 12,000 lines of dactylic hexameter. In Alexander Pope's 18th-century translation — the source text for this wiki — it is rendered in heroic couplets that capture both the grandeur and the intimacy of Homer's verse.

> The man for wisdom's various arts renown'd [001-018]

> The man for wisdom's various arts renown'd,
> Long exercised in woes, O Muse! resound;
> Who, when his arms had wrought the destined fall
> Of sacred Troy, and razed her heaven-built wall,
> Wandering from clime to clime, observant stray'd,
> Their manners noted, and their states survey'd. [001-018–024]

### Structural Features

The *Odyssey*'s narrative architecture is famously sophisticated [P01-346]:

- **In medias res opening**: The poem begins near the end of Odysseus's journey, with the hero trapped on [[calypso|Calypso]]'s island [005-001]. The first ten years of wandering are related through flashback — Odysseus's own narration to the [[the-phaeacians|Phaeacians]] [009–012].
- **Dual plot**: The Telemachy (Books 1–4) and Odysseus's return (Books 5–24) run in parallel, converging in Book 16 when father and son reunite at [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]]'s hut [016-001].
- **Divine frame**: The [[the-council-of-the-gods|Council of the Gods]] (Books 1 and 5) brackets the action, establishing the theological stakes [001-028, 005-003].
- **Embedded narratives**: Stories nest within stories — Demodocus's songs at the Phaeacian court [008-001], Odysseus's *Apologoi* [009–012], Menelaus's tale of Proteus [004-015], the shade of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s warning [011-024].
- **Ring composition**: The poem begins and ends with divine assemblies; Odysseus leaves Ithaca as a young king and returns as a disguised beggar; the bow that was left behind becomes the instrument of restoration [P01-418].

## Relationship to the Iliad

The *Odyssey* is the *Iliad*'s sequel, but more importantly, it is its moral revision [P01-394]:

| Dimension | Iliad | Odyssey |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Central value | [[kleos]] — immortal glory | [[nostos]] — mortal homecoming |
| Heroic quality | *Biē* (force) | [[metis]] (cunning) |
| Setting | Battlefield | Household, sea, and court |
| Emotional register | Wrath and grief | Longing, endurance, and reunion |
| View of mortality | Tragic — the greatest heroes die young | Chosen — Odysseus chooses mortality over immortality [005-016] |
| Women | Captives and prizes (Briseis, Chryseis) | Agents and partners (Penelope, [[nausicaa|Nausicaa]], [[arete|Arete]]) |

The *Odyssey* does not repudiate the *Iliad*'s values — it complicates them. [[achilles|Achilles]]'s shade in the Underworld confirms his *Iliad* choice was wrong: "I would rather be a slave on earth to another man, to one with no land or livelihood, than rule over all the perished dead" [011-609]. The bow contest re-stages the *Iliad*'s archery competition but redirects it toward domestic restoration rather than martial glory [021-001].

## The Pope Translation

This wiki uses Alexander Pope's translation (1725–1726), a landmark of English neoclassical poetry [P01-061]. Pope's version is characterized by:

- **Heroic couplets**: Rhymed iambic pentameter pairs that give the narrative a stately, musical flow.
- **Neoclassical diction**: Elevated, Latinate vocabulary that transforms Homer's directness into Augustan grandeur. "Ulysses" for Odysseus, "Jove" for Zeus, "Neptune" for Poseidon.
- **Moralizing tendency**: Pope amplifies the poem's ethical dimensions, making explicit what Homer leaves implicit.
- **PN system**: This wiki's paragraph numbering follows Pope's edition: `[001-018]` means Book 1, paragraph 18.

## Significance

The *Odyssey* is the ancestor of every journey narrative, every homecoming story, every tale of the survivor who endures by wit rather than strength [P01-139]. Its influence extends from Virgil's *Aeneid* through Dante's *Divine Comedy*, Joyce's *Ulysses*, and countless contemporary narratives of displacement and return.

More than any single literary descendant, the *Odyssey* established a narrative grammar: the hero who is defined not by what he conquers but by what he endures; the home that is won not by arrival but by recognition; the identity that must be proven, piece by piece, before it can be restored. As the poem's first line announces, it is about "the man" — not the warrior, not the king, but the human being whose defining quality is not strength but wisdom [001-018].

The comparative critical tradition has long debated the Odyssey's relationship to the Iliad. Where the *Iliad* is a poem of youth and death, the *Odyssey* is a poem of middle age and survival. Its hero is not the most beautiful or the strongest but the most resourceful — a revolutionary choice in a culture that had elevated physical excellence above all other virtues [P01-394].
