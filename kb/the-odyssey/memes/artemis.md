# Artemis

**Artemis** (Roman: Diana) is the Olympian goddess of the hunt, wild animals, the wilderness, and the moon. She is the twin sister of Apollo, daughter of Zeus and Leto. In the _Odyssey_, she appears not as an active character but as a divine benchmark — the standard of beauty and grace against which mortal women are measured.

## Role in the Odyssey

Artemis is invoked five times in the _Odyssey_, always in similes praising female beauty or stature. When Odysseus marvels at Nausicaa, he compares her to Artemis: "So moves the goddess of the silver bow, / O'er the tall mountains, as she bends her brow" (006-148). The comparison emphasizes Nausicaa's stature, grace, and maidenly dignity.

In Book 17, Penelope is described as beautiful "as Artemis or golden Aphrodite" (017-043). In Book 19, the same comparison recurs: "like Artemis or golden Aphrodite" (019-060), reinforcing Penelope's queenly radiance even in her sorrow.

Artemis also appears in mythological references within the poem. When Odysseus recalls Tityus's punishment in the Underworld, Homer notes that the giant was condemned for attempting to violate Leto (011-723). Artemis, alongside Apollo, is said to have slain Tityus in defense of their mother: the goddess of the hunt as divine protector.

> "So moves the goddess of the silver bow, / O'er the tall mountains, as she bends her brow" (006-148)

## Mythological Background

Artemis is one of the twelve Olympians. She chose eternal maidenhood and is associated with the moon (as Apollo is with the sun), archery, and the protection of young girls and wild animals. Her epithets include "of the silver bow" and "huntress." She was worshipped throughout the Greek world, with her great temple at Ephesus counted among the Seven Wonders. In Homer, she often appears in similes alongside Aphrodite as the two poles of feminine beauty: Artemis the maiden huntress, Aphrodite the goddess of love.
