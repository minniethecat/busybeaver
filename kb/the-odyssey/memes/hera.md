# Hera

Hera, called Juno throughout Pope's translation, is the queen of the Olympian gods and the wife of Zeus (Jove). Though she plays a far more prominent role in the *Iliad* — where her enmity toward the Trojans drives much of the divine action — she appears sparingly in the *Odyssey*. The two references to her name are deliberately placed: one marks her as a guardian of voyagers, the other as a bestower of feminine majesty. Together they sketch a goddess whose powers of protection and conferral operate at the margins of the poem's mortal dramas.

## Identity and Names

In Greek tradition Hera (Ἥρα, *Hḗra*) is the daughter of Cronus and Rhea, sister and wife of Zeus, and the principal goddess of marriage, childbirth, and queenship. Pope consistently renders her name as *Juno*, following the Latin convention established by Virgil and the Roman poets. His epithets for her — "imperial Juno" [020-094] and "by Juno's guardian aid" [004-694] — emphasize her sovereignty and protective function rather than the jealousy and vindictiveness that characterize her in the *Iliad*.

## Appearances in the Odyssey

Hera is mentioned by name in only two passages, both in contexts that align her with mortal welfare rather than divine discord.

### Book 4 — Guardian of Seafarers

When Menelaus recounts his return from Troy to Telemachus, he describes how the Lesser Ajax (Oilean Ajax) boasted of escaping the storm that wrecked the Greek fleet, only to be destroyed for his impiety. Proteus, the Old Man of the Sea, tells Menelaus that it was "by Juno's guardian aid" [004-694] that his own brother Agamemnon safely passed the storm-waters. The invocation places Hera in her traditional role as protector of Greek kings — and, by extension, of the institution of legitimate sovereignty that Menelaus and Agamemnon represent:

> "By Juno's guardian aid, the watery vast, / Secure of storms, your royal brother pass'd" [004-694]

This is Hera as the divine guarantor of kingship, ensuring that Agamemnon — flawed as he is — reaches Argos before his murder.

### Book 20 — Bestower of Majesty

The second reference appears in a miniature theogony of feminine gifts. Penelope, sleepless and praying for death, recalls the daughters of Pandareus, orphaned girls whom the goddesses collectively raised. The passage distributes feminine excellences among four deities:

> "Imperial Juno to their youth assigned / A form majestic, and sagacious mind" [020-094]

Venus nourished them, Diana gave them beauty, and Pallas (Athena) taught them craft — but it was Juno who bestowed *majesty* and a *sagacious mind*. The pairing is significant: Hera's gift is not merely physical beauty but the quality of regal presence combined with wisdom, qualities that Penelope herself embodies throughout the poem.

## Hera and the Odyssey's Divine Economy

Hera's marginal presence in the *Odyssey* reflects the poem's shifted divine focus. In the *Iliad*, she is a primary agent — scheming, seducing Zeus to distract him from the battlefield, and relentlessly pursuing Troy's destruction. The *Odyssey* replaces this combative Hera with Athena as the active divine protagonist, while Poseidon takes the antagonistic role. Hera recedes to a background deity whose powers are invoked in formulaic or commemorative contexts.

Yet her two appearances are thematically coherent: she guards the legitimate king (Agamemnon) across the sea and confers majesty upon mortal women. Both functions resonate with the *Odyssey*'s central concerns — the restoration of Odysseus's kingship and the celebration of Penelope's regal wisdom. What Hera does overtly and at scale in the *Iliad*, she does sparingly and symbolically in the *Odyssey*: her name alone conveys the weight of sovereign order.
