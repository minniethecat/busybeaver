## Description

Mount Parnassus is a mountain in central Greece, sacred to Apollo and the Muses. In the *Odyssey*, it appears as the site of a pivotal childhood episode — the boar hunt during which the young [[odysseus]] received his distinctive scar. The mountain is described as wooded, rugged terrain suitable for hunting.

> "This on Parnassus (combating the boar)." [019-475]

The mountain lies near Delphi, in the territory of [[odysseus]]'s maternal grandfather [[autolycus]], who lived at its foot.

## In the Odyssey

Parnassus appears exclusively in Book 19, during the extended recognition scene between [[odysseus]] and his former nurse [[eurycleia]]. While washing the feet of the disguised beggar, Eurycleia discovers the scar on his thigh — a wound he received during a boar hunt on Parnassus in his youth. This triggers a lengthy digression that constitutes one of the poem's most important backstory episodes.

### Book 19 — The Scar Episode

The narrative flashes back to Odysseus's youth, when he visited his maternal grandfather [[autolycus]] on Parnassus. Autolycus, the famous thief and trickster, had come to Ithaca shortly after Odysseus's birth and named the infant. When Odysseus came of age, he travelled to Parnassus to receive the promised gifts from his grandfather.

> "The hunting of the wild boar on Parnassus." [019-011]

During the visit, the young Odysseus joined his uncles (Autolycus's sons) in a boar hunt on the mountain's wooded slopes. A great boar charged out of its lair, gashing Odysseus's thigh with its tusk before Odysseus drove his spear into its shoulder. The sons of Autolycus bound his wound and tended him, and he returned to Ithaca bearing the scar that would one day prove his identity.

> "Parnassus, thick perplex'd with horrid shades." [019-516]

### The Recognition

Back in the present of Book 19, Eurycleia's discovery of the scar triggers her recognition of the beggar as her master. [[odysseus]] seizes her throat and swears her to silence — the time has not yet come to reveal himself to [[penelope]].

## Significance

Parnassus is the geographical anchor of Odysseus's foundational childhood experience. The boar hunt on the mountain serves multiple narrative functions. It marks Odysseus's transition from boyhood to manhood — the scar is a warrior's wound, earned in a rite-of-passage encounter with a dangerous beast. The episode also links Odysseus to his maternal lineage through [[autolycus]], from whom he inherits his gifts for deception and storytelling (Greek: *metis*).

More immediately within the poem's structure, the Parnassus episode is the dramatic pivot of Book 19. The scar — earned on that mountain — is the physical proof that confirms the beggar's true identity to [[eurycleia]], setting in motion the chain of recognitions that will culminate in the slaughter of the suitors and reunion with [[penelope]].

The episode is one of the *Odyssey*'s most celebrated narrative set-pieces, praised since Aristotle for its skillful use of analepsis (flashback) — the poet interrupts the climactic foot-washing scene to deliver a long backstory, heightening rather than dissipating the dramatic tension.
