# Troy

## Description

Troy is the absent center of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*. Though the epic never depicts the city directly — the *[[iliad|Iliad]]* covers those events — Troy's fall radiates through every book. It is the place [[odysseus|Odysseus]] left, the site of the suffering that earned him his fame (*kleos*), and the shared reference point for every character's story.

> Of sacred Troy, and razed her heaven-built wall, [001-021]

| Figure | Role at Troy | Fate After Troy |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Odysseus | Devised the Trojan Horse; sacker of cities | Ten years wandering; finally returns |
| Agamemnon | Commander of Greek forces | Murdered by Aegisthus and Clytemnestra |
| Menelaus | Cuckolded husband; reclaimed Helen | Returns to Sparta after seven years |
| Nestor | Wise elder counselor | Returns safely to Pylos |
| Achilles | Greatest warrior; killed Hector | Dies at Troy; buried on the shore |
| Ajax | Second-greatest warrior | Suicide after losing Achilles's arms |
| Aeneas | Trojan prince | Escapes the burning city |

## In the Odyssey

### The Trojan War as Shared Memory

Every major character's identity is shaped by their role at Troy. When [[nestor|Nestor]] recounts the returns, when [[menelaus|Menelaus]] tells of the Trojan Horse, when [[demodocus|Demodocus]] sings of the quarrel of Odysseus and [[achilles|Achilles]], when the shades in [[hades|the Underworld]] tell their stories — all of them are telling the story of Troy and its aftermath. The war is the origin story of the *Odyssey*'s entire world, and every kingdom [[telemachus|Telemachus]] visits is a post-Trojan-War kingdom.

### Demodocus's Songs (Book 8)

The blind bard of the [[scheria|Phaeacians]] sings two songs of Troy at the court of [[alcinous|Alcinous]]:

1. **The Quarrel of Odysseus and Achilles** — The one favorable omen [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] received at Aulis, foretelling Troy's fall. Odysseus weeps at this first song, hiding his face.

2. **The Trojan Horse** — At Odysseus's request, Demodocus sings of the great wooden horse: how the Greeks left it on the beach, how the Trojans debated taking it inside, and how the Greeks poured out at night to sack the city. Odysseus weeps again — "as a woman weeps, falling upon the body of her husband who has fallen before his city" — and it is this display of grief that prompts Alcinous to finally ask his guest's identity.

### The Underworld (Book 11)

In the [[the-underworld|Nekyia]], Troy's dead assemble around Odysseus. Agamemnon tells of his murder, comparing his faithless wife to Odysseus's faithful [[penelope|Penelope]]. Achilles laments his choice of glory over long life: "I would rather be a slave on earth to another man, than rule over all the dead." [[ajax|Ajax]], still nursing his grievance over Achilles's arms, refuses to speak and turns away in silence. The parade of the dead is a reunion of the Trojan War generation — and Odysseus is one of the last ones left alive.

> Wish your Ulysses shared an equal doom! [001-471]

## Geography

Troy (Hisarlik, in modern Turkey) is one of the most extensively excavated Bronze Age sites in the world. The city identified as Homeric Troy (Troy VI/VIIa) was destroyed around 1250 BCE, consistent with the traditional dating of the Trojan War. The plain of the Scamander River (modern Karamenderes) and the beach where the Greek ships would have been drawn up are visible from the citadel mound. The *Odyssey*'s geography sets Troy across the Aegean from the Greek kingdoms, a journey of several days by sail — close enough for regular contact, far enough for a ten-year war.

| Layer | Period | Destruction | Homeric Association |
|-------|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| Troy VI | 1750–1300 BCE | Earthquake? | Wealthy citadel with impressive walls |
| Troy VIIa | 1300–1190 BCE | Fire and siege | Most likely Homeric Troy |
| Troy VIIb | 1190–1100 BCE | Gradual abandonment | Post-war settlement |

## Significance

Troy's narrative function in the *Odyssey* is to be the past that defines the present. The war's shadow falls across the poem: [[the-suitors|the suitors]] are the generation that did not fight, and their corruption is partly a consequence of the absence of the heroic generation. When Odysseus reclaims [[ithaca|Ithaca]], he is reclaiming not just his household but the meaning of the war that consumed twenty years of his life.

Troy also serves as a cautionary counterpoint. The poem repeatedly contrasts Odysseus's successful [[nostos]] with the disastrous returns of other Greek heroes — Agamemnon murdered, Ajax dead by his own hand, Menelaus blown off course for seven years. Troy is the crucible from which some emerged to rebuild and others to destruction. The *Odyssey* is the story of the one who made it home.
