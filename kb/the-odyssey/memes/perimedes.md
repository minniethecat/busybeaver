## Overview

Perimedes is a member of [[odysseus|Odysseus's]] crew who appears by name at a critical juncture: the rites to summon the dead at the shore of the [[the-cimmerians|Cimmerians]] in Book XI. Though his role is brief, his naming places him among the select companions entrusted with ritual duties at the boundary between the worlds of the living and the dead.

## The Underworld Rites

When Odysseus reaches the Cimmerian shore — "the doleful passage to the infernal sky" [^1] — he performs an elaborate necromantic ritual prescribed by [[circe|Circe]]. The crew disembark sheep intended as sacrificial offerings to the powers of the underworld. In this charged, liminal moment, two crew members are named as bearing the victims: "Eurylochus and Perimedes bore" [^1].

Perimedes is paired here with [[eurylochus|Eurylochus]], Odysseus's second-in-command — a detail that suggests Perimedes holds a position of some trust among the crew, entrusted with handling sacred offerings at a moment of great spiritual danger.

## Ritual Context

The underworld rite involves digging a pit, pouring libations of honey, wine, water, and barley, and sacrificing sheep so that their blood may attract the shades of the dead. Odysseus stands guard with his sword drawn to prevent any shade from approaching the blood before [[teiresias|Tiresias]] has drunk. Perimedes and Eurylochus, as the ritual assistants, perform the physical labor that enables this solemn encounter.

## Significance

Perimedes exemplifies the anonymous majority of Odysseus's crew — men whose names appear only once or twice in the epic, but whose presence is essential to the execution of the hero's tasks. His sole named appearance at the threshold of Hades fixes him permanently in one of the poem's most haunting scenes.
