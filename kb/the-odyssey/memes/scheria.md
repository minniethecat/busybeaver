# Scheria

## Geography

Scheria (Greek: Σχερίη, *Scheriē*) is the island of the Phaeacians, a blessed people who dwell at the world's edge, remote from other mortals. Homer describes it as a place of miraculous abundance: perpetual orchards, two springs in the palace, gold and silver guard dogs crafted by Hephaestus himself. In antiquity, Scheria was identified with Corcyra (modern Corfu), though Homer's geography is deliberately mythic.

## In the Odyssey

Scheria functions as the bridge between the world of adventure and Ithaca. Odysseus washes ashore after Poseidon destroys his raft, and the Phaeacians — particularly Nausicaa, Alcinous, and Arete — receive him with extraordinary xenia. At Alcinous's feast, Odysseus narrates his adventures (Books 9–12), the bard Demodocus sings of Troy, and the Phaeacians provide the ship that finally carries Odysseus home. Poseidon's anger at this aid — "the Phaeacians have given him passage" — results in the returning ship being turned to stone, a permanent warning that even the most hospitable people cannot fully shield a mortal from divine wrath.

## Ruler & Inhabitants

Scheria is jointly ruled by King Alcinous and Queen Arete — a rare model of shared authority in Homer. The Phaeacians are a transitional people: once neighbors of the Cyclopes, they fled to Scheria to escape their violence. Their ships are magical — guided by thought, needing no steersman — and they serve as the mythic ferrymen between the worlds of adventure and home.

## Significance

Scheria is the poem's most idealized community — a place where xenia is perfect, art (Demodocus's songs) is valued, and the stranger is honored without question. Yet it is also a place Odysseus must leave. The Phaeacians offer every comfort except the one thing he wants: home.
