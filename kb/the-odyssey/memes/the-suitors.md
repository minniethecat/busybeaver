# The Suitors

## Identity

The Suitors — also called the Wooers, Rivals, or Proci in Latin — are a group of 108 noblemen from [[ithaca|Ithaca]] and the neighboring islands (Dulichium, Same, Zacynthus) who occupy the palace of [[odysseus|Odysseus]] during his twenty-year absence from the Trojan War and subsequent wanderings. Numbered by [[telemachus|Telemachus]] in Book 16, they represent the young aristocratic generation that did not fight at Troy — men who have grown up without the discipline of war or the example of their fathers.

Pope describes them collectively as a consuming force: "High in Ulysses' social list enroll'd" [001-545] — men who have inserted themselves into a household not their own, treating its wealth as common property and its queen as a prize to be claimed.

Their defining characteristic is the systematic violation of [[xenia]]: they are guests who have become parasites, consuming the host's food and wine while abusing the host's son, seducing the host's servants, and plotting the host's murder should he ever return. This inversion of the guest-host relationship is the moral crime that the [[slaughter-of-the-suitors]] avenges.

## In the Odyssey

### Occupation (Books 1–4)

The suitors are introduced through the eyes of [[telemachus|Telemachus]], who sits among them helplessly as they "devour his substance." They feast daily on Odysseus's cattle, sheep, and wine, and mock the young prince when he calls the [[ithacan-assembly]]. "Since great Ulysses sought the Phrygian plains" [002-049], they have acted as if the king will never return.

### The Ambush Plot (Book 4)

Learning of Telemachus's secret voyage to Pylos, the suitors — led by [[antinous|Antinous]] — plot to ambush and kill him on his return. They set a ship with twenty men to wait in the strait between Ithaca and Same. This act of attempted murder marks their transition from parasites to active conspirators.

### The Beggar-King (Books 17–20)

When the disguised Odysseus enters his own hall, the suitors treat him as an object of sport. Antinous throws a stool at him; Eurymachus mocks him; Ctesippus throws an ox-hoof. Their treatment of the stranger — the ultimate test of [[xenia]] — reveals their moral bankruptcy. Only Amphinomus shows kindness, and even he is doomed.

### The Slaughter (Books 21–22)

When [[penelope|Penelope]] sets the bow contest — whoever can string Odysseus's great bow and shoot through twelve axe-heads shall be her husband — every suitor fails. The disguised Odysseus strings it effortlessly, then turns the bow upon them. The slaughter is total: all 108 are killed, their bodies piled in the courtyard. The poet emphasizes that not one escapes.

## Characteristics

| Trait | Manifestation |
|-------|--------------|
| Violation of [[xenia]] | Consume host's wealth; abuse host's son; seduce servants |
| Arrogance ([[hubris]]) | Mock the stranger (Odysseus in disguise); dismiss omens and warnings |
| Physical indulgence | Perpetual feasting, drinking, sexual predation |
| Moral blindness | Ignore Halitherses's prophecy; ignore Telemachus's demands; ignore the beggar's warnings |
| Cowardice | 108 men cannot string one bow; flee before two warriors and a god |

The suitors are the antithesis of the Homeric ideal. Where Odysseus embodies [[metis]] and [[time]], they embody mindless consumption and unearned status. They are the generation that inherited wealth without inheriting the values that created it — a cautionary portrait of aristocracy in decay.

## Key Members

- **[[antinous|Antinous]]** — The de facto leader; the most arrogant and cruel. First to die, shot through the throat while drinking.
- **Eurymachus** — The most cunning; attempts to negotiate after the slaughter begins, offering restitution. Killed second.
- **Amphinomus** — The one decent suitor; shows kindness to the disguised Odysseus. Warned by Odysseus to leave, but Athena holds him in place — even virtue cannot save a member of a doomed group.
- **Ctesippus** — Throws an ox-hoof at the disguised Odysseus, the most gratuitous act of cruelty.
- **Leiodes** — The suitors' seer; the only one to condemn their behavior. Pleads for mercy but is killed by Odysseus nonetheless.

## Significance

The suitors serve as the poem's negative moral exemplar. Their destruction is not merely revenge — it is the restoration of cosmic order. Every violation of [[xenia]], every act of [[hubris]], every cruelty to the stranger and the son is repaid. The poet frames the slaughter not as excess but as [[dike]] — justice made visible.

Their fate also carries a generational message. These are the young men who did not fight at Troy, who grew up in the shadow of absent fathers and dead heroes. Their corruption is partly a consequence of the heroic generation's absence — a vacuum that [[odysseus|Odysseus]] must return to fill.
