[022-001] BOOK XXII.

[022-002] ARGUMENT.

[022-003] THE DEATH OF THE SUITORS.

[022-004] [[odysseus|Ulysses]] begins the slaughter of the suitors by the death of [[antinous|Antinous]].

[022-005] He declares himself, and lets fly his arrows at the rest. [[telemachus|Telemachus]]

[022-006] assists and brings arms for his father, himself, [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]], and

[022-007] Philaetius. [[melanthius|Melanthius]] does the same for the wooers. [[athena|Minerva]] encourages

[022-008] Ulysses in the shape of [[mentor|Mentor]]. The suitors are all slain, only [[medon|Medon]]

[022-009] and [[phemius|Phemius]] are spared. Melanthius and the unfaithful servants are

[022-010] executed. The rest acknowledge their master with all demonstrations of

[022-011] joy.

[022-012] Then fierce the hero o’er the threshold strode;

[022-013] Stripp’d of his rags, he blazed out like a god.

[022-014] Full in their face the lifted bow he bore,

[022-015] And quiver’d deaths, a formidable store;

[022-016] Before his feet the rattling shower he threw,

[022-017] And thus, terrific, to the suitor-crew:

[022-018] “One venturous game this hand hath won to-day,

[022-019] Another, princes! yet remains to play;

[022-020] Another mark our arrow must attain.

[022-021] Phœbus, assist! nor be the labour vain.”

[022-022] Swift as the word the parting arrow sings,

[022-023] And bears thy fate, Antinous, on its wings:

[022-024] Wretch that he was, of unprophetic soul!

[022-025] High in his hands he rear’d the golden bowl!

[022-026] E’en then to drain it lengthen’d out his breath;

[022-027] Changed to the deep, the bitter draught of death:

[022-028] For fate who fear’d amidst a feastful band?

[022-029] And fate to numbers, by a single hand?

[022-030] Full through his throat Ulysses’ weapon pass’d,

[022-031] And pierced his neck. He falls, and breathes his last.

[022-032] The tumbling goblet the wide floor o’erflows,

[022-033] A stream of gore burst spouting from his nose;

[022-034] Grim in convulsive agonies be sprawls:

[022-035] Before him spurn’d the loaded table falls,

[022-036] And spreads the pavement with a mingled flood

[022-037] Of floating meats, and wine, and human blood.

[022-038] Amazed, confounded, as they saw him fall,

[022-039] Up rose the throngs tumultuous round the hall:

[022-040] O’er all the dome they cast a haggard eye,

[022-041] Each look’d for arms—in vain; no arms were nigh:

[022-042] “Aim’st thou at princes? (all amazed they said;)

[022-043] Thy last of games unhappy hast thou play’d;

[022-044] Thy erring shaft has made our bravest bleed,

[022-045] And death, unlucky guest, attends thy deed.

[022-046] Vultures shall tear thee.” Thus incensed they spoke,

[022-047] While each to chance ascribed the wondrous stroke:

[022-048] Blind as they were: for death e’en now invades

[022-049] His destined prey, and wraps them all in shades.

[022-050] Then, grimly frowning, with a dreadful look,

[022-051] That wither’d all their hearts, Ulysses spoke:

[022-052] “Dogs, ye have had your day! ye fear’d no more

[022-053] Ulysses vengeful from the Trojan shore;

[022-054] While, to your lust and spoil a guardless prey,

[022-055] Our house, our wealth, our helpless handmaids lay:

[022-056] Not so content, with bolder frenzy fired,

[022-057] E’en to our bed presumptuous you aspired:

[022-058] Laws or divine or human fail’d to move,

[022-059] Or shame of men, or dread of gods above;

[022-060] Heedless alike of infamy or praise,

[022-061] Or [[kleos|Fame]]’s eternal voice in future days;

[022-062] The hour of vengeance, wretches, now is come;

[022-063] Impending fate is yours, and instant doom.”

[022-064] Thus dreadful he. Confused the suitors stood,

[022-065] From their pale cheeks recedes the flying blood:

[022-066] Trembling they sought their guilty heads to hide.

[022-067] Alone the bold [[eurymachus|Eurymachus]] replied:

[022-068] “If, as thy words import (he thus began),

[022-069] Ulysses lives, and thou the mighty man,

[022-070] Great are thy wrongs, and much hast thou sustain’d

[022-071] In thy spoil’d palace, and exhausted land;

[022-072] The cause and author of those guilty deeds,

[022-073] Lo! at thy feet unjust Antinous bleeds

[022-074] Not love, but wild ambition was his guide;

[022-075] To slay thy son, thy kingdom to divide,

[022-076] These were his aims; but juster [[zeus|Jove]] denied.

[022-077] Since cold in death the offender lies, oh spare

[022-078] Thy suppliant people, and receive their prayer!

[022-079] Brass, gold, and treasures, shall the spoil defray,

[022-080] Two hundred oxen every prince shall pay:

[022-081] The waste of years refunded in a day.

[022-082] Till then thy wrath is just.” Ulysses burn’d

[022-083] With high disdain, and sternly thus return’d:

[022-084] “All, all the treasure that enrich’d our throne

[022-085] Before your rapines, join’d with all your own,

[022-086] If offer’d, vainly should for mercy call;

[022-087] ’Tis you that offer, and I scorn them all;

[022-088] Your blood is my demand, your lives the prize,

[022-089] Till pale as yonder wretch each suitor lies.

[022-090] Hence with those coward terms; or fight or fly;

[022-091] This choice is left you, to resist or die:

[022-092] And die I trust ye shall.” He sternly spoke:

[022-093] With guilty fears the pale assembly shook.

[022-094] Alone Eurymachus exhorts the train:

[022-095] “Yon archer, comrades, will not shoot in vain;

[022-096] But from the threshold shall his darts be sped,

[022-097] (Whoe’er he be), till every prince lie dead?

[022-098] Be mindful of yourselves, draw forth your swords,

[022-099] And to his shafts obtend these ample boards

[022-100] (So need compels). Then, all united, strive

[022-101] The bold invader from his post to drive:

[022-102] The city roused shall to our rescue haste,

[022-103] And this mad archer soon have shot his last.”

[022-104] Swift as he spoke, he drew his traitor sword,

[022-105] And like a lion rush’d against his lord:

[022-106] The wary chief the rushing foe repress’d,

[022-107] Who met the point and forced it in his breast:

[022-108] His falling hand deserts the lifted sword,

[022-109] And prone he falls extended o’er the board!

[022-110] Before him wide, in mix’d effusion roll

[022-111] The untasted viands, and the jovial bowl.

[022-112] Full through his liver pass’d the mortal wound,

[022-113] With dying rage his forehead beats the ground;

[022-114] He spurn’d the seat with fury as he fell,

[022-115] And the fierce soul to darkness dived, and hell.

[022-116] Next bold [[amphinomus|Amphinomus]] his arm extends

[022-117] To force the pass; the godlike man defends.

[022-118] Thy spear, Telemachus, prevents the attack,

[022-119] The brazen weapon driving through his back.

[022-120] Thence through his breast its bloody passage tore;

[022-121] Flat falls he thundering on the marble floor,

[022-122] And his crush’d forehead marks the stone with gore.

[022-123] He left his javelin in the dead, for fear

[022-124] The long encumbrance of the weighty spear

[022-125] To the fierce foe advantage might afford,

[022-126] To rush between and use the shorten’d sword.

[022-127] With speedy ardour to his sire he flies,

[022-128] And, “Arm, great father! arm (in haste he cries).

[022-129] Lo, hence I run for other arms to wield,

[022-130] For missive javelins, and for helm and shield;

[022-131] Fast by our side let either faithful swain

[022-132] In arms attend us, and their part sustain.”

[022-133] “Haste, and return (Ulysses made reply)

[022-134] While yet the auxiliar shafts this hand supply;

[022-135] Lest thus alone, encounter’d by an host,

[022-136] Driven from the gate, the important past be lost.”

## Section 54

[022-137] With speed Telemachus obeys, and flies

[022-138] Where piled in heaps the royal armour lies;

[022-139] Four brazen helmets, eight refulgent spears,

[022-140] And four broad bucklers to his sire he bears:

[022-141] At once in brazen panoply they shone.

[022-142] At once each servant braced his armour on;

[022-143] Around their king a faithful guard they stand.

[022-144] While yet each shaft flew deathful from his hand:

[022-145] Chief after chief expired at every wound,

[022-146] And swell’d the bleeding mountain on the ground.

[022-147] Soon as his store of flying fates was spent.

[022-148] Against the wall he set the bow unbent;

[022-149] And now his shoulders bear the massy shield,

[022-150] And now his hands two beamy javelins wield:

[022-151] He frowns beneath his nodding plume, that play’d

[022-152] O’er the high crest, and cast a dreadful shade.

[022-153] There stood a window near, whence looking down

[022-154] From o’er the porch appear’d the subject town.

[022-155] A double strength of valves secured the place,

[022-156] A high and narrow, but the only pass:

[022-157] The cautious king, with all-preventing care,

[022-158] To guard that outlet, placed Eumaeus there;

[022-159] When Agelaus thus: “Has none the sense

[022-160] To mount yon window, and alarm from thence

[022-161] The neighbour-town? the town shall force the door,

[022-162] And this bold archer soon shall shoot no more.”

[022-163] Melanthius then: “That outlet to the gate

[022-164] So near adjoins, that one may guard the strait.

[022-165] But other methods of defence remain;

[022-166] Myself with arms can furnish all the train;

[022-167] Stores from the royal magazine I bring,

[022-168] And their own darts shall pierce the prince and king.”

[022-169] He said; and mounting up the lofty stairs,

[022-170] Twelve shields, twelve lances, and twelve helmets bears:

[022-171] All arm, and sudden round the hall appears

[022-172] A blaze of bucklers, and a wood of spears.

[022-173] The hero stands oppress’d with mighty woe,

[022-174] On every side he sees the labour grow;

[022-175] “Oh cursed event! and oh unlook’d for aid!

[022-176] Melanthius or the women have betray’d—

[022-177] Oh my dear son!”—The father with a sigh

[022-178] Then ceased; the filial virtue made reply;

[022-179] “Falsehood is folly, and ’tis just to own

[022-180] The fault committed: this was mine alone;

[022-181] My haste neglected yonder door to bar,

[022-182] And hence the villain has supplied their war.

[022-183] Run, good Eumaeus, then, and (what before

[022-184] I thoughtless err’d in) well secure that door:

[022-185] Learn, if by female fraud this deed were done,

[022-186] Or (as my thought misgives) by [[dolius|Dolius]]’ son.”

[022-187] While yet they spoke, in quest of arms again

[022-188] To the high chamber stole the faithless swain,

[022-189] Not unobserved. Eumaeus watchful eyed,

[022-190] And thus address’d Ulysses near his side:

[022-191] “The miscreant we suspected takes that way;

[022-192] Him, if this arm be powerful, shall I slay?

[022-193] Or drive him hither, to receive the meed

[022-194] From thy own hand, of this detested deed?”

[022-195] “Not so (replied Ulysses); leave him there,

[022-196] For us sufficient is another care;

[022-197] Within the structure of this palace wall

[022-198] To keep enclosed his masters till they fall.

[022-199] Go you, and seize the felon; backward bind

[022-200] His arms and legs, and fix a plank behind:

[022-201] On this his body by strong cords extend,

[022-202] And on a column near the roof suspend:

[022-203] So studied tortures his vile days shall end.”

[022-204] The ready swains obey’d with joyful haste,

[022-205] Behind the felon unperceived they pass’d,

[022-206] As round the room in quest of arms he goes

[022-207] (The half-shut door conceal’d his lurking foes):

[022-208] One hand sustain’d a helm, and one the shield

[022-209] Which old [[laertes|Laertes]] wont in youth to wield,

[022-210] Cover’d with dust, with dryness chapp’d and worn,

[022-211] The brass corroded, and the leather torn.

[022-212] Thus laden, o’er the threshold as he stepp’d,

[022-213] Fierce on the villain from each side they leap’d,

[022-214] Back by the hair the trembling dastard drew,

[022-215] And down reluctant on the pavement threw.

[022-216] Active and pleased the zealous swains fulfil

[022-217] At every point their master’s rigid will;

[022-218] First, fast behind, his hands and feet they bound,

[022-219] Then straighten’d cords involved his body round;

[022-220] So drawn aloft, athwart the column tied,

[022-221] The howling felon swung from side to side.

[022-222] Eumaeus scoffing then with keen disdain:

[022-223] “There pass thy pleasing night, O gentle swain!

[022-224] On that soft pillow, from that envied height,

[022-225] First may’st thou see the springing dawn of light;

[022-226] So timely rise, when morning streaks the east,

[022-227] To drive thy victims to the suitors’ feast.”

[022-228] This said, they, left him, tortured as he lay,

[022-229] Secured the door, and hasty strode away:

[022-230] Each, breathing death, resumed his dangerous post

[022-231] Near great Ulysses; four against an host,

[022-232] When lo! descending to her hero’s aid,

[022-233] Jove’s daughter, Pallas, War’s triumphant maid:

[022-234] In Mentor’s friendly form she join’d his side:

[022-235] Ulysses saw, and thus with transport cried:

[022-236] “Come, ever welcome, and thy succour lend;

[022-237] O every sacred name in one, my friend!

[022-238] Early we loved, and long our loves have grown;

[022-239] Whate’er through life’s whole series I have done,

[022-240] Or good, or grateful, now to mind recall,

[022-241] And, aiding this one hour, repay it all.”

[022-242] Thus he; but pleasing hopes his bosom warm

[022-243] Of Pallas latent in the friendly form.

[022-244] The adverse host the phantom-warrior eyed,

[022-245] And first, loud-threatening, Agelaus cried:

[022-246] “Mentor, beware, nor let that tongue persuade

[022-247] Thy frantic arm to lend Ulysses aid;

[022-248] Our force successful shall our threat make good,

[022-249] And with the sire and son commix thy blood.

[022-250] What hopest thou here? Thee first the sword shall slay,

[022-251] Then lop thy whole posterity away;

[022-252] Far hence thy banish’d consort shall we send;

[022-253] With his thy forfeit lands and treasures blend;

[022-254] Thus, and thus only, shalt thou join thy friend.”

[022-255] His barbarous insult even the goddess fires,

[022-256] Who thus the warrior to revenge inspires:

[022-257] “Art thou Ulysses? where then shall we find

[022-258] The patient body and the constant mind?

[022-259] That courage, once the Trojans’ daily dread,

[022-260] Known nine long years, and felt by heroes dead?

[022-261] And where that conduct, which revenged the lust

[022-262] Of Priam’s race, and laid proud [[troy|Troy]] in dust?

[022-263] If this, when [[helen|Helen]] was the cause, were done;

[022-264] What for thy country now, thy queen, thy son?

[022-265] Rise then in combat, at my side attend;

[022-266] Observe what vigour gratitude can lend,

[022-267] And foes how weak, opposed against a friend!”

[022-268] She spoke; but willing longer to survey

[022-269] The sire and son’s great acts withheld the day!

[022-270] By farther toils decreed the brave to try,

[022-271] And level poised the wings of victory;

[022-272] Then with a change of form eludes their sight,

[022-273] Perch’d like a swallow on a rafter’s height,

[022-274] And unperceived enjoys the rising fight.

[022-275] Damastor’s son, bold Agelaus, leads,

[022-276] The guilty war, Eurynomus succeeds;

[022-277] With these, Pisander, great Polyctor’s son,

[022-278] Sage Polybus, and stern Amphimedon,

[022-279] With Demoptolemus: these six survive:

[022-280] The best of all the shafts had left alive.

[022-281] Amidst the carnage, desperate as they stand,

[022-282] Thus Agelaus roused the lagging band:

[022-283] “The hour has come, when yon fierce man no more

[022-284] With bleeding princes shall bestrew the floor;

[022-285] Lo! Mentor leaves him with an empty boast;

[022-286] The four remain, but four against an host.

[022-287] Let each at once discharge the deadly dart,

[022-288] One sure of six shall reach Ulysses’ heart:

[022-289] The rest must perish, their great leader slain:

[022-290] Thus shall one stroke the glory lost regain.”

[022-291] Then all at once their mingled lances threw,

[022-292] And thirsty all of one man’s blood they flew;

[022-293] In vain! Minerva turned them with her breath,

[022-294] And scattered short, or wide, the points of death!

[022-295] With deaden’d sound one on the threshold falls,

[022-296] One strikes the gate, one rings against the walls:

[022-297] The storm passed innocent. The godlike man

[022-298] Now loftier trod, and dreadful thus began:

[022-299] “‘Tis now (brave friends) our turn, at once to throw,

[022-300] (So speed them Heaven) our javelins at the foe.

[022-301] That impious race to all their past misdeeds

[022-302] Would add our blood, injustice still proceeds.”

[022-303] He spoke: at once their fiery lances flew:

[022-304] Great Demoptolemus Ulysses slew;

[022-305] Euryades received the prince’s dart;

[022-306] The goatherd’s quiver’d in Pisander’s heart;

[022-307] Fierce Elatus by thine, Eumaeus, falls;

[022-308] Their fall in thunder echoes round the walls.

[022-309] The rest retreat: the victors now advance,

[022-310] Each from the dead resumes his bloody lance.

[022-311] Again the foe discharge the steely shower;

[022-312] Again made frustrate by the virgin-power.

[022-313] Some, turn’d by Pallas, on the threshold fall,

[022-314] Some wound the gate, some ring against the wall;

[022-315] Some weak, or ponderous with the brazen head,

[022-316] Drop harmless on the pavement, sounding dead.

[022-317] Then bold Amphimedon his javelin cast:

[022-318] Thy hand, Telemachus, it lightly razed:

[022-319] And from [[ctesippus|Ctesippus]]’ arm the spear elanced:

[022-320] On good Eumaeus’ shield and shoulder glanced;

[022-321] Not lessened of their force (so light the wound)

[022-322] Each sung along and dropped upon the ground.

[022-323] [[moira|Fate]] doom’d thee next, Eurydamus, to bear,

[022-324] Thy death ennobled by Ulysses’ spear.

[022-325] By the bold son Amphimedon was slain,

[022-326] And Polybus renown’d, the faithful swain.

[022-327] Pierced through the breast the rude Ctesippus bled,

[022-328] And thus Philaetius gloried o’er the dead:

[022-329] “There end thy pompous vaunts and high disdain;

[022-330] O sharp in scandal, voluble and vain!

[022-331] How weak is mortal pride! To Heaven alone

[022-332] The event of actions and our fates are known:

[022-333] Scoffer, behold what gratitude we bear:

[022-334] The victim’s heel is answered with this spear.”

[022-335] Ulysses brandish’d high his vengeful steel,

[022-336] And Damastorides that instant fell:

[022-337] Fast by Leocritus expiring lay,

[022-338] The prince’s javelin tore its bloody way

[022-339] Through all his bowels: down he tumbled prone,

[022-340] His batter’d front and brains besmear the stone.

[022-341] Now Pallas shines confess’d; aloft she spreads

[022-342] The arm of vengeance o’er their guilty heads:

[022-343] The dreadful aegis blazes in their eye:

[022-344] Amazed they see, they tremble, and they fly:

[022-345] Confused, distracted, through the rooms they fling:

[022-346] Like oxen madden’d by the breeze’s sting,

[022-347] When sultry days, and long, succeed the gentle spring,

[022-348] Not half so keen fierce vultures of the chase

[022-349] Stoop from the mountains on the feather’d race,

[022-350] When, the wide field extended snares beset,

[022-351] With conscious dread they shun the quivering net:

[022-352] No help, no flight; but wounded every way,

[022-353] Headlong they drop; the fowlers seize their prey.

[022-354] On all sides thus they double wound on wound,

[022-355] In prostrate heaps the wretches beat the ground,

[022-356] Unmanly shrieks precede each dying groan,

[022-357] And a red deluge floats the reeking stone.

[022-358] Leiodes first before the victor falls:

[022-359] The wretched augur thus for mercy calls:

[022-360] “Oh gracious hear, nor let thy suppliant bleed;

[022-361] Still undishonoured, or by word or deed,

[022-362] Thy house, for me remains; by me repress’d

[022-363] Full oft was check’d the injustice of the rest:

[022-364] Averse they heard me when I counselled well,

[022-365] Their hearts were harden’d, and they justly fell.

[022-366] O spare an augur’s consecrated head,

[022-367] Nor add the blameless to the guilty dead.”

[022-368] “Priest as thou art! for that detested band

[022-369] Thy lying prophecies deceived the land;

[022-370] Against Ulysses have thy vows been made,

[022-371] For them thy daily orisons were paid:

[022-372] Yet more, e’en to our bed thy pride aspires:

[022-373] One common crime one common fate requires.”

[022-374] Thus speaking, from the ground the sword he took

[022-375] Which Agelaus’ dying hand forsook:

[022-376] Full through his neck the weighty falchion sped;

[022-377] Along the pavement roll’d the muttering head.

[022-378] Phemius alone the hand of vengeance spared,

[022-379] Phemius the sweet, the heaven-instructed bard.

[022-380] Beside the gate the reverend minstrel stands;

[022-381] The lyre now silent trembling in his hands;

[022-382] Dubious to supplicate the chief, or fly

[022-383] To Jove’s inviolable altar nigh,

[022-384] Where oft Laertes holy vows had paid,

[022-385] And oft Ulysses smoking victims laid.

[022-386] His honour’d harp with care he first set down,

[022-387] Between the laver and the silver throne;

[022-388] Then prostrate stretch’d before the dreadful man,

[022-389] Persuasive thus, with accent soft began:

[022-390] “O king! to mercy be thy soul inclined,

[022-391] And spare the poet’s ever-gentle kind.

[022-392] A deed like this thy future fame would wrong,

[022-393] For dear to gods and men is sacred song.

[022-394] Self-taught I sing; by Heaven, and Heaven alone,

[022-395] The genuine seeds of poesy are sown:

[022-396] And (what the gods bestow) the lofty lay

[022-397] To gods alone and godlike worth we pay.

[022-398] Save then the poet, and thyself reward!

[022-399] ’Tis thine to merit, mine is to record.

[022-400] That here I sung, was force, and not desire;

[022-401] This hand reluctant touch’d the warbling wire;

[022-402] And let thy son attest, nor sordid pay,

[022-403] Nor servile flattery, stain’d the moral lay.”

## Section 55

[022-404] The moving words Telemachus attends,

[022-405] His sire approaches, and the bard defends.

[022-406] “O mix not, father, with those impious dead

[022-407] The man divine! forbear that sacred head;

[022-408] Medon, the herald, too, our arms may spare,

[022-409] Medon, who made my infancy his care;

[022-410] If yet he breathes, permit thy son to give

[022-411] Thus much to gratitude, and bid him live.”

[022-412] Beneath a table, trembling with dismay,

[022-413] Couch’d close to earth, unhappy Medon lay,

[022-414] Wrapp’d in a new-slain ox’s ample hide;

[022-415] Swift at the word he cast his screen aside,

[022-416] Sprung to the prince, embraced his knee with tears,

[022-417] And thus with grateful voice address’d his ears

[022-418] “O prince! O friend! lo, here thy Medon stands

[022-419] Ah stop the hero’s unresisted hands,

[022-420] Incensed too justly by that impious brood,

[022-421] Whose guilty glories now are set in blood.”

[022-422] To whom Ulysses with a pleasing eye:

[022-423] “Be bold, on friendship and my son rely;

[022-424] Live, an example for the world to read,

[022-425] How much more safe the good than evil deed:

[022-426] Thou, with the heaven-taught bard, in peace resort

[022-427] From blood and carnage to yon open court:

[022-428] Me other work requires.” With timorous awe

[022-429] From the dire scene the exempted two withdraw,

[022-443] And now the king commands his son to call

[022-444] Old Euryclea to the deathful hall:

[022-445] The son observant not a moment stays;

[022-446] The aged governess with speed obeys;

[022-447] The sounding portals instant they display;

[022-448] The matron moves, the prince directs the way.

[022-449] On heaps of death the stern Ulysses stood,

[022-450] All black with dust, and cover’d thick with blood.

[022-451] So the grim lion from the slaughter comes,

[022-452] Dreadful he glares, and terribly he foams,

[022-453] His breast with marks of carnage painted o’er,

[022-454] His jaws all dropping with the bull’s black gore.

[022-455] Soon as her eyes the welcome object met,

[022-456] The guilty fall’n, the mighty deed complete;

[022-457] A scream of joy her feeble voice essay’d;

[022-458] The hero check’d her, and composedly said.

[022-459] “Woman, experienced as thou art, control

[022-460] Indecent joy, and feast thy secret soul.

[022-461] To insult the dead is cruel and unjust;

[022-462] Fate and their crime have sunk them to the dust.

[022-463] Nor heeded these the censure of mankind,

[022-464] The good and bad were equal in their mind

[022-465] Justly the price of worthlessness they paid,

[022-466] And each now wails an unlamented shade.

[022-467] But thou sincere! O Euryclea, say,

[022-468] What maids dishonour us, and what obey?”

[022-469] Then she: “In these thy kingly walls remain

[022-470] (My son) full fifty of the handmaid train,

[022-471] Taught by my care to cull the fleece or weave,

[022-472] And servitude with pleasing tasks deceive;

[022-480] “Not so (Ulysses, more sedate, replied),

[022-483] “Now to dispose the dead, the care remains

[022-484] To you, my son, and you, my faithfull swains;

[022-491] So with the suitors let them mix in dust,

[022-492] Stretch’d in a long oblivion of their lust.”

[022-494] Each vents a groan, and drops a tender tear;

[022-495] Each heaved her mournful burden, and beneath

[022-496] The porch deposed the ghastly heap of death.

[022-498] Urged the dire task imperious from above;

[022-500] (The swains unite their toil); the walls, the floor,

[022-503] To the base court the females take their way;

[022-504] There compass’d close between the dome and wall

[022-505] (Their life’s last scene) they trembling wait their fall.

[022-506] Then thus the prince: “To these shall we afford

[022-507] A fate so pure as by the martial sword?

[022-508] To these, the nightly prostitutes to shame,

[022-509] And base revilers of our house and name?”

[022-511] A ship’s tough cable from a column hung;

[022-512] Near the high top he strain’d it strongly round,

[022-513] Whence no contending foot could reach the ground.

[022-514] Their heads above connected in a row,

[022-515] They beat the air with quivering feet below:

[022-516] Thus on some tree hung struggling in the snare,

[022-517] The doves or thrushes flap their wings in air.
