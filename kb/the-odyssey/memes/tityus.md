# Tityus

**Tityus** is a giant of Greek mythology, the son of Gaia (Earth), whose eternal punishment Odysseus witnesses when visiting the Underworld in Book 11 of the _Odyssey_. His torment is one of the most vivid images in Homer's catalog of sinners.

## Role in the Odyssey

Odysseus sees Tityus among the great sufferers of the Underworld: "There Tityus large and long, in fetters bound" (011-723). His body stretches across nine acres — a measure of his gigantic size. Two vultures sit on either side, perpetually tearing at his liver and gnawing into his bowels, and his hands are powerless to drive them away.

Homer explains the reason: Tityus had attempted to assault Leto, the mother of Apollo and Artemis, as she traveled toward Pytho. For this crime against a divine consort, he suffers the same type of eternal organ-devouring punishment later made famous by Prometheus.

The passage groups Tityus with Tantalus and Sisyphus: "He then beholds Tityus, Tantalus, Sisyphus, / And each the hateful price of torment pays" (011-012).

> "There Tityus large and long, in fetters bound, / O'erspreads nine acres of infernal ground" (011-723)

## Mythological Background

Tityus was a son of Gaia (Earth), though some traditions name Zeus as his father. He was born in Euboea or Panopeus. At Hera's instigation, he attempted to rape Leto as she traveled to Delphi. Apollo and Artemis killed him with their arrows, and in Tartarus his punishment continued — a cautionary tale about the inviolability of the gods.
