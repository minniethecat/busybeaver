# Phemius

Phemius is the bard of Odysseus's household, a figure who embodies the ambiguous position of the artist under occupation. Forced to entertain the suitors with his lyre, he is nevertheless spared in the slaughter — both because Telemachus vouches for him and because his sacred status as a poet places him under divine protection.

## Identity & Names

The name Phemius (Greek: Φημιος, *Phemios*) derives from *pheme* (speech, utterance, fame). He is described as "Phemius the sweet, the heaven-instructed" [022-379], marking him as divinely inspired. In Pope's prefatory material, Phemius is noted as Telemachus's "preceptor in wisdom" who gave him "both sustenance and instruction" [P01-171], [P01-308].

## Role & Significance

Phemius is a mortal bard of Ithaca. His role in the narrative is to dramatize the tension between art and power:

1. **Artist under compulsion**: He sings for the suitors because he must — "with heavy heart he sang for men who wasted his master's substance" [001-014].
2. **Survivor by sacred office**: His life is spared because the bard's person is inviolable — killing a poet is an offense against the Muse.
3. **Witness to restoration**: With Medon, he survives to testify to the justice of Odysseus's return.

## Appearances

### Book 1 — The Bard and the Suitors

Phemius first appears in the great hall of Odysseus's palace, singing of the Achaeans' return from Troy while the suitors feast. Penelope, hearing his song from her chamber, comes down and asks him to choose a different theme — the song of loss is too painful. Telemachus defends Phemius:

> "To Phemius was consign'd the chorded lyre" [001-214]

> "Phemius, whose voice divine could sweetest sing" [001-216]

Telemachus argues that the bard is not to blame — Zeus, not singers, determines men's fates. He tells his mother to let Phemius sing what the Muse inspires [001-450].

### Books 16–17 — Mentioned

Phemius is mentioned alongside Medon as one "skill'd in heavenly song" [016-281] and appears in the palace playing his lyre [017-319].

### Book 22 — The Sparing

When the slaughter of the suitors begins, Phemius faces a choice. He considers whether to flee or to beg for mercy. He chooses the latter, kneeling before Odysseus with his lyre in hand. Telemachus intervenes:

> "Phemius alone the hand of vengeance spares" [022-378]

> "Phemius the sweet, the heaven-instructed bard" [022-379]

Odysseus grants him his life, and Phemius joins the loyal remnant in the purified palace.

### Book 24 — The Final Assembly

Phemius and Medon appear together at the assembly after the slaughter:

> "Sage Medon came, and Phemius came along" [024-516]

Together they bear witness to the gods' justice.

## Relationships

### Patrons

- **Odysseus**: His true master, whose return restores Phemius's proper role.
- **Telemachus**: The young prince who twice defends him — first to Penelope [001-450], then to Odysseus [022-378].
- **The Muse**: The source of his art and his sacred protection.

### Fellow Survivors

- **Medon**: The herald who shares his fate as a spared innocent.

## Analysis

### The Bard's Sacred Immunity

Phemius's survival is one of the epic's most significant statements about the status of poetry. The bard is *theios* (divine) — inspired by the Muse and therefore under divine protection. Killing a poet is not merely murder but sacrilege. This sacred immunity would have resonated with Homer's original audience, for whom bards were the living repositories of cultural memory.

### Art Under Occupation

Phemius's predicament — forced to sing for those who destroy his master's house — is a powerful metaphor for the artist's position under tyranny. His song gives pleasure to the suitors, but his heart is not in it. Penelope's request that he change his theme and Telemachus's defense of his autonomy together articulate a theory of artistic freedom: the bard sings what the god inspires, not what the powerful demand.

### The Double Bard

With Demodocus, the Phaeacian bard, Phemius forms one half of a diptych of Homeric poets. Demodocus sings freely at a legitimate feast; Phemius sings under duress at a usurped one. Together they define the range of the bard's experience — from honored guest to captive artist.
