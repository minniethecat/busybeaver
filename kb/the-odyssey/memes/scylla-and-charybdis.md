# Scylla and Charybdis

## Summary

The passage between Scylla and Charybdis is the most harrowing navigational peril in the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*. Having escaped the Sirens, [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s ship enters a narrow strait: on one side, [[charybdis|the whirlpool]] [[charybdis|Charybdis]] swallows and regurgitates the sea three times daily; on the other, the cave-dwelling monster [[scylla|Scylla]] — "Dire [[scylla|Scylla]] there a scene of horror forms" (012-290) — reaches down with six long necks and snatches a man from the deck for each of her six heads. [[circe|Circe]] had warned [[odysseus|Odysseus]] not to fight [[scylla|Scylla]], for she is immortal. The hero, armed and ready on the deck, steers closer to [[scylla|Scylla]]'s cliff as the crew rows in terror past [[charybdis|Charybdis]]. [[scylla|Scylla]] strikes — "When lo! fierce [[scylla|Scylla]] stoop'd to seize her prey" (012-304) — and six men are devoured, the worst single loss of life under [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s command since the [[telepylus|Laestrygonia]]ns.

## Context & Causes

The [[scylla|Scylla]]-[[charybdis|Charybdis]] passage represents the culmination of Book 12's sequence of escalating perils: the Sirens (temptation overcome by preparation), the [[the-planctae|Wandering Rocks]] (avoided by route selection), and finally the strait (loss unavoidable). [[circe|Circe]]'s warning about [[scylla|Scylla]] is philosophically distinct from her other advice: she tells [[odysseus|Odysseus]] that [[scylla|Scylla]] is immortal and cannot be fought, and that he must accept the loss of six men as the price of saving the ship and the rest of the crew. This violates the heroic code — a hero does not sacrifice his men — but [[odysseus|Odysseus]], for once, follows the pragmatic divine counsel rather than his warrior instincts.

## Key Moments

1. **The Approach**: After the Sirens, the crew hears the roar of [[charybdis|Charybdis]] and sees smoke rising. Fear paralyzes the rowers until [[odysseus|Odysseus]] rallies them with a speech recalling their escape from [[polyphemus|the Cyclops]].

2. **The Strategic Choice**: Following [[circe|Circe]]'s warning — "Ah, shun the horrid gulf! by [[scylla|Scylla]] fly" (012-147) — [[odysseus|Odysseus]] steers toward [[scylla|Scylla]]'s cliff. He suppresses the name of [[scylla|Scylla]] from the crew: "Cautious the name of [[scylla|Scylla]] I suppress'd" (012-276), knowing panic would make them unable to row.

3. **[[charybdis|Charybdis]]'s Maw**: As they pass, [[charybdis|Charybdis]] sucks down the sea, exposing the ocean floor. The ship trembles at the edge of the vortex. The crew rows with desperate strength.

4. **[[scylla|Scylla]]'s Strike**: While all eyes are on [[charybdis|Charybdis]], [[scylla|Scylla]]'s six heads descend from above — "Fell [[scylla|Scylla]] rises, in her fury roars" (012-133) — and each seizes a crewman. The victims cry out [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s name as they are lifted into the air, their last moments echoing [[eurylochus|Eurylochus]]'s earlier accusation that [[odysseus|Odysseus]] leads men to their deaths.

5. **The Aftermath**: The ship emerges from the strait. The surviving crew grieves but rows on toward the island of [[helios|the Sun]]. Six men lost — the price of passage.

## Aftermath & Consequences

The [[scylla|Scylla]] episode is the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*'s starkest statement of the limits of metis. Unlike every other peril [[odysseus|Odysseus]] faces, there is no clever solution, no ruse, no divine intervention that can avert loss. [[circe|Circe]] tells him this explicitly: [[scylla|Scylla]] is not mortal; she cannot be fought. The hero's only choice is between losing six men ([[scylla|Scylla]]) or losing everyone ([[charybdis|Charybdis]]). [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s decision — to sail closer to [[scylla|Scylla]], to arm himself despite [[circe|Circe]]'s warning (a futile gesture of defiance), and to conceal the danger from his crew — represents a complex moral calculus. He violates the heroic imperative of transparency and protection, but his choice saves the ship and the remaining crew. The episode thus prefigures the central ethical tension of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*: survival sometimes requires a leader to make choices that the heroic code cannot justify.
