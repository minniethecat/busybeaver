# Eurymachus

**Eurymachus**, son of Polybus, is the most dangerous of Penelope's suitors after Antinous — and in some ways more dangerous still. Where Antinous is openly violent and hostile, Eurymachus wields speech as his weapon: charming, diplomatic, and skilled at manipulation. His rhetorical gifts make him the suitors' most effective spokesman and the most insidious corrupter of Ithaca's social order.

## Identity & Names

Eurymachus is identified by patronymic throughout: "Oh son of Polybus!" Telemachus addresses him [001-538], anchoring identity in lineage. Among the suitors he ranks second to Antinous, functioning as the faction's diplomatic face.

Pope's epithets trace a revealing arc. He is "great Eurymachus, of worth confess'd" [004-861] — "confess'd" signaling that even opponents acknowledge his talents. Telemachus reports with irony that "He woos the queen with more respectful flame, / And emulates her former husband's fame" [015-570]. Early books grant "great Eurymachus" [004-861] and "Respectful thus Eurymachus rejoin'd" [021-359]; later books strip the courtesy, leaving only a doomed man's name.

## Character and Methods

### The Persuasive Face of Corruption

Eurymachus' defining trait is his mastery of deceptive speech. When Telemachus publicly rebukes the suitors in Book 1, it is Eurymachus who responds with apparent courtesy — "To this Eurymachus: 'To Heaven alone / The sceptre is intrusted'" [001-526] — deflecting the young prince's challenge with pious-sounding platitudes while maintaining the suitors' predatory occupation of the household.

His rhetorical skill is acknowledged even by those who oppose him. Penelope's suitors include "great Eurymachus, of worth confess'd" [004-861], recognizing his talents even as they serve corrupt ends.

### The Manipulator

Eurymachus' manipulation extends beyond public speech. He pursues Penelope "through both" sentiment and material persuasion: "Through both, Eurymachus pursues the dame" [015-032]. His gifts to the queen — "Eurymachus presents: and ear-rings bright" [018-352] — are calculated attempts to win her favor, not through honest courtship but through the relentless pressure of obligation.

His secret dealings are hinted at darkly: "Nocturnal with Eurymachus" [018-380] suggests clandestine meetings that contribute to the atmosphere of conspiracy in Odysseus' household.

### Rhetorical Strategy

Eurymachus deploys a consistent tactical repertoire. When Telemachus demands the suitors depart, Eurymachus pivots to questioning the stranger-guest's identity rather than address the challenge [001-532]. He invokes piety as shield: "To Heaven alone / Refer the choice" [001-526], reframing occupation as divine will.

When piety fails, he shifts register entirely. Confronted with [[halitherses|Halitherses]]'s prophecy, courtesy evaporates: "Fly, dotard fly, / With thy wise dreams, and fables of the sky" [002-223]. The sudden shift reveals his politeness as purely instrumental — deployed or discarded as tactics demand.

### Key Episode: The Plot and the Lie (Book 16)

When the suitors' ambush against Telemachus fails, Eurymachus leads the strategic response: "At length Eurymachus: 'Our hopes are vain'" [016-370]. Yet confronted by Penelope moments later, he performs an extraordinary reversal, swearing a sacred oath of protection:

> "Far hence those fears (Eurymachus replied,) / O prudent princess! bid thy soul confide. / Breathes there a man who dares that hero slay, / While I behold the golden light of day?" [016-460]

Pope's narrator interjects: "Thus smooth he ended, yet his death conspired" [016-472] — the scene crystallizing his defining quality: the complete divorce between speech and intent.

### Key Episode: Flattery, Mockery, Violence (Book 18)

Book 18 exhibits Eurymachus in three modes. As flatterer: "Then to the queen Eurymachus replies: / 'O justly loved, and not more fair than wise!'" [018-292]. As mocker: "Thoughtless and gay, Eurymachus began" to ridicule the disguised Odysseus [018-405]. As bully: "While yet he speaks, Eurymachus replies, / With indignation flashing from his eyes" [018-435] and hurls a footstool. It misses the beggar, striking a cup-bearer — his violence, like his rhetoric, striking the wrong target and spreading collateral harm.

## Relationship with Other Suitors

Eurymachus serves as Antinous' diplomatic counterpart. While Antinous threatens Telemachus with violence and plots his murder, Eurymachus offers false reassurance: "Far hence those fears (Eurymachus replied)" [016-460]. This good-cop/bad-cop dynamic makes the suitors' collective menace more insidious — Antinous provides the threat, Eurymachus provides the cover of civility.

In council, Eurymachus often takes the lead in framing decisions. When the suitors debate how to respond to Telemachus' growing assertiveness, "At length Eurymachus: 'Our hopes are vain'" [016-370], he articulates their collective assessment and shapes their strategy.

His influence extends through patronage. [[melanthius|Melanthius]] sits "Beneath Eurymachus, his patron lord" [017-315]. His affair with [[melantho|Melantho]] binds the household's corruption at every level.

## The Bow Contest and Final Confrontation

In Book 21, Eurymachus is among the first to attempt stringing Odysseus' bow. Despite his confidence — "The force of great Eurymachus alone" [021-202] — he fails, and his failure is telling: "Before the flame Eurymachus now stands" [021-273], warming and oiling the bow in vain, his physical incapacity exposing the hollowness beneath his rhetorical prowess.

When he cannot string the bow, his response reveals his character: "Not so, Eurymachus: that no man draws / The bow" [021-285] — rather than accept the contest's terms, he immediately seeks to manipulate the outcome, proposing to postpone the trial.

## Death

In the climactic slaughter of Book 22, Eurymachus is the first to speak after Antinous falls, attempting his final manipulation — offering Odysseus compensation and blaming everything on the dead Antinous: "Alone the bold Eurymachus replied" [022-067]. His attempt at negotiation fails; Odysseus refuses, and "Alone Eurymachus exhorts the train" [022-094] to fight. He dies in the ensuing battle, his silver tongue finally silenced by bronze.

## Analysis

### Speech as Weapon

Eurymachus is the dark mirror of Odyssean eloquence. Both men are masters of persuasive speech, yet where Odysseus uses rhetoric for survival and just restoration, Eurymachus deploys it for exploitation. Telemachus's ironic report captures the precise parallel — "He woos the queen with more respectful flame, / And emulates her former husband's fame" [015-570] — imitation that is also violation.

The structural irony runs deeper: Eurymachus's greatest weapon makes him Odysseus's natural prey. The hero who talked past [[polyphemus|Polyphemus]], [[circe|Circe]], and the [[the-sirens|Sirens]] cannot be talked past by a suitor, however silver-tongued. Eurymachus plays a game the master won long ago.

### The Failure of Persuasion

Every strategy that sustained Eurymachus collapses in Book 22. Blaming Antinous cannot distract from collective guilt. Offering twofold restitution cannot repay twenty years of violation. Rallying the train to fight cannot overcome a man defending his home.

His rhetoric works only within shared assumptions — that the occupation is legitimate, that wealth substitutes for justice, that eloquence outweighs truth. Odysseus's return shatters those assumptions. Pope underscores this with brutal juxtaposition: the suitor's most eloquent speech [022-068] immediately precedes his least dignified moment — the sprawling death "extended o'er the board" [022-108]. The man who talked his way to power dies without a final word worth recording.
