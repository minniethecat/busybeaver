# Sisyphus

**Sisyphus** is a figure of Greek mythology whose eternal punishment in Tartarus is witnessed by Odysseus during his journey to the Underworld in Book 11 of the _Odyssey_. He is condemned to roll a massive boulder up a hill, only to watch it roll back down each time he nears the summit.

## Role in the Odyssey

In Book 11, Odysseus sees Sisyphus among the great sinners in the Underworld, grouped with Tantalus and Tityus: "He then beholds Tityus, Tantalus, Sisyphus, / And each the hateful price of torment pays" (011-012). Homer describes the torment in detail: Sisyphus strains with both hands and feet to push a huge stone up a steep slope. Just as it reaches the top, its own weight drives it back down, and "the restless stone rolls rebounding to the plain" (011-739). Drenched in sweat and covered in dust, Sisyphus must begin again — forever.

The image is one of the most famous passages in the _Odyssey_, a powerful emblem of futile, unending labour.

> "With many a weary step, and many a groan, / Up the high hill he heaves a huge round stone" (011-737)

## Mythological Background

Sisyphus was a king of Corinth (Ephyra), the son of Aeolus. His crimes varied across traditions: he betrayed Zeus's secrets, cheated death by trapping Thanatos, and tricked Hades and Persephone into releasing him from the Underworld — claiming his wife had failed to perform proper funeral rites. When the gods finally reclaimed him, his punishment was designed to be not merely painful but absurd: an eternity of effort with zero progress.
