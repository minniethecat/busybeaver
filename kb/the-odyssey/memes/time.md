# Time (Honor)

## Definition

**Time** (Greek: τιμή, *timḗ*) is the public recognition of a person's worth — the honor, status, and esteem that society accords to an individual based on their achievements, birth, and character. Unlike [[kleos]] (posthumous fame), time is enjoyed during life: it is the respect shown by peers, the deference of inferiors, the material privileges (choice cuts of meat, prizes of war, seats of honor) that mark a person's standing.

The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* is, among other things, the story of a man recovering his time. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] returns not just to his home but to his rightful status — and the suitors' primary crime is not theft of property but usurpation of honor:

> Unmark'd, unhonour'd, at a monarch's gate [001-175]

| Level | Who | Time Status at Start | Time Status at End |
|-------|-----|---------------------|-------------------|
| King | [[odysseus|Odysseus]] | Stripped — disguised as beggar | Restored — acknowledged king |
| Queen | [[penelope|Penelope]] | Preserved through fidelity | Restored with husband |
| Prince | [[telemachus|Telemachus]] | Unrecognized, passive | Proven in battle, acknowledged heir |
| Servant | [[eumaeus|Eumaeus]] | Honored for loyalty | Rewarded with freedom and wealth |
| Suitor | [[antinous|Antinous]] | Illegitimate honor (usurpation) | Dishonored in death |

## Greek Context

In the competitive world of Homeric aristocracy, time was the currency of social life. It could be won (through deeds in battle), lost (through defeat or disgrace), inherited (through noble birth), and contested (through rivalry). The quarrel between [[achilles|Achilles]] and [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in the *[[iliad|Iliad]]* — the poem's originating conflict — is a dispute over time: who deserves the greater prize, the greater honor.

The *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* transposes this concern from the battlefield to the household. [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s time is usurped by the suitors, who treat his house, his wife, and his stores as their own. The poem asks: how does a man recover his honor when he must first conceal his identity?

> Had with transmissive honour graced his son [001-325]

## In the Odyssey

### The King Without Honor

[[odysseus|Odysseus]] returns to [[ithaca|Ithaca]] stripped of all visible time. He enters his own palace as a beggar — the lowest-status figure in Homeric society — and must endure insults, thrown stools, and the mockery of men who do not know who he is. His entire strategy depends on concealing his identity, which means concealing his claim to time:

> Should great Ulysses stern appear in arms [002-297]

The dramatic irony is acute: the audience knows the beggar is the king, but the suitors see only a ragged man they can abuse with impunity. Their treatment of the disguised [[odysseus|Odysseus]] is the measure of their violation of [[xenia]] — and thereby the measure of their guilt.

### The Restoration of Status

The poem's second half is a step-by-step recovery of time, structured through recognition scenes:

1. **[[eumaeus|Eumaeus]]** (Book 14) — The swineherd does not recognize his king but treats the beggar with perfect [[xenia]], proving his loyalty and thus his claim to honor.

2. **[[telemachus|Telemachus]]** (Book 16) — The recognition between father and son restores [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s status as father and the household's generational continuity.

3. **[[eurycleia|Eurycleia]]** (Book 19) — The old nurse recognizes the scar; her recognition is forced (she spots the wound), not chosen, and [[odysseus|Odysseus]] must silence her to preserve his strategy.

4. **[[the-bow-contest|The Bow Contest]]** (Book 21) — Stringing the great bow is a public demonstration of unique worth. No suitor can even string it; [[odysseus|Odysseus]] does so effortlessly and shoots through the axes.

5. **[[penelope|Penelope]]** (Book 23) — The final recognition, hinging on intimate knowledge of the marriage bed, restores [[odysseus|Odysseus]] as husband and the marriage as the household's foundation.

6. **[[laertes|Laertes]]** (Book 24) — The aged father recognizes his son through knowledge of the orchard trees, closing the generational circle.

### Time Across the Social Spectrum

The poem explores time at every social level, creating a comprehensive map of honor and dishonor:

- **[[odysseus|Odysseus]]** earns time through deeds: the Trojan Horse, [[polyphemus|the Cyclops]], the bow, the battle.
- **[[penelope|Penelope]]** preserves time through fidelity: her cleverness (the shroud trick) maintains household honor.
- **[[telemachus|Telemachus]]** grows into time: the Telemachy is a time-acquisition journey.
- **[[eumaeus|Eumaeus]]** exemplifies earned time: loyalty confers honor regardless of birth status.
- **[[the-suitors|The Suitors]]** embody usurped time: they consume another man's wealth and claim his wife — their honor is theft, not earning.
- **[[melanthius|Melanthius]]** represents lost time: the traitorous goatherd is stripped of all honor and dies a dishonorable death.

## Significance

- **Motivation engine**: Time drives character behavior throughout the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*. [[odysseus|Odysseus]] wants his household back not just for comfort but for honor; the suitors want [[penelope|Penelope]] partly for the status she confers; [[telemachus|Telemachus]] seeks recognition as his father's son.
- **Social order**: Time maps the proper hierarchy of Homeric society. The crisis in [[ithaca|Ithaca]] is a crisis of misplaced honor — the wrong people have it, and justice means putting honor back where it belongs.
- **Moral foundation**: Unlike modern notions of self-worth, Homeric time is entirely social and public. You do not "feel" honored — you *are* honored, or you are not, and the difference is visible to all. This externality is the source of the poem's dramatic power: honor must be demonstrated, not merely claimed.
