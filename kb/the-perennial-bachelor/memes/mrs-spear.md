## Overview

Mrs. Spear is the wife of Bayard Spear, the wealthy family who purchases The Maples after the Campions are forced to sell their ancestral home. She embodies the new money that displaces old gentility in the novel's quietly brutal economic arc: she is unfailingly polite to the Campion sisters, crediting them for the garden they lost, yet her every improvement to the property and her casual dismissals of Maggie underscore the irrevocable transfer of place and status. She is not a villain — the novel has no villains — but she is the face of the Campions' dispossession.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Spear enters the novel with the purchase of The Maples, the event that crystallizes the Campion family's financial decline. "The Maples sold! The rich Bayard Spears bought the place," the narrator announces, and immediately Mrs. Spear's improvements begin: the new walled garden, the English columbine, the tea-roses "with their melting colors of sunsets and peaches and cream," and most vividly, "Mrs. Spear's tool-house like a little stone house in the woods in a German fairy tale, with all the tools Maggie had always wanted, with their handles painted moss-green" [028-069]. Every detail of the Spears' enhancements is a detail the Campions could never have afforded, and the narrator makes this explicit: "It would have been easier to bear, if the Spears had gone in for peanut brittle stone walls and round beds of cannas, like Mrs. Detweiler" [028-069].

Mrs. Spear's public manner is gracious, even deferential. "Mrs. Spear was always sweet about giving the Campions the credit when people praised her garden" [028-070]. But to her friends, she has other things to say — the text leaves the content of those private remarks suspended in an ominous dash.

At the housewarming, Mrs. Spear plays the welcoming hostess with an edge of condescension. "Sweet of you to come," she murmurs to Lily, her "eyes wandering absently" [028-077]. She gestures vaguely: "Just poke around. See what we've done upstairs" [028-082]. The invitation is generous in form but careless in substance — the Spears have filled the house with so many guests that the Campion sisters are merely faces in a "swarm."

Years later, when Maggie — now faded and thin — pays a call, Mrs. Spear is in the midst of bridge. "'Oh, bother!' she said. 'Still, I suppose I'll have to see the poor old thing'" [031-006]. She finds Maggie's behavior inexplicable, her refusal to comment on the improvements almost rude. "Not much like her brother, who was almost painfully polite when he came to dine or to play bridge." Her final appearance is a brief domestic snapshot: "Mrs. Spear paused at the door on the way upstairs for a nap" [033-024], a woman whose life at The Maples has settled into comfortable routine, untroubled by the ghosts of the family who came before.

## Relationships

- **Bayard Spear** — Her husband, the wealthy man who purchases The Maples. The purchase is attributed to him ("the rich Bayard Spears bought the place"), but it is Mrs. Spear who animates the property with her garden improvements and social events.
- **Dorothy Spear ("Dotty")** — Her daughter, introduced to Lily at the housewarming as a little girl [028-077], later seen as a young woman of the 1920s, "slender legs in flesh-colored silk stockings," waving a cigarette holder and discussing suppressed desires [033-024].
- **The Campion Sisters** — The displaced former owners. Mrs. Spear is publicly deferential to them, privately dismissive. Her relationship to Maggie, in particular, is one of mutual incomprehension: Maggie cannot bear the Spears; Mrs. Spear cannot fathom Maggie's intensity.
- **Lily Campion** — The one sister who accepts the housewarming invitation, driven by curiosity and a desire not to "hurt their feelings" [028-077]. Mrs. Spear receives her with practiced, absent warmth.

## Significance

Mrs. Spear is the novel's most sustained portrait of the new wealth that displaces the old. She is not caricatured — the novel is too subtle for that — but her every appearance is shadowed by what the reader knows the Campions have lost. Her tool-house with its moss-green handles, her walled garden, her bridge parties: each is a small monument to the fact that The Maples no longer belongs to the people who loved it first. The narrator's parenthetical aside — that her new rubber reducing corset "wasn't by any means the dream of comfort she had expected" — is a flicker of the novel's quiet irony, a suggestion that even the victors in the economic game are not quite comfortable in their winnings.
