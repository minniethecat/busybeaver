# Social Class and Gentility

## Overview

Social class in *The Perennial Bachelor* is a matter of relentless, anxious calculation. The Campion family occupies an unstable position — genteel by ancestry and pretension, but increasingly impoverished in fact — and much of the novel's dramatic tension arises from the gap between the family's social aspirations and its economic reality. Anne Parrish, writing from within this class herself, captures its nuances with ethnographic precision: the significance of a top hat, the mortification of a shabby parlor, the elaborate choreography of calling cards and returned visits.

The novel is set in a small town near Wilmington, a milieu in which everyone knows everyone else's business and social standing is maintained through an endless performance of gentility. This performance requires money — for clothes, for entertainments, for the conspicuous leisure that signals a lady's status — and the Campions have very little. The result is a chronic low-grade panic about appearances, a persistent fear of being found out, that shapes every character's decisions and constrains every character's possibilities.

## Treatment in the Novel

Parrish establishes the class coordinates early. The Campions are "good family" — Papa was a gentleman, Mamma is a Southmayd — but good family without money occupies a precarious position in a town where new commercial wealth is beginning to challenge old gentility. The novel is set at a historical inflection point: the post-Civil War period saw the decline of the old planter aristocracy and the rise of a new mercantile and industrial elite. The Campions belong to the former world but must navigate the latter.

Mr. Alfred Lacey serves as the novel's representative of prosperous middle-class respectability. His courtship of Mamma is conducted through material display — wine, compliments, the implicit promise of financial security. He is not a villain; Parrish treats him with the same gentle irony she applies to all her characters. But his presence in the novel makes visible the economic logic that underlies the rituals of courtship and marriage. A gentleman, the novel reminds us, is defined partly by his manners and entirely by his income (004-038).

The theme of gentility as performance runs throughout. Victor's top hat — "the price of a gentleman's top hat alone!" (004-038) — is both a symbol of his class aspirations and an emblem of the financial strain those aspirations impose. The novel is full of such details: the carefully preserved wedding dress, the shabby furniture disguised by dim lighting, the entertainments that cannot be returned. Parrish understands that gentility is not a quality but a display, and that maintaining the display requires resources the Campions do not possess.

The novel's treatment of servants — Martha, Albert, Chloe, Caesar — provides another lens on class. The Campions have servants, but barely: the servants are old, the wages are low, and the relationship is marked by a mutual dependency that complicates the usual master-servant hierarchy. Parrish does not romanticize this relationship, but she registers its human complexity in a way that more polemical fiction might not.

Cousin Lizzie Blow represents a different class trajectory: married to a man she did not love, living in eccentric independence, she has made peace with a social position that is neither exalted nor shameful. Her embrace of Spiritualism suggests both the intellectual restlessness of a woman with too little to do and the freedom that comes with not caring what the neighbors think.

## Character Perspectives

Mamma experiences class anxiety most acutely because she was raised for a position she can no longer sustain. Her beauty is her only remaining asset, and the novel shows how precarious that asset is — subject to age, illness, and the changing fashions that determine what kind of beauty is valued.

Maggie, by contrast, is largely indifferent to class performance. Her plainness, her practicality, her fierce loyalty to her family — these insulate her from the anxieties that consume her mother. But her indifference is itself a class marker: only someone secure in her gentility can afford to disregard its performance.

Victor is the novel's study in class failure. Raised as a gentleman but incapable of supporting himself as one, he drifts from job to job, always expecting a respect that his achievements do not warrant. His perennial bachelorhood is partly a class phenomenon — he cannot afford to marry, but he cannot admit that he cannot afford it.

## Literary and Historical Context

The decline of the genteel class in the post-Civil War South is a major theme in American literature, treated with varying degrees of nostalgia and critique by writers from Thomas Nelson Page to William Faulkner. Parrish, writing in 1925, belongs to the generation that could see this decline with clarity but also with sympathy. Her novel is neither an elegy for a lost aristocracy nor a celebration of its passing, but a clear-eyed portrait of people caught in a historical transition they do not fully understand.
