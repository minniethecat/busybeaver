# Mrs. Clark

## Overview

Mrs. Clark is an elderly widow living across the road from The Maples, the Campion family home. Her husband Lewis Clark is buried in the cemetery adjacent to the Methodist Church, and his grave becomes the pretext — and genuine object — of Lily Campion's weekly charity visits. Mrs. Clark is a figure at once comic and poignant: she is opinionated, lonely, and fiercely proud of the floral displays on her husband's grave.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Clark serves a dual narrative function. On the surface, she is the object of Lily's charitable visitations — the "poor old neighbor" to whom Lily carries armfuls of flowers every Saturday evening. But her deeper function is as an unwitting prop in Lily's romantic scheme: Lily times her visits hoping to encounter Mr. Marshall, a potential suitor who calls at the Campion house. Mrs. Clark's loneliness is thus both genuine and instrumentalized — a tension the novel handles with its characteristic blend of sympathy and irony.

## Key Appearances

### The Saturday Evening Visits (Chapter 23)

Lily takes to "running across the road to see old Mrs. Clark on Saturday evenings, hoping that Mr. Marshall would come to call." When Mr. Marshall arrives at the Campion house and asks after Lily, Maggie tells him she is visiting "a poor old neighbor," leading Mr. Marshall to think reverently, "Sweet Saint Charity!" — an ironic beat, since Lily's charity serves a romantic ulterior motive.

### The Taj Mahal Conversation (Chapter 23)

Lily attempts to broaden Mrs. Clark's horizons by telling her about the Taj Mahal — "a wonderful building called the Taj Mahal, all carved out of marble so that it looks as fine as lace, that an Indian rajah built in memory of his wife." Mrs. Clark misconstrues "Indian" (from India) with Native Americans, replying that her nephew's wife sent a picture from the World's Fair of "Chief Big Crow and his squaw Little Cloud." Lily corrects her — "this is a different kind of Indian" — but Mrs. Clark remains skeptical: "Well, different kind or not, I wouldn't trust 'em. Scalp you as soon as look at you." The exchange captures Mrs. Clark's provincial worldview with affectionate humor.

### The Weekly Flowers (Chapter 23)

Every Saturday, Lily carries flowers to Mrs. Clark so that "Mr. Clark's grave, conveniently next to the Methodist Church, might be admired on Sunday." Mrs. Clark receives each bouquet with critical appraisal — "Thank you kindly. Them white roses last week was as beautiful as if they'd been artificial" — maintaining a reputation to keep up through her husband's grave. Her comment that "Everybody always admires poor Lewis's grave, it has the handsomest bouquets of any in the graveyard" reveals a fierce pride that survives into widowhood.

### The Lonesome Confession (Chapter 23)

When Lily arrives one evening, Mrs. Clark greets her with unguarded honesty: "I was just settin' here in the dark feelin' so lonesome, and hopin' my uttermost best that you'd come!" The moment is the novel's most direct glimpse of Mrs. Clark's interior life — an elderly woman sitting alone in the dark, waiting for the young neighbor whose visits are, in part, a romantic stratagem.

## Character and Significance

Mrs. Clark is a minor character drawn with Dickensian vividness. Her dialect ("settin'," "uttermost best," "Patience guide me!"), her conflation of India with the American frontier, and her critical eye for floral arrangements make her memorable. But beneath the comedy lies a portrait of old age and solitude — a widow whose social world has contracted to her husband's grave and the Saturday visits of a neighbor's daughter. The novel does not judge Lily for using these visits as cover for romantic hopes; instead, it lets both realities coexist: the genuine loneliness of Mrs. Clark and the genuine self-interest of a young woman in search of a husband.
