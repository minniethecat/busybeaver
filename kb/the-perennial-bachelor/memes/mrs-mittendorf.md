## Overview

Mrs. Mittendorf arrives at the Campion household as a friend of Mrs. Hopper's, settling into the boarding-house community with a comfortable, sleepy warmth [026-022]. Unlike the other boarders — Miss Snaith with her desperate longings, Mrs. Hopper with her complaints, Mr. Neff with his droll performances — Mrs. Mittendorf occupies a quieter register: she yawns enormously, rocks back and forth, and serves as the household's receptive ear. When Mr. Neff confides his concerns about May Campion, it is Mrs. Mittendorf who receives them, her rocking chair becoming a confessional of sorts.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Mittendorf's first action in the novel is to arrive at the Campion home "because she was a friend of Mrs. Hopper's" [026-022]. She integrates quickly into the boarding-house rhythm: at the evening entertainments she "patted an enormous yawn" [026-034] and later "sat smiling sleepily into the fire" [026-046] — a portrait of genial, undemanding presence.

Her most significant scene comes when Mr. Neff confides in her about May Campion's troubling behavior. "It's Miss May," he explains, describing how she "sort of smoulders all the time" and cries through the night [027-008]. Mrs. Mittendorf responds not with alarm but with a rocking, philosophical agreement: "She's a funny one" [027-009]. When Mr. Neff makes one of his outrageous remarks — that May has "bats in her belfry" — Mrs. Mittendorf "swayed backward and forward, almost bursting" with a mixture of shock and amusement [027-011].

Beneath the gossip, a deeper current surfaces: asked about what she would like for supper, Mrs. Mittendorf says "boiled cherry pudding," and the narrator reveals she is "really thinking of her dead husband" [027-012]. This single line transforms her from comic background figure into a widow whose losses are carried quietly, surfacing only in moments of unguarded association. When the boarders eventually disperse, Mrs. Mittendorf "followed them," and though she says "I hate to go," she goes anyway — like the others [027-015].

## Relationships

- **Mr. Neff** — Her primary interlocutor. He confides his concerns about May to her, and she receives them with rocking-chair equanimity. Their exchanges form the novel's most direct discussion of May's psychological state.
- **Mrs. Hopper** — Her friend whose presence at the Campion house draws her there in the first place [026-022].
- **May Campion** — Though we never see them interact directly, Mrs. Mittendorf is positioned as an observer of May's deterioration, hearing Mr. Neff's reports and offering her own assessment.
- **Her dead husband** — Absent but present in her thoughts, invoked by the memory of boiled cherry pudding — one of the novel's quietest and most affecting registrations of widowhood [027-012].

## Significance

Mrs. Mittendorf is the novel's most economical portrait of widowhood. In a book filled with unmarried women and one confirmed bachelor, she is a rare married woman — and a widow, no less — whose grief surfaces not in dramatic confession but in the homely association of a remembered dessert. Her rocking, her yawning, her sleepy smiling are the outward signs of a woman who has made peace with diminished things. She functions as the household's emotional absorbent: Mr. Neff's anxieties, Mrs. Hopper's complaints, the general loneliness of the boarding house all seem to settle into her rocking rhythm without requiring anything of her in return. When she leaves, the household loses its quietest and most stabilizing presence.
