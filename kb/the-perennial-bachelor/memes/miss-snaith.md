# Miss Snaith

## Overview

Miss Snaith appears in *The Perennial Bachelor* as one of the many unmarried women who populate the novel's social periphery — figures whose presence registers the demographic reality of post-Civil War spinsterhood without ever becoming the novel's focus. She is mentioned in passing, attends social gatherings, and fades from the narrative without having been fully introduced. Her very marginality is significant: the novel's world is full of women like Miss Snaith, and their ubiquity is the statistical background against which the Campion sisters' fates must be understood.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Snaith participates in the social events that structure the Campion family's limited public life. She is present at gatherings, part of the community that surrounds The Maples, one of the many women whose lives parallel the Campion sisters' without intersecting with the novel's central concerns.

Her function is atmospheric rather than narrative. She exists to populate the world, to remind the reader that the Campion sisters are not unique — that their fates were shared by thousands of women of their generation, that [[spinsterhood]] was not a personal failure but a demographic condition.

## Significance

Miss Snaith is important precisely because she is not important. The novel's attention flows toward the Campion family; women like Miss Snaith exist in the peripheral vision, acknowledged but never examined. Her presence — and the presence of figures like [[miss-martin]], [[miss-perry]], and [[miss-hessie-farley]] — constitutes the novel's unwritten history of female singleness.

Reading against the grain of the novel's attention, Miss Snaith represents the story Parrish did not tell: the story of the woman who was not beautiful, not wealthy, not central enough to be narratively interesting, and whose life was therefore invisible to the literature of her time.
