# Cousin Lizzie's Spiritualist Turn

## Overview

Cousin [[cousin-lizzie-blow|Lizzie Blow]]'s embrace of the spiritualist movement is one of the novel's most colorful subplots — a late-life transformation that sees the already formidable Lizzie become a medium, a seeker, and a source of considerable anxiety to her husband [[sam-blow|Sam]]. The episode is treated with the novel's characteristic blend of satire and affection: Lizzie's spiritualism is ridiculous, and also genuine, and also a kind of liberation.

## The Turn

Lizzie's conversion to spiritualism arrives without warning, as her enthusiasms always do. She had ridden horses wildly, criticized Mamma freely, and lived with an energy that set her apart from the more languid Campion women. Spiritualism was the natural extension of her temperament — a belief system that legitimate dramatic self-expression under the guise of communion with the dead.

The episode's comedy derives from the gap between Lizzie's conviction and her family's response. Sam Blow, long accustomed to his wife's intensities, is reportedly driven to distraction. Aunt Priscilla, whose own relationship to reality was always tenuous, narrates the developments with wide-eyed credulity.

## Significance

Lizzie's spiritualist turn is more than comic relief. It represents a form of female self-assertion that the novel's more constrained characters cannot achieve. Lizzie does not wait for permission; she does not consult her husband; she does not worry about appearances. She becomes a spiritualist because she wants to, and the novel, for all its mockery, registers the freedom in her choice.

The episode also illuminates the spiritual vacuum at the center of the Campion world. The formal religion of [[st-stephens-episcopal|St. Stephen's]] provides no sustenance for Lizzie; she needs something more dramatic, more personally involving, more hers. Her turn toward the occult is an implicit critique of the respectable Christianity that fails to nourish its adherents.
