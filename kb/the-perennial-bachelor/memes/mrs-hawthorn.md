## Overview

Mrs. Hawthorn is Lucy Hawthorn's mother, a woman of "opulent curves" whose New York sophistication and French maid set her apart from the quieter society of Wilmington [017-004]. She visits Mrs. Leaf and becomes the gatekeeper through whom Victor Campion must pass to reach her daughter — a role she performs with the tactical instinct of a woman who understands that romantic access is a form of social currency. Her violet draperies, her French maid Elise, and her proprietary grip on Lucy embody the worldly glamour that makes the Campion sisters' life seem provincial by comparison.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Hawthorn first appears as Mrs. Leaf's guest from New York, presiding over an afternoon tea where the Campion sisters' reduced circumstances are made painfully visible. Mrs. Leaf apologizes for the sparse refreshments — "there wasn't anything on the tea table but graham wafers and water" — because "her guest from New York, Mrs. Hawthorn, had afternoon tea, with bread and butter, every day of her life" [017-003]. The contrast between Mrs. Hawthorn's opulent world and the Campions' genteel poverty is sharpened when Mrs. Leaf mentions that "Elise is Mrs. Hawthorn's French maid," a detail that leaves the Campion sisters speechless [017-009].

When Victor Campion calls on Lucy, it is Mrs. Hawthorn who first receives him — "sitting in the lovely, liquid golden dusk" in her violet draperies, a butterfly brooch perched on her shoulder [018-010] [019-026]. She recognizes his infatuation immediately and watches it with the detachment of a woman who has seen many young men come and go. As Victor stammers his request to see Lucy, "Would the goose never go?" Mrs. Hawthorn thinks, letting a calculated pause pass before responding [019-024].

When Victor startles her by appearing suddenly at the hall, she cries "Mercy!" and "flaps her hands with exasperation," her composure briefly cracking [019-030]. The novel ultimately frames her, alongside her husband, as one of "those three ivies, Mr. and Mrs. Hawthorn and Miss Hawthorn, to cling to" — a family unit so self-contained and formidable that Victor, imagining himself the strong oak for them to cling to, is revealed as deluded [019-086].

## Relationships

- **Lucy Hawthorn** — Her daughter and social protégée. Mrs. Hawthorn manages Lucy's romantic possibilities with the same proprietorship she exercises over her household, and her departure for Europe with Lucy removes Victor's last chance at love.
- **Mrs. Leaf** — Her Wilmington hostess and friend. Mrs. Leaf uses Mrs. Hawthorn's visit as a status marker, eagerly explaining the French maid to the Campions and seeking validation: "Mrs. Hawthorn was lovely, wasn't she?" [017-010].
- **Victor Campion** — She regards him with amused condescension, a provincial suitor beneath her daughter's prospects. Her silent judgment — waiting for him to leave, flapping her hands at his clumsiness — communicates what politeness forbids her to say aloud.

## Significance

Mrs. Hawthorn functions in the novel as an emissary from a world the Campion sisters can never enter. Her French maid, her afternoon teas, her violet draperies, and her Grand Tour plans for Lucy represent a cosmopolitan femininity built on wealth and social confidence — everything the Campion sisters, with their dwindling inheritance and provincial horizons, cannot access. When she and Lucy depart for Europe, they take with them not just Victor's hopes but the novel's last vision of a wider world beyond Wilmington. Her presence also illuminates the social machinery of nineteenth-century courtship: a mother's role as gatekeeper, the performative display of leisured womanhood, and the quiet cruelty of letting a young man know, through silence and flapping hands, that he does not belong.
