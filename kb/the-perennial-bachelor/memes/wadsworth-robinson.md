## Overview

Wadsworth Robinson appears briefly in the novel's later chapters as a suitor to May Campion — one of the few men who shows romantic interest in the aging sisters. His courtship is the occasion for a rare moment of protective intervention from Victor, who helps dissuade the match. Years afterward, May looks back and credits Victor with having saved her from the marriage, a judgment delivered with the complicated gratitude of a woman reconciling herself to spinsterhood.

## Role in the Novel

Wadsworth Robinson enters the narrative through Lily's skeptical eye. She reports to the household that May has "a new beau named Wadsworth Robinson" and adds, with characteristic bluntness, "he seems kind of silly to me" [024-009]. The name itself — Wadsworth — marks him as faintly ridiculous, a comic figure in a novel that treats most suitors as disappointments.

At a party, May watches the scene "seeing herself as Wadsworth Robinson saw her" — a moment of self-consciousness that reveals how much she craves the validation of male attention [024-044]. He participates in the evening's hijinks when someone "slipped a piece of ice down Wadsworth Robinson's collar" — a prank that underscores his role as a figure of fun rather than romantic gravity [024-066].

Lily, ever the pragmatist, warns: "You mark my words, Lily — Wadsworth Robinson is going to make May an offer" [025-001]. The prediction carries the weight of the household's accumulated experience with disappointed hopes.

The courtship's resolution is revealed retrospectively. Years later, May reflects that Victor "kept me from marrying Wadsworth Robinson. Oh, yes, he did; if it hadn't been for Victor I'd be Mrs. Wadsworth Robinson this minute" [027-032]. The tone is ambiguous — gratitude mixed with something like regret, the recognition that Victor's intervention, however well-intentioned, preserved her in the state of waiting that defines her life.

## Relationships

- **May Campion** — His primary romantic interest. May is momentarily captivated by the attention, seeing herself through his eyes, but ultimately accepts Victor's judgment that the match is unsuitable.
- **Lily Campion** — She dismisses him as "kind of silly" and anticipates his proposal with the weary wisdom of a woman who has watched her sister's romantic possibilities dwindle [024-009].
- **Victor Campion** — The brother who intervenes to prevent the marriage. May's later recollection frames Victor as both protector and obstacle — the man who saved her from a foolish match and, in doing so, ensured she would remain unmarried.

## Significance

Wadsworth Robinson is the novel's last explicit suitor for any of the Campion sisters, and his dismissal marks the final closing of the door on romantic possibility. His name — comic, faintly pretentious — signals that the sisters' matrimonial prospects have declined from the eligible Victor Campion of their youth to men who are "kind of silly" and can have ice slipped down their collars at parties. May's retrospective gratitude to Victor for preventing the marriage is one of the novel's most psychologically complex moments: she credits him with having saved her, yet the "saved" life is precisely the one of lonely waiting she has been trying to escape. That she can laugh about it — "I'd be Mrs. Wadsworth Robinson this minute" — is a measure of the dark humor with which the novel treats its characters' accommodations to disappointment.
