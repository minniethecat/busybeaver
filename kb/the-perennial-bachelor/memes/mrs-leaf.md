## Overview

Mrs. Leaf is the mother of Fannie and Robert, a Wilmington matron whose social ambitions find their fullest expression in her friendship with the wealthy New Yorker Mrs. Hawthorn. When the Campion sisters call at her home, she presides over an afternoon tea that becomes a quiet theater of social one-upmanship — bragging about Fannie, name-dropping Mrs. Hawthorn's French maid, and offering the sisters graham wafers while apologizing that her distinguished guest demands bread and butter every day.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Leaf first appears as a hostess receiving the Campion sisters for an awkward afternoon call. The scene is staged around deprivation and display: "there wasn't anything on the tea table but graham wafers and water" — an apology of a tea laid "on a small spidery table by Mrs. Leaf" [017-002] [017-001]. She explains that her guest from New York "had afternoon tea, with bread and butter, every day of her life," a detail that simultaneously boasts of her connection to cosmopolitan luxury and excuses the meager provisions offered to the Campions [017-003].

Her social maneuvering is transparent to the sisters. She "knew what they were feeling" — the humiliation of being offered graham wafers while Mrs. Hawthorn gets bread and butter — and presses on regardless [017-003]. She extends an invitation: "Fannie's so anxious to see you. Couldn't you come home for a visit, May?" — an offer that reads as charity thinly disguised as hospitality [017-005].

Her masterstroke is the revelation about Elise: "'Elise is Mrs. Hawthorn's French maid,' Mrs. Leaf explained" [017-009]. The narrator notes that "that made up for cracker crumbs and spidery table," the Campion sisters rendered momentarily speechless by this evidence of a world of servants and sophistication they cannot access. After the sisters leave, Mrs. Leaf seeks validation from her guest: "'Mrs. Hawthorn was lovely, wasn't she?'" [017-010].

At a later gathering, Mrs. Hawthorn directs her attention to Mrs. Leaf — "Look at the amount of ankle that girl is showing, Bessie!" — the use of her first name confirming the intimacy between the two women [018-037].

## Relationships

- **Fannie Leaf (Page)** — Her daughter, of whom she is conspicuously proud. She brags about Fannie to the Campion sisters, whose own marriage prospects have failed to materialize [017-015].
- **Mrs. Hawthorn** — Her prized friend from New York, whose presence she leverages for social status. The friendship is real but also instrumental — Mrs. Hawthorn's French maid is as much a prop in Mrs. Leaf's theater as in Mrs. Hawthorn's own.
- **The Campion sisters** — She extends hospitality that is also condescension, inviting them into her home while reminding them of everything they lack. The sisters laugh about her afterward: "Did you hear Mrs. Leaf bragging to me about Fannie and her baby?" [017-015].

## Significance

Mrs. Leaf is the novel's most precisely drawn portrait of small-town social climbing. Her friendship with Mrs. Hawthorn, her bragging about Fannie, her spidery tea table with its graham wafers — every detail contributes to the picture of a woman who understands status as a zero-sum game and who has calculated that the Campion sisters, with their dwindling fortune and unmarried state, are on the losing side. She is not cruel; she is merely strategic, and her strategy requires the Campions to know their place. The sisters' laughter at her expense afterward is one of the novel's small victories — the powerless reclaiming dignity through mockery.
