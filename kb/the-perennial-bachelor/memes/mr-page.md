## Overview

Mr. Page is the schoolmaster who runs a small school for boys in the Rectory — one of the few figures of institutional authority to appear in Victor Campion's childhood. Gentle, mildly shortsighted, and quietly firm, he presides over the reluctant education of young Victor, who dreads the schoolroom and invents elaborate excuses to escape it. Decades later, a yellowed photograph of "old Mr. Page in his vestments" surfaces as Victor confronts the accumulated weight of his past [030-037]. He is distinct from the younger Prentice Page, who moves through the novel's social scenes a generation later.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Page appears exclusively in the chapters concerning Victor's boyhood. His school at the Rectory is the destination Maggie drags her reluctant younger brother toward: Victor "had to go to the Rectory, where Mr. Page had a school for little boys" [007-008]. On the day of their walk, they find old Mr. Page absorbed in his garden, "tying up his dahlias" — a detail that softens the figure of authority into something approaching the pastoral [007-018].

The schoolroom scene is rendered through Victor's childish perspective: Maggie prompts him to promise he will "be good and study very hard, Mr. Page," and Victor mechanically repeats the words [007-020] [007-024]. Mr. Page, "looking through him mildly," seems to understand the performance for what it is [007-021]. When Victor does manage a correct answer, Mr. Page pats his head and calls him "Young Meritorious" — a moment of benign approval that Victor, even as a child, recognizes as condescension wrapped in kindness [007-025].

The schoolmaster's most vivid appearance comes not in the classroom but in pursuit: when Victor bolts from school, he knows "it was Mr. Page running after him — Mr. Page grabbing him by the collar, thud, thud, thud!" [007-046]. This image of the old man chasing a fleeing boy is the novel's sharpest memory of childhood resistance to imposed order.

In the novel's final chapter, Victor, now old himself, comes across "a faded yellowed photograph of old Mr. Page in his vestments" and retrieves it — an act of salvage that links the schoolmaster to the novel's broader meditation on memory, loss, and the artifacts that outlive a community [030-037].

## Relationships

- **Victor Campion** — His reluctant pupil. Victor's relationship with Mr. Page is defined by evasion and mild coercion, the schoolroom representing the first in a lifelong series of demands Victor would rather flee than face.
- **Maggie Campion** — She delivers Victor to the schoolroom and prompts his promises of good behavior, acting as the enforcer of the social contract Victor instinctively resists [007-023].
- **Prentice Page** — No established familial relationship in the text, though the shared surname suggests a possible connection across generations. Prentice moves in the Campion sisters' social world while old Mr. Page belongs to Victor's childhood.

## Significance

Mr. Page is the novel's representative of institutional benignity — the school, the church, the polite coercion of childhood. His mildness ("looking through him mildly") and his garden dahlias make him a gentler figure than the stern schoolmasters of Victorian stereotype, yet his function in Victor's life is the same: he stands for the world of rules, schedules, and expectations that Victor's temperament is fundamentally unsuited to meet. The photograph in the final chapter transforms him from a figure of childhood dread into an object of elegy — one more relic of a vanished world that Victor, in his old age, can only retrieve and gaze upon.
