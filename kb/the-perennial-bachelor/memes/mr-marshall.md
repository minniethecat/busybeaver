# Mr. Marshall

## Overview

Mr. Marshall is a young man in Wilmington society who becomes the object of Lily Campion's romantic hopes in Chapter 23. He is first encountered at Hessie Farley's Valentine euchre party and subsequently calls at the Campion house, prompting Lily to orchestrate a scheme of proximity: she visits the elderly Mrs. Clark across the road every Saturday evening, hoping to be summoned home with the words "Lily! Mr. Marshall's over at the house!"

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Marshall's role is defined more by Lily's anticipation than by his own presence. He is one of the novel's several suitor-figures — young men who exist in the narrative primarily as objects of female attention and speculation. He is polite, dutifully impressed by Lily's charity toward "a poor old neighbor," and apparently unaware that his visits have been transformed, in Lily's imagination, into the central drama of her Saturday evenings.

The novel handles Mr. Marshall with characteristic restraint: he is neither hero nor villain, merely a possibility. The pathos of the episode lies not in what Mr. Marshall does but in what Lily does for him — the flowers carried across the road, the ears straining for the click of the gate, the elaborate architecture of hope built around a man who may or may not ever arrive.

## Key Appearances

### The Valentine Euchre Party (Chapter 23)

Lily meets Mr. Marshall at Hessie Farley's Valentine euchre party, amid "a romantic setting of red cardboard hearts, heart-shaped sandwiches, heart-shaped cakes with beetroot-reddened icing, and large crimson hearts of geranium blossoms." Mrs. Farley, the hostess, "talked for a year about how much trouble it had all been." The setting is saturated with romantic signification, and Lily's hopes attach themselves to Mr. Marshall in this atmosphere of constructed sentiment.

### The Saturday Evening Calls (Chapter 23)

Mr. Marshall calls at the Campion house on Saturday evenings. When he arrives and finds Lily absent, Maggie tells him she is visiting "a poor old neighbor" across the road. His response — a reverent "Sweet Saint Charity!" — invests Lily's actions with a sanctity that the reader knows is only half-deserved. Lily is indeed charitable; she is also strategic. Mr. Marshall sees only the former.

### The Missed Encounter (Chapter 23)

The central dramatic irony of the episode is that Mr. Marshall never arrives while Lily is actually at Mrs. Clark's. She listens "for the click of the gate, steps on the path, Maggie's breathless voice" — but "he never came, and it just let her in for a tiresome custom." The visits to Mrs. Clark, begun as pretext, become obligation; the hoped-for summons never materializes. Mr. Marshall's absence is more consequential than his presence would have been.

## Character and Significance

Mr. Marshall is a minor figure rendered significant by the narrative architecture of hope built around him. He never speaks at length, never reveals an interior life, never even knows the role he plays in Lily's imagination. He is a space that Lily fills with possibility — and the novel's treatment of that space, suspended between hope and disappointment, is one of its more delicate renderings of the unmarried woman's emotional economy.

## Relationships

- **Lily Campion**: The Campion sister who hopes for his attention
- **Maggie Campion**: Social acquaintance; covers for Lily during his calls
- **Mrs. Clark**: Unwittingly, Lily's visits to her are timed to coincide with Mr. Marshall's potential arrival
