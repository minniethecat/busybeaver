## Overview

Cobina is Aunt Priscilla's cook, known to the reader through three brief but warmly drawn mentions in Chapter 15. Unlike the novel's other servant characters, who are rendered largely through their physical labor, Cobina enters the narrative through prayer and food — through the corn-fritters her mistress loves and the mushrooms she herself refuses to eat.

## Role in the Novel

Cobina appears first in Aunt Priscilla's grace: "'Our Father which art in Heaven, thank thee ever so much for making Cobina a good cook and thank thee for Willie. Amen'" [015-006]. The prayer is characteristic of Aunt Priscilla — artless, affectionate, blurring the sacred and the domestic — and it elevates Cobina to the company of the things Priscilla is most grateful for: good food and her husband Willie.

When Aunt Priscilla gathers mushrooms for Mamma Campion, Cobina is the practical counterweight to her mistress's enthusiasm. Priscilla offers the mushrooms to Mamma, adding that "Cobina says she wouldn't be paid to eat them. Toads, she calls them — did you ever? Short for toadstools, I guess" [015-014]. The remark captures Cobina's voice at one remove — sensible, skeptical, unwilling to trust what grows wild in the damp. Where Aunt Priscilla sees treasure, Cobina sees poison, and the novel's sympathies, in this small domestic comedy, lie quietly with the cook.

Her final mention is a request for more food: "Co‑o‑bina! Oh, Cobina, you might just let us have a few more of the corn-fritters" [015-014]. The drawn-out vowels of "Co‑o‑bina!" capture Aunt Priscilla's characteristic mode of address — affectionate, beseeching, treating her cook as both servant and intimate.

## Relationships

- **Aunt Priscilla Campion** — Her mistress and, in Priscilla's guileless way, her admirer. Priscilla's prayer of thanks for Cobina's cooking is one of the novel's purest expressions of unselfconscious gratitude.
- **Willie Campion** — Aunt Priscilla's husband, paired with Cobina in Priscilla's prayer as the two beings she is most thankful for.

## Significance

Cobina occupies perhaps three sentences in the novel, yet she is drawn with a warmth granted to few of Parrish's servant characters. The prayer of thanks, the refusal of mushrooms, the corn-fritters — these details sketch a presence that is defined not by labor performed but by food made and judgment exercised. Cobina's refusal to eat the mushrooms is a small assertion of autonomy, the cook declining what the mistress has gathered. That Aunt Priscilla reports this refusal without resentment — indeed, with a kind of amused respect — suggests a household in which the asymmetries of race and class are softened, if not erased, by the daily intimacies of shared meals and long acquaintance.
