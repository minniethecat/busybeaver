# Cousin Lizzie Blow

## Overview

Cousin Lizzie Blow is the Campion relative who says what others only think — a sharp-tongued, socially ambitious woman whose appearances in the novel reliably introduce critical perspective into the family's self-enclosed world. Married to Sam Blow, she moves through the novel as a kind of one-woman Greek chorus, her commentary on Mamma's household revealing truths the Campions would prefer to leave unspoken.

## The Critical Relation

Lizzie's defining characteristic is her willingness to criticize. "She would rather have died than tear around the country as Lizzie did on Sam's wildest horses, with her cheeks like fire, making such a figure of herself" [002-036] — Mamma's thought about Lizzie captures the ambivalence: disapproval mixed with a kind of reluctant admiration for Lizzie's vitality.

Lizzie asked the questions others avoided: "'Do you think you ought to have us, Margaret?'" [001-026] — the practical concern about imposing that Mamma's politeness forbade. She "lifted her eyebrows" [001-048] at Mamma's many daughters, the silent commentary of a woman who understood the social liability of a household without sons. Her presence introduces an outside perspective that the Campion family, left to its own devices, would never generate.

## Social Energy

Unlike the Campion women, Lizzie moved through the world with physical confidence. She rode horses wildly; she "came rustling in" [001-045] to rooms with theatrical presence; she "got up and moved about" [001-058] while making conversational raids. Her energy is the antithesis of Mamma's dreamy delicacy, and the contrast is pointed.

The novel never entirely condemns Lizzie, even as it registers her sharpness. She is, in her way, more alive than any of the Campion women — more engaged with the world, more willing to assert herself, less paralyzed by the gentility that immobilizes Mamma's household. Her critical remarks are often accurate, however unkind.

## Significance

Cousin Lizzie represents the wider social world that judges the Campions — and finds them wanting. Her presence in the novel serves as a reality check: the family's internal narratives about their own gentility and distinction are not universally shared. Lizzie sees the cracks in the Campion facade, and she is not too polite to mention them.

She is also a counter-example to the Campion model of femininity. Where Mamma is delicate and passive, Lizzie is vigorous and assertive. Where the Campion daughters sacrifice themselves to family duty, Lizzie claims her own pleasures. The novel does not endorse her — Parrish is too subtle for simple moralizing — but it uses her to illuminate, by contrast, what the Campion women are missing.
