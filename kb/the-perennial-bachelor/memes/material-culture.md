# Material Culture

## Overview

Anne Parrish's attention to the material world — to dresses and dance cards, china and chafing-dishes, top hats and trousseaux — is one of the distinguishing features of *The Perennial Bachelor*. Objects in this novel are never merely decorative; they are dense with social meaning, marking class position, gender expectations, and the passage of time with a precision that the characters themselves cannot articulate. Parrish, the daughter of a prominent Delaware family and the wife of a successful businessman, knew this world from the inside, and her novel is an ethnography of its material practices.

The novel's material culture is organized around display. The objects that matter are the ones that can be seen: the wedding dress, the calling card, the properly set table. What cannot be displayed — the worn linens, the mended gloves, the economies of the kitchen — is hidden, and the labor of hiding it falls to the women of the household. The novel registers this hidden labor without making it its primary subject, but the accumulation of detail — Maggie's practicality, the servants' complaints, the chronic shortage of money — tells the story beneath the story.

## Treatment in the Novel

Fashion functions in the novel as a system of social signaling. Mamma's wedding dress, preserved in lavender, represents a moment of triumph that can never be repeated (015-020). May's careful attention to her appearance — the pompadours, the "bursts of chiffon" — is both a strategy and a necessity; her beauty is her only asset, and maintaining it requires constant material investment (026-010). Parrish understands that fashion is not frivolous but fundamental: it is how women in this world communicate their value.

Domestic space is equally charged. The Campion house, The Maples, is both home and stage — a place where the family performs its gentility for an audience of neighbors and potential suitors. The shabbiness that cannot be concealed, the entertainments that cannot be returned, the "things growing more Southern every minute" (027-063) — these details accumulate into a portrait of a family whose material resources are inadequate to its social aspirations.

Food serves as another register of class and care. The novel's descriptions of meals — the elaborate holiday spreads, the meager everyday fare — distinguish between the family's public face and its private reality. The servants — old Chloe in the kitchen, Caesar in the garden — are part of this material economy, their labor sustaining a gentility that depends on the work of people who are barely acknowledged.

## Character Perspectives

Mamma's relationship to material culture is aspirational and anxious. Her belongings — Papa's desk, the silver tea-set, the wedding slippers — are talismans of a status she can no longer afford to maintain. Her attachment to these objects is genuine but also desperate: they are evidence that she was once someone who mattered.

Maggie's relationship to material culture is practical verging on indifferent. She has no time for the performance of gentility because she is too busy doing the work that makes the performance possible. Her plainness extends to her attitude toward objects: they are useful or they are not, and most of the objects her mother treasures are not.

Victor's material consumption — the top hats, the steins, the dance cards — represents everything the family cannot afford (004-038). His purchases are investments in an identity — gentleman, bachelor, man about town — that his income cannot sustain. The novel registers the cost of his consumption without ever letting him feel it.

## Literary and Historical Context

Parrish's attention to material culture belongs to a tradition of American realism — from Howells to Wharton — that understood objects as carriers of social meaning. Her distinctive contribution is the tenderness with which she treats the objects themselves: the wedding dress is not mocked, the shabby parlor is not sneered at. Parrish understands that people love their things, and that this love is neither foolish nor trivial — it is how human beings attach meaning to the physical world they inhabit.
