## Overview

Mrs. Kelsey is a Wilmington society matron, the wife of Judge Kelsey, whose appearances in the novel are glancing but vivid. She materializes at a debutante ball in gold antennæ, chaperones dances, and later surfaces in the local newspaper's classified section. She belongs to the established provincial aristocracy — not the new-money Spears but the older stratum of judges and their wives, solid and slightly ridiculous, whose social position is secure enough to survive a pair of gold antennæ with diamond dangles on the tips.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Kelsey first appears at May Campion's debutante ball, described through the filter of May's report to Maggie afterward. "Everyone's wearing things in their hair, Maggie — I was glad I wore my gold lace butterfly, though it was very modest and meek compared to the diamond stars and crescents and things most of them had. Mrs. Kelsey had gold antennæ with diamond dangles on the tips" [028-007]. The detail is exquisite and faintly absurd — a matron wearing insect antennae as jewelry — and Maggie's response is immediate and scornful: "'Mrs. Kelsey with antennæ!' cried Maggie. 'She's a right hefty butterfly, is all I have to say'" [028-007]. The exchange captures the Campion sisters' particular brand of wit: observant, astringent, and directed at the social world they inhabit but do not quite belong to.

Mrs. Kelsey serves a practical function at the ball as well: when Maggie is stranded without partners, she "talked brightly, feverishly to Mrs. Kelsey through one dance — two dances — three dances" [028-005]. The chaperone becomes a refuge, a safe harbor for a young woman who is not being asked to dance. Mrs. Kelsey's role here is entirely structural — she exists to be talked to — but the repetition of "one dance — two dances — three dances" conveys the desperation of the situation with quiet force.

Her husband, Judge Kelsey, is a dance partner of May's — "a mature demure hop with old Judge Kelsey" — and the couple appears together on May's card: "Judge Kelsey and Raymond Line, but they weren't exactly thrilling" [028-005].

Mrs. Kelsey's final mention is domestic and bathetic. Reading the local paper, the Campion sisters note that "Mrs. Kelsey was advertising for a cook" [029-020]. From gold antennæ at a ball to a classified ad for domestic help: the arc is comic and entirely typical of the novel's way of reminding us that even society matrons have kitchens to staff.

## Relationships

- **Judge Kelsey** — Her husband, a dance partner of May's and a figure of the older generation in Wilmington society.
- **Maggie Campion** — The sister who clings to Mrs. Kelsey's company through three dances when no partners appear, and who later delivers the verdict on her antennæ.
- **May Campion** — The debutante who reports on Mrs. Kelsey's hair ornaments with a mixture of awe and gentle mockery.

## Significance

Mrs. Kelsey is a minor figure of the provincial establishment — a judge's wife, a chaperone at balls, a woman whose social world is secure and whose domestic world occasionally requires a new cook. The gold antennæ are her defining detail, and they are revealing: she has the confidence (or the obliviousness) to wear insect-themed jewelry with diamond dangles to a debutante ball. Maggie's verdict — "a right hefty butterfly" — is a small act of social revenge, the wit of the declining gentry trained upon the solid bourgeoisie. Mrs. Kelsey, one suspects, would not care what Maggie Campion thought of her antennæ. She has a cook to find.
