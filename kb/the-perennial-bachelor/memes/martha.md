# Martha

## Overview

Martha appears in *The Perennial Bachelor* as part of the household staff at The Maples. Her presence is registered primarily through her labor — helping in the kitchen, assisting with household tasks, forming part of the quiet infrastructure of domestic service that made the Campion family's way of life possible.

## Role in the Household

Martha works alongside [[chloe|Chloe]] in the kitchen and assists [[maggie-campion|Maggie]] with household tasks. When Maggie "had been helping Martha" [013-001] with domestic work, the shared labor between the white daughter and the Black servant is noted without comment — a detail that reveals both the household's dependence on Martha's competence and the easy integration of Maggie's practical nature with the servant economy.

The household at The Maples, like all Southern households of its class and era, was sustained by domestic workers whose labor was essential and whose presence was rendered nearly invisible by the conventions of the literature that recorded it. Martha is the novel's acknowledgment of that labor — partial, limited by its era's vision, but present.

## Significance

Martha's significance, like Chloe's, is structural rather than dramatic. She represents the labor that the novel's central characters depend upon but rarely think about. Her presence in the margins of the narrative is itself a kind of testimony: the Southern domestic novel could not exist without the servants who made the domestic world function, even when it could not imagine their inner lives.
