## Overview

Jake is Chloe's great-grandson, first encountered as a boy who goes fishing with the young Victor Campion [010-018]. As the novel spans decades, Jake grows from a child companion into an adult who delivers the mail, freezes ice-cream for gatherings, and eventually marries and has children of his own. His trajectory — boyhood freedom to adult responsibility, from fishing the river to bringing the post — mirrors in miniature the novel's larger arc of time's passage and the settling of lives into their adult shapes.

## Role in the Novel

Jake's first appearance captures a moment of boyhood idyll: Victor has "run away, gone fishing with Jake, old Chloe's great-grandson" [010-018]. The two boys share the world of the river and the outdoors, a realm far from the parlor where Mamma and the girls hold their tableaux and receive callers. A later comic detail — "the stirring that had gone on until Jake hit it over the head" [011-028] — preserves the rough physicality of boyhood adventure.

As the years pass, Jake transitions into adult roles within the household's extended economy. In Chapter 24, "Jake's going to freeze it" — the ice-cream for a family gathering [024-046] — marks his integration into the rhythms of domestic service. By Chapter 27, "She could hardly wait for Jake to bring the mail on St. Valentine's Day" [027-041] shows him as postman, a role that connects the household to the wider world. The novel's final glimpses of Jake show him fully adult, married to Ida, with children of his own: "Jake and Ida were turning into" [027-063], and a gift of books "for the children" [028-053] confirms the next generation.

## Relationships

- **Chloe** — His great-grandmother, a long-serving servant at The Maples. Jake's presence extends Chloe's lineage within the household's world.
- **Victor Campion** — Boyhood fishing companion; their paths diverge as Victor goes to school and Jake enters service, but the connection endures through the household.
- **Ida** — His wife in the novel's later chapters [027-063].

## Significance

Jake is one of the novel's smallest but most structurally elegant characters. His appearances span the full chronological sweep of the narrative — from boyhood to adulthood, from fishing companion to postman to father — tracking the passage of time in a handful of vivid, economical details. Unlike the white Campion characters whose lives are dissected at length, Jake is rendered entirely through action: he fishes, he stirs, he freezes ice-cream, he delivers mail. This spareness is not incidental but historically revealing: Black servants in early twentieth-century fiction are so routinely rendered through their labor that the accumulation of these small, concrete details — a great-grandmother, a wife, children, books — becomes, in its quiet way, a counter-narrative to erasure.
