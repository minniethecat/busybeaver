## Overview

Mr. Neff is one of the boarders who takes up residence at the Campion family home after Mamma Campion's death forces the sisters to take in paying guests. A man of peculiar humor — he nicknames the ladies' teddy bears "Theodore bears" — he serves as photographer, parlor entertainer, and reluctant confidant during the household's brief communal chapter. His departure in the spring marks the beginning of the boarders' dispersal and the sisters' return to isolation.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Neff arrives as a tenant in the old schoolroom, where Maggie prepares his quarters "with a sword turning in her heart" as the family home converts into a boarding house [026-023]. He quickly becomes a fixture of the household's social life, joining the evening gatherings around the fire where Miss Snaith shows her teddy bear pictures and the boarders sing together [026-036].

His defining trait is a droll, gently mocking humor. He dubs the ladies' teddy bears "Theodore bears" — Miss Hopper's with a blue ribbon, Miss Snaith's with a pink — and through these toys the "tragic virgins" project their unspoken longing toward him [027-005]. He takes their photograph as they pretend to gather snowballs in their piqué skirts and chatelaine bags, a comic tableau of middle-aged courtship rituals [027-003].

Beneath the humor, Mr. Neff serves as a witness to the household's unraveling. He confides in Mrs. Mittendorf his concern about May Campion: "I don't know what's the matter with her, she just sort of smoulders all the time" [027-010]. When he leaves in the spring, the boarders' fragile community begins to dissolve — Miss Hopper and Miss Snaith grow fond of each other only after he is gone [027-014].

## Relationships

- **Maggie Campion** — She prepares his room with painful reluctance, the conversion of the old schoolroom into a boarder's quarters symbolizing the family's decline [026-023].
- **Mrs. Mittendorf** — His primary confidant in the house; he shares his concerns about May with her, prompting her indignant responses [027-011].
- **Miss Snaith** — One of the "tragic virgins" who, through her teddy bear, silently calls to him for rescue from spinsterhood [027-005]. Her heart flutters at the mere sight of Victor's empty room after Mr. Neff departs.
- **Miss Hopper** — Fellow boarder whose teddy bear also becomes a "Theodore bear" under Mr. Neff's naming, and who, alongside Miss Snaith, participates in the snowball photographs [027-003].

## Significance

Mr. Neff embodies the comic-pathetic texture of the boarding-house chapters — the only period in the novel when the Campion home fills with outside life. His humor (the "Theodore bear" joke, the snowball photographs, the wry observations about May) provides the novel's sharpest comic relief, yet his presence also intensifies the pathos: through the teddy bears and parlor songs, three aging women project their longing onto the one available man in the house. His departure in spring signals that the boarding-house experiment is unsustainable, and with him goes the household's last infusion of masculine presence and levity. That Miss Hopper and Miss Snaith only become "quite fond of each other since Mr. Neff had gone" [027-014] underscores how his presence, however amusing, also functioned as a distraction from the loneliness that would define the rest of their lives.
