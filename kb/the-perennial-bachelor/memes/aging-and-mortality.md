# Aging and Mortality

## Overview

Anne Parrish's *The Perennial Bachelor* spans approximately six decades, from the 1860s through the 1920s, and the passage of time is not merely the novel's backdrop but its central subject. The narrative opens with Maggie Campion at age ten, "with light eyes looking out" at a world still radiant with childhood (001-003), and closes with the Campion sisters in middle age, their youth consumed by the family that depended on them. Parrish traces this arc without sentimentality, registering the small losses that accumulate into a life — the fading of beauty, the narrowing of possibilities, the quiet arrival of death.

The novel treats mortality as both a personal reality and a historical force. Papa's death, which occurs before the novel's opening, shapes every subsequent event: the family's poverty, Mamma's helplessness, the daughters' sacrifices (005-011). Individual deaths — Papa, old Toot, Aunt Priscilla's children — mark the narrative like stations on a via dolorosa, each one diminishing the world a little further.

## Treatment in the Novel

Parrish's treatment of aging is notable for its attention to the body. Her characters age in specific, physical ways: Mamma's beauty fades incrementally, registered in the diminishing returns of her social power; Victor's youthful charm curdles into middle-aged petulance; Maggie's plainness, which was a disadvantage in youth, becomes in maturity a kind of dignity. The novel does not console its characters — or its readers — with the promise that age brings wisdom. What it brings, Parrish suggests, is clarity, and clarity can be a painful gift.

The death of Papa functions as the novel's originating wound. "I do not realize consciously that his death meant a strain removed" (005-011) — the narrator's language is precise about the ambivalence of grief. Papa's absence is both a liberation (from his expectations, his authority) and a catastrophe (the family has no provider, no protector). This double quality — loss as both freedom and disaster — recurs throughout the novel's treatment of mortality.

Aunt Priscilla's story arc provides a concentrated study in grief. Her children die; her sweetness survives but is hollowed out, becoming a kind of absent-mindedness, a failure to fully inhabit the present. Parrish is interested in what grief does to people over time — not the dramatic anguish of fresh loss but the slow erosion of self that prolonged mourning produces.

The novel's later chapters accelerate the reckoning with mortality. The First World War brings death on an industrial scale, transforming it from a personal tragedy into a historical condition (030-053). Maggie's health crisis in Chapter 30 brings the threat of death directly into the Campion household, forcing a confrontation with everything the family has avoided. The novel's final image — a new generation occupying the house where "three old maid sisters used to live" (033-037) — makes the cycle explicit: one generation's life is another's memory.

## Character Perspectives

Maggie ages into her role as the family's anchor. The girlish plainness that made her invisible in the marriage market becomes, in middle age, a kind of solidity — she is the one who remains, who endures, who does the work. Her aging is an achievement, not a defeat.

Mamma's aging is the novel's most sustained meditation on the loss of beauty as a social death. Her identity was founded on being admired; when admiration stops, who is she? Parrish answers this question with terrible gentleness: she is no one, or no one in particular — a woman whose social function has been withdrawn but who lacks the resources to invent a new one.

Victor's aging is the most uncomfortable to witness. The charming bachelor becomes, by the novel's end, a figure of pathos — still expecting the deference that youth and charm once commanded, but receiving only pity or impatience (029-029). His refusal to grow up is, the novel implies, a refusal to face mortality itself.

## Literary and Historical Context

Parrish belonged to a generation of writers — Proust, Woolf, Mann — who made the experience of time itself a central literary subject. Her contribution is distinctively American and distinctively female: she examines aging not as a philosophical problem but as a material condition, shaped by economics, gender, and the specific historical circumstances of the post-Civil War South. For a woman of the Campion sisters' class, aging meant the loss of whatever social power beauty had provided, with nothing to replace it except the dignity of endurance.
