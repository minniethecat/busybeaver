# Armistice and Peace Celebrations

## Overview

The Armistice celebrations that mark the end of the First World War are the novel's last great scene of the Campion family engaging with the world beyond The Maples. Bells ring; crowds gather; the war that had structured years of Maggie's life through [[war-committees|committee work]] comes to an end. The celebrations are joyful — and also, for the Campion women, a kind of loss. The war that had given them purpose is over; the domestic enclosure that awaits them is permanent.

## The Celebration

The novel renders the Armistice through the accumulation of sensory detail characteristic of Parrish's method: the sound of bells, the movement of crowds, the peculiar quality of public joy. The Campion family participates — briefly, tentatively — in the collective emotion. For a moment, the household's internal dramas are subsumed into something larger.

The celebrations mark the last time the novel will show the Campions as part of a community rather than isolated within their own declining estate. After the Armistice, the novel's focus contracts; the world outside The Maples recedes; the family's self-enclosure becomes complete.

## Aftermath

The most significant consequence of the Armistice is the dissolution of the war committees that had given [[maggie-campion|Maggie]] purpose. The war's end is, for her, an ambiguous blessing — relief at the cessation of violence, loss at the cessation of meaningful work. The novel does not state this directly; it is communicated through the quiet that follows the celebrations, through Maggie's return to the routines of domestic caretaking, through the novel's diminishing interest in anything outside the household's walls.

## Significance

The Armistice celebrations close the novel's engagement with history. The Civil War had shaped the Campion children's earliest memories; the Great War had structured their middle age. After 1918, history recedes from the novel, and what remains is pure domesticity — the slow, undramatic decline of The Maples and its inhabitants.

The celebrations are the last moment when the Campions are part of something larger than themselves. Their retreat afterward is not just physical but existential: the family becomes its own world, and that world is shrinking.
