## Overview

Sam Blow occupies a peculiar position in the world of *The Perennial Bachelor*: he is the man Lizzie Campion married to prove she did not care that Victor Campion had chosen Margaret Southmayd over her. This origin story, established in the novel's first chapter, casts a long shadow over Sam's every appearance. He is by turns a hunting companion, a witness to tragedy, and — by Chapter 24 — the husband of a second, very different wife. Sam Blow is never the focus of a scene, but his presence across four widely spaced chapters makes him one of the more persistent minor figures in the novel's social landscape.

## Role in the Novel

Sam Blow is introduced not in person but through backstory. In Chapter 1, the narrator explains that "everyone knew that Lizzie Campion had been madly in love with her cousin, and had only married Sam Blow to show she didn't care when Victor married Margaret Southmayd" [001-049]. This revelation — delivered with the casualness of small-town gossip — defines Sam from the outset as a second choice, a consolation prize accepted in a fit of pique. The Campion family's awareness of this fact colors every interaction with him.

By Chapter 2, Sam appears as part of the masculine social world of hunting and sport. He goes "with his brother Willie and Sam Blow to shoot reedbirds, and railbirds" [002-034], participating in the rituals of male companionship that the novel depicts as a counterpoint to its domestic interiors. These expeditions are never dramatized directly; they exist only in passing reference, part of the background hum of male activity.

Sam's most dramatic moment comes in Chapter 3, during a carriage accident. As the horse bolts and the wagon overturns, " 'God, his neck's broken!' Sam Blow cried: and Lizzie began to scream, thin and high above the wind, like a rabbit in a trap" [003-048]. Sam's cry is the first spoken reaction to the disaster — a raw, unmediated exclamation that cuts through the scene's chaos. The moment also underscores the Blows' proximity to the Campion family during moments of crisis.

Chapter 24 reveals that Sam has remarried. His new bride, Daisy Blow, arrives at the Campion household with armfuls of decorative items, and the Campion women's reactions to her — half-amused, half-appalled — implicitly reflect on Sam as well. "We don't want Mrs. Sam Blow's things!" [024-061] becomes the household's refrain, a rejection that extends, however indirectly, to Sam himself.

## Relationships

**Lizzie Campion Blow** — Sam's first wife, who married him to spite Victor Campion [001-049]. Their marriage, shadowed by Lizzie's unresolved feelings for her cousin, represents one of the novel's several portraits of romantic compromise.

**Daisy Blow** — Sam's second wife, whose flashy style and overwhelming presence in Chapter 24 suggest that Sam has moved from one form of marital difficulty to another. Daisy's visit to the Campions — bringing her lace tablecloth and pink china — registers as an intrusion the household tolerates but does not welcome [024-048, 024-061].

**Willie Blow** — Sam's brother, with whom he hunts reedbirds [002-034]. The Blow brothers belong to the network of male relatives and acquaintances who form the novel's offstage masculine world.

**The Campion Family** — Sam's relationship with the Campions is defined by his marriage to Lizzie. He is family by law but never quite family by feeling, and the Campions' knowledge of the circumstances of his marriage ensures he will always be regarded with a trace of pity or condescension.

## Significance

Sam Blow is a figure of quiet pathos. He is introduced as a man his wife did not love, and he exits the narrative as a man whose second wife's social blunders provoke the Campions' barely concealed disdain. In a novel preoccupied with the gap between romantic idealism and marital reality, Sam embodies the cost of that gap — not for the person who settled (Lizzie) but for the person who was settled for.

Structurally, Sam's appearances mark some of the novel's most dramatic moments: the carriage accident of Chapter 3 and the comic social ordeal of Chapter 24. He is a witness to crisis and a subject of comedy, never the protagonist of either. In this, he resembles many of Parrish's minor male characters — present, useful to the plot, but never granted the interiority that the Campion women receive.
