# Aunt Priscilla

## Overview

Aunt Priscilla is Mamma's sister — warm-hearted, catastrophically disorganized, and treated by the novel with an affection that stops just short of cruelty. She is the Campion relative who most vividly illustrates the family's pattern of gentle incompetence. Married to Uncle Willie, she presides over a household of cheerful chaos, and her appearances in the novel reliably inject comedy into the accumulating melancholy of The Maples.

## A Creature of Chaos

Priscilla's defining quality is her inability to manage practical life. Her conversation "rippled around" [001-037] inconsequential topics; she talked "in a mild stream" [001-035] while following Mamma through the conservatory. Her domestic efforts are well-intentioned and reliably disastrous.

She once made caramels for Willie and hid them in the attic under her grandmother's lace wedding-veil — "to keep off the flies" [028-???]. Then she forgot about them entirely, remembering only during Sunday school while screaming "Work, for the night is coming," at which point she spent the rest of the service repeating "Caramels — caramels" to herself through the psalms and sermon.

This is vintage Priscilla: her intentions are impeccable, her execution is absurd, and her self-awareness is nonexistent. She is the novel's purest example of a character who means well and cannot help being what she is.

## Mamma's Foil

The relationship between Priscilla and Mamma is one of the novel's most economically drawn contrasts. Where Mamma is beautiful and self-possessed, Priscilla is plain and flustered. Where Mamma's selfishness is elegant, Priscilla's is muddled. Mamma thinks "Poor Priscilla" [001-049] with automatic condescension, and the phrase captures the dynamic perfectly: Priscilla exists as a reassurance to Mamma that she herself, however impractical, is at least not *that*.

Yet Priscilla is, in her way, more generous than Mamma. She offers her grandmother's wedding veil to May; she makes decorations for Papa's birthday; she comes to help, however ineffectually. Her incompetence is paired with genuine warmth in a combination the novel treats as both comic and strangely admirable.

## Significance

Aunt Priscilla is the novel's gentlest satirical portrait — a character whose absurdity is rendered with such affection that it becomes a kind of tribute. She represents the Campion family's capacity for muddling through, for maintaining warmth despite chaos, for surviving despite every evidence of unfitness for survival.

She is also a cautionary figure for the Campion daughters: a woman who, like them, was shaped by the same forces of [[southern-womanhood|Southern womanhood]] and [[gender-roles|gender expectations]], and who emerged as a kind of permanent child. Her fate — married to Uncle Willie, perpetually overwhelmed, loved despite everything — is the comic version of the tragedy that the novel traces through the Campion sisters.
