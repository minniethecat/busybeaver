## Overview

Fannie (née Leaf, later Page) belongs to the Campion sisters' wider social circle in the small-town world of *The Perennial Bachelor*. First seen in the tableaux vivants at The Maples, where she and Prentice Page perform as a charming couple [012-008], Fannie is consistently described as "pretty" — a quality that sets her apart and shapes her social trajectory [016-037]. Her life follows the expected arc of her milieu: courtship, marriage, children, and a settled domesticity that contrasts with the Campion sisters' more complicated paths.

## Role in the Novel

Fannie appears in the novel primarily as a point of social comparison. During the tableaux, she and Prentice Page are "The Dumb Oarsman" in Fannie's tan cape [012-009], and Maggie, watching, asks with characteristic bluntness: "Do you think she's pretty?" [012-026]. The answer is clearly yes — and Fannie's prettiness, freely acknowledged, opens doors that remain closed to the plain-featured Maggie.

By Chapter 17, Fannie has married: "Fannie and Prentice and their two little girls" [017-005] serve as a quiet reproach to the unmarried Campion sisters. Mamma feels the sting directly: "Here's Fannie been married four years" [017-007]. The tennis matches of Chapter 18 pair May and pretty Fannie against Maggie, with Maggie in her "manly straw hat," a visual shorthand for the different forms of femininity on display [018-011].

As the years pass, Fannie's prettiness settles into contented domesticity: "Fannie's getting fat!" [018-078], though her "pretty ringed hand" [018-090] remains a detail Maggie notices during goodbyes in the hall. Her later appearance as Fannie Page in Chapter 29, "tilting the side of her fan" and discussing a banquet speech [029-001], shows her fully established in middle-aged respectability.

## Relationships

- **Maggie Campion** — A friendly acquaintance; Maggie observes Fannie with a mixture of admiration and detachment, aware that Fannie's easy prettiness represents a path she cannot walk.
- **May Campion** — Tennis partner and social peer; May and Fannie are paired as the "pretty" team against Maggie [018-011].
- **Prentice Page** — Her husband; they appear together in the tableaux and later as a married couple with children.

## Significance

Fannie embodies the conventional feminine ideal against which the Campion sisters — especially Maggie — are measured and found wanting. Her prettiness, her early marriage, her children, and her placid contentment trace the outline of a life that society expects and rewards. That she is drawn with economy rather than interiority is itself significant: Fannie is seen from the outside, as the Campion sisters see her, a mirror reflecting what they are not. Her transformation from Fannie Leaf to Fannie Page, from pretty girl to matron with a "ringed hand," marks the passage of time in the novel as surely as the changing fashions and the graying of Mamma's hair.
