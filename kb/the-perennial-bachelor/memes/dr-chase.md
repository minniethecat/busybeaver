## Overview

Dr. Chase was the local physician serving the Campion family and the wider Wilmington community. He appears in the novel's early chapters attending to the birth of Victor Campion and reappears later during Aunt Priscilla's final illness. Though a minor character, his presence anchors the domestic world of the novel in the tangible rhythms of nineteenth-century small-town medicine—the doctor with his black bag, summoned to births and deathbeds alike [004-016].

## Role in the Novel

Dr. Chase first enters the narrative in the context of Victor Campion's birth. When the Campion children ask where babies come from, the family offers competing explanations: Aunt Priscilla attributes Victor's arrival to Dr. Chase and his black bag, a theory young Maggie finds persuasive because she witnessed the doctor entering her mother's room carrying it [004-019]. Cousin Jennie Blodgett offers the stork story, while Mamma prefers the angelic version—a layered domestic comedy that introduces the character through the eyes of children puzzling over adult mysteries [004-016].

Dr. Chase reappears much later in the novel under graver circumstances. When Aunt Priscilla falls fatally ill from a poisoned dish she had lovingly prepared, Dr. Chase is summoned but can do nothing. "Nothing could counteract the poison of that dish Aunt Priscilla had offered her so lovingly. Dr. Chase couldn't help her, no one could help her" [015-044]. This appearance frames the doctor as a figure present at both the beginnings and endings of the Campion family story.

## Relationships

Dr. Chase's relationships in the novel are professional rather than personal. He is the attending physician for Mamma Campion during Victor's birth, glimpsed through young Maggie's eyes as a figure of authority with his black bag [004-019]. Later, he attends Aunt Priscilla during her terminal illness, his inability to save her underscoring the limits of medical intervention in the era [015-044]. The Campion household staff—Martha in particular—assist him during the crisis, working "good as gold, feeling sad and excited and bursting with importance" [015-044].

## Significance

Dr. Chase functions as a structural presence in the novel's domestic landscape, representing the medical realities of nineteenth-century American small-town life. His appearances bookend the narrative's emotional range: the comic speculation of children about where babies come from, and the tragic helplessness of a family at a deathbed. Anne Parrish uses him sparingly but effectively—the black bag that fascinated Maggie as a child becomes, by implication, a symbol of both life's arrival and its departure. The character's minimal presence in the novel reflects the historical reality of the general practitioner whose role, while socially central, was often limited to attending births, certifying deaths, and offering what comfort was available in between.
