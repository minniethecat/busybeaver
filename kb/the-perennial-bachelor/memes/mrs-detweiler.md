# Mrs. Detweiler

## Overview

Mrs. Detweiler is a prosperous neighbor of the Campion family in Wilmington. She is a figure of material comfort and social energy — the proud owner of a phonograph (a technological marvel of the era), a hostess of box-parties in Philadelphia, and a gardener whose elaborate floral displays reflect both her generosity and her competitive instincts in neighborhood display culture.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Detweiler appears primarily in the later chapters as part of the social fabric of Wilmington. She represents the new prosperity of the Gilded Age — a woman whose household possesses the latest inventions and whose entertainments span from at-home phonograph demonstrations to theater excursions in Philadelphia. Her presence illuminates the social world in which the Campion daughters navigate courtship expectations.

She is significant in two emotional registers. During May's illness and death, Mrs. Detweiler sends lilies, and Maggie, in her grief, regrets ever having "made fun of her" — a small moment of retrospective generosity that underscores the community's shared mourning (Chapter 28). Her garden, with its "red and yellow parrot tulips, all feathered and fringed," becomes a quiet legacy passed to the Campion household.

## Key Appearances

### The Phonograph Demonstration (Chapter 26)

Mrs. Detweiler introduces the Campion sisters to her phonograph — a talking machine that leaves a powerful impression. The household's decor — "tapestry squares of gaming cavaliers and drinking monks" and a deerskin with antlers — conveys a particular style of late-Victorian interior abundance. Her laughter during an Uncle Josh Whitcomb monologue is unforgettable: "little bright tears stood on her bulges of cheek, and her diamond eardrops quivered."

### The Philadelphia Box-Party (Chapter 27)

Mrs. Detweiler hosts a box-party in Philadelphia where the party sees a production of *Everyman*. The narrative notes her reaction with characteristic humor: she "nearly cracked her jaw yawning, but she knew it was 'artistic,' and genteelly patted the back of her gloved wrist at the end of each act." This episode captures the tension between genuine cultural aspiration and the performative aspects of Gilded Age social climbing.

### May's Death (Chapter 28)

After May Campion drowns, Mrs. Detweiler sends lilies. Maggie, overwhelmed by grief, exclaims: "Mrs. Detweiler sent all these lilies — I wish I'd never made fun of her." The moment is brief but revealing — the neighbor's gesture of sympathy transcends the petty social judgments that previously defined their relationship.

### The Garden Legacy (Chapter 31)

Mrs. Detweiler contributes bulbs from her own garden to the Campion household: "red and yellow parrot tulips, all feathered and fringed." Maggie plants them, and the tulips become a tangible connection between the two households, a small but enduring mark of neighborly exchange.

## Character and Significance

Mrs. Detweiler embodies the social world of late-nineteenth-century Wilmington — a world of phonographs and moving pictures, box-parties and chafing-dish suppers, where prosperity manifested in material display and social one-upmanship. Yet the novel does not treat her merely as satire. Her laughter is genuine, her grief at May's death is expressed through the tangible generosity of flowers, and her garden continues blooming in the Campions' soil. She is a minor but vividly realized figure in the novel's social landscape.

## Relationships

- **The Campion sisters (Maggie, May, Lily)**: Neighbors whom she entertains and, in the case of May, mourns
- **The Spear family**: Mentioned in contrast to Mrs. Detweiler's taste; the Spears' restrained garden aesthetic is implicitly compared with her more exuberant style
