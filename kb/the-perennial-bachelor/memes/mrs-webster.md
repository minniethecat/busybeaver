# Mrs. Webster

## Overview

Mrs. Webster is a fixture of Wilmington's musical life, a soprano whose performances of sentimental parlor songs accompany the novel's key social gatherings. Her voice is characterized by an extravagant tremolo — the narrative notes that "the staff and the stems of the notes on her sheet of music must be scalloped instead of straight" — and she is frequently paired with Mr. Bates, whose bass provides a grounding counterpoint to her soaring high notes.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Webster appears in Chapters 12 and 14, providing the musical texture for two significant events: an evening of tableaux vivants and amateur theatricals (Chapter 12) and May Campion's wedding (Chapter 14). She is never developed as a character in her own right; rather, she is part of the social furniture — the singer whose warblings fill the background while courtships, flirtations, and family dramas play out in the foreground.

Her function is partly atmospheric and partly structural. The songs she sings — "Sweet and Low," "Tears, Idle Tears," hymns of "contemplation" — provide a soundtrack that both complements and ironically comments on the action. While she sings of "the tender grace of a day that is dead," the young people below are absorbed in the very much living business of romance.

## Key Appearances

### The Evening of Tableaux (Chapter 12)

At an evening gathering featuring the "Lady of Shalott" tableau, Mrs. Webster sings "Sweet and Low" with such pronounced tremolo that the narrative's description becomes a gentle satire: the sheet music itself seems affected. Mr. Bates follows with "Break, break, break," and the ladies "sigh and smile." Later, the two combine for a duet of "Tears, Idle Tears," during which Edward Post and May exchange barbs about a fly on someone's nose — the sublime lyrics about "the days that are no more" floating above a flirtation conducted in whispers.

The placement of her music against the foreground action illustrates the novel's technique of ironic counterpoint: the profundity of Tennyson's verse set against the comedy of a young woman hunting for lost slippers and a young man pretending not to care.

### May's Wedding (Chapter 14)

At May's wedding, Mrs. Webster's voice "soared higher than the steeple" on the phrase "Beneath thy contemplation," an acoustical feat that marks the ceremony's emotional peak. The moment is immediately undercut by Stewy Grant's sneeze, muffled through the closed door, followed by the murmured "Amen" — the sacred and the comic jostling together in the novel's characteristic register.

## Character and Significance

Mrs. Webster is not a character in the psychological sense; she is a presence, a voice, a recurring element of the social soundscape. Her tremolo is a joke the novel shares with the reader, but it is an affectionate joke — she is not mocked so much as observed with the same wry precision the novel brings to every social performance. In a world where everyone is performing something — courtship, grief, piety, charm — Mrs. Webster's performance is at least honest in its artifice: she sings, she trembles, and the notes land somewhere near their target.

## Relationships

- **Mr. Bates**: Her frequent duet partner; together they form the musical backbone of Wilmington's social gatherings
- **The Campion family**: Present at their social events, including May's wedding
