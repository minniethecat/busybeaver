## Overview

Miss Martin is the organist at St. Stephen's Episcopal Church in Wilmington, where the Campion family worships. She is a minor but recurring figure whose presence helps establish the texture of parish life in the town. Physically, she is described as "small and grey and whiskered like a mouse" [006-012], an image that evokes her quiet, unassuming place within the congregation. Beyond her church duties, she gives music lessons to the town's children — a practical necessity for a woman of modest means in late-nineteenth-century America.

Miss Martin first appears in Chapter 6, hurrying out of church with her music under her arm after Sunday services [006-012]. Her role, though modest, recurs across the novel's middle chapters and illuminates the social fabric within which the Campion family moves.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Martin serves a functional role at St. Stephen's: she plays the organ for Sunday services and, presumably, for weddings and funerals. During one service, the bass soloist Mr. Bates booms "up and down — much too loud, thought Miss Martin at the organ, it really drowned out all the rest of the choir. Something tactful would have to be said again — oh dear!" [014-016]. This moment of private exasperation, rendered through free indirect discourse, reveals a woman who cares about musical decorum but dreads confrontation — a small, telling detail of character.

She is present at the conclusion of the same service, setting "the organ pealing" as the congregation files out [014-022], and receives a cheerful "Good morning, Miss Martin! Thank you! Yes, indeed — !" from a parishioner [014-024]. These brief glimpses establish her as a fixture of church life, reliable and unobtrusive.

Her role extends beyond Sunday mornings. In Chapter 17, it is noted that a character has been "giving up her music lessons with Miss Martin, and working alone, with a notebook" [017-019]. The lessons Miss Martin provides are evidently a standard part of a middle-class girl's upbringing — and their abandonment marks a shift toward independence.

## Relationships

**St. Stephen's Congregation** — Miss Martin interacts with the parishioners as their organist, receiving polite greetings and enduring the vicissitudes of amateur choir performances. Her relationship with the church is professional but familiar; she is a known quantity.

**Mr. Bates** — The over-loud bass soloist whose volume troubles Miss Martin's musical sensibilities [014-016]. She finds herself in the uncomfortable position of needing to correct him but dreading the conversation.

**Music Pupils** — Miss Martin teaches music to local children, including one who eventually gives up lessons to work independently [017-019]. These lessons represent both her supplemental income and her contribution to the town's cultural life.

## Significance

Miss Martin belongs to the class of minor townsperson characters who populate the margins of *The Perennial Bachelor*. She contributes no dramatic incident and advances no plot; her significance is atmospheric. Like Miss Perry the seamstress and Miss Hessie Farley the Sunday school teacher, Miss Martin helps establish Wilmington as a real community — one where the organist hurries out of church with her music under her arm, where someone must tactfully tell Mr. Bates he is singing too loud, and where music lessons are a rite of passage for young ladies.

Her characterization — "small and grey and whiskered like a mouse" [006-012] — is typical of Anne Parrish's unsentimental portraiture. Parrish sketches these minor figures with a few deft strokes, granting them a flicker of interiority (Miss Martin's "oh dear!") before returning to the Campions. In this, Miss Martin exemplifies the novel's quiet investment in the world beyond its central family — a world of small anxieties, small duties, and small dignities.
