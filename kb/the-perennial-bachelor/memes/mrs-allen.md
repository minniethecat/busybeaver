# Mrs. Allen

## Overview

Mrs. Allen is a figure in the Wilmington social circle frequented by the Campion family. She appears at two key gatherings: an evening of theatrical tableaux (Chapter 12), where she recites Tennyson, and a Fourth of July fireworks party (Chapter 14), where she and her husband Mr. Allen are among the guests. Her presence in the novel is brief but illustrative of the social world in which the Campion daughters' courtships unfold.

## Key Appearances

### The Lady of Shalott Tableau (Chapter 12)

At an evening gathering featuring amateur theatricals, Mrs. Allen reads "The Lady of Shalott" with "great expression." Her recitation provides the backdrop for May Campion's tableau — "May was so beautiful that Lily wept unrestrainedly, and Edward Post looked down at her as if he really loved her." Mrs. Allen's Tennyson, delivered with conviction, frames one of the novel's pivotal romantic moments.

### The Fourth of July Fireworks (Chapter 14)

Mrs. Allen and her husband are among the guests at the Campions' Independence Day celebration: "the Blows, and the Willie Campions, and Mr. and Mrs. Allen and Edward Post were coming for the fireworks." The evening is described with the novel's characteristic attention to the lingering summer light — "The light never lingered so long as on Fourth of July; but at last it grew dark" — and the fireworks display provides a communal occasion for the social bonds that structure Wilmington life.

## Relationships

- **Mr. Henry Allen**: Her husband; together they form part of the married couples who populate the novel's social landscape
- **Edward Post**: A social connection; Mrs. Allen is associated with the circle that includes May's future husband
