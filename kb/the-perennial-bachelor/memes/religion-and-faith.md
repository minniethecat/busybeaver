# Religion and Faith

## Overview

Religion in *The Perennial Bachelor* is less a matter of belief than of practice — the quiet rhythms of the Episcopal Church that structure the Campion family's week, mark their rites of passage, and provide the moral language in which they understand their obligations to one another. Anne Parrish treats religion with the same clear-sighted gentleness she brings to all her subjects: the Church is a genuine source of consolation for her characters, and it is also one of the institutions that teaches women that their suffering has spiritual meaning.

The novel's religious sensibility is distinctive. It is not the evangelical fervor of the revival meeting or the intellectual complexity of theological debate; it is the domesticated Christianity of the prayer book and the parish, the faith of a community that has never seriously considered not believing. Parrish's narrator never mocks this faith, but she registers its limitations — particularly its inability to offer women anything beyond the consolations of sacrifice (030-053).

## Treatment in the Novel

The Catechism that opens Chapter 6 establishes the novel's moral framework. "What is thy duty towards thy neighbor?" the question runs, and the answer: "To love him as myself" (006-001, 006-002). The language of the prayer book provides the characters with a vocabulary for obligation — duty, neighbor, sacrifice, love — that shapes how they understand their lives. But Parrish is attentive to the gap between the ideal and the practice: the duty to love one's neighbor falls most heavily on those with the least power to resist it.

The Episcopal Church serves as a social institution as much as a spiritual one. Church attendance marks class membership — "nice people, no doubt, but not Church people" (026-002) — and denominational distinctions carry social weight. The novel's reference to "High Church" practice (020-035) is both an observation about ritual preference and a marker of social aspiration. Aunt Priscilla's "very High Church" turn (023-008) is presented with affectionate irony: the aesthetic satisfactions of ritual are real, even if their theological content is thin.

The novel's most explicit theological statement comes through the language of sacrifice. "Before the wheat becomes bread it must be ground, before the grapes become wine they must be crushed. They must be sacrificed, as Christ on the Cross was sacrificed" (030-053). This passage, which describes Maggie's suffering, draws a direct parallel between the Eucharist and women's self-sacrifice. The imagery dignifies the suffering — it is, after all, compared to Christ's — while also making visible its horror. What kind of religion, the novel implicitly asks, sanctifies the systematic destruction of women's lives?

The novel's final religious note is ambiguous. The language of sacrifice persists to the end (031-012), but whether it represents genuine consolation or simply the exhausted piety of people who have nothing else is left for the reader to decide. Parrish does not offer a verdict on her characters' faith; she simply shows what it costs them and what, if anything, it gives them in return.

## Character Perspectives

Maggie's relationship to religion is the novel's most fully realized. She does not question the faith she inherited, but she lives it with an integrity that exposes its contradictions. Her sacrifice is real and costly; whether the Church that taught her to sacrifice has anything to offer her in return is a question the novel raises but does not answer.

Mamma's religion is decorative — a matter of attendance, of social performance, of the aesthetic pleasures of liturgy. Parrish does not condemn this; she simply notes that a religion of beauty and consolation is a luxury available only to those whose lives are otherwise comfortable.

Cousin Lizzie Blow's turn to Spiritualism represents the novel's only departure from orthodox Christianity — and it is treated as eccentricity rather than heresy, one more expression of a restless temperament that convention cannot contain.

## Literary and Historical Context

The Episcopal Church occupied a distinctive position in nineteenth-century American society: the church of the establishment, of the old Anglo-American families who had lost the Puritan intensity of their ancestors but retained the forms of Protestant piety. In the post-Civil War South, the Episcopal Church offered a version of Christianity compatible with gentility — undemanding, aesthetically pleasing, socially impeccable.

Parrish's treatment of religion belongs to a tradition that includes Jane Austen's clerical comedies and George Eliot's earnest examinations of doubt. But Parrish's Christianity is neither comic nor doubtful; it is simply present, a fact of her characters' world, operating beneath the level of conscious choice to shape their understanding of duty, sacrifice, and meaning.
