## Overview

Prentice Page moves through the social world of *The Perennial Bachelor* as a charming, slightly elusive young man — handsome enough to catch May Campion's eye, agreeable enough to play tennis and exchange banter, and conventional enough to marry the pretty Fannie Leaf and settle into contented domesticity. He appears at the tableaux vivants, on the tennis lawn, in garden flirtations, and finally as a married man whose wife puzzles over why Victor Campion never married. Across these appearances he serves as both a romantic possibility that flickers and fades, and a marker of the ordinary married life the Campion sisters watch from the sidelines.

## Role in the Novel

Prentice first appears at the tableaux vivants held at The Maples, where "Mr. Bates and Prentice Page stood ready to pull back the hitchy curtains" [012-001]. His role as stagehand — part of the machinery of the entertainment rather than its center — foreshadows his function in the novel: he is always present at the edges of the drama, never its subject. Moments later, the curtain jerks back on "what a charming tableau! Fannie Leaf and Prentice Page were 'Come Into the Garden, Maud'" [012-008], already paired with the girl he will eventually marry.

Years later, in Chapter 17, Mrs. Leaf announces "Fannie and Prentice and their two little girls are home for a visit" [017-005], and the news ripples through the Campion household as a reminder of the domestic normalcy that has passed the sisters by. The tennis matches of Chapter 18 bring Prentice into sharper focus: "Prentice Page with ferns inside his hat to keep off the sun, looking like a faun from the forest" [018-011] — a vignette that catches his boyish vanity and charm in a single image. He plays against Maggie, who cuts a less romantic figure in her "manly straw sailor" [018-011].

It is with May, however, that Prentice's most charged scenes unfold. Cooling off in the shade after tennis, May tells him with studied nonchalance, "Oh, I've settled down to being an old maid, Prentice. I've decided I'd rather paddle my own canoe" [018-033]. Her performance of indifference is belied by what follows: Mrs. Hawthorn observes May "making such eyes at Prentice" and notes that "you can see he's laughing at her" [018-037]. Later, in the moonlit garden, May lets Prentice "say those things, flattering and insolent" [018-090], and then lies awake tormented: "Why had she acted like such a cheap fool in the garden after supper? Oh, why had she let Prentice say those things?" [018-090].

> "Oh, I've settled down to being an old maid, Prentice. I've decided I'd rather paddle my own canoe."
> — [018-033]

The novel's final glimpse of Prentice comes decades later, in Chapter 29. He and Fannie are married, settled, reading letters and newspapers in their home. Fannie, discussing Victor Campion's bachelorhood, presses Prentice for agreement: "You know it isn't only Victor himself, but I believe the girls wouldn't have been old maids if it hadn't been for him" [029-025]. Prentice's response is characteristically noncommittal: "'Mm,' said Prentice, reading" [029-026]. He has become a man who deflects his wife's speculations with vague sounds, more interested in his newspaper than in the existential puzzles of the Campion household. And yet Fannie's final question — "And can you imagine — even imagine life without any children, Prentice?" [029-029] — hangs in the air, unanswered, a rhetorical question that is really about the Campions' unlived lives.

## Relationships

- **Fannie Leaf (later Fannie Page)** — His wife. They appear together first in the tableaux vivants [012-008] and later as a married couple with two daughters [017-005]. Fannie's pretty "ringed hand" through his arm [018-090] signals their settled union.
- **May Campion** — A romantic near-miss. Prentice flirts with May on the tennis court and in the garden [018-033, 018-090], and May is wounded to discover the flirtation meant nothing serious when she sees him afterward "in the lamplight again, saying goodbye in the hall, with Fannie's pretty ringed hand dangling through his arm" [018-090].
- **Maggie Campion** — Tennis opponent [018-011]; their interactions are cordial but underscored by Maggie's awareness that she does not belong to the world of pretty girls and easy flirtation that Prentice inhabits.
- **Victor Campion** — A subject of dinner-table speculation; Fannie wonders aloud why Victor never married [029-021], and Prentice's indifference to the question underscores the distance between his settled domestic life and Victor's protracted bachelorhood.

## Significance

Prentice Page is a minor character, but in a novel about lives that do not follow the expected script, his very ordinariness carries meaning. He marries the pretty girl, has children, reads the newspaper, deflects his wife's questions with murmurs — he is the unexamined life, the path of least resistance that the Campion sisters, for reasons both chosen and unchosen, cannot take. His flirtation with May is the novel in miniature: a possibility glimpsed in moonlight, renounced in lamplight, and dissolved into the ordinary texture of marriage to someone else. May's humiliation — "poor old May!" [018-090] — is Prentice's obliviousness, and that asymmetry is precisely the point.
