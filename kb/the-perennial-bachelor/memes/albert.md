## Overview

Albert is the outdoor man at The Maples, the Campion family home. His responsibilities span the physical maintenance of the property — sweeping, firewood, gardening, and running errands — and he is a quiet but steady presence throughout the novel's early and middle chapters. Along with Martha the indoor servant, Albert represents the stratum of domestic labor that sustains the Campions' genteel life, his work so woven into the household's rhythm that he is noticed chiefly in the small sensory details he produces: the swish of his broom [001-022], the baskets of cherries [013-017], the freezer of raspberry water-ice for summer parties [013-043].

## Role in the Novel

Albert's first appearance establishes the morning routine at The Maples: "from below the swish, swish of Albert's broom, sweeping the porch" [001-022]. He is part of the household's sensory fabric, his labor registered in sound and temperature — logs carried in for the fire [003-001], a pail of water fetched from the pump [007-049], cherries picked from the trees [008-001]. When Victor comes home, it is Albert who tells Martha, sending her running with joy [007-054].

Albert's work enables the novel's set-piece social occasions. For the fireworks evening, he makes "a big freezer of raspberry water-ice" [013-043]; for gatherings, he brings "a big basket" of cherries to the porch [013-017]. His presence recedes in the novel's later chapters as the family's fortunes shift — "they could only afford to keep Albert outside and Martha in the house" [014-001] — and the quiet threat of his absence registers the Campions' declining circumstances.

A small but telling detail emerges in Chapter 16, when the Campion women are "both thinking of Albert's back and Albert's ears" [016-031] — a moment that hints at the intimate, unspoken awareness of a servant's physical presence that characterizes the domestic life of the period.

## Relationships

- **The Campion Family** — Albert serves the household as outdoor man, his tenure spanning years. His work is taken for granted yet essential.
- **Martha** — Fellow servant at The Maples; Martha handles indoor work while Albert manages the grounds.
- **Caesar** — The gardener, another outdoor servant with whom Albert shares the rhythms of the property's upkeep [007-055].
- **Victor Campion** — Albert announces Victor's homecoming, sending Martha running with the news [007-054].

## Significance

Albert belongs to the novel's quiet economy of service — the domestic workers whose labor makes possible the leisure in which the Campion sisters' dramas unfold. Parrish renders him almost entirely through action rather than speech or interiority: he sweeps, carries, fetches, and makes water-ice, his presence noted in the results of his work rather than in his personhood. This narrative treatment is itself a historical document of early twentieth-century domestic arrangements, where servants were visible chiefly through what they produced. That the Campion women can afford to "keep Albert outside" [014-001] — and the implication that they might one day not be able to — makes him an index of the family's economic trajectory, as telling in its way as the fading of Mamma's wedding slippers or the selling of Papa's books.
