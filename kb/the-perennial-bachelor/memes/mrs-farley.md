## Overview

Mrs. Farley is a Wilmington townswoman who appears in several communal scenes — at church, at the Tennyson Tableaux, at a Valentine euchre party — always in the role of helper, organizer, or background commentator. She is the mother of Hessie Farley, whose sharper observations ("Such a fuss — !" at Fannie Leaf's engagement announcement) give the Farley women a small but distinct presence in the novel's social fabric. Mrs. Farley herself is less acerbic than her daughter, more the practical mother who asks "Why didn't you hold on to them?" when Hessie loses her costume pieces, and who later "talked for a year about how much trouble" her Valentine party had been.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Farley first appears as a name in the congregation, grouped with "people like Miss Hessie Farley and Mr. and Mrs. Almond" — the churchgoing townspeople whose lives form the background hum of the community [006-012]. Her role, like most of the minor townswomen, is to populate the social world that surrounds the Campion sisters.

Her most vivid appearance is backstage at the Tennyson Tableaux. Her daughter Hessie "was going to be Airy Fairy Lilian in half a minute, and was in a panic because she couldn't find her blue scarf and parasol anywhere. Mrs. Farley, who was helping behind the scenes, asked, 'Why didn't you hold on to them?' and tears of fury sprang to her daughter's eyes" [012-001]. The moment is small and perfect: the practical mother asking the practical question, the daughter responding with theatrical fury, the whole miniature drama playing out while "the ladies seethed" and Mr. Bates and Prentice Page peered through the curtains.

Later, Mrs. Farley and Hessie appear together outside church after Fannie Leaf's engagement is announced: "'Goodness! You'd think nobody in the world had ever got engaged before,' Miss Hessie Farley said gloomily to Mrs. Farley as they walked away. 'Such a fuss — !'" [014-010]. Mother and daughter, walking away together, offering the wry commentary that the principals cannot.

Mrs. Farley's social ambitions surface indirectly through a later description: Mrs. Almond "thought she certainly washed her hair with soda to make it golden, and told Hessie Farley so; and Hessie murmured back that that was probably what gave her face that queer artificial look" [017-020]. The gossip flows from mother to daughter — Mrs. Almond tells Hessie, and one imagines Hessie reporting it to Mrs. Farley later, the two of them dissecting the pretensions of Mrs. Hawthorn over tea.

Her most sustained enterprise is the Valentine euchre party: "a romantic setting of red cardboard hearts, heart-shaped sandwiches, heart-shaped cakes with beetroot-reddened icing, and large crimson hearts of geranium blossoms. Mrs. Farley talked for a year about how much trouble it had all been" [027-005]. The detail is comic and affectionate — the elaborate themed refreshments, the year of complaint that is also a year of pride.

## Relationships

- **Hessie Farley** — Her daughter, sharper-tongued than her mother, who appears in scenes from Sunday school to engagement announcements to gossip about Mrs. Hawthorn's hair. Their relationship is one of the novel's quiet mother-daughter duos.
- **Mrs. Almond** — Fellow churchgoer who confides her suspicions about Mrs. Hawthorn's hair to Hessie, linking the Farley women to the novel's network of female gossip.
- **Lily Campion** — Briefly connected through the Valentine euchre party, where Lily meets Mr. Marshall "in a romantic setting of red cardboard hearts."

## Significance

Mrs. Farley is one of the novel's many background mothers — practical, slightly put-upon, proud of her parties, absorbed in the small dramas of church and town. Her role is structural: she adds depth to the social world, provides a foil for her daughter's sharper commentary, and represents the kind of woman whose life is measured in tableaux costumes and heart-shaped sandwiches. The novel grants her no interiority, but it grants her the dignity of being named, of having a daughter who walks beside her, of having talked for a year about how much trouble it had all been.
