## Overview

Washington is Aunt Priscilla's servant, memorable chiefly for his reluctance to be found when wanted and his girth — "fat and black as a blackbird too fat and old to fly" [005-016]. His appearances in the novel are few but vivid, each one a small comic portrait of the asymmetrical relationship between a scatterbrained mistress and the servant who has learned to manage her.

## Role in the Novel

Washington's most extended scene occurs in Chapter 5, when Aunt Priscilla calls for him to ready the carriage: "'Washington! Oh, Washington!' But Washington, fat and black as a blackbird too fat and old to fly, felt the afternoon was over-hot for a drive, and remained where he was, comfortably hidden" [005-016]. His hiding is not rebellion so much as quiet self-preservation — the afternoon is hot, the drive unnecessary, and Washington knows his mistress well enough to predict that the impulse will pass.

The narrative gives us a startlingly intimate detail: Washington sits "mimicking his mistress under his breath" [005-016], repeating her words back to himself in a private performance that inverts the power relationship, if only for a moment. Aunt Priscilla, unaware, rationalizes his absence: "I couldn't find Washington — I guess he was busy somewhere, and I don't like to stop him. He says the work's almost too much for him anyway" [005-021]. The comedy lies in the gap between her charitable interpretation and the reality of Washington hiding from her.

Later, when Aunt Priscilla kisses her little peach tree, she looks around "in a panic to make sure Washington hadn't seen her" [015-007]. The servant's gaze, even when absent, exerts a disciplinary pressure — the mistress embarrassed before the man she employs, aware at some level that her sentimentality would not survive his witness.

## Relationships

- **Aunt Priscilla Campion** — His mistress; their relationship is one of mutual accommodation — she makes excuses for his absences, he manages his labor to match her fluctuating demands.
- **The Campion Family** — Peripheral connections through his service to Aunt Priscilla.

## Significance

Washington is one of several Black servants who appear at the edges of the novel's domestic scenes — Caesar, Martha, Albert, Chloe, Jake, Cobina. Like them, he is rendered through action rather than interiority: he hides, he mimics, he remains comfortably out of reach. What distinguishes Washington is the note of subversive comedy in his portrayal. His mimicry of Aunt Priscilla [005-016] is a small act of resistance, performed in private but shared with the reader. In a novel that largely takes its white characters' perspectives for granted, this moment — a Black servant mocking his mistress's words back to himself — opens a brief window onto a consciousness the novel otherwise declines to enter.
