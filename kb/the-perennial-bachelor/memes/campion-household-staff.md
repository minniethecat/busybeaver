# The Campion Household Staff

## Overview

The Campion household was sustained by generations of domestic workers — cooks, housemen, and servants whose names appear in the novel's margins and whose labor was essential to the family's way of life. [[chloe|Chloe]], the elderly cook, and [[martha|Martha]], her kitchen assistant, are the most visible; Carpus the houseman appears briefly; and numerous unnamed workers pass through the novel's periphery. Together they constitute the quiet infrastructure upon which the Campion family's gentility depended.

## The Household Economy

The novel registers the household's dependence on its staff primarily through the effects of reduction. As The Maples declined, the staff contracted. Mamma considered letting Susannah go: "I might possibly let Susannah go — she's very lazy" [007-???] — the casual dismissal of a worker whose labor had been taken for granted. The observation reveals both the household's economic pressures and its easy assumption that servants could be dispensed with when convenience required.

Carpus, the houseman, appears at a critical moment during a Guild meeting at Mrs. Leaf's house: "Carpus the houseman in his white jacket, with his black forehead all puckered with perplexity, brought in a tray" [017-???]. The detail — his white jacket, his perplexity, his role as the bearer of disappointing refreshments — captures the complex visibility of the Black domestic worker in the novel's world: seen, described, essential, and never known.

## The Limits of the Novel's Vision

Parrish's treatment of the household staff reflects both the conventions of 1925 fiction and the racial limitations of her perspective. The servants are rendered with a degree of physical specificity — Chloe's age, Carpus's white jacket, Martha's presence in the kitchen — but they are never given interior lives. They exist as functions: cooking, cleaning, bearing trays, sustaining the household that would collapse without them.

This limitation is itself historically significant. The novel's inability to imagine Black domestic workers as full characters is a feature of the literary tradition it inhabits, and reading against that limitation reveals the structural reality the novel cannot articulate: the Campion family's gentility was built on labor it could not fully acknowledge.

## Significance

The household staff page exists to make visible what the novel renders invisible. By collecting the scattered references to domestic workers across the novel's chapters, it documents the labor economy that the Campion story depends upon but cannot see. The staff is the novel's structuring absence — the presence without which nothing in The Maples would function, and which the novel's own conventions prevent it from honoring.
