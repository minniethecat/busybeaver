# Gender Roles

## Overview

The central structural insight of *The Perennial Bachelor* is conveyed through its title: a man who never marries is a "perennial bachelor," a figure of affectionate comedy, while his three unmarried sisters are "old maids," figures of pity or contempt. This asymmetry — the double standard that assigns radically different social meanings to the same condition based on the gender of the person experiencing it — is the novel's most sustained critique of the society it depicts.

Anne Parrish does not argue this point; she demonstrates it. Over the course of the novel, the reader watches the Campion family's resources — financial, emotional, social — flow toward Victor and away from his sisters, not through anyone's conscious malice but through the operation of a gender system so naturalized that no one in the family thinks to question it. The result is a quiet tragedy: three women's lives consumed in the service of a man who has done nothing to earn their sacrifice except be born male.

## Treatment in the Novel

The novel opens by establishing gendered expectations through the contrast between the Campion children. Maggie, at ten, is already taking responsibility; Victor, the only son, is already receiving indulgences. The pattern intensifies as the children grow. Maggie's practicality, her plainness, her competence — these are presented as disqualifications from femininity even as they make her the family's most valuable member. May's beauty qualifies her for the marriage market but traps her in a role defined entirely by male desire. Lily's delicacy exempts her from labor but also from agency.

Victor's arc is the novel's most devastating study in gender privilege. He is not cruel, not consciously exploitative; he is simply the product of a system that has never asked him to consider the costs his existence imposes on others. The novel traces his career — such as it is — as a series of failures that are absorbed by his sisters' sacrifices. He cannot hold a job; his sisters do without. He cannot support himself; his sisters support him. He cannot marry; his sisters cannot marry either, but for different reasons and with different social consequences.

The novel's treatment of the "New Woman" phenomenon is characteristically indirect. The character who notes that there is "nothing of the new woman about Margaret" (002-006) is not praising her — the remark registers the new woman as an available alternative, a path not taken. The Campion sisters are too embedded in their family's expectations to imagine independence; the tragedy of their situation is not that they chose wrongly but that they never had the conceptual resources to imagine a choice.

## The Double Standard in Practice

Parrish is meticulous in documenting the material operations of gender inequality. The family's limited funds go to Victor's clothes, Victor's entertainments, Victor's business ventures. The daughters' needs — for dresses, for social events, for the dowries that would make marriage possible — are perpetually deferred. This is not presented as theft because no one in the family sees it as theft; it is simply the natural order of things, in which sons are investments and daughters are expenses.

The novel's most pointed commentary on the double standard comes through May's remark about Victor: "Of course! He's a bachelor. Wait till he gets married and see if he stays so popular" (026-010). The remark is casual but devastating: Victor's social value is enhanced by his bachelorhood, while his sisters' social value is destroyed by theirs. The same condition that makes him charming makes them pitiable. Parrish lets the observation stand without commentary, trusting the reader to draw the obvious conclusion.

## Literary and Historical Context

Gender roles in the post-Civil War South were undergoing transformation even as the culture clung to traditional ideals. The war had disrupted family structures, the suffrage movement was gaining momentum, and the "New Woman" of the 1890s represented a visible alternative to domesticity. Parrish, born in 1888, came of age during this transition, and her novel can be read as a document of the old order's last years — or as a brief for its abolition.
