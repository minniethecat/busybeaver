# Lily Campion

## Overview

Lily is the youngest of the four Campion children — plump, sentimental, clumsy where her sister May is graceful, and possessed of a tender-heartedness that the novel treats with a mixture of affection and gentle mockery. At four years old she "lay in a cot" [001-021] while Maggie and May raced ahead; as an adult she remains the baby of the family, never quite achieving the independence her older sisters manage. Where Maggie becomes the household's practical anchor and May escapes into marriage, Lily drifts into permanent daughterhood — the Campion sister for whom the pattern of arrested development claims its gentlest victim.

## Appearance and Temperament

Lily's physical presence is defined by softness. She was "fat and her ankles were weak" [008-019]; she skated poorly and "gave up desserts, too" [008-024] in futile attempts at self-improvement. Mamma dressed her in blue "to match her eyes" [008-006] and perpetually corrected her posture: "Don't slouch, Lily. Will you ever learn to stand up straight?" [008-007].

Her emotional life was equally soft-edged. She wept easily — over Victorian novels whose "pathos reduced Lily to pleasurable tears" [007-008], over May's beauty at a party [012-014], and at any moment of heightened feeling. Her "nervous giggle escaped before she could stop it" [011-021] at tense moments, marking her as the sister least able to control her self-presentation.

> Lily liked best to play the piano with its fan of puckered green silk in the front, and the swinging candle-holders. But it was so hot, and four flats were so hard. [010-032]

This passage captures Lily's essential quality: she wants to do the graceful thing, but finds it effortful, and her attention wanders. She is not lazy — she is constitutionally unsuited to the performative demands of her social world.

## Childhood and the War

As a small child, the Civil War passed through Lily's world as a confusion of images. She once "thought she saw Papa marching past, and ran out to him. But she got mixed up in the marching legs and knew she was lost forever until a kind soldier with a grey mustache picked her up" [005-004]. The memory encapsulates Lily's relationship to the world: a reaching toward connection, a getting lost, a rescue by kindness.

She and Victor made Christmas decorations together — "two sprays of laurel and one of cedar, and then two sprays of cedar and one of laurel" [009-002] — their hands blackened with evergreen sap. These sibling scenes, rendered in Parrish's accumulative detail, build the world of childhood that the Campion children never fully leave.

## The Unmarried Daughter

Lily's romantic prospects are the thinnest of all the Campion sisters. She is too plump, too clumsy, too prone to nervous giggling. When [[alfred-lacey|Mr. Lacey]] courts Mamma, Lily is an awkward bystander — she "would have quite loved kind Mr. Lacey" [010-038] in her own way, but there is never a question of his noticing her as anything but Mamma's youngest daughter.

Her role in the household solidifies around caregiving and companionship. She makes lemonade when asked: "'Lily! Be an angel and make some lemonade, will you?'" [010-046]. She is useful, sweet-natured, and perpetually available — the qualities that ensure she will never leave.

## Relationship with Victor

As the baby of the family, Lily's bond with her brother [[victor-campion|Victor]] is less freighted than Maggie's or May's. He treats her with the affectionate condescension of an older brother accustomed to her mild peculiarities: "'Yes,' said Victor, used to Lily's mildly tossed beanbags" [007-039]. She admires him without the competition or resentment that colors his relationships with others. In the novel's emotional economy, Lily is the purest example of the Campion capacity for loving without expectation — which is also, the novel suggests, a form of self-erasure.

## Significance

Of all the Campion daughters, Lily represents the gentlest form of the novel's central tragedy. Maggie sacrifices herself to duty; May escapes through marriage but remains tethered by guilt; Lily simply stays. She is not bitter, not rebellious, not even particularly aware of what she has missed. She is the daughter who never outgrew the nursery, and Parrish renders her with a tenderness that makes her fate — a life lived entirely within the gravitational field of The Maples — seem less like failure than like the quietest form of survival.
