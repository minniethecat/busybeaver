## Overview

Miss Hopper is a peripheral figure in the later chapters of *The Perennial Bachelor*, one of the unmarried townswomen whose social rounds intersect with the Campion family's declining years. Described as "thin old-maid Miss Hopper, with her cheap little rings" [026-021], she belongs to the novel's quiet catalog of what the Campion sisters might become — or, in the case of the unmarried Maggie, what she is already becoming. Her friendship with Miss Snaith, the Campions' former governess, draws her into the family's orbit for photographs, porch visits, and the small consolations of shared solitude.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Hopper appears first at a Campion gathering in Chapter 26, arriving with "Mr. and Mrs. Hopper and Miss Hopper" [026-019]. Her designation as "Miss" — the unmarried daughter accompanying her married parents — immediately places her in the social category the novel examines with such unsparing clarity: the spinster, the surplus woman, the one who was not chosen.

Her physical description is economically damning: "thin old-maid Miss Hopper, with her cheap little rings" [026-021]. The rings are the detail that lingers — cheap, little, worn by a woman who knows they are cheap and little and wears them anyway. Parrish captures in a phrase the particular pathos of small adornments on a woman who has nothing grand to adorn.

When a man at the party disappears, Miss Hopper reacts with the reflexive anxiety of someone accustomed to social slights: she "was sure she had followed him" [026-023], interpreting his absence as a judgment on her company. Later, her rings come off for an impromptu repair: "Oop! There went some gathers! Miss Hopper took off her rings" [026-043] — the gesture at once practical and symbolic, a stripping of her small armor.

She reappears the following summer, visiting with Miss Snaith "in piqué skirts, with chatelaine watches pinned to their belts" [027-003]. The two spinster friends share a world of small rituals: Miss Hopper exclaims "I take an awful photograph" [027-004], and owns a Teddy bear — "Miss Hopper had a Teddy bear too" [027-005] — the "too" suggesting Miss Snaith has one as well, two grown women holding stuffed animals in the summer twilight.

Her final quoted line, directed at Miss Snaith, carries the weight of years of unspoken understanding: "I don't see how you stand it, Ethel" [027-014]. What "it" is — the Campions, the summer heat, the accumulating years, the life neither of them chose — Parrish leaves unglossed.

## Relationships

- **Miss Snaith** — Her closest friend in the novel's later chapters; they share summer visits, photographs, and the unspoken fellowship of unmarried women past their youth.
- **The Campion Family** — Social acquaintances; Miss Hopper attends their gatherings and visits with the family, though always as a guest rather than an intimate.
- **Mr. and Mrs. Hopper** — Her parents, with whom she arrives at social functions [026-019].

## Significance

Miss Hopper is one of the novel's small but essential portraits of what the Campion sisters might have become — and, in Maggie's case, largely did become. Her "cheap little rings" and her Teddy bear, her anxiety about having "followed him" and her summer visits in a piqué skirt, compose a life of small consolations and smaller disappointments. She is not a major character; she is not even particularly memorable to the other characters. But Parrish's willingness to look at her — really look, with the unsparing clarity she brings to all her women — makes Miss Hopper more than a type. She is a reminder that every spinster in the novel's world was once a girl who thought the rings might be real.
