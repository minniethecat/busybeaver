# May Campion

## Overview

May is the beautiful middle daughter of the Campion family — the one Mamma acknowledged as elegant, the one who managed to look graceful even in the fashions that defeated her sisters. Six years old when the novel opens, "May slept beside her in the big bed" [001-004] with Maggie; she alone among the sisters would eventually marry and leave [[the-maples]]. But Parrish complicates the escape narrative: May's departure through [[mays-marriage|marriage]] is shadowed by the gravitational pull of the family she cannot fully leave behind.

## Beauty and Its Burdens

May's beauty is her defining characteristic, noted by the narrative and by every character who encounters her. Mamma thought with a sigh that "May was the only one who managed to look well" [008-006] in the elaborate dresses of the period. At the family theatricals, "May was so beautiful that Lily wept unrestrainedly" [012-014]. Playing the Lady of Shalott, "May was the Lady, lying all in white" [012-009] — the image capturing both her ethereal quality and her essential passivity.

> "Who's the prettiest?" asked May, reviving like a wilted flower. [012-029]

The question, asked with the artlessness of someone who already knows the answer, reveals the economy May inhabits. Her beauty is her currency, her identity, and her obligation — something to be maintained and displayed for the family's benefit. She is the Campion daughter whose value is most visibly external, most publicly assessed.

## The Sensitive One

Beneath the beauty, May is the most emotionally fragile of the sisters. She blushed "brightly, delicately" [010-036] at any mention of romance; her "hands shook" [010-044] when conversation turned toward [[alfred-lacey|Mr. Lacey]] and Mamma's possible remarriage. She is "very high-strung," as Mamma described her with "gently complacent" pride [001-020] — a diagnosis that served to classify May's emotional intensity as a kind of accomplishment.

She was also the sister most capable of aesthetic absorption. While Lily struggled through piano practice, May "was cutting out a picture of a statue of Psyche to paste on green velvet cardboard" [010-032], her "quick flashing of the sharp scissors" [010-032] revealing a manual precision her sisters lacked. "If she had been doing it she would have torn the delicate winged figure," Lily thought, recognizing in May a grace she could not match.

## The Only One to Marry

May's [[mays-marriage|marriage]] is a landmark in the Campion family history — proof that the gravitational field of The Maples could, in theory, be escaped. But Parrish presents her departure as deeply ambivalent. May marries but remains tethered; she leaves but never fully arrives elsewhere.

The external world's assessment is captured in a passing remark: "That is May Campion, the stepdaughter of the rich" [010-040] — an outsider's summary that reduces her to a financial relationship. The question "What became of May Campion?" [027-019], asked later in the novel, hangs unanswered, suggesting that marriage, for all its promise of escape, was not a destination but a diffusion.

May's beauty, which seemed to promise so much, delivers her into a life that the novel treats as only marginally freer than her sisters'. She is not a spinster — but the distinction, Parrish implies, may matter less than the culture insists.

## Significance

May occupies a crucial position in the novel's typology of feminine fates. Maggie sacrifices romance for duty; Lily drifts into permanent daughterhood; May marries — and yet the marriage does not liberate her. She is the sister who "got out," and the novel quietly undermines the premise that getting out was ever really possible.

Her beauty, the quality that seemed to guarantee her a different fate, becomes in retrospect a kind of trap — a gift that placed her on display but never in control. May's story is the novel's most elegant demonstration that the problem was not individual choice but systemic constraint: the Campion daughters were shaped by a world that offered them no real exits, only different forms of enclosure.
