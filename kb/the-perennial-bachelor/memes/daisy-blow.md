## Overview

Daisy Blow is the newly married wife of Sam Blow, appearing prominently in Chapter 24. She bursts into the narrative as a figure of comic excess: heavily perfumed, fashionably (if questionably) dressed, and armed with an armful of decorative household items she insists on lending for a party. Through the Campion women's reactions to her — Aunt Priscilla's alarm, Maggie's barely concealed dislike, Victor's sardonic commentary — Parrish sketches a sharp portrait of class anxiety, female competition, and the tension between old-fashioned gentility and the assertive new woman of the early twentieth century.

## Role in the Novel

Daisy Blow's role is concentrated in a single chapter, but her visit is rendered with such vivid detail that she leaves a lasting impression. Her arrival is announced by Aunt Priscilla, who pants breathlessly to Maggie: "Daisy Blow's in the parlor!" [024-013]. Maggie's reaction is immediate and visceral — she begs Aunt Priscilla to entertain the visitor while she composes herself, groaning, "Oh, bother — oh, dear! What did she have to come for?" [024-013].

Parrish's narrator lingers on Maggie's uncharitable appraisal: "She didn't like Daisy Blow. She looked fast, and she certainly was painted black around the eyes, for it had smudged a little. And what a lot of 'Héliotrope Blanc' perfume, the woman was drenched in it" [024-016]. The smudged eye-paint and overwhelming perfume mark Daisy as trying too hard — a woman who has money but not the taste to deploy it subtly. Maggie's instinctive recoil is followed by a moment of self-awareness: she feels "a sudden warm gush of affection for shabby, shy old Aunt Priscilla" [024-016] by contrast.

Daisy has come bearing goods: a lace tablecloth, pink china, silver candlesticks with pink candles and silk shades, which she presents with proprietary pride [024-048]. She speaks in a torrent — "I simply ran over with some little things for tonight" [024-048] — and her largesse, while genuinely helpful, carries an undercurrent of social one-upmanship. After she leaves, Maggie flings open the windows crying "Whew, whew, whew! I never smelled so much perfume in my life!" [024-027], and the cook Martha emerges from the kitchen, sniffing, to pronounce "Daisy Blow's been here" [024-057] with evident disapproval.

## Relationships

**Sam Blow** — Daisy is Sam's bride. The marriage marks Sam's second; his first wife was Lizzie Campion, who married him to spite Victor Campion [001-049]. Daisy thus enters the Blow-Campion orbit as a successor to a woman whose own marriage was shadowed by romantic disappointment.

**The Campion Women** — Daisy's relationship with Maggie and Aunt Priscilla is one of awkward social obligation. She clearly wishes to impress, but her efforts produce the opposite effect. Victor drily observes of her hair, "Methinks, my lady's auriferous tresses are too good to be true" [024-027], a sardonic aside that places Daisy firmly outside the Campions' genteel circle.

**Martha the Cook** — Even the household staff register Daisy's visit as an intrusion. Martha's sniffing verdict — "Daisy Blow's been here" [024-057] — speaks volumes about the social distance between the Campion household and its visitor.

## Significance

Daisy Blow functions as a comic foil to the Campion women. Where they are reserved, she is effusive; where they are shabby-genteel, she is ostentatiously new-rich. Parrish uses Daisy to explore the fault lines of class and taste in early-twentieth-century small-town America. Her "Héliotrope Blanc" perfume, her smudged eye-paint, and her pink silk tablecloth all announce a woman who has read the rules of fashion but doesn't quite understand them — a figure of aspirational striving that the older-money Campions view with a mixture of condescension and unease.

At the same time, Daisy's visit serves a narrative function: her loaned tablecloth, candlesticks, and china swan materially enable the evening's entertainment, and her presence reminds the reader that the Campion household, for all its pretensions, depends on the goodwill of relatives by marriage. The chapter ends with the rueful acknowledgment that "though they didn't want Daisy Blow's lace tablecloth and bisque Cupid, they had to have something" [024-053] — a small, sharp comment on the compromises of social life.
