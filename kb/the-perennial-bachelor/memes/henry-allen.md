## Overview

Henry Allen is a minor presence in the Campion family's social world — a friend who drops in for tea with Willie in the novel's opening chapter, and later the uncle with whom a new young man comes to live while working in Wilmington. His role is structural: he anchors two small domestic scenes, one comic and one romantic, each of which depends on his presence without requiring him to do much more than exist. He is part of the novel's network of uncles, family friends, and background gentlemen who populate the edges of the Campion sisters' world.

## Role in the Novel

Henry Allen makes his first and most memorable appearance in Mamma's rambling recollection of a domestic crisis. Speaking to her daughters, Mamma recounts the afternoon with affectionate exasperation: "the first thing I knew, there was Willie home and Henry Allen with him — come to tea — and me in my old crocheted Zouave because it was so chilly, and my hoops off. And nothing for tea but chip-beef because I told the girl I'd make some whips for dessert" [001-038]. Henry Allen himself says nothing in this scene; he is simply there, the unexpected guest whose arrival exposes the household's unpreparedness. Mamma's mortification — caught in her old jacket, her hoops off, nothing to serve but chip-beef — is the comedy of provincial hospitality, and Henry Allen is its silent occasion.

His second appearance is reported rather than dramatized. At the Tennyson Tableaux, a new young man catches someone's eye: "'Who's that pretty girl in pink?' He was a new young man, and had just come to live with his Aunt Jo and Uncle Henry Allen while he worked in Wilmington" [012-025]. A few lines later, Mamma identifies him: "'Sh, Mamma! He'll hear you. Post. He's the Henry Allens' nephew'" [012-044]. Henry Allen is invoked as kinship — the nephew is identified entirely by his relationship to the Allens — and the household of "Aunt Jo and Uncle Henry Allen" becomes, in these brief mentions, a node in the town's social geography: a place where young men from elsewhere come to live while establishing themselves.

## Relationships

- **Willie Campion** — The Campion son who brings Henry Allen home for tea at an inopportune moment, precipitating Mamma's domestic comedy [001-038].
- **Aunt Jo Allen** — His wife, mentioned as co-host with "Uncle Henry Allen" for the nephew who comes to Wilmington [012-025].
- **The Nephew (Mr. Post)** — A young man who lives with the Allens while working in Wilmington, identified as "the Henry Allens' nephew" [012-044]. He appears at the Tennyson Tableaux and asks after "that pretty girl in pink."
- **Mamma (Margaret Campion)** — The matriarch whose domestic unpreparedness is exposed by Henry Allen's unexpected visit, and whose recollection of the incident years later still carries the sting of embarrassment.

## Significance

Henry Allen is one of the novel's many named but undeveloped figures — the family friend, the uncle, the man who comes to tea. His function is entirely relational: he exists so that Mamma can be embarrassed, so that a nephew can be identified by kinship, so that the social world of Wilmington feels thick with names and connections. The novel's willingness to name him, to give him a wife ("Aunt Jo") and a nephew and a house where young men board, is part of its commitment to density — a world in which even the most peripheral figures have names and tea invitations and nephews who ask after pretty girls in pink.
