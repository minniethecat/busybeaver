# War and Social Change

## Overview

*The Perennial Bachelor* spans a period of extraordinary historical transformation: from the Civil War through Reconstruction, the Gilded Age, the Progressive Era, and the First World War, into the jazz age of the 1920s. The Campion family's decline is not merely personal but historical — they are the representatives of an old order that is being swept away by forces they cannot comprehend. Anne Parrish registers these forces indirectly, through the accumulation of small changes in manners, assumptions, and material circumstances, rather than through explicit historical commentary.

The Civil War exists in the novel as memory and legacy. Papa fought in it; the family's social position was shaped by its outcome. The war is present in the novel less as a military event than as a social rupture — the dividing line between the world the Campions were raised for and the world they actually inhabit. By the time of the First World War, the rupture has become an abyss: the old certainties about class, gender, and family are dissolving, and the Campions are among the last people to notice.

## Treatment in the Novel

The Civil War is invoked early through the mention of specific battles — Seven Days', Fredericksburg, Gettysburg, the Wilderness — but these references function less as history than as family lore, the stories that constitute the Campions' sense of who they are. Papa's service is a source of pride, but the war's economic consequences — the destruction of the plantation economy, the impoverishment of the Southern gentry — are the unspoken context of the family's decline.

The novel's treatment of the First World War is more direct and more devastating. The war arrives in the later chapters as an intrusion of history into the domestic sphere. The war committees, the shortages, the casualty lists — these transform the small town's rhythms and expose the fragility of the world the Campions have constructed. The Armistice celebrations register as anticlimax: the war is over, but nothing will be the same.

Parrish is particularly attentive to the war's effect on women. The First World War created a generation of "surplus women" — women for whom marriage was statistically impossible because so many men of their generation had died. This demographic reality is the unspoken context of the novel's later chapters, in which the Campion sisters' spinsterhood shifts from personal tragedy to historical condition. They are not alone in their unmarried state; they are part of a generation of women for whom the old scripts no longer apply.

The novel's treatment of social change is cumulative rather than argumentative. The new generation that appears in the final chapter — young people who speak casually of "sex repression" and dismiss the old house as a relic (033-037) — represents a world in which the Campion family's values are not just irrelevant but incomprehensible. The novel does not celebrate this new world or mourn the old one; it simply registers the transition with the clarity of someone who knows that both worlds have their costs.

## Character Perspectives

Maggie experiences social change as loss — the loss of the world her father fought for, the loss of the certainties that gave her sacrifices meaning. But she is also, paradoxically, the character most capable of adapting to change. Her practicality, which the old order undervalued, is exactly what the new order demands.

Victor experiences change as bewilderment. He was raised to be a gentleman in a world that no longer needs gentlemen, and his serial professional failures are as much historical as personal. He is, in the novel's implicit judgment, a casualty of the transition between orders — a man who was trained for a role that no longer exists.

The new generation, glimpsed briefly at the novel's end, experiences the Campion house not as a home but as a curiosity — a place where "he and three old maid sisters used to live" (033-037). Their casual dismissal is both cruel and clarifying: the old order is over, and the people who inhabited it are already becoming exhibits in a museum of social history.

## Literary and Historical Context

Parrish wrote *The Perennial Bachelor* in 1925, on the far side of the First World War, at a moment when American fiction was grappling with the meaning of the changes the war had accelerated. Her novel belongs to a tradition that includes Willa Cather's *One of Ours* and Edith Wharton's *The Age of Innocence* — novels that examine the collision between old orders and new, and that find in individual lives the traces of historical transformation.
