# Courtship and Marriage

## Overview

Courtship and marriage form the central social drama of Anne Parrish's *The Perennial Bachelor*, structuring the lives of the Campion sisters and defining the novel's emotional landscape. In the world Parrish depicts — a small town near Wilmington from the 1860s through the 1920s — marriage is not merely a personal choice but an economic necessity, a social performance, and the primary mechanism by which women's worth is measured. The novel traces both successful and failed courtships, using them to expose the gap between romantic ideals and material realities.

Parrish is unusually clear-eyed about the transactional nature of marriage in her characters' world. The novel presents courtship as a marketplace in which beauty, charm, and family connections are currency, and in which the absence of a dowry or the presence of dependent relatives can prove fatal to a woman's prospects. Yet she also captures the genuine emotional texture of romantic hope — the flutter of a dance card, the significance of a glance, the devastating weight of a proposal that never comes.

## Treatment in the Novel

The novel opens with marriage already established as the expected outcome and its absence as a looming threat. The Campion sisters' grandmother "had only married Sam Blow to show she didn't care" when Victor married another woman (001-049), establishing a pattern in which marriage serves purposes far beyond love. Parrish's narrative voice is gently ironic about the rituals of courtship — the German dances, the chafing-dish fudge, the carefully penciled dance cards — while never dismissing their genuine power over her characters' lives (026-010).

Mamma's own marriage to Papa provides the novel's template: a beautiful woman married to a man who adored her, she was left widowed with four children and no practical skills for survival. Her widowhood shapes her daughters' prospects directly — there is no money for the dresses, entertainments, and social display that the marriage market requires. The wedding itself appears in the novel as both memory and aspiration: Margaret's tears on the night before her wedding (002-008), the treasured white satin wedding slippers (012-009), the grandmother's lace veil that cannot be found (014-027). These artifacts of bridal hope accumulate into a portrait of marriage as the one great event toward which a woman's life is directed.

Parrish is attentive to the economic infrastructure beneath the romance. Mr. Lacey's courtship of Mamma — with his wine, his compliments, his air of prosperous good nature — makes visible what is usually unspoken: that marriage is a financial transaction in which a woman exchanges youth, beauty, and domestic service for material security (010-020). When this transaction fails — when a suitor proves unwilling, or a family cannot produce the necessary social capital — the woman is left stranded in a dependency that is both economic and existential.

The marriage service itself becomes a site of narrative meaning. Parrish notes the words "where the woman says she will obey" (020-028), a detail that encapsulates the power relations at the heart of the institution she examines. The novel's treatment is not polemical — Parrish is too subtle a writer for that — but cumulative, building a case through the accretion of small observations about who pays, who waits, who sacrifices, and who is left behind.

## Character Perspectives

Maggie's broken engagement is the novel's central courtship tragedy. The narrative implies that she ended her engagement to stay home and care for her mother and brother — a sacrifice that the community understands but will not name (029-025). Her later declaration that she has "settled down to being an old maid" represents a hard-won self-knowledge, a rejection of the romantic economy that had no place for her.

May, by contrast, succeeds in the marriage market through beauty and strategic self-presentation. Her success is the novel's implicit critique of what the market rewards — not virtue, not intelligence, but the performance of desirability. May's marriage provides her with position and security, yet Parrish's treatment is ambiguous: escape from spinsterhood through marriage may simply be a different form of constraint.

Mamma embodies the widow's dilemma — dependent on her children, incapable of remarriage without betraying the memory of a husband she genuinely loved, she drifts through the novel as a cautionary figure. Her beauty, which once secured her a husband, becomes in widowhood a kind of reproach — a reminder of what was and what can never be again.

## Literary and Historical Context

Parrish published *The Perennial Bachelor* in 1925, at a moment when the institution of marriage was under unprecedented scrutiny. The suffrage movement, the "New Woman" of the 1890s, the sexual radicalism of the Greenwich Village bohemians, and the demographic shock of the First World War — which created a generation of women for whom marriage was statistically impossible — had all destabilized the assumption that marriage was every woman's destiny.

The novel participates in a tradition of American fiction — most prominently associated with Edith Wharton and Ellen Glasgow — that subjected the marriage plot to skeptical analysis. Parrish's contribution is her focus on the unremarkable, the provincial, the lives that history overlooks. Her characters are not New Women or radicals; they are ordinary people caught in a system they did not design and cannot escape, whose small dramas of hope and disappointment constitute a quiet indictment of the world that made them.
