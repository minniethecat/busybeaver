## Overview

Robert Leaf belongs to a family closely connected to the Campions' social circle — his sister Fannie is a friend of the Campion sisters and later marries Prentice Page. Robert himself appears in only two brief mentions across the novel, yet those mentions sketch the outline of a conventional life: a young man who takes part in amateur theatricals, marries, and produces the next generation.

## Role in the Novel

Robert's first appearance is in the tableaux vivants at The Maples, where he performs as the Dumb Oarsman in a scene adapted from Tennyson: "Edward Post was Sir Lancelot in a Tam o' Shanter with a plume from his Aunt Jo's best hat; and Robert Leaf was the Dumb Oarsman (for they had mixed in some of Lancelot and Elaine) in Fannie's tan cape, with the packing-box draperies arranged to hide his boots and trousers" [012-009]. The detail of the "packing-box draperies arranged to hide his boots and trousers" captures the make-do charm of small-town theatricals — a world of borrowed costumes and improvised props in which Robert is a willing participant.

Years later, in Chapter 17, Robert has followed the expected path into marriage and fatherhood. Mrs. Leaf, speaking to the Campion sisters, notes that "Robert married a year and a half" ago and that both Robert and Fannie "think they know more about bringing up babies than I do!" [017-007]. A photograph of "Isabel holding little Robbie — he certainly looks like his daddy" [017-007] confirms the domestic tableau. A later mention, in Maggie's resentful recollection, confirms that Robert's life has proceeded along conventional lines: "He and Isabel can have a million babies for all I care — it certainly isn't of the slightest importance to me" [017-015].

## Relationships

- **Fannie Leaf** — His sister, who marries Prentice Page. The Leaf siblings both make conventional marriages and produce children, following the life script that the Campion sisters observe from the outside.
- **Isabel** — His wife, with whom he has a son, Robbie [017-007].
- **Robbie** — His infant son, whose photograph Mrs. Leaf shows to the Campion sisters [017-007].

## Significance

Robert Leaf is an almost entirely structural character — he exists in the novel not as an individual but as a data point in the social calculus that measures the Campion sisters' lives against the conventional norm. His marriage, his child, his participation in the tableaux vivants — these are the ordinary markers of a life lived along expected lines, and their very ordinariness is what gives them their quiet reproachful force when set beside the Campions' arrested trajectories.
