# The Local Store

## Overview

The local store in Wilmington serves as one of the novel's few points of contact between the Campion household and the commercial world beyond its gates. It is where the family shops, where community news circulates, where the economic realities that The Maples tries to keep at bay make themselves felt. Parrish renders it with the same accumulative detail she brings to domestic interiors — and the contrast between the store's practical bustle and The Maples' genteel decline is pointed.

## Commercial and Social Hub

The store functions as both marketplace and social center. It is where Mrs. Trewhitt, the storekeeper's wife, dispenses news alongside merchandise [006-008]; where the Campion daughters encounter the town's broader population; where the economic transactions that sustain the household become visible as transactions rather than the invisible infrastructure of domestic life.

The store's presence in the novel is intermittent but significant. It represents the practical world that the Campions inhabit without fully belonging to — a world of prices and exchanges, of labor made visible, of social relations not mediated by gentility.

## Significance

The local store is the novel's most direct representation of the economic reality that the Campion family's gentility obscures. At The Maples, money is never discussed; at the store, everything has a price. The contrast illuminates the central tension of the Campion world: they live within an economy they refuse to acknowledge.
