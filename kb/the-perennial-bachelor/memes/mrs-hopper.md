## Overview

Mrs. Hopper arrives at the Campion family home as one of the first boarders after Mamma Campion's death, accompanied by her husband and her daughter Miss Hopper [026-019]. Described as "old Mrs. Hopper," she navigates the boarding-house chapter in a perpetual state of mild physical complaint — tired feet, cold drafts, biting mosquitoes. Her presence, alongside the other boarders, fills the Campion home with a brief, comic vitality before the household's inevitable dispersal.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Hopper is introduced as part of the first wave of boarders inspecting the Campion home. Maggie, "praying in her heart" that they would not like the rooms, shows them Mamma's room and the tiny room where Victor slept as a boy [026-019]. Mrs. Hopper's response is characteristically physical: "Ho-hum!" she yawns, her feet tired from the walk [026-020].

She settles into the household routine as a figure of low-grade discontent. During the cold weather she complains, "Ain't it cold? I hope I haven't caught a cold in my head" — the kind of quotidian grumble that fills the boarding-house silence [026-032]. At the evening entertainments, she yawns and applauds on cue, a dutiful but unenthusiastic audience member [026-046]. When summer brings mosquitoes, "Mrs. Hopper used up bottles of citronella" — her battle against insects becoming a running domestic joke [027-013].

Her teddy bear, distinguished by its blue ribbon (Miss Snaith's wears pink), becomes one of the "Theodore bears" christened by Mr. Neff — a prop in the tragicomic theater of middle-aged longing that defines the boarding-house chapters [027-005].

## Relationships

- **Miss Hopper** — Her daughter, with whom she tours the house and settles into boarder life. Miss Hopper removes her rings before singing around the fire while Mrs. Hopper looks on.
- **Mr. Hopper** — Her husband, who accompanies her when they first inspect the Campion home [026-019].
- **Mr. Neff** — Fellow boarder whose "funny way" of calling teddy bears "Theodore bears" amuses the household. He rouses her to call to her daughter during the evening gatherings [026-038].
- **Maggie Campion** — The reluctant landlady who prays Mrs. Hopper will not like the rooms, but must accept her as a tenant nonetheless [026-019].
- **Mrs. Mittendorf** — A friend who arrives at the Campion house "because she was a friend of Mrs. Hopper's" [026-022].

## Significance

Mrs. Hopper is the novel's most fully realized portrait of the aging boarder — a woman who has outlived whatever drama her life once contained and now fills her days with yawns, complaints, and citronella. Her teddy bear with its blue ribbon, her tired feet, and her dutiful applause at Mr. Neff's performances together sketch a life of diminished expectations. She is neither tragic nor comic, but something in between: a representative of the ordinary, unglamorous drift into old age that the Campion sisters themselves are navigating. When the boarders eventually disperse, Mrs. Hopper's departure leaves behind only the faint scent of citronella and the memory of her yawns — an absence barely registered, like so many of the losses that accumulate through the novel.
