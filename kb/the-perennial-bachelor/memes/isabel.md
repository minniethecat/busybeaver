## Overview

Isabel Leaf (née unknown, later Mrs. Robert Leaf) belongs to the generation of women who married — the path not taken by Maggie Campion and the other spinsters who populate the novel's later chapters. Her appearances are brief but pointed: a new photograph of her baby [017-007], a telephone call about a doctor [030-018], a remark about someone aging [029-055]. Through these small, scattered moments, Parrish sketches the outline of a life that has followed the expected script — marriage, motherhood, domestic management — and in doing so provides a quiet counterpoint to the Campion sisters' more complicated trajectories.

## Role in the Novel

Isabel first enters the narrative through the voice of Mrs. Leaf, her mother-in-law, who shows off "a new photograph of Isabel holding little Robbie" [017-007]. The photograph — Isabel holding her child — is the image of completed womanhood that the novel's spinsters can only observe from the outside. Mamma Campion feels the comparison keenly: "He and Isabel can have a million babies for all I care" [017-015], she thinks, the defensiveness of her internal retort revealing the wound.

In the novel's final chapters, Isabel becomes a more active presence. At a social gathering, "Isabel Leaf said they had had a dreadful time getting a cook" [029-034] — the domestic complaint of a woman whose problems are the right kind of problems, the problems of running a household rather than surviving without one. She passes judgment on a mutual acquaintance: "Isabel Leaf says she's aged awfully" [029-055], the comment at once casual and cutting, the kind of remark that only a woman secure in her own position can afford to make.

Her most significant appearance comes near the novel's end, when the ailing Mamma needs a doctor. Maggie resolves to "telephone Isabel Leaf and ask her who was good" [030-018] — a small but telling detail. Isabel is the one who knows which doctor to call, who manages the practicalities of illness and recovery. Her response — "I hope it's nothing serious!" [030-019] — is the conventional expression of concern, socially adequate if emotionally thin.

## Relationships

- **Robert Leaf** — Her husband; the Leafs appear as a settled married couple, their union the achieved state that the novel's unmarried women cannot reach.
- **Robbie** — Her baby son, whose photograph becomes a token of completed womanhood [017-007].
- **Maggie Campion** — A family friend; Maggie turns to Isabel for practical help in crisis, acknowledging Isabel's competence in the domestic sphere [030-018].
- **The Campion Family** — Social connections maintained across decades.

## Significance

Isabel Leaf functions in the novel as a structural foil: the woman who married, who had the baby, who knows which doctor to call. She is not drawn with interiority — Parrish gives us her remarks, her photograph, her telephone voice — because her interiority is not what the novel needs from her. What it needs is her surface: the smooth, unruffled surface of a life that followed the expected path. Against that surface, the Campion sisters' deviations — Maggie's sacrifice, Lily's delicacy, May's disappointed ambitions — show more sharply. Isabel is not a criticism of the path taken, nor is she an advertisement for it. She is simply evidence that the path existed, that some women walked it, and that walking it did not necessarily lead to depths that the novel's spinsters, in their more complicated solitude, did not reach.
