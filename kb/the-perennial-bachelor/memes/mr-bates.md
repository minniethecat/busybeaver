## Overview

Mr. Bates is a minor but vividly sketched member of the St. Stephen's choir, memorable for a single quality rendered with comic precision: his voice is too loud. In a novel full of delicate social negotiations, Mr. Bates's booming bass represents the kind of small communal friction that no one quite knows how to address — Miss Martin, the organist, frets that "something tactful would have to be said again — oh dear!" — and that no one ever does.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Bates first appears at the Tennyson Tableaux, a church fundraiser where he and Prentice Page "stood ready to pull back the hitchy curtains" while the ladies backstage seethed with costume crises [012-001]. His vocal debut comes later in the same chapter: "Mrs. Webster sang 'Sweet and Low' with such tremolo it seemed as if the staff and the stems of the notes on her sheet of music must be scalloped instead of straight; and Mr. Bates' voice welling out in 'Break, break, break' made the ladies sigh and smile" [012-004]. The pairing of Mrs. Webster's tremolo and Mr. Bates's boom is a miniature comic duet — each voice excessive in its own direction.

He and Mrs. Webster later perform a duet of "Tears, Idle Tears" to close the evening, while Maggie helps Aunt Priscilla hunt for her lost slippers [012-015]. The image is domestic and slightly shambolic — exactly the texture of small-town church entertainments in the novel.

Mr. Bates reappears briefly in a later chapter, still in church, still too loud: "boomed Mr. Bates, up and down — much too loud, thought Miss Martin at the organ, it really drowned out all the rest of the choir. Something tactful would have to be said again — oh dear!" [014-016]. The "again" is the operative word — this is a recurring problem, never resolved, the kind of low-grade communal irritant that churches and small towns sustain indefinitely because no one wants to be the one to say something.

## Relationships

- **Mrs. Webster** — His fellow chorister, whose tremolo and his boom form a mismatched duet. They perform together at the Tableaux and presumably at regular services.
- **Miss Martin** — The church organist, "small and grey and whiskered like a mouse," who bears the burden of Mr. Bates's volume with silent exasperation, perpetually resolving to say something and perpetually not doing so.
- **Prentice Page** — Fellow curtain-puller at the Tableaux, the two men sharing a "good look at the audience through the sides" while the women panicked backstage.

## Significance

Mr. Bates is in the novel for perhaps half a dozen sentences, but those sentences are characteristic of Anne Parrish's method: a minor character rendered in a single indelible trait, placed in a scene just long enough to make it breathe, then released. His booming voice is a small comic grace note in a novel whose dominant key is elegiac, and his presence at the Tennyson Tableaux helps build the sense of a whole community — not just the Campions but the Websters and Bateses and Martins who fill the pews and the choir loft, each with their own small vanities and vexations.
