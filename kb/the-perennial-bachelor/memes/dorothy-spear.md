## Overview

Dorothy Spear belongs to the new generation that displaces the old at The Maples. When her wealthy parents — Bayard and Violet Spear — purchase the Campion family estate, Dorothy becomes the inheritor of a world the Campion sisters have lost. She is modern, impatient, and casually rude: a flapper-era young woman whose silk stockings, cigarettes, and bored insolence mark her as belonging to a social order fundamentally different from the one that produced Maggie, May, and Lily Campion.

## Role in the Novel

Dorothy first appears as a child at the Spears' housewarming, where her mother presents her to Lily Campion: "You know my little Dorothy, don't you? Just think, Dotty, Miss Lily used to live here when she was a little girl like you" [028-077]. The encounter is a study in social awkwardness and shifting power. Lily, who has come against her better judgment — Maggie "couldn't stand the Spears just because they had bought The Maples" [028-065] — is met not with the gracious hospitality she hoped for but with the bored rudeness of a spoiled child. When Dorothy misbehaves, Mrs. Spear snaps: "Dorothy! If you can't speak politely to Mother's friends, you can go up to Fraulein!" [028-080]. The presence of a German governess — "Fraulein" — signals the Spears' nouveau-riche aspirations, their reach toward European gentility that sits uneasily beside the old Southern simplicity of The Maples.

The house party scene in Chapter 33 brings the adult Dorothy into sharp focus, and the portrait is unsparing. "Four members of Dorothy Spear's house party were playing mah-jongg at the top of their lungs, four more were dancing to 'It Ain't Goin' to Rain No More,' played by the gramophone. Dorothy herself lay stretched on the sofa, slender legs in flesh-colored silk stockings showing to the knees, waving a cigarette in a long holder at Gregory Hart to emphasize a few remarks on suppressed desires" [033-016]. The image is a compendium of 1920s signifiers — mah-jongg, gramophone, silk stockings, cigarettes, Freudian jargon ("suppressed desires") — and the tone is distinctly satiric. Dorothy is performing modernity, and the performance is conspicuously shallow.

Her mother's response is the novel's most economical judgment on the generation gap: Mrs. Spear pauses at the door and says resignedly, "Dorothy, your legs" [033-025]. In four words, an entire world of maternal exasperation, social propriety, and generational conflict is compressed. When Victor Campion arrives — "Mr. Victor Campion in his high silk hat, out making Sunday afternoon calls" [033-034] — Dorothy looks over the heads of her guests, registering his presence with the same imperious indifference with which she treats everything else.

## Relationships

- **Violet Spear** — Her mother, who alternates between showing Dorothy off and correcting her manners. The tension between Violet's social ambition and Dorothy's casual insolence defines their relationship.
- **Bayard Spear** — Her father, the wealthy man whose purchase of The Maples [028-001] sets the Spear family's ascendancy in motion. He is mentioned but never appears directly with Dorothy.
- **Lily Campion** — Dorothy meets Lily at the housewarming as a child [028-077]; the encounter dramatizes the transfer of The Maples from the old family to the new.
- **Victor Campion** — Dorothy looks over his head when he arrives at her house party [033-034]; theirs is a relationship of mutual indifference across the chasm between old Wilmington and new money.

## Significance

Dorothy Spear is the novel's emblem of a new generation that has inherited a world it did not build and does not particularly value. Her silk stockings, her cigarettes, her Freudian patter, her casual rudeness — all mark her as belonging to the Jazz Age, a world of speed, noise, and surface that has replaced the slower, quieter, more decorous world of the Campions' youth. The Spears' purchase of The Maples is the novel's central symbol of historical change, and Dorothy is its human face: the child who plays in a garden she did not plant, the young woman who hosts parties in rooms she did not furnish. Her mother's weary "Dorothy, your legs" is more than a reprimand about posture — it is the voice of an older order, already half-resigned to its own obsolescence.
