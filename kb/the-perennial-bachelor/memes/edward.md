## Overview

Edward Post is a young man of the Campions' social circle who appears in the novel's middle chapters as a romantic interest for the Campion sisters. First introduced in Chapter 12 during the tableaux vivants at The Maples, he initially seems drawn to the beautiful May Campion but gradually develops a deeper, more complicated connection with her sister Maggie [012-009]. His presence brings to the surface the tensions between the sisters — May's expectation of admiration and Maggie's quieter, more authentic appeal [013-008]. By the novel's later chapters, Edward has married another woman [022-080], becoming one of several roads not taken in Maggie's life of sacrifice.

## Role in the Novel

Edward's first appearance casts him as Sir Lancelot in the Campions' tableaux vivants, "in a Tam o' Shanter with a plume from his Aunt Jo's best hat" [012-009]. The choice of role is fitting: like Lancelot, Edward will prove a romantic figure who does not ultimately choose the woman who loves him most. During the performance, May is so beautiful as the Lady of Shalott that "Lily wept unrestrainedly, and Edward Post looked down at her as if he really loved her" [012-014].

The following summer brings Edward closer to Maggie. During an afternoon of archery on the lawn at The Maples, Edward and May shoot against Maggie and Victor [013-008]. But Edward's attention shifts: "Edward had to work hard to beat her. He looked at her with dawning interest as she drew her bow" [013-009]. A scene of cherry-eating deepens their intimacy when Maggie matter-of-factly tells him to "just throw your stones into the lily-of-the-valley bed — we always do," and they eat "ever so many" cherries together [013-018].

> "Goodbye," Edward said to Maggie, smiling at her, taking her hand in his.
> — [013-036]

This quiet moment of farewell, with its understated physical gesture, carries more emotional weight than any of the theatrical declarations surrounding May. Edward becomes a regular presence, coming "on Sunday evening to sing hymns with the Campions" [013-040], and Lily teaches him to play chopsticks on the piano.

The swimming expedition at the river marks a turning point. May, seeking Edward's attention, begs him to teach her to swim: "Oh! Oh! Edward! Oh, I'm going down! Oh, don't let me go down!" [013-058]. Yet afterward it is Maggie and Edward who "sat together, swinging their feet, looking at the blue river" [013-066]. Their conversation in canvas chairs under the trees grows charged with meaning: "Maggie felt light as a mounting flame, and Edward too was flaming" [014-014].

Despite this connection, the romance does not endure. By Chapter 22, Edward has married: Maggie finds herself "looking at the presents with Edward Post and his wife" [022-080]. His wedding gifts become a quiet emblem of what Maggie has relinquished. Years later, a letter signed "Ever your friend, Edward Post" [028-034] confirms his permanent place in her past.

## Relationships

- **Maggie Campion** — His most significant relationship in the novel. Their connection builds through archery, cherry-eating, river-side conversations, and shared intellectual spark. Mamma "handed Edward around like a plate of delicious cake" [014-053], but the bond between Edward and Maggie was genuine rather than managed.
- **May Campion** — The initial object of his admiration during the tableaux. May performs for his gaze, but her theatrics cannot compete with Maggie's authenticity.
- **Lily Campion** — A peripheral connection; Lily teaches him chopsticks and cries at his handsomeness during the tableaux.
- **Mamma Campion** — Treats him as a prized social acquisition, "beaming" as she introduces him to other young ladies [014-053].

## Significance

Edward Post functions as the novel's most fully realized portrait of romantic possibility and its quiet dissolution. Unlike the other suitors who are merely sketched, Edward is given interiority and a recognizable character arc — from the theatricality of the tableaux to the intimate Sunday evenings at the Campions', to his eventual conventional marriage. His trajectory mirrors the novel's larger argument: that life's most consequential passages are not marked by dramatic rejection but by gradual drift, by cherries eaten and rivers watched and moments of flame that settle, without announcement, into ash. His signed letter — "Ever your friend" — is the epitaph of a love that never quite declared itself, and one of the novel's most quietly devastating turns.
