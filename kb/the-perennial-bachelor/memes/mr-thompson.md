# Mr. Thompson

## Overview

Mr. Thompson is a passenger aboard the *Hiawatha* during the Atlantic crossing that takes the Campion sisters to England. Described as the life of the party — "awfully jolly," "full of fun" — he provides comic relief during the voyage but is ultimately exposed as a man whose social performance masks a failure of genuine gentility. His proposal to May Campion at Hampton Court, undertaken with unearned presumption, becomes a minor but instructive episode in May's transatlantic education.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Thompson appears in Chapter 20, which takes the form of May Campion's long letter home from London. He is part of the shipboard society that makes the Atlantic crossing an intense social experience — a floating world of flirtations, games, and temporary intimacies. His narrative function is twofold: he contributes to the vivid texture of the voyage, and his eventual unmasking as "not a gentleman" provides May with a lesson in the difference between charm and character.

The episode also serves as a foil to May's more serious romantic attachments. Where Mr. Thompson is jolly but shallow, Edward Post and the other genuine suitors are measured against a higher standard.

## Key Appearances

### The Atlantic Crossing (Chapter 20)

May resolves to be "awfully jolly" — a phrase she borrows from Mr. Thompson, "a Mr. Thompson on board was always saying" — and not shed a tear at departure. The ship carries a lively company: "There were some desperate flirtations on board, of course. Everyone said Mr. Thompson ought to be named Mr. Spoony, but he was full of fun and the life of the party."

The voyage is slow ("An old donkey could go faster," Mr. Thompson quips), but the days slip by in games of "Ruth and Jacob," four daily meals, and the abandon of shipboard society.

### The Theatre Outing in London (Chapter 20)

After arriving in England, Mr. Thompson escorts May and her mother to the theatre to see Geneviéve Ward in *Forget-me-not*. This London appearance suggests he maintains a social connection with the Campions beyond the voyage itself, positioning himself as an ongoing presence in May's English experience.

### The Hampton Court Proposal (Chapter 20)

The relationship sours at Hampton Court, where Mr. Thompson manipulates an opportunity to separate May from the group — "he made an excuse to get me away from the others, to feed the swans, he said" — and proposes marriage. Critically, he does so "apparently taking it for granted that my answer would, of course, be 'yes.'" May refuses, and her verdict is definitive:

> "I never was so disappointed in anyone in my whole life. He is not a gentleman."

The episode is a miniature education in the difference between surface charm and genuine respect. Mr. Thompson's assumption of May's assent reveals an underlying vulgarity that his shipboard jollity had masked.

## Character and Significance

Mr. Thompson is one of the novel's minor cautionary figures — a man whose social gifts prove to be performance rather than substance. His nickname "Mr. Spoony" (a period term for a foolishly amorous man) hints at what the voyage's company already perceives: his charm is indiscriminate, his joviality a strategy rather than a temperament.

For May, the episode at Hampton Court is a small but significant moment of discernment. She has been the object of genuine romantic attention from Edward Post; Mr. Thompson's presumption clarifies, by contrast, what genuine gentility looks like. His role in the novel is brief — a handful of paragraphs in a single chapter — but his failure registers as a minor key in the novel's larger meditation on courtship, character, and the difference between what a man *appears* to be and what he *is*.
