# The Carnival

The Carnival — the last day of feasting before the Lenten fast — provides the backdrop for the second chapter of *An Attic Philosopher in Paris* (002-121). Through the lens of this annual festival of masks and misrule, the philosopher observes not only the "troops of masks" that fill the Parisian streets (002-143) but the deeper masquerade that governs everyday life: the social roles, pretenses, and self-deceptions that turn "our whole life" into "but an unsightly Carnival" (002-149).

## Overview

The philosopher is roused from his reflections by the noise of the street below. "I recollect," he writes, "this is the last day of the Carnival, and the maskers are passing" (002-121). The shouts in the square grow louder; the "troops of masks increase" (002-143), filling the boulevards with the anarchic energy of a festival that briefly suspends the hierarchies and proprieties of ordinary life.

He traces the Carnival's origins to pagan Rome. The Church, he observes, "did not abolish the festivals of the pagan times, but it has changed the names" (002-124). What was once the Saturnalia — a season of license when "all is given up to the 'fools of the family'" (002-142) — became the pre-Lenten Carnival. The very name carries its meaning: "carn-ival means, literally, 'farewell to flesh'" (002-126), marking the transition from feasting to the fasting of Lent.

Yet the philosopher's tone shifts from scholarly detachment to moral unease. "Must we believe," he asks, "that it requires such an effort to amuse us?" (002-132). The forced gaiety of the Carnival, the desperate pursuit of pleasure, strikes him as evidence not of joy but of its absence. The Carnival, he concludes, is a door opened to the "incongruous fancies" that are normally kept hidden — and in this, it holds a mirror to the human condition (002-139).

## Narrative Account

The philosopher illustrates his reflections with three vignettes drawn from the Carnival streets.

**The Ball and the Workingman.** In a neighboring apartment, a workman named chaufour is suffering from a toothache so severe he can barely speak. Yet his wife is determined not to miss the Carnival ball. "Bah!" she declares, "he may keep in his bed till to-morrow, as I'm going to the ball" (003-034). The scene captures the desperate hunger for pleasure among the working poor — a hunger so intense that even a husband's illness cannot dampen it. Another workman, Pierre, laments: "Well! must I miss a party of pleasure at Courtville?" (003-036). The planned excursion to "dine at Courtville Gardens" (003-062) represents one of the few bright points in a year of labour, and to miss it is a genuine calamity. When Pierre's employer demands hot water, Pierre "stamped his foot" and cried: "Am I to spend my Carnival in heating water for" others? (003-049).

**The Epicure's Feast.** In a quieter corner of the city, the philosopher encounters a different kind of Carnival celebration. An old man — an epicure of modest means — has prepared his own private feast. "Not all feasts are meant to regale the palate," the philosopher observes (003-083). The old man has bought a single luxury: a pineapple, which he contemplates with "nostrils expanded." "It was indeed," the philosopher records, "a feast for the eyes" (003-108). The man declares with "an air of triumph": "Others may run after dinners and balls; as for me, this is the pleasure I give myself for my Carnival" (003-111, 003-112). In this solitary connoisseur, the philosopher finds the truest spirit of the festival — not the forced gaiety of the crowd but the quiet cultivation of a simple delight.

**The Masquerade of Daily Life.** Returning to his window, the philosopher watches the maskers pass and reflects that the Carnival is not confined to a single day. "Each of us is like these masqueraders," he thinks (002-148). The pretenses worn in the street — the costumes of rank, profession, and respectability — are worn every day, not just on Shrove Tuesday. "Our whole life is often but an unsightly Carnival!" (002-149). The philosopher's wish is that humanity might "invent a Carnival that will please everybody, and bring shame to no one" (002-158) — a festival of genuine joy rather than desperate escapism.

## The Philosopher's Reflection

The Carnival leads the philosopher to a meditation on the nature of pleasure itself. The workingman's longing for the ball, the young wife's determination to dance, Pierre's fury at missing Courtville — these are not signs of frivolity but symptoms of a deeper poverty. When a life offers so few pleasures, each one becomes precious. "What a history," the philosopher reflects, "may be contained in one single day of pleasure!" (003-044).

The philosopher's own response to the Carnival is characteristically quiet. He shuts his window, stirs up his fire (002-159), and turns inward, seeking not the "brilliant maskers" of the street (002-176) but a peace that does not depend on noise and costume. The true alternative to the unsightly Carnival of daily life, he suggests, is not withdrawal but sincerity — the courage to face the world without a mask.
