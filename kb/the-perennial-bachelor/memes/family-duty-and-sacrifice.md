# Family Duty and Sacrifice

## Overview

Family duty in *The Perennial Bachelor* operates as both a moral imperative and a mechanism of entrapment. The Campion family is held together not by affection alone — though affection is abundant — but by a web of obligations that bind each member to the others, and most tightly bind the daughters to their mother and brother. Parrish examines this framework with a clarity that refuses both sentimentality and cynicism, showing how the genuine virtue of self-sacrifice becomes, when institutionalized, a kind of slow violence against those who practice it.

The novel's treatment of duty is deeply informed by its religious context. The Episcopal catechism, with its questions about "duty towards thy neighbor" (006-001), provides the moral language in which the characters understand their obligations. But Parrish shows how this language can be weaponized: the call to sacrifice is always directed at the women, never at Victor, and the rewards of sacrifice — spiritual consolation, the approval of the community — are real but insufficient.

## Treatment in the Novel

Parrish establishes the framework of duty early through the catechism recitation that opens Chapter 6. The question "What is thy duty towards thy neighbor?" and its answer — "To love him as myself" (006-002) — provide the novel's moral baseline. But the narrative immediately complicates this ideal by showing how unequally the burden of neighborly love is distributed. Maggie, the eldest daughter, bears nearly all of it; Victor, the son and heir, bears almost none.

The economic dimension of family duty is central. Victor's financial failures — his inability to hold a job, his drain on family resources — are subsidized by his sisters' sacrifices. They cannot marry because they cannot afford the social display that courtship requires; they cannot afford the display because the family's resources are directed toward Victor. This is not presented as villainy but as system: everyone in the family acts according to their understanding of their role, and the result is an arrangement that slowly consumes the lives of the women who sustain it.

Maggie's loyalty to Papa's memory — "fiercely loyal to Papa, she had been forbiddingly" (010-051) — represents another dimension of duty: the obligation to the dead. The novel is haunted by Papa's absence, and his daughters' sense of what he would have wanted shapes their choices long after his death. This loyalty to the dead is noble but paralyzing; it keeps the family suspended in a past that can never be recovered.

The novel's most devastating articulation of the sacrifice theme comes in Chapter 30, where Parrish draws an explicit parallel between the crushing of grapes for wine and the crushing of women's lives for the sake of family. "Before the wheat becomes bread it must be ground, before the grapes become wine they must be crushed. They must be sacrificed, as Christ on the Cross was sacrificed" (030-053). The religious imagery dignifies the suffering while also exposing its horror: what kind of God, Parrish implicitly asks, demands this kind of sacrifice from those who have already given everything?

## Character Perspectives

Maggie is the novel's primary embodiment of the sacrifice ethic. Her broken engagement — accepted by the community as a necessary sacrifice for her mother and brother — defines her adult life. She becomes, in effect, the family's emotional and practical anchor, the one who manages the household, nurses the sick, and absorbs the disappointments that others are too delicate to bear. Her self-description as an "old maid" who would "rather paddle my own canoe" (018-033) is both a statement of independence and an acknowledgment of what duty has cost her.

Lily, the youngest sister, represents a different relationship to duty: she is too fragile to bear its full weight, and so is partly exempted. But this exemption is itself a form of diminishment — she is spared sacrifice because she is not considered capable of anything else. The novel suggests that the ethics of duty, however sincerely held, ultimately serve to keep each woman in her assigned place.

Victor, the perennial bachelor, is the novel's case study in what happens when duty is not demanded. Pampered by his mother, indulged by his sisters, he drifts through life without ever being asked to sacrifice anything for anyone. His freedom is purchased by his sisters' constraint, and the novel's most damning judgment is simply to show this transaction without comment.

## Literary and Historical Context

The ethic of female self-sacrifice that Parrish examines was deeply embedded in nineteenth-century American culture, reinforced by both religious teaching and the "cult of true womanhood" that defined feminine virtue in terms of piety, purity, submissiveness, and domesticity. The post-Civil War South, with its cult of the Lost Cause and its idealization of the sacrificial Southern lady, added additional layers of expectation and mythology.

Parrish wrote from within this tradition but also against it. Published in 1925, after the disruptions of the First World War and the achievement of women's suffrage, *The Perennial Bachelor* can be read as an elegy for a world in which women's sacrifices had meaning — or as an exposé of a world in which those sacrifices were simply taken for granted. The novel's refusal to choose between these readings is the source of its lasting power.
