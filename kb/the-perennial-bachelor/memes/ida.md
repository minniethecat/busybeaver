## Overview

Ida appears in only a single sentence of *The Perennial Bachelor*, mentioned alongside her husband Jake as part of the servant community at The Maples. Her brief presence in the text is nonetheless significant: she represents the domestic world of the Black servants whose labor sustains the Campion household, and her marriage to Jake completes the small family unit — Jake, Ida, and their children — that mirrors, in a lower key, the family structures of the white characters whose lives occupy the novel's foreground.

## Role in the Novel

Ida's sole appearance occurs in Chapter 27, during a passage that describes how Victor Campion, visiting Wilmington with a pair of Chicago acquaintances, sees his family home transformed through the eyes of outsiders. By this point in the novel, Jake has been a recurring presence for many chapters: he first appears as a boy fishing with Victor [010-018], and by the later chapters he has grown into the adult who delivers the mail on St. Valentine's Day [027-041]. It is in this context — Jake the postman, Jake the family man — that Ida enters the narrative:

> The Maples was becoming an old plantation as it entered their sympathetic pinkness, Jake and Ida were turning into any number of old family servants, speaking in anecdotes and full of devotion for "Mr. Victor."
> — [027-063]

The sentence is layered with irony. The phrase "sympathetic pinkness" — a wry description of the sentimental Northern gaze — frames what follows as a performance of Southern nostalgia. Jake and Ida are "turning into" stock characters: the loyal old family retainer, the devoted servant speaking in anecdotes. The quotation marks around "Mr. Victor" underscore the artificiality of the tableau; this is not how Jake and Ida speak of Victor among themselves but how the Chicago visitors imagine they speak. The passage is a rare moment in which the novel acknowledges its own representational strategies — the way it, too, turns the Black characters into background figures in someone else's story.

## Relationships

- **Jake** — Her husband, Chloe's great-grandson, first seen fishing with the young Victor [010-018] and later serving as postman who brings the mail on St. Valentine's Day [027-041]. Their marriage [027-063] places Ida within a family lineage of household service that spans the novel's full chronology.
- **Chloe** — Jake's great-grandmother, a long-serving servant at The Maples; Ida marries into this family lineage of household service.

## Significance

Ida is present in the novel only as a name attached to a husband, a shadow-figure in a single sentence. Yet that sentence is one of the novel's most self-aware moments: it acknowledges, however briefly, that the Black characters who populate the margins of the story are being seen through a distorting lens — turned into types, anecdotes, and props in a nostalgic fantasy of the Old South. Ida's near-invisibility is not a failure of characterization but a structural feature of the novel's point of view, and her presence, however fleeting, gestures toward the fuller lives — of labor, of family, of community — that exist just beyond the narrative's frame.
