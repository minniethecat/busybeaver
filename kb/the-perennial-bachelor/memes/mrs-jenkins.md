# Mrs. Jenkins

## Overview

Mrs. Jenkins is the director of the amateur theatrical production that occupies much of Chapter 27. She is a figure of formidable authority — sharp-tongued, skeptical, and unimpressed by the pretensions of her cast. Her voice "shattered through an atmosphere of magnolia trees and mocking birds" as she marshals the Campion household and its guests through a chaotic rehearsal that includes "The Bachelor's Revery," Gibson Tableaux, and a cakewalk by the Sunflower Belles and Beaux.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Jenkins appears solely in Chapter 27, but her presence dominates the rehearsal scenes. She functions as the novel's comic portrait of the amateur theatrical director — part bully, part impresario, entirely convinced of her own authority. Her running commentary on the performers provides much of the chapter's humor, and her folded lips and dubious looks serve as a running critique of everyone's efforts.

She also serves a structural function: the rehearsal she directs brings together virtually the entire social circle of the Campions, creating a stage (literal and figurative) on which courtships, rivalries, and social performances play out simultaneously.

## Key Appearances

### The Rehearsal (Chapter 27)

Mrs. Jenkins opens the rehearsal with a cry that sets her tone: "Where's the Bachelor? Bachelor! Mr. Campion!" When Victor answers "Present!" she plunges forward without pleasantries: "Well! I've been calling till I — Margaret! Margaret Johnson! You stay right here! We're going to run through the 'Bachelor's Revery.'"

The rehearsal proceeds under her cold watch. When Victor performs "The Bachelor's Revery" — "Tomorrow is my wedding day, / Tonight, in the firelight's glow, / I'll sit and dream of bygone days, / And the girls I used to know" — her only feedback is: "You'll have to talk louder than that." When he promises to do better tomorrow, she replies "dubiously": "Well, I hope you do."

### The Gibson Tableaux (Chapter 27)

Mrs. Jenkins shifts to directing the Gibson Tableaux, a series of living pictures based on Charles Dana Gibson's illustrations. She calls for the missing Bride — "Bride! Bride! Where's the Bride? Where's Marguerite?" — and when told Marguerite is late, delayed by an automobile journey with Scudder Tait, she "folded her lips and sighed." Her efficiency is undimmed: "Well, the ones of you that are going to be in the Gibson Tableaux needn't wait any longer, but the Sunflower Belles and Beaux might go through their cakewalk again while we're waiting."

The production is a microcosm of the era's amateur theatrical culture: Gibson Girls, cakewalks, "Hello, Mah Baby" on the piano, and Mrs. Jenkins at the center of it all, "watched coldly" by a director who has clearly decided "she wasn't going to like it, no matter what anyone did."

## Character and Significance

Mrs. Jenkins is a minor character drawn with sharp comic strokes. She embodies the type of the small-town theatrical director — a role that confers enormous temporary authority and incites both fear and resentment in its holder's volunteer cast. Her voice "shatters," her eye is "cold," and her judgment is preemptive: nothing anyone does will satisfy her, because her authority is constituted precisely by her dissatisfaction.

Yet there is something admirable in her energy. She keeps the chaos of an amateur production moving forward — cues, costumes, cakewalks, and all. In a novel about performance — social, romantic, and theatrical — Mrs. Jenkins is the one character who treats performance as a job to be done, not a role to be inhabited.

## Relationships

- **Victor Campion**: Her "Bachelor" — the lead performer whose volume she finds wanting
- **Maggie Campion**: The "School Girl" who must stay on her cues
- **The Campion social circle**: The cast of the production, all subject to her direction
