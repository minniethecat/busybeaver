## Overview

Stewy Grant occupies the margins of the novel's communal scenes — the church choir, the tableaux vivants, the Sunday school — a minor male figure whose presence rounds out the town's social fabric without ever commanding center stage. He is remembered chiefly for nearly playing Sir Lancelot, for a sneeze that punctuates the church service, and for a little boy who passes through Lily Campion's Sunday school class.

## Role in the Novel

Stewy Grant first appears by negative reference: someone observes, "I thought Stewy Grant was going to be Sir Lancelot" at the tableaux vivants, implying he was cast, replaced, or simply imagined for the role [012-045]. The remark is casual, but it fixes him in the novel's taxonomy as a man who might-have-been — appropriate for a book about lives that never quite take the shape they seem to promise.

During the church service at St. Stephen's, Stewy is present in the choir procession. As the choir marches out, "Stewy Grant wondered if he could possibly make it to the door before he sneezed" [014-018]. The detail is trivial and intensely human — a man calculating the distance between his nose and the exit, a small comic struggle embedded in the solemnity of the service.

His later appearance is vicarious: Lily's Sunday school class includes "Sissie and Mary Holly, Stewy Grant's little boy, and Miss Taylor, the postmaster's daughter" [023-007]. The possessive — "Stewy Grant's little boy" — is how he enters the record, defined by fatherhood rather than any accomplishment of his own.

## Relationships

- **St. Stephen's choir** — He sings in the choir under Miss Martin's organ and Mr. Bates's booming voice, part of the church's musical ensemble [014-018].
- **Lily Campion** — His son attends Lily's Sunday school class, making Stewy one of the parents under her pedagogical authority [023-007].
- **His son** — Mentioned only as "Stewy Grant's little boy," unnamed, a child who passes through the Sunday school room as part of the town's generational continuity [023-007].

## Significance

Stewy Grant is the novel's representative of the ordinary man — a choir member, a father, a figure so unremarkable that his most vivid moment is nearly sneezing in church. He is the kind of person the Campion sisters see every Sunday and never think about, and the novel's willingness to name him, to record his near-sneeze and his son's presence in Lily's class, is characteristic of its documentary impulse: everyone counts, even the people who do nothing worth recounting.
