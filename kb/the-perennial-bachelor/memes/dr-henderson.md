## Overview

Dr. Henderson was a physician practicing on Delaware Avenue in Wilmington, mentioned in the later chapters of the novel. He represents the changing medical landscape of the early twentieth century—a doctor with an office in the city rather than the family physician making house calls with a black bag like Dr. Chase of the earlier generation [030-021].

## Role in the Novel

Dr. Henderson appears in a single episode near the end of the novel, when a member of the extended Campion circle pays him a visit on the recommendation of a friend: "We always go to Dr. Henderson on Delaware Avenue. Why don't you come out to lunch, and then I'll send you down to him in the motor?" [030-021]. The offer of a motor car ride to the doctor's office signals the modernity of the later chapters—a marked contrast with the earlier scenes of Dr. Chase arriving at the house with his black bag.

The patient, feeling unexpectedly well by the time she arrives, hesitates at the threshold: "She felt so well by the time she got to Dr. Henderson's that it seemed ridiculous to go in—still, she was here, and she might as well" [030-023]. The nervous glance into her purse, wondering what he charges, grounds the visit in the economic anxieties that run throughout the novel. After waiting her turn, "she picked up her market basket and went in to Dr. Henderson" [030-026]—a detail that places the medical consultation within the fabric of ordinary domestic errands.

## Relationships

Dr. Henderson's sole connection to the novel's world is professional: he is the physician regularly consulted by the friend or relative who recommends him. His office on Delaware Avenue places him in the commercial-medical district of Wilmington, distinct from the domestic settings where Dr. Chase practiced a generation earlier. The Campion family's movement from home visits by Dr. Chase to office consultations with Dr. Henderson subtly traces the professionalization and urbanization of American medicine across the novel's timespan.

## Significance

Though a fleeting presence, Dr. Henderson serves as a quiet marker of historical change. The contrast between Dr. Chase (the old-fashioned family doctor arriving at the house with his black bag) and Dr. Henderson (the city physician with an office on Delaware Avenue, reached by motor car) charts the modernization of medical practice from the mid-nineteenth century to the 1920s. Anne Parrish embeds this shift without commentary, letting the domestic details—a market basket, a ride in a motor, a nervous glance at a purse—carry the weight of social history. The character also reinforces the novel's attention to the physical vulnerabilities of its characters, whose bodies age, sicken, and require professional attention as the decades pass.
