# Memory and Nostalgia

## Overview

The opening sentence of *The Perennial Bachelor* establishes memory as the novel's governing mode: "between sleeping and waking, Maggie Campion knew, without remembering why, that it was a happy day" (001-001). The distinction between knowing and remembering — between the felt residue of the past and the conscious act of recollection — runs throughout the novel. Parrish's characters live in a perpetual negotiation with memory: the golden past they remember is partly real and partly invented, and the gap between the two is where much of the novel's emotional complexity resides.

Nostalgia in Parrish's hands is not an escape from reality but a way of managing it. Her characters remember the past not because it was better — though they believe it was — but because remembering is how they make sense of the present. The past provides the stories they tell about themselves: Papa was wonderful, Mamma was beautiful, the family was happy. Whether these stories are true matters less than the fact that they are necessary.

## Treatment in the Novel

The novel's narrative structure enacts the workings of memory. It moves associatively rather than chronologically, drifting from present to past and back again, following the currents of character recollection. When Maggie remembers "now what it was. It was Papa's birthday" (001-002), the sentence's structure — the delayed recognition, the specific detail — mimics the process by which memory surfaces into consciousness.

Parrish is particularly attentive to material memory: the objects that carry the past into the present. Mamma's wedding dress, Papa's desk, the family silver — these things are talismans, physical anchors for stories that might otherwise drift away. The novel's treatment of these objects is neither sentimental nor dismissive; Parrish understands that people need their talismans, even when — perhaps especially when — the reality they commemorate was more complicated than the memory.

Mamma's memories of Papa are the novel's most sustained study in selective recollection. "Mamma, remembering, thought, 'Poor Lizzie!' comfortably" (001-049) — the memory of Papa's marriage to her is transformed into a story in which she is the fortunate one, the chosen one, the happy ending. The comfort this memory provides is real; its accuracy is beside the point. Parrish understands that memory serves emotional needs that have nothing to do with factual truth.

The novel's treatment of nostalgia is complicated by its historical setting. The post-Civil War South was a culture built on memory — the memory of a lost cause, a lost civilization, a lost way of life. The Campion family's nostalgia for its own past participates in this larger cultural nostalgia, and Parrish registers the connection without making it explicit. The family's decline mirrors the region's, and their memories serve the same function: to make the present bearable by insisting that the past was glorious.

## Character Perspectives

Maggie's memory is practical and corporeal — she remembers events, duties, the things that must be done. Her relationship to the past is less nostalgic than responsible: she is the keeper of the family's continuity, the one who ensures that traditions are maintained and debts honored.

Mamma's memory is aesthetic and emotional — she remembers feelings, atmospheres, the way things looked. Her nostalgia is genuinely felt but also strategically deployed: remembering the past is a way of avoiding the present's demands.

Victor's memory is the most impoverished. He lives almost entirely in the present, drifting from one enthusiasm to the next without the ballast of recollection. His inability to learn from the past is presented as both a character flaw and a survival strategy — remembering too clearly what he has failed to become would be unbearable.

## Literary and Historical Context

Parrish's treatment of memory anticipates the modernist preoccupation with subjective time that would reach its fullest expression in Proust and Woolf. Her innovation is to ground this philosophical concern in the specific material and social circumstances of her characters' lives. Memory in *The Perennial Bachelor* is not a luxury of consciousness but a necessity of survival — the tool that people with very little use to make sense of lives that have not gone as planned.
