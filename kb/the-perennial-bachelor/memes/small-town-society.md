# Small-Town Society

## Overview

The unnamed small town near Wilmington, Delaware, in which most of *The Perennial Bachelor* takes place is not merely a setting but an agent — a collective consciousness that observes, judges, and remembers everything its inhabitants do. Parrish, who grew up in a similar milieu, renders this world with the precision of an insider: everyone knows everyone else's business, social standing is a matter of perpetual negotiation, and the past is never truly past because the community keeps it alive through gossip and collective memory.

The town operates as a panopticon of sorts — every action is witnessed, every failure recorded, every pretension noted. This visibility is both protective and suffocating. It enforces standards of behavior that maintain social order, but it also forecloses the possibility of reinvention. Once the town has decided who you are — the beauty, the old maid, the spendthrift, the eccentric — there is no appeal.

## Treatment in the Novel

Parrish establishes the town's social geography through accumulation. The names — Miss Snaith, Cousin Lizzie Blow, Mrs. Hawthorn, Mr. Page, Miss Hopper — appear and reappear, creating a sense of a populated world in which everyone is known to everyone else. The novel does not describe the town directly; it allows the town to emerge through the characters' interactions, through the shared references and unspoken assumptions that constitute communal life.

The mechanism of social control is gossip, and Parrish treats it with characteristic ambivalence. Gossip is cruel — it enforces conformity through the threat of exclusion — but it is also a form of social knowledge, a way of making sense of a small world. When Cousin Lizzie says something scandalous, or when a character's marriage prospects are discussed, the novel registers both the cruelty of the judgment and the pleasure of the telling.

The town's relationship to time is one of the novel's most subtle observations. In the small town, the past is never over; it is preserved in memory, invoked in conversation, deployed as precedent. The Campion family's history — Papa's gallantry, Mamma's beauty, the children's promise — is public property, a story that the community tells about itself. When the family declines, the town notices; when the daughters fail to marry, the town knows why — or believes it knows, which amounts to the same thing.

## Character Perspectives

Mamma experiences the town as audience — she performs for it, worries about its judgments, and measures her worth by its approval. Her beauty was a public asset, admired by the community; its loss is a public event, registered in the diminishing frequency of calls and invitations.

Maggie is largely indifferent to the town's opinion — a privilege purchased at the cost of being considered hopeless anyway. Her plainness and practicality exempt her from the marriage market and therefore from the community's most intense scrutiny. She is seen but not watched, and this invisibility is one of her few freedoms.

Cousin Lizzie Blow represents the possibility of opting out. Her eccentricity — Spiritualism, outspokenness, indifference to convention — is tolerated precisely because she no longer cares whether it is tolerated. She has achieved the small town's rarest commodity: the freedom of not needing to be liked.

## Literary and Historical Context

The small town as literary subject has a distinguished American pedigree, from Sarah Orne Jewett's Dunnet Landing to Sherwood Anderson's Winesburg to Sinclair Lewis's Gopher Prairie. Parrish's contribution to this tradition is her focus on the specifically female experience of small-town life — the way the community's scrutiny falls most heavily on women, whose reputations are their only currency and whose every action is judged by standards they did not create and cannot contest.
