# Mr. Lacey's Courtship of Mamma

## Overview

Mr. [[alfred-lacey|Alfred Lacey]]'s courtship of [[mamma-campion|Mamma Campion]] is the novel's most fully rendered romantic narrative — and its quietest tragedy. A widower of warmth and genuine affection, Mr. Lacey enters the Campion household as a skating partner, a Christmas decoration helper, and a teller of puns. He leaves as a possibility that was never quite refused but also never quite accepted. The courtship's failure is the moment when the Campion family's self-enclosure becomes permanent.

## The Courtship

Mr. Lacey's presence in the household builds across several chapters through accumulation of domestic detail. At Christmas, he helped the children make church decorations: "Even Mr. Lacey's hands were black" [009-002] with evergreen sap. He bundled the girls on winter outings, "came bounding in, all chill and snowy" [009-013], and brought the outside world into the parlor with an energy the Campion household could not generate on its own.

His relationship with Mamma developed through shared laughter. He made her feel young again — "she had begun to make little jokes herself, and grow girlish and arch and saucy" [009-002]. Where Papa's warmth had been familial, domestic, diffuse, Mr. Lacey's was directed: "gazing earnestly and admiring" [009-043] at Mamma, he offered the specific attention of a man choosing a partner.

> Mr. Lacey liked everybody so much, he couldn't help laughing at them, and they couldn't help laughing back. [009-027]

The children observed with mixed feelings. [[lily-campion|Lily]] "would have quite loved kind Mr. Lacey" [010-038]; [[may-campion|May]] blushed at any mention of the romance; [[maggie-campion|Maggie]] watched with the complicated awareness of someone who understood the stakes. The household held its breath.

## The Failure

The courtship's failure is never dramatized. Parrish, faithful to her method, records it through indirection and silence. Mr. Lacey simply stops appearing. The reasons are suggested rather than stated: Mamma's inability to share herself after [[papas-death|Papa's death]], her absorption in [[victor-campion|Victor]], the gravitational pull of the household that tolerated no outside attachments.

Maggie, who had been picking raspberries when she sensed something was wrong, "went on picking raspberries from the hot vines" [010-053] — the domestic labor continuing as if nothing had happened, because in the Campion household nothing was ever allowed to have happened.

## Significance

The failed courtship is the novel's most important turning point that is not an event. Mr. Lacey's departure confirms that The Maples will remain a closed system. Mamma will not remarry; Victor will not gain a stepfather; the daughters will not have the model of a mother who chose partnership over absorption.

The courtship's warmth — its genuine affection, its laughter, its possibility — makes its failure more painful than any melodramatic rejection could have been. Mr. Lacey was good, Mamma was capable of happiness with him, and it still was not enough. The Campion pattern was stronger than any individual desire to escape it.
