## Overview

Miss Perry is the town seamstress, one of the small-scale working women whose livelihoods are intertwined with the Campion family's fortunes. She appears only in passing, but each mention adds a distinct sensory or economic detail: the sweetness of her mock-orange bushes, the pathos of her lost savings, the gossip she shares with Mrs. Trewhitt. In a novel largely centered on the leisured gentility of the Campion sisters, Miss Perry represents the laboring woman whose modest independence is as precarious as the sisters' own.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Perry is first mentioned in the context of Sunday school conversation, grouped with "people like Mrs. Trewhitt and Miss Perry, the seamstress" — the kind of churchgoing townspeople whose lives form the background hum of the community [006-012]. The appositive "the seamstress" is her entire identity in the novel's social taxonomy: she is what she does.

Her most vivid appearance is sensory rather than narrative. Passing through town, the narrator notes "the mock-orange bushes in Miss Perry's yard. Queer how their fragrance made you feel so happy and yet as if you wanted to cry at the same time" [012-070]. The yard, the bushes, the feeling — Miss Perry herself never appears in the scene, yet her presence is the occasion for one of the novel's most evocative passages.

Her third mention is economic and elegiac. When the Campion sisters can no longer afford their former expenditures, the narrator notes "poor Miss Perry, who had counted on saving her earnings from the Campion sewing" [017-002]. Like the sisters themselves, Miss Perry has "counted on" a future that did not arrive. Her disappointment mirrors theirs, scaled down to the dimensions of a seamstress's ledger.

## Relationships

- **Mrs. Trewhitt** — Fellow churchgoer with whom she shares conversation, the two of them representing the town's network of women whose social world revolves around St. Stephen's [006-012].
- **The Campion sisters** — Her clients and economic dependents. When the sisters' fortunes decline, Miss Perry loses income she had counted on, a small-scale echo of the larger economic forces reshaping the town [017-002].

## Significance

Miss Perry occupies perhaps three sentences in the novel, yet those sentences do concentrated work. She is a figure of modest independence — a woman who owns her own yard, grows her own mock-orange, earns her own living — and also of vulnerability, since her livelihood depends on the patronage of families like the Campions. The mock-orange passage is the novel in miniature: beauty that makes you "so happy and yet as if you wanted to cry." Her lost savings, noted in a single subordinate clause, gesture toward the economic precarity that the novel's leisured protagonists are only beginning to taste.
