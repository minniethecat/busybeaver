## Overview

Cousin Jennie Blodgett belongs to the extended family network that orbits the Campion household — a relation who appears during times of crisis and whose material traces (party slippers, Christmas gloves, an embroidered motto) outlast her presence in the narrative. She is one of the novel's many minor women whose lives are glimpsed through domestic objects rather than dramatic scenes, and her "God Bless Our Home" motto becomes, in the novel's final chapters, an artifact of a home that no longer exists.

## Role in the Novel

Cousin Jennie is first mentioned as part of the household during Mamma Campion's final illness: Victor recalls the bag of horehound candy the doctor "had brought him in his bag; and Cousin Jennie Blodgett, who stayed with them while Mamma was so ill" [004-016]. Her presence is associated with childhood crisis — she is the relative who materializes when a family needs extra hands, the kind of woman whose life is defined by service to others' emergencies.

Years later, her gifts reappear as relics. When the Campion sisters attend a party, they wear "their party slippers, and Cousin Jennie Blodgett's Christmas present gloves" — the possessions of women who rely on gifts to supplement wardrobes they cannot afford to replenish [022-026].

In the novel's final chapter, as the household is dismantled, someone holds up an embroidered motto: "Here's Cousin Jennie Blodgett's 'God Bless Our Home' motto" [028-014]. The object, like the home it was meant to bless, has become a curiosity — something to be sorted, kept, or discarded. The irony is gentle but unmistakable: God did not bless this home, or if He did, the blessing took the form of endurance rather than prosperity.

## Relationships

- **The Campion sisters** — Her cousins, with whom she stays during Mamma's illness and to whom she gives Christmas gloves. The relationship is one of familial obligation quietly fulfilled.
- **Mamma Campion** — She stays with the household during Mamma's final illness, part of the vigil that defines the sisters' girlhood [004-016].

## Significance

Cousin Jennie Blodgett never speaks a line of dialogue in the novel, yet her embroidered motto — "God Bless Our Home" — delivers one of the book's most quietly devastating ironies. The home she blessed with her needlework has been sold, its contents dispersed, its family reduced to three aging women who can no longer afford to maintain it. The motto survives as a material remnant of a domestic ideal that the novel has spent thirty-three chapters dismantling. Cousin Jennie, like the motto, is a figure of well-meaning conventional piety, preserved in the narrative not for what she did but for what her embroidered words, held up in the clearing-out, come to mean.
