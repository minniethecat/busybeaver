## Overview

Miss Hessie Farley is one of the more fully realized minor characters in *The Perennial Bachelor*. She appears across five chapters, always in connection with St. Stephen's Episcopal Church and its social life. She teaches Sunday school, performs in tableaux vivants, hosts a Valentine euchre party, and dispenses gloomy pronouncements about the marital prospects of her acquaintances. Parrish sketches Hessie with a combination of affection and gentle mockery — she is ridiculous in her theatrical ambitions and her fashion anxieties, but her presence is essential to the communal fabric the novel depicts.

## Role in the Novel

Hessie Farley's first appearance establishes both her role and her manner. In Chapter 6 she is teaching Sunday school, asking the children "What is thy duty towards thy neighbor?" while "brushing a bluebottle fly away from her nose" [006-001]. The juxtaposition of solemn catechism and mundane distraction is characteristic of Parrish's lightly ironic treatment of small-town piety.

After church, Hessie mingles with the congregation. She discusses her new "postillion-coat" and frets over whether to adopt "the Egyptian fashion" for its buttons — "did they think the Egyptian fashion was going to take? Because if so, she would have triangular buttons, like the Pyramids, but one didn't want to be extreme" [006-012]. This small comic monologue reveals a woman acutely conscious of fashion trends but uncertain in her taste — a blend of aspiration and anxiety that Parrish treats with amused sympathy.

Chapter 12 gives Hessie her moment on stage — literally. She is to appear as "Airy Fairy Lilian" in the Tennyson tableaux, but is thrown into a panic when she cannot find her blue scarf and parasol. "Tears of fury sprang to her daughter's eyes" [012-001] when her mother asks why she didn't hold onto them. The props are eventually discovered beneath Aunt Priscilla, who has been sitting on them, and Hessie takes her position with her smile in place [012-001].

Hessie also serves as a barometer of local opinion. In Chapter 14, she pronounces gloomily to her mother that no one "had ever got engaged before" [014-025], a remark that captures both her investment in the romantic lives of others and her tendency toward melodramatic pronouncement. Chapter 17 finds her dispensing advice about hair-lightening [017-004], and by Chapter 23, she hosts a Valentine euchre party where significant social encounters occur [023-013].

## Relationships

**Mrs. Farley** — Hessie's mother, who assists behind the scenes at the tableaux and receives her daughter's tearful outbursts with weary practicality [012-001].

**The St. Stephen's Congregation** — Hessie is deeply embedded in church life as a Sunday school teacher [006-001] and participant in parish entertainments [012-001, 023-013]. Her social world is almost entirely defined by the church.

**Maggie Campion** — It is Maggie who spots Hessie's lost blue scarf under Aunt Priscilla during the tableaux panic [012-001], a small moment of cross-class assistance that links the younger generation of church women.

**The Almonds and Mrs. Trewhitt** — Hessie's social circle includes Mr. and Mrs. Almond, with whom she discusses fashion and parish gossip after Sunday services [006-012].

## Significance

Miss Hessie Farley is a representative figure: the unmarried church woman whose life revolves around Sunday school, tableaux, and euchre parties. In a novel centrally concerned with the predicament of the unmarried woman — the "perennial bachelor" and "spinster" alike — Hessie offers a glimpse of how such a life might look from the inside: anxious, sometimes ridiculous, but not without its pleasures and its dignities.

Her "Airy Fairy Lilian" episode is particularly revealing. The Tennyson tableaux offer Hessie a chance to be someone else — ethereal, poetic, romantic — and the gap between that aspiration and the reality (lost props, hitchy curtains, a mother asking practical questions) is the gap the novel everywhere explores. Parrish's treatment of Hessie is never cruel; it is, rather, clear-eyed about the limitations of small-town life while honoring the genuine feeling — the tears of fury, the determination to smile — that Hessie brings to her modest stage.
