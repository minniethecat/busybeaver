# Lincoln's Assassination

## Overview

President Lincoln's assassination in April 1865 — occurring just days after Lee's surrender at Appomattox — was the defining national trauma of the post-Civil War era. In *The Perennial Bachelor*, the event exists as part of the historical atmosphere that surrounds the Campion family's early years: a public grief that paralleled and amplified the private losses the family would soon experience.

## Historical Context

Lincoln was shot by John Wilkes Booth at Ford's Theatre on April 14, 1865, and died the following morning. The assassination, coming at the moment of Union victory, transformed the national mood from celebration to mourning. For families like the Campions, whose lives had already been shaped by the [[civil-war-battles|Civil War]], the assassination deepened the sense of a world in irreversible transformation.

## Role in the Novel

The assassination appears in the novel as historical background — the kind of public event that filters into the Campion household through conversation, newspapers, and the general cultural atmosphere without becoming the subject of dramatized scenes. It belongs to the same category as the war itself: history that shapes the characters' world without being fully processed by them.

The event's narrative function is to locate the Campion story within a specific historical moment and to establish the larger context of loss and transformation against which the family's private decline unfolds. The nation lost its president; The Maples would lose its patriarch. The public and private griefs run parallel, connected by the historical moment but experienced at entirely different scales.

## Significance

Like the [[civil-war-battles|Civil War]], Lincoln's assassination is part of the novel's historical infrastructure — the real events that shape the fictional world without becoming its subject. Parrish's decision to keep such events in the narrative's background reflects her method: the Campions are not historical actors but historical subjects, shaped by forces they cannot control and do not fully understand.
