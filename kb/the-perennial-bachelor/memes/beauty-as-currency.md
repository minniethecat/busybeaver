# Beauty as Currency

## Overview

In the economy of *The Perennial Bachelor*, beauty is the primary form of capital available to women. It can be exchanged for marriage, security, and social position; its absence condemns a woman to dependency, obscurity, or worse. Anne Parrish traces the operation of this economy across three generations of Campion women, showing how the distribution of beauty — who has it, who loses it, who never had it — determines the shape of each life with a precision that is almost cruel.

The novel's treatment of beauty is notable for its lack of sentimentality. Parrish does not pretend that inner worth compensates for outward plainness, or that character matters more than appearance. In the world she depicts, beauty is power, and the women who lack it — or who lose it — are powerless in ways that virtue cannot remedy. This clear-sightedness is what gives the novel its moral force: it insists on seeing the world as it is, not as we might wish it to be.

## Treatment in the Novel

The novel establishes the beauty hierarchy early. Mamma is beautiful — this is the central fact of her identity and the explanation for her marriage (001-052). May inherits this beauty and learns to use it: "She was the beauty of the family, with her long" (008-012). Maggie is plain — this is the central fact of her identity too, and it determines everything that follows. Lily is pale, delicate, interesting-looking rather than beautiful — she occupies an intermediate position in the economy of appearance, neither valuable enough to trade nor worthless enough to ignore.

Parrish is attentive to the labor that beauty requires. May's beauty is not natural but achieved — maintained through dress, deportment, and the constant performance of desirability. The dance cards, the pompadours, the "bursts of chiffon at the backs of their necks" (026-010) — these are the tools of the trade. The novel understands that beauty is work, and that women who succeed at this work are not lucky but skilled.

The relationship between beauty and happiness is treated with sustained irony. Mamma, the most beautiful character, is also the most helpless — her beauty secured her a husband but left her incapable of surviving without one. May's beauty earns her a marriage but the novel is ambiguous about whether this marriage constitutes happiness. Maggie's plainness, which seems at first to be her tragedy, becomes by the novel's end a kind of freedom: she has nothing to lose because she never had the currency to play the game.

The loss of beauty through aging is the novel's most painful subject. Beauty "made her so happy!" (010-014), but happiness founded on beauty is fragile by definition. Time is the enemy of beautiful women in a way it is not the enemy of anyone else, and the novel traces the slow erosion of Mamma's power with a tenderness that does not diminish the harshness of the observation.

## Character Perspectives

Maggie's plainness is the novel's most sustained meditation on the beauty economy. She is described as "light eyes looking out" (001-003) — the focus is on what she sees, not how she is seen. Her role in the family — practical manager, emotional anchor, perpetual sacrifice — is partly a consequence of her appearance: a plain woman's labor is less valuable than a beautiful woman's presence, and so Maggie works while May is displayed.

May's beauty is both gift and trap. It gives her power in the marriage market, but it defines her entirely in terms of male desire. She is never seen as anything other than beautiful, and the novel leaves open the question of whether she is anything other than beautiful — whether the performance has consumed the self that performed it.

Mamma's fading beauty is the novel's most elegiac thread. The woman who was once the center of every room becomes, by the novel's end, a ghost at the edge of the action — still present, still loved, but no longer powerful. The novel mourns this loss without ever suggesting that the power of beauty was a good thing.

## Literary and Historical Context

The treatment of beauty as a form of female capital has a long history in literature, from the fairy tales in which beauty is the heroine's only asset to the marriage-plot novels of the nineteenth century. Parrish's contribution is to treat the beauty economy with the analytic precision of an economist: she shows how it works, who benefits, who pays, and what happens when the currency is devalued by time.
