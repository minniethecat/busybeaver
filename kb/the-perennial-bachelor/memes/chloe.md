# Chloe

## Overview

Chloe is the elderly cook at The Maples, present in the household across the decades the novel spans. She belongs to the generation of Black domestic workers whose labor sustained Southern white households, and Parrish's treatment of her is characteristic of the novel's limitations: she is rendered with affection but never interiority, present as a force of care rather than a fully realized character.

## Domestic Presence

Chloe's presence is most vivid in the novel's childhood scenes. She told stories to the Campion children and was part of the fabric of the household that Maggie remembered from earliest consciousness. Old Chloe appeared alongside "old Toot" [001-046] in the stable-yard — the paired figures of Black labor that framed the Campion children's world.

She baked on Saturdays, providing "real food to put on the heavy little china plates" [006-???] for the children's play — baked dough mice with currant eyes, "grimy grey and hard as rock." The detail captures the affection in her labor: food made not just for sustenance but for childhood pleasure, however humble the materials.

## The Limits of Representation

Parrish's portrayal of Chloe reflects the racial blind spots of a 1925 novel by a white author. Chloe is kind, loyal, and essentially voiceless — a figure of sentimentalized domestic service. She is never given speech of any substance, never shown to have a life outside the Campion kitchen, never accorded the interiority the novel lavishes on its white characters.

The novel does, however, record the structural reality of her position. She is "old Chloe" [001-046] from the first pages, her age marking a lifetime of service. She is part of the household economy that the Campion daughters inherit and maintain — the labor that makes their gentility possible.

## Significance

Chloe represents the presence that the novel registers but cannot fully see: the Black domestic workers whose labor was the foundation of the Southern white household. Her significance is structural rather than narrative — she is there because the Campion world required her, and the novel's inability to imagine her inner life is itself a historical document.

Within the novel's own terms, Chloe is the most enduring presence at The Maples. Children are born and grow; Papa dies; the household declines. But Chloe remains, cooking, sustaining, witnessing. She is the continuity that the Campions take for granted and the novel never fully honors.
