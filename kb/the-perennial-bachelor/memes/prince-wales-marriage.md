# Marriage of the Prince and Princess of Wales

## Overview

The marriage of the Prince and Princess of Wales (the future King Edward VII and Queen Alexandra, married in 1863) appears in the novel as a distant cultural reference — the kind of public event that filters into the consciousness of the Campion household through newspapers, conversation, and the general cultural atmosphere. It is not a plot point but a timestamp: the royal wedding locates the Campion story within a specific historical moment and establishes the distance between the grand events of the world and the circumscribed lives of the novel's characters.

## The Public and the Private

The royal wedding serves as a counterpoint to the novel's private dramas. While princes marry princesses in distant cathedrals, the Campion daughters navigate their own romantic possibilities — or lack thereof — in the parlors and gardens of The Maples. The gap between the public event and the private lives is the novel's subject: history happens elsewhere, while the Campions live within the small circumference of their domestic world.

## Significance

The royal wedding is the novel's most extreme example of history as background radiation. It is mentioned, it locates the narrative in time, and it has no effect whatsoever on the characters' lives. This is precisely the point: the Campion story is not a story of great events but of the small, accumulating details of domestic existence. The royal wedding, by its very irrelevance, illuminates the novel's true subject.
