# May's Marriage

## Overview

[[may-campion|May Campion]]'s marriage is a landmark in the Campion family history — the moment when one of the daughters achieves what her sisters cannot. She is the beautiful one, the one Mamma acknowledged as elegant, and her wedding represents the family's single successful transaction with the outside world. Yet Parrish complicates the escape narrative: May marries but remains tethered, leaves but never fully arrives elsewhere.

## The Event

The novel does not dramatize the wedding itself. As with [[papas-death|Papa's death]] and [[maggie-broken-engagement|Maggie's broken engagement]], the crucial event occurs in the narrative's gaps. What we see instead are the ripples: the preparations, the family's responses, the changed atmosphere at The Maples after May's departure.

"May Campion! Who's A. J.? Every waltz with him!" [023-080] — the question, asked at a dance before her marriage, captures the social scrutiny that attended May's romantic life. She was the Campion daughter whose beauty was publicly assessed, whose choices were publicly tracked.

## Aftermath

The question "What became of May Campion?" [027-019], asked later in the novel, hangs in the air. The answer is ambiguous: she married, she left, she lived elsewhere — and yet the novel's interest in her wanes after her departure, as if the marriage, having been accomplished, ceases to be narratively relevant.

This is itself a comment on the novel's investment in entrapment over escape. May's story — the one daughter who got out — is the one the novel cannot sustain interest in. The narrative energy of *The Perennial Bachelor* flows toward those who stay, not those who leave.

## Significance

May's marriage proves that escape from The Maples was possible — and also that escape was never complete. She married, but the gravitational field of the Campion family continued to exert its pull. Her departure is the exception that proves the rule: the Campion daughters were shaped for a world that offered no real exits, only different forms of accommodation.

The marriage also illuminates, by contrast, the fates of [[maggie-campion|Maggie]] and [[lily-campion|Lily]]. May got what they did not — and the novel, having granted her this distinction, loses interest. The story Parrish wanted to tell was not the story of the daughter who escaped, but the story of those who could not.
