## Overview

Caesar is the gardener at The Maples, the Campion family home. His domain is the grape-house — a warm, fragrant space where he tends the vines and cuttings — and his skills extend to driving Mamma to church in the family carriage [006-007]. As the novel traces the Campion family's slow decline, Caesar represents the continuity of the household's working life: the garden tended, the horse harnessed, the seasons turning in their accustomed rhythm.

## Role in the Novel

Caesar enters the narrative early in the novel's domestic chronology. When the children's pony dies, "his place was taken by Caesar, who, under Mamma's direction, planted the whole garden in tuberoses" [005-002]. This detail establishes his role as both laborer and agent of transformation — the pony's death and the tuberoses' planting mark one of childhood's small, irrevocable transitions.

In spring, "Caesar was showing the children a nest of young mice" [005-009] — a moment of gentleness amid the household's practical routines. His regular duty of driving Mamma to church places him at the threshold between the domestic sphere and the public world of the town: he waits "in the carriage sheds" [006-007], a figure poised between service and the wider landscape.

The grape-house is Caesar's particular province. Victor, returning home, finds Stella the horse "in the grape-house with Caesar. The leaves and the heavy bunches of small green grapes made it moist and warm and fragrant" [007-053]. The scene gathers the household's working members — Martha, Albert, and "Caesar with his scissors" [007-055] — in a tableau of productive labor that contrasts with the emotional dramas unfolding in the parlor. Caesar "stood on the old broken chair" [007-054], his pruning a quiet counterpoint to the Campions' tangled relationships.

## Relationships

- **The Campion Family** — Caesar serves as gardener and occasional coachman, his work sustaining the property's beauty and the family's mobility.
- **Albert** — Fellow outdoor servant; they share the grounds work at The Maples, their labors complementary [007-055].
- **Martha** — Indoor servant who joins the grape-house gathering when Victor comes home [007-055].
- **Mamma Campion** — Gives Caesar direction in the garden, particularly after the pony's death [005-002].

## Significance

Caesar, like Albert and Martha, belongs to the novel's stratum of domestic labor — the servants whose steady work forms the invisible infrastructure of the Campions' gentility. Parrish renders him through physical detail: the grape-house's "moist and warm and fragrant" air, the "old broken chair" he stands on, the scissors in his hand [007-054]. These sensory specifics do more than establish atmosphere; they make Caesar's labor tactile, present, real. In a novel preoccupied with the emotional weather of its white protagonists, these glimpses of Black working life are necessarily partial — but their partiality is itself historically significant, a record of what was seen and what was overlooked in the domestic arrangements of the early twentieth-century American household.
