## Overview

Cousin Sam is a peripheral but persistent member of the Campion family circle in *The Perennial Bachelor*. He appears at family dinners, brings gifts of fruit, talks fishing with Victor and Uncle Willie, and in the novel's later chapters provides a quiet mirror for Victor's own aging and decline. His flaws — particularly his drinking — are noted with the frankness that characterizes Parrish's treatment of family shortcomings [016-072], yet he is drawn with an affection that deepens as the novel progresses.

## Role in the Novel

Cousin Sam enters the novel in its opening pages, arriving for dinner alongside Aunt Priscilla and Cousin Lizzie [001-025]. He is immediately established as a vigorous, opinionated presence: at a family gathering in Chapter 3, "Cousin Sam shouted" his assessment of Victor's temperament — "'nervous as a witch!'" [003-046] — capturing in a phrase the family's frank, unsentimental talk about one another.

His gifts punctuate the domestic scenes: "the black grapes Cousin Sam brought, with a queer bright beetle on them" [015-056] are a small sensory detail that lingers. But darker notes accumulate. The family acknowledges that "Cousin Sam drinks dreadfully" [016-072], and his troubles are spoken of with the same unflinching directness.

In the novel's later chapters, Cousin Sam takes on new weight. His daughter Daisy, mentioned with affection [024-005], connects him to a younger generation. His presence in New York [024-018] and his illness — "Cousin Sam doubled up groaning one day" [025-025] — chart the physical toll of the years. Parrish draws him in these late passages with an economy that is almost tender: "Whatever it was he saw, Cousin Sam saw it too. Old and tired" [024-068].

## Relationships

- **Victor Campion** — The two men share fishing talk and quiet understanding, particularly in the novel's middle and later chapters [014-060], [024-068].
- **Daisy** — His daughter, whose youth and vitality contrast with Sam's decline [024-005].
- **Cousin Lizzie Blow** — Fellow relative and co-conspirator in family commentary; they attend dinners together [001-025].
- **Uncle Willie** — Part of the trio of older male relatives who talk fishing with Victor [014-060].

## Significance

Cousin Sam belongs to the novel's rich cast of secondary relatives — the aunts, uncles, and cousins who fill the Campion house for dinners and then recede, their foibles catalogued and accepted as part of the family's emotional furniture. His drinking is a flaw the family names openly but never disowns him for; his illness is met with concern rather than judgment. In this, he exemplifies one of the novel's quiet moral arguments: that family love consists less in admiration than in the simple fact of continued presence. When he and Victor, both "old and tired," see the same thing [024-068], it is a moment of wordless kinship — two men who have lived long enough to recognize in each other what age has taken and what it has left.
