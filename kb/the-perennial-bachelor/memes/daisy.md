# Daisy

## Overview

Daisy is a brief presence in the novel's extensive cast of characters — mentioned in connection with the childhood world of the Campion sisters, part of the social fabric that Parrish renders with precise detail but without narrative emphasis. Her very marginality is characteristic of the novel's method: she exists as a name, a presence, a thread in the tapestry, without ever becoming a subject.

## Role in the Novel

Daisy appears in the chapters depicting the Campion children's early years. She is part of the world of play and social visits that structures the novel's opening sections — one of the many children and neighbors who populate the Campion sisters' expanding consciousness of their social world.

Her function is documentary rather than dramatic. The novel's power derives from its accumulation of such figures — the sense that The Maples exists within a dense network of relationships, that every name represents a life, that the Campion story is only one thread in a larger social fabric that the novel can gesture toward but never fully capture.

## Significance

Daisy matters because the novel's method matters. Parrish's genius is for making the peripheral feel present — for giving every named figure enough specificity to suggest a full life beyond the narrative's boundaries. Daisy is one such figure: mentioned, glimpsed, and then absorbed into the novel's background, her presence a testament to the density of the social world the Campions inhabited and the novel can only partially record.
