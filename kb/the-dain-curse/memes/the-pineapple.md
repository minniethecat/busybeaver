# The Pineapple

"The Pineapple" is the colloquial term used in the novel for the hand grenade thrown into a room in Chapter XVIII, and by extension the scene of this violent attack. The term "pineapple" was common 1920s underworld slang for a fragmentation grenade.

## Overview

Chapter XVIII of *The Dain Curse* opens with the Continental Op and Owen Fitzstephan debating the case, trying to make sense of the tangled chain of events. The chapter's title refers to the grenade attack that occurs during their investigation, demonstrating that the violence surrounding the Dain curse has escalated from stealthy murders to outright explosive assault.

## In the Novel

### Key Scenes

The Op and Fitzstephan spend much of the chapter arguing about the case. The Op insists on methodically turning over the facts: "You've got to sit down to all the facts you can get and turn them over and turn them over till they click" (018-005). Fitzstephan mocks this methodical approach, calling it "the Nick Carter school" of detective work (018-008).

The investigation's rhythm is shattered when an explosive device is thrown into a room. The Op's immediate identification of the weapon is terse: "Pineapple," I said (018-062). The aftermath of the explosion is investigated with forensic precision: the Op notes that the window was closed, there was "no noise as of something being thrown through the glass just before the explosion," and "there's no broken window-glass inside the room." The screen was still over the window, leading the Op to conclude that "the pineapple wasn't chucked in through the window" (018-078).

This forensic detail is critical — it proves the attacker had access to the interior of the building, narrowing the field of suspects and implicating someone close to the investigation.

The incident connects to Dull Point, as the Op later notes: "There's no use thinking Mrs. Collinson chucked the bomb. She's been in bed since we brought her back from Dull Point yesterday. She couldn't have had the bomb planted there because she had no way of knowing that she was going to occupy the room" (018-082).

## Description

The physical setting is described primarily through the forensic details of the bombing scene: a room with a closed window, an intact screen, and no broken glass inside. These details suggest an interior space in a building or house where the Op and others were staying during the investigation. The absence of external entry points for the explosive is the critical physical fact that drives the investigation forward, proving internal access and therefore an inside attacker.
