# Edgar Leggett

Edgar Leggett is the scientist whose diamond theft report sets the novel's investigation
in motion. Born Maurice Pierre de Mayenne in Fecamp, France, he is a fugitive, a
researcher, and a father who dies protecting the daughter he could not save.

## Role in the Novel

Leggett is the novel's primary catalyst. His report of stolen diamonds brings the
Continental Op into the case, and his death in the laboratory sets off the chain of
revelations that expose Alice Dain Leggett's crimes. Though Leggett dies early, the
entire plot revolves around the secrets he carried and the people who pursued him.

## Biography

### Background

Maurice Pierre de Mayenne was born in Fecamp, France, on March 6, 1883. He studied
painting in Paris, where in 1907 he met the orphaned Dain sisters, Alice and Lily. He
married Lily in 1908; their daughter Gabrielle was born in 1909. After Lily was murdered
in 1913, Leggett was convicted of the killing and sent to Devil's Island — though Alice
Dain was the true murderer.

Leggett escaped from Devil's Island, fled through Central America and Mexico, and reached
San Francisco in 1920, where he assumed the name Edgar Leggett. He established himself as
a legitimate scientist, pursuing experiments with color that he had begun as a young
artist in Paris: "developing experiments with color that I had attempted as a young
artist in Paris" (006-030). The jeweler Collinson recognized commercial potential in
Leggett's glasswork and hoped "the same method could be applied to diamonds" (002-028).

### In the Novel

When Alice and Gabrielle appeared on his San Francisco doorstep in August 1923, Leggett
"turned absolutely white" — Mrs. Begg "thought he was going to faint" (003-095). He had
not sent for them; Alice had traced him. Mrs. Begg testified: "he was the most surprised
man she had ever seen when you arrived from New York" (007-023).

Leggett lived in a top-floor laboratory, sleeping in a cubbyhole bedroom: "a man of
simple tastes" who "spent nearly all his time on the top floor" (003-094). His laboratory
contained "an X-ray machine — or something similar," a forge, glassware, and chemical
equipment (001-051). The diamonds were stored in a green steel cabinet, from which they
were apparently stolen, launching the Op's investigation.

When blackmailer Upton arrived demanding payment for his silence about Leggett's past,
Alice handled the negotiation while keeping Leggett in the dark. After Ruppert killed
Upton and demanded money from Leggett, Leggett killed Ruppert in self-defense — "His body
is in the cellar" (006-033). Trapped, Leggett wrote a lengthy confession and shot
himself at his laboratory table. Beside his body the police found "seven unset diamonds
grouped by his head" and a "small black automatic pistol" (006-013).

His final letter assumed responsibility for everything, shielding both Alice and
Gabrielle — though the Op quickly detected that the letter was "simply the best story he
could think of that would cover his wife and daughter" (007-023).

## Relationships

- **[[gabrielle-leggett]]** — daughter, whom he shielded from the truth of her
  mother's murder and whose curse he tried to protect her from (006-030, 008-008)
- **[[alice-dain-leggett]]** — sister-in-law/murderer of his first wife, who traced
  him to San Francisco and married him (007-023)
- **[[lily-dain]]** — first wife, murdered by Alice in Paris in 1913 (007-028)
- **[[continental-op]]** — detective who investigated the diamond theft and exposed
  the truth behind Leggett's death (007-017)
- **[[eric-collinson]]** — son of the jeweler whose interest in Leggett's scientific
  work linked the families (002-028)
- **[[joseph-haldorn]]** — friend of Leggett's who later exploited Gabrielle (009-008)
