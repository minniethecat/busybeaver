# Devil's Island

The French penal colony, formally the Iles du Salut (Salvation Islands), off the coast of French Guiana, where Maurice Pierre de Mayenne (later Edgar Leggett) was sentenced to life imprisonment for the 1913 murder of his wife Lily Dain in Paris.

## Overview

Devil's Island was part of the French penal system in South America, one of the most notorious prison colonies in the world. In the novel, it serves as the point of origin for the Dain family's American trajectory. Maurice Leggett was sentenced there in 1913 and later escaped, eventually making his way to the United States.

## In the Novel

### Key Scenes

Maurice Leggett's confession details his conviction and sentence: he was tried in Paris, found guilty of Lily Dain's murder, and sentenced to life imprisonment on the Iles du Salut (006-026). He later escaped from the penal colony and made his way through Central America and Mexico before arriving in the United States.

The Continental Op reconstructs Leggett's path, noting that Ruppert had traced Leggett's course "from Devil's Island to Mexico City" (006-032). The detective later uses this knowledge to pressure Leggett, asking him pointedly about his escape and his trail from Devil's Island to San Francisco (007-025).

Fitzstephan, in a later analysis of Gabrielle's character, compares her toughness to her father's: "It's the same toughness that carried him through Devil's Island, Central America, and Mexico to the United States" (019-071). This establishes the escape from Devil's Island as a foundational ordeal that shaped the Leggett-Dain family's survival in America.

## Description

The novel provides few direct physical descriptions of Devil's Island itself, treating it primarily as a backstory location. The penal colony is referenced obliquely through the characters' accounts of escape and pursuit. The harshness of the colony is implied through the characterization of Leggett's toughness being forged by his experience there. The geography of Leggett's escape route — Devil's Island to Central America to Mexico to the United States — traces the historical path of escapees from the French Guiana penal system.
