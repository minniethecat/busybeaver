# The Curse Speech

Alice Dain Leggett's curse speech is the most dramatically charged moment in *The Dain Curse*. Standing in the laboratory over her dead husband's body, accused by the Continental Op of murder, Alice turns on her stepdaughter Gabrielle and delivers a venomous, incantatory proclamation of hereditary doom — the speech that gives the novel its title.

## Overview

The curse speech occurs in Chapter 7, immediately after the Op accuses Alice of murdering her husband. In response to the accusation, her demeanor transforms from grief to a terrifying display of hatred. She turns to face Gabrielle and delivers a chanting, rhythmic tirade, declaring that Gabrielle is cursed with the "black soul and rotten blood" of the Dain family, with her mother's blood on her hands, and with a future of perpetual pain and sorrow (007-048).

## Details

### Circumstances

The speech was delivered in the Leggett laboratory, where Edgar Leggett's body still lay at his work table. The room contained eight people: the Op, O'Gar, Pat Reddy, a uniformed policeman, Fitzstephan, Collinson, Gabrielle, and Alice herself (007-046). Alice had just been accused of murdering her husband and forcing him to write the confession letter.

The atmosphere was charged. Alice's eyes, previously sorrowful, now burned with "mad hatred." As the Op described it, "this mad hatred — and she as part of it — seemed the only live thing in the room. The eight of us who looked at and listened to her didn't, for the moment, count: we were simply the audience" (007-046).

### Key Participants

- **Alice Dain Leggett** — The speaker, unleashing years of accumulated hatred upon her stepdaughter (007-048)
- **Gabrielle Leggett** — The target of the curse; she screamed and collapsed in terror (007-050)
- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — His accusation of murder triggered Alice's outburst (007-021)
- **Eric Collinson** — Tried to stop Alice, gasping "Make her stop" (007-049)
- **Owen Fitzstephan** — Silent observer; later revealed as the true manipulator behind the entire scene (023-010)

### Sequence of Events

Alice turned from the Op, flinging an arm toward Gabrielle. Her voice became throaty and vibrant, with "savage triumph in it," her words separated into groups by brief pauses so that she seemed to be chanting (007-047).

The curse itself was a layered pronouncement. First, inheritance: Gabrielle was cursed with the "same black soul and rotten blood" shared by all the Dains. Second, original sin: she was cursed with her mother Lily's blood on her hands from babyhood — the Paris murder. Third, personal affliction: the "twisted mind and the need for drugs" that Alice claimed to have given her. Fourth, doom: her life would be "all pain and sorrow" and her death "double pain and double sorrow" (007-048).

Eric Collinson gasped, "Stop! Make her stop!" (007-049). Gabrielle, both hands pressed to her ears, her face twisted with terror, screamed once — horribly — and fell forward out of her chair (007-050). In the momentary chaos as everyone looked at the fallen girl, Alice drew a pistol and began backing toward the door (007-052).

## Dramatic Function

The curse speech serves multiple narrative purposes. It externalizes the psychological burden that has been placed on Gabrielle throughout her life — the belief that she carries an inherited taint, that she is somehow responsible for her mother's death, that her suffering is predestined. It also reveals Alice's true character: not a grieving widow but a woman consumed by hatred. Finally, it provides Fitzstephan with the central metaphor he later attempts to exploit as a legal defense — the idea that the Dain blood itself is criminally insane, and that as a Dain by descent, he cannot be held responsible (022-090, 022-094).
