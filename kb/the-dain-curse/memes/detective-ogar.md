# Detective O'Gar

Detective-Sergeant O'Gar is the San Francisco Police Department homicide detective who
works alongside the Continental Op throughout the Leggett investigation. A burly,
experienced professional, he represents the official law enforcement apparatus that the
Op must navigate, cooperate with, and sometimes outmaneuver.

## Role in the Novel

O'Gar serves as the novel's institutional anchor. While the Op operates in the gray
zones of private investigation, O'Gar represents the weight and procedure of the police
department. He is competent, cooperative, and professional — a foil, not an antagonist.
His presence validates the Op's work while also constraining it: the Op shares
information with O'Gar, but also withholds some, knowing that police procedure and
private investigation operate on different timelines.

## Biography

### Background

O'Gar is "a burly, stolid man of fifty, who wore wide-brimmed black hats of the
movie-sheriff sort. There was a lot of sense in his hard bullet-head, and he was
comfortable to work with" (002-054). He works with a younger partner, Pat Reddy, "a big,
jovial youngster, with almost brains enough to make up for his lack of experience"
(002-059).

### In the Novel

O'Gar first appears when the Op seeks him out at the Hall of Justice: "See O'Gar"
(002-036). The Op is surprised to find a homicide detective involved in what he thought
was a simple diamond theft, but the discovery of a dead man in Golden Gate Avenue — the
apparent burglar — has brought the homicide detail into the case.

At the crime scene, O'Gar is methodical and undramatic: "Looks like him and another guy
glaumed the ice, and then the other guy decided to take a one-way split" (002-058). He
shares evidence freely with the Op, including the empty diamond envelopes and the
description of the "tall guy with a long nose" who escaped down the fire escape.

O'Gar is present at Leggett's suicide scene, standing with Reddy by the window reading
Leggett's confession when the Op arrives (006-012). He hands the Op part of the
confession to read, and cooperates when the Op suggests reading it aloud in front of
everyone (006-020).

When Alice Leggett draws a gun in the laboratory, O'Gar blames himself for the lapse:
"Pat Reddy was young at manhunting, but O'Gar and I should have known better than to
stop watching Mrs. Leggett even for a half-second" (007-051). Alice is killed in the
ensuing struggle.

O'Gar also handles the arrest of Rhino Tingley — "That dinge of yours — Rhino Tingley —
was picked up in a hock-shop last night trying to unload some jewelry" (005-005) — and
coordinates with the New York police on the backgrounds of Upton and Ruppert (004-019).

## Relationships

- **[[continental-op]]** — the private detective with whom he maintains a productive,
  if sometimes guarded, working relationship (002-054)
- **Pat Reddy** — his younger partner, experienced but still learning (002-059)
- **[[alice-dain-leggett]]** — the suspect he failed to watch closely enough,
  allowing her to draw a hidden gun (007-051)
