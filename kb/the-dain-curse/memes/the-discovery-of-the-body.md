# The Discovery of the Body

The discovery of a murdered man's body in a shabby apartment on Golden Gate Avenue marks the first homicide in the Leggett diamond theft case. The dead man is identified as Upton, a blackmailer who had been extorting Edgar Leggett, and his death transforms a routine theft investigation into a murder case.

## Overview

While the Continental Op is investigating the diamond theft, the police summon the Leggett family to a Golden Gate Avenue apartment to identify a dead man. The Op arrives to find the homicide detail already on the scene: O'Gar and Pat Reddy are examining the body of a man shot twice, with bullet holes over his navel and high on the left side of his chest (002-055). The diamond envelopes are found in the apartment, but the diamonds themselves are gone (002-058).

## Details

### Circumstances

The Op learned of the body when he went to the Hall of Justice and was told that the Leggetts had been called to identify a man in Golden Gate Avenue. Leggett had not gone, saying he had not seen the supposed burglar (002-039). The location was a small apartment house; a uniformed officer stood at the door, and inside, in a shabbily furnished room, a dead man lay on a wall bed (002-051).

The body was that of a man of about forty, with a heavy pale face, short gray-touched hair, a scrubby dark mustache, and stocky arms and legs. He had been shot twice: once just over the navel, and once high on the left side of his chest (002-055).

### Key Participants

- **Upton** — The victim; a blackmailer who had demanded money from Edgar Leggett to keep his past secret (006-031)
- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — Investigated the body and connected it to the Leggett case (002-051)
- **O'Gar** — Homicide detective-sergeant leading the investigation (002-056)
- **Pat Reddy** — O'Gar's partner, guarding the scene (002-051)
- **Edgar Leggett** — Declined to identify the body, though he knew Upton well (002-039)

### Sequence of Events

The Op examined the body and the scene. O'Gar briefed him: the diamond envelopes were present but empty — "Looks like him and another guy glaumed the ice, and then the other guy decided to take a one-way split." The other man had apparently gone down the fire escape with the diamonds (002-058).

The dead man was identified as Upton. Further investigation revealed that Upton had a criminal record — he and a man named Harry Ruppert had been convicted in 1923 for trying to fix a jury (004-005). Upton had traced Leggett's true identity and had been blackmailing him. Late one Saturday night, Upton had called on Leggett and demanded money as the price of silence. Leggett, having no cash, put him off until Tuesday, when he gave Upton the diamonds as partial payment (006-031).

## Investigation

The Op's investigation of Upton led to a broader criminal network. Upton had been working with — or against — Harry Ruppert, who had "a grudge of his own to settle." Ruppert had traced Leggett's path from Devil's Island to Mexico City and had also learned the Leggett secret, either from Upton or by spying on him (006-032).

The Op sent a wire to the New York branch for more information on Upton and Ruppert, and began investigating their connections to Leggett (004-008). He learned from Mrs. Priestly and other neighbors that a man matching Ruppert's description had been seen near the Leggett house (005-004).

Ruppert soon turned up dead himself, killed in the Leggett kitchen, and Leggett's confession letter later claimed that Leggett had killed him (006-033). The Op, however, later determined that Fitzstephan was responsible for much of the violence, manipulating all parties for his own ends.

## Aftermath

The discovery of Upton's body transformed the diamond theft case into a homicide investigation. It brought O'Gar and the police homicide detail into what had been a private insurance matter, and it began the chain of deaths that would claim Ruppert, Leggett, Alice, Riese, Collinson, and others before the case was finally resolved. The body on Golden Gate Avenue was the first visible sign that the diamond theft was connected to something far more deadly than a simple burglary.
