# The Paris Murder 1913

The murder of Lily Dain in Paris on June 6, 1913, by her husband Maurice Pierre de Mayenne is the foundational crime of *The Dain Curse*'s backstory. This event — and its contested account — creates the family history of violence that becomes the "Dain curse" mythology, shaping the identities and fates of all the principal characters.

## Overview

According to the confession letter written by Edgar Leggett (Maurice de Mayenne) shortly before his death, he murdered his wife Lily Dain in their Paris home on June 6, 1913 (006-026). His stated motive was that he had fallen in love with Lily's sister Alice, and Lily had refused to grant him a divorce. He killed Lily, then fled with Alice and the four-year-old Gabrielle to London, where he was soon arrested and returned to Paris for trial. He was convicted and sentenced to life imprisonment on the Iles du Salut — Devil's Island (006-026).

## Details

### Circumstances

Maurice de Mayenne was born in Fecamp, France, in 1883. He went to Paris in 1903 to study painting and there met Alice and Lily Dain, orphan daughters of a Franco-English family (006-024). He married Lily, but shortly afterward discovered he had made "a most horrible mistake" — it was Alice, not Lily, whom he truly loved. He kept this secret until the child Gabrielle was nearly five years old, then told his wife and asked for a divorce. Lily refused (006-025).

On June 6, 1913, de Mayenne murdered Lily. His letter claimed he acted alone, and that Alice had no part in the murder and no knowledge of it until afterward. He fled with Alice and the four-year-old Gabrielle to London, was arrested, tried in Paris, found guilty, and sentenced to life imprisonment on Devil's Island (006-026).

### Key Participants

- **Lily Dain** — The victim, murdered in her Paris home (006-026)
- **Maurice de Mayenne (Edgar Leggett)** — Lily's husband and confessed murderer (006-024)
- **Alice Dain (later Alice Leggett)** — Lily's sister, with whom de Mayenne was in love; her actual role in the murder is disputed (023-029)
- **Gabrielle Dain (later Gabrielle Leggett)** — Lily's four-year-old daughter, present in the house at the time of the murder (006-026)

### Sequence of Events

De Mayenne's confession described a crime of passion: having been rejected in his request for a divorce, he killed his wife and fled with her sister and child. He was captured, tried, and sentenced to the notorious Devil's Island penal colony in French Guiana.

In 1918, de Mayenne escaped from Devil's Island with a fellow convict named Jacques Labaud on a crude raft. Labaud died of starvation and exposure during the voyage (006-027). De Mayenne made his way to Venezuela, assumed the name Walter Martin, and became involved in a mining company fraud. When his co-conspirator killed the company manager, de Mayenne fled again (006-028-029).

In 1920, he arrived in San Francisco under yet another name — Edgar Leggett — and began building a new life. He developed experiments with coloring glass and eventually made contact with Alice and Gabrielle, who had moved to San Francisco as well. In 1923, believing his new identity was secure, he married Alice (006-030).

## Disputed Accounts

The truth of the Paris murder is contested. Alice Leggett, in her curse speech, claimed that the four-year-old Gabrielle had killed her own mother, taking a pistol from a drawer while Lily slept (007-035). According to Alice, Maurice covered up the child's role to protect her.

Owen Fitzstephan, in his later account, said he did not believe Alice's version. He thought Alice herself had done the killing and had lied to hurt Gabrielle with the accusation. "Everybody took it for granted that he was right — everybody, including Gabrielle — though he didn't have any more reason for his belief than the rest of them had" (023-029).

Alice had been acquitted of any role in her sister's murder by the French courts, and under French law could not be tried again regardless of what she later confessed (008-022).

## Aftermath

The Paris murder is the origin point for the "Dain curse" — the belief that the Dain family carries a hereditary taint of violence, madness, and moral corruption. This mythology is invoked repeatedly throughout the novel: by Alice in her curse speech (007-048), by Fitzstephan as a literary conceit, and ultimately by Fitzstephan himself as a potential legal defense, claiming that as a Dain by blood, he was not criminally responsible for his actions (022-090, 022-094).

The murder also set in motion the chain of false identities and criminal evasions that would eventually bring Upton to San Francisco as a blackmailer, triggering the diamond theft and the entire sequence of violent events in the novel (006-031).
