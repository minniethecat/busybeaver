# The Diamonds

Eight unmounted diamonds, belonging to the firm of Halstead and Beauchamp and
entrusted to Edgar Leggett for experimental work on coloring processes, whose theft
from a green-painted steel cabinet in the Leggett home triggers the Continental Op's
involvement in the Leggett case.

## Overview

The diamonds are described through the novel as unset, loose stones kept in small
manila envelopes, each marked with a number and the weight of the stone written in
pencil (001-036). The stone first discovered by the Op is "not more than a quarter of
a carat in weight, and unmounted" (001-001). Leggett describes them collectively:
"The largest weighed only a trifle more than half a carat, some of the others only a
quarter, and except for two they were all of poor color" (001-019).

## Narrative Role

### Plot Significance

The diamonds function as the novel's MacGuffin. Their alleged theft sets the
investigation in motion: the Op is employed by the Continental Detective Agency on
behalf of the insurance company to recover them. Leggett claims they were stolen from
his cabinet overnight (001-027), but the Op's discovery of a single diamond in the
grass (001-001) leads him to suspect the theft was staged — the stone was deliberately
planted as misdirection (001-054). The diamonds are recovered by the police near the
novel's conclusion (008-025), allowing the agency to close its file, though by that
point the human cost has far exceeded the value of the stones.

### Associated Characters

- **[[Edgar Leggett]]** — entrusted with the diamonds for experimental color-work; invented the theft as cover for giving the stones to Upton as blackmail payment (006-031)
- **[[The Continental Op]]** — assigned to recover the diamonds for the insurance company; discovers one planted in the lawn (001-001)
- **[[Owen Fitzstephan]]** — introduced Leggett's glass-coloring work to Halstead, setting up the diamond connection (002-028)

- **001-054**: The Op confronts the Leggetts with the planted diamond
- **001-068**: The Op and Leggett search the lawn for more diamonds
- **002-028**: Halstead explains how Leggett came to possess the diamonds for experimental work
- **006-013**: Seven diamonds are found grouped by Leggett's head at the scene of his apparent suicide
- **008-025**: The recovery of the diamonds by the police allows the Continental Detective Agency to close its file
