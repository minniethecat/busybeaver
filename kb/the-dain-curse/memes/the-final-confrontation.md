# The Final Confrontation

The final confrontation between the Continental Op and Owen Fitzstephan in the county hospital room is the dramatic climax of the investigation. Having assembled the evidence piece by piece, the Op lays out the full case against Fitzstephan, who responds with mock surprise, gradually shifts to ironic admission, and finally reveals his deepest secret: he is a Dain by blood, and intends to use the curse as his legal defense.

## Overview

The confrontation in Fitzstephan's hospital room (022-070 to 022-094) represents the resolution of the novel's central mystery. The Op, having driven over from the county seat, presents a methodical case: Fink passed Fitzstephan the bomb, Fitzstephan engineered Riese's murder, he hired Whidden to kill Collinson, and Aaronia Haldorn's testimony ties him to the Temple. Fitzstephan's responses evolve from theatrical denial to a chilling revelation — "Suppose, my boy, I were to tell you I'm a Dain?" — and a preview of the insanity defense he plans to mount (022-090, 022-094).

## Details

### Circumstances

Fitzstephan is recovering from injuries sustained in the bomb blast at the Cotton house — the bomb that Fink had passed to him, and that was intended to eliminate both the Op and Fitzstephan himself. He is bandaged heavily, with only "one eye and a fraction of a mouth" visible (022-089). Despite his physical condition, his mind is sharp, and he engages the Op in a verbal duel that reveals his intellectual vanity even in defeat.

### Key Participants

- **[[owen-fitzstephan|Owen Fitzstephan]]** — The mastermind, novelist, and secret Dain, confronted with his crimes (022-070)
- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — Methodically presents the evidence and draws out the confession (022-076)
- **[[aaronia-haldorn|Aaronia Haldorn]]** — Her testimony, given before her death, provided key evidence (022-085)
- **Fink** — The Temple mechanic who passed Fitzstephan the bomb, unwittingly exposing their connection (022-076)
- **Harvey Whidden** — Fitzstephan's hired killer, whose kidnapping of Gabrielle exposed the scheme (022-080)

### Sequence of Events

The Op began with the physical evidence. "Here's the first point: Fink handed you that bomb when he shook hands with you. That's the only way it could have got in without my seeing it. His back was to me then" (022-076). This established Fitzstephan's direct connection to the Temple's mechanical operator.

Next came the broader pattern. "You engineered Riese's murder. The others were your accomplices. When Joseph died the blame was put all on him, the supposed madman. That's enough to let the others out, or ought to be. But here you are killing Collinson and planning God knows what else" (022-078).

Fitzstephan deflected: "Better and better. So I killed Collinson?" The Op corrected him: "You had him killed — hired Whidden and then didn't pay him. He kidnapped the girl then, holding her for his money, knowing she was what you wanted" (022-080).

When the Op mentioned Aaronia's role, Fitzstephan's demeanor changed. "Ah, Aaronia helped tie me? What has she been up to?" His eye narrowed — he was calculating, not denying (022-085).

The Op then delivered his summation: "And so ends the Great Dain Curse" (022-088). This was the pivot point. Fitzstephan laughed and made his revelation: "Suppose, my boy, I were to tell you I'm a Dain?" He explained his blood connection: "My mother and Gabrielle's maternal grandfather were brother and sister" (022-090, 022-092).

Fitzstephan laid out his planned defense: he would "insist on the curse," presenting a "most remarkable defense, a circus" that would use his literary works as evidence of inherited degeneracy, and would argue that as a Dain, he was not legally responsible — "I shall wave my madness in the jury's face and they'll call me mad" (022-094).

## The Confession's Full Meaning

The Op's Chapter 23 narrative filled in what Fitzstephan only hinted at. The Dain blood connection meant that Fitzstephan was family to Gabrielle Leggett — a cousin by blood. His obsession with her was not merely predatory but carried a dynastic, incestuous dimension. The "curse" he planned to use as a defense was the same mythology he had cultivated through Alice's speech and his own literary posturing, now turned to his legal advantage.

Fitzstephan's intellectual vanity was his undoing. He could not resist the theatrical gesture — revealing his Dain ancestry, previewing his courtroom strategy — even when silence would have served him better. The Op's deadpan response to his dramatic revelation — "Huh?" — and "I'll be damned" — punctured Fitzstephan's self-dramatization and underscored the gap between his literary sensibility and the brutal reality of his crimes (022-091, 022-093).

## Aftermath

Fitzstephan's confession did not result in his immediate arrest — he was still hospitalized — but it completed the investigation. The full truth, laid out in Chapter 23, confirmed everything the Op had presented in the hospital room and more. Fitzstephan's scheme, born of obsession with Alice Dain and transferred to her niece Gabrielle, had claimed at least six lives across two continents and three decades.

The novel closes with the Op reflecting on the case's resolution. Fitzstephan's "remarkable defense" was never needed: the evidence, including his confession to replacing Gabrielle's morphine with powdered sugar, was damning regardless of his Dain ancestry (022-112).
