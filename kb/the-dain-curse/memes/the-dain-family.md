# The Dain Family

The Dain family serves as the novel's central social unit of destruction — a kinship group whose internal bonds produce murder, psychological abuse, and the transmission of what its members call "the Dain curse" across two generations. This page examines the family as an organization: its members, their relationships, and how the family structure functions as a mechanism of harm, distinct from [[the-dain-curse]], which addresses the curse as a thematic concept.

## Overview

The Dain family consists of two British sisters, Alice and Lily Dain, "orphan daughters of a British naval officer" (006-024), and their entanglement with Maurice Leggett (also known as Maurice Pierre de Mayenne). Maurice marries Lily in Paris in 1908; their daughter Gabrielle is born in 1909. But Maurice is in love with Alice, and when he asks Lily for a divorce, she refuses. Alice then arranges Lily's murder, frames Maurice, and raises Gabrielle as her own — all while cultivating the belief in a hereditary curse that she uses to control the girl.

The family reconstitutes in San Francisco in 1923, when Alice and Gabrielle track down Edgar Leggett (Maurice's new identity) and Alice marries him. As the housekeeper Mrs. Begg recalls: "With the Dains there, and with Mrs. Dain's ability as a housekeeper, there was no longer any place for Mrs. Begg" (003-096).

## Key Members

- **[[alice-dain-leggett]]** — matriarch of destruction, architect of her sister's murder, Gabrielle's psychological destroyer
- **Lily Dain** — Alice's sister, Maurice's first wife, murdered in Paris in 1913
- **[[gabrielle-leggett]]** — Lily's daughter, raised by Alice, the inheritor of the family's trauma and the belief in the curse
- **[[edgar-leggett]]** / Maurice Pierre de Mayenne — married to Lily, then Alice, father of Gabrielle
- **[[owen-fitzstephan]]** — Alice Dain's cousin, who "had known Alice Dain, his cousin, in New York" (023-004)

## In the Novel

### Activities

The Dain family's primary activity is destroying its own members. Alice murders Lily. She raises Gabrielle in a state of psychological imprisonment, erasing the girl's memory of the murder and addicting her to morphine. When Gabrielle is five, Alice extracts "the last trace of memory" from the child's mind, achieving "a child of, one might say, limited mentality" (007-037). She later marries Edgar Leggett under false pretenses, maintaining control over him through shared secrets until she arranges his death.

Alice's explicit ideology is the Dain curse itself: "you're cursed with the same black soul and rotten blood that she and I and all the Dains have had" (007-048). She weaponizes this belief to make Gabrielle complicit in her own destruction.

### The Dain Household in San Francisco

The family reconstitutes physically in San Francisco in 1923 when Alice and Gabrielle arrive at Leggett's home. Mrs. Begg, the former housekeeper, describes the scene: Leggett "turned absolutely white, and she thought he was going to fall down, he shook that bad" (003-095). Within a week, a marriage license had been issued "to Edgar Leggett and Alice Dain" (003-096), reconstituting the family unit that would produce the novel's cascade of murders.

### The Curse as Family Structure

The Dain family exemplifies the novel's argument that a "curse" is not supernatural but social — a pattern of abuse and violence passed through family relationships. Alice creates the curse through action (murder, psychological manipulation, drug addiction), mythologizes it through language ("the cursed Dain blood"), and transmits it through the ordinary channels of family life. At the novel's conclusion, the true murderer's plan to claim "the cursed Dain blood" as a legal defense (022-094) reveals how completely the family's self-destruction has become a weapon available to anyone who can claim kinship.
