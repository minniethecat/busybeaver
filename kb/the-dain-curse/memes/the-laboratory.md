# The Laboratory

Edgar Leggett's personal laboratory occupied nearly the entire third floor of the Leggett house in San Francisco. It served multiple roles: Leggett's workplace for color and light experiments, the storage site for the eight diamonds, and the setting of his wife Alice's dramatic confession.

## Overview

The laboratory was a large, well-equipped workspace where Leggett, formerly a painter in Paris, conducted experiments with color using X-ray equipment and chemical apparatus. It also doubled as the secure storage for the valuable Halstead and Beauchamp diamonds. The laboratory thus combines Leggett's scientific ambition, his criminal past, and the domestic tensions that ultimately destroy his family.

## In the Novel

### Key Scenes

The laboratory is first described when the Continental Op is taken there to inspect the scene of the diamond theft. It is a large room covering nearly all of the third floor, with charts hung between windows on whitewashed walls and an uncovered wooden floor. The equipment includes an X-ray machine, four or five smaller machines, a forge, a wide sink, a large zinc table, and smaller porcelain work surfaces (001-051).

The diamonds were normally left lying around in the open within the laboratory, but for several days before the theft, Leggett had begun locking them in a cabinet following his "last unsuccessful experiment" (001-023). This change in routine is a key detail in the investigation, suggesting that Leggett anticipated trouble.

The laboratory also serves as a location for Fitzstephan's reconnaissance. He proposes visiting the Leggett house "to borrow a book," knowing Leggett would be working in the laboratory and would refuse to be disturbed, giving Fitzstephan an opportunity to question Alice alone (004-045).

In the novel's climax, Alice Leggett reveals that her husband "guided by him, Alice completely demolished the Leggett household, thinking, until the very last, when he pursued her after giving her the pistol in the laboratory" (023-010). This reference to the pistol in the laboratory connects Leggett's workspace to the chain of violence that engulfs the family.

## Description

The laboratory's physical description suggests a hybrid space: part artist's studio, part scientific workshop. The X-ray machine coexists with a forge, chemical sinks, zinc and porcelain tables. The whitewashed walls and uncovered wooden floor give it a utilitarian character. The room's size — covering nearly the entire third story — indicates the primacy of Leggett's work in his life and the marginalization of domestic space in the household.
