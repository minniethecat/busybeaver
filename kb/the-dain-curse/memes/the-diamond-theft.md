# The Diamond Theft

The theft of seven unset diamonds from Edgar Leggett's locked laboratory cabinet, discovered on the morning the Continental Op first arrives at the Leggett household, sets the entire plot of *The Dain Curse* in motion.

## Overview

The diamond theft is the inciting incident of the novel. The Continental Op, employed by the insurance company that covered the diamonds, arrives at the Leggett residence to investigate. Before he even rings the bell, he finds a small unmounted diamond lying in the grass near the front walk (001-001). Inside, he learns that the diamonds had been stored in a locked steel cabinet in Leggett's laboratory, and that the cabinet drawer had been forced open with a jimmy or chisel (001-052).

## Details

### Circumstances

The theft occurred overnight. Mrs. Leggett reported that she came downstairs in the morning to find the front door ajar. She woke her husband, and together they searched the house and discovered the diamonds missing (001-028). The kitchen door showed marks of forced entry similar to those on the cabinet, suggesting the burglar entered through the back and exited through the front (001-053). Nothing else in the house was taken.

Edgar Leggett had been using the diamonds experimentally, attempting to adapt his glass-coloring techniques to improve off-color stones (001-019). The diamonds belonged to Halstead and Beauchamp, who had provided them on the understanding that Leggett's process might enhance their value (001-018). For several days before the theft, Leggett had begun locking the diamonds in the cabinet after his last unsuccessful experiment (001-023).

### Key Participants

- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — Insurance company investigator who discovers a diamond in the grass and begins the investigation (001-001)
- **Edgar Leggett** — Scientist and victim of the theft; had been using the diamonds for color-enhancement experiments (001-019)
- **Mrs. Alice Leggett** — First to discover the open front door and missing diamonds (001-028)
- **Gabrielle Leggett** — Daughter who had seen a suspicious man near the house on a previous night (001-032)
- **Minnie Hershey** — The Leggetts' mulatto servant, initially suspected of involvement (001-050)

### Sequence of Events

The Op arrived at the Leggett house and found a diamond in the grass before entering (001-001). Once inside, Mrs. Leggett explained the discovery: an open front door, a forced cabinet drawer, and missing diamonds (001-028). She also reported that her daughter Gabrielle had seen a man loitering near the house late one night (001-032).

The Op examined the crime scene. The cabinet drawer had been pried open with a tool, and the kitchen door showed similar marks (001-052, 001-053). He found it suspicious that the burglar knew exactly which drawer to target, having wasted no time on the other drawers (001-060). The Op showed the Leggetts the diamond he had found in the grass, but Leggett was uncertain whether it was one of the stolen stones (001-054).

Minnie Hershey, the household servant, was questioned. She tearfully protested her innocence and resigned on the spot, refusing to work where she was suspected of theft (001-069). The Op and Leggett searched the lawn for more diamonds but found none (001-072).

## Investigation

The Op immediately suspected an inside job. He noted that the burglar had known exactly which drawer held the diamonds, suggesting inside knowledge of the cabinet's contents (001-060). The diamond found in the grass appeared to have been planted there deliberately to misdirect the investigation (001-060).

The Op began tracing Upton, a man who had been seen near the house. He learned that Upton had a criminal record and had recently arrived in San Francisco. Meanwhile, Gabrielle Leggett identified Eric Collinson as the man who had driven her home on the night she saw the suspicious figure, and suggested he might have been the loiterer (001-047).

## Aftermath

The diamond theft set off a chain of increasingly violent events. The Op's investigation into Upton led to the discovery of Upton's body in a Golden Gate Avenue apartment, shot to death (002-051). The diamonds themselves were not recovered until after Edgar Leggett's death, when seven unset stones were found grouped by his head on his laboratory table, with a suicide letter confessing to a complex criminal past (006-013). The eventual recovery of the diamonds allowed the Continental Detective Agency to close the case (008-025).
