# The Eight Diamonds

The theft of eight unmounted diamonds from Edgar Leggett's home serves as the novel's
inciting incident. While the diamonds themselves are ultimately a relatively minor
element in the larger conspiracy, they function as the narrative's MacGuffin — the
object that brings the Continental Op into the Leggett household and sets the
investigation in motion.

## Overview

The novel opens with the Op finding a single diamond in the grass outside the Leggett
home: "It was a diamond all right, shining in the grass half a dozen feet from the
blue brick walk" (001-001). This discovery leads to his involvement in what initially
appears to be a simple burglary case but quickly expands into a complex web of murder,
drug addiction, religious cult activity, and family tragedy.

## In the Novel

### The Theft

The diamonds belonged to Halstead and Beauchamp, who had given them to Edgar Leggett
for experimental purposes. Leggett, a scientist working on methods of coloring glass,
hoped to adapt his process to diamonds — "improving off-color stones, removing
yellowish and brownish tinges" (001-019). The diamonds were stolen from a locked
green steel cabinet in his home, apparently by a burglar who entered through the
kitchen door.

### The Planted Diamond

The Op's discovery of a single diamond in the lawn — "not more than a quarter of a
carat in weight, and unmounted" (001-001) — becomes a crucial clue. The diamond was
deliberately planted to misdirect the investigation, and the Op recognizes this: he
suspects the theft was an inside job rather than a random burglary.

### Beyond the Diamonds

Owen Fitzstephan dismisses the diamond case as trivial: "I've been thinking of Leggett
in terms of Dumas, and you bring me a piece of gimcrackery out of O. Henry"
(003-032). His literary sensibilities are offended by the mundane nature of the crime,
but the Op recognizes that the diamond theft is merely the surface of a much darker
story. The recovery of the diamonds by the police later in the novel allows the
Continental Detective Agency to close its file, but the Op's involvement has already
gone far beyond the original brief.

### Narrative Function

The diamonds serve as a classic detective-fiction MacGuffin — an object that drives
the plot through its theft and recovery but whose intrinsic value is less important
than the human drama it exposes. By the novel's end, the diamonds are barely
remembered, having been overshadowed by the murders, the cult, and the curse.

## Analysis

Hammett uses the diamond theft as a deliberately understated entry point into a story
of Gothic proportions. The contrast between the mundane burglary investigation and the
lurid family drama it uncovers is characteristic of the hard-boiled tradition: the
detective enters through the service door and finds himself in a nightmare. The
diamonds' eventual return to their owners — noted almost in passing — reinforces the
Op's professional detachment: the case is closed, regardless of what else has happened.
