# The Murder of Edgar Leggett

Edgar Leggett is found shot dead at his laboratory table, apparently a suicide, with a
[[the-dagger|small black automatic pistol]] near his hand, [[the-diamonds|seven unset
diamonds]] grouped by his head, and a long confession letter revealing his true identity
as Maurice de Mayenne — a French convict who escaped from
[[devils-island|Devil's Island]]. The [[continental-op|Continental Op]] immediately
suspects murder, accusing [[alice-dain-leggett|Alice Leggett]] on the spot. The truth,
revealed only in the final chapter, is more cynical: [[owen-fitzstephan|Owen Fitzstephan]]
killed Leggett himself, then used the death to position himself closer to
[[gabrielle-leggett|Gabrielle Leggett]].

## Overview

Edgar Leggett's death is the first major murder in the Leggett household and the inaugural
crime in Fitzstephan's campaign. The death is elaborately staged: Leggett's body is found
seated at his laboratory table, a pistol near his hand, seven unset diamonds on the table,
and a multi-page confession letter beside him (006-013). The letter reveals that "Edgar
Leggett" was actually Maurice Pierre de Mayenne, a Frenchman who had murdered his first
wife [[lily-dain|Lily Dain]] in Paris in 1913, escaped from Devil's Island, and assumed a
new identity in America (006-024).

## Circumstances

### The Discovery

Fitzstephan arrived at the Leggett house and met the Op at the door with a whisper:
"Leggett's committed suicide" (006-006). In the laboratory, the Op found a scene already
under police observation:

| Person | Position/Role | Detail |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| Edgar Leggett | Victim | Seated at table, head and arms sprawled, shot |
| Mrs. Alice Leggett | Alleged discoverer | Bent forward in chair, sobbing into handkerchief |
| O'Gar & Reddy | Police | Reading the confession letter by window |
| Uniformed officer | Guard | Standing beside the door |
| Gray-faced man | Unknown | Beside zinc table, twiddling eyeglasses |
| Fitzstephan | Arrived "just after the shot" | "Hot and bright" eyes, keenly interested |
| Gabrielle Leggett | Brought in by the Op | Drugged, barely conscious |
| Eric Collinson | Accompanied Gabrielle | Bloody-faced from car accident |

The Op read Leggett's confession letter aloud to the assembled group (006-022). The letter
detailed Leggett's true identity as Maurice de Mayenne, his marriage to Lily Dain, his
murder of Lily in Paris on June 6, 1913, his escape from Devil's Island, and his career of
fraud across Venezuela and Mexico before arriving in San Francisco (006-024–030).

### The Op's Accusation

The Op did not believe the suicide narrative for a moment. He told Mrs. Leggett directly:
"He didn't commit suicide. He was murdered" (007-009). His reasoning was instinctive: the
confession letter read too perfectly as a document designed to clear Alice Leggett of
suspicion. It was "a confession designed to clear Alice," not a suicide note written by a
man about to die.

When pressed, the Op accused Alice of killing Leggett and forcing him to write the
confession letter to shoulder her crimes (007-021). This accusation triggered Alice's
legendary curse speech — her transformation from "Fitzstephan's serene sane soul" into a
hunting cat — and her flight to the stairs where she, too, would be killed moments later.

## The Confession Letter

The letter was "four stiff white sheets covered with very small, precise, and regular
handwriting in black ink" (006-015). It contained:

1. **Identity revelation**: Leggett was actually Maurice Pierre de Mayenne
2. **The Paris murder**: He had killed his first wife Lily Dain in Paris, June 6, 1913
3. **Escape**: He escaped from Devil's Island and assumed a new identity
4. **Fraudulent career**: Venezuela → Mexico → San Francisco, using his glass-coloring
   expertise to pass as a legitimate scientist
5. **The blackmail**: Upton had discovered his identity and demanded payment
6. **The diamonds**: He gave Upton the diamonds as partial blackmail payment
7. **Ruppert's death**: He claimed responsibility for killing Ruppert
8. **Suicide declaration**: He declared he was now ending his own life

## Investigation

The Op's suspicion was based on several inconsistencies:

- The confession letter's phrasing was too careful, too exculpatory of Alice
- Fitzstephan's arrival timing — immediately after the shot — was suspicious
- Mrs. Leggett's composure was theatrical rather than genuinely grief-stricken
- The entire scene felt "staged" — a literary tableau rather than a spontaneous tragedy

The truth came out only in [[fitzstephans-confession|Fitzstephan's confession]]. When
Gabrielle left the house after witnessing Ruppert's murder, she left a note saying she had
gone for good. This broke the arrangement as far as Leggett was concerned. He told Alice
he was through, was going away, and volunteered to write a letter that would take the blame
for everything — a genuinely convenient final act. Fitzstephan, seeing Leggett as an
obstacle to his pursuit of Gabrielle, killed him after the letter was written (023-011).

## Aftermath

Leggett's death had cascading consequences:

- **Gabrielle** was left without a father, her mental state further fractured
- **Alice** was without a husband, and would be killed on the stairs within hours
- The **confession letter** exposed the family's dark past to the police and eventually to
  the newspapers
- The **diamonds** were recovered at the death scene, allowing the
  [[continental-detective-agency|Continental Detective Agency]] to close the original theft
  case (008-025)
- **Fitzstephan**'s success in "getting rid of Leggett, and in escaping detection by
  killing Alice, encouraged him" to continue his murderous plan (023-012)

## Key Participants

- **[[edgar-leggett|Edgar Leggett]]** — The victim, revealed to be Maurice de Mayenne, a
  convicted murderer living under a false identity (006-024)
- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — Immediately doubted the suicide narrative
  and accused Alice Leggett of murder (007-009)
- **[[alice-dain-leggett|Alice Leggett]]** — Edgar's wife, who claimed to have discovered
  the body; her composure collapsed under the Op's accusation, and she was killed on the
  stairs minutes later (007-020)
- **[[owen-fitzstephan|Owen Fitzstephan]]** — Present at the scene; later confessed to
  murdering Leggett himself, having used the letter Leggett volunteered to write as cover
  (023-011)
- **[[gabrielle-leggett|Gabrielle Leggett]]** — Edgar's daughter, brought into the
  laboratory in a drugged state to witness her father's body (006-011)
- **[[eric-collinson|Eric Collinson]]** — Accompanied Gabrielle, his face bloody from the
  car accident on the way
