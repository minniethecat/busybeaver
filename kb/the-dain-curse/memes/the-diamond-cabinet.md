# The Diamond Cabinet

A green-painted steel cabinet with six locking drawers, from which the eight Halstead
and Beauchamp diamonds were allegedly stolen — the physical focal point of the
Leggett burglary investigation and the first crime scene the Continental Op examines.

## Overview

The cabinet is described in detail during the Op's initial investigation of the
Leggett home: "The cabinet the diamonds had been taken from was a green-painted steel
affair with six drawers all locking together. The second drawer from the top — the one
the diamonds had been in — was open. Its edge was dented where a jimmy or chisel had
been forced between it and the frame. The other drawers were still locked. Leggett
said the forcing of the diamond drawer had jammed the locking mechanism so that he
would have to get a mechanic to open the others" (001-052). The back kitchen door
showed matching marks "apparently by the same tool" (001-053).

## Narrative Role

### Plot Significance

The cabinet is the first crime scene element the Op examines, and its physical state
immediately raises questions. The jimmy marks on the drawer and the matching marks on
the kitchen door (001-053) are consistent with a break-in — but the Op's discovery of
a planted diamond in the lawn (001-001) suggests the burglary was staged. Leggett's
explanation that the diamonds had been kept in the cabinet "since my last unsuccessful
experiment" (001-023) — whereas previously they were "left lying around in the
open... always in the laboratory" (001-023) — adds to the Op's suspicion. The cabinet
thus embodies the central mystery of the novel's opening: whether the theft was real
or fabricated by Leggett as part of his scheme.
