# Fitzstephan's Confession

Owen Fitzstephan, recovering in a county hospital from the bomb that was meant to silence him, is confronted by the Continental Op with the full evidence of his crimes. In a remarkable scene, Fitzstephan first denies, then obliquely admits his guilt, and finally reveals that he himself is a Dain — making him, in his own ironic logic, a victim of the same curse he manufactured.

## Overview

Fitzstephan's confession unfolds in two stages. First, in the hospital room, the Op lays out the evidence: Fink handed Fitzstephan the bomb when they shook hands, Fitzstephan engineered Riese's murder through Joseph and Minnie, he hired Whidden to kill Collinson, and he had been pursuing Gabrielle all along. Fitzstephan, with "one visible gray eye" growing narrow, parries each accusation with mock surprise before turning serious and revealing his deepest secret: he is a Dain (022-090).

## Details

### Circumstances

The confrontation takes place in Fitzstephan's room at the county hospital, where he is recovering from the bomb blast at the Cotton house. He is bandaged — "one eye and a fraction of a mouth" visible (022-089). The Op has driven over from Quesada specifically to extract the truth.

The Op had assembled the pieces: Fink's involvement in passing the bomb, Aaronia Haldorn's testimony about Fitzstephan's role in the Temple, Whidden's kidnapping of Gabrielle as payment for the murder Fitzstephan commissioned, and the pattern of deaths that all pointed toward one objective — Fitzstephan's possession of Gabrielle.

### Sequence of Events

The Op began methodically. "Here's the first point," he said: Fink handed Fitzstephan the bomb when they shook hands. Fitzstephan's back was turned to the Op at that moment, and he didn't know what he was being handed, but had to take it to avoid revealing his connection to Fink (022-076).

"You engineered Riese's murder," the Op continued. "The others were your accomplices. When Joseph died the blame was put all on him, the supposed madman. That's enough to let the others out, or ought to be. But here you are killing Collinson and planning God knows what else" (022-078).

Fitzstephan responded with ironic equanimity: "Better and better. So I killed Collinson?" The Op corrected him: "You had him killed — hired Whidden and then didn't pay him" (022-080). When the Op mentioned Aaronia's role in tying him to the crimes, Fitzstephan's eye narrowed — he was "busy with other thoughts behind it" (022-085).

Then came the turn. "And so ends the Great Dain Curse," the Op said (022-088). Fitzstephan laughed and asked: "Suppose, my boy, I were to tell you I'm a Dain?" (022-090). He explained: "My mother and Gabrielle's maternal grandfather were brother and sister" (022-092).

Fitzstephan then outlined his planned defense: he would "insist on the curse," using it to "save my dear neck." He would present a "most remarkable defense, a circus" that would invoke his literary works — *The Pale Egyptian* and *Eighteen Inches* — as evidence of "authorial degeneracy," proving his inherited insanity (022-094).

## The Full Truth (Chapter 23)

The Op's subsequent narrative in Chapter 23 fills in the complete story that Fitzstephan's hospital-room performance had only hinted at. Fitzstephan had been in love with Alice Dain in New York, and they exchanged letters after she moved to San Francisco. He then created the Temple of the Holy Grail, made Aaronia his mistress, and used the cult to approach Gabrielle (023-005, 023-006, 023-007).

When Leggett discovered Fitzstephan's attempts to seduce Gabrielle, a three-way confrontation ended with Fitzstephan banned from the house. His revenge came through Alice: when Upton arrived to blackmail Leggett, Fitzstephan advised Alice to handle it herself, setting in motion the chain of events that destroyed the Leggett household (023-009).

Fitzstephan killed Leggett personally, then Alice, then — through Joseph and Minnie — Riese. Each death "increased her price, her value to him" (023-014). When Eric Collinson married Gabrielle, Fitzstephan hired Whidden to kill him. When Whidden demanded payment, Fitzstephan refused — and Whidden's retaliation by kidnapping Gabrielle finally exposed the entire scheme.

## Aftermath

Fitzstephan's confession — and his claim of Dain blood — did not save him. The Op noted that "nobody but an idiot would have confessed that for two days the folded papers had held powdered sugar instead of the original morphine" (022-112), referring to Fitzstephan's plan to keep Gabrielle addicted and dependent. The full truth, laid out in Chapter 23, made Fitzstephan's guilt inescapable regardless of his Dain ancestry.
