# Halstead and Beauchamp

A diamond dealing firm on Geary Street in San Francisco, Halstead and Beauchamp supplied the eight unset diamonds that Edgar Leggett was using experimentally and that were stolen in the novel's opening theft. The firm plays a small but essential role as the origin point for the story's central MacGuffin.

## Overview

Halstead and Beauchamp occupy a store on Geary Street (002-020) and are managed by Watt Halstead, a "suave, pale, bald, fat man, with tired eyes and a too tight collar" (002-021). The firm's diamonds — unset stones in small manila envelopes, each labeled with a number and weight — are the objects around which the novel's first half revolves. Though Halstead and Beauchamp appear only briefly on stage, their professional relationship with Edgar Leggett connects the diamond theft to Leggett's scientific research and, ultimately, to the deeper criminal history behind the theft.

## Key Members

- **Watt Halstead** — partner in the firm, described as suave and tired (002-021). He identifies Leggett's purchases for the police and provides the Op with Leggett's known address

## In the Novel

### Activities

Five weeks before the novel's opening, Watt Halstead — aware of Leggett's work with glass coloring — became "interested in the possibility of the process being adapted to diamonds" and gave Leggett the stones for experimental use (001-019). The diamonds were "unset, of course, and in small manila envelopes — Halstead and Beauchamp's — each in a separate envelope, with a number and the weight of the stone written in pencil" (001-036). When the Op calls on the firm, Halstead provides Leggett's Nob Hill apartment address from the telephone book (002-032).

After the diamonds are recovered — found on Upton's body — Halstead identifies them, and their recovery closes the Leggett insurance case: "The recovery of Halstead and Beauchamp's diamonds let the Continental Detective Agency out" (008-025). Later, Halstead also helps the police by identifying pieces — "a string of pearls and a topaz brooch" (005-007) — as articles Leggett had purchased at the store for his daughter.

### Narrative Role

Halstead and Beauchamp serve as the novel's gateway to the legitimate commercial world that Leggett's criminal activities intersect with. The firm represents the respectable business sphere — a Geary Street jewelry store — whose property becomes entangled in a web of theft, suicide, and cult conspiracy. Their role is small but precisely drawn, anchoring the diamond plot in a recognizable commercial reality.
