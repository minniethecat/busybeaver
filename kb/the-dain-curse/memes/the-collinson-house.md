# The Collinson House

Eric Collinson's residence, which becomes the site of his murder. This location features in the novel's complex chain of events involving multiple killings, mistaken identities, and the unfolding investigation by the Continental Op.

## Overview

The Collinson house serves as both a character's residence and ultimately a crime scene. As the home of Eric Collinson, Gabrielle Leggett's fiance, it is a locus of the romantic subplot that becomes entangled with the murder investigation. The house gains prominence in the novel's later chapters when tragedy strikes within its walls.

## In the Novel

### Key Scenes

Eric Collinson, the young stockbroker engaged to Gabrielle Leggett, is first introduced as a clean-cut Princeton graduate who works at Spear, Camp and Duffy on Montgomery Street (002-009). His home becomes significant when the investigation turns toward him.

The tragedy at the Collinson house connects to the broader pattern of death that follows the Dain and Leggett families. In the novel's climax, the various threads converge: Fitzstepphan's manipulations, the Haldorns' schemes at the Temple, and the violence that reaches the Collinson home. The reference to "Collinson trouble" in Mrs. Whidden's testimony underscores how the Collinson house became enmeshed in the criminal web (017-024).

## Description

The novel provides limited direct architectural description of the Collinson house. References are oblique, embedded in the investigation rather than in scenic description. The house is contextualized through Eric Collinson's social position: his father was "the lumber Collinson," suggesting family wealth that would be reflected in their San Francisco residence. Eric himself is characterized as belonging to the world of polo, shooting, and flying — pursuits associated with affluent living standards (002-009).
