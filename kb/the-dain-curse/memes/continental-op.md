# The Continental Op

The Continental Op is the unnamed first-person narrator and protagonist of The Dain
Curse. An employee of the Continental Detective Agency's San Francisco office, he is
assigned to investigate the theft of eight diamonds from the Leggett household and
finds himself drawn into a Gothic web of murder, drug addiction, religious fraud, and
family conspiracy.

## Role in the Novel

The Op functions as the novel's epistemological anchor. While other characters operate
through faith, superstition, or self-interest, the Op maintains professional
skepticism and methodological rigor. His investigation systematically dismantles each
supernatural claim — the curse, the visions, the inherited madness — and reveals the
rational crimes beneath. He is not a genius detective but a working professional whose
success comes from persistence and clear thinking.

## Biography

### Background

The Op's personal history is never revealed. He exists as a professional function
rather than a private individual — we do not learn his name, his background, or
anything about his life outside the agency. This anonymity is characteristic of
Hammett's Continental Op stories: the detective is defined entirely by his work.

### In the Novel

The Op enters the case through a routine assignment: investigating the theft of
diamonds from Edgar Leggett's home. He finds a single diamond in the grass outside —
"a diamond all right, shining in the grass half a dozen feet from the blue brick
walk" (001-001) — and recognizes it as a planted clue. From this modest beginning, the
case expands into a series of murders that the Op investigates with characteristic
method.

The Op's most important intellectual contribution is his refusal to accept the curse
narrative. When Fitzstephan argues for the "poetic necessity of the curse" (008-007),
the Op reduces it to "words in an angry woman's mouth" (008-006). This rationalism is
not naivete — he understands that belief in the curse has real effects — but a
professional commitment to factual explanation.

The Op is physically active throughout the novel: he is beaten, shot at, and involved
in multiple violent confrontations. He kills at least one person (Alice Dain Leggett)
in a struggle on the stairs. Despite the violence, he maintains professional composure,
treating physical danger as an occupational hazard.

His closing line — "And so ends the Great Dain Curse" (022-088) — is characteristic:
understated, professional, and slightly ironic. The case is solved; the mystery is
dispelled; the job is done.
