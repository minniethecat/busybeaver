# The Temple Altar

The altar of the Temple of the Holy Grail is the most dramatic crime scene in the novel — a place of ostensible spiritual purity that becomes the setting for a shocking murder. The discovery of Doctor Riese's body on the altar steps transforms the Temple from a fashionable religious institution into a locus of violence.

## Overview

The altar is the focal point of the Temple's chapel. Described as a wide structure of brilliant white, crystal, and silver, it is approached by three steps (010-046). The altar embodies the Temple's aesthetic of stark white purity that masks its darker operations.

## In the Novel

### Key Scenes

The Continental Op and Eric Collinson break into the Temple's chapel at night, using an axe to force their way. When the light falls on the altar, it "glittered and glistened on a wide altar of brilliant white, crystal, and silver" (010-046). On the lowest of the three altar steps, they discover Doctor Riese lying dead on his back, his face composed as if sleeping, his arms straight at his sides, his clothes not rumpled despite four stab wounds to his chest (010-047).

The detective examines the scene with his flashlight. The chapel walls are white, smooth, and unbroken except for two identical doors on opposite sides. The walls are "four straight whitewashed walls, undecorated" (010-067), creating an atmosphere of austere blankness that heightens the horror of the corpse.

When the Op returns to the altar later, the scene has been sanitized: Riese's body is gone, the dagger has vanished, and all blood traces have been cleaned except a faint yellow stain on the white floor (010-100). This erasure of evidence demonstrates the Temple's control over its physical space and the difficulty of proving what occurred there.

In a later scene, the Op finds the altar again illuminated: "The altar was glaring white, crystal, and silver in an immense beam of blue-white light that slanted down from an edge of the roof" (011-066). The theatrical lighting underscores the Temple's use of spectacle and stagecraft to create religious awe.

## Description

The altar dominates the Temple chapel through the contrast between its brilliance and the featureless white walls surrounding it. The use of crystal and silver suggests both wealth and a particular aesthetic of spiritual purity. The three steps create a hierarchical approach to the altar, with Riese's body placed on the lowest step — a detail that emphasizes the desecration of a sacred space. The chapel's architecture, with its two identical doors on opposite walls and its complete lack of decoration, is designed for maximum theatrical effect when the lighting is activated.
