# The Murder of Alice Dain

Alice Dain Leggett, after delivering a venomous curse speech to her stepdaughter Gabrielle, attempts to flee the Leggett house but dies in a struggle on the stairs with the Continental Op. Her death is later revealed to have been orchestrated by Owen Fitzstephan, the true mastermind behind the Dain curse murders.

## Overview

The murder of Alice Leggett occurs in the immediate aftermath of her husband Edgar Leggett's death. Confronted in the laboratory where her husband's body lies, Alice delivers a theatrical, hate-filled proclamation of the Dain curse upon Gabrielle (007-048). She then tries to escape, brandishing a pistol, and is killed during a struggle with the Continental Op on the staircase (007-064). The Op later realizes that Fitzstephan had given Alice the pistol and engineered the confrontation, then killed her to silence her (023-010).

## Details

### Circumstances

The event unfolded in the Leggett house on the same day Edgar Leggett's body was discovered. After the assembled group — including the Op, O'Gar, Reddy, Fitzstephan, Collinson, and Gabrielle — gathered in the laboratory to hear Leggett's confession letter read aloud, Mrs. Leggett was questioned about the circumstances of his death. She claimed to have heard a shot and found her husband dead; she said Fitzstephan had arrived at the doorbell moments later (007-020).

The Op, however, did not believe Leggett had committed suicide. He accused Mrs. Leggett of murder, presenting the evidence that Leggett's letter was not a suicide note but a confession designed to shield her (007-021). At this accusation, Mrs. Leggett's composure shattered, and she launched into the infamous curse speech.

### Key Participants

- **Alice Dain Leggett** — Edgar Leggett's wife, the speaker of the curse, killed on the stairs (007-048)
- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — Accused Alice of murder and struggled with her on the stairs (007-021)
- **Gabrielle Leggett** — Target of the curse speech; screamed and collapsed during Alice's tirade (007-050)
- **Eric Collinson** — Attempted to protect Gabrielle during the confrontation (007-049)
- **Owen Fitzstephan** — Present at the scene; later revealed as Alice's manipulator and actual killer (023-010)

### Sequence of Events

After the Op accused Alice of killing her husband and forcing him to write the confession letter, she transformed. The "mad hatred" that had been behind her eyes now consumed her entirely. She turned to Gabrielle and delivered a chanting, theatrical curse, declaring that Gabrielle was cursed with "the same black soul and rotten blood" as all the Dains, cursed with her mother's blood on her hands, and cursed with a twisted mind and need for drugs (007-048).

Gabrielle, both hands pressed to her ears, screamed horribly and fell forward out of her chair (007-050). In the momentary distraction — everyone looked at the fallen girl — Alice drew a pistol. She backed toward the door, glaring over the black gun at everyone in the room (007-052). The Op signaled to Pat Reddy not to move; the hall and stairs would be a better place to catch her than the crowded room (007-053).

Alice backed over the door sill, made a hissing, spitting sound, and fled down the hall (007-054). The Op pursued her. On the stairs, a struggle ensued. By the time it was over, Alice Leggett was dead (007-064).

## Investigation

The Op initially assumed Alice had died in the struggle with him. But in Chapter 23, Fitzstephan's own account revealed the truth: he had given Alice the pistol in the laboratory and pursued her after she fled. He killed her to prevent her from revealing his role as the mastermind behind the entire series of crimes (023-010). Fitzstephan's success in getting rid of Leggett — and in escaping detection by killing Alice — encouraged him to continue his murderous plan to claim Gabrielle for himself (023-012).

## Aftermath

Alice's death and her curse speech became the central mythology of the case. The "Dain curse" — the idea that the Dain family carried a hereditary taint of madness and violence — was used by various characters, most significantly by Fitzstephan himself, who planned to use it as an insanity defense (022-094). Her accusation that Gabrielle had killed her own mother as a child, though widely disbelieved (including by Fitzstephan, who thought Alice herself had done the killing), cast a lasting shadow over Gabrielle's sense of identity (023-029).
