# San Francisco Police Department

The San Francisco Police Department serves as the official law enforcement presence in the novel, headquartered in the Hall of Justice downtown. Its detectives operate in parallel with and sometimes in competition with the Continental Op, reflecting the uneasy relationship between public and private investigative institutions in Hammett's fiction.

## Overview

The police department's involvement in the novel begins with the Leggett diamond theft — the police investigate the crime scene, "found marks on the kitchen door" (001-027) — and extends through the string of murders that follow. The Op's professional relationship with the department is pragmatic: he shares information when it serves his purposes but maintains the independence of the Continental Detective Agency. The department's homicide detail, led by Detective-Sergeant O'Gar, becomes increasingly important as the case shifts from theft to murder.

## Key Members

- **Detective-Sergeant O'Gar** — a homicide detail detective-sergeant who the Op encounters at multiple crime scenes. The Op initially wonders what he — "a homicide detail detective-sergeant" (002-037) — has to do with the diamond theft, signaling the shift from property crime to murder investigation
- **Pat Reddy** — O'Gar's partner in the homicide detail (002-037)
- **Lieutenant Duff** — officer at the detective bureau in the Hall of Justice (002-035)
- **McCracken** — the San Francisco police department's bomb expert, who examines the fragments after the bomb attack on the Op's apartment (019-024)

## In the Novel

### Activities

The department's involvement spans the full novel. Patrolmen secure crime scenes; detectives at the Hall of Justice handle pawnshop inquiries; O'Gar and Reddy track the escalating murders. When the Leggett household is called to "a place in Golden Gate Avenue to see if they could identify a man" (002-039), the police have already located a body — Upton's — that will reveal the case's deeper dimensions. The department also confirms alibis: telegrams "from the San Francisco police department and the Marin County sheriff's office" (017-008) verify Fitzstephan's story.

### Relationship with the Continental Op

The Op's relationship with the department is characterized by guarded cooperation. He identifies himself as "not a police detective" (001-014), establishing the private-public distinction, but freely uses police resources — checking pawnshop records, consulting detectives, and coordinating investigations. O'Gar appears periodically, functioning as a professional peer whose deference to the Op's expertise reflects the respect the Continental Agency commands even from the official police.
