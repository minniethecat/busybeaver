# Vernon

Vernon is the district attorney of the county where the Leggett-Collinson murders
take place. Described as "sharp-featured, aggressive, and hungry for fame" (015),
he is one of three local law enforcement officials — along with
[[ben-rolly|Sheriff Feeney]] and [[marshal-cotton|Marshal Dick Cotton]] — who
become involved in the investigation. His primary motivation throughout the case
is not justice but self-promotion, turning the investigation into what one
character calls "a contest between Vernon, Feeney, and Cotton to see which can
get his name and picture printed most" (016).

## Role in the Novel

Vernon serves as a satirical portrait of small-county officialdom in Hammett's
California. He is competent enough to recognize the importance of evidence when
it is presented to him, but his judgment is consistently clouded by his rivalry
with the other lawmen. He appears primarily in the middle section of the novel
(chapters XV–XVI), during the investigation of [[eric-collinson|Eric
Collinson]]'s death and the subsequent manhunt for the marshal. The
[[continental-op|Continental Op]] treats him with professional courtesy but
little respect, using Vernon's hunger for information as leverage to stay
involved in the case.

## Biography

### Background

Little is revealed about Vernon's past. He is the district attorney for the
county encompassing Quesada, the small town near which the Leggett house is
located. His position gives him jurisdictional authority over the murder
investigations, though he shares power uneasily with Sheriff Feeney and Marshal
Dick Cotton.

### Physical Appearance

Vernon is described only as "sharp-featured" — a phrase that captures both his
physical appearance and his temperament. When the Op visits him in his hotel
room, Vernon is found reading newspapers, "a pink, green, and white pile on the
bed," and the room is "blue-gray with cigar smoke" (016), suggesting a man who
spends more time monitoring his press coverage than actively investigating.

### In the Novel

Vernon first appears at the scene of Eric Collinson's death, arriving from the
county seat with Sheriff Feeney (015). He immediately becomes one of the
officials who conclude that [[gabrielle-leggett|Gabrielle Collinson]] killed her
husband.

His most significant scenes occur during the night investigation that follows.
When the Op brings him information from [[owen-fitzstephan|Fitzstephan]], Vernon
receives it with theatrical self-importance: "Vernon posed in front of me, hands
in pockets, feet far apart" (016). He then summons Feeney and orders the Op to
repeat the information — establishing his dominance over the sheriff.

During the search that follows, Vernon participates reluctantly in the physical
work, slipping in the mud and needing to be caught by the Op. His impatience
with Cotton's theatrics is evident, but he lacks the authority or will to
override the marshal completely.

Vernon's final significant appearance comes during the confrontation with Mrs.
Cotton, where she begs him not to believe her husband's lies about Harvey
Whidden. Vernon's discomfort — he "looked at his feet and didn't say anything"
(016) — reveals a man who, beneath his aggressive exterior, is fundamentally
weak when faced with genuine human distress.

## Relationships

- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — Vernon views the Op as a useful
  source of information but resents his competence. The Op, in turn, treats
  Vernon with minimal deference, using him as a conduit to stay connected to the
  official investigation.
- **Sheriff Feeney** — Professional rival. The two compete for press attention,
  but Vernon consistently asserts his superiority, treating Feeney as a
  subordinate.
- **Marshal Dick Cotton** — Open antagonist. Vernon repeatedly tries to shut
  down Cotton's theories and investigations, culminating in their confrontation
  after Mrs. Cotton's death.
