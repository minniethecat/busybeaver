# The Recovery of the Diamonds

The seven unset diamonds stolen from Edgar Leggett's laboratory cabinet are recovered at the scene of his death, found grouped by his head on the laboratory table beside his suicide letter. Their recovery allows the Continental Detective Agency to formally close the Leggett investigation.

## Overview

The diamonds, which had belonged to Halstead and Beauchamp and had been lent to Leggett for experimental color-enhancement work, were the initial reason for the Continental Op's involvement in the case. Their theft set the entire investigation in motion. Their recovery — found at the death scene alongside Leggett's body, the confession letter, and the pistol — resolved the insurance company's interest in the matter, though the murder investigation continued (006-013, 008-025).

## Details

### Circumstances

When the Op entered Leggett's laboratory after being told of the suicide, he saw the diamonds immediately: "Passing the table on my way to join them at the window, I saw blood, a small black automatic pistol lying close to one of Leggett's hands, and seven unset diamonds grouped by his head" (006-013).

The diamonds' presence at the death scene was consistent with Leggett's confession letter, which explained that he had given the diamonds to Upton as partial payment of the blackmail demand, and that after Upton's death the diamonds had come into Ruppert's possession. Ruppert, in turn, had tried to blackmail Leggett, and Leggett claimed to have killed Ruppert and recovered the diamonds (006-032, 006-033).

### Key Participants

- **Edgar Leggett** — Had the diamonds at his death scene; his letter explained their journey (006-013)
- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — Observed the diamonds at the crime scene (006-013)
- **Halstead and Beauchamp** — The diamond merchants who owned the stones and insured them (001-018)
- **Upton** — Received the diamonds as blackmail payment; killed by Ruppert (006-031)
- **Harry Ruppert** — Took the diamonds after killing Upton; killed in Leggett's kitchen (006-032)

### Sequence of Events

The diamonds had an eventful journey. Originally lent to Leggett by Halstead and Beauchamp for experimental work on improving off-color stones (001-019), they were stolen from Leggett's locked cabinet in what the Op suspected was an inside job (001-060).

Leggett's confession letter traced their subsequent movements. Upton, who had traced Leggett's criminal past and was blackmailing him, had been given the diamonds as partial payment (006-031). When Upton was killed — by Ruppert, according to Leggett — the diamonds passed to Ruppert, who also began blackmailing Leggett. Leggett claimed to have killed Ruppert in the kitchen and recovered the stones (006-033).

The diamonds were found at Leggett's death scene, and their recovery resolved the insurance aspect of the case. The Continental Detective Agency, having been hired by the insurance company, had no further contractual obligation once the stones were accounted for.

## Aftermath

The recovery of the diamonds allowed the agency to close the case formally. As the Op recorded: "The recovery of Halstead and Beauchamp's diamonds let the Continental Detective Agency out: we wrote 'Discontinued' at the bottom of the Leggett record" (008-025).

However, the Op did not consider the larger case resolved. He recognized that the diamond theft was merely the surface layer of a much deeper pattern of crime. He went "up in the mountains" to rest (008-025), but the murders continued — Doctor Riese, Eric Collinson — and he was drawn back into the investigation, which ultimately led to Fitzstephan.

The diamonds' recovery thus marks the boundary between the insurance investigation (Phase 1 of the novel) and the murder investigation (Phase 2), though the connection between the two — Fitzstephan's hidden role — would not become clear until the final chapters.
