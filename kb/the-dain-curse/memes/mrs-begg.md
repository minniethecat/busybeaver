# Mrs. Begg

Mrs. Begg is Edgar Leggett's former housekeeper, a plainspoken woman whose eyewitness
testimony becomes a key piece of evidence in the Continental Op's reconstruction of the
truth. Her account of Alice and Gabrielle's unannounced arrival in 1923 contradicts
Leggett's written confession and exposes Alice's lies.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Begg appears only briefly — in a single interview in Chapter 3 — but her testimony
is structurally essential. The Op uses her account of Leggett's shocked reaction to his
family's arrival to prove that Leggett's confession letter was fabricated: "He didn't
send for you and his daughter. You traced him here. Mrs. Begg said he was the most
surprised man she had ever seen when you arrived from New York" (007-023). This
contradiction is one of the "half a dozen lies" the Op identifies in the confession.

## Biography

### Background

Mrs. Begg was hired through an employment agency as housekeeper in 1921. Leggett lived
simply: "At first she had a girl to help her, but there wasn't enough work for two, so,
at Mrs. Begg's suggestion, they let the girl go" (003-094). Leggett spent nearly all his
time in his third-floor laboratory and cubbyhole bedroom.

### In the Novel

When the Op tracks her down, Mrs. Begg is working for a family named Freemander in
Berkeley. She is described as "a scrawny, big-boned woman, with not much gray hair
packed close around a bony skull, hard gray eyes, and hard, capable hands. She was sour
and severe, but plainspoken enough to let us talk turkey" (003-091).

Once started, "she told me a great deal, damned near talking me earless" (003-093). Her
key testimony concerns August 1923 — "a rainy morning, she remembered" — when "a woman
and a girl of fifteen, with a lot of suitcases" arrived at the house. She fetched
Leggett from his laboratory. "Never in all her born days had she seen such a surprised
man as he was when he saw them. He turned absolutely white, and she thought he was going
to faint" (003-095).

With Alice Dain's arrival and "Mrs. Dain's ability as a housekeeper, there was no longer
any place for Mrs. Begg." The family "helped her find a new place and gave her a
generous bonus when she left" (003-096). She had kept track of the family since through
"the careful watch she habitually kept on the marriage, death, and birth notices in the
morning papers" (003-096).

She also confirmed that neighbors considered Gabrielle "a bit wild" but that the
Leggetts "seemed to be nice people, but kept to themselves" (002-004).
