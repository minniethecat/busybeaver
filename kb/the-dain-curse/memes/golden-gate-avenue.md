# Golden Gate Avenue

Golden Gate Avenue is a San Francisco street where a critical early scene unfolds: the identification of a dead man whose body is connected to the diamond theft at the Leggett house. This location marks the transition of the case from a simple burglary investigation to a homicide inquiry.

## Overview

The location on Golden Gate Avenue appears to be a police facility or morgue where bodies are taken for identification. The police summon the Leggett women there to identify a man whose body may be connected to the burglary case.

## In the Novel

### Key Scenes

The Continental Op learns of the location when he telephones the Leggett house and is told by Mrs. Begg that "the police called up a little while ago and asked my wife and daughter to come to a place in Golden Gate Avenue to see if they could identify a man there" (002-039). The Op immediately asks for the specific location: "Whereabouts in Golden Gate Avenue?" (002-040).

The discovery of this body fundamentally changes the scope of the investigation. What began as a diamond theft becomes entangled with a homicide. The Op summarizes the situation for Fitzstephan: "I told him all I knew about the Leggetts, the diamonds, and the dead man in Golden Gate Avenue" (003-031). The case rapidly evolves from a property crime to a murder investigation centered on the Leggett household.

## Description

The novel provides limited physical description of the Golden Gate Avenue location itself. It is presented primarily as a functional police site — a place where bodies are held for identification. The fact that it is not the Leggett house but a police-controlled location signals the escalation of the case from a private investigation to a matter involving the homicide department, specifically Detective-Sergeant O'Gar and his partner Pat Reddy.
