# Gabrielle's Suicide Attempt

After finding her husband Eric Collinson's shattered body at the base of the Quesada cliffs, Gabrielle Leggett attempts suicide twice with his pistol — once aiming at her temple, once at her breast — but each time she jerks the weapon away at the last moment, unable to overcome her fear.

## Overview

Gabrielle's suicide attempt is one of the most psychologically revealing moments in the novel. Having sat up all Friday night waiting for Eric to return from his walk, she went looking for him at daylight and found his body where it had fallen from the cliff path. The discovery drove her to the edge of self-destruction, but her survival instinct — which she interpreted as cowardice — prevented her from following through. This scene is recounted by Gabrielle herself after her rescue from Whidden's cave at Dull Point (017-059).

## Details

### Circumstances

After Eric Collinson was pushed from the cliff by Harvey Whidden on Friday night, Gabrielle waited for him through the night. At daylight, frantic at his failure to return, she went to look for him and found him — as the Op had — at the base of the cliff, his body broken on the rocks (017-059).

The shock of finding her husband dead, combined with her long history of psychological trauma and drug dependency, drove Gabrielle to take Eric's pistol and attempt to end her own life.

### Key Participants

- **Gabrielle Leggett (Collinson)** — The grieving widow who attempted suicide (017-059)
- **Eric Collinson** — Her dead husband, whose body she discovered (013-031)
- **[[continental-op|The Continental Op]]** — Rescued her from Whidden and heard her confession (017-058)
- **Harvey Whidden** — Had pushed Eric off the cliff and later kidnapped Gabrielle (023-017)

### Sequence of Events

Gabrielle told the Op and Fitzstephan her story after being rescued from Whidden's cave at Dull Point. She had known nothing of Whidden's first attempt on Eric's life Thursday night, and nothing of the telegram Eric had sent to summon the Op. She sat up all Friday night waiting for Eric to return, and at daylight, frantic, went searching (017-059).

She found Eric's body — as the Op had found it — at the foot of the cliff. The discovery shattered her. She took Eric's pistol and tried to shoot herself.

"I tried twice," she whispered, "but I couldn't. I couldn't. I was too much a coward. I couldn't keep the pistol pointing at myself while I did it. I tried the first time to shoot myself in the temple, and then in the breast; but I hadn't the courage. Each time I jerked it away just before I fired" (017-060).

After failing to kill herself, she went back to the house and was subsequently kidnapped by Whidden, who was seeking payment for the murder Fitzstephan had commissioned. Whidden held her in a cave at Dull Point until the Op, Fitzstephan, Sheriff Feeney, and District Attorney Vernon tracked him down (017-036, 017-042).

## Psychological Significance

Gabrielle's perception of her own survival as "cowardice" is characteristic of the self-loathing that the Dain curse mythology had instilled in her. Her inability to take her own life, which a healthier person might interpret as a healthy survival instinct, became in her mind another proof of her unworthiness — she was not even capable of ending her suffering.

The Op, however, did not share this interpretation. His response to Gabrielle's confession was characteristically pragmatic: he focused on the factual information she provided and on ensuring her safety going forward.

## Aftermath

After her rescue, Gabrielle was returned to Quesada, and from there eventually transferred to the care of the Collinson family. Her suicide attempt, combined with her drug addiction and the cumulative trauma of witnessing multiple murders, left her in a fragile state. The Op later noted that she had been "in bed since we brought her back from Dull Point" (018-082).

The Op also recognized that Gabrielle's account of finding Eric's body confirmed the timeline and location of the murder, helping to establish Whidden as the killer and, ultimately, Fitzstephan as the mastermind.
