# Leggett's Letter

The written statement left by Edgar Leggett at his death, addressed to the police,
confessing to a lifetime of crimes and deaths — found alongside his body and seven
unset diamonds, and central to the investigation's understanding (and
misunderstanding) of the Leggett case.

## Overview

The letter is a lengthy handwritten document, portions of which are read aloud by the
Op in the presence of the gathered characters (006-020). It spans several paragraphs
and is signed "Maurice de Mayenne," revealing Leggett's true identity (006-034). The
letter traces Leggett's life from the murder of his first wife Lily in Paris in 1913,
through his escape from Devil's Island, the deaths of Labaud and Edge in South
America, and his resettlement in San Francisco, to the immediate events surrounding the
diamond case — Upton's blackmail, Ruppert's murder, and the hiding of Ruppert's body
in the cellar (006-033). The letter is discovered with "blood, a small black automatic
pistol lying close to one of Leggett's hands, and seven unset diamonds grouped by his
head" (006-013).

## Narrative Role

### Plot Significance

The letter serves as Leggett's attempt to control the narrative after his death —
taking responsibility for crimes he committed while exculpating his wife Alice and
daughter Gabrielle. The Op immediately recognizes contradictions in the statement: the
tone suggests a man preparing to flee rather than commit suicide, and certain details
do not hold up to scrutiny (007-017, 007-023). The Op's analysis of the letter
directly leads to his accusation of Alice Dain Leggett as the actual killer: "He wrote
this statement, shouldering your crimes... Your husband's letter sounded enough like a
suicide letter to pass for one, you thought; so you murdered him" (007-021). The
letter thus functions as both evidence and a piece of misdirection — a document that
reveals while it conceals.
