# Paris, 1913

Paris in 1913 is the site of the original crime that shadows every character in *The Dain Curse*. On June 6, 1913, Lily Dain was murdered, and her husband Maurice Pierre de Mayenne was convicted and sentenced to life imprisonment. The Paris murder is the foundational event of the novel's intricate plot.

## Overview

The Paris of the novel is primarily the Paris of 1903–1913, where Maurice Pierre de Mayenne came as a young artist. He studied painting there, met the orphaned Dain sisters Alice and Lily in 1907, and married Lily the following year. Their daughter Gabrielle was born in Paris. The city represents both artistic ambition and romantic tragedy.

## In the Novel

### Key Scenes

Maurice Leggett's written confession provides the most detailed account of his Paris years. He arrived in Paris in 1903 to study painting and four years later made the acquaintance of Alice and Lily Dain, orphan daughters of a British naval officer. He married Lily in 1908 (006-024).

On June 6, 1913, he murdered Lily and fled with Alice and Gabrielle to London, where he was soon arrested and returned to Paris for trial. He was found guilty and sentenced to life imprisonment (006-026).

The newspapers later sensationalized Alice Leggett's account of what happened in Paris in 1913, sparking a brief public scandal (008-025). In the novel's climax, Fitzstephan casts doubt on Alice's version of events, suggesting that she — not Maurice — was the actual killer, and had lied to hurt Gabrielle (023-029).

The Paris murder echoes through the novel in multiple retellings. When the deputy sheriff at Quesada demands the full story, the Op gives him everything "from Paris in 1913 to the cliff road" (013-069), underscoring how the Paris crime is the narrative's starting point.

## Description

The novel provides few physical descriptions of Paris itself, treating it primarily through the characters' memories and confessions. The city is evoked as a place of artistic study and bohemian life, where a young painter could meet the daughters of a British naval officer. The contrast between Paris as a city of art and romance and Paris as the site of a brutal murder creates the central irony that pervades the Dain family's history.
