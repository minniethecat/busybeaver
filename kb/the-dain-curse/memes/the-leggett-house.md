# The Leggett House

The San Francisco residence of Edgar Leggett and his family, where the theft of the Halstead and Beauchamp diamonds sets the novel's plot in motion. The house serves as a key crime scene and the detective's point of entry into the Leggett family's secrets.

## Overview

The Leggett house is a three-story residence in San Francisco, where Edgar Leggett lived with his wife Alice and daughter Gabrielle. Leggett maintained his scientific laboratory on the third floor, where he conducted experiments with color and light (001-011). The household also included a housekeeper, Mrs. Begg, and various servants including Minnie Hershey (001-050).

## In the Novel

### Key Scenes

The novel opens at the Leggett house when the Continental Op is called to investigate the theft of eight diamonds that had been kept in Leggett's laboratory (001-023). The diamonds were normally left lying in the open but had recently been locked in a cabinet. The front door was found ajar on the morning of the discovery, and Mrs. Leggett reported seeing a man outside the house (001-028).

The laboratory itself occupies nearly the entire third floor. It is described as a large room with charts between windows on whitewashed walls, an uncovered wooden floor, and equipment including an X-ray machine, several smaller machines, a forge, a wide sink, a large zinc table, and smaller porcelain work surfaces (001-051).

Mrs. Begg, the housekeeper hired through an employment agency in 1921, worked alone after the household determined there was not enough work for two servants. Leggett spent nearly all his time on the top floor in his laboratory and adjoining bedroom (003-094). In August 1923, Alice Leggett and fifteen-year-old Gabrielle arrived at the house with suitcases. Mrs. Begg later testified that Leggett was the most surprised man she had ever seen when they appeared (003-095).

## Description

The house interior includes a green, orange, and chocolate room on the second floor furnished with a dull orange oriental rug and walnut furniture (001-011). The third floor is dominated by the laboratory. The property has a front lawn bordered by a blue brick walk, where the Continental Op finds a small diamond, not more than a quarter of a carat, shining in the grass (001-002).

The neighborhood is described by Mrs. Priestly, a neighbor, who observed that the Leggetts kept to themselves but seemed to be nice people (002-004). Leggett moved into the house in 1921, initially alone except for the housekeeper.
