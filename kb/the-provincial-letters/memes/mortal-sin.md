## Definition

A **mortal sin** (Latin: *peccatum mortale*) is, in Catholic moral theology, a gravely wrongful act committed with full knowledge and deliberate consent, which destroys the supernatural life of charity in the soul and, unless absolved through sacramental confession, merits eternal separation from God. It is distinguished from **venial sin**, which wounds but does not destroy charity and does not entail eternal damnation.

In the *Provincial Letters*, the mortal–venial distinction becomes a flashpoint because Jesuit casuists are shown to have reclassified acts traditionally regarded as mortal sins into the category of venial sins, or to have exempted certain classes of persons from what would otherwise be mortal guilt.

## Role in the Controversy

The classification of sins lies at the core of Pascal's polemic. He accuses the Jesuit casuists of systematically downgrading the gravity of sinful acts through probabilistic reasoning, such that conduct the Church Fathers and Scripture regarded as mortally sinful becomes, under casuistic analysis, merely venial—or even permissible.

A central example is the obligation of almsgiving. Pascal cites Jesuit authorities who teach that the rich are "not guilty of mortal sin by refusing to give alms out of their superfluity" ([010-040]), a position he presents as directly contradicting the Gospel and the Church Fathers. In another passage, he reports the Jesuit teaching that an incumbent may "without any mortal sin, desire the decease of a life-renter on his benefice" ([008-016]).

Pascal further highlights the casuistic treatment of falsehood. He reports, with evident indignation, the maxim that "a falsehood is not a mortal sin, either against justice or charity" ([016-009]), a position he treats as sanctioning systematic dishonesty under the cover of theological refinement.

The cumulative effect, Pascal argues, is to render the concept of mortal sin nearly inoperative: "the world will get rid of mortal sins altogether" ([010-051]).

## Positions

### Jesuit Casuist Position

The Jesuit authorities cited by Pascal held that:

- Rich persons are not guilty of mortal sin by refusing alms from their superfluous wealth ([010-040])
- An incumbent may desire the death of a life-renter on his benefice without mortal sin ([008-016])
- A falsehood, under certain conditions, does not constitute a mortal sin against justice or charity ([016-009])
- Priests are obliged on pain of mortal sin to absolve penitents who follow probable opinions ([007-053])
- The distinction between mortal and venial sins can be managed through the use of two confessors ([011-008])

### Pascal's Critique

Pascal's response is sustained and satirical. He treats these maxims not as careful theological refinements but as calculated relaxations designed to accommodate the vices of the powerful:

- The alms doctrine, he argues, directly contradicts patristic teaching and permits the rich to ignore the cry of the poor
- The benefice doctrine sanctions murderous desires under the guise of permitted self-interest
- The falsehood doctrine removes the fear of eternal punishment from systematic dishonesty
- The cumulative effect of these relaxations is to empty "mortal sin" of meaning, leaving a moral theology that serves the world rather than God
