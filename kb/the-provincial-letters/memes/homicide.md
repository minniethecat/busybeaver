## Definition

Homicide, in the *Provincial Letters*, refers to the unjustified killing of a human being — an act absolutely prohibited by divine and natural law, yet one that Jesuit casuists subjected to an elaborate probabilistic analysis that effectively permitted it in a startling range of circumstances.

Pascal's treatment of homicide spans Letters VII through XIV and constitutes the most sustained and rhetorically powerful single-issue critique in the entire work. The casuistic approach to homicide serves as Pascal's primary exhibit of moral theology run amok.

## Role in the Controversy

The topic of homicide is introduced in Letter VII through the doctrine of "directing the intention." The Jesuit monk explains that this method — diverting one's intention from the evil of an act to some permissible end — justifies homicide "in a thousand instances" (008-008).

The monk proceeds to catalogue the circumstances under which killing is permitted: to defend one's honour after a buffet, to protect one's property, to preempt slander. The catalogue is delivered with the complacent confidence of a system-builder exhibiting the elegance of his classifications.

Pascal devotes Letters XIII and XIV to a detailed rebuttal. He identifies the key conceptual manoeuvre: the distinction between *speculation* and *practice*. The casuists maintain that certain homicides are permissible "in speculation" but not "in practice," thereby appearing to restrain their own permissions while leaving the speculative permission intact.

But Escobar, in his later work, closes this gap: "I cannot see how it can be that an action which seems allowable in speculation should not be so likewise in practice" (014-016). The speculative permission becomes, by Escobar's logic, a practical license.

Pascal's most devastating observation concerns the effect of probabilism on moral prohibitions. When one casuist condemns what another permits, probabilism dictates that the lenient opinion may be followed.

Thus Vasquez's condemnation of homicide is neutralised by Lessius's permission of it — not because Lessius is more authoritative, but because any probable opinion is a safe opinion (014-027). The structure of probabilism ensures that the most permissive view always prevails.

In Letter XIV, Pascal appeals to the most fundamental principles of natural law and revelation: the prohibition of murder given to Noah, renewed in the Decalogue, and confirmed by the Gospel (015-007).

Against this unchanging moral law, the casuistic permissions appear not as legitimate theological development but as apostasy — the language of "the spiritual Sodom" rather than "the mystical Jerusalem" (015-029).

## Positions

**Jesuit casuists (Lessius, Molina, Escobar, Reginald, Filiutius)**: Homicide is permissible in a wide range of circumstances through the direction of intention: to avenge a buffet, to defend honour, to protect property, to preempt slander.

The distinction between speculation and practice provides rhetorical cover while the probabilistic structure ensures that permissive opinions are always available (008-008, 014-014, 014-016).

**Counter-casuists (Vasquez, Suarez)**: Certain forms of homicide condemned by their colleagues are indeed impermissible. But their condemnations are rendered practically inert by probabilism, which permits following the lenient opinion regardless (014-027).

**Scripture and natural law**: The prohibition of homicide is absolute, grounded in the imago Dei and the covenant with Noah (Genesis 9:5–6), renewed in the Decalogue, and confirmed by Christ. The only exceptions are those established by divine authority itself: capital punishment by lawful magistrates and killings commanded by special divine mandate (015-007).

**Pascal's position**: The Jesuit casuistry on homicide is not merely erroneous but diabolical — it speaks the language of "the devil, who has been a murderer from the beginning" (015-029).

The structure of probabilism, combined with the distinction between speculation and practice, creates a system in which the most permissive moral opinion always wins, and the Church's absolute prohibition is reduced to a suggestion.

## Key Passages

> "Know, then, that this marvellous principle is our grand method of directing the intention — the importance of which, in our moral system, is such, that I might almost venture to compare it with the doctrine of probability ... I will now show you this grand method in all its glory, as it applies to the subject of homicide — a crime which it justifies in a thousand instances." (008-008)

> "Is it allowable to kill him who has given me a buffet? Lessius says it is permissible in speculation, though not to be followed in practice ... Others, however, have judged that, by avoiding these inconveniences, this is permissible and safe in practice." (014-014)

> "I cannot see how it can be that an action which seems allowable in speculation should not be so likewise in practice; because what may be done in practice depends on what is found to be lawful in speculation, and the things differ from each other only as cause and effect." (014-016)

> "Thus an opinion which would be condemned under the name of practice, comes out quite safe under the name of speculation. But this basis once established, it is not difficult to erect on it the rest of your maxims. There is an infinite distance between God's prohibition of murder, and your speculative permission of the crime; but between that permission and the practice the distance is very small indeed." (014-016)

> "What can be a plainer dictate of nature than that no private individual has a right to take away the life of another?" (015-007)

> "Whoso sheddeth man's blood, by man shall his blood be shed; for man is made in the image of God." (015-007)
