## Definition

Conscience (*conscientia*) is the internal faculty by which a person applies universal moral principles to particular actions, judging them as right or wrong, obligatory or forbidden.

In Catholic moral theology, conscience is binding: a person must always follow the certain judgment of conscience, and to act against conscience — even an erroneous conscience — is sin.

The *Provincial Letters* engage conscience at a specific point of tension: what happens when the certain judgment of conscience is replaced by the uncertain guidance of probable opinions? If a confessor tells the penitent that a probable opinion justifies his act, is his conscience genuinely at peace — or has the very concept of conscience been emptied of meaning?

## Role in the Controversy

Conscience is the internal forum (*forum internum*) that the confessor is supposed to form and govern. In the rigorist tradition, this means instructing the penitent in the moral law and binding him to follow it, even at personal cost.

Pascal's critique is that the Jesuit system of probabilism inverts this relationship. Instead of conscience being bound by the moral law, the moral law is adjusted to whatever will leave the penitent's conscience undisturbed.

The effect, Pascal argues, is a corruption of conscience itself. The penitent learns not to examine his actions against an objective standard but to seek out a confessor who will supply a probable opinion that permits what he wishes to do. Conscience ceases to be a guide and becomes a rubber stamp. (006-052)

The Letters are themselves an act of conscience — Pascal writing to "awaken" the conscience of his provincial correspondent, to expose what the casuists have done, and to restore the distinction between a genuinely formed conscience and a conscience pacified by probable opinions.

## Positions

**The Jesuit position (as presented by Pascal)**. The Jesuit casuists taught that a person satisfies the obligation of conscience by following any genuinely probable opinion — even if the opposite opinion is more probable.

Pascal quotes the casuist Emmanuel Sà: "a single doctor may turn consciences round about and upside down, and yet always keep them in safety." (006-052) 

The key claim is that conscience is satisfied by probability, not certainty: if a respectable doctor holds an opinion, the penitent may follow it, and his conscience is at peace — regardless of what the Church, Scripture, or tradition might teach.

**Pascal's critique**. Pascal's spokesman in the Letters presents this as a systematic corruption of conscience. He asks: what is the difference between a conscience that follows a probable opinion and a conscience that follows its own desires?

> "Speaking, then, against your conscience?" Pascal asks. "No," the casuist replies, "not according to my own conscience, but according to that of Pontas and Bauny." (006-044, 006-045)

The point is devastating: the casuist has substituted the opinion of a doctor for his own conscience. He has not followed his conscience — he has outsourced it.

**The rigorist position**. The rigorist tradition maintained that conscience must follow not probability but certainty. Where the moral law is unclear, the safer course (*tutior pars*) must be followed — not because the stricter opinion is always true, but because conscience requires moral certainty, not mere probability.

Pascal endorses this implicitly: the whole thrust of the Letters is that the Jesuits have traded the safety of conscience for the comfort of the penitent.

## Key Passages

> "A single doctor may turn consciences round about and upside down, and yet always keep them in safety." (006-052)

> "Speaking, then, against your conscience?" "Not according to my own conscience, but according to that of Pontas and Bauny." (006-044, 006-045)

> "The Jesuits teach that they should govern all consciences." (006-010)

> "Is it not enough to have one grave doctor on your side? By my conscience, such an authority! It is Escobar." (006-019)

> "It does not affect my conscience in the least whether M. Arnauld is a heretic or not." (001-011)
