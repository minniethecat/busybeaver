## Definition

Probabilism is a system of moral theology holding that in cases of moral doubt, a person may licitly follow any "probable" opinion — one supported by reputable authorities or arguments — even if the contrary opinion appears more probable.

The doctrine was systematized by Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]s in the early 17th century and became the primary target of [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s satire. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] portrays probabilism as a mechanism by which confessors could absolve virtually any sin by finding some theologian who had expressed a lenient opinion. (006-019)

The key logical structure of probabilism is: if an opinion is supported by at least one recognized authority, it is "probable"; if it is probable, it is safe to follow in conscience; therefore, any action with at least one authority in its favor becomes licit.

## Role in the Controversy

Probabilism is the unifying theme of Letters V–X, where [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s narrator is instructed by a Jesuit monk in the "new system of morals." The monk's candid exposition forms the satirical core of the work.

Key critiques [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] advances:

| Critique | [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s Argument |
|----------|-------------------|
| Authority accumulation | A single "grave doctor" makes an opinion probable, regardless of opposing authorities |
| Probabilism about probabilism | The doctrine itself rests on probabilistic reasoning — circular justification |
| Moral minimalism | Encourages seeking minimum obligation, not maximum good |
| Corrosive effect | Gradually erodes all moral prohibitions through exception-stacking |

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] contrasts Jesuit probabilism with the rigorist [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist approach to conscience. The [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists, following [[Jansenius]], held that in cases of doubt, the safer (stricter) course must be followed.

> "So, father, your doctrine is a very convenient one! Only think of being able to answer yes or no as you please! How happy is the power of probabilism!" (006-023, paraphrase)

## Positions

Probabilism occupied a middle position in Catholic moral theology, flanked by stricter and looser doctrines:

**Probabiliorism** held that the more probable opinion must be followed — mere probability was insufficient. This was the dominant position among Dominicans and many secular clergy.

**Tutiorism** maintained that the safest or strictest opinion must always be followed, regardless of probability. The [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists generally favored this rigorist approach.

**Laxism** held that even a slightly probable opinion could be followed. While the Church condemned laxism, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] argued that probabilism as practiced by the Jesuits was indistinguishable from it in effect.

Jesuit defenders of probabilism, including [[Escobar]] and [[Molina]], argued it provided necessary pastoral flexibility for confessors dealing with complex moral situations in the lives of ordinary penitents.

## Historical Context

Probabilism emerged from the development of moral theology as a distinct discipline at the University of Salamanca and in Jesuit institutions during the 16th and 17th centuries.

The doctrine's rise coincided with the expansion of sacramental confession and the need for practical guides for priests. Earlier medieval [[casuistry|casuist]]ry had relied heavily on the authority of Thomas Aquinas and canon law; probabilism represented a shift toward reasoning from multiple authorities.

| Period | Development |
|--------|------------|
| 1577 | Bartolomé de Medina formulates the first clear statement of probabilism |
| 1588 | [[molina|Molina]]'s *Concordia* provides theological framework |
| 1644 | [[Escobar]]'s *Liber theologiae moralis* systematizes probabilist [[casuistry|casuist]]ry |
| 1656–57 | [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s *Provincial Letters* attack probabilism as moral laxity |
| 1679 | [[innocent-x|Pope Innocent X]]I condemns 65 laxist propositions |
| 18th c. | St. Alphonsus Liguori develops equiprobabilism as a moderate alternative |

> "By means of probabilism, which is held both by Vasquez and by Sanchez, one may do what one considers allowable according to a probable opinion, though the contrary may be the more probable." (014-027, paraphrase)

## Relationship to Casuistry

Probabilism and [[casuistry]] are distinct but complementary. [[casuistry|Casuistry]] provides the analytical framework for examining moral cases through reasoning from principles, precedents, and authorities. Probabilism provides the decision rule for resolving doubt when authorities conflict.

The two are often conflated in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s polemic, but the distinction matters: one can practice [[casuistry|casuist]]ry without being a probabilist (as did many Dominican rigorists), and one can hold probabilist principles without extensive case analysis.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s critique targets both, but his deepest objection is to the probabilist decision rule, which he sees as making moral truth a function of scholarly opinion rather than divine law.
