## Definition

A decree (*decretum*) in canon law is an authoritative determination issued by a competent ecclesiastical authority. Decrees may be legislative (establishing general norms), judicial (resolving particular cases), or administrative (executing existing law). The decrees that figure in the *Provincial Letters* are primarily of two kinds: (1) decrees of the Roman Congregations — particularly the Holy Office — condemning propositions extracted from Jansenius; and (2) decrees of the Faculty of Theology of Paris (the Sorbonne) censuring Antoine Arnauld.

What distinguishes a decree from an ordinary opinion is its binding character. A decree obliges the faithful to internal assent, not merely external obedience. This is precisely what Pascal contests: he argues that certain decrees — particularly those that purport to decide questions of fact rather than doctrine — exceed the legitimate scope of ecclesiastical authority. (006-014)

## Role in the Controversy

The Jansenist controversy was, in significant part, a controversy *about decrees* — their authority, their interpretation, and their limits. The 1653 papal bull *Cum occasione* (itself a form of decree) condemned five propositions allegedly drawn from Jansenius's *Augustinus*. The 1656 Sorbonne decree censured Arnauld for questioning whether those propositions were, in fact, in Jansenius.

Pascal's critique operates on two levels. First, he contests the *competence* of the Sorbonne decree: the power to issue binding decrees does not extend to questions of fact, which are accessible to ordinary human inquiry and do not require supernatural discernment. (018-035) Second, he contests the *manner* in which decrees are obtained: the Sorbonne's censure of Arnauld was, he alleges, procured through the manipulation of the Dominican votes and the exclusion of opposing voices. (019-006)

The broader implication is constitutional: Pascal is arguing, without quite saying so, that decree-making power in the Church is not absolute. It is bounded by tradition, by the nature of the question at issue, and by the procedural requirements of natural justice. A decree that exceeds these bounds is not binding — not because papal authority is denied, but because the decree itself is ultra vires. (019-032)

## Positions

**The ultramontane position**. Decrees of the Holy See are binding in conscience, and their authority extends to both questions of doctrine (de jure) and questions of fact (de facto). To question a papal decree is to question the pope's divinely instituted authority. The Sorbonne and the Jesuit order, broadly speaking, held this view.

**Pascal's Gallican-Augustinian position**. Pascal, drawing on the Gallican tradition of the French Church, distinguishes between the pope's doctrinal authority (which is supreme but not infallible on questions of fact) and his disciplinary authority (which is limited by canon law and tradition). A decree that condemns a proposition is binding; a decree that asserts, without evidence, that the proposition appears in a particular book is not — because the pope has no special charism for locating words in texts. (018-035)

**The procedural objection**. Even if papal decrees could, in principle, decide questions of fact, the specific decrees condemning Jansenius were, Pascal alleges, obtained by fraud — the propositions were misrepresented to the pope, their Jansenist context was suppressed, and the condemnation was procured through political rather than theological channels. (019-039)
