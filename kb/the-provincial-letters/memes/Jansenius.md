## Role in the Controversy

Cornelius Jansen (Jansenius), Bishop of Ypres, is the foundational figure behind the Jansenist movement that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] defends throughout the *Provincial Letters*. His posthumous work *Augustinus* (1640) articulated a strict Augustinian theology of grace that became the doctrinal basis for the Jansenist position.

Jansenius's five propositions — allegedly extracted from *Augustinus* and condemned by the Sorbonne — form the central point of dispute in Letters I–IV. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s defence of [[Arnauld|Antoine Arnauld]] revolves around whether these propositions were in fact contained in Jansenius's book. (001-005)

The controversy over whether the condemned propositions were actually "in Jansenius" (a question of fact) versus whether they were heretical (a question of faith) becomes the key distinction that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] exploits throughout the letters. (001-012)

> "They have found the secret of satisfying the world, by telling them that it is not a question of faith, but a question of fact only, touching Jansenius, which could not possibly be deemed a matter of heresy." (018-023)

## Key Positions

Jansenius held that divine grace is granted according to God's will alone, not proportioned to human merit. This placed him in direct opposition to [[Molinism|Molinist]] doctrine advanced by the Jesuits.

| Doctrine | Jansenius's Position | Opposing View |
|----------|---------------------|---------------|
| Efficacious grace | Grace infallibly produces its effect by divine power | Grace depends on human free consent (Molinism) |
| Sufficient grace | Without interior movement from God, "sufficient grace" is insufficient | God gives all people grace sufficient for salvation |
| Free will | Wounded by sin, requires efficacious grace to choose good | Free will cooperates with grace |
| Predestination | Rooted in God's eternal decree | Conditioned on foreseen human response |

His theology drew rigorously on Saint Augustine, emphasizing the necessity of interior grace that God gives inwardly and that unfailingly produces its effect. (001-011)

The five propositions condemned by the Sorbonne concerned the nature of grace, free will, and merit — core issues dividing Jansenists and Jesuits throughout the 17th century.

## Biography

Cornelius Jansen was born in 1585 at Accoy, near Leerdam in the Netherlands. He studied at the University of Louvain, where he distinguished himself in theological studies and formed a lasting friendship with Jean du Vergier de Hauranne (later the Abbé de Saint-Cyran), the future spiritual director of Port-Royal.

Jansen and Saint-Cyran devoted years to the study of Augustine, reportedly reading the entire corpus of his works multiple times. This intensive engagement laid the foundation for *Augustinus*, Jansen's magnum opus.

Appointed Bishop of Ypres in 1636, Jansen served only two years before dying of plague in 1638. He did not live to see the controversy his work would ignite, nor the movement that would bear his name.

## Writings

Jansen's literary output was concentrated in his final years:

| Work | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| *Augustinus* (3 vols.) | 1640 (posthumous) | Systematic exposition of Augustine's theology of grace; source of the Five Propositions |
| *Mars Gallicus* | 1635 | Political treatise criticizing French foreign policy under Richelieu |
| *Commentarius in Proverbia* | 1614–1617 | Early exegetical work on the Book of Proverbs |

*Augustinus* was condemned by Pope Urban VIII in 1642 in the bull *In eminenti*, but the bull's enforcement in France was contested, fueling the controversy that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] would enter two decades later.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Jansen's thought was shaped by his deep engagement with Saint Augustine and his partnership with Saint-Cyran, who brought Jansenist theology to Port-Royal.

The Abbé de Saint-Cyran became confessor to the nuns of Port-Royal and made the convent the institutional center of Jansenist spirituality in France. Through Saint-Cyran, Jansen's theology influenced [[Arnauld|Antoine Arnauld]], [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]], and the broader Port-Royal circle.

Jansen's theological opponents included [[Molina|Luis de Molina]], whose *Concordia* (1588) articulated the Molinist doctrine of grace that Jansen considered semi-Pelagian. The Jesuit polemicists — particularly [[annat|Père Annat]] and [[meynier|Père Meynier]] — attacked Jansen's doctrines throughout the controversy that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] chronicles.

## Legacy

Jansenius's *Augustinus* shaped French Catholic theology for over a century. The movement it spawned led to the destruction of Port-Royal (1710), the papal bull *Unigenitus* (1713), and a lasting tension between Augustinian and Molinist theologies.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s *Provincial Letters* remain the most widely read product of this theological battle, ensuring Jansenius a place in literary as well as theological history. Through [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s pen, a deceased bishop of Ypres became the symbolic center of a controversy that engaged the finest minds of 17th-century France.
