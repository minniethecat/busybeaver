## Role in the Controversy

Francisco Suárez was the most important Jesuit theologian of the early modern period, and his appearance in the *Provincial Letters* reflects both his immense authority and the dilemma this authority posed for [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s polemic. Suárez was no minor [[casuistry|casuist]] — he was a thinker of the first rank whose metaphysical and legal writings influenced the whole of Catholic theology. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] could not dismiss him as a marginal figure; instead, he had to show that even Suárez's great authority had been co-opted by the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic system and made to serve morally lax conclusions.

Suárez appears in the *Letters* in two distinct roles. First, he is named by [[Escobar]] among the "four living creatures" — the four supreme Jesuit authorities (Suárez, Vasquez, [[molina|Molina]], and [[valencia|Valencia]]) whose collective work forms the foundation of Jesuit moral theology. (006-021) Second, he is cited on specific moral questions: the sufficiency of attrition in the sacrament of penance, the obligations of confessors, the morality of simony, and the resolution of doubtful cases. In each context, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s treatment is more respectful than his treatment of lesser [[casuistry|casuist]]s — but the conclusion is the same: the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic method has corrupted even the greatest minds.

The most striking exchange involving Suárez occurs in Letter X, where [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] has the Jesuit monk present Suárez's teaching on attrition as evidence that the Jesuits require very little of penitents. When the narrator objects that Suárez's actual teaching is more demanding, the monk concedes the point but immediately shifts ground to another authority — illustrating the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic habit of seeking whichever opinion yields the desired result. (011-023 through 011-054)

## Key Positions

Suárez's doctrines cited in the *Letters* address several of the central disputes:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Four living creatures | Named by [[Escobar]] as one of the four foundational Jesuit authorities alongside Vasquez, [[molina|Molina]], and [[valencia|Valencia]] | 006-021 |
| Attrition and confession | Suárez's teaching on the sufficiency of attrition without charity for sacramental absolution | 011-023, 011-027, 011-046, 011-052, 011-054 |
| Simony and benefices | Suárez's treatment of simoniacal contracts and the obligations of beneficiaries | 009-025 |
| Two confessors | Suárez and [[Escobar]] on the permissibility of having two confessors — one for mortal sins and one for venial | 011-008 |
| Restitution obligations | Suárez addressed, with other authorities, whether beneficiaries must make restitution for simoniacally obtained benefices | 011-037 |

The core controversy over Suárez in the *Letters* concerns the doctrine of attrition. In Letter X, the Jesuit monk initially claims Suárez as an authority for the position that attrition without any love of God is sufficient for the sacrament of penance. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s narrator challenges this: "You told me that your Father Suarez had taught this doctrine. But I have read the passage, and he teaches a very different one." (011-023) The monk responds that Suárez "has not handled the question in the place you refer to" — but the narrator has the passage "word for word." (011-023)

> "you last cited Father Suarez, I beg you will inform me if he maintains this doctrine?" (011-054)

The debate over Suárez's actual teaching continues through several exchanges, with the narrator pressing the monk on whether Suárez really requires so little of penitents and the monk eventually conceding that he does not — but insisting that "[[filiutius|Filiutius]], you know, regulates the matter." (011-027) This sequence illustrates [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s core argument: the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic system allows one to escape the demands even of the most authoritative theologian by simply finding another authority who says something different.

## Biography

Francisco Suárez was born in Granada, Spain, in 1548 and entered the Society of Jesus at the age of sixteen. He studied at the University of Salamanca, where he absorbed the Scholastic tradition in its most rigorous form. After ordination, he taught philosophy and theology at several Jesuit colleges in Spain, at the Roman College, and finally at the University of Coimbra in Portugal, where he held the principal chair of theology from 1597 until his retirement in 1615.

Suárez's intellectual achievement was monumental. His *Disputationes Metaphysicae* (1597) was the first systematic treatise on metaphysics in the Western tradition that was not a commentary on Aristotle, and it influenced philosophers from Descartes to Leibniz to Schopenhauer. His *De Legibus* (1612) was a foundational text in the development of natural law theory, and his *Defensio Fidei* (1613) engaged the political-theological controversies over papal authority and the divine right of kings. His moral and sacramental theology, collected in his works on the sacraments and on theological virtues, was the standard reference point for Jesuit moral teaching throughout the seventeenth century.

Suárez was known in his lifetime as *Doctor Eximius* — the Exceptional Doctor — and his authority within the Catholic theological tradition was rivaled only by that of Thomas Aquinas. The [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists, including [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]], respected Suárez's intellectual stature even as they contested the uses to which his doctrines were put by lesser [[casuistry|casuist]]s. Suárez died in Lisbon in 1617, nearly four decades before the *Provincial Letters*.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Suárez's authority places him at the apex of the Jesuit theological hierarchy in the *Letters*:

- **Gabriel Vásquez**: The most frequently paired authority with Suárez; together they are called "the most illustrious of our fathers" and their joint authority is invoked repeatedly. (006-068, 014-027, 014-030)
- **[[molina|Luis de Molina]]**: Fellow member of the "four living creatures" and co-authority on grace and free will. Suárez and [[molina|Molina]] represent the great Jesuit theological tradition from which [[casuistry|casuist]]ry derived. (006-021)
- **[[Escobar|Antonio Escobar]]**: [[Escobar]]'s *Moral Theology* is presented as a synthesis of the tradition Suárez helped found. The relationship is that of compiler to source. (006-021)
- **Vincenzo [[filiutius|Filiutius]]**: In Letter X, the monk shifts from Suárez to [[filiutius|Filiutius]] when the former proves inconvenient, illustrating the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic method of authority-shopping. (011-027)
