## Definition

A sacrament is an efficacious sign of grace, instituted by Christ and entrusted to the Church, by which divine life is dispensed to the faithful.

The Council of Trent defined seven sacraments: Baptism, Confirmation, Eucharist, Penance, Extreme Unction, Holy Orders, and Matrimony. Each consists of matter (the sensible element) and form (the words that confer the grace). (013-020)

The sacraments are not merely symbolic but actually confer the grace they signify — *ex opere operato*, by the very act of being performed — provided the recipient places no obstacle in the way. This principle, defined at Trent against the Protestant Reformers, is the theological foundation for the casuistical questions in the *Letters*: what constitutes an "obstacle" to sacramental grace?

## Role in the Controversy

The sacrament of penance is the one most directly at issue in the *Provincial Letters*, but Pascal's critique extends to sacramental theology in general.

The underlying question is the same across all the sacraments: what are the minimum conditions for valid reception, and who determines them? (011-055)

The Jesuit casuists, in Pascal's account, systematically lower the minimum conditions to make the sacraments maximally accessible. The Jansenists insist that the conditions are objective and demanding, and that lowering them profanes the sacraments.

This is not merely a technical theological dispute. It determines whether the ordinary Christian can approach the sacraments with confidence or must approach them with fear — whether the Church's sacramental system is a mechanism of consolation or a tribunal of judgment. (011-058)

## Positions

**The Jesuit sacramental theology**. The Jesuits emphasized the efficacy of the sacraments *ex opere operato*.

If the sacrament itself confers grace, then the subjective disposition of the recipient is less important than the objective performance of the rite. The priest, acting in the person of Christ, confers forgiveness by the very words of absolution, provided the penitent places no *formal* obstacle.

The Jesuit casuists defined "obstacle" narrowly: only a deliberate intention to persist in unconfessed mortal sin constitutes an obstacle. Imperfect sorrow, habitual weakness, and repeated falls do not. (011-048)

**The Jansenist sacramental theology**. The Jansenists emphasized that the *ex opere operato* efficacy of the sacraments does not eliminate the need for subjective disposition.

The Council of Trent itself taught that the recipient must "place no obstacle" (*obex non ponere*). The Jansenist argument is that the Jesuit definition of "obstacle" is so narrow that it renders the condition meaningless.

For the Jansenists, a penitent who confesses without genuine contrition, who has no firm purpose of amendment, and who habitually falls into the same sins has placed an obstacle — and the sacrament, while objectively valid, does not confer grace because the recipient is not properly disposed. (011-055)

**The pastoral stakes**. The sacramental question is ultimately pastoral. If the sacraments are primarily medicine for the sick, the Church should make them as accessible as possible — the Jesuit position.

If they are primarily privileges of the righteous, the Church should guard them carefully — the Jansenist position. Pascal's *Provincial Letters* are, at their deepest level, an argument for the second view.
