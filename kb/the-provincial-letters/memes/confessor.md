## Definition

A confessor (*confessarius*) is a priest authorized by his bishop to hear sacramental confessions and grant absolution. In Catholic sacramental theology, the confessor acts *in persona Christi* (in the person of Christ) when he absolves the penitent of sins.

The confessor's role involves three interrelated functions: he is a judge, determining whether the conditions for absolution are met; a physician of souls, prescribing spiritual remedies (penances); and a teacher, instructing the penitent in moral and doctrinal matters.

In the *Provincial Letters*, Pascal focuses on the confessor's role as judge — and on the claim, advanced by Jesuit casuists, that the confessor is bound to absolve a penitent who follows a probable opinion, even if the confessor himself considers that opinion false.

## Role in the Controversy

The confessor is a recurring figure in the *Provincial Letters*, appearing whenever Pascal discusses the practical operation of probabilism. The central question is: what is a confessor obliged to do when a penitent invokes a probable opinion to justify conduct that the confessor considers sinful?

The Jesuit casuists' answer — and Pascal's target — is that the confessor must absolve. The principle is stated repeatedly in the Letters:

> "When a penitent follows a probable opinion, the confessor is bound to absolve him, though he should be of a contrary opinion." (006-066)

Pascal's spokesman reacts with alarm: "I see nothing to fear now. No confessor can dare to be refractory after that." (006-069) The irony is palpable — the confessor, who should be the guardian of moral standards, is transformed by probabilism into a passive administrator of absolution.

The logic extends from the confessional to civil society. Pascal reports that Jesuit casuists have "secured the magistrates as well as the confessors" (007-053) — meaning that the system of probable opinions governs both spiritual and temporal judgment. The confessor who might once have withheld absolution until the penitent amended his life is now "obliged to refer to" the opinion of the casuist rather than his own judgment (007-054).

Pascal asks rhetorically: "You are the judges of the confessors, are you not?" (007-055) The inversion is complete — the casuists judge the confessors, rather than the confessors judging the penitents.

## Positions

**The Jesuit/casuist position**. Jesuit casuists developed an extensive body of teaching on the obligations of confessors. Their core principle was that the confessor's judgment is subordinate to the authority of approved theological opinions. If a reputable casuist has declared an opinion probable, the confessor may not substitute his own contrary judgment.

The Jesuit father in the Letters cites Escobar's compendium: "Let confessors observe that they are bound to absolve a penitent who follows a probable opinion." (007-043) The rationale is pastoral: if confessors could impose their own stricter views, the sacrament of penance would become an instrument of arbitrary judgment rather than a channel of divine mercy.

The casuists also addressed the practical question of the confessor's own conscience. The Jesuit position was that the confessor could follow a probable opinion in absolving even if he personally doubted it — because "it is not necessary that the confessor should be convinced that the opinion he follows is the true one" (011-027).

**Pascal's critique**. Pascal's critique of the Jesuit doctrine on confessors operates on multiple levels.

First, he argues that it makes the sacrament of penance meaningless. If the confessor is "bound to absolve" anyone who can cite a probable opinion, then absolution is automatic — and the penitent has no incentive to reform (006-066).

Second, he argues that it corrupts the confessor. By obliging priests to absolve against their own consciences, the system forces confessors into moral complicity with the sins they are supposed to remedy: "Confessors are bound to believe them for the purpose of absolution." (011-025)

Third, he argues that it undermines the credibility of the Church. A system in which "no confessor can dare to be refractory" (006-069) is one in which no one can trust that absolution means anything at all.

**The Thomist/Dominican position**. The Dominicans, whose theology of grace was Augustinian like the Jansenists', held a stricter view of the confessor's obligations — that the confessor must form his own judgment and may not absolve if he believes the penitent's opinion to be false. Pascal aligns himself with this position but notes that the Dominicans were under pressure to conform to the Jesuit view (007-043).

## Key Passages

> "When a penitent follows a probable opinion, the confessor is bound to absolve him, though he should be of a contrary opinion." (006-066)

> "I see nothing to fear now. No confessor can dare to be refractory after that." (006-069)

> "Let confessors observe that they are bound to absolve a penitent who follows a probable opinion." (007-043)

> "You have secured the magistrates as well as the confessors, by obliging priests, on the pain of mortal sin, to absolve penitents who act according to probable opinions." (007-053)

> "Confessors are bound to believe them for the purpose of absolution." (011-025)

> "It is not necessary that the confessor should be convinced that the opinion he follows is the true one." (011-027)

> "[The confessor] ought to sit in judgment on the disposition of his penitent, as he would have to answer for it if he absolved him without evidence of contrition." (011-038)
