## Definition

Actual grace (*gratia actualis*) is a temporary supernatural assistance by which God moves the human will to perform a particular good act — whether an act of faith, contrition, prayer, or obedience to a commandment. It is distinguished from *habitual grace* (the stable state of sanctification) and from *efficacious grace* (which infallibly produces its effect).

The specific question contested in the Provincial Letters is whether actual grace is *always* available to every person in every circumstance. (005-005)

## Role in the Controversy

The dispute over actual grace lies at the intersection of the Jesuits' commitment to moral accessibility and the Jansenists' insistence on divine sovereignty. If actual grace is always available, then every sinner can, at any moment, fulfill the commandments — and failure to do so is purely the sinner's fault.

If, however, actual grace is not always given, then some failures to obey may reflect the absence of divine assistance rather than purely human refusal. (005-007)

Pascal exposes the Jesuit position through the words of a Jesuit father who claims that "God bestows actual grace on all men in every case of temptation" (005-007).

The Jansenist rebuttal, which Pascal presents through the voice of his interlocutor, is that this claim is empirically false and theologically unsupported: the Scriptures and the Fathers attest that grace is distributed according to God's inscrutable will, not according to a mechanical guarantee. (005-028)

## Positions

**The Jesuit position**. The Jesuits maintained that actual grace is universally available — God never permits a temptation without simultaneously offering the grace to resist it. This position, associated with Molina and Lessius, is motivated by the conviction that God cannot justly condemn a person for failing to do what they lacked the grace to accomplish.

It makes moral obligation coextensive with divine assistance. (005-007)

**The Jansenist position**. Pascal's Jansenist spokesman rejects the Jesuit claim as a dangerous innovation. Drawing on Augustine's anti-Pelagian writings, the Jansenists held that after the Fall, humanity lost the constant availability of grace. Some persons, at some times, lack the actual grace required for certain acts — including the act of contrition.

This is not injustice on God's part but a consequence of original sin and a manifestation of divine sovereignty. (005-017)

**Practical implications for confession**. The dispute over actual grace had direct consequences for sacramental practice. If actual grace is always available, then every penitent can produce genuine contrition at any moment, and the confessor need only verify the external signs.

If actual grace is not universally guaranteed, then the confessor must exercise greater discernment — and some penitents may be incapable of the contrition required for valid absolution. This practical dimension is what makes the abstract theological question urgent in the Letters. (005-039)

## Key Passages

> "We hold that God bestows actual grace on all men in every case of temptation." (005-007)

> "The person who uses the acts of love to God or contrition, has no actual grace for exercising those acts." (005-017)

> "In plain terms, they have not always actual grace. Say no longer, with your modern authors, that they have it whenever they please." (005-039)
