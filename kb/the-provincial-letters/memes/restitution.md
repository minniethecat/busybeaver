## Definition

**Restitution** (Latin: *restitutio*) is the act of commutative justice by which a person who has unjustly taken or retained what belongs to another is obliged to return it or to compensate for the loss. In Catholic moral theology, the obligation of restitution is among the most stringent requirements of natural and divine law: without restitution, the sin of theft or fraud is not forgiven, regardless of sacramental absolution.

In the *Provincial Letters*, Pascal deploys the doctrine of restitution as a test case for the integrity of Jesuit moral theology—with devastating effect.

## Role in the Controversy

Letter IX of the *Provincial Letters* contains one of Pascal's most concentrated polemical assaults. He devotes the entire letter to examining Jesuit teachings on restitution, treating the topic not as a peripheral casuistic question but as the measure of whether a moral system serves God or human convenience.

Pascal's method is to take a clear moral principle—that ill-gotten gains must be restored—and then show how Jesuit casuists have surrounded it with so many exceptions, distinctions, and probable opinions that the obligation is effectively annihilated. He reports that, according to the casuists, a person who has acquired money through criminal means may retain it if restoring it would cause him "considerable inconvenience" ([009-003]).

The most striking example is the treatment of a man who has burned down a neighbour's barn. Pascal cites the casuistic opinion that the arsonist is not obliged to make restitution, because the act was not "expressly prohibited by any law" ([009-036]). He then multiplies examples: the diviner who may keep his fees, the judge who may retain bribes, and so on—each instance demonstrating the same pattern of a clear obligation dissolved through casuistic refinement.

Pascal presents the Jesuit position as a kind of practical atheism: a moral system whose cumulative effect is to assure the wealthy and powerful that they need not fear the material consequences of their injustices. "Relieved from the burden of restitution," he remarks with heavy irony, "what have they left to fear?" ([009-064]).

## Positions

### Jesuit Casuist Position

Pascal attributes the following positions to Jesuit authorities:

- A person resolved to make restitution of ill-gotten gains is not obliged to follow through if a probable opinion relieves him of the obligation ([006-012], [006-072])
- A person who has gained money through usury is not bound to make restitution of the profit ([009-029])
- An arsonist who burns down a barn is not obliged to make restitution, because arson is not expressly addressed by the law of restitution ([009-036])
- A diviner who takes money for his services need not restore it ([009-048])
- The only case in which restitution is "positively enjoined" is when money is received "as a price" for a benefice—all other transfers are exempt ([009-041])

### Pascal's Critique

Pascal's response is sustained and methodical:

- The casuistic method takes a universal moral obligation and carves out exceptions so numerous that the rule becomes meaningless
- The reasoning treats property crimes as technical legal questions rather than violations of divine and natural justice
- The cumulative effect is to assure the rich that they may retain ill-gotten wealth with a clear conscience, provided they consult the right casuist
- The Jesuit system effectively abolishes the obligation of restitution ([009-064])
