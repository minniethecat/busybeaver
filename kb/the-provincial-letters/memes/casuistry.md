## Definition

Casuistry is the method of ethical reasoning that resolves moral problems by applying theoretical rules to particular instances (cases or "cases of conscience"). In the context of 17th-century Catholic theology, it became associated with the Jesuit practice of providing detailed guidance for confessors.

The method proceeds by analogy: a novel moral situation is compared to established cases, and principles from those precedents are applied. This requires a body of authoritative opinions — which Jesuit theologians compiled extensively.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] uses the term "casuist" over thirty times in the *Provincial Letters*, always with satirical intent. The casuist, in his portrait, is a moral technician who deploys distinctions to dissolve obligations. (006-017)

## Role in the Controversy

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s *Provincial Letters* are a sustained attack on Jesuit casuistry as a moral system. He argues that the case-based approach led to moral laxity, where almost any action could be justified through clever reasoning and doctrinal loopholes.

The Jesuit monk in Letters V–X serves as a dramatic device: by having the casuist candidly explain his method, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] lets the system indict itself. Key targets include:

| Technique | Description | [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s Objection |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| [[probabilism\|Probabilism]] | Follow any [[probabilism|probable opinion]] | Makes morality a function of scholarly fashion |
| Directing the intention | Justify acts by redirecting purpose | Allows evil means for ostensibly good ends |
| Mental reservation | Speak with hidden meaning | Destroys the integrity of language |
| Distinction multiplication | Split concepts to create exceptions | Gradually erases every moral prohibition |

> "The new casuists, to accommodate the law of God to the interests of men, have invented a method of evading it which must be highly acceptable to all reasonable people." (006-011, paraphrase)

## Positions

Casuistry as a method was not uniquely Jesuit. Different schools employed it to different ends:

**Jesuit casuistry** emphasized pastoral accommodation. Confessors were trained to find reasons for absolution rather than grounds for refusal, on the principle that penitents should not be driven away from the sacrament by excessive rigor.

**[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist rigorism** rejected this approach. The [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists, following [[Jansenius]], held that the ancient Church's penitential discipline was the norm and that modern casuistry had abandoned it in favor of laxity.

**Dominican casuistry** occupied a middle position. Dominican theologians like Cajetan developed extensive case analyses but generally applied stricter standards than the Jesuits, particularly on matters of economic ethics.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] exploits these differences by citing Dominican authorities who agree with [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist criticisms of Jesuit moral theology — a rhetorical strategy that prevents his adversaries from dismissing him as merely partisan.

## Relationship to Probabilism

Casuistry and [[probabilism]] are distinct but complementary doctrines often conflated in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s polemic.

Casuistry is the **method**: case-based moral reasoning from authorities, precedents, and analogies. [[probabilism|Probabilism]] is the **decision rule**: when authorities conflict, one may follow any [[probabilism|probable opinion]].

One can practice casuistry without probabilism — Dominican rigorists did extensive case analysis but required following the more [[probabilism|probable opinion]]. And one can theoretically hold [[probabilism|probabilist]] principles without elaborate casuistry — though in practice, probabilism requires the archive of opinions that casuistry generates.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s deepest objection targets the combination: a method that generates lenient opinions, coupled with a rule that makes any such opinion sufficient for conscience. Together, they form what he portrays as a machine for manufacturing moral permission.

## Legacy

The *Provincial Letters* permanently associated casuistry with moral laxity in the European imagination. The very word acquired pejorative connotations — "mere casuistry" became shorthand for sophistical reasoning that evades moral truth.

Within Catholic moral theology, the critique had lasting effects. [[innocent-x|Pope Innocent X]]I's condemnation of 65 laxist propositions (1679) validated some of [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s concerns, though without endorsing [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ism. St. Alphonsus Liguori's development of equiprobabilism in the 18th century represented an attempt to chart a middle course.

In philosophy, casuistry was rehabilitated in the late 20th century by bioethicists who argued that case-based reasoning is essential for applied ethics. This revival treats casuistry not as a system for evading rules but as a method for applying principles to complex realities — returning to the practice's pre-[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]ian meaning.
