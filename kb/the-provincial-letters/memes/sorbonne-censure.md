## Overview

The Sorbonne censure of Antoine [[Arnauld]] was the theological proceeding that directly provoked [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] to write the *Provincial Letters*. Between November 1655 and January 1656, the Faculty of Theology of Paris examined propositions drawn from [[Arnauld]]'s published writings and ultimately condemned him — a decision [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s letters contest on grounds of both procedure and substance.

The dispute centered on two questions: whether five propositions attributed to [[Jansenius|Cornelius Jansen]] were actually in his book *Augustinus* (a question of fact), and whether [[Arnauld]]'s own theological opinions were heretical (a question of faith).

## Background

The roots of the censure lay in the long-running conflict over [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ism at the University of Paris. In 1649, Nicolas Cornet, syndic of the Faculty of Theology, extracted five propositions from [[Jansenius|Jansen]]'s *Augustinus* and submitted them for condemnation. These propositions concerned the nature of efficacious grace, free will, and merit.

The Faculty was deeply divided. Some doctors, including [[Arnauld]], argued that while the propositions might be heretical in certain senses, they were not actually taught by [[Jansenius|Jansen]] in the sense condemned. Others — allied with the Jesuits — pressed for swift condemnation of both the propositions and anyone who defended [[Jansenius|Jansen]].

The dispute escalated because it involved not merely abstract theology but institutional power: who controlled the teaching authority of the most prestigious theological faculty in Catholic Europe.

## The Proceedings

In November 1655, [[Arnauld]] published his *Second Letter to a Duke and Peer of France*, in which he defended the orthodoxy of his positions on grace. The letter contained two assertions that became the basis for the censure:

1. That [[Arnauld]] could not find the five propositions in [[Jansenius]]'s book (a **question of fact**)
2. That the doctrine of efficacious grace was the teaching of Saint Augustine and the Church (a **question of faith**)

The Faculty examined these assertions through a series of assemblies. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] reports on the proceedings with vivid detail:

> "I was under the impression that the disputes in the Sorbonne were vastly important, and deeply affecting the interests of religion." (001-003)

The proceedings were contentious. Seventy-one doctors initially undertook [[Arnauld]]'s defense, but when the question of fact and the question of faith were separated, the majority voted to censure [[Arnauld]] on the question of fact while acknowledging his orthodoxy on the question of faith. (001-007)

The *Provincial Letters* are dated precisely to this timeline — the first letter appeared on January 23, 1656, while the proceedings were ongoing, making the letters a form of real-time intervention in a live controversy.

## Role in the Letters

The censure is the narrative frame for the entire *Provincial Letters*, but its prominence varies across the sequence:

**Letters I–IV** are set at the Sorbonne and report directly on the proceedings. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s narrator visits theologians, reports on factional maneuvers, and documents the procedural irregularities that, in his telling, render the censure illegitimate.

**Letters V–X** shift focus from the censure itself to the broader system of Jesuit moral theology that, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] argues, lies behind the proceedings. The Sorbonne becomes a backdrop against which the real stakes of the controversy — the nature of Christian morality — are explored.

**Letters XI–XVI** continue the attack on Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]ry while periodically returning to the Sorbonne as a reference point. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents the censure as the product of a theological system that has abandoned rigor for accommodation.

**Letters XVII–XIX** return to the doctrinal core of the dispute, with [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] defending the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist position on grace and arguing that the censure rests on a confusion between questions of fact and questions of faith.

## Outcome and Significance

[[Arnauld]] was formally censured and excluded from the Sorbonne, but the *Provincial Letters* transformed the controversy from an academic dispute into a public debate. The censure became symbolic of the conflict between [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist rigor and Jesuit accommodation, and between institutional authority and intellectual conscience.

The procedural questions [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] raised — about the separation of fact and faith, the role of majority voting in theological judgments, and the legitimacy of condemning a person rather than a doctrine — had lasting resonance in Catholic theological discourse.

The censure also demonstrated the political dimensions of theological conflict in 17th-century France. The Jesuits, the Sorbonne Faculty, the French bishops, the papacy, and the Crown all had interests in the outcome, making the dispute far more than an academic exercise.

> "It is an established fact that the five propositions are not in Jansenius. They are innocent of all error, and it is a strange kind of heresy to be innocent." (018-044, paraphrase)
