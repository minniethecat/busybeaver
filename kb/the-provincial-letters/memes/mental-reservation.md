## Definition

Mental reservation (*restrictio mentalis*) is a doctrine in moral theology holding that a person may make a statement that is literally false if the speaker internally qualifies or "reserves" part of the meaning in their own mind. For example, saying "I did not do it" while mentally adding "today" or "in the way you mean" makes the external statement technically "true" in the speaker's conscience. The doctrine was developed by Jesuit casuists to address situations where telling the literal truth would cause harm, but Pascal condemns it as a systematic justification of lying.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal treats mental reservation as perhaps the most shocking example of Jesuit casuistry's moral laxity. In Letter IX, he quotes Father Sanchez's teaching that "a man may swear that he has not done a thing, though he has actually done it, by understanding within himself that he did not do it on a certain day, or before he was born" (010-064). For Pascal, this is not a nuanced resolution of a difficult case but a license to commit perjury — a doctrine that destroys the very possibility of truthful speech and oath-taking.

The doctrine of mental reservations is closely linked to the broader doctrine of equivocations (010-060), which Pascal presents as a standing resource that the Jesuits deploy whenever their other casuistical arguments fail. Together, these doctrines represent the endpoint of the probabilist logic: if any probable opinion justifies an action, and if words can be made to mean whatever the speaker intends, then no moral prohibition can survive.

## Positions

**Jesuit View (as presented by Pascal)**: Mental reservation is a legitimate application of the principle that speech is governed by intention. As long as the speaker's internal qualification makes the statement true in their own mind, the external falsehood is not a lie. Father Sanchez is cited as the key authority, teaching that the use of mental reservations requires only that one "be expert in the use of these reservations" (010-068). The doctrine is defended on the grounds that truth-telling obligations are not absolute when serious harm would result from honest speech.

**Pascal's Critique**: Pascal presents mental reservation as self-evidently scandalous. His strategy is to quote the doctrine in the casuists' own words and let the reader recoil. The doctrine, he argues, makes perjury permissible, destroys the social bond of truthful speech, and contradicts both Scripture and natural law. He contrasts the "subtleties of your modern equivocations" (017-012) with the straightforward honesty required by Christian morality, asking rhetorically: "Who ever thought of using" such devices before the rise of Jesuit casuistry?

**Traditional View**: Christian moral theology has always condemned lying as intrinsically evil. Pascal invokes this tradition against the Jesuit innovations, arguing that the early Church Fathers and medieval theologians would have been horrified by the doctrine of mental reservation.

## Key Passages

> "I mean the doctrine of mental reservations. 'A man may swear,' as Sanchez says, 'that he has not done a thing, though he has actually done it, by understanding within himself that he did not do it on a certain day, or before he was born.'" (010-064)

> "In such cases, our doctrine of equivocations has been found of admirable service." (010-060)

> "It is only necessary that one be expert in the use of these reservations." (010-068)

> "Who ever thought of using the wicked subtleties of your modern equivocations?" (017-012)

Pascal's quotation of Sanchez at length (010-064) is characteristic of his method throughout the *Letters*: he lets the Jesuit authorities condemn themselves out of their own mouths. The phrase "it is only necessary that one be expert" (010-068) carries heavy irony — moral theology, which should guide the conscience toward virtue, has become a technical skill in deception.
