## Role in the Controversy

Gregorio de Valencia occupies a foundational position in the *Provincial Letters* not as a direct participant in the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy but as one of the great authorities of early Jesuit moral theology whose writings provided the intellectual framework for the [[casuistry|casuist]]ic positions [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] attacks. When the Jesuit monk introduces [[Escobar]]'s *Moral Theology*, he names Valencia among the "four living creatures" to whom "Jesus presents it thus sealed" — alongside [[suarez|Suarez]], Vasquez, and [[molina|Molina]] — identifying Valencia as one of the four pillars of the Jesuit moral-theological tradition from which [[Escobar]] synthesized his doctrines. (006-021)

Valencia died in 1603, four decades before the composition of the *Provincial Letters*, yet his authority pervades the work. He appears across three distinct letters on three distinct topics — simony, attrition, and [[casuistry|casuistic method]] — demonstrating the breadth of his influence on the moral theology [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] critiques. Unlike contemporaries who engaged directly in the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist polemics, Valencia's role is that of a remote authority: his arguments are invoked by later Jesuits to justify positions that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] finds morally indefensible, placing him in the genealogy of error the *Letters* construct.

## Key Positions

Valencia's moral theology, as refracted through the *Letters*, addresses several of the core disputes of the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Palliative for simony | Valencia developed arguments allowing beneficiaries to avoid simony charges through interpretation of terms and circumstances | 007-028 |
| Attrition and sacrament | Valencia's views on attrition were cited in College of Clermont theses on the sufficiency of merely natural attrition | 011-050 |
| Founding authority of [[casuistry|casuist]]ry | Named by [[Escobar]] as one of the four principal Jesuit theologians whose work underlies the *Moral Theology* | 006-021 |

The most detailed citation of Valencia in the *Letters* concerns his treatment of simony among ecclesiastical beneficiaries. The Jesuit monk explains that "it was rendered highly necessary for our fathers to exercise their prudence in finding out a palliative" for the traffic in benefices, and "with what success they have done so will appear from the following words of Valencia." (007-028) [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents Valencia's reasoning as emblematic of the [[casuistry|casuistic method]]: the application of ingenious distinctions to render permissible what the plain sense of traditional moral teaching would condemn.

> "the following words of Valencia, who is one of Escobar's 'four living creatures'" (007-028)

In Letter X, Valencia reappears in connection with the theology of attrition. The narrator cites "the celebrated Jesuit Father Valencia" (Tom. iv., disp. 7) to illustrate the extreme positions Jesuit theologians had reached on the sufficiency of attrition for the sacrament — a topic at the heart of the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist charge that Jesuit moral theology lowered the requirements for absolution. (011-050)

## Biography

Gregorio de Valencia was born in Medina del Campo, Spain, in 1549 and entered the Society of Jesus in 1565. After studying at the University of Salamanca, he was appointed professor of theology at the Jesuit college in Dillingen, Bavaria, where he taught from 1575 to 1597. He later held the chair of theology at the Roman College, the principal Jesuit institution of higher learning.

Valencia was among the most influential Jesuit theologians of the late sixteenth century. His four-volume *Commentarii Theologici* (published 1591–1597) addressed the full range of Scholastic theology and became a standard reference work in Jesuit seminaries throughout Europe. His treatment of moral questions — particularly on probabilism, simony, and the theology of the sacraments — shaped the [[casuistry|casuist]]ic tradition that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] would later subject to sustained critique. Valencia's death in 1603 at Naples placed him beyond the reach of the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy, but his intellectual legacy ensured his prominence in the *Letters*.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Valencia's principal relationship in the *Letters* is to [[Escobar]], whose *Moral Theology* explicitly names Valencia among its four founding authorities alongside [[Suarez]], [[Vasquez]], and [[Molina]]. (006-021) This grouping places Valencia at the summit of the Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]ic tradition, as the source from which [[Escobar]]'s systematization descends.

The citation of Valencia in Letter X alongside the College of Clermont theses (011-050) links his theology to the institutional teaching of the French Jesuits at their flagship college — a connection [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] exploits to argue that the laxist positions he attacks are not marginal aberrations but the official doctrine of the Society of Jesus. Valencia's status as a theologian who died before the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy began makes him, in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s construction, an unwitting architect of the moral system the *Letters* seek to dismantle.
