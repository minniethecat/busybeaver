## Definition

A **vow** (Latin: *votum*) is, in Catholic theology, a deliberate and free promise made to God concerning a possible and better good, by which a person binds themselves under pain of sin to fulfil what they have promised. Religious vows—particularly those of poverty, chastity, and obedience—constitute the foundation of consecrated religious life. In the *Provincial Letters*, Pascal addresses vows primarily in the context of casuistic reasoning that releases the faithful from their binding force.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal's treatment of vows forms part of his broader critique of Jesuit casuistry as a system that systematically weakens moral obligation. He focuses on two specific applications: the Lenten vow and the vow of obedience to religious superiors.

In Letter VII, Pascal cites Jesuit authorities who teach that a person "bound by a particular vow to a Lenten life" may nevertheless eat flesh during Lent ([007-015])—effectively nullifying the vow's practical content. More broadly, he reports the maxim that a religious who finds obedience to superiors burdensome "is no longer bound by his vow of obedience" ([007-039]).

Pascal presents these relaxations not as isolated instances but as symptoms of a systematic approach to moral obligation. The casuistic method, he argues, treats vows not as binding commitments to God but as provisional arrangements that can be set aside whenever they become inconvenient. This approach, he contends, reflects the same logic that permits the rich to ignore alms obligations and penitents to substitute attrition for genuine contrition: the reduction of Christian morality to a set of manageable minimums.

The theme of obedience runs throughout the *Letters*. Pascal contrasts the Jesuit approach—which finds ways to release the faithful from vows and obligations—with the Jansenist insistence that authentic Christian life demands wholehearted submission to divine and ecclesiastical authority.

## Positions

### Jesuit Casuist Position

The Jesuit authorities cited by Pascal held:

- A person bound by a vow to keep Lent may nevertheless eat flesh during the penitential season ([007-015])
- A religious who finds obedience difficult "is no longer bound by his vow of obedience" ([007-039])
- The obligation of vows can be relaxed through casuistic distinctions about the circumstances and intention of the person bound
- Religious superiors may be circumvented through the use of probable opinions that release subordinates from obedience

### Pascal's Critique

Pascal treats these positions as fundamentally destructive of religious life:

- A vow is a promise to God; releasing oneself from it through human reasoning usurps divine authority
- The relaxation of the vow of obedience strikes at the foundation of monastic and religious discipline
- The pattern mirrors the broader Jesuit tendency to substitute human accommodation for divine commandment
- The obligation of religious obedience is not dissolved by the difficulty of fulfilling it ([007-039])
