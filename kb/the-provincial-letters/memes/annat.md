## Role in the Controversy

Father François Annat is one of the few figures in the *Provincial Letters* who appears not merely as a historical authority to be cited but as a living, active adversary engaged in the same controversy as [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]. As confessor to King Louis XIV and a prominent Jesuit polemicist, Annat represented the institutional power of the Society of Jesus at the highest levels of the French state. His writings against [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ism provided the theological justification for the campaign of repression that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] sought to resist.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] introduces Annat in Letter V through a classic polemical gesture: the Jesuit monk, eager to demonstrate the authority of his [[casuistry|casuist]]s, produces Annat's book, and the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist narrator discovers in it a doctrine that appears to exempt a vast swath of humanity from ever having committed sin. (005-017, 005-025) This pattern — the Jesuit citing Annat as an authority, the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist exposing the absurdity of Annat's doctrine — recurs throughout the letters.

Annat's unique role as both theologian and agent of state power makes him an especially dangerous adversary in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s portrayal. When Annat writes against the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists, he does so not merely as a private theologian but with the implicit backing of the Crown. His arguments carry the threat of temporal as well as spiritual penalties. This fusion of theological and political authority is, for [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]], emblematic of the corruption that the *Letters* seek to expose.

Annat's theological interventions in the controversy, as documented and contested by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Righteous and sinless pagans | According to Annat, the righteous before the Law never committed sin because their ignorance of the Law excused them | 005-017, 005-025 |
| Defense of Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]ry | Along with Pintereau, [[le-moine|Le Moine]], and Antoine [[le-moine|Le Moine]], defended Jesuit moral theology against [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist charges | 011-055 |
| Opposition to [[Arnauld]] | Active participant in the campaign against [[Arnauld|Antoine Arnauld]] and the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist cause | 017-023 |
| Anti-[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist polemics | Published works attacking [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist theology, cited and refuted by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] | 017-023 |

> "Here is the book of Father Annat." (005-017)

The most consequential of Annat's doctrines for [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s polemic is the claim concerning the righteous before the Law. Annat holds that "they have never committed sin, because the righteous were excused by their ignorance of the Law." (005-025) This position, which extends the Baunian doctrine of ignorance-excusing-sin to the entirety of pre-Mosaic humanity, becomes in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s hands a reductio ad absurdum of the Jesuit method: if ignorance excuses sin, and the righteous before the Law were ignorant, then they never sinned — a conclusion that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents as self-evidently incompatible with Christian teaching on original sin and universal human fallenness.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s treatment of Annat differs from his treatment of earlier Jesuit theologians like [[Escobar]] or [[Bauny]] in one crucial respect: Annat is a contemporary who can respond. This gives the polemic an edge of immediacy absent from the historical citations. When [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] exposes a contradiction in Annat's reasoning, he is not merely scoring a point against a dead authority but challenging a living adversary to answer.

## Biography

François Annat was born in 1590 in Rodez, France. He entered the Society of Jesus in 1607 and pursued a distinguished ecclesiastical career, serving as professor of theology at the College of Clermont in Paris, rector of several Jesuit colleges, and provincial superior of the French Jesuits.

In 1654, Annat was appointed confessor to King Louis XIV, a position of immense influence that placed him at the intersection of theology, politics, and court life. It was from this position of institutional power that he engaged the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy. His 1656 work *Le Rabat-joie des Jansénistes* (*The Killjoy of the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists*) was a direct response to the early *Provincial Letters*, and [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s Letter XVII is structured as a rebuttal to Annat's arguments.

Annat's tenure as royal confessor lasted until 1670, the year of his death. Throughout this period, he remained a consistent opponent of [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ism, using his influence at court to advocate for the enforcement of anti-[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist bulls and the suppression of Port-Royal. His longevity in office — sixteen years, spanning the entire period of the *Provincial Letters* controversy and beyond — testifies to his political skill and to the alignment of his theological commitments with the Crown's interest in religious uniformity.

Letter XVII represents [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s most sustained engagement with Annat. Here [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] abandons the satirical mode that dominates the middle letters and adopts a more directly argumentative style, accusing Annat of systematically misrepresenting [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist doctrine. The charge is grave: Annat, a royal confessor speaking with the authority of both Church and Crown, is engaged in what [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] characterizes as deliberate falsification of his opponents' positions. (017-023)

## Relationship to Other Figures

As royal confessor, Annat occupied a position fundamentally different from that of the [[casuistry|casuist]]s [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] attacks in the middle letters. Where [[Escobar]] and [[Bauny]] are discredited academic authorities whose writings can be mined for absurdities, Annat is a living wielder of institutional power whose arguments must be engaged and refuted in the present tense. His alignment with the Crown makes him, in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s construction, the most dangerous kind of adversary: one who can translate theological error into political persecution.

Annat's relationship with his fellow Jesuits is one of mutual reinforcement. Pintereau, [[le-moine|Le Moine]], and Antoine [[le-moine|Le Moine]] are grouped with him as defenders of the Society's moral theology (011-055), and [[meynier|Meynier]] is named alongside him as a "perverter" of [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist texts (017-023). This collective framing is essential to [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s polemical strategy: Annat is not an isolated figure but the most prominent representative of a coordinated Jesuit campaign.

His relationship with [[Arnauld]] is one of direct antagonism. As [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] reminds his readers, the whole controversy began with the Sorbonne's censure of [[Arnauld]] — an action in which Annat, as a leading theological voice of the Society, was almost certainly involved. The *Provincial Letters* can be read, at one level, as an extended reply to Annat and his fellow anti-[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist polemicists, written in a form that reached a public far wider than theological tracts could command.
