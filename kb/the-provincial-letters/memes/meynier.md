## Role in the Controversy

Father Meynier enters the *Provincial Letters* not as a theologian or [[casuistry|casuist]] but as a polemicist — the author of a scurrilous anti-[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist pamphlet that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] treats as Exhibit A of the Jesuit method of controversy. In Letter XVI, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] identifies Meynier as having "the impudence" to make false accusations against Port-Royal, and his pamphlet becomes the occasion for one of [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s most sustained defenses of [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist orthodoxy and personal integrity. (016-019)

Meynier's pamphlet, published under his own name with ecclesiastical approval, accused [[Arnauld]] and other Port-Royal figures of heresy and moral deviance. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s response, occupying the better part of Letters XVI and XVII, transforms Meynier's attack into an opportunity to demonstrate what [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents as the systematic dishonesty of Jesuit controversial method. The narrator charges that Meynier falsified quotations, misrepresented doctrines, and attributed to the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists positions they had never held.

In the dramatic structure of the *Letters*, Meynier serves as the personal antagonist whose excesses justify [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s escalation from satirical critique to direct accusation of calumny. Where earlier letters had mocked the absurdities of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic reasoning, the Meynier episode introduces a graver charge: that the Jesuit campaign against Port-Royal was conducted through deliberate falsehood. (017-023, 017-024)

## Key Positions

Meynier's role in the controversy is defined not by theological doctrines but by polemical methods that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] characterizes as systematically dishonest:

| Charge | Evidence | Key PN |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Falsification of quotations | Meynier misrepresented [[Arnauld]]'s words to make them appear heretical | 017-014, 017-024 |
| Calumny against Port-Royal | Meynier accused [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists of doctrines they explicitly rejected | 016-019 |
| Falsification of [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] | Meynier tampered with conciliar texts to convict [[Arnauld]] | 017-024 |
| Attack on St. Cyran | Meynier distorted the teachings of the Abbé de Saint-Cyran | 017-018 |
| Defense of Jesuit positions | Meynier maintained controversial doctrines on grace and free will | 017-029 |

The most serious accusation [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] levels against Meynier is the falsification of the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]]. The narrator charges that Meynier, "conscious of the wretched deficiency of your proofs," had "recourse to a new artifice, which is nothing less than to falsify the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]], in order to convict [[Arnauld|M. Arnauld]]." (017-024) This charge — that a Jesuit polemicist would tamper with the text of an ecumenical council — is among the gravest [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] makes in the entire *Letters*.

> "Conscious, however, of the wretched deficiency of your proofs, you have had recourse to a new artifice, which is nothing less than to falsify the Council of Trent, in order to convict M. Arnauld." (017-024)

The Meynier episode also illuminates the institutional dimension of the controversy. Meynier's pamphlet carried ecclesiastical approval, meaning that the calumnies [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] attributes to him were, in the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist view, sanctioned by the Jesuit order's hierarchy. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] names Meynier alongside [[Annat]] — the Jesuit provincial of Paris and confessor to the king — as co-authors of "the capital count in your indictment" (017-023), linking Meynier's pamphlet to the highest levels of Jesuit authority.

## Biography

The biographical record of Father Meynier is sparse. He was a French Jesuit active in the 1650s, during the height of the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy. His anti-[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist pamphlet, published with the approval of his Jesuit superiors, was one of many such publications that appeared during the period of the *Provincial Letters* — part of a broader campaign by the Society of Jesus to secure the condemnation of [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist theology by both the Sorbonne and the Holy See.

Meynier's pamphlet targeted not only [[Arnauld]]'s theology but also the memory of the Abbé de Saint-Cyran, the spiritual father of Port-Royal who had died in 1643. (017-018) This targeting of the deceased — a figure revered by the Port-Royal community — was particularly offensive to [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] and his allies, and [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s response to Meynier's attack on Saint-Cyran is among the most emotionally charged passages in the *Letters*.

The pamphlet's specific title and publication details are not preserved in the *Letters* themselves, and Meynier's subsequent career is not well documented. His significance to the historical record derives almost entirely from [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s use of him as the embodiment of Jesuit controversial malpractice — a role that has ensured his name survives, if only as a footnote to one of the great polemical works of French literature.

## References in the Letters

Meynier appears primarily in Letters XVI and XVII, where [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] devotes sustained attention to refuting his pamphlet:

| Letter | NNN | Content | Key PN |
|--------|-----|---------|--------|
| Letter XVI | 016 | Impudence of Meynier's accusations; source of Jesuit slanders against Port-Royal | 016-019 |
| Letter XVII | 017 | Meynier's pamphlet on its front; falsification of [[Arnauld]]'s words; attack on St. Cyran; falsification of [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] | 017-012, 017-014, 017-018, 017-023, 017-024, 017-029 |

The concentration of Meynier's appearances in two letters reflects [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s decision to treat his pamphlet as a case study in Jesuit controversy rather than to distribute the refutation across the entire series. This concentrated treatment allows [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] to build a cumulative case: each successive falsification he attributes to Meynier adds weight to the charge of systematic dishonesty.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Meynier is positioned in the *Letters* as part of a coordinated Jesuit campaign against Port-Royal, operating alongside [[Annat]] — "your Fathers [[annat|Annat]] and Meynier, which forms the capital count in your indictment" (017-023). This pairing links Meynier's pamphlet to the highest institutional authority of the French Jesuits, since [[annat|Annat]] was the Jesuit provincial. The implication is clear: Meynier's calumnies were not the work of a rogue polemicist but part of an officially sanctioned strategy.

Meynier's attack on Saint-Cyran (017-018) and on [[Arnauld]] (017-014) places him in direct opposition to the two founding figures of Port-Royal. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s defense of both men through his rebuttal of Meynier serves to reinforce the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist claim to represent authentic Catholic tradition against Jesuit innovation.

The Meynier episode also illuminates the relationship between [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] and his provincial addressee. By devoting two full letters to the refutation of a single pamphlet, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] signals to his provincial correspondent that the controversy is not merely a theological dispute but a moral contest over truthfulness itself — a framing that raises the stakes of the entire *Letters*.
