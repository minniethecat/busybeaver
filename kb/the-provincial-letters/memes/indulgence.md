## Definition

In the *Provincial Letters*, Pascal uses "indulgence" (*indulgence*) primarily in a non-technical sense, referring to the excessive leniency and permissiveness that he attributes to Jesuit moral theology. While the term has a precise theological meaning in Catholic doctrine — the remission of temporal punishment due to sins already forgiven — Pascal's usage is broader and more polemical. He employs it to characterize the entire Jesuit project of relaxing moral obligations under the cover of probable opinions. The Jesuits, he charges, have transformed the confessional into a place of "comforts and indulgences" (009-067) rather than a tribunal of penitence.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal deploys the language of indulgence to frame the Jesuit moral system as a systematic accommodation of human weakness rather than a call to conversion. When the Jesuit father boasts of the "comforts and indulgences which our fathers allow" (009-067), Pascal treats this as an admission that the Society measures its success by how effectively it relieves the faithful of moral burdens — not by how faithfully it transmits the demands of the Gospel.

The charge of indulgence functions as a unifying theme in Pascal's polemic. Whether he is discussing the relaxation of fasting obligations, the permission to fight duels, the reduction of the duty to love God, or the softening of the requirements for sacramental absolution, Pascal consistently characterizes the Jesuit approach as "indulgence" — a word that simultaneously suggests pastoral accommodation and moral laxity. He warns the Jesuit father that "this indulgence of yours will shock more people than you imagine" (010-091), predicting that the faithful themselves will recognize the excess when they see it clearly.

Pascal also applies the concept to the broader dynamics of the controversy. He speaks of a "grace which seems to increase by indulgence" (018-007), referring to how the Jesuits' permissive attitude toward their own polemical methods — including calumny and misrepresentation — appears to grow bolder the more it is tolerated. This usage links the moral-theological sense of indulgence to the rhetorical conduct of the Society in the controversy itself.

While Pascal does not extensively discuss the technical theological concept of indulgences (the remission of temporal punishment), his choice of this particular term carries weight in a seventeenth-century Catholic context. The sale of indulgences had been one of the precipitating causes of the Protestant Reformation a century earlier, and the word retained its resonance as a symbol of ecclesiastical corruption. By characterizing Jesuit moral teaching as a system of "indulgences," Pascal implicitly associates his opponents with the very abuses that had torn Christendom apart.

## Positions

**Jesuit position**: The Jesuits would have defended their accommodations as exercises of pastoral prudence, not indulgence in the pejorative sense. They argued that adapting the rigor of the moral law to the weakness of the penitent was an act of mercy, not laxity. The "indulgences" Pascal condemns were, in their view, legitimate applications of the Church's power to bind and loose.

**Jansenist / Pascal's position**: Pascal treats the Jesuit "indulgence" as a corruption of genuine pastoral mercy. True mercy, in his Augustinian framework, does not lower the moral standard to accommodate sin but provides the grace necessary to meet the standard. By lowering the standard itself, the Jesuits have confused indulgence with charity and have left penitents in their sins rather than drawing them out of them.

**Theological context**: The Council of Trent had reformed the practice of indulgences in response to Protestant critiques, affirming their theological validity while strictly regulating their application. Pascal's polemic does not challenge the doctrine of indulgences as such but appropriates the word's negative connotations to characterize Jesuit moral theology as a whole.

## Key Passages

> "The comforts and indulgences which our fathers allow." (009-067)

Pascal's Jesuit interlocutor speaks these words with pride; Pascal reports them with irony, treating the boast as self-condemnation.

> "This indulgence of yours will shock more people than you imagine." (010-091)

Pascal predicts that the laity themselves will recoil from Jesuit permissiveness once they understand its full extent — a rhetorical strategy that positions Pascal as the defender of ordinary Catholic conscience against elite casuistical sophistication.

> "A grace which seems to increase by indulgence." (018-007)

Pascal observes a pattern in Jesuit polemical conduct: the more their methods are tolerated, the bolder they become — a dynamic he links metaphorically to the moral-theological concept of indulgence.
