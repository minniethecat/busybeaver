## Role in the Controversy

Étienne Bauny is one of the most frequently cited and satirized Jesuit [[casuistry|casuists]] in the *Provincial Letters*, appearing by name across no fewer than eight letters. His *Summary of Sins* (1633) serves as [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s principal quarry for the laxist maxims attributed to the Jesuits in Letter IV, where the entire satire on "sins of ignorance" is built around Bauny's doctrines.

Within the dramatic economy of the letters, Bauny enters the scene with theatrical effect: the Jesuit monk, "laden with books," thrusts Bauny's *Summary of Sins* into the narrator's hands, announcing it as "the fifth edition too, you see, which shows that it is a good book." (005-012) This moment inaugurates the sustained satirical examination of Jesuit moral theology that occupies the following letters.

The historical introduction to the *Provincial Letters* records that Bauny's influence extended beyond the text into popular culture: his "grotesque maxims furnished topics for perpetual badinage," making him, alongside [[Escobar]], a byword for Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]ic laxity. (P03-041) His doctrines were formally condemned by the General Assembly of the French clergy in 1642 as "leading to licentiousness, and the corruption of good manners." (FN 120)

## Key Positions

Bauny's moral theology, as presented by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]], centers on doctrines that radically narrow the scope of what constitutes sin and loosen the obligations of confessors:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Sin requires prior knowledge of evil | No act is sinful unless the agent perceives, knows, and comprehends its good and evil qualities beforehand | 005-014, 005-045 |
| Proximate occasions of sin | Absolution may not be refused to those who remain in proximate occasions of sin if leaving would cause inconvenience | 006-039, 011-039 |
| Directly seeking occasion of sin | One may seek an occasion of sin "directly and designedly" (*primo et per se*) for spiritual or temporal advantage | 006-041, 011-041 |
| Confessor's obligation | When the penitent follows a [[probabilism|probable opinion]], the confessor is bound to absolve him, under pain of mortal sin | 006-066 |
| Tacit Church approbation | If an opinion has been advanced by a [[casuistry|casuist]] and not impugned by the Church, the Church approves it | 007-021 |
| Absolution without amendment | Absolution must be granted even to those living in habitual sins with no prospect of future amendment | 011-029, 011-031 |
| Women's adornment | A woman does not sin by adorning herself even knowing the bad effect on the virtue of beholders | 010-078 |
| Envy distinction | Envy of spiritual good is mortal; envy of temporal good is only venial | 010-046 |
| Restitution | One who asks a soldier to beat a neighbor or burn a barn is not bound to make restitution | 009-035 |
| Servants' wages | Servants dissatisfied with their wages may take property to make them equivalent | 007-043 |

The overarching theme is a **pastoral laxism** that, in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s telling, dissolves moral responsibility through elaborate conceptual distinctions. Bauny's doctrine that an action is not voluntary unless the agent "perceives, knows, and comprehends what is good and what is evil in it" (005-045) is the most philosophically ambitious of these, resting on what the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist interlocutor demonstrates to be a misreading of Aristotle's *Ethics* on the nature of voluntary action and ignorance. (005-047, 005-048)

> "In order to sin and become culpable in the sight of God, it is necessary to know that the thing we wish to do is not good, or at least to doubt that it is---to fear or to judge that God takes no pleasure in the action which we contemplate, but forbids it; and in spite of this, to commit the deed, leap the fence, and transgress." (005-014)

This definition became the object of intense ridicule. [[hallier|M. Hallier]], before his reconciliation with the Jesuits, famously applied to Bauny the words of John the Baptist: *Ecce qui tollit peccata mundi* --- "Behold the man that taketh away the sins of the world." (005-015)

> "What has the censure of Rome to do with that of France?" (007-019)

Bauny's defiant reply when his books were censured at Rome exemplifies, in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s portrayal, the Jesuit habit of evading ecclesiastical authority through procedural finesse. It is complemented by his grand maxim on tacit approbation: "if an opinion has been advanced by some [[casuistry|casuist]], and has not been impugned by the Church, it is a sign that she approves of it." (007-021)

## Biography

Étienne Bauny (also spelled Bauni) was born in 1564 in France. He entered the Society of Jesus and became a prominent moral theologian and [[casuistry|casuist]], publishing his major work, the *Somme des péchés* (*Summary of Sins*), in 1633. Written in French rather than Latin "that it may be understood by everybody" (009-019), the *Summary* was a comprehensive manual for confessors, cataloguing sins and their degrees of culpability.

The work enjoyed considerable popularity, running through at least five editions. However, it also attracted severe criticism. In 1642, the General Assembly of the French clergy formally censured Bauny's moral theology, finding in it propositions "leading to licentiousness, and the corruption of good manners, violating natural equity, and excusing blasphemy, usury, simony, and other heinous sins, as trivial matters." (FN 120)

Bauny died in 1649, seven years before [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] began publishing the *Provincial Letters*. He thus did not live to see his name become, through [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s satire, synonymous with the laxist tendency in Jesuit [[casuistry]]. His doctrines were nonetheless vigorously defended by fellow Jesuits in the *Apology for the Casuists* (1657), written by Father Pirot under the Society's direction. (FN 120)

The *Summary of Sins* was designed as a practical guide for confessors rather than a work of speculative theology. Its French language and accessible format contributed to its wide circulation but also made it more vulnerable to [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s polemical use, since its maxims could be read and ridiculed by a lay audience.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] returns to Bauny in Letter XVI, the last completed letter, where he exposes the Jesuits' contradictory defenses: Father Pintereau condemned Bauny's maxim on absolution without amendment as "damnable" while denying it was Bauny's; [[brisacier|Father Brisacier]] simultaneously admitted it was Bauny's while defending it as sound. This demonstration of bad faith in Bauny's defenders serves as a coda to [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s engagement with the [[casuistry|casuist]] and a summary indictment of Jesuit polemical method. (016-016, 016-017)
