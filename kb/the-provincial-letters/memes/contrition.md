## Definition

Contrition (*contritio*) is, in Catholic sacramental theology, the perfect sorrow for sin that proceeds from the love of God above all things. It is distinguished from *attrition* (imperfect contrition), which is sorrow for sin motivated chiefly by fear of divine punishment, the ugliness of sin, or the loss of eternal reward.

The Council of Trent defined contrition as "a sorrow of the soul and a detestation of the sin committed, with the purpose of sinning no more" (Session XIV, Chapter IV). (011-045)

The crucial practical question — and the one that dominates Pascal's treatment in the *Letters* — is whether contrition is *necessary* for the valid reception of the sacrament of penance, or whether attrition suffices when accompanied by absolution.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal's critique of Jesuit confessional practice turns decisively on the contrition–attrition distinction. The traditional rigorist position, maintained by the Dominicans and the Jansenists, held that contrition (perfect sorrow from love of God) is necessary for justification in the sacrament of penance.

The Jesuit casuists, whose manuals Pascal quotes extensively in Letter X, maintained that attrition (imperfect sorrow) is sufficient — and that attrition can be elicited by the mere fear of hell, or even by the mechanical recitation of a formula. (011-048)

Pascal presents this as the theological mechanism by which the Jesuits make confession easy and absolution automatic. If attrition suffices, and if attrition is as easy to produce as reciting a few words, then no penitent need ever be refused absolution. The confessor becomes not a judge but a dispenser of guaranteed forgiveness. (011-050)

## Positions

**The traditional rigorist position**. The Dominicans and Jansenists, following the medieval tradition and the Council of Trent, maintained that contrition — sorrow flowing from charity — is the ordinary means of justification. Attrition, being motivated by servile fear, is merely a disposition toward contrition, not a substitute for it.

This position makes the sacrament of penance genuinely demanding: the penitent must truly love God above all things, not merely fear his punishments. (011-045)

**The Jesuit position**. The Jesuit casuists cited by Pascal — including Bauny, Escobar, and Filiutius — taught that attrition is sufficient for valid absolution. Some went further, maintaining that attrition need not even include a firm purpose of amendment; the resolution to confess and accept absolution was itself sufficient.

Pascal quotes Escobar's dictum that contrition, far from being necessary, is actually not even desirable in certain cases, because the effort to achieve it might discourage the penitent from confessing at all. (011-052)

**The pastoral argument**. The Jesuits defended their position on pastoral grounds: requiring perfect contrition from every penitent would effectively bar most Christians from the sacrament. The ordinary faithful cannot reliably distinguish contrition from attrition, and demanding what they cannot produce would drive them away from confession altogether.

Pascal's response, implicit throughout Letter X, is that this pastoral argument is a rationalization for laxity — the Church's role is not to lower the standard to what people can easily achieve but to call them to what they ought to become. (011-058)
