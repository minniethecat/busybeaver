## Definition

In the *Provincial Letters*, "ecclesiastical" refers to matters pertaining to the Church's institutional authority — its laws, courts, judgments, and governance structures. Pascal invokes the term to distinguish Church authority from philosophical opinion or personal moral reasoning, and to argue that the Jesuits' casuistical methods undermine legitimate ecclesiastical jurisdiction.

## Role in the Controversy

The concept of ecclesiastical authority is central to Pascal's argumentative strategy. Throughout the *Letters*, he contrasts the binding force of ecclesiastical judgments with the flexible opinions of casuists, arguing that the Jesuits systematically weaken Church discipline by substituting private casuistical opinions for established ecclesiastical law. The Sorbonne's censure of Antoine Arnauld — an ecclesiastical act — becomes the immediate occasion for the controversy, and Pascal repeatedly appeals to papal and conciliar authority as the proper standard for doctrinal judgment.

## Positions

**Jansenist (Pascal's) View**: Ecclesiastical authority — expressed through papal bulls, council decrees, and episcopal judgments — is the supreme and binding norm for doctrinal questions. Pascal argues that the Jesuits evade ecclesiastical judgments by appealing to "probable opinions" of individual theologians, thereby subordinating Church law to private interpretation. He cites historical examples from ecclesiastical history to demonstrate that the Church has always exercised strict judgment over doctrinal error (005-035).

**Jesuit View (as presented by Pascal)**: The Jesuit casuists acknowledge ecclesiastical authority in principle but develop interpretive techniques that effectively neutralize its binding force. By distinguishing between matters of "right" and matters of "fact," and by arguing that a probable opinion suffices to act contrary to ecclesiastical law, the Jesuits create a system in which ecclesiastical statutes become optional guidelines rather than binding commands (015-020).

**Dominican/Thomist View**: The Dominicans occupy an intermediate position, upholding the binding force of ecclesiastical judgments while engaging in theological debate over the interpretation of specific decrees. Pascal presents them as allies in affirming ecclesiastical authority against Jesuit probabilism.

## Key Passages

> "I showed you how far the Jesuits have departed from the decisions of popes and ecclesiastical laws." (014-007)

> "How different must the contrast be with ecclesiastical laws, which must be incomparably more severe." (015-020)

Ecclesiastical history provides Pascal with models of strict pastoral discipline. He notes that the Church has always recognized certain persons as "unworthy of admission into the ecclesiastical state who have been convicted of" serious offenses (017-031), and that even "ecclesiastical judges" were historically barred from attending criminal proceedings to avoid entanglement in bloodshed (008-057).

The authority of ecclesiastical statutes is further illustrated by Pascal's reference to a decree "of Alexander III" which "enacted an ecclesiastical statute" binding on the whole Church (019-032), demonstrating that papal legislative authority is the proper source of binding moral norms.
