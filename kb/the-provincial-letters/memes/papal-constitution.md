## Definition

A papal constitution is a solemn decree issued by the Pope, typically broader in scope than a bull and often addressed to the universal Church. In the *Provincial Letters*, the term appears in connection with the papal documents condemning the five propositions attributed to Jansenius.

Pascal's treatment of papal constitutions is closely intertwined with his treatment of papal bulls; both raise the same fundamental question about the scope of papal teaching authority and its susceptibility to procedural abuse.

## Role in the Controversy

The papal constitution appears at a critical moment in Letter XVII, where Pascal responds to the charge of heresy leveled against him by the Jesuit party. His accusers had argued: the author of the *Letters* is a Port-Royalist; Port-Royal is declared heretical; therefore the author is a heretic.

Pascal's rebuttal turns on the distinction between the doctrinal authority of papal documents and their factual presuppositions.

He demands: "What council have I contradicted? What papal constitution have I violated?" (018-008). The rhetorical force of the question lies in its implied answer: *none*. The papal constitution condemns heretical propositions — propositions Pascal does not hold and has never defended. The Jesuit accusation relies on a chain of imputations, not on any direct contravention of papal teaching.

Pascal further notes that the Jansenist party received the papal constitution "with all possible respect" and "offered to subscribe it" (018-020). Arnauld's second letter explicitly affirmed this.

The refusal of absolution to Arnauld's followers, despite their submission to the constitution, reveals that the Jesuit campaign was never truly about securing doctrinal compliance — it was about destroying a rival party.

## Positions

**Jesuit party**: The papal constitution authoritatively condemns Jansenism, and all who associate with Port-Royal or defend Jansenius fall under this condemnation. Compliance with the constitution entails accepting not only its doctrinal content but also the Jesuit interpretation of its scope.

**Arnauld and the Jansenist defenders**: The papal constitution binds on matters of doctrine (the five propositions are heretical) but not on matters of fact (whether Jansenius taught them). Full and respectful submission to the constitution's doctrinal authority is compatible with contesting the factual attributions that accompanied it (018-020).

**Pascal's rhetorical position**: The accusation that he has violated a papal constitution is baseless by its own terms, since he has contradicted no council and violated no decree. The charge of heresy is a procedural weapon, not a theological argument (018-008).

## Key Passages

> "What council have I contradicted? What papal constitution have I violated? You *must* answer, father, else — You know what I mean." (018-008)

> "When I saw that they afterwards received that decree with all possible respect, that they offered to subscribe it, and that M. Arnauld had declared all this in his second letter, in stronger terms than I can report him, I should have considered it a sin to doubt their soundness in the faith." (018-020)
