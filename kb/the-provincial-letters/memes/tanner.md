## Role in the Controversy

Adam Tanner appears in the *Provincial Letters* as a Jesuit theological authority whose rulings on simony, homicide, and the moral obligations of churchmen provided [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] with material for his critique of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic laxity. Though cited less frequently than [[Escobar]], [[diana|Diana]], or [[reginald|Reginald]], Tanner occupies a significant position in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s argument because his authority extends across the two central themes of the *Letters*: the morality of benefices and simony (Letters VI and XII), and the justification of homicide through the direction of intention (Letter VII).

Tanner's reputation within the Society of Jesus was that of a careful and relatively moderate theologian, yet [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents his rulings as part of the same pattern of moral relaxation that characterizes the entire Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]ic tradition. The juxtaposition of Tanner's rulings with those of more obviously lax [[casuistry|casuist]]s serves [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s rhetorical strategy: if even a "moderate" authority like Tanner endorses these positions, the system itself must be corrupt.

## Key Positions

Tanner's rulings cited in the *Letters* address three main areas:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Money as principal object | Tanner is identified as "a member of our Society" who held that a judge may receive money as the principal object of a judgment without committing simony | 007-028 |
| Homicide by churchmen | Tanner ruled, with [[reginald|Reginald]], that the value of the article must be "of great value in the estimation of everyone" for homicide to be justified | 008-050 |
| Churchmen and homicide | Tanner declared that "Churchmen, and even Religious, may lawfully kill a calumniator" when certain conditions are met | 008-056 |

In Letter VI, Tanner appears in connection with the Jesuits' palliative for simony among ecclesiastical judges. The monk explains that "Tanner, who is also a member of our Society," is among those who developed the doctrine that a judge may receive money as "the principal object" of a judicial act without committing simony, provided certain conditions regarding the intention and the nature of the payment are satisfied. (007-028) [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] treats this as characteristic of the [[casuistry|casuistic method]]: the multiplication of technical distinctions that effectively nullify the moral prohibition.

> "According to Reginald and Tanner, 'the article must be of great value in the estimation of everyone'" (008-050)

Letter VII presents Tanner alongside [[reginald|Reginald]] on the question of homicide by churchmen. The monk cites Tanner's ruling that churchmen may lawfully kill when their reputation is threatened, with the qualification that "the article must be of great value in the estimation of everyone." (008-050) The irony [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] intends is clear: a system in which churchmen debate the precise monetary threshold above which killing becomes permissible has abandoned the Gospel's moral teaching.

Letter XII returns to Tanner in the context of simony, where [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s narrator engages in an extended point-by-point refutation of the Jesuit interpretation of Tanner's rulings. The narrator accuses the Jesuit of selectively quoting Tanner and misrepresenting the state of the question, arguing that "Tanner, you say, has, at any rate, decided the question" in the Jesuits' favor — but a careful reading of Tanner's actual text shows otherwise. (013-019 through 013-022) This exchange represents one of the rare moments in the *Letters* where [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] engages in detailed textual argument over a specific [[casuistry|casuist]]ic authority, rather than relying on ironic quotation.

## Biography

Adam Tanner was born in Innsbruck, in the Tyrol region of the Holy Roman Empire, in 1572. He entered the Society of Jesus in 1589, at the age of seventeen, and pursued theological studies at the University of Ingolstadt, one of the principal centers of Jesuit learning in Germany. After ordination, Tanner taught philosophy and theology at Ingolstadt, Dillingen, and other Jesuit colleges in southern Germany.

Tanner's major work, the *Universa Theologia Scholastica* (Universal Scholastic Theology), was published in four volumes between 1626 and 1627. The work covered the full range of Scholastic theology — God, creation, sin, grace, sacraments, and moral theology — and was widely used in Jesuit seminaries. Unlike the specialized [[casuistry|casuist]]ic manuals of [[Escobar]] or [[diana|Diana]], Tanner's *Universa Theologia* was a comprehensive theological synthesis in the tradition of Thomas Aquinas, though it incorporated the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic methodology characteristic of post-Tridentine Jesuit moral theology.

Tanner also engaged in the major theological controversies of his era. He wrote against the Protestant Reformers and participated in the Catholic theological debates over grace and free will that prefigured the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy. His reputation among contemporaries was that of a balanced and scholarly theologian, not a partisan of laxity — which made his appearance in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s catalogue of lax [[casuistry|casuist]]s particularly pointed.

Tanner died in 1632 at Unken, near Salzburg, more than two decades before the *Provincial Letters* were written.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Tanner's connections within the [[casuistry|casuist]]ic network of the *Letters*:

- **[[reginald|Valerius Reginaldus]]**: The most frequently paired authority with Tanner; their joint rulings on homicide by churchmen are cited together. (008-050)
- **Emanuel Sa**: Cited alongside Tanner on the question of homicide by churchmen; "our great and famous fathers Tanner and Emanuel Sa" are invoked as joint authorities. (008-030)
- **[[Escobar|Antonio Escobar]]**: Tanner appears in the same lists of authorities that include [[Escobar]], [[molina|Molina]], and [[lessius|Lessius]], representing the breadth of the Jesuit consensus on contested moral questions. (008-056)
