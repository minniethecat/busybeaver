## Definition

Simony is the deliberate exchange of a temporal good (money, property, influence) for a spiritual good (a sacrament, a benefice, an ecclesiastical office, or a spiritual favor).

It takes its name from Simon Magus, who attempted to purchase the power of conferring the Holy Spirit from the Apostles and was rebuked by Peter: "Your money perish with you, because you thought that the gift of God could be purchased with money" (Acts 8:20). (007-028)

In canon law, simony renders the transaction invalid and may incur excommunication. The question treated in the *Provincial Letters* is whether the Jesuit casuists had devised distinctions that effectively permit what the law nominally forbids.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal devotes Letter VII to the topic of simony, treating it as a case study in how Jesuit casuistry undermines canon law through verbal distinctions.

The specific question is whether a churchman who gives a benefice to another in exchange for a reciprocal benefit commits simony. (013-018)

The Jesuit casuists, particularly Escobar and Sanchez, draw a distinction between giving the benefice *as the price* of the reciprocal benefit (which would be simony) and giving it *as the motive* for the reciprocal benefit (which, they argue, is not).

Pascal's critique is that this distinction is purely verbal — the practical result is identical, and the distinction exists only to provide canonical cover for what is substantively simony. (013-020)

## Positions

**The traditional canonical position**. Canon law unambiguously prohibits the exchange of spiritual goods for temporal ones.

The prohibition is absolute: any deliberate exchange of spiritual for temporal is simony, regardless of the intention with which it is done. The traditional rigorist position is that the Church's law on this point admits no exceptions. (013-019)

**The Jesuit position**. The Jesuit casuists do not deny the prohibition of simony in principle, but they narrow its definition to the point where few actual transactions qualify.

Escobar teaches that if a benefice is given "not as the price but merely as the motive" for a reciprocal benefit, no simony occurs. (013-021)

Sanchez goes further, maintaining that simony requires an explicit contractual intention, and that a tacit understanding between the parties does not constitute the canonical crime. (013-023)

**Pascal's rebuttal**. Pascal argues that these distinctions are sophistical. If two churchmen exchange benefices with the understanding that each benefits the other, the substance of simony is present regardless of the verbal formula.

The Jesuit distinctions serve only to reassure consciences while leaving the corrupt practice intact. (013-022)

## Key Passages

> "If he gives it merely as a motive, it is not simony." (007-028)

> "This is not simony in point of conscience, seeing that he did not give it as the price." (013-021)

> "These are two very different things — whether it was sin, and whether it was simony." (013-022)

> "Is it simony when two Churchmen become mutually obligated to procure benefices for each other? — It is the common opinion that it is not." (013-024)
