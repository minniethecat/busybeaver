## Definition

Excommunication (*excommunicatio*) is the most severe ecclesiastical censure, by which a baptized person is excluded from the communion of the faithful and deprived of the right to receive or administer the sacraments.

In seventeenth-century church discipline, excommunication was understood as a medicinal penalty — intended to bring about repentance, not to destroy the offender. The Council of Trent reaffirmed that it should be used sparingly and only for grave offenses.

The *Provincial Letters* discuss excommunication in a specific context: whether the Jesuit system of probable opinions effectively nullifies the force of ecclesiastical sanctions, including excommunication.

## Role in the Controversy

Excommunication figures in Pascal's broader critique of Jesuit casuistry. The question is whether the system of probable opinions grants immunity from ecclesiastical sanctions to those who follow a probable opinion that their action is licit.

In Letter VI, Pascal notes that Pope Gregory XIV "forbid the Jesuits, on pain of excommunication, to permit the worship of idols" in China — citing this as evidence that Jesuit missionary accommodations had drawn formal ecclesiastical censure. (006-014)

In Letter VII, the discussion turns to whether Jesuit casuists can effectively discharge the effects of excommunication through confessional practice. Pascal's spokesman challenges Father Bauny on whether monks who abandon their habit are "discharged from excommunication" simply because a probable opinion permits the act. (007-008)

The implication is that the Jesuits' laxity is not merely theological opinion — it has provoked the highest ecclesiastical penalty, which their own casuistic methods then claim to neutralize.

## Positions

**The rigorist (Jansenist) position**. Pascal presents excommunication as an objective sanction that cannot be circumvented by casuistic reasoning. If an act is forbidden under pain of excommunication, no probable opinion can make it licit — ecclesiastical law stands outside the probabilist calculus. (007-007)

He cites papal denunciations of excommunication against monks who abandon their habit, asking rhetorically whether the casuists would claim that such censures no longer bind because a probable opinion says so. (007-007)

**The Jesuit position (as presented by Pascal)**. Pascal portrays Jesuit casuists as treating excommunication as a penalty that can be neutralized by the direction of intention or the invocation of a probable opinion.

Once a penitent has been absolved following a probable opinion, the censure is effectively discharged — because the penitent's conscience is at peace, and as one casuist argues, "the pope himself cannot bind where there is no obligation in conscience." (007-008)

This subordinates ecclesiastical law to the internal forum — and, by extension, to the confessor who governs it.

**The Thomist (Dominican) middle ground**. The Dominican tradition maintained excommunication's binding force while recognizing objective exemptions (ignorance, impossibility, grave danger). Unlike the Jesuit approach as Pascal depicts it, this exemption is grounded in the nature of the act, not in the subjective opinion of the confessor.

## Key Passages

> "The popes have denounced excommunication on monks who lay aside their habit. Are they discharged from excommunication on such occasions?" (007-007)

> "Forbid the Jesuits, on pain of excommunication, to permit the worship of idols." (006-014)

> "Are they discharged from excommunication on such occasions? I put it to Father Bauny to answer." (007-008)
