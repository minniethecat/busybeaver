## Role in the Controversy

Nicolas Caussin appears in the *Provincial Letters* as one of the many Jesuit authorities whose writings, in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s telling, exemplify the moral-theological positions the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists found indefensible. Unlike [[Annat]] or [[Meynier]], Caussin was not a direct participant in the anti-[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist polemics of the 1650s — he died in 1651 — but his earlier writings on [[casuistry|casuist]]ry and moral theology provided ammunition for [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s critique of the Jesuit tradition.

Caussin's most notable appearance is in Letter X, where the Jesuit monk cites him defending the proposition "that were this not true, confession would be interdicted to the greater part of mankind; and the only resource left for poor sinners would be a branch and a rope." (011-031) The proposition Caussin was defending — that a penitent may be absolved even when the hope of absolution induced him to sin more freely — is presented by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] as a reductio ad absurdum of the Jesuit pastoral method.

Caussin's earlier career as confessor to Louis XIII (appointed 1637, dismissed the same year) adds a layer of biographical irony to his appearance in the *Letters*. A theologian who had briefly held the most sensitive confessional office in France is quoted defending the proposition that the sacrament of penance would be rendered inaccessible if rigorist standards were applied — an argument that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s readers would recognize as a charter for moral laxity at the highest levels of power.

## Key Positions

Caussin's positions, as [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents them, concern the boundaries of sacramental absolution:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Absolution and motive | Defended [[bauny|Bauny]]'s proposition that absolution may be given even when the hope of absolution induced the sin | 011-031 |
| Confession and despair | Argued that without this doctrine, "confession would be interdicted to the greater part of mankind" | 011-031 |
| Occasion of sin | His response to charges concerning [[bauny|Bauny]]'s maxim on seeking proximate occasion of sin | 016-015 |

The most striking feature of Caussin's presentation is the dramatic language [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] attributes to him: "the only resource left for poor sinners would be a branch and a rope" — that is, hanging as the sole escape from a rigorist confessional that denied absolution. (011-031) This appeal to pastoral desperation — the idea that moral rigor drives sinners to suicide — is presented by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] as a rhetorical sleight of hand that replaces the question of what is true with the question of what is convenient.

> "were this not true, confession would be interdicted to the greater part of mankind; and the only resource left for poor sinners would be a branch and a rope!" (011-031)

In Letter XIV, Caussin reappears in connection with [[bauny|Bauny]]'s maxim on seeking a proximate occasion of sin "for the spiritual or temporal good of ourselves or our neighbor." (016-015) The context suggests that Caussin was among the Jesuit theologians whose defenses of this maxim had drawn criticism in the decade before the *Letters*.

## Biography

Nicolas Caussin was born in Troyes, France, in 1583 and entered the Society of Jesus in 1609. He became professor of rhetoric at the Jesuit colleges of Rouen and La Flèche before his appointment as confessor to King Louis XIII in March 1637 — a position of extraordinary influence that made him, briefly, the most powerful Jesuit in France.

Caussin's tenure as royal confessor was short-lived. His efforts to influence French foreign policy toward a peace with Spain and his involvement in court intrigues surrounding the queen and Cardinal Richelieu led to his dismissal and exile to Quimper in December 1637, after only nine months in office. He spent the remainder of his life in literary and theological work, producing devotional writings, Latin tragedies, and works of moral theology.

His most famous work, *La Cour Sainte* (*The Holy Court*, 1624), was a widely read treatise on the spiritual life of courtiers that went through multiple editions and translations. Caussin's reputation as a spiritual writer and [[casuistry|casuist]] ensured that his opinions carried weight in the theological debates of the mid-century, including those [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] addresses in the *Provincial Letters*.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Caussin's principal relationship in the *Letters* is to [[Bauny]], the Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]] whose most controversial propositions Caussin defended in print. (011-031, 016-015) This relationship illustrates the collaborative character of Jesuit moral theology as [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents it: individual [[casuistry|casuist]]s' maxims were not isolated opinions but positions defended by the collective authority of the Society of Jesus.

Caussin's former status as royal confessor — an office later held by [[Annat]], [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s principal antagonist — connects him to the institutional nexus of Jesuit influence at the French court. The fact that two successive royal confessors were implicated in the defense of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic propositions that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] considered scandalous reinforces his argument that laxist moral theology was not the province of obscure academics but the official doctrine of the Society at its highest levels.
