## Role in the Controversy

Pierre Le Moyne is one of the most frequently cited Jesuit adversaries in the *Provincial Letters*, appearing across nine letters (I, III, IV, IX, X, XI, XII, XIII, and the Reply). As a doctor of the Sorbonne and a leading [[Molinism|Molinist]] theologian, Le Moyne occupied a strategic position at the intersection of academic theology and pastoral practice.

His role in the controversy is twofold. First, as an examiner in the Sorbonne proceedings against [[Arnauld|Antoine Arnauld]], he was instrumental in the political machinery of the censure. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] reports that Le Moyne was "the most zealous of the examiners" (004-012) and reveals the cynical logic behind the condemnation:

> "This proposition would be orthodox in the mouth of any other---it is only as coming from M. Arnauld that the Sorbonne have condemned it!" (004-012)

Second, Le Moyne served as the Jesuit order's public intellectual, producing devotional works aimed at making piety attractive to worldly audiences. His book *Devotion made Easy* became a primary target of [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s satire, embodying what [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] saw as the reduction of Christian virtue to worldly sensibility.

## Key Positions

Le Moyne's theological and moral positions, as presented and critiqued by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]], span several domains:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| [[Molinism\|Proximate power]] | The righteous may pray without efficacious grace; all that is needed is "proximate power" | 001-015 |
| [[Sin of ignorance\|5-step sin-ignorance test]] | An act is not a sin unless five divine inspirations pass through the soul | 005-011 |
| Devotion made easy | Piety should be agreeable and adapted to worldly tastes | 010-006 |
| Old women and adornment | Aging women should retire from ornament at "the first approach of the shades of night" | 011-013 |
| Penance softened | Penance rendered "easier than vice, and more agreeable than pleasure" | 011-018 |
| Dispensation from loving God | Defended the doctrine that Christians are not strictly bound to love God | 012-007 |

The overarching theme of Le Moyne's theology is **accommodation**: adapting Christian moral demands to the sensibilities and comfort of worldly elites. Where [[Escobar]] systematizes this accommodation through [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic distinctions, Le Moyne achieves it through stylistic re-branding — recasting austerity as poor taste and devotion as refined pleasure.

### Proximate Power

In Letter I, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] recounts his visit to a disciple of M. le Moine, who explains that "proximate power" means having everything necessary to act, without requiring any additional efficacious grace from God. (001-015) The [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist narrator exposes this as a deliberate verbal agreement masking substantive disagreement:

> "Do you mean, then, to renew our broils? Have we not agreed not to explain that word *proximate*, but to use it on both sides without saying what it signifies?" (001-023)

### The Five-Step Ignorance Doctrine

In Letter IV, Le Moyne provides [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] with one of his most devastating exhibits. According to Le Moyne, before an action can be considered a sin, five distinct operations must pass through the soul: God must shed abroad a measure of love, inspire knowledge of weakness, reveal the physician, inspire desire for healing, and inspire desire to pray. (005-011) [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] draws the satirical conclusion that hardened sinners who never think of God at all are, by this logic, still in a state of baptismal innocence.

### Devotion Made Easy

In Letter IX, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] quotes Le Moyne's manifesto for a new kind of piety:

> "Virtue has never as yet been seen aright; no portrait of her, hitherto produced, has borne the least verisimilitude. It is by no means surprising that so few have attempted to scale her rocky eminence. She has been held up as a cross-tempered dame, whose only delight is in solitude; she has been associated with toil and sorrow; and, in short, represented as the foe of sports and diversions, which are, in truth, the flowers of joy and the seasoning of life." (010-006)

Le Moyne contrasts two types of devout persons: the "pale and melancholy" type with "phlegm instead of blood in their veins," and the "happier complexion" type who possess "that sweet and warm humor, that genial and rectified blood, which is the true stuff that joy is made of." (010-008) His *Moral Pictures* ridicules ascetic saints as moping idiots insensitive to beauty, honor, and pleasure. (010-009)

### Adornment and Penance

In Letter X, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] cites Le Moyne's rule on female adornment: old women should consult "good sense and a good mirror" and retire "at the first approach of the shades of night." (011-013) The rule grants youth a natural right to ornament while rendering it unseemly for the elderly — a pastoral accommodation that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents as frivolous moral reasoning.

The same letter quotes Le Moyne's claim that the Jesuits, by "demolishing the bugbear which the devil had placed at its threshold," have rendered devotion "easier than vice, and more agreeable than pleasure; so that, in fact, simply to live is incomparably more irksome than to live well." (011-018)

### Dispensation from Loving God

Letter XI records that Le Moyne was among the Jesuit theologians (alongside [[annat|Annat]], Pintereau, and [[sirmond|Sirmond]]) who defended the doctrine that Christians are not bound to an actual affection for God, provided they do not hate him. (012-007) This dispensation, according to Father Pintereau, was purchased by the blood of Jesus Christ and constitutes the privilege of the Evangelical law over the Judaical.

## Biography

Pierre Le Moyne was born in 1602 in France. He entered the Society of Jesus and became a doctor of the Sorbonne, where he taught theology and participated actively in the faculty's proceedings.

By the mid-1650s, Le Moyne had established himself as one of the most prominent Jesuit publicists in France. His devotional works, particularly *Devotion made Easy* (1652) and *Moral Pictures* (*Peintures morales*), aimed to reconcile Christian piety with the tastes of the cultured Parisian elite. These works made him a natural target for [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]], who saw in Le Moyne's elegant prose the spiritual danger of a Christianity stripped of its cross.

During the censure of [[Arnauld|Antoine Arnauld]] (1656), Le Moyne served as one of the examiners in the Sorbonne. His role in the proceedings earned him particular mention in Letter III, where his remark that [[Arnauld]]'s proposition "would be orthodox in the mouth of any other" exposed the political character of the condemnation. (004-012)

Le Moyne died in 1671, outliving [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] by nearly a decade. His reputation, like that of [[Escobar]], was indelibly shaped by the *Provincial Letters*.

The stylistic character of Le Moyne's writing — ornate, gallant, and self-consciously literary — made it especially vulnerable to [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s satirical method. Where [[Escobar]] provided the [[casuistry|casuist]]ical content, Le Moyne provided the aesthetic frame: a vision of piety as worldly refinement rather than self-denial.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Le Moyne's place in the *Provincial Letters* is defined by his position within the Jesuit-Molinist network and his role in the Sorbonne proceedings.

His theological framework derives from [[Molina|Luis de Molina]] (1535–1600), whose doctrine of scientia media provided the metaphysical basis for the "sufficient grace" that Le Moyne defended in the Sorbonne. Le Moyne's insistence that the righteous may pray without efficacious grace places him on the Molinist side of the [[grace]] controversy.

Within the Sorbonne proceedings, Le Moyne worked alongside [[nicolai|Father Nicolai]] and the New Thomists, though they disagreed on the meaning of "proximate power." As [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] shows, Le Moyne and the Jacobins had agreed to use the term without defining it, allowing each party to claim victory while forming a united front against [[Arnauld]]. (001-023)

In the moral theology sections of the letters, Le Moyne appears alongside [[Escobar]], [[bauny|Bauny]], [[lessius|Lessius]], and other Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]s. While [[Escobar]] supplies the [[probabilism|probabilist]] reasoning and the scholastic distinctions, Le Moyne supplies the stylistic polish — the re-branding of moral laxity as refined devotion. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] treats them as complementary figures in a single project: [[Escobar]] makes sin logically permissible; Le Moyne makes piety aesthetically agreeable.

His principal opponents are the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists, particularly [[Arnauld|Antoine Arnauld]] and [[Jansenius|Cornelius Jansen]], whose Augustinian rigorism on grace and penance stands in direct opposition to Le Moyne's accommodating theology.
