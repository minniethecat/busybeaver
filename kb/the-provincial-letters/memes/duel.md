## Definition

In the context of the *Provincial Letters*, duelling refers to the early modern practice of honor-driven single combat between gentlemen. By the seventeenth century, the Church had repeatedly and explicitly condemned duelling as gravely sinful, involving the double guilt of homicide and the defiance of lawful authority. The Council of Trent imposed excommunication on those who engaged in duels. Pascal's treatment of duelling serves as a paradigmatic case study in how Jesuit casuistry, in his view, effectively nullifies even the Church's clearest moral prohibitions through the mechanism of probable opinions.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal presents the Jesuit treatment of duelling as one of the most damning demonstrations of the moral consequences of probabilism. He devotes a sustained passage in the *Letters* to showing how the casuists, while formally acknowledging that the Church condemns duelling, have constructed a web of exceptions so capacious that "all that duellists are disposed to ask" is effectively granted (008-022).

The argument proceeds through a series of increasingly permissive opinions. Pascal cites the casuist who holds that a gentleman challenged to a duel may accept, on the grounds that declining would damage his honor — and that one who "is well known to have no religion" would be particularly justified, since the loss of honor would carry no spiritual compensation (008-020). He further records the opinion that one may "fight a duel to save his life, his honor, or his fortune" (008-026), effectively covering every possible motive a gentleman might have for accepting a challenge.

Pascal traces how these exceptions multiply: from accepting a challenge, to seeking out a duel, to killing one's opponent. He summarizes the cumulative effect: under Jesuit casuistry, a man may "accept a challenge to a duel, to give one sometimes, to kill one's adversary" (008-031) — all while remaining in good standing with the Church, provided he directs his intention away from the sinful act itself.

Having established the pattern through the duelling case, Pascal later returns to it as a proven example of the system's corruption, noting that he has "shown by a great number of passages" how the Jesuits "authorize duelling" (015-016).

## Positions

**Jesuit position**: The Jesuit casuists maintained that they did not "permit duelling" in any positive sense (008-021). Their approach was to analyze the specific circumstances under which duelling might lose its character as a mortal sin — for instance, when a gentleman acts purely to defend his honor rather than from hatred or revenge. They argued that this was not permission but precise moral analysis, distinguishing between the objective gravity of duelling and the subjective culpability of the duellist.

**Jansenist / Pascal's position**: Pascal treats the duelling case as the reductio ad absurdum of the entire casuistical method. A practice that the Church has unambiguously condemned as mortal sin becomes, through a chain of casuistical refinements, an option available to any gentleman who values his worldly reputation. For Pascal, the fact that casuists can construct a chain of probable opinions that effectively neutralizes an excommunicable offense demonstrates that the entire probabilist edifice is morally bankrupt. The Church has "taken the surest method to prevent, and abolish the practice of duelling in the State" (008-023) — but the casuists have undone that work through their exceptions.

**Secular / honor-code position**: The gentleman's code of honor, which Pascal's interlocutor challenges, treated the refusal of a duel as a loss of social standing tantamount to civil death. Pascal's argument implicitly addresses this culture as well: by showing that the Jesuits accommodate rather than challenge worldly honor codes, he suggests that the Society has abandoned its prophetic role of moral witness.

## Key Passages

> "It is allowable to fight a duel." (008-019)

Pascal opens the duelling discussion with this blunt assertion, attributed to the Jesuit casuists, designed to shock the reader and establish the gravity of the moral permission at stake.

> "'Who is challenged to fight a duel, is well known to have no religion to lose, may accept the challenge without sin.'" (008-020)

Pascal quotes the casuist distinguishing between religious and irreligious gentlemen, implying that the Church's moral teaching is treated as optional — a luxury available only to those who have faith to lose.

> "We authorize duelling, as I have shown by a great number of passages." (015-016)

Pascal's later summary, treating the duelling case as definitively proved and returning to it as a touchstone of Jesuit moral corruption.
