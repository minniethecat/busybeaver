## Role in the Controversy

Pope Innocent X is the supreme ecclesiastical authority whose 1653 condemnation of the Five Propositions extracted from [[Jansenius]]'s *Augustinus* frames the theological controversy at the heart of the *Provincial Letters*. His bull *Cum Occasione* (31 May 1653) declared five propositions heretical, and this papal judgment became the juridical pivot around which the entire [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy turned.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s treatment of Innocent X is marked by careful deference to papal authority combined with equally careful distinction between what the pope condemned and what the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists actually taught. The narrator repeatedly affirms respect for the Holy See (019-028) while arguing that the Five Propositions, as condemned by Innocent X, are not in fact found in [[Jansenius]]'s *Augustinus* — a distinction known as the "question of fact" versus the "question of right."

This distinction — between the pope's right to condemn heretical propositions (which [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] accepts) and the factual question of whether [[Jansenius]] taught those propositions (which [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] contests) — is the theological fulcrum of the later *Letters*. Innocent X's bull thus serves as both the authority [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] must respect and the text he must interpret in a manner consistent with [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist orthodoxy. (018-036)

## Key Positions

Innocent X's role in the controversy is defined by the bull *Cum Occasione* and its contested interpretation:

| Event/Action | Significance | Key PN |
|--------------|-------------|--------|
| Condemnation of Five Propositions (1653) | Declared five propositions extracted from [[Jansenius]] heretical | 016-011, 018-036 |
| Question of fact vs. right | [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] distinguishes papal authority on doctrine from factual determination of what [[Jansenius]] wrote | 018-036 |
| Examination of [[Jansenius]] | Innocent X's examination focused on the propositions, not the *Augustinus* as a whole | 019-028 |
| Predecessor and successor | Innocent X's actions contrasted with those of his predecessor Urban VIII and successor Alexander VII | 019-028, 019-030 |

The bull *Cum Occasione* condemned the following five propositions:
1. Some of God's commandments are impossible for the just, even when they will and strive
2. In the state of fallen nature, one never resists interior grace
3. For merit and demerit in the state of fallen nature, freedom from necessity is sufficient; freedom from coercion is not required
4. The Semi-Pelagians admitted the necessity of prevenient interior grace for each act, but were heretics for holding that this grace is such that the human will can resist or obey it
5. It is Semi-Pelagian to say that Christ died for all men without exception

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s argument, developed across Letters XVII and XVIII, is that the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists accept the condemnation of these propositions as heretical while denying that [[Jansenius]] taught them. This distinction, known as the "question of fact" (did [[Jansenius]] teach these propositions?) versus the "question of right" (are these propositions heretical?), became the central theological-juridical controversy of the 1650s.

> "Pope Innocent X. has declared that the error of the five propositions is to be found in Jansenius." (018-036)

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s response to this papal determination is subtle: he argues that Innocent X's examination was limited to the five propositions themselves and did not extend to a full examination of [[Jansenius]]'s *Augustinus*. (019-028) The pope "had merely examined into the five propositions" without determining whether [[Jansenius]] had actually taught them in the sense condemned — a distinction that preserved papal authority while leaving room for [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist interpretation.

## Biography

Giovanni Battista Pamphili was born in Rome in 1574 to a noble family with deep connections to the papal court. He studied law at the Collegio Romano and entered the papal diplomatic service, serving as nuncio to Naples and later to Spain. His diplomatic career gave him extensive experience in the political complexities of Catholic Europe, experience that would shape his pontificate.

Pamphili was created cardinal in 1629 and elected pope in 1644, taking the name Innocent X. His pontificate was dominated by the negotiations that led to the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which Innocent X condemned in the bull *Zelo Domus Dei* for its concessions to Protestant powers. The pope's protest against the peace settlement — which effectively ended the Thirty Years' War on terms favorable to Protestant states — reflected his conviction that the papacy retained a voice in the political ordering of Christendom.

The [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy was the major theological issue of Innocent X's pontificate. The bull *Cum Occasione* (1653) was issued after extensive examination of the Five Propositions by a commission of theologians, and it represented the first formal papal judgment on the theological questions raised by [[Jansenius]]'s *Augustinus*. Innocent X died in 1655, before the publication of the *Provincial Letters* and before the full unfolding of the controversy his bull had helped to shape.

The concentration of Innocent X's appearances in the later letters reflects the theological escalation of the *Letters*: from satire of [[casuistry|casuist]]ry (Letters IV–X) through defense of Port-Royal's orthodoxy (Letters XI–XVI) to the high theological argument about grace and papal authority (Letters XVII–XVIII). Innocent X is the authority before whom [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] must make his case that [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist theology is Catholic.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Innocent X's relationship to the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist community is defined by the question of what his bull actually determined. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s narrator insists that "your antagonists give sufficient evidence of their resolution ever to abide by its decisions" (019-028), affirming [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist submission to papal authority while contesting the Jesuits' interpretation of what the pope decided. This dual stance — submission to authority combined with resistance to its claimed extension — is the defining posture of [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist theology in its relation to Rome.

The relationship between Innocent X and his predecessor [[Urban VIII]] is significant: Urban VIII had prohibited discussion of the questions raised by [[Jansenius]]'s *Augustinus*, and Innocent X's bull *Cum Occasione* was the papal determination that Urban VIII's prohibition had postponed. (019-028) [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s reference to this history suggests that Innocent X's condemnation was the result of sustained Jesuit pressure on the Holy See — a suggestion that respects the pope's authority while questioning the process that led to his judgment.

Innocent X's relationship to the Jesuit controversialists — particularly [[Annat]], who as confessor to Louis XIV was instrumental in securing royal support for the anti-[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist campaign — illuminates the political dimension of the controversy. The Jesuits claimed Innocent X's bull as a complete vindication of their position; [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s response is that the bull, properly understood, condemns only propositions the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists themselves reject. This hermeneutical dispute — what did Innocent X actually decide? — is the unresolved question at the heart of the *Letters*.
