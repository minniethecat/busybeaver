## Definition

In Catholic theology, confession (or the sacrament of penance) is the sacrament by which sins committed after baptism are forgiven through the absolution of a priest. The sacrament requires contrition (sorrow for sin), confession of mortal sins by kind and number, and satisfaction (performing the assigned penance). In the *Provincial Letters*, Pascal argues that the Jesuit casuists have systematically undermined every element of this sacrament — making confession easy for the penitent but empty of genuine penitence — and have thereby corrupted the primary mechanism of moral accountability in the Church.

## Role in the Controversy

Confession lies at the heart of Pascal's critique of Jesuit moral theology. The casuistical system operates primarily through the confessional: it is there that probable opinions are applied, that moral obligations are relaxed, and that the penitent receives assurance that conduct the Church once condemned is now permissible. Pascal's account traces how the Jesuits transformed confession from an instrument of moral rigor into a mechanism of accommodation.

The structural problem, as Pascal presents it, is self-reinforcing. The Jesuits recognized that "there is no remedy but confession" for sin (011-004) and that the difficulty of confession deterred penitents. Their response was to "alleviate" the burdens of the sacrament by minimizing what was required: reducing the obligation to love God, softening the requirement of contrition to mere attrition, and offering dispensations from the works of satisfaction. Pascal's Jesuit interlocutor boasts that "the most squeamish" penitents now find nothing objectionable in the confessional (011-013), and that these accommodations "will draw people to your confessionals" (011-032) — a claim that Pascal treats as self-indictment, revealing that the Jesuits measure their success by popularity rather than by genuine conversion.

The consequences extend beyond the confessional itself. Pascal charges that the Jesuit system makes confession the universal solvent of moral obligation: when a penitent can confess any sin and receive easy absolution, the deterrent force of the moral law is effectively negated. The casuist Father Brisacier even warns that if the Jesuits are silenced, "fewer people will come to your confessionals" (017-016) — a threat that Pascal frames as revealing the institutional self-interest behind the entire laxist project.

## Positions

**Jesuit position**: The Jesuit casuists defended their confessional practice as an exercise of pastoral mercy. They argued that excessive rigor drives penitents away from the sacrament entirely, resulting in greater spiritual harm. By accommodating the weaknesses of the faithful — reducing the required dispositions, offering easy paths to absolution — they kept the laity within the sacramental economy of the Church. The confessional, in their view, was properly a tribunal of mercy, not of condemnation.

**Jansenist / Pascal's position**: Pascal does not reject the sacrament of confession itself; his critique targets its abuse. He argues that the Jesuit system hollows out the sacrament by eliminating its essential conditions. When attrition substitutes for contrition, when the obligation to love God is dispensed with, and when absolution is granted without genuine amendment of life, the sacrament becomes a formality that ratifies rather than remedies sin. Pascal points to the casuists' own admissions — that confession without true penance is sufficient for saying Mass (017-026), that the sacrament can be administered without the penitent loving God (011-054) — as proof that the Jesuits have abandoned Catholic sacramental theology.

**Augustinian / Patristic position**: The Church Fathers and the Council of Trent insisted on the integrity of the sacrament: genuine contrition, integral confession, and satisfaction. Pascal's position is that he is defending this traditional teaching against Jesuit innovation, not proposing anything new.

## Key Passages

> "There is no remedy but confession; it became necessary to alleviate its terrors." (011-004)

Pascal traces the Jesuit logic: confession is necessary but difficult, therefore the difficulty must be removed — a logic he argues corrupts the sacrament rather than saving it.

> "We ought no longer to call confession the sacrament of penance." (011-014)

Attributed to a Jesuit casuist, this claim encapsulates Pascal's charge: that the Jesuits have so transformed confession that it no longer deserves the name of the sacrament of penance, since penance — genuine sorrow and amendment — has been removed from it.

> "Fewer people will come to your confessionals." (017-016)

Pascal quotes Father Brisacier's warning as an admission that the Jesuit confessional practice is driven by institutional competition, not theological principle.
