## Definition

The Five Propositions are five doctrinal statements concerning the relationship between divine grace and human free will, extracted — or allegedly extracted — from Cornelius Jansenius's *Augustinus* (1640) and condemned as heretical by Pope Innocent X in the bull *Cum occasione* (31 May 1653).

The five condemned propositions are: (1) that certain commandments of God are impossible for the just to fulfill given their present strength, and grace is lacking to make them possible; (2) that in the state of fallen nature, interior grace is never resisted; (3) that for merit or demerit in the state of fallen nature, freedom from necessity is sufficient, without freedom from coercion; (4) that the Semi-Pelagians erred in teaching that the human will can resist or obey grace; (5) that it is Semi-Pelagian to say Christ died for all men without exception.

The question that dominates the *Provincial Letters* is not whether these propositions are heretical — both parties agree they are — but whether they are actually found in Jansenius's work.

## Role in the Controversy

The Five Propositions are the pivot on which the entire Jansenist controversy turns.

After Innocent X's condemnation in 1653, the debate shifted from the abstract question of grace to the factual question of attribution. The Jansenists accepted the condemnation of the propositions while denying that Jansenius had taught them *in the sense condemned*. This distinction between *factum* (the fact of Jansenius having taught the propositions) and *jus* (the right of the pope to condemn heretical propositions) became the central juridical dispute.

In the *Provincial Letters*, the Five Propositions are introduced in Letters XVII–XIX, where Pascal mounts a sustained argument that the pope's infallibility extends to doctrinal judgments (*jus*) but not to questions of fact (*factum*):

> "The pope has condemned the doctrine of the five propositions, which was represented to him as the doctrine of Jansenius; but the question of fact — whether Jansenius actually held that doctrine — is a point on which the pope may be mistaken." (018-039)

Pascal reports that the Jansenists "maintain the five propositions condemned by the pope" are not in Jansenius in the heretical sense (018-014).

## Positions

**The Jesuit/Roman position**. The Jesuits and the papal court maintained that the Five Propositions are in Jansenius *word for word*, and that the pope's authority extends to determining this factual question as well as the doctrinal one. To deny the fact is to deny the pope's authority, which is itself heretical.

Pascal's Jesuit interlocutor states: "It is sufficient for you that they condemn the five propositions, and everything in Jansenius that is conformable to them and contrary to St. Augustine." (018-025)

**The Jansenist/Port-Royal position**. The Jansenists drew a distinction between the propositions *in the sense condemned* (Calvinist, denying free will) and what Jansenius actually taught (Augustinian, affirming the necessity but not the irresistibility of grace).

Pascal cites the Jansenist argument that "the error of the five propositions is to be found in Jansenius" only if one first assumes that the propositions express Calvin's doctrine — which Jansenius himself opposed (018-036). The "doctrine of Jansenius in these five propositions" was never his own doctrine (018-037).

**The distinction between fact and right**. The core Jansenist argument, developed by Arnauld and deployed by Pascal, is that the Church has never claimed infallibility on questions of fact:

> "It would be easy to conclude, that if the five propositions are not in Jansenius, it is impossible that any should have taken them from thence." (019-042)

Pascal argues that if the pope condemned the propositions as Calvinist, and Jansenius also condemned Calvin's doctrine, then what the pope condemned was not Jansenius's actual teaching (019-006, 019-009).
