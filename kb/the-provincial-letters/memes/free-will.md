## Definition

Free will (*liberum arbitrium*) is the human faculty by which a person chooses between alternatives without external compulsion. In Catholic theology, free will is affirmed as essential to moral responsibility: without it, neither merit nor demerit, neither virtue nor sin, is possible.

The Council of Trent (Session VI, Canon 5) anathematized the proposition that "man's free will, after the sin of Adam, is lost and extinguished" — directly rejecting the Calvinist doctrine of total depravity.

The *Provincial Letters* engage free will not as an abstract philosophical question but as the practical crux of the grace controversy: does efficacious grace determine the will infallibly, or does it operate in a way that preserves genuine freedom?

## Role in the Controversy

The free-will question runs through the entire Jansenist–Jesuit controversy. The Jansenists, following the Augustinian tradition, emphasized the sovereignty of efficacious grace to the point that their opponents accused them of denying free will — of teaching that grace is "irresistible" and that human beings "have not free will to do either good or evil." (018-014)

Pascal's spokesman in the Letters rejects this characterization. The Jansenist position, he insists, does not deny free will but only asserts that efficacious grace moves the will in a way that "infallibly consents" — that is, grace is always effective, but it operates through the will, not against it. (019-017)

The Jesuit opponents, by contrast, are presented as defending free will in a way that makes grace dependent on human consent — effectively subordinating the divine action to the human response.

## Positions

**The Jansenist (Augustinian) position**. Pascal's Jansenist spokesman maintains that efficacious grace moves the free will infallibly while preserving its freedom. The will consents not because it is compelled but because grace gives it the delight (*delectatio*) that draws it freely toward the good. (019-017)

He argues that the Council of Trent itself taught that "free will may always act or not act, will or not will" — but that this is compatible with the infallible operation of grace, because grace works from within the will, not from without. (019-021)

This careful distinction did not satisfy the Jesuits, who accused the Jansenists of Calvinism — of teaching that free will is destroyed by the Fall and unable to resist grace.

**The Molinist (Jesuit) position**. The Jesuits, following Luis de Molina, taught that God's grace is congruent with the circumstances of each person (*scientia media*) and that the human will retains the power to accept or resist it.

Pascal presents this as making grace dependent on human foresight — God gives grace to those He foresees will cooperate with it. The Jansenist objection is that this reduces grace to a reward for foreseen merit, which is precisely the semi-Pelagian position condemned by the Church.

**The Thomist (Dominican) middle position**. The Dominican tradition, represented in the Letters by figures like Nicolai, taught that grace is intrinsically efficacious (*gratia per se efficax*) — it produces its effect by its own nature, not by external circumstances.

This position affirms both the sovereignty of grace and the reality of free will, arguing that the tension between them is a mystery, not a contradiction. Pascal acknowledges this position but argues that the Jesuit Molinists have departed from it.

## Key Passages

> "They maintain that grace is irresistible — that we have not free will to do either good or evil." (018-014)

> "When efficacious grace moves the free will, it infallibly consents; because the effect of grace is that the will, without compulsion, consents." (019-017)

> "According to the Council, free will may always act or not act, will or not will." (019-021)

> "God regulates the free will of man without encroaching on its liberty." (019-011)

> "Our actions are ours in respect of the free will which produces them; but they are God's in respect of the grace which enables them." (019-016)
