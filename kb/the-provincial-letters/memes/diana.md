## Role in the Controversy

Antonino Diana is the single most frequently cited [[casuistry|casuist]] in the *Provincial Letters*, referenced across Letters V through XIII — more than fifteen distinct citations spanning eight letters. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] treats Diana not as an original theological thinker but as the great compiler and systematizer of the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic moral tradition: a writer whose enormous output (twelve folio volumes of *Resolutiones Morales*) provided a comprehensive catalogue of moral permissions that Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]s could invoke as authoritative precedents.

The Jesuit monk introduces Diana through [[caramuel|Caramuel]]'s dedication: "Hence the learned [[caramuel|Caramuel]], in dedicating his Fundamental Theology to Diana, declares that this great Diana has rendered many opinions probable which were not so before — *quæ antea non erant*." (007-023) This formulation captures the core of [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s critique: Diana, by collecting and endorsing a vast range of lax opinions, functioned as a kind of permission-manufacturing engine. Under probabilism, the mere existence of Diana's endorsement made an opinion "probable," and therefore safe to follow in conscience.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s narrator adopts a tone of ironic deference toward Diana, calling him "our worthy friend" and "our cordial friend" while systematically exposing the moral consequences of his rulings. (007-021, 011-048) The irony serves a serious polemical purpose: Diana's authority was so widely accepted in [[casuistry|casuist]]ic circles that simply quoting him was sufficient to establish any opinion as a legitimate moral option.

## Key Positions

Diana's *Resolutiones Morales* addressed an extraordinarily wide range of moral questions. Those cited in the *Letters* fall into several categories:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Ecclesiastical benefices | Diana ruled that beneficiaries who received benefices through simony were not obliged to relinquish them | 007-005, 007-015 |
| Killing for insult | Diana endorsed the opinion that one may kill for a buffet or an insult, founding on less rigorous authors | 007-005 |
| Fasting obligations | Diana provided elaborate exemptions from fasting, cited approvingly by the Jesuit monk | 011-048 |
| [[probabilism|Probabilism]] in general | Diana's method of collecting opinions and declaring them probable exemplified the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic mechanism | 006-057 |
| Confessors and absolution | Diana addressed questions of whether beneficiaries must make restitution and whether confessors may absolve simoniacal penitents | 007-008 |
| Duelling and honor | Diana's rulings on duelling and the point of honor were cited in Letters VII and XIII | 007-017, 008-020, 013-013 |

A central theme in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s use of Diana is the [[casuistry|casuist]]'s method of aggregating authorities. "There is Diana, for instance, a most voluminous writer on these questions," [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] notes, describing how Diana's approach consists in citing a range of prior [[casuistry|casuist]]ic opinions without independent examination of their moral soundness. (006-074) The circularity is deliberate: "When Diana, for example, who does not bestow his approbation on all sorts of opinions, tells us that a certain sentiment is probable, it forthwith becomes so. So thoroughly has he digested his own opinions! So admirably does he succeed in giving currency to other authors with applause!" (006-082)

> "we may always say with Diana, on his finding that Father Bauny is on his side" (006-064)

The most pointed structural critique appears in Letter VI, where [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] has the Jesuit monk acknowledge that "Diana, who generally founds on our fathers, sometimes, however, goes counter to them; but we serve them as a kind of foil to his opinions." (007-005) Even when Diana contradicts specific Jesuits, his authority is such that the Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]s work around the contradiction rather than challenging him — a demonstration of the self-reinforcing character of the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic system. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s narrator observes: "He showed me the same thing in Diana, in these terms: *Ut eat incommodum* — that he may go at large, or without impediment." (007-008)

## Biography

Antonino Diana was born in Palermo, Sicily, in 1586 and entered the Theatine order at a young age. The Theatines were a reform-oriented clerical order founded in 1524, distinct from the Jesuits but sharing their commitment to theological education and moral instruction. Diana studied at Palermo and later at Rome, where he was exposed to the full range of post-Tridentine moral theology.

Diana's literary output was prodigious. His *Resolutiones Morales* eventually filled twelve folio volumes, addressing thousands of specific moral cases. The work went through multiple editions across Europe, and Diana's name became synonymous with comprehensive [[casuistry|casuist]]ic coverage. He was the most cited moral authority of his generation, and his opinions regularly appeared in confessional manuals, theological disputations, and ecclesiastical court proceedings.

Despite his prominence, Diana was not an original systematic thinker. His method was compilatory: he collected opinions from earlier [[casuistry|casuist]]s (both Jesuit and non-Jesuit), classified them, and pronounced on their [[probabilism|probability]]. This method, which [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] attacked as intellectually vacuous, was precisely what made Diana so useful to the [[casuistry|casuist]]ic tradition — he provided a one-stop reference for practitioners seeking authoritative authorization for lenient rulings.

Diana died in Rome in 1663, while the controversy ignited by the *Provincial Letters* was still active. His reputation survived [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s attack; the *Resolutiones Morales* continued to be reprinted well into the eighteenth century.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Diana stands at the center of the network of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic authorities in the *Letters*:

- **[[caramuel|Juan Caramuel]]**: Dedicated his *Fundamental Theology* to Diana, calling him the man who "rendered many opinions probable which were not so before." (007-023)
- **[[bauny|Étienne Bauny]]**: Diana frequently endorsed [[bauny|Bauny]]'s opinions, and [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] notes that Diana "on his finding that [[bauny|Father Bauny]] is on his side" would declare an opinion probable. (006-064)
- **Gaspar Hurtado**: Diana cited Hurtado's opinions, and [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] traces chains of citation (Hurtado → Diana → later [[casuistry|casuist]]s) to expose the circularity of [[probabilism|probabilist]] authority. (008-016)
- **[[Escobar|Antonio Escobar]]**: Both are compilers of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic opinion; [[Escobar]]'s *Moral Theology* draws on the same tradition Diana systematized. The two are often paired in the *Letters* as representatives of the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic method.
