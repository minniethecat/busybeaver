## Role in the Controversy

Henrique Henriquez appears in the *Provincial Letters* as one of the senior authorities of the Jesuit moral tradition — a theologian whose opinions carry weight across the full range of topics [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] subjects to critique. Introduced in Letter V among the "four-and-twenty" Jesuits whose collective authority is invoked to outweigh Church Fathers (006-078), Henriquez serves as a durable anchor for the [[casuistry|casuist]]ic positions [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] challenges: homicide, the frequency of confession, and the structure of [[probabilism|probable opinion]].

Unlike more frequently cited figures such as [[Escobar]] or [[Molina]], Henriquez appears at strategic moments when the Jesuit monk wishes to invoke the consensus of multiple authorities. In Letter VII, the monk reports that the permission to kill a person who has given "a box on the ear" has been "satisfactorily proved by the learned Henriquez" (008-032). In Letter X, Henriquez is cited on the minimal frequency of confession — "once in five years" (011-052) — a position that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] treats as an example of how [[casuistry|casuist]]ry loosens the sacramental obligations of the faithful.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s treatment of Henriquez is notably restrained compared to his satire of [[Bauny]] or [[Escobar]]. Henriquez is presented as a weighty authority whose conclusions, while extreme, are reached through recognizable theological reasoning rather than the absurdity [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] attributes to the more egregious [[casuistry|casuist]]s. This restraint serves [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s rhetorical purpose: if even a theologian of Henriquez's stature reaches such conclusions, the problem lies not in individual excess but in the [[casuistry|casuistic method]] itself.

## Key Positions

Henriquez's moral theology spans homicide, sacramental discipline, and the structure of [[probabilism|probable opinion]]:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Homicide for a box on the ear | It is "perfectly right" to kill a person who has given a box on the ear, even if the offender runs away | 008-032 |
| Infrequent confession | Confession once every five years is sufficient; grouped with [[filiutius|Filiutius]] and others on minimal sacramental obligation | 011-052 |
| Probable opinion on calumny | Alongside Navarre, held the opinion on calumny to be "probable and safe" | 014-008, 014-014 |
| Part of the 24 authorities | Listed among the twenty-four Jesuit theologians whose collective authority is presented as outweighing individual Church Fathers | 006-078 |

The doctrine on homicide "for a box on the ear" is perhaps the most arresting of Henriquez's positions as [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents them. The qualification that killing is permitted "provided it is not done through hatred or revenge" and "there is no danger of giving rise to murders of too great consequence" (008-032) is treated by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] as a paradigm case of how [[casuistry|casuist]]ic qualifications undermine the moral law they purport to interpret.

> "It is perfectly right to kill a person who has given us a box on the ear, although he should run away, provided it is not done through hatred or revenge, and there is no danger of giving rise to murders of too great consequence." (008-032)

Henriquez's position on the frequency of confession — "once in five years" — appears in Letter X alongside a cascade of similarly minimal requirements from other [[casuistry|casuist]]s: [[Filiutius]] (once in three or four years), and others cited by [[Escobar]]. (011-052) [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s point is cumulative: the multiplication of authorities each advocating minimal sacramental practice creates a de facto permission to neglect the sacraments entirely.

## Biography

Henrique Henriquez was born in 1536 in Portugal. He entered the Society of Jesus and became professor of moral theology at the University of Salamanca, one of the premier theological faculties of Catholic Europe. His position at Salamanca placed him at the center of the scholastic revival of moral theology that characterized the Counter-Reformation period, and his opinions carried particular weight in the Iberian theological world.

Henriquez's major work was the *Summa Theologiae Moralis* (Summary of Moral Theology), a comprehensive treatise that addressed the full range of questions in moral theology. The work went through multiple editions and was widely cited by later [[casuistry|casuist]]s, including [[Escobar]], who quotes Henriquez as an authority on homicide. (008-032) Henriquez's incorporation into [[Escobar]]'s anthology of opinions ensured his continuing influence within the Jesuit moral tradition well into the seventeenth century.

Henriquez died in 1608, half a century before the *Provincial Letters*. His doctrines, however, remained current: the Jesuit monk in the *Letters* cites him as a living authority, and [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] treats his opinions as representative of the tradition he seeks to discredit. The gap between Henriquez's death and [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s satire illustrates the durability of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic opinion within the Society of Jesus.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Henriquez occupies a middle tier in the Jesuit theological hierarchy as [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents it. He is not as frequently cited as [[Escobar]] or [[Molina]], but his authority is invoked at crucial moments when the Jesuit monk needs to establish that a particular opinion commands broad consensus. His pairing with Navarre on the question of calumny (014-008) is significant: Navarre (Martín de Azpilcueta) was one of the most respected canonists of the sixteenth century, and Henriquez's association with him lends weight to the opinion that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] considers scandalous.

Henriquez's relationship to [[Filiutius]] is defined by their joint appearance in Letter X on the frequency of confession (011-052), where Henriquez's "once in five years" is juxtaposed with [[filiutius|Filiutius]]'s "once in three or four years." The effect, in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s presentation, is to suggest that the [[casuistry|casuist]]s are engaged in a competition to see who can require the least of the faithful.

Henriquez's inclusion in the list of twenty-four Jesuits in Letter V (006-078) — alongside [[suarez|Suarez]], Vasquez, and other major theologians — positions him as part of the collective authority the Jesuit monk claims outweighs individual Church Fathers. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s response to this claim — that it substitutes the opinion of men for the tradition of the Church — is one of the central arguments of the *Letters*, and Henriquez, by his inclusion in the list, becomes a symbol of this substitution.
