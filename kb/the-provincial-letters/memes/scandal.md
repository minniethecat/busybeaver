## Definition

In the context of the *Provincial Letters*, scandal (*scandale*) refers to conduct or teaching that causes spiritual harm to others by leading them into sin. In Catholic moral theology, scandal is distinguished between *active scandal* (intentionally leading another to sin) and *passive scandal* (where the observer takes offense at an objectively innocent act due to ignorance or weakness). Pascal repeatedly invokes the concept to argue that Jesuit casuistical doctrines — by relaxing moral obligations under the cover of probability — produce widespread scandal among the faithful.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal deploys the charge of scandal as a rhetorical and theological weapon against the Jesuits throughout the *Provincial Letters*. He contends that the Society of Jesus, by permitting morally dubious practices under the authority of probable opinions, creates scandal in at least three respects: first, by undermining the integrity of moral teaching through doctrines that shock the conscience of ordinary believers (007-009); second, by publishing works that contain "scandalous falsehoods" and calumnies against their opponents (013-028); and third, by conducting their polemics in a manner that brings the Church herself into disrepute (016-010). Pascal's friend in the dialogues repeatedly expresses astonishment at the "scandalous and unmeasured license" which the Jesuit fathers grant to their followers (012-025).

The term also figures centrally in Pascal's account of the calumny controversy. He accuses the Jesuits of spreading a "scandalous lie" about Jansenist teaching on grace, alleging that they resorted to equivocal formulations to escape the guilt of their falsehood — confessing it "not publicly, indeed, for fear of scandal, but in a private way" (014-010, 014-018). This distinction between public and private acknowledgment becomes, in Pascal's hands, evidence of bad faith: the Jesuits know their accusations are false but refuse to retract them openly.

Pascal further contends that the scandal reaches its peak when the Jesuits attempt to silence discussion of the very controversies they have provoked. When the Jesuit father complains about the public airing of these disputes, Pascal's interlocutor retorts: "You cannot, it would appear, bear that men should speak of a scandal which you have caused" (014-023).

## Positions

**Jesuit position**: The Jesuit casuists recognized scandal as a genuine moral category but argued that their probabilist system did not generate scandal, because the opinions they endorsed were supported by reputable authorities. They maintained that private circulation of probable opinions among confessors did not constitute public scandal, and that the charge of scandal was being misapplied by rigorist critics who failed to appreciate the legitimate diversity of moral-theological opinion.

**Jansenist / Pascal's position**: Pascal insists that the Jesuit doctrines are intrinsically scandalous, regardless of their private or public character. He points to specific cases as evidence that probabilism systematically generates scandal: the Jesuit teaching that a monk may kill a detractor who threatens to publish scandalous crimes of his community (008-056), the publication of "such a scandalous book" in Paris bearing Jesuit approbation (017-012), and the "scandalous and unmeasured license" introduced into the Church (012-025). For Pascal, the scandal lies not merely in individual opinions but in the entire system that treats moral truth as a matter of majority vote among casuists. He also invokes the scandal caused by calumnious attacks on the Jansenists, noting the "false and scandalous tales" that circulated throughout the court (016-010).

**Dominican / Thomist position**: The Dominicans, while sharing Pascal's concern about laxism, approached scandal through the traditional Thomist framework, emphasizing the objective nature of scandal and the duty to avoid even the appearance of giving offense in matters of faith and morals.

## Key Passages

> "Only think what a scandal it would be, were a monk surprised in the act of turning detractor and assassin." (007-009)

Pascal imagines the scandal of a religious committing violence, using hyperbole to illustrate the contradiction between monastic profession and the moral permissions granted by casuistry.

> "The scandalous and unmeasured license which they have introduced into the Church." (012-025)

This passage captures Pascal's core accusation: that Jesuit permissiveness constitutes a systemic corruption of Christian morality, not merely isolated errors.

> "You cannot, it would appear, bear that men should speak of a scandal which you have caused." (014-023)

Pascal's interlocutor confronts the Jesuit father directly, accusing him of seeking to silence discussion of the very scandal the Society has provoked.
