## Definition

The direction of intention (*directio intentionis*) is a principle of Jesuit casuistry holding that the morality of an act is determined not solely by the act itself but by the end (*finis*) toward which the agent directs his intention.

Pascal's casuist interlocutor describes it as "the grand method of directing the intention — the importance of which, in our system of morality, is such that I could almost venture to compare it with the doctrine of probabilism." (008-008)

In its strictest formulation, an act that would be sinful if done for a sinful end may become licit — or at least not mortally sinful — if the agent "directs the intention" toward a lawful end.

Pascal seizes on this doctrine as emblematic of the entire Jesuit moral system: a mechanism by which any act, however objectively evil, can be justified by a mental redirection of purpose.

## Role in the Controversy

The direction of intention is one of Pascal's most effective satirical targets in the *Provincial Letters*. He presents it as the master key of Jesuit casuistry — the principle that enables all the other laxities. (008-008)

Letter VIII is almost entirely devoted to exposing the doctrine's applications. The casuist explains that "by thus regulating the intention" one may lawfully engage in dueling — not with "the express intention of fighting a duel, but merely of defending one's honor." (008-012, 008-020)

Similarly, a person challenged to a duel "directs his intention solely to the preservation of his honor" — the external act of killing is identical, but the internal direction of intention transforms its moral character. (008-022)

Pascal's spokesman repeatedly punctures this logic: "with all your directing of the intention, that it is all one whether a man fights a duel or not." (008-019) The doctrine effectively collapses the distinction between good and evil acts — since any act can be directed toward a good end, and the direction of intention is an invisible, private mental act inaccessible to external verification.

The casuist's refrain throughout Letter VIII is "you constantly forget the main point — the direction of the intention." (008-030) Pascal presents this as the casuist's universal escape clause: whenever a moral problem seems insoluble, one simply redirects the intention.

## Positions

**The Jesuit position (as presented by Pascal)**. Pascal presents the Jesuit casuists as teaching that the direction of intention can transform the moral character of any act. 

The classic example is the servant sent to deliver a bribe: he may licitly carry the money if he directs his intention not toward the bribe but toward "the profit that will accrue to him." The external act is identical; only the internal direction of intention changes. (008-056)

In the casuistic system, one "must be always taken to direct the intention properly." (008-056) The confessor's role is to instruct the penitent in the proper direction of intention — teaching not how to avoid the sinful act, but how to perform it with a rectified intention.

The casuist even suggests that a person may "so direct his intention as lawfully to kill another for a slight affront." (008-035) It is precisely these extreme applications that make Pascal's satire so devastating.

**Pascal's critique**. Pascal's critique is that the direction of intention makes morality entirely subjective. If the morality of an act depends on an internal mental state that no one else can verify, then there is no external standard by which any act can be condemned.

He treats the doctrine as self-evidently absurd — a reductio ad absurdum of the whole probabilist system. If this is what moral theology has become, he implies, then moral theology has ceased to be a guide to virtue and become a technology of self-deception.

**The rigorist response**. The rigorist position, which Pascal's spokesman endorses, is that the morality of an act is determined primarily by the act itself (*ex objecto*) — not by the agent's subjective intention. A bribe is a bribe, a duel is a duel, regardless of what the agent is thinking while performing it.

The Thomist tradition recognized that intention can affect the morality of an act, but only when the act itself is morally neutral — not when it is intrinsically evil. To claim that directing the intention can make killing licit is to deny that killing is intrinsically evil, which is contrary to natural law.

> "By thus regulating the intention, you may lawfully engage in a duel — not, indeed, with the express intention of fighting a duel, but merely of defending your honor." (008-012, 008-020)
