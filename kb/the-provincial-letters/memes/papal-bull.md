## Definition

A papal bull is a formal decree issued by the Pope, typically bearing a lead seal (*bulla*) and addressing matters of doctrine, discipline, or governance.

In the context of the *Provincial Letters*, papal bulls are central to the controversy over Jansenism: the bull *Cum Occasione* (1653)
of Innocent X condemned five propositions attributed to Jansenius, and subsequent bulls sought to enforce acceptance of this condemnation.

Pascal examines the bulls not only as theological instruments but as procedural artefacts — documents whose authority is contingent on the integrity of the process by which they were obtained.

## Role in the Controversy

Papal bulls appear at multiple junctures in the Jansenist controversy. The Dominicans recall how Clement VIII and Paul V were prevented from publishing bulls on efficacious grace — Clement by death, Paul by Italian political affairs — with the result that "our arms still sleep in the Vatican" (002-021).

This unfinished doctrinal adjudication created the vacuum into which the Jesuit campaign against Jansenius would later move.

The bull *Cum Occasione* forms the juridical backbone of the anti-Jansenist campaign. The five propositions it condemns are attributed to Jansenius's *Augustinus*, but Pascal's central contention is that the question of whether Jansenius actually taught these propositions is a *question of fact*, not of faith.

The bull condemns the propositions as heretical (which Pascal accepts), but does not — and canonically cannot — settle the factual question of their presence in Jansenius's text. The Jesuits, however, systematically conflate the two, treating the papal condemnation of the propositions as a condemnation of Jansenius himself (019-009).

Pascal further argues that bulls can be obtained by "artifice and surprise" (*artifice et surprise*), citing the canonical principle articulated by Alexander III: when there is reason to suspect a decree was procured by deception, its execution may be suspended (019-032).

This established canonical safeguard, Pascal contends, is routinely ignored by those who wield papal authority as a weapon against their theological opponents.

## Positions

**Jesuit party**: Papal bulls condemning the five propositions are definitive and binding in both faith and fact. To question whether Jansenius taught the condemned propositions is to resist papal authority. The bulls settle the controversy; continued debate is disobedience (007-004, 007-007).

**Jansenist defenders (Pascal, Arnauld)**: The bulls bindingly condemn the five propositions as heretical — this is accepted without reservation. But the attribution of those propositions to Jansenius is a factual question, not a doctrinal one, and bulls do not enjoy infallibility on matters of fact.

Moreover, bulls obtained through partisan manipulation enjoy diminished authority under canon law itself (019-032, 019-039).

**Canonical tradition (Alexander III)**: Papal decrees may be legitimately suspended when there is evidence they were procured by "artifice and surprise." The pope's authority is exercised for the settlement of disputes, not for the persecution of one party by another (019-032).

## Key Passages

> "Don't you know what happened under Clement VIII. and Paul V., and how the former having been prevented by death, and the latter hindered by some Italian affairs from publishing his bull, our arms still sleep in the Vatican?" (002-021)

> "We were all ready, without the aid of bulls or briefs, to join with you in condemning that error. What urgent necessity there was for such an explanation! What a host of difficulties has it removed!" (019-009)

> "If we sometimes send decretals to your fraternity which are opposed to your sentiments, give yourselves no distress on that account. We shall expect you either to carry them respectfully into execution, or to send us the reason why you conceive they ought not to be executed; for we deem it right that you should not execute a decree, which may have been procured from us by artifice and surprise." (019-032)

> "It is quite clear, if the popes themselves give no force to their bulls, except in so far as they are founded on genuine facts, that it is not the bulls alone that prove the truth of the facts, but that, on the contrary, even according to the canonists, it is the truth of the facts which renders the bulls lawfully admissible." (019-032)
