## Role in the Controversy

Thomas Sanchez is one of the most frequently cited Jesuit [[casuistry|casuists]] in the *Provincial Letters*, appearing across at least eight letters (V through XII). His authority is invoked by the Jesuit monk to justify a remarkably wide range of moral positions, from probabilism itself to equivocation, duelling, usury, and the treatment of sorcerers.

In the polemical architecture of the letters, Sanchez serves a dual function. First, he is a foundational authority on [[probabilism]] — it is Sanchez who provides the core definition that "the authority of a single good and learned doctor renders an opinion probable." (006-008) Second, his opinions on specific moral cases supply [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] with some of his most striking examples of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic laxity: the permissibility of killing a false witness, the Mohatra contract for usury, and the doctrine of mental reservation.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] groups Sanchez among the "four-and-twenty worthies" whose opinions were compiled by [[Escobar]] in his *Liber theologiae moralis*. (008-012)

## Key Positions

Sanchez's moral theology spans an unusually broad range of topics, making him a versatile authority for the Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]ic system:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| [[probabilism\|Probabilism]] | Authority of a single good and learned doctor renders an opinion probable | 006-008 |
| Confessor's advice | Confessor may give advice contrary to his own opinion if more favorable to the penitent | 007-008 |
| Refusal of absolution | It is a mortal sin to refuse absolution to a penitent acting on a [[probabilism|probable opinion]] | 008-007 |
| Monastic obedience | A monk may follow his own [[probabilism|probable opinion]] over that of his superior | 009-007 |
| Duelling | It is permissible to fight a duel to save one's life, honor, or property | 010-008 |
| Private killing | It is better to privately kill an enemy than to fight a duel | 010-009 |
| Killing false witnesses | One may kill a false witness to protect one's reputation | 010-010 |
| Equivocation | Permitted to use ambiguous terms, leading people to understand them in another sense | 011-008 |
| Mental reservation | One may swear "I never did such a thing" while inwardly meaning "on a certain day" | 011-009 |
| Usury (Mohatra) | The Mohatra contract provides a licit means around the prohibition on usury | 012-007 |
| Sorcerers' restitution | Sorcerers are not obliged to restore money gained through their arts | 013-007 |

The underlying pattern, as with [[Escobar]], is **redefinition by exception**: Sanchez's method extends the scope of morally permissible action by introducing distinctions that carve out ever-wider exceptions from traditional prohibitions.

> "To refuse absolution to a penitent who acts according to a probable opinion is a sin which is in its nature mortal." (008-007)

> "He has also shown them how those who are in disgrace may lawfully use ambiguous terms, leading people to understand them in another sense from that in which they themselves understand them, and that this is always permissible when it is necessary or useful for one's health, honor, or property." (011-008)

## Biography

Thomas Sanchez was born in Cordoba, Spain, in 1550. He entered the Society of Jesus in 1567 and pursued theological studies at the University of Salamanca, the preeminent center of scholastic moral theology in early modern Catholic Europe.

He taught moral theology at the Jesuit college in Granada and later served as master of novices. His reputation as a moral theologian grew steadily through his publications, and he became one of the most respected [[casuistry|casuist]]ic authorities within the Spanish Jesuit province.

Sanchez died in 1610, well before the *Provincial Letters* were written (1656–1657), making him an authority whose opinions were transmitted secondhand through the compilations of later [[casuistry|casuist]]s like [[Escobar]]. Unlike [[Escobar]] — who lived to see himself satirized — Sanchez's posthumous reputation was shaped entirely by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s use of his writings.

## Writings

| Work | Year | Content |
|------|------|---------|
| *De sancto matrimonii sacramento* | 1602 | Comprehensive treatise on the sacrament of marriage, in three volumes |
| *Opus morale in praecepta Decalogi* | 1613 | Moral theology organized around the Ten Commandments (posthumously published) |
| *Consilia moralia* | — | Collected moral counsels for confessors |

Sanchez's treatise on marriage (*De sancto matrimonii sacramento*) was his most influential work during his lifetime and went through multiple editions. It treated the theology, canon law, and moral [[casuistry|casuist]]ry of marriage with encyclopedic thoroughness and was widely cited by subsequent Jesuit moralists, including [[Escobar]].

His *Opus morale*, organized around the Decalogue rather than the order of cases, reflected a systematic approach to moral theology that aimed to provide comprehensive guidance to confessors — precisely the kind of manual that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] would later subject to satirical scrutiny.
