## Definition

Efficacious grace (*gratia efficax*) is the supernatural divine motion that not only enables a good act but infallibly brings it about.

Unlike sufficient grace, which provides the power to act while leaving the act contingent on human consent, efficacious grace determines the will to the act while preserving the will's freedom — because God, as the author of the will, moves it in a manner perfectly congruent with its nature. (001-042)

The concept is foundational to the Thomist and Jansenist theologies, both of which maintain that after original sin, efficacious grace is necessary for any supernaturally meritorious act, including prayer, faith, and contrition. (001-051)

## Role in the Controversy

The necessity of efficacious grace is the theological line that separates the Jansenists and Dominicans from the Jesuits in the *Provincial Letters*. The Jesuits, following Molina, maintain that sufficient grace is given to all, and that it becomes efficacious *through human consent* — the efficacy comes from the will's cooperation, not from the grace itself.

The Jansenists, following Augustine and Jansenius, hold that grace is efficacious *in itself* — it carries its effect within it by the sovereign power of God. (002-007)

Pascal dramatizes this dispute in Letter I through the figure of the Jansenist spokesman who explains to his interlocutor that the entire controversy reduces to a single question: whether efficacious grace is necessary for every good act. The Jansenist says yes; the Jesuit says that sufficient grace suffices.

Pascal's rhetorical strategy is to show that the Jesuit position, while superficially moderate, in practice reduces grace to a mere offer that the autonomous will may accept or reject — a position indistinguishable from the semi-Pelagianism condemned at the Council of Orange (529). (002-013)

## Positions

**The Thomist/Jansenist position**. Both Dominicans and Jansenists affirm that efficacious grace is necessary for supernaturally good acts. God does not merely offer grace; he effects the act through grace.

The difference between the two schools is that Dominicans locate the efficacy in a *physical premotion* — a real modification of the will by God — while Jansenists emphasize the *delectatio victrix* (victorious delight), a movement of the affections that overcomes concupiscence.

Both agree against the Jesuits that grace is efficacious *ex se* (of itself), not *ex consensu* (from consent). (002-009)

**The Jesuit position**. The Jesuits do not deny the existence of efficacious grace; they redefine it. In the Molinist system, "efficacious grace" is simply sufficient grace *plus* the divine foreknowledge that the recipient will freely consent to it.

The efficacy lies not in the grace itself but in the congruence between the grace and the recipient's disposition, which God knows through middle knowledge. Pascal presents this as a distinction without a difference — a verbal concession to tradition that preserves the substance of the Pelagian position. (002-021)

**The political stakes**. The dispute over efficacious grace was not merely academic. The papacy had repeatedly attempted to suppress it: Clement VIII and Paul V both issued decrees forbidding the parties from accusing each other of heresy on this point.

Pascal's treatment of the topic in the *Letters* is therefore a deliberate provocation — he reopens a question that Rome had tried to close, and he does so in the vernacular, for a lay audience, in the midst of the Arnauld censure. (002-024)

## Key Passages

> "Is it not necessary that they have an efficacious grace, in order to pray to God?" — "That is not the doctrine of the Society," replied the father. (001-042)

> "The Dominicans hold that a man can never act without efficacious grace." — "And the Jesuits?" — "They hold the contrary." (002-007)

> "If I admit the sufficient grace without efficacious grace, I am a Pelagian. If I admit it with the Dominicans, I am a hypocrite." (002-013)
