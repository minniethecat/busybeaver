## Definition

The sacrament of penance is the ordinary means by which the Catholic Church grants forgiveness for sins committed after baptism.

Its matter consists of the three acts of the penitent — contrition (or attrition), confession of sins, and satisfaction (performing the assigned penance) — and its form is the words of absolution pronounced by the priest. (011-055)

The Council of Trent defined the sacrament as necessary for salvation for those who have fallen into mortal sin after baptism, and it anathematized those who denied that the priest acts as a judge in the confessional rather than merely as a declarer of forgiveness.

## Role in the Controversy

The sacrament of penance is the institutional context for many of the casuistical questions that Pascal treats in the *Provincial Letters*.

The debate over contrition vs. attrition, the question of what constitutes sufficient satisfaction, and the limits of the confessor's power of absolution are all debates about the nature and requirements of this sacrament. (011-058)

Pascal's central accusation is that the Jesuit casuists have transformed the sacrament of penance from a tribunal of divine justice into a mechanism of cheap grace.

By minimizing the requirements for valid reception — attrition instead of contrition, minimal satisfaction, absolution on demand — they have made the sacrament accessible to the point of meaninglessness. (011-050)

The Jansenist counter-proposal, articulated in Arnauld's *De la fréquente communion*, is that the sacrament should be approached with fear and trembling, and that worthy reception requires genuine conversion of heart — not merely the mechanical performance of prescribed acts.

## Positions

**The Jesuit pastoral approach**. The Jesuits emphasized the accessibility of the sacrament.

Since the Council of Trent had defined penance as necessary for salvation, making it too difficult to receive would endanger souls. The confessor's role, in this view, is primarily medicinal: to heal the penitent through absolution, not to judge whether the penitent deserves healing. (011-048)

**The Jansenist rigorist approach**. The Jansenists, following the ancient penitential discipline, emphasized that the sacrament is a tribunal in which the priest acts as judge.

A judge must determine whether the conditions for absolution are met — genuine sorrow, full confession, firm purpose of amendment — and must refuse absolution when they are not.

Pascal and Arnauld argue that the Jesuit approach abolishes the judicial character of the sacrament. (011-055)

**The confessor's dilemma**. The practical question is whether the confessor should err on the side of mercy (granting absolution when in doubt) or on the side of rigor (refusing it when in doubt).

The Jesuits chose mercy; the Jansenists chose rigor. Pascal's rhetorical achievement is to make the choice of mercy appear not as charity but as corruption.

## Key Passages

> "The ancient schoolmen maintained that contrition was necessary as soon as one had committed a mortal sin; but the moderns hold that attrition is sufficient with the sacrament." (011-048)

> "To exercise an act of perfect contrition, in order to be justified — and this is what the Council of Trent requires." (011-055)

> "Without any sign of contrition except promises a hundred times violated." (011-058)
