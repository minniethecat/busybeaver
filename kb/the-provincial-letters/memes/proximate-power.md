## Definition

Proximate power (*pouvoir prochain*, Latin: *proxima potestas*) is a scholastic theological term denoting the power or capacity that a person possesses to perform a specific act, as distinct from a remote or merely potential capacity. In the context of the grace controversy, it refers to the claim that all the righteous possess the proximate power to observe God's commandments — a formula intended to reconcile the Thomist insistence on the necessity of efficacious grace with the Molinist claim that sufficient grace is given to all.

## Role in the Controversy

The debate over proximate power occupies the entire first Letter and serves as Pascal's entry point into the Jansenist-Jesuit controversy. The dispute begins when the Sorbonne condemns Antoine Arnauld for denying that the righteous have the proximate power to keep the commandments. Pascal's narrative persona — a naive inquirer — visits the Sorbonne doctors to understand what "proximate power" means, only to discover that the term is an empty formula adopted for political peace rather than theological clarity.

Pascal's satirical treatment of proximate power establishes the rhetorical strategy of the entire *Letters*: exposing theological language that sounds orthodox but dissolves into meaninglessness under scrutiny. The Thomists use "proximate power" to mean one thing (power that requires only the addition of efficacious grace), the Molinists use it to mean another (power that is sufficient in itself), and both parties agree on the word while disagreeing on the substance — a verbal peace that conceals doctrinal contradiction.

## Positions

**Thomist/Dominican View**: The Thomists, represented at the Sorbonne, hold that the righteous have the proximate power to keep the commandments, but they add that efficacious grace is necessary to translate this proximate power into actual action. In their system, proximate power is real but incomplete — it is the capacity that becomes operative when God adds efficacious grace. Pascal quotes M. Le Moine as a representative of this position (001-053).

**Jansenist View (Arnauld's position)**: Arnauld denied that the righteous always have proximate power in the sense required by the Molinists. The Jansenists accept that the righteous can keep the commandments when God grants efficacious grace, but they reject the claim that this constitutes a permanent "proximate power" independent of grace. Pascal's narrator, playing the naive inquirer, exposes the ambiguity by asking practical questions: does a man in a dark room have the "proximate power" of seeing (001-046)?

**Molinist/Jesuit View**: The Jesuits insist that all the righteous have the proximate power of observing the commandments (001-038), meaning that sufficient grace is always given and that the failure to act is due to human resistance rather than lack of divine assistance. This position preserves human responsibility but, Pascal argues, makes grace unnecessary.

**The Verbal Compromise**: Pascal's most devastating insight is that the Thomists and Molinists "agreed on this term proximate, which both parties might use in common, though they understood it in different senses" (001-026). This is not a theological resolution but a diplomatic one — a formula that allows both sides to sign the same document while meaning different things by it.

## Key Passages

> "I desired them to inform me what it was to have the proximate power of doing a thing." (001-032)

> "And so, to have the proximate power of crossing a river, for example, is to have everything necessary to cross it." (001-034)

> "Would you say, for instance, that a man in the dark, and without any light, had the proximate power of seeing?" (001-046)

> "M. le Moine calls that power proximate power." (001-053)

> "If he call that power proximate power, he will be a Thomist, and therefore a Catholic; if not, he will be a Jansenist, and therefore a heretic." (001-057)

The final passage (001-057) encapsulates Pascal's critique: the determination of orthodoxy or heresy depends not on what one actually believes but on whether one uses the approved formula. The term "proximate power" has become a shibboleth — a word that separates the orthodox from the heretical regardless of its meaning.
