## Definition

Fasting, in the context of the *Provincial Letters*, refers to the ecclesiastical obligation to abstain from food during prescribed seasons (such as Lent), as required by canon law and Church tradition.

The practice appears in Pascal's satire not as a subject of theological dispute in itself, but as a case study of how Jesuit casuistry systematically undermines ascetic obligations by multiplying grounds for dispensation.

The *Letters* use fasting to illustrate the broader pattern: what the Church presents as a binding duty, the casuists transform into an optional counsel by the ingenuity of their distinctions.

## Role in the Controversy

Fasting enters the *Provincial Letters* in Letter V, where Pascal narrates a visit to a Jesuit casuist. The narrator, seeking guidance on the Lenten fast, is met not with exhortation to self-discipline but with a search for excuses.

The episode functions as an entry point into the larger critique of Jesuit moral theology: instead of holding the faithful to the Church's disciplinary standards, the casuists devote their intellectual resources to finding ways around them (006-017).

The casuist's method is revealed through his consultation of Escobar, who provides a compendium of dispensations. The question "If a man cannot sleep without taking supper, is he bound to fast?" receives the answer "By no means!" — a characteristic example of how a plausible hardship is elevated into a peremptory exemption (006-022).

Pascal's narrator observes this process with mounting irony, noting that the same casuistic machinery could generate dispensations for virtually any circumstance.

## Positions

**Jesuit casuists (Escobar, Filiutius)**: Fasting obligations admit of extensive dispensation. Escobar catalogues numerous grounds for exemption, including difficulty sleeping, fatigue, and even deliberately induced exhaustion.

Filiutius goes further, maintaining that a person who exhausts himself *expressly to obtain a dispensation from fasting* is still not bound to fast — "He will not, even though he should have had that design" (006-035). The intention to evade the obligation does not nullify the exemption.

**The Church's traditional discipline**: Fasting is a binding penitential obligation, grounded in Scripture and the practice of the early Church. Dispensations exist for genuine necessity (illness, age, hard labour), but they are exceptions to be granted by authority, not entitlements to be claimed by individual self-assessment.

**Pascal's implied position**: The casuistic multiplication of dispensations effectively abolishes fasting as a meaningful discipline. When every difficulty generates an exemption, and when even the deliberate manufacture of a difficulty qualifies, the obligation becomes a dead letter. This is not pastoral accommodation but systematic subversion.

## Key Passages

> "I took occasion from the present season, to learn something from him about fasting, and thus slip insensibly into the main subject. I told him, therefore, that I had difficulty in supporting the fast. He exhorted me to do violence to my inclinations; but as I continued to murmur, he took pity on me, and began to search out some ground for a dispensation." (006-017)

> "At length, having sought out the passage on fasting, 'O here it is!' he said; 'treatise 1, example 13, no. 67: If a man cannot sleep without taking supper, is he bound to fast? Answer: By no means! Will that not satisfy you?'" (006-022)

> "'Is a man who has exhausted himself any way — by profligacy, for example — obliged to fast? By no means. But if he has exhausted himself expressly to procure a dispensation from fasting, will he be held obliged? He will not, even though he should have had that design.' There now! would you have believed that?" (006-035)
