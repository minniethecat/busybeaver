## Definition

Absolution is the sacramental formula pronounced by a priest over a penitent: "I absolve you from your sins in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit."

It is the *form* of the sacrament of penance — the words by which Christ, acting through the priest, effects the forgiveness of sins.

The Council of Trent defined that the priest acts as a judge, not merely as a declarer, and that absolution is a judicial act requiring the priest to assess whether the penitent has satisfied the conditions for valid reception. (011-055)

## Role in the Controversy

The question of when a priest may — and must — grant or refuse absolution is at the heart of Pascal's critique of Jesuit confessional practice.

If the conditions for valid reception are minimal (attrition rather than contrition, any confession rather than integral confession), then absolution must be granted in virtually every case.

The confessor ceases to be a judge and becomes what Pascal calls "a dispenser of guaranteed forgiveness." (011-050)

Pascal's specific complaint is that the Jesuit casuists have systematically reduced the conditions for absolution until there is no case in which it can be refused.

A penitent who confesses without genuine sorrow, who has no firm purpose of amendment, who has committed the same sins repeatedly — all are absolved, because the casuists have defined the minimum requirements downward to the lowest possible level. (011-058)

## Positions

**The Jesuit position on the confessor's power**. The Jesuits maintained that the confessor's power of absolution is extensive and should be exercised generously.

The priest acts *in persona Christi*, and Christ came to save, not to condemn. The presumption should always be in favor of absolution. The conditions for valid reception — sorrow, confession, purpose of amendment — are to be interpreted leniently, so that no penitent who desires forgiveness is turned away. (011-048)

**The Jansenist position on the confessor's duty**. The Jansenists maintained that the confessor's first duty is to judge.

A priest who absolves an unworthy penitent participates in the sin and profanes the sacrament. The conditions for valid reception are objective and must be verified — it is not enough that the penitent claims to fulfill them.

Arnauld's *De la fréquente communion* argues that the ancient Church required lengthy periods of public penance before absolution, precisely to ensure that the penitent's conversion was genuine. (011-055)

**The question of deferred absolution**. The most practically contested point was whether a confessor may defer absolution — that is, require the penitent to demonstrate amendment over time before receiving forgiveness.

The Jesuits generally opposed deferral as contrary to the pastoral purpose of the sacrament. The Jansenists insisted that deferral is sometimes necessary as an exercise of the confessor's judicial function — precisely the function the Jesuits had, in their view, abandoned.

## Key Passages

> "The Council of Trent requires an act of perfect contrition in order to be justified." (011-055)

> "Without any sign of contrition except promises a hundred times violated, they are absolved." (011-058)

> "Attrition, induced by the mere dread of temporal punishment, is sufficient with the sacrament." (011-051)
