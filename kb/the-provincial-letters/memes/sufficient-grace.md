## Definition

Sufficient grace (*gratia sufficiens*) is the divine assistance that God grants to all human beings, enabling them to fulfill his commandments and resist temptation. In the theological vocabulary of the seventeenth century, it is distinguished from *efficacious grace* (*gratia efficax*), which not only enables but infallibly produces the good act.

The distinction turns on whether grace requires the free consent of the human will to become effective. (002-006)

## Role in the Controversy

The doctrine of sufficient grace became a flashpoint in the controversy between the Jesuits and the Jansenists because it crystallized the deeper disagreement about human free will after the Fall. The Jesuits, following Luis de Molina, maintained that sufficient grace is genuinely given to all and becomes efficacious through human consent.

The Jansenists, appealing to Augustine, held that after original sin the human will requires *efficacious* grace from the outset — what the Jesuits called "sufficient grace" was, in their view, never truly sufficient without an additional divine motion. (002-007)

Pascal devotes much of Letter II to exposing what he regards as a semantic compromise: the Dominicans (Jacobins) and the Jesuits both use the phrase "sufficient grace," but they mean entirely different things by it.

The Dominicans, pressed by papal authority, adopted the terminology while preserving their Thomist theology through a distinction between grace that is sufficient *in itself* and grace that is sufficient *in effect*. Pascal portrays this as an equivocation designed to preserve the appearance of doctrinal unity. (002-009)

## Positions

**The Jesuit position**. The Jesuits maintained that a sufficient grace is given to all human beings without exception. This grace is truly adequate for the performance of good acts; it becomes efficacious when the human will freely consents to it.

The Jesuit theologians cited by Pascal — including Le Moine, Lessius, and Bauny — grounded this view in their commitment to human free will and the universal salvific will of God. (002-008)

**The Jansenist position**. The Jansenists, following the Augustinian tradition, held that the grace given to fallen humanity must be intrinsically efficacious. A grace that merely "enables" without actually producing the good act is, for the fallen will, no grace at all.

Pascal — writing as the Jansenist spokesman — argues that the Jesuits have emptied the term of meaning by making sufficient grace dependent on human consent rather than divine power. (002-013)

**The Dominican (Thomist) compromise**. The Dominicans, represented in the Letters by figures such as Nicolai, attempted to occupy a middle ground.

They accepted the language of "sufficient grace given to all" while maintaining, through the distinction between grace sufficient *in sensu composito* and grace sufficient *in sensu diviso*, that this grace only becomes operative through an additional efficacious motion from God.

Pascal presents this as a tactical ambiguity — the Dominicans adopted the Jesuits' vocabulary to avoid censure while preserving their traditional theology. (002-014)

**The political dimension**. The controversy over sufficient grace was not merely theological. The term "sufficient grace" became a litmus test of orthodoxy: to deny it was to risk condemnation as a Jansenist, while to affirm it in the Jesuit sense was to abandon the Augustinian tradition.

Pascal's critique is that the Church authorities, particularly the Sorbonne, exploited this ambiguity to condemn Arnauld without resolving the substantive theological question. (002-022)

## Historical Background

The sufficient-grace controversy has its roots in the *de auxiliis* controversy (1582–1607), in which the Dominicans and Jesuits debated the relationship between divine grace and human free will.

Pope Clement VIII presided over the Congregatio de Auxiliis but died before issuing a definitive ruling; his successor Paul V closed the proceedings without resolving the dispute, decreeing only that neither side should accuse the other of heresy.

This unresolved tension provided the theological backdrop for the Jansenist revival of Augustinian rigorism and for Pascal's critique of Jesuit "sufficient grace" as a hollow formula. (004-021)

> "The Jesuits content themselves with admitting a sufficient grace given to all men; but they maintain that this grace is never efficacious without a further motion from God." (002-008)

> "If I deny the sufficient grace, I am a Jansenist. If I admit it like the Jesuits, I am a Pelagian. If I admit it like the Dominicans, I speak one way and think another." (002-013)

> "The Dominicans reply, that it has no sufficient grace until God is pleased to bestow an additional aid, which they call efficacious grace." (002-017)

> "If the term sufficient grace be once established, it will be easy for them to slip in their efficacious grace under its cover." (002-022)
