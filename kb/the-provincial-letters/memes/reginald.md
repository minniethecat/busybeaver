## Role in the Controversy

Valerius Reginaldus appears in the *Provincial Letters* as one of the principal Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]ic authorities whose moral rulings [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] subjects to sustained critique. Cited across Letters V through XIV on a remarkable range of topics — homicide, calumny, sorcery, fasting, and probabilism itself — Reginald exemplifies the [[casuistry|casuistic method]] [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] attacks: the application of subtle distinctions to render permissible what traditional moral teaching would condemn.

Reginald's authority within the Jesuit tradition is underscored by his repeated pairing with other major [[casuistry|casuist]]s. The Jesuit monk cites him alongside [[cellot|Cellot]], [[tanner|Tanner]], [[filiutius|Filiutius]], [[molina|Molina]], [[Escobar]], and [[lessius|Lessius]], treating his opinions as established reference points in the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic landscape. (006-072, 008-056) [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s strategy with Reginald, as with other [[casuistry|casuist]]s, is to quote his actual rulings and let their moral implications speak for themselves, while the narrator's ironic commentary frames the reader's judgment.

## Key Positions

Reginald's rulings cited in the *Letters* span the full range of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic moral theology:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| [[probabilism|Probabilism]] in morals | Reginald asserted with [[cellot|Cellot]] that "in matters of morality we are to follow, not the most probable, but the least probable and most benign opinions" | 007-034 |
| Homicide against slanderers | Reginald maintained that "the opinion that we may kill a man for a simple buffet is probable in practice" | 008-042 |
| Homicide by churchmen | Reginald and [[tanner|Tanner]] ruled "the article must be of great value in the estimation of everyone" for homicide to be justified | 008-050 |
| Sorcery and the arts | Reginald ruled that "a sorcerer who has employed the diabolical art" is not obliged to reveal it | 011-011 |
| Killing for money | Reginald addressed, with [[filiutius|Filiutius]], the question of killing one who threatens to reveal a secret unless paid money | 014-016 |
| Usury | Reginald ruled on *mohatra* contracts and other usurious practices alongside [[Escobar]] and [[lessius|Lessius]] | 008-056 |

The most characteristic passage appears in Letter VI, where the Jesuit monk explains the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic principle in Reginald's own words: "according to our fathers [[cellot|Cellot]] and Reginald, 'in matters of morality we are to follow, not the most probable, but the least probable and most benign opinions.'" (007-034) This formulation — following the *least* [[probabilism|probable opinion]] because it is the most lenient — captures the inversion of moral reasoning that [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s entire polemic targets.

> "According to Reginald and Tanner, 'the article must be of great value in the estimation of everyone'" (008-050)

In Letter XIII, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] devotes sustained attention to Reginald's rulings on homicide. He notes that "your authors, [[filiutius|Filiutius]], Reginald, and others, reply: 'This is permissible,'" regarding killing to prevent the revelation of a secret, and that "your fathers Reginald, [[filiutius|Filiutius]], and others, have handled the question of killing for money." (014-016, 014-018) [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] contrasts Reginald's permissive rulings with Scripture's prohibition of homicide, arguing that the [[casuistry|casuistic method]] has effectively nullified divine law through its system of exceptions and distinctions. "Reginald has not, perhaps, broken the bonds; but [[filiutius|Filiutius]] certainly has," the narrator remarks, tracing the progressive relaxation of moral constraints from one [[casuistry|casuist]] to the next. (014-021)

## Biography

Valerius Reginaldus (French: Valère Regnauld) was born around 1545 in France and entered the Society of Jesus as a young man. After completing his theological studies, he taught moral theology at Jesuit colleges in France and Italy, eventually holding a chair at the Collegium Romanum, the principal Jesuit educational institution.

Reginald's major work, the *Praxis Fori Poenitentialis* (Practice of the Penitential Forum), was first published in 1616 and became a standard reference work for confessors throughout Catholic Europe. The *Praxis* addressed the full range of moral questions that arose in the administration of the sacrament of penance: the conditions for absolution, the assessment of mortal versus venial sin, the obligations of restitution, and the evaluation of doubtful cases. Like other comprehensive [[casuistry|casuist]]ic manuals, it was organized by topic and provided summaries of authoritative opinions on each question, typically with the author's own judgment on the most probable resolution.

Reginald's approach was characteristically Jesuit: he emphasized the liberty of the penitent and the latitude of the confessor, drawing on the principle of probabilism to resolve doubtful cases in favor of the less rigorous opinion. This earned him a prominent place in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s catalogue of lax [[casuistry|casuist]]s, though within the Society of Jesus his work was regarded as balanced and responsible. He died around 1623, nearly three decades before the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist controversy erupted, but his writings remained in active use and were frequently cited by the [[casuistry|casuist]]s [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] attacked.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Reginald functions within the *Letters* as part of a dense network of mutually-reinforcing Jesuit authorities:

- **[[cellot|Louis Cellot]]**: Reginald is frequently cited alongside [[cellot|Cellot]]; [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] quotes their joint formulation that the "least probable and most benign" opinion should be followed in morals. (006-072, 007-034)
- **[[tanner|Adam Tanner]]**: Reginald and [[tanner|Tanner]] are paired on the question of what value of article justifies homicide. (008-050)
- **Vincenzo [[filiutius|Filiutius]]**: The most closely-linked pair in the *Letters*; [[filiutius|Filiutius]] and Reginald are cited together on killing for money, sorcery, and the progressive relaxation of homicide prohibitions. (014-016 through 015-029)
- **[[molina|Molina]], [[Escobar]], [[lessius|Lessius]]**: Reginald appears alongside these authorities in the rosters of [[casuistry|casuist]]s [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] enumerates to demonstrate the breadth of support for lax moral opinions. (008-056, 015-014)
