## Overview

The Council of Trent (1545–1563) was the 19th ecumenical council of the Catholic Church and the centerpiece of the Counter-Reformation. Convened in response to the Protestant Reformation, it defined Catholic doctrine on Scripture, original sin, justification, the sacraments, and other contested topics.

In the *Provincial Letters*, both Jesuits and [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists invoke the Council as a doctrinal authority. The question is not whether Trent is authoritative but what it actually teaches — and whether 17th-century theological innovations are faithful to its decrees.

## Background

By the early 16th century, the Protestant Reformation had challenged core Catholic doctrines. Martin Luther's teaching on justification by faith alone and the rejection of several sacraments created an urgent need for the Church to clarify its own positions.

The Council was delayed for decades by political conflicts between the papacy and the Holy Roman Empire, and by wars between France and the Habsburgs. It finally convened in 1545 under Pope Paul III in the city of Trent (Trento), chosen as a compromise location between Rome and the German lands.

The Council met in three distinct periods (1545–47, 1551–52, 1562–63) under three popes, with lengthy interruptions due to political tensions and plague. Its decrees were confirmed by Pope Pius IV in 1564.

## Role in the Letters

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] cites the Council of Trent at strategic moments to demonstrate that [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist positions are consistent with established Church teaching while Jesuit innovations depart from it.

In Letter XVII, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] appeals to Trent's teaching on the Eucharist: "There is nothing more in them but what the Council of Trent teaches (sess. xiii., c. 8)." (017-020) This passage is part of [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s defense of [[Arnauld|Antoine Arnauld]] against charges of heresy.

In Letter XIX, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] returns to Trent to anchor his argument about grace: "Our merits are the gifts of God," as the Council of Trent says. (019-013) By citing Trent, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] positions [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ism not as an innovation but as fidelity to conciliar teaching.

> "There is nothing more in them but what the Council of Trent teaches (sess. xiii., c. 8), that Jesus Christ is in the Eucharist in a singular manner." (017-020)

## Key Decrees Relevant to the Letters

| Decree | Session | Content | Relevance |
|--------|---------|---------|-----------|
| Decree on Justification | Session VI (1547) | Defined the role of grace and free will | Central to [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist–Jesuit debates |
| Decree on the Eucharist | Session XIII (1551) | Defined real presence and transubstantiation | Cited by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] at 017-020 |
| Decree on Penance | Session XIV (1551) | Defined sacramental confession | Relevant to [[casuistry|casuist]]ry and confessional practice |
| Decree on the Sacrifice of the Mass | Session XXII (1562) | Defined the Mass as propitiatory | Background to [[simony]] debates |

The Decree on Justification was especially significant because it rejected Protestant sola fide while also implicitly rejecting Pelagianism — creating a middle path that both Jesuits and [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists claimed to represent faithfully.

## Significance in the Controversy

Both parties in the *Provincial Letters* claimed the Council of Trent as their authority. The Jesuits argued that their moral theology — including [[probabilism]] — was consistent with Trent's pastoral orientation. The [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists argued that Trent's teaching on grace and justification supported their Augustinian rigorism.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s rhetorical strategy exploits this shared reference point: by demonstrating that Jesuit positions cannot be reconciled with Trent's actual language, he forces the Jesuits to choose between abandoning their innovations or diminishing their conciliar authority.

This appeal to Trent reflects a broader strategy in the letters — [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] consistently argues that [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ism is orthodox Catholicism, not a sectarian deviation, and that it is the Jesuits who have departed from the tradition of the Fathers and Councils.
