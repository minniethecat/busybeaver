## Definition

Venial sin (*peccatum veniale*) is, in Catholic moral theology, a sin that wounds the soul's relationship with God but does not destroy sanctifying grace. It is distinguished from *mortal sin* by three criteria: (1) the matter is light rather than grave; (2) full advertence of the intellect may be absent; (3) full consent of the will may be lacking. Venial sin merits temporal rather than eternal punishment and can be remitted by penitential acts other than sacramental confession — though confession remains the ordinary means.

The practical boundary between venial and mortal sin is precisely what Pascal contests in the *Letters*. The Jesuit casuists, he charges, systematically reclassify grave sins as venial by manipulating the criteria of grave matter, full knowledge, and complete consent. (010-038)

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal's attack on Jesuit casuistry in the later *Letters* (especially Letters X–XVI) devotes sustained attention to the venial–mortal distinction. His central charge is that the Jesuit confessors, under the guise of pastoral accommodation, have rendered the distinction meaningless — classifying calumny, gluttony, and even homicide as "merely venial" through clever doctrinal maneuvers. (016-009)

The mechanism of reclassification Pascal describes is systematic: the casuists deploy doctrines such as probabilism, mental reservation, and direction of intention to show that the penitent lacked either full knowledge or full consent, thereby downgrading an act that appears mortally sinful into something venial. (010-078)

Pascal's rhetorical strategy is to let the casuists' own words indict them. He quotes their manuals verbatim, allowing the reader to see for themselves how "gluttony is not even a venial sin" under certain constructions, or how "calumny is only a venial sin" when the false accusation is made under a particular set of mental qualifications. (010-060)

## Positions

**The traditional position**. The Church's traditional teaching, reaffirmed at Trent, holds that the distinction between mortal and venial sin is objective — it follows from the nature of the act, not from the confessor's ingenuity. Grave matter, full knowledge, and deliberate consent are the three conditions for mortal sin, but these conditions are not infinitely malleable.

**The Jesuit position (as represented by Pascal)**. The Jesuit casuists treat the three conditions as variables that the skilled confessor can manipulate. If the penitent did not fully understand the gravity of calumny, his calumny is venial. If the penitent's intention was directed toward a benevolent outcome, his otherwise-grave act becomes venial. Pascal quotes Escobar and Bauny to this effect. (010-078)

**The pastoral counter-argument**. The Jesuits would respond that the confessional is a court of mercy, not a court of law, and that the confessor's role is to bind and loose — to apply the law pastorally, which sometimes means finding reasons to treat sins as venial so that the penitent is not driven away. Pascal's reply, implicit throughout Letters X–XI, is that mercy without truth is cruelty — it leaves the sinner in his sin while promising him peace.

## Key Passages

> "'For this would be only a venial sin, unless they sought after greater gain.'" (010-038)

> "Is gluttony, then, not even a venial sin?" (010-059)

> "According to the same author, it would be a venial sin, 'were a person to gorge himself, from the mere love of eating, even to the point of vomiting.'" (010-060)

> "According to our Father Bauny, this is only a venial sin, or rather no sin at all." (010-078)

> "What is it but a venial sin to calumniate and forge false crimes to ruin a brother's reputation?" (016-009)
