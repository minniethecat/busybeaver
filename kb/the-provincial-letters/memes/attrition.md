## Definition

Attrition (*attritio*) is imperfect sorrow for sin, motivated by the fear of divine punishment, the ugliness of sin, or the loss of eternal reward.

It is distinguished from *contrition*, which is perfect sorrow flowing from the love of God above all things. (011-045)

The Council of Trent taught that while contrition is the ordinary means of justification, attrition "prepares the sinner to obtain the grace of God in the sacrament of penance" (Session XIV, Chapter V).

The practical question contested in the *Provincial Letters* is whether attrition alone — without contrition — is sufficient for valid absolution.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal's critique of Jesuit confessional practice in Letter X hinges on the contrition–attrition distinction. The Jesuits taught that attrition is sufficient for the sacrament of penance when accompanied by priestly absolution.

Pascal presents this as a theological mechanism for making confession effortless and absolution automatic. (011-048)

If attrition can be elicited by the mere fear of hell — and if the fear of hell can be elicited by the mere recitation of a formula — then every penitent can satisfy the minimum requirement.

The confessor becomes not a judge of the penitent's disposition but a dispenser of guaranteed forgiveness. (011-050)

Pascal contrasts this with the traditional rigorist position: that true sorrow from charity is necessary, and that failing to require it turns the sacrament into a mechanical transaction.

## Positions

**The Jesuit position**. The Jesuit casuists, including Bauny, Escobar, and Filiutius, maintained that attrition is sufficient for valid absolution.

Some went further, arguing that contrition is not merely unnecessary but actually undesirable — because the effort to achieve perfect contrition might discourage penitents from confessing at all. (011-054)

The Jesuit position was defended on pastoral grounds: requiring contrition from every penitent would effectively bar most Christians from the sacrament, since the ordinary faithful cannot reliably distinguish contrition from attrition. (011-049)

**The Jansenist position**. Pascal's Jansenist spokesman argues that the Jesuit doctrine of attrition empties the sacrament of its moral content.

If the minimum requirement for absolution can be met by a fear that any baptized person can mechanically produce, then there is no meaningful standard left.

The sacrament becomes not a remedy for sin but a guarantee of impunity — exactly the "laxism" that Pascal attributes to the entire Jesuit moral system. (011-051)

**The Dominican (Thomist) middle ground**. The Dominican tradition maintained that attrition, while insufficient on its own, becomes sufficient *in the sacrament* — that is, the grace of the sacrament elevates attrition to the level required for justification.

This position preserves the pastoral accessibility of the sacrament without reducing it to a mechanical formula. Pascal does not attack this view directly but implies that the Jesuit casuists have abandoned even this moderate standard.

## Key Passages

> "It is almost an article of faith, that attrition, induced merely by fear of punishment, is sufficient with the sacrament." (011-046)

> "If attrition with the sacrament did not suffice, there would be very few who would be saved." (011-048)

> "Attrition, induced by the mere dread of temporal punishment, is sufficient with the sacrament." (011-051)

> "Attrition in such cases is quite enough; for contrition would only disturb the peace of the penitent." (011-054)
