## Definition

**Piety** (Latin: *pietas*) is the theological virtue of religious devotion, filial reverence toward God, and the lived expression of faith through worship and moral conduct. In the context of the *Provincial Letters*, Pascal distinguishes between authentic Christian piety—rooted in the spirit of charity and obedience to divine law—and a superficial, accommodating piety promoted by certain Jesuit casuists.

## Role in the Controversy

Piety stands at the centre of Pascal's polemic against Jesuit moral theology. Pascal charges that the casuists' probabilistic system, far from guiding the faithful toward greater devotion, systematically relaxes moral obligations to the point of undermining genuine piety. The controversy crystallises around Father Le Moine's *La Dévotion Aisée* ("Devotion Made Easy"), which Pascal presents as emblematic of a theology that adapts Christian morality to worldly comfort rather than elevating the world to Christian standards.

Pascal argues that the Jesuit approach substitutes an external, manageable piety for the internal transformation that the Gospel demands. He contends that by teaching men they can satisfy religious obligations without genuine contrition, without restitution of ill-gotten goods, and without abandoning sinful attachments, the casuists "strike at the very heart of piety" and "rob it of the spirit that gives it life" ([011-059]).

The *Provincial Letters* repeatedly invoke the contrast between the "spirit of piety"—which prompts truthfulness, charity, and rigour in moral matters—and the "spirit of the world," which seeks accommodations and easy paths ([012-027]). Pascal treats this distinction as the fundamental choice facing 17th-century French Catholicism.

## Positions

### Jesuit Position (as characterised by Pascal)

The Jesuit casuists, particularly Father Le Moine, advanced what Pascal characterises as an accessible, indulgent model of piety:

- Piety can be made "easy" through casuistic distinctions that reduce the scope of moral obligation ([012-032])
- The rich are not guilty of mortal sin by refusing alms, since their obligations are less stringent than traditionally taught ([010-040])
- Religious practices can be adapted to worldly lifestyles without compromising salvation
- The "spirit of piety" permits a measure of raillery and wit even in theological controversy ([012-029])

### Jansenist / Pascal's Position

Pascal advances a rigorous Augustinian conception of piety:

- True piety is inseparable from charity and the spirit of the Gospel, not merely external observance
- Casuistic accommodations that weaken moral obligation corrupt piety itself ([011-059])
- The "spirit of piety always prompts us to speak with truth and sincerity," forbidding the equivocations and mental reservations that casuists permit ([012-027])
- Piety demands restitution and genuine contrition, not merely probable opinions that excuse from these obligations
