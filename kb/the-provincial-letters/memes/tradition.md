## Definition

Tradition (*traditio*) is, in Catholic theology, the living transmission of the apostolic deposit of faith — doctrines, practices, and institutions handed down from the apostles through the Church's teaching authority under the guidance of the Holy Spirit. The Council of Trent defined tradition as a source of divine revelation co-equal with Scripture, "received by the apostles from the mouth of Christ himself or dictated by the Holy Spirit, and preserved in the Catholic Church by a continuous succession."

In the *Provincial Letters*, the term functions as a polemical weapon. Both Pascal and his Jesuit adversaries claim tradition as their ally; the dispute turns on the *content* of tradition — whether it is an unchanging doctrinal inheritance (the Jansenist view) or a living, developing organism capable of accommodating new formulations (the implicit Jesuit view). (004-015)

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal's primary appeal to tradition is forensic: he accuses the Jesuits of innovating where they should be preserving, of replacing the ancient faith of the Church with "new opinions" and "modern authors." (005-011) The charge is rhetorically devastating because the Jesuits, as defenders of papal and conciliar authority, cannot openly admit to innovating — yet Pascal's extensive quotation of their casuistic manuals shows doctrines that bear little resemblance to the patristic sources they claim to inherit.

The flashpoint is the interpretation of the Council of Trent. The Jansenists insisted that Trent's decree on grace and free will must be interpreted in conformity with the earlier Augustinian tradition, especially Augustine's anti-Pelagian writings. The Jesuits, by contrast, read Trent as a new doctrinal synthesis that need not be constrained by the views of individual Church Fathers. Pascal characterizes this as a rejection of tradition in favor of novelty. (004-019)

Pascal also deploys tradition as a criterion for recognizing heresy. If a proposition contradicts the unanimous teaching of the Fathers, it is heretical — regardless of papal or conciliar approbation. This is Pascal's strongest implicit claim: that the Jesuits, not the Jansenists, are the true innovators, and that the papal condemnation of the five propositions must be read in light of tradition, not against it. (006-046)

## Positions

**The Jansenist position**. Tradition is the definitive norm of Catholic orthodoxy, and Scripture must be interpreted according to the unanimous consent of the Fathers. Since Augustine is the preeminent Latin Father on questions of grace, his anti-Pelagian writings provide the interpretive key to Trent's decrees. Any teaching that contradicts Augustine on grace, free will, or predestination is a departure from tradition — and therefore heretical.

**The Jesuit position (as represented by Pascal)**. The Jesuits pay lip-service to tradition while systematically subordinating it to what Pascal calls "human prudence." Their casuistic method — in which the opinions of modern authors carry decisive weight — effectively replaces the patristic consensus with a new magisterium of probabilist theologians. (006-015) When a Jesuit confessor follows Escobar rather than Augustine, he is, in Pascal's view, abandoning the apostolic tradition for a recent fabrication.

**The Molinist middle**. The Molinists sought to reconcile the Jesuit emphasis on free will with the Augustinian emphasis on grace, arguing that their system preserves both tradition and the Church's developing understanding. Pascal dismisses this as incoherent: either the Molinist "sufficient grace" is what Augustine meant by grace (which is historically indefensible), or it is a novelty that contradicts Augustine (which is doctrinally unacceptable).

> "It is powerfully supported by the tradition of the Church." (004-019)
