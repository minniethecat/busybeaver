## Role in the Controversy

Father Nicolai occupies a distinctive position in the *Provincial Letters*: he is introduced in the very first letter as the representative of a mediating theological school — the Dominican "New Thomists" — whose positions, in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s telling, expose the arbitrariness of the Sorbonne's condemnation of [[Arnauld]].

The narrator's [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist informant identifies Nicolai as a test case: "let us see if you are acquainted with any of the Dominicans whom they call the 'New Thomists,' for they are all the same with Father Nicolai." (001-031)

Nicolai's role is structural rather than biographical. He appears in Letter I not for his own doctrines but as a marker of theological pluralism within the Church.

The informant's argument runs: the New Thomists hold positions on grace substantially similar to those for which [[Arnauld]] is being condemned, yet the Dominicans are not accused of heresy. The discrepancy reveals that the condemnation is political rather than doctrinal.

The informant's earlier remark — that the Jesuits' "malicious designs" against the Molinists are a matter "which I am unwilling to believe on his word" (001-027) — sets up the framework within which Nicolai is introduced. The narrator is being educated in the arcane vocabulary of the controversy.

## Key Positions

Nicolai's theological positions, as presented in Letter I, are defined by his identification with the New Thomist school:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| New Thomism | Dominican school that attempted to reconcile Thomistic theology with contemporary debates on grace | 001-031 |
| Proximate power | The New Thomists' position was substantially identical to that for which [[Arnauld]] was condemned | 001-031 |
| Theological equivalence | Nicolai's doctrines serve as evidence that the Sorbonne censure of [[Arnauld]] was inconsistent | 001-031 |

The informant's identification of Nicolai is presented as a decisive refutation of the consistency of the Sorbonne's censure. (001-031) If the Dominicans may hold a given position without censure, on what grounds is [[Arnauld]] condemned for holding the same view? The question is never answered directly; it is left to hang over the entire subsequent controversy.

> "Well, let us see if you are acquainted with any of the Dominicans whom they call the 'New Thomists,' for they are all the same with Father Nicolai." (001-031)

## Biography

The biographical details of Father Nicolai are sparse. He was a Dominican theologian active in the mid-seventeenth century, associated with the "New Thomist" movement within the Order of Preachers. The New Thomists sought to defend the theology of Thomas Aquinas on grace and free will while engaging with the intra-Catholic disputes between Molinists and [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists.

Nicolai's exact identity is not fully established in the historical record. He may be identified with Jean Nicolai (1594–1673), a French Dominican and doctor of the Sorbonne who wrote on Thomistic theology, or with another Dominican of the same surname.

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s use of his name as a shorthand for the entire New Thomist school suggests that he was sufficiently well known to [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s intended audience to serve as a recognizable representative of the Dominican position.

## References in the Letters

Nicolai appears exclusively in Letter I, where he serves as a test case in the narrator's theological education:

| Letter | NNN | Content | Key PN |

| Letter I | 001 | Introduced as representative of Dominican New Thomists; his position contrasted with Sorbonne's censure of [[Arnauld]] | 001-027, 001-031 |

The concentration of Nicolai's appearances in a single letter reflects his function in the argument. He is introduced to establish a point about theological inconsistency, and once that point is established, he is not needed again. His cameo in Letter I is nonetheless structurally significant, introducing a theme sustained throughout the *Letters*.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Nicolai's principal relationship is to the Dominican "New Thomist" school, of which he is presented as the archetypal representative. (001-031) This school is positioned between the Jesuit Molinists and the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists, whose position on grace is the immediate subject of the controversy.

By invoking Nicolai, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] appeals to the authority of the Dominican theological tradition — historically the guardians of Thomistic orthodoxy within the Catholic Church — as a counterweight to Jesuit dominance at the Sorbonne. The argument implies that if the Dominicans may teach what [[Arnauld]] teaches, the condemnation of [[Arnauld]] cannot rest on theological principle alone.
