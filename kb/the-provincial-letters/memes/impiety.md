## Definition

**Impiety** (Latin: *impietas*; French: *impiété*) is the vice contrary to the virtue of piety—a lack of due reverence toward God, sacred persons, sacred things, or sacred doctrine. In theological discourse, impiety is distinguished from heresy (which involves doctrinal error) and blasphemy (which involves verbal offense), though the terms frequently overlap in polemical usage.

## Role in the Controversy

"Impiety" functions in the *Provincial Letters* as one of the most powerful rhetorical weapons deployed by both sides of the Jansenist controversy. Pascal's Jesuit adversaries accused the Jansenists of impiety for allegedly teaching that the commandments of God are impracticable and that grace is irresistible—positions they characterized as disrespectful to divine justice and human freedom.

Pascal turns the accusation back on his opponents with considerable force. He identifies the "spirit of buffoonery, impiety, and heresy" ([012-029]) as characteristic of Jesuit polemical methods. More substantively, he charges that the Jesuit casuists' moral teachings—which permit killing, theft, and false witness under probabilistic justifications—constitute a genuine impiety far more serious than the doctrinal errors of which the Jansenists stand accused.

The climax of Pascal's impiety argument appears in his treatment of Jesuit casuistry on homicide: the notion that a religious may kill a detractor "without violating the commandment not to kill" represents, for Pascal, the very "climax of impiety" ([011-059]).

## Positions

### Jesuit Accusation

- The Jansenist doctrine of grace is impious because it makes God the author of sin
- Teaching that the commandments are impracticable is an impiety against divine goodness
- The Jansenist resistance to papal condemnation constitutes impiety against ecclesiastical authority

### Pascal's Counter-Accusation

Pascal identifies the following as genuinely impious:

- The Jesuit moral system that permits homicide, calumny, and theft under subtle distinctions ([011-059])
- The "spirit of buffoonery, impiety, and heresy" that mocks sacred things ([012-029])
- The accusation of impiety against the innocent, which itself constitutes a form of impiety ([016-018])

## Key Passages

> "[011-059] …this, sir, is the very climax of impiety. The price of the blood of Jesus Christ, and the sealing of the Holy Spirit—these are the things that are trifled with!"

> "[012-029] …whereas the spirit of buffoonery, impiety, and heresy, mocks at all that is most sacred…"

> "[016-018] …them—by imposture or by impiety; and when the most innocent men are accused of the blackest crimes…"

> "[018-029] …to confound Jansenism with heresy would be an act of impiety. Now, all those whom you condemn as heretics profess to be nothing but Catholics…"
