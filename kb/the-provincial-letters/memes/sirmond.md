## Role in the Controversy

Antony Sirmond appears in the *Provincial Letters* as the author whose work represents, in [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s telling, the furthest reach of Jesuit moral theology on the question of the obligation to love God. When the Jesuit monk introduces Sirmond's *The Defence of Virtue*, he declares that "our Father Antony Sirmond surpasses all on this point" — the point being the systematic relaxation of the commandment to love God — and quotes Sirmond's own boast that he "speaks French in France" as evidence of his pastoral accessibility. (011-054)

Sirmond's role in the controversy is that of the practical popularizer. Where other Jesuit theologians addressed the question of divine love in Scholastic Latin, Sirmond produced a vernacular work designed to reach the lay reader and the confessional. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s satire of Sirmond's tone — the claim to "speak French in France" — targets precisely this attempt to make theologically radical conclusions appear commonsensical and native to the French Catholic tradition.

The *Letters* present Sirmond as a key member of a coordinated Jesuit defense of laxist moral theology on the question of divine love, naming him alongside [[Annat]], Pintereau, and [[Le-Moine]] as among those who "have strenuously defended it when it has been attacked." (011-055) This grouping places Sirmond within the mainstream of French Jesuit polemics rather than at their fringe.

## Key Positions

Sirmond's moral theology, as [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents it, centers on the question of whether Christians are obligated to love God:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Obligation to love God | Argued that St. Thomas's view (obligation arises at age of reason) is "rather too soon"; Scotus's (every Sunday) is also too demanding | 011-054 |
| Defence of Virtue | Wrote a vernacular treatise discharging men from the "painful obligation" of actually loving God | 011-054 |
| Defense of probabilism | Alongside [[annat|Annat]], Pintereau, and [[le-moine|Le Moine]], vigorously defended dispensations from moral obligation | 011-055 |
| Defense of Theodoret | Defended Theodoret of Cyrus from charges of Nestorian heresy | 018-034 |

The centerpiece of [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s presentation of Sirmond is the extended quotation from *The Defence of Virtue*, in which Sirmond reviews and dismisses successive theological opinions on the obligation to love God. St. Thomas's view — that the obligation arises as soon as one attains the use of reason — is judged "rather too soon." Scotus's view — that it arises every Sunday — is dismissed with "pray, for what reason?" Others, who hold that the obligation arises under severe temptation, are met with "yes, if there be any other way of escape." (011-054)

> "St. Thomas says that we are obliged to love God as soon as we come to the use of reason: that is rather too soon! Scotus says, every Sunday: pray, for what reason?" (011-054)

[[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s rhetorical strategy is to let Sirmond's own words convict him. The breezy, colloquial tone in which Sirmond dispatches centuries of theological reflection on the greatest commandment is presented as evidence of a moral theology that has lost its bearings.

## Biography

Antony Sirmond (also rendered Antoine Sirmond) was born in 1591 and entered the Society of Jesus in France. He was the nephew of the more famous Jesuit scholar Jacques Sirmond, the distinguished patristics editor and confessor to Louis XIII. Antony Sirmond's own career was devoted to moral theology and controversy rather than patristic scholarship.

Sirmond's principal work, *The Defence of Virtue* (published in French as *La Défense de la Vertu*), was a vernacular treatise on moral theology that addressed questions of probabilism and moral obligation for a lay readership. The work became notorious through [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s citation in the *Provincial Letters* and remains known primarily through this hostile presentation. Sirmond also wrote in defense of the Jesuit position on grace and free will, participating in the polemical exchanges that surrounded the condemnation of [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ism.

Sirmond died in 1643, thirteen years before the first *Provincial Letter* appeared, and thus could not reply to [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s use of his work. His posthumous presence in the *Letters* is characteristic of [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s method: citing authorities whose deaths placed them beyond the reach of correction.

## References in the Letters

Sirmond appears in two letters, on two distinct topics:

| Letter | NNN | Content | Key PN |
|--------|-----|---------|--------|
| Letter X | 011 | *The Defence of Virtue* cited on the obligation to love God; defended alongside [[annat|Annat]], Pintereau, [[le-moine|Le Moine]] | 011-054, 011-055 |
| Letter XVIII | 018 | Defense of Theodoret of Cyrus from charges of Nestorian heresy | 018-034 |

In Letter X, Sirmond is the culminating figure in the Jesuit monk's catalogue of authorities who have dispensed Christians from the obligation to love God. In Letter XVIII, his appearance in defense of Theodoret serves a different function: [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] uses Sirmond's willingness to defend a figure accused of heresy as a contrast to the Jesuits' unwillingness to extend the same charity to the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Sirmond is presented in the *Letters* as part of a coordinated group of Jesuit defenders of laxist moral theology. He is named alongside [[Annat]] — the Jesuit provincial of Paris and [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s principal antagonist — as well as Pintereau and [[Le-Moine]] as among those who "have strenuously defended" the doctrine dispensing with the obligation to love God. (011-055) This grouping links Sirmond's popularizing work to the highest institutional authority of the French Jesuits.

His defense of Theodoret of Cyrus in Letter XVIII (018-034) connects Sirmond to the patristic scholarship tradition represented by his uncle Jacques Sirmond and to [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s broader argument about the double standard the Jesuits apply to theological evaluation — generous to pre-[[Jansenius|Jansen]]ist figures accused of heresy, relentless toward the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists themselves.
