## Definition

**Satisfaction** (Latin: *satisfactio*) is, in Catholic sacramental theology, the third act of the penitent in the sacrament of penance, following **contrition** (sorrow for sin) and **confession** (the oral accusation of sins). It consists in the performance of the works of penance assigned by the confessor—prayer, fasting, almsgiving, or other pious acts—by which the penitent makes amends for the temporal punishment that remains after the guilt of sin has been forgiven.

The Council of Trent (1551, Session XIV) defined satisfaction as an integral part of the sacrament, affirming that the priest acts as judge in the confessional and must impose a "just and proportionate" penance. Theologically, satisfaction serves not only as punishment but as medicine: it heals the disorder left by sin and strengthens the penitent against future temptation.

In the *Provincial Letters*, satisfaction becomes a key measure of whether Jesuit confessional practice takes the sacrament seriously or reduces it to an empty ritual.

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal's treatment of satisfaction is embedded within his broader critique of the Jesuit approach to the sacrament of penance. He argues that having already relaxed the requirement of genuine contrition—substituting attrition (imperfect sorrow) for contrition (perfect sorrow)—the Jesuits further diminish the sacrament by rendering satisfaction a negligible formality.

The evidence Pascal marshals is stark. He reports the teaching that "for the honor of the sacrament, the confessor may impose a very light penance" ([011-013]), and that it suffices to "administer something in the way of penance, for the form's sake" ([011-015]). The implication is clear: satisfaction has ceased to be a genuine act of reparation and has become a ritual gesture devoid of moral content.

Pascal locates this relaxation within the larger Jesuit strategy of making the sacraments maximally accessible. The casuists, he notes, have "softened the use of the sacraments, and particularly that of penance" ([010-090]). This pastoral leniency may appear merciful, but Pascal insists that it renders the sacrament a mechanism of false assurance—assuring sinners of forgiveness while leaving their attachment to sin untouched.

The contrast Pascal draws is between the ancient penitential discipline of the Church—which imposed prolonged, public penances proportionate to the gravity of the sin—and the Jesuit practice, in which satisfaction is diminished to the point of vanishing.

## Positions

### Jesuit Pastoral Position

The Jesuit authorities cited by Pascal held:

- The confessor may impose only a light penance "for the honor of the sacrament" ([011-013])
- It is sufficient to "administer something in the way of penance, for the form's sake" ([011-015])
- Penitents may refuse any penance unless they "choose to accept it" ([011-058])
- The severity of ancient penances need not be imitated; modern pastoral practice is governed by mercy rather than rigour
- Attrition together with the sacrament suffices for justification, without the demanding works of satisfaction that contrition would require ([011-050])

### Pascal's Critique

Pascal's response draws on the Church's ancient penitential tradition:

- Satisfaction without genuine reparation is an empty form, a mockery of divine justice
- The relaxation of satisfaction is not mercy but a dereliction of the confessor's duty as judge
- A system that demands nothing of the penitent teaches him that sin has no real consequence
- The ancient Church imposed severe and lengthy penances precisely because it took sin seriously ([015-021])
