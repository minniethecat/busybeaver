## Role in the Controversy

Juan Caramuel y Lobkowitz occupies a distinctive position in the *Provincial Letters*: though not a Jesuit himself, he is presented by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] as one of the foremost champions of the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic moral theology the Jesuits promoted. The Jesuit monk in the dialogue introduces Caramuel as "our famous champion" who quotes and amplifies the doctrines of Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]s, and [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] uses Caramuel's writings — particularly his *Fundamental Theology* (*Theologia Fundamentalis*) — to trace the logical consequences of Jesuit moral principles to their most alarming conclusions. (008-058)

Caramuel appears across two distinct sections of the *Letters*: first in Letters VI–VII on the direction of intention and the justification of homicide against slanderers, and later in Letter XV on the morality of calumny. In both contexts, [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents him not as an originator of the doctrines but as the systematizer who draws out their extreme implications — what Caramuel himself calls "the conclusion of conclusions" (*conclusionum conclusio*). (008-058) This makes him a uniquely useful witness for [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s polemical strategy: Caramuel demonstrates that the principles established by the Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]s, when followed honestly to their logical end, produce morally monstrous results.

Caramuel's dedication of his *Fundamental Theology* to [[diana|Antonino Diana]] — the prolific Sicilian [[casuistry|casuist]] — is repeatedly cited by [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] to underline the mutual endorsement among [[probabilism|probabilist]] writers. (007-023) In this dedication Caramuel declares that [[diana|Diana]] "has rendered many opinions probable which were not so before" (*quæ antea non erant*), serving [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] as evidence that probabilism was an engine for manufacturing moral permission: what was once sinful becomes, through the accumulation of [[casuistry|casuist]]ic opinions, permissible.

## Key Positions

Caramuel's contributions to the moral theology critiqued in the *Letters* center on three interconnected doctrines:

| Doctrine | Summary | Key PN |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Homicide against slanderers | Caramuel concluded that a priest may not only kill a slanderer but that circumstances exist where it becomes his duty (*etiam aliquando debet occidere*) | 008-058 |
| Calumny as venial sin | Caramuel defended the [[probabilism|probability]] of the opinion that calumniating another to preserve one's reputation involves no mortal sin | 016-009 |
| [[probabilism|Probabilism]] as permission-generator | Caramuel's praise of [[diana|Diana]] for rendering opinions probable that were not so before (*quæ antea non erant*) exemplifies the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic mechanism | 007-023 |

The most striking passage in the *Letters* concerning Caramuel appears in Letter VII, where the Jesuit monk explains that Caramuel, "quoting it in his Fundamental Theology, p. 543, thinks it so certain, that he declares the contrary opinion to be destitute of [[probabilism|probability]], and draws some admirable conclusions from it." (008-058) Among these conclusions, Caramuel extends [[lamy|Father Lamy]]'s doctrine on killing slanderers to ask, with apparent seriousness: "*May the Jesuits kill the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists?*" (008-058) [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s narrator responds with heavy irony: "A curious point of divinity that, father! I hold the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists to be as good as dead men, according to [[lamy|Father Lamy]]'s doctrine."

> "Caramuel infers the very reverse from the same principles." (008-063)

The monk's retort — that Caramuel actually concludes the [[Jansenius|Jansen]]ists may *not* be killed — does not rescue the framework for [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]. The narrator's reply sharpens the critique: the very fact that the question is debated within a system where "the intention of the wounder conveys no comfort to the wounded" demonstrates the moral bankruptcy of the [[casuistry|casuistic method]]. (008-060)

In Letter XV, Caramuel reappears as an authority on calumny. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] quotes him directly: "There can be no doubt that it is a [[probabilism|probable opinion]] that we contract no mortal sin by calumniating another, in order to preserve our own reputation." (016-009) Caramuel lists more than twenty grave doctors who support this position, adding that "were this doctrine not probable, it would be difficult to find any one such in the whole compass of theology" — a remark [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] seizes upon to indict the entire [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic system. (016-009)

Caramuel also provides [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] with a concrete illustration of the doctrine's social consequences. He recounts how a German countess communicated Dicastille's maxim on calumny to the daughters of the empress, with the result that "in the course of a few days ... such an immense number of false and scandalous tales" spread through the court that a Capuchin friar had to be called in to undo the damage. The anecdote serves [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]]'s argument that these are not merely abstract theological positions but doctrines with real, corrupting social effects. (016-010)

## Biography

Juan Caramuel y Lobkowitz was born in Madrid in 1606 to a distinguished family with ties to both Spanish and Bohemian nobility. A child prodigy, he mastered Latin, Arabic, and mathematics before adolescence. He entered the Cistercian order and studied at the University of Salamanca and the University of Alcalá, where he absorbed the full range of Scholastic theology.

Caramuel's intellectual range was extraordinary even by seventeenth-century standards. He published on theology, philosophy, mathematics, astronomy, linguistics, music theory, and architecture — earning him comparisons to Leibniz among some historians. His theological works, particularly the *Theologia Fundamentalis* and the *Theologia Moralis*, engaged extensively with the [[probabilism|probabilist]]ic moral theology that dominated Catholic ethical discourse in the mid-seventeenth century.

Though a Cistercian rather than a Jesuit, Caramuel aligned himself closely with the Jesuit theological tradition. He corresponded with and admired the great Jesuit [[casuistry|casuist]]s, and his *Fundamental Theology* was dedicated to [[diana|Antonino Diana]]. This alliance made him a natural target for [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]], who treated Caramuel's extreme conclusions as the logical endpoint of the Jesuit moral system.

Caramuel served as vicar-general of the Archbishopric of Prague and later became bishop of Vigevano in Lombardy (1673–1682). He died in Vigevano in 1682, leaving behind a corpus of over 260 printed works.

## Relationship to Other Figures

Caramuel's position in the intellectual network of the *Letters* is defined by three key relationships:

- **[[diana|Antonino Diana]]**: Caramuel dedicated his *Fundamental Theology* to [[diana|Diana]], praising him for multiplying [[probabilism|probable opinion]]s. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] treats this mutual endorsement as evidence of a closed system of self-authorizing probabilism. (007-023)
- **[[lamy|François Lamy]]**: Caramuel built upon [[lamy|Lamy]]'s doctrine that a priest may kill a slanderer, extending it to claim the act may sometimes be a *duty*. [[Blaise-Pascal|Pascal]] presents this as the logical trajectory of the entire tradition. (008-058)
- **Dicastille**: Caramuel cited Dicastille's defense of calumny as a venial sin, and the anecdote of the German court's moral collapse under Dicastille's maxim appears in Caramuel's own work. (016-009, 016-010)
