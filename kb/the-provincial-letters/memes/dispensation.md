## Definition

In Catholic canon law, a dispensation (*dispensatio*) is the relaxation of a merely ecclesiastical law in a particular case, granted by a competent authority for a just cause. Unlike an abrogation, which permanently removes a law, a dispensation suspends the law's binding force for a specific person or situation. In the *Provincial Letters*, Pascal criticizes what he sees as the Jesuit abuse of the concept — extending dispensations far beyond their proper scope to excuse moral obligations that the Church has never authorized relaxing.

## Role in the Controversy

Dispensation plays a specific but significant role in Pascal's attack on Jesuit moral theology. He argues that the Jesuit casuists effectively create a system of private dispensations from divine and ecclesiastical law, allowing the faithful to evade obligations under the guise of probable opinions. The most striking example Pascal cites is the claim that the Jesuits have effectively granted a "dispensation from loving God" (011-059) — a privilege that Pascal's interlocutor finds shocking, as love of God is a fundamental precept of Christian life.

Pascal also highlights the practical mechanism through which such dispensations operate. He describes how Jesuit confessors, rather than imposing the full rigor of moral law, search out grounds for dispensation: the casuist "suggested a number of ways" by which a penitent might obtain release from obligations (006-017). This instrumental approach to dispensation — where the casuist actively seeks pretexts to relax the law rather than requiring a genuine just cause — is presented as characteristic of Jesuit moral method.

The economic dimension of dispensation does not escape Pascal's notice. He draws attention to the cost of such privileges, remarking that one may form "some idea of the value of this dispensation, from the price" the Jesuit father names (011-055). By linking dispensation to monetary transaction, Pascal suggests that the Jesuit system commodifies spiritual goods, reducing the moral law to a purchasable commodity.

## Positions

**Jesuit position**: The Jesuit casuists defended their use of dispensation on two grounds. First, they argued that probable opinions, backed by reputable authorities, provided the requisite just cause for relaxing ecclesiastical obligations. Second, they maintained that the pastoral context demanded flexibility — the rigor of the law must be tempered by mercy, and dispensation was a legitimate instrument of pastoral accommodation. The dispensation from fasting, for instance, was justified by reference to the varying capacities of penitents (006-035).

**Jansenist / Pascal's position**: Pascal contends that the Jesuit practice of dispensation exceeds canonical limits. When a casuist expressly procures a dispensation from fasting for himself, Pascal asks rhetorically whether such a person truly acts for a just cause (006-035). He treats the "dispensation from loving God" (011-059) as a reductio ad absurdum of the entire system: if the greatest commandment can be dispensed with, no obligation is secure. For Pascal, the Jesuit dispensations are not pastoral accommodations but systematic evasions of moral responsibility.

**Canonical / Thomist position**: Traditional canon law recognized dispensation as a legitimate exercise of ecclesiastical authority but insisted on strict conditions: a competent superior, a just cause proportionate to the law being dispensed, and the good of souls. The lax application of dispensation without these conditions was widely criticized by rigorist theologians, not only by Jansenists.

## Key Passages

> "He suggested a number of ways" by which to obtain a dispensation. (006-017)

Pascal portrays the Jesuit casuist as actively manufacturing grounds for dispensation rather than requiring genuine just cause — an inversion of the canonical principle that the superior judges the cause, not the petitioner.

> "This dispensation from loving God is the privilege of the Society of Jesus." (011-059)

The most rhetorically charged passage on dispensation in the *Letters*. Pascal transforms a technical canonical concept into a symbol of Jesuit spiritual presumption — claiming for their followers an exemption from the most fundamental Christian obligation.
