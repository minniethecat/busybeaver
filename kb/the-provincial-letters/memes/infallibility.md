## Definition

In the *Provincial Letters*, infallibility operates at two levels. At the theological level, it describes the certainty and irresistible efficacy of divine grace — whether God's operation in the soul produces its effect infallibly (the Thomist/Augustinian position) or depends on human consent (the Molinist position). At the ecclesiastical level, it concerns whether papal and conciliar judgments on matters of faith are binding and incapable of error.

## Role in the Controversy

The concept of infallibility is central to the grace controversy that animates the *Letters*. The Jansenists, following Augustine and Thomas Aquinas, hold that efficacious grace works infallibly — that "the infallibility of the divine operation" (019-010) ensures its effect without overriding human freedom. The Jesuits (Molmists) reject this, holding that sufficient grace is given to all but becomes efficacious only through human consent. Pascal presents the Jansenist position as the traditional doctrine and the Jesuit position as an innovation that undermines the certainty of salvation.

At the ecclesiastical level, infallibility bears on the authority of the papal condemnation of the Five Propositions. Pascal must navigate between affirming the pope's authority to condemn error while questioning whether the propositions as condemned are actually found in Jansenius's *Augustinus*. This distinction between "right" and "fact" becomes a key argumentative strategy.

## Positions

**Jansenist/Augustinian View**: Efficacious grace works infallibly. "The infallibility of the divine operation does not hinder the liberty of the will" (019-010). The Jansenists hold that when God grants efficacious grace, the will freely and certainly consents. This preserves both divine sovereignty and human freedom. "The grace of Jesus Christ insures infallible perseverance in piety" (019-017) — the effect of grace is certain, not probabilistic.

**Jesuit/Molinist View**: The Jesuits deny the infallible efficacy of grace, teaching instead that sufficient grace is given to all and that its efficacy depends on human free consent. Pascal presents this as making salvation dependent on human will rather than divine gift, and as introducing uncertainty into the economy of salvation.

**Papal/Conciliar Infallibility**: Pascal affirms the binding authority of papal and conciliar judgments on matters of faith. He references "the decisions of the popes, the councils, and the Scripture" (007-003) as normative sources. However, he distinguishes between matters of "right" (doctrinal condemnation) — where the Church's judgment is binding — and matters of "fact" (whether Jansenius actually taught the condemned propositions) — where fallible human judgment applies. Pascal notes that even popes can be "in this matter not infallible" (018-030) when judging factual questions.

## Key Passages

> "The infallibility of the divine operation does not hinder the liberty of the will." (019-010)

> "The grace of Jesus Christ insures infallible perseverance in piety." (019-017)

> "The Word of God being infallible in the facts which it records..." (019-035)

On ecclesiastical authority, Pascal argues through his Jesuit interlocutor that the casuists' opinions are "so infallible, that no other sign is needed" to follow them (011-024) — an ironic statement that contrasts the Jesuits' claimed certainty with their actual methodological flexibility.
