## Definition

Obedience (*oboedientia*) is the moral virtue by which a person submits his will to the legitimate command of a superior for the sake of the common good or the divine will. In the Catholic tradition, obedience is not mere external compliance but an interior act of the will — a free submission to authority recognized as legitimate. The object of obedience is the superior's command; its motive is the duty owed to lawful authority, ultimately grounded in the authority of God.

The *Provincial Letters* explore the limits of this virtue. Pascal's Jesuit interlocutors invoke obedience to justify the practice of following one's superior's opinion even when one's own conscience disagrees. Pascal's response is that obedience to men — whether to a religious superior, a confessor, or a papal decree — never absolves a person of the duty to obey God. An erroneous conscience binds; a command that contradicts conscience does not. (007-037)

## Role in the Controversy

The doctrine of obedience appears in the *Letters* in two interconnected contexts: the vow of religious obedience and the obedience owed to ecclesiastical decrees.

**Religious obedience**. Pascal quotes Jesuit casuists who teach that a religious who follows his superior's opinion — even when that opinion is probabilistically doubtful — acts virtuously, because the merit of obedience outweighs the defect of the opinion followed. This is Pascal's reductio of probabilism: if any probable opinion is a safe guide, and if the superior's command makes an opinion probable, then the religious can never be blamed for following a command — no matter how morally dubious. (007-039)

**Ecclesiastical obedience**. The second context is the Jansenist response to the papal condemnation.

Pascal's party insists that it has "yielded all due obedience" to the decrees condemning the five propositions, while distinguishing between the doctrinal condemnation (which is accepted) and the factual assertion that the propositions are in Jansenius (which is a question of fact, not of faith). (019-009)

The issue is whether obedience requires assent to every particular in a decree, or only to its doctrinal substance. Pascal argues that it requires only the latter — and that the Jesuits' insistence on total submission is a political maneuver, not a theological argument.

Pascal's deeper point is that the Jesuit doctrine of obedience is incoherent: it exalts the virtue of submission while simultaneously providing casuistic devices that allow the subject to evade every concrete obligation. The religious obeys his superior — but only after the casuist has defined "obedience" so narrowly that almost nothing counts as disobedience. (007-041)

## Positions

**The Jesuit position (as represented by Pascal)**. Obedience to religious superiors is virtually absolute, and the merit of obedience can sanctify an act that would otherwise be sinful. This follows from the probabilist framework: if the superior's command creates a probable opinion that the act is lawful, the subject may follow it safely. Moreover, obedience to papal decrees requires complete submission, including to the factual assertions embedded in them.

**The Jansenist position**. Obedience is a genuine virtue but is bounded by the prior duty to God and to conscience. An erroneous conscience binds; a command that contradicts a certain conscience does not. The Jansenists accept the papal condemnation of the five propositions but deny that the pope has the authority to bind the faithful to a factual claim — that these words appear in this book — that is accessible to ordinary reading.

**The Dominican via media**. The Dominicans, whose votes were crucial in the Sorbonne censure, sought to affirm the binding character of papal decrees while preserving the traditional limits on obedience. This middle position collapsed under pressure from both sides: the Jesuits accused the Dominicans of Jansenist sympathies, while Pascal accused them of sacrificing truth to institutional peace. (014-022)
