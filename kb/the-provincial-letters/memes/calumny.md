## Definition

Calumny, in the *Provincial Letters*, designates the deliberate making of false and injurious statements about another person. The term carries a double significance in Pascal's work.

On one level, it is the subject of casuistic analysis: Jesuit theologians, applying their probabilistic method, treated calumny against a calumniator as a merely venial sin — a position Pascal presents as emblematic of their moral corruption.

On another level, calumny is the defining crime of the Jesuit party itself: their entire campaign against Jansenius, Arnauld, and Port-Royal is, in Pascal's analysis, one sustained act of calumny, perpetrated under cover of theological orthodoxy.

## Role in the Controversy

Calumny first appears in the *Letters* as a subsidiary example of casuistic moral logic. In Letter VII, the Jesuit monk notes in passing that "we may kill a man for calumny" — the opinion being "not without its probability" (008-042). The casual brutality of the remark exemplifies the moral world Pascal is exposing.

The theme receives its fullest treatment in Letters XV and XVI, where Pascal accuses the Jesuits of a systematic campaign of calumny against their theological opponents. The accusation is carefully constructed.

Pascal first establishes that the Jesuit casuists themselves treat calumny as a trivial matter: they hold that "calumny was only a venial sin" and that "calumny against your enemies is no crime" (016-010, 017-032). This moral minimalisation of calumny, Pascal argues, explains the Jesuits' own behaviour: they calumniate because their theology tells them calumny does not matter.

The calumny in question is the imputation of heresy to Jansenius, Arnauld, and their defenders. Pascal contends that the Jesuits know — or ought to know — that this charge is false.

The five propositions are indeed heretical, and Jansenius's defenders accept their condemnation; but the attribution of those propositions to Jansenius is factually contestable, and the Jesuits' refusal to produce textual evidence for the attribution reveals that their campaign is political, not theological.

"Why did you not take the plan which I followed in bringing to light the wicked maxims of your authors — which was to cite faithfully the passages of their writings from which they were extracted?" (019-026). The Jesuits cannot cite chapter and verse because the verses do not exist.

The structural irony is deep: the Jesuits, who minimise calumny in their moral theology, are themselves the great calumniators; their victim is Jansenius, whom they calumniate by attributing to him propositions he did not teach;

and Pascal's own method — the method of exact quotation from published sources — is the antithesis of calumny, because it rests on verifiable evidence rather than partisan assertion.

## Positions

**Jesuit casuists**: Calumny is a venial sin when committed against a calumniator. More broadly, "calumny against your enemies is no crime" — an opinion that, however qualified in the casuistic literature, effectively licenses defamation as a tool of ecclesiastical politics (016-010, 017-032).

**Pascal's moral analysis**: Calumny is a grave sin, explicitly prohibited by the Eighth Commandment. The casuistic minimisation of calumny is self-serving: it provides theological cover for the Jesuits' own campaign of defamation against Jansenius and Arnauld.

"A single calumny may prove mortal to an infinite number of souls" (017-033) — the harm of calumny is measured not by the intention of the calumniator but by its effect on its victims and the public.

**Pascal's methodological rebuttal**: The antidote to calumny is the method of exact citation. Pascal's own practice in the *Letters* — quoting Jesuit authors by book, treatise, and page number — demonstrates that factual claims about what someone has written can and should be verified by reference to the text.

The Jesuits' refusal to do the same for their accusations against Jansenius reveals those accusations as calumnies (019-026).

## Key Passages

> "I am aware that those who are not very well acquainted with you, are at a great loss what to think on this subject, as they find themselves under the painful necessity, either of believing the incredible crimes with which you charge your opponents, or (what is equally incredible) of setting you down as slanderers." (016-007)

> "They had quite a different idea of calumny from that which they suppose you to have, and that you actually believe that in blackening and defaming your adversaries you are working out your own salvation." (016-007)

> "It was the general opinion of all your writers, that calumny was only a venial sin." (016-010)

> "Maintaining, as you have done, that calumny against your enemies is no crime." (017-032)

> "A single calumny may prove mortal to an infinite number of souls." (017-033)

> "Why did you not take the plan which I followed in bringing to light the wicked maxims of your authors — which was to cite faithfully the passages of their writings from which they were extracted?" (019-026)

> "Instead of acceding to this, they had gone off to Rome, and procured a bull, ordaining all men to acknowledge the truth of their statement ... they would never have had recourse to this extraordinary method, but for want of the natural means of substantiating the truth." (019-026)
