## Definition

Molinism is the theological system developed by the Spanish Jesuit Luis de Molina in his 1588 treatise *De concordia*. Its central innovation is the doctrine of *scientia media* (middle knowledge): God knows, before any decree of his will, what every possible free creature would freely choose in every possible circumstance.

This knowledge allows God to arrange circumstances such that free creatures freely choose what God has predestined, without violating their freedom. (004-027)

## Role in the Controversy

Pascal invokes Molinism as the intellectual foundation of the entire Jesuit moral system that he attacks in the *Provincial Letters*. In his view, Molinism is not merely a speculative theology of grace — it is the engine that produces the lax casuistry of Escobar, Bauny, and their colleagues.

By making human freedom the decisive factor in salvation, Molinism generates an optimistic anthropology in which the commandments are always fulfillable (002-007) and the confessor's task is to minimize rather than to judge. (004-028)

The specific connection Pascal draws is this: if God's grace is always resistible (the Molinist position), then the decisive factor in salvation is human choice. And if human choice is decisive, then the Church should make salvation as accessible as possible — which means making moral obligations as light as possible.

Pascal sees this logic unfolding inevitably from Molina's premises through the casuist manuals to the confessional practices he satirizes.

## Positions

**The Molinist system (Jesuit)**. Molina taught that God possesses three types of knowledge: natural knowledge (of all possibles), free knowledge (of his own decrees), and middle knowledge (of what every free creature would do in every circumstance).

Using middle knowledge, God can predestine the elect without predetermining their free acts — he simply creates the circumstances in which he knows they will freely choose salvation.

The Jesuits presented this as a middle way between the Dominican doctrine of physical premotion (which seemed to destroy freedom) and the Pelagian denial of grace (which destroyed divine sovereignty).

**The Jansenist critique**. Pascal's critique, echoing Jansenius's *Augustinus*, is that Molinism is Pelagian in effect if not in name. By making grace resistible and human freedom the decisive factor, Molinism renders grace ornamental rather than efficacious (002-021). The system's "middle knowledge" is a philosophical fiction with no basis in Scripture or tradition (004-028).

Pascal presents Molinism not as a solution to the grace–freedom problem but as a rhetorical evasion of it — and as the root cause of the moral laxity he documents throughout the Letters. (004-028)

**The Dominican (Thomist) alternative**. The Dominicans maintained the traditional Thomist doctrine of physical premotion: God moves the will intrinsically and infallibly to the good act, while preserving freedom because the divine motion is congruent with the will's nature.

They rejected middle knowledge as an unnecessary and unprovable addition to the traditional attributes of God. The Congregatio de Auxiliis (1598–1607) was convened to adjudicate this dispute but ended inconclusively, leaving both positions permitted.
