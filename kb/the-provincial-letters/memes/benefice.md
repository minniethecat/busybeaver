## Definition

A **benefice** (Latin: *beneficium*) is an ecclesiastical office—such as a parish, bishopric, or canonry—to which a fixed income or endowment is attached. In 17th-century France, benefices were frequently subject to trafficking, patronage abuse, and simony, making them a key target of Pascal's critique of ecclesiastical corruption.

## Role in the Controversy

In the *Provincial Letters*, the question of benefices arises within the broader discussion of simony—the buying and selling of spiritual goods. Pascal exposes Jesuit casuists who developed elaborate distinctions to permit practices that amounted to purchasing benefices while technically avoiding the canonical definition of simony.

The Jesuit casuists argued that giving money "as a motive" to resign a benefice was distinct from purchasing it outright—a distinction Pascal treats with evident sarcasm. He further reports their teaching that receiving money "as an acknowledgment" after a benefice has been conferred does not constitute simony, since no prior contract existed.

Pascal frames the benefice question as emblematic of a larger pattern: the Jesuit application of probabilistic reasoning to erode clear moral prohibitions. What canon law plainly forbids becomes permissible through a cascade of subtle distinctions.

## Positions

### Jesuit Casuist Position

The Jesuit casuists cited by Pascal held that:

- Money given "as a motive to resign a benefice" is not simony, because the resignation is a distinct act from the conferral ([012-019])
- Receiving payment "as an acknowledgment" after a benefice has been received does not constitute simony ([013-019])
- A benefice worth four thousand livres might be given in exchange for money if structured as a "gratitude" rather than a price ([013-021])
- Promising money to obtain a benefice is distinct from actually paying for it ([013-024])

### Pascal's Critique

Pascal treats these distinctions as manifestly self-serving, asking whether giving "money as a motive to resign a benefice, is to purchase the benefice" ([012-019]). He presents the Jesuit reasoning as sophistry designed to legitimize what any honest observer would recognize as simony.

## Key Passages

> "[012-019] …money as a motive to resign a benefice, is to purchase the benefice?"

> "[013-021] …simoniacal, were he to give a benefice worth four thousand livres of rent for a sum of money, even though it should be given merely as an acknowledgment?"

> "[008-008] …those who give money for benefices might be downright simoniacs…"

> "[013-024] …simony to get possession of a benefice by promising a sum of money, without the intention of paying it—because this is only an ingenious method of acquiring a benefice without giving anything for it!"
