## Definition

Equivocation (*aequivocatio*), also known as mental reservation (*restrictio mentalis*), is the doctrine by which a speaker may use words that, taken in their ordinary sense, would be false, but which are made true by a mental qualification that the speaker reserves to himself and does not communicate to the hearer.

The doctrine distinguishes between "pure" mental reservation (where no external sign is given — universally condemned by theologians) and "broad" mental reservation (where the words themselves can bear an alternative interpretation). Jesuit casuists developed the latter into a sophisticated system for navigating conflicts between the obligation to speak truthfully and other obligations, such as the seal of confession or the protection of reputation.

Pascal treats equivocation as the emblematic abuse of Jesuit casuistry — a technique that allows priests to say one thing while meaning another, and that systematically undermines the trustworthiness of speech.

## Role in the Controversy

Equivocation is one of the central targets of Pascal's satire in the *Provincial Letters*. He presents it as the mechanism by which Jesuit confessors enable sin while appearing to condemn it.

In Letter V, Pascal's spokesman confronts the Jesuit father with the charge of equivocation directly, and the father flatly denies it: "I assure you there is no such thing as equivocation here." (005-020)

But in subsequent letters, Pascal documents a system of equivocations used to soften moral obligations — by redirecting intention, by distinguishing the letter from the spirit of the law, and by employing definitions that are technically correct but substantively deceptive.

In Letter X, the Jesuit father describes equivocation as a practical tool: "In such cases, our doctrine of equivocations has been found of admirable service." (010-060) The context is the system by which priests can absolve penitents while appearing to maintain doctrinal rigor.

By Letter XVII, Pascal's tone has shifted from satirical to polemical. He contrasts the "barefaced subtleties of your modern equivocations" with the straightforwardness of early Christian moral teaching (017-012), and observes that if the Jesuits were "the best Catholics alive," then "no equivocations would have been discovered in their writings" (017-016).

## Positions

**The Jesuit defense**. Jesuit casuists presented equivocation as a legitimate application of the principle that speech exists to serve truth and charity, not to entrap the speaker. If a direct answer would violate the seal of confession, endanger an innocent person, or reveal a state secret, the doctrine of equivocation provides a morally permissible alternative to lying.

The Jesuit father in the Letters defends the practice by arguing that it is based on the distinction between the words spoken and the speaker's intention — a distinction that, he claims, St. Augustine himself recognized (010-060).

**Pascal's critique**. Pascal's critique operates on several levels.

First, he argues that equivocation destroys the very possibility of trustworthy communication. If a speaker can always reserve a mental qualification, no statement can be relied upon — and the sacrament of confession itself becomes meaningless.

Second, he argues that the Jesuit doctrine of equivocations is not an exceptional measure for extreme cases but a routine technique for circumventing moral obligations. In Letter VIII, Pascal describes a Jesuit who practices "equivocation" to avoid acknowledging an error in his published writings (008-031).

Third, Pascal presents equivocation as part of a broader pattern of Jesuit laxity — a system that appears to maintain doctrinal orthodoxy while in practice permitting everything it nominally prohibits.
