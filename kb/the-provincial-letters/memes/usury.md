## Definition

Usury, in the theological context of the *Provincial Letters*, refers to the practice of charging interest on loans in a manner condemned by traditional Christian morality. Pascal addresses usury as a test case for Jesuit casuistry: where the Church has historically prohibited usury as sinful, the Jesuit casuists develop interpretive techniques — particularly the "Mohatra contract" and redirection of intention — that effectively permit what the letter of the law forbids.

## Role in the Controversy

Usury serves in the *Letters* as a concrete illustration of Pascal's broader critique of Jesuit moral theology. In Letter VIII, he presents Father Escobar's treatment of usury as emblematic of how casuistry undermines moral absolutes. The discussion demonstrates that Jesuit methods do not merely address borderline cases but systematically dismantle established moral prohibitions. Pascal's rhetorical strategy is to show that the same logic that permits usury also operates in more consequential matters such as homicide and calumny (014-016).

## Positions

**Traditional Church Teaching**: Usury is a sin against justice and charity. The prohibition is rooted in Scripture and canon law, and the Church has consistently taught that charging interest on loans to the poor is gravely wrong. The traditional view holds that money is sterile and that profit should come from labor, not from money itself.

**Jesuit Casuist View (as presented by Pascal)**: The Jesuit casuists do not openly deny the prohibition on usury but develop techniques to circumvent it. They teach that one may avoid usury "by a simple shift of the intention" (009-021), redirecting the lender's purpose so that the transaction is no longer formally usurious. The "Mohatra contract" — a complex arrangement in which goods are bought and immediately resold at a different price to disguise a loan — is presented as a legitimate way to evade the usury prohibition. Pascal quotes Escobar acknowledging that "some are allured into usury merely from the love of gain" (009-015), yet the casuist response is to accommodate rather than condemn.

**Pascal's Critique**: Pascal argues that these casuistical devices make a mockery of moral theology. If a "simple shift of intention" can transform a usurious loan into a legitimate transaction, then all moral prohibitions become empty forms. The Jesuit claim to keep people "from usury" (009-013) is ironic, since their own doctrines enable it. The "demon of usury" (009-020) — a phrase Pascal uses sardonically — is not exorcised but accommodated.

## Key Passages

> "Their great object in dealing with gentlemen is to keep them from usury — an object to accomplish which they have discovered an infallible method." (009-013)

> "It may be feared, he says, that some are allured into usury merely from the love of gain." (009-015)

> "Father Escobar shows you how you may avoid usury by a simple shift of the intention." (009-021)

Pascal's tone in these passages is deeply ironic. The "infallible method" for avoiding usury turns out to be a set of legal fictions that permit it. The concern that people might be "allured into usury" is framed as a problem the casuists solve, when in fact their solution is to redefine usury so that almost nothing qualifies.
