## Definition

Heresy (*haeresis*) is the obstinate post-baptismal denial or doubt of a truth that must be believed with divine and Catholic faith (*de fide divina et catholica*).

It is distinguished from simple error by the element of obstinacy: the heretic is one who, having been admonished by legitimate ecclesiastical authority, persists in denying a defined doctrine. It is distinguished from schism by its object: heresy concerns doctrine, schism concerns communion.

In the *Provincial Letters*, heresy is the central charge that the Jesuit party levels against the Jansenists — and the charge that Pascal spends the Letters contesting.

## Role in the Controversy

The Sorbonne's censure of Antoine Arnauld in 1656 was the immediate occasion for the *Provincial Letters*. The question was whether Arnauld's propositions — specifically, that he could not find the Five Propositions condemned by the pope in Jansenius's *Augustinus* — constituted heresy.

Pascal's first letter is an extended examination of what, precisely, the Sorbonne judged to be heretical in Arnauld's statement. His spokesman discovers that the censors cannot coherently identify the heresy:

> "Tell me where, then, lay the heresy of M. Arnauld's proposition?" (001-017)

The Sorbonne doctors, Pascal reports, condemned Arnauld not for what he actually said but for what they assumed he must have meant — because they had already determined that Jansenius was a heretic, and Arnauld was a Jansenist, so Arnauld must be a heretic too.

This circular reasoning — the "heresy" is in the reader's inference, not the text — is the satirical engine of the first several letters.

## Positions

**The Jesuit/Sorbonne position**. The Jesuits and their allies at the Sorbonne maintained that Jansenius's *Augustinus* contained the Five Propositions condemned by Innocent X — and that anyone who denied this, as Arnauld did, was ipso facto a heretic. (004-006)

Pascal reports their position: that Arnauld's statement was "heresy — formal heresy" because it implied "indifference to the faith." (002-012, 002-021)

The juridical logic is that the pope has the authority to determine both the fact (*factum*) of whether the propositions are in the book and the right (*jus*) of whether they are heretical. To deny the fact is as heretical as to deny the right — because both are exercises of papal authority. (004-012)

**Pascal's Jansenist defense**. Pascal's defense operates on two levels. 

First, he argues that Arnauld's statement is factually true: the Five Propositions, *in the sense in which they were condemned*, are not in Jansenius. The propositions condemned by Innocent X express a Calvinist doctrine of grace that Jansenius himself rejected. (001-016)

Second, he argues that the distinction between fact and right is a real one, long recognized by the Church. The pope's authority extends to the doctrinal question (are these propositions heretical?) but not to the factual question (are they in this book?). (004-012)

**The Calvinist backdrop**. Pascal repeatedly distinguishes the Jansenist position from the Calvinist heresy, which denied free will entirely.

He cites a Sorbonne doctor who says: "Since the introduction of the heresy of Luther and Calvin, all the heresies have turned upon grace." (002-021) The implication is that the Jansenists are being falsely assimilated to the Protestant Reformation — that their Augustinian theology of grace is being misrepresented as Calvinism.

## Key Passages

> "That is not a heresy — it is an orthodox opinion." (001-016)

> "Tell me where, then, lay the heresy of M. Arnauld's proposition?" (001-017)

> "Indifference! — it is heresy — formal heresy. The necessity of efficacious grace is a point of faith." (002-012)

> "Since the introduction of the heresy of Luther and Calvin, all the heresies have turned upon grace." (002-021)

> "Condemning the most shocking heresy in the world, but your wonder is that the heretics are not converted by it." (004-006)

> "Where is the difference between condemning as heresy a proposition that contains no heresy, and condemning as heretical a proposition that does contain heresy?" (004-012)
