## Definition

**Charity** (Latin: *caritas*; Greek: *agape*) is the theological virtue by which God is loved above all things and one's neighbor is loved for God's sake. In Catholic moral theology, charity is the "form of all virtues" and the essential animating principle of the Christian life. The loss of charity through mortal sin constitutes the primary state from which the sacrament of penance restores the soul.

## Role in the Controversy

The concept of charity plays a critical role in Pascal's critique of Jesuit casuistry on penitence. The Jesuit theologians had developed a distinction between *attrition* (imperfect sorrow for sin, motivated by fear of punishment) and *contrition* (perfect sorrow motivated by love of God—charity). Pascal's Jansenist position held that true penitence requires charity, while the Jesuit casuists permitted absolution based on attrition alone.

Pascal reports the Jesuit position that "sins are remitted through the sacrament of penance even without charity" and treats this as a dangerous relaxation of moral requirements. He presents charity as "the life and soul of virtue" ([006-016]), without which penitence is merely external compliance.

Beyond the penitential debate, Pascal invokes charity to critique the Jesuit use of "charitable" language to mask moral laxity. He ironically describes as "novel charity" the Jesuit practice of accusing Pascal himself of lacking charity for exposing their moral maxims ([012-023]).

## Positions

### Jesuit Casuist Position

The Jesuit authors cited by Pascal taught that:

- The sacrament of penance remits sins even without the penitent possessing charity ([005-025])
- Attrition (fear-based sorrow) suffices for valid absolution without contrition (love-based sorrow)
- The "rule of charity does not bind us to deprive ourselves" of property for the benefit of others ([009-039])

### Pascal's Jansenist Critique

Pascal argues that:

- True penitence requires charity—the love of God—not merely fear of punishment
- The Jesuit position "strips" penitence of "the charity which is the life and soul of virtue" ([006-016])
- Jesuit casuists misuse the language of charity to shield morally problematic teachings from scrutiny
- The Jesuit accusation that Pascal lacks charity for exposing error is itself a perversion of the concept

## Key Passages

> "[006-016] …so completely stripped of the charity which is the life and soul of virtue…"

> "[005-025] …committed sin through the want of charity and penitence. Their life is spent in sin and in the desire of sin…"

> "[012-023] …Novel charity this, which groans at seeing those who expose the vices of others, while it never groans at the vices themselves!"

> "[009-039] …the rule of charity does not bind us to deprive ourselves of our own property for the benefit of our neighbor…"
