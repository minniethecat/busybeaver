## Definition

A condemnation (*damnatio*) is a formal act of ecclesiastical authority — typically a pope or an ecumenical council — by which a specific proposition, doctrine, book, or person is declared to contain or profess theological error.

In the technical vocabulary of post-Tridentine Catholicism, a condemnation is distinguished from a censure by its source: a condemnation issues from the papal or conciliar level, while a censure may issue from a lower authority such as a theological faculty. A condemnation may specify the grade of error (heresy, error, temerity) or may issue a generic reprobation.

In the *Provincial Letters*, the papal condemnation of the Five Propositions is the background against which every theological argument unfolds, and Pascal devotes his final letters (XVII–XIX) to contesting its scope and authority.

## Role in the Controversy

The papal condemnation of the Five Propositions by Innocent X in *Cum occasione* (1653) was the event that transformed the Jansenist controversy from a theological dispute into an ecclesiastical crisis.

Pascal's treatment of the condemnation is strategically layered. He never denies the pope's authority to condemn heretical propositions. Instead, he argues that the condemnation of the Five Propositions *as heretical* does not entail that Jansenius actually taught them:

> "The pope has condemned the doctrine of the five propositions, which was represented to him as the doctrine of Jansenius." (018-039)

The key distinction is between the pope's authority over doctrine (*jus*) and his authority over facts (*factum*). Pascal argues that the pope is infallible on the former but not the latter. A pope may be misinformed about what a particular author wrote, and his condemnation does not make the factual claim true.

He develops this argument through historical precedent: "Many councils and two popes confirmed the condemnation of the Letters of Honorius" — and yet "the Church has always held that a pope may err in a question of fact" (018-036).

Pascal also accuses the Jesuits of exploiting the condemnation for political ends. They use the papal condemnation of the Five Propositions as a weapon against the Jansenists while simultaneously promoting moral systems that Pascal considers far more dangerous:

> "You have nothing more to do than to conjoin for your condemnation the maxims which you have discovered." (014-027)

## Positions

**The Jesuit/ultramontane position**. The Jesuits and their Roman allies maintained that the pope's condemnation of the Five Propositions was an exercise of the supreme magisterium to which all Catholics owe unconditional submission — including submission on the factual question of whether Jansenius taught the condemned doctrine.

Their argument rested on the claim that the pope could not effectively govern the Church if his subjects could second-guess his factual determinations. The papacy's authority to condemn heresy implies the authority to determine what is and is not heretical in particular texts.

**Pascal's Jansenist position**. Pascal does not reject the condemnation. He accepts that the Five Propositions, "had they borne that sense" of Calvinism, "would not have merited condemnation" — meaning he affirms the condemnation of the Calvinist sense of the propositions (018-028).

His argument is jurisdictional rather than doctrinal. The pope can condemn doctrines; he cannot make facts true by declaring them. The question of whether Jansenius taught the Five Propositions is a question of fact, and "the pope may be mistaken" on questions of fact (018-039).

Pascal warns that requiring Catholics to affirm a factual claim as a matter of faith forces them into hypocrisy:

> "What will it serve you, in the sight of God, by signing that condemnation against their consciences?" (018-038)

**The Calvinist backdrop**. Pascal repeatedly distinguishes the Jansenist position from Calvin's doctrine, which the Jansenists also condemn. He argues that by "joining with them in the condemnation of Calvin's doctrine," the Jansenists have demonstrated their orthodoxy — and that the Jesuits, by continuing to pursue them, reveal that their real target is not heresy but Augustinian theology itself (019-009).
