## Definition

Presumption (*praesumptio*) is, in Catholic moral theology, a sin against the virtue of hope, consisting in an inordinate confidence in one's own capacity to attain salvation without divine assistance, or in a rash expectation of God's mercy without conversion of life. It is the mirror opposite of despair and shares with it the character of a sin directly against God.

In the context of the *Provincial Letters*, presumption takes on a more specific forensic meaning: the rashness of asserting a theological proposition without sufficient warrant from Scripture and tradition. Pascal's interlocutors charged Arnauld with "presumption" for having denied that the five propositions of Jansenius are contained in the Augustinus; Pascal's defense turns on demonstrating that Arnauld's caution was precisely the opposite of presumptuous. (001-005)

## Role in the Controversy

The charge of presumption lay at the heart of the Sorbonne censure of Antoine Arnauld (1656). The faculty declared that Arnauld had acted with "presumption" in asserting that the five propositions — though condemned as heretical by the papal bull *Cum occasione* — were not, in fact, found in Jansenius's *Augustinus*. The procedural question (whether Arnauld's statement was presumptuous) eclipsed the substantive question (whether the propositions actually appear in Jansenius).

Pascal's response in the early *Letters* is twofold. First, he argues that the Sorbonne's reasoning is circular: the faculty presumes Arnauld guilty of presumption before examining the evidence. (001-006) Second, he distinguishes de facto presumption (asserting something without sufficient inquiry) from doctrinal error (asserting a heresy). Arnauld may, at worst, have been mistaken about a question of fact — but a mistake of fact is not a sin of presumption, least of all a heresy. (018-008)

The dispute over presumption thus crystallizes the larger procedural critique that runs through the *Letters*: that the Sorbonne and the Jesuits have substituted juridical maneuvering for theological reasoning, condemning persons rather than propositions.

## Positions

**The Sorbonne position**. The censure of Arnauld treated his denial as presumptuous because it appeared to contradict the papal bull *Cum occasione* — and to contradict a papal judgment, even on a question of fact, is to exceed one's competence as a private theologian. Presumption, in this view, consists in the arrogation of magisterial authority by a non-magisterial person. (018-023)

**Pascal's defense of Arnauld**. Pascal insists that questions of fact (whether certain words appear in a book) are discernible by ordinary reading and do not require episcopal or papal discernment. To call a simple factual observation "presumptuous" is to weaponize a theological category against a procedural right — the right to examine evidence. (018-030) Pascal goes further: if anyone is presumptuous, it is the accusers who pronounce a man heretical without producing the text that supposedly damns him.

**The Jesuit view**. The Jesuits, as Pascal represents them, treat the charge of presumption as a flexible instrument of ecclesiastical discipline. Since probabilism allows the confessor to follow any probable opinion, and since the opinion that Arnauld is presumptuous is "probable" once the Sorbonne has said so, the Jesuit confessor may safely treat Arnauld as a sinner — without ever reading Jansenius for himself. (013-020)

## Key Passages

> "They are determined to pronounce him guilty of presumption, for having asserted in his second letter that he had read Jansenius thoroughly, and that he did not find in him the condemned propositions." (001-005)

> "Is it possible, father, that M. Arnauld could, without presumption, entertain a doubt that these five propositions are in Jansenius?" (001-006)

> "I must tell you, father, that it is an act of high presumption to advance, without being prepared to prove, so grave a charge." (018-008)

> "Not but that there was some presumption that you were speaking the truth — for who could imagine you would lie in a printed book?" (018-023)

> "It is true, father, though it may be an act of presumption, to disbelieve certain particular facts — but it can never be heresy to refuse belief to facts not revealed by God." (018-030)
