## Definition

A censure (*censura*) is a formal judgment by an ecclesiastical authority — a pope, a council, a bishop, or a theological faculty — that a particular proposition, writing, or teaching contains doctrinal error.

Theological censures are graded by severity. The most serious is the censure of heresy, which declares a proposition contrary to divine and Catholic faith. Lesser censures include "erroneous," "temerarious," "offensive to pious ears," "scandalous," and "suspected of heresy."

In the *Provincial Letters*, the term refers primarily to the Sorbonne's censure of Antoine Arnauld in January–February 1656, which Pascal treats as the immediate occasion for the Letters and as a case study in ecclesiastical abuse of power.

## Role in the Controversy

The Sorbonne's censure of Arnauld is the event that opens the *Provincial Letters*. Pascal's first letter is an account of the proceedings, narrated by a witness who reports the confusion and procedural irregularities surrounding the censure.

Pascal describes the censure as a foregone conclusion. The outcome was determined not by the content of Arnauld's propositions but by the political alignment of the voters. He distinguishes two factions among the Sorbonne doctors: the "Molinists" (Jesuit allies who voted for the censure) and the "Neutrals" — "those who were not in favor of the censure, and who are called Neutrals" (001-010).

Pascal's ultimate judgment on the censure is severe. He has his spokesman predict that "though the censure should pass, peace will not be restored" (001-062). And when the censure is finally published, Pascal treats it not as a vindication of orthodoxy but as evidence of institutional decay:

> "It is a much easier, and much less troublesome, task to censure than to reply — it being a much shorter process to say 'that is heretical' than to answer arguments." (004-019)

He quotes a friend who says: "If this be the case, their censure is not worth a straw; for who would pay any regard to it?" (004-020)

## Positions

**The Sorbonne/Molinist position**. The Molinist doctors of the Sorbonne argued that Arnauld's propositions — specifically, that he could not find the Five Propositions in Jansenius, and that the grace without which Peter fell was lacking to Peter — were heretical or at least temerarious.

Their argument rested on the claim that the pope had already condemned the Five Propositions as Jansenist, that Arnauld's refusal to acknowledge the propositions were in Jansenius was therefore an implicit denial of papal authority, and that such denial constituted heresy.

**Pascal's critique**. Pascal's critique is procedural, theological, and ecclesiological.

Procedurally, he argues that the censure was achieved through manipulation: the Molinists packed the assembly, excluded Dominican allies of Arnauld, and changed the voting rules mid-process. (001-010, 001-012)

Theologically, he argues that Arnauld's propositions were orthodox. The statement that he could not find the Five Propositions in Jansenius is a statement of fact, not doctrine; the statement about Peter's fall is supported by Augustine and the Church Fathers. (001-016)

Ecclesiologically, Pascal argues that the Sorbonne's censure is self-defeating. A censure achieved by procedural manipulation and applied to orthodox propositions undermines the authority it purports to defend: "Their censure, censurable as it is, will produce the contrary effect to what they intended." (004-021)

**The Dominican position**. The Dominican theologians (Thomists), while doctrinally closer to the Jansenists on grace, were initially inclined to vote for the censure to avoid being associated with Jansenism. Pascal treats their position with sympathy but also as a cautionary tale about the pressure to conform. (001-010)
