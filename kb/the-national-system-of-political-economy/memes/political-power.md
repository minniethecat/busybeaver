## Definition

Political power, in List's political economy, is the collective capacity of a nation to pursue its interests in the international arena—through diplomacy, military strength, and economic policy. List rejected the classical economists' separation of political power from economic analysis, insisting that the two are inextricably linked: a nation's political power depends on its productive powers, and its productive powers depend on the political framework within which they develop. (004-010)

## In List's System

Political power occupies a foundational position in List's system because it bridges the economic and the political. The classical economists, List charged, treated political power as external to their subject matter—a matter for statesmen, not for economists. But this separation was artificial and misleading. A nation that neglects its political power will soon find that it has lost the capacity to make sovereign economic decisions, while a nation that builds its political power through manufacturing development acquires the means to shape the terms of international trade in its own favor. (012-005)

List identified a dynamic relationship between political power and productive powers. Manufacturing development strengthens political power by increasing population, wealth, and technological capacity. Political power, in turn, protects and extends the sphere within which productive powers can develop—through the defense of national territory, the negotiation of favorable commercial treaties, and the maintenance of internal order. (013-011)

## List's Argument

List's central argument is that political power is not a luxury that nations can afford to neglect while pursuing economic growth—it is an essential instrument of economic development itself. He traced the historical interaction between political power and economic forces to show that nations which allowed their political power to decay invariably saw their economic prosperity follow.

> She has shown us the intimate connection between the forces of production and political power. (004-012)

The relationship between manufacturing and political power was, for List, particularly important. A purely agricultural nation, he argued, "will never acquire important political power, or be placed in a position to exert any considerable influence on the civilization and the affairs of the world." (015-005) Manufacturing nations, by contrast, command the material resources, technological capacity, and concentrated population that translate into military and diplomatic strength. England's political power, List observed, was the direct consequence of its manufacturing supremacy. (015-010)

List also argued that political power has a qualitative dimension beyond mere material resources. A manufacturing nation develops the institutional capacity—administration, law, education, finance—that enables sustained collective action. These institutions are themselves productive powers, and they are cultivated most fully in nations that have passed through the manufacturing stage of development. (015-004)

## Historical Examples

List provided numerous illustrations of the political-power/productive-powers nexus:

- **England's Rise**: England's political power, List argued, was built on the foundation of its manufacturing development. The Navigation Acts, protective tariffs, and strategic promotion of industry were simultaneously economic and political measures—they strengthened both England's productive capacity and its ability to project power abroad. (015-010)

- **The Hanseatic League's Weakness**: The Hanseatic cities amassed great commercial wealth but never developed corresponding political power because they lacked the territorial base and national unity of a modern state. Their commercial prosperity, unprotected by adequate political power, proved vulnerable to the rising nation-states. (002-009)

- **Spain and Portugal's Decline**: Spain and Portugal possessed immense political power in the sixteenth century—empires, navies, colonial possessions—but failed to develop domestic manufacturing. As a result, their political power proved hollow: it rested on the extraction of colonial resources rather than on domestic productive capacity, and it eroded as other nations developed superior manufacturing. (004-012)

- **The Netherlands**: The Dutch Republic achieved a remarkable combination of commercial prosperity and political power in the seventeenth century, but its political power was ultimately limited by its small territorial base and population. List saw the Dutch case as demonstrating both the potential of commercial-manufacturing development and the limits of achieving it without the scale of a great nationality. (003-006)

- **Germany's Fragmentation**: The German lands, List argued, suffered from a deficit of political power precisely because of their political fragmentation. Divided into numerous small states, Germany could not pursue a coherent national economic policy or project power internationally. The Zollverein represented a first step toward remedying this deficit in the economic sphere. (015-002)
