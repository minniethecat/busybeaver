## Definition

> Her merchant shipping and her foreign commerce rest on the solid foundation of her manufacturing industry; and her manufacturing industry has been built up on the basis of her protected home market. (002-011)

Merchant shipping refers to the commercial transport of goods by sea using nationally owned and operated vessels.

In List's economic framework, merchant shipping is not merely a service industry but a **strategic national asset** — it connects domestic manufacturing to foreign markets, supports naval power through the training of seamen, and serves as a carrier of national commerce that must be protected and promoted through state policy.

List distinguishes merchant shipping from simple maritime commerce: a nation may conduct foreign trade using foreign vessels (passive commerce), or it may carry its own goods in its own ships (active commerce). The latter requires a developed shipping industry and yields far greater national benefit.

## In List's System

Merchant shipping occupies a critical junction in List's system, connecting **manufacturing**, **foreign trade**, and **national defense**. It functions as both an enabler of manufacturing exports and a direct expression of national productive power.

List integrates shipping into his theory of productive powers through the concept of **navigation as protected industry**. Drawing on the historical example of England's Navigation Acts, he argues that shipping must be actively promoted through policy: "Her merchant shipping and her foreign commerce rest on the solid foundation of her manufacturing industry" (002-011).

The causal arrow runs in both directions — manufacturing creates demand for shipping, while a strong merchant marine secures the trade routes that manufacturing depends on.

Shipping also connects to List's argument about **national independence**.

A nation that relies on foreign vessels to carry its exports and imports is vulnerable to disruption and forfeits the ancillary benefits of shipping: seamen training for naval defense, shipbuilding industry development, and the commercial intelligence that comes from direct engagement with foreign markets.

## List's Argument

List develops a multi-dimensional argument for merchant shipping as a national economic priority:

**Navigation Laws as Policy Model.** List treats England's Navigation Acts as the archetypal maritime protection policy. The English navigation laws restricted foreign shipping, reserving English trade for English vessels: "The English navigation laws were copied from those of the Hanseatic League" (002-002).

These laws, List argues, were instrumental in building England's merchant marine and, by extension, its naval power and manufacturing dominance.

**Shipping and Manufacturing Interdependence.** List insists that merchant shipping cannot thrive without domestic manufacturing to generate cargo, nor can manufacturing reach foreign markets without domestic shipping. The two reinforce each other: "Her merchant shipping and her foreign commerce rest on the solid foundation of her manufacturing industry" (002-011).

This interdependence is central to List's critique of free trade — nations that open their shipping to foreign competition before developing their own manufacturing forfeit both.

**Fisheries as Nursery for Seamen.** List notes the role of sea fisheries in training seamen who later serve in merchant shipping and naval defense. Fisheries were valued "as nurseries for seamen" (001-001), and England protected its fisheries alongside its shipping precisely because the two formed an integrated maritime economy.

**The Hanseatic Precedent.** The Hanseatic League provides List's primary historical model of shipping-based commercial power. The League united "under one flag all the shipping from Dunkirk as far as Riga" (003-004), demonstrating how coordinated shipping policy could create commercial dominance.

But the League's decline also illustrates the vulnerability of shipping when detached from territorial manufacturing and state support.

**German Shipping as National Project.** List argues that Germany must develop its own merchant shipping as part of its industrial catch-up.

He calls for "special advantages to their shipping" (034-014) and for Germany to "protect and promote their foreign trade through their own vessels" (034-014). The Zollverein alone is insufficient — Germany must also have a merchant marine to carry its goods to world markets.

## Historical Examples

List draws on an extensive range of historical cases to illustrate the role of merchant shipping in national development:

- **The Italian Republics (Medieval Period).** Venice, Amalfi, and Genoa built their commercial empires on shipping. Venice "by her industry and her shipping had become the centre of the commerce of the South" (003-002). Italian shipping dominated Mediterranean trade, and the republics' naval power grew directly from their merchant marine.

List notes that "navigation along Italy's extensive sea-coast" (001-001) gave these republics a natural advantage they exploited through policy.

- **The Hanseatic League (13th–16th Centuries).** The Hanseatic towns of Northern Germany created a shipping-based commercial network spanning the North Sea and Baltic. List records that "the Hanseatic shipping trade had already begun to decline" (003-003) by the time Venice reached its zenith, illustrating the shifting geography of maritime power.

The League "united under one flag all the shipping from Dunkirk as far as Riga" (003-004), demonstrating the power of coordinated maritime policy.

- **England (17th–18th Centuries).** England's Navigation Acts systematically built English merchant shipping at the expense of Dutch and Hanseatic competitors. List notes that England "reduced restrictions on foreign shipping" only after its own merchant marine had achieved dominance (002-002).

The acts ensured that English manufacturing exports traveled in English vessels, capturing the full value chain from production to delivery.

- **Holland (17th Century).** The Dutch Republic's "success in maritime trade in competition with the Hanseatic League" (003-004) demonstrated the effectiveness of combining shipping, finance, and manufacturing.

However, Holland's subsequent decline illustrated the risk of relying on carrying trade without sufficient domestic manufacturing — the shipping remained but the economic foundation eroded.

- **Germany (19th Century).** List's contemporary Germany lacked a significant merchant marine. He advocated for "differential duties in favour of German shipping" (036-004) and argued that a German flag should "float over German shipping" (P04-010) as a symbol of national economic maturity.
  German shipping would both carry German exports and serve as a training ground for naval defense.
