# Richard Cobden

## Life and Career

Richard Cobden (1804–1865).

Richard Cobden (1804–1865) was a British manufacturer and Radical politician who led the Anti-Corn Law League (1838–1846). He became the most prominent British advocate of unilateral free trade in the mid-19th century.

A self-made calico printer from Manchester, Cobden entered Parliament in 1841. He dedicated his political career to the abolition of protective tariffs — first the Corn Laws (repealed 1846), then the Navigation Acts (repealed 1849).

Cobden was List's contemporary and, in many ways, his direct antagonist. Where List argued that free trade should come only after industrial maturity, Cobden insisted that Britain should adopt free trade immediately and unilaterally.

List "resembled Cobden, who also excelled in exaggerating the benefits of his own system" (P03-004). This comparison highlights their shared conviction but opposite conclusions.

## Role in "The National System"

Cobden appears in List's analysis as the embodiment of the cosmopolitan free trade doctrine that List spent his career opposing. List met Cobden personally — "Cobden, who jokingly asked him, 'Have you written a book too?'" (P04-009). This encounter exemplified the gulf between the British free-trader and the German economic nationalist.

List's critique of Cobden's position: Britain, having achieved manufacturing supremacy through centuries of protection, was now converting to free trade at the moment it had the least to lose and the most to gain. Cobden's unilateral free trade was a policy that only an already-dominant manufacturing nation could afford — one that would prevent other nations from ever achieving similar dominance.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **The Anti-Corn Law League**: Cobden's successful campaign to repeal the Corn Laws demonstrated the political power of organized manufacturing interests allied with urban consumers against agricultural landlords.

- **Unilateral Free Trade**: Cobden argued that Britain should abolish tariffs regardless of reciprocity — a doctrine List considered disastrous for nations still developing their manufacturing.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment was respectful but sharply critical. He recognized Cobden's sincerity and organizing genius, but saw Cobden's free trade absolutism as the product of British national interest masquerading as universal principle. Cobden, in List's view, was a sophisticated instrument of British insular supremacy — persuading other nations to disarm economically.
