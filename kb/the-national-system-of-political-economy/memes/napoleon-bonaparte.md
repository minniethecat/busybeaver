# Napoleon Bonaparte

## Life and Career

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) rose from Corsican minor nobility to become Emperor of the French and master of continental Europe. His military campaigns reshaped the European political map, but from List's perspective, his most significant action was the **Continental System** (1806–1814) — the economic blockade of Britain that Napoleon imposed on all territories under French control.

The Continental System prohibited the importation of British goods into any port under French influence, aiming to strangle Britain's manufacturing economy by denying it access to European markets. Napoleon "said in his trenchant style, 'I will conquer England by means of her commerce'" (006-005) — a strategy that List considered fundamentally sound in concept but fatally flawed in execution.

## Role in "The National System"

Napoleon's Continental System occupies a prominent place in List's analysis as a real-world experiment in economic nationalism. List argued that the blockade demonstrated both the power and the danger of economic warfare:

- **The power**: The Continental System showed that Britain's manufacturing supremacy was not invincible. "The Continental blockade extended Napoleon's system across all of Europe" (006-005), and during its operation, continental manufacturing expanded rapidly — particularly in France and the German states.

- **The failure**: Napoleon's system ultimately collapsed because it was imposed by military force on unwilling populations, not adopted voluntarily by nations pursuing their own industrial development. It generated resentment, smuggling, and eventually military resistance.

- **The lesson**: List drew the conclusion that economic nationalism must be *voluntary and national*, not imperial and coercive. The Zollverein (a voluntary customs union of German states) was List's preferred alternative to Napoleon's imperial blockade.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **Economic Warfare**: Napoleon was the first modern ruler to systematically use economic blockade as a weapon of war, anticipating the 20th-century intersection of economics and military strategy.

- **Continental Integration**: The Continental System inadvertently stimulated industrial development in regions that had previously been raw material suppliers to Britain, particularly the Rhineland and Belgium.

- **The Limits of Empire**: Napoleon's system showed that economic policy imposed by a foreign power generates resistance, while the same policies adopted voluntarily (as in the Zollverein) produce economic development.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment was nuanced. He wrote positively of "the powerfully favourable effects of Napoleon's Continental system" (P05-001) on continental manufacturing, but acknowledged that its coercive character doomed it. Napoleon understood the strategic importance of manufacturing but lacked the theoretical framework to pursue it through sustainable national policies rather than imperial decree.

The Continental System, in List's analysis, was a half-success: it proved that continental Europe *could* industrialize without British imports, but it failed to establish the institutional foundation (national customs unions, infrastructure investment, educational reform) that would make industrialization permanent.
