# Theory of Values

## Definition

The **Theory of Values** (or Theory of Exchangeable Values), as Friedrich List characterizes it, is the classical economic doctrine that evaluates all economic phenomena solely through the lens of market exchange prices. In this framework, a nation's economic welfare is measured by the total exchangeable value of goods it possesses — without regard to the *causes* that produce those goods.

List argued that Adam Smith's work "teaches nothing else than the theory of values." (012-003) The theory treats wealth accumulation as the central aim of economic activity, but ignores the distinction between the *capacity* to produce wealth and wealth itself.

## In List's System

List positioned the theory of values as the fundamental error of classical political economy, against which his entire *National System* was constructed. In his Author's Preface, List stated that he sought to establish "the distinction between the theory of values and the theory of the powers of production." (P05-003)

The theory of values, List charged, is "a mere shopkeeper's or individual merchant's theory" — not a science capable of guiding national policy. (031-003) It evaluates transactions as if nations were individual merchants exchanging goods, oblivious to the long-term developmental consequences.

List identified three critical failures of the theory of values:

1. **It ignores productive capacity**: A nation that deindustrializes by importing cheap manufactures may increase short-term exchange value but destroys its ability to produce in the future.

2. **It assumes universal peace and unity**: The theory presumes a world without nation-states or conflict — what List called "cosmopolitical" economics. In reality, nations compete for industrial supremacy and military security. (012-010)

3. **It confuses private and national economy**: What is prudent for an individual merchant (buying the cheapest goods) may be ruinous for a nation (allowing its manufacturing base to collapse). (014-001)

## List's Argument

List developed his critique through both theoretical argument and historical evidence:

- **The individual vs. the nation**: A private merchant profits by purchasing goods at the lowest price regardless of origin. A nation, however, must concern itself with *how* goods are produced — whether its citizens are developing skills, whether its institutions are strengthening, and whether its productive powers are growing. "That may be wisdom in national economy which would be folly in private economy." (014-003)

- **The productive powers distinction**: List used examples from private economy to illustrate his point. A father who spends his capital on immediate consumption leaves his children destitute; a father who invests in education and skills (even at the cost of present consumption) increases his family's productive powers. The theory of values cannot capture this distinction. (012-003)

- **The historical evidence**: England, France, and the United States all achieved industrial development by *violating* the prescriptions of the theory of values — imposing protective tariffs precisely when free trade would have been most "efficient" by the narrow exchange-value calculus.

## Contrast with Productive Powers

| Aspect | Theory of Values | Theory of Productive Powers |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Measure of welfare | Exchange value of goods | Capacity to produce goods |
| Unit of analysis | Individual (merchant) | Nation |
| Time horizon | Present market prices | Multi-generational development |
| Policy implication | Free trade always optimal | Protection during industrial infancy |
| What it misses | Mental capital, institutions, skills | — |

J.B. Say himself, List noted, "endeavoured to obviate the fundamental error of the theory of values" by adding the concept of productive services, though ultimately insufficiently. (032-002)
