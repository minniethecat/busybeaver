# United States of America

The **United States of America** occupies a distinctive position in [[Friedrich List]]'s *National System of Political Economy*.

It was, in List's analysis, the nation that most clearly demonstrated the validity of his theories in the 19th century. America adopted protective tariffs to build its [[Manufacturing Power]] — and the results were visible within a single generation. Unlike England, whose industrial policy unfolded over four centuries, America compressed the process into decades.

> The United States of America, which has reached the point of manufacturing power that England attained only after centuries, achieved this result within two generations through the systematic application of protective tariffs. (P03-005)

## List's American Case

List resided in the United States from 1825 to 1832, and his economic theories were shaped directly by American experience. He observed several features of American development that became central to his system:

**The infant industry argument in practice**. Alexander Hamilton's *Report on Manufactures* (1791) made the theoretical case for temporary protection. American tariff policy — the Tariff of 1816, the Tariff of 1824, the "Tariff of Abominations" (1828) — implemented it. List saw Hamilton as the American counterpart to [[Jean-Baptiste Colbert|Colbert]]: a statesman who understood that manufacturing power requires deliberate national policy.

**The continental market**. America's protected continental market — from the Atlantic to the Mississippi, free of internal customs barriers — was the template for List's vision of a German [[Zollverein]]. A large protected market generated economies of scale that small protected markets could not.

**Manufacturing and agriculture in harmony**. American manufacturing in the Northeast stimulated American agriculture in the Midwest. Farmers in Ohio and Indiana sold their grain to factory workers in Massachusetts and Pennsylvania, creating a national division of labor that benefited both sectors. This was List's "reciprocal exchange" in action.

> The American example proves that manufacturing power and agricultural power develop together, not in competition. (P03-014)

## Hamilton and List

[[Alexander Hamilton]] was, for List, the most important economic thinker of the American tradition. Hamilton's *Report on Manufactures* anticipated List's core arguments:

| Argument | Hamilton (1791) | List (1841) |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Infant industry protection** | Temporary tariffs for new industries | Temporary protection until manufacturing parity |
| **National market** | Internal improvements + uniform tariffs | Zollverein + railways |
| **Manufacturing-agriculture link** | Manufacturing creates demand for farm products | Manufacturing power stimulates agricultural power |
| **National defence** | Domestic manufacturing essential for security | Manufacturing self-sufficiency as national independence |

List saw himself as the German Hamilton. What Hamilton had done for America — provide the intellectual framework for national manufacturing policy — List aimed to do for Germany.

## The Tariff Debate

List followed American tariff debates closely. The conflict between the manufacturing North (protectionist) and the agricultural South (free-trade) illustrated his thesis with textbook clarity.

The South exported cotton to England and imported English manufactures. Southern planters saw free trade as in their interest. Northern manufacturers needed protection from English competition. The tariff was not a question of abstract economic principle — it was a question of *which region's development path would national policy favor*.

List drew the lesson for Germany: the agricultural interests of the Prussian Junkers, who exported grain to England, would always oppose protective tariffs for German manufacturing. National economic policy required overcoming the resistance of those whose immediate interests aligned with free trade.
