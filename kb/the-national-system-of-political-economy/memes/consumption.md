# Consumption

## Definition

**Consumption**, in Friedrich List's *National System of Political Economy*, is not treated as a simple final stage of economic activity but as a dynamic force whose character — productive or unproductive — determines whether a nation strengthens or weakens its long-term capacity to create wealth. List rejected the classical school's blanket condemnation of all non-reproductive consumption, arguing that certain forms of consumption (defense, education, infrastructure) build productive powers even though they consume present material values. (012-003)

List's key insight is that consumption must be evaluated by its *effect on productive powers*, not merely by whether it directly reproduces the material values consumed. "All expenditure in the instruction of youth, the promotion of justice, defence of nations, &c. is a consumption of present values for the behoof of the productive powers." (012-003)

## In List's System

Consumption occupies a central position in List's theoretical architecture. Chapter XXV of *The National System* is explicitly devoted to "The Manufacturing Power and the Incentives to Production AND Consumption," treating the two as complementary forces within a developed manufacturing economy.

List analyzed consumption at three interconnected levels:

1. **Individual consumption** — the private economy level, where the agriculturist or manufacturer consumes goods for subsistence and enjoyment. At this level, List observed that "the producer becomes accustomed to a certain scale of consumption; and certain enjoyments, which under other circumstances he would have regarded as luxuries, become necessaries to him."

2. **National consumption** — the aggregate consumption of a nation, which List saw as the foundation for domestic manufacturing. "The maintenance of the foundation of" national industry depends on domestic consumption providing a reliable market. (P04-007)

3. **Consumption as productive stimulus** — "Production renders consumption possible, and the desire to consume incites to production." This reciprocal relationship is most fully realized within a manufacturing nation.

## Productive vs. Unproductive Consumption

List departed from the classical economists' rigid distinction between productive and unproductive consumption. He argued:

> The dominant school are in the right when they contend that all consumption which is not directly reproductive — for instance, that of war — is absolutely injurious without qualification. [This we deny.] The equipment of armies, wars, and the debts contracted for these purposes, may under certain circumstances very greatly conduce to the increase of the productive powers of a nation. (004-016)

**Productive consumption** includes:
- Education and instruction of youth (012-003)
- Administration of justice and national defense (012-003)
- Investment in machinery and manufacturing capital
- Consumption by manufacturing workers that sustains industrial skills

**Consumption injurious to productive powers** includes:
- Excessive taxes on articles of general consumption that "embitter" working-class existence (009-005)
- Luxury consumption by absentee rentiers in nations without manufacturing industry
- Consumption dependent on foreign manufactures that weakens domestic industry

## Consumption and the Manufacturing Power

List identified a virtuous cycle between manufacturing development and consumption:

| Without Manufacturing | With Manufacturing |
|-----------------------|-------------------|
| Consumption dependent on foreign supply | Home production supplies home consumption |
| Agricultural nation's consumption limited by export earnings | Manufacturing nation's consumption grows with productive capacity |
| Consumption shocks from war/trade disruption | Resilient domestic consumption base |

"Every nation which gains entire possession of its own home market for manufactures, gains in the course of time, by its home production and consumption of manufactured goods, infinitely more than the nation which has hitherto provided the former with manufactured goods loses by being excluded." (015-014)

## Historical Examples

- **The Hanseatic League** (Chapter II): The Hansards neglected to base their commerce on "the production and consumption, the agriculture or the manufactures, of the land to which their merchants belonged." (002-010) Their consumption was purely commercial, not productive — it collapsed when trade routes shifted.

- **England's domestic consumption base**: England's manufacturing power was built on a large home consumption market. English woolens were produced "for home consumption and for export to England" (of France, 006-001), demonstrating how domestic consumption provided the base for export competitiveness.

- **France under free trade (1786)**: When the Eden Treaty opened French markets to English goods, French consumption shifted toward English manufactures while domestic industry contracted — a case List cited as consumption patterns destroying productive power. (006-004)

## Consumption Taxes and National Policy

List was sharply critical of regressive consumption taxes. He argued that "taxes upon articles of consumption, by which the existence of the working classes is embittered beyond the point of endurance," undermined the nation's productive foundation. He proposed reducing consumption taxes "by one-half or two-thirds" and replacing the revenue with income taxes, arguing this would strengthen both working-class welfare and national productive power. (009-005)
