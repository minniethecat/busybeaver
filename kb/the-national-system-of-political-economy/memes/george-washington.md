# George Washington

## Life and Career

George Washington (1732–1799).

George Washington (1732–1799), commander of the Continental Army during the American Revolution and first President of the United States (1789–1797), appears in List's analysis as an early American practitioner of economic nationalism.

List cited Washington's support for domestic manufacturing as evidence that the founders understood the connection between industrial independence and political sovereignty.

"Washington, on the day of his inauguration, wore a suit of American-manufactured cloth" (009-002) — a symbolic act that List interpreted as a deliberate statement of economic policy. Washington's first annual message to Congress advocated the promotion of domestic manufactures.

"In his Message in 1791 Washington was able to congratulate Congress on the progress of American industry" (009-003).

## Role in "The National System"

Washington represents, in List's framework, the American counterpart to Edward III and Elizabeth I. He established the principle that national independence requires manufacturing capacity. List drew a direct parallel: "Washington like Napoleon" (012-011) understood that economic policy and national power are inseparable.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment was admiring. He saw Washington's (and Hamilton's) American System as the successful application of the principles List was advocating for Germany. The United States, by protecting its infant industries behind the tariff wall of 1816 and subsequent legislation, had demonstrated that a former colony could develop manufacturing and challenge British industrial supremacy.
