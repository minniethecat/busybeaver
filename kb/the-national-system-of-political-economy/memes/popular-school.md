## Definition

The Popular School is List's collective designation for the dominant tradition in classical political economy—the school of Adam Smith, Jean-Baptiste Say, and their Continental and British followers. The term is not neutral: by calling it "popular," List underscored its wide diffusion among educated publics while simultaneously implying its intellectual superficiality—a set of doctrines appealing to common sense but inadequate to the complexity of national economic development. (P05-003)

## In List's System

The Popular School functions in List's system as the primary intellectual antagonist. List's entire theoretical apparatus—productive powers, stages of development, protective tariffs, national economy—was constructed in explicit opposition to what he considered the fundamental errors of the Smithian tradition. (014-001)

List identified three core errors of the Popular School. First, it substituted cosmopolitan economy for political economy, analyzing wealth creation as if nations, borders, and wars did not exist. Second, it reduced the sources of wealth to the division of labor and exchange, ignoring the productive powers—mental, social, institutional—that determine a nation's capacity to produce. (014-004) Third, it universalized the temporary interests of England—then the only fully developed manufacturing nation—into eternal principles valid for all nations at all stages of development. (027-001)

## List's Argument

List's critique of the Popular School was systematic and multi-layered. At the theoretical level, he argued that the School had conflated the causes of wealth (productive powers) with wealth itself (exchange values), leading to policy prescriptions that maximized current exchange at the expense of future productive capacity. (014-002)

> The Popular School has concealed its misunderstanding of the nature of productive powers behind the doctrine of free trade. (014-001)

At the historical level, List charged that the Popular School ignored the actual experience of nations. England had built its manufacturing supremacy through centuries of protection—yet the Popular School, writing from within that protected supremacy, now urged other nations to adopt free trade. (027-001) "The Popular School does not discriminate in respect of the different stages of national development," List complained; it prescribed the same policy to England and to Germany, to the manufacturing giant and to the agricultural dependency. (027-001)

At the practical level, List argued that the Popular School's doctrines served England's commercial interests. By convincing other nations to open their markets to free competition, the Popular School—whatever the intentions of its individual adherents—functioned as an instrument of English commercial supremacy. (012-005) List was particularly critical of how the Popular School mixed up the concepts of individual economy and national economy, treating the maxims valid for the private merchant as if they applied equally to the nation. (011-007)

Despite his critique, List did not reject the Popular School wholesale. He acknowledged Smith's genius in articulating the division of labor and Say's contributions to the theory of value. His objection was not to the School's positive discoveries but to its claim to universality—its pretense that principles valid for a world without nations constituted a complete political economy. (013-001)

## Historical Examples

- **Adam Smith** was "the first who successfully applied the analytical method to political economy." (P03-002) List praised Smith's analysis of the division of labor while criticizing his neglect of the national dimension.

- **J.B. Say** systematized Smith's doctrines and spread them across the Continent. List credited Say with clarifying the theory of value but faulted him for extending Smith's cosmopolitan framework rather than correcting it. (P03-006)

- **England as the Popular School's implicit model**: List argued that the Popular School's doctrines were not genuinely universal but were generalizations from England's unique historical position. England was the first nation to complete the transition to the agricultural-manufacturing-commercial state—and the Popular School, List charged, mistook England's exceptional trajectory for a universal law. (013-010)

- **German economists** of List's generation had been "trained up in the cosmopolitical school" and consequently failed to develop a political economy suited to German conditions. This intellectual subordination was, for List, as damaging to German development as the absence of protective tariffs. (P05-003)
