# George Canning

## Life and Career

George Canning (1770–1827) was a British Tory statesman who served as Foreign Secretary (1807–1809, 1822–1827) and briefly as Prime Minister (1827). In List's analysis, Canning represents a pivotal — and, from List's perspective, cautionary — figure: the British statesman who began dismantling the protective system that had built British manufacturing supremacy.

Canning, together with his colleague William Huskisson at the Board of Trade, initiated what List called the "Canning-Huskisson system" of trade liberalization in the 1820s. They reduced tariffs, simplified the Navigation Acts, and moved Britain toward the free trade policy it would fully embrace with the repeal of the Corn Laws (1846). List referenced "the cosmopolitical tendencies of Canning and Huskisson" (032-005) as evidence that Britain, having achieved manufacturing supremacy, was now preaching free trade to lock in its advantage.

## Role in "The National System"

Canning and Huskisson appear in List's *National System* as a turning point: the moment when Britain transitioned from *practicing* protection to *preaching* free trade. List's analysis was characteristically strategic:

- **Britain's position had changed**: By the 1820s, British manufacturing was so dominant that it no longer needed protection. Free trade would benefit Britain by opening foreign markets to British goods while foreign manufacturers (in Germany, America, France) still needed protection to survive.

- **The Canning-Huskisson reforms were rational for Britain**: List acknowledged that, from a purely British perspective, the reforms made sense. Britain's manufacturers could now compete globally. The problem was that continental nations were being urged to reciprocate — to open their markets — while their industries were still in infancy.

- **The cosmopolitical rhetoric**: Canning presented his reforms in the language of universal principle (free trade benefits all nations), but List saw them as Britain pursuing its national interest under cosmopolitan cover.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **Reciprocity Treaties**: Canning negotiated commercial treaties that reduced tariffs bilaterally — a step toward free trade that, List argued, benefited the stronger manufacturing partner disproportionately.

- **The Navigation Acts reform**: Canning and Huskisson began liberalizing the navigation laws that had reserved British trade for British ships since Cromwell's time.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment of Canning was critical but not dismissive. He understood Canning's policies as the logical expression of British national interest at a particular historical moment. The error was not Canning's (he served Britain) but that of German and American policymakers who imitated British free trade without having achieved British manufacturing maturity.

"The economic measures promoted by Canning, Huskisson, and their successors" (P04-008) represented, List argued, a sophisticated form of economic warfare — persuading competitors to disarm (remove tariffs) when they most needed protection.
