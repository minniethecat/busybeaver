## Definition

Moral forces, in List's political economy, are the ethical, religious, and intellectual qualities of a population considered as factors of production. List treats morality, religious character, prudence, and enlightenment not as virtues to be praised in sermons but as forms of national capital — productive assets that accumulate through good institutions and deplete through bad ones. A nation whose population possesses a "considerable natural measure of morality, prudence, and circumspection" (007-005) will produce more from the same material resources than a nation whose population lacks these qualities. The moral dimension of productive powers is what enables a nation to undertake long-term projects, maintain institutions, transmit skills across generations, and resist the temptations of short-term gain that lead to long-term ruin.

List's treatment of moral forces as productive capital is one of his most distinctive contributions, setting him apart from classical economists who generally treated morality as outside the scope of economic analysis.

## In List's System

Moral forces occupy a foundational position in List's theory of productive powers. He enumerates them alongside material forces and institutional conditions as the third pillar of national productive capacity. In Chapter XII, List integrates moral forces into his causal framework:

> It were difficult to estimate the influence which liberty of thought and conscience has on the productive powers of nations ... others promoting the morality and religious character of the population. (012-004, 012-007)

This passage establishes that moral forces are not merely correlated with prosperity but causally contribute to it: they are among the conditions that "promote" productive powers. List explicitly ranks the factors: security for person and property, freedom and justice, the promotion of religious character, morality and intelligence — each is a distinct productive force that may be stronger or weaker in a given nation, with economic consequences that can be traced and compared.

The manufacturing system itself generates moral improvement, creating a virtuous cycle. List observes that factory work demands "the strictest morality, cleanliness, and neatness" of workers (009-005), while agricultural labour — particularly under servile conditions — permitted lower standards. Manufacturing thus educates the population morally as well as technically, and this moral education in turn raises the quality of all economic activity.

List's integration of moral forces into his system culminates in the declaration that the state must "promote religion, morality, and prosperity" simultaneously — not as separate domains of policy but as a single integrated project of national development. (015-002)

## List's Argument

**Moral forces as capital.** List's most radical claim is that moral forces are forms of capital — assets that produce returns over time and can be accumulated or dissipated. He writes:

> The nation must make notable progress in its moral, intellectual, social, and political conditions before it can undertake the transition to manufacturing. (015-005)

This is not a pious observation but a causal claim: moral development is a prerequisite for economic development. Nations that lack the moral discipline, the trust in contracts, the willingness to defer gratification, and the respect for law that manufacturing requires cannot build a manufacturing economy regardless of their tariff policy.

**The manufacturing-morality cycle.** List identifies a two-way relationship between manufacturing and morality. Manufacturing demands morality of its workers — punctuality, cleanliness, honesty, sobriety — and in demanding these qualities it cultivates them. The "manufacturing population" develops habits of discipline and prudence that the purely agricultural population lacks. (009-005) Conversely, a population that has already achieved a high level of morality is better positioned to develop manufacturing, because manufacturing requires the cooperation of large numbers of people in complex chains of production that depend on trust and reliability. (010-001)

**Religion as moral infrastructure.** List treats religion primarily as a source and sustainer of moral forces. The "religious and moral character of the people" appears repeatedly in his enumeration of productive conditions (004-012), and he argues that the Reformation — by freeing individual conscience and promoting literacy and moral seriousness — was an economic event of the first magnitude. The countries that adopted Protestantism (England, Holland, parts of Germany, the northern United States) developed manufacturing more rapidly than those that remained Catholic, not because of any theological superiority but because Protestantism's emphasis on individual conscience, literacy, and worldly calling reinforced the moral disciplines that manufacturing required.

**The limits of moral forces.** List does not treat moral forces as a substitute for material development or institutional reform. A nation cannot moralize its way to prosperity without manufacturing; morality without industry produces a virtuous but poor people. Nor can moral forces operate without supporting institutions: "proportion to the intelligence, morality, and industry of its citizens" (010-001) is the nation's productive capacity, but only when those citizens are free, secure in their property, and governed by constitutional institutions. Morality is one pillar among several, not a sufficient condition.

## Historical Examples

**England** again provides List's central illustration. The "religious and moral character of the people" — forged by centuries of Protestant discipline, parliamentary liberty, and commercial experience — was, in List's analysis, as important to England's rise as its coal deposits or its navy. (004-012) The English factory system, with its demands for punctuality, sobriety, and attention to quality, both drew upon and deepened this moral capital.

**The Hanseatic cities** demonstrate moral forces at the municipal level. The Hanseatic merchants developed a reputation for honesty and reliability in contract that was itself a commercial asset, lowering the costs of trade and attracting partners from across Europe. (007-005) Their "natural measure of morality, prudence, and circumspection" was not a cultural accident but the product of institutions — municipal self-government, merchant guilds, and commercial law — that rewarded moral behavior and punished its absence.

**Spain and Portugal** illustrate the negative case. List argues that the influx of American gold and silver "will only acquire and maintain political liberty" if deployed productively (005-003); without the moral and institutional framework to channel wealth into productive investment, the precious metals merely financed consumption, war, and the maintenance of a parasitic aristocracy. Spain's decline was, in this reading, as much a moral as an economic failure — the failure to develop the productive discipline that manufacturing requires.

**Germany** in List's own time presented both problems and promise. The German population possessed strong moral qualities — "a considerable natural measure of morality, prudence, and circumspection" (007-005) — but these qualities could not be fully expressed economically because Germany lacked the national unity, protective policy, and manufacturing base that would have converted moral capital into material prosperity. The Zollverein was, in part, a project to give German moral forces the economic framework they needed to become fully productive.
