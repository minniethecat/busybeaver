# Torrid Zone

## Definition

The **Torrid Zone** — the tropical regions of the earth between the Tropic of Cancer and the Tropic of Capricorn — occupies a distinct economic role in Friedrich List's theory of international trade and national development. List argued that the countries of the torrid zone were naturally suited to producing agricultural commodities — sugar, coffee, cotton, rice, tobacco, indigo — that the manufacturing nations of the temperate zone required.

This geographic division of production was, in List's view, a natural and mutually beneficial arrangement: "The countries of the torrid zone will offer to all nations which have established manufacturing power a large and increasing market for their manufactured goods." (015-016)

## In List's System

List's treatment of the torrid zone reveals his adaptation of Montesquieu's climate theory to political economy:

1. **Natural comparative advantage**: Unlike the manufacturing/agricultural division within the temperate zone — which List believed could and should be overcome through protection — the torrid/temperate division was rooted in climate, not policy. Tropical crops simply could not be grown efficiently in Europe.

2. **Complementary trade**: Trade between the temperate manufacturing nations and the torrid zone was genuinely mutually beneficial in List's framework. The torrid zone provided raw materials and colonial produce; the temperate zone provided manufactured goods. Neither could produce what the other offered.

3. **A limit to protection**: List's argument for protectionism was geographically bounded. Protection was for nations of the temperate zone seeking to develop manufacturing — not for tropical colonies, whose economic role was naturally complementary.

## The Division of Labor Between Zones

List described a global economic order structured around the relationship between the two zones:

> "This constitutes the division of labor and the confederation of productive powers between the countries of the torrid zone and those of the temperate zone." (021-004)

The torrid zone would specialize in raw materials and exotic agricultural products, while the temperate zone would specialize in manufacturing. This division was:
- **Natural**, not imposed by colonial policy
- **Mutually beneficial**, unlike the relationship between an agricultural and manufacturing nation within the temperate zone
- **Increasing over time**, as manufacturing nations grew wealthier and consumed more tropical products

## Consumption Patterns

List observed that manufacturing nations consumed vastly more colonial produce than agricultural nations:

> "The English on average consume two to three times more colonial produce than the French, three to four times more than the Germans." (021-006)

This became an argument for industrialization: only a wealthy manufacturing nation could afford to consume tropical products at scale, thus benefiting the torrid-zone producers. The torrid zone therefore had an interest in the industrialization of the temperate zone.

## Political Implications

List's torrid zone theory had political dimensions:

- **Anti-colonialism**: List was skeptical of direct colonial rule. He predicted that "the Asiatic countries of the torrid zone will pass gradually under the dominion" of manufacturing powers not through military conquest but through economic dependence. (022-003)

- **Free trade with the tropics**: Unlike his opposition to free trade within the temperate zone, List accepted — and even encouraged — free trade between the temperate and torrid zones. This was trade between genuinely different productive systems, not unequal competition within the same system.

- **The American hemisphere**: List saw the Americas as encompassing both zones — the United States and Canada in the temperate zone, the Caribbean and South America in the torrid zone — creating the conditions for a self-contained hemispheric trading system.
