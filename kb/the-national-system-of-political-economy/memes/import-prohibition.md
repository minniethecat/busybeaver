## Definition

Import prohibition is the **complete legal exclusion of specific foreign goods** from a nation's market — the most restrictive instrument in the spectrum of trade policy measures. In Friedrich List's framework, import prohibition represents the **maximum degree of protection**, deployed when customs duties alone are insufficient to shelter a strategically important nascent industry from overwhelming foreign competition.

List distinguished between **absolute prohibition** (total ban on specified articles) and **prohibitive duties** (tariffs set so high as to effectively preclude imports). He regarded prohibition as a legitimate but **exceptional and temporary** measure, suitable only for the early stages of industrial development and only for industries deemed essential to the nation's long-term productive powers. Unlike crude mercantilism, which treated prohibition as a permanent feature of trade policy, List insisted that prohibitions should be progressively replaced by moderate protective duties as domestic industry matured.

## In List's System

Import prohibition occupies the **extreme end of List's protective policy spectrum**, beyond protective tariffs and customs duties. It is a tool of last resort for nations at the earliest stages of industrialisation — what List called the transition from the agricultural to the agricultural-manufacturing stage of development.

List's position on prohibition was carefully calibrated. He acknowledged that "our system" allowed for "prohibitions of, or duties on, exports" in specific circumstances, but he did not advocate blanket prohibition. (026-001) His policy recommendations were always context-dependent: what was appropriate for Germany in the 1840s differed from what had been appropriate for England under Henry VII or for the United States after independence.

List was particularly attentive to the **graduation principle**: a nation that begins with prohibitions should plan from the outset to transition to moderate protection as its industries mature. The goal was not permanent exclusion of foreign goods but the creation of conditions under which domestic industry could eventually compete.

## List's Argument

**Prohibition as a developmental tool.** List argued that prohibition could be justified when a nation possessed all the natural prerequisites for a particular industry — raw materials, labour, potential capital — but could not initiate production because of the overwhelming competitive advantage of established foreign producers. In such cases, temporary prohibition could provide the sheltered space needed for the industry to take root and develop.

**The graduation imperative.** List was sharply critical of permanent prohibition, which he associated with the excesses of the mercantile system. "A system of prohibition" should, in his framework, give way to "a moderate system of protection" once the protected industry had established itself. (026-003) The transition from prohibition to protection was itself a measure of industrial progress.

**England's strategic use of prohibition.** List documented how England had systematically used import prohibitions to build its textile industry. The prohibition on the import of Indian cotton fabrics was, he argued, a decisive factor in the development of England's own cotton manufacturing: "The prohibition was complete and peremptory." (004-007) Once English cotton manufactures had achieved dominance, England reversed its position and began advocating free trade — a sequence that List regarded as instructive rather than hypocritical.

**The limits of prohibition.** List was aware of the risks of prohibition: it could breed inefficiency by eliminating competitive pressure, provoke smuggling and retaliation, and, if maintained too long, lock the nation into inferior production methods. "The evasion of customs duties by smuggling and false declarations" increased when duties or prohibitions were set too high relative to market realities. (026-004)

## Historical Examples

**The Hanseatic prohibition episode.** List recounted how the Hanseatic League, at the height of its power, secured from the King of England a prohibition against the wearing of foreign-manufactured cloth — an early instance of import prohibition used to protect domestic industry. However, once the Hansards' political power waned, "the Hansards [failed] to remove this prohibition," and the English cloth industry continued to develop behind protective walls. (002-005)

**England's cotton prohibition.** The most consequential example in List's narrative was England's absolute prohibition on Indian cotton and silk fabrics — a policy that, by excluding superior Indian textiles, created the protected space in which England's own cotton industry could develop from infancy to global dominance.

**Napoleon's Continental System.** Though List did not examine it in depth, the Continental System represented an attempt at large-scale import prohibition directed against British manufactures. Its partial failure illustrated the practical difficulties of enforcing prohibitions across extensive territories with porous borders.

**The Dutch case.** List noted that "the Dutch would not" accept permanent prohibition (032-004), reflecting the tension between the commercial interests of established trading nations and the protective needs of industrialising ones — a tension that runs throughout List's analysis.
