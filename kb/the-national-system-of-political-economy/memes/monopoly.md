## Definition

Monopoly, in List's political economy, is not primarily the private monopoly of a firm or individual but the structural monopoly that one nation can achieve over manufacturing industry to the disadvantage of other nations. List argued that England, through its early industrialization and strategic commercial policy, had established a de facto world-manufacturing monopoly—a position that free trade rhetoric served to legitimize and perpetuate rather than to challenge. (015-014)

## In List's System

The concept of monopoly occupies a critical position in List's critique of classical free trade doctrine. The classical economists, List charged, condemned private monopolies as distortions of the market while remaining silent about the far more consequential national monopoly that England had established over world manufacturing. This asymmetry was not accidental: England's advocacy of free trade, List argued, was in fact a strategy for preserving its national manufacturing monopoly against emerging competitors. (014-005)

List distinguished between natural and artificial monopoly. Agriculture, he acknowledged, possesses a "natural monopoly"—climate and soil conditions give certain regions inherent advantages in producing particular crops. (018-005) But manufacturing monopoly, List insisted, is largely artificial: it results from historical accident and deliberate policy, and it can be challenged and overcome by nations that adopt the right protective measures. (027-001)

## List's Argument

List's argument about monopoly is inseparable from his theory of national development. He contended that England's manufacturing monopoly was not a permanent natural endowment but the outcome of centuries of strategic policy—from Edward III's promotion of woolen manufacturing to the Navigation Acts and the protective tariffs of the eighteenth century. Having achieved industrial supremacy through these measures, England then urged other nations to adopt free trade, which would have the effect of locking in England's monopoly by preventing competitors from developing their own manufacturing capacity.

> The school treats the question as if a world-manufacturing monopoly were indispensable for the progress of the human race. (015-016)

The injustice of this arrangement, List argued, lay in the fact that England's monopoly deprived other nations of the opportunity to develop their own productive powers. The monopoly was self-reinforcing: England's manufacturing dominance gave it the wealth and power to suppress competition, and the suppression of competition preserved the monopoly. (014-006) Only deliberate protective policy—the temporary creation of a protected domestic market—could break this cycle and enable other nations to become manufacturing powers in their own right.

List was careful to distinguish protective tariffs from the creation of private monopoly. Protection, properly calibrated, "becomes mere private monopoly" only when it is excessive and permanent. (015-002) The goal of protection should be to create conditions of managed competition within the domestic market, not to insulate domestic producers from all competitive pressure.

## Historical Examples

List drew on extensive historical evidence to illustrate the dynamics of national monopoly:

- **England's Navigation Acts**: List identified the Navigation Acts as the cornerstone of England's manufacturing monopoly. By reserving English carrying trade for English ships, the Acts simultaneously built up English maritime power and suppressed the commercial fleets of rival nations. (015-010) The Acts demonstrated how a nation could use commercial policy to establish monopoly in one sector as a platform for broader industrial dominance.

- **The Hanseatic League's Decline**: The Hanseatic League had once enjoyed a near-monopoly over Northern European trade, but its commercial power lacked a manufacturing base and a unified national state to sustain it. As nation-states developed their own manufacturing and shipping, the League's monopoly eroded. The lesson List drew was that commercial monopoly without manufacturing power is inherently fragile. (002-009)

- **Venice's Manufacturing Monopoly**: Venice maintained a manufacturing monopoly in the Mediterranean for centuries through a combination of technical secrecy, strategic prohibitions, and naval power. But its monopoly depended on the absence of manufacturing competitors; once other nations developed their own industries, Venice's position became untenable. (001-007)

- **The Colonial Monopoly System**: List observed that European powers had used colonial monopolies—exclusive trading rights with their colonies—as a means of building up domestic manufacturing. These colonial monopolies were, in List's analysis, a transitional instrument: they served their purpose during the early stages of national development but became obstacles to broader prosperity once manufacturing had taken root. (P03-005)

- **The American Response**: The United States, List noted, had recognized the threat of British manufacturing monopoly and had responded with protective tariffs designed to foster American industry. The American System of Henry Clay was precisely an attempt to break free of British manufacturing monopoly through deliberate national policy. (023-015)

## Contrast with Classical Economics

The classical economists condemned monopoly in all its forms as a distortion of the natural order of free competition. Adam Smith's famous hostility to monopolies—whether guild monopolies, chartered company monopolies, or tariff-protected monopolies—was rooted in the conviction that monopoly raised prices and reduced output, harming consumers for the benefit of a privileged few.

List did not dispute that private monopoly could be harmful. His objection was that the classical school applied the anti-monopoly principle selectively—zealously condemning the protective tariffs that developing nations used to foster their industries, while ignoring the far larger structural monopoly that England had achieved and that free trade would preserve. The classical economists' campaign against monopoly, List charged, was in practice a campaign against the industrial development of nations other than England. (027-001)
