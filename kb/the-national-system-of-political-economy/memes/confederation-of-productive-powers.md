# Confederation of Productive Powers

## Definition

The **Confederation of Productive Powers** (*Konföderation der Produktivkräfte*) is Friedrich List's term for the organic integration of all sectors within a national economy — agriculture, manufacturing, commerce, transportation, science, and institutions — into a mutually reinforcing system. When a nation's productive powers are "confederated," each sector stimulates and supports the others, creating a whole greater than the sum of its parts.

This concept is List's alternative to Adam Smith's "division of labour." Where Smith saw specialization as the key to productivity, List saw *integration* — the confederation of diverse productive powers within a single national territory. (013-004)

## In List's System

The confederation of productive powers is the desired end-state of national economic development. It represents the condition in which:

- **Manufacturing** provides tools, machinery, and consumer goods to agriculture
- **Agriculture** supplies food and raw materials to manufacturing
- **Commerce** distributes goods between sectors and to foreign markets
- **Transportation** (canals, railways, roads) connects regions and reduces costs
- **Science and education** continuously improve methods and skills
- **Political institutions** provide stability, law, and national defence

When these powers are confederated, the nation achieves a self-sustaining cycle of development. The "division of the commercial operations and the confederation of the productive powers will be developed" only when all these elements coexist within one national territory. (013-004)

## Contrast with the Division of Labor

List's confederation concept is a deliberate alternative to Smith's division of labor. Smith emphasized *specialization* — breaking production into ever-finer tasks. List emphasized *integration* — connecting diverse sectors into a unified whole. Both increase productivity, but List argued that Smith's framework provided no basis for opposing an international division of labor that would assign manufacturing to some nations and agriculture to others. The confederation concept shows why such a division is destructive: it breaks the organic connection between sectors that drives national development.
