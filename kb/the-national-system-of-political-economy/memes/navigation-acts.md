# The Navigation Acts

The **Navigation Acts** were a series of English laws, originating under Cromwell's Commonwealth (1651), that reserved English foreign trade for English-owned and English-built ships crewed predominantly by English sailors.

Foreign vessels were prohibited from carrying goods between English ports or between England and its colonies. The Acts remained the foundation of British maritime policy for nearly two centuries, until their repeal in 1849.

[[Friedrich List]] identified the Navigation Acts as one of three pillars of British manufacturing supremacy, alongside protective tariffs on woolens and the deliberate importation of skilled artisans.

> England by her political power gained productive power, and by her productive power gained political power. (004-010)

## List's Analysis

List saw the Navigation Acts as a comprehensive system of national economic development disguised as maritime regulation. Their effects were multi-dimensional:

| Domain | Mechanism | Long-term Effect |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Shipbuilding** | Captive demand from English merchants | World's largest merchant fleet |
| **Naval power** | Trained seamen available for wartime | Royal Navy supremacy |
| **Trade profits** | Foreign carriers excluded | Profits retained in England |
| **Colonial system** | Colonies trade only with England | Integrated imperial economy |
| **Manufacturing** | Shipbuilding stimulated iron, timber, sailcloth, rope | Backward linkages to industry |

The Acts were not isolated legislation but part of a coherent national strategy. They worked in concert with the protective tariffs [[Elizabeth I of England|Elizabeth I]] had established for woolens and the systematic acquisition of Continental manufacturing technology.

## Historical Context

The Navigation Acts were England's response to Dutch commercial supremacy. In the early 17th century, the Dutch Republic dominated European carrying trade — Dutch ships were cheaper to operate and available for hire by merchants of any nation. England exported its woolen goods and colonial products largely on Dutch bottoms.

The Acts of 1651 changed this. They created a protected market for English shipping, raised the cost of using foreign carriers, and ensured that the profits of England's growing colonial trade stayed in English hands. Dutch merchants protested; the Anglo-Dutch Wars (1652–1674) were fought partly over the Acts' commercial restrictions.

List's insight: the Navigation Acts were not "free trade" — they were deliberate discrimination in favor of national industry. And they worked. By 1700, England had overtaken the Dutch Republic as Europe's leading commercial and naval power.

## The Acts and Free Trade

The Navigation Acts posed a problem for the free trade doctrines of [[Adam Smith]] and [[Jean-Baptiste Say]].

If free trade was universally beneficial, as classical economics taught, then the Navigation Acts — which restricted trade — should have harmed England. But List pointed out the contrary: England's rise to manufacturing and maritime supremacy occurred *under* the Navigation Acts, not after their repeal. The Acts were protectionist, discriminatory, and economically nationalist — and they succeeded.

Smith himself acknowledged this tension. In *The Wealth of Nations*, he famously praised the Navigation Acts as "perhaps the wisest of all commercial regulations of England" — not on economic grounds, but on grounds of national defence. List seized on this admission: if Smith conceded that national security justifies protection, the principle of free trade absolutism was already breached.
