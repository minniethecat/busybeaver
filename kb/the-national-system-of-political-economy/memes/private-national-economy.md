# Private Economy and National Economy

## Definition

The distinction between **Private Economy** and **National Economy** is one of Friedrich List's most important methodological contributions. Private economy concerns the economic logic of individuals, firms, and merchants — maximizing profit by buying cheap and selling dear. National economy concerns the economic logic of an entire nation across generations — developing productive powers, even at the temporary expense of individual profits.

List argued that classical economists had "confounded the principles of private economy with those of national economy" (014-001), applying the merchant's calculus to nations and thereby reaching disastrous policy conclusions.

## In List's System

List devoted Chapter XIV of his Second Book specifically to this distinction. His thesis: what is rational for an individual operating in a market is often irrational for a nation pursuing long-term development.

The classical school, List charged, had taken principles valid for private economy — buy the cheapest goods, maximize present exchange value — and elevated them to universal economic laws applying equally to individuals, nations, and the world. This conflation produced the doctrines of unconditional free trade and cosmopolitan laissez-faire.

List insisted on three distinct levels:
1. **Private economy** — principles governing individual exchange
2. **National economy** — principles governing the development of a nation's productive powers
3. **Cosmopolitical economy** — principles that would govern a hypothetical world federation

Classical economics, by conflating all three, gave advice appropriate only to a merchant to entire nations.

## List's Argument

### The Merchant's Logic vs. The Nation's Logic

> "That may be wisdom in national economy which would be folly in private economy." (014-003)

A private merchant who passes up the opportunity to buy cheap goods is a fool. But a nation that passes up the opportunity to *produce* those goods — allowing its manufacturing base to die while importing cheap substitutes — may be acting wisely. The merchant maximizes present profit; the nation must maximize productive power across generations.

### The Temporal Dimension

Private economy operates on a short time horizon — quarterly profits, annual returns. National economy operates across centuries. The English textile industry was built over 400 years of deliberate protection; no private investor could have sustained such a time horizon. Only the nation, as a continuous entity, can pursue multi-generational development strategies.

### The Spillover Dimension

A private manufacturer considers only the direct costs and revenues of his enterprise. The nation must consider spillovers: the skills developed by workers that diffuse throughout the economy, the scientific advances stimulated by industrial problems, the transportation infrastructure built to serve factories that benefits farmers as well. These spillovers are invisible to private calculation but central to national welfare. "Private economy," List noted, "is almost synonymous with the private economy of all individuals; politics (national economy) is the economy of the nation." (014-002)

## Practical Examples

- **Importing cheap manufactures**: From the private economy perspective, buying British textiles at half the price of domestic ones is rational. From the national economy perspective, doing so destroys the domestic textile industry, eliminates the skills of weavers, reduces demand for domestic wool, and weakens the nation's productive powers permanently.

- **Infant industry investment**: From the private economy perspective, investing in an industry that cannot yet compete internationally is wasteful. From the national economy perspective, the temporary subsidy is an investment in future productive capacity that will repay itself many times over.

- **Education and infrastructure**: These are unprofitable from a narrow private perspective (the returns are too diffuse and long-term to be captured by any single investor), but essential from a national perspective.
