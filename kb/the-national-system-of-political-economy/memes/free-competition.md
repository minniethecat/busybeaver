## Definition

Free competition, as critiqued by List, is the doctrine that all nations should be subjected to unrestricted economic competition in international markets without protective measures. List distinguished between free competition among equal industrial powers—which he acknowledged as beneficial—and free competition between nations at vastly different stages of industrial development, which he condemned as a mechanism by which advanced manufacturing nations destroy the infant industries of less developed ones. (P05-002)

## In List's System

Free competition is the operational mechanism of the cosmopolitical economy that List rejected. The classical school, he argued, abstracted away from national differences and treated the world as a single economic community in which free competition would naturally produce optimal outcomes. List countered that in a world divided into nations at different stages of development, free competition does not produce harmony but domination—the established manufacturing powers capture foreign markets while preventing the industrialization of latecomers. (027-001)

List placed free competition in a developmental sequence. For a nation that has already achieved manufacturing maturity, free competition is an advantageous policy because it opens foreign markets to its superior products. For a nation still building its industrial capacity, free competition is self-destructive—it exposes nascent industries to rivals with decades of accumulated experience, capital, and scale. (029-001) The appropriate moment for free competition is therefore not determined by abstract principle but by the relative industrial development of the competing nations.

## List's Argument

List developed a systematic critique of free competition across multiple dimensions. His primary objection was historical: the nations that preached free competition to others—particularly England—had themselves achieved industrial supremacy through centuries of protection. England, List noted, had "steadily ranged under the free competition of the foreigner" the industries of other nations while carefully protecting its own. (014-006) To demand that latecomers adopt free competition was, in List's view, to "kick away the ladder" by which England itself had climbed.

> Free competition between two nations which are highly civilized can only be mutually beneficial in case both of them are in a nearly equal degree of industrial development. (P05-002)

List's second objection was structural. Industries in advanced nations enjoy advantages that have nothing to do with natural efficiency: accumulated capital, established distribution networks, skilled workforces, and institutional knowledge built over generations. An infant industry in a developing nation cannot "hope to succeed in free competition with the old and long-established manufactures of England" without the breathing space that protection provides. (010-004)

Third, List argued that free competition between unequal powers is not genuinely free. The dominant manufacturing nation can use its superior scale to underprice competitors temporarily, driving them out of business before raising prices. The dependent nation's producers are "brought to the brink of ruin by free competition with foreigners" and lose the capital and skills they had accumulated. (010-006)

## Historical Examples

- **England** demonstrated the strategic use of free competition rhetoric. After building its industry behind protection, England advocated free competition for other nations while maintaining its own Navigation Acts and tariffs until its supremacy was unassailable. List cited English commercial diplomacy as a systematic project to expose foreign home markets to English free competition. (035-010)

- **Germany** illustrated the costs of free competition. Before the Zollverein, German states were exposed to free competition from English and French manufactures. German industry remained stunted, and Germany was, in List's phrase, a nation whose manufacturing market was "exposed to the free competition of a nation which has attained a high degree of manufacturing supremacy." (023-001)

- **The United States** provided List's example of a nation that recognized the danger. The American states understood that they could never build their own industry under free competition with England, and therefore adopted a protective system. The result was rapid industrial growth that would have been impossible under free competition. (026-003)

- **France under Colbert** showed that even temporary exposure to free competition could undo decades of industrial development. When France's protections were removed after Colbert, French manufactures were "sacrificed in favour of English free competition," destroying industries that had taken generations to build. (033-013)
