## Definition

Rent of land is the annual income that accrues to the owner of land from its productive use or its scarcity value. In classical political economy, rent was analysed primarily through the lens of differential fertility (Ricardo) or as one of the three factor shares (Smith). List, while engaging with the classical treatment, approached rent of land from the perspective of his productive powers theory: rent is not a fixed natural magnitude but a social product that rises in proportion to the development of manufacturing and commerce in the vicinity of the land. (020-002)

## In List's System

List integrated rent of land into his theory of the interdependence between agriculture and manufacturing. The rent of agricultural land, he argued, is not determined solely by its natural fertility but by the proximity and vigour of manufacturing industry. A fertile field in an isolated agricultural country may command low rent, while a less fertile field near a thriving manufacturing town commands high rent—because manufacturing creates a dense population of consumers with purchasing power and generates demand for agricultural produce. (020-004)

> The selling price of a given amount of rent of land is always disproportionately influenced by the presence of manufacturing industry in the neighbourhood. (020-002)

This insight was central to List's argument against the classical school's doctrine of comparative advantage in agriculture. If land rent is partly a function of manufacturing development, then nations that specialise exclusively in agriculture—as the classical school recommended for countries with fertile land—are systematically undervaluing their agricultural potential by forgoing the manufacturing development that would raise rents and land values. (020-016)

## List's Argument

List developed his analysis of rent of land in explicit opposition to the classical treatment, particularly Ricardo's theory of rent. Ricardo had argued that rent arises from differences in soil fertility: as population grows and cultivation extends to less fertile land, the more fertile land yields a rent equal to the difference in productivity. List did not deny differential fertility but argued that it was a secondary factor compared to the transformative effect of manufacturing.

List pointed out that the rent of land in manufacturing countries like England was far higher than in purely agricultural countries with superior natural fertility. This disparity could not be explained by differential fertility alone—it reflected the vastly greater demand for agricultural produce created by a dense manufacturing population with high wages. (020-004)

List also argued that the increase in rent of land from manufacturing development benefits the agricultural interest as a whole, not merely landowners. Higher rents mean higher land values, which mean greater collateral for agricultural improvement loans, greater incentives for land improvement, and ultimately greater productivity per acre. This was part of List's broader argument that the agricultural and manufacturing interests are complementary, not antagonistic. (020-016)

## Historical Examples

- **England** provided List's principal illustration. English agricultural rents were the highest in Europe not because English soil was the most fertile—it was not—but because England's manufacturing industry created an enormous domestic market for food and raw materials. The English landlord benefited from industrial protection as much as the manufacturer did. (020-002)

- **Germany** in List's time demonstrated the opposite pattern. German agricultural land was naturally fertile, particularly in the northern plains, but rents remained low compared to England because Germany lacked a dense manufacturing population to absorb agricultural produce at remunerative prices. This was precisely the condition that List's protective programme sought to address.

- **Poland and Russia** represented extreme cases: vast tracts of fertile agricultural land commanded negligible rents because they produced primarily for distant export markets rather than for a proximate manufacturing population. The absence of manufacturing development depressed the value of their most abundant natural resource.
