# Stages of Economic Development

## Definition

The **Stages of Economic Development** is Friedrich List's historical framework for understanding how national economies evolve over time and what trade policies are appropriate at each stage. List identified a progression through which all developing nations pass:

> Nations must "pass through the following stages of development: original barbarism, pastoral life, agriculture, agriculture combined with manufactures, and finally agriculture, manufactures, and commerce combined." (015-003)

This framework is central to List's policy argument: different stages require different trade policies. Free trade is beneficial for nations in the agricultural-manufacturing-commercial stage (they can dominate global markets), but destructive for nations still in the purely agricultural stage (their infant manufacturing cannot survive foreign competition).

## In List's System

The stages framework provides the historical and empirical foundation for List's policy prescriptions. It explains *why* protection is temporary and *when* it should be applied:

1. **Barbarism → Pastoral**: Hunting and gathering give way to herding. Trade is minimal; policy is largely irrelevant.

2. **Pastoral → Agricultural**: Settled farming develops. Some trade in raw materials and foodstuffs emerges. Free trade in agricultural exports is generally beneficial at this stage.

3. **Agricultural → Agricultural-Manufacturing**: The critical transition. Manufacturing begins to develop alongside agriculture. This is when protective tariffs are necessary — the infant industries cannot compete with established foreign manufacturers. Without protection, the nation may be locked permanently in the agricultural stage.

4. **Agricultural-Manufacturing → Agricultural-Manufacturing-Commercial**: The nation has achieved manufacturing maturity. It now has a diversified economy with competitive industry, productive agriculture, and an active merchant marine. At this stage, the nation can safely adopt free trade — and indeed benefits from it, as its efficient manufacturers can compete globally.

## List's Argument

- **Protection is stage-specific**: "To communities in the early stages of their civilisation, so long as they remain in them, free trade with more advanced nations is beneficial." (004-008) But for nations in the agricultural-manufacturing transition stage, protection is essential.

- **England's trajectory**: England passed through all stages — from raw wool exporter (agricultural) to protected textile manufacturer (agricultural-manufacturing) to global industrial and commercial power (agricultural-manufacturing-commercial). Only after reaching the final stage did England advocate free trade.

- **Germany's position**: Germany in List's time was in the agricultural-manufacturing transition. The Zollverein and protective tariffs were instruments to advance to the commercial stage.
