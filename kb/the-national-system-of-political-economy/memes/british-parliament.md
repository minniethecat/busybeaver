# British Parliament

The **British Parliament** is, in [[Friedrich List]]'s analysis, the institutional mechanism through which England's national economic interest was translated into law across four centuries.

List did not analyze Parliament as a political institution in the abstract. He analyzed its *economic function*: the body that enacted the protective tariffs, [[Navigation Acts]], colonial monopolies, and manufacturing regulations that built English [[Manufacturing Power]].

> At the opening of Parliament in 1721, the King is made to declare that the encouragement of manufactures is the surest foundation of national prosperity. (004-004)

## Parliament as Economic Instrument

List identified several features of Parliament that made it an effective instrument of national economic policy:

**Continuity across regimes**. England's economic policy — protective tariffs, navigation laws, manufacturing promotion — persisted across Tudor, Stuart, Commonwealth, and Hanoverian governments. Parliament provided institutional continuity that continental absolutist states lacked.

**Mercantile representation**. The commercial and manufacturing classes were represented in Parliament, particularly after the Glorious Revolution (1688). This ensured that economic legislation served the interests of the productive classes rather than solely the Crown or landed aristocracy.

**Legislative precision**. Parliamentary statutes could calibrate protection with precision: specific tariff rates for specific industries, exemptions for raw materials, bounties for exports. Continental monarchies, by contrast, often issued broad decrees that were poorly enforced.

**Colonial integration**. Parliament integrated colonial trade into a coherent imperial system. The Navigation Acts ensured that colonial raw materials flowed to England and colonial markets consumed English manufactures — a closed loop enforced by parliamentary legislation.

> Elizabeth treated her Parliaments not as adversaries but as partners in the project of national manufacturing development. (004-013)

## List's Implicit Comparison: Reichstag vs. Parliament

List wrote for a German audience and his analysis of Parliament carried an implicit comparison with German political institutions. The [[German States]] were fragmented across thirty-nine customs territories. The German Confederation's Diet (Bundestag) in Frankfurt had no power to enact uniform commercial legislation.

England had Parliament — a single legislative body for a unified national market. Germany had thirty-nine separate parliaments and diets, each protecting its own local interests. The [[Zollverein]] (1834) was the closest German equivalent to Parliament's economic function: a single institution that could enact uniform commercial policy across multiple territories.

## The Free Trade Turn

Parliament's role shifted in the 19th century. After the Industrial Revolution had made British manufacturing the world's most efficient, Parliament became the instrument of *free trade* legislation: the repeal of the Corn Laws (1846), the repeal of the Navigation Acts (1849), the Cobden-Chevalier Treaty (1860).

List noted the timing with irony. For four centuries, Parliament had enacted protection. Only when British manufacturing supremacy was unassailable did Parliament discover the virtues of free trade. The institution was constant; the policy changed with national interest.

This, List argued, is what a national legislature *should* do — adapt economic policy to the nation's stage of development. The error was not Parliament's adaptability but the claim by [[Adam Smith]] and his followers that free trade was a universal principle rather than a policy appropriate to a specific stage of national development.
