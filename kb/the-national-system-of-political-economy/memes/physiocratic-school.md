# The Physiocratic School

## Overview

The Physiocratic School, founded by François Quesnay in the 1750s, was the first systematic school of economic thought. Its core doctrines — that agriculture alone produces a *produit net* (net product), that manufacturing is "sterile" (merely transforming inputs without adding value), and that a single tax on agricultural land should replace all other taxes — made it both an important predecessor and a target of List's critique.

List acknowledged the Physiocrats' historical importance: "before Quesnay and the French economists there had been no systematic political economy" (011-001). But he rejected their central doctrine as the mirror-image error of Adam Smith's system. Where Smith overvalued exchange and undervalued productive conditions, Quesnay overvalued agriculture and undervalued the transformative power of manufacturing.

## Role in List's Analysis

List treated the Physiocratic School as a cautionary example in two senses:

- **Methodological**: The Physiocrats were the first to attempt a systematic theory of the economy, demonstrating the power of theoretical reasoning. List credited them with establishing economics as a discipline.

- **Substantive**: Their doctrine that only agriculture is productive was, List argued, empirically false and practically dangerous. France's relative industrial backwardness compared to England was partly attributable to the anti-manufacturing bias of Physiocratic thought. "The erroneous view of the physiocratic school" (020-013) had real consequences for French economic policy.

- **The physiocratic revival**: List was accused by some critics of "reviving the long-exploded physiocratic system" (007-009) — a charge he vigorously rejected. His system emphasized *manufacturing* as the engine of productive power, precisely the opposite of Physiocratic doctrine.
