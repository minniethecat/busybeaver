## Definition

Emigration, in List's political economy, is the permanent relocation of population across national boundaries. Unlike the classical economists who treated emigration as a matter of individual choice responding to wage differentials, List analyzed emigration as a phenomenon with profound national consequences—it transfers human capital, skills, and productive powers from one nation to another, altering the relative strength of nations in the process. (P04-008)

## In List's System

Emigration occupies an ambivalent position in List's framework. On one hand, emigration can be a symptom of national weakness: when a nation's citizens leave because they cannot find productive employment at home, the nation loses the human capital in which it has already invested (education, upbringing, early training). On the other hand, emigration can be an instrument of national strength: when a nation systematically colonizes new territories, it extends its economic reach and creates markets for its manufactures. (036-004)

List was particularly attentive to the asymmetric character of emigration between nations at different stages of development. Emigration from an agricultural nation to a manufacturing nation transfers population from an economy where labor is underemployed to one where it can be more productively deployed—but the benefits of this productivity gain accrue mainly to the receiving nation. The sending nation loses workers in whom it has invested without receiving any return on that investment. (023-014)

## List's Argument

List's principal argument about emigration is that it must be managed as a matter of national policy rather than left to individual decision. He advocated for "national supervision of emigration"—the deliberate direction of population flows to serve national economic interests. (P04-008)

> The business of arranging their emigration must be taken in hand by the state. (021-002)

The case of England illustrated both sides of the emigration phenomenon. England had used emigration strategically to populate its colonies, creating overseas markets for English manufactures and sources of raw materials for English industry. At the same time, England had lost population to the United States, where emigrants carried English skills and industrial knowledge that helped build a manufacturing competitor. List observed this tension and argued that nations must be strategic about when to encourage and when to discourage emigration.

For Germany, List advocated a program of organized emigration to Central and South America, where German settlers could establish communities that would become markets for German exports. (036-006) This was not mere colonialism in the traditional sense but an extension of the national economy: the emigrants would remain culturally and commercially linked to Germany, creating a network of economic relationships that would benefit the homeland.

## Historical Examples

List drew on several historical cases to illustrate the economic significance of emigration:

- **English Emigration to America**: The emigration of English artisans and workers to the American colonies transferred industrial skills across the Atlantic. List noted that English laws prohibiting the emigration of skilled artisans were attempts to prevent this transfer—an implicit acknowledgment that emigration was not merely a matter of individual movement but of national productive power. (009-006)

- **German Emigration**: List was deeply concerned about the emigration of Germans to the United States and other destinations. Each emigrant represented a loss of human capital that Germany had nurtured but could not productively employ at home—a consequence of Germany's failure to develop its manufacturing industry. (036-005)

- **The Hanseatic Experience**: The Hanseatic cities had used emigration and settlement to extend their commercial networks across Northern Europe, but these settlements lacked the political backing of a unified national state and were eventually absorbed by the territorial powers. (002-004)

- **Colonial Settlement**: List studied the colonial settlement policies of various European powers and concluded that organized colonization, backed by national policy, was a more effective instrument of economic development than haphazard individual emigration. The Dutch and English experiences demonstrated the value of systematic colonization. (036-007)

## Contrast with Classical Economics

The classical economists treated emigration as a self-correcting mechanism: workers move from low-wage to high-wage regions until wages equalize. In this framework, emigration is always beneficial—workers improve their own condition, and the efficiency of labor allocation increases globally.

List's objection was that this analysis ignored the national dimension. The emigrating worker takes with them skills, knowledge, and productive capacities that were cultivated at national expense. The receiving nation gains these assets without having invested in them. Over time, the cumulative effect of emigration can drain an agricultural nation of its most enterprising citizens while concentrating talent and skill in already-advanced manufacturing nations. What the classical economists described as an efficient allocation of labor was, in List's analysis, a mechanism by which the rich nations grew richer and the poor nations grew poorer.
