# David Ricardo

## Life and Career

David Ricardo (1772–1823).

David Ricardo (1772–1823) was a British political economist, stockbroker, and Member of Parliament. His *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817) systematized classical economics around the labor theory of value, the theory of rent, and the doctrine of comparative advantage — the theoretical foundation for universal free trade.

List mentioned Ricardo only briefly, noting that "Ricardo in his Principles of Political Economy" (032-007) had developed the rent theory that List considered flawed. List's deeper objection was to Ricardo's comparative advantage doctrine: that nations should specialize in whatever they produce relatively most efficiently, regardless of the long-term developmental consequences.

## Role in "The National System"

Ricardo represents the theoretical culmination of the classical school List opposed. Ricardo's comparative advantage provided the rigorous analytical basis for free trade that Smith's looser arguments had lacked. List's counter-argument — that comparative advantage is static, ignoring the dynamic benefits of manufacturing — was directed as much against Ricardo as against Smith.

## List's Assessment

List considered Ricardo's system even more dangerous than Smith's because of its analytical rigor: Ricardo gave free trade a mathematical precision that made it harder to challenge. But the precision was bought at the cost of ignoring everything List considered essential — institutions, history, productive powers, and the nation as the unit of analysis.
