## Definition

Political economy, in List's system, is the science that investigates how a particular nation, given its specific historical circumstances, institutional inheritance, and stage of development, can best promote its productive powers and advance toward the agricultural-manufacturing-commercial state. List distinguished his "political economy" from the "cosmopolitical economy" of Adam Smith: where Smith's science assumed a universal community of nations, List's studied the economy of a specific nation situated among rival powers. (P05-002)

## In List's System

Political economy is the overarching framework within which all of List's specific doctrines—protective tariffs, productive powers, stages of development—find their place. List argued that Smith had given the name "political economy" to what was in fact "cosmopolitical economy"—a theory of how wealth is created in a world without nations, borders, or wars. (011-003) True political economy, List insisted, must address the nation as it actually exists: politically organized, historically situated, and engaged in perpetual competition with other nations.

The national system of political economy thus has three components: a theory of productive powers (how nations create the capacity to produce), a theory of stages (how nations progress from savagery to the agricultural-manufacturing-commercial state), and a theory of policy (how the state intervenes to accelerate this progression). (P03-006) Together, these constitute what List called "the natural system of political economy"—natural because it follows from the actual condition of nations, not from cosmopolitan abstractions. (P05-005)

## List's Argument

List advanced his conception of political economy through a sustained critique of the prevailing Smithian orthodoxy. His central charge was that Smith and his followers had committed a category error: they had substituted a cosmopolitan theory of universal wealth for a political theory of national development. (012-003) In a world of competing nations, List argued, the relevant question is not "how is wealth created in general?" but "how can this nation, under these conditions, develop its productive powers?"

> The best work on political economy which one can read in that modern land of classical political economy is a treatise on the national system of political economy. (P05-004)

List traced the intellectual lineage of political economy through three phases: the practical statecraft of Venice, the Hanseatic League, and England (a "practice of political economy" exercised by statesmen before the science existed); the mercantile system (a flawed but nationally oriented theory); and the Smithian school (a cosmopolitan theory that abandoned the national standpoint). (011-001) List positioned his own system as a recovery and synthesis—restoring the national perspective that Smith had discarded while incorporating Smith's genuine insights about the division of labor and exchange. (011-002)

A key implication of List's conception was methodological: political economy cannot be a deductive science derived from axioms about human nature, as the classical school attempted. It must be an inductive and historical science, reasoning from the actual experience of nations across different epochs and stages of development. (016-001)

## Historical Examples

List grounded his conception of political economy in the historical record of nations that had successfully developed their productive powers:

- **England** practiced political economy for centuries before the science existed. From Edward III onward, English statesmen pursued a coherent strategy of protecting domestic manufacturing, restricting raw material exports, and using navigation laws to build national carrying trade. This "practice of political economy which was exercised by the State authorities" was the true source of English prosperity—not the abstract principles later articulated by Smith. (011-001)

- **Venice** demonstrated that political economy requires adaptation to changing circumstances. Venice's early success came from a national commercial policy suited to its position as a maritime republic. Its decline came when it failed to adapt this policy to the rise of competing Atlantic powers. (001-006)

- **France under Colbert** showed the transformative power of a nationally oriented political economy. Colbert's system was imperfect—it overemphasized manufactures at the expense of agriculture—but it represented a genuine attempt to apply political economy to national development. (006-005)

- **Germany** represented the negative case: a nation without a unified political economy, divided into dozens of customs territories, each pursuing its own narrow interests. The Zollverein was, for List, the first German experiment in deliberate national political economy. (034-010)
