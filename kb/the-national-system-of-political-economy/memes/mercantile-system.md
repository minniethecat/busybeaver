## Definition

The mercantile system is the body of commercial policy doctrines and practices that prevailed in Europe from approximately the sixteenth through the eighteenth centuries. Its core principle held that a nation's economic policy should be directed toward maximising exports and minimising imports, thereby accumulating precious metals and achieving a favourable balance of trade. The classical economists from Adam Smith onwards subjected the mercantile system to comprehensive theoretical refutation, treating it as a tissue of fallacies built on the confusion of money with wealth. List, while not endorsing the mercantile system in its entirety, argued that the classical condemnation was excessive and that the mercantile system had played an indispensable role in the industrial development of the nations—above all England—that later became its harshest critics. (023-009)

## In List's System

The mercantile system occupies an ambivalent position in List's national system. On one hand, List agreed with the classical economists that the mercantile doctrine of identifying wealth with precious metals was theoretically mistaken. On the other hand, he argued that the classical school had thrown out the baby with the bathwater: in refuting the theoretical errors of mercantilism, they had also dismissed the practical policies—protective tariffs, navigation laws, the deliberate nurturing of manufactures—that had actually built the industrial power of nations like England. (023-012)

List's rehabilitation of the mercantile system was not theoretical but historical. He judged it not by the logical consistency of its doctrines but by its practical results. By that standard, the mercantile system had been a spectacular success: it had transformed England from a raw-material exporter into the world's dominant manufacturing power. The classical economists' condemnation of mercantilism was, in List's view, a form of ingratitude—or worse, a deliberate attempt to kick away the ladder by which England had climbed to industrial supremacy, preventing other nations from following the same path. (004-005)

## List's Argument

List developed his qualified defence of the mercantile system across several dimensions:

**The mercantile system as industrial policy.** List argued that whatever its theoretical errors, the mercantile system functioned in practice as a system of industrial promotion. By restricting imports of manufactured goods and encouraging exports, it created the protected space within which infant industries could develop. The principle of the mercantile system, List wrote, "in any case would have been much nearer the truth than that of the popular school." (004-005) The mercantilists may have been wrong about why protection worked—attributing its benefits to the accumulation of specie rather than to the development of productive powers—but they were right that it did work:

> It would be of course false to trace back to the errors of the so-called mercantile system the extraordinary progress which England has made. But it would be equally false to deny that the mercantile system, whatever its theoretical errors, contained a practical wisdom that the cosmopolitan system of Adam Smith entirely lacks. (023-009)

**The historical indispensability of mercantilism.** List argued that the industrial development of England would not have occurred without the mercantile system. Beginning under Edward III and continuing through Elizabeth I, James I, and beyond, England systematically restricted the export of raw materials, prohibited or taxed the import of manufactured goods, and attracted foreign artisans to establish domestic manufactures. These were mercantilist policies, and they were essential:

> England has been indebted for her manufacturing industry not to the cosmopolitan but to the mercantile system. (009-006)

The classical economists, writing after England had already achieved manufacturing supremacy, could afford to dismiss the mercantile system as a historical error. But for nations still at earlier stages of development—as Germany was in List's time—the practical wisdom embedded in the mercantile system remained directly relevant.

**The cosmopolitan system as mercantilism in disguise.** List's most provocative argument was that the classical school's "cosmopolitan system" of free trade was itself a form of mercantilism—the mercantilism of the dominant power. By preaching free trade to the world while having built its own manufacturing supremacy through centuries of protection, England was practising a kind of imperial mercantilism:

> The strictest and most consistent 'mercantile system' is, and it is incomprehensible how a nation which has become powerful through the mercantile system can expect other nations to adopt the cosmopolitan system. (031-004)

**The theoretical value of the mercantile system.** List did not advocate a return to the crude doctrines of the mercantile writers. He acknowledged their theoretical errors—the confusion of money with wealth, the belief that the gains of trade were zero-sum, the excessive focus on the balance of trade as an end in itself. But he argued that the classical economists had failed to extract the valid practical insights embedded in mercantilist thought. The mercantile system had correctly understood that national economic development requires deliberate state action, that infant industries need protection, and that the structure of a nation's foreign trade affects its internal productive development. These were truths that classical political economy, in its cosmopolitan abstraction, had lost sight of. (032-002) (032-005)

## Historical Examples

- **England**: List's central case study. From Edward III (fourteenth century) through the reign of George I (eighteenth century), England systematically applied mercantilist policies: prohibiting the export of raw wool, attracting Flemish weavers, taxing or prohibiting the import of manufactured goods, and using the Navigation Acts to monopolise carrying trade. These policies built the manufacturing base that made England the workshop of the world. When England later advocated free trade, it was because its manufacturing supremacy was so complete that free trade served its interest—not because the mercantile system had been a mistake. (004-005) (009-006)

- **The Hanseatic League**: List treated the Hanseatic League's commercial system as a precursor to mercantilism—a system that understood the value of commercial monopoly but failed to develop the manufacturing base and political unity that would have made its commercial power durable. The Hanseatic cities practised a form of mercantilism without manufactures, and the result was commercial wealth without productive power. (004-010)

- **France under Colbert**: Colbert's mercantilist policies in seventeenth-century France represented a conscious attempt to replicate England's industrial development. List acknowledged that Colbert's system had significant successes in establishing French manufactures, though it was ultimately undermined by the revocation of the Edict of Nantes and the expulsion of Huguenot artisans—a self-inflicted wound that demonstrated the dependence of mercantilist development on the attraction and retention of skilled labour. (004-005)

- **Germany and the Zollverein**: List presented the Zollverein's protective tariff as a modernised, theoretically refined version of the mercantile system—one that had shed the theoretical errors (the identification of wealth with money) while retaining the practical wisdom (the necessity of protecting infant industry). (035-004)

## Contrast with Classical Economics

The classical economists' critique of the mercantile system was comprehensive and, in List's view, excessive. Adam Smith devoted a substantial portion of *The Wealth of Nations* to refuting mercantilist doctrines—the confusion of money with wealth, the obsession with the balance of trade, the zero-sum conception of international commerce. David Hume's price-specie flow mechanism demonstrated the logical impossibility of permanently accumulating precious metals through trade surpluses.

List did not dispute the logical validity of these critiques as abstract theory. What he disputed was their practical relevance. The classical refutation of mercantilism served, in practice, as an argument against protective tariffs—as if the refutation of the mercantile theory of money automatically refuted the case for protecting infant industries. It did not. One could accept that the mercantilists were wrong about why protection works while insisting that they were right that it does work:

> He who would reform the (so-called) mercantile system is himself nothing else than a reformer of the mercantile system. (032-002)

This was List's characteristically dialectical conclusion: the debate between the mercantile system and free trade was a false antithesis. The true position—List's position—was a reformed mercantilism: a system that protected infant industries for the purpose of developing productive powers, without the theoretical errors of the original mercantilist doctrine.
