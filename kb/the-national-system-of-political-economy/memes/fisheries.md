# Fisheries

## Definition

**Fisheries** — particularly coast and deep-sea fisheries — occupy a foundational role in Friedrich List's historical analysis of national economic development. List saw prosperous fisheries not merely as a source of food, but as a nursery for maritime power: "Prosperous coast fisheries served everywhere as nurseries for the mercantile marine." (001-001)

In List's framework, fisheries were the first rung on the ladder from agricultural to commercial and manufacturing power. They created the seafaring population, shipbuilding industry, and maritime infrastructure upon which larger commercial empires were built.

## In List's System

List identified fisheries as a critical early-stage productive power for maritime nations:

1. **Naval nursery**: Fisheries trained generations of seamen who would later serve in both merchant shipping and naval warfare. This human capital — practical maritime skill — could not be acquired through any other means.

2. **Economic catalyst**: The fisheries required shipbuilding, net-making, salt production, and barrel-making — each a small manufacturing industry that contributed to the broader development of productive powers.

3. **Trade foundation**: Fish products — particularly salted herring and whale oil — were among the earliest bulk commodities traded internationally, creating the first accumulation of commercial capital.

## Historical Examples

List traced the role of fisheries across every major maritime power:

- **Ancient Rome**: Even the Romans recognized the connection between coast fisheries and naval strength. (001-001)

- **The Hanseatic League**: The League's commercial empire was built partly on herring fisheries in the Baltic and North Sea. The Hansards traded salted fish across Europe, accumulating the capital that funded their wider commercial operations. (002-002, 002-004)

- **Holland**: The Dutch Republic's rise to commercial supremacy in the 17th century was intimately tied to its herring and whale fisheries. Dutch fishermen dominated the North Sea herring grounds, and "England's herring and whale fisheries, which the Dutch had previously monopolized" became a point of national competition. (004-006)

- **England**: Recognizing the strategic importance of fisheries, England systematically developed its own fishing fleets and passed laws to encourage domestic fisheries over foreign competition. The fisheries, along with the coasting trade, formed the foundation of England's merchant marine. (004-003, 004-005)

- **Venice and the Italian Republics**: Early Italian maritime power also rested on fisheries, combined with dairy production and other agricultural exports. (003-002)

## Strategic Importance

List argued that fisheries had several unique economic properties:

- **Capital accumulation**: Unlike agriculture, which was limited by land availability, fisheries offered virtually unlimited potential for expansion, limited only by shipping capacity and market demand.

- **Risk-taking culture**: Fishermen developed the skills and temperament for long-distance commerce — navigation, weather-reading, risk management — that directly transferred to merchant shipping.

- **Shipbuilding synergy**: The demand for fishing vessels supported shipyards, which in turn enabled naval construction. "Both the coasting trade and the fisheries constituted the foundation of naval power." (004-003)

- **Food security**: Fisheries provided a critical protein source independent of agricultural land, enabling higher population densities in coastal trading cities.
