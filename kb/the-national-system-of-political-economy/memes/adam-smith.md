# Adam Smith

Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish moral philosopher and economist, widely regarded as the founder of modern economics.

Educated at the University of Glasgow and Balliol College, Oxford, he served as Professor of Moral Philosophy at Glasgow (1751–1764) before publishing his two major works: *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759) and *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776).

*The Wealth of Nations* established the framework of classical political economy: the [[Division of Labor]] as the source of productivity, the invisible hand of market competition, and the case for free trade based on comparative advantage. Smith's system treated the economy as a self-regulating mechanism in which individual self-interest produces outcomes beneficial to society.

## Role in "The National System"

Adam Smith is the single most important intellectual opponent in [[Friedrich List]]'s *National System of Political Economy*. List did not reject Smith wholesale — he acknowledged Smith's genius and borrowed freely from his analysis. But List identified a fundamental error in Smith's system that made it unsuitable as a guide for national economic policy.

List's critique of Smith operates on three levels:

| Level | Smith's Position | List's Counter-Argument |
|-------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Method** | Cosmopolitical: analyzes the economy of the entire human race as if nations did not exist | National: the world is divided into competing nations; economic analysis must start from this fact |
| **Value theory** | Exchange values: the wealth of nations consists of the sum of exchangeable goods | [[Productive Powers]]: the *capacity to produce* wealth matters more than the stock of wealth itself |
| **Policy** | Free trade is universally beneficial; protection is always harmful | Free trade benefits all only when nations are at comparable development stages; infant industries need temporary protection |

List summarized his objection: Smith's system is "a cosmopolitical economy, not a political economy — it assumes a universal republic, not a world of competing nations" (P01-002).

## The Productive Powers Critique

List's most original criticism of Smith concerns the theory of value. Smith, following the physiocrats, defined national wealth as the sum total of exchangeable goods and services produced in a country. List argued that this misses what truly matters: the *capacity* to produce — what he called [[Productive Powers]].

> Adam Smith had included under the fixed capital of a nation not only material goods but also the acquired abilities of all inhabitants — yet he failed to develop this insight into a systematic theory of productive powers. (P03-006)

List's analogy became famous: a father who spends his savings on his son's education reduces exchangeable wealth in the short term but vastly increases the son's productive powers.

A nation that sacrifices exchange value today (through protective tariffs) to build its [[Manufacturing Power]] does the same at national scale. Smith's value theory, List argued, cannot capture this trade-off.

## The Nationality Gap

List's most fundamental objection to Smith was that Smith's system has no place for *nationality*. It analyzes "the economy" as if humanity were a single community — what List called "cosmopolitical economy."

The reality, List argued, is that the world is organized into competing nation-states. Each nation has its own history, institutions, level of development, and interests. An economic doctrine that ignores these differences — that prescribes the same policy (free trade) to England (the manufacturing superpower) and Germany (still building its industry) — serves the strong against the weak.

> It is Adam Smith who wants to introduce the principle of free trade as if nations did not exist. (P03-001)

## Historical Context

Smith wrote *The Wealth of Nations* in 1776, when Britain was already the world's leading manufacturing nation. His free trade doctrine, List noted, served British interests perfectly: it persuaded potential competitors to open their markets to British manufactures while discouraging them from adopting the protective policies Britain itself had used to build its industry.

List charged that Smith's English followers — [[David Ricardo]], [[Thomas Malthus]], [[Jean-Baptiste Say]] — turned Smith's analytical framework into an ideology that justified British manufacturing supremacy as a natural outcome of market forces rather than the product of centuries of deliberate national policy.

> The cosmopolitical theory of Adam Smith and J. B. Say, in its political application, is nothing but a continuation of the commercial policy of England under a scientific guise. (P04-006)
