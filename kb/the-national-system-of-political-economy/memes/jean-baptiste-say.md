# Jean-Baptiste Say

Jean-Baptiste Say (1767–1832) was a French economist and businessman who popularized [[Adam Smith]]'s ideas in continental Europe.

His *Traité d'économie politique* (*Treatise on Political Economy*, 1803) became the standard economics textbook across Europe for much of the 19th century. He held the first chair of political economy at the Collège de France.

Say is best known for "Say's Law" — the proposition that supply creates its own demand. General overproduction (gluts) is impossible, he argued, because the production of goods simultaneously creates the purchasing power to buy them. This doctrine was central to classical economics' faith in market self-regulation.

## Role in "The National System"

Say occupies a secondary but significant position in [[Friedrich List]]'s critique. If [[Adam Smith]] was the architect of the cosmopolitical system, Say was its most effective popularizer — the writer who brought Smith's doctrine to French and German readers in accessible form.

List's criticisms of Say fall into three categories:

| Critique | Say's Position | List's Response |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Cosmopolitical method** | Economics applies universally, regardless of nation | National differences are the starting point of economic analysis |
| **Free trade absolutism** | "We should buy from abroad whatever costs less than making it ourselves" | This ignores the productive powers lost when manufacturing capacity is sacrificed |
| **Theory of values** | Exchange value is the measure of wealth | [[Productive Powers]] — the capacity to produce — matters more than current exchange value |

> J. B. Say openly demands that we should buy from abroad whatever costs less than making it ourselves. (011-002)

List's objection: this principle, applied consistently, would keep Germany an agricultural exporter and raw material supplier indefinitely. It is sound logic within a cosmopolitical framework — but pernicious advice for a nation seeking to develop its manufacturing capacity.

## Say's Law and Productive Powers

List's most penetrating criticism of Say concerns Say's Law. If supply creates its own demand, then the economic disruptions caused by sudden free trade — factory closures, unemployment, the destruction of infant industries — are temporary adjustment problems that markets will resolve.

List rejected this. The destruction of manufacturing capacity is not self-correcting because [[Productive Powers]] are cumulative. Skills, institutions, supply chains, and technical knowledge built over generations can be destroyed in a few years of unprotected competition. Once lost, they must be rebuilt from scratch — at far greater cost than maintaining them through temporary protection.

> The theories of Adam Smith and J. B. Say, the Theory of Values — for, as we shall show in the course of this treatise, this theory fails to account for the productive powers of nations. (004-007)

## The French Context

Say's influence in France gave List's critique particular urgency. France, unlike England, was not yet a fully mature manufacturing power. French policymakers who adopted Say's free-trade doctrine were, in List's analysis, acting against their own national interest.

List noted a paradox: French statesmen under [[Napoleon Bonaparte|Napoleon]] had pursued aggressive industrial policy (the [[Continental System]]), while French economists under Say's influence advocated the opposite. The economists won the intellectual battle — and List believed France's industrial development suffered as a result.

> Monsieur J. B. Say, Adam Smith's most famous pupil, hailed the announcement of the Continental System's end as a triumph of economic science over political folly. (006-006)
