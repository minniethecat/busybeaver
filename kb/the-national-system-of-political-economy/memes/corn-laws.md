## Definition

The Corn Laws were a series of English statutes, reaching their most restrictive form in the early nineteenth century, that imposed tariffs and import restrictions on foreign grain. Their purpose was to protect English agriculture—and the rents of English landowners—from foreign competition. In List's political economy, the Corn Laws served as a case study in the complex relationship between agricultural protection and manufacturing development. (009-004)

## In List's System

The Corn Laws occupy an ambivalent position in List's analysis. On one hand, they exemplified the protective principle that List advocated: a nation using tariffs to defend a domestic industry from foreign competition. On the other hand, they demonstrated the potential for protection to serve narrow class interests rather than national development. List used the Corn Laws to distinguish between protective tariffs that foster manufacturing—which he endorsed—and protective tariffs that merely sustain agricultural rents—which he viewed with skepticism. (018-005)

The Corn Laws were particularly instructive because they created a conflict between two domestic interests: agriculture and manufacturing. The manufacturing interest opposed the Corn Laws because they raised the price of food and thus the cost of labor; the agricultural interest supported them for precisely the same reason. List saw in this conflict a microcosm of the broader tension between the agricultural and manufacturing stages of national development. (034-006)

## List's Argument

List argued that the Corn Laws were a double-edged sword for England. They protected English agriculture and preserved the social position of the landed aristocracy, but they also raised the cost of subsistence for manufacturing workers, potentially undermining the competitiveness of English industry. The Corn Laws, in List's analysis, represented the persistence of agricultural interests in a nation that had already become a manufacturing power—a political and economic contradiction that would eventually have to be resolved.

> Through the Corn Laws, no doubt, a quantity of unfed land was brought into cultivation. (018-005)

The eventual abolition of the Corn Laws in 1846 confirmed List's analysis: as manufacturing interests gained political power relative to agricultural interests, the protection of agriculture became politically unsustainable. But List warned that the abolition of the Corn Laws would have consequences beyond England's borders. By opening the English market to foreign grain, repeal would increase the dependence of agricultural nations on the English market, reinforcing the very pattern of agricultural-manufacturing dependency that List sought to break. (034-011)

For Germany, List drew a clear lesson: protective tariffs should be designed to foster manufacturing, not to preserve agricultural rents. The Corn Laws showed what happened when protection served the wrong interests—it became a source of internal conflict rather than national development. (034-007)

## Historical Examples

- **The Corn Laws and English Agriculture**: The Corn Laws encouraged the cultivation of marginal land in England, bringing "a quantity of unfed land" into production. But this benefit came at a cost: higher food prices for manufacturing workers, which created pressure for higher wages and reduced the competitiveness of English exports. (018-005)

- **The Corn Laws and American Development**: List observed that the Corn Laws had the paradoxical effect of encouraging American manufacturing. By restricting the American grain exports to England, the Corn Laws pushed the United States toward developing its own manufacturing industry as an alternative outlet for its productive energies. (033-011)

- **The Repeal and Its Consequences**: The abolition of the Corn Laws in 1846 was, List predicted, a watershed moment. It would deepen the dependence of agricultural nations on the English market while removing one of the last protections for English agriculture itself. The repeal reflected the triumph of manufacturing interests over agricultural interests within England—a shift that List saw as inevitable but whose international consequences he regarded with concern. (034-010)

- **Germany and the Corn Law Lesson**: List argued that Germany should learn from England's experience: protection should be calibrated to serve national development rather than class interests, and the transition from agricultural to manufacturing protection should be managed deliberately rather than left to political conflict. (034-007)
