## Definition

National wealth is the total stock of material and immaterial resources—land, capital, labour, institutions, skills, and productive capacities—that a nation possesses and can draw upon for its continued existence and development. In List's framework, national wealth is not simply the sum of the private wealth of individual citizens, nor is it reducible to the aggregate of exchangeable values in the national territory. It includes the productive powers, mental capital, social institutions, and political arrangements that enable a nation to generate future wealth—causes of wealth that are, as List insisted, "something totally different from wealth itself." (012-003)

List's most important theoretical contribution to the concept of national wealth was his insistence that a nation can increase its stock of wealth while diminishing its capacity to produce wealth, or vice versa. A nation that sells off its manufacturing base in exchange for cheap foreign goods may temporarily increase its consumable wealth while destroying the productive powers that are the true source of national wealth. (012-003)

## In List's System

National wealth is the central object of List's political economy—but with a crucial qualification. Where Adam Smith titled his great work *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*, List argued that Smith and his successors had inquired only into the nature of wealth, neglecting the causes. The classical economists had treated wealth as an accumulation of exchangeable values, ignoring the productive powers—the skills, institutions, technologies, and social arrangements—that alone make the creation of wealth possible. (032-001)

List positioned his concept of national wealth at the intersection of economics and statecraft. A nation's wealth is not merely an economic magnitude to be maximised; it is the material foundation of national existence, independence, power, and culture. The measures that increase a nation's exchangeable wealth in the short term may undermine its capacity to generate wealth in the long term—as when free trade destroys infant industries that would, if protected, eventually produce far more wealth than the temporary consumer surplus gained from cheap imports.

This perspective led List to reconceptualise the relationship between private wealth and national wealth. What is rational for an individual merchant—buying where goods are cheapest—may be irrational for a nation, because the merchant's calculation does not include the effect of his purchasing decision on the nation's productive powers. A nation that consistently buys foreign manufactures because they are cheaper than domestic ones will, over time, lose the manufacturing capacity that is itself a form of national wealth far greater than the savings on individual purchases. (019-009)

## List's Argument

List developed his theory of national wealth through a sustained critique of the classical treatment and an original constructive account.

**The critique of classical value theory.** List argued that Adam Smith, J.B. Say, and their followers had mistaken the nature of the investigation. By defining wealth exclusively in terms of exchangeable values, they had excluded from their analysis the very factors that determine whether a nation grows richer or poorer over time:

> Say found himself opposed to those who consider the productive powers of the nation, apart from the possession of exchangeable values, as national wealth. (032-001)

What Say dismissed as a conceptual confusion was, in List's view, the essential insight that classical political economy had lost. A nation's productive powers—its manufacturing capacity, its skilled workforce, its transport infrastructure, its legal and political institutions—are themselves forms of wealth, and forms more important than any particular stock of exchangeable goods. A merchant who owns a warehouse full of grain is wealthy; a nation that possesses the capacity to produce grain indefinitely, in any quantity, is wealthy in a more fundamental sense.

**The causes of wealth are different from wealth.** List's most famous formulation of this argument appears early in *The National System*:

> The causes of wealth are something totally different from wealth itself. A person may possess wealth, i.e. exchangeable value; but if he does not possess the power of producing objects of more value than he consumes, he will become poorer. A person may be poor, but if he possesses the power of producing objects of greater value than he consumes, he will become rich. (012-003)

Applied to nations, this principle yields a radically different policy calculus from that of classical free trade. A nation may temporarily increase its stock of consumable wealth by importing cheap foreign manufactures, but if doing so destroys its own manufacturing power—one of the primary causes of national wealth—it will ultimately become poorer. Conversely, a nation that temporarily sacrifices consumable wealth by paying higher prices for protected domestic manufactures may, by building up its productive powers, become vastly wealthier in the long run. (010-005)

**The composition of national wealth.** List provided an empirical illustration of his theory using English data. The English national wealth, he noted, was composed overwhelmingly of agricultural capital—"only about 1/6 of the English national wealth" consisted of manufacturing and commercial capital. Yet it was this small fraction that generated the immense agricultural prosperity of England:

> It is those 218 millions of manufacturing capital which have principally called into existence those 3,311 millions of agricultural capital. (020-013)

This was List's empirical demonstration that the composition of national wealth matters more than its quantity. The small stock of manufacturing capital was the generative force behind the much larger stock of agricultural capital. One part of the national wealth was the cause of all the rest. (020-003) (020-004)

**Fertility of the soil is not enough.** List explicitly rejected the claim, common among classical economists, that the fertility of the soil is the primary source of a nation's wealth:

> The fertility of the soil is, according to him, the chief source of national wealth. (028-004)

He pointed to Poland, with its rich agricultural land, as a counter-example: a nation with fertile soil but no manufacturing power remained poor and politically weak. Conversely, nations with relatively poor soil—such as parts of Germany—could become wealthy through the development of manufacturing and the productive powers that manufacturing brings. (028-004) (029-002)

**National wealth as a policy objective.** List concluded that the maximisation of national wealth, properly understood, must be the guiding objective of commercial policy. But this objective cannot be pursued through the simple maxim of buying cheap and selling dear. It requires the deliberate cultivation of productive powers through protective tariffs, infrastructure investment, educational institutions, and the legal and political arrangements that enable manufacturing to develop:

> The augmentation of the national wealth is dependent on the augmentation of the manufacturing capital; and (inversely) the latter on the former. (019-006)

## Historical Examples

- **England**: List's primary example of successful national wealth accumulation. England built its national wealth not through free trade but through centuries of deliberate protection, the attraction of foreign manufacturing skill, and the systematic development of productive powers. By the time England began advocating free trade, it had already accumulated the manufacturing supremacy that made free trade advantageous. (004-004) (037-004)

- **Poland**: List's counter-example of a nation that, despite fertile soil and abundant natural resources, failed to develop national wealth because it failed to develop manufacturing. Polish nobles exported raw materials and imported foreign manufactures, and the result was not prosperity but national extinction. (018-008)

- **Italy**: Venice and the Italian republics accumulated commercial wealth through trade but failed to develop the manufacturing power and national unity that would have made that wealth durable. When the trade routes shifted, their commercial wealth evaporated, demonstrating that wealth without productive power is precarious. (004-010)

- **The United States**: List cited estimates of American national wealth and income to demonstrate the transformative effect of manufacturing development. The growth of American manufactures under the protective tariff of 1828 produced a rapid increase in national wealth that far exceeded what agriculture alone could have achieved. (037-004)
