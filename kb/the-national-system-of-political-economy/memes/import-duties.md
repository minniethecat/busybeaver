# Import Duties

## Definition

**Import duties** in Friedrich List's *National System of Political Economy* (1841) are taxes levied on foreign goods entering a nation's territory, serving a dual purpose: as instruments of industrial protection and as sources of government revenue. List treated import duties not as an end in themselves but as a calibrated policy tool whose level and scope must be adjusted according to the manufacturing development stage of the nation imposing them.

> It increases or lessens import duties according to circumstances; it grants premiums, advances loans, and undertakes works which private capital would be unable to carry out alone. (033-009)

List distinguished sharply between *revenue duties* — low, uniform tariffs designed only to raise government income — and *protective duties* — graduated tariffs designed to nurture domestic industry by raising the price of competing foreign goods.

## In List's System

Import duties are the central policy instrument of List's protective system. They are not, however, a universal or permanent measure. List insisted that import duties should be:

**Moderate**: List warned against prohibitory duties that eliminate all foreign competition. Such duties breed complacency and inefficiency in domestic industry. The correct level, he argued, is one that enables domestic manufacturers to compete while maintaining sufficient competitive pressure to drive improvement. (026-002)

**Graduated by industry**: Different industries require different levels of protection. Raw materials (coal, wool, corn, cattle) should face only modest import duties, because over-protecting inputs raises costs for downstream manufacturing. (026-003) Manufactured goods, particularly those in early stages of development, warrant higher duties.

**Scaled by development stage**: As domestic industry matures, import duties should be gradually reduced. The goal is not permanent protection but the development of competitive capacity. "The scale by which the import duties are increased should be determined beforehand," List argued, so that industry has a predictable path toward open competition. (026-004)

**Reciprocal in spirit**: Import duties are a legitimate response to the protectionism of more advanced nations. When England protects its own industry while preaching free trade to others, List charged, the continental nations have every right to respond with their own import duties to level the playing field. (034-013)

## List's Argument

List's case for import duties rests on his developmental theory of national economies. He identified several distinct arguments:

**The infant industry argument**: In the early stages of manufacturing development, domestic industry cannot survive against the competition of more advanced nations with lower production costs. Even moderate import duties may be insufficient to enable domestic manufacturers to meet this competition. (026-006) The state must therefore calibrate duties to the specific needs of developing industries.

**The strategic argument**: Import duties are not merely economic tools but instruments of national power. A nation that allows its industries to be destroyed by foreign competition sacrifices not only its present wealth but its future productive capacity. The temporary higher prices paid by consumers are an investment in national productive power that will pay dividends for generations.

**The fiscal argument**: Beyond protection, import duties provide government revenue. List was pragmatic about this: he acknowledged that revenue from import duties could fund infrastructure, education, and other investments in productive power. However, he insisted that revenue should be a secondary consideration, never the primary purpose of the duty structure.

**The empirical evidence**: List pointed to the historical experience of nations that had successfully employed import duties to build industrial power. England raised import duties on manufactured goods while reducing them on raw materials throughout its rise to industrial dominance. (036-008) The United States, List argued, had followed a similar path. Free trade, in this light, was a policy that served the interests of the already-industrialized, not a universal principle.

## Historical Examples

List drew extensively on historical cases to support his theory of import duties:

- **England under Edward III and Elizabeth I** (Chapter IV): England's early protective system relied heavily on import duties on woolen manufactures. These duties were calibrated to exclude Flemish and French competition from the domestic market while allowing English manufacturers to develop their skills. The result was the transformation of England from a raw wool exporter into the world's leading producer of manufactured woolens.

- **England's grain duties and the Corn Laws debate**: List discussed the political struggle over import duties on grain between English landowners (who favored high duties to protect agricultural rents) and manufacturers (who favored low duties to reduce the cost of labor). This internal debate, List argued, demonstrated that import duties must be calibrated across sectors in a coherent national strategy. (034-005)

- **France under Colbert** (Chapter VI): Colbert's *dirigiste* industrial policy used import duties as one instrument among many — alongside state subsidies, quality regulations, and the attraction of skilled foreign artisans. The French case showed both the potential and the limits of import duties as a development tool.

- **The German Zollverein** (Chapter VIII): The Prussian-led customs union harmonized import duties across the German states, creating a protected internal market. List saw this as the essential precondition for German industrial development. The Zollverein's import duty structure was designed to protect German manufacturing while allowing the free circulation of goods *within* the union.

- **The United States**: List praised the American protective tariff of 1828 as a model of calibrated protection. By imposing duties on manufactured imports while keeping raw material duties low, the United States followed the same developmental path that England had pioneered.
