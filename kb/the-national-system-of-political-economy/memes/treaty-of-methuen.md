# The Treaty of Methuen

## Overview

The 1703 trade treaty between England and Portugal, which List cited as the classic example of a free-trade agreement that permanently subordinated the weaker party to raw material production.

The Treaty of Methuen (1703), negotiated between England and Portugal, permitted English woolen goods to enter Portugal duty-free in exchange for Portuguese wines (particularly port) entering England at reduced duties. It is one of the most famous trade treaties in economic history and List's paradigmatic example of a free-trade agreement that permanently subordinates the weaker party.

List's analysis was devastating: Portugal, by opening its market to English woolens without protecting its own nascent textile industry, abandoned manufacturing entirely. It became a raw-material exporter (wine, cork, olive oil) dependent on English manufactured goods. England, by contrast, gained a captive export market that stimulated its woolen industry and a source of cheap wine for its growing urban population.

The Treaty of Methuen was "celebrated by free traders as a model of mutually beneficial commerce" but in List's analysis, it was a catastrophe for Portugal. Two centuries later, Portugal remained one of the least industrialized nations in Western Europe — a direct consequence, List argued, of the structural dependence created by the 1703 treaty.
