# The Mercantile System

## Overview

The Mercantile System was the dominant economic doctrine and policy framework of European states from approximately the 16th through the late 18th centuries. It emphasized the accumulation of national wealth through trade surpluses (importing gold, exporting goods), state regulation of commerce, protective tariffs, and colonial monopolies.

List carefully distinguished his own system from mercantilism, even as he acknowledged its historical contributions. The mercantile system was correct in its *national* orientation — it recognized that nations compete economically and that state policy matters — but wrong in its *theoretical* foundations: it confused money (gold) with wealth, it overregulated the internal economy, and it lacked a coherent theory of productive powers.

## Role in List's Analysis

List's relationship with the mercantile system was complex:

- **What List borrowed**: The mercantile emphasis on national economic interest, the use of protective tariffs, the recognition that manufacturing matters for national power — all of these anticipated List's system. Colbert's France and Cromwell's England were, in List's view, practicing a crude but directionally correct form of economic nationalism.

- **What List rejected**: The mercantile obsession with gold accumulation, the zero-sum assumption (one nation's gain is another's loss), the stifling of internal competition, and the absence of a theory of productive development. List's system was developmental and dynamic; mercantilism was static and acquisitive.

- **The historical transition**: List argued that Adam Smith's critique of mercantilism had been too sweeping — in rejecting mercantilism's errors, Smith had also rejected its correct insight: that national economic policy must serve national productive development. List aimed to recover the mercantile insight within a sound theoretical framework.
