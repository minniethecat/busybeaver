# Customs Union

## Definition

A **Customs Union** (*Zollverein* in German) is an agreement among independent political entities — typically neighboring states or principalities — to eliminate internal tariffs and trade barriers while maintaining a common external tariff toward non-member nations. Friedrich List championed the customs union as the essential first step toward German national economic unification, and more broadly as the optimal mechanism for medium-sized nations to achieve the scale necessary for manufacturing development.

List argued that a customs union creates the largest possible protected market without requiring immediate political unification — it achieves economic integration first, with political union to follow. (007-011)

## In List's System

The customs union occupies a strategic position in List's policy framework. It solves a practical problem: how can the German states, individually too small to support competitive manufacturing, collectively build industrial capacity?

List identified several advantages of the customs union:

- **Market scale**: A customs union of multiple states creates a market large enough to support efficient manufacturing. Individual German principalities were too small; the Zollverein created a market comparable to France or England.

- **Internal competition with external protection**: Within the union, competition drives efficiency. Against external competitors (primarily England), the common tariff provides protection. This combines the benefits of competition with the necessity of protection.

- **Graduated path to political unity**: Economic integration creates material incentives for political cooperation. "Germany is associated in a single customs union, which has abolished the separate tariff systems." (007-011) The commercial union preceded and enabled the political unification of 1871.

## Historical Example: The German Zollverein

The Zollverein (1834) was the practical realization of List's vision. Beginning with Prussia's internal tariff reform (1818) and expanding through treaties with neighboring states, it eventually encompassed most of the German Confederation. By eliminating internal tolls and establishing a common external tariff, the Zollverein:

- Created a protected market of ~25 million consumers
- Stimulated German railway construction (goods needed to move within the enlarged market)
- Enabled German manufacturing to compete with British imports
- Laid the economic foundation for the political unification of Germany in 1871

List actively campaigned for the Zollverein through his journalism and political advocacy, making him one of its most influential intellectual champions.
