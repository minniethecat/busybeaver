# England (as Economic Unit)

England is the central empirical case in [[Friedrich List]]'s *National System of Political Economy*.

It is the nation that most fully exemplifies the power of deliberate national economic policy.

From [[Edward III of England|Edward III]] through [[Elizabeth I of England|Elizabeth I]], through Cromwell's [[Navigation Acts]] to the Industrial Revolution — England built its supremacy over four centuries of continuous industrial policy.

List's conclusion: England achieved supremacy not through free trade but through protection — and only advocated free trade *after* its manufacturing superiority was unassailable.

> England by her political power gained productive power, and by her productive power gained political power. (004-010)

## The Four-Century Strategy

List identified a coherent national strategy operating across English history, despite changes in dynasty and political regime:

| Century | Key Policy | Architect | Outcome |
|---------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **14th** | Import Flemish weavers, ban raw wool export | Edward III | Woolen manufacturing established |
| **16th** | Expel Hanseatic merchants, protect woolens | Elizabeth I | Domestic manufacturing consolidated |
| **17th** | Navigation Acts, colonial monopoly | Cromwell | Maritime and commercial supremacy |
| **18th** | Protective tariffs + technology acquisition | Walpole, Pitt | Industrial Revolution |
| **19th** | Advocate free trade abroad | Huskisson, Peel | Lock in manufacturing advantage |

This was not laissez-faire. It was deliberate, sustained, nationally-oriented economic policy — precisely what List advocated for Germany.

> The English nation had understood perfectly well that manufacturing power is the mother of all other powers. (P04-006)

## Insular Supremacy

England's geography reinforced its policy. As an island, England could defend itself with a navy rather than a standing army. The navy, in turn, protected trade routes and colonial possessions, which provided raw materials and captive markets for English manufactures.

This created what List called **"insular supremacy"** — a self-reinforcing cycle:

Manufacturing → demand for shipping → merchant marine → naval power → colonial expansion → raw materials + markets → manufacturing growth

No continental nation could replicate this exact path, but the principle — that policy creates power, and power reinforces policy — was universal. [[Germany]] needed different instruments (the [[Zollverein]], railways, commercial treaties) but the same strategic logic.

## The Free Trade Pivot

List's most provocative argument about England concerns timing. England protected its manufacturing for four centuries — and only began advocating free trade in the 19th century, after the Industrial Revolution had made English industry the world's most efficient.

This was not hypocrisy, List argued. It was rational national interest. England's statesmen understood that free trade benefits the strongest producer. Adam Smith and his followers provided the intellectual framework that made this national interest appear as universal economic science.

> The cosmopolitical theory of Adam Smith and J. B. Say, in its political application, is nothing but a continuation of the commercial policy of England under a scientific guise. (P04-006)

List urged German readers to see through the scientific veneer: when the world's dominant manufacturing power tells developing nations to adopt free trade, it is acting in its own interest, not theirs. The same England that preached free trade to Germany had built its own industry behind protective walls.
