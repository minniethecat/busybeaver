## Definition

Liberty, in List's political economy, is not a luxury purchased by wealth but a productive force that generates it. Civil and political liberty — the freedom of person from arbitrary coercion, the freedom of thought and conscience, the right to participate in government — are institutional conditions that enable the full development of human productive powers. List treats liberty as what modern economists would call an institutional precondition for growth: it channels individual energy toward productive activity rather than toward defence against arbitrary power, and it creates a citizenry capable of sustaining the long-term national projects that build prosperity. (012-004)

List distinguishes liberty from mere absence of restraint. Real liberty requires positive institutions — municipal self-government, representative assemblies, legal protection of person and property — that collectively constitute what List calls "municipal liberty" when embodied in town charters and civic institutions. (010-001) This distinction is central to his historical analysis: nations that achieved the institutional forms of liberty prospered; those that did not stagnated regardless of their natural endowments.

## In List's System

Liberty occupies a foundational layer in List's hierarchy of productive powers. Before a nation can develop manufacturing, commerce, or agriculture to their fullest extent, it must establish the liberty of its citizens — their security of person and property, their freedom of thought and economic action, and their participation in the making of the laws that govern them. (012-002)

List places liberty in direct opposition to despotism as explanations for divergent economic outcomes. He writes:

> The people's innate love of liberty and of justice, the energy, the religious and moral character of the people, have a share in it. The constitution of the country, its institutions, the wisdom and power of its government and its aristocracy ... are the conditions under which productive powers develop. (004-012)

This passage encapsulates List's integration of liberty into economic analysis. Liberty is not a separate domain from economics; it is a factor of production as real as soil or capital. A nation that lacks liberty will find its productive powers stunted regardless of its natural wealth (as Spain demonstrated). A nation that secures liberty will find its productive powers amplified (as England and Holland demonstrated).

The connection between liberty and manufacturing is particularly important. List argues that civic liberty — the freedom and self-government of towns — was essential to the emergence of manufacturing because manufacturing requires a concentration of free labour, secure property, and the ability to enter contracts and form associations. (010-005) Agriculture, in contrast, could be conducted under feudal or servile conditions, which is why purely agricultural nations could tolerate despotism while manufacturing nations could not.

## List's Argument

**Liberty as a productive force.** List argues directly that liberty of thought and conscience has an economic effect:

> It were difficult to estimate the influence which liberty of thought and conscience has on the productive powers of nations. (012-004)

The mechanism is twofold. First, liberty of thought enables innovation and the diffusion of useful knowledge, without which manufacturing remains stuck at a low level of technique. Second, liberty of conscience channels religious energy into moral and social capital that supports economic activity, rather than into persecution and civil war that destroy it. (012-007)

**Liberty and the balanced national economy.** List argues that the natural liberty of individuals — left unguided by national policy — will not produce a balanced national economy. He observes that "the exertions of private individuals in the exercise of their natural liberty are opposed to one another" when different sectors compete for resources. (011-003) The state must harmonize these individual liberties through national policy, particularly through protection of manufacturing, so that the collective liberty of the nation is not sacrificed to the uncoordinated liberty of individuals. This distinction — between individual liberty and national liberty — is fundamental to List's critique of classical political economy.

**The historical geography of liberty.** List traces a geography of liberty across Europe: the Italian republics, the Hanseatic cities, the Dutch provinces, and England are the nodes where liberty flourished and where, consequently, manufacturing and commerce developed. (005-003) The regions that lacked this tradition — Spain, much of Germany, Eastern Europe — remained agricultural and comparatively poor. List's "liberty-loving democracy" of ancient Italy — the energetic democracy from which Rome sprang — provides the primordial example of liberty generating power. (001-004)

**Liberty and the manufacturing spirit.** List develops a historical theory in which liberty and manufacturing develop together. The spirit of industry, commerce, and liberty "attained full influence" in certain cities and regions (002-001), and from those centers radiated outward, transforming the economic and political life of surrounding territories. England achieved this synthesis most completely: "liberty kept pace at all times with the advance of industry, commerce, and navigation." (010-005) The English Parliament, the common law, and municipal self-government together created an environment in which manufacturing could flourish as nowhere else.

## Historical Examples

**The Italian republics** formed the first nexus of liberty and prosperity. Venice, Genoa, Florence, and the other free cities of medieval Italy were both the freest and the richest communities of their time. Their liberty — their independence from feudal lords, their self-government through councils and guilds, their protection of merchants' property — directly enabled their commercial and manufacturing supremacy. (001-004)

**Holland** demonstrated the same pattern. "The struggle for liberty begot in Holland an heroic spirit of industry and enterprise." (003-004) The Dutch revolt against Spain was simultaneously a struggle for political liberty, religious toleration, and economic freedom. The result was the Dutch Golden Age — a small nation that became the commercial and financial center of Europe.

**England** provided List's most developed example. England's liberty was not a single event but a continuous process: the Magna Carta, the development of Parliament, the common law, the settlement of 1688, and the gradual extension of the franchise all deepened the institutional foundations of English liberty. Each step made capital more secure, enterprise more rewarded, and innovation more likely. (004-014) England's economic supremacy was, in List's analysis, a direct consequence of its political liberty — not, as the classical economists argued, merely the result of free trade.

**The Hanseatic cities** demonstrated how liberty could be achieved at the municipal level even without a liberal national government. "Their protection of civic liberty in Germany" — through town charters, self-governing councils, and leagues for mutual defence — created islands of freedom and prosperity in a sea of feudal oppression. (007-003)

**Spain** illustrated the negative case. Spain possessed immense colonial wealth and natural resources, but "gold and silver will only acquire and maintain political liberty" if they are deployed through institutions that promote productive powers rather than merely financing consumption and war. (005-003) Spanish despotism, the Inquisition, and the expulsion of the industrious Jewish and Moorish populations destroyed the conditions under which liberty and prosperity could flourish together.
