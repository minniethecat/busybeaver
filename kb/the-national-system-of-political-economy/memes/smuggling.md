## Definition

Smuggling is the clandestine importation or exportation of goods in violation of customs laws and trade restrictions. In List's economic framework, smuggling is not treated as a mere criminal activity but as a **systemic phenomenon** inherent in the operation of protective tariff regimes — a practical constraint that shapes the design and effectiveness of trade policy.

List analyzes smuggling from the perspective of the policymaker: protectionist measures create incentives for evasion, and the success of a tariff system depends partly on its ability to minimize smuggling through enforcement, administrative design, and the calibration of duty rates. Smuggling thus functions in his system as a limiting factor on the effectiveness of protection.

## In List's System

Smuggling occupies a pragmatic, administrative dimension of List's theory of protection. It is the **shadow side of the protective system** — the unintended consequence that policymakers must anticipate and manage.

List integrates smuggling into his framework through three analytical connections. First, smuggling serves as a **diagnostic of tariff design**: high duties on easily concealed goods invite evasion, while moderate duties on bulk goods are more enforceable. This informs List's preference for carefully calibrated rather than maximally high protective tariffs.

Second, smuggling illustrates the **limits of state capacity**: a nation's ability to protect its industries depends not only on the legal framework but also on administrative competence, border control, and the legitimacy of the tariff system among the population.

Third, smuggling provides an argument for **tariff moderation**: excessively high duties create perverse outcomes — they "serve merely to favour contraband" (027-002) rather than protecting domestic industry. The optimal tariff is one high enough to protect but low enough to make smuggling unprofitable.

## List's Argument

> The evasion of customs duties by smuggling and false declarations of value is a necessary consequence of excessive duties, which can be most readily evaded by smuggling. (026-004)

List develops his analysis of smuggling through several interconnected arguments:

**Smuggling as Systemic Response.** List treats smuggling not as individual criminality but as a predictable market response to price differentials created by tariffs. High duties create a price gap between legal and illegal imports; that gap is the smuggler's profit margin.

"The evasion of customs duties by smuggling and false declarations of value" (026-004) follows inevitably from the economic incentive structure created by the tariff system.

**Calibration of Tariff Rates.** List argues that the optimal protective tariff must account for smuggling risk. Duties that "can be most readily evaded by smuggling" (026-005) provide little real protection regardless of their nominal rate.

The policymaker must consider the enforceability of duties, not just their protective intent: excessively high rates on easily smuggled goods are self-defeating, as they "serve merely to favour contraband" (027-002).

**Historical Evidence of Smuggling Under Protection.** List documents cases where protectionist policies generated large-scale smuggling: "immense quantities of goods" were smuggled (005-008) when tariff barriers created sufficient incentive.

The Canadian smuggling trade with the United States became "unendurable" (033-012), illustrating how smuggling across a long, difficult-to-patrol border can undermine even well-intentioned protection.

**Administrative Remedies.** List notes that nations have developed administrative techniques to reduce smuggling: "By this means not only were smuggling and too low valuations obviated" (007-010).

These include accurate valuation systems that prevent under-declaration and enforcement mechanisms that raise the cost of evasion. Effective administration is as important as the tariff rate itself.

**Smuggling and National Development Stage.** Implicit in List's analysis is a developmental dimension: advanced manufacturing nations with strong administrative capacity and clear borders can enforce more sophisticated tariff systems.

Nations at earlier stages of development, with porous borders and limited administrative reach, must design simpler systems with lower rates to keep smuggling manageable.

## Historical Examples

List draws on several historical cases to illustrate the relationship between smuggling and trade policy:

- **Germany Before the Zollverein (Pre-1834).** List describes the pre-Zollverein German states as a smuggler's paradise: "every province was closed against the other" by internal tariffs, and "every opportunity was afforded them of smuggling immense quantities of goods" (005-008).

The fragmentation of German territory into dozens of customs jurisdictions made enforcement impossible — goods could be smuggled across internal borders with impunity. The Zollverein resolved this partly by eliminating internal tariffs entirely, removing the incentive for internal smuggling.

- **England's Tariff Administration.** List notes that England developed administrative techniques to combat customs evasion. Measures were adopted "by this means not only were smuggling and too low valuations obviated" (007-010). The English experience demonstrated that tariff protection required not just legislation but an administrative apparatus capable of enforcement.

- **Canadian-American Border.** List observes that "the Canadian smuggling trade became unendurable" (033-012) along the long, porous border with the United States. This case illustrates how geographic factors — border length, terrain, population density — constrain tariff policy.

A tariff that works for an island nation like Britain may fail for a continental nation with long land borders.

- **Protective Tariffs and Evasion.** List notes the general principle that customs duties "can be most readily evaded by smuggling" (026-005) when rates are set without regard to enforceability. He uses this observation to argue for moderate, well-enforced tariffs rather than prohibitive duties that invite widespread evasion.

- **Import Prohibitions.** List discusses smuggling in connection with import prohibitions, noting that outright bans on imported goods create the strongest smuggling incentive of all: prohibition "serves merely to favour contraband" (027-002) because the eliminated legal market is replaced entirely by illegal supply.

This is one reason List generally prefers moderate protective tariffs to outright import prohibitions.
