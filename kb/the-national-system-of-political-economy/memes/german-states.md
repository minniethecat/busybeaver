# The German States

The politically fragmented German territories whose economic unification through the Zollverein List championed as the essential condition for German manufacturing development

## In List's Analysis

The German States are the subject — and intended audience — of List's *National System*. In List's time (the 1830s–1840s), "Germany" was a cultural and linguistic entity but not a political one: the German Confederation comprised 39 sovereign states, each with its own tariffs, currencies, and commercial regulations. Goods traveling from Hamburg to Vienna faced dozens of internal customs barriers.

List diagnosed Germany's problem as political fragmentation preventing economic development. The German states, individually too small to support competitive manufacturing, were collectively large enough to become a major industrial power — *if* they could unite economically through a customs union (Zollverein) and politically through confederation.

List's analysis was both descriptive and prescriptive. He documented the costs of fragmentation (internal tariffs raised prices, small markets prevented economies of scale, foreign manufactures dominated German markets) and advocated the solution (the Zollverein, protective tariffs, railway construction, national education, and eventually political unification). "By its Zollverein, the German nation first obtained one of the chief essentials of its nationality" (015-003).
