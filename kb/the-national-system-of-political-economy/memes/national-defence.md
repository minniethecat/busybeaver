# National Defence

## Definition

**National defence** in Friedrich List's *National System of Political Economy* (1841) is not merely a military concern but a fundamental dimension of national productive power. List argued that a nation's capacity to defend itself is inseparable from its manufacturing capability, because modern warfare depends on industrial resources — iron, machinery, gunpowder, and the skilled labor to produce them. Defence, List contended, is "of much more importance than opulence." (P03-009)

> ...the State, as a new basis for national defence. By means of their commercial... (002-001)

For List, the link between economic policy and national security was not a secondary consideration but a primary justification for the protective system. A nation that relies on foreign suppliers for its manufactured goods, he warned, is placing its means of defence in foreign hands. (027-003)

## In List's System

National defence occupies a strategic position in List's argument for protective tariffs. List's reasoning proceeds from a simple premise: in a world of competing nation-states, a nation that cannot produce its own weapons, ships, and industrial materials is not truly sovereign.

List identified three layers of the defence–economy nexus:

1. **Industrial self-sufficiency**: A nation must be able to manufacture the goods essential for its defence — iron, steel, gunpowder, ships, and machinery. Free trade, by encouraging specialization in raw material exports, may maximize short-term wealth but at the cost of this vital self-sufficiency.

2. **Maritime and commercial power**: List observed that commercial nations, from Venice to Holland to England, built their military power on commercial foundations. The same ships that carried trade in peacetime became instruments of naval power in wartime. "By her political power England gained productive power, and by her productive power gained political power." (004-010)

3. **The balance of defensive and offensive capacity**: The European state system, List argued, was characterized by a perpetual tension between defensive and offensive forces. A nation that neglects its defensive productive powers invites domination by those who have cultivated theirs. (011-005)

## List's Argument

List's argument on national defence unfolds across multiple dimensions. He insisted that the promotion of justice, defence of nations, and national security are not mere costs to be minimized but essential functions of the state that create the conditions for all economic activity. (012-003)

The manufacturing capacity of a nation, List argued, is "infinitely" more important for its means of national defence than raw material wealth. (027-003) This is because:

1. **War relies on manufacturing**: Modern armies require iron for cannons, steel for rifles, coal for steam-powered ships, and machinery for mass production. A nation that imports all these goods is strategically vulnerable.

2. **Manufacturing creates skilled labor**: The workers who produce manufactured goods in peacetime constitute a reserve of skilled labor for defence production in wartime. This "mental capital" of technical skill is as important to defence as material stockpiles.

3. **Transportation infrastructure serves dual purposes**: Railroads and canals built for commerce also enable rapid mobilization. List viewed transport infrastructure as a form of instrumental power that serves both economic and strategic ends.

The self-maintenance and self-defence of the nation, List concluded, counsels nations to develop their own manufacturing power even at the temporary expense of cheaper imports. (024-007)

List did not argue for autarky. His position was more nuanced: for national defence, a nation must possess *the capacity* to produce essential goods, even if it does not *always* exercise that capacity. The protective system builds this latent capacity; free trade, by dismantling it, creates strategic dependency.

## Historical Examples

List marshaled extensive historical evidence for the defence–economy link:

- **The Hanseatic League** (Chapter II): The Hansards built a vast commercial empire, but their cities lacked the territorial base and manufacturing capacity to defend themselves. When political conditions shifted — when territorial states like England and Denmark asserted control over their coasts — the League's commercial wealth proved militarily useless without a corresponding manufacturing base. (002-001)

- **England** (Chapter IV): England's Navigation Acts and protective tariffs on woolen manufactures simultaneously built industrial and naval power. The same policies that nurtured domestic industry created the merchant marine that became the Royal Navy's reserve. List cited this as the archetypal case of defence and productive power growing together.

- **Continental Europe** (Chapters VI–X): The German states, politically fragmented and economically divided, demonstrated the opposite. Without national unity and protective tariffs, they could develop neither the manufacturing capacity nor the military strength to resist Napoleonic or British pressure. List argued that the German Zollverein was as much a project of national defence as of economic integration.
