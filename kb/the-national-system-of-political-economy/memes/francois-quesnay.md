# François Quesnay

## Life and Career

François Quesnay (1694–1774) was a French physician and economist who served as the personal doctor to Madame de Pompadour at the court of Louis XV. He founded the Physiocratic school of economics — the first systematic school of economic thought — which held that agriculture is the sole source of net wealth (*produit net*) and that manufacturing and commerce merely transform or circulate existing value without adding to it.

Quesnay's *Tableau Économique* (1758) was the first attempt to model the circular flow of income through an economy. Based on his medical training (he viewed the economy as analogous to the circulatory system), it depicted how agricultural surplus flows through landlords, farmers, and artisans.

## Role in "The National System"

Quesnay and the Physiocrats appear in List's analysis as both predecessors and cautionary examples. List acknowledged that "before Quesnay and the French economists there had been no systematic political economy" (011-001) — the Physiocrats established economics as a discipline. But their central doctrine (that only agriculture is productive) was, in List's view, the mirror-image error of Smith's system.

Where Smith overvalued exchange and undervalued productive conditions, Quesnay overvalued agriculture and undervalued manufacturing. List saw himself as synthesizing what was correct in both systems while correcting their shared blindness to the nation as the proper unit of analysis.

List noted that Quesnay, like Smith, operated within a cosmopolitical framework. The Physiocrats advocated free trade in grain (*laissez-faire, laissez-passer*) not from Smithian principles of exchange value but from the belief that free agricultural trade would maximize the *produit net*. Both schools, List argued, failed to see that the nation — not the world — is the relevant economic unit.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **The *Tableau Économique***: The first macroeconomic model, tracing the circulation of income between productive (agricultural), proprietary (landlord), and sterile (manufacturing) classes.

- **The *produit net***: The doctrine that only agriculture generates a net surplus beyond the cost of production. Manufacturing merely transforms inputs without creating new value.

- **Single Tax**: The Physiocrats advocated replacing all existing taxes with a single tax on agricultural land rent, the only true source of surplus.

- **Free Trade in Grain**: The policy of *laissez-faire* originated with the Physiocrats' demand for unrestricted grain trade within France.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment of Quesnay was mixed. He credited the Physiocrats with founding political economy as a systematic discipline. But he rejected their central doctrine — that manufacturing is "sterile" — as fundamentally wrong.

The deeper error, in List's view, was shared by both Quesnay and Smith: the assumption that the economic principles valid for an individual or the world apply to nations. Quesnay, like Smith, "had not analyzed the causes of wealth" (011-001) in the sense that List meant — the institutional, political, and historical conditions that enable nations to develop productive powers.

List also noted that "Quesnay in his writings has adduced examples from private economy which were afterwards repeated by Smith" (030-003) — indicating that Smith borrowed more from the Physiocrats than he acknowledged.
