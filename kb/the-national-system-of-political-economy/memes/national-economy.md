# National Economy

## Definition

The **National Economy** (or Political Economy), in Friedrich List's system, is the economy of an entire nation considered as a unified entity with its own developmental interests, historical trajectory, and strategic imperatives. It occupies an intermediate position between two other levels of economic organization: the private economy of individuals and the cosmopolitical economy of all humanity.

List argued that classical economists had erred by conflating these three distinct levels. Adam Smith and his followers treated private economy principles (buy cheap, sell dear) as universally applicable to nations, while simultaneously assuming a peaceful world federation that did not yet exist. List insisted that "political or national economy must be" recognized as a distinct discipline with its own principles. (011-003)

## In List's System

The concept of national economy is the organizing framework for List's entire theoretical project. His major work after *The National System* was to be titled *The Politics of National Economy*. (P04-008)

List identified national economy as the level where:

1. **Productive powers are cultivated**: The nation provides the legal, educational, and infrastructural conditions that enable individual productivity. "The greater part of the productive powers of individuals are derived from the social and political institutions of the nation." (003-006)

2. **Intergenerational development occurs**: Only a nation, persisting across centuries, can pursue the long-term industrial policies that build up manufacturing capacity. England's rise from wool exporter to industrial power took over 400 years. (004-001)

3. **Trade-offs between sectors are managed**: Agricultural and manufacturing interests may conflict in the short run; only the national perspective can balance them for long-run development.

## The Three Levels of Economy

List distinguished three distinct spheres, each with its own logic:

| Level | Unit | Principle | Discipline |
|-------|------|-----------|------------|
| Private economy | Individual / firm | Maximize profit; buy cheapest | Business administration |
| **National economy** | **Nation-state** | **Develop productive powers** | **Political Economy (List)** |
| Cosmopolitical economy | All humanity | Universal free trade | Classical economics (Smith) |

Classical economists, List charged, "confounded the principles of private economy with those of national economy." (014-001) They applied the merchant's logic of cost minimization to nations, ignoring that "that may be wisdom in national economy which would be folly in private economy." (014-003)

## List's Argument

List made the case for national economy as the proper unit of analysis through several lines of argument:

- **Historical**: Every nation that achieved industrial greatness (England, France, the United States) did so through *national* economic policies — protective tariffs, navigation laws, infrastructure investment — that defied the prescriptions of cosmopolitan free trade.

- **Institutional**: The laws, customs, educational systems, and political stability that make economic activity possible are national in scope. A merchant in London and a merchant in Constantinople operate under entirely different conditions, even if they trade the same goods.

- **Strategic**: In a world of competing nation-states, economic capacity determines military security and political independence. A nation that subordinates its productive development to short-term consumer savings may find itself defenseless. "By her political power England gained productive power, and by her productive power gained political power." (004-010)

- **Developmental**: Nations pass through distinct stages — pastoral, agricultural, agricultural-manufacturing, agricultural-manufacturing-commercial. Different trade policies are appropriate at different stages. A nation in the agricultural stage needs protection to reach the manufacturing stage; once fully industrialized, it may benefit from free trade.
