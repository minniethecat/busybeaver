# Free Trade

## Definition

**Free Trade** — the unrestricted exchange of goods across national borders without tariffs, quotas, or other barriers — is both an ultimate ideal and a strategic danger in Friedrich List's system. List did not reject free trade as a final end; he argued that "his ultimate ideal is universal free trade." (P03-013) But he insisted that free trade between nations at vastly different levels of industrial development was destructive to the less developed nation's productive powers.

List distinguished between two forms of free trade:
- **Cosmopolitical free trade**: The doctrine that free trade should be adopted immediately and universally, regardless of national circumstances — which List opposed
- **Free trade among equals**: Commerce between nations at similar stages of industrial development — which List supported

## In List's System

Free trade occupies a complex position in List's framework. It is simultaneously:

1. **The long-term goal**: List envisioned a future world federation where all nations had achieved manufacturing maturity and could trade freely without danger.

2. **A present danger**: Premature free trade between England (fully industrialized) and Germany or the United States (agricultural) would permanently lock the latter into raw material production, preventing them from ever developing manufacturing industry.

3. **A policy to be timed**: Nations should transition to free trade only after achieving manufacturing maturity through a period of protection. "England only commenced to advocate free trade after she had reached manufacturing supremacy." (004-012)

List observed that England itself "only commenced to advocate free trade after she had reached manufacturing supremacy" — having spent centuries protecting its own infant industries. The call for universal free trade was, List charged, a strategy to "kick away the ladder" by which England had climbed to industrial dominance.

## List's Argument

List's critique of premature free trade rested on several pillars:

- **Historical precedent**: Every major commercial and industrial power — Venice, the Hanseatic League, Holland, England — used protection and navigation laws during its rise. Free trade was embraced only after manufacturing supremacy was achieved. (002-011)

- **Unequal competition**: When an advanced manufacturing nation trades freely with an agricultural nation, the agricultural nation's nascent industries are destroyed before they can develop. The short-term gains from cheaper imports are overwhelmed by the long-term loss of productive capacity.

- **The manufacturing imperative**: Manufacturing is not just another sector — it is the engine of national development. It stimulates science, skill formation, transportation infrastructure, and the division of labor across the entire economy. A nation without manufacturing is permanently stunted.

- **National security**: Military power in the modern era depends on manufacturing capacity. A nation that imports all its manufactured goods is strategically vulnerable. "The manufacturing power and national defence are intimately connected." (026-001)

## Historical Examples

- **England**: From Edward III (14th century) through Elizabeth I to the Navigation Acts, England systematically protected its woolen manufactures and shipping. Only after achieving industrial dominance under George I and II did England begin preaching free trade.

- **The Hanseatic League**: A purely commercial free-trade network that dominated Northern Europe for centuries — but never built national manufacturing, and collapsed when political conditions changed.

- **The United States**: Alexander Hamilton's *Report on Manufactures* (1791) advocated protection for American infant industries. The American System of Henry Clay continued this tradition through the early 19th century.
