## Definition

National prosperity, in List's system, is the comprehensive measure of a nation's political-economic health. It is not reducible to the sum of individual wealth or exchangeable values held by a nation's members. Rather, it encompasses the steady and durable advancement of productive powers, the rising value of land, the growth of population in rising well-being, the development of mental and social institutions, and the nation's capacity to secure its own independence and future. List deploys "national prosperity" as the ultimate criterion against which every policy, institution, and commercial arrangement must be tested. (014-001)

List explicitly distinguishes national prosperity from mere "national wealth" understood as an aggregate of exchange values. A nation may possess abundant exchangeable wealth — through natural resource exports or favourable terms of trade — yet lack prosperity if its productive powers remain undeveloped or if its land values fluctuate wildly under the pressure of foreign commercial dependence. (020-007)

## In List's System

National prosperity occupies the conceptual center of List's national system of political economy. In Chapter XXXII, List enumerates the chain of national goods that the science of political economy, properly understood, must serve:

> The statesman will know and must know ... how by means of those national productive powers the national resources can be utilised in the wisest and best manner so as to produce national existence, national independence, national prosperity, national strength, national culture, and a national future. (032-004)

National prosperity thus sits in the middle of a sequence that runs from bare existence through independence to prosperity, and onward to strength, culture, and the nation's long-term future. It is the pivotal term — the point at which a nation's material, institutional, and cultural development converges into a self-reinforcing condition of well-being.

The fundamental precondition of national prosperity is national unity. List opens Chapter XIV with the declaration that "the unity of the nation forms the fundamental condition of lasting national prosperity," and that only where individual interests have been subordinated to those of the nation do productive powers develop harmoniously across generations. (014-001) This link between unity and prosperity recurs throughout the work: nations that fail to achieve political and economic integration — whether through internal division or subordination to foreign commercial interests — sacrifice their prosperity. (009-006)

## List's Argument

List's argument for national prosperity as a distinct analytical category proceeds on several fronts.

**First, the distinction from private wealth.** The classical school, following Adam Smith, conflated the prosperity of the nation with the prosperity of individuals. List insists that "that may be wisdom in national economy which would be folly in private economy, and vice versa." (014-003) National prosperity requires institutions and policies — including protective tariffs, investment in transport infrastructure, and the deliberate fostering of manufacturing — that no individual acting alone would undertake, because individuals cannot account for the needs of future generations, national defence, or the coordinated development of the nation's full productive capacity. (014-009)

**Second, the role of manufacturing power.** List argues that manufacturing is the engine of national prosperity because it alone can produce the continuous, regular, and lasting increase in land values and rents that signals genuine and durable prosperity. Foreign trade may produce temporary surges in agricultural prices, but such gains are fragile and reversible — "like the stimulant of opium or strong drink, stimulating merely for a moment, but weakening for a whole lifetime." (020-009) Only a home manufacturing power guarantees steady and permanent increase.

**Third, the test of land values.** List proposes a concrete empirical test for national prosperity:

> We believe it essential to maintain that there is no surer test of national prosperity than the rising and falling of the value of the land, and that fluctuations and crises in that are to be classed among the most ruinous of all plagues that can befall a country. (020-007)

This passage, which the Index identifies as the definitive statement on the subject (037-003), encapsulates List's method: national prosperity is not an abstract ideal but a measurable condition, observable in the long-run trajectory of land values. Land rises in value when manufacturing and agriculture develop together in a balanced national economy; it collapses when a nation depends precariously on foreign demand for its raw products.

**Fourth, the relationship to national power.** National prosperity and national power are linked but distinct. Manufacturing power simultaneously augments both: "all the mental powers of a nation, its State revenues, its material and mental means of defence, and its security for national independence, are increased in equal proportion by establishing in it a manufacturing power." (017-010) A nation that possesses prosperity without the power to defend it risks losing both; a nation that pursues power without building the productive foundation of prosperity builds on sand. (032-004)

## Historical Examples

List finds the historical record rich with illustrations of his theory of national prosperity.

**Venice** exemplified how a prohibitory commercial policy could build national prosperity through manufacturing and carrying trade. However, the prosperity proved fragile: once competing nations developed their own manufacturing capacity, Venice's advantage eroded, demonstrating that lasting prosperity requires continuous development of productive powers. (001-003)

**The Hanseatic League** demonstrated how united commercial policy could raise a confederation of cities to a high degree of prosperity, achieving manufacturing and commercial supremacy across Northern Europe. (005-001)

**England** provided List's central positive example. Through the systematic application of protective commercial policy — the Navigation Acts, import prohibitions on manufactured goods, and export duties on raw wool — England built a manufacturing power that raised its land values and agricultural prosperity to levels unmatched in purely agricultural nations. List estimates that without its manufacturing power, England's land values would fall to "the tenth or twentieth part of its present value." (020-005)

**Spain**, conversely, illustrated the negative case. A nation rich in colonial silver and possessing apparent national wealth failed to develop domestic productive powers, and its prosperity proved illusory. The influx of precious metals inflated consumption without building the manufacturing capacity that alone can sustain prosperity. (027-004)

**Germany**, in List's own time, presented the problem he sought to solve. Divided into numerous states with conflicting commercial policies, Germany could not achieve the unity that is the precondition of national prosperity. The Zollverein represented the first step toward the unified national economy that List believed was essential. (009-006)
