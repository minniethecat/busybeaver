## Definition

Commercial policy is the deliberate use of government power to shape a nation's foreign trade relations. In List's framework, it encompasses tariffs, import prohibitions, navigation laws, and commercial treaties—the full arsenal of state instruments for protecting and fostering domestic productive powers. Unlike the classical economists who viewed commercial policy as an artificial interference with natural economic laws, List regarded it as an indispensable tool of nation-building. (P01-001)

## In List's System

Commercial policy occupies a central position in List's national system of political economy. It is the primary mechanism through which the state intervenes to protect nascent industries from foreign competition during the critical transition from an agricultural to an agricultural-manufacturing-commercial state. (010-005)

List placed commercial policy within a historical framework: nations pass through stages of development, and the appropriate commercial policy varies with each stage. Free trade benefits advanced manufacturing nations (like England in List's time), while protective commercial policy is essential for nations striving to develop their own manufacturing capacity. (015-012) A uniform commercial policy across a confederation of states—as List advocated for the German Zollverein—amplifies the protective effect by creating a large internal market. (P05-002)

## List's Argument

List argued that commercial policy is not a matter of abstract principle but of historical circumstance and national fitness. He rejected the cosmopolitan school's claim that free trade is always and everywhere beneficial, insisting that "a restrictive commercial policy can be operative for good only where it corresponds with the degree of the industrial development of a nation." (009-002)

> The causes of wealth are something totally different from wealth itself. A nation may be impoverished by the very commercial policy that enriches another. (015-013)

List traced the success of England, Venice, and the Hanseatic League to their strategic use of protective commercial policy. He contended that England's Navigation Acts and import prohibitions were "the masterpiece of English commercial policy," deliberately designed to transfer manufacturing capability from the Continent to England. (005-005) Conversely, he argued that nations like Portugal and Spain failed because they adopted commercial policies unsuited to their stage of development, merely transplanting foreign systems without building domestic productive powers. (005-008)

The core of List's argument is that commercial policy must serve the development of productive powers, not merely the accumulation of exchange value. A nation that subordinates its commercial policy to the immediate gains of free trade may enjoy cheap imports in the short term but will sacrifice its long-term capacity to produce. (023-002)

## Historical Examples

List drew extensively on historical cases to illustrate his theory of commercial policy:

- **Venice** employed a prohibitory commercial policy that enabled its rise as a manufacturing and commercial power, but this policy benefited it only so long as competing nations had not yet developed their own manufacturing capacity. (001-007)

- **The Hanseatic League** established and developed a commercial policy that achieved a degree of manufacturing and commercial supremacy in Northern Europe, demonstrating what united commercial policy could accomplish. (002-001)

- **England** provided List's most elaborate example. From Edward III onward, England systematically used commercial policy—import restrictions on manufactured goods, export duties on raw wool, and the Navigation Acts—to build its manufacturing industry. List argued that England's commercial policy was the foundation of its subsequent industrial supremacy. (004-005)

- **The German Zollverein** represented List's positive program: the adoption of a common uniform commercial policy toward foreigners, creating a protected internal market large enough to sustain industrial development. (P05-003)

- **France under Colbert** demonstrated both the potential and the limits of commercial policy. Colbert's protective system fostered French manufacturing, but the policy was later undermined by the removal of protections before French industry had fully matured. (006-005)

## Contrast with Classical Economics

The classical economists, particularly Adam Smith and J.B. Say, treated commercial policy as a matter of cosmopolitan principle: free trade was universally beneficial, and any government interference was a distortion of natural economic order. List rejected this as a doctrinaire position that ignored the reality of national competition.

Where Smith saw commercial restrictions as a tax on consumers, List saw them as an investment in national productive capacity. Where the classical school advocated a uniform policy of free trade for all nations, List insisted that commercial policy must be calibrated to each nation's stage of development. The classical position, List charged, served the interests of England—already the dominant manufacturing power—by discouraging other nations from adopting the very protective policies England itself had used to achieve supremacy. (027-003)
