## Definition

The Physiocratic system (or Agricultural System) was the economic doctrine developed by François Quesnay and his followers in eighteenth-century France. It held that agriculture was the sole productive sector of the economy—the only activity that generated a net surplus over and above the costs of production. Manufacturing and commerce, in the Physiocratic view, were "sterile" activities that merely transformed or circulated wealth without creating it. List analyzed the Physiocratic system as a significant but flawed precursor to both Adam Smith's classical economics and his own national system. (030-001)

## In List's System

The Physiocratic system occupies an important place in List's intellectual history of political economy. List treated it as a transitional doctrine: it represented an advance over mercantilism in recognizing that wealth consists of goods rather than precious metals, but it erred in the opposite direction by confining productive activity to agriculture alone. The Physiocrats correctly identified the material foundation of national wealth, but they failed to grasp the productive character of manufacturing and the mental powers it cultivates. (P05-005)

List saw the Physiocratic system as particularly instructive for understanding the limits of doctrines developed in specific national contexts. Physiocracy emerged in France—a nation that was overwhelmingly agricultural—and it reflected French economic conditions. Its error was to universalize these conditions into a general theory, just as Smith later universalized English conditions into classical economics. Both doctrines, List argued, failed the test of nationality: they assumed that what was true for one nation at one stage of development must be true for all nations at all stages. (031-001)

## List's Argument

List's primary argument about the Physiocratic system was that it represented an incomplete theory of productive powers. The Physiocrats were right to focus on production rather than exchange—this was their great advance over mercantilism—but they identified production too narrowly. By treating only agriculture as productive, they missed the cumulative and self-reinforcing character of manufacturing development.

> The Physiocratic system would hardly have ever come to be formulated if French agriculture had not been in a state of deep depression. (030-001)

List argued that the Physiocratic system contained a kernel of truth that was lost in subsequent classical economics. The Physiocrats understood that national wealth ultimately rests on the productive powers of the land and its people, not on the accumulation of gold or the balance of trade. Where they went wrong was in failing to see that manufacturing multiplies the productive powers of agriculture itself—that the most productive agriculture is found not in purely agricultural nations but in manufacturing nations where farming benefits from machinery, scientific knowledge, and a dense internal market. (030-001)

Adam Smith, List noted, had been influenced by the Physiocrats but had corrected their error by recognizing manufacturing and commerce as productive activities. However, Smith had then committed a different error: by treating all productive activities as equally beneficial regardless of their national context, he had lost the Physiocrats' insight that the structure of production matters. List's own system, he claimed, synthesized the Physiocrats' concern with the material foundations of wealth and Smith's recognition of manufacturing productivity, while adding the national and developmental dimension that both predecessors lacked. (031-001)

## Historical Examples

- **France under the Physiocrats**: The Physiocratic system emerged in a France where agriculture was depressed by heavy taxation, internal trade barriers, and the neglect of infrastructure. The Physiocrats' policy prescriptions—free internal trade in grain, tax reform, investment in agricultural improvement—were sensible responses to these conditions, but their theoretical claims about the sterility of manufacturing were unwarranted extrapolations. (030-001)

- **The Physiocratic Influence on Smith**: Adam Smith encountered the Physiocrats during his stay in France and was influenced by their emphasis on production and their critique of mercantilist trade restrictions. Smith adopted their view that wealth consists of goods rather than money, but he rejected their claim that only agriculture is productive—a rejection that List endorsed while criticizing Smith for then going too far in the opposite direction by neglecting the qualitative differences between economic activities.
