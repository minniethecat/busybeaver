# Instrumental Powers

## Definition

**Instrumental powers** (*instrumentale Kräfte*) in Friedrich List's *National System of Political Economy* (1841) refer to the material and physical component of a nation's productive powers. They encompass natural resources, tools, machinery, transport infrastructure, and all physical means by which labor is rendered more effective. List distinguishes instrumental powers from the mental and social dimensions of productive capacity:

> ...and natural resources, and of instrumental powers too (which latter the prevailing school terms capital). (012-011)

The term is List's deliberate counterpart to what the classical economists simply called "capital." By using "instrumental powers" instead of "capital," List embedded the concept within his broader theory that productive capacity is a national, not merely individual, phenomenon. (019-001)

## In List's System

Instrumental powers occupy a distinct position in List's multi-dimensional framework of productive forces. List identified several categories of productive power — mental, social, moral, political — and instrumental powers represent the tangible, physical layer upon which all others operate.

List argued that the accumulation of instrumental powers — machines, roads, canals, harbors — is essential but insufficient for national prosperity. A nation rich in instrumental powers but poor in mental capital (scientific knowledge, technical skill) or political institutions (rule of law, national unity) cannot sustain economic development. The greater part of the productive powers of individuals, List consistently maintained, derives from the social and political institutions of the nation. (003-006)

The relationship is symbiotic: mental capital directs the creation of instrumental powers, and instrumental powers in turn create the material surplus that supports scientific inquiry, education, and institutional development.

## List's Argument

List's treatment of instrumental powers is embedded in his critique of the classical school's narrow concept of capital. Adam Smith and his followers, List charged, conflated all productive forces under the single heading of "capital," obscuring the crucial distinction between material instruments and the mental, social, and political conditions that determine whether those instruments are effectively employed.

> What we understand by the term "instrumental powers" is called "capital" by the prevailing school of political economy. (019-001)

List identified several categories of instrumental power:

1. **Natural endowments** — fertile soil, mineral deposits, navigable rivers, and harbors. These are the given material base that every nation must work with.

2. **Manufacturing capital** — factories, machinery, and tools that multiply the productivity of human labor. Manufacturing industry, List argued, is the most dynamic form of instrumental power because it continuously stimulates invention and improvement across all sectors. (P03-008)

3. **Transport infrastructure** — roads, canals, railways, and shipping. These enable the circulation of goods and labor, transforming a collection of local markets into an integrated national economy.

4. **Material stock** — accumulated raw materials, fuel, and intermediate goods that sustain continuous production.

The critical point of List's argument is that instrumental powers are *created*, not merely accumulated. A nation that imports cheap manufactures while neglecting its own industrial development is not merely forgoing short-term profit — it is systematically failing to generate the instrumental powers that constitute the material basis of future prosperity. (012-003)

## Historical Examples

List drew on European history to illustrate the role of instrumental powers in national development:

- **Venice and the Italian Republics** (Chapter I): The Italian city-states accumulated immense instrumental powers in the form of ships, warehouses, and commercial infrastructure. However, because these were not complemented by national political unity and a protected home market, they proved fragile. When trade routes shifted, instrumental powers in one city found no employment and were dispersed rather than redeployed. (003-006)

- **The Hanseatic League** (Chapter II): The Hansards built instrumental powers in the form of a vast commercial fleet, trading posts from London to Novgorod, and financial networks. Yet their instrumental powers served *commerce*, not *manufacturing* — they were tools of exchange rather than tools of production. This proved their fatal weakness.

- **England under Elizabeth I** (Chapter IV): England's systematic policy of attracting skilled artisans from the Continent and protecting domestic woolen manufactures built instrumental powers in manufacturing, not merely in trade. The Navigation Acts created instrumental powers in shipping that served both commercial and strategic purposes.
