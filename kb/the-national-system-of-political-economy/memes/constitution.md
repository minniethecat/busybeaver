## Definition

The constitution of a country, in List's political economy, is the set of fundamental political institutions that determine how power is exercised and how citizens relate to the state. List treats constitutional government — representative institutions, the rule of law, limitations on executive power, security of person and property — as a productive force that channels national energy toward economic development rather than toward the protection of privilege or the waging of dynastic wars. A nation "under a rightly ordered constitutional form of government" (024-002) will outproduce and outcompete a nation under despotism, even if the latter possesses superior natural resources.

List's concept of constitution is not narrowly legal but broadly institutional: it encompasses written charters and unwritten conventions, the balance between crown and parliament, municipal self-government, the independence of courts, and the customs and expectations that govern political behaviour. All of these together form the "political constitution of the government" (003-006) that enables or disables economic development.

## In List's System

Constitutional government occupies a critical position in List's institutional framework. He treats it as both a precondition and a consequence of manufacturing development. Manufacturing requires constitutional government because manufacturing depends on free labour, secure property, enforceable contracts, and a state that can credibly commit to long-term policies — all conditions that absolutist governments supply unreliably. Conversely, manufacturing generates constitutional government because the manufacturing class — the bourgeoisie — demands political representation and will not indefinitely accept taxation without consent.

List articulates this connection explicitly when discussing England:

> The constitution of the country, its institutions, the wisdom and power of its government and its aristocracy ... are the conditions under which productive powers develop. (004-012)

The English constitution — the product of centuries of gradual evolution from Magna Carta through the Glorious Revolution to the parliamentary supremacy of the eighteenth century — was, in List's analysis, as important to England's economic supremacy as its coal, its navy, or its Navigation Acts. The constitution was not a superstructure built upon an economic base; it was a productive force in its own right.

List is careful to distinguish genuine constitutional government from nominal forms. He notes critically that classical political economy, as taught by Adam Smith, assumed that "the constitution of countries cannot be taken into consideration" in economic analysis (028-004) — an assumption List regards as fatal to the scientific value of Smith's system. List insists that "countries only whose political constitution (whether it bear the name of republic or of limited monarchy) secures to their citizens a high degree of personal liberty and of security of property" can develop productive powers to their fullest extent. (028-005)

## List's Argument

**Constitution and productive powers.** List argues that constitutional government affects productive powers through multiple channels. First, constitutional government provides security of property — the assurance that the state will not arbitrarily confiscate wealth — without which capital accumulation is irrational. Second, constitutional government enables the coordination of individual efforts toward national goals: citizens who participate in government are more willing to accept the sacrifices (taxation, military service, temporary protection costs) that long-term national development requires. Third, constitutional government disciplines the state itself, preventing the waste of national resources on dynastic wars, court extravagance, and the maintenance of a parasitic aristocracy.

**The English constitutional model.** England provides List's exemplary case of constitutional development in the service of national prosperity. He traces the process through which "municipal and provincial constitutions" evolved into national parliamentary government, with each stage deepening the institutional foundations of liberty and property. (008-003) The key moments in this evolution — Magna Carta, the Reformation Parliament, the settlement of 1688, the union with Scotland — were simultaneously constitutional and economic events. England's "constitutional freedom" under the Tudors, imperfect though it was by later standards, was sufficient to set England on a path that diverged from the absolutist monarchies of the continent. (004-013)

**The German constitutional deficit.** List's analysis of Germany's economic backwardness is inseparable from his diagnosis of its constitutional deficiencies. The Holy Roman Empire's decay left Germany without effective national institutions; the Peace of Westphalia confirmed the sovereignty of hundreds of petty states, each with its own laws, tariffs, and currencies. "The faulty constitution of the free states" — a reference to the weak constitutional arrangements of the German Confederation after the Congress of Vienna — "made the internal commerce of Germany a labyrinth of conflicting regulations." (009-002) Germany's economic problem was, at root, a constitutional problem: the absence of national political institutions that could enact and sustain a national economic policy.

**Constitution and the national future.** List's most forward-looking argument concerns the link between constitutional government and the nation's capacity to invest in its future. He argues that constitutional states alone can undertake the long-term projects — the building of canals and railways, the establishment of national education systems, the protection of infant industries over decades — that build national prosperity across generations. (015-002) A despotism may occasionally undertake such projects under an enlightened ruler, but it cannot sustain them across successions; only constitutional government provides the institutional continuity that makes long-term investment rational.

## Historical Examples

**The Italian republics** provided the earliest European examples of constitutional government enabling prosperity. Venice, Florence, and Genoa developed constitutional forms — elected councils, checks on executive power, commercial law — that protected the interests of the merchant class and enabled the accumulation of capital that financed the Renaissance. (001-001)

**England** is List's central case. The English constitution evolved through a series of settlements — Magna Carta, the Bill of Rights, the Act of Settlement — each of which limited arbitrary power and expanded the security of property. The result was not merely political liberty but an economic environment in which capital could accumulate and manufacturing could develop without fear of confiscation. (004-014) List is particularly struck by the English capacity for "undisturbed consummation" of constitutional development — the absence of the revolutionary ruptures that interrupted French development. (004-015)

**The Hanseatic cities** operated under municipal constitutions that provided the institutional stability necessary for commercial capitalism. Their town charters guaranteed self-government, commercial law, and protection from feudal exactions, creating oases of constitutional order in a feudal landscape. (007-003)

**The United States** represented the most complete realization of constitutional government in List's time. The American Constitution — with its separation of powers, its federal structure, its protection of individual rights — created a political framework within which the American System of protection and internal improvements could be debated and enacted through representative institutions. (009-007)

**Spain and France** demonstrate constitutional failures. Spain, despite its immense wealth, never developed effective constitutional limits on royal power, with the result that colonial revenue was squandered on dynastic wars and court extravagance rather than invested in productive development. France's constitutional development, though more advanced than Spain's, was repeatedly interrupted by revolution and reaction, preventing the "undisturbed consummation" that England achieved. (004-015)
