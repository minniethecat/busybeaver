# The British East India Company

## Overview

The British East India Company was chartered in 1600 by Elizabeth I and gradually transformed from a commercial trading company into a territorial empire controlling most of the Indian subcontinent. List cited the Company as evidence of how commercial power, backed by the British state and navy, could be wielded as an instrument of national economic dominance.

List noted that "the English East Indian territory, its fertility, and its immense population" (021-006) constituted a captive market for British manufactures and a source of raw materials — the colonial complement to Britain's insular supremacy. The Company's monopoly ensured that Indian textiles (once competitive with British production) were suppressed while British cotton goods flooded the Indian market.

## Role in List's Analysis

The East India Company exemplifies List's argument about the relationship between commercial organization and national power. The Company was nominally a private enterprise, but its operations were inseparable from British state power — the Royal Navy protected its ships, the British Army defended its territories, and British diplomacy secured its monopolies.

List's analysis focused on the destructive asymmetry: Indian handicraft manufacturing (particularly cotton textiles) was deliberately suppressed to create a market for British machine-made goods. This was not free trade between equal partners — it was the systematic destruction of a competitor's productive powers through the combination of commercial monopoly and state power.
