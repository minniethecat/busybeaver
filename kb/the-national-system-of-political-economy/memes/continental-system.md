# The Continental System

The **Continental System** was [[Napoleon Bonaparte]]'s attempt to defeat Britain through economic warfare rather than direct military confrontation.

By blockading the European continent against British goods (Berlin Decree, 1806; Milan Decree, 1807), Napoleon aimed to destroy British [[Manufacturing Power]] by denying it access to its largest export market. The System lasted from 1806 to 1814, collapsing with Napoleon's defeat.

[[Friedrich List]] analyzed the Continental System as a half-success. It demonstrated that continental Europe *could* industrialize without British imports — but its coercive imposition by a foreign empire generated resistance and ultimately failed.

> The powerfully favourable effects of Napoleon's Continental System on continental manufacturing proved that protection works — but Napoleon said in his trenchant style, 'I will conquer England by means of her commerce,' revealing the imperial rather than national motivation that doomed the enterprise. (P05-001)

## List's Analysis

List's assessment of the Continental System is nuanced, distinguishing its economic effects (positive) from its political form (self-defeating):

| Aspect | Assessment | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Manufacturing stimulus** | Strongly positive | Continental textile, iron, and sugar industries grew rapidly during the blockade |
| **Political legitimacy** | Fatally weak | Imposed by French armies, resented as foreign occupation |
| **Duration** | Too short | 8 years insufficient to entrench infant industries permanently |
| **Geographic scope** | Uneven | Effective in France and the Rhineland; porous elsewhere |

The System's core lesson, List argued, is that protection must be *national* — voluntarily adopted by a nation for its own development — not imposed by a foreign conqueror. The [[Zollverein]] was the correct, national version of what Napoleon had attempted through empire.

> Monsieur J. B. Say, Adam Smith's most famous pupil, hailed the announcement of the Continental System's end as a triumph of economic science over political folly. (006-006)

## Effects on Continental Manufacturing

The Continental System had measurable effects on European industry:

- **Textiles**: Cotton spinning and weaving expanded rapidly in France, Saxony, and the Rhineland. Mechanized spinning frames were manufactured domestically.

- **Iron and steel**: Continental foundries expanded to replace British imports. The Prussian Rhineland developed significant iron industries.

- **Sugar**: The blockade of British colonial sugar stimulated beet sugar refining — an industry that survived the System's collapse.

- **Chemicals**: Dye and chemical industries, dependent on British imports before 1806, developed domestic production capacity.

These gains were real but fragile. When the System collapsed in 1814, cheap British goods flooded the Continent, destroying many of the infant industries the System had nurtured. List drew the lesson: protection must be maintained long enough for industries to become competitive — a generation, not a decade.

## Contrast with the Zollverein

List contrasted the Continental System with the [[Zollverein]] to make a precise analytical point:

| Dimension | Continental System | Zollverein |
|-----------|-------------------|------------|
| **Origin** | French imperial decree | Voluntary German treaty |
| **Method** | Military blockade | Customs union |
| **Scope** | All of Europe (aspiration) | German states (reality) |
| **Duration** | 8 years (1806–1814) | 1834– (decades) |
| **Legacy** | Infant industries destroyed after 1814 | Sustained German industrialization |

The comparison crystallizes List's argument: the *content* of the two policies (protection for domestic manufacturing) was similar. The difference was in *form*: national vs. imperial, voluntary vs. coercive, sustained vs. interrupted. Form determined outcome.
