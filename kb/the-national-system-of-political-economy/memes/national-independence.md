## Definition

National independence is the condition in which a nation exercises sovereign control over its own political and economic affairs, free from subjection to or dependency upon foreign powers. In List's system, national independence is not a static political fact but a dynamic condition that must be actively cultivated through economic development. A nation may possess formal political independence yet fail to realise its substance if it does not develop the manufacturing capacity and productive powers that make independence meaningful and durable. (023-015)

List treats independence as one of the fundamental outputs of a well-functioning national economy—ranking it alongside national existence, prosperity, strength, culture, and future as the purposes for which productive powers must be organised and protected. (032-004)

## In List's System

National independence occupies a foundational position in List's national system of political economy. It is simultaneously the starting point and the end goal of economic policy. Without independence, a nation cannot set its own commercial policy, protect its infant industries, or pursue a course of development calibrated to its own historical stage. Yet independence without economic substance—without manufacturing power and developed productive forces—is precarious and incomplete.

> Power is more important than wealth. And why? Simply because national power is a dynamic force by which new productive resources are opened out, and because the forces of production are the tree on which wealth grows ... because a nation, by means of power, is enabled not only to open up new productive sources, but to maintain itself in possession of former and of recently acquired wealth, and because the reverse of power—namely, feebleness—leads to the relinquishment of all that we possess, not of acquired wealth alone, but of our powers of production, of our civilisation, of our freedom, nay, even of our national independence, into the hands of those who surpass us in might. (004-010)

This passage encapsulates the reciprocal logic at the heart of List's treatment of independence: political power secures economic development, economic development reinforces political power, and the loss of either leads to the loss of all.

In List's framework, specific industries are of strategic importance to national independence. The great branches of manufacture—cotton, woollen, and linen production—matter not merely for their exchange value but precisely because they bear directly on a nation's capacity to maintain its independence in a competitive world of rival states. (015-005)

## List's Argument

List advanced a multi-layered argument for why national independence must be the organising principle of political economy:

**Independence as the precondition of policy.** A nation that lacks independence cannot choose its own commercial policy and will inevitably have its economic course dictated by stronger powers. This is why List insisted that the German states must achieve genuine political and commercial unity through the Zollverein: only an independent and unified nation could adopt the protective commercial policy necessary to develop manufacturing power. (035-004)

**Independence as the fruit of manufacturing power.** List argued that manufacturing power is the ultimate guarantor of national independence. A nation that establishes a manufacturing capacity multiplies its material means of defence and its security for independence in direct proportion to its industrial development. (017-010) Naval power, sustained by a strong manufacturing base and foreign commerce, provides "the guarantee of national independence and influence over other nations." (009-009)

**The failure to utilise independence.** List was sharply critical of nations that possessed formal independence but failed to use it for economic development. He pointed to the United States as an instructive case: if Americans "do not know how to utilise their national independence so as to cultivate and develop a national industry of their own, and a self-supporting system of commerce and credit which is independent of the world outside," then their political independence would amount to little. (023-015) He further warned that sectional economic interests could become actively hostile to national greatness—observing that Southern slaveholders and cotton planters found their economic alignment with British free trade "more desirable than the national independence and greatness of the United States." (023-016)

**National independence as a system output.** In his critique of J.B. Say's system of values, List enumerated national independence as one of the essential products of a properly organised national economy, alongside national existence, prosperity, strength, culture, and future. (032-004) By elevating independence to this systematic level, List rejected the classical economists' treatment of independence as an exogenous political given and insisted that it is produced—or destroyed—by the very structure of a nation's economic policy.

## Historical Examples

List drew on a wide range of historical cases to illustrate his thesis that national independence depends on economic strength:

- **The Italian Republics**: Venice and the other Italian republics provide List's cautionary example of how commercial prosperity without sustained manufacturing development can prove fragile. They lost their national independence not through military defeat alone but through the enfeeblement that comes when productive powers are allowed to wither. (004-010)

- **The Hanseatic League**: Like the Italian republics, the Hanseatic cities achieved commercial supremacy but failed to build the manufacturing base and political unity necessary to preserve their independence. Their decline demonstrates that commercial wealth without productive power is an insecure foundation for national existence. (004-010)

- **England**: England is List's paramount positive example. Through deliberate commercial policy—the Navigation Acts, protective tariffs, and the systematic fostering of manufacturing—England built the productive powers that secured its national independence and made it the dominant power of the age. England understood that power is more important than wealth and that independence must be actively produced through economic policy. (004-010) (009-009)

- **The United States**: List saw the United States as a nation at a crossroads, where the choice between free trade and protection was simultaneously a choice between de facto colonial dependency and genuine national independence. The sectional conflict between the manufacturing North and the agrarian South was, in List's analysis, fundamentally a contest over whether the nation would use its independence to build productive powers or squander it. (023-015) (023-016)

- **The German Zollverein**: List's positive program for Germany centred on the proposition that the German states must achieve true commercial and political unity to exercise national independence. Germany's geographical position at the centre of Europe, its federal constitution, and its cultural endowments qualified it for a leading role in securing "national independence and power" on the Continent. (035-004)

- **South American States**: List noted that the newly independent states of South America, animated by "jealousy for national independence," were inclined to favour commercial relations with Germany over England or France—an observation that underscored both the universality of the drive for independence and the strategic opportunity it presented for German commercial policy. (036-006)

## Contrast with Classical Economics

Classical political economy, in List's critique, treated national independence as a given—an exogenous political fact outside the proper scope of economic analysis. Adam Smith and J.B. Say focused on the creation of exchange values and the efficiency of the global division of labour, assuming that free trade among nations would produce universal benefit regardless of the disparities in national power.

List rejected this cosmopolitan assumption as naive and, in practice, self-serving for the already-dominant manufacturing power of England. He argued that a nation that subordinates its independence to the principle of free trade before developing its own manufacturing capacity will find itself reduced to a de facto colony—exporting raw materials and importing manufactured goods, with its political sovereignty hollowed out from within. Where the classical economists saw the pursuit of national independence through protective policy as an irrational interference with the natural harmony of economic interests, List saw it as the sine qua non of any defensible economic order. A nation that does not actively produce its own independence through the cultivation of productive powers will eventually lose it.
