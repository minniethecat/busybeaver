## Definition

The temperate zone is List's geographical-economic concept designating the climatic region naturally endowed for manufacturing civilisation. In List's framework, the temperate zone encompasses the regions of Europe, North America, and temperate Asia whose moderate climate, fertile soil, and seasonal variation create conditions uniquely suited to bodily and mental exertion, the accumulation of capital, and the development of manufacturing industry. (013-011) It is defined in opposition to the torrid (tropical) zone, which List characterises as naturally suited to the production of agricultural commodities and raw materials but not to advanced manufacturing. (015-014)

The concept is not merely descriptive but prescriptive: nations inhabiting the temperate zone have both a natural vocation and a duty to develop their manufacturing power, failing which they squander the natural advantages bestowed upon them. (018-003)

## In List's System

The temperate zone occupies a foundational position in List's national system of political economy. It provides the geographical and climatic justification for his entire argument that nations must pursue industrialisation through protective commercial policy. The division of the world into temperate and torrid zones underpins List's theory of the international division of labour and his vision of a harmonious global economic order based on complementary national specialisations. (013-011)

List's core argument runs as follows: the temperate zone is naturally fitted for manufacturing, the torrid zone for the production of colonial and agricultural commodities. The "great commerce of the world" consists in the exchange between these two zones — manufactured goods from the temperate zone for raw materials and tropical products from the torrid zone. (021-007) Any disturbance of this natural order — such as one nation monopolising the manufacturing power of the entire temperate zone — is both economically inefficient and politically unjust. (015-016)

The concept also serves as a limiting criterion for List's protective policy. Not every nation can or should pursue protection; only those in the temperate zone, possessing adequate territory, national unity, and educated population, qualify. (033-017) The temperate zone thus defines the set of nations for which List's prescriptions are valid. (035-005)

## List's Argument

List develops his argument about the temperate zone across multiple chapters, building from descriptive observation to prescriptive policy.

He begins in Chapter 13 by establishing the natural determination of the division of labour: climate and Nature herself determine what each region can produce. The temperate zone emerges as uniquely privileged:

> The countries of the world most favoured by nature, with regard to both national and international division of labour, are evidently those whose soil brings forth the most common necessaries of life of the best quality and in the largest quantity, and whose climate is most conducive to bodily and mental exertion, and these are the countries of the temperate zone; for in these countries the manufacturing power especially prospers, by means of which the nation not merely attains to the highest degree of mental and social development and of political power, but is also enabled to make the countries of tropical climates and of inferior civilisation tributary in a certain measure to itself. (013-011)

In Chapter 15, List makes the concept central to his case for protection. He argues that the temperate zone countries are "specially fitted by nature for manufacturing" and therefore have a special field for their manufacturing exports in supplying the torrid zone. (015-014) The potential for growth is enormous: List calculates that barely a fiftieth part of the land suitable for tropical production was then in use, meaning both the torrid zone's production and the temperate zone's consumption of colonial produce could increase five- to tenfold. (015-015)

Most forcefully, List frames the choice between English manufacturing monopoly and a protected multi-national industrial order as a choice about the destiny of the entire temperate zone:

> It is evident that a world-manufacturing monopoly such as is at present established by the free competition of English manufactured goods on the European and American continents is not in the least more conducive to the welfare of the human race than the system of protection, which aims at developing the manufacturing power of the whole temperate zone, for the benefit of the agriculture of the whole torrid zone. (015-016)

List extends the argument in Chapters 21 and 22 by showing that the commercial intercourse between the agriculturists of the torrid zone and the manufacturers of the temperate zone constitutes "the great commerce of the world." (021-007) He predicts that the Asiatic countries of the torrid zone "will pass gradually under the dominion of the manufacturing commercial nations of the temperate zone" — a geopolitical consequence of the natural division of labour. (022-003)

The concept also appears in the Introductory Essay, where the editor notes that List "calmly assumes that Canada will secede as soon as she has reached the point of manufacturing power attained by the United States when they seceded, and that independent agricultural manufacturing commercial states will also arise in the countries of temperate climate in Australia in the course of time." (P03-005) This demonstrates how the temperate zone concept shapes List's predictions about colonial and national development in every temperate region.

## Historical Examples

List does not provide discrete historical examples for the temperate zone concept itself — it is a theoretical-geographical framework rather than a historically observed institution. However, the concept is applied throughout his historical analysis:

- **England** serves as the paradigmatic temperate-zone manufacturing power. List traces England's rise to its systematic use of protective policy to develop the manufacturing capacity for which its temperate climate and insular position had fitted it. (015-014) England's failure, in List's view, lies not in developing its own manufacturing but in seeking to monopolise manufacturing for the whole temperate zone, thereby denying other temperate nations their natural vocation.

- **Germany** represents the temperate-zone nation that must claim its natural inheritance. List's advocacy for the Zollverein and protective tariffs is framed as enabling Germany to fulfill the manufacturing destiny that its temperate climate and ample territory make possible. (035-005)

- **The United States** exemplifies a temperate-zone nation that successfully developed manufacturing power through protection, demonstrating the validity of List's temperate-zone thesis for new as well as old nations. (036-005)

- **The torrid zone countries** — India, the East Indies, South America, and Africa — appear throughout List's analysis as the natural agricultural and raw-material counterpart to the temperate manufacturing zone. List argues that their economic development depends on being able to exchange their products freely with multiple temperate-zone manufacturing nations rather than being captive to a single metropolitan power. (022-003)

- **Canada and Australia** are cited by List (as noted in the Introductory Essay) as temperate-climate colonies destined to develop independent manufacturing power and eventually secede, following the American pattern. (P03-005)
