## Definition

Population, in List's political economy, is not a simple demographic fact but a qualitative variable whose character and composition determine the productive powers of a nation. List treats population neither as a Malthusian burden to be restrained nor as a neutral factor of production, but as a resource that grows in value only when embedded in a manufacturing state. A growing population in a purely agricultural nation signals impending poverty; the same population growth in a manufacturing nation signals rising prosperity. (011-009) The key distinction is not how many people a nation contains, but whether its institutional and economic structure enables the majority of that population to contribute to and benefit from the development of productive powers.

List explicitly measures national prosperity partly by the growth of population — alongside rising land values and increasing national power — but always with the qualification that population growth must occur in a context of rising well-being, not merely rising numbers. (004-012)

## In List's System

Population occupies a pivotal position in List's chain of national development. It is simultaneously a cause and a consequence of prosperity: a nation with developed manufacturing powers attracts and sustains a larger and more productive population, which in turn deepens the division of labour and expands the home market.

List places population in his enumeration of the fundamental conditions of national prosperity:

> The statesman will know and must know ... how the national resources can be utilised in the wisest and best manner so as to produce national existence, national independence, national prosperity, national strength, national culture, and a national future. (032-004)

Population figures implicitly in every term of this sequence. A nation without sufficient population cannot sustain independence; a nation whose population is divided or servile cannot achieve prosperity; a nation whose population is educated and industrially engaged will develop culture and a national future.

The natural conditions — geographical position, climate, and soil fertility — set the initial limits on population capacity (P03-006), but List insists that these limits are not fixed. A nation that moves from agriculture to manufacturing can support a far larger population on the same territory because manufacturing multiplies the value produced per person and per acre, and creates demand for agricultural products that makes farming more intensive and productive. (009-008)

## List's Argument

List's argument for the centrality of population proceeds along several lines.

**Population growth through manufacturing.** List argues that manufacturing is the only sector capable of absorbing a growing population without depressing wages and living standards. In a purely agricultural nation, each additional person competes for a limited supply of land, driving down marginal returns. In a manufacturing nation, each additional person expands the market, deepens the division of labour, and increases the capital stock — raising wages rather than depressing them. (011-009) The former path leads to Malthusian pressure; the latter to rising prosperity.

**The surplus population problem.** List identifies emigration as the symptom of a nation that has failed to develop its manufacturing power. When a nation must export its "surplus population" to foreign countries because domestic industry cannot absorb them, it exports not merely people but "material, capital, and mental powers." (009-008) The receiving nation — in List's analysis, primarily England and the United States — gains the productive capacity that the sending nation squanders. This was a central concern in List's analysis of Germany's position relative to Britain: "the surplus population of those states would have to emigrate to England, and by settling there they increase the manufacturing and commercial, the political and national power of that country." (009-006)

**Depopulation as civilizational regression.** At the extreme, List identifies depopulation as one of the "ruinous plagues" that can befall a nation alongside foreign invasion and desolation. (004-013) A nation that loses its population — whether through emigration, war, or economic collapse — loses its future. The Thirty Years' War, which reduced Germany's population to a quarter of its former level, stands as List's primary historical warning of the catastrophic effects of population collapse on national power. (007-008)

**Quality over quantity.** List distinguishes between free and servile populations. The serfdom of the rural population in parts of Germany, prior to the reforms of the early nineteenth century, rendered that population incapable of contributing to productive powers despite their numerical presence. (007-002) A free population, secure in its property and rights, is — person for person — vastly more productive than a servile one. Liberty is thus a multiplier of population value, not merely an abstract ideal. (001-004)

## Historical Examples

List draws extensively on comparative history to illustrate his population thesis.

**The Hanseatic towns** began as a small population of German fishermen, sailors, and traders on a marshy coast. Through the development of commerce and manufacturing, this "small population" achieved wealth and power disproportionate to its numbers, demonstrating that the economic organization of a population matters more than its raw size. (003-006)

**England** provides List's central positive case. The Navigation Acts and systematic import prohibitions built a manufacturing power that absorbed a growing population at rising wages and generated "the increase of population resulting from" prosperity. (004-005) Through protective policy, England preserved "its own seafaring population from sloth" and directed their energies toward national enrichment. (004-009) List contrasts England's experience with that of nations that lost their manufacturing populations to emigration.

**The Thirty Years' War and Germany** provide the cautionary tale. The war reduced the German population to a fourth of its pre-war level through slaughter, disease, and emigration. "Wars were often wished for" by the nobility as a means of reducing population pressure — a barbarous logic that reflected the absence of a manufacturing economy capable of absorbing population growth productively. (007-008)

**The United States** in List's own time represented a nation that had learned the lesson. By protecting its nascent manufacturing through tariffs, the United States was transforming from an exporter of raw materials and importer of manufactures into a nation capable of "attracting the surplus population" of other nations and converting immigrants into productive citizens. (009-007)
