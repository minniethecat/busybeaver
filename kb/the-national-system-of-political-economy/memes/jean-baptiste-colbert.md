# Jean-Baptiste Colbert

## Life and Career

Jean-Baptiste Colbert (1619–1683) served as the Controller-General of Finances under Louis XIV of France from 1665 until his death. He directed French economic policy during the period of France's greatest power and cultural influence, implementing a systematic program of state-directed industrial development known as Colbertism — a form of mercantilism that prioritized domestic manufacturing, infrastructure investment, and commercial regulation.

Colbert's policies included: state-owned manufactories (the Gobelins tapestry works, the Sèvres porcelain works), protective tariffs on imported manufactures, subsidies for domestic industry, infrastructure investment (canals, roads, ports), the establishment of the French merchant marine, and the regulation of quality standards for French export goods.

## Role in "The National System"

Colbert appears throughout List's *National System* as a historical exemplar — an early practitioner of the kind of national industrial policy List advocated. While Colbert's theoretical framework was mercantilist (emphasizing gold accumulation), his practical policies anticipated List's system of manufacturing promotion.

List cited Colbert's achievements: "Colbert had estimated that the entire manufacturing industry of France at the time of Mazarin's death employed fewer than 50,000 workers; within 20 years he had increased this number eightfold." (006-002) France's manufacturing base expanded dramatically under Colbert's direction.

However, List also noted Colbert's limitations: "If Colbert was in error in opposing the export of agricultural products and the import of raw materials, this error was understandable in the context of his times." (006-003) Colbert lacked the theoretical framework to distinguish between beneficial and harmful trade restrictions.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **State-directed Industrialization**: Colbert demonstrated that a determined state could accelerate manufacturing development through targeted policies — a lesson List considered crucial for Germany in his own time.

- **Infrastructure Investment**: Colbert's canal and road building program showed the importance of transportation infrastructure for economic integration.

- **Quality Standards**: Colbert established strict quality regulations for French exports, understanding that national reputation affects the terms of trade.

- **Mercantilism**: Colbert's theoretical framework — emphasizing the accumulation of gold and the suppression of imports — was flawed, but his practical industrial policies were sound.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment of Colbert was largely positive, tempered by historical awareness. He saw Colbert as a pioneer who understood the necessity of manufacturing development but lacked the theoretical tools to pursue it optimally.

"Colbert's commercial policy was the first systematic attempt to develop the manufacturing powers of a great nation" (006-001). Where England's industrial development happened somewhat organically (aided by geography and political continuity), France's required deliberate state direction.

List considered Colbert's system a proof-of-concept for his own policy prescriptions: a continental nation with agricultural resources and a large population *can* develop manufacturing power through deliberate national policy. That Colbert's system was imperfect (it overregulated and sometimes stifled competition) did not discredit the principle.
