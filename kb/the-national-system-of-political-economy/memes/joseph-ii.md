# Joseph II

## Life and Career

Joseph II (1741–1790).

Joseph II (1741–1790), Holy Roman Emperor from 1765 to 1790, was the most radical of the "enlightened despots" of the 18th century. He abolished serfdom, promoted religious toleration, reformed the legal system, and pursued economic modernization across the Habsburg domains. List noted that "Austria had formerly suffered under Charles VI and Maria Theresa, but even more under Joseph II" (007-006) — referring to the economic disruption caused by Joseph's rapid reforms, despite their good intentions.

## Role in "The National System"

Joseph II appears in List's argument as a cautionary example: a ruler who understood the goal (economic modernization) but pursued it through imperial decree rather than national economic development. "Frederick the Great like Joseph II" (012-011) understood protection, but Joseph's reforms were imposed on unwilling populations and lacked the institutional foundation — a national customs union, a unified market, a protected manufacturing sector — that would make them durable.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment distinguished between Joseph's intentions (correct) and his methods (flawed). Joseph demonstrated the limitations of enlightened absolutism in economics: top-down reform without national economic integration produces resistance, not development. The Zollverein, by contrast, was voluntary and gradual — German states joining from economic self-interest rather than imperial command.
