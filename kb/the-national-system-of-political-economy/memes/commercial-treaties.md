## Definition

Commercial treaties, in List's political economy, are formal agreements between nations that establish the terms under which their citizens may trade. List analyzed commercial treaties not as neutral instruments of international cooperation but as tools of national policy whose effects depend entirely on the relative power and stage of development of the signatory nations. A commercial treaty between equals can be mutually beneficial; a commercial treaty between a manufacturing nation and an agricultural nation, List argued, typically serves the interests of the stronger party. (027-007)

## In List's System

Commercial treaties occupy a strategic position in List's analysis of international economic relations. They are the formal mechanism through which the cosmopolitan principles of free trade are implemented, and they therefore deserve the same critical scrutiny that List applied to free trade doctrine itself. List was particularly attentive to the asymmetry inherent in commercial treaties between nations at different stages of development: the manufacturing nation gains access to raw materials and markets for its finished goods, while the agricultural nation locks itself into a pattern of specialization from which it may find it difficult to escape. (P04-008)

The negotiation of commercial treaties, List argued, requires the backing of political power. A weak nation cannot negotiate favorable terms; it can only accept what stronger nations offer. List therefore advocated that Germany should strengthen its negotiating position by developing its manufacturing industry and unifying its commercial policy before entering into commercial treaties with England or France. (004-011)

## List's Argument

List's argument about commercial treaties is an extension of his broader critique of free trade. The classical economists treated commercial treaties as instruments for reducing trade barriers—unambiguously beneficial because they moved the world closer to the ideal of free exchange. List countered that the actual effects of commercial treaties depend on the stage of development of the signatory nations.

> A nation that binds itself to foreign competition by commercial treaties before its manufacturing industry has matured sacrifices its future productive powers for present commercial convenience. (027-007)

The historical record, List argued, supported his skepticism. Treaties that locked agricultural nations into supplying raw materials to manufacturing nations—such as the Methuen Treaty between England and Portugal—had enriched the manufacturing partner while suppressing industrial development in the agricultural partner. These were not accidents of poor negotiation but the predictable consequences of the structural asymmetry between nations at different stages of development.

For Germany, List advocated a policy of selective and strategic commercial treaties: negotiate favorable terms with the United States, Holland, and other nations that could supply raw materials and tropical products, but avoid treaties that would expose German manufacturing to English competition before it had achieved maturity. (P04-008) The key was to ensure that commercial treaties served national development rather than cosmopolitan ideology.

## Historical Examples

- **The Methuen Treaty (1703)**: List cited the commercial treaty between England and Portugal as a cautionary example. The treaty gave English manufactured goods access to the Portuguese market in exchange for preferential access to Portuguese wine. The result, List argued, was that Portugal's nascent manufacturing industry was suppressed, while England gained both a market for its goods and a source of raw materials. (004-011)

- **The Hanseatic Commercial Treaties**: The Hanseatic League had negotiated commercial treaties with numerous European powers, securing trading privileges that formed the basis of Hanseatic commercial supremacy. But these treaties lacked the backing of a unified national state and proved difficult to enforce once territorial powers grew strong enough to challenge them. (002-003)

- **German Zollverein Treaties**: The Zollverein's commercial treaties with neighboring states represented, for List, the correct approach: negotiate from a position of unified commercial strength, secure access to raw materials and markets, but protect the domestic manufacturing base until it could compete on equal terms. (P04-008)
