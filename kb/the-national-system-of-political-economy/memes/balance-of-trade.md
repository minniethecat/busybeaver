## Definition

The balance of trade is the accounting relationship between the total value of a nation's exports and the total value of its imports over a given period. A favourable balance occurs when exports exceed imports, resulting in a net inflow of specie or foreign exchange. In classical political economy after Adam Smith, the balance of trade was widely dismissed as a mercantilist obsession—a relic of the pre-scientific era of economic thought. List, however, argued that this dismissal was itself unscientific, because the balance of trade serves as a critical indicator of whether a nation is developing its productive powers or allowing them to be undermined by foreign competition. (014-007)

## In List's System

The balance of trade occupies a dual position in List's national system. On one hand, List acknowledged that the classical economists were right to reject the crude mercantilist doctrine that equated a favourable balance with national prosperity in all circumstances. On the other hand, he insisted that the balance of trade is a real phenomenon with real consequences, and that the classical school's refusal to discuss it amounted to a refusal to confront the distributional effects of international trade between nations at different stages of development. (023-012)

List positioned the balance of trade as one of the key empirical indicators that a nation must monitor when designing its commercial policy. A persistent unfavourable balance in manufactured goods signals that a nation's productive powers in manufacturing are being suppressed by more advanced foreign competitors—a signal that, if ignored, leads to the progressive enfeeblement of the national economy. The classical economists' insistence that the balance of trade would automatically equilibrate through the price-specie flow mechanism was, in List's view, a theoretical abstraction that ignored the irreversible damage done to productive structures in the meantime. (029-003)

## List's Argument

List advanced a nuanced argument that neither endorsed the crude mercantilist view nor accepted the classical dismissal. His position was that the balance of trade matters—not as a fetish, but as a diagnostic tool for assessing the health of a nation's productive powers.

List observed that the classical economists had made the balance of trade into a taboo subject:

> The popular theory maintains that the balance of trade must be favourable to a country, and the popular school of political economy maintains the contrary. The popular school has gone so far as to assert that the Minister must not even utter the term 'balance of trade,' while this term is, notwithstanding, in constant use among practical men and in all legislative assemblies. (014-007)

List rejected this intellectual prohibition. He argued that however much the doctrine of the balance of trade had been scorned by the scientific school, the underlying reality it pointed to had never ceased to operate. The classical economists had refuted the mercantilist version of the doctrine without replacing it with an adequate account of how trade imbalances affect the development of national productive powers. (023-012)

List identified a crucial distinction that the classical school overlooked: the difference between the balance of trade that arises from natural comparative advantage and the balance that arises from differential stages of industrial development. When a nation at the agricultural stage trades freely with a nation at the manufacturing-commercial stage, the resulting trade balance will tend to be structurally unfavourable to the agricultural nation in manufactured goods. This is not a self-correcting phenomenon—it is a structural effect of unequal development that, if not corrected through protective policy, perpetuates and deepens the inequality. (023-013)

List illustrated this with the American case. The Compromise Bill of 1833 exposed the American economy to unrestricted British competition, and the result was a balance of trade so unfavourable that for a series of years it shook the American economy to its foundations:

> In the United States of North America, under the so-called 'Compromise Bill,' the balance of trade was for a series of years so unfavourable that the greatest commercial crisis arose which that country had ever experienced. (023-018)

This crisis was not, in List's analysis, a temporary disequilibrium that the market would naturally correct. It was the direct consequence of exposing infant American manufactures to the full force of British manufacturing supremacy—a policy that drained the country of specie, disrupted credit, and threatened to undo the industrial progress made under the protective tariff of 1828.

List also linked the balance of trade to monetary crises, arguing that a persistently unfavourable balance could trigger financial instability in ways that the classical theory of automatic adjustment failed to capture. The drain of specie caused by a structural trade deficit could produce credit contractions, bank failures, and general economic distress long before the posited equilibrating mechanisms had time to operate. (023-016) (029-003)

## Historical Examples

List drew on historical experience to demonstrate the real-world significance of the balance of trade:

- **England under mercantilism**: List acknowledged that England had consciously pursued a favourable balance of trade for centuries through the Navigation Acts and protective tariffs. The favourable balance lowered the rate of exchange and facilitated the accumulation of capital that financed England's manufacturing expansion. (005-004) List did not endorse the mercantilist doctrine in its crude form, but he refused to dismiss the historical evidence that England's deliberate management of its trade balance had contributed to its rise as a manufacturing power.

- **The United States under the Compromise Bill**: As noted above, the unfavourable balance of trade that followed the Compromise Bill produced the greatest commercial crisis in American history to that date. This demonstrated, in List's view, that an agricultural nation cannot safely expose its infant manufactures to unrestricted competition from a more advanced manufacturing power without suffering severe trade imbalances and financial disruption. (023-018)

- **Germany**: List's concern with the balance of trade was directly practical for the German Zollverein. He argued that Germany's position at the centre of Europe, surrounded by more advanced manufacturing nations, meant that its trade balance required active management through protective commercial policy. Without such management, Germany would remain a supplier of raw materials and agricultural produce, importing manufactured goods on terms that progressively impoverished its productive powers. (035-005)

- **The broader pattern**: List noted that all nations that had successfully transitioned from the agricultural to the manufacturing-commercial stage had, at some point, experienced unfavourable trade balances that they corrected through protective policy. The classical school's theoretical demonstration that the balance of trade must eventually equilibrate was, in practice, irrelevant—because the damage done to productive powers during the disequilibrium period could be permanent. (023-017)

## Contrast with Classical Economics

The classical economists from Adam Smith onwards treated the balance of trade as a non-problem. The price-specie flow mechanism, first articulated by David Hume, demonstrated that a trade surplus would cause an inflow of specie, which would raise domestic prices, which would reduce exports and increase imports, automatically restoring equilibrium. The entire apparatus of mercantilist concern with the balance of trade was dismissed as based on a fallacy.

List did not dispute the logical validity of the Humean mechanism as an abstract model. What he disputed was its applicability to a world of nations at vastly different stages of industrial development. In a world where one nation possesses overwhelming manufacturing superiority—as England did in the nineteenth century—the equilibrating mechanism does not operate neutrally. The influx of cheap manufactured goods from the superior power can destroy the less-developed nation's infant manufacturing capacity before prices have time to adjust, producing not equilibrium but de-industrialisation and permanent dependency.

This argument was central to List's broader critique of the cosmopolitan assumptions of classical political economy. The classical school's dismissal of the balance of trade was not wrong as pure theory; it was wrong as practical policy for nations that had not yet developed their manufacturing powers. For such nations, the balance of trade remained a vital indicator—not of whether they were accumulating precious metals, but of whether their productive powers were developing or being destroyed by foreign competition.
