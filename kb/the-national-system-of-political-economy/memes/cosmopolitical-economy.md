# Cosmopolitical Economy

## Definition

**Cosmopolitical Economy** (or Cosmopolitan Economy) is Friedrich List's term for the classical economic doctrine that assumes the entire world operates as a single, peaceful, unified market — as if national borders, competing political interests, and unequal levels of development did not exist. List used this term to name what he considered the foundational error of Adam Smith and his school.

List distinguished sharply between three levels of economic organization. Cosmopolitical economy — "or world-wide economy, or economy of the whole human race" (011-002) — is the level that classical economists mistakenly applied to all economic questions, ignoring the intermediate reality of the nation.

## In List's System

Cosmopolitical economy serves as the intellectual antagonist throughout List's *National System*. It represents the theoretical framework List sought to supplant with his own political or national economy.

List's core charge was that Adam Smith, by giving "his cosmopolitical economy the name political, he confounded the ideas." (011-003) Smith's system claimed to be about the wealth of *nations*, but it actually analyzed the world as a single undifferentiated market, assuming universal peace and free trade as both the natural state and the normative ideal.

List acknowledged that cosmopolitical economy would be correct *if*:
1. All nations were united in a perpetual peace
2. All nations had reached the same level of industrial development
3. Free trade existed universally

But none of these conditions obtained in the real world. The doctrine was therefore "nothing else but cosmopolitical or world-wide economy" — a utopian vision presented as practical science. (011-002)

## List's Argument

- **The nation-state reality**: The world is divided into competing nation-states with distinct interests, laws, languages, and levels of development. Economic policy must start from this fact, not from an imagined world republic.

- **The manufacturing gap**: Free trade between an industrial power (England) and an agricultural nation (Germany, America) does not produce mutual benefit — it permanently subordinates the weaker nation to raw material production.

- **The historical evidence**: No nation achieved industrial development through cosmopolitical free trade. England, France, Holland, and the United States all used protection. Free trade advocacy always came *after* industrial supremacy was achieved.

- **The confusion of levels**: Classical economists applied principles valid for individuals (buy the cheapest) to nations (allow your industries to die for cheaper imports), treating fundamentally different entities as identical.

List's own ultimate ideal was also universal free trade — but only after all nations had reached manufacturing maturity. His difference with the cosmopolitical school was about *timing* and *sequence*, not ultimate ends. "His ultimate ideal is universal free trade. His difference with the laissez-faire school is that he would prepare the way for it by a fostering national policy." (P03-013)
