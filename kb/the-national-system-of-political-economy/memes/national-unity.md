## Definition

National unity, in List's political economy, is the condition in which a people occupying a contiguous territory are organized under a single political authority with the capacity to enact and enforce a uniform economic policy. It is not merely a political ideal but a functional prerequisite: without national unity, the instruments of national economic development—protective tariffs, transport infrastructure, commercial treaties—cannot be effectively deployed. (034-013)

## In List's System

National unity occupies a foundational position in List's developmental framework. It is the political precondition that must be satisfied before economic development can begin. A nation divided into multiple sovereign entities—as Germany was before the Zollverein—cannot pursue a coherent commercial policy, cannot build an integrated transport network, and cannot protect its home market from foreign competition. (028-001)

List elevated national unity from a background condition to an object of explicit theoretical analysis. He argued that the absence of national unity explained the economic decline of Italy after the Renaissance and the economic weakness of Germany before the nineteenth century. Conversely, the achievement of national unity explained the rise of England, France, and Holland as economic powers. (001-006)

## List's Argument

List's argument for the economic significance of national unity proceeds from historical comparison. Nations that achieved political unification early—England under the Tudors, France under the Bourbons—were able to pursue sustained policies of industrial development. Nations that remained politically fragmented—Italy, Germany—saw their early commercial advantages dissipate as they were outcompeted by unified rivals. (002-010)

> National unity is the rock on which the edifice of national industry must be built. (034-013)

The mechanism through which national unity enables economic development is straightforward. Protective tariffs require a unified customs territory; without it, goods enter through the weakest point and circulate freely. Transport infrastructure requires coordinated investment across regions; without national unity, each principality builds roads and canals to serve its own narrow interests. Commercial treaties require a single negotiating authority; a divided nation cannot bargain effectively with unified rivals. (003-003)

List distinguished between mere political unification—the formal subordination of territories to a single crown—and genuine national integration—the creation of common institutions, a shared market, and a national consciousness. England, he argued, had achieved the latter; Spain, despite its early political unification, had not, and consequently failed to develop a sustained manufacturing capacity despite its vast colonial wealth. (002-011)

> An incarnation of the idea of national unity which widely pervades the country is the indispensable foundation of all permanent industrial progress. (034-010)

## Historical Examples

- **Italy** provided List's most elaborate negative example. The Italian city-states had pioneered modern commerce, manufacturing, and banking. But they remained politically divided, "surrounded by great consolidated nations," and their early lead was lost when unified nation-states with larger home markets and coherent commercial policies overtook them. (028-001) The absence of national unity, List concluded, was the primary cause of Italy's industrial decline.

- **Germany** illustrated the costs of disunity in List's own time. Thirty-nine customs territories, each with its own tariffs, weights, measures, and currencies, prevented the emergence of a German national market. The Zollverein was, in List's analysis, the first step toward remedying this—an economic approximation of national unity that preceded and prepared the ground for political unification. (031-002)

- **England** demonstrated the benefits of early unification. The Tudor consolidation of national authority enabled the systematic commercial policy—Navigation Acts, protective tariffs, colonial monopoly—that built English manufacturing supremacy. England "grew up without prejudice to national unity or to national power," and this unity was the foundation of its subsequent economic success. (002-011)

- **The Netherlands** showed that commercial wealth without full national unity was vulnerable. The Dutch Republic was a federation of provinces with competing interests; its commercial empire, List argued, ultimately proved fragile because it lacked the unified political authority that England and France could deploy. (008-001)

- **The United States** demonstrated national unity as a conscious project. The federal Constitution created a single commercial space, and the early Congresses used protective tariffs to bind the nation together economically as well as politically. List cited the American example to argue that Germany should pursue national unity through the Zollverein and later through political consolidation. (034-013)
