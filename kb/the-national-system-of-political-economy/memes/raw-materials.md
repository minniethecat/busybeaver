## Definition

Raw materials, in List's political economy, are the unprocessed or minimally processed products of agriculture, forestry, mining, and fishing that enter international trade. List's distinctive contribution was to analyze raw materials not merely as commodities but as indicators of a nation's position in the international division of labor: nations that export raw materials and import manufactures occupy a subordinate position, while nations that import raw materials and export manufactures occupy a dominant one. (013-003)

## In List's System

The raw materials question is central to List's critique of free trade between unequal partners. The classical economists argued that free trade benefits all parties by allowing each nation to specialize in what it produces most efficiently—for agricultural nations, raw materials; for manufacturing nations, finished goods. List countered that this "natural" specialization is in fact a trap: a nation that accepts the role of raw material supplier condemns itself to permanent economic subordination. (013-004)

List located the raw materials problem within his theory of productive powers. Agriculture produces raw materials; manufacturing transforms them. But manufacturing also develops the mental and social productive powers—technical knowledge, organizational capacity, scientific inquiry—that agriculture alone cannot generate. A nation confined to raw material production therefore forgoes the developmental benefits of manufacturing, remaining not merely poorer but less capable of becoming richer. (013-005)

## List's Argument

List developed a three-part argument about raw materials. First, he distinguished between the "natural advantage" that some nations possess in raw material production—fertile soil, mineral deposits, favorable climate—and the "acquired advantage" that manufacturing nations develop through investment in skills, machinery, and infrastructure. The latter, List insisted, is far more important for long-term national prosperity. (013-003)

Second, List argued that the terms of trade between raw material exporters and manufacturing exporters systematically favor the latter. Manufacturing nations can choose among multiple sources of raw materials, playing suppliers against each other. Raw material exporters, by contrast, depend on manufacturing nations as the sole buyers of their products and the sole suppliers of the manufactured goods they need. This asymmetry means that raw material exporters are "mere providers of food and raw materials" on terms dictated by others. (P03-007)

> A nation that exports agricultural products and raw materials for foreign manufactured goods is an individual who sells his fields to buy clothes. (015-004)

Third, List demonstrated historically that the wealthiest and most powerful nations are those that import raw materials and export manufactures—not the reverse. England imported raw cotton, wool, and timber and exported finished textiles, machinery, and metal goods. Venice imported raw materials from the Levant and exported manufactured goods. The nations that remained raw material exporters—Portugal, Poland, the American South—remained poor and politically weak. (005-008)

## Historical Examples

- **England** systematically shaped its trade policy to favor the import of raw materials and the export of manufactures. English tariffs were structured to admit raw materials duty-free while imposing heavy duties on manufactured imports. The Navigation Acts ensured that raw materials from colonies were carried in English ships and processed in English factories. (004-006)

- **Portugal** exemplified the fate of a raw material exporter. The Methuen Treaty (1703) locked Portugal into exporting wine and raw materials to England in exchange for English manufactured goods—a relationship that, List argued, prevented Portugal from developing its own manufacturing industry and left it economically dependent. (032-004)

- **The Hanseatic League** built its prosperity on the exchange of raw materials from the Baltic and Russia for manufactured goods from Western Europe. But this prosperity proved fragile: when nation-states developed their own manufacturing and carrying trade, the Hanseatic intermediary role collapsed. (002-003)

- **Germany**, before the Zollverein, was largely an exporter of raw materials—timber, grain, wool—to England and France. List argued that this pattern was impoverishing Germany, draining it of the productive powers that only manufacturing could develop. (009-007)

- **The United States** represented the successful transition from raw material exporter to manufacturing nation. Through protective tariffs, the United States was progressively shifting from exporting raw cotton and importing English textiles to processing its own raw materials domestically. (015-003)
