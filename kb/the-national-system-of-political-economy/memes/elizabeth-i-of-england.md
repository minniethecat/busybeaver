# Elizabeth I of England

## Life and Career

Elizabeth I (1533–1603) reigned as Queen of England and Ireland from 1558 until her death. Her reign — the Elizabethan era — is traditionally celebrated for its cultural achievements (Shakespeare, Marlowe) and naval triumph (the Spanish Armada, 1588). For Friedrich List, however, Elizabeth's significance lay in her economic policy: she was the monarch who consolidated England's transition from a raw wool exporter to a manufacturing nation.

Under Elizabeth, the "woollen manufacture, which had been introduced under Edward III, was now developed into a national industry" (004-002), protected by tariffs, supported by the immigration of skilled Protestant artisans from the Spanish Netherlands, and promoted by navigation laws that reserved English trade for English ships.

## Role in "The National System"

Elizabeth appears in List's historical narrative as the ruler who institutionalized England's protective system. "Elizabeth acted towards these merchants with a firmness and consistency which later English governments would have done well to emulate" (002-008), List wrote, referring to her expulsion of Hanseatic merchants who had dominated English trade for centuries.

List's key observations about Elizabeth's policy:

- **Protective continuity**: Elizabeth built on Edward III's foundation (importing Flemish weavers) and added her own measures: the complete expulsion of Hanseatic privileges, navigation laws, and tariffs on manufactured imports.

- **National over cosmopolitan**: Elizabeth consistently prioritized English national economic interest over cosmopolitan free trade principles. She understood, List argued, that a nation's wealth depends on its productive capacity, not on the cheapness of imported goods.

- **The Protestant artisan immigration**: The persecution of Protestants in the Spanish Netherlands drove skilled textile workers to England. Elizabeth welcomed them, understanding that skilled immigrants are "mental capital" imported at no cost to the receiving nation.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **The Elizabethan Poor Laws**: While List did not discuss these directly, Elizabeth's economic legislation included the first systematic English poor laws (1601), establishing the principle of national responsibility for the indigent.

- **Commercial Diplomacy**: Elizabeth's foreign policy consistently served English commercial interests — from the Muscovy Company charter to the support of Drake and Hawkins's privateering against Spanish trade.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment of Elizabeth was admiring. He ranked her alongside Edward III as one of the two monarchs who understood that national economic policy must serve productive development. "Edward III comprehended this like Elizabeth" (012-011) — both understood the necessity of protecting infant industries.

List contrasted Elizabethan economic nationalism favorably with the free trade doctrines that later prevailed in Britain. Elizabeth built; her successors, having achieved manufacturing supremacy, preached free trade to prevent others from following the same path.
