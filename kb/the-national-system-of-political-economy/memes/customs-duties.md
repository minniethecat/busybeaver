## Definition

Customs duties are taxes levied on goods crossing national borders, primarily on imports. In Friedrich List's system, customs duties serve a dual purpose: as a **protective instrument** to shelter nascent domestic manufacturing from foreign competition, and as a source of state revenue. List distinguished between *revenue duties* (moderate taxes on luxury or colonial goods that do not compete with domestic production) and *protective duties* (carefully calibrated tariffs designed to allow domestic industries to develop until they can compete internationally).

List emphasised that customs duties are not an end in themselves but a **temporary educative measure**: once a nation's manufacturing power has matured to the point of international competitiveness, duties should be progressively reduced, ultimately moving toward free trade among equals.

## In List's System

Within the framework of the National System of Political Economy, customs duties are the **primary operational mechanism** of the protective system. They occupy a central position in List's policy prescriptions for nations in the second and third stages of economic development — those transitioning from agriculture to manufacturing.

List argued that customs duties must be applied **strategically and selectively**, not universally. The duty structure should reflect a nation's specific stage of development and the particular industries it seeks to nurture. He was critical of both indiscriminate high tariffs (which breed inefficiency) and premature free trade (which condemns less-developed nations to permanent agricultural subservience). The proper calibration of customs duties — high enough to protect, low enough to incentivise improvement — constitutes what List called the "science of political economy in practice."

## List's Argument

List developed a nuanced argument about customs duties that distinguished him from both absolute free traders and crude protectionists.

**The protective function.** Customs duties, List argued, are the most practical means by which a nation can protect its manufacturing industry during its infancy. "By navigation laws and customs duties in each case native vessels and native manufactures were protected against the competition of the foreigner." (001-006) The objective is not permanent protection but temporary shelter that enables native industry to reach the level of its foreign competitors.

**The calibration problem.** List was keenly aware that poorly designed customs duties could harm rather than help development. Duties set too high remove the competitive pressure that drives efficiency; duties set too low provide no meaningful protection. He advocated a system of moderate, gradually declining duties that would incentivise domestic producers to continuously improve. (026-003)

**The revenue dimension.** Beyond protection, customs duties provide the state with fiscal resources needed to fund infrastructure, education, and other productive investments. This revenue function is particularly important for nations that have not yet developed a mature internal taxation system.

**Against internal customs barriers.** List sharply criticised internal customs lines — such as the provincial duties that fragmented the German states before the Zollverein — as destructive to national economic integration. "Oppressed by provincial customs duties," he noted, "under such circumstances no national economy could develop." (030-002) External duties could be justified; internal duties were an obstacle to national unity.

## Historical Examples

List drew extensively on historical evidence to support his theory of customs duties:

**England's protective system.** List documented how England, after observing the success of Henry VII's early protectionist measures, systematically deployed customs duties from the Navigation Acts onward to build its manufacturing supremacy. "Stimulated by high customs duties and by bounties, the English manufactures attained an ever higher degree of perfection." (004-005) England's eventual transition to advocating free trade came only after it had achieved industrial dominance — a sequence List considered paradigmatic.

**The German Zollverein.** The abolition of internal customs lines among German states and the establishment of a common external tariff through the Zollverein was, for List, the most important economic reform in German history. It created a unified home market and enabled German industry to develop under moderate external protection.

**The American example.** List noted that the newly independent United States used customs duties both for revenue and for the protection of its nascent manufactures. The adjustment of American customs duties after independence demonstrated how a developing nation could use tariff policy to build industrial capacity. (023-005)
