## Definition

Nationality, in List's political economy, is the principle that a nation—a community bound by common language, territory, history, and institutions—constitutes the proper unit of economic analysis and policy. List elevated nationality from a descriptive category to an analytical foundation: the nation is not an arbitrary boundary imposed on economic life but the natural and necessary framework within which productive powers develop. (P05-005)

## In List's System

Nationality stands at the very foundation of List's theoretical project. He described it as "the characteristic of my system"—the organizing concept that distinguishes his national system of political economy from the cosmopolitan economics of Adam Smith and J.B. Say. (P03-006) Where the classical economists addressed themselves to "the wealth of nations" in the abstract, List insisted that wealth can only be understood and cultivated within the concrete context of a particular nation's history, institutions, and stage of development.

The principle of nationality carries direct policy implications in List's system. A nation that lacks political unity cannot develop a coherent national economy; a nation that neglects its national interests in favor of cosmopolitan ideals will see its productive powers undermined by more strategically minded rivals. (014-002) List therefore argued that the first duty of statesmanship is to secure the conditions of national existence—territorial integrity, political independence, and economic self-determination—before pursuing the cosmopolitan goal of universal free trade. (015-001)

## List's Argument

List's argument for the primacy of nationality rests on a historical and empirical claim: economic life has never been, and cannot be, conducted on a cosmopolitan basis. The nation is not "a grammatical invention," as he accused Adam Smith of implying, but a living community with shared interests, collective memory, and a common stake in the future. (011-003)

> The school ignores the very existence of nationality, and leaves entirely out of consideration the special conditions and requirements of separate nations. (015-009)

The classical economists' error, List charged, was to reason as if national boundaries did not exist—as if capital, labor, and goods moved frictionlessly across borders and all nations benefited equally from free exchange. In reality, List argued, nations compete. A more advanced manufacturing nation can use free trade to suppress the industrial development of its rivals, and only a nation conscious of its distinct national interests can resist this dynamic. (016-001)

List distinguished his concept of nationality from narrow nationalism. The principle of nationality did not imply hostility toward other nations or a rejection of international commerce. It meant only that each nation must organize its own economic development before it can participate in a genuinely beneficial international division of labor. A confederation of independent nations, each with developed productive powers, was List's ideal—not a world of atomized individuals trading under cosmopolitan rules written by the strongest power. (023-014)

## Historical Examples

List drew extensively on European history to illustrate the role of nationality in economic development:

- **The Hanseatic League**: List argued that the League's ultimate failure demonstrated the limits of commercial power without a national foundation. The Hansards amassed great wealth but lacked the political and military backing of a unified nationality, making them vulnerable to the rising nation-states. Had "the German nationality instead of the English" prevailed in Northern Europe, the entire course of economic history would have been different. (011-010)

- **The Decay of the Italian Cities**: The Italian city-states—Venice, Genoa, Florence—possessed advanced commerce and manufacturing but failed to unite under a common nationality. Their rivalries allowed larger national powers to overpower them one by one. List saw this as a cautionary tale: commercial prosperity without national unity is inherently fragile. (001-006) The absence of a great Italian nationality left each city to "expose their nationality to foreign attacks." (027-005)

- **Germany's Fragmentation**: List's own political career was devoted to the cause of German national unity. He argued that Germany's division into numerous small states had prevented it from developing a national economy on the scale of England or France. The Zollverein, Germany's customs union, represented a partial realization of the nationality principle in the economic sphere—a step toward the unified national market that List considered essential. (P05-005)

- **England's National Development**: England, List observed, had achieved its economic supremacy precisely by pursuing national—not cosmopolitan—policies. The Navigation Acts, protective tariffs, and strategic promotion of manufacturing were all expressions of the nationality principle in action. (007-005) England's classical economists, List acidly noted, preached free trade to other nations only after England itself had used national protection to reach the summit of industrial power.

## Contrast with Classical Economics

The classical economists, particularly Adam Smith, treated the nation as a convenient unit of description rather than an essential category of analysis. Smith's "wealth of nations" was, in List's reading, really the wealth of individuals aggregated within arbitrary political boundaries. The classical school assumed that what was good for the individual was good for the nation, and what was good for one nation was good for all.

List's rebuttal was both theoretical and empirical. Theoretically, he argued that the nation possesses interests that are irreducible to the sum of individual interests—foremost among them, the interest in developing and preserving national productive powers. Empirically, he pointed to the historical record: every nation that had achieved industrial greatness had done so through policies that deliberately favored national interests over cosmopolitan principles. The nationality principle was not an invention of List's but a discovery—a fact of economic life that the classical school had simply refused to see. (015-002)
