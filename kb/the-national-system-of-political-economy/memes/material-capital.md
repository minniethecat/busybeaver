# Material Capital

## Definition

**Material capital** (*materielles Kapital*), also termed **instrumental powers** in List's system, refers to the physical instruments and infrastructure of production: machinery, tools, factories, ships, railways, canals, and the accumulated physical stock that enables labor to produce more than it could with unaided hands. List devoted Chapter XIX of *The National System* specifically to "The Manufacturing Power and the Instrumental Powers (Material Capital) of the Nation."

List placed material capital within his broader productive powers framework, arguing that material capital alone cannot explain national prosperity. "That which we understand by the term 'instrumental powers' is called 'capital' by the school." (019-001) The classical economists, List charged, treated material capital accumulation as the sole driver of wealth, neglecting the mental, social, and institutional conditions that make capital accumulation possible and productive.

## In List's System

Material capital occupies a secondary but essential role in List's theoretical architecture. It is the *instrument* through which productive powers are applied, not the *source* of productive powers themselves:

> The causes of wealth are something totally different from wealth itself. A man may possess wealth, i.e. exchangeable values, but if he does not possess the power of producing more valuable objects than he consumes, he will become poorer. (012-001)

This distinction is fundamental: material capital is *accumulated wealth*, while productive powers are the *capacity to create wealth*. A nation rich in material capital but lacking productive powers (e.g., through deindustrialization) will consume its capital without replacing it.

## Material Capital and Manufacturing

List's most distinctive contribution to the theory of material capital is his argument that **manufacturing is the primary engine of material capital formation**:

1. **Manufacturing transforms agricultural produce into capital** — "Agricultural produce is not material capital. By means of manufactures it is turned into manufacturing capital." (019-008) Raw grain, timber, and wool become capital only when transformed into machinery, ships, and factory buildings.

2. **Manufacturing increases the productivity of existing capital** — "Every new manufacture established in a country increases the quantities of mental and material capital existing in the nation." (020-001) Each new factory not only adds its own capital but multiplies the value of surrounding land, transport infrastructure, and labor skills.

3. **Manufacturing prevents capital flight** — In agricultural nations, accumulated savings often flow abroad seeking manufacturing returns. A domestic manufacturing sector retains and reinvests national savings.

## Material Capital Across Stages of Development

List traced how material capital accumulation differs across developmental stages:

| Stage | Material Capital Characteristic |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| **Pastoral** | "Material capital increases quickly, but only so far as the increase of flocks and herds." (019-005) |
| **Agricultural** | Capital fixed in land improvements; limited reinvestment multipliers |
| **Agricultural-Manufacturing** | Rapid capital accumulation; agricultural surplus transformed into industrial capital |
| **Manufacturing-Commercial** | Maximum capital density; capital circulates rapidly between sectors |

## The Federation of Material and Mental Capital

List insisted that material and mental capital are inseparable in practice. The introductory essay to *The National System* identifies List's "position on immaterial capital and productive powers" as second in importance only to his doctrine of nationality. (P03-006)

Mental capital — scientific knowledge, technical skill, institutional wisdom — directs material capital toward productive uses. Without mental capital, material capital is misallocated or wasted. Without material capital, mental capital cannot be applied. The two grow together within a manufacturing nation:

> The present state of the nations is the result of the accumulation of all discoveries, inventions, improvements, perfections, and exertions of all generations which have lived before us; they form the mental capital of the present human race. (P03-006)

## List's Critique of Classical Capital Theory

List's main criticism of the classical school's treatment of capital was reductive materialism:

- Adam Smith and his followers "speak of capital" as if it were only "the material capital, the material instruments of production." (019-001)
- They assumed that national wealth depended "only on the sum of its material capital," neglecting the organizational, institutional, and mental conditions that make capital productive. (019-003)
- The classical theory could not explain why nations with similar material capital endowments (e.g., England vs. Spain in the 17th century) achieved radically different economic outcomes.

The difference, List argued, lay in productive powers — the mental, social, and political framework that directed material capital toward nation-building rather than consumption or export.
