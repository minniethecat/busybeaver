# Wages

## Definition

**Wages**, in Friedrich List's national economic system, are understood not as a fixed share of a static national product but as a dynamic outcome of manufacturing development. List rejected the classical wages-fund doctrine, which treated wages as determined by the ratio of capital to population. Instead, he argued that wages are fundamentally shaped by whether a nation has developed its own manufacturing power — manufacturing nations sustain structurally higher wages than purely agricultural nations.

List's core insight was that "the value of land and the rate of wages in these states everywhere rose" with the introduction of manufacturing, and that "with a high rate of wages in agriculture, manufactures could flourish" when both sectors developed within the same national economy. (009-002, 009-007)

## In List's System

Wages occupy a central position in List's argument for protective industrialization. The classical free-trade school argued that protection raised consumer prices and thus lowered real wages. List countered that manufacturing development raised *both* nominal and real wages by increasing the nation's total productive power:

1. **Manufacturing increases labor demand** — factories create competition for labor that bids up agricultural wages as well. "The rents, farming profits, and wages are increased" across the entire economy when manufacturing takes root. (012-006)

2. **Higher productivity supports higher wages** — manufacturing workers are more productive than agricultural laborers, and this higher productivity translates into higher wages without requiring lower profits.

3. **Wages and the home market** — higher wages create a larger home market for both manufactured and agricultural goods, completing a virtuous cycle of growth.

## Wages in the Agricultural vs. Manufacturing Nation

| Condition | Agricultural Nation | Manufacturing Nation |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Wage level | Low, constrained by export prices | High, supported by domestic productivity |
| Wage stability | Vulnerable to foreign market shocks | Buffered by internal market |
| Agricultural wages | Depressed by surplus labor | Raised by manufacturing labor demand |
| Real wages | High import costs for manufactures | Lower manufactured goods costs (domestic) |

"The mere agricultural nation is in its consumption dependent on foreign conditions, and if these are not favourable to it, that production which has been incited by consumption is checked." Wage employment collapses when foreign demand for agricultural exports falters.

## High Wages as a Competitive Advantage

List turned the classical argument on its head. Where free traders warned that high wages made a nation uncompetitive, List argued that high wages were a *signal* of high productive power:

> Nations in which wages are high, and whose population is [large], possess a natural advantage in manufacturing. (015-005)

High-wage manufacturing nations could compete successfully because their superior productive powers — better machinery, more skilled labor, superior organization — generated more output per worker. The United States, List noted, had disproportionately high wages yet was an increasingly formidable manufacturing competitor, precisely because those high wages reflected and reinforced high productivity.

## Wages and Protection

List argued that protective tariffs, far from depressing real wages as the free-trade school claimed, were essential to wage growth:

- Without protection, domestic manufacturing cannot develop, and labor remains trapped in low-wage agricultural employment
- Once manufacturing is established under temporary protection, wages rise across both sectors
- "The United States, as long as wages are disproportionately higher" than in England, needed protection to offset England's head start in manufacturing skill and capital (015-012)
- As manufacturing power matures, wages rise further, and the need for protection diminishes

The Hanseatic cities illustrated the alternative: their policy "naturally was warmly opposed by the landowners" because it affected "the price of provisions, and the rate of wages" — they suppressed domestic agricultural development, keeping wages low and fragile. (002-006)
