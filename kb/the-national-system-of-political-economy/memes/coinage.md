## Definition

Coinage is the state-authorized minting of precious metals into standardized currency, serving as the monetary foundation of commercial exchange.

In List's economic framework, coinage is analyzed not as an abstract monetary phenomenon but as a **policy instrument** embedded in the historical rivalry between nations — a tool through which states regulate internal circulation, manage trade balances, and assert economic sovereignty.

List discusses coinage primarily through the lens of the mercantile system's concern with precious metal flows. He distinguishes between the mercantilist obsession with accumulating coined treasure and his own productive-powers framework, which treats money as a means rather than an end of economic policy.

## In List's System

> All the coin which passed current in Italy was brought into circulation by the commerce and navigation of these republics. (001-003)

Coinage occupies a secondary but revealing position in List's theoretical architecture. It serves as a bridge concept between the **mercantile system** (which List critiques) and his own **theory of productive powers** (which subordinates monetary questions to productive capacity).

List's treatment of coinage reflects his broader historical method: he traces how the concept of national wealth evolved from the simple accumulation of coined precious metals (mercantilism) through the exchange-value framework of Adam Smith to his own productive-powers analysis.

Coinage thus functions in his system as a marker of **economic development stage** — nations at lower stages of development fixate on coin accumulation, while advanced manufacturing nations understand that productive capacity, not money supply, constitutes real national wealth.

List also analyzes coinage in the context of **monetary sovereignty**: the right of a nation to "coin money" (036-011) is one of the attributes of state power, alongside the ability to regulate commerce, construct infrastructure, and protect domestic industry. A nation that cannot control its own currency is not fully sovereign in economic terms.

## List's Argument

List's argument about coinage operates on two levels — a historical critique of mercantilist monetary doctrine and a constructive analysis of currency within his productive-powers framework:

**Critique of Mercantilist Coin Fetishism.** List rejects the mercantile system's equation of national wealth with the accumulation of coined precious metals.

He notes that mercantilists treated "the prfecious metals, both coined and uncoined" (002-005) as the measure of national prosperity. This fixation, List argues, led to policies that restricted trade and hoarded specie at the expense of the productive capacity that actually generates wealth.

The Italian republics accumulated "all the coin which passed current in Italy" (001-003) through their commercial dominance — but this coin was a symptom of their productive power, not its cause.

**Coin Flows as Trade Barometer.** List treats international coin movements as a diagnostic indicator of trade balances rather than an end in themselves. He notes that "extraordinary exports of coin take place" during trade imbalances (023-010), and that nations must sometimes "pay for it in specie which we have acquired elsewhere" (036-002).

These coin flows signal underlying productive asymmetries between nations — a manufacturing nation draws in specie through export surpluses; an agricultural nation loses it through import dependence.

**Monetary Sovereignty and State Power.** List identifies the power to "coin money" (036-011) as one of the fundamental attributes of national sovereignty, alongside the regulation of commerce and the protection of industry. This reflects his broader view that economic policy instruments — including monetary ones — must be under national control.

A nation that relies on foreign coin or cannot regulate its own currency surrenders a dimension of its economic independence.

**From Coin to Credit.** List's analysis of coinage points toward his treatment of more advanced financial instruments. He notes the role of "money changers and bankers" (004-017) in medieval London, recognizing that as economies develop, credit instruments supplement and partially replace coined money.

This transition from metallic currency to credit-based exchange is, in List's framework, a marker of economic advancement — manufacturing nations develop sophisticated financial systems, while agricultural nations remain dependent on simple coin circulation.

## Historical Examples

List draws on a range of historical episodes to illustrate the role of coinage in national economic development:

- **Medieval Italy.** The Italian republics accumulated vast quantities of coined precious metals through their dominance of Mediterranean trade routes. List records that "all the coin which passed current in Italy" flowed through Italian commercial centers (001-003). This accumulation was a consequence — not the cause — of Italian manufacturing and commercial power.

When Italian industry declined, the coin flowed elsewhere.

- **Medieval England.** List notes the presence of "money changers and bankers" from Lombardy operating in London as early as the 14th century (004-017). He also records that "formerly all the coin in circulation in England was foreign" (002-003), a situation remedied by the development of English manufacturing and the protection of English commerce.

The transition from dependence on foreign coin to a national currency paralleled England's broader economic maturation.

- **Mercantilist Specie Policies.** List discusses mercantilist "laws against the exportation of specie and the importation of manufactured goods" (005-002) as a characteristic policy of nations at an early stage of economic development.

He acknowledges that these policies served a protective function while arguing that they were based on an incomplete understanding of national wealth. The real goal, List insists, is not to hoard coin but to build the productive capacity that generates wealth.

- **Suspension of Specie Payments.** List observes that nations under financial pressure may "avoid their fall by suspending specie payments" (023-007), a policy response to crises that reflects the tension between metallic currency constraints and the needs of a developing economy. He treats this as a pragmatic measure rather than a theoretical ideal.

- **German Monetary Sovereignty.** List asserts that a developed German nation must exercise the right to "coin money" (036-011) as part of its economic sovereignty.

This claim situates coinage within his broader program of German economic unification — alongside the Zollverein, the railway system, and protective tariffs, a national monetary regime is one pillar of economic nation-building.
