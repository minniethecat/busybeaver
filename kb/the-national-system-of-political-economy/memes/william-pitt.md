# William Pitt the Elder

## Life and Career

William Pitt the Elder (1708–1778), first Earl of Chatham, was a British Whig statesman who served as Prime Minister (1766–1768) and as Secretary of State during the Seven Years' War (1756–1763). Known as "the Great Commoner" for his refusal of a title until late in life, Pitt was the architect of British imperial strategy during the critical period when Britain displaced France as the dominant global power.

Pitt's foreign policy combined naval expansion, colonial conquest (Canada, India), and support for Britain's commercial interests. He understood, perhaps more clearly than any British statesman before him, the connection between commercial policy, naval power, and national greatness.

## Role in "The National System"

William Pitt appears in List's *National System* as the exemplary statesman — the figure who embodies List's argument that economic policy cannot be separated from national power. List wrote that "William Pitt was the first English statesman who acted on the principle that the commercial and manufacturing supremacy of England must be maintained by political measures." (033-004)

List contrasted Pitt with his predecessor Walpole. Where Walpole had prioritized peace and commercial stability, Pitt understood that Britain's manufacturing supremacy depended on naval power, and that naval power depended on active cultivation of British maritime and commercial interests. This was List's own argument: economic development and national power are mutually reinforcing.

List also noted that "England since William Pitt's time had really cast away the policy of merely commercial treaties and had adopted one of manufacturing and commercial supremacy." (033-005) Pitt represented the transition from a purely commercial to an industrial-imperial conception of British national interest.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **Naval-Commercial Synthesis**: Pitt understood that naval power protects commerce, commerce funds naval power, and manufacturing provides the goods that commerce carries. This was the self-reinforcing system List analyzed as "insular supremacy."

- **Continental Strategy**: Pitt's strategy of subsidizing continental allies (Prussia) while Britain focused on naval and colonial warfare anticipated List's argument that insular powers can pursue strategies unavailable to continental powers.

- **The Navigation System**: Pitt maintained and extended the Navigation Acts that had built British maritime power since Cromwell's time.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment of Pitt was unreservedly admiring. He presented Pitt as proof that successful statesmen intuitively understand what List was trying to make explicit in theory: that national economic policy must serve national productive development, not abstract cosmopolitan principles.

Pitt features in List's comparison of England's statesmen with those of continental powers. Where German princes fought over dynastic claims and territorial borders, English statesmen pursued commercial and manufacturing supremacy with single-minded consistency. The result, after two centuries, was England's global dominance and Germany's fragmentation.
