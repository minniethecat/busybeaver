## Definition

The Navigation Laws (or Navigation Acts) were a series of English statutes, most notably the Navigation Act of 1651, that restricted the carriage of goods to and from England to English-owned and English-crewed ships. In List's political economy, the Navigation Laws represented the strategic use of commercial policy to build national maritime power, protect domestic shipping from foreign competition, and create the conditions for manufacturing supremacy. (015-010)

## In List's System

The Navigation Laws occupy a privileged position in List's analysis as perhaps the single most successful example of protective commercial policy in modern history. List regarded them as a model of strategic policy-making: they simultaneously strengthened England's merchant marine, provided a nursery for naval seamen, suppressed Dutch commercial competition, and channeled colonial trade through English ports. (022-001)

List drew particular attention to the fact that England's Navigation Laws were copied from those of the Hanseatic League and the Dutch Republic—earlier maritime powers that had used similar measures to build their commercial strength. (002-002) The Navigation Laws thus exemplified List's broader argument that successful nations learn from their predecessors and adapt proven policies to their own circumstances.

## List's Argument

List's argument about the Navigation Laws is that they demonstrate the power of deliberate commercial policy to reshape the economic geography of the world. Before the Navigation Acts, the Dutch dominated European carrying trade; after them, English shipping rose to supremacy. This transformation was not the result of natural comparative advantage but of strategic government intervention.

> England has obtained political power by means of her Navigation Laws; and by means of political power she has extended her manufacturing and commercial supremacy. (015-010)

The Navigation Laws, List argued, were not merely a commercial measure but a comprehensive instrument of national development. They built English shipbuilding, trained English seamen, accumulated English capital in shipping and related industries, and ensured that the profits of colonial trade accrued to English rather than foreign merchants. "Priestley said of the English Navigation Laws, it would be just as wise to expect a man to walk without legs as to expect a nation to carry on foreign trade without ships of its own." (033-005)

List also noted the coercive dimension of the Navigation Laws. They were used not merely to protect English shipping but to suppress the shipping of rival nations—most notably the Dutch, whose commercial power was broken in large part by the Navigation Acts and the naval wars they engendered. (022-001) The Navigation Laws were, in List's terms, an instrument of national monopoly as much as of national protection.

## Historical Examples

- **The Dutch Precedent**: The English Navigation Laws were modeled on Dutch and Hanseatic precedents. The Dutch had used similar measures to build their own commercial supremacy in the early seventeenth century, and the English adapted these measures to their own circumstances and ultimately used them to surpass the Dutch. (002-002)

- **England's Maritime Transformation**: Before the Navigation Acts, English foreign trade was largely carried in foreign ships—predominantly Dutch. Within a generation of the Navigation Act of 1651, English shipping had eclipsed Dutch shipping, and London had replaced Amsterdam as the center of European commerce. List saw this transformation as a direct consequence of deliberate policy. (015-010)

- **The American Navigation Laws**: The United States, List noted, had adopted its own navigation laws to protect American shipping from British competition. These measures had "greatly increased the carrying trade" of the United States, demonstrating that the principles underlying the English Navigation Laws were transferable to other nations. (014-002)

- **Germany's Maritime Deficit**: Germany, by contrast, lacked both a unified navigation policy and a significant merchant marine. List argued that Germany must learn from the English and American examples and develop its own maritime capacity through strategic commercial policy. (022-001)

## Contrast with Classical Economics

Adam Smith, despite his general hostility to protective measures, made a notable exception for the Navigation Laws. In a passage that List quoted with approval, Smith acknowledged that "defence is of much more importance than opulence" and that the Navigation Laws, though economically inefficient, were justified on grounds of national security. List seized on this concession as evidence that even the arch-defender of free trade recognized the limits of his own doctrine when confronted with the realities of national competition.
