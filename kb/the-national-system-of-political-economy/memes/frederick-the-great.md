# Frederick the Great

## Life and Career

Frederick II (1712–1786), known as Frederick the Great, ruled Prussia from 1740 until his death. Under his reign, Prussia expanded territorially (Silesia, Polish partitions) and developed the bureaucratic, military, and economic institutions that would later underpin German unification. List cited Frederick as the exemplar of the enlightened monarch who understood the connection between economic development and state power.

List noted "the countless measures whereby Frederick II attracted to his dominions foreign agriculturists and artisans, brought waste lands into cultivation, and promoted manufactures" (007-007). Frederick's policy anticipated List's own prescriptions: immigration of skilled workers, infrastructure investment, protective tariffs, and state support for industry.

## Role in "The National System"

Frederick the Great appears in List's analysis as proof that successful German economic development was possible — that the German states were not doomed to remain agricultural dependencies of industrial England and France. Frederick's Prussia showed what a determined state could achieve.

List's specific observations:

- **Skilled immigration**: Like Edward III, Frederick actively recruited skilled workers (Huguenots, Protestants from Catholic territories) whose knowledge enriched Prussian manufacturing.

- **State capacity**: Frederick built the Prussian bureaucracy into an instrument of economic policy — collecting accurate statistics, enforcing commercial regulations, and planning infrastructure.

- **The lesson for Germany**: If one Prussian king could develop manufacturing through deliberate policy, a unified German customs union could do so at vastly greater scale.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **Enlightened Absolutism in Economics**: Frederick applied the rational, state-directed methods of enlightened absolutism to economic development, treating manufacturing promotion as a function of state.

- **Agricultural-Industrial Balance**: Frederick understood that agricultural improvement and manufacturing development must proceed together — a point List developed systematically.

## List's Assessment

List's assessment of Frederick was positive but contextual. Frederick demonstrated the *possibility* of state-directed development in German lands, but his achievement was limited by the small scale of Prussia and the absence of a national German market. The task List set for his own generation — the Zollverein and national economic unity — would extend Frederick's methods to all of Germany.

"Frederick the Great like Joseph II" (012-011) understood the necessity of protection, but their efforts were constrained by the political fragmentation List sought to overcome.
