# Edward III of England

## Life and Career

Edward III (1312–1377) reigned as King of England from 1327 until his death. While traditionally remembered for his military campaigns in the Hundred Years' War (Crécy, Poitiers), List focused on Edward's economic policy — specifically, his decision to import Flemish weavers to establish a domestic English woolen manufacturing industry.

Before Edward III, England's economic role was that of a raw material exporter: it produced the finest wool in Europe but exported it to Flanders to be woven into cloth. English wool was the foundation of Flemish wealth while English farmers and landowners captured only a fraction of the value. Edward "conceived the opinion that a nation might perform much more useful and noble functions than those of exporting raw wool and importing woollen cloth" (002-004).

## Role in "The National System"

Edward III is, in List's historical narrative, the founding figure of English industrial policy. He was the first English monarch to understand that national wealth depends not on natural endowments (fine wool) but on the *capacity to transform* raw materials into finished goods (woolen cloth).

Edward's key actions included:

- **Importation of Flemish weavers**: Edward actively recruited skilled textile workers from Flanders, offering them protection, privileges, and freedom from guild restrictions. This was, in List's terms, the deliberate importation of "mental capital."

- **Protective measures**: Edward restricted the export of raw wool and the import of finished cloth, creating a protected market for the nascent English textile industry.

- **Long-term vision**: "The measures of Edward III" (010-004) set in motion a multi-century process of industrial development. List estimated that it took over 400 years for English manufacturing to reach full maturity — a time horizon no private investor could contemplate.

## Key Ideas or Contributions

- **Infant Industry Protection**: Edward III's policy was the first systematic example of what List would later theorize as infant industry protection — temporary trade restrictions to nurture domestic manufacturing until it can compete internationally.

- **Mental Capital Acquisition**: By importing skilled workers, Edward demonstrated that nations can accelerate development by acquiring knowledge from more advanced regions.

## List's Assessment

"Edward III comprehended this like Elizabeth" (012-011) — List paired Edward and Elizabeth as the two monarchs who understood the connection between manufacturing and national power. Where Edward initiated the protective system, Elizabeth consolidated it. Together, they spanned the 14th to 17th centuries, the period during which England transformed from a peripheral wool exporter to a manufacturing power.

List's assessment reflects his broader argument: England's industrial supremacy was not a natural outcome of free markets but the deliberate product of centuries of national economic policy. The free trade doctrines England later preached to the world were developed only *after* protection had done its work.
