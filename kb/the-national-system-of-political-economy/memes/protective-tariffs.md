# Protective Tariffs

## Definition

**Protective Tariffs** (or Protective Duties) are import taxes designed not primarily for revenue but to shield a nation's developing manufacturing industries from competition by more advanced foreign producers. In Friedrich List's system, they are a *temporary* instrument of national development — to be gradually reduced and eventually eliminated once domestic industry achieves manufacturing maturity.

> "Any tariff system which completely excludes competition is harmful; a moderate protective system which allows competition while cushioning its destructive effects is the correct policy." (P04-007)

List distinguished protective tariffs from both prohibitive mercantilism (which stifles all competition and breeds inefficiency) and absolute free trade (which destroys infant industries before they can develop).

## In List's System

Protective tariffs are the policy consequence of List's theory of productive powers. If manufacturing power is the engine of national development, and if free trade between unequal partners destroys the weaker partner's manufacturing, then temporary protection is a rational investment in future productive capacity.

List identified the conditions under which protective tariffs are justified:

1. **The nation must have the natural prerequisites for manufacturing**: A temperate climate, sufficient population, access to raw materials, and a coastline or navigable rivers for commerce. Nations lacking these conditions (e.g., tropical colonies, sparsely populated territories) should not attempt protection.

2. **Protection must be moderate and graduated**: Duties should be high enough to give domestic producers a price advantage, but low enough to maintain competitive pressure. List specifically opposed prohibitive duties that create monopolies.

3. **Protection must be temporary**: Once domestic manufacturing reaches the point where it can compete internationally, duties should be progressively reduced. The goal is free trade among equals, not permanent protection.

4. **Protection should target manufacturing, not agriculture**: Agricultural protection raises food prices for industrial workers and undermines the manufacturing sector it is meant to serve.

## List's Argument

- **The infant industry logic**: The cost of protective tariffs (higher prices for consumers in the short run) is "made good by the gain in productive power." (P03-009) A nation that pays slightly more for manufactured goods while building its own industry is investing in its future, not wasting resources.

- **Historical precedent**: Every major industrial power used protection during its rise. England's Navigation Acts and woolen duties, Colbert's tariffs in France, Hamilton's protective system in America — none would have industrialized without temporary protection.

- **The value sacrifice is temporary**: "The sacrifice of value is caused by protective duties, but it is made good by the gain in productive power." (P03-009) Once domestic manufacturing matures, prices fall below what imports would have cost, and the nation has gained both cheaper goods and productive capacity.

- **Manufacturing begets everything else**: Protective tariffs are not a subsidy to a single sector but an investment in the sector that stimulates development across the entire economy — agriculture, commerce, transportation, science, and national defence.

## Historical Examples

- **England (Edward III to Victoria)** : The classic case. England protected its woolen manufactures for over 400 years, from the 14th century through the Industrial Revolution, only turning to free trade advocacy after achieving undisputed manufacturing supremacy.

- **The German Zollverein**: The customs union that List championed as the first step toward German industrial unity. Internal free trade combined with external protection created the largest protected market in continental Europe.

- **The United States (Hamilton to Clay)** : The American System of protective tariffs built New England's textile industry and Pennsylvania's iron industry behind tariff walls through the early 19th century.
