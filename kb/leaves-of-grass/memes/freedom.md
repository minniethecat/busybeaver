## Overview

Freedom in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* operates on multiple registers simultaneously: political (the abolition of slavery, democratic self-governance), existential (the soul's liberation from convention and constraint), and aesthetic (free verse as formal freedom). Whitman's freedom is not merely negative (freedom from) but exuberantly positive — "freedom and extrication" (002-0643), the release into fuller being. Throughout the poems, freedom is the condition in which the self, the nation, and the soul achieve their fullest expression.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Freedom is a persistent theme, woven through the political and spiritual dimensions of the poetry. Whitman speaks of "life untold, and of freedom and extrication" (002-0643) — freedom as the release of pent-up possibilities. His celebration reaches an ecstatic pitch: "I swear I think now that every thing without exception has an eternal soul! … We have voided all but freedom and all but our own joy" (002-2235). Freedom is not a condition to be achieved but a reality to be recognized — it is already present in every soul.

The theme of physical and political freedom — emancipation from chattel slavery — runs through the collection with moral urgency. Whitman's free verse itself enacts freedom at the formal level: the long, unrhymed, un-metered lines break from European poetic traditions as America broke from European political traditions.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's concept of freedom synthesizes several traditions. Jeffersonian democratic theory — the rights of man, the dignity of the individual — provides the political foundation. Transcendentalist emphasis on self-reliance and nonconformity (Emerson: "Whoso would be a man must be a nonconformist") informs the existential dimension. Abolitionist discourse gives Whitman's freedom its most urgent political referent. And Romanticism's celebration of the unfettered imagination — the poet as liberator — provides the aesthetic framework.
