## Overview

"Song of the Answerer" occupies BOOK IX of the 1891–92 Deathbed Edition of *Leaves of Grass*, though its first version appeared as "Poem of the Poet" in the 1856 edition. The poem is one of Whitman's most direct meditations on the nature and function of the poet — not the poet as maker of beautiful verses, but the poet as "Answerer," a figure who receives the signs of the world and translates them for humanity. The poem belongs to the 1855–56 cluster that established Whitman's prophetic persona, alongside the early "Song of Myself" and "Starting from Paumanok."

The Answerer is both shaman and democrat: he stands before every seeker "face to face," takes their hand, and answers not only for individuals but "for him that answers for all" (006-001). The poem articulates a theory of poetic knowledge in which the poet's authority derives not from learning or technique but from an almost occult receptivity to the "signs" that emanate from nature, society, and the cosmos.

## Themes

### The Poet as Cosmic Mediator

Whitman's Answerer is an intermediary between the world's meanings and humanity's need for meaning. The poem opens, "Now list to my morning's romanza, I tell the signs of the Answerer" (006-001), establishing the poet as one who reads and transmits signs that others cannot decipher. This is a radically democratic version of the prophetic role: the Answerer does not possess privileged revelation but rather an acute sensitivity to what is already present — "the profound earth and its attributes and the unquiet ocean" (006-001).

### Signs and Interpretation

The poem develops a semiotics of the natural and social world. Everything is a sign: "Beautiful women, the haughtiest nations, laws, the landscape, people, animals" — all "tell" something that only the Answerer can fully receive. Whitman collapses the distance between sign and meaning, asserting that signs are not arbitrary but carry their meanings within themselves for those who can listen.

### Democratic Knowledge

Crucially, the Answerer's knowledge is accessible: "Him all wait for, him all yield up to, his word is decisive and final, / Him they accept, in him lave, in him perceive themselves as amid light" (006-001). The Answerer does not rule over others but serves them; his answers are not impositions but recognitions of what people already dimly know. This is the democratic poet — one who speaks for all because he listens to all.

## Structure & Form

"Song of the Answerer" is structured as a sustained apostrophe to the figure of the Answerer, blending declaration, catalogue, and lyric meditation. Key formal features include:

- **"Morning's romanza" frame**: The poem is presented as a morning song (romanza), linking it to traditions of aubade and dawn poetry while grounding it in Whitman's characteristic emphasis on freshness and beginning.

- **Catalogic enumeration**: The poem employs Whitman's signature technique of listing — nations, landscapes, people, animals — to create a sense of total inclusiveness.

- **Direct address**: The second-person mode ("Tell him to send me the signs") creates an intimate, conversational quality that enacts the poem's theme of face-to-face encounter.

- **Circular structure**: The poem returns to its "morning's romanza" refrain, creating a sense of ritual recurrence — the Answerer's song is eternal, sung anew each day.

## Key Passages

The poem's opening establishes its programmatic statement about the nature of the poet:

> Now list to my morning's romanza, I tell the signs of the Answerer,
> To the cities and farms I sing as they spread in the sunshine before me.
> — [006-001]

Whitman's description of the Answerer's universal receptivity creates a compelling image of poetic consciousness:

> Him all wait for, him all yield up to, his word is decisive and final,
> Him they accept, in him lave, in him perceive themselves as amid light,
> Him they immerse and he immerses them.
> — [006-001]

The poet's transformative vision appears in the concluding catalogue of what the Answerer receives and reorders:

> He puts things in their attitudes,
> He puts to-day out of himself with plasticity and love,
> He places his own times, reminiscences, parents, brothers and
> sisters, associations, employment, politics, so that the rest
> — [006-001]

## Publication History

"Song of the Answerer" first appeared as "Poem of the Poet" in the **1856 edition** of *Leaves of Grass*, the second edition of the book. The 1856 edition was smaller than the landmark 1855 first edition but contained several important new poems that developed Whitman's theory of poetry. The poem underwent revisions and its current title in subsequent editions before reaching its final form in the 1891–92 **Deathbed Edition**, where it occupies BOOK IX (chapter 6).

## Critical Reception

"Song of the Answerer" has received less individual critical attention than "Song of Myself" or the Lincoln elegies, but it occupies an important place in Whitman's poetological statements — poems that theorize the nature of poetry itself. Scholars of Whitman's 1850s period recognize it as part of the cluster of poems (alongside sections of the 1855 Preface) in which Whitman worked out his conception of the poet's role in a democratic society. The poem's emphasis on "signs" has attracted attention from critics working in semiotic and hermeneutic traditions.
