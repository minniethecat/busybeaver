## Overview

The Soul is one of the most pervasive and foundational concepts in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*. Whitman presents the soul not as an abstract theological principle but as an intimate, palpable presence — a "mistress," a companion, the immortal essence that animates the body and connects every individual to the cosmos. The soul is simultaneously personal and universal: "Clear and sweet is my soul, and clear and sweet is all that is not my soul" (002-0070).

## In Whitman's Poetry

The soul appears across virtually every major poem in *Leaves of Grass*, but it is in "Starting from Paumanok" and "Song of Myself" that Whitman most directly establishes its role. In the opening book, he declares that every object in the universe "has reference to the soul" (001-240), establishing the soul as the universal point of convergence. He personifies it dramatically: "here comes my mistress the soul" (001-086), and ties it directly to his poetic project — "I think I shall then supply myself with the poems of my soul and of immortality" (001-094).

In "Song of Myself," the soul becomes the dialogue partner: "I loafe and invite my soul" (002-0006) opens the poem's meditative journey. Whitman frames the soul-body relationship as one of mutual respect: "I believe in you my soul, the other I am must not abase itself to you, / And you must not be abased to the other" (002-0110). The soul is also the vehicle of mystical vision: "My voice goes after what my eyes cannot reach" (002-0729), and "I fly those flights of a fluid and swallowing soul" (002-1026).

Whitman repeatedly links soul to body — "life return in the body and the soul" (001-255) — refusing the Puritan separation of these realms. The soul "includes and is the soul" (001-258) of every person, democratizing spiritual experience.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's treatment of the soul draws deeply from Transcendentalism, particularly Ralph Waldo Emerson's concept of the Over-Soul — a universal spiritual essence in which every individual soul participates. But where Emerson's Over-Soul is abstract and philosophical, Whitman's soul is embodied, erotic, and democratic. It belongs equally to every person ("how superb and how divine is your body, or any part of it," 001-259). This democratization of the soul reflects both Quaker notions of inner light and the radical egalitarianism of 19th-century American democracy. Whitman also echoes Romanticism's celebration of subjective experience, but grounds it in the physical — the soul is not opposed to the body but continuous with it.

## Related Concepts

The Soul in Whitman's cosmos is inseparable from several other key concepts:

- **The Body**: Whitman refuses the soul/body dualism — the body is "superb and divine" (001-259), and soul and body return together after death
- **Democracy**: The universal possession of soul by every individual underpins Whitman's democratic vision
- **Death & Immortality**: The soul is the immortal element — it "includes and is the soul" (001-258), indifferent to death
- **Nature**: The soul is evidenced through natural observation — "the unseen is proved by the seen" (002-0071)
- **The Self**: The soul is the deepest layer of Whitman's expansive, cosmic self
