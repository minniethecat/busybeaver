# Virginia

**Virginia**, the "Old Dominion," occupies a complex place in Walt Whitman's poetic geography. Named in 15 corpus passages, it is among the most frequently referenced states in *Leaves of Grass*. For Whitman, Virginia was simultaneously the birthplace of American liberty — the soil of Washington, Jefferson, and the Revolution — and the heart of the slaveholding South. This duality gives Virginia's appearances in the poetry a particular charge, especially in the Civil War years and their aftermath.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Virginia enters Whitman's catalogues in varied guises. Early in *Leaves of Grass*, the "Virginian" appears alongside the Pennsylvanian and Carolinian in Whitman's roll call of American types (001-288). The state surfaces in more specific contexts as the collection unfolds: a "planter's son returning" to Virginia soil (007-069), and darkly, a "scaffold in Virginia" — an allusion to the state's history of punishment and, by implication, the violence of the slave system (013-513).

Whitman's wartime perspective transforms the Virginia landscape into a theater of conflict. The state, home to Richmond (the Confederate capital) and the site of more Civil War battles than any other, becomes in Whitman's imagination a ground of national sacrifice. His reference to "Virginia — The West" (014-1290) captures the state's transitional geography — neither fully North nor South, neither coastal nor frontier — a borderland where the nation's contradictions were fought out in blood.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Virginia was the first permanent English colony in North America (Jamestown, 1607) and the largest state by population at the time of the American Revolution. In Whitman's era, it had become deeply divided: the western counties, opposed to secession, would break away to form West Virginia in 1863. The state was the primary battleground of the Civil War, with major campaigns fought at Bull Run, Fredericksburg, Chancellorsville, the Wilderness, and Petersburg. Richmond fell to Union forces in April 1865, days before Lee's surrender at Appomattox Court House — both events occurring on Virginia soil.

## Symbolic Meaning

Virginia embodies the **American paradox** in Whitman's symbolic landscape. It is the state of founding fathers and enslaved people, of democratic ideals and aristocratic plantations, of Union and secession. When Whitman names "the Virginian" among his American types, he is claiming even the divided South for the democratic whole. The "scaffold in Virginia" and the returning planter's son are fragments of a story Whitman never fully tells — the story of American slavery and its legacy — but their presence in the poetry marks the fault line that runs through the republic's foundation.
