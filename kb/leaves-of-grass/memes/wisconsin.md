# Wisconsin

**Wisconsin** appears in 4 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it occupies the northernmost reach of Whitman's Midwestern catalogue. Opening with "Chants of Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Iowa, Wisconsin and Minnesota" (001-054), the state is named among the first cohort of the American interior — those states whose very names, in Whitman's hands, become a kind of music.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Wisconsin is named in the foundational catalogue of "Starting from Paumanok" (001-054), appearing as the penultimate state in a list that moves westward and northward across the Midwest. The "Wisconsinese'" joins Whitman's roll call of American types (008-140), the demonym — like "Ohioan's" and "Illinoisian's" — celebrating regional identity as a form of democratic citizenship. The poet's geographic intention is explicit: "To Wisconsin, Iowa, Minnesota, to sing their songs" (014-1025), the voice moving from state to state, gathering each into the American chorus. And in the agricultural catalogue, Wisconsin contributes its harvest: "Harvest the wheat of Ohio, Illinois, Wisconsin, every barbed spear" (015-0647), the wheat imagined as a weapon of peace — the "barbed spear" that feeds rather than wounds.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Wisconsin was admitted as the 30th state in 1848, carved from the Northwest Territory. In Whitman's era, it was a frontier of wheat cultivation, dairy farming, and timber harvesting — its landscape shaped by glaciation (the state's thousands of lakes) and its economy by German and Scandinavian immigration. The state was a free state, and its identity was shaped by progressive politics — the Republican Party was founded in Ripon, Wisconsin, in 1854 — and by the labor movements of its industrializing cities. Wisconsin's location on the Great Lakes (bounded by Lake Superior to the north and Lake Michigan to the east) gave it a distinctive geography of water and forest that set it apart from the prairie states to the south.

## Symbolic Meaning

Wisconsin embodies the **northern reach** of the Midwestern catalogue in Whitman's symbolic geography. If the catalogue begins with Ohio and moves westward, Wisconsin and Minnesota mark the northern limit — the place where the prairie begins to transition into the forests and lakes of the North Woods. The "Wisconsinese'" is a citizen of this transitional zone, neither fully of the prairie nor of the eastern woodlands, but a distinct type in the American mosaic. The state's wheat, named alongside Ohio's and Illinois's, participates in the agricultural abundance that Whitman's poetry celebrates as the material basis of democratic community.
