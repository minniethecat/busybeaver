# Georgia

**Georgia** appears in 8 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it figures as a representative southern state in the poet's panoramic catalogues of American geography. For Whitman, Georgia was the cotton South — a place of agricultural abundance, coastal harbors, and the sweet potato fields of the Atlantic seaboard. The state participates in his democratic vision of a continent united by commerce, agriculture, and shared national identity.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman's Georgia references function within his catalogue technique — the enumerative lists of American places that constitute one of his signature poetic devices. In "Starting from Paumanok," the Georgian stands alongside the Louisianian as equally close to the poet: "The Louisianian, the Georgian, as near to me, and I as near to him" (001-301). This democratic proximity collapses geographical distance, asserting that regional identity is no barrier to shared American fellowship.

In the Song of Myself catalogues, Georgia participates in the great inventory of American types: "leggings, a Louisianian or Georgian" (002-0456) — the state reduced to metonym, a single figure standing for a whole culture. The war-haunted "Our Old Feuillage" places Georgia wagoners among the nocturnal landscape: "The camp of Georgia wagoners just after dark, the supper-fires" (007-053), evoking the logistics of the Civil War that cut through the southern states.

Georgia also appears in Whitman's litanies of agricultural production: "golden the sweet potato of Georgia and the Carolinas" (015-0654), and in his gestures of poetic offering: "to Kentucky, to the Carolinas and Georgia to sing theirs" (014-1028). The Atlantic coastline provides the final locus: "I coast off Georgia, I coast up the Carolinas" (021-0470), the poet imagining himself aboard a coaster vessel tracing the American shoreline.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Georgia, the last of the original Thirteen Colonies (founded 1733) and the fourth state to ratify the Constitution (1788), was in Whitman's era a state defined by the cotton economy, the plantation system, and its Atlantic port at Savannah. The state seceded in 1861 and suffered General Sherman's March to the Sea in 1864, which devastated its infrastructure and economy. By the time of the 1891–92 "Deathbed" Edition of *Leaves of Grass*, Georgia was deep into Reconstruction and the beginnings of the New South. The sweet potato, which Whitman singles out for praise, was a staple crop of the Georgia coastal plain, cultivated alongside cotton, rice, and indigo.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Whitman's symbolic geography, Georgia represents the **agricultural South** — the warm, fertile Atlantic seaboard that produces "golden" harvests. It is one face of the American whole, neither condemned nor idealized, but simply named and claimed as part of the continent-spanning democratic project. The Georgian wagoner beside his supper-fire, the sweet potato fields, the Atlantic coasting vessels — these are fragments of a larger mosaic, each contributing its particularity to Whitman's vision of national unity through diversity. Georgia is what Whitman's catalogues affirm: that every place has its place in the American song.
