## Overview

[[democracy|Democracy]] is arguably the single most encompassing concept in [[walt-whitman|Walt Whitman]]'s poetic universe. More than a political system, democracy for Whitman was a spiritual principle, an aesthetic method, and a vision of human relationship. His famous declaration in the 1855 Preface to *Leaves of Grass* — that "the United States themselves are essentially the greatest poem" — rests on the conviction that democracy is not merely a form of government but a way of being in the world, one that the poet must both celebrate and help bring into existence.

Whitman's democracy is radically inclusive. It embraces not only citizens and voters but the body, sexuality, the natural world, and even death itself. The democratic self that Whitman sings is porous, receptive, and egalitarian — a self that contains multitudes because democracy requires it. This vision distinguishes Whitman from nearly every other 19th-century American writer: his democracy is not a compromise between competing interests but a cosmic principle of connection and mutual recognition.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Democracy appears across the entire span of Whitman's work, from the earliest editions to the Deathbed Edition. Rather than a single "democracy poem," Whitman's democratic vision is distributed throughout the book, manifesting in:

- **Catalogues as democratic form**: The long enumerations of places, peoples, occupations, and things in "Song of Myself," "Starting from Paumanok," and "Salut au Monde!" are formal enactments of democratic inclusivity — every item receives equal attention, no hierarchy of significance.

- **"Song of Myself"** (BOOK III): The poem's central gesture — "I celebrate myself, and sing myself, / And what I assume you shall assume, / For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you" — is a democratic declaration. The self that Whitman celebrates is not an exceptional individual but a representative one, a self whose very openness makes democracy possible.

- **"For You O Democracy"**: A shorter poem that explicitly links democratic commitment to comradeship and affection: "I will make divine magnetic lands, / With the love of comrades, / With the life-long love of comrades."

- **"By Blue Ontario's Shore"** (BOOK XXIII): Contains Whitman's most sustained prose-like meditation on the poet's role in a democracy, arguing that "the poets of the kosmos" must "advance through all the laws of the material world" to give democratic nations their spiritual foundation.

- **"Song of the Broad-Axe"** (BOOK XII): The axe shapes not only material civilization but democratic character, producing "the great city" where "the citizen is always the head and ideal."

The "resolute warlike One" of "Starting from Paumanok" (PN 001-105) is an early intimation of democracy's martial defense — a note that would become tragically relevant with the Civil War.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's democratic vision draws on several intellectual currents:

- **Transcendentalism**: Ralph Waldo Emerson's insistence on the divinity of the individual self ("Self-Reliance," 1841) provided Whitman with the philosophical foundation for his democratic individualism. But where Emerson's self-reliance tends toward solitude, Whitman's democratic self is constitutively social — it needs others to be itself.

- **Jacksonian Democracy**: The expansion of white male suffrage in the 1820s–1840s created a political culture in which the "common man" was the central figure. Whitman's celebration of the working person — the carpenter, the boatman, the blacksmith — is a poetic extension of Jacksonian rhetoric, but Whitman radicalizes it by extending democratic dignity to women, African Americans (however imperfectly), and even the non-human world.

- **Quaker Thought**: Whitman's mother's family was Quaker, and the Quaker emphasis on the "inner light" — the direct, unmediated access of every soul to the divine — informs his democratic spirituality. Every person, in Whitman's vision, has an inner light worth singing.

- **French Revolutionary Thought**: The ideals of *liberté, égalité, fraternité* echo throughout Whitman's poetry, particularly in his emphasis on comradeship (fraternité) as democracy's emotional core.

What distinguishes Whitman from all these sources is his insistence that democracy requires a new kind of poetry — not verse about democratic themes but a poetic form that is itself democratic: open, inclusive, non-hierarchical, and endlessly capacious.

## Key Passages

> I celebrate myself, and sing myself,
> And what I assume you shall assume,
> For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you.
> — [002-001]

The opening of "Song of Myself" is democracy in three lines: the self is celebrated not as an exception but as a representative, and its atoms — its very substance — are shared property.

> I will make the continent indissoluble,
> I will make the most splendid race the sun ever shone upon,
> I will make divine magnetic lands,
> With the love of comrades,
> With the life-long love of comrades.
> — [021-770]

From "Thou Mother with Thy Equal Brood" (BOOK XXXI): democracy's ultimate expression is not law but love — "the life-long love of comrades" — a spiritual bond that makes political union possible.

> Where the citizen is always the head and ideal, and President, Mayor, Governor, and what not, are agents, and no more—
> — [009-200]

From "Song of the Broad-Axe," Whitman's most direct statement of democratic political theory: the citizen is the sovereign; elected officials are servants, not masters.

## Related Concepts

- **[[liberty|Liberty]]**: The freedom that democracy guarantees and requires — but for Whitman, liberty is also an inward condition of the soul.
- **Equality**: The principle that every self is worthy of celebration, which undergirds Whitman's catalogic method.
- **[[comrade|Comradeship]]**: Democracy's emotional core — the "love of comrades" that binds citizens to one another.
- **[[self|The Self]]**: The democratic individual who contains multitudes and through whose expansiveness democracy becomes possible.
- **[[freedom|Freedom]]**: The political condition and spiritual state that democracy enables.
