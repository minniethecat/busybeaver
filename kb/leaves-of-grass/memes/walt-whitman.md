## Overview

Walt Whitman (1819–1892) is the "I" at the center of *Leaves of Grass* — simultaneously the historical person born on Long Island and the mythic persona who declares himself "a kosmos, of Manhattan the son" (002-0649). This doubling is the engine of the entire poetic project: the actual Whitman (printer's apprentice, newspaper editor, Civil War hospital volunteer) becomes the archetypal Democratic Self, large enough to contain multitudes.

Whitman published the first edition of *Leaves of Grass* in 1855 — twelve untitled poems, including what would become "Song of Myself" — and spent the remaining thirty-seven years of his life expanding, revising, and rearranging the collection through six major editions, culminating in the 1891–92 "Deathbed" Edition that serves as this wiki's source text. No other figure in American letters so thoroughly fused life and work: the book *is* the man, and the man insisted on this identification.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman inserts himself into the poetry with unprecedented directness. "Walt Whitman, a kosmos, of Manhattan the son" (002-0649) — the self-naming inaugurates a new poetic mode where the poet is protagonist, chorus, and stage.

"Salut au Monde!" (BOOK VI) opens with an intimate address to the poet himself: "O take my hand Walt Whitman! / Such gliding wonders! such sights and sounds!" (003-003). The poem proceeds through a series of questions — "What widens within you Walt Whitman?" (003-007), "What do you hear Walt Whitman?" (003-029), "What do you see Walt Whitman?" (003-061) — turning the poet into a sensorium for the entire world.

By "By Blue Ontario's Shore" (BOOK XXIII), the self-reference has become political assertion: "be at ease with me — I am Walt Whitman, liberal and / lusty as Nature" (015-1263). The poet's name is a credential, a guarantee of democratic authenticity.

Throughout the collection, the name "Walt Whitman" functions as more than authorial signature — it is a character, a philosophical claim, and a democratic act. To name oneself in poetry is to assert that a common American life deserves epic treatment.

## Historical & Biographical Context

| Event | Date | Significance |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Born, West Hills, Long Island | 1819 | "Starting from Paumanok" — the fish-shaped island becomes Whitman's mythic origin |
| Leaves of Grass, 1st edition | 1855 | Twelve poems, self-published, Emerson's famous letter of praise |
| Civil War hospital service | 1862–1865 | Volunteer nurse in Washington D.C. hospitals; *Drum-Taps* (1865) and the Lincoln elegies |
| Lincoln assassination | 1865 | "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd" and "O Captain! My Captain!" |
| Leaves of Grass, "Deathbed" Edition | 1891–92 | The final, authoritative text — 389 poems, the life's work complete |
| Death, Camden, New Jersey | 1892 | Buried in Harleigh Cemetery in a tomb he designed himself |

Whitman's working life — printer, teacher, journalist, carpenter — grounded his poetry in the labor and craft of common Americans. His volunteer nursing during the Civil War (visiting tens of thousands of wounded soldiers) deepened the poetry's confrontation with suffering, death, and comradeship. His lifelong project of expanding a single book through successive editions enacted his belief in organic, evolving identity.

## Themes & Significance

Whitman the person embodies the central themes he writes about:

- **The Democratic Self**: Whitman made his own life the test case for democratic individuality — a common man whose inner life proves as vast and worthy of epic as any king's.

- **The Body**: His frank celebration of the body, sexuality, and physical existence scandalized his era and prefigured modernist candor.

- **Comradeship**: The Civil War hospitals taught Whitman a love he called "adhesiveness" — the deep bond between men that could, he believed, anchor democracy itself.

- **The Poet-Prophet**: Whitman consciously assumed the role of America's national poet, answering Emerson's 1843 call for an American bard who would sing "the new world."

- **Death and Immortality**: His late poems face mortality with equanimity — "Has any one supposed it lucky to be born? / I hasten to inform him or her it is just as lucky to die."
