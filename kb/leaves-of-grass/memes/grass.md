## Overview

Grass is the master symbol of Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* — the image that gives the collection its title and its deepest organizing principle. Grass is democratic (it grows everywhere, on every soil, among all peoples), mortal (it springs from graves), immortal (it renews itself endlessly), and utterly ordinary. Whitman's genius is to take the humblest of plants and make it the vehicle for his most ambitious philosophical claims: that death is continuous with life, that all people are equal, and that the smallest thing contains the universe.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Grass appears most famously in Section 6 of "Song of Myself," where a child's question — "What is the grass?" — prompts the poet's most sustained meditation on symbolism, mortality, and democracy. The grass is "the flag of my disposition, out of hopeful green stuff woven." It is "the handkerchief of the Lord." It grows "among black folks as among white." And most hauntingly, it is "the beautiful uncut hair of graves" — growing from the bodies of the dead, transforming death into new life.

Beyond this celebrated passage, grass imagery runs throughout the collection as a quiet, persistent presence. The "grass-fields of the world" (001-271) represent the pastoral expansiveness of the American continent. The "spear of summer grass" that the poet observes while loafing (002-0007) is the occasion for the entire poetic journey that follows.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's choice of grass as his central symbol is itself a democratic philosophical statement. Unlike the traditional poetic symbols — the rose, the nightingale, the star — grass is common, overlooked, and ubiquitous. This reflects Whitman's conviction that the divine is found in the ordinary, not the exceptional. Transcendentalism's reverence for nature as divine revelation informs the treatment, but Whitman's grass is more embodied and more political — it is not just nature's beauty but nature's democracy. The grass also carries Biblical echoes: "All flesh is grass" (Isaiah 40:6), which Whitman transforms from a memento mori into an affirmation of death's continuity with life.

## Key Passages

> A child said What is the grass? fetching it to me with full hands;
> How could I answer the child? I do not know what it is any more than he.
> I guess it must be the flag of my disposition, out of hopeful green stuff woven.
> — (002-0098–002-0100)

> I bequeath myself to the dirt to grow from the grass I love,
> If you want me again look for me under your boot-soles.
> — (002-1338)

> I lean and loafe at my ease observing a spear of summer grass.
> — (002-0007)
