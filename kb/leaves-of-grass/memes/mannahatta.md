## Overview

Mannahatta — Walt Whitman's preferred indigenous-derived name for Manhattan — is the urban center of gravity in *Leaves of Grass*. "Dweller in Mannahatta my city" (001-006) — the city is personal ("my city"), a possession of the heart. For Whitman, Mannahatta is not just a place but a principle: the democratic city where all peoples, trades, and energies converge in productive chaos.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Mannahatta appears throughout the collection as the poet's home ground. Whitman celebrates its harbor, its crowded streets, its ferries (the Brooklyn Ferry was his daily commute), and its endless diversity. The city is the human counterpart to the natural landscape — equally sacred, equally worthy of celebration. The catalogues of urban life — mechanics, boatmen, immigrants, shoppers, lovers — are Mannahatta's population rendered as poetry.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Mid-19th-century New York was America's largest and most dynamic city, a major port of entry for immigrants, and the commercial capital of the nation. Whitman lived and worked there as a journalist, printer, and poet. He knew its streets intimately. The Brooklyn Bridge had not yet been built, but the ferry that crossed the East River became the occasion for one of his greatest poems ("Crossing Brooklyn Ferry").

## Symbolic Meaning

Mannahatta symbolizes: **democratic urbanism**, **diversity and mixture**, **human energy**, **the poet's home and identity**, and **the city as sacred space**.
