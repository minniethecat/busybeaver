# Illinois

**Illinois**, the Prairie State, appears across 8 corpus passages in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it embodies the agricultural heartland of the American interior. For Whitman, who traveled through the Midwest and was deeply moved by the vast prairie landscapes, Illinois was not merely a political jurisdiction but a state of mind — a place where the scale of the American continent became visible and where the democratic promise of space and abundance found its fullest expression.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Illinois enters Whitman's poetry at the very beginning of his grand catalogue, in one of the most famous enumerations in American verse: "Chants of Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Iowa, Wisconsin and Minnesota" (001-054). This opening list, from "Starting from Paumanok," announces Whitman's intention to sing every state into being — a poetic act of national unification through language. The "Illinoisian" appears later among Whitman's catalogue of American types (008-140), the demonym itself becoming a kind of music.

In the geographic passages, Illinois is a landscape of movement and cultivation: "crossing the prairies out of Illinois and Indiana" (014-0982), the poet (or his persona) traversing the open country that defines the state. The possessive intimacy of "my Illinois fields, and my Kansas fields" (014-1178) claims the land for the poet and, by extension, for the democratic masses he represents. And in the agricultural catalogues, Illinois contributes its harvest: "Harvest the wheat of Ohio, Illinois, Wisconsin, every barbed spear" (015-0647), the wheat imagined as a weapon of peace, feeding the nation.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Illinois was admitted as the 21st state in 1818. In Whitman's lifetime, it was transformed by three forces: the opening of the prairie to commercial agriculture, the rise of Chicago as the great metropolis of the Midwest, and the legacy of Abraham Lincoln — Illinois's most famous son, whose presidency and assassination defined Whitman's political consciousness. The state was a free state during the antebellum period but bordered slave states (Kentucky, Missouri), making it a terrain of the Underground Railroad and sectional tension. Its prairies — once considered barren "desert" by early settlers — proved to be some of the most productive agricultural land on earth, fueling the nation's growth and export economy.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Whitman's symbolic geography, Illinois represents the **democratic interior** — the vast, fertile middle of the continent that sustains the republic. Unlike the coastal states that face outward toward Europe or the Pacific, Illinois faces inward, its significance rooted in the land itself. The prairie, for Whitman, is a democratic landscape: no peaks or valleys assert hierarchy, no ocean defines a boundary. Instead, the land stretches level and open, a physical correlative to democratic equality. "My Illinois fields" — the poet claims as his own not a private estate but the common ground of the nation, held in trust for all.
