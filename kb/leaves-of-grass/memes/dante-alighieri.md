# Dante Alighieri

**Dante Alighieri** (c. 1265–1321), the Florentine poet whose *Divine Comedy* stands as one of the foundational works of Western literature, appears across 3 corpus passages in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*. For Whitman, Dante represented the epic journey of the soul — a literary ancestor whose vision of the afterlife and moral cosmos shaped the tradition Whitman sought to democratize and embody.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman invokes Dante by name in three key passages. In the opening cluster, he places Dante alongside Virgil as one of the "shades" — the great poetic voices whose "myriad memories, poems" are both honored and surpassed by the American bard's living presence (010-038). A later catalogue of literary giants positions Dante in a lineage stretching from Job and Homer through Shakespeare and Tennyson to Emerson (021-1588). And in a third passage, Dante is named with Chaucer among the "flocks of singing birds" — the European tradition that Whitman absorbs and answers with his own expansive song (021-2088). These mentions are brief but strategic: each places Dante within a genealogy Whitman simultaneously reveres and exceeds.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Dante Alighieri was born in Florence around 1265 and died in exile in Ravenna in 1321. His *Divine Comedy* (completed just before his death) reimagined the Christian afterlife as a three-part journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise. Written in vernacular Italian rather than Latin, Dante's epic democratized poetry — a move Whitman would later radicalize by writing in the American vernacular of free verse. Politically active in Florence's factional struggles, Dante knew exile and loss firsthand, experiences that deepened his vision of justice, love, and cosmic order.

## Themes & Significance

Dante matters to Whitman not as a direct influence but as a precedent. The *Divine Comedy* demonstrated that a single poet could attempt a comprehensive vision of human existence — the same ambition that drives *Leaves of Grass*. Dante's journey through the afterlife parallels Whitman's own journey through the American landscape: both are spiritual voyages in which the self encounters the full spectrum of human experience. Where Dante required an elaborate theological architecture, Whitman found his cosmos in a leaf of grass. The contrast illuminates Whitman's democratic mysticism: salvation not through a structured afterlife but through full presence in the here and now.
