# Texas

**Texas** appears in 10 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it serves as a marker of the American continent's vast western and southern reach. For Whitman, Texas was the frontier state par excellence — a place of cotton fields and cattle, of expansive landscapes and the nation's unfinished business of expansion. The state's presence in his catalogues of American places anchors his vision of a continent-spanning democratic republic.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman's Texas references span the personal and the panoramic. One of the most intriguing passages is autobiographical: "Now I tell what I knew in Texas in my early youth" (002-1117) — a line that has puzzled scholars, since Whitman is not known to have visited Texas in his youth. The statement may be fictional, a poetic persona's claimed experience, or trace evidence of a lost episode in the poet's early life.

In the catalogues, Texas appears among the cotton states: "cotton-fields of Alabama and Texas" (007-004), "Down in Texas the cotton-field, the negro-cabins" (007-089). These references place Texas within the plantation economy of the pre-Civil War South, acknowledging the labor — and by implication, the enslaved people — upon which that economy rested. In a later passage, Texas joins the continental sweep: "To Texas and so along up toward California" (014-1029), mapping the westward arc of American movement.

The state also participates in Whitman's vision of American agricultural abundance: "The varied products of Ohio, Pennsylvania, Missouri, Georgia, Texas, and the rest" (010-240). In this litany, Texas is one note among many in the symphony of American production.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Texas entered the Union in 1845 as the 28th state, having existed as an independent republic for nine years following its break from Mexico in 1836. In Whitman's lifetime, Texas was a frontier of cotton cultivation, cattle ranching, and settler expansion — a state defined by its sheer size (the largest in the Union until Alaska's admission) and its contested identity between South and West. The state seceded in 1861 and was readmitted in 1870. Its cotton economy, built on enslaved labor, tied it firmly to the antebellum South; its vast open ranges and mythos of independence tied it equally to the emerging mythology of the West.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Whitman's symbolic geography, Texas represents the **continental vastness** of the American experiment. It is a terminal point of the catalogue — the far southwestern reach of the nation's inhabited space before the land gives way to Mexico. The state's association with cotton roots it in the agrarian America Whitman celebrated, while its scale and frontier character point toward the expansionist future he envisioned. Texas is both a specific place and a measure of national magnitude: to name Texas is to claim the continent.
