## Overview

The Mother occupies a uniquely elevated position in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*. "I say there is nothing greater than the mother of men" (001-115) — Whitman places the mother above all other human roles, including the poet's own. The mother is not merely a domestic figure but a cosmic principle: the generative source, the perfect nurturer, the body from which all life emerges. She is both intensely personal (Whitman's own mother, Louisa Van Velsor Whitman, whom he adored) and archetypally universal.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Mothers appear throughout the collection as figures of primal significance. "And here you are the mothers' laps" (002-0153) — the mother's body is the first ground of human experience. The mother is associated with fertility, earth, and the continuity of generations. Whitman links the mother to the nation itself: America is a mother, nourishing her diverse children.

In the war poems, the mother becomes a figure of grief — the mother who loses her soldier-son, the mother who receives the letter announcing death. This maternal grief is sacred, and Whitman's nursing in the hospitals can be understood as a kind of maternal care: he mothers the wounded soldiers as a mother tends her children.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Whitman's own mother, Louisa Van Velsor Whitman, was the most important person in his life. He lived with her for long periods, cared for her in her old age, and wrote of her with unwavering devotion. His father, Walter Whitman Sr., was a more distant and difficult figure. This biographical reality infuses the poetry's elevation of the maternal — the mother is not an abstraction but a lived reality of unconditional love and nurture.

## Themes & Significance

The Mother embodies: **generation and nurture** (the source of all life), **unconditional love** (the mother's love as the model for democratic affection), **the body** (the mother's body as the first home), **national identity** (America as mother to diverse peoples), and **grief** (the mother's loss as the war's deepest wound).
