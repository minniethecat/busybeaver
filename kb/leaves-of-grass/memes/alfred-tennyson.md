# Alfred Tennyson

**Alfred, Lord Tennyson** (1809–1892), Poet Laureate of Great Britain during much of Victoria's reign, was Walt Whitman's most significant English contemporary. Across 4 corpus passages, Whitman engages with Tennyson as both peer and contrast — the established voice of English poetry against which the American bard defined his own democratic aesthetic.

## Whitman and Tennyson: Parallel Lives

Born in the same year (1809), Whitman and Tennyson traced remarkably different arcs through the 19th century. Tennyson ascended to the laureateship, institutional recognition, and a peerage; Whitman remained an outsider, self-published, controversial, and only gradually recognized. Their parallel lives embody the difference between English literary establishment and American democratic innovation.

## Poetic Contrast

Tennyson's poetry — metrically masterful, melancholic, steeped in classical and medieval reference — represents everything Whitman's free verse rejected. "In Memoriam" and "Ulysses" are poems of exquisite control; *Leaves of Grass* is a poetry of exuberant overflow. Yet Whitman recognized Tennyson's achievement: the English poet had mastered the forms Whitman felt America needed to transcend.

## The Challenge of Influence

For Whitman, Tennyson represented the gravitational pull of English tradition — the temptation to write poetry that sounded "poetic" by inherited standards. Whitman's rejection of meter and rhyme was in part a rejection of Tennyson's model: America required its own music, not English melody transplanted. The "barbaric yawp" was, among other things, an answer to Tennysonian polish.

## Tennyson in Leaves of Grass

Whitman's references to Tennyson are respectful but distant. The American poet acknowledges the Englishman's craft while insisting on a different path. This is not rivalry but differentiation: two poets, one language, two nations, two visions of what poetry can be.

| Aspect | Tennyson | Whitman |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Form | Metered, rhymed | Free verse |
| Tone | Elegiac, controlled | Expansive, ecstatic |
| Audience | Victorian establishment | Democratic masses |
| Nation | England | America |
| Legacy | Victorian poetry | Modern American poetry |
