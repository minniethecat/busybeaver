## Overview

Beauty in *Leaves of Grass* is never merely decorative. For Whitman, the beautiful is a mode of truth — a sensory revelation of the real that carries moral and spiritual force. "A gigantic beauty of a stallion, fresh and responsive to my caresses" [002-0889] exemplifies his characteristic move: beauty is physical, immediate, and encountered in particulars rather than abstract ideals. Yet this physical beauty is never merely physical; it opens onto what Whitman calls "the wonderful beauty" [002-1037] — a radiance that inheres in all things when perceived with sufficient attention and love. His democratic aesthetic extends beauty to bodies and objects that European tradition had excluded from the category: the laborer's physique, the factory district, the "beauty of wood-boys and wood-men" [009-047]. "The flow of beauty is not more welcome or universal than the flow of health" [006-045] — beauty is democratically distributed, a current that runs through all existence rather than a rare quality reserved for the exceptional.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman's treatment of beauty evolves across editions but consistently links it to vigor, truth, and democratic inclusiveness. In the 1855 *Song of Myself*, the "gigantic beauty of a stallion" [002-0889] establishes the paradigm: beauty is first encountered through the senses, in the presence of a magnificent animal, then internalized as the poet refuses the horse's offer of speed and instead contains it within his own being. *I Sing the Body Electric* extends this to the human form: "the beauty of the waist, and thence of the hips, and thence downward toward the knees" [002-2029] anatomizes beauty without prurience, treating the body as a site of wonder rather than shame. "Sanity, beauty" [002-1909] appear as paired qualities — beauty is a form of health, and health is beautiful. In *Song of the Broad-Axe*, beauty shifts to a civic register: "the beauty of all adventurous and daring persons ... the beauty of independence, departure, actions" [009-046–048] locates beauty in character and conduct rather than appearance. The "beauty of person" [002-1850] emerges from "wonderful vigor, calmness" — an integrated excellence of body and spirit. By the later poems, beauty becomes explicitly cosmic: "the soul of earth's, activity's, beauty's, heroism's" [010-043] — beauty is no longer a quality that objects possess but a cosmic principle, co-equal with activity and heroism.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's aesthetics synthesizes several traditions. From Romanticism, particularly Keats ("Beauty is truth, truth beauty") and Shelley, he inherits the conviction that beauty has metaphysical significance. But where Keats's Grecian urn freezes beauty into timeless stasis, Whitman's beauty is always in motion — "the flow of beauty" [006-045]. From Transcendentalism, he takes the idea that beauty is a mode of spiritual perception: "true poets are not followers of beauty but the august masters of beauty" [006-105] echoes Emerson's doctrine of the poet as seer. The classical tradition's association of beauty with proportion and harmony is democratized: beauty resides as fully in "the group of laborers" [002-1827] as in any classical statue. Whitman's most radical departure is his insistence that beauty can coexist with — indeed can inhere in — what convention deems ugly: the body of an old woman, the face of a laborer weathered by sun, the "beauty of independence, departure" [009-048] that is the beauty of refusal and self-determination.

## Key Passages

> A gigantic beauty of a stallion, fresh and responsive to my caresses,
> Head high in the forehead, wide between the ears,
> Limbs glossy and supple, tail dusting the ground,
> Eyes full of sparkling wickedness, ears finely cut, flexibly moving.
> — [002-0889]

> They do not seek beauty, they are sought,
> Forever touch'd by them or close upon them follows beauty, longing, fain, love-sick.
> — [006-119–120]

> The beauty of all adventurous and daring persons,
> The beauty of wood-boys and wood-men with their clear untrimm'd faces,
> The beauty of independence, departure, actions that rely on themselves.
> — [009-046–048]
