## Overview

God in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* undergoes a radical democratization. Whitman's God is not the transcendent, judging deity of Puritan Christianity but an immanent presence diffused through all creation — and most intensely present in the human self. "And nothing, not God, is greater to one than one's self is" (002-1621). This is not atheism but a thoroughgoing pantheism in which every person, every object, and every experience participates in divinity.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman's treatment of God is characteristically audacious. He places God on equal footing with the self — or subordinates God to the self entirely. In "Song of Myself," he declares that "the gods of the antique wars" (002-1327) are lesser than the living, breathing present. He "honor[s] the gods, saluting the sun" (002-1409) but always returns to the immediate and the human. Most tellingly, Whitman includes God in his catalogues of things to be adopted and absorbed: "I adopt each theory, myth, god, and demi-god" (013-579). God is not worshipped but included — one element among many in the democratic assemblage of being.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's unorthodox theology draws from multiple sources. Transcendentalism's concept of the Over-Soul — a universal spirit in which all individual souls participate — is perhaps the closest parallel. Quaker notions of "inner light" (the divine presence within each person) resonate with Whitman's insistence on the self's divinity. Eastern religious thought, particularly Vedantic Hinduism's identification of Atman (individual soul) with Brahman (universal soul), offers striking parallels. Whitman's God is also shaped by his rejection of Calvinist predestination and punitive theology — his God judges no one and excludes no one.

## Related Concepts

- **The Self**: The self is the most complete embodiment of the divine — "nothing, not God, is greater"
- **The Soul**: The soul is the divine element within each person
- **Religion**: Whitman's spirituality rejects institutional religion for direct, embodied experience
- **Nature**: The natural world is saturated with divinity — "the earth and all the stars are for religion's sake"
- **Democracy**: God's presence in every person underwrites democratic equality
