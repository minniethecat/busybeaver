# Pennsylvania

**Pennsylvania**, the Keystone State, appears in 6 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it occupies a central place — both geographically and symbolically — in the poet's vision of America. Founded by William Penn as a haven of religious liberty, Pennsylvania was in Whitman's era an industrial powerhouse and agricultural breadbasket, its identity woven from the coal mines of the anthracite region, the steel mills of Pittsburgh, and the fertile farms of the Great Valley.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Pennsylvania enters Whitman's catalogue through its people: "The Pennsylvanian! the Virginian! the double Carolinian!" (001-288). This opening invocation — placing the Pennsylvanian first — reflects the state's primacy in Whitman's conception of the American type. A later passage names "the Seaside State and of Pennsylvania" (002-2823) in a pairing that links coast and interior. In the economic catalogue, Pennsylvania contributes its varied products alongside Ohio, Missouri, Georgia, and Texas (010-240). The poet's persona moves "down from the great lakes or in Pennsylvania, or on deck along" (014-0984), traversing the state as part of a larger continental journey. And in the agricultural litany, Pennsylvania yields its wool: "clip the wool of California or Pennsylvania" (015-0655), the state's pastoral economy registered in a single tactile image.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Pennsylvania was the second state to ratify the Constitution (1787) and hosted the nation's capital in Philadelphia during the 1790s. By Whitman's era, the state had become an industrial colossus: the anthracite coal fields of the northeast fueled the nation's railroads and factories; Pittsburgh's iron and steel mills shaped the built environment of industrial America; and the state's farmland — especially in the Great Valley and the German-settled southeast — was among the most productive in the nation. Pennsylvania was a free state and a major route on the Underground Railroad. Its role in the Civil War was pivotal: the Battle of Gettysburg (July 1863), fought on Pennsylvania soil, marked the turning point of the conflict.

## Symbolic Meaning

Pennsylvania embodies the **productive republic** in Whitman's symbolic geography. It is a state of making — of coal mined, steel forged, wool clipped, crops harvested. In Whitman's catalogues, Pennsylvania is never merely scenery; it is always a source of value, a contributor to the national abundance. The Pennsylvanian, named first among the American types in Whitman's roll call, represents the citizen as producer — the person whose labor, whether in mine, mill, or field, sustains the democratic experiment. This is Whitman's materialism at its most democratic: the nation is built not of ideas alone but of the tangible goods its people create.
