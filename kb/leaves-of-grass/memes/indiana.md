# Indiana

**Indiana** appears in 6 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, occupying a privileged position in Whitman's poetic geography. It is one of the very first states named in the great catalogue that opens Whitman's project: "Chants of Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Iowa, Wisconsin and Minnesota" (001-054). This placement — second in a list that will eventually encompass the entire continent — grants Indiana a foundational status in the American song.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Indiana's appearances cluster around Whitman's vision of the American interior as the heartland of democratic life. The state is named in the catalogue of chants (001-054), then reappears in the geographic passages that trace movement across the prairies: "crossing the prairies out of Illinois and Indiana" (014-0982), the flat, open landscape of the Midwest imagined as a space of transit and transformation. A later passage makes explicit Whitman's intention to sing each state: "Then to Ohio and Indiana to sing theirs, to Missouri" (014-1026), the poet moving from state to state like a circuit rider of democracy. The "land of Ohio's waters and of Indiana" (014-1296) pairs the two states in a shared landscape of rivers and fields. And in the poem's later reaches, Indiana's people are numbered among the democratic millions: "Ohio's, Indiana's millions, comrades, farmers" (021-0770) — the state's inhabitants claimed as companions in the great American project.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Indiana was admitted as the 19th state in 1816, carved from the Northwest Territory. In Whitman's era, it had been transformed from a frontier of Anglo-American settlement — displacing the Miami, Potawatomi, and other Native peoples — into a settled agricultural state. Its flat, fertile landscape, shaped by glaciation and drained by the Wabash and Ohio rivers, proved ideal for corn and wheat cultivation. The state was a free state, and its location north of the Ohio River placed it squarely within the Union during the Civil War; Indiana contributed over 200,000 soldiers to the Union cause. The state's identity as the "Hoosier State" — a term of uncertain origin — reflected a self-conscious regional culture that Whitman's catalogues helped to codify in the national imagination.

## Symbolic Meaning

Indiana represents the **democratic middle** in Whitman's symbolic geography. It is not a state of dramatic landscapes or coastal commerce; it is a state of farms, small towns, and steady labor — the unglamorous but essential ground of American life. When Whitman places Indiana second in his opening catalogue, he is making a claim: the center matters. The democratic republic is not sustained by its edges but by its middle — by the Indianas that feed the nation, send their sons to its wars, and embody the quiet persistence of American community. "Indiana's millions, comrades, farmers" — in this phrase, Whitman collapses the distinction between citizen and poet, between the state and the people who compose it.
