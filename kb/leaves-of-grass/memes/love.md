## Overview

Love in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* is not confined to romantic or erotic attachment — it expands into a cosmic, democratic, and spiritual force that binds all beings. Whitman's love encompasses the intimate and the universal: the "manly love" of comrades (001-115), the "perfect love" for all nations and peoples (001-290), and the self-love that forms the foundation of his poetic persona. Love is the emotional engine of Whitman's democracy — the force that makes equality felt rather than merely declared.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman announces love as a primary theme from the opening poems. In "Starting from Paumanok," he positions himself as the poet of love in all its dimensions: "And I say it is as great to be a woman as to be a man, / And I say there is nothing greater than the mother of men" (001-115). He pledges to "write the evangel-poem of comrades and of love" (001-121), and asks with startling directness: "who but I should understand love with all its sorrow and joy?" (001-122).

The comradely love — what Whitman called "adhesiveness" — runs throughout the collection as a democratic principle. "Dear son do you think it is love?" (001-163) he asks, before answering that love is painful, excessive, and all-consuming: "It is a painful thing to love a man or woman to excess" (001-165). Yet this excess is the point — love cannot be moderated or rationed. In a magnificent closing gesture, Whitman gathers all humanity: "O all and each well-loved by me! my intrepid nations! … I include you all with perfect love!" (001-289, 001-290).

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's treatment of love synthesizes several traditions. The Platonic ideal of love as ladder to the divine is present but embodied — Whitman's love always returns to the physical. The Christian concept of agape (universal, selfless love) informs the democratic love of all persons equally. But Whitman's most original contribution is the fusion of erotic love with democratic philosophy: the passion that draws lover to lover is the same passion that should bind citizen to citizen in a democracy. This "adhesiveness" — a term borrowed from phrenology — became Whitman's code for the homosocial bonds he saw as essential to American national cohesion.

## Related Concepts

- **Comradeship**: Love between equals — "the manly love of comrades" — is the emotional basis of democracy
- **The Body**: Love is embodied — "the love of the body of man or woman balks account"
- **Democracy**: Love as universal, non-hierarchical connection is the affective core of democratic life
- **The Self**: Self-love is not narcissism but the foundation for loving others
- **Death**: Love persists beyond death — the ultimate affirmation
