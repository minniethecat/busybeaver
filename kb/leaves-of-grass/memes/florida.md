# Florida

**Florida** appears in 8 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it is depicted as a lush, subtropical peninsula of distinctive natural beauty. For Whitman, Florida was a place of green peninsulas, transparent lakes, winter refuge, and orange blossoms — a sensual landscape at the far southeastern reach of the American continent, marking the warm-water frontier of the nation's geographical imagination.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman's Florida references are notable for their sensory richness. The earliest appearance comes in "Song of Myself," where he declares the state's physical form with striking concision: "Florida as it hung trailing down" (002-2461). This image of Florida as a pendant or appendage, "trailing" southward from the continent, captures the peninsula's distinctive geography with the precision of a map rendered in poetry.

"Our Old Feuillage" opens with Florida as a defining marker of the American landscape: "Always Florida's green peninsula — always the priceless delta of Louisiana" (007-003). The "green" qualifier is important — it distinguishes Florida from the cotton-and-tobacco agriculture of the other southern states, emphasizing instead a tropical lushness. The same poem later notes "wintering along the coasts of Florida" (007-133), marking the state's emerging identity as a refuge from northern cold.

In "A Broadway Pageant," Whitman promises that "Florida perfumes shall tenderly come" to Michigan (014-1863) — an olfactory bridge spanning half the continent. The most sustained Florida passage appears in "Thou Mother with Thy Equal Brood": "Again in Florida I float on transparent lakes, I float on the Okeechobee" (021-0465), followed by visions of "upland, or Florida's glades" (021-0028). The final references are domestic and commercial: "Orange Buds by Mail from Florida" (021-1822) and "a sprig of orange buds by mail from Florida" (021-1829) — the state's citrus industry already a fixture of American commerce by the 1880s.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Florida became a U.S. territory in 1821 and the 27th state in 1845, the same year as Texas. In Whitman's lifetime, Florida was sparsely populated and largely undeveloped — a frontier of wetlands, pine barrens, and Seminole resistance. The state seceded in 1861 and was readmitted in 1868. The wintering mentioned by Whitman refers to the early tourism that would eventually transform the state: wealthy northerners escaping the cold, drawn by the climate and the "transparent lakes" the poet evokes. The orange buds mailed from Florida capture the emergence of the citrus industry that would define the state's agricultural identity. Lake Okeechobee, which Whitman names, is the largest lake in the southeastern United States.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Whitman's symbolic geography, Florida represents the **tropical margin** of the American continent — the warm, green, water-rich extreme that balances the cold northern reaches of his catalogues. Where states like Dakota represent the continental interior's austerity, Florida represents abundance and fecundity. The "green peninsula," the "transparent lakes," the perfumes that travel north — these images construct Florida as a place of sensory richness and natural generosity. In Whitman's democratic poetics, even this remote subtropical edge belongs to the whole: its perfumes reach Michigan, its oranges travel by mail, its landscapes are claimed and celebrated in the great inventory of American places.
