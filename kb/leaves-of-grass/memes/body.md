## Overview

The Body in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* is not merely a physical container for the soul — it is celebrated as sacred in its own right, fully equal to the immortal spirit. Whitman revolutionizes 19th-century poetics by refusing the body/soul dualism that dominated Western thought: "I have said that the soul is not more than the body, / And I have said that the body is not more than the soul" (002-1619, 002-1620). Throughout the collection, the body becomes the primary vehicle for democratic, erotic, and spiritual experience.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The body is announced as a central theme from the very opening of the collection. In "Starting from Paumanok," Whitman declares his poetic mission: "I will make the poems of my body and of mortality" (001-093). He immediately poses a rhetorical challenge to the reader: "How can the real body ever die and be buried?" (001-246), followed by an intimate appeal to the reader's own physical reality: "Of your real body and any man's or woman's real body" (001-247).

The body reaches its most sustained celebration in later sections of "Song of Myself." Whitman addresses the reader directly with an almost shocking intimacy: "how superb and how divine is your body, or any part of it!" (001-259). He inverts the traditional hierarchy of spirit over flesh: "the body includes and is the meaning" (001-257), and most radically — "And if the body were not the soul, what is the soul?" (002-1804). The famous declaration "I sing the body electric" (002-1797) captures the body as a site of vitality, energy, and connection to the cosmos. The body is simultaneously personal ("my body," "your body") and universal ("the body electric," "the armies of those I love").

Whitman's treatment extends to laboring bodies — "fill'd a tub for his sweated body and bruis'd feet" (002-0253) — and to the mortal body's final transformation: "life return in the body and the soul, / Indifferently before death and after death" (001-255, 001-256).

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's celebration of the body draws from several intellectual currents. Transcendentalism, particularly Emerson's reverence for nature, provided license to find the divine in the physical. But Whitman goes far beyond Emerson, rejecting any spiritual hierarchy that subordinates the body. His embodied spirituality also echoes Quaker emphasis on inner experience over doctrinal abstraction. The radical egalitarianism of the body — every body is "superb and divine" — mirrors the democratic political philosophy that runs through all of *Leaves of Grass*. Unlike the Romantic poets who often treated the body as a site of decay or a prison for the soul, Whitman presents the body as inseparable from spiritual experience and fully sufficient for transcendence.

## Key Passages

> And nothing, not God, is greater to one than one's self is,
> — (002-1619–002-1621)

> I sing the body electric,
> The armies of those I love engirth me and I engirth them,
> — (002-1797)

> — (001-259)

## Related Concepts

The Body in Whitman's cosmos is inseparable from several other key concepts:

- **The Soul**: Whitman's central innovation is the body/soul equality — "if the body were not the soul, what is the soul?" (002-1804)
- **Democracy**: The sacredness of every individual body underwrites Whitman's democratic egalitarianism
- **Love & Sexuality**: The body is the site of erotic connection — "the love of the body of man or woman balks account" (002-1806)
- **Death & Immortality**: The body returns after death, indifferent, continuous with the soul
- **Labor**: Working bodies — the blacksmith, the boatman, the farmer — are celebrated as fully as contemplative ones
