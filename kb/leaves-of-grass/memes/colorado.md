# Colorado

**Colorado** appears in 8 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it is evoked as a place of dramatic geography — canyons, mountains, high plains, and perpetual snow. For Whitman, Colorado was the vertical West, a landscape defined by its altitude and geological spectacle. His Colorado references cluster around the Platte Canyon and the state's distinctive identity as a realm of mountain men and canyon winds, a counterpoint to the horizontal vastness of the Great Plains.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman's Colorado references begin with elemental force: "Colorado winds!" (001-275), a three-word exclamation that captures the state's essence in sheer meteorological energy. In "Song of the Broad-Axe," Colorado appears in a catalogue of river settlements: "settlements of the Arkansas, Colorado, Ottawa, Willamette" (009-044) — the Arkansas River having its headwaters in the Colorado Rockies.

"Song of the Redwood-Tree" extends the poet's vision "from Puget sound to Colorado south" (011-103), mapping the continent's diagonal reach from the Pacific Northwest to the southern Rockies. In "A Song of the Rolling Earth," Whitman affirms a regional identity: "Colorado men are we" (013-275) — the mountain men of the high country, distinct and self-naming. The most vivid geographical passage appears in "By Blue Ontario's Shore": "Some down Colorado's canons from sources of perpetual snow" (015-0476), evoking the meltwater-fed gorges that carve the Rocky Mountain landscape.

The most specific Colorado reference comes in "Thou Mother with Thy Equal Brood," where Whitman includes a poem subtitle: "[Written in Platte Canyon, Colorado]" (021-0775). Platte Canyon, southwest of Denver, was a well-known scenic destination by the late 19th century, accessible by the Denver, South Park and Pacific Railroad. The poet returns to the canyon imagery in the same poem: "your huge rifts of canyons, Colorado" (021-1474), and envisions the future: "will show there in Nebraska, Colorado, Nevada" (021-0957) — Colorado as part of the unfolding American destiny across the western interior.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Colorado became a territory in 1861 (the same year the Civil War began) and the 38th state in 1876, earning it the nickname "Centennial State." In Whitman's lifetime, Colorado was a frontier of mining (the 1859 Pike's Peak Gold Rush), railroad expansion, and settler migration. The Denver, South Park and Pacific Railroad reached Platte Canyon in the 1870s, opening the region to tourism. The state's economy centered on mining (gold, silver, lead), ranching, and the railroads that connected the mountain West to the national network. The "Colorado men" Whitman invokes were miners, ranchers, railroad workers, and mountaineers — the archetypal western types that populated the American imagination of the frontier.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Whitman's symbolic geography, Colorado represents the **vertical sublime** of the American continent. Where states like Kansas or Iowa embody the horizontal sweep of the Great Plains, Colorado embodies altitude — the "perpetual snow" of the peaks, the "huge rifts of canyons," the winds that sweep down from the mountains onto the high desert. Colorado is the continent's backbone made visible, a place where geology asserts itself with unmistakable force. In Whitman's democratic vision, the Colorado men — the miners and mountaineers of the high country — are as essential to the American whole as the farmers of the prairie and the sailors of the coasts. The state's inclusion in his catalogues affirms that elevation, like latitude and longitude, is a coordinate of American identity.
