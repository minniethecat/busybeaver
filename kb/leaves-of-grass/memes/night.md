## Overview

Night in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* is a richly ambivalent presence — not merely the absence of daylight but a positive realm of mystery, soul, and intimate encounter. Where day is the time of action, labor, and public life, night is the time of the soul's deeper journeys: erotic union, dream vision, cosmic merger, and the contemplation of death. "The damp of the night drives deeper into my soul" (002-0833) — night penetrates where daylight cannot reach.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Night imagery pervades the collection, particularly in the more introspective and mystical passages. In "The Sleepers," night becomes the occasion for a shamanic journey through all forms of sleep — the peaceful, the tormented, the dead. In "Song of Myself," night is the setting for the soul's most intimate encounters: "I believe in you my soul … Loafe with me on the grass, loose the stop from your throat." The night is erotic, secret, and cosmically vast — the darkness is not empty but full of invisible presences.

Whitman's night is also democratic — it falls equally on all, rich and poor, and the sleepers are united in their nightly journey regardless of their daytime stations. This makes night a powerful symbol of the equality that Whitman's poetry seeks to enact.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Night as a poetic topos has a long lineage — from the Biblical "night of the soul" to the Romantic celebration of nocturnal sublimity (Novalis's "Hymns to the Night," Coleridge's midnight meditations). Whitman inherits this tradition but democratizes it: his night is not the privilege of the solitary genius but the common experience of all sleepers. The Quaker emphasis on inner light visible in darkness may also inform Whitman's treatment. And the night journey — the descent into darkness that precedes renewal — echoes mythological patterns (the hero's journey, the shaman's vision quest) that Whitman intuitively recapitulates.
