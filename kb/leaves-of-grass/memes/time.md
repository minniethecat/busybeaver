## Overview

Time in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* is radically reconfigured. Rather than a linear progression of past→present→future, Whitman's time is an eternal present in which all moments coexist. The poet "contain[s] multitudes" not only of identities but of eras — he is simultaneously ancient and newborn, "both old and young, foolish and wise." This collapse of temporality is essential to Whitman's democratic vision: if all moments are equally present, all people in all times are equally alive and equally significant.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman lists "time" among the foundational realities he catalogues — "history, union, faith, identity, time" (001-025) — placing it as a primordial given rather than a philosophical problem. In "Song of Myself," the poet moves freely across time: "I am an acme of things accomplish'd, and I an encloser of things to be." The past is not behind him but within him; the future is already present. This temporal expansiveness mirrors the spatial expansiveness of the democratic self — just as Whitman's "I" encompasses all geographies, it encompasses all eras.

The poem "To Think of Time" is the collection's most sustained meditation on temporality and mortality, but time as a theme pervades the entire work. Whitman's characteristic catalogue technique itself enacts a kind of temporal simultaneity — listing things from different times and places as though they coexist in a single eternal moment.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's treatment of time reflects several influences. Romanticism's valorization of the present moment (Wordsworth's "spots of time") provides a literary precedent. Transcendentalist emphasis on the eternal Now — Emerson's "these roses under my window make no reference to former roses" — is a direct source. Eastern concepts of cyclical time and the illusory nature of temporal sequence (particularly from Hindu and Buddhist thought) offer striking parallels. Whitman's innovation is to make temporal simultaneity a democratic principle: every person in every era has equal claim on presence and significance.

## Related Concepts

- **Immortality**: Time's collapse into eternity makes immortality a present reality
- **Death**: Death is a temporal passage, not an ending
- **The Self**: The self encompasses all times — "I am large, I contain multitudes"
- **History**: All historical eras coexist in the democratic present
- **Nature**: Natural cycles (seasons, tides) model non-linear time
