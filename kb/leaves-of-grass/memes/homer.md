# Homer

**Homer** (c. 8th century BCE), the legendary author of the *Iliad* and *Odyssey*, stands at the fountainhead of Western poetry. In *Leaves of Grass*, Whitman invokes Homer not as a remote classic but as a living precedent — the original bard who demonstrated that poetry could encompass an entire world, a model for Whitman's own ambition to sing the totality of American life.

## Homer as Epic Model

Whitman's catalogues — those long, breathless lists of American types and scenes — have their distant ancestor in Homer's catalog of ships, his enumeration of warriors and nations. Where Homer listed the Greek contingents sailing for Troy, Whitman lists the American workers, landscapes, and experiences that constitute the democratic whole. The impulse is the same: to name everything, to omit nothing.

## The Bardic Tradition

Whitman claimed a place in the line of bards that begins with Homer. The poet as national voice, as keeper of collective memory, as singer of the tribe's identity — this is the Homeric inheritance Whitman consciously assumed. What Homer was to Greece, Whitman would be to America: the poet who gives a people their myth.

## Homer in Whitman's America

Whitman's references to Homer place the classical within the democratic. The ancient bard and the modern American poet share a task: to make the particular universal, to find in local materials — whether the plains of Troy or the streets of [[Mannahatta]] — the stuff of enduring poetry.

## Epic Ambition, Democratic Form

Where Homer's epic required the hexameter and the conventions of oral-formulaic composition, Whitman's democratic epic required free verse — a form as unbounded as the nation it celebrated. The Homeric ambition persists, but the form is transformed: America's epic cannot wear Greece's meter.

| Aspect | Homer | Whitman |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Subject | Heroic age, gods, war | Democratic America, body, self |
| Form | Dactylic hexameter | Free verse, catalog |
| Hero | Achilles, Odysseus | The common person, the self |
| World | Mediterranean, mythic | American continent, actual |
| Legacy | Western epic tradition | Modern American poetry |
