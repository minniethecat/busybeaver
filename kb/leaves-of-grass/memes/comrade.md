## Overview

Comradeship — what Whitman called "adhesiveness" — is one of the most radical and personal concepts in *Leaves of Grass*. It names the intense, passionate bond between equals that Whitman saw as the emotional substrate of democracy. More than friendship, less institutional than marriage, comradeship is a love that binds citizens to one another across differences of class, region, and background. "I will write the evangel-poem of comrades and of love" (001-121) — Whitman announces this as his sacred mission.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The theme of comradeship runs through the entire collection but is concentrated in the "Calamus" cluster. Whitman asks directly: "who but I should be the poet of comrades?" (001-123). The comrade is addressed with startling intimacy — "My comrade!" (001-173) — a cry of recognition and embrace. In "Song of Myself," comradeship expands geographically: "Comrade of Californians, comrade of free North-Westerners … comrade of raftsmen and coalmen, comrade of all who shake hands" (002-0463, 002-0465). The comrade is everywhere, in every trade and region, bound by the adhesive love that makes a nation more than a contract.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman borrowed the term "adhesiveness" from phrenology — the pseudo-science of skull-reading that located different mental faculties in specific brain regions. Adhesiveness was the faculty of attachment, friendship, and social bonding. Whitman transformed this clinical term into a poetic and political concept: adhesiveness was the force that could hold the United States together across its immense geography and fractious politics. The concept also has roots in classical ideals of male friendship (the Sacred Band of Thebes, Achilles and Patroclus) and in the democratic theory of fraternité from the French Revolution.

## Related Concepts

- **Love**: Comradeship is love between equals — "the manly love of comrades"
- **Democracy**: Adhesiveness is the affective glue of democratic society
- **The Body**: Comradeship is embodied — handshakes, embraces, walking together
- **War**: Civil War hospitals deepened Whitman's lived experience of comradeship
- **Equality**: Comradeship presupposes and enacts radical equality
