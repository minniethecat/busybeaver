# Dakota

**Dakota** appears in 5 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it occupies a distinctive register: the remote, the far, the mythic. "Far Dakota" is Whitman's phrase, and the qualifier is essential — Dakota is not a settled place but a distant horizon, a landscape of woods, canyons, and the encounter between American expansion and Native presence. Its most famous appearance is in the title and body of "From Far Dakota's Canyons" (021-0702), a poem dated June 25, 1876 — the date of Custer's defeat at the Little Bighorn, fought on what was then Dakota Territory.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Dakota enters Whitman's verse in the opening poem: "Or rude in my home in Dakota's woods, my diet meat, my drink from the spring" (001-009) — the poet imagining himself into a primitive, self-sufficient life on the remote frontier. This persona is a performance, a Whitman who has shed civilization for the raw materials of existence. A later passage notes "Italian Music in Dakota" (015-1580, 015-1593), a striking juxtaposition of European high culture and American wilderness that captures Whitman's vision of cultural circulation — nothing is too refined for the frontier, nothing too remote for art. The most significant Dakota passage is "From Far Dakota's Canyons [June 25, 1876]" (021-0702), which explicitly links the landscape to the historical event of Custer's Last Stand — a moment of national trauma and Native American victory that resonated through the closing decades of the 19th century.

## Geographic & Historical Context

The Dakota Territory was organized in 1861, encompassing what would later become North and South Dakota, as well as parts of Montana and Wyoming. In Whitman's era, it was a frontier of conflict and transformation: the Black Hills Gold Rush (1874), the Great Sioux War (1876–1877), and the progressive displacement of the Lakota and other Native peoples. The territory was not divided into the two Dakotas until 1889, three years before Whitman's death. Throughout his lifetime, "Dakota" remained a name for a vast, largely unsettled region — a landscape more defined by its indigenous inhabitants and the U.S. military presence than by the farms and towns that would later characterize it.

## Symbolic Meaning

Dakota represents the **remote frontier** in Whitman's symbolic geography — a place not yet domesticated by the agricultural order of the Midwest or the urban energy of the East. The "far" in "Far Dakota" is both geographic and existential: it marks a limit of the known American world, a place where the national project encounters its own violence and its own limits. Whitman's "home in Dakota's woods" is a fantasy of primitive simplicity; his "Far Dakota's Canyons" is a site of historical trauma. Together, they reveal Dakota as a landscape of American imagination as much as American geography — a place onto which the nation projected its dreams of renewal and its nightmares of conflict.
