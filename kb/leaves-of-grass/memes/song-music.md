## Overview

Song and music are the primary metaphors through which Walt Whitman understands the act of poetry. *Leaves of Grass* announces itself as a "song" from its opening pages — "Song of Myself" [002-0001] is not a poem about the self so much as the song the self sings. Whitman's preference for "song" over "poem" or "verse" is deliberate: song implies breath, body, communal participation, and oral immediacy — all qualities essential to his democratic aesthetic.

For Whitman, music is not just metaphor but a presence in the world. "Music rolls, but not from the organ" [002-1358] — the true music is the sound of life itself, the rhythms of the city, the cadences of speech, the "varied carols" of America. The poet's task is to transcribe this cosmic music into language.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The word "Song" titles more poems in *Leaves of Grass* than any other single word: "Song of Myself," "Song of the Open Road" [004-001], "Song of the Answerer" [006-001], "A Song of Joys" [008-001], "Song of the Broad-Axe" [009-001], "Song of the Exposition" [010-001], "Song of the Redwood-Tree" [011-001], "A Song for Occupations" [012-001], "A Song of the Rolling Earth" [013-001]. This proliferation is itself a statement — every subject, from the self to the axe to the redwood tree, deserves its song.

In "Proud Music of the Storm" [016-001], Whitman listens to the sounds of a storm and hears in them all the music of the world — opera, martial marches, religious chorales, dance tunes. The storm becomes a cosmic orchestra, and the poet the listener-transcriber. "Italian Music in Dakota" [015-1580] captures a moment of cultural encounter: European opera reaching the American plains, music dissolving geographic and cultural boundaries.

"Song of Myself" is structured like a musical composition — themes introduced, developed, varied, and recapitulated. The long lines have the breath-control of a singer; the catalogues build like crescendos; the "I" that contains multitudes is like a chord that holds many notes simultaneously. "Songs, commands, health, pride, the maternal mystery, the seminal milk" [002-2042] — song is listed first among the offerings, the primary gift.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's elevation of song over verse has roots in multiple traditions. From the Hebrew Bible he inherits the psalm — David's lyre, the singing of praise as the highest spiritual act. From classical antiquity comes the figure of the bard or rhapsode, who sings the deeds of heroes. From opera (which Whitman loved) comes the fusion of music, drama, and narrative into a single overwhelming experience.

But Whitman's song is distinctly American. It owes as much to the democratic hymn-singing of camp meetings and the work songs of laborers as to high culture. The poet hears music in the carpenter's hammer and the ferryboat's whistle. This democratization of music parallels his democratization of poetry: every person, every trade, every object has its note in the universal chorus.

## Related Concepts

Song is the mode through which **The Self** expresses its democratic multiplicity. It connects to **Democracy** through the idea that everyone has a song worth singing. The musical structure of catalogues links song to **Nature** — the poet listens to the world's music in waves, leaves, and storms. Song is also the vehicle for **Joy** ("A Song of Joys"), for **Labor** ("A Song for Occupations"), and for the cosmic vision of equality where every thing has its note.
