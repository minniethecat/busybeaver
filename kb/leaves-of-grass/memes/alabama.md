# Alabama

**Alabama** appears in 5 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it figures primarily in association with cotton — the crop that defined the state's economy, landscape, and social order in the 19th century. For Whitman, Alabama was a southern coordinate in his continental map, a place where the agrarian abundance he celebrated was inextricably linked to the labor system — slavery — that his poetry both acknowledges and cannot resolve.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Alabama enters Whitman's poetry through the poet's imagined movement across the continent: "As I have walk'd in Alabama my morning walk" (001-009) — the first-person assertion claiming even the Deep South as territory for the democratic poet's perambulations. The state's defining crop appears in a line that names the labor behind it: "always the cotton-fields of Alabama and Texas" (007-004), the adverb "always" suggesting the relentless, seasonless character of plantation agriculture. A striking image of avian life — "Two feather'd guests from Alabama, two together" (014-0143), later "The solitary guest from Alabama" (014-0169) — uses birds from Alabama as emissaries of the natural world entering human consciousness. The agricultural catalogue returns: "the cotton in Mississippi or Alabama, dig and hoard the" (015-0653), the labor of cotton cultivation registered in the verbs "dig" and "hoard."

## Geographic & Historical Context

Alabama was admitted as the 22nd state in 1819, its economy built on cotton cultivation using enslaved labor. By 1860, enslaved people constituted 45% of the state's population, and Alabama was one of the wealthiest cotton-producing regions in the world — wealth built entirely on the institution of slavery. The state seceded in 1861; Montgomery briefly served as the first capital of the Confederacy. Reconstruction brought brief political empowerment to freedpeople, followed by the violent reassertion of white supremacy that defined the post-Reconstruction South. Whitman, writing both before and after the war, registers Alabama primarily through its landscape and agriculture rather than its politics — the cotton fields and their avian visitors, the morning walk through a terrain the poet claims for his democratic vision.

## Symbolic Meaning

Alabama represents the **agrarian South** in Whitman's symbolic geography — the landscape of cotton and morning walks that is also, implicitly, a landscape of unfree labor. Whitman's "morning walk" in Alabama is a democratic claim: the poet can walk anywhere in the nation and call it his own. But the cotton fields that surround that walk tell a more complicated story. The "two feather'd guests from Alabama" — birds, not people — are emissaries from a landscape whose human inhabitants are largely absent from Whitman's verse. This absence, this silence around the people who worked the Alabama cotton fields, is itself a form of presence in the poetry — a gap that the reader is left to fill.
