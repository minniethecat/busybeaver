## Overview

The South in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* is inseparable from the Civil War. "Southern savannas" (001-006) appear in the early catalogues as one more American landscape. But after 1861, the South becomes the site of battle, wounding, and ultimately — in Whitman's vision — reconciliation. The poet's hospital nursing brought him into intimate contact with Southern soldiers, whom he treated with the same compassion as Union men.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The South appears in the pre-war poems as a geographical fact: "southern savannas," the Gulf coast, the cotton fields. The war transforms it into a site of suffering and national trial. In the Lincoln elegies and "Drum-Taps," the South is not demonized but included — it is part of the body of the nation, wounded but still part of the whole. Whitman's democratic faith requires that the South be re-embraced.

## Geographic & Historical Context

The American South of Whitman's era was defined by slavery, secession, Civil War, defeat, and Reconstruction. Whitman was a staunch Unionist but not an abolitionist in the radical sense. His poetry treats the South less as a political problem than as a spiritual one — a region that must be healed and re-integrated into the national body.

## Symbolic Meaning

The South symbolizes: **national fracture**, **suffering and healing**, **reconciliation**, **the body wounded and restored**, and **the limits of Whitman's democratic embrace**.
