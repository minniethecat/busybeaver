# Tennessee

**Tennessee** appears in 8 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, figuring in his catalogues of American rivers, agricultural landscapes, and the people who inhabit them. As both a state and a river, Tennessee occupies a distinct position in Whitman's geography — a border state of the Upper South, divided during the Civil War, whose waterways connect the Appalachian interior to the great Mississippi system.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Tennessee's appearances in *Leaves of Grass* are predominantly geographical. The Tennessee River is named among the continent's great waterways — "those drain'd by the Tennessee" (002-0431) — placing it within Whitman's continental hydrological imagination. The state is linked with its neighbor Kentucky in multiple passages: "In Tennessee and Kentucky slaves busy in the fields" (007-067), a line that acknowledges the labor system of the antebellum South without editorial comment, letting the image speak.

Later passages map the state's riverscape: "southward along the Tennessee or Cumberland rivers" (014-0986), and trace the movement of Whitman's poetic attention "To Tennessee and Kentucky, to the Carolinas" (014-1028). In the agricultural catalogues, Tennessee appears among the grain-producing states: "maize of Missouri, Kentucky, Tennessee, each ear in its" (015-0649), contributing its harvest to the continental abundance Whitman celebrates.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Tennessee was admitted as the 16th state in 1796, carved from territory west of the Appalachian Mountains. In Whitman's era, it was defined by three distinct regions: the mountainous east (Appalachian), the fertile central basin (Nashville and the bluegrass region), and the cotton-growing west (Memphis and the Mississippi Delta). This internal diversity made Tennessee a microcosm of the nation's divisions. The state was the last to secede from the Union and the first to be readmitted after the Civil War. Its rivers — the Tennessee and the Cumberland — were vital arteries of commerce and movement, connecting the Appalachian interior to the Ohio and Mississippi systems.

## Symbolic Meaning

Tennessee embodies the **borderland condition** in Whitman's American geography. Neither wholly of the Deep South nor of the Midwest, divided between mountain and delta, between Unionist east and Confederate west, Tennessee is a state of transition. In Whitman's catalogues, this liminal quality becomes a virtue: Tennessee is one more note in the democratic symphony, its particularity — its rivers, its maize, its people — contributing to the whole without being subsumed by it. The state's river system, flowing into the Ohio and then the Mississippi, participates in Whitman's larger vision of **connection and flow** — the waterways that bind the continent into a single body.
