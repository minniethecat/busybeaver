## Overview

Nature in [[walt-whitman|Walt Whitman]]'s poetry is never merely scenery. It is the fundamental reality — the physical universe in its totality — from which all else derives: [[self|the self]], [[democracy]], [[soul|the soul]], even [[god|God]]. Whitman's nature is not the domesticated landscape of the English Romantics but a teeming, often overwhelming presence: the "long-running [[mississippi|Mississippi]]," "mighty [[niagara|Niagara]]," the prairies, the sea, and above all, [[grass|the grass]] — humble, ubiquitous, and mysteriously suggestive of the cycle of death and rebirth.

Whitman's natural world is democratic in its very structure. Every element — from the "spear of summer grass" to the "suffusion of the sun" — receives equal poetic attention. The catalogue, Whitman's signature formal device, is an ecological form: an enumeration in which every item belongs, no hierarchy of significance, everything participating in the whole. In this sense, Whitman's nature poetry IS his democratic poetry — both are expressions of a single vision of inclusive, interconnected being.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Nature pervades every section of *Leaves of Grass*:

- **"Song of Myself"** (BOOK III): The poem's opening movement grounds the self in nature — "I loafe and invite my soul, / I lean and loafe at my ease observing a spear of summer grass." The grass becomes the poem's central symbol, answering the child's question "What is the grass?" with a cascade of interpretations that connect nature to death, regeneration, and democratic equality.

- **"Starting from Paumanok"** (BOOK II): Whitman names the natural features of the American continent — "the flowing Missouri," "mighty Niagara," "the buffalo herds" (PN 001-013–015) — as his poetic material. Nature IS America in this opening movement.

- **"Song of the Redwood-Tree"** (BOOK XIV): A late poem in which a dying California redwood speaks, merging Whitman's nature mysticism with elegy for the passing of the wild continent.

- **"Out of the Cradle Endlessly Rocking"**: Nature as teacher — the sea whispers the "delicious word" of death to the boy-poet, initiating him into his vocation.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's nature philosophy synthesizes Transcendentalist pantheism (Emerson's "Nature," 1836) with a distinctively American empiricism. Unlike Emerson, for whom nature is primarily a symbol of spirit, Whitman insists on the dense materiality of the natural world — its tastes, smells, sounds, the "fragrance of the grass." His nature is not a ladder to transcendence but a realm to be inhabited and celebrated on its own terms.

## Key Passages

> A child said What is the grass? fetching it to me with full hands;
> How could I answer the child? I do not know what it is any more than he.
> — [002-099]

The grass — humble, ubiquitous, democratic — becomes Whitman's master symbol for the interconnectedness of all life and the mystery of death and regeneration.

> Smile O voluptuous cool-breath'd earth!
> Earth of the slumbering and liquid trees!
> Earth of departed sunset — earth of the mountains misty-topt!
> Earth of the vitreous pour of the full moon just tinged with blue!
> — [002-437]

From "Song of Myself" — Whitman's earth is not passive but actively "voluptuous," a living presence that the poet addresses as lover and equal.
