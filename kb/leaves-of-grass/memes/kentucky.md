# Kentucky

**Kentucky** appears in 5 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it occupies the ambiguous position of a border state — neither wholly of the North nor of the Deep South, a landscape of bluegrass pastures and corn-fields whose identity was shaped by the fracture lines of American history. For Whitman, Kentucky represented the fertile middle ground of the continent, where the agrarian abundance he celebrated was shadowed by the legacy of slavery.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Kentucky is most often paired with its neighbor Tennessee in Whitman's catalogues. A line that registers the antebellum reality without flinching: "In Tennessee and Kentucky slaves busy in the fields" (007-067) — the image of enslaved labor placed before the reader without editorial comment, the plainness of the observation itself a kind of witness. The poet's geographic intention is announced in a later passage: "To Tennessee and Kentucky, to the Carolinas and Georgia" (014-1028), the states listed as destinations for the singing voice. In the agricultural catalogues, Kentucky contributes its harvest: "Harvest the maize of Missouri, Kentucky, Tennessee, each ear in its" (015-0649), the corn imagined not as commodity but as living form. And in one of the most vivid images: "A Kentucky corn-field, the tall, graceful, long-leav'd corn" (021-0491) — the specificity of the observation revealing Whitman's deep attention to the American landscape in its particularity. A final passage links the state with its neighbors in a gesture of continental inclusion: "Of what Indiana, Kentucky, Arkansas, and the rest, are" (021-0956).

## Geographic & Historical Context

Kentucky was admitted as the 15th state in 1792, the first state west of the Appalachian Mountains. Its identity as a border state — slave state but Union, Southern in culture but Northern in economic orientation — was forged in the crucible of the Civil War. Kentucky never formally seceded, though a Confederate shadow government briefly claimed authority. Both Abraham Lincoln and Jefferson Davis were born in Kentucky, making the state literally the birthplace of the conflict's opposing leaders. Its landscape — the bluegrass region of central Kentucky, the Appalachian east, the Mississippi lowlands of the west — gave rise to a distinctive agrarian culture, famous for thoroughbred horses, tobacco, and corn.

## Symbolic Meaning

Kentucky embodies the **border condition** in Whitman's symbolic geography — not the border as line of separation but as zone of meeting and mixing. Its location between North and South, between the settled East and the frontier West, makes it a place where the nation's contradictions and possibilities are held in suspension. The "Kentucky corn-field, the tall, graceful, long-leav'd corn" is at once a specific observation and a symbol of American abundance — the land yielding its increase to the people who tend it. That Whitman's gaze could rest on a Kentucky corn-field with the same attention he brought to the streets of Mannahatta testifies to his democratic aesthetic: no place is too humble to be seen, none too remote to be sung.
