## Overview

For Walt Whitman, life is not an abstraction but a physical, sensual, and immediate reality. Throughout *Leaves of Grass*, "life" denotes the animating principle that courses through the body, the earth, and the cosmos — inseparable from matter yet transcendent in its endless renewal. Whitman refuses the traditional Western dichotomy that subordinates the living body to an immortal soul; instead, he insists that life in the flesh is itself sacred and sufficient. "This then is life," he declares in *Song of Myself* [001-028], presenting ordinary embodied existence as the highest miracle. His conception of life encompasses everything from the microscopic processes of organic growth to the vast currents of human history, all bound together by what he calls the "brine of life" — a salty, generative medium that dissolves distinctions between self and world [002-0600].

## In Whitman's Poetry

Life permeates every section of *Leaves of Grass*, but certain passages foreground it with particular intensity. In *Song of Myself*, Whitman ties life directly to the body: "more than a man's substance and life or a woman's substance and life return in the body and the soul" [001-254–255]. Life here is not separable from its material container — the body is life's expression, not its prison. The "Sea of the brine of life" in Section 22 of *Song of Myself* [002-0600] images life as an oceanic medium in which all beings are suspended, "unshovell'd yet always ready for the shoveller." This metaphor recurs in *Starting from Paumanok*, where life is "always a breed of life" [002-0064], self-propagating and inexhaustible. Across the *Calamus* cluster, life takes on an erotic and relational dimension: "For your life adhere to me" [001-317] expresses the poet's conviction that lives intertwine and sustain one another. In later poems such as *Proud Music of the Storm*, life acquires a more elegiac register — "Twenty-eight years of womanly life and all so lonesome" [002-0263] — acknowledging the solitude that even a life-affirming philosophy cannot fully dispel.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's philosophy of life draws on several intellectual currents. From Transcendentalism, particularly Emerson's essay *Nature* (1836), he inherits the idea that the natural world is infused with spiritual significance and that each living thing participates in an overarching "Over-Soul." But where Emerson tends toward the abstract, Whitman anchors life in the literal body — in sweat, sex, and sensation. His Quaker-inflected egalitarianism leads him to see life as equally sacred in all its forms: "the caresser of life wherever moving" [002-0304] embraces high and low without distinction. The Romantic celebration of vital force — Goethe's *Lebenskraft*, the *élan vital* later theorized by Bergson — offers another parallel, though Whitman's version is distinctively American in its democratic reach and its refusal of European pessimism. Where Schopenhauer saw life as suffering, Whitman sees it as "the print I have read in my life" [002-0310] — legible, meaningful, and ultimately joyous.

## Related Concepts

Life is inseparable from [[death]] in Whitman's cosmology — the two form a cycle rather than an opposition. The [[body]] is life's material theatre, and [[health]] is life at its most vigorous. The regenerative power of life connects to [[nature]] and [[earth]], while its spiritual dimension links to [[soul]] and [[immortality]]. The democratic distribution of life's value across all persons ties this concept to [[democracy]] and the celebration of the common.
