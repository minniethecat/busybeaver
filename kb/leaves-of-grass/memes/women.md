# Women

**Women** in *Leaves of Grass* appear as objects of celebration, figures of beauty, and participants in democratic equality. Whitman's treatment of women is complex: he affirms feminine presence and strength while working within — and sometimes against — the gender conventions of 19th-century America.

## "Beautiful Women" and the Gaze

Whitman's most direct address to femininity comes in "Beautiful Women" [[014-0832]], a brief but striking passage that places women at the center of the poet's democratic vision. The poem does not describe women in detail but names them as part of the American panorama, alongside laborers, landscapes, and civic life.

> Women sit or move to and fro, some old, some young,
> The young are beautiful — but the old are more beautiful than the young
> — [[014-0833]]

## Women, Wisdom, and Innocence

In [[021-0416]], Whitman places "Women and men in wisdom innocence and health" side by side — the pairing is characteristic. Gender difference exists but does not imply hierarchy; wisdom and innocence are equally available to women and men. This vision of parity, while not yet the full equality later feminism would demand, exceeds the conventions of Whitman's time.

## The Feminine and the Maternal

Whitman's treatment of women is closely linked to his concept of the [[Mother]]. The "Mother of All" represents the cosmic feminine — generative, powerful, and ultimately beyond any single gender role. Yet this elevation of the maternal risks reducing women to their reproductive function. Whitman's women are celebrated primarily as mothers and beauties, not as workers, thinkers, or citizens in their own right.

## Limitations and Horizons

Whitman's 19th-century context marks his representation of women. He rarely depicts women in professional or public roles, and his celebration of the "beautiful" can tip toward objectification. Yet his democratic vision contains within it the seeds of fuller equality: if "women and men" share "wisdom innocence and health" [[021-0416]], the logic of Whitman's own expansive inclusivism points beyond the limits of his moment.
