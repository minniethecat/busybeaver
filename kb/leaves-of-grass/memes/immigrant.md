## Overview

The Immigrant is a foundational figure in Walt Whitman's vision of American democracy. "See, in my poems immigrants continually coming and landing" (001-350) — the immigrant is not a visitor or outsider but the very substance of the nation's ongoing creation. For Whitman, America is defined not by its past or its native-born but by its continual renewal through the arrival of people from every nation. The immigrant embodies democracy's promise: that anyone can become American, that diversity strengthens rather than weakens the nation.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Immigrants appear throughout the collection as figures of vitality and renewal. "The groups of newly-come immigrants cover the wharf or levee" (002-0371) — this is not a threat but a celebration. The immigrant is the living proof of America's universal appeal and democratic promise. Whitman catalogues immigrants by nationality and trade, celebrating their diversity as the nation's wealth.

The immigrant also represents a personal dimension for Whitman. As a New Yorker, he witnessed the constant arrival of immigrants at the city's docks. As a democrat, he saw in this endless influx the very mechanism of American renewal — each new arrival brings new energy, new perspectives, and new commitment to the democratic experiment.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Whitman wrote during the greatest wave of immigration in American history. Between 1840 and 1860, over four million immigrants arrived in the United States — primarily Irish, German, and later Italian, Eastern European, and Chinese. This transformation of the American population was controversial (the Know-Nothing Party campaigned on anti-immigrant platforms), but Whitman embraced it wholeheartedly. His democratic faith required that America be open to all, and his poetry enacts this openness at the level of form — the catalogues welcome every type of person into the poem's embrace.

## Themes & Significance

The Immigrant embodies: **democratic renewal** (each new arrival refreshes the nation), **diversity as strength** (difference enriches rather than divides), **universalism** (America belongs to all humanity), **the open frontier** (America as land of opportunity), and **transformation** (the immigrant's journey recapitulates the American story).
