# BOOK XXV: Proud Music of the Storm

[016-001]Proud Music of the Storm

[016-002]1

[016-003]Proud music of the storm,

[016-004]Blast that careers so [[freedom|free]], whistling across [[prairie|the prairies]],

[016-005]Strong hum of forest tree-tops--wind of the mountains,

[016-006]Personified dim shapes--you hidden orchestras,

[016-007]You serenades of phantoms with instruments alert,

[016-008]Blending with Nature’s rhythmus all the tongues of nations;

[016-009]You chords left as by vast composers--you choruses,

[016-010]You formless, free, religious dances--you from the Orient,

[016-011]You undertone of rivers, roar of pouring cataracts,

[016-012]You sounds from distant guns with galloping cavalry,

[016-013]Echoes of camps with all the different bugle-calls,

[016-014]Trooping tumultuous, filling the midnight late, bending me powerless,

[016-015]Entering my lonesome slumber-chamber, why have you seiz’d me?

[016-016]2

[016-017]Come forward O my [[soul]], and let the rest retire,

[016-018]Listen, lose not, it is toward thee they tend,

[016-019]Parting the midnight, entering my slumber-chamber,

[016-020]For thee they sing and dance O soul.

[016-021]A festival song,

[016-022]The duet of the bridegroom and the bride, a marriage-march,

[016-023]With lips of [[love]], and hearts of lovers fill’d to the brim with love,

[016-024]The red-flush’d cheeks and perfumes, the cortege swarming full of

[016-025]friendly faces young and old,

[016-026]To flutes’ clear notes and sounding harps’ cantabile.

[016-027]Now loud approaching drums,

[016-028]Victoria! seest thou in powder-smoke the banners torn but flying?

[016-029]the rout of the baffled?

[016-030]Hearest those shouts of a conquering army?

[016-031](Ah soul, the sobs of women, the wounded groaning in agony,

[016-032]The hiss and crackle of flames, the blacken’d ruins, the embers of cities,

[016-033]The dirge and desolation of mankind.)

[016-034]Now airs antique and mediaeval fill me,

[016-035]I see and hear old harpers with their harps at Welsh festivals,

[016-036]I hear the minnesingers singing their lays of love,

[016-037]I hear the minstrels, gleemen, troubadours, of the middle ages.

[016-038]Now the great organ sounds,

[016-039]Tremulous, while underneath, (as the hid footholds of [[earth|the earth]],

[016-040]On which arising rest, and leaping forth depend,

[016-041]All shapes of beauty, grace and strength, all hues we know,

[016-042]Green blades of [[grass]] and warbling birds, children that gambol and

[016-043]play, the clouds of heaven above,)

[016-044]The strong base stands, and its pulsations intermits not,

[016-045]Bathing, supporting, merging all the rest, maternity of all the rest,

[016-046]And with it every instrument in multitudes,

[016-047]The players playing, all the world’s musicians,

[016-048]The solemn hymns and masses rousing adoration,

[016-049]All passionate heart-chants, sorrowful appeals,

[016-050]The measureless sweet vocalists of ages,

[016-051]And for their solvent setting earth’s own diapason,

[016-052]Of winds and woods and mighty [[sea|ocean]] waves,

[016-053]A new composite orchestra, binder of years and climes, ten-fold renewer,

[016-054]As of the far-back days the poets tell, the Paradiso,

[016-055]The straying thence, the separation long, but now the wandering done,

[016-056]The journey done, the journeyman come home,

[016-057]And man and art with Nature fused again.

[016-058]Tutti! for earth and heaven;

[016-059](The Almighty leader now for once has signal’d with his wand.)

[016-060]The manly strophe of the husbands of the world,

[016-061]And all the wives responding.

[016-062]The tongues of violins,

[016-063](I think O tongues ye tell this heart, that cannot tell itself,

[016-064]This brooding yearning heart, that cannot tell itself.)

[016-065]3

[016-066]Ah from a little child,

[016-067]Thou knowest soul how to me all sounds became music,

[016-068]My [[mother]]’s voice in lullaby or hymn,

[016-069](The voice, O tender voices, memory’s loving voices,

[016-070]Last miracle of all, O dearest mother’s, sister’s, voices;)

[016-071]The rain, the growing corn, the breeze among the long-leav’d corn,

[016-072]The measur’d sea-surf beating on the sand,

[016-073]The twittering bird, the hawk’s sharp scream,

[016-074]The wild-fowl’s notes at [[night]] as flying low migrating north or south,

[016-075]The psalm in the country church or mid the clustering trees, the

[016-076]open air camp-meeting,

[016-077]The fiddler in the tavern, the glee, the long-strung [[sailor]]-song,

[016-078]The lowing cattle, bleating sheep, the crowing cock at dawn.

[016-079]All songs of current lands come sounding round me,

[016-080]The German airs of friendship, wine and love,

[016-081]Irish ballads, merry jigs and dances, English warbles,

[016-082]Chansons of France, Scotch tunes, and o’er the rest,

[016-083]Italia’s peerless compositions.

[016-084]Across the stage with pallor on her face, yet lurid passion,

[016-085]Stalks Norma brandishing the dagger in her hand.

[016-086]I see poor crazed Lucia’s eyes’ unnatural gleam,

[016-087]Her hair down her back falls loose and dishevel’d.

[016-088]I see where Ernani walking the bridal garden,

[016-089]Amid the scent of night-roses, radiant, holding his bride by the hand,

[016-090]Hears the infernal call, the [[death]]-pledge of the horn.

[016-091]To crossing swords and gray hairs bared to heaven,

[016-092]The clear electric base and baritone of the world,

[016-093]The trombone duo, Libertad forever!

[016-094]From Spanish chestnut trees’ dense shade,

[016-095]By old and heavy convent walls a wailing song,

[016-096]Song of lost love, the torch of youth and life quench’d in despair,

[016-097]Song of the dying swan, Fernando’s heart is breaking.

[016-098]Awaking from her woes at last retriev’d Amina sings,

[016-099]Copious as stars and glad as morning light the torrents of her [[joy]].

[016-100](The teeming lady comes,

[016-101]The lustrious orb, Venus contralto, the blooming mother,

[016-102]Sister of loftiest [[god|gods]], Alboni’s [[self]] I hear.)

[016-103]4

[016-104]I hear those odes, symphonies, operas,

[016-105]I hear in the William Tell the music of an arous’d and angry people,

[016-106]I hear Meyerbeer’s Huguenots, the Prophet, or Robert,

[016-107]Gounod’s Faust, or Mozart’s Don Juan.

[016-108]I hear the dance-music of all nations,

[016-109]The waltz, some delicious measure, lapsing, bathing me in bliss,

[016-110]The bolero to tinkling guitars and clattering castanets.

[016-111]I see religious dances old and new,

[016-112]I hear the sound of the Hebrew lyre,

[016-113]I see the crusaders marching bearing the cross on high, to the

[016-114]martial clang of cymbals,

[016-115]I hear dervishes monotonously chanting, interspers’d with frantic

[016-116]shouts, as they spin around turning always towards Mecca,

[016-117]I see the rapt religious dances of the Persians and the Arabs,

[016-118]Again, at Eleusis, home of Ceres, I see the modern Greeks dancing,

[016-119]I hear them clapping their hands as they bend their bodies,

[016-120]I hear the metrical shuffling of their feet.

[016-121]I see again the wild old Corybantian dance, the performers wounding

[016-122]each other,

[016-123]I see the Roman youth to the shrill sound of flageolets throwing and

[016-124]catching their weapons,

[016-125]As they fall on their knees and rise again.

[016-126]I hear from the Mussulman mosque the muezzin calling,

[016-127]I see the worshippers within, nor form nor sermon, argument nor word,

[016-128]But silent, strange, devout, rais’d, glowing heads, ecstatic faces.

[016-129]I hear the Egyptian harp of many strings,

[016-130]The primitive chants of the Nile boatmen,

[016-131]The sacred imperial hymns of China,

[016-132]To the delicate sounds of the king, (the stricken wood and stone,)

[016-133]Or to Hindu flutes and the fretting twang of the vina,

[016-134]A band of bayaderes.

[016-135]5

[016-136]Now Asia, Africa leave me, Europe seizing inflates me,

[016-137]To organs huge and bands I hear as from vast concourses of voices,

[016-138]Luther’s strong hymn Eine feste Burg ist unser Gott,

[016-139]Rossini’s Stabat Mater dolorosa,

[016-140]Or floating in some high cathedral dim with gorgeous color’d windows,

[016-141]The passionate Agnus Dei or Gloria in Excelsis.

[016-142]Composers! mighty maestros!

[016-143]And you, sweet singers of old lands, soprani, tenori, bassi!

[016-144]To you a new bard caroling in [[the-west|the West]],

[016-145]Obeisant sends his love.

[016-146](Such led to thee O soul,

[016-147]All senses, shows and objects, lead to thee,

[016-148]But now it seems to me sound leads o’er all the rest.)

[016-149]I hear the annual singing of the children in St. Paul’s cathedral,

[016-150]Or, under the high roof of some colossal hall, the symphonies,

[016-151]oratorios of Beethoven, Handel, or Haydn,

[016-152]The Creation in billows of godhood laves me.

[016-153]Give me to hold all sounds, (I madly struggling cry,)

[016-154]Fill me with all the voices of the universe,

[016-155]Endow me with their throbbings, Nature’s also,

[016-156]The tempests, waters, winds, operas and chants, marches and dances,

[016-157]Utter, pour in, for I would take them all!

[016-158]6

[016-159]Then I woke softly,

[016-160]And pausing, questioning awhile the music of my dream,

[016-161]And questioning all those reminiscences, the tempest in its fury,

[016-162]And all the songs of sopranos and tenors,

[016-163]And those rapt oriental dances of religious fervor,

[016-164]And the sweet varied instruments, and the diapason of organs,

[016-165]And all the artless plaints of love and grief and death,

[016-166]I said to my silent curious soul out of the bed of the slumber-chamber,

[016-167]Come, for I have found the clew I sought so long,

[016-168]Let us go forth refresh’d amid the day,

[016-169]Cheerfully tallying life, walking the world, the real,

[016-170]Nourish’d henceforth by our celestial dream.

[016-171]And I said, moreover,

[016-172]Haply what thou hast heard O soul was not the sound of winds,

[016-173]Nor dream of raging storm, nor sea-hawk’s flapping wings nor harsh scream,

[016-174]Nor vocalism of sun-bright Italy,

[016-175]Nor German organ majestic, nor vast concourse of voices, nor layers

[016-176]of harmonies,

[016-177]Nor strophes of husbands and wives, nor sound of marching [[soldier|soldiers]],

[016-178]Nor flutes, nor harps, nor the bugle-calls of camps,

[016-179]But to a new rhythmus fitted for thee,

[016-180]Poems bridging the way from Life to Death, vaguely wafted in night

[016-181]air, uncaught, unwritten,

[016-182]Which let us go forth in the bold day and write.
