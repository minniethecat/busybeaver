# BOOK XV: A Song for Occupations

[012-001]A Song for Occupations

[012-002]1

[012-003]A song for occupations!

[012-004]In the labor of engines and trades and the labor of fields I find

[012-005]the developments,

[012-006]And find the eternal meanings.

[012-007]Workmen and Workwomen!

[012-008]Were all educations practical and ornamental well display’d out of

[012-009]me, what would it amount to?

[012-010]Were I as the head teacher, charitable proprietor, wise statesman,

[012-011]what would it amount to?

[012-012]Were I to you as the boss employing and paying you, would that satisfy you?

[012-013]The learn’d, virtuous, benevolent, and the usual terms,

[012-014]A man like me and never the usual terms.

[012-015]Neither a servant nor a master I,

[012-016]I take no sooner a large price than a small price, I will have my

[012-017]own whoever enjoys me,

[012-018]I will be even with you and you shall be even with me.

[012-019]If you stand at work in a shop I stand as nigh as the nighest in the

[012-020]same shop,

[012-021]If you bestow gifts on your brother or dearest friend I demand as

[012-022]good as your brother or dearest friend,

[012-023]If your lover, husband, wife, is welcome by day or [[night]], I must be

[012-024]personally as welcome,

[012-025]If you become degraded, criminal, ill, then I become so for your sake,

[012-026]If you remember your foolish and outlaw’d deeds, do you think I

[012-027]cannot remember my own foolish and outlaw’d deeds?

[012-028]If you carouse at the table I carouse at the opposite side of the table,

[012-029]If you meet some stranger in the streets and [[love]] him or her, why

[012-030]I often meet strangers in the street and love them.

[012-031]Why what have you thought of yourself?

[012-032]Is it you then that thought yourself less?

[012-033]Is it you that thought the President greater than you?

[012-034]Or the rich better off than you? or the educated wiser than you?

[012-035](Because you are greasy or pimpled, or were once drunk, or a thief,

[012-036]Or that you are diseas’d, or rheumatic, or a prostitute,

[012-037]Or from frivolity or impotence, or that you are no scholar and never

[012-038]saw your name in print,

[012-039]Do you give in that you are any less immortal?)

[012-040]2

[012-041]Souls of men and women! it is not you I call unseen, unheard,

[012-042]untouchable and untouching,

[012-043]It is not you I go argue pro and con about, and to settle whether

[012-044]you are alive or no,

[012-045]I own publicly who you are, if nobody else owns.

[012-046]Grown, half-grown and babe, of this country and every country,

[012-047]in-doors and out-doors, one just as much as the other, I see,

[012-048]And all else behind or through them.

[012-049]The wife, and she is not one jot less than the husband,

[012-050]The daughter, and she is just as good as the son,

[012-051]The [[mother]], and she is every bit as much as the father.

[012-052]Offspring of ignorant and poor, boys apprenticed to trades,

[012-053]Young fellows working on farms and old fellows working on farms,

[012-054][[sailor|Sailor]]-men, merchant-men, coasters, [[immigrant|immigrants]],

[012-055]All these I see, but nigher and farther the same I see,

[012-056]None shall escape me and none shall wish to escape me.

[012-057]I bring what you much need yet always have,

[012-058]Not money, amours, dress, eating, erudition, but as good,

[012-059]I send no agent or medium, offer no representative of value, but

[012-060]offer the value itself.

[012-061]There is something that comes to one now and perpetually,

[012-062]It is not what is printed, preach’d, discussed, it eludes discussion

[012-063]and print,

[012-064]It is not to be put in a book, it is not in this book,

[012-065]It is for you whoever you are, it is no farther from you than your

[012-066]hearing and sight are from you,

[012-067]It is hinted by nearest, commonest, readiest, it is ever provoked by them.

[012-068]You may read in many languages, yet read nothing about it,

[012-069]You may read the President’s message and read nothing about it there,

[012-070]Nothing in the reports from the State department or Treasury

[012-071]department, or in the daily papers or weekly papers,

[012-072]Or in the census or revenue returns, prices current, or any accounts

[012-073]of stock.

[012-074]3

[012-075]The sun and stars that float in the open air,

[012-076]The apple-shaped [[earth]] and we upon it, surely the drift of them is

[012-077]something grand,

[012-078]I do not know what it is except that it is grand, and that it is [[joy|happiness]],

[012-079]And that the enclosing purport of us here is not a speculation or

[012-080]bon-mot or reconnoissance,

[012-081]And that it is not something which by luck may turn out well for us,

[012-082]and without luck must be a failure for us,

[012-083]And not something which may yet be retracted in a certain contingency.

[012-084]The light and shade, the curious sense of [[body]] and identity, the

[012-085]greed that with perfect complaisance devours all things,

[012-086]The endless pride and outstretching of man, unspeakable joys and sorrows,

[012-087]The wonder every one sees in every one else he sees, and the wonders

[012-088]that fill each minute of [[time]] forever,

[012-089]What have you reckon’d them for, camerado?

[012-090]Have you reckon’d them for your trade or farm-work? or for the

[012-091]profits of your store?

[012-092]Or to achieve yourself a position? or to fill a gentleman’s leisure,

[012-093]or a lady’s leisure?

[012-094]Have you reckon’d that the landscape took substance and form that it

[012-095]might be painted in a picture?

[012-096]Or men and women that they might be written of, and songs sung?

[012-097]Or the attraction of gravity, and the great laws and harmonious combinations

[012-098]and the fluids of the air, as subjects for the savans?

[012-099]Or the brown land and the blue [[sea]] for maps and charts?

[012-100]Or the stars to be put in constellations and named fancy names?

[012-101]Or that the growth of seeds is for agricultural tables, or

[012-102]agriculture itself?

[012-103]Old institutions, these arts, libraries, legends, collections, and

[012-104]the practice handed along in manufactures, will we rate them so high?

[012-105]Will we rate our cash and business high? I have no objection,

[012-106]I rate them as high as the highest--then a child born of a woman and

[012-107]man I rate beyond all rate.

[012-108]We thought our Union grand, and our Constitution grand,

[012-109]I do not say they are not grand and good, for they are,

[012-110]I am this day just as much in love with them as you,

[012-111]Then I am in love with You, and with all my fellows upon the earth.

[012-112]We consider bibles and religions divine--I do not say they are not divine,

[012-113]I say they have all grown out of you, and may grow out of you still,

[012-114]It is not they who give the life, it is you who give the life,

[012-115]Leaves are not more shed from the trees, or trees from the earth,

[012-116]than they are shed out of you.

[012-117]4

[012-118]The sum of all known reverence I add up in you whoever you are,

[012-119]The President is there in the White House for you, it is not you who

[012-120]are here for him,

[012-121]The Secretaries act in their bureaus for you, not you here for them,

[012-122]The Congress convenes every Twelfth-month for you,

[012-123]Laws, courts, the forming of States, the charters of cities, the

[012-124]going and coming of commerce and malls, are all for you.

[012-125]List close my scholars dear,

[012-126]Doctrines, politics and civilization exurge from you,

[012-127]Sculpture and monuments and any thing inscribed anywhere are tallied in you,

[012-128]The gist of histories and statistics as far back as the records

[012-129]reach is in you this hour, and myths and tales the same,

[012-130]If you were not breathing and walking here, where would they all be?

[012-131]The most renown’d poems would be ashes, orations and plays would

[012-132]be vacuums.

[012-133]All architecture is what you do to it when you look upon it,

[012-134](Did you think it was in the white or gray stone? or the lines of

[012-135]the arches and cornices?)

[012-136]All music is what awakes from you when you are reminded by the instruments,

[012-137]It is not the violins and the cornets, it is not the oboe nor the

[012-138]beating drums, nor the score of the baritone singer singing his

[012-139]sweet romanza, nor that of the men’s chorus, nor that of the

[012-140]women’s chorus,

[012-141]It is nearer and farther than they.

[012-142]5

[012-143]Will the whole come back then?

[012-144]Can each see signs of the best by a look in the looking-glass? is

[012-145]there nothing greater or more?

[012-146]Does all sit there with you, with the mystic unseen [[soul]]?

[012-147]Strange and hard that paradox true I give,

[012-148]Objects gross and the unseen soul are one.

[012-149]House-building, measuring, sawing the boards,

[012-150]Blacksmithing, glass-blowing, nail-making, coopering, tin-roofing,

[012-151]shingle-dressing,

[012-152]Ship-joining, dock-building, fish-curing, flagging of sidewalks by flaggers,

[012-153]The pump, the pile-driver, the great derrick, the coal-kiln and brickkiln,

[012-154]Coal-mines and all that is down there, the lamps in the darkness,

[012-155]echoes, songs, what meditations, what vast native thoughts

[012-156]looking through smutch’d faces,

[012-157]Iron-works, forge-fires in the mountains or by river-banks, men

[012-158]around feeling the melt with huge crowbars, lumps of ore, the

[012-159]due combining of ore, limestone, coal,

[012-160]The blast-furnace and the puddling-furnace, the loup-lump at the

[012-161]bottom of the melt at last, the rolling-mill, the stumpy bars

[012-162]of pig-iron, the strong clean-shaped Trail for railroads,

[012-163]Oil-works, silk-works, white-lead-works, the sugar-house,

[012-164]steam-saws, the great mills and factories,

[012-165]Stone-cutting, shapely trimmings for facades or window or door-lintels,

[012-166]the mallet, the tooth-chisel, the jib to protect the thumb,

[012-167]The calking-iron, the kettle of boiling vault-cement, and the fire

[012-168]under the kettle,

[012-169]The cotton-bale, the stevedore’s hook, the saw and buck of the

[012-170]sawyer, the mould of the moulder, the working-knife of the

[012-171]butcher, the ice-saw, and all the work with ice,

[012-172]The work and tools of the rigger, grappler, sail-maker, block-maker,

[012-173]Goods of gutta-percha, papier-mache, colors, brushes, brush-making,

[012-174]glazier’s implements,

[012-175]The veneer and glue-pot, the confectioner’s ornaments, the decanter

[012-176]and glasses, the shears and flat-iron,

[012-177]The awl and knee-strap, the pint measure and quart measure, the

[012-178]counter and stool, the writing-pen of quill or metal, the making

[012-179]of all sorts of edged tools,

[012-180]The brewery, brewing, the malt, the vats, every thing that is done

[012-181]by brewers, wine-makers, vinegar-makers,

[012-182]Leather-dressing, coach-making, boiler-making, rope-twisting,

[012-183]distilling, sign-painting, lime-burning, cotton-picking,

[012-184]electroplating, electrotyping, stereotyping,

[012-185]Stave-machines, planing-machines, reaping-machines,

[012-186]ploughing-machines, thrashing-machines, steam wagons,

[012-187]The cart of the carman, the omnibus, the ponderous dray,

[012-188]Pyrotechny, letting off color’d fireworks at night, fancy figures and jets;

[012-189]Beef on the butcher’s stall, the slaughter-house of the butcher, the

[012-190]butcher in his killing-clothes,

[012-191]The pens of live pork, the killing-hammer, the hog-hook, the

[012-192]scalder’s tub, gutting, the cutter’s cleaver, the packer’s maul,

[012-193]and the plenteous winterwork of pork-packing,

[012-194]Flour-works, grinding of wheat, rye, maize, rice, the barrels and

[012-195]the half and quarter barrels, the loaded barges, the high piles

[012-196]on wharves and levees,

[012-197]The men and the work of the men on ferries, railroads, coasters,

[012-198]fish-boats, canals;

[012-199]The hourly routine of your own or any man’s life, the shop, yard,

[012-200]store, or factory,

[012-201]These shows all near you by day and night--[[mechanic|workman]]! whoever you

[012-202]are, your daily life!

[012-203]In that and them the heft of the heaviest--in that and them far more

[012-204]than you estimated, (and far less also,)

[012-205]In them realities for you and me, in them poems for you and me,

[012-206]In them, not yourself-you and your soul enclose all things,

[012-207]regardless of estimation,

[012-208]In them the development good--in them all themes, hints, possibilities.

[012-209]I do not affirm that what you see beyond is futile, I do not advise

[012-210]you to stop,

[012-211]I do not say leadings you thought great are not great,

[012-212]But I say that none lead to greater than these lead to.

[012-213]6

[012-214]Will you seek afar off? you surely come back at last,

[012-215]In things best known to you finding the best, or as good as the best,

[012-216]In folks nearest to you finding the sweetest, strongest, lovingest,

[012-217]Happiness, knowledge, not in another place but this place, not for

[012-218]another hour but this hour,

[012-219]Man in the first you see or touch, always in friend, brother,

[012-220]nighest neighbor--woman in mother, sister, wife,

[012-221]The popular tastes and employments taking precedence in poems or anywhere,

[012-222]You workwomen and workmen of these States having your own divine

[012-223]and strong life,

[012-224]And all else giving place to men and women like you.

[012-225]When the psalm sings instead of the singer,

[012-226]When the script preaches instead of the preacher,

[012-227]When the pulpit descends and goes instead of the carver that carved

[012-228]the supporting desk,

[012-229]When I can touch the body of books by night or by day, and when they

[012-230]touch my body back again,

[012-231]When a university course convinces like a slumbering woman and child

[012-232]convince,

[012-233]When the minted gold in the vault smiles like the night-watchman’s daughter,

[012-234]When warrantee deeds loafe in chairs opposite and are my friendly

[012-235]companions,

[012-236]I intend to reach them my hand, and make as much of them as I do

[012-237]of men and women like you.
