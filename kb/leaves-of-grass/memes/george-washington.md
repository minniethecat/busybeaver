# George Washington

**George Washington** (1732–1799), first President of the United States and commander of the Continental Army, appears in *Leaves of Grass* as an archetype of American leadership and republican virtue. Whitman invokes Washington not as a remote icon but as a living presence in the democratic imagination — a figure whose legacy shapes the nation the poet champions.

## Washington as Founding Archetype

Whitman's Washington is less a historical individual than a symbolic presence: the father of the country whose authority derives not from birth or conquest but from the consent of the governed. In the poet's democratic vision, Washington represents the possibility of legitimate power exercised without tyranny — leadership that serves rather than dominates.

## The General and the Republic

Washington's military leadership during the Revolution — holding the Continental Army together through years of privation — established a model of American command: citizen-soldier rather than professional general. Whitman, who witnessed the Civil War's testing of that model, understood Washington's precedent as foundational to the American experiment in republican self-governance.

## Washington in the American Landscape

For Whitman, Washington belongs to the same American geography as [[America]] itself — the Potomac, Mount Vernon, the capital city that bears his name. The physical landscape of the republic is inseparable from the figures who shaped it, and Washington's name recurs across the continent as a marker of national identity.

## Legacy in Democratic Poetry

Whitman's Washington is ultimately a figure of democratic possibility — proof that the republican experiment can produce leaders worthy of the people's trust. In an age when Whitman saw that experiment threatened by disunion and slavery, Washington's legacy provided both standard and rebuke.
