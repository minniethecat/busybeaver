## Overview

The Prairies are one of Walt Whitman's most potent geographic symbols in *Leaves of Grass*. "Chants of the prairies" (001-052) — the prairie is not merely a landscape but a voice, a mode of being. Whitman's prairies are vast, open, and democratic: they erase old boundaries and hierarchies, offering a blank slate for the American experiment. "Crossing the prairies, dwelling again in Chicago" (001-297) — the prairie is both passage and destination.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Prairie imagery runs throughout the collection, associated with freedom, expansiveness, and the uniquely American sublime. The "Prairie States" and "Prairie Sunset" appear as Whitman catalogues the nation's interior. The prairie is democratic space — flat, unbounded, belonging to no one and everyone.

## Geographic & Historical Context

In Whitman's era, the prairies were the frontier — being settled, cultivated, and traversed by wagon trains and, increasingly, railroads. The 1862 Homestead Act accelerated settlement. Native peoples were being displaced. Whitman's celebration of the prairies elides this violence, focusing on the landscape's democratic promise rather than its contested history.

## Symbolic Meaning

The Prairies symbolize: **democratic openness**, **spiritual vastness**, **the American future**, **nature's democracy** (grass covering all equally), and **the frontier as possibility**.
