# Ulysses S. Grant

**Ulysses S. Grant** (1822–1885), commanding general of the Union Army during the American Civil War and the 18th President of the United States, appears in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* through a single powerful reference: "Death of General Grant" (021-1522). This brief mention, embedded within a catalogue of American events and figures, signals Grant's place in Whitman's vision of national history — a figure whose military leadership and presidential service shaped the America Whitman celebrated.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The single corpus reference to Grant — "Death of General Grant" (021-1522) — is characteristically terse. It appears in a sweeping catalogue that gathers American events, places, and historical moments into Whitman's poetic embrace. The phrase captures a specific historical moment (Grant died on July 23, 1885, after a prolonged battle with throat cancer, just days after completing his *Personal Memoirs*) while also elevating it to symbolic status. For Whitman, Grant's death was not merely a biographical event but a national one — the passing of a figure who had saved the Union and led its reconstruction.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Born Hiram Ulysses Grant in Point Pleasant, Ohio, Grant graduated from West Point in 1843 and served with distinction in the Mexican-American War. After a difficult period in civilian life, he returned to military service at the outbreak of the Civil War in 1861. Rising rapidly through the ranks, Grant became the first full general since George Washington to command all Union armies. His relentless campaigns — culminating in Robert E. Lee's surrender at Appomattox Court House in April 1865 — ended the war and preserved the Union. Elected President in 1868, Grant served two terms marked by Reconstruction efforts, civil rights enforcement, and economic turmoil. His final act — completing his memoirs while dying of cancer to secure his family's financial future — was itself a triumph of American character.

## Themes & Significance

Grant embodies several of Whitman's central themes: democratic leadership, military valor in service of union, and the common man elevated by circumstance and character. Whitman, who had served as a volunteer nurse in Washington's military hospitals during the war, understood the human cost Grant had managed and the moral weight of command. The "Death of General Grant" is thus more than a news item; it is an elegy compressed into three words, a recognition that the passing of such a figure marks the closing of an era. Grant's trajectory — from obscure clerk to victorious general to president — also enacts Whitman's democratic faith: that greatness can emerge from ordinary American soil.
