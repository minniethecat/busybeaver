## Overview

Union is one of the most multivalent concepts in [[walt-whitman|Walt Whitman]]'s poetic vocabulary, operating simultaneously on political, spiritual, and erotic registers. Politically, Union refers to the preservation of the United States against the threat of secession — a cause for which Whitman gave three years of his life as a hospital volunteer. Spiritually, Union names the merging of the individual [[self|self]] with the cosmos, the "merge" that Whitman celebrates throughout *Leaves of Grass* as the highest experience available to the soul. Erotically, Union figures the physical intimacy through which separate bodies become one, a theme Whitman treats with unprecedented candor.

These three meanings are not separate in Whitman's imagination. The political Union is sustained by the spiritual union of citizens who recognize their shared being; spiritual union is modeled on and expressed through bodily union; and all forms of union are threatened by the same forces — separation, isolation, the refusal of connection. "Crossing Brooklyn Ferry" makes this explicit: the ferry crossing becomes a figure for the union of past and present, self and other, living and dead, all "disintegrated yet part of the scheme."

## In Whitman's Poetry

- **"Crossing Brooklyn Ferry"** (BOOK VIII): The central poem of union, in which the ferry passage across the East River becomes a meditation on the continuity of human experience across time. "It avails not, time nor place — distance avails not, / I am with you, you men and women of a generation, or ever so many generations hence" — this is union across history.

- **"Song of Myself"** (BOOK III): Section 5's ecstatic merging of body and soul — "I believe in you my soul, the other I am must not abase itself to you" — is Whitman's earliest and most radical statement of spiritual union.

- **Civil War poems**: The "Drum-Taps" cluster and Lincoln elegies are poems about the threat to Union and its costly restoration. The war taught Whitman that union is not a given but something that must be defended and, when broken, painfully rebuilt.

## Key Passages

> I am with you, you men and women of a generation, or ever so many generations hence,
> Just as you feel when you look on the river and sky, so I felt,
> Just as any of you is one of a living crowd, I was one of a crowd...
> — [005-020]

"Crossing Brooklyn Ferry" — union across time, the physical fact of shared experience binding all humans who have ever looked at the same water, the same sky.
