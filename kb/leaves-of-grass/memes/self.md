## Overview

The Self is the organizing center of Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* — the "I" that speaks the poems, celebrates itself, and expands to encompass all humanity, nature, and cosmos. This is not the narrow ego of Romantic confession but a radically inclusive identity: "I am myself just as much evil as good" (001-130). Whitman's self is democratic in structure — it refuses hierarchy, contains contradictions, and extends itself to identify with every person, every object, and every experience.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The self is declared as the poem's subject from the very first line of "Song of Myself": "I celebrate myself, and sing myself." Whitman's self is performative — it inflates and expands as the poem progresses. "A throat is now inflating itself" (001-206) captures the self as a growing, swelling presence. The self is not confined to Whitman's own biography — it is a representative self, a democratic self that speaks for and as all people. The self is both the source of poetic authority ("I think I shall then supply myself with the poems of my soul," 001-094) and the field of experience where all things converge.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's expansive self draws on Transcendentalist notions of the individual as conduit to the divine — Emerson's "transparent eyeball" is a clear precursor. But Whitman's self is more embodied, more erotic, more populous. It also draws on democratic theory: in a democracy, every individual's voice carries equal weight, and the poet's "I" can legitimately speak for all. Psychologically, Whitman anticipates later notions of the self as constructed, performative, and multiple — "I am large, I contain multitudes" — decades before modernist explorations of fractured identity.

> I am myself just as much evil as good, and my nation is — and I say there is in fact no evil,
> — (001-130)

> A throat is now inflating itself and undulating as I sing,

> And what I assume you shall assume,

## Related Concepts

- **The Soul**: The self's deepest layer — "I believe in you my soul, the other I am must not abase itself to you"
- **Democracy**: The democratic self speaks for all — "what I assume you shall assume"
- **The Body**: The self is embodied — not abstract consciousness but physical presence
- **Love**: Self-love is the foundation of universal love — "I celebrate myself"
- **Nature**: The self merges with natural processes — grass, sea, earth
