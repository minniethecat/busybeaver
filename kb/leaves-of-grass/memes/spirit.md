## Overview

Spirit in [[walt-whitman|Walt Whitman]]'s poetry is not the enemy of the body but its completion. Rejecting the Puritan dualism that opposed flesh to soul, Whitman insists on their inseparability: "I am the poet of the Body and I am the poet of the Soul" ("Song of Myself," PN 002-422). The spirit, for Whitman, is immanent within the material world — it does not transcend the physical but radiates through it. Every sensual experience is potentially a spiritual one; every body is "the procession of the soul."

This incarnational spirituality sets Whitman apart from both orthodox Christianity and the more abstract Transcendentalism of Emerson. Where Emerson's "Over-Soul" tends toward the disembodied and philosophical, Whitman's spirit is always located — in the "fragrance of the grass," the touch of a lover, the sound of the sea. The spiritual life is not achieved by turning away from the senses but by entering into them more fully.

## In Whitman's Poetry

- **"Song of Myself"** (BOOK III): Section 5's dialogue between body and soul is the central statement of Whitman's spiritual vision. The soul does not command or transcend the body but lies alongside it — "I believe in you my soul, the other I am must not abase itself to you."

- **"Proud Music of the Storm"** (BOOK XXV): The poem traces the soul's journey from sensory experience (the "proud music" of the storm) to spiritual transcendence, a journey that passes THROUGH rather than around the body's perceptions.

- **"Passage to India"** (BOOK XXVI): The soul's ultimate voyage — "Passage to more than India!" — beyond physical exploration to spiritual discovery.

## Key Passages

> I believe in you my soul, the other I am must not abase itself to you,
> And you must not be abased to the other.
> — [002-082]

The great democratic gesture extended to the relationship between body and soul — neither is subordinate to the other.
