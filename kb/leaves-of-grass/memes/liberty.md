## Overview

For Walt Whitman, liberty is never merely political — it is existential, cosmic, and embodied. "I announce uncompromising liberty and equality" (021-1200) he declares in the closing poems of *Leaves of Grass*, yoking the two great democratic ideals into a single utterance. Liberty in Whitman's lexicon spans from the personal (the soul's freedom from convention) to the planetary ("liberty, the earth, the elements" at 004-172) to the historical (the "fierce French liberty songs" at 003-037).

This expansiveness distinguishes Whitman's liberty from the narrower Enlightenment conception. Where Jefferson located liberty in natural rights and limited government, Whitman locates it in the body, the open road, and the cosmic order. Liberty is not a political condition but a mode of being — "immensity, liberty, action" (003-168) form an indivisible triad.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Liberty surfaces across the geographical and philosophical sweep of *Leaves of Grass*. In "Salut au Monde!" (BOOK VI), Whitman hears "fierce French liberty songs" (003-037) — the revolutionary echo of 1789, 1830, and 1848 — and extends his salute to liberty-lovers across nations: "You Belge! you liberty-lover of the Netherlands!" (003-243).

"Song of the Open Road" (BOOK VII) fuses liberty with motion: "Allons! with power, liberty, the earth, the elements" (004-172). The open road IS liberty — the freedom to move, to depart, to shed the accumulated weight of settlement and respectability.

"Song of the Broad-Axe" (BOOK XII) gives liberty a historical narrative: "revolted lieges determin'd for liberty" (009-117) — the axe that clears forests also clears tyranny.

The most concentrated treatment comes in "By Blue Ontario's Shore" (BOOK XXIII), where Whitman addresses liberty as a national crisis: "When liberty goes out of a place it is not the first to go" (015-0849), and its restoration requires more than laws: "Then only shall liberty or the idea of liberty be disinfected from the slightest taint" (015-0855). Liberty, for Whitman, is contagious — it spreads or contracts by example, not decree.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's liberty draws from three intersecting traditions:

**Revolutionary republicanism**. The American and French Revolutions furnish the vocabulary — "uncompromising liberty," "equality," "the rights of man" — but Whitman pushes beyond constitutional liberty toward something more absolute. Political liberty is a floor, not a ceiling.

**Romantic individualism**. Wordsworth's "unchartered freedom" and Byron's defiant autonomy echo in Whitman's "Song of the Open Road," but Whitman's individualist liberty is generative, not oppositional — it creates new selves rather than merely defending existing ones.

**Abolitionist thought**. Writing across the decades of slavery and Civil War, Whitman's liberty is shadowed by its negation. The "rage divine for liberty" (015-1480) is the rage of the enslaved, the fugitive, the abolitionist. "What of liberty and slavery among them" (015-0878) — the question hangs over the entire democratic project.

## Key Passages

> Allons! with power, liberty, the earth, the elements,
> Health, defiance, gayety, self-esteem, curiosity;
> Allons! from all formules!
> From your formules, O bat-eyed and materialistic priests.
>
> — [004-172]

> When liberty goes out of a place it is not the first to go,
> Nor the second or third to go,
> It waits for all the rest to go, it is the last.
>
> — [015-0849]

> I announce uncompromising liberty and equality,
> I announce the justification of candor and the justification of pride.
>
> — [021-1200]
