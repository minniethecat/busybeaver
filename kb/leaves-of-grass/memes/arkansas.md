# Arkansas

**Arkansas** appears in 6 corpus passages across Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*, where it serves as a marker of the lower Mississippi region — a land of rivers and settlements, of the "Arkansian" type that Whitman catalogues among the American multitude. Less prominent in the poetry than Ohio or Virginia, Arkansas nonetheless contributes a distinctive note to Whitman's continental symphony: the note of the deep interior South, where the great river shapes the land and its people.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Arkansas enters Whitman's verse through its waterways — "those drain'd by the Tennessee, or through those of the Arkansas" (002-0431) — the Arkansas River named as one of the continent's defining hydrological features. The state appears among Whitman's catalogue of American settlements: "The settlements of the Arkansas, Colorado, Ottawa, Willamette" (009-044), a list that spans the continent from the South to the Northwest. In the geographic roll calls, Arkansas is swept into the westward momentum: "From Nebraska, from Arkansas" (013-279), and the poet's intention to sing each state is announced: "Arkansas to sing theirs" (014-1027). A more enigmatic reference — "Kanada, Arkansas?" (015-0290) — pairs the state with the northern neighbor in a gesture of continental scope, the question mark suggesting open possibility rather than fixed meaning.

## Geographic & Historical Context

Arkansas was admitted as the 25th state in 1836, a slave state whose economy was built on cotton cultivation in the lowlands of the Mississippi Delta. The Arkansas River, a major tributary of the Mississippi, gave the state its name (derived from a Siouan term for the Quapaw people) and its geographic identity. In Whitman's era, Arkansas was still a frontier of settlement — much of its western uplands remained sparsely populated, and the state's development was shaped by the tension between the plantation economy of the Delta and the subsistence agriculture of the highlands. Arkansas seceded from the Union in 1861 and was readmitted in 1868, its postwar history marked by the struggles of Reconstruction and the rise of Jim Crow.

## Symbolic Meaning

Arkansas embodies the **riverine interior** in Whitman's symbolic geography. Its identity is inseparable from the waterway that bears its name — a tributary of the great Mississippi, part of the hydrological web that Whitman's catalogues celebrate as the circulatory system of the continent. The "settlements of the Arkansas" suggest a landscape in the process of being inhabited, a frontier not yet fully transformed into the settled agricultural order of the older states. This in-between quality — neither old South nor new West — gives Arkansas its distinctive resonance in Whitman's poetry: it is a place of becoming, still in the process of joining the national whole.
