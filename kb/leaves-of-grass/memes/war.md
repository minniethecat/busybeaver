## Overview

War, and specifically the American Civil War (1861–1865), marks the great watershed in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*. The early editions (1855, 1856, 1860) are dominated by celebration, cosmic affirmation, and the expansive self. After Whitman's experience as a volunteer nurse in Washington, D.C. military hospitals, the later editions introduce the "Drum-Taps" poems and Lincoln elegies — work that confronts suffering, death, and national fracture with a gravity absent from the pre-war poetry. War becomes the crucible in which Whitman's democratic faith is tested and deepened.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Even before the war, Whitman's poetry carries martial energies. In "Starting from Paumanok," he declares himself "Resolute warlike One including and over all" (001-105) — the poet as spiritual warrior. But this pre-war bellicosity is abstract; the actual war grounds it in blood and bandages.

The "Drum-Taps" sequence (incorporated into later editions) and the Lincoln elegies represent Whitman's most direct engagement with war. "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd" transmutes Lincoln's assassination into a national elegy that moves through grief toward reconciliation. "The Wound-Dresser" documents the poet's hospital service with unflinching detail. Throughout, Whitman refuses both jingoism and despair — the war is portrayed as a necessary trial through which the Union proves its worth. The suffering of soldiers — North and South alike — is rendered with equal compassion, reflecting Whitman's insistence that democratic love transcends political division.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's war poetry draws from several traditions. The classical elegy (pastoral setting, procession of mourners, apotheosis of the deceased) structures "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd." The tradition of battlefield poetry — from Homer to Tennyson — provides formal precedents, but Whitman's free verse and democratic perspective transform the genre. Most distinctively, Whitman's hospital experience gives his war poetry a medical, embodied quality: he catalogs wounds, amputations, and the intimate gestures of nursing care. This is war poetry from the ward, not the battlefield — uniquely Whitman's contribution to the genre.

## Related Concepts

- **Democracy**: The Civil War was the supreme test of democratic government — "whether that nation, or any nation so conceived … can long endure"
- **Death**: War forced Whitman to confront death on an unprecedented scale
- **Comradeship**: The "manly love of comrades" was forged in the crucible of war
- **Healing**: Whitman's nursing experience made healing a central poetic theme
- **The Union**: The preservation of national unity became Whitman's overriding political commitment
