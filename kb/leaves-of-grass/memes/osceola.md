# Osceola

**Osceola** (1804–1838), the Seminole leader who organized resistance against the United States government's forced removal policies during the Second Seminole War, appears in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* through a single, resonant word: "Osceola" (021-2156). Listed without elaboration among other American figures and reference points, the name carries the weight of Native American history — a presence that haunts and complicates Whitman's celebration of the American experiment.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The single mention of Osceola — the name standing alone in a verse catalogue (021-2156) — is typical of Whitman's approach to Native American subjects. He rarely elaborates; instead, he names. The effect is incantatory: Osceola's name, placed among other American signifiers, becomes a marker of presence and loss. Whitman does not narrate the Seminole Wars or Osceola's betrayal and imprisonment. But the name, once spoken, cannot be unheard. It registers the Native American as an indelible part of the American whole — not absent, not forgotten, but named into the poetic record.

## Historical / Biographical Context

Osceola was born Billy Powell around 1804 in present-day Alabama to a Creek mother and a white trader father. When the United States attempted to forcibly remove the Seminole from their Florida lands under the Indian Removal Act of 1830, Osceola emerged as a resistance leader. He led guerrilla campaigns against U.S. forces during the Second Seminole War (1835–1842), achieving notable victories against a superior military. In 1837, Osceola was captured while under a flag of truce — an act of deception by U.S. authorities that drew international condemnation. Imprisoned at Fort Moultrie in South Carolina, he died of illness in January 1838, aged about 34. His death cemented his status as a symbol of Native American resistance and the injustices of removal.

## Themes & Significance

Osceola's place in *Leaves of Grass* speaks to one of Whitman's most complex and unresolved themes: the Native American presence in the American story. Whitman's poetry celebrates the continent's indigenous names — "Monongahela," "Mississippi," "Mannahatta" — even as it envisions an America built by settlers and immigrants. Osceola, named without narrative, embodies this tension. He is present in the poem — acknowledged, recorded — yet his story goes untold. For contemporary readers, the single name is an invitation: to remember what Whitman could not or did not fully say, to hold the Native American history that the democratic panorama both includes and obscures.
