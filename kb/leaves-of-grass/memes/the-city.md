## Overview

The city occupies a central and affectionate place in Walt Whitman's poetic imagination. Whitman was, by biography and sensibility, a poet of the city — born on Long Island but formed in Brooklyn and Manhattan, where he worked as a printer, journalist, and editor. The urban landscape of *Leaves of Grass* — its crowded streets, ferry boats, omnibuses, workshops, and waterfronts — is not mere backdrop but active participant in the democratic vision.

For Whitman, the city is democracy made visible. Its teeming diversity of races, trades, classes, and languages is the nation in microcosm. "City of the world! (for all races are here)" [014-1308] — the urban center is where the democratic experiment is most intense, most chaotic, and most promising. The city's noise and motion are not defects but evidence of vitality.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman's urban poetry reaches its apex in "Crossing Brooklyn Ferry" (section 3 of *Song of Myself* in some editions), where the ferry ride between Brooklyn and Manhattan becomes a meditation on time, community, and the shared experience of city-dwellers across generations. The ferry is the democratic vehicle par excellence — carrying all classes, all races, all purposes across the same water.

"City of Orgies" [002-2640] reclaims urban pleasure from moralistic condemnation. "City of orgies, walks and joys" [002-2641] — the city's pleasures are enumerated without shame, part of the full acceptance of life that characterizes Whitman's vision. "City whom that I have lived and sung in your midst will one day make you illustrious" [002-2642] — the poet's presence sanctifies the city, and the city's life nourishes the poet.

"City of Ships" [014-1304] celebrates the maritime commerce that made New York a global hub. "City of ships! / City of the sea! city of hurried and glittering tides!" [014-1310] — the harbor is the city's connection to the wider world, the point where America meets the globe. The ships bring goods, peoples, and languages, enriching the democratic mixture.

In "Once I Pass'd Through a Populous City" [002-2262], the urban encounter becomes the occasion for a fleeting but indelible romantic connection — the city as the setting for human intimacy amid the crowd.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's urban vision stands in contrast to both the pastoral tradition (which saw the city as corrupt) and the Gothic imagination (which saw it as monstrous). His closest predecessor is William Blake's London — also seen as a spiritual landscape — but Whitman lacks Blake's apocalyptic dread. For Whitman, the city is fundamentally healthy: its crowds are not threatening but affirming; its noise is not chaos but music.

The city also represents America's difference from Europe. European cities carried the weight of feudal and monarchical history; American cities were being built in real time, their character still forming. Whitman's urban poetry is part of this construction — naming the city, celebrating it, giving it a mythology.

## Related Concepts

The city is the spatial embodiment of **Democracy** — diverse individuals living in proximity, negotiating difference through daily encounter. It connects to **Journey** through the ferry, the omnibus, the pedestrian's wandering path. The city's commerce links it to **Labor** and its crowds to **Comrade**-ship. The urban landscape — docks, streets, buildings — is part of the American **Nature** that Whitman celebrates, no less sacred than the prairie.
