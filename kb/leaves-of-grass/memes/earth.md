## Overview

The Earth in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* is the shared physical foundation of all human experience — "the whole earth and all the stars in the sky are for religion's sake" (001-140). Whitman's earth is not a distant, abstract planet but the immediate soil beneath one's feet, the "grass-fields of the world" (001-271), the rocks and flowers and rivers that he catalogs with loving attention. The earth is the democratic ground — literally the ground — on which all people stand equally.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Whitman's engagement with the earth is simultaneously cosmic and granular. He opens "Starting from Paumanok" with a catalogue of earthly particulars: "Of earth, rocks, Fifth-month flowers experienced, stars, rain, snow, my amaze" (001-017). The earth is the source of all poetic material. He speaks of "the life of the earth" (001-158) as an ongoing, generative force, and imagines dropping into it "the germs of a greater religion" (001-171).

In "Song of Myself," the earth becomes a conversational partner: "Have you reckon'd the earth much?" (002-0041). Whitman's answer is that the earth's value cannot be quantified — it is the foundation of all value. The earth provides the good things of life freely: "you shall possess the good of the earth and sun" (002-0046). The earth is also the repository of all human history — the graves, the battles, the civilizations that have risen and fallen upon it.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's earth-centered spirituality reflects several influences. Transcendentalism's reverence for nature as a direct revelation of the divine is present throughout. The Romantic sublime — nature as overwhelming, awe-inspiring — appears in Whitman's cosmic passages, but he equally values the ordinary and proximate: a spear of grass, a leaf, a pebble. American exceptionalism also plays a role: the vast, "unclaimed" American continent becomes the stage for Whitman's democratic vision, with its "pastoral plains" and "prairies" (001-271). The earth is America, and America is the earth's destined expression.

## Related Concepts

- **Nature**: Earth is the foundation of nature — the planet that hosts all living things
- **Democracy**: The earth is the one thing all people share equally — the ultimate democratic commons
- **The Body**: The body is made of earth and returns to earth — "I bequeath myself to the dirt to grow from the grass I love"
- **Grass**: The grass is the earth's democratic flag — growing everywhere, on every grave
- **America**: The American continent is the earth's chosen stage for democratic experiment
