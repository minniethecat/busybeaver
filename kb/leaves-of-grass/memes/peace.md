## Overview

Peace in Whitman's poetry operates on multiple registers: as a personal spiritual state, as a political aspiration for a nation recovering from Civil War, and as a cosmic condition underlying the apparent strife of nature. "Peace and knowledge that pass all the argument of the earth" [002-0121] — the phrase echoes the biblical "peace that passeth understanding" (Philippians 4:7) while grounding it in earthly experience rather than otherworldly transcendence. Whitman's peace is not the absence of conflict but a deeper reconciliation that includes and transforms it: he is "at peace about God and about death" [002-1634] not because he has resolved theological questions but because he has accepted mystery. The peace he envisions is "practical, peaceful life, the people's life" [010-153] — embodied in daily existence rather than achieved through doctrinal consensus. After the Civil War, peace acquires an urgent political dimension: the nation must be "bathed in peace — elate, secure in peace" [010-154] if the democratic experiment is to endure.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The theme of peace runs throughout *Leaves of Grass*, shifting in tone and emphasis across editions. In the 1855 *Song of Myself*, peace is primarily a personal attainment: "peace and knowledge that pass all the argument of the earth" [002-0121] describes a state of intuitive acceptance that transcends rational dispute. The *Calamus* poems of 1860 extend this to interpersonal relations: "Return in peace to the ocean my love" [002-2195] images peace as both departure and reunion, a rhythm of attachment and release. The Civil War transforms peace from a philosophical ideal into a political necessity. The Drum-Taps poems (1865) grieve the war's cost while gesturing toward reconciliation. The *Sequel to Drum-Taps* (1865–66), later absorbed into the main collection, includes the elegy for Lincoln — "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd" — where peace arrives through mourning: the "peaceful child" [014-0278] the speaker was before the war can never fully return, but a more mature peace, informed by loss, becomes possible. "Health, peace, salvation universal" [013-241] in *Song of the Universal* elevates peace to a cosmic principle.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's conception of peace draws on Quaker theology, with its emphasis on the "inner light" and peaceful witness, though Whitman himself was not a practicing Quaker. The biblical language of peace — *shalom* in Hebrew, *eirene* in Greek — resonates through his diction, secularized but not emptied of spiritual force. Emerson's Transcendentalism offers a model of inner peace achieved through alignment with nature and the Over-Soul. After 1865, Whitman's peace-writing enters dialogue with the broader literature of Reconstruction: his vision is less punitive than the Radical Republicans' and more spiritually inflected than the political compromises of 1877. His insistence on "agitation and peace" together [013-475] — "I hold up agitation and conflict" alongside peace — distinguishes him from quietist traditions: peace must be achieved through engagement, not withdrawal.

## Key Passages

> Swiftly arose and spread around me the peace and knowledge that pass all the argument of the earth,
> And I know that the hand of God is the promise of my own,
> And I know that the spirit of God is the brother of my own,
> And that all the men ever born are also my brothers, and the women my sisters and lovers.
> — [002-0121]

> Practical, peaceful life, the people's life, the life of business, of labor, of affairs,
> Lifted, illumin'd, bathed in peace — elate, secure in peace.
> — [010-153–154]

> I raise eminent men and hold up peace, I hold up agitation and conflict,
> I do not praise the peaceful and the studious, nor the wealth and the slipper.
> — [013-475]

## Related Concepts

Peace is the resolution of [[war]] — not its negation but its transcendence. In the personal sphere, peace relates to [[soul]] and [[joy]] as a state of spiritual equilibrium. The democratic peace Whitman envisions connects to [[union]] — the national reconciliation after Civil War — and to [[democracy]] as a system that channels conflict into peaceful resolution. Peace achieved through mourning links this concept to [[death]] and [[immortality]], as in the Lincoln elegies.
