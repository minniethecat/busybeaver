## Overview

"Song of the Exposition" occupies BOOK XIII of the 1891–92 Deathbed Edition of *Leaves of Grass*. Originally written for the American Institute's 1871 Industrial Exhibition in New York City, the poem is Whitman's most direct engagement with the material culture of American progress — the machines, products, and industrial processes that were transforming the nation in the decades after the Civil War.

The poem gathers the products of American labor — agricultural, industrial, and artisanal — into a poetic exhibition that rivals the physical fair it was written to accompany. Like the exposition it celebrates, the poem is a display case: an arrangement of objects meant to demonstrate the vitality and variety of American productive power. In Whitman's hands, the exposition becomes a metaphor for democratic culture itself — a public space where the fruits of labor are displayed not for private profit but for collective wonder.

## Themes

### Industry as Poetry

Whitman insists that American industry is a fit subject for poetry — a radical claim in a literary culture that still associated poetry with elevated, classical, or pastoral themes. The poem enumerates "the varied products of Ohio, Pennsylvania, Missouri, Georgia, Texas, and the rest" (010-240), treating agricultural produce and manufactured goods as equivalent to the traditional subjects of verse.

### Democratic Productivity

The poem's vision of production is fundamentally democratic: every region, every occupation, every product contributes to the national whole. The exposition is not a competition but a collaboration — a gathering of "the States" in their material aspect, each bringing its distinctive gifts to the common display.

### America as Exhibition

Whitman recasts the American continent itself as a perpetual exposition — a display of natural and human abundance that requires no formal fair to be seen. The poem's ultimate subject is not the 1871 exhibition but the ongoing exhibition of American life that Whitman's poetry itself aims to document.

## Structure & Form

"Song of the Exposition" employs Whitman's characteristic catalogic method, organized around the conceit of the exhibition. Key formal features include:

- **Exhibition frame**: The poem is structured as a walk through the exhibition, moving from display to display, product to product, region to region.

- **Invocation of the Muse**: Unusually for Whitman, the poem opens with a classical invocation — "After all not to create only" — that explicitly redefines the Muse for the industrial age: the Muse is now the spirit of American productivity.

- **Geographic catalogue**: Like "Our Old Feuillage," the poem maps the continent through its products, using the exhibition as an organizing principle for geographical enumeration.

- **Rhetorical address**: The poem frequently addresses its audience directly, reproducing the experience of a public oration delivered at the exhibition itself.

## Key Passages

The poem's opening redefinition of the Muse for industrial America:

> After all not to create only, or found only,
> But to bring perhaps from afar what is already founded,
> To give it our own identity, average, limitless, free
> — [010-001]

The catalogue of American agricultural abundance draws the continent into the exhibition hall:

> The varied products of Ohio, Pennsylvania, Missouri, Georgia, Texas, and the rest,
> The large, rich crops, with grass and trees,
> The fertile earth itself, the products of the rivers, lakes, and mines
> — [010-240]

## Publication History

"Song of the Exposition" was first composed for the **1871 Industrial Exhibition** of the American Institute in New York City. Whitman, then living in Washington, D.C., and working as a government clerk, saw the commission as an opportunity to reach a broader public. The poem was delivered at the exhibition's opening ceremonies and later incorporated into *Leaves of Grass*. It underwent revisions before reaching its final form in the 1891–92 **Deathbed Edition**, where it occupies BOOK XIII (chapter 10).

## Critical Reception

"Song of the Exposition" has been read primarily within studies of Whitman's relationship to nineteenth-century material culture and industrial capitalism. Critics have debated whether the poem's celebration of American productivity represents an embrace of industrial modernity or a more complex negotiation with the economic transformations of the Gilded Age. The poem's occasional origin — written for a specific event — has led some scholars to treat it as a minor work, though recent criticism has reassessed its significance as a document of Whitman's engagement with the public culture of post-war America.
