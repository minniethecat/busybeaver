## Overview

"Our Old Feuillage" occupies BOOK X of the 1891–92 Deathbed Edition of *Leaves of Grass*. The poem was first published in the 1860 (third) edition, a transitional volume that marked Whitman's deepening engagement with national themes on the eve of the Civil War. The French-derived title word "feuillage" — meaning foliage or leafage — is a deliberate coinage that links the poem to the book's controlling metaphor: *Leaves* of Grass.

The poem is one of Whitman's most ambitious geographical catalogues, sweeping across the American continent to enumerate its regions, types, occupations, and natural features. The "old feuillage" of the title is both the perennial green of the American landscape and the accumulated cultural diversity that, in Whitman's vision, constitutes the nation's true wealth. Like the leaves that return each spring, the poem suggests, American identity is always ancient and always new, rooted in the land and perpetually renewing itself.

## Themes

### American Unity Through Diversity

"Our Old Feuillage" is arguably Whitman's most concentrated poetic argument for the unity of American diversity. The poem catalogues an extraordinary range of regions, peoples, and activities — from "Florida's green peninsula" to "the priceless delta of Louisiana," from "the camp of Georgia wagoners" to the Pacific coast — and asserts that all are "always" part of one feuillage. The word "always" recurs throughout the poem as a structuring refrain, insisting on the permanence of this vision against the centrifugal forces of sectionalism and disunion that would soon erupt into Civil War.

### The Geography of Democracy

The poem maps democratic citizenship onto the physical geography of the continent. Every region, every occupation, every ecosystem participates in the feuillage — the Whitmanian whole that is greater than its parts. The poem's catalogic method enacts its theme: no single item is privileged over others, and meaning emerges from the accumulation and juxtaposition of particulars.

### Nature as National Metaphor

The central metaphor of feuillage — foliage — roots national identity in natural process. Leaves grow, fall, and return; the feuillage is old because it is perennial, regenerating through seasons of crisis and renewal. This organic metaphor for national continuity would prove tragically prescient as the country descended into war the year after the poem's first publication.

## Structure & Form

"Our Old Feuillage" is structured as an extended catalogue, one of Whitman's most elaborate. Key formal features include:

- **Anaphoric "Always"**: The word "Always" (or "Always") opens dozens of lines, creating an incantatory rhythm that asserts permanence and continuity. This is Whitman at his most liturgical.

- **Geographic sweep**: The poem moves systematically across the American continent — south to north, east to west — mapping the nation through enumeration.

- **Parenthetical intimacy**: Brief parenthetical asides — "Always our old feuillage!" (007-002) — break the catalogue's forward momentum with moments of lyric intensity, as if the poet cannot contain his enthusiasm for the vision he is creating.

- **Crescendo structure**: The poem builds through accumulation toward a culminating vision of unity: "bouquets of the incomparable feuillage of these States" (007-168).

## Key Passages

The poem's opening establishes its geographical and metaphorical scope:

> Always our old feuillage!
> Always Florida's green peninsula — always the priceless delta of Louisiana
> — [007-002]

The catalogue encompasses the continent's agricultural variety:

> Always the varied products of Ohio, Pennsylvania, Missouri,
> Georgia, Texas, and the rest
> — [007-020]

The poem's climactic image transforms enumeration into vision:

> How can we but offer our calyx and leaf and fruit,
> bouquets of the incomparable feuillage of these States?
> — [007-168]

## Publication History

"Our Old Feuillage" first appeared in the **1860 edition** of *Leaves of Grass*, the third and most elaborately organized edition, published by the Boston firm Thayer and Eldridge. The 1860 edition introduced several new clusters of poems that responded to the gathering national crisis. The poem survived subsequent reorganizations and revisions to reach its final form in the 1891–92 **Deathbed Edition**, where it occupies BOOK X (chapter 7).

## Critical Reception

"Our Old Feuillage" has been discussed primarily within studies of Whitman's geographical imagination and his response to the sectional crisis of the 1850s. Critics have noted the poem's almost desperate insistence on "always" — the repetition betraying anxiety about the very permanence it asserts. The poem is valued as a primary document of Whitman's antebellum nationalism and his conviction that poetry could serve as a unifying force in a fragmenting nation.
