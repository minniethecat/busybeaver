## Overview

Equality is one of the most radical and pervasive principles in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*. For Whitman, equality is not merely a political ideal enshrined in the Declaration of Independence but a cosmic law — a "divine average" [001-188] that extends to every person, every object, and every moment of existence. Where Jeffersonian democracy limited equality to the political sphere (equal rights before the law), Whitman extended it into the metaphysical: the grass blade is equal to the star, the prostitute equal to the president.

Whitman's conception of equality is inseparable from his poetics of catalogues and his democratic voice. The long enumerative lines of *Song of Myself* enact equality at the level of form — no item in a Whitman catalogue is subordinate to another; each receives its own line, its own moment of attention. This formal egalitarianism is the aesthetic counterpart to the political vision.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Equality appears throughout *Leaves of Grass* not as an abstract slogan but as an embodied practice. In *Song of Myself*, the poet declares himself "the companion and equal" [001-312] of every person he encounters, dissolving hierarchies of class, race, and gender. The famous assertion "I am the poet of the woman the same as the man" [001-220] extends equality across the gender divide.

In "By Blue Ontario's Shore," equality is explicitly tied to liberty: "without retrograde is Equality" [015-0219] — equality is not something achieved and then preserved, but a continuing forward motion. The poem links equality with comradeship: "The continuance of Equality shall be comrades" [014-1868], suggesting that equality is sustained not by laws or institutions but by the lived solidarity of individuals.

The "Calamus" poems deepen this vision by grounding equality in affection and intimacy. The "need of comrades" [002-2325] is at once personal and political: a society of equals must be built on actual bonds of love and admiration between individuals, not merely on abstract principles. Whitman's catalogues — the long lists of people, trades, landscapes, and objects — are the formal enactment of equality. In these passages, the poet grants equal attention to the president and the mechanic, the bride and the prostitute. Each item "fills its period and place and is equal to any" [002-1468].

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's egalitarianism has roots in several intellectual currents. From Quakerism — via his mother's family — he inherited the doctrine of the inner light, the belief that divine truth is accessible to every person without priestly mediation. From Transcendentalism and Emerson's "Self-Reliance" he absorbed the radical valuation of individual experience. From Thomas Paine and the revolutionary tradition he took the political language of rights and equality.

But Whitman went further than any of his sources. Emerson's individualism remained hierarchical at the level of intellect (the "representative man" stands above the ordinary); Whitman democratized Emerson by insisting that "a grain of sand is equally perfect" [002-0845]. His equality was not just for exceptional souls but for all beings and all things. Where the French Revolution's *égalité* was primarily juridical, Whitman's was ontological: equality is a fact about the nature of reality, not a political concession.

## Related Concepts

Equality is the conceptual foundation upon which Whitman builds his vision of **Democracy** — if democracy is the political form, equality is its metaphysical substance. It connects directly to **Comrade**-ship and the "Calamus" vision of adhesive love between equals. The principle that every being is equally valuable ties equality to **The Self** (each self is sovereign), to **The Body** (the body is equal to the soul), and to **Nature** (all natural things are equally perfect). Equality also stands in productive tension with **Individualism** — the equal value of each person enables rather than suppresses individual distinctiveness.
