# Columbia

**Columbia** carries a double meaning in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass*: it is both the poetic personification of America — the goddess Columbia, invoked since the Revolutionary era as the embodiment of national ideals — and the great river of the Pacific Northwest, the Columbia River that winds from the Canadian Rockies to the Pacific Ocean. Across 9 corpus passages, Whitman deploys both senses, weaving geography and national myth into a single resonant name.

## In Whitman's Poetry

The Columbia River anchors Whitman's vision of the continent's farthest reach: "Lands where the north-west Columbia winds, and where the south-west Colorado winds" (001-274). This pairing of the Columbia and the Colorado — two great rivers of the Far West — maps the nation's territorial extremes. The river reappears in later passages: "lakes, or on the Columbia" (009-272), naming it among the waters that define the American landscape.

But Columbia is also Whitman's name for the nation itself, in the older, personified sense. "To thee Columbia" (010-081) addresses the nation as a living entity, a being to whom the poet offers his songs. And in a martial register: "They shall yet make Columbia victorious" (014-1854), "No danger shall balk Columbia's lovers" (014-1857) — the name echoes the rhetoric of national destiny and patriotic resolve. Whitman's Columbia is both a river and a goddess, both geography and idea.

## Geographic & Historical Context

The Columbia River, named after Captain Robert Gray's ship *Columbia Rediviva* (which entered its mouth in 1792), drains a basin of some 258,000 square miles across seven U.S. states and one Canadian province. In Whitman's era, the Columbia was the great waterway of the Oregon Country — a region jointly occupied by the United States and Britain until the Oregon Treaty of 1846. The river represented the farthest reach of American territorial ambition, the Pacific edge of the continent that Whitman's catalogues were determined to encompass. The personification Columbia, meanwhile, was an 18th-century creation — a female embodiment of the Americas, sister to Britannia, widely used in poetry, political cartoons, and the naming of the nation's new capital district ("District of Columbia").

## Symbolic Meaning

Whitman's Columbia embodies the **unity of nation and landscape**. That a single name can denote both the country and a specific river is, for Whitman, not a coincidence but a truth: America is its geography, and its geography is America. The Columbia River marks the nation's northwest limit — the point where the continental catalogue must pause. And "Columbia" as national personification transforms patriotic abstraction into something intimate, a being to whom the poet can speak directly. Both meanings converge in Whitman's democratic mysticism: the land is sacred, and the nation is a living body made of its rivers, mountains, and people.
