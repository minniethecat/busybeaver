## Overview

Abraham Lincoln (1809–1865) stands as the single most consequential historical figure in [[walt-whitman|Walt Whitman]]'s poetic universe. Whitman, who lived in Washington D.C. during the Civil War and witnessed Lincoln's presidency firsthand, transformed the assassinated president into a symbol of national sacrifice, democratic redemption, and the tragic cost of [[liberty]]. No other person in *Leaves of Grass* receives the sustained elegiac treatment that Whitman devoted to Lincoln — two complete poems that rank among the greatest elegies in the English language.

Whitman never met Lincoln personally, but he observed him regularly on the streets of Washington and felt a profound spiritual kinship. In his prose writings, Whitman described Lincoln as "the greatest, best, most characteristic, artistic, moral personality" in American life. The poet's Lincoln is not merely a biographical subject but a [[democracy|democratic]] martyr whose death completes the Civil War's meaning.

## In Whitman's Poetry

Lincoln appears directly and indirectly across multiple poems in *Leaves of Grass*. The most significant treatments are the two great elegies written in the immediate aftermath of his assassination on April 15, 1865:

- **"When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd"** — Whitman's pastoral elegy, composed in the months following Lincoln's death, uses the symbols of lilacs, the evening star (Venus), and the hermit thrush to process grief on a cosmic scale. The funeral train's journey from Washington to Springfield becomes a meditation on death, rebirth, and national healing.

- **"O Captain! My Captain!"** — The more direct, public-facing elegy, written in conventional meter and rhyme (uncharacteristic for Whitman), became his most widely anthologized poem. The captain who "lies fallen cold and dead" is unmistakably Lincoln, the ship of state having weathered the Civil War's storm.

A lesser-known but revealing direct tribute appears in the cluster "Thou Mother with Thy Equal Brood" (BOOK XXXI, PN 021-1373), a short poem titled simply "Abraham Lincoln, Born Feb. 12, 1809" that places Lincoln in a trinity with Washington and Jefferson:

> Swell'd Washington's, Jefferson's, Lincoln's sails.
> — [021-1493]

> Victor's and vanquish'd — Lincoln's and Lee's — now thou with them,
> Man of the mighty days — and equal to the days!
> — [021-1528]

The poem "How Solemn As One by One," written in Washington City in 1865, also reflects the wartime atmosphere in which Whitman encountered Lincoln's presence.

Whitman moved to Washington in December 1862 after learning his brother George had been wounded at the Battle of Fredericksburg. He remained in the capital throughout the remainder of the war, working as a volunteer nurse in military hospitals and as a clerk in the Army Paymaster's Office and later the Attorney General's Office. During this period, Whitman saw Lincoln riding on horseback or in his carriage on numerous occasions. In his prose memoir *Specimen Days*, Whitman recorded these sightings with reverence, describing Lincoln's "deep-cut haggard" face and "eyes, always to me, with a latent sadness in their expression."

Lincoln's assassination on April 14, 1865 — just five days after Lee's surrender at Appomattox — devastated Whitman. The poet channeled his grief into the two elegies that would become cornerstones of his reputation.

## Themes & Significance

Lincoln in Whitman's poetry embodies three interconnected themes:

1. **[[democracy]] and the Common Man** — Lincoln, the rail-splitter turned president, represents Whitman's ideal of democratic leadership: a man of the people elevated not by birth but by character and capacity. Whitman saw Lincoln as the living proof that American democracy could produce greatness from ordinary soil.

2. **Martyrdom and National Redemption** — Whitman frames Lincoln's death as a blood sacrifice that purifies and reunites the nation. In "When Lilacs Last," the president's death becomes cosmically significant, echoed by the evening star's descent and the thrush's song. The personal grief of the poet expands to embrace national — then universal — mourning.

3. **[[death]] and Transcendence** — Lincoln's assassination gave Whitman the occasion for his most profound meditation on death. "When Lilacs Last" moves from the shock of loss through grief to a hard-won acceptance, offering what Whitman called "the sure-enwinding arms of cool-enfolding death." The elegy transforms Lincoln from a historical figure into a permanent presence in the American psyche.

The pairing of the two elegies reflects Whitman's understanding of audience: "O Captain!" for the public, hymn-like and accessible; "When Lilacs Last" for the initiate, a private ritual of mourning that rewards deep reading. Together they demonstrate Whitman's range — the public poet of democratic celebration and the private visionary of [[self|the self]]'s interior landscape.
