# Edgar Allan Poe

**Edgar Allan Poe** (1809–1849) occupies a complex position in *Leaves of Grass*. Whitman acknowledged Poe as a distinctly American voice — one whose "melancholy" and "lurid" genius, while temperamentally opposite to Whitman's own expansive optimism, represented an authentically native literary achievement. Across 5 corpus passages, Whitman engages with Poe as both predecessor and foil.

## Whitman's Recognition of Poe

Whitman, who rarely praised contemporaries, made a notable exception for Poe. He recognized in Poe's work a quality uniquely American — not imitative of European models but born from the nation's own psychic landscape. Where Whitman's America is sunlight and democracy, Poe's is shadow and introspection; yet Whitman saw both as necessary expressions of the national character.

## The "Melancholy" Genius

Poe's characteristic tone — what Whitman called his "lurid" quality — represented for Whitman something the American literary tradition needed to absorb rather than reject. The darkness in Poe's vision was not un-American but deeply American: the underside of the democratic optimism Whitman himself championed.

## Poe and the American Poetic Tradition

In Whitman's historical scheme, Poe stands as a transitional figure: after the European-influenced poets of the early republic but before the full flowering of a native democratic poetry that Whitman himself claimed to inaugurate. Poe cleared ground — proved that American materials and American psychology could generate powerful literature without European sanction.

## Death and Legacy

Poe's death at forty — under still-mysterious circumstances in Baltimore — became part of his legend. Whitman, who wrote extensively about [[Death]], would have recognized in Poe's premature end both tragedy and the kind of myth-making that surrounds certain American figures.
