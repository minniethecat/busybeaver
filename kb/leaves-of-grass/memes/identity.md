## Overview

"Identity" in Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* is a dynamic, paradoxical force — at once the irreducible kernel of the individual self and the medium through which all selves merge into a greater whole. Whitman declares "Always a knit of identity, always distinction, always a breed of life" (002-0064), capturing the double movement: identity distinguishes each person, yet simultaneously knits them together. This tension between singularity and universality runs through the entire poetic sequence and underpins Whitman's democratic vision.

Unlike the Cartesian "I think, therefore I am," Whitman's identity is embodied: "I too had receiv'd identity by my body" (005-094). The physical self is not a vessel for identity but its very source — flesh, sensation, and desire constitute who we are. This corporeal foundation extends to the national body: "I announce that the identity of these States is a single identity only" (021-1202).

## In Whitman's Poetry

Identity appears across the full span of *Leaves of Grass*, from the inaugural "Inscriptions" through the culminating "Songs of Parting." In "Starting from Paumanok" (BOOK II), Whitman introduces the "knit of identity" as a cosmic principle: "Always a knit of identity, always distinction, always a breed of life" (002-0064). The word "knit" suggests textile — threads woven together without losing their individual strands.

In "Crossing Brooklyn Ferry" (BOOK VIII), identity becomes fluid: "I too had receiv'd identity by my body" (005-094). The ferry passage — bodies moving across water, reflections shimmering — becomes a metaphor for identity in transit, never fixed, always receiving itself anew.

"Song of the Broad-Axe" (BOOK XII) extends identity to material labor: "the curious sense of body and identity, the / wonderment at oneself" (012-084). Here identity is discovered not through introspection but through craft — the axe that shapes wood also shapes the self.

The late poems intensify the philosophical stakes. In "To Think of Time" (BOOK XXIX), identity confronts mortality: "That may-be identity beyond the grave is a beautiful fable" (002-2501). And in "Songs of Parting" (BOOK XXXI), the grand synthesis arrives: "no birth, identity, form — no object of the world" (021-1633) — identity dissolves into pure being.

## Philosophical & Literary Context

Whitman's treatment of identity draws on three major currents:

**Transcendentalism**. Emerson's "Self-Reliance" (1841) posited an original, divine self that society corrupts. Whitman radicalizes this: the self is not merely to be trusted but actively celebrated — "I celebrate myself, and sing myself."

**Hegelian dialectics**. Though Whitman may not have read Hegel directly, the triadic rhythm of thesis-antithesis-synthesis permeates his identity thinking: individual identity (thesis) encounters the other (antithesis) and merges into cosmic identity (synthesis). The "knit of identity" is dialectical weaving.

**Quaker spirituality**. The Quaker concept of the "Inner Light" — the divine presence within each person — resonates throughout Whitman's treatment of identity. Every self houses the sacred; democracy is the political expression of this spiritual equality.

Unlike his Romantic contemporaries (Wordsworth's solitary self, Byron's heroic ego), Whitman's identity is constitutively social — it exists only in relation to others, to the city, to the nation, to the cosmos.

> To elaborate is no avail, learn'd and unlearn'd feel that it is so.
>
> — [002-0064]
