# Renaissance

## Overview

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **Renaissance** occupies a position of tragic significance: it was the moment when the classical [[Master Morality|aristocratic]] [[ideals|ideal]] — the value system of [[Greece]] and [[Rome]] — staged its last great counter-offensive against the triumphant [[slave-morality|slave morality]] of [[Christianity]]. The Renaissance, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is the great "what if" of Western history: what if the classical revival had succeeded? What if [[Rome]] had defeated [[Jews|Judaea]] after all?

> At any rate there took place in the Renaissance a brilliantly sinister revival of the classical ideal, of the aristocratic valuation of all things. (015-002)

## Nietzsche's Interpretation

### The Revival of the Classical Ideal

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] characterizes the Renaissance not as a peaceful cultural flowering but as a "brilliantly sinister" (*glänzend-unheimliche*) resurgence — an event that was both magnificent and dangerous. The Renaissance aristocrat, like his Roman predecessor, affirmed himself without reference to Christian [[guilt]] or [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic]] self-denial. For a brief historical moment, the [[Master Morality|master]] walked the e[[artist|art]]h again.

> At any rate there took place in the Renaissance a brilliantly sinister revival of the classical ideal, of the aristocratic valuation of all things: Rome itself bent like one awakening from a trance under the pressure of the new, Judaized Rome. (015-002)

### Attempts Towards Honesty

In a later passage, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] credits the Renaissance with "attempts towards honesty" — a drive toward truthfulness that broke with the Christian habit of self-deception. This intellectual honesty (*Redlichkeit*) is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the one virtue that [[christianity|Christianity]] unwittingly cultivated and that eventually turned against [[christianity|Christianity]] itself.

> Attempts towards honesty at the Renaissance, for example: always for the sake of the ascetic ideal. (071-004)

## Role in Moral Genealogy

In the genealogical narrative, the Renaissance functions as the *counter-event* to the [[slave-morality|slave revolt in morals]]. If Essay 1 narrates the victory of [[Jews|Judaea]] over [[Rome]], the Renaissance is the moment when [[rome|Rome]] briefly reawakened — when the classical [[ideals|ideal]], buried but not dead, attempted to reclaim its throne.

The Renaissance failed. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] attributes this failure to the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] — the German religious movement that, in his view, restored the [[Jews|Judean]]-[[Christianity|Christian]] value system precisely when it was most vulnerable. The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] was the "German" response to the Italian Renaissance, and it ensured that the slave revolt in morals would continue to dominate [[europe|European civilization]].

The Renaissance thus marks a genealogical fork in the road: the path not taken. Its defeat by the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] sealed the fate of [[europe|Europe]]an culture for the next four centuries — and, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s view, set the stage for the crisis of [[nihilism]] that defines modernity.

## Key Passages

> At any rate there took place in the Renaissance a brilliantly sinister revival of the classical ideal, of the aristocratic valuation of all things: Rome itself bent like one awakening from a trance under the pressure of the new, Judaized Rome, which presented the aspect of an oecumenical synagogue and was called the "Church." (015-002)

> Attempts towards honesty at the Renaissance, for example: always for the sake of the ascetic ideal — one does not yet dare to will the *contrary* ideal. (071-004)
