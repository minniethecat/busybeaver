## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]''s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, **knowledge** (*Erkenntnis*, *Wissen*) is not a neutral accumulation of facts but a *value* — and a problematic one. The "will to knowledge" that drives [[science|modern science]] and philosophy is, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, the latest metamorphosis of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]: the belief that truth is unconditionally valuable, that knowing is inherently good, and that life should be sacrificed to the pursuit of knowledge.

> We finders of knowledge, provided that we know how to keep silent long enough. (P05-001, paraphrase)

## Nietzsche''s Analysis

### Knowledge as Ascetic Discipline

The pursuit of knowledge requires the same self-denial that characterizes religious [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]]. The scholar renounces pleasure, withdraws from the world, and submits to a discipline:

> The will to truth which first became conscious of itself as a problem... more and more heavily on the human conscience. (002-003, paraphrase)

The scholar''s devotion to objectivity, to method, to "the facts" — this is [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] in scientific dress. The "disinterested" pursuit of knowledge is still an *interest*, a valuation: it values truth above life.

### The Philosopher and the Will to Knowledge

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] examines the philosopher as a type shaped by the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]:

> What matters that to us, us the philosophers? (P04-002)

The philosopher''s pose of disinterested contemplation masks a [[will-to-power|will to power]] expressed as the will to knowledge. The philosopher does not *discover* truth — he *creates* values, then calls them truths. The ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] provides the philosopher with the mask of objectivity behind which he exercises his creative power.

### Knowledge and the Intellectual Conscience

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] distinguishes the "will to knowledge" (which he critiques) from the "[[conscience|intellectual conscience]]" (which he values). The [[conscience|intellectual conscience]] is the rigorous honesty that refuses to deceive oneself — even when the truth is uncomfortable:

> Nowadays the intellectual conscience dwells and is alive — in point of fact it is even the *most ticklish* of all moral things. (065-001)

But this [[conscience|intellectual conscience]], pushed to its extreme, becomes self-destructive: "the will to truth" that would rather will *nothingness* than not will at all.

### The Problem of Truth

The culmination of the will to knowledge is the recognition that *truth itself* is a value — and perhaps a life-denying one:

> The will for deception has good conscience on its side, is much more fundamental, more primeval, more *holy* than the will to truth. (066-001, paraphrase)

Art, appearance, and deception are, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], more life-affirming than truth. The ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]''s triumph is the elevation of truth to the supreme value — and this, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] suggests, is where the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] finally defeats itself, for the will to truth eventually questions *its own* value.

## Key Passages

> Where stand the hives of our knowledge. It is to those hives that we are always on our way, we born honey-gatherers of the spirit. (P03-001, paraphrase)

> From a fundamental "*fiat*" of knowledge, whose empire reached to the very depths of the logical instincts. (P04-001, paraphrase)

> The will to truth which first became conscious of itself as a problem. (002-003, paraphrase)
