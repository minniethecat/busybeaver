# Honoré Gabriel Mirabeau

## Overview

Honoré Gabriel Riqueti, Comte de Mirabeau (1749–1791), was a French revolutionary statesman, orator, and writer whose tumultuous life and prodigious political energy made him one of the most celebrated figures of the early French Revolution. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Mirabeau appears in a single but strategically crucial passage: as the modern exemplar of the [[noble|aristocratic]] capacity to forget — a psychological trait [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] opposes to the resentful man's inability to let go of injury. Mirabeau, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] writes, "had no memory for any insults and meannesses which were practised on him, and who was only incapable of forgiving because he forgot" (010-002). This formulation crystallizes one of the [[genealogy|Genealogy]]'s central psychological claims: that genuine forgiveness is not a moral achievement but a [[health|physiological]] capacity — the overflow of strength that spontaneously expels injury, leaving no trace behind.

> A good example of this in the modern world is Mirabeau, who had no memory for any insults and meannesses which were practised on him, and who was only incapable of forgiving because he forgot. (010-002)

## Role in the Genealogy

Mirabeau enters the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] in Essay One, §10, as [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] elaborates the psychological contrast between [[noble|aristocratic]] and slave moralities. The section is devoted to the concept of *[[forgetfulness|forgetting]]* — not as a failure of memory but as an active, positive faculty, a "door-keeper" of psychic health that preserves consciousness for what is new and vital by expelling what is spent and injurious. Mirabeau is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s empirical proof that such [[forgetfulness|forgetting]] is not a philosophical abstraction but a real human capacity, observable in the strongest natures. The invocation is strategic: by choosing a figure from the French Revolution — the event that, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], represented the political triumph of [[ressentiment]] — [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] locates [[noble|aristocratic]] health even within modernity, as a remnant of an older, stronger humanity.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### Forgetting as Active Force

The passage in which Mirabeau appears is one of the [[genealogy|Genealogy]]'s most philosophically dense. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is arguing that [[forgetfulness|forgetting]] is not a passive "inertia" but "an active and in the strictest sense positive faculty of repression" (010-002). Without this faculty, consciousness would be overwhelmed by the accumulated residue of every insult, every disappointment, every slight. The man of [[ressentiment]] — the slave type — lacks this faculty: "His soul *squints*; his mind loves hidden crannies, tortuous paths and back-doors" (010-002). Mirabeau is his antithesis. Where the resentful man nurses every wound, Mirabeau simply does not register injury as injury. His [[forgetfulness|forgetting]] is not a moral decision to forgive but a [[health|physiological]] incapacity to hold a grudge.

> Such a man indeed shakes off with a shrug many a worm which would have buried itself in another. (010-002)

### The French Revolution Context

Mirabeau's appearance in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] is enriched by [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s broader treatment of the French Revolution elsewhere in the text. In Essay One, §16, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] analyzes the Revolution as the political eruption of [[ressentiment]] — the moment when "the *[[ressentiment]]* of the masses becomes *creative* and gives birth to values" (015-002). Mirabeau, a moderate revolutionary who sought constitutional monarchy rather than the Terror, occupies an ambiguous position: a figure of [[noble|aristocratic]] health operating within a movement driven by [[slave-morality|slave morality]]. His early death in 1791 — before the Revolution descended into the Terror — makes him, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s implicit narrative, a symbol of what the Revolution might have been had [[noble|aristocratic]] instincts, rather than [[ressentiment]], guided it.

### Forgetting vs. Forgiving

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s distinction between [[forgetfulness|forgetting]] and forgiving is crucial. "Forgiveness" in the Christian sense — the [[obligation|moral obligation]] to pardon one's enemies — is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], a disguised form of the very [[ressentiment|resentment]] it claims to overcome. The command to forgive presupposes that one has been injured, that one remembers the injury, and that one chooses, through an act of moral will, to set it aside. Mirabeau represents something entirely different: the absence of the injury itself as a psychological event. He "was only incapable of forgiving because he forgot" — forgiveness is redundant when there is nothing to forgive. This is the [[noble|aristocratic]] [[ideals|ideal]]: a life so full that injuries cannot take root.

### The Strong Nature as Moulding Force

The Mirabeau passage culminates in one of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most vivid images of psychological health: "the full strong natures who possess a superfluity of moulding plastic force, that heals completely and produces forgetfulness" (010-002). The strong nature is a sculptor of its own experience: it shapes the past into what it needs, discarding what it does not. Mirabeau's [[forgetfulness|forgetting]] is not absence but abundance — an excess of vitality that continually remakes the self, leaving no scar tissue behind.
