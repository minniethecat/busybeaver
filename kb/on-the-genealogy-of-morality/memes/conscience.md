## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **conscience** (*Gewissen*) is not a divine voice or innate moral faculty but a historically evolved phenomenon — the product of [[cruelty]] turned inward. The "[[bad-conscience|bad conscience]]" (*schlechtes Gewissen*) arises when external constraints force human aggressive instincts to be repressed and redirected against the self. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] distinguishes this from the "intellectual conscience" (*intellektuelle Gewissen*), which he values as the rigorous honesty of the free spirit.

> The bad conscience is an illness, but an illness like pregnancy is an illness. (035-001)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Origin of Bad Conscience

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] locates the origin of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] in the transition from nomadic to settled society. When humans were enclosed within the walls of civilization, their predatory instincts — previously discharged outward — found no outlet. These instincts turned inward, creating an "internal world" (*innere Welt*) that grew in depth and complexity:

> All instincts that are not discharged outwardly *turn themselves inward* — this is what I call the *internalization* of man: thus first of all grows in man that which he later calls his "soul." The whole inner world, originally as thin as if stretched between two membranes, has expanded and extended itself, gained depth, breadth, and height, in proportion as the discharge of human instinct has been *obstructed*. Those fearful bulwarks with which the state organisation protected itself against the old instincts of freedom (punishments belong pre-eminently to these bulwarks) brought it about that all those instincts of the wild, free, roving man were turned backwards, *turned against man himself*. (032-002)

This internalization is the mechanism that produces the entire inner life — soul, depth, and the capacity for self-torture.

### Conscience and Cruelty

The [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] is fundamentally an expression of [[cruelty]], redirected. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that seeing others suffer once brought pleasure; when external targets became unavailable, man made himself suffer and found pleasure in his own pain:

> That will to self-torment, that repressed cruelty of the animal-man who had been internalized and pushed back into the conscience, invented the bad conscience so as to hurt himself, after the *more natural* outlet of this will to hurt had been blocked. (038-001)

This self-directed [[cruelty]] gave rise to the entire apparatus of [[guilt]], sin, and moral self-laceration.

### The Sovereign Individual's Conscience

At the opposite pole of development stands the "sovereign individual" — the human being who has mastered the long discipline of responsibility and whose conscience is no longer a source of torment but of pride:

> The proud knowledge of the extraordinary privilege of *responsibility*, this consciousness of this rare freedom, this power over oneself and over fate, has sunk into his deepest depth and become instinct, the dominating instinct: — what will he call this dominating instinct, assuming that he feels the need to have a word for it? This sovereign man calls it his *conscience*. (018-001)

### Intellectual Conscience

Beyond the moral conscience, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] valorizes the "intellectual conscience" — the rigorous honesty of thought that refuses self-deception:

> Nowadays the intellectual conscience dwells and is alive — in point of fact it is even the *most ticklish* of all moral things. (065-001)

This intellectual conscience is the standard by which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] judges [[philosopher|philosophers]], scientists, and the "free spirits" of the future.

## Key Passages

> How much blood and horror is at the bottom of all "good things"! ... And more and more heavily on the human conscience — it is, to use my own language, the *will to truth* which first became conscious of itself as a problem. (002-003)

> His conscience? — One apprehends at once that the concept "conscience," which we here meet with in its highest, almost startling form, has already behind it a long history and metamorphosis. (019-001)

> That stroke of genius called Christianity: — God personally immolating himself for the guilt of man, God himself exacting payment of himself, God as the only being who can redeem man from what has become irredeemable to man himself — the creditor sacrificing himself for his debtor, out of *love* (can one credit it?), out of love for his debtor! (037-001)
