# Stendhal

## Overview

Stendhal (Marie-Henri Beyle, 1783–1842) was a French novelist and essayist whose psychological realism and aesthetic philosophy earned [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s deep admiration. In the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] enlists Stendhal as a counter-witness against [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] and [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]ian aesthetics, and places him among the great [[europe|Europe]]an minds who transcended national narrowness.

## Role in the Genealogy

Stendhal appears in two significant passages. In the Third Essay, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts Stendhal's definition of beauty with [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s, using the French novelist to advance his argument that authentic aesthetic experience is deeply personal and rooted in desire. In "Peoples and Countries," Stendhal is named among the "broad-minded and deep-thinking men" who worked toward a unified [[europe|Europe]]an culture.

> Compare this definition with this other one, made by a real "spectator" and "artist" — by Stendhal, who once called the beautiful *une promesse de bonheur*. (047-001)

> I am thinking of men like Napoleon, Heinrich Heine, Goethe, Beethoven, Stendhal, Schopenhauer. (087-001)

## Nietzsche's Admiration

### The Promise of Happiness

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most sustained engagement with Stendhal centers on his aesthetic dictum: "the beautiful is a promise of happiness" (*la beauté est une promesse de bonheur*). Against [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s definition of the beautiful as that which "pleases without interest," Stendhal's formulation locates beauty in the realm of desire, anticipation, and embodied experience. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], this makes Stendhal a truer psychologist of [[artist|art]] than any German philosopher.

### A "Happier" Nature Than Schopenhauer

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] explicitly compares Stendhal to [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] as "an equally sensual but more happily constituted nature." Where [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] used [[artist|art]] as an escape from the torments of the will, Stendhal experienced beauty as an *excitement* of the will — an intensification of life rather than a retreat from it. This contrast is central to [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s argument that the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] distorts philosophical engagement with [[artist|art]].

### European Spirit

In the closing section of the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, Stendhal is numbered among the rare individuals who resisted the pull of nationalism and worked toward a "United [[europe|Europe]]" of the spirit. This places Stendhal in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s personal pantheon of free spirits who transcend their national limitations.

## Key Passages

> ...by Stendhal, who once called the beautiful *une promesse de bonheur*. Here, at any rate, the one point which Kant makes prominent in the aesthetic position is repudiated and eliminated — *le désintéressement*. Who is right, Kant or Stendhal? (047-001)

> Stendhal, an equally sensual but more happily constituted nature than Schopenhauer, gives prominence to another effect of the "beautiful." "The beautiful *promises* happiness." To him it is just the *excitement* of the "will" (the "interest") by the beauty that seems the essential fact. (047-001)

> I am thinking of men like Napoleon, Heinrich Heine, Goethe, Beethoven, Stendhal, Schopenhauer. Perhaps Richard Wagner likewise belongs to their number... (087-001)
