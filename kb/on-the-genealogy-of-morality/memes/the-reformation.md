# The Reformation

## Overview

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **Reformation** (*die Reformation*) is a pivotal event in the history of Western morality — a missed opportunity of world-historical proportions. At the moment when the [[renaissance|Renaissance]] appeared ready to restore [[noble|aristocratic]], life-affirming values to [[Europe]], [[martin-luther|Martin Luther]]'s Reformation intervened, reviving the [[Ascetic Ideal]] and deepening the [[Slave Morality|slave revolt]] that had begun with the [[Jews]] and early [[christianity|Christianity]].

> The Reformation, that is, the ressentiment of the German spirit against the Renaissance. (060-001, paraphrased)

## The Reformation as Ressentiment

### Against the Renaissance

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] frames [[martin-luther|the Reformation]] not as a progressive liberation from ecclesiastical authority but as a reactionary movement of [[ressentiment]] against the [[renaissance|Renaissance]]. The [[renaissance|Renaissance]], with its recovery of classical antiquity, represented a genuine alternative to Christian values — a revaluation of the [[noble|aristocratic]] [[ideals|ideal]]. The Reformation, driven by [[martin-luther|Luther]]'s visceral hatred of [[renaissance|Renaissance]] [[rome|Rome]], destroyed this possibility:

> When Luther saw the Rome of the Renaissance, he turned away in disgust. What he saw was the *triumph of life* over the ascetic ideal — and he could not bear it. (060-001, paraphrased)

### The German Character of the Revolt

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] emphasizes [[martin-luther|the Reformation]]'s specifically German character. It is, in his genealogy, a manifestation of the same [[revenge|spiritual revenge]] that characterized the [[Jews|Jewish]] [[transvaluation|revaluation of values]] — a northern, "barbarian" reaction against Mediterranean culture and its sensuous, life-affirming qualities:

> The German Reformation... that revolt of the more simple needs against the "higher values" of the Renaissance, against all that the Renaissance stood for: the revaluation of aristocratic values. (015-002, paraphrased)

## Luther as Ambivalent Figure

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s assessment of [[martin-luther|Martin Luther]] is characteristically ambivalent. On one hand, [[martin-luther|Luther]] belongs to [[germany|Germany]]'s "best blood" — a figure of individual courage and spiritual intensity. On the other hand, his historical effect was to preserve the very ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] he claimed to oppose:

| Aspect | [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Positive View | [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Negative View |
|--------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Individual courage | Stood alone against [[church|the Church]] | His courage served [[ressentiment]] |
| Spiritual intensity | Revived personal [[religion|faith]] | Deepened the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] |
| Historical effect | Broke papal authority | Replaced it with an even more internalized [[guilt]] |
| Cultural impact | German language, music | German moral vulgarity, anti-Roman [[ressentiment]] |

## Historical Consequences

### The Thirty Years' War and German Devastation

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] notes the concrete historical costs of [[martin-luther|the Reformation]] — the Thirty Years' War that "infected half [[germany|Germany]] with evil diseases" — as evidence that the movement, far from being a "renewal," was a catastrophe:

> The Thirty Years' War, which infected half Germany with evil diseases, and thereby prepared the way for the moral and physical degradation of the German spirit. (058-001, paraphrased)

### The Deepening of the Ascetic Ideal

The Reformation's deepest consequence was not institutional but psychological. By eliminating the external apparatus of the Catholic [[church|Church]] (confession, indulgence, intercession), it forced the believer into an even more intense relationship with his own [[guilt]]. The [[Bad Conscience]] that [[church|the Church]] had managed through ritual was now left raw and unmediated:

> The Reformation: the ressentiment movement *par excellence*, which is called the Reformation, and taking also into account what it brought with it — the re-establishment of the Church, and so the re-burial of the Renaissance? (015-002, paraphrased)
