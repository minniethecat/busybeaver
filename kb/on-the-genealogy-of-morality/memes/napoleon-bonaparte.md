## Overview

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) appears in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s _On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality_ as the supreme modern exemplar of the [[will-to-power]] in the political sphere.

For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], Napoleon is not merely a historical figure but a philosophical event—the "synthesis of _Unmensch_ and _Übermensch_," the proof that even in the age of democratic mediocrity, the exceptional individual can emerge and reshape the world according to his own values.

## Role in the Genealogy

Napoleon appears in two key passages spanning Essay 1 and Essay 2. In Essay 1, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] introduces Napoleon as the end-point of a historical sequence that begins with the Jewish "slave revolt in morality" and culminates, paradoxically, in the French Revolution and its imperial aftermath.

The French Revolution, born of [[ressentiment]]-driven egalitarianism, somehow gave rise to Napoleon—"the most unique and violent manifestation" of [[noble|aristocratic]] individuality (015-002). This is history's irony: the slave revolt produces its own antithesis.

In Essay 2, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] groups Napoleon with [[Heinrich Heine]], [[Johann Wolfgang von Goethe|Goethe]], and [[Ludwig van Beethoven|Beethoven]] as examples of men whose creative power transcended the limitations of their national and cultural contexts (087-001). Napoleon is "the first man, and the man of a new age"—the pioneer of a post-Christian, post-democratic possibility for human greatness.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Napoleon is not the historical Napoleon of scholarly biography but a philosophical symbol—the "Napoleon" who represents everything that modern [[europe|Europe]]an culture, with its Christian-democratic values, seeks to suppress:

1. **Napoleon as the _Übermensch_-type**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees in Napoleon the closest modern approximation to his [[ideals|ideal]] of human greatness. Napoleon's indifference to moral conventions, his capacity for world-historical action, and his synthesis of destruction and creation embody the [[will-to-power]] in its most concentrated political form.

The phrase "the most unique and violent manifestation" (015-002) is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s highest praise.

2. **The dialectic of the Revolution**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy of Napoleon is dialectical: the French Revolution, born of slave-morality's demand for equality, paradoxically created the conditions for Napoleon's emergence. The mass movement of [[ressentiment]] produced, as if by historical irony, the supreme individualist.

This is one of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s deepest political insights: egalitarian movements, by destroying old hierarchies, may inadvertently clear the ground for new and more radical forms of [[aristocracy]].

3. **Napoleon and the [[artist|art]]ists**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s grouping of Napoleon with [[artist|art]]ists like [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]] and [[ludwig-van-beethoven|Beethoven]] (087-001) is significant. The great statesman, like the great [[artist|art]]ist, is a creator of values, a legislator of new forms of life. Napoleon's military campaigns were, in this sense, works of [[artist|art]]—the violent imposition of form upon the formless matter of [[europe|Europe]]an history.

4. **The "synthetic" man**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] describes Napoleon as a "synthesis" of seemingly contradictory qualities: the beast of prey and the lawgiver, the destroyer and the creator, the plebeian and the aristocrat. This synthetic character is precisely what makes Napoleon the "first man" of a new age—he transcends the binary oppositions that structure Christian-moral thinking (087-002).

## Key Passages

> ...to other ways, there appeared Napoleon, the most unique and violent manifestation of the modern soul—and in him the problem of the aristocratic ideal itself made flesh. (015-002)

> I am thinking of men like Napoleon, [[Heinrich Heine]], [[Johann Wolfgang von Goethe|Goethe]], [[Ludwig van Beethoven|Beethoven]]—men in whom the "German" element was merely the raw material for a higher synthesis. (087-001)

> ...following in the footsteps of Napoleon—the first man, and the man of a new age, in whom the _Unmensch_ and the _Übermensch_ are—for the first time—joined in a single figure. (087-002)
