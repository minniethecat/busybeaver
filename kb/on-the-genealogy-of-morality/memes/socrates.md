# Socrates

## Overview

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher whose dialectical method and ethical inquiries laid the foundation for [[europe|the West]]ern philosophical tradition. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Socrates appears only once, but that single appearance is characteristically pointed. In the Third Essay, amid a discussion of the philosopher's necessary hostility to marriage, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] names Socrates as the exception — the one great philosopher who married, but who did so *ironice*, maliciously, precisely to demonstrate the rule that philosophy and marriage are incompatible. This brief but dense reference encapsulates [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s complex attitude toward Socrates: a figure who, by turning philosophy into a dialectical contest, initiated the rationalist tradition that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy seeks to overcome.

> As for that exception of a Socrates — the malicious Socrates married himself, it seems, ironice, just to prove this very rule. (048-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Socrates occupies a peculiar position in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s philosophical genealogy. He is the progenitor of the entire post-Socratic tradition — the inventor of the "theoretical man" who believes that thinking, pursued according to the rules of dialectic, can penetrate the depths of existence and even correct it. This [[religion|faith]] in reason, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is the secular descendant of the [[Ascetic Ideal]]: the conviction that truth is redemptive, that knowledge can heal what life has wounded. In the [[genealogy|Genealogy]], Socrates functions as the ancient origin point of the philosophical [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] that culminates in [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], whose categorical imperative is dialecticism moralized into universal law. Yet [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment contains an undertone of admiration for Socrates' "malice" — his willingness to expose the contradictions in his interlocutors' certainties, a proto-genealogical gesture.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### The Married Philosopher — Ironice

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most direct engagement with Socrates in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] occurs in Essay Three, §7, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] enumerates the great [[philosopher|philosophers]] who never married. Heracleitus, [[plato|Plato]], [[rene-descartes|Descartes]], [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], [[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz|Leibniz]], [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] — all celibate. Socrates is the sole exception, and [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] interprets this exception not as a counterexample but as confirmation. Socrates married *ironice* — with Socratic irony, the knowing adoption of a position one does not hold in order to expose its absurdity. By marrying, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] suggests, Socrates performed a reductio ad absurdum on the very idea of the married philosopher: "A married philosopher belongs to *comedy*, that is my rule; as for that exception of a Socrates — the malicious Socrates married himself, it seems, *ironice*, just to prove this *very* rule" (048-001).

### Greek Dialectic and the Will to Truth

Although Socrates is named only once in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]], his presence haunts the text through [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s sustained critique of Greek philosophical rationalism. The Socratic equation of reason with virtue — the claim that to know the good is to do it — is the founding gesture of the moral-intellectual tradition [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] aims to dismantle. In the Preface, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] aligns [[plato|Plato]] (Socrates' intellectual heir) with [[christianity|Christianity]] as the two great forces that taught [[europe|the West]] to despise the body and its instincts (001-001). Behind [[plato|Plato]] stands Socrates, the "theoretical optimist" who first persuaded philosophy that dialectic could correct existence.

### The Greek Context — Nobility Before Dialectic

To understand [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Socrates, one must grasp what Socrates displaced. The pre-Socratic Greek [[aristocracy|nobility]], as [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] describes it in Essay One, possessed an instinctive morality of strength: the "well-born" simply *felt* themselves the happy, and their value judgments arose from overflowing vitality, not from rational deduction (010-002). Socrates, by introducing dialectic — the demand that every value justify itself before the tribunal of reason — initiated the process by which Greek instinct was gradually replaced by Greek logic. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], this was not progress but a symptom of decline: the strong do not need to prove their values; they enact them.

### Socrates and the Ascetic Ideal

The philosopher's celibacy is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], one of the most visible signs of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s operation (048-001). That Socrates married at all distinguishes him from the pure ascetic type; that he married *ironice* keeps him within it. Socrates' irony is the philosophical mode of the ascetic: a detachment from life that observes it, dissects it, and finds it wanting. In this sense, Socrates is the first "[[priests|priest]]" of reason — the prototype of the ascetic philosopher who sacrifices his own vitality on the altar of truth, and who teaches others to do the same.
