## Overview

Frederick the Great (1712–1786), King of Prussia, was the preeminent monarch of the German Enlightenment — a military commander, patron of Voltaire, and architect of Prussia's emergence as a [[europe|Europe]]an power. In "Peoples and Countries," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Frederick not as a historical figure to be analysed, but as a symbol of the Mephistophelian dimension of the German character — a force of dangerous, cunning vitality that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] finds more authentic than the domesticated Mephistopheles of [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]]'s "Faust."

The reference to Frederick the Great occurs in a passage (074-001) where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is revaluing [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]]'s conception of the demonic. [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]], [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, *diminished* his Mephistopheles in order to magnify his Faust — a gesture of moral timidity. The "true German Mephistopheles" is something wilder, and Frederick the Great — along with the medieval Hohenstaufen emperor Frederick II — is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s exhibit.

## Role in the Genealogy

Frederick appears in Peoples and Countries, Section 5 (074-001), a section concerned with the underappreciated depth and danger of the German character. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s theme is the gap between the domesticated, bourgeois [[germany|Germany]] of his own time and what he sees as the deeper, darker German vitality that figures like Frederick the Great embodied.

The rhetorical move is characteristic of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]: he takes a familiar cultural symbol ([[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]]'s Mephistopheles) and argues that [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]] himself failed to do justice to its power. "The true German Mephistopheles is much more dangerous, bold, wicked, and cunning, and *consequently* more open-he[[artist|art]]ed" (074-001). Frederick the Great is offered as evidence — a historical instance of the genuinely Mephistophelian.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**Mephistophelian vitality.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s praise of Frederick as "Mephistophelian" is ambivalent but essentially affirmative. The qualities he enumerates — dangerousness, boldness, wickedness, cunning, open-he[[artist|art]]edness — are, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s vocabulary, signs of *health*, not corruption. They belong to the psychology of the noble, the master, the value-creator. Frederick's "wickedness" is the wickedness of a force of nature, not of a diseased conscience.

**Against [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]]'s diminishment.** The real target of the passage is [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]] — or rather, what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] takes to be [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]]'s residual moral timidity. By making his Mephistopheles a diminished, ironic figure (relative to the "inner Faust"), [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]] domesticated the demonic. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], by invoking Frederick the Great, is attempting to restore its full stature.

**The two Fredericks.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] pairs Frederick the Great with the Hohenstaufen Frederick II ("that much greater Frederick"), the medieval emperor known for his intellectual curiosity, religious scepticism, and political ruthlessness. The pairing spans centuries to suggest a persistent type — the German ruler as anti-Christian force, as Mephistophelian intelligence — that runs beneath the official history of Prussian and German piety.

## Key Passages

> What I observe with pleasure in the German is his Mephistophelian nature; but, to tell the truth, one must have a higher conception of Mephistopheles than Goethe had, who found it necessary to *diminish* his Mephistopheles in order to magnify his "inner Faust." (074-001)

> The true German Mephistopheles is much more dangerous, bold, wicked, and cunning, and *consequently* more open-hearted: remember the nature of Frederick the Great, or of that much greater Frederick, the Hohenstaufen, Frederick II. (074-001)

> The real German Mephistopheles crosses the Alps, and believes that everything there belongs to him. Then he recovers himself, like Winckelmann, like Mozart. (074-002)
