## Overview

**Saint Paul** (c. 5 – c. 64–67 CE), also known as Paul the Apostle and Saul of Tarsus, is among the most influential figures in the history of [[Christianity]]. A Hellenistic Jew and Roman citizen who initially persecuted the early Christian movement, Paul underwent a dramatic conversion experience on the road to Damascus and became [[christianity|Christianity]]'s most energetic missionary and theologian. His epistles — which form a substantial portion of the New Testament — shaped Christian doctrine on grace, [[religion|faith]], law, sin, and the relationship between Judaism and the emerging Christian movement.

## Role in the Genealogy

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] mentions Paul once in *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality* — but the reference is laden with historical and philosophical significance. Paul appears in the First Essay's climactic passage on the historical struggle between [[rome|Rome]] (representing [[noble|aristocratic values]]) and Judaea (representing [[slave-morality|slave values]]):

> Which of them has been provisionally victorious, Rome or Judaea? … just consider to whom in Rome itself nowadays you bow down, as though before the quintessence of all the highest values — and not only in Rome, but almost over half the world, everywhere where man has been tamed or is about to be tamed — to *three Jews*, as we know, and *one Jewess* (to Jesus of Nazareth, to Peter the fisher, to Paul the tent-maker, and to the mother of the aforesaid Jesus, named Mary). (015-002)

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s designation of Paul as "the tent-maker" — using his trade rather than his apostolic title — is a deliberate rhetorical choice. It demystifies the Apostle, presenting him as an ordinary laborer rather than a sanctified figure, while simultaneously underscoring the historical irony: the Roman world, which prided itself on [[noble|aristocratic]] [[noble|nobility]], now genuflects before a Jewish tent-maker.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

Paul occupies a complex position in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s thought that extends well beyond this single *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* reference. Throughout his works — most extensively in *The Antichrist* — [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies Paul as the decisive figure in [[christianity|Christianity]]'s "transvaluation" of [[noble]] values into [[slave-morality]].

In the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, Paul's significance is primarily historical-symbolic rather than theological. He represents, alongside [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]], Peter, and Mary, the "Judaean" victory over [[rome|Rome]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy of [[morality]] is simultaneously a genealogy of Western civilization's value system, and in this narrative, Paul is not merely an apostle — he is a world-historical agent of the [[slave-morality|slave revolt in morals]].

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s curt reference to "Paul the tent-maker" condenses a larger argument: that the triumph of [[Christianity]] was not a victory of truth over falsehood, or of goodness over wickedness, but a victory of one configuration of [[will-to-power]] over another. The Roman [[noble|aristocratic]] values of strength, pride, and self-assertion were overcome — not through superior force but through the psychological cunning of [[ressentiment]] — by a movement led, in large p[[artist|art]], by a Jewish tent-maker from Tarsus.

The passage places Paul within [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s three-stage historical schema: (1) classical [[noble|aristocratic]] [[rome|Rome]], (2) the Jewish-Christian "slave revolt" that overcame it, and (3) the modern world that inherits this inverted value system. Paul, as the architect of Gentile [[christianity|Christianity]], is the bridge between stages 2 and 3.
