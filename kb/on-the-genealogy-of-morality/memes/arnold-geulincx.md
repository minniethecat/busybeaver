## Overview

Arnold Geulincx (1624–1669) was a Flemish philosopher associated with the [[rene-descartes|Cartesian]] tradition and known as one of the principal exponents of Occasionalism — the doctrine that God is the sole true cause of all events, and that created substances (mind and body) serve merely as "occasions" for divine action. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] references Geulincx in Essay 3 of the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality* for his concept of *despectus sui* ([[contempt|self-contempt]] or self-[[contempt|disdain]]), which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] interprets as a philosophical crystallization of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s psychological mechanism: the turning of [[cruelty]] inward against the self.

## Role in the Genealogy

Geulincx appears in Essay 3, §15 (PN=059-002), within [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s extended analysis of the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] and the psychological machinery of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is describing how the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] manipulates the suffering of the "herd" — redirecting their [[ressentiment]] away from external targets and back upon themselves. The concept of *despectus sui*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] suggests, is the philosophical [[artist|art]]iculation of this inward turn: the sick person, unable to discharge their [[cruelty]] outward, learns to despise themselves and to experience this [[contempt|self-contempt]] as a form of moral seriousness or spiritual depth.

> Nietzsche cites Geulincx as the philosophical source of *despectus sui* — the systematic devaluation of the self that ascetic Christianity transforms into a virtue. The ascetic priest, in Nietzsche's analysis, does not invent suffering — suffering is already universal — but he *interprets* it, giving the sufferer a meaning for their pain by teaching them to see their own existence as the cause. This self-contempt becomes, paradoxically, a narcotic: it anesthetizes the deeper pain of meaninglessness. (059-002)

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of Geulincx is characteristically compressed: a single parenthetical reference embedded in a dense paragraph on ascetic psychology. Yet the invocation is strategically significant. Geulincx represents the philosophical wing of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] — the translation of Christian self-negation into the language of metaphysics. Occasionalism, with its radical denial of creaturely efficacy, becomes for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] a philosophical analogue of the [[priests|priest]]ly doctrine that man is powerless to save himself and must rely entirely on divine grace. The *despectus sui* is at once a psychological state (self-loathing), a theological virtue (humility), and a metaphysical thesis (the creature is nothing; God alone acts).

This aligns Geulincx with other figures [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] treats as philosophical expressions of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] — [[Immanuel Kant]] (whose categorical imperative [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] reads as a secularized form of self-[[cruelty]]) and [[Arthur Schopenhauer]] (whose pessimism [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] regards as the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] in modern philosophical dress). Geulincx is not a major figure in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s pantheon, but his inclusion in the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* reveals the breadth of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s reading and his determination to trace the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] across its theological, philosophical, and psychological manifestations.

## Key Passages

> The ascetic priest gives the sufferer a *meaning* for their suffering — the meaning that *they themselves* are its cause. This self-contempt (*despectus sui*, as Geulincx named it) is the great narcotic of the ascetic ideal: it numbs the wound of existence by making the wound itself into a virtue. To despise oneself is still to take oneself seriously — it is, for the ascetic, the last remaining form of self-assertion. (059-002)

The surrounding passage in Essay 3, §15 elaborates the mechanism: the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] does not *cure* suffering ([[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is explicit that the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] makes the sick *sicker*), but he changes its *direction*. [[ressentiment|Ressentiment]], which in the noble mode would be discharged outward against enemies, is turned inward — the sufferer learns to see *himself* as the enemy. Geulincx's term *despectus sui* captures this with philosophical precision: it is not merely low self-esteem, but a systematic project of self-devaluation, a discipline of self-hatred that the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] elevates to the status of wisdom.
