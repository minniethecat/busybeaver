## Overview

Thucydides (c. 460–400 BCE) was an Athenian historian and general, author of the *History of the Peloponnesian War*. His work is celebrated for its analytical rigor, its refusal of mythological explanation, and its unflinching portrayal of power politics.

In the appendix "Peoples and Countries," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Thucydides as the moral antipode to [[christianity|Christianity]] — the historian whose values "grew up among the governing castes" and who therefore embodies a pre-Christian, pre-[[ressentiment]] ethical sensibility.

## Role in the Genealogy

Thucydides appears in Peoples and Countries §2 (071-003), within a broader argument about the class origins of moral systems. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts Hellenic morals — which arose naturally within a ruling caste — with Christian morals, which abandoned the class from which they sprang.

The single sentence "Thucydides' morals are the same as those that exploded everywhere with [[plato|Plato]]" (071-003) is dense with genealogical implication: it links the historian's [[noble|aristocratic]] realism to the philosopher's [[noble|aristocratic]] [[ideals|ideal]]ism, identifying both as expressions of the same pre-democratic value-orientation.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**Morals of the governing caste.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Thucydides is not a neutral observer but a moral agent whose very mode of seeing is shaped by [[noble|aristocratic]] self-certainty. The historian's famous objectivity — his refusal to moralize about power — is itself a moral stance: the stance of one who does not need to condemn the strong because he is among them.

**Thucydides contra Christian [[ressentiment]].** The implicit contrast is with the "slave revolt in morality" [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] anatomizes in Essay 1. Where [[christianity|Christian morality]] judges power from below — as evil, as sinful — Thucydides' morality simply records power's operations without the need for metaphysical condemnation. This is not amoralism but the morality natural to a ruling caste: confidence, candor, and indifference to the judgment of the weak.

**The [[plato|Plato]] connection.** The phrase "exploded everywhere with [[plato|Plato]]" suggests that the [[noble|aristocratic]] valuation, once systematized by philosophy, becomes a cultural force that radiates outward — unlike the inward-turning, reactive dynamic of [[ressentiment]]-based morality.

## Key Passages

> This is the difference between us and the Hellenes: their morals grew up among the governing castes. Thucydides' morals are the same as those that exploded everywhere with Plato. (071-003)

> This inconsistency is explained by the fact that Christianity has abandoned the class from which it sprang. (071-002)

> Attempts towards honesty at the Renaissance, for example: always for the benefit of the arts. Michael Angelo's conception of God as the "Tyrant of the World" was an honest one. (071-004)
