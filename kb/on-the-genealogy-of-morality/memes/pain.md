## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s psychology, **pain** (*Schmerz*) is not merely a sensation to be avoided but the fundamental instrument of human memory and social organization. Before conscience, before [[guilt]], before morality itself, there was pain — the "[[memory|mnemotechnics]]" (*Mnemotechnik*) by which promises were made binding and contracts enforced. "Only that which never ceases to *hurt* remains in the memory" (019-001).

> Its most potent mnemonic was pain. (019-001, paraphrase)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### Pain as Memory-Technology

The central thesis of Essay 2 is that human memory — the capacity to make and keep promises across time — had to be created through the systematic infliction of pain. The human animal is naturally forgetful; to make it calculable, regular, and predictable required "burning in" the memory of [[debt|obligation]]:

> "A thing must be burnt in so that it remains in the memory: only that which never ceases to *hurt* remains in the memory" — this is a leading principle of the oldest (unfortunately also the most enduring) psychology on earth. (019-001)

This is the dark prehistory of all moral concepts: the most terrible rituals, sacrifices, and [[punishment]]s were not aberrations but the necessary conditions for creating a being capable of promise.

### Pain as Currency of Exchange

Pain functioned as the earliest form of compensation. When a debtor could not repay in goods, the creditor was repaid in the pleasure of witnessing the debtor's suffering:

> To what extent can suffering be a compensation for "owing"? In the degree to which *to make suffer* was the highest degree of pleasure, in the degree to which the injured party exchanged for the loss, including the displeasure over the loss, an extraordinary *counter-pleasure*: the *making suffer*. (022-001)

This economy of [[cruelty]] is the foundation of early law, in which every infraction had a precise tariff of pain. [[punishment|Punishment]] was not about deterrence or reform but about the creditor's right to take pleasure in the debtor's suffering.

### The Festivals of Cruelty

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] emphasizes that the infliction of pain was once a *festival* — a source of collective joy. Public executions, torture, and [[punishment]] were spectacles that reinforced social bonds:

> Without cruelty, no festival: so teaches the oldest and longest history of man — and in punishment, too, there is so much that is *festive*! (019-001, paraphrase)

This insight reverses the modern humanitarian assumption that [[cruelty]] is exceptional or pathological. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account, the pleasure in [[cruelty]] is fundamental, and civilization consists in its gradual sublimation and internalization.

### Pain and the Origin of Bad Conscience

When external targets for [[cruelty]] become unavailable — when the state monopolizes [[punishment]] — the instinct for [[cruelty]] turns inward. This internalized [[cruelty]] is the origin of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]]:

> Those fearful bulwarks with which the state organisation protected itself against the old instincts of freedom brought it about that all those instincts of the wild, free, roving man were turned backwards, *turned against man himself*. (032-002, paraphrase)

Pain, once inflicted on others for pleasure, becomes self-inflicted pain — and this is experienced as [[guilt]].
