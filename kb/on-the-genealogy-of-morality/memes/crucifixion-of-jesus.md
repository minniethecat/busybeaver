# Crucifixion of Jesus

## Overview

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **Crucifixion of Jesus** is not discussed as a historical event to be verified or disputed. It appears as the supreme *symbol* — the crystallization of the entire logic of the slave revolt in morals. The crucifixion represents what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls "the self-crucifixion of a god for the *salvation* of man" — the theological stroke of genius through which [[christianity|Christianity]] transforms suffering into meaning, weakness into virtue, and the execution of a Jewish preacher into the pivot of world history.

> What is the meaning of the paradoxical and utter horror of the self-crucifixion of a god for the *salvation of man*? (008-001, paraphrased)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Paradox of Divine Self-Sacrifice

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis of the crucifixion centers on its paradoxical character: God sacrifices *himself* to *himself* to pay a debt that *he* created. This circular logic is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the ultimate expression of the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship gone mad — the logic of [[Guilt]] and [[Punishment]] elevated to a cosmic scale.

> The maximum of the feeling of guilt on earth was accompanied by the deed of a god who sacrifices himself for the guilt of mankind — the creditor sacrificing himself for his debtor, out of *love* (can one believe it?), out of love for his debtor! (039-001, paraphrased)

### The Exploitation of Bad Conscience

The crucifixion, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account, is the instrument through which [[Bad Conscience]] is universalized. Every human being is now a debtor who owes an infinite, unpayable debt to a God who has already paid it — producing not gratitude but an intensification of [[guilt]]:

> Now the worst and most painful secret of the conscience is once more removed, thrown outside — man has again a *scapegoat*. (039-001, paraphrased)

### The Stroke of Genius of Christianity

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] regards the crucifixion — the interpretation of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s death as cosmic redemption — as the "stroke of genius" of [[christianity|Christianity]]. It takes the primitive logic of creditor and debtor, [[punishment]] and compensation, and transforms it into a metaphysical drama in which the weakest values (suffering, submission, sacrifice) become the most powerful:

> The advent of the Christian God as the maximum God hitherto attained, was accompanied by the maximum of the feeling of guilt on earth. (034-001)

## The Crucifixion in Nietzsche's Genealogical Schema

| Element | Pre-Christian Meaning | Post-Crucifixion Meaning |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| [[Punishment]] | Creditor's right to [[cruelty]] | God's justice upon sin — internalized as [[guilt]] |
| [[Guilt]] (*[[guilt|Schuld]]*) | Material debt | Infinite, unpayable moral debt |
| [[suffering|Suffering]] | Meaningless; to be endured or inflicted | Redemptive; p[[artist|art]]icipation in Christ's passion |
| The Victim | Weak; contemptible | Holy; the model of virtue |

## Relationship to the Ascetic Ideal

The crucifixion is the symbolic center of the [[Ascetic Ideal]]. It transforms the most extreme form of life-denial — the torture and execution of an innocent — into the highest value. The cross, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is the sign under which the [[Slave Morality|slave revolt in morals]] achieved its definitive victory over the [[Aristocracy|aristocratic]] values of [[Rome]].

> Rome found in the Jew the incarnation of the unnatural, as though it were its antithetical *monstrum*. (015-001)

To *three [[jews|Jews]]* and *one Jewess* — through the Christian [[religion|faith]] centered on the cross — it was granted to conquer [[rome|Rome]]. (015-002, paraphrased)
