## Overview

Johann Joachim Winckelmann (1717–1768) was a German [[artist|art]] historian and archaeologist, a foundational figure of neo-classicism whose writings on Greek [[artist|art]] shaped the aesthetic [[ideals|ideal]]s of the German Enlightenment.

In *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] names Winckelmann in a recurring trio — Winckelmann, [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]], [[wolfgang-amadeus-mozart|Mozart]] — representing the latent Hellenic potential in the German character, activated by southern exposure.

## Role in the Genealogy

Winckelmann appears in the "Peoples and Countries" appendix (Sections 3–4). For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], Winckelmann's career — a German scholar who found his vocation through immersion in Italian antiquity — exemplifies the argument that the German spirit contains something "Hellenic" only the South can awaken (074-002, 075-001).

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s invocation of Winckelmann is wholly affirmative. Winckelmann serves as positive evidence for the thesis that geographical displacement can produce spiritual recovery. "The real German Mephistopheles crosses the Alps... Then he recovers himself, like Winckelmann, like [[wolfgang-amadeus-mozart|Mozart]]" (074-002).

Winckelmann's significance lies in his role as a precedent. Before [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]]'s Italian journey or [[wolfgang-amadeus-mozart|Mozart]]'s Italian operas, Winckelmann had demonstrated the German spirit could be transformed by southern exposure. After this recovery, northern gloom — "Faust and Hamlet" — appears as "caricatures, invented to be laughed at" (074-002).

Yet [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s framing contains a note of lament. These Hellenic moments are fleeting: "We should not forget, however, that we are still young. [[martin-luther|Luther]] is still our last event" (075-001). Winckelmann's achievement remains a promise unfulfilled by German culture as a whole.

## Key Passages

> The real German Mephistopheles crosses the Alps, and believes that everything there belongs to him. Then he recovers himself, like Winckelmann, like Mozart. He looks upon Faust and Hamlet as caricatures, invented to be laughed at, and upon Luther also. (074-002)

> Perhaps the Germans have only grown up in a wrong climate! There is something in them that might be Hellenic! — something that is awakened when they are brought into touch with the South — Winckelmann, Goethe, Mozart. (075-001)
