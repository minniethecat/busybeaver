# Disease

## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **Disease** (*Krankheit*) is far more than a metaphor — it is the diagnostic frame through which all moral phenomena are examined. Man is "the diseased animal" (069-001), and every moral system — p[[artist|art]]icularly [[christianity|Christianity]] — is analyzed as a symptom, a contagion, or a failed therapy of this fundamental sickness. The medical idiom is systematic: moral values are "symptoms," the [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] is "a disease as pregnancy is a disease" (038-001), the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] is a "treatment" that makes the patient sicker (062-001), and the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] is an "inoculator of disease" (050-001). [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s [[genealogy|genealogical method]] is, in essence, a diagnostic practice — tracing moral phenomena back to the diseased condition of the human animal and exposing the self-proclaimed "physicians of the soul" as agents of infection.

> Man, the boldest animal and the one most accustomed to pain, does not repudiate suffering in itself; he *wills* it, he even seeks it out, provided that he is shown a *meaning* for it. The senselessness of suffering, *not* suffering itself, was the curse which lay over mankind up to now — *and the ascetic ideal gave it a meaning!* (069-001)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Diseased State of Man

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s fundamental anthropological claim is that man is constitutively diseased. This is not a historical accident or a state from which man can recover — it is the condition of being human. The disease is not bodily but psychological and spiritual: the animal that has become conscious, that has developed memory and promise-making, that has internalized its [[cruelty]] as [[Bad Conscience]], has made itself sick.

> The diseased state of man up to the present — the *man* is diseased, there is no doubt about it. (054-001)

The "disease" is the internal contradiction of a p[[artist|art]]ly-tamed animal — a creature of instinct forced into the "straitjacket" of society, law, and morality, whose undischarged instincts turn inward and poison the organism. What is called "morality" is in large p[[artist|art]] an epiphenomenon of this disease — a set of symptoms rationalized after the fact.

> Here is *disease*, undubitably, the most ghastly disease that has as yet raged in man. (038-001)

### Medical Metaphor as Genealogical Method

Throughout the [[genealogy|Genealogy]], [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] systematically deploys medical language as an analytic tool. The method is not decorative but structural — he reads moral systems the way a physician reads symptoms:

- **[[pity|Pity]]** is diagnosed as "the most sinister symptom of a disease which had seized even [[philosopher|philosophers]]" (P07-001)
- The **slave revolt** in morals produces a "diseased refinement and moralisation" (023-001)
- The **[[bad-conscience|bad conscience]]** is "a disease — as pregnancy is a disease" (038-001)
- The ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] is an epidemic: [[christianity|Christianity]] "infected half [[germany|Germany]] with evil diseases" (058-001)

This medical framing allows [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] to bypass moral evaluation and instead conduct a clinical diagnosis — asking not whether [[christianity|Christian morality]] is "right" or "wrong," but whether it is a symptom of health or sickness, whether it strengthens or weakens the organism.

### The Ascetic Priest as Inoculator of Disease

The [[Ascetic Priest]] is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most damning deployment of the disease metaphor. The [[priests|priest]] presents himself as a physician, healer, and savior, but in fact he is the opposite: a deliberate propagator of the very disease he claims to cure.

> The ascetic priest does not belong to any single race; he prospers everywhere and grows from every social class. But he *must* be the natural *adversary* and the *despiser* of all rude health and power. He is the "inoculator of disease." (050-001, paraphrased)

The [[priests|priest]]'s method is to make the sufferer sicker — to deepen the wound, intensify the [[guilt]], poison the natural instincts — so that the sufferer becomes utterly dependent on the [[priests|priest]] for relief. The relief offered is itself a further dose of sickness: the narcotic of the [[Ascetic Ideal]], the anesthesia of [[contempt|self-contempt]], the "cure" of sin and redemption that perpetuates the disease.

> Through this treatment of the sick, disease has on every occasion spread further and more dangerously. (062-001)

### The Will to Power of the Sick

Far from being passive victims, the sick possess their own form of [[Will to Power]] — and it is a p[[artist|art]]icularly virulent one. The sick person's [[will-to-power|will to power]] operates through "crooked paths" — not through direct assertion but through the moral manipulation of the [[health|healthy]], through the invention of "sin" and the imposition of [[guilt]].

> All sick and diseased people strive instinctively for a herd organisation, out of a longing to shake off their dull discomfort and their feeling of weakness. (059-002)

The sick organize, form herds, and become the dominant moral force precisely because their [[will-to-power|will to power]] — denied [[health|healthy]] expression — has become cunning, patient, and spiritually sophisticated. The history of Western morality, on [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s reading, is the history of the victory of the sick over the [[health|healthy]], of the diseased herd over the noble individual.

### Health as a Counter-Ideal

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] does not offer a systematic "cure" for the diseased condition of man, but he does gesture toward health as a counter-[[ideals|ideal]]. [[health|Health]] is not the absence of illness but the capacity to overcome it — the strength to metabolize sickness into growth. The "[[health|great health]]" that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes elsewhere in his work is implicit in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]]'s critique: it would be the condition of a being whose instincts are not at war with consciousness, whose [[will-to-power|will to power]] acts outwardly and productively, and whose self-overcoming is an expression of abundance rather than lack.

> We must guard against confusing the *cause* of an action with its *purpose*. The cause may lie in a disease of the *nervus sympathicus*, or in an over-richness of blood — but the purpose is always something *imagined*, projected as the "motive." (056-001, paraphrased)
