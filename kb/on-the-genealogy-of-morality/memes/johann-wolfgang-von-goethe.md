# Johann Wolfgang von Goethe

## Overview

**Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** (1749–1832) is the most frequently cited cultural figure in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Goethe as a counter-[[ideals|ideal]] to the ascetic tradition — a figure who embodied health, wholeness, and the affirmation of life against the forces of moral [[ressentiment]] and life-denial. Alongside figures like [[ludwig-van-beethoven|Beethoven]] and [[napoleon-bonaparte|Napoleon]], Goethe represents for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] the highest possibility of [[europe|Europe]]an culture.

> What future is prepared for us in Goethe, Beethoven, and Bismarck rather than in that "greatest" of all phenomena, the ascetic ideal? (081-001, paraphrased)

## Role in the Genealogy

### Exemplar of Noble Culture

Throughout the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] uses Goethe as shorthand for a certain kind of cultural excellence — one that is self-contained, "distinguished," and untouched by the rancor of the weak. Goethe appears in the Third Essay as an [[artist|art]]ist who, unlike [[Richard Wagner]], did not succumb to the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]:

> So, for example, did *Richard Wagner* take, "when the time had come," the philosopher Schopenhauer for his "fatherly and leading man." Who would even think it possible that he did *not* take Goethe for that role? (046-001, paraphrased)

### The Mephistopheles Insight

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] finds in Goethe's treatment of Mephistopheles a profound psychological insight: that the devil figure must be portrayed not as purely evil but as an intimate of the divine — a recognition that "evil" is not external to life but woven into its highest expressions.

> It would be a misconception of Mephistopheles than Goethe had, who found it necessary to bring the highest devil into relationship with the divine, "to the highest degree." (074-001, paraphrased)

### The "Good German" Moment

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s relationship to Goethe is not uncritical. He notes that even Goethe had his "good *German* moments" — moments where he laughed at figures like [[martin-luther|Luther]], a laughter [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees as a symptom of German coarseness rather than genuine superiority.

> Goethe had his good *German* moments, when he laughed at Luther. But he laughed from a higher plane. (074-002, paraphrased)

## Nietzsche's Goethe vs. Wagner's Goethe

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts Goethe implicitly with [[richard-wagner|Wagner]] throughout the Third Essay. The difference illuminates his broader critique:

| Aspect | Goethe | [[richard-wagner|Wagner]] |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Relationship to [[artist|art]] | Art as affirmation of life | Art as narcotic (ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]) |
| Sensuality | Natural, unashamed | Repressed, then [[ideals|ideal]]ized as "chastity" |
| Cultural role | Self-sufficient genius | Dependent on philosophy ([[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]) |
| Late style | Ripening, completion | Decline into Christian symbolism (*Parsifal*) |

## Key Passages

> Who would create an Achilles, nor Goethe a Faust, if Homer had been an Achilles or Goethe a Faust? (045-001)

> Goethe, in the most natural way in the world, possesses distinction; he attains what others scarcely attain by education and effort. (078-002, paraphrased)
