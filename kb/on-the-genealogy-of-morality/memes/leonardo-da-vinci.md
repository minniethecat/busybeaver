## Overview

Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519) was an Italian painter, sculptor, architect, scientist, and engineer — the archetypal [[renaissance|Renaissance]] polymath. His works, including the *Mona Lisa* and *The Last Supper*, are among the most recognized in [[europe|the West]]ern canon.

In the appendix "Peoples and Countries," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] makes a remarkable claim about Leonardo: that he alone among [[renaissance|Renaissance]] [[artist|art]]ists possessed a "really super-Christian outlook." This judgment appears within [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s comparison of [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]] and [[raphael|Raphael]], where Leonardo is introduced as the exception who transcends the Christian horizon that even [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]] could only intermittently escape.

## Role in the Genealogy

Leonardo appears at the conclusion of Peoples and Countries §1 (072-001), after [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] has argued that [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]] — despite his titanic efforts — ultimately "broke down before" the "eternal feminine in [[christianity|Christianity]]." The paragraph pivots to Leonardo as the [[artist|art]]ist who did not break down, who achieved what [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]] could not sustain.

The reference is brief but freighted: three sentences that elevate Leonardo above all other [[renaissance|Renaissance]] [[artist|art]]ists as the one figure whose vision was genuinely post-Christian, not merely wrestling with [[christianity|Christianity]] from within.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**The super-Christian outlook.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s coinage "super-Christian" (*überchristlich*) parallels his concept of the *Übermensch*: it designates not a rejection of [[christianity|Christianity]] from below (via [[ressentiment]]) but an overcoming from above — a perspective that has absorbed and transcended the Christian framework. Leonardo, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s reading, does not fight [[christianity|Christianity]]; he simply no longer inhabits its horizon.

**[[knowledge|Knowledge]] of the East.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] attributes to Leonardo an internal familiarity with "the East, the 'land of dawn'" — a phrase that evokes the pre-Socratic, pre-Christian, [[zarathustra|Zarathustra]]n orientation toward life-affirmation. This is not scholarly orientalism but a metaphor for the psychological capacity to access modes of valuation older and deeper than [[christianity|Christianity]].

**The super-[[europe|Europe]]an silence.** The closing characterization — "There is something super-[[europe|Europe]]an and silent in him: a characteristic of every one who has seen too wide a circle of things good and bad" — links Leonardo to the figure of the wanderer, the knower who has transcended parochial attachments. "Silent" here is a term of praise: the silence of one who has seen too much to be captured by any single evaluative vocabulary.

## Key Passages

> But he was not thinker and philosopher enough for that. Perhaps Leonardo da Vinci alone of those artists had a really super-Christian outlook. He knows the East, the "land of dawn," within himself as well as without himself. There is something super-European and silent in him: a characteristic of every one who has seen too wide a circle of things good and bad. (072-001)

> Michael Angelo was naturally only at certain moments so high and so far beyond his age and Christian Europe: for the most part he adopted a condescending attitude towards the eternal feminine in Christianity; it would seem, indeed, that in the end he broke down before her, and gave up the ideal of his most inspired hours. (072-001)

> I rate Michael Angelo higher than Raphael, because, through all the Christian clouds and prejudices of his time, he saw the ideal of a culture _nobler_ than the Christo-Raphaelian. (072-001)
