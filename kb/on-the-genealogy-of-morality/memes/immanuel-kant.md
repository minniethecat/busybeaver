# Immanuel Kant

## Overview

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) was a German Enlightenment philosopher whose critical philosophy — p[[artist|art]]icularly the categorical imperative and the distinction between the phenomenal and noumenal worlds — represents, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the most intellectually formidable version of the moral worldview he seeks to dismantle. In the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Kant is the chief modern representative of philosophical morality: the attempt to ground ethics in pure reason rather than in instinct, history, or life. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] treats Kant with a mixture of intellectual respect and genealogical contempt — respecting the sophistication of the Kantian apparatus while exposing its hidden origins in the same [[slave-morality|slave morality]] it claims to transcend.

> How hostile to the Kantian article, and how pregnant with new needs! (P05-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Kant appears in all three Essays as a recurring target and reference point. In the Preface, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] aligns Kant with a tradition of philosophical hostility to pity — a rare point of agreement. In the First and Second Essays, the "Kantian Thing-in-itself" and the categorical imperative are cited as prime examples of the metaphysical illusions generated by moral consciousness. In the Third Essay, Kant's aesthetic theory and his confession about the "deteriorating effect" of the categorical imperative become key exhibits in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis of philosophy as a disguised form of the [[Ascetic Ideal]].

> Not even in old Kant; the categorical imperative reeks of cruelty. (022-001)

## Nietzsche's Critique

### The Categorical Imperative as Cruelty

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most visceral critique of Kant targets the categorical imperative — the rational moral law that commands unconditionally. Where Kant sees pure practical reason, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees the internalized [[cruelty]] of [[Bad Conscience]]. The imperative's "unconditional" character — its demand for obedience without regard for consequence — is, genealogically, the voice of the internalized master, the residue of millennia of violent discipline now experienced as the voice of Reason itself.

> The categorical imperative reeks of cruelty. (022-001)

### Thing-in-Itself and the Metaphysical Need

Kant's distinction between the phenomenal world (as it appears to us) and the noumenal world (the "thing-in-itself" beyond experience) is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], a symptom of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s grip on philosophy. The postulation of an unknowable "true world" behind appearances is [[plato|Platonism]] in modern dress — a life-denying gesture that devalues the only world we actually inhabit in favor of a metaphysical phantom.

> A changeling, just as the Kantian "Thing-in-itself." (012-001)

### Kant and the Philosophers' Unmarried State

In a characteristically irreverent passage, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] notes that Kant — like [[plato|Plato]], [[rene-descartes|Descartes]], [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], [[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz|Leibniz]], and [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] — never married. This is not mere biographical gossip but an illustration of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] at work in philosophy: the philosopher's "purity" is purchased at the cost of life, a sublimation of the [[will-to-power|will to power]] that expresses itself through systematic thought rather than through living.

> Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz, Kant, Schopenhauer — they were not married. (048-001)

### The Deteriorating Effect

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] seizes on Kant's own admission that the categorical imperative has a "deteriorating effect" — a remarkable confession that the moral law, far from elevating humanity, actually diminishes it. This is the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s signature: a moral system that makes its adherents sicker while convincing them they are being cured.

> The deteriorating effect of which Kant has made a remarkable confession. (066-001)

### Pia Fraus Revisited

In the Appendix, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] explicitly condemns Kant alongside [[plato|Plato]] for "pious fraud" — the derivation of the Categorical Imperative being, genealogically, the philosophical laundering of the slave's [[ressentiment]] into a universal moral law.

> We would condemn Kant for the derivation of his Categorical Imperative. (073-002)
