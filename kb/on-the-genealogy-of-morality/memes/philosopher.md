## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **philosopher** is analyzed not as a discoverer of truth but as a psychological type—one whose relationship to the [[Ascetic Ideal|ascetic ideal]] reveals the hidden motives behind philosophical activity.

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s central claim is that the philosopher, far from being the opposite of the [[Ascetic Priest|ascetic priest]], shares his fundamental structure: both are driven by a [[will-to-power|will to power]] that disguises itself as a will to truth. The philosopher's [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] is not a rejection of life but a condition for his most intense form of life—the life of thought.

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Philosopher and the Ascetic Ideal

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] opens his analysis of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] by asking what it means for different types. For philosophers and scholars, it means "a kind of 'flair' and instinct for the conditions most favourable to advanced intellectualism" (042-001). Philosophy, in other words, has always used the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] as its disguise.

The philosopher adopts ascetic habits—solitude, poverty, chastity—not because he believes in ascetic values but because these conditions are optimal for philosophical work. The philosopher's [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] is a tool, not a creed.

### The Philosopher as Animal

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] insists on seeing the philosopher as an animal—a specific type of life with specific instincts. "What does a philosopher demand of himself first and last? To overcome his age in himself, to become 'timeless'" (Preface). The philosopher's apparent detachment from his age is itself an instinctual strategy, a way of securing the conditions for his own flourishing.

The English moral philosophers, by contrast, fail as philosophers precisely because they remain trapped in the prejudices of their age. "Like all the English moral philosophers, [they] see the ethical 'Thing-in-itself'" (P06-001) rather than recognizing morality as a problem to be investigated.

### The Will to Truth

The deepest riddle of the philosopher is the [[Will to Truth|will to truth]] itself. Why does the philosopher value truth above all else? [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s answer is that the will to truth is the latest and most refined form of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]—"that [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] of virtue which never says 'no,' which never says 'yes' to reality" (Essay 3).

The philosopher who pursues truth at all costs, including the cost of life itself, is the heir of the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]]. The unconditional will to truth is "the kernel of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] itself" (Essay 3 §27)—it is [[religion|faith]] in a metaphysical value that it cannot itself justify.

### The Philosophers of the Future

Against the ascetic philosopher, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] gestures toward a new type: the philosopher of the future, who has overcome the will to truth as an unconditional value. This philosopher recognizes that all philosophy is a form of interpretation, an expression of the [[will-to-power|will to power]].

"All the sciences have now to pave the way for the future task of the philosopher; this task being understood to mean that he has to fix the *hierarchy of values*" (016-004). The genuine philosopher is not a seeker of truth but a creator of values—a legislator.

| Type of Philosopher | Relationship to Truth | Relationship to [[ascetic-ideal|Ascetic Ideal]] |
|--------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| Ascetic philosopher | Seeks unconditional truth | Uses ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] as disguise |
| English moral philosopher | Sees "Thing-in-itself" in morality | Unconscious vehicle of ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] |
| Philosopher of the future | Creates values / fixes hierarchy | Overcomes the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] |

## Key Passages

> What is the meaning of ascetic ideals? — In artists, nothing, or too much; in philosophers and scholars, a kind of "flair" and instinct for the conditions most favourable to advanced intellectualism. (042-001)

> Schopenhauer has made use of the Kantian treatment of the æsthetic problem... We get now, at any rate, a first hint; he wishes to *escape from a torture*. (047-001)

> All the sciences have now to pave the way for the future task of the philosopher; this task being understood to mean that he has to fix the *hierarchy of values*. (016-004)

The philosopher's confession, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is never found in his explicit doctrines but in the hidden motives that his philosophy serves. Every great philosophy is "the personal confession of its author, a kind of involuntary and unconscious memoir." The genealogist reads philosophy not for its truth but for what it reveals about the philosopher.
