# Blaise Pascal

## Overview

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) was a French mathematician, physicist, inventor, and religious philosopher whose *Pensées* — a posthumously published collection of fragments arguing for the truth of [[christianity|Christianity]] — made him one of the most influential Christian apologists in [[europe|the West]]ern tradition. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Pascal appears in the Third Essay as the intellectual spokesperson for religious self-abasement. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] quotes Pascal's dictum "*il faut s'abêtir*" ("one must make oneself stupid") as the clearest formulation of the ascetic strategy for combating psychological suffering: reduce consciousness to its lowest possible degree, starve the intellect, and the pain of existence will diminish. Pascal, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is not a critic of [[christianity|Christianity]] but its most honest exponent — a man who understood, and accepted, that Christian [[religion|faith]] requires the sacrifice of the intellect.

> As far as the intellect is concerned, Pascal's principle, "il faut s'abêtir." To put the result in ethical and psychological language, "self-annihilation," "sanctification"; to put it in physiological language, "hypnotism." (058-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Pascal appears in Essay Three, §16, the section in which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] analyzes the "medication" offered by the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] to the suffering masses. The [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] does not cure the cause of suffering — that would require changing the conditions of life — but instead treats the *feeling* of suffering, the consciousness of misery. Pascal's principle is the intellectual formula for this treatment: make yourself stupid, cease to think, and the pain of thinking will cease with it. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] treats Pascal not as an opponent to be refuted but as a witness whose honesty exposes the mechanism he serves. Pascal does not pretend that [[christianity|Christianity]] elevates the intellect; he openly prescribes its diminution.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### "Il faut s'abêtir" — The Honesty of Self-Abasement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s citation of Pascal's principle is strategically placed within the Third Essay's analysis of religious "medication." The [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]], [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, combats the *feeling* of depression — the leaden fatigue, the black melancholy — by reducing the "consciousness of life itself to the lowest degree" (058-001). The methods are familiar: fasting, sleep deprivation, sensory deprivation, the repetition of prayers, the suppression of desire. Pascal's "*il faut s'abêtir*" is the philosophical [[artist|art]]iculation of these practices. To "make oneself stupid" is to voluntarily reduce one's intellectual life to the level of an animal — precisely what the fakir, the monk, and the saint have always done, though they gave it holier names. Pascal's honesty consists in calling the thing by its right name.

> Wherever possible, no more wishes, no more wants; shun everything which produces emotion, which produces "blood" ... no love; no hate; equanimity; no revenge; no getting rich; no work; begging; as far as possible, no woman, or as little woman as possible. (058-001)

### The Physiological Basis of Religious Practice

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis of Pascal is embedded in a broader [[health|physiological]] argument about religion. The "dominant depression" that Pascal's method combats is, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] insists, a [[health|physiological]] condition — the result of "the crossing of too heterogeneous races," of "a mistaken emigration," of "old age and fatigue" (058-001). But because those who suffer from it lack [[health|physiological]] knowledge, they experience it as a *moral* or *spiritual* problem and seek the cure in moral psychology — that is, in religion. Pascal's genius, from [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s perspective, is that he intuited the [[health|physiological]] mechanism even while misidentifying its cause. The method works, in a sense: hypnotic self-abasement really does provide relief from the pain of consciousness. The cost is the intellect itself.

### Pascal and the Philosophers' Unmarried State

Like [[rene-descartes|Descartes]], [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], [[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz|Leibniz]], and [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], Pascal never married. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] lists him among the celibate [[philosopher|philosophers]] in Essay Three, §7 (048-001), but Pascal's case is distinctive. Where [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' celibacy expressed the rationalist's preference for pure thought over embodied life, Pascal's celibacy was religious — a Jansenist's renunciation of the world. Jansenism, the Augustinian reform movement within French Catholicism to which Pascal adhered, was itself a form of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] in its most rigorous Christian mode: the conviction that human nature is radically corrupt, that the world is a temptation to be resisted, and that salvation is available only through divine grace, not through any excellence of intellect or character. Pascal's famous wager — that it is rational to bet on God's existence because the stakes are infinite — is, genealogically, the intellect's final surrender: reason itself, pushed to its limit, argues for its own abdication.

### Pascal vs. Epicurus: Two Responses to Suffering

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts Pascal's method implicitly with that of [[epicurus|Epicurus]], who appears in the same section of the Third Essay (058-003). [[epicurus|Epicurus]] also sought relief from suffering, but his method was the opposite of Pascal's: not the reduction of consciousness but its refinement, not self-abasement but the cultivation of intellectual pleasure. "The clear, cold, Greekly cold, but yet suffering [[epicurus|Epicurus]]" (058-003) represents a noble alternative to Pascal's Christian abasement — an alternative that [[europe|the West]], having chosen [[christianity|Christianity]], largely abandoned. That [[epicurus|Epicurus]]' method requires intellectual strength while Pascal's accommodates intellectual weakness is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], p[[artist|art]] of the explanation for [[christianity|Christianity]]'s historical victory.

### The Ascetic Ideal's Most Honest Voice

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of Pascal is unusual in its restraint. There is no polemic, no mockery. Pascal is treated as a figure of intellectual integrity — a man who saw what [[christianity|Christianity]] required and accepted it without evasion. "*Il faut s'abêtir*" is not a hypocrisy but a confession. In this, Pascal anticipates [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own method: the genealogist, too, is willing to look without flinching at what morality actually demands. The difference is that Pascal submits to the demand; [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] exposes it.
