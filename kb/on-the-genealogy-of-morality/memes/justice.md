## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **justice** (*Gerechtigkeit*) is not a transcendental principle but a historical development rooted in the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship. It begins as the measured exchange of equivalents — damage compensated by pain — and gradually transforms into an impersonal legal system as communities grow powerful enough to suppress private [[revenge|vengeance]].

> The sentiment of justice appeared on earth, is in point of fact, according to the most valid historiography, everywhere at home in the same sphere of the reactive feelings. (020-002, paraphrase)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### Justice as Economic Exchange

The earliest form of justice is simply the enforcement of contracts. When a debtor fails to pay, the creditor is entitled to extract compensation — and the earliest compensation was the infliction of pain:

> The relationship between buyer and seller, creditor and ower: here it was that individual confronted individual for the first time, here it was that individual *measured itself* against individual. (024-001)

This measuring — the establishment of equivalence between damage and suffering — is the germ of all legal thinking.

### From Private Vengeance to Public Law

As communities grow in power, they intervene to regulate the creditor's [[revenge|vengeance]]. What was once a private right becomes a public function:

> The justice which began with the maxim, "Everything can be paid off, everything must be paid off," ends by *winking* at the insolvent and letting him go free. The creditor has always grown more humane proportionately as he has grown richer. (026-001, paraphrase)

This is a paradox: the more powerful the community, the less severe its [[punishment]]s. Strength enables mercy, not [[cruelty]].

### Justice as Active Force

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] distinguishes true justice from mere reactive feeling. The man of [[ressentiment]] cloaks revenge in the language of justice, but genuine justice is active and impersonal:

> The man who seeks justice is not the man who cries "they shall be damned," but the man who is strong enough for it. (026-001, paraphrase)

This active justice, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] suggests, requires overcoming the reactive affects — a capacity associated with the sovereign individual and the philosopher of the future.

## Key Passages

> The way in which the sentiment of justice appeared on earth, is in point of fact, according to the most valid historiography, everywhere at home in the same sphere of the reactive feelings. (020-002)

> Here it was that individual confronted individual for the first time, here it was that individual *measured itself* against individual. No grade of civilisation, however low, has been found in which something of this relationship has not been noticeable. (024-001)

> The most naive moral canon of *justice*, the beginning of all "kindness of heart" on earth — to what extent can suffering be a compensation for "owing"? In the degree to which *to make suffer* was the highest degree of pleasure. (024-001, paraphrase)
