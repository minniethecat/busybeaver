## Overview

Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900) is the author of _On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality_ (_Zur Genealogie der Moral_, 1887), the text that serves as the sole corpus for this wiki. The _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_ is one of Nietzsche's late works, written after _Thus Spoke [[zarathustra|Zarathustra]]_ and _Beyond [[good-and-evil|Good and Evil]]_, and represents the most systematic [[artist|art]]iculation of his mature philosophical method.

It is composed of a Preface and three Essays, each developing a distinct genealogical argument: the origin of "good and evil" in the slave revolt in morality (Essay 1), the origin of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] and [[guilt]] in the internalization of [[cruelty]] (Essay 2), and the meaning of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] as the will-to-nothingness (Essay 3).

## Role in the Genealogy

As the author, Nietzsche does not appear directly in the text of the _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_, but his philosophical voice—polemical, aphoristic, psychologically penetrating—structures every page.

The _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_ is written in the first person, with Nietzsche addressing his readers directly, challenging them to follow his "subterranean" investigations into the origins of their most cherished moral convictions. Key autobiographical elements include:

- The **Preface**, in which Nietzsche reflects on the genesis of his philosophical project, dating his early doubts about the value of morality to his thirteenth year and his first published work, _The Birth of Tragedy_ (1872).

- His relationship to **[[Arthur Schopenhauer]]**, whose pessimism profoundly influenced Nietzsche's early thought before he broke with it to develop his own life-affirming philosophy.

- His engagement with **[[Richard Wagner]]**, the composer with whom Nietzsche enjoyed an intense friendship and later a bitter estrangement—a relationship that shaped his understanding of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] and the function of [[artist|art]].

- His intellectual debts to French psychology and German philology, acknowledged in his references to **[[paul-ree|Paul Rée]]** (the "English psychologist" with whom he broke) and to the classical tradition of [[Homer]] and [[Thucydides]].

## Nietzsche's Key Concepts

The _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_ introduces or significantly develops several of Nietzsche's most important philosophical concepts:

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **[[master-morality]]** | The spontaneous self-affirmation of the noble, powerful, and life-affirming type; "good" = noble, powerful, beautiful; "bad" = base, common, contemptible |
| **[[slave-morality]]** | The reactive morality of the weak, born of [[ressentiment]]; "good" = the suffering, the poor, the powerless; "evil" = the powerful, the [[aristocracy|masters]] |
| **[[ressentiment]]** | The creative, value-producing revenge of the powerless against the powerful; the psychological mechanism by which weakness is reinterpreted as merit and strength as [[guilt]] |
| **[[bad-conscience]]** | The internalization of the [[will-to-power]] when its external discharge is blocked by civilization; the origin of [[guilt]], self-torture, and the "inner life" |
| **[[ascetic-ideal]]** | The [[priests|priest]]ly [[will-to-power]] that turns against life itself; the valuation of nothingness over existence, of the "beyond" over the "here" |
| **[[will-to-power]]** | The fundamental drive underlying all organic and psychological phenomena; the will to grow, to overcome, to impose form, to create values |
| **[[pathos-of-distance]]** | The affective structure of [[noble|aristocratic]] culture: the felt superiority of the noble over the base, the "pathos of [[aristocracy|nobility]] and distance" that grounds all hierarchical value-creation |
| **[[eternal-return]]** | Although not explicitly developed in the _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_, the thought of eternal recurrence underlies Nietzsche's demand for an unconditional affirmation of life |

## Historical Context

The _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_ was written in 1887, during Nietzsche's most productive period (1883–1888), when he produced _Thus Spoke [[zarathustra|Zarathustra]]_, _Beyond [[good-and-evil|Good and Evil]]_, _The [[genealogy|Genealogy]]_, _Twilight of the Idols_, _The Antichrist_, and _Ecce Homo_ in rapid succession. It was published by C.G. Naumann in Leipzig in November 1887.

Nietzsche intended the _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_ as a "supplement and clarification" of _Beyond [[good-and-evil|Good and Evil]]_, making the latter's aphoristic insights more accessible through sustained, systematic argumentation.

The text engages with several contemporary intellectual currents:
- **[[charles-darwin|Darwinism]]**: Nietzsche critiques the "English psychologists" ([[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]], [[paul-ree|Paul Rée]], and others)

for their naive utilitarianism and evolutionary assumptions about the origin of morality
- **German historicism**: The _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_ deploys the methods of classical philology—Nietzsche's academic training—in the service of a radical philosophical critique

- **Anti-Semitism and nationalism**: Nietzsche's text is, among other things, a sustained polemic against the [[germany|German nation]]alist and [[anti-semites|anti-Semitic movement]]s of his time, which he saw as expressions of [[ressentiment]]

- **The "death of God"**: Although not explicitly stated in the _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_, Nietzsche's earlier proclamation that "God is dead" (from _The Gay [[science|Science]]_, 1882)

is the unstated premise of the entire work: the genealogy of morality is possible only once morality's transcendental guarantee has collapsed

## Key Passages

> We are unknown to ourselves, we men of knowledge—and with good reason. We have never sought ourselves—how could it happen that we should ever _find_ ourselves? (Preface, 001)

> A certain amount of historical and philological training is required for this, together with an innate fastidiousness of taste regarding _moral_ questions... (Preface, 007)

> —Was that the end of it? That greatest of all anti-Semitic absurdities, did it end there? (Essay 1, 016)

> Let us immediately state that this phenomenon of a "bad conscience" is the gravest and strangest illness from which humanity has never yet recovered. (Essay 2, 016)

> Apart from the ascetic ideal, man—the animal man—had no meaning up to now. His existence on earth had no purpose... (Essay 3, Section 28)
