# Michael Angelo

## Overview

Michelangelo Buonarroti (1475–1564) was the towering figure of the Italian High [[renaissance|Renaissance]] — sculptor of the *David* and the *Pietà*, painter of the Sistine Chapel ceiling, architect of St. Peter's dome. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Michelangelo (rendered in the English text as "Michael Angelo") appears in the final appendix, "Peoples and Countries," as a case study in the [[artist|art]]ist's relationship to [[christianity|Christianity]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] rates Michelangelo "higher than [[raphael|Raphael]]" because Michelangelo glimpsed a nobler, post-Christian culture — but he also argues that Michelangelo ultimately "broke down" before what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls "the eternal feminine in [[christianity|Christianity]]," failing to sustain the super-Christian vision that only [[leonardo-da-vinci|Leonardo da Vinci]] fully achieved.

> I rate Michael Angelo higher than Raphael, because, through all the Christian clouds and prejudices of his time, he saw the ideal of a culture *nobler* than the Christo-Raphaelian. (072-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Michelangelo appears in two passages of Peoples and Countries. In §1 (071-004), [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] cites Michelangelo's conception of God as the "Tyrant of the World" as an example of [[renaissance|Renaissance]] honesty — an "attempt towards honesty" that placed [[artist|art]] in the service of truth rather than piety. In §2 (072-001), [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] develops a comparison between Michelangelo, [[raphael|Raphael]], and [[leonardo-da-vinci|Leonardo da Vinci]] that serves as a compressed typology of the [[artist|art]]ist's relationship to the Christian tradition.

The comparison is structured as an ascent: [[raphael|Raphael]] represents the [[artist|art]]ist wholly within [[christianity|Christianity]], producing a "Christo-[[raphael|Raphael]]ian" culture that reconciles classical form with Christian content. Michelangelo represents the [[artist|art]]ist who sees *beyond* [[christianity|Christianity]] — who glimpses "the [[ideals|ideal]] of a culture *nobler* than the Christo-[[raphael|Raphael]]ian" — but who cannot sustain that vision. [[leonardo-da-vinci|Leonardo]] represents the [[artist|art]]ist who has fully transcended the Christian horizon, possessing "a really super-Christian outlook" and knowing "the East, the 'land of dawn,' within himself as well as without himself."

Michelangelo occupies the middle position: he is the transitional figure, the [[artist|art]]ist who breaks with Christian aesthetics but cannot complete the break.

## Nietzsche's Critique / Engagement

### The "Tyrant of the World": Michelangelo's Honesty

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s first reference to Michelangelo occurs in a passage that contrasts ancient Greek morality — which "grew up among the governing castes" — with [[christianity|Christian morality]], which grew up among the governed. The [[renaissance|Renaissance]], [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, represented an "attempt towards honesty" that operated "for the benefit of the [[artist|art]]s." Michelangelo's conception of God as the "Tyrant of the World" (071-004) is cited as an instance of this honesty: to depict God as a tyrant rather than a loving father is to tell the truth about the Christian God as [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] understands it — a being whose absolute power makes human freedom impossible. The Sistine Chapel's God, in this reading, is not a comforting presence but a terrifying one, and Michelangelo's honesty consists in rendering that terror visible.

### Higher Than Raphael, But Not High Enough

The central critical judgment comes in Peoples and Countries §2. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] "rates Michael Angelo higher than [[raphael|Raphael]]" — a ranking that reverses conventional [[artist|art]]-historical wisdom, which tended to place [[raphael|Raphael]] above Michelangelo as the more "perfect" [[artist|art]]ist. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s criterion is not formal perfection but *cultural [[aristocracy|nobility]]*: Michelangelo is superior because he saw beyond the Christian horizon that [[raphael|Raphael]] never questioned.

But the judgment is qualified. Michelangelo's vision was intermittent: "Michael Angelo was naturally only at certain moments so high and so far beyond his age and Christian [[europe|Europe]]: for the most p[[artist|art]] he adopted a condescending attitude towards the eternal feminine in [[christianity|Christianity]]; it would seem, indeed, that in the end he broke down before her, and gave up the [[ideals|ideal]] of his most inspired hours" (072-001).

The "eternal feminine in [[christianity|Christianity]]" is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s term for the sentimental, comforting, maternal aspect of Christian piety — the Virgin, the Pietà, the promise of redemption through suffering. Michelangelo, despite his titanic ambition, could not free himself from this gravitational pull. The *Pietà* is the emblem of this failure: the [[artist|art]]ist who painted God as Tyrant also sculpted the Virgin cradling her dead son, and the latter image represents not honesty but capitulation.

### The Transitional Figure: Michelangelo Between Two Worlds

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Michelangelo is a tragic figure — the [[artist|art]]ist who sees the future but cannot reach it. This is the structural position of the transitional intellectual in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s thought: like [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]], who diagnosed the will but remained trapped in pessimism, Michelangelo saw beyond [[christianity|Christianity]] but remained trapped in Christian feeling. The tragedy is that Michelangelo possessed the *vision* but lacked the *strength* to live according to it.

> But he was not thinker and philosopher enough for that. (072-001)

This sentence, applied to Michelangelo in the comparison with [[leonardo-da-vinci|Leonardo]], is devastating in its precision. Michelangelo's limitation is not [[artist|art]]istic but philosophical: he lacked the conceptual apparatus to complete the break that his [[artist|art]] had begun. [[leonardo-da-vinci|Leonardo]], by contrast, had "seen too wide a circle of things good and bad" — had achieved a philosophical breadth that made the Christian framework no longer necessary. Michelangelo's greatness is aesthetic; his limitation is intellectual. The [[artist|art]]ist who painted the Sistine ceiling could not think his way out of the Christian cosmos that ceiling adorned.
