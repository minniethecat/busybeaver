# Pity

## Definition

**Pity** (*Mitleid*) is, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s moral psychology, not the selfless virtue it appears to be but a disguised form of power — an emotion that extends suffering rather than relieves it. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of pity is especially directed at [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]], who had elevated pity to the foundation of all morality. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], pity is the signature emotion of the "pity-morality" that defines modernity and [[christianity|Christianity]] alike, and it is fundamentally hostile to life.

> This problem of the value of pity and of the pity-morality (I am an opponent of the modern disgraceful sentimentality)... (P08-001)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### Pity as Life-Denial

The Preface announces pity as one of the core problems the book will address. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] positions himself against the "modern [[shame|disgrace]]ful sentimentality" that treats pity as an unqualified good. Pity, he argues, multiplies suffering: it adds the suffering of the pitier to the suffering of the pitied, creating a net increase in suffering in the world.

> ...the value of the "un-egoistic" instincts, the instincts of pity, self-denial, and self-sacrifice — it is these instincts which he fundamentally calls into question. (P07-001)

### The Great Pity for Man

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] distinguishes between ordinary pity and what he calls "the great pity for man" — the cosmic pity that sees human existence itself as a problem to be solved through transcendence or redemption. This great pity is, for him, the hidden engine of the [[Ascetic Ideal]]: it is what makes the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] irresistible to the suffering masses.

> ...and equally so the great pity for man. Supposing that both of them together once joined hands... (055-001)

### Against the Great Pity

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s deepest hostility is reserved not for pity as a personal emotion but for pity as a civilizational force. "The great pity for man" is what fuels the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]]'s project of turning life against itself — of convincing the suffering that their suffering is a *redeemable debt* rather than an irreducible p[[artist|art]] of existence.

> ...with man! against the great pity for man! (055-002)

### Mastery Over Pity

The figure [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] holds up as an [[ideals|ideal]] — the "man of the future" described in the Third Essay — is precisely the one who has overcome pity, not through callousness but through an abundance of strength that no longer needs to communicate itself as pity. This figure is "master even of his pity."

> ...pedestal, master even of his pity, who mercilessly shatters and breaks in pieces whatever... (072-001)
