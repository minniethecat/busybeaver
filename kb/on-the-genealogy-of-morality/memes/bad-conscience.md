# Bad Conscience

## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **bad conscience** (*schlechtes [[conscience|Gewissen]]*) is the psychological condition in which the human animal, having been forcibly pacified and confined within the walls of society, turns its instinct for [[cruelty]] *inward* against itself. This internalization of aggression becomes the origin of [[guilt]], remorse, and the entire apparatus of moral self-torment.

> This instinct for freedom forced to become latent... this instinct for freedom forced back, trodden back, incarcerated within, and finally able to discharge and unleash itself only against itself: that, and that alone, is the *bad conscience* in its first beginning. (032-002)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### Origin in Socialization

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that bad conscience did not arise gradually or voluntarily. It was a violent rupture — the sudden imposition of "peace" and social order upon a creature shaped by millennia of wild, predatory existence. The oldest instincts — [[cruelty]], aggression, the joy in inflicting suffering — could no longer discharge outward. They turned inward.

> I look on bad conscience as a serious illness to which man was forced to succumb by the pressure of the most fundamental of all changes which he experienced — that change whereby he finally found himself imprisoned within the pale of society and peace. (032-001)

### The Internalization of Cruelty

The key mechanism is *internalization* (*Verinnerlichung*). All those instincts that had previously been discharged externally — in hunting, war, conquest, and domination — now had no outlet. They turned against the self, creating an inner world of self-laceration.

> All instincts which are not discharged outwardly *turn inwards* — this is what I call the *internalization* of man: thus first grows in man that which later is called his "soul." The whole inner world, originally as thin as if stretched between two membranes, has expanded and extended, has acquired depth, breadth, and height, in proportion as the outward discharge of human instincts has been *obstructed*. (027-001)

### Relationship to Guilt and Debt

Bad conscience is conceptually linked to the notion of *[[guilt|Schuld]]* — which in German means both "[[guilt]]" and "debt." The [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship, according to [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is the primitive model from which all moral concepts of [[guilt]] derive. Bad conscience is what happens when this [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] logic is turned inward.

> The feeling of guilt, of personal obligation... had its origin, as we saw, in the oldest and most primitive personal relationship, in the relationship of buyer and seller, creditor and debtor. (030-001)

### The Role of Religion

[[christianity|Christianity]], in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account, seized upon bad conscience and exacerbated it. The Christian doctrine of original sin and the image of a God who sacrifices himself for the [[guilt]] of mankind represents the most extreme expression of bad conscience — the "stroke of genius" of [[christianity|Christianity]].

> The advent of the Christian God as the maximum God hitherto attained, was accompanied by the maximum of the feeling of guilt on earth. (034-001)

## Key Passages

> The breeding of an animal that *can promise* — is this not just that very paradox of a task which nature has set itself in regard to man? Is not this the very problem of man? (017-001)

> The consciousness of sin, the whole "bad conscience," came into the world... at the beginning there was that thoroughly efficient and thoroughly fatal work of the slave-morality, that campaign of revenge led by the priestly nation against its masters. (020-001)
