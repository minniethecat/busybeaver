## Definition

**Genealogy** (*Genealogie*) is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s signature philosophical method. Unlike traditional moral philosophy, which treats moral values as eternal, self-evident, or rationally grounded, genealogy investigates the historical, psychological, and [[health|physiological]] *origins* of those values—and in doing so, exposes their contingent, interested, and often brutal foundations.

"My thoughts concerning the *genealogy* of our moral prejudices—for such is the subject of this polemic" (P04-001). The genealogical method is not neutral history; it is polemic, an intervention that changes the value of the values it investigates.

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### Genealogy vs. Traditional Philosophy

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts his genealogical approach with that of the "English psychologists" and moral [[philosopher|philosophers]] who, in his view, have completely failed to understand the historical nature of morality.

"The crass ineptitude of their genealogy of morals is immediately apparent" (002-001). These [[philosopher|philosophers]] treat morality as a given, a "Thing-in-itself" whose value is beyond question. Genealogy, by contrast, makes morality itself into a problem—it asks: what is the *value* of our moral values?

### The Historical Sense

Genealogy requires what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls the "historical sense"—an ability to see moral concepts not as eternal truths but as products of specific historical conditions. "We need a *critique* of moral values, *the value of these values is itself to be called into question*" (Preface §6).

This historical sense is what distinguishes the genealogist from the traditional philosopher. Where the philosopher sees "good" as a self-evident category, the genealogist traces its transformation from the noble's "I am good" to the slave's "you are evil, therefore I am good."

### Genealogy as Physiology

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy is not merely historical but [[health|physiological]]. Moral values are traced back to the bodily conditions that produce them: health or [[disease|sickness]], strength or weakness, plenitude or lack.

Every philosophy, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] suggests, is "the personal confession of its author, a kind of involuntary and unconscious memoir" (Beyond [[good-and-evil|Good and Evil]] §6). The genealogist reads moral systems as symptoms of the [[health|physiological]] condition of those who create them.

### Genealogy as Revaluation

Genealogy is not a purely scholarly enterprise—it is an act of revaluation. By exposing the lowly origins of our highest values, genealogy strips them of their apparent necessity and opens the possibility of creating new values.

"All the sciences have now to pave the way for the future task of the philosopher; this task being understood to mean that he has to fix the *hierarchy of values*" (016-004). Genealogy clears the ground for the philosopher of the future, the creator of values.

| Traditional Philosophy | Genealogical Method |
|----------------------|-------------------|
| Treats values as given | Questions the value of values |
| Seeks rational justification | Investigates historical origin |
| Universal and timeless | Historical and contingent |
| Detached from body | Rooted in physiology |
| Affirms existing values | Opens possibility of revaluation |

## Key Passages

> My thoughts concerning the *genealogy* of our moral prejudices—for such is the subject of this polemic—had their first, meagre, provisional expression in that collection of aphorisms entitled *Human, all-too-Human*. (P04-001)

> The crass ineptitude of their genealogy of morals is immediately apparent. (002-001)

> We need a *critique* of moral values, *the value of these values is itself to be called into question*, and for this purpose a knowledge of the conditions and circumstances of their growth, development, and displacement is necessary. (Preface §6)

The genealogical method is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s greatest contribution to philosophy. By asking not "Are these values true?" but "What produced these values, and what do they serve?", genealogy exposes the [[will-to-power|will to power]] that operates behind every claim to moral truth.
