## Overview

Herbert Spencer (1820–1903) was an English philosopher, biologist, and sociologist, a prominent exponent of evolutionary theory and utilitarian ethics in Victorian [[england|England]].

In *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] engages with Spencer's ideas at two critical junctures: his account of the origin of "good" (Essay 1, §3) and his definition of life as adaptation (Essay 2, §12).

## Role in the Genealogy

Spencer appears as a representative of the English psychological tradition that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] critiques. In Essay 1, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] challenges the utilitarian claim that "good" originally meant "useful" (003-001).

In Essay 2, Spencer resurfaces in the methodological he[[artist|art]] of the book, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] attacks "adaptation" as life's defining principle — a definition he attributes directly to Spencer (028-001).

## Nietzsche's Critique

**Against utilitarian ethics.** In Essay 1, §3, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] concedes Spencer's theory is "more logical" than its rivals — equating "good" with "useful-purposive" — but insists this does not make it "truer." The error is assuming utility could be forgotten when it "has rather been experienced every day at all times" (003-001).

**Against the adaptation paradigm.** In Essay 2, §12, Spencer's definition of life as "an increasingly effective internal adaptation to external circumstances" is rejected for failing to grasp "the real essence of life, its [[will-to-power|will to power]]" (028-001).

For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], adaptation is "a second-class activity, a mere capacity for 'reacting'," while true life consists in "plastic forces of spontaneity, aggression, and encroachment" (028-001). He notes [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]]'s reproach to Spencer of "administrative Nihilism," adding the problem is "something much more than 'administration'" (028-001).

## Key Passages

> How much more logical is that contrary theory (it is not the truer for that) which is represented, for instance, by Herbert Spencer, who places the concept "good" as essentially similar to the concept "useful," "purposive"... (003-001)

> ...life itself has been defined (by Herbert Spencer) as an increasingly effective internal adaptation to external circumstances. This definition, however, fails to realise the real essence of life, its will to power. (028-001)

> One remembers Huxley's reproach to Spencer of his "administrative Nihilism": but it is a case of something much *more* than "administration." (028-001)
