# Master Morality

## Definition

**Master morality** (*Herrenmoral*) is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s reconstruction of the original form of valuation — the spontaneous self-affirmation of the [[Aristocracy|aristocratic]] caste, who called their own qualities (power, beauty, [[aristocracy|nobility]], happiness) "good" and the qualities of the low, the base, and the plebeian "bad." Master morality does not arise in reaction to anything external; it is the natural expression of strength looking upon itself and finding itself excellent.

> The judgment "good" did not originate among those to whom "goodness" was shown. On the contrary, it was the "good" themselves, that is, the aristocratic, the powerful, the high-stationed, the high-minded, who felt and ranked themselves and their doings as good — in contradistinction to all that was base, low-minded, and plebeian. (002-002)

## Characteristics

### The Pathos of Distance

The fundamental experience of master morality is what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls the *[[pathos-of-distance|Pathos der Distanz]]* — the "pathos of distance." The aristocrat does not argue for his superiority; he lives it as a fact. This distance is not maintained through oppression but through an unshakeable self-certainty.

> It is out of this *pathos of distance* that they first arrogated the right to create values for their own profit, and to coin the names of such values. (002-002)

### Active Forgetfulness

The noble type possesses what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls "[[forgetfulness|active forgetfulness]]" — the capacity to digest experience, to let go of injury, to not brood. This is precisely what distinguishes the noble from the man of [[Ressentiment]], whose memory is a catalogue of slights and injuries.

> The aristocratic man lives in trust and openness with himself. The man of ressentiment is neither upright nor naive, nor honest and straightforward with himself. (010-002)

### The "Good/Bad" Distinction

Master morality operates with the pair "good" (*gut*) and "bad" (*schlecht*). The concept of "evil" (*böse*) — which is central to [[Slave Morality]] — is largely absent from the [[noble|aristocratic]] vocabulary. The noble does not need to imagine his enemies as cosmically evil; he simply regards them as beneath him.

> The aristocratic morality springs from a triumphant saying of "yes" to one's own self — it is the self-glorification and self-affirmation of life. (010-001)

## Etymological Evidence

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] supports his reconstruction with etymological analysis, tracing the word "good" back to its roots in words for "noble," "powerful," "rich":

| Language | "Good" | Root Meaning |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| German | *gut* | From *Gote* (the god-like, the noble) |
| Latin | *bonus* | From *duonus* (warrior) |
| Greek | *esthlos* | The brave, the powerful |

> Everywhere "aristocrat," "noble" (in the social sense), is the root idea out of which there necessarily developed "good" in the sense of "with aristocratic soul." (004-001)
