# William Shakespeare

## Overview

William Shakespeare (1564–1616) was an English playwright and poet, widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language. In the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] references Shakespeare in two contexts: as a cultural authority whose characters embody psychological types, and as a representative of the [[artist|art]]istic greatness that Christian [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] has sought to suppress.

## Role in the Genealogy

Shakespeare appears in the Third Essay, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] examines the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]]'s war against the [[health|healthy]] instincts. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] uses Shakespeare — specifically the character of Sir Christopher — to illustrate the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]]'s hostility toward vitality, sensuality, and [[artist|art]]istic greatness.

> ...as a clumsy species of roast-beef-eating "freethinkers" and Sir Christophers are fain to do... (058-001)

In a later passage, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] describes [[christianity|Christianity]]'s historical campaign against the cultural legacy of antiquity, framing it as a battle waged against figures like Shakespeare:

> ...the "Salvation Army" wages its fight against Shakespeare and other "heathens" with an implacable hatred... (063-001)

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### Shakespeare as Artistic Standard

For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], Shakespeare represents the summit of [[artist|art]]istic achievement — the kind of vital, psychologically penetrating [[artist|art]] that stands in direct opposition to the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. The Christian hostility to Shakespeare is, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis, not merely a matter of taste but a structural necessity: the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] cannot coexist with [[artist|art]] that affirms the fullness of life, including its [[cruelty]], passion, and ambiguity.

### The Sir Christopher Reference

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s passing reference to "Sir Christopher" in Shakespeare alludes to the character of Sir Christopher Urswick from *Richard III* — a minor figure whose name [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] deploys metonymically to represent the unreflective, self-satisfied "freethinker" who dismisses ascetic practices without understanding their psychological depth.
