## Overview

Henry Thomas Buckle (1821–1862) was an English historian, author of the unfinished *History of Civilization in [[england|England]]* (1857–1861). A radical empiricist, Buckle sought to apply statistical methods to history, arguing that human actions are governed by fixed laws as rigid as those of the physical world.

In *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] singles out Buckle — by name — as the definitive exemplar of a methodological error that pervades modern thought: the democratic prejudice that blinds inquiry to the [[noble|aristocratic]] origins of value.

## Role in the Genealogy

Buckle appears in Essay 1, §4, precisely at the moment [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] transitions from etymological evidence to a broader methodological critique. Having demonstrated that the concept "good" everywhere traces back to "[[noble|aristocratic]]" and "noble" roots, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] indicts the modern spirit for refusing to see what the linguistic record plainly shows.

Buckle is the "notorious case" — the exhibit A — of how democratic prejudice systematically distorts historical inquiry. The passage (004-001) links Buckle's English plebeianism directly to the Thirty Years' War era semantic shift in which *schlecht* (originally "plain, simple") became "bad."

## Nietzsche's Critique

**Democratic prejudice as methodological blindness.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s attack on Buckle is not ad hominem but methodological: Buckle represents an entire intellectual tradition — English empiricism wedded to democratic egalitarianism — that cannot perceive [[noble|aristocratic]] value-distinctions because its own presuppositions rule them out in advance.

The "mischief" [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies is that democratic prejudice, once freed of all constraints "except those of its own malice," extends even into natural science and physiology, "which, *prima facie* is the most objective" (004-001). [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s rhetoric here — "slimy volcano," "salted, rampant, and vulgar eloquence" — performs the [[noble|aristocratic]] [[contempt|disdain]] whose conceptual possibility Buckle's framework denies.

**[[genealogy|Genealogy]] contra positivism.** Buckle's statistical historiography treats human action as law-governed in the physical sense — precisely the flattening of qualitative distinction that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s entire [[genealogy|genealogical method]] is designed to resist. Where Buckle seeks laws, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] seeks origins in acts of valuation and revaluation.

## Key Passages

> The extent of the mischief which is caused by this prejudice (once it is free of all trammels except those of its own malice), particularly to Ethics and History, is shown by the notorious case of Buckle: it was in Buckle that that *plebeianism* of the modern spirit, which is of English origin, broke out once again from its malignant soil with all the violence of a slimy volcano, and with that salted, rampant, and vulgar eloquence with which up to the present time all volcanoes have spoken. (004-001)

> From the standpoint of the Genealogy of Morals this discovery seems to be substantial: the lateness of it is to be attributed to the retarding influence exercised in the modern world by democratic prejudice in the sphere of all questions of origin. (004-001)

> The most eloquent proof of this last contention is the German word "*schlecht*" itself: this word is identical with "*schlicht*" ... which, originally and as yet without any sinister innuendo, simply denoted the plebeian man in contrast to the aristocratic man. (004-001)
