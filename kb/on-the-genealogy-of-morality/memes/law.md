## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]''s genealogy, **law** (*Recht*) originates not in justice or morality but in the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship — the most primitive form of interpersonal [[debt|obligation]]. Law codifies equivalences: every damage has a tariff, every debt a [[punishment|penalty]]. As communities grow in power, they intervene to regulate private [[revenge|vengeance]], gradually transforming personal revenge into impersonal legal order.

> The severity of the penal laws affords in particular a gauge of how much trouble they had in conquering the *forgetfulness* of man. (019-001)

## Nietzsche''s Analysis

### Law as Codified Revenge

The earliest law is simply a schedule of equivalences: for this injury, that quantum of pain. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] insists that this is not justice but measured revenge:

> The more *Roman* conception of law, when the Roman Code of the Twelve Tables... (021-001, paraphrase)

Roman law, with its precise tariffs and contractual framework, represents the fullest development of this [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] model. Every crime is a debt; every [[punishment]] is a repayment.

### The Community as Mediator

As communities become stronger, they no longer tolerate unlimited private [[revenge|vengeance]]. Law emerges when the community interposes itself between creditor and debtor:

> The justice which began with the maxim, "Everything can be paid off, everything must be paid off," ends by *winking* at the insolvent and letting him go free. (026-001, paraphrase)

This is the paradox of legal development: the more powerful the community, the more *merciful* it becomes. Strength produces leniency, not severity — because the powerful no longer *need* to punish to assert their dominance.

### Law and Morality

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is at pains to separate law from morality. The earliest law has no moral content — it is purely economic, concerned with debts and payments, not with good and evil:

> Similarly, concerning the origin of law... the contract, consequently of all law. (P06-001, paraphrase)

The moralization of law — the interpretation of crime as *sin* rather than debt — is a later development, driven by the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. The [[priests|priest]]ly reinterpretation of legal [[debt|obligation]] as [[guilt|moral guilt]] is the decisive step by which law becomes an instrument of the [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]].

### Law and the State

The state monopolizes law — and in doing so, it monopolizes [[revenge|vengeance]]. The individual is forbidden to take revenge; that right belongs to the state alone:

> The state, conceived of as sovereign and universal, not as a weapon in the struggle of powers. (027-001, paraphrase)

This monopoly is the foundation of civil peace, but it also disarms the individual. The [[freedom|instinct of freedom]], denied the outlet of personal [[revenge|vengeance]], turns inward — producing the [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] that the state then exploits.
