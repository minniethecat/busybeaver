## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis in *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **artist** occupies a peculiar and revealing position in relation to [[Ascetic Ideal|ascetic ideals]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] opens Essay 3 with the provocative question—"What is the meaning of [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]]?"—and his first answer concerns artists: "In artists, nothing, or too much" (042-001). The artist, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is neither a genuine adherent of [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]] nor entirely independent of them. Rather, the artist's relationship to [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]] reveals something about the nature of artistic creation itself: the artist is always dependent on something outside himself—a philosophy, a moral framework, a metaphysical system—that provides the ground for his art.

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Artist's Dependence

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s central claim about the artist is that the artist is never truly independent. He "always stands in a far too dependent relationship to some morality, philosophy, or religion" to be the source of his own values. The artist's apparent devotion to [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]] is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], a symptom of this deeper dependence. When [[Richard Wagner]] turned to ascetic [[christianity|Christianity]] in *Parsifal*, or when musicians became mouthpieces for [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]ian metaphysics, they were not expressing their own authentic values but borrowing authority from [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]].

This is why [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] can say that in artists, [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]] mean "nothing"—because the artist does not genuinely believe in them—or "too much"—because the artist's entire work can become a vehicle for ascetic values that are not truly his own (042-001).

### The Musician as Metaphysician

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] pays particular attention to the figure of the musician, tracing how the [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]ian philosophy elevated music to metaphysical significance. "This extraordinary rise in the value of music... was at once accompanied by an unprecedented rise in the estimation in which the *musician* himself was held: he became now an oracle, a [[priests|priest]], nay, more than a [[priests|priest]], a kind of mouthpiece for the 'intrinsic essence of things'" (046-001). The musician, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account, became a "ventriloquist of God" who "one day he eventually talked *[[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]]*" (046-001). This trajectory—from artist to oracle to mouthpiece of [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]]—illustrates how artistic creation can be co-opted by the ascetic framework.

### The Wagner Case

The case of [[Richard Wagner]] serves as [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s primary exhibit. [[richard-wagner|Wagner]]'s late turn to Christian themes in *Parsifal* represents, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], a betrayal of authentic artistic instinct. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] expresses the wish that [[richard-wagner|Wagner]] "had taken farewell of us and of his art in a *different* manner, not with a *Parsifal*, but in more victorious, more self-confident, more [[richard-wagner|Wagner]]ian style—a style less misleading... less [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]ian, less Nihilistic" (045-001). The artist who succumbs to [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]] abandons his own creative power for borrowed authority.

### The Nobler Artist

Not all artists fall into this trap. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts the dependent artist with figures like [[van-dyck|Van Dyck]], who "was nobler in this respect: who in all those whom he painted added a certain amount of what he himself most highly valued: he did not descend from himself, but rather lifted up others to himself when he 'rendered'" (078-001). The nobler artist does not submit to external values but projects his own values outward through his work—a model of artistic creation that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] implicitly endorses.

## Key Passages

> What is the meaning of ascetic ideals? — In artists, nothing, or too much; in philosophers and scholars, a kind of "flair" and instinct for the conditions most favourable to advanced intellectualism; in women, at best an *additional* seductive fascination... in priests, the actual priestly faith, their best engine of power, and also the supreme authority for power; in saints, finally a pretext for hibernation, their *novissima gloriæ cupido*, their peace in nothingness ("God"), their form of madness. (042-001)

> This extraordinary rise in the value of music (a rise which seemed to grow out of the Schopenhauerian philosophy) was at once accompanied by an unprecedented rise in the estimation in which the *musician* himself was held: he became now an oracle, a priest, nay, more than a priest, a kind of mouthpiece for the "intrinsic essence of things," a telephone from the other world—from henceforward he talked not only music, did this ventriloquist of God, he talked metaphysic; what wonder that one day he eventually talked *ascetic ideals*. (046-001)

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis extends to the psychology of artistic creation itself. The aesthetic state, he notes, does not exclude sensuality—"it may be, consequently, that sensuality is not annulled on the appearance of the æsthetic condition... but only transmutes itself and ceases to enter the consciousness as sexual excitement" (049-001). The artist's creative power is grounded in [[health|physiological]] vitality, not in the denial of the body that [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideals]] demand.
