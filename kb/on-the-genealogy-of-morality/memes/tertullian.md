## Overview

**Tertullian** (c. 155 – c. 240 CE) was a prolific early Christian author from C[[artist|art]]hage in Roman North Africa. Often called "the father of Latin [[christianity|Christianity]]," he was the first major Christian writer to compose in Latin, coining key theological terms including *Trinitas* (Trinity). His works span apologetics, polemics against heresies, and moral treatises. Later in life he associated with the Montanist movement, a rigorist prophetic sect.

## Role in the Genealogy

Tertullian appears in the First Essay of *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality* in one of the most rhetorically charged passages in the entire work. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is arguing that the [[Christianity|Christian]] promise of heavenly bliss is inseparable from the imagined spectacle of the damned suffering in hell. The [[priestly]] inversion of [[noble]] values culminates, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contends, in the fantasy of watching one's enemies tormented for eternity.

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] quotes Tertullian at length to prove his point — specifically Tertullian's treatise *De Spectaculis* (On the Spectacles), where [[church|the Church]] Father argues that Christians need not attend Roman public spectacles because far greater entertainment awaits them at the Last Judgment:

> "at enim supersunt alia spectacula, ille ultimas et perpetuus judicii dies … Quæ tunc spectaculi latitudo! Quid admirer! quid rideam! Ubigaudeam! Ubi exultem, spectans tot et tantos reges, qui in cœlum recepti nuntiabantur, cum ipso Jove et ipsis suis testibus in imis tenebris congemescentes! …" (014-001)

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] explicitly identifies this passage — and Tertullian — as a vehicle for Christian [[cruelty]], presenting [[church|the Church]] Father's ecstatic anticipation of watching kings, [[philosopher|philosophers]], poets, and actors burning in hell as the ultimate expression of [[ressentiment]].

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s deployment of Tertullian is among his most devastating rhetorical strategies in the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*. Rather than arguing abstractly that [[christianity|Christianity]] is motivated by [[revenge]], [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] lets a revered [[church|Church]] Father testify against himself. The extensive Latin quotation — which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] provides untranslated, trusting his readers to feel the horror in the original — shows Tertullian enumerating, with evident relish, the categories of the damned he expects to see:

- Kings who claimed to have been received into heaven, groaning in darkness alongside Jupiter
- Provincial governors who persecuted Christians, "liquefying in flames fiercer than those with which they proudly raged against the Christians"
- [[philosopher|Philosopher]]s blushing before their disciples as they burn together
- Poets trembling not before mythical judges Rhadamanthus and Minos but before the tribunal of Christ
- Charioteers "reddening on a wheel of flame"
- Athletes "hurled not in the gymnasium but in the fire"

The passage culminates in a specifically anti-Jewish barb: Tertullian, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] notes, is "referring to the [[jews|Jews]]" when he mocks the "son of a carpenter or a harlot," the "destroyer of the Sabbath, a Samaritan possessed by a demon."

For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], Tertullian's text demonstrates that the [[ascetic-ideal]] is not a renunciation of [[cruelty]] but its spiritualization and deferral. The Christian who abstains from watching gladiators in the arena is not less cruel — he has merely postponed his enjoyment to the eschatological spectacle, where the [[cruelty]] is infinite and eternal.

## Key Passages

The sole reference to Tertullian in the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* (014-001) is one of the longest block quotes in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s entire corpus. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] introduces it after quoting [[Thomas of Aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]] on the blessed watching the damned suffer, then escalates to Tertullian for the "stronger tone." The passage is cited with precise scholarly apparatus (*De Spectaculis*, chapter 29ff.), suggesting [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] worked directly with the source text.
