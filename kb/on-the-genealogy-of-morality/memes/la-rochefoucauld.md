# La Rochefoucauld

## Overview

François VI, Duc de La Rochefoucauld (1613–1680) was a French moralist, memoirist, and author of the *Maxims* (*Réflexions ou sentences et maximes morales*, 1665), one of the founding texts of the French moralist tradition. His aphoristic explorations of self-interest, vanity, and the hidden motives behind human virtue made him the great anatomist of *amour-propre* (self-love). [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes La Rochefoucauld in the Preface to the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality* as evidence for a historical claim: that before the modern age, [[philosopher|philosophers]] were unanimous in their contempt of pity. La Rochefoucauld stands, in this genealogy, as the representative of a pre-modern psychological clarity that modernity — with its "morality of pity" — has lost.

> I need only mention Plato, Spinoza, La Rochefoucauld, and Kant—four minds as mutually different as is possible, but united on one point; their contempt of pity. (P07-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

La Rochefoucauld appears in Preface §7, at the climax of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s autobiographical account of how he came to write the [[genealogy|Genealogy]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is explaining his early break with [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]'s ethics of pity — the philosophical system that had once "attracted" him but that he came to see as the "great danger of mankind." The argument requires [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] to show that the modern *valuation* of pity (as an intrinsic good) is historically anomalous. This is where La Rochefoucauld enters.

The four names [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] lists — [[plato|Plato]], [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], La Rochefoucauld, [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] — span twenty-three centuries of Western philosophy, and [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] insists on their "mutual difference" to strengthen the point: if thinkers as diverse as these agree on something, the agreement cannot be explained by shared premises; it must reflect something deeper about the pre-modern evaluation of pity. La Rochefoucauld is the most modern of the four (writing in the 1660s), which makes his contempt of pity especially telling — it comes from the era closest to the modern "morality of pity" that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is attacking, yet it remains psychologically pre-modern.

## Nietzsche's Engagement with La Rochefoucauld

### The Maxims as Genealogical Method

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s interest in La Rochefoucauld goes deeper than the single mention in the Preface suggests. Throughout his career, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] admired the French moralists — La Rochefoucauld, La Bruyère, Chamfort, Montaigne — as practitioners of a psychological method that anticipated his own. The maxim form, with its compression, its refusal of system, and its willingness to uncover unflattering truths about human motivation, is a precursor to [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own aphoristic style and to the [[genealogy|genealogical method]] itself.

La Rochefoucauld's central insight — that what we call virtue is almost always self-interest (amour-propre) in disguise — is a proto-genealogical move. It does not simply reject moral claims as false; it asks what function those claims serve, what interest they express, what [[will-to-power|will to power]] they represent. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] radicalizes this procedure: where La Rochefoucauld traces virtue back to self-love, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] traces self-love itself back to the [[will-to-power|will to power]]. But the structure of the unmasking is the same.

### Contempt of Pity as Psychological Hygiene

Why, for La Rochefoucauld, is pity contemptible? The *Maxims* offer several answers. [[pity|Pity]] is "often a sense of our own misfortunes in those of others" (Maxim 264) — it is self-referential, not genuinely altruistic. [[pity|Pity]] is "a clever foresight of evils that may befall us" (Maxim 264) — it is prudence, not [[pity|compassion]]. And most importantly, pity is weakness masquerading as virtue: "We have strength enough to endure the ills of others" (Maxim 19). The aphorism is devastating: pity, far from being a moral achievement, is merely the failure of imagination required to fully enter into another's suffering.

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own critique of pity in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] extends and deepens this analysis. Where La Rochefoucauld sees pity as self-deception, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees it as a weapon: the weak use the morality of pity to make the strong feel [[guilt]]y about their strength. [[pity|Pity]] is not merely an error about human psychology; it is a strategy in the war between master and slave moralities. But [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own contempt of pity owes an acknowledged debt to the French moralist who first taught that the "[[virtue|virtues]]" are lost in self-interest as rivers are lost in the sea.
