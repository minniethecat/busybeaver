## Overview

**Thomas Moore** (1779–1852) was an Irish poet, singer, songwriter, and biographer, best known for his *Irish Melodies* (1808–1834), which set his lyrics to traditional Irish airs, and for his oriental romance *Lalla Rookh* (1817). A close friend of [[lord-byron|Lord Byron]] — whose memoirs Moore famously burned after [[lord-byron|Byron]]'s death — Moore was one of the most popular literary figures of the Romantic era, celebrated for his lyrical gift and his ability to give voice to Irish national sentiment.

In the Third Essay of *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Moore in a discussion of autobiography and the psychology of self-revelation. Moore, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] suggests, was "too good" to write the kind of ruthless autobiography that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] values — a judgment that illuminates [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s broader critique of modern morality and its inhibition of psychological honesty.

## Role in the Genealogy

Moore appears in the Third Essay, §19 (060-001), in the context of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis of the "[[guilt]]y methods" employed by the [[ascetic-priest]] to combat depression and psychic suffering. These "[[guilt]]y methods" involve producing *emotional excess* — using strong emotions as an anesthetic against chronic pain.

The passage discussing Moore appears to be p[[artist|art]] of a digression on autobiography and self-knowledge. The truncated English text reads:

> …nal autobiography, but Thomas Moore was "too good" for it; he buried… (060-001)

The full context, reconstructed from the German original, indicates that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is contrasting two types of autobiography: the ruthless self-examination that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] values, and the polite, self-censored memoir that Moore represents. Moore, in this reading, exemplifies the modern "innocence" — the intellectual dishonesty that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies as the chief characteristic of modern souls and modern books.

> For, do not deceive yourself: what constitutes the chief characteristic of modern souls and of modern books is not the lying, but the *innocence* which is part and parcel of their intellectual dishonesty. (060-001)

This sentence, which immediately follows or precedes the Moore reference, provides the interpretive key. Moore's failure to write a genuinely revealing autobiography is not a lie — it is an *innocence*, a cultivated blindness to uncomfortable truths about the self, which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] considers more insidious than straightforward lying.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### Autobiography and Psychological Honesty

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of Moore must be understood within the context of his lifelong preoccupation with autobiography and self-knowledge. In *Ecce Homo* (written shortly after the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*), [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] would produce one of the most extraordinary autobiographies in Western literature — a work that transforms the genre into an instrument of philosophical self-creation. Moore, by contrast, represents the *suppression* of autobiography: the decision not to reveal oneself, or to reveal only what is socially acceptable.

The phrase "too good" — which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] places in quotation marks — is deeply ironic. Moore's "goodness" is not a moral virtue but a form of cowardice: the inability or unwillingness to confront the darker dimensions of the self. This is consistent with [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of modern morality throughout the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*: what passes for "goodness" is often a disguised form of weakness, timidity, or self-deception.

### Moore and Byron

Moore's relationship with [[Lord Byron]] is the biographical key to [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s reference. After [[lord-byron|Byron]]'s death in 1824, Moore, who had been entrusted with [[lord-byron|Byron]]'s memoirs, burned them — an act that has been controversial ever since. Moore acted at the urging of [[lord-byron|Byron]]'s family and friends, who considered the memoirs scandalous, but the destruction has been seen by many (including, apparently, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]) as an act of literary vandalism and moral cowardice.

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s "buried" powerfully evokes this episode. Moore did not simply refrain from writing his own autobiography — he buried [[lord-byron|Byron]]'s. The act of burial is the opposite of the [[genealogy|genealogical method]]: where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] digs, Moore covers over. Where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] seeks the "nasty ditch" (*Pfuhl*) of origins, Moore preserves the polished surface of respectability.

### The "Innocence" of Modern Literature

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s judgment on Moore is consistent with his broader critique of 19th-century [[europe|Europe]]an culture. In the Third Essay, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that modern literature, philosophy, and morality are saturated with a "moralised language" that renders genuine psychological insight impossible. Moore, the popular and respectable poet, is a representative figure of this cultural condition: a writer whose very decency prevents him from telling the truth about himself or others.
