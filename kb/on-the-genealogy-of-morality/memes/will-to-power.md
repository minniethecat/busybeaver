# Will to Power

## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **Will to Power** (*Wille zur Macht*) is introduced as the fundamental principle of life itself — the active, plastic force that shapes, interprets, and subordinates. It is not the mere desire to dominate others, but the intrinsic drive of every living thing to impress its own form and meaning upon its environment, to grow, to overcome resistance, and to impose interpretation. In the [[genealogy|Genealogy]], the concept appears at three strategic junctures: as an explanation of historical change (028-001), as the force behind self-formation and [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] (034-001), and as the will that operates even in the weakest (055-002).

> The real essence of life, its will to power. (028-001)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### Will to Power as Historical Method

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] introduces the will to power in Essay 2 §12 as p[[artist|art]] of his critique of the "End in [[law|Law]]" approach to history. Against the utilitarian assumption that things evolve toward their present function, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that every organ, custom, or institution is a site of continuous struggle — a "sign-chain" of successive interpretations and subjugations, each imposed by a will to power that has mastered a less powerful force.

> All ends and all utilities are only *signs* that a Will to Power has mastered a less powerful force, has impressed thereon out of its own self the meaning of a function; and the whole history of a "Thing," an organ, a custom, can on the same principle be regarded as a continuous "sign-chain" of ever new interpretations and adjustments. (028-001)

The form of a thing is fluid, but its "meaning" is even more so. What appears as "progress" is in fact a succession of conquests — each new will to power seizing the existing material and reinterpreting it for its own purposes. What is lost or discarded — p[[artist|art]]ial degeneration, loss of function — is not a failure of progress but a condition of it.

> The magnitude of a "progress" is gauged by the greatness of the sacrifice that it requires: humanity as a mass sacrificed to the prosperity of the one *stronger* species of Man — that *would be* a progress. (028-001)

### The Active Force: Artists, Organisers, and Self-Formation

In Essay 2 §18, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] draws a parallel between the "secret self-tyranny" of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] and the will to power that operates on a grand scale in state-builders and [[artist|art]]ists. Both are expressions of the same active force — the drive to impose form upon resistant material, whether that material is other people, marble, or one's own soul.

> At bottom it is the same active force which is at work on a more grandiose scale in those potent artists and organisers, and builds states, which here, internally, on a smaller and pettier scale and with a retrogressive tendency, makes itself a bad science in the "labyrinth of the breast." (034-001)

This "[[artist|art]]ist's [[cruelty]]" — the delight in giving form to oneself as a piece of "difficult, refractory, and suffering material," in burning in a will, a critique, a contradiction — is the will to power turned inward. The same force that builds civilizations, when denied external objects, turns against the self and becomes [[Bad Conscience]].

### The Will to Power of the Weakest

In the Third Essay, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] asks a provocative question: does the will to power exist only in the strong? His answer is no. The sick, the weak, the resentful — they too possess will to power, and it manifests with p[[artist|art]]icular intensity and cunning.

> The sick man's will to represent *some* form or other of superiority, his instinct for crooked paths, which lead to a tyranny over the healthy — where can it not be found, this will to power of the very weakest? (055-002)

The [[Ascetic Priest]] is the supreme example of this — a figure whose physical weakness is compensated for by an extraordinary will to power that operates through spiritual means: the invention of sin, [[Guilt]], and the [[Ascetic Ideal]]. The will to power in the weak does not disappear; it takes crooked paths. The sick person "spares nothing living, nothing dead" and wages a silent war of "minute poisoned powders, with pin-pricks, with spiteful grimaces of patience" against the [[health|healthy]] (055-002).
