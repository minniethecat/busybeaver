## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, **Christianity** is not primarily a theological system but a moral-psychological phenomenon — the historical vehicle through which the "slave revolt in morality" achieved world-historical victory. Christianity perfected the revaluation of [[noble|aristocratic]] values, universalized the [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]], and gave the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] its most seductive and destructive form.

> That stroke of genius called Christianity: — God personally immolating himself for the guilt of man, God himself exacting payment of himself, God as the only being who can redeem man from what has become irredeemable to man himself — the creditor sacrificing himself for his debtor, out of *love* (can one credit it?), out of love for his debtor! (037-001)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Jewish Origin

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] traces Christianity's moral logic to its Jewish roots. It was Judaism that first dared the inversion of [[noble|aristocratic]] values:

> It was the Jews who, in opposition to the aristocratic equation (good = noble = powerful = beautiful = happy = loved by the gods), dared with a terrifying logic to suggest the contrary equation and to hold it fast with the tenacity of a boundless hatred. (007-001)

Christianity universalized this inversion: what was originally a specifically Jewish revenge against their oppressors became a universal moral code applicable to all humanity.

### The God on the Cross

The crucifixion represents, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the ultimate paradox of the slave revolt — the elevation of weakness, suffering, and death to the status of divinity:

> That awful paradox of a "god on the cross," that mystery of an unimaginable ultimate cruelty and self-crucifixion of God *for the salvation of man*. (008-001)

This image encapsulates the entire Christian revaluation: power appears as [[sacrifice|self-sacrifice]], strength as submission, and the execution of a criminal becomes the redemption of the world.

### The Economy of Redemption

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] reads Christian theology through the lens of the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship. Original sin creates an infinite debt; God's [[sacrifice|self-sacrifice]] is an infinite payment. The entire drama is an economic transaction inflated to cosmic proportions:

> The creditor sacrificing himself for his debtor, out of *love* — can one credit it? — out of love for his debtor! (037-001)

This "stroke of genius" transforms an unpayable debt into the occasion for infinite gratitude — the most effective instrument of psychological control ever devised.

### Christianity and the Ascetic Ideal

In Essay 3, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that Christianity is the most complete expression of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. The [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] channels the suffering of the masses into self-directed [[cruelty]], interpreted as "sin":

> Christianity in particular should be dubbed a great treasure-house of the cleverest consolations: so much that is comforting, soothing, narcotising is piled up in it; so many of the most dangerous and audacious expedients have been risked for it. (058-001)

The ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] gives meaning to suffering — and Christianity's version of that meaning (sin, [[guilt]], redemption) has been the most successful in Western history.

### The Decline of Christian Dogma

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] observes that Christian *belief* has weakened, but Christian *morality* persists — and this is more dangerous:

> Christianity *went to ruin as a dogma*, through its own morality: in the same way Christianity *must go to ruin as a morality* — we stand on the threshold of *this* event. (068-002)

The death of God does not automatically free humanity from Christian morality; that liberation requires a further, more difficult revaluation.
