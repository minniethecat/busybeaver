# Germans

## Overview

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **Germans** occupy a distinctive diagnostic position. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] — himself a German writing in German — subjects his own people to the same genealogical scrutiny he applies to the [[Greeks]], the [[Rome|Romans]], and the [[Jews]]. The result is a complex portrait: the old [[Germans]] embody the barbarian [[nobility]] that underlies all [[noble|aristocratic]] values, while modern [[Germans]] exemplify the cultural confusion and moral decay of contemporary [[europe|Europe]].

## Nietzsche's Characterization

### The Old Germans and Aristocratic Origins

In his etymological analysis, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] traces the concept of "good" through the linguistic record of Germanic peoples. The old Germans, like the [[Greeks|Greek]] and [[Rome|Roman]] nobilities, understood themselves as "the good" — the powerful, the real, the true.

> Between the old Germans and ourselves there exists scarcely an idea in common, let alone an identity of words. (010-004)

This gap between old and modern Germans is precisely the point. The old Germans are alien to us — they belong to a world before the [[slave-morality|slave revolt in morals]], a world where value was synonymous with power. Modern Germans, by contrast, are products of the revolt.

### Modern German Self-Regard

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] repeatedly punctures German self-satisfaction. Where Germans pride themselves on their "depth" and "seriousness," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees only confusion and a dangerous lack of self-knowledge.

> We Germans do certainly not regard ourselves as a particularly cruel and hard-natured people. (019-001)

The irony is biting: the German refusal to recognize their own capacity for [[cruelty]] is itself a symptom of the moral self-deception that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy aims to expose.

### The German as "Dangerous"

One of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most provocative characterizations calls Germans "a dangerous people" — not as a condemnation but as a genealogical observation. The German's capacity for inner turmoil, for questioning, for overturning established values, makes them unpredictable.

> The Germans are a dangerous people: they understand how to intoxicate themselves. (076-001)

This "dangerousness" is ambivalent. It is the source of both the German capacity for philosophical radicalism ([[Immanuel Kant|Kant]], [[Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel|Hegel]], [[Arthur Schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]) and the German vulnerability to moral fanaticism and [[ressentiment]]-driven politics.

## Role in Moral History

In the genealogical narrative, the Germans occupy a transitional position. They inherit the [[noble|aristocratic]] values of the Indo-[[europe|Europe]]an past (preserved in Germanic languages and legal traditions), but they have been reshaped by two millennia of [[Christianity|Christian]] moralization. The result is a people at war with themselves — possessing the "memory of the will" that characterizes the master races, yet harnessed to the [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic ideal]] that defines [[slave-morality|slave morality]].

This internal contradiction makes the Germans the potential site of a future [[transvaluation|revaluation of values]] — or of the most destructive form of modern [[nihilism]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s ambivalent relationship to his own people is one of the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*'s most charged subtexts.
