## Overview

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) was a German philosopher, mathematician, and polymath. Co-inventor of calculus and author of the *Theodicy* (1710), Leibniz represents the high-water mark of seventeenth-century rationalist metaphysics.

In "Peoples and Countries," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] names Leibniz in a list of German achievements that reads as an indictment. The catalogue — "Gothic, rococo, the historical sense and exoticism, [[hegel|Hegel]], [[richard-wagner|Richard Wagner]] — Leibniz, too (dangerous at the present day)" (076-001) — positions Leibniz among the intoxicants a dangerous people exports to the world.

The parenthetical "(dangerous at the present day)" is the key: Leibniz is not merely a historical figure but a living danger, an intellectual intoxicant that still seduces.

## Role in the Genealogy

Leibniz appears in Peoples and Countries §7 (076-001), whose thesis is blunt: "The [[germans|Germans]] are a dangerous people: they are experts at inventing intoxicants."

The list that follows catalogues German cultural exports that intoxicate rather than clarify. Leibniz's placement between [[richard-wagner|Wagner]] and the "[[ideals|ideal]]ised serving soul" of German scholars suggests [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees rationalist metaphysics as continuous with the German talent for self-deceptive sublimation.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**Philosophy as intoxicant.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s classification of Leibniz as an "intoxicant" is a genealogical provocation. The *Theodicy* — the rational proof that evil is compatible with divine goodness — is not a sober exercise of reason but a species of philosophical inebriation.

**The German danger.** What makes Leibniz "dangerous at the present day" is that his mode of thinking remains operative. The subordination of experience to system, of suffering to rational justification — these are not dead doctrines but living temptations.

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s target is the German philosophical instinct Leibniz exemplifies: conceptual structures so elegant that one forgets to ask whether they are true.

**Theodicy and genealogy.** Leibniz's *Theodicy* is the inverse of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy. Where Leibniz asks "how can evil be rationally justified?", [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] asks "under what conditions did man invent the concepts good and evil?"
