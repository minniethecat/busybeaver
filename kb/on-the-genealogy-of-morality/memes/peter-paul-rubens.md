## Overview

Peter Paul Rubens (1577–1640) was a Flemish painter of the Baroque period, celebrated for his sumptuous canvases of mythological, religious, and [[noble|aristocratic]] subjects. In "Peoples and Countries," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] discusses Rubens within a larger argument about the servility of [[artist|art]]ists — the thesis that most of the greatest [[artist|art]]ists have been, at bottom, servants of their patrons' tastes.

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts Rubens unfavourably with [[van-dyck|Anthony van Dyck]]. Where Rubens "portrayed the [[aristocracy|nobility]] of his age; but only according to *their* vague conception of taste, not according to his own measure of beauty" (078-001), [[van-dyck|Van Dyck]] "did not descend from himself, but rather lifted up others to himself when he 'rendered.'" The distinction maps onto [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s larger philosophical concern with creation versus compliance, self-legislation versus submission.

## Role in the Genealogy

Rubens appears in Peoples and Countries, Section 9 (078-001), a passage that diagnoses [[artist|art]]istic servility as a structural feature of [[europe|the West]]ern [[artist|art]]istic tradition. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s claim is sweeping: "Most [[artist|art]]ists, even some of the greatest (including the historians) have up to the present belonged to the serving classes" (078-001).

The specific analysis of Rubens exemplifies this thesis. Rubens's patrons — the courts, [[church|the Church]], the [[aristocracy]] — determined not only his subjects but also the aesthetic standards by which his work was judged. He "portrayed the [[aristocracy|nobility]] of his age," but he did so according to their values, their conception of taste. The result is [[artist|art]] that flatters its audience rather than challenging or elevating it.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**The servant-[[artist|art]]ist.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of Rubens is not primarily aesthetic — he does not deny Rubens's technical mastery — but moral-psychological. The question is one of *self-relation*: does the [[artist|art]]ist project his own values outward through his work, or does he submit to values imposed from without? Rubens, on [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s reading, submitted.

**[[van-dyck|Van Dyck]] as counter-example.** The comparison with [[van-dyck|Van Dyck]] is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s positive standard. [[van-dyck|Van Dyck]] "added a certain amount of what he himself most highly valued" — he gave to his subjects something of himself, rather than merely reflecting their own self-image back to them. This is the [[artist|art]]istic equivalent of the [[master-morality|noble morality]] [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] describes in the First Essay: the capacity to create values rather than to receive them.

**[[church|Church]] and moral law.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] extends the analysis beyond individual patrons to institutional dependence: "not to speak of their dependence upon [[church|the Church]] and upon moral law" (078-001). Rubens's famous religious commissions — his altarpieces, his crucifixions — were produced under the authority of the Counter-[[the-reformation|Reformation]] [[church|Church]], an institution [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] regarded as the institutional embodiment of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. The [[artist|art]]ist who paints for [[church|the Church]] paints, necessarily, under moral constraint.

## Key Passages

> Most artists, even some of the greatest (including the historians) have up to the present belonged to the serving classes (whether they serve people of high position or princes or women or "the masses"), not to speak of their dependence upon the Church and upon moral law. (078-001)

> Thus Rubens portrayed the nobility of his age; but only according to *their* vague conception of taste, not according to his own measure of beauty — on the whole, therefore, against his own taste. (078-001)

> Van Dyck was nobler in this respect: who in all those whom he painted added a certain amount of what he himself most highly valued: he did not descend from himself, but rather lifted up others to himself when he "rendered." (078-001)
