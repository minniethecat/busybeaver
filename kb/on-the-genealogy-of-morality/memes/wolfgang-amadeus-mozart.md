## Overview

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756–1791) was an Austrian composer of the Classical period.

In *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Mozart as p[[artist|art]] of a recurring trio — [[johann-joachim-winckelmann|Winckelmann]], [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]], Mozart — representing the "Hellenic" potential latent in the German spirit, awakened through contact with the Mediterranean South.

## Role in the Genealogy

Mozart appears in the "Peoples and Countries" appendix (Sections 3–4), where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] reflects on the German character. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that [[germans|Germans]] possess something "Hellenic" — a classical, southern sensibility — activated only through exposure to the South, across the Alps (074-002, 075-001).

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of Mozart is an affirmation, not a critique. Mozart, [[johann-joachim-winckelmann|Winckelmann]], and [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]] together demonstrate that the German spirit is not inherently Philistine. Rather, it contains "something that might be Hellenic" (075-001).

The mechanism is geographical crossing: "The real German Mephistopheles crosses the Alps, and believes that everything there belongs to him. Then he recovers himself, like [[johann-joachim-winckelmann|Winckelmann]], like Mozart" (074-002). This "recovery" returns the German to a truer self obscured by the northern climate.

After this southern recovery, "He looks upon Faust and Hamlet as caricatures, invented to be laughed at, and upon [[martin-luther|Luther]] also" (074-002). Mozart thus serves as a counterweight to the "cloudy moods" of German romanticism — though [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] adds that [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]] "fell back again into his cloudy moods" (074-002).

## Key Passages

> The real German Mephistopheles crosses the Alps, and believes that everything there belongs to him. Then he recovers himself, like Winckelmann, like Mozart. He looks upon Faust and Hamlet as caricatures, invented to be laughed at, and upon Luther also. (074-002)

> Perhaps the Germans have only grown up in a wrong climate! There is something in them that might be Hellenic! — something that is awakened when they are brought into touch with the South — Winckelmann, Goethe, Mozart. (075-001)
