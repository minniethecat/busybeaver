# Nazareth

## Overview

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **Nazareth** is not a tourist destination but the symbolic point of origin for the most consequential event in Western moral history: the transformation of the [[Jews|Jewish]] [[transvaluation|revaluation of values]] into a universal religion. The figure of "[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus of Nazareth]]" embodies, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the paradox at the he[[artist|art]] of [[Christianity]] — a gospel of love that functions as an instrument of [[ressentiment]], a message of redemption that condemns the [[health|healthy]] to eternal [[guilt]].

## Role in the Genealogy

### Jesus of Nazareth and the Slave Revolt

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is notably restrained in the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* — he appears only twice, and each time in the context of a larger argument about the victory of [[Jews|Judaea]] over [[Rome]]. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus of Nazareth]] is not analyzed as a psychological type (as [[Paul of Tarsus|Paul]] might be) but invoked as the symbolic condensation of the slave revolt:

> This Jesus of Nazareth, the incarnate gospel of love, this "Saviour" bringing holiness and victory to the poor, to the sick, to the sinners — was he not just seduction in its most sinister and irresistible form? (008-001)

The question mark is crucial: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] does not assert but *asks*, leaving open the possibility that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] himself was something other than what [[christianity|Christianity]] made of him. But the historical effect is clear — whatever [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] was, his name became the banner under which the slave revolt marched to victory.

### Nazareth in the Rome-Judaea Conflict

In the climactic passage of Essay 1, Nazareth appears in a list of names — [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], Peter, Paul — that together constitute the "Judaean" forces arrayed against the classical [[ideals|ideal]]:

> To Jesus of Nazareth, to Peter the fisher, to Paul the tent-maker — to these Judaea owes its victory over Rome. (015-002)

Nazareth, the insignificant Galilean village, thus becomes — in the genealogical narrative — the most consequential place in Western history. From this provincial origin emerged the value system that would conquer the [[rome|Roman Empire]] and shape [[europe|European civilization]] for two millennia.
