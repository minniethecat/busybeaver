## Definition

In the third essay of the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that modern **science** (*Wissenschaft*) is not the enemy of religion but its secret ally — the latest form of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. Both share an unconditional [[religion|faith]] in *truth* as the supreme value. The scientist no less than the [[priests|priest]] says: "Life is worth nothing; truth is worth everything." Science is [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] without God.

> The will to truth which first became conscious of itself as a problem. (002-003, paraphrase)

## Nietzsche''s Analysis

### Science as Ascetic Ideal

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]''s claim is deliberately provocative: science, which prides itself on having overcome religion, is the continuation of religion by other means:

> Science and the ascetic ideal — they rest on the same foundation... on the same overestimation of truth (more correctly: on the same belief in the *inestimability*, the *uncriticizability* of truth). (066-001, paraphrase)

The scientist believes in truth just as unconditionally as the believer believes in God. The *content* of belief has changed but the *form* — absolute [[religion|faith]] in a supreme value — remains identical.

### The Honesty of Science

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] grants science one virtue: its "[[conscience|intellectual conscience]]," its commitment to honesty. But he immediately shows how this virtue undermines science itself:

> The will to truth, that still uncomprehended *ideal* whose servants we are. (065-001, paraphrase)

The rigorous honesty of science, pushed to its conclusion, must eventually question the *value* of truth. And at that point, science self-destructs — or transforms into something beyond itself.

### Science and Nihilism

Science does not cure nihilism; it deepens it. By reducing the world to mechanism, causality, and law, science strips existence of meaning:

> The *meaninglessness* of suffering, *not* suffering itself — that was the curse that lay over mankind. (035-001, paraphrase)

The ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] at least *gave meaning* to suffering (as [[punishment]] for sin). Science offers no meaning at all — only explanation. This is why [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls science a "will to nothingness" — it preserves the ascetic *will* (the will to truth at any cost) while emptying it of all content.

### The Self-Overcoming of Science

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees a dialectical possibility: the honesty cultivated by science may eventually turn *against* the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] itself. The will to truth, having exposed [[christianity|Christianity]] as a fable, may finally expose *itself* as a value — and question whether truth is worth the sacrifice of life:

> Christianity *went to ruin as a dogma*, through its own morality: in the same way Christianity *must go to ruin as a morality*. (068-002)

Similarly, science may go to ruin through its own [[conscience|intellectual conscience]] — and what emerges on the other side may be a new possibility: knowledge in the service of life rather than against it.

## Key Passages

> A certain asceticism, a grimly gay whole-hearted renunciation, is, as we have seen, one of the most favorable conditions of the highest intellectualism. (050-001, paraphrase)

> The will to truth, that still uncomprehended *ideal*. (065-001, paraphrase)

> Science and the ascetic ideal — they rest on the same foundation. (066-001, paraphrase)
