## Overview

George Gordon, Lord Byron (1788–1824), was the most celebrated English poet of the Romantic movement — a figure of immense [[europe|Europe]]an fame, scandal, and literary influence. In the "[[genealogy|Genealogy]]," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Byron not for his poetry but for his *biography* — specifically, for the autobiography that Byron wrote and that his literary executor, [[thomas-moore|Thomas Moore]], subsequently burned.

The burning of Byron's autobiography exemplifies the central [[disease|pathology]] of modern culture: its incapacity for truth about human beings. Modern "good men," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, cannot bear an honest account of a human life.

Byron's fate at the hands of his "virtuous" executor is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s first exhibit in a gallery of biographical vandalism. His autobiography was destroyed not out of malice but out of moral squeamishness.

## Role in the Genealogy

Byron appears in the Third Essay, Section 19 (060-001), a passage that diagnoses the "moral sugariness and falsity" of modern culture. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that the modern psychologist's task is obstructed at every turn by the "innocent" dishonesty of those who cannot bear to look at human beings as they are.

Byron's case is the first of four: his autobiography was burned by [[thomas-moore|Thomas Moore]], who was "too good" for it; [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]'s papers were allegedly destroyed by his executor Gwinner; [[ludwig-van-beethoven|Beethoven]]'s biography was abandoned mid-stream by the "virtuous American" Thayer.

[[richard-wagner|Richard Wagner]]'s promised autobiography, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] predicts, will be a "clever" one — that is, dishonest. Each case shows the same point: the modern world prevents true biographies from surviving.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**The impossibility of honesty.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] asks: "What sensible man nowadays writes one honest word about himself?" (060-001). The implied answer — no one — sets the terms for his larger critique of modern "virtue." Byron's burned autobiography is the emblematic case.

**The "good man" as enemy of truth.** The destruction of Byron's autobiography is an act of moral vandalism performed in the name of goodness. [[thomas-moore|Moore]] was not malicious; he was "too good" — and this goodness is precisely the problem. The modern "good man" is an "innocent liar" (060-001).

**The psychologist's predicament.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] positions himself as a psychologist in a world that systematically obstructs psychological truth. Byron's burned autobiography is emblematic: the material the psychologist needs has already been reduced to ashes by the "good" before he can read it.

## Key Passages

> Which of them *could stand* a further truth "about man"? or, put more tangibly, which of them could put up with a true biography? One or two instances: Lord Byron composed a most personal autobiography, but Thomas Moore was "too good" for it; he burnt his friend's papers. (060-001)

> Dr. Gwinner, Schopenhauer's executor, is said to have done the same; for Schopenhauer as well wrote much about himself, and perhaps also against himself. The virtuous American Thayer, Beethoven's biographer, suddenly stopped his work: he had come to a certain point in that honourable and simple life, and could stand it no longer. (060-001)

> Moral: What sensible man nowadays writes one honest word about himself? He must already belong to the Order of Holy Foolhardiness. (060-001)
