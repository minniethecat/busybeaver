# Paul Rée

## Overview

Paul Ludwig Carl Heinrich Rée (1849–1901) was a German philosopher and physician whose 1877 book *The Origin of the Moral Emotions* (*Der Ursprung der moralischen Empfindungen*) attempted to provide a naturalistic, [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ian account of the origin of morality. Rée was a close friend of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] during the late 1870s — the two shared a house in Sorrento with Lou Salomé — and his influence on [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s middle-period works (*Human, All Too Human*, *The Dawn of Day*) was substantial. In the Preface to the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] acknowledges Rée as the initial "impulse" for his own genealogical project while simultaneously declaring his complete rejection of every conclusion Rée reached. Rée thus occupies a unique position in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]]: he is both the intellectual catalyst [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] credits and the intellectual error [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] corrects.

> My first impulse to publish some of my hypotheses concerning the origin of morality I owe to a clear, well-written, and even precocious little book, in which a perverse and vicious kind of moral philosophy (your real *English* kind) was definitely presented to me for the first time. (P06-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Rée appears in the Preface, not in the body of the [[genealogy|Genealogy]], which is itself significant. The Preface locates the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s intellectual biography — it tells the story of how [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] came to write the book, and Rée is the central figure in that story. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] presents his relationship with Rée as a progression through three phases:

First, *attraction*: Rée's book "attracted me — with that magnetic attraction, inherent in that which is diametrically opposed and antithetical to one's own ideas" (P06-001). [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] was drawn to Rée's project precisely because he found it wrong — the opposition clarified his own thinking.

Second, *negation*: "I may almost say that I have never read anything in which every single dogma and conclusion has called forth from me so emphatic a negation" (P06-001). The negation is not hostile but productive — it generates [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own alternative hypotheses.

Third, *disappointment*: "If, in this context, I thought, amongst others, of the aforesaid Dr. Rée, I did so because I had no doubt that from the very nature of his questions he would be compelled to have recourse to a truer method, in order to obtain his answers. Have I deceived myself on that score?" (P09-001). [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] had hoped Rée would become an ally in the genealogical project; instead, Rée remained committed to the "English" method.

## Nietzsche's Critique / Engagement

### The "English" Moral Philosophy and Its Limitations

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of Rée is, at one level, a critique of the entire tradition of "English" moral psychology — Hobbes, Hume, Smith, Bentham, Mill, [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] — that seeks to explain morality in terms of utility, sympathy, or adaptation. Rée, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account, belongs to this tradition despite being German: his book presents "a perverse and vicious kind of moral philosophy (your real *English* kind)." The charge is that this tradition never genuinely investigates origins but instead projects modern, democratic, utilitarian values backward onto history, assuming that what morality *is* now is what it has always been.

Rée's specific error, as [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] diagnoses it, is twofold. First, Rée assumes that [[punishment]] originated as a *deterrent* — that its "deterrent object" is "essential and original" (P06-001) — whereas [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy shows that [[punishment]] is far older than deterrence and originated in the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship. Second, Rée identifies morality with altruism — "the altruistic ethics (in which Dr. Rée, like all the English moral [[philosopher|philosophers]], sees the ethical 'Thing-in-itself')" (P06-001) — whereas [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy reveals morality as the product of a historical struggle between master and slave valuations.

### Darwin and the "Blue Vacuum of Heaven"

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most vivid critique of Rée is also his most telling. Rée "had read [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]: — and so in his philosophy the [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ian beast and that pink of modernity, the demure weakling and dilettante, who 'bites no longer,' shake hands politely" (P09-001). The image is devastating: [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s ferocious vision of nature — the struggle for existence, the war of all against all — is domesticated in Rée's hands into a story of progressive gentility. The beast becomes a gentleman; evolution becomes moral progress.

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts this with his own method: "Other colours, of course, rise immediately to one's mind as being a hundred times more potent than blue for a [[genealogy|genealogy of morals]]: — for instance, *grey*, by which I mean authentic facts capable of definite proof and having actually existed" (P09-001). The "blue" of the English sky — the abstract, optimistic, theoretical coloration of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]ian explanation — is replaced by the "grey" of the genealogical document: legal codes, [[punishment]] practices, philological evidence, the "long hieroglyphic script" of human moral history. Rée looked up at the sky; [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] looks down at the documents.

### Rée as Catalyst: The Productive Value of Error

For all his emphatic negation of Rée's conclusions, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is careful to acknowledge Rée's positive role. The "impulse" Rée provided was real and productive. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s method emerged through opposition to Rée's — the genealogical hammer was forged against the anvil of English moral psychology. This is a recurring pattern in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s intellectual biography: [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]], [[richard-wagner|Wagner]], Rée — each was, at a different stage, the figure [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] needed to oppose in order to become himself.

> In that early period I gave, as I have said, the first public expression to those theories of origin to which these essays are devoted, but with a clumsiness which I was the last to conceal from myself, for I was as yet cramped, being still without a special language for these special subjects. (P06-001)

The acknowledgment of "clumsiness" is important. The [[genealogy|Genealogy]] does not pretend to have sprung fully formed from [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s head; it acknowledges its own history — the false st[[artist|art]]s, the borrowed language, the gradual development of a "special language for these special subjects." Rée is p[[artist|art]] of that history, and the [[genealogy|Genealogy]]'s Preface honors him even as it refutes him.

### The Autobiographical Subtext

Beneath the philosophical argument, the Preface to the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] contains a submerged autobiographical narrative. Rée was not merely an intellectual antagonist but a former friend — the third point of the triangle with Lou Salomé that ended in painful estrangement. The [[genealogy|Genealogy]] was published in 1887, five years after the break with Rée and Salomé. When [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] writes of having been "deceived" in his hope that Rée would adopt a "truer method," there is more at stake than methodology. The intellectual break and the personal break are intertwined, and the Preface's tone — respectful, regretful, but resolute — reflects both dimensions.
