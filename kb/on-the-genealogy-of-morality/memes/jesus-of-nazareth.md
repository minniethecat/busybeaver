## Overview

Jesus of Nazareth occupies a pivotal and ambivalent position in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s _On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality_. On one hand, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] treats Jesus as the symbolic origin of the "slave revolt in morality"—the historical turning point at which the values of the weak, the suffering, and the powerless were elevated to the status of universal morality.

On the other hand, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s relationship to the figure of Jesus is more complex than simple rejection; he distinguishes between Jesus himself and the institutional [[Christianity]] that [[Saint Paul]] and the early [[church|Church]] built in his name.

## Role in the Genealogy

Jesus appears in all three Essays, but most prominently in Essay 1, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] develops his theory of the "slave revolt in morality." The phrase "Jesus of Nazareth, the incarnate gospel of love" (008-001)

is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s ironic designation: the "love" that Jesus preaches is, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogical reading, a sublimated form of hatred—the [[ressentiment]] of the powerless against the powerful, transformed into a moral system.

The genealogy of [[christianity|Christian morality]] begins, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account, with the Jewish [[priests|priestly class]] and reaches its consummation in the figure of Jesus. The "transvaluation of values" that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] attributes to the [[jews|Jews]]—whereby "the wretched alone are the good; the poor, the impotent, the lowly alone are the good"—finds its most powerful and enduring expression in the Christian gospel.

In Essay 3, Jesus is implicated in the broader phenomenon of the [[ascetic-ideal]]: the [[priests|priest]]ly [[will-to-power|will to power]] that turns against life itself. The [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]], whose archetype is ultimately the figure of Christ, exercises power by cultivating [[guilt]], sin-consciousness, and the longing for redemption in his flock.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of Jesus involves several distinct argumentative strands:

1. **Jesus as the "incarnate gospel of love"**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] reads Christian _agape_ not as genuine love but as the ultimate weapon of the weak. The command to "love one's enemies" is, genealogically speaking, the most refined form of revenge: it forces the enemy into a moral debt he cannot repay, making him feel [[guilt]]y for his own strength.

2. **The psychology of the Redeemer**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is interested in the psychological type that Jesus represents—the "sublime" variant of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]], in which the [[will-to-power|will to power]] turns so completely inward that it negates the world entirely.

This type of psychology, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, is driven by an extreme sensitivity to suffering and a consequent devaluation of the entire realm of the senses and the body.

3. **Jesus and the [[jews|Jews]]**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s discussion of Jesus is inseparable from his larger and more ambivalent treatment of Judaism. In a provocative passage, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] refers to "that Jewess" (Mary) and groups Jesus with "Peter the fisherman" and other Jewish figures, emphasizing the Jewish origins of [[christianity|Christianity]] against anti-Semitic narratives that sought to de-Judaize Jesus (015-002).

4. **The Talmudic mother**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] draws on Talmudic traditions about Mary to suggest that the Christian narrative of virgin birth conceals a more complex—and for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], more psychologically interesting—story about the relationship between religious genius and its origins (014-001).

## Key Passages

> ...and increasing desire. This Jesus of Nazareth, the incarnate gospel of love, the "Saviour" who brought blessedness and victory to the poor, the sick, the sinners—was he not precisely seduction in its most sinister and irresistible form? (008-001)

> One should read the description of the mother of Jesus from the Talmud, Tertullian included, with the attention it deserves as a document of the psychology of _ressentiment_. (014-001)

> ...we know, and _one Jewess_ (to Jesus of Nazareth, to Peter the fisherman, to that arch-Jew Paul of Tarsus)... (015-002)
