## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **shame** is not an innate moral sentiment but a product of the "[[virtue|morality of custom]]"—the prehistoric system through which communities enforced conformity through public [[cruelty]] and spectacle.

Shame is the internalization of the community's gaze. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] describes how, in the [[virtue|morality of custom]], "being pitied passed for shame, work for shame" (050-003 area). What a community despises becomes what the individual despises in himself—shame is social control become self-control.

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### Shame and the Morality of Custom

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy locates shame in the prehistoric "[[virtue|morality of custom]]" (*Sittlichkeit der Sitte*). In this primordial moral system, values were not internal convictions but public enforcements.

"Throughout the world suffering passed for virtue, [[cruelty]] for virtue, deceit for virtue, revenge for virtue, repudiation of the reason for virtue; and when, conversely, well-being passed current for danger, the desire for knowledge for danger, pity for danger, peace for danger, being pitied for shame, work for shame" (050-003). Shame is whatever the community makes shameful—and the community can make anything shameful through the threat of [[punishment]] and exclusion.

### The Public Character of Primitive Morality

Before the invention of conscience, morality was entirely public. "[[virtue|Virtue]] without witnesses was something quite inconceivable" (023-002 area). The community needed to *see* virtue and vice—and shame was the mechanism that made the individual's conformity visible.

The debtor who defaulted, the oath-breaker, the coward—all were subjected to public degradation. Their shame was not a private feeling but a public spectacle, performed before the community and often before [[gods|the gods]].

### Shame and the Rise of Bad Conscience

With the internalization of [[cruelty]]—the historical shift that produces [[Bad Conscience|bad conscience]]—shame too is internalized. What was once the public gaze becomes the inner eye of conscience.

The individual no longer needs the community to witness his shame; he witnesses himself. "The 'ower' is now the self" (037-001): the self becomes its own creditor, its own judge, its own punisher. Shame becomes [[guilt]]—a debt owed not to the community but to oneself.

### Shame and the Noble Type

The [[Noble|noble]] type, by contrast, is characterized by a relative absence of shame. "This is a heritage of the old noble [[master-morality|aristocratic morality]], which... possessed no memory for any insults and meannesses" (010-002).

The noble does not feel shame because he does not internalize the judgment of others. His values are his own—spontaneous, self-affirming, indifferent to the opinions of inferiors. Shame, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is the mark of the slave: the one who has been forced to see himself through the eyes of his master.

| Form of Shame | Historical Context | Mechanism |
|-------------|-------------------|----------|
| Public shame | Morality of custom | Community spectacle, [[punishment]] |
| Internalized shame | Rise of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] | Self-witnessing, [[guilt]] |
| Nobility's absence | Pre-moral [[aristocracy]] | Self-affirmation, indifference to others' judgment |

## Key Passages

> Being pitied passed for shame, work for shame, madness for divinity, and *change* for immorality and incarnate corruption! (050-003)

> He who can still hear (but man turns now deaf ears to such sounds), how in this night of torment and nonsense there has rung out the cry of *love*, the cry of the most passionate ecstasy, of redemption in *love*. (038-001)

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis of shame does not call for its abolition—shame, like all moral phenomena, has its uses. But genealogy exposes shame as a historical product, a tool of social control, rather than an eternal moral truth. The question is not whether shame exists but *who* feels shame, *for what*, and *in whose service*.
