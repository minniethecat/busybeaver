## Definition

The **creditor-debtor relationship** (*Schuldverhältnis*) is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s proposed origin for the entire realm of moral concepts: [[guilt]] (*[[guilt|Schuld]]*), [[obligation|duty]], [[debt|obligation]], justice, and even the idea of a divine creditor (God). The German word *[[guilt|Schuld]]* means both "[[guilt]]" (moral) and "debt" (financial) — a linguistic coincidence that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] takes as archaeological evidence for the economic origin of moral concepts.

> The contractual relationship between *creditor* and *ower*, that is as old as the existence of "legal subjects" at all, and on its part points back to the fundamental forms of buying, selling, barter, trade, and truck. (020-002)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Economic Origin of Guilt

The genealogy traces how the mundane economic relation of lending and repayment became the template for all [[obligation|moral obligation]]. When a debtor fails to repay, the creditor is entitled to compensation — and the earliest form of compensation was the debtor's body:

> The debtor pledged something by virtue of a contract with his creditor to meet the contingency of his not paying: his body, his wife, his freedom, or his life (or under certain religious conditions even his blessedness, the salvation of his soul, finally even his peace in the grave). (021-001)

This corporeal security gave the creditor the right to inflict pain on the debtor — a right that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies as the origin of [[punishment]]. The pleasure of seeing another suffer was the creditor's compensation for the unpaid debt.

### The Equation of Pain and Debt

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most radical thesis is that the equivalence "damage = pain" was the foundation of all early law:

> To what extent can suffering be a compensation for "owing"? In the degree to which *to make suffer* was the highest degree of pleasure, in the degree to which the injured party exchanged for the loss, including the displeasure over the loss, an extraordinary *counter-pleasure*: the *making suffer*. (022-001)

This economy of [[cruelty]] is the basis of the oldest legal systems, in which every infraction was a debt to be paid in pain, with tariffs specifying exactly what body p[[artist|art]] or degree of suffering corresponded to each offense.

### From Economic Debt to Moral Guilt

The decisive transformation occurs when the creditor-debtor relationship is projected onto the largest scale: the relationship between the living and their ancestors. The ancestors' power grows in memory, and their demands for sacrifice increase, until:

> The conviction reigns that it is only through the sacrifices and works of the ancestors that the race *continues* — and that one has to *pay them back* with sacrifices and works. One thus acknowledges a *debt* that continually grows... because the ancestors in their continued existence as powerful spirits do not cease to confer on the race advantages and advances of their power. (025-001)

The ancestor eventually becomes a god, and the debt becomes infinite — culminating in [[christianity|Christianity]]'s drama of an unpayable debt redeemed by God's [[sacrifice|self-sacrifice]].

### The Origin of Justice

[[justice|Justice]] (*[[justice|Gerechtigkeit]]*) begins in the creditor-debtor relationship as a form of measured compensation. The earliest law (*[[law|Recht]]*) was not punitive but economic — a system of equivalences:

> The sentiment of justice appeared on earth, is in point of fact, according to the most valid historiography, everywhere at home in the same sphere of the reactive feelings. (020-002, paraphrase)

As communities grew more powerful, they intervened to limit the creditor's [[revenge|vengeance]], replacing private revenge with public law — a regularization of [[cruelty]] rather than its elimination.

## Key Passages

> The relationship between buyer and seller, creditor and ower: here it was that individual confronted individual for the first time, here it was that individual *measured itself* against individual. (024-001)

> Man lives in a continual process of reckoning with his past, of balancing accounts — the creditor has always grown more humane proportionately as he has grown richer; finally the *degree* of his wealth becomes the measure of how much injury he can *endure* without suffering from it. (026-001)

> The feeling of guilt, of personal obligation, had its origin, as we saw, in the oldest and most primitive personal relationship, that between buyer and seller, creditor and ower. (035-001)
