# State

## Overview

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **State** is not a social contract or a rational institution — it is theorized as the product of a primordial act of conquest and subjugation. This violent origin is central to [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account of how [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] emerged: the State provided the "cage" within which human instincts, denied external outlet, turned inward.

## Nietzsche's Characterization

### The Violent Origin of the State

Against [[social-contract|social contract]] theories that imagine the State arising from rational agreement, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] insists on its bloody birth. The oldest State appeared as a "terrible tyranny," imposed by a conquering master-race upon a weaker population. This is not a critique but a genealogical claim: the State's essence is not law but *force*.

> The oldest "State" appeared consequently as a ghastly tyranny, as a grinding, pitiless machinery, and went on working, until the raw material of a semi-animal populace was ready and had been completely kneaded. (033-001)

### The State as Cage

The State's most significant genealogical function is to serve as an enclosure. Once humans are imprisoned within its walls, their aggressive instincts — formerly discharged outward in hunting, war, and competition — have no natural outlet. These instincts turn inward, creating the psychological interior that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]].

> Encaged as he was in "the State," as part of his taming process, the semi-animal man was reduced to thinking, inferring, calculating. (038-001)

This caging is not incidental to civilization — it *is* civilization. What we call "interiority," "soul," "conscience" are, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account, the scars left by the State's original violence.

### Small States and Modern Decline

In a late passage, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] reflects on the modern political condition of "small States" — the fragmentation of [[europe|Europe]] into nation-states — as a symptom of cultural exhaustion rather than vitality.

> The small States of Europe — I refer to all our present kingdoms and empires — are no longer capable of producing anything great. (087-002)

## Role in Moral History

In the genealogical narrative, the State occupies a paradoxical position. It is the most violent of all human inventions — and yet it is the necessary precondition for the emergence of the human *soul*. Without the State's enclosure, there would be no [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]]; without [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]], there would be no morality as [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] understands it. The State is thus both the original sin of civilization and the womb of the human interior.

This paradox is essential to understanding [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s relationship to political institutions. He neither defends the State (its origin is barbaric) nor simply denounces it (its product — the human soul — is the object of his genealogical inquiry). The State is what it does, and what it does is *transform* the human animal into the human subject.

## Key Passages

> The oldest "State" appeared consequently as a ghastly tyranny, as a grinding, pitiless machinery, and went on working, until the raw material of a semi-animal populace was ready and had been completely kneaded — kneaded, indeed, into a people. (033-001)

> Encaged as he was in "the State," as part of his taming process, the semi-animal man was reduced to thinking, inferring, calculating, combining cause and effect — to his "consciousness," in short. (038-001)

> The small States of Europe — I refer to all our present kingdoms and empires — are no longer capable of producing anything great: the soil has become too poor. (087-002)
