## Overview

Heinrich Heine (1797–1856) was a German poet, essayist, and literary critic, one of the most significant figures of post-Romantic German literature. A Jewish convert to [[christianity|Christianity]] living in Parisian exile, Heine embodied the transnational [[europe|Europe]]an spirit [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] admired.

In "Peoples and Countries," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] places Heine in distinguished company — alongside [[napoleon-bonaparte|Napoleon]], [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]], [[ludwig-van-beethoven|Beethoven]], [[stendhal|Stendhal]], and [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] — as one of the "broad-minded and deep-thinking men" whose real work was the preparation of a United [[europe|Europe]]. The inclusion is telling: Heine's cosmopolitanism, his ironic intelligence, and his freedom from [[germany|German nation]]alist pieties all align with [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own values.

## Role in the Genealogy

Heine appears in Peoples and Countries §18 (087-001), a passage in which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] prophesies the coming unification of [[europe|Europe]] as an economic and political necessity. Heine is cited as evidence that the genuine intellectual currents of the nineteenth century flowed toward synthesis and integration, not toward the national fragmentation celebrated by "patriots."

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s observation that these thinkers "only in their weaker moments, or when they grew old, did they fall back again into the national narrowness of the 'Fatherlanders'" (087-001) is a backhanded compliment: the default, vital orientation of such minds was [[europe|Europe]]an, not national.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**[[europe|Europe]]an against the national.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Heine is an argument against patriotism as a serious intellectual position. The "United [[europe|Europe]]" vision [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] espouses locates Heine among those who understood — earlier and more clearly than their contemporaries — that the nation-state was becoming economically untenable and spiritually provincial.

**The company Heine keeps.** The list in 087-001 is carefully composed: [[napoleon-bonaparte|Napoleon]] (will and action), [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]] (comprehensive vision), [[ludwig-van-beethoven|Beethoven]] (creative power), [[stendhal|Stendhal]] (psychological acuity), [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] (philosophical pessimism), and Heine (poetic irony). Each represents a dimension of the [[europe|Europe]]an spirit [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] champions. Heine's specific contribution is the marriage of lyricism to critical intelligence — the poet as scourge of philistinism.

**The "perhaps" of [[richard-wagner|Wagner]].** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s addition of "Perhaps [[richard-wagner|Richard Wagner]] likewise belongs to their number, concerning whom, as a successful type of German obscurity, nothing can be said without some such 'perhaps'" (087-001) is characteristically barbed: [[richard-wagner|Wagner]], the would-be [[europe|Europe]]an, may only have been another instance of German obscurity after all.

## Key Passages

> What I am concerned with — for I see it preparing itself slowly and hesitatingly — is the United Europe. It was the only real work, the one impulse in the souls, of all the broad-minded and deep-thinking men of this century — this preparation of a new synthesis, and the tentative effort to anticipate the future of "the European." (087-001)

> I am thinking of men like Napoleon, Heinrich Heine, Goethe, Beethoven, Stendhal, Schopenhauer. Perhaps Richard Wagner likewise belongs to their number, concerning whom, as a successful type of German obscurity, nothing can be said without some such "perhaps." (087-001)

> Only in their weaker moments, or when they grew old, did they fall back again into the national narrowness of the "Fatherlanders" — then they were once more "patriots." (087-001)
