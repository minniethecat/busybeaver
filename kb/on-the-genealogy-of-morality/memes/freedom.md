## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]''s genealogy, **freedom** (*Freiheit*) is not a political right or a metaphysical property of the will but a fundamental *instinct* — the "instinct of freedom" (*Instinkt der Freiheit*) that drives all living things to resist constraint and seek the discharge of power. This instinct underlies the most diverse phenomena: the [[cruelty]] of [[punishment]], the self-torture of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]], the creativity of the [[artist|art]]ist, and the self-overcoming of the philosopher.

> That identical *instinct of freedom* (to use my own language, the will to power): which is the will to power. (034-001, paraphrase)

## Nietzsche''s Analysis

### The Instinct of Freedom as Will to Power

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies the instinct of freedom with the [[will-to-power|will to power]] itself. Every living thing seeks to expand, to overcome resistance, to discharge its force. Freedom is not the absence of constraint but the active overcoming of it:

> A tremendous quantity of freedom been expelled from the world, or at least from the visible world, by the hammer-blows of this process of "civilising." (033-001, paraphrase)

This "expelled" freedom does not disappear — it turns inward, creating the internal world of conscience, depth, and self-overcoming.

### Freedom and Punishment

The earliest form of the instinct of freedom is [[cruelty]] — the pleasure of making others suffer. [[punishment|Punishment]] is the institutionalized form of this instinct, giving the powerful the "right" to exercise their freedom on the body of the transgressor:

> Punishments belong pre-eminently to these bulwarks — brought it about that all those instincts of the wild, free, roving man were turned backwards, turned against man himself. (032-002)

When external targets become unavailable (through the state''s monopoly on violence), the instinct of freedom turns inward — and this is the origin of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]].

### The Sovereign Individual''s Freedom

At the apex of development, the instinct of freedom is neither discharged outward (as [[cruelty]]) nor inward (as self-torture) but transformed into genuine self-mastery. The sovereign individual''s freedom is the power to keep a promise, to answer for oneself, to be the master of one''s own measure of value:

> The proud knowledge of the extraordinary privilege of *responsibility*, this consciousness of this rare freedom, this power over oneself and over fate. (018-001)

This is freedom as *achievement* — not a given but something won through the long discipline of the [[virtue|morality of custom]].

### Freedom and the State

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is suspicious of modern political freedom. The state does not liberate the instinct of freedom but channels and neutralizes it:

> They enjoy there freedom from all social control, they compensate themselves for the tension produced by long enclosure and confinement in the peace of the community. (010-004, paraphrase)

The "freedom" of the modern individual is often merely release — the temporary discharge of accumulated instinct, not genuine self-mastery.

## Key Passages

> That identical *instinct of freedom* (to use my own language, the will to power)... this instinct for freedom, pushed back, repressed, incarcerated within, and finally able to discharge and unleash itself only against itself: that, and that alone, is what the *bad conscience* is in its beginnings. (034-001)

> Not a tremendous quantity of freedom been expelled from the world, or at least from the visible world, by the hammer-blows of this process of "civilising." (033-001, paraphrase)

> The sovereign man calls it his *conscience*. (018-001)
