## Overview

Ludwig Feuerbach (1804–1872) was a German philosopher of the Young [[hegel|Hegelian]] movement, known for his materialist critique of religion.

In *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Feuerbach in the context of [[richard-wagner|Wagner]]'s intellectual biography — his early enthusiasm for "[[health|healthy]] sensuality" and his later repudiation of it.

## Role in the Genealogy

Feuerbach appears in Essay 3, §3, within [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis of [[richard-wagner|Wagner]]'s *Parsifal*. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] asks whether [[richard-wagner|Wagner]]'s final work is genuine Christian re-conversion or a parody — "his secret laugh of superiority over himself" (044-001).

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of Feuerbach is indirect — a reference point in [[richard-wagner|Wagner]]'s trajectory. The key passage: "Just remember with what enthusiasm [[richard-wagner|Wagner]] followed in the footsteps of Feuerbach. Feuerbach's motto of '[[health|healthy]] sensuality' rang in the ears of [[richard-wagner|Wagner]]... like the word of redemption" (044-001).

This [[memory|recollection]] establishes a baseline for measuring [[richard-wagner|Wagner]]'s distance traveled — from Feuerbachian materialism to the ascetic [[christianity|Christianity]] of *Parsifal*. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], this movement is the central problem (044-001).

Feuerbach represents the 1830s–1840s German moment when the Young [[hegel|Hegelian]] critique of religion promised liberation from Christian [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]]. That [[richard-wagner|Wagner]], devoted to "the *highest* [[artist|art]]istic expression of soul and body," should end preaching "retrogression, conversion, [[christianity|Christianity]], mediævalism" represents not merely personal apostasy but a symptom of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s enduring power (044-001).

## Key Passages

> Just remember with what enthusiasm Wagner followed in the footsteps of Feuerbach. Feuerbach's motto of "healthy sensuality" rang in the ears of Wagner during the thirties and forties of the century, as it did in the ears of many Germans (they dubbed themselves "*Young* Germans"), like the word of redemption. (044-001)

> Did he eventually *change his mind* on the subject? For it seems at any rate that he eventually wished to *change his teaching* on that subject... (044-001)
