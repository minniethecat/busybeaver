# Plato

## Overview

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher, student of [[socrates|Socrates]], and founder of the Academy. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Plato is not merely an ancient thinker to be historically assessed but the archetypal philosophical adversary — the originator of the metaphysical dualism that, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy, evolves into [[christianity|Christian morality]] and culminates in the [[Ascetic Ideal]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of Plato in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] is genealogical: Platonism is treated as the philosophical prototype of the life-denying will to truth that subordinates the actual, sensuous world to a fictitious "true world" of Forms, Ideas, and eternal truths.

> Our enmity and rancour against Christianity (and Plato), that has conceivably never been overcome. (001-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Plato appears across all three Essays of the [[genealogy|Genealogy]], invoked at strategic moments in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s argument about the origins and evolution of morality. In the First Essay, Plato is identified with the [[noble|aristocratic]] Greek valuation that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] both admires and critiques — the Greek noble who affirmed himself as "good" before the slave revolt in morals introduced reactive valuation. In the Third Essay, Plato becomes the central exhibit in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s case that philosophy itself, from its inception, has been a disguised form of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]].

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] positions Plato as the turning point in Western thought: the moment when the instinct for truth — originally a tool of life — begins to turn against life, valuing the supersensuous "true world" over the actual, embodied, becoming world. The "Platonic error" is the archetype of all subsequent metaphysical and moral errors.

> Plato's instinct felt this — Plato, the greatest enemy of art which Europe has produced up to the present. (066-001)

## Nietzsche's Critique

### Plato and the Ascetic Ideal

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most penetrating critique of Plato in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] is found in the Third Essay, where he argues that philosophy, from Plato onward, has always been a form of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. The philosopher's will to truth — the drive to reach beyond the world of appearance to a "true" reality — is structurally identical to the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]]'s will to transcend the body and its passions. Both are expressions of a life that has become hostile to itself.

> The ascetic ideal has thus corrupted the taste and virtue of our philosophic men no less than of the rest of mankind. Our cultured men of to-day, our "good" men, do not tell lies — that is true; but it is not to their credit! The real lie, the genuine resolute "honest" lie... would prove too tough and strong for them. (060-001)

### The Erroneous Belief That God Is Truth

In a crucial passage, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies Plato as the philosophical source of the Christian equation of God with Truth — a conflation that makes the death of God (the loss of Christian [[religion|faith]]) paradoxically strengthen rather than weaken the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. [[science|Science]] inherits Plato's will to truth even as it dismantles [[christianity|Christianity]]'s dogma.

> Christian belief, which was also Plato's belief, the belief that God is Truth, that Truth is Divine. (065-001)

### Pia Fraus — The "Pious Fraud"

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] accuses Plato of *pia fraus* (pious fraud) — the philosophical equivalent of the [[priests|priest]]'s manipulation. By inventing a "true world" beyond this one, Plato engaged in a noble lie that devalued the actual world and prepared the ground for two millennia of metaphysical and moral nihilism.

> We would condemn Plato for his *pia fraus*. (073-002)

### The Greek Context

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s view of Plato is not uniformly hostile. He acknowledges Plato as a genuine Greek type — the "Greek" who "shows it in a hundred places" (059-002). Plato's philosophical instinct belongs to the late Greek world, a world already in decline but still possessed of the [[noble|aristocratic]] self-certainty that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] admires. The tragedy, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s view, is that Plato's genius was employed in the service of a life-denying metaphysics rather than a life-affirming one.

> Such was the Greek; Plato shows it in a hundred places. (059-002)
