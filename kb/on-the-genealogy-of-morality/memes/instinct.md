# Instinct

## Definition

**Instinct** (*Instinkt*) is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], not a mere biological reflex but the embodied intelligence of life itself — the accumulated wisdom of millennia encoded in the body and expressed as spontaneous valuation. Instinct is what guides the "noble" type of human being, whose actions flow directly from an affirmative self without the mediation of abstract moral reasoning. The history of morality, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s account, is largely the history of the war against instinct.

> ...where the life instinct fixes truth with the greatest positiveness... (053-001)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### Instinct vs. Consciousness

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] inverts the traditional hierarchy that places consciousness and reason above instinct. For him, consciousness is a late, fragile evolutionary development, while instinct represents the deep, reliable wisdom of the organism. "The great intelligence of the body" functions far more effectively without conscious oversight.

> ...of the governing unconscious instincts, or as indeed a certain lack of knowledge, something which is not necessarily conscious... (010-002)

### The Herd Instinct

In the First Essay, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies what he calls the "herd instinct" (*Heerden-Instinkt*) — the deeply rooted tendency of the weak to band together for protection and to elevate their collective values into universal moral law. This instinctive gregariousness is the psychological foundation of [[Slave Morality]].

> ...in my own language, the herd instinct which finds in this antithesis its highest expression. (002-003)

### The "Un-egoistic" Instincts

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] singles out the instincts of pity, self-denial, and [[sacrifice|self-sacrifice]] as those most highly valued by the morality he critiques. These "un-egoistic" instincts are not, he argues, more natural or more moral — they have simply been selected for by the historical victory of [[Slave Morality]] over [[Master Morality]].

> ...the value of the "un-egoistic" instincts, the instincts of pity, self-denial, and self-sacrifice... (P07-001)

### Instinct as a Dominating Force

In the Second Essay, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] describes how a p[[artist|art]]icular instinct can become so deeply ingrained that it functions as a "dominating instinct" — an automatic, unquestioned response that shapes an entire civilization's value system. [[Bad Conscience]] is itself such an instinct: the instinct for freedom turned inward.

> ...and has become an instinct, a dominating instinct — what name shall I give it? (018-001)

### Life Instinct and the Ascetic Ideal

The ultimate target of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique is the [[Ascetic Ideal]], which he diagnoses as the life instinct turned against itself. The [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] uses the power of instinct to attack instinct, creating a paradoxical form of life that wills its own diminishment.
