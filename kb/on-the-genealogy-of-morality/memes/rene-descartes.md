# René Descartes

## Overview

René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist whose methodological skepticism and dualism of mind and body established the framework for modern philosophy. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Descartes appears only in a list — the roll call of great [[philosopher|philosophers]] who never married — but this placement is itself an argument. By grouping Descartes with Heracleitus, [[plato|Plato]], [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], [[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz|Leibniz]], [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], and [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] as philosophical celibates, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] makes a genealogical claim: that the *cogito* — the thinking self that Descartes isolated as the foundation of certain knowledge — is itself an expression of the [[Ascetic Ideal]]. The philosopher who declares "I think, therefore I am" has already, in the act of privileging thought over embodiment, performed the ascetic gesture: the denial of the body in favor of a purified, rational substance.

> Heracleitus, Plato, Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz, Kant, Schopenhauer — they were not married, and, further, one cannot imagine them as married. (048-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Descartes is a minor figure in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]]'s dramatis personae, but his significance is structural. He represents the early modern consolidation of the philosophical ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] — the moment when philosophy, having emerged from its medieval subordination to theology, reconstituted itself on a new, ostensibly secular foundation. The Cartesian *cogito* appears to break with religious authority; in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy, it merely replaces one form of [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] with another. The thinking substance (*res cogitans*) is a purified abstraction, a philosopher's fantasy of existence without a body. That Descartes himself lived a celibate, retiring life — he never married, and his most productive years were spent in self-imposed isolation in the Netherlands — is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], not biographical coincidence but the lived expression of his philosophy.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### The Unmarried Philosopher

Descartes' celibacy is the central fact about him in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]]. In Essay Three, §7, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] enumerates the great [[philosopher|philosophers]] who never married, and the list — Heracleitus, [[plato|Plato]], Descartes, [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], [[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz|Leibniz]], [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] — spans the entire history of Western philosophy from the pre-Socratics to [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own immediate predecessor. Descartes occupies the midpoint of this lineage: after the ancients (Heracleitus, [[plato|Plato]]) and before the modern [[germans|Germans]] ([[immanuel-kant|Kant]], [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]). His presence in the list demonstrates the continuity of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] across the supposed rupture of modernity. The scientific revolution did not liberate philosophy from [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]]; it gave [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] a new, rationalist form.

> A married philosopher belongs to comedy, that is my rule. (048-001)

### Cogito as Ascetic Gesture

Although [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] does not explicitly analyze the *cogito* in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]], the framework of the Third Essay provides the tools for such an analysis. The *cogito* is the philosopher's declaration of independence from the body: "I think" is the assertion of a self that is pure thought, uncontaminated by desire, sensation, or instinct. This is the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] in its philosophical mode — not the mortification of the flesh through fasting and prayer, but the transcendence of the flesh through thought. Descartes' dualism, which splits the human being into thinking substance and extended substance, is the metaphysical [[artist|art]]iculation of the philosopher's instinctive hostility to sensuality, which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] discusses at length in the same section (048-001).

### Descartes and the Will to Truth

Descartes' methodological skepticism — the systematic doubt that strips away every belief that can be questioned until only the *cogito* remains — is a paradigmatic expression of what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls the [[Will to Truth]]. The will to truth is the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s latest and most refined form: the conviction that truth is unconditionally valuable, that it must be pursued at any cost, including the cost of life itself. Descartes' doubt is, genealogically, a form of self-torture — the philosopher turning against his own beliefs with the same [[cruelty]] that the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] turns against the flesh. The reward is certainty, the "clear and distinct idea" — the philosophical equivalent of the saint's beatific vision.

### The French Philosophical Tradition

Descartes' presence in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] also serves to locate French philosophy within [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy of [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]]. Alongside [[blaise-pascal|Pascal]] — whose principle "il faut s'abêtir" [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] quotes in Essay Three (058-001) — Descartes represents the French contribution to the philosophical ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. Where [[blaise-pascal|Pascal]]'s [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] is religious and self-abasing, Descartes' is rational and self-affirming — but both, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], are forms of the same life-denying impulse, the same flight from the body and its claims.
