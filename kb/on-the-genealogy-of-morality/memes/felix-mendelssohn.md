## Overview

**Felix Mendelssohn** (1809–1847) was a German composer, pianist, organist, and conductor of the early Romantic period. A prolific composer across genres — symphonies, concertos, piano music, organ music, and chamber works — Mendelssohn was among the most celebrated [[artist|musician]]s of his era. He revived interest in the music of [[Johann Sebastian Bach]], conducted the first performance of [[johann-sebastian-bach|Bach]]'s *St. Matthew Passion* since the composer's death, and founded the Leipzig Conservatory.

## Role in the Genealogy

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] mentions Mendelssohn once in *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, in a passage discussing the relationship between [[artist|art]]ists and their public. The context is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s observation that most [[artist|art]]ists have historically been "serving classes" — dependent on [[church|the Church]], on princes, on the masses, and on moral law for their patronage and self-conception. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts this servile disposition with a handful of [[artist|art]]ists who maintained their own internal standard of taste.

Mendelssohn appears as one of the rare figures who possessed innate distinction, alongside [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]]:

> The slavish humility of the artist to his public (as Sebastian Bach has testified in undying and outrageous words in the dedication of his High Mass) is perhaps more difficult to perceive in music; but it is all the more deeply engrained. … Chopin possesses distinction, like Van Dyck. The disposition of Beethoven is that of a proud peasant; of Haydn, that of a proud servant. Mendelssohn, too, possesses distinction — like Goethe, in the most natural way in the world. (078-002)

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of Mendelssohn is notable for what it omits: Mendelssohn's Jewish heritage — a topic [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] discusses at length elsewhere in the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* in connection with [[Jews|the Jews]] and [[slave-morality]] — is not mentioned. Instead, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] classifies Mendelssohn purely on aesthetic grounds: he is one of the few [[artist|musician]]s who did not compromise his [[artist|art]]istic vision to please patrons or the public.

This placement is significant within [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s broader aesthetic philosophy. The distinction [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] attributes to Mendelssohn aligns with the [[noble]] mode of valuation that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] traces throughout the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*: an [[artist|art]]ist who "does not descend from himself" (to use [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s description of [[van-dyck|Van Dyck]]) but rather lifts others up to his own standard. Mendelssohn, like [[Johann Wolfgang von Goethe|Goethe]], represents the possibility of an [[artist|art]]ist who creates from inner necessity rather than external expectation.

## Key Passages

The sole reference to Mendelssohn in the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* occurs in a dense passage on [[artist|art]]istic character (078-002), p[[artist|art]] of the "Peoples and Countries" appendix. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] uses Mendelssohn to complete a taxonomy of musical temperaments:

| Composer | [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Characterization |
|----------|------------------------------|
| [[johann-sebastian-bach|Sebastian Bach]] | Slavish humility to his public |
| [[frederic-chopin|Chopin]] | Possesses distinction, like [[van-dyck|Van Dyck]] |
| [[ludwig-van-beethoven|Beethoven]] | Disposition of a proud peasant |
| [[joseph-haydn|Haydn]] | Disposition of a proud servant |
| **Mendelssohn** | **Possesses distinction — like [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]], naturally** |
