## Overview

Johann Sebastian Bach (1685–1750) was a German composer and organist of the Baroque period, whose works — including the *Mass in B minor*, the *St. Matthew Passion*, and *The Art of Fugue* — are widely regarded as among the supreme achievements of Western music.

In the appendix "Peoples and Countries," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] cites Bach in a single, pointed parenthetical: "as Sebastian Bach has testified in undying and outrageous words in the dedication of his High Mass." The reference serves as the opening illustration for an argument about the psychology of [[artist|artistic creation]] under [[christianity|Christianity]].

## Role in the Genealogy

Bach appears in Peoples and Countries §7 (078-002), within a section that examines the relationship between [[artist|art]]ists and their publics. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] opens the paragraph with a general thesis — "The slavish humility of the [[artist|art]]ist to his public" — and immediately names Bach as the exemplary case, citing the obsequious dedication of the *Mass in B minor* as enduring testimony to this servility.

Bach is the first example in a sequence that moves through [[frederic-chopin|Chopin]] ("distinction, like [[van-dyck|Van Dyck]]"), [[ludwig-van-beethoven|Beethoven]] ("the disposition of a proud peasant"), [[joseph-haydn|Haydn]] ("that of a proud servant"), and [[felix-mendelssohn|Mendelssohn]] ("distinction — like [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]], in the most natural way in the world"). Bach anchors the lower end of this spectrum: the [[artist|art]]ist whose humility toward his audience is absolute and, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s word, "outrageous."

## Nietzsche's Critique

**The [[artist|art]]ist as servant.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s phrase "slavish humility" (*sklavische Demut*) is deliberately provocative in a text centrally concerned with the genealogy of [[slave-morality|slave morality]]. By applying it to Bach — the most revered figure in the German musical canon — [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] extends his critique of Christian values from the moral into the aesthetic domain. Bach's humility before his patron-public is not accidental piety but structural: it reveals the [[artist|art]]ist's position within a Christian economy where creativity is understood as service, not sovereignty.

**Music and the invisibility of servility.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] immediately acknowledges a complicating factor: "The slavish humility of the [[artist|art]]ist to his public ... is perhaps more difficult to perceive in music; but it is all the more deeply engrained." Music, as the most abstract of the [[artist|art]]s, conceals the social relations embedded in its production. Bach's obsequious dedication, preserved in text, makes visible what musical sound alone would obscure — the "deeply engrained" subordination that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] takes to be constitutive of Christian [[artist|art]].

**The dedication as permanent testimony.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s choice of the word "undying" (*unsterblich*) carries a double edge. Bach's dedication is undying in the sense that his music has achieved immortality; but it is also undying in the sense that it permanently records the [[artist|art]]ist's self-abasement. The Mass itself becomes a monument to the servile psychology [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] anatomizes throughout the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*.

## Key Passages

> The slavish humility of the artist to his public (as Sebastian Bach has testified in undying and outrageous words in the dedication of his High Mass) is perhaps more difficult to perceive in music; but it is all the more deeply engrained. (078-002)

> Chopin possesses distinction, like Van Dyck. The disposition of Beethoven is that of a proud peasant; of Haydn, that of a proud servant. Mendelssohn, too, possesses distinction—like Goethe, in the most natural way in the world. (078-002)

> Van Dyck was nobler in this respect: who in all those whom he painted added a certain amount of what he himself most highly valued: he did not descend from himself, but rather lifted up others to himself when he "rendered." (078-001)
