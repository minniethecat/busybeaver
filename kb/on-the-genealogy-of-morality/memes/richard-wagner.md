# Richard Wagner

## Overview

**Richard Wagner** (1813–1883) occupies a uniquely charged position in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*. He appears not merely as a historical figure but as a personal symbol — the admired mentor who became the embodiment of everything [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] came to oppose. In the Third Essay, Wagner serves as the primary case study of how an [[artist|art]]ist, despite his creative power, can submit to the [[Ascetic Ideal]] and thereby betray his own life-affirming potential.

> In the case of an artist like Richard Wagner paying homage to chastity in his old age — that is a piece of acting, a *tour de force*. (043-001, paraphrased)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### From Admiration to Critique

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s early devotion to Wagner is well-documented. In *The Birth of Tragedy* (1872), Wagner appears as the rebirth of Greek tragic culture. By the time of the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* (1887), Wagner has become the primary example of the [[artist|art]]ist who capitulates to the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] — exchanging Dionysian affirmation for Christian redemption.

> The same velleity to which Wagner fell a victim in his old age, the velleity to *not* conclude a work, to leave it unfinished, to make it impossible. (045-001, paraphrased)

### Parsifal and the Ascetic Ideal

Wagner's last opera, *Parsifal* (1882), is for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] the decisive evidence of Wagner's decline. Where Wagner had once celebrated sensual love (*Tristan und Isolde*), he now celebrates chastity and Christian redemption. This turn from sensuality to [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], not a development but a *betrayal* — of [[artist|art]], of life, and of Wagner's own earlier insights.

> It would be unnecessary, even to wish it — that the Wagnerian *Parsifal* was meant joyously, as a *Satyr-play*, with which the tragedian Wagner wished to take leave of us, also of himself, above all of *tragedy*, in a fitting and worthy manner. (044-001, paraphrased)

### The Schopenhauer Connection

Wagner's turn to the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] was mediated by his embrace of [[Arthur Schopenhauer]]'s philosophy. Where [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]] would have been the "natural" father figure for an [[artist|art]]ist of Wagner's stature, Wagner instead chose [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] — the philosopher of pessimism and life-denial:

> So, for example, did *Richard Wagner* take, "when the time had come," the philosopher Schopenhauer for his "fatherly and leading man." Who would even think it possible that he did *not* take Goethe for that role? (046-001)

## Wagner in Nietzsche's Typology

| Period | Representative Work | [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s Evaluation |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Early (to 1859) | *Tristan und Isolde* | Dionysian, life-affirming |
| Middle (1860s) | *Die Meistersinger* | Ambivalent — "German" in both good and bad senses |
| Late (1870s–1883) | *Parsifal* | Ascetic, Christian, decadent |

## Broader Cultural Significance

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees Wagner's arc as symptomatic of a broader [[europe|Europe]]an [[disease|pathology]]. The "Wagnerian" becomes shorthand for a certain kind of modern German culture — intoxicated, grandiose, and secretly life-denying:

> We modern men are already beginning to unlearn the *senses* in this intoxicated in contemporary Germany, *the species anarchistica* within the educated... (067-001, paraphrased)
