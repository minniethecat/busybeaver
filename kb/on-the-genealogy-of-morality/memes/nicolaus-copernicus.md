## Overview

Nicolaus Copernicus (1473–1543) was a Polish astronomer whose heliocentric model displaced the E[[artist|art]]h from the centre of the universe. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], Copernicus is not merely a figure in the history of astronomy — he is the pivotal symbol of a process through which [[science|modern science]] has progressively stripped humanity of its dignity and self-importance.

In Essay 3, Section 25 (066-001), [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes the Copernican revolution not as a triumph of reason, but as the initiating catastrophe of a long self-belittling of man. Since Copernicus, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, humanity has been rolling "away from the centre — whither? into nothingness."

## Role in the Genealogy

Copernicus appears in the climactic passage of the Third Essay, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] interrogates the relationship between science and the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. The question at stake is whether science truly opposes the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]], or whether — as [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contends — science is itself an expression of that very [[ideals|ideal]], driven by the same "over-appreciation of truth" (066-001).

The reference to Copernicus serves as [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s central exhibit. The Copernican revolution, celebrated by moderns as a victory of science over theological dogma, did not — [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] insists — weaken the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] at all. On the contrary: by removing man from the centre of the cosmos, astronomy supplied the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] with a new, more sophisticated instrument of [[contempt|self-contempt]]. The old theological conviction that man was the image of God was replaced by the scientific conviction that man is a negligible speck — and this, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, is not a refutation of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]], but its most insidious expression.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**[[science|Science]] as ally of the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]].** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of Copernicus is embedded in a broader argument about science's hidden complicity with the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] (066-001). [[science|Science]] prides itself on objectivity and disinterestedness, but these very [[virtue|virtues]], [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contends, are ascetic [[virtue|virtues]] — they require the suppression of instinct, the cooling of emotion, the "impoverishment of life" that the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] has always demanded.

**The steep plane of self-belittling.** "Since Copernicus," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] writes, "man seems to have fallen on to a steep plane — he rolls faster and faster away from the centre — whither? into nothingness?" (066-001). The progression from Copernicus through [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] to [[science|modern science]] is, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s telling, a continuous degradation: man who was "almost God" has become "animal, literal, unqualified, and unmitigated animal."

**The will to truth as self-destruction.** The deeper point is that the scientific "will to truth" — the drive that produced Copernicus's heliocentrism and every subsequent scientific correction to human vanity — is itself a disguised form of the ascetic will. [[science|Science]] believes it is opposing religious dogmatism; in reality, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, it is perfecting the ascetic project of teaching man to despise himself, now under the respectable banner of "objectivity."

## Key Passages

> Has there not been since the time of Copernicus an unbroken progress in the self-belittling of man and his will for belittling himself? Alas, his belief in his dignity, his uniqueness, his irreplaceableness in the scheme of existence, is gone — he has become animal, literal, unqualified, and unmitigated animal, he who in his earlier belief was almost God ("child of God," "demi-God"). (066-001)

> Since Copernicus man seems to have fallen on to a steep plane — he rolls faster and faster away from the centre — whither? into nothingness? into the "thrilling sensation of his own nothingness" — Well! this would be the straight way — to the old ideal? (066-001)

> All science (and by no means only astronomy, with regard to the humiliating and deteriorating effect of which Kant has made a remarkable confession, "it annihilates my own importance"), all science, natural as much as unnatural — by unnatural I mean the self-critique of reason — nowadays sets out to talk man out of his present opinion of himself. (066-001)
