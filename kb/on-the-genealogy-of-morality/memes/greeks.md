# Greeks

## Overview

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **ancient Greeks** function as the primary historical counter-example to [[Christianity|Christian]] civilization. Where Christian [[europe|Europe]] is sick, inward-turned, and [[ressentiment]]-driven, the Greeks represent health, outwardness, and the spontaneous self-affirmation of [[Master Morality|master morality]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] returns to the Greeks repeatedly — not as antiquarian nostalgia but as a diagnostic contrast that reveals the [[disease|pathology]] of the present.

## Nietzsche's Characterization

### The Greek Relationship to the Gods

Perhaps [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most striking claim about the Greeks concerns their relationship to their gods. Unlike the Christian who uses God to judge himself, the Greek used [[gods|the gods]] to *protect* himself from [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] — to deflect responsibility for misfortune onto divine whim and thereby preserve psychological health.

> These Greeks long utilised their gods as simply a means of warding off the "bad conscience," so as to be able to enjoy their freedom of soul. (039-001)

This is the opposite of the Christian relationship to divinity. The Greek gods do not judge; they provide an *alibi* for human imperfection. This allows the Greek to remain on good terms with himself — a capacity [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] considers the mark of true [[aristocracy|nobility]].

### Greek Cruelty as Spectacle

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] does not [[ideals|ideal]]ize the Greeks as gentle humanitarians. On the contrary, he emphasizes their frank relationship to [[cruelty]] — a relationship that is, in his view, healthier than the Christian internalization of [[cruelty]] as [[guilt]] and self-[[punishment]].

> Even the Greeks knew no more piquant seasoning for the happiness of their gods than the joys of cruelty. (023-002)

The Greek acceptance of [[cruelty]] as p[[artist|art]] of existence — as spectacle, as festival, as divine pleasure — is a sign of strength, not barbarism. The Greek does not need to *deny* [[cruelty]]; he can *affirm* it.

### The Greek as Standard of Health

The Greeks provide [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] with his standard of what a [[health|healthy]] civilization looks like. Against the Greeks, modern [[europe|Europe]]an life appears shrunken, timid, and exhausted.

> By the standard of the ancient Greeks, our whole modern life, in so far as it is not weakness but power and the consciousness of power, appears as sheer hubris and godlessness. (050-001)

Yet [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is not recommending a return to [[greece|ancient Greece]]. The Greek world is forever lost; its function in the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* is to show that a different mode of human existence *was* possible — and therefore might be possible again.

## Role in Moral History

In the genealogical narrative, the Greeks represent the "before" of the slave revolt in morals. They are the civilization that the [[Jews|Jewish]] [[transvaluation|revaluation of values]] had to overcome. The historical triumph of [[Christianity]] over Hellenism is, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s view, the triumph of [[disease|sickness]] over health, of the [[slave-morality|slave]] over the [[Master Morality|master]].

The Greeks also provide the etymological evidence for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s central claim about the origin of "good." The Greek word *esthlos* — meaning "good" — originally meant "one who *is*," "the real," "the true." The Greek [[aristocracy|nobility]] called *themselves* good, and this self-designation is the origin of all value.
