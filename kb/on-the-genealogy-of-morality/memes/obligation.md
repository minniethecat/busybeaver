## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **[[debt|obligation]]** (*Verpflichtung*) traces its origin to the economic relationship between creditor and debtor. What appears as a purely moral phenomenon—the sense of duty, the binding force of a promise—is revealed by genealogy to be the internalized form of a [[creditor-debtor-relationship|contractual relationship]].

"The feeling of [[guilt]], of personal [[debt|obligation]]," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] writes, "had its origin in the oldest and most primitive personal relationship, in the relationship between buyer and seller, creditor and debtor" (Essay 2 §8). Moral [[debt|obligation]] is economic debt transformed into conscience.

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Economic Origin of Obligation

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] locates the origin of [[debt|obligation]] in the most fundamental of human transactions: exchange. The [[Creditor-Debtor Relationship|creditor-debtor relationship]] is "the oldest and most primitive personal relationship that exists" (Essay 2 §8).

In this primitive economy, the debtor pledges something as security for his debt—his body, his freedom, his life. "To inspire trust in his promise to repay... the debtor pledges something which he still possesses, something over which he still has power, for instance, his body, or his wife, or his freedom, or even his life" (Essay 2 §5). Obligation begins as a bodily contract.

### From Contract to Conscience

The crucial transformation in the history of [[debt|obligation]] is its internalization. What was originally an external, contractual bond becomes an internal, moral one. "The 'ower' is now the self" (037-001).

The debtor who cannot repay his debt to the community becomes an "outlaw"—cast out from the protection of the social contract. This primitive experience of being "owed" to the community is the seed from which the later concept of moral obligation grows. "Duty" (*[[guilt|Schuld]]*) and "[[guilt]]" (*[[guilt|Schuld]]*) share a common root in the German language—a linguistic fact that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] exploits to demonstrate their common origin.

### Obligation and the Promise

Obligation reaches its highest form in the act of [[promise|promising]]. The [[Promise|promise]] is the self-imposed [[debt|obligation]]—the individual binding himself to a future course of action. This capacity, which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] associates with the [[Sovereign Individual|sovereign individual]], is the "ripest fruit" of the long mnemotechnical history.

"To be able to guarantee oneself, and to do so with pride, and so also to be permitted to say 'yes' to oneself—that is a ripe fruit, but also a late fruit" (Essay 2 §2). The sovereign individual's freedom consists precisely in his capacity to impose [[debt|obligation]]s on himself and to honor them.

### Obligation and Bad Conscience

The most fateful development in the history of [[debt|obligation]] is its transformation into [[Bad Conscience|bad conscience]]. When the debtor-creditor relationship is applied to [[gods|the gods]]—when humanity conceives of itself as indebted to divine ancestors—the debt becomes unpayable.

"The impossibility of paying the debt" produces "the idea of an irredeemable debt" (037-001)—eternal [[debt|obligation]], eternal [[guilt]]. This is the birth of the Christian [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]]: an [[debt|obligation]] so absolute that it can never be discharged.

## Key Passages

> The feeling of guilt, of personal obligation, had its origin in the oldest and most primitive personal relationship, in the relationship between buyer and seller, creditor and debtor. (Essay 2 §8)

> To inspire trust in his promise to repay... the debtor pledges something which he still possesses, something over which he still has power, for instance, his body, or his wife, or his freedom, or even his life. (Essay 2 §5)

> With the impossibility of paying the debt, there becomes conceived the idea of an irredeemable debt, the idea that it cannot be paid off—the idea of "eternal punishment." (037-001)

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy of [[debt|obligation]] is a masterwork of reductive analysis: the most sacred moral feelings—duty, conscience, the binding force of a promise—are traced back to the most mundane of human practices: lending and borrowing. The creditor's ledger becomes the book of conscience.
