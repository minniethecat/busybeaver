## Overview

Theognis of Megara was a 6th-century BCE Greek elegiac poet whose verses — preserved in the collection known as the *Theognidea* — [[artist|art]]iculate an [[noble|aristocratic]] worldview centered on hereditary [[aristocracy|nobility]], loyalty among the *agathoi* (the "good" or noble), and contempt for the *kakoi* (the "base" or common). [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] references Theognis in the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality* as a paradigmatic voice of pre-Socratic [[master-morality|aristocratic morality]], making him a key textual witness in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s historical reconstruction of how "good" and "bad" originally emerged as social markers of rank rather than as moral categories.

## Role in the Genealogy

Theognis appears in Essay 1, §5, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is tracing the etymological and conceptual origins of moral terms. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies Theognis as the "mouthpiece" (*Mundstück*) of the Megarian [[aristocracy]] — a poet who gives voice to the spontaneous self-affirmation of the [[aristocracy|noble class]], who call *themselves* "the good" and designate the low-born as "the bad." For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], Theognis embodies the pre-moral stage of value creation: the aristocrat does not *judge* the commoner through comparison or [[ressentiment|resentment]], but simply *feels* his own superiority and expresses it directly in language. Theognis's poetry provides [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] with philological evidence that *gut* (good) and *schlecht* (bad) originally referred to social standing, not ethical qualities.

> The word ἐσθλός (*esthlos*), the root of which implies "one who *is*," "the real," "the true," and then, as a subjective development, "the true man," "the noble," undergoes an evolution typical of the aristocratic mode of valuation. Nietzsche notes that this trajectory is visible in Theognis, "whose mouthpiece is found in Theognis, the Megarian poet." (005-001)

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s interest in Theognis dates back to his early philological work. As a student at Schulpforta and later at Bonn and Leipzig, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] studied the Theognidean corpus extensively. His valedictorian thesis at Schulpforta (1864) was on Theognis, and he later published an [[artist|art]]icle "Zur Geschichte der Theognideischen Spruchsammlung" (On the History of the Theognidean Gnomic Collection) in *Rheinisches Museum für Philologie* (1867).

In the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] draws on this deep philological familiarity but deploys it in service of his [[genealogy|genealogical method]]. Theognis matters not as a literary figure per se, but as a *document*: his verses preserve, in fossilized form, the pre-moral [[noble|aristocratic]] value-scheme that [[christianity|Christianity]] would later invert through the slave revolt in morals. Theognis represents what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] calls the *vornehme* (noble) mode of valuation — spontaneous, affirmative, and rooted in the experience of plenitude — before it was corrupted by [[priests|priest]]ly [[ressentiment]].

## Key Passages

> Nietzsche identifies Theognis as the authentic voice of the Megarian nobility, whose poetry distinguishes the noble (*esthlos*) from the base (*kakos*) along lines of birth and character rather than moral desert. The word Theognis uses for the noble man, ἐσθλός, originally signified one who "is" — who possesses genuine, substantial being — in contrast to the mere appearance or deficiency of the commoner. (005-001)

The broader passage in Essay 1, §5 situates Theognis within [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s argument that the language of morality originated as a language of rank. The Greek terms ἀγαθός (*agathos*, good) and κακός (*kakos*, bad) originally distinguished the well-born warrior-aristocrat from the low-born commoner. Theognis's elegiac verses — admonishing a young nobleman named Cyrnus to associate only with the *agathoi* and shun the *kakoi* — provide [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] with primary textual evidence for this thesis. The moralization of these terms (where "good" becomes equivalent to "altruistic" and "bad" to "selfish") is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the historical achievement of the slave revolt in morals, which Theognis's poetry predates and thus helps to illuminate by contrast.
