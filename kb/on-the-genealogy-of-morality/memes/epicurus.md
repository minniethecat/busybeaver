# Epicurus

## Overview

Epicurus (341–270 BC) was an ancient Greek philosopher whose ethical hedonism identified the absence of pain (*ataraxia*) as the highest good. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] references Epicurus in the Third Essay of the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]* as p[[artist|art]] of his analysis of how [[philosopher|philosophers]] have conceived of happiness, suffering, and the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]].

## Role in the Genealogy

Epicurus appears in the Third Essay, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] examines the relationship between suffering and the various consolations that religions and philosophies have offered. Epicurus's [[ideals|ideal]] of painlessness serves as an early philosophical [[artist|art]]iculation of the desire to escape suffering — a desire that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees as underlying all ascetic and pessimistic systems.

> ...the painless state which Epicurus praised as the highest good and as the state of the gods... (047-001)

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### The Ideal of Painlessness

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] discusses Epicurus in the context of [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]'s aesthetics. The Epicurean [[ideals|ideal]] of painlessness represents, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], one variant of the philosophical tendency to value freedom from disturbance over the active engagement with life. This "negative" conception of happiness — defined by what is absent rather than what is present — becomes a target of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s broader critique of life-denying philosophies.

> The hypnotic sensation of nothingness, the peace of deepest sleep, anaesthesia in short — that is what passes with the sufferers and the absolutely depressed for, forsooth, their supreme good... (058-003)

### Epicurus and the Ascetic Ideal

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] situates Epicurus within a genealogy of philosophical responses to suffering. The Epicurean retreat from pain, while not explicitly ascetic in the world-denying sense, shares with the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] a fundamental orientation: the preference for the diminishment of experience over its intensification. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], this is a symptom of [[health|physiological]] depression disguised as philosophical wisdom.

## Key Passages

> ...the painless state which Epicurus praised as the highest good and as the state of the gods; his gods had no concern either with the world or with the suppliant... (047-001)

> The hypnotic sensation of nothingness, the peace of deepest sleep, anaesthesia in short — that is what passes with the sufferers and the absolutely depressed for, forsooth, their supreme good, their value of values... (058-003)
