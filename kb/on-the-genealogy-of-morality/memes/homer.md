## Overview

Homer, the legendary ancient Greek poet to whom the _Iliad_ and _Odyssey_ are attributed, serves as a central cultural reference in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s _On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality_.

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] treats Homer not merely as a literary figure but as the foundational voice of the [[noble|aristocratic]] value system—what he calls "[[master-morality|master morality]]." The Homeric world, with its warrior-heroes, its gods who look down upon human struggles with detached amusement, and its poetry of power, embodies everything that the later Judeo-Christian "slave revolt in morality" would overturn.

## Role in the Genealogy

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] references Homer across all three Essays of the _[[genealogy|Genealogy]]_, with p[[artist|art]]icular density in Essay 1 (on "Good and Bad" vs. "[[good-and-evil|Good and Evil]]") and Essay 3 (on the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]). In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogical scheme, Homer stands at the origin point of Western morality: the pre-Socratic, pre-[[plato|Platonic]] world where "good" meant noble, powerful, and life-affirming.

In Essay 1, Homer's heroes—alongside the "Scandinavian Vikings" and "Japanese [[aristocracy|nobility]]"—exemplify the original meaning of "good" as a self-designation of the powerful. The Homeric epithet "sweeter than honey" (013-011) captures the spontaneous self-affirmation of the noble soul, who does not need to compare himself to inferiors to feel good.

In Essay 3, the opposition between [[Plato]] and Homer becomes [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s shorthand for the entire history of Western philosophy: "[[plato|Plato]] _versus_ Homer, that is the complete, the true antagonism" (066-001). [[plato|Plato]] represents the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s triumph—the philosopher who banishes the poets, who subordinates the body to the soul, appearance to reality.

Homer represents the pre-philosophical affirmation of life in its fullness.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s engagement with Homer is profoundly affirmative. Unlike his treatment of [[Socrates]], [[Plato]], or [[Christianity]], [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] scarcely criticizes Homer. Instead, Homer functions as a positive counter-example:

1. **Homeric gods as aesthetic spectators**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] admires the Homeric conception of [[gods|the gods]] as witnesses rather than judges. In Essay 2, he notes that Homer "makes his gods look down upon the destinies of men" not with moral outrage but with aesthetic appreciation—they enjoy the spectacle of human struggle without condemning it (023-002).

2. **Homeric poetry as [[noble|aristocratic]] [[artist|art]]**: Against the modern democratic prejudice that [[artist|art]] arises from "the people," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] insists: "No Homeric poetry as 'popular' poetry!" (073-001). The Homeric epics, like all great [[artist|art]], are the product of [[noble|aristocratic]] culture, not folk creativity.

3. **Homeric Zeus and the [[artist|art]]ist's metaphysics**: In Essay 3, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes "the Homeric Zeus" as an authority for the [[artist|art]]ist's right to "make things real" through creative will (039-001). This anticipates [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own concept of the [[will-to-power]] as world-making activity.

4. **The aesthetic justification of existence**: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] suggests that Homer "would not have created an Achilles" if he had been a moralist rather than an [[artist|art]]ist (045-001). The ability to create powerful, even terrifying figures without moral judgment is the mark of the [[artist|art]]ist's affirmation of life.

## Key Passages

> ...and Japanese nobility, the Homeric heroes, the Scandinavian Vikings—all of them are, in this respect, creatures of the same substance. (010-004)

> ("sweeter than honey," did Homer call it?), but the victory of the "good" over the "bad" was always already decided... (013-011)

> I think, was the mood with which Homer makes his gods look down upon the destinies of men—as a sublime spectacle, not as a moral tribunal. (023-002)

> The artist's metaphysics: the Homeric Zeus makes things real by naming them, by conceiving them, by willing them into existence. (039-001)

> Homer would not have created an Achilles—nor Goethe a [[Faust]]—if they had been driven by moral indignation. (045-001)

> Plato _versus_ Homer, that is the complete, the true antagonism—the "beyond" against the "here," the ascetic ideal against the tragic affirmation. (066-001)

> The notion that great art emerges from "the people" is refuted! No Homeric poetry as "popular" poetry! (073-001)
