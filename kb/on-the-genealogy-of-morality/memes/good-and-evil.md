## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy, **"good and evil"** (*gut und böse*) is not a timeless moral distinction but the product of a specific historical revaluation — the "slave revolt in morality." The earlier [[noble|aristocratic]] value-pair "good and bad" (*gut und schlecht*) distinguished noble from base. The [[slave-morality|slave morality]] inverted this: what the nobles called "good" (power, pride, strength) became "evil," and what they [[contempt|scorn]]ed (humility, pity, weakness) became "good."

> It was the Jews who, in opposition to the aristocratic equation (good = noble = powerful = beautiful = happy = loved by the gods), dared with a terrifying logic to suggest the contrary equation and to hold it fast with the tenacity of a boundless hatred — namely: "the wretched are alone the good; the poor, the weak, the lowly are alone the good; the suffering, the needy, the sick, the loathsome are the only pious ones, the only blessed, for them alone is there salvation — you, on the other hand, you the noble, the powerful, you are to all eternity the evil, the horrible, the covetous, the insatiable, the godless; eternally also shall you be the unblessed, the cursed, the damned!" (007-001)

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Aristocratic Origin of "Good"

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues that "good" originally had no moral content. It was a designation the noble and powerful applied to *themselves* — a mark of social distance, not ethical evaluation:

> The pathos of nobility and distance, the prolonged and domineering fundamental total feeling on the part of a higher ruling order in relation to a lower order, to a "below" — *that* is the origin of the opposition "good" and "bad." (010-002)

The German word *schlecht* (bad) is etymologically related to *schlicht* (plain, simple) — it originally designated the common man as opposed to the noble, with no moral implication.

### The Slave Revolt in Morality

The decisive event is the "slave revolt in morality" (*Sklavenaufstand in der Moral*), which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] attributes to the [[jews|Jews]] and their Christian heirs. Unable to act directly against their oppressors, the weak enact a "[[revenge|spiritual revenge]]":

> The slave revolt in morality begins when *ressentiment* itself becomes creative and gives birth to values: the ressentiment of beings denied the true reaction, that of the deed, who compensate themselves with an imaginary revenge. (010-002)

This revaluation transforms the noble [[virtue|virtues]] — strength, pride, self-assertion — into vices ("evil"), while elevating the slave's condition — humility, suffering, powerlessness — into [[virtue|virtues]] ("good").

### "Good and Evil" vs. "Good and Bad"

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] insists on the distinction between two fundamentally different value systems:

> "Good and bad," "good and evil," have fought a dreadful, thousand-year struggle on earth... The symbol of this fight, written in a script that has remained legible through all human history up to the present, is called "Rome against Judaea, Judaea against Rome." (015-001)

The [[noble|aristocratic]] "good/bad" evaluates actions and character from within — the noble affirms himself and calls "bad" what is unlike him. The slave "good/evil" evaluates from without — it first identifies the "evil" other (the powerful), then defines itself as "good" by opposition. This reactive origin is the genealogy's key diagnostic.

### The Invention of the "Evil Enemy"

Central to the [[slave-morality|slave morality]] is the invention of the "[[enemy|evil enemy]]" (*der böse Feind*):

> He has conceived "the evil enemy," the "evil one," and this is his basic concept, from which he derives as its opposite and after-image a "good one" — himself! (010-004)

This projection of evil onto the other is the psychological mechanism of [[ressentiment]]. The slave needs an [[enemy|evil enemy]] to make sense of his own suffering — and this need gives birth to the moral interpretation of the world.

## Key Passages

> While the aristocratic man lives in trust and openness with himself, the man of ressentiment is neither sincere, nor naive, nor honest and frank with himself. His soul *squints*; his spirit loves hiding places, secret paths, and back doors. (010-004)

> Supposing that "beyond good and evil" there are goddesses who can grant every favor, let the philosophers of the future try if they can obtain a little leave from this compulsion to moral evaluation. (011-001)

> On the other hand, what a delight there is in the very thought of a power which could say "beyond good and evil," at any rate of a power before which one might speak out. (012-001)
