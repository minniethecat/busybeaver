# Rome

## Overview

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, **Rome** functions not merely as a historical empire but as a *symbol* — the ultimate embodiment of the [[Master Morality|aristocratic]] value equation: good = noble = powerful = Roman. The conflict between Rome and [[Jews|Judaea]] is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the master narrative of Western moral history — a two-thousand-year war whose outcome defines everything that [[europe|European civilization]] has become.

## Nietzsche's Characterization

### Rome as the Archetype of Noble Values

In the etymological analysis of Essay 1, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] uses Rome as the definitive example of how "good" originally meant "noble." The Latin *bonus* traces back to *duonus* — the warrior, the man of strife. To be good in ancient Rome was to be powerful, to be a master, to impose one's will.

> One sees what in ancient Rome "the good" meant for a man. The Roman aristocrat called himself *vir bonus* — the good man — and no one doubted that this meant: the brave man, the capable man, the man who knows how to command. (005-001)

### Rome versus Judaea

The conflict between Rome and Judaea is, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s view, not a clash of empires but a clash of *value systems*. Rome represents the [[Master Morality|aristocratic]] mode: spontaneous self-affirmation, the equation of power with value, the frank acceptance of hierarchy. Judaea represents the [[slave-morality|slave]] mode: reactive negation, the transvaluation of weakness into virtue, the moralization of impotence.

> Rome against Judaea, Judaea against Rome: there has hitherto been no greater event than *this* war, *this* formulation of the problem, *this* contradiction of mortal enemies. (015-001)

This is not merely a historical observation — it is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s diagnosis of the fundamental tension that has structured the entire development of Western civilization.

### The Provisional Victory of Judaea

The genealogical question is not whether Rome fell — it did — but *who won*. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s answer is characteristically unsettling: Judaea won. The slave revolt in morals triumphed over the classical [[ideals|ideal]], and this triumph was so complete that we no longer recognize it *as* a triumph — we take Christian-moral values to be simply "values" rather than the historical product of a specific [[ressentiment]]-driven revaluation.

> Which of them has been provisionally victorious, Rome or Judaea? But there is not a shadow of doubt: just consider to whom one bows today in Rome itself as to the embodiment of the *highest* values. (015-002)

The irony is bitter: in Rome itself — the physical city — people now bow to the representative of the very value system that destroyed Roman civilization. The victory of Judaea is inscribed in the geography of the Eternal City.

## Role in Moral History

Rome occupies a unique position in the genealogical narrative because it is both the *highest expression* of the [[noble|aristocratic]] value system and the *site of that system's defeat*. Rome's fall is not a military event but a moral one — it is the moment when the [[slave-morality|slave revolt in morals]] achieved its definitive historical victory, a victory so complete that the victors came to be seen as the universal standard of value.

This makes Rome the pivot point of the entire *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*. Everything before Rome is the development of [[noble|aristocratic]] values; everything after Rome is the triumph and universalization of slave values. To understand Rome is to understand what was lost — and what might, perhaps, be recovered.
