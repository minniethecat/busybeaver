# Charles Darwin

## Overview

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) was an English naturalist whose theory of evolution by natural selection transformed modern biology and had profound repercussions across philosophy, ethics, and the human sciences. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Darwin appears indirectly but significantly in the Preface, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] critiques the "Darwinian" influence on [[paul-ree|Dr. Paul Rée]]'s moral philosophy. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s objection is not to Darwin's biology per se but to the application of Darwinian categories to morality — a move that, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, substitutes a benign English fable about adaptation for the violent, bloody reality of how moral values actually emerged. The "Darwinian beast" that [[paul-ree|Rée]] imported into his philosophy "shakes hands politely" with the modern demure weakling; this is precisely the sentimentalized picture of human nature that genealogy aims to correct.

> He had read Darwin: — and so in his philosophy the Darwinian beast and that pink of modernity, the demure weakling and dilettante, who "bites no longer," shake hands politely. (P09-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Darwin enters the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] through the figure of [[paul-ree|Dr. Paul Rée]], whose 1877 book *The Origin of the Moral Sensations* was an early attempt to give morality a naturalistic, evolutionary explanation. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], who was personally close to [[paul-ree|Rée]] during the period of the book's composition, initially admired [[paul-ree|Rée]]'s project but came to see its Darwinian framework as an obstacle to genuine genealogical inquiry. In the Preface, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] explains his break with [[paul-ree|Rée]]: [[paul-ree|Rée]] had "read Darwin," and the Darwinian lens had led him to see in morality a story of gradual adaptation and utility, rather than the history of conquest, violence, and the internalization of [[cruelty]] that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy reveals.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### The Darwinian Beast and Modern Comfort

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of Darwin in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] is compressed into a single devastating image. The "Darwinian beast" — humanity understood as the product of natural selection, shaped by the pressures of survival and adaptation — is made to "shake hands politely" with the "demure weakling and dilettante, who 'bites no longer'" (P09-001). The image is satirical: Darwinism, in [[paul-ree|Rée]]'s hands, becomes a story of progressive domestication in which humanity's violent origins are smoothed over into gentility. The beast becomes a gentleman; adaptation becomes moral progress. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy offers a different picture: morality emerged not from adaptation but from conquest, not from utility but from the violent imposition of form on formless human material. The "blond beast" of the First Essay is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s counter-image to the Darwinian beast — not a creature adapting to its environment but a predator imposing itself upon it.

### English Moral Psychology and Its Limits

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of Darwin is p[[artist|art]] of a broader critique of English moral psychology — the tradition running from Hobbes through Hume, Smith, and the Utilitarians to [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] and Darwin. What unites this tradition, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], is its commitment to explaining morality in terms of utility, sympathy, and adaptation — in short, in terms that make morality appear *reasonable*. The opening sentence of the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] targets precisely this tradition: "We are unknown to ourselves, we men of knowledge" — the English psychologists, for all their empiricism, have never truly looked at the *origins* of morality because they have assumed that those origins must be something they could respect. Darwin's contribution is the most powerful version of this error because it appears to have the authority of science.

> Other colours, of course, rise immediately to one's mind as being a hundred times more potent than blue for a genealogy of morals: — for instance, grey, by which I mean authentic facts capable of definite proof and having actually existed. (P09-001)

### The Genealogical Method vs. Evolutionary Method

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s contrast between "blue" (the English-Darwinian sky) and "grey" (the genealogical document) is methodological. Darwinian evolutionary explanation works with abstract mechanisms — selection, adaptation, fitness — that operate over geological time and are inferred rather than documented. [[genealogy|Genealogy]] works with the "grey" of "authentic facts capable of definite proof and having actually existed" — legal codes, [[punishment]] practices, religious rituals, linguistic evidence. The genealogist is closer to the historian and the philologist than to the biologist. Darwin explains how species change; [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] explains how values change. The methods are distinct, and confusing them — as [[paul-ree|Rée]] did — produces a philosophy that is neither good science nor good history.

### Rée, Darwin, and the Break with Nietzsche's Past

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s mention of Darwin in the Preface is also autobiographical. [[paul-ree|Rée]] was a close friend during [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s middle period, and *The Origin of the Moral Sensations* influenced [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own *Human, All Too Human*. The [[genealogy|Genealogy]] marks [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s decisive break with this earlier, more positivist phase. "I had no doubt that from the very nature of his questions he would be compelled to have recourse to a truer method" (P09-001) — but [[paul-ree|Rée]] did not. Darwin, in this narrative, is the intellectual obstacle that prevented [[paul-ree|Rée]] from making the genealogical turn. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique of Darwin is thus also a critique of his own earlier self, the [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] who once believed that morality could be explained by the same methods that explained the natural world.
