# Baruch Spinoza

## Overview

Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677) was a Dutch-Jewish rationalist philosopher whose ethics, metaphysics, and critique of religion made him one of the most radical thinkers of the early modern period. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Spinoza appears as a significant but understated presence — a philosophical predecessor whom [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] treats with unusual respect. Unlike [[plato|Plato]] or [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], Spinoza is not a target of sustained polemic but is instead invoked as an ally in the project of exposing the hidden mechanisms of morality. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s engagement with Spinoza in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] centers on three themes: Spinoza's "interested malice" (*sympathia malevolens*), his intellectual courage in the face of suffering, and his philosophical honesty — a rare commodity in the history of moral philosophy.

> I need only mention Plato, Spinoza, La Rochefoucauld, and Kant — four minds as mutually different as possible, but united on one point: their contempt of pity. (P07-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Spinoza's most important appearance in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] is in the Second Essay, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] draws on Spinoza's concept of *sympathia malevolens* — the pleasure taken in another's misfortune — to support his argument about the centrality of [[cruelty]] in human psychology. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] treats Spinoza not as an authority to be cited uncritically but as a predecessor whose psychological insights, when pushed further, lead toward the [[genealogy|genealogical method]]. Spinoza also appears in the list of great [[philosopher|philosophers]] who never married — a biographical detail [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] uses to illustrate the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s grip on the philosophical life.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### Sympathia Malevolens — The Pleasure in Cruelty

In the Second Essay, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Spinoza's phrase "sympathia malevolens" (which [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] translates as "interested malice") to name the phenomenon he is analyzing: the human capacity to derive pleasure from the suffering of others. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], this is not a perversion but a fundamental psychological fact, rooted in the [[will-to-power|will to power]].

> "Interested malice" (or, to use Spinoza's expression, the *sympathia malevolens*). (022-001)

Spinoza's recognition of this phenomenon — his willingness to name it without moralizing — earns [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s respect. Where [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] moralizes [[cruelty]] out of existence (the categorical imperative), Spinoza acknowledges it as a fact of human psychology to be understood rather than condemned. In this, Spinoza anticipates the [[genealogy|genealogical method]].

### Contempt of Pity

In the Preface, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] groups Spinoza with [[plato|Plato]], [[la-rochefoucauld|La Rochefoucauld]], and [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] as one of the "four minds" united in their contempt of pity. This alignment is significant: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] positions Spinoza within a subterranean philosophical tradition that has always suspected pity of being something other than a pure moral virtue — a tradition that the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] aims to bring to full consciousness.

> Four minds as mutually different as possible, but united on one point: their contempt of pity. (P07-001)

### Causa Sui and Intellectual Courage

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] references Spinoza's concept of *causa sui* (self-caused being) in the context of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] and the human relationship to suffering. Spinoza's willingness to confront the problem of suffering without recourse to supernatural consolation — his insistence that understanding the causes of suffering is the path to overcoming it — represents for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] a model of philosophical health.

> Not far enough from the consciousness of Spinoza (to the disgust of his commentators). (031-001)

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s parenthetical remark — "to the disgust of his commentators" — signals his appreciation for Spinoza's intellectual courage: the willingness to think thoughts that polite philosophical opinion finds repugnant.

### The Unmarried Philosopher

Like [[plato|Plato]], [[rene-descartes|Descartes]], [[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz|Leibniz]], and [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], Spinoza never married. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis, this is not a biographical accident but a symptom of philosophy's deep entanglement with the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]. The philosopher's celibacy is the body's tribute to the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] who lurks within every systematic thinker.

> Heracleitus, Plato, Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz, Kant, Schopenhauer — they were not married. (048-001)
