## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the concept of the **enemy** is fundamentally dual: there is the enemy as conceived by [[Master Morality|master morality]] — an object of respect, a worthy opponent whose defeat is a source of pride — and the enemy as conceived by [[Slave Morality|slave morality]] — the "evil enemy," a projection of [[Ressentiment|ressentiment]] onto which all negative qualities are displaced.

This duality is central to [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy because the transformation of the enemy-concept is one of the primary mechanisms through which the slave revolt in morals was achieved. The noble says "my enemy is strong and I respect him"; the slave says "my enemy is evil and I hate him." The history of Western morality is, in p[[artist|art]], the triumph of the second conception over the first.

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Noble Enemy

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s description of the noble relationship to the enemy appears in Essay 1, section 10, where he describes the "blond beast" at the he[[artist|art]] of all noble races. The noble type "regains its innocence" through its encounters with enemies — not by forgiving them, but by respecting them as equals in the struggle for power.

For the noble, the enemy is not an object of moral condemnation but a natural feature of the competitive landscape. The noble "returns from an orgy of murder, incendiarism, violation, and torture" (010-002) not with [[guilt]] but with the satisfaction of having discharged natural instincts against a worthy opponent.

### The Slave's Evil Enemy

Against the noble conception, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sets the slave's transformation of the enemy into an object of moral hatred. "The 'barbarian,' of the 'evil enemy,' perhaps of the 'Goth' and the 'Vandal'" (010-004) — the [[slave-morality|slave morality]] takes the noble's enemy and reinterprets him as morally evil.

This is one of the key operations of [[ressentiment]]: the inability to act against one's oppressor is transformed into a moral condemnation of the oppressor. The weak do not say "I cannot defeat my enemy"; they say "my enemy is evil, and his power is proof of his evil."

This transformation is described in the famous passage contrasting noble and slave modes of valuation: "What they hate is not their enemy, no, they hate 'unrighteousness,' 'godlessness'" (013-011). The [[slave-morality|slave morality]] abstracts from the concrete enemy to a moral category — not this p[[artist|art]]icular opponent but "evil" as such.

### The Conquered Enemy

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] analyzes the psychological significance of the conquered enemy in Essay 2. The treatment of a "vanquished, [[contempt|disdain]]ed, and conquered enemy" (025-001) is one of the primary sites where [[Cruelty|cruelty]] expresses itself — and, simultaneously, where [[Bad Conscience|bad conscience]] begins to develop.

The "oppression and humiliation of an enemy that has at last been subdued" (029-001) produces a specific form of pleasure — the pleasure of [[cruelty]] — that is central to [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s understanding of pre-moral human psychology. The internalization of this [[cruelty]], when outward enemies are eliminated by the state, is the origin of [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]].

### The Enemy Within

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] extends the enemy concept inward: sexuality, the body, and [[artist|art]] all appear as "enemies" from the perspective of the [[Ascetic Ideal|ascetic ideal]].

"Treated sexuality as a personal enemy (including its tool, woman, the instrument and the 'devil's gate')" (048-001) — the [[ascetic-priest|ascetic priest]] identifies internal drives as enemies and wages war against them. The [[Ascetic Priest|ascetic priest]] himself is "this apparent enemy of life, this denier" (054-001) — but one who, paradoxically, serves life by making [[disease|sickness]] bearable.

"[[plato|Plato]], the greatest enemy of [[artist|art]] which [[europe|Europe]] has produced" (066-001) — the philosophical tradition inherits the [[priests|priest]]ly hostility to sensuous experience, making [[artist|art]] into an enemy of truth.

|-----------|--------|--------|---------|---------|
| [[noble|Noble]] enemy | [[Master Morality]] | Worthy opponent | Respect, aggression | Discharge of instinct, self-affirmation |
| Evil enemy | [[Slave Morality]] | Moralized opponent | Hatred, condemnation | Projection of [[ressentiment]], self-justification |
| Internal enemy | [[Ascetic Ideal]] | Body, sexuality, [[artist|art]] | Denial, war | Management of suffering, will to nothingness |

### The Genealogical Significance

The transformation of the enemy from worthy opponent to evil other is not a theoretical shift but a practical one — it changes how human beings act, punish, and evaluate themselves. The noble enemy can be defeated, respected, even reconciled with; the evil enemy can only be condemned and, ultimately, destroyed.

This transformation is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], one of the central mechanisms through which [[Christianity]] conquered the ancient world. The Roman who regarded the barbarian as a worthy enemy was replaced by the Christian who regarded the pagan as an embodiment of evil.

## Key Passages

> Whether against danger or the enemy, or as those ecstatic bursts of rage, love, reverence, gratitude, and revenge, by which in noble souls all the natural emotions break loose and discharge themselves. (010-002)

> The "barbarian," of the "evil enemy," perhaps of the "Goth" and the "Vandal." (010-004)

> What they hate is not their enemy, no, they hate "unrighteousness," "godlessness." (013-011)

> A vanquished, disdained, and conquered enemy, who is not only deprived of his weapons, but also has had his rights and claims abolished. (025-001)

> The oppresion and humiliation of an enemy that has at last been subdued. (029-001)

> The ascetic priest, this apparent enemy of life, this denier — he actually belongs to the really great *conservative* and *affirmative* forces of life. (054-001)

The duality of the enemy-concept — noble respect versus slave hatred — is one of the clearest demonstrations of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s broader thesis: that morality is not a single thing with a single origin but a field of contestation between incompatible modes of valuation.
