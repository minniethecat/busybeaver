## Definition

In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, the **gods** are a genealogical formation whose origin lies in the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship and the fear of ancestors. Rather than treating gods as primordial objects of worship, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] traces their emergence from concrete socio-economic relations: the living generation's sense of indebtedness to founding ancestors, which grows over time into a sense of divine [[debt|obligation]]. The gods are not discovered but *invented* — and their invention serves specific psychological and social functions within the unfolding of [[Bad Conscience|bad conscience]].

This genealogical account distinguishes [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s approach from both theological affirmations of divine reality and Enlightenment dismissals of religion as mere error. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the gods are real as psychological and cultural formations, and their history reveals the deep structure of human morality.

## Nietzsche's Analysis

### The Origin of Gods in Fear

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most direct statement on the origin of gods appears in Essay 2: "This is the very origin of the gods, that is, an origin from *fear*" (035-001). Fear is not mere superstition — it is the psychological product of the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship extended to the ultimate scale: the living community's [[debt|obligation]] to its founding ancestors.

The ancestor, as the original founder of the community, is experienced as a creditor whose loan can never be fully repaid. As the community prospers, the ancestor's power grows in memory until it becomes divine.

### The Invention of Gods

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is explicit that gods are *invented* — but this invention is not arbitrary. It is "almost compulsory" (023-002) given the psychological logic of the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship.

The invention of gods serves a double function. First, it provides a transcendent witness to human suffering, satisfying the psychological need for suffering to be *seen*. "It is so as to *have a spectator*" (023-003).

Second, the invention of a hierarchy of intermediate beings — "gods and a hierarchy of intermediate beings" (023-002) — creates a structure through which the debtor's [[debt|obligation]] can be mediated and, eventually, internalized as [[guilt]].

### The Moralization of the Gods

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] traces a crucial historical development: the transformation of gods from amoral tribal powers into moral judges. This process culminates in the Christian God, who represents the maximum development of the [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] — a God who sacrifices himself for the [[guilt]] of humanity.

"The conception of gods is not bound to lead necessarily to this degradation of the imagination" (039-001). [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] contrasts the Greek gods, who "subserved the function of keeping off the '[[bad-conscience|bad conscience]]'" (039-005), with the Christian God, who intensifies it.

| Stage | Divine Type | Function | Relation to [[bad-conscience|Bad Conscience]] |
|-------|------------|----------|--------------------------|
| Tribal ancestors | Founding forebears | Source of [[debt|obligation]] | Pre-moral; debt owed to founders |
| Tribal gods | Amoral powers | Witness to suffering | Keeps [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] at bay (Greek model) |
| Universal God | Moral judge | Internalized [[guilt]] | Maximum [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] (Christian model) |

### The Greek Exception

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] presents the Greek relationship to their gods as a crucial counterexample to the Christian development. The Greek gods did not intensify [[guilt]] — they absorbed it.

"Those [[greeks|Greeks]] in fact used their gods for a long time precisely so as to keep the '[[bad-conscience|bad conscience]]' at bay, so as to be able to continue to enjoy their freedom of soul" (039-005). The Greek gods served to justify even transgression: "they took upon themselves the consequences of human wrongdoing."

### Gods and the Bad Conscience

The relationship between gods and [[bad-conscience|bad conscience]] reaches its culmination in [[christianity|Christianity]]. "The maximum god hitherto achieved on e[[artist|art]]h" (047-001) — the Christian God — represents the complete internalization of the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] relationship. Humanity owes a debt it can never repay, and this infinite debt becomes the psychological foundation of Christian [[guilt]].

This maximum god was produced by "forcing down the gods and the traditions of their own" (051-001) — the [[Ascetic Ideal|ascetic ideal]]'s appropriation and transformation of existing divine concepts into instruments of self-denial.

### The Death of Gods

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis also implies the *death* of gods as a genealogical event. If gods are invented to serve specific psychological functions — to witness suffering, to absorb [[guilt]], to provide an object for the [[creditor-debtor-relationship|creditor-debtor]] instinct — then the collapse of those functions (through the [[Will to Truth|will to truth]], the heir of [[christianity|Christian morality]] itself) necessarily entails the death of the God they sustained.

## Key Passages

> This is the very origin of the gods, that is, an origin from *fear*. (035-001)

> It is almost compulsory to invent gods and a hierarchy of intermediate beings... it is so as to *have a spectator*. (023-002)

> The conception of gods is not bound to lead necessarily to this degradation of the imagination... the Greeks, ... accordingly the gods in those times subserved the function of keeping off the "bad conscience". (039-001, 039-005)

> The maximum god hitherto achieved on earth... we are during that moment from the very bottom of our hearts the most supremely good and as the state of the gods. (047-001)

> They were obliged to force down the gods and the traditions of their own. (051-001)

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy of gods is inseparable from his genealogy of [[Debt|debt]], [[Guilt|guilt]], and [[Bad Conscience|bad conscience]]. The gods are not a separate domain of religious history but the ultimate expression of the moral-psychological structures that constitute human social life.
