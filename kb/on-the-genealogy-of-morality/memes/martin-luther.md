# Martin Luther

## Overview

Martin Luther (1483–1546) was a German theologian and the central figure of the [[the-reformation|Protestant Reformation]]. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Luther occupies an ambivalent position: he is at once a figure of German vitality and linguistic power, the liberator of [[europe|Europe]]an consciousness from [[rome|Rome]]'s spiritual monopoly, and yet also a representative of the plebeian [[ressentiment|resentment]] that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies as the driving force of the slave revolt in morals. [[the-reformation|Luther's Reformation]] did not overthrow [[christianity|Christianity]] — it renewed it, transposing the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]] from the monastery into everyday life and from Latin into the vernacular. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], Luther is the supreme example of how even rebellion against authority can serve the very forces it claims to oppose.

> Luther is still our last event; our last great event. (075-001)

## Role in the Genealogy

Luther appears primarily in the Third Essay and the Appendix, where [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] analyzes the Reformation as a historical case study in the dynamics of [[Ressentiment]]. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] frames Luther within a broader argument about the relationship between religious movements, the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]], and the health of peoples. Luther's German e[[artist|art]]hiness — his coarse language, his rejection of ascetic practices, his marriage — makes him a more "honest" Christian than the Roman clergy, but it does not emancipate him from the Christian framework. Instead, his rebellion against [[rome|Rome]] proves to be a strategic renewal of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s hold on the [[europe|Europe]]an soul.

## Nietzsche's Engagement

### The Eloquence of the Plebeian

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] admires Luther's rhetorical power — his German, his e[[artist|art]]hiness, his ability to speak to and for "the people." In a passage contrasting Luther with the "cultured" reformers of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own day, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] presents Luther as a genuine force: coarse, violent, and alive.

> Just recollect Luther, the most "eloquent" and insolent peasant that Germany has had. (063-001)

Luther's linguistic vitality — his "greuliche thier" (hideous beast) rhetoric — represents a kind of health that the anemic "good men" of modernity have lost. But this vitality is deployed in the service of [[ressentiment]], not liberation.

### Luther's Wedding and the Demystification of Asceticism

In a remarkable passage, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] references "Luther's Wedding" as a symbolic event — the moment when the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s grip on [[europe|Europe]]an sexuality was publicly broken. Luther's marriage to Katharina von Bora was a deliberate repudiation of clerical celibacy and, in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s reading, a blow against the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s most intimate stronghold.

> Who knows what chances would have been in store for the idea of "Luther's Wedding"? (043-001)

Yet [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s tone is ambiguous: the "Wedding" liberated [[europe|Europe]]an sexuality, but only to re-channel it within the domesticated, bourgeois form that Protestantism perfected.

### Luther and the Ascetic Ideal

Despite his rebellion against [[rome|Rome]], Luther remains, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], within the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s logic. His rejection of "works" in favor of "[[religion|faith]] alone" is another form of the same self-negation — the devaluation of human agency and the glorification of a transcendent standard. Luther replaced the [[priests|priest]] with the Bible, but the structure of obedience remained.

> All of Reason, which even Luther chose to call *Frau Klüglin* (Mrs. Clever). (050-001)

Luther's anti-intellectualism — his derogatory feminization of Reason as "Frau Klüglin" (the clever dame) — exemplifies the ascetic [[ideals|ideal]]'s strategic hostility to the intellect, even as it paradoxically employs intellectual means.

### "God Help Me! Amen"

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] quotes Luther's famous declaration before the Diet of Worms — "Here I stand; I can do no other. God help me! Amen" — as an exemplar of the Protestant conscience: the individual standing alone against institutional authority, but only by appealing to a higher authority that is equally transcendent and equally life-denying.

> "God help me! Amen" — were Luther's words before the Reichstag at Worms. (069-006)

### Luther and German Identity

In the Appendix, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] places Luther in a lineage of German vital blood — alongside [[otto-von-bismarck|Bismarck]], Niebuhr, [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]], and [[ludwig-van-beethoven|Beethoven]] — suggesting that Luther's true significance is not theological but ethno-cultural: he was an eruption of German vitality that broke the Roman Mediterranean's hold on the Northern soul.

> Blood in Germany — for example, Luther, Niebuhr, Bismarck. (082-001)
