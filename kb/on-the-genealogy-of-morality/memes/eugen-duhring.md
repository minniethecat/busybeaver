# Eugen Dühring

## Overview

Eugen Karl Dühring (1833–1921) was a German philosopher, positivist, and economist whose work gained a significant following among German socialists and intellectuals in the late nineteenth century. Dühring's *Value of Life*, *Course of Philosophy*, and related writings advanced a materialist worldview grounded in what he called "reality philosophy" (*Wirklichkeitsphilosophie*). In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s *[[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morality*, Dühring appears three times — always as a polemical target, yet with a nuance that distinguishes [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s treatment of him from his treatment of other contemporaries. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] despises Dühring's [[ressentiment]]-driven moralism, but he also recognizes in Dühring a kind of intellectual honesty absent from the "contemplative" historians of the modern type.

> The revolting use in all present-day Germany of moral refuse; Dühring, the first apostle of revenge in Germany. (055-002)

## Role in the Genealogy

Dühring enters the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] at three distinct moments, each illuminating a different dimension of [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s critique.

In the Second Essay (§11, 027-001), [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] deploys Dühring as the representative of a theory of justice grounded in *[[ressentiment]]* — the idea that justice originates from the reactive feelings of the injured p[[artist|art]]y. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] quotes Dühring's own self-confession ("the creed of revenge has run through all my works and endeavours like the red thread of [[justice|Justice]]") and inverts his thesis: justice, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, is not born from revenge but is rather the war *against* reactive feelings, waged by the strong to contain the "senseless fury of [[ressentiment|resentment]]."

In the Third Essay (§14, 055-002), Dühring is named in the climactic passage on the "sick" — those [[health|physiological]] distortions whose [[will-to-power|will to power]] takes the form of moral bluster, revenge disguised as righteousness. "That Berlin apostle of revenge, Eugen Dühring, who makes the most disreputable and revolting use in all present-day [[germany|Germany]] of moral refuse" — [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] identifies Dühring as the paramount example of the man of [[ressentiment]], even among his own "kidney, the [[anti-semites|Anti-Semites]]."

In the Third Essay (§26, 067-001), however, a surprising shift occurs. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], railing against the "contemplative" school of modern historians who gaze at the past with "objective" detachment, declares that he would far rather wander with the historical Nihilists through the "gloomiest, grey, cold mist" — and Dühring is named as one such "completely unhistorical and anti-historical" man, a figure whose periods have "grown intoxicated in contemporary [[germany|Germany]]." The preference is backhanded: Dühring is wrong, but he is wrong with conviction; the contemplatives are wrong with style.

## Nietzsche's Critique / Engagement

### Justice as Revenge: The Inversion of Dühring

[[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s most sustained engagement with Dühring occurs in the Second Essay, where Dühring's theory of justice serves as the polemical foil for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s own genealogy of law. Dühring had argued that the "home of [[justice|Justice]] is to be found in the sphere of the reactive feelings" — that law originates in the injured p[[artist|art]]y's claim for redress. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] counters by historicizing the concept: law, he argues, is not born from the reactive feelings but from the *active* powers, who establish legal frameworks as a means of *containing* revenge rather than expressing it. The foundation of law is "the imperative declaration of what in its eyes is to be regarded as just and lawful, and what unjust and unlawful" — an act of strength, not of grievance.

> Everywhere where justice is practised and justice is maintained, it is to be observed that the stronger power, when confronted with the weaker powers which are inferior to it, searches for weapons to put an end to the senseless fury of resentment. (027-001)

The most telling move in this passage is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s quotation of Dühring's own words: "the creed of revenge has run through all my works and endeavours like the red thread of [[justice|Justice]]." [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] lets Dühring convict himself — the "red thread of [[justice|Justice]]" is, in truth, the red thread of revenge. [[genealogy|Genealogy]] exposes what Dühring's own language reveals.

### The Anti-Semite's Moral Bluster

In the Third Essay, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] groups Dühring with the [[anti-semites|Anti-Semites]] — not as a racial accusation but as a psychological diagnosis. The [[anti-semites|Anti-Semites]], in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s analysis, are exemplary cases of [[ressentiment]]: they require an enemy to sustain their sense of moral superiority, and they deploy the rhetoric of righteousness to mask what is, in reality, a [[will-to-power|will to power]] born of weakness. Dühring, "the paramount moral blusterer that there is to-day," is the most accomplished practitioner of this [[artist|art]]. His moral indignation is not a response to genuine evil but a performance — a "disreputable and revolting use" of the language of morality that [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sees as symptomatic of a culture in decline.

### The Anti-Historical Man: A Backhanded Preference

In a passage of the Third Essay (§26) that anticipates [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s later critique of "objective" historiography, Dühring reappears in an unexpected light. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] has been attacking the "contemplative" school of historians — those who "describe" without judging, who ogle the past with "voluptuous" detachment — when he suddenly pivots:

> Nay! The devil take these "contemplative" folk! How much liefer would I wander with those historical Nihilists through the gloomiest, grey, cold mist! — nay, I shall not mind listening (supposing I have to choose) to one who is completely unhistorical and anti-historical (a man, like Dühring for instance...). (067-001)

The logic of this preference reveals something essential about [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s epistemology: it is better to be *wrong* with conviction — to be an anti-historical dogmatist — than to be *right* with the bloodless neutrality of the "objective" scholar. Dühring's "species anarchistica" at least *wills* something; the contemplative wills nothing. In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s hierarchy of values, the former ranks above the latter.

### The Dühring-Case and the Genealogical Method

Dühring's triple appearance in the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] is not accidental but structural. Each citation maps onto a different function of the [[genealogy|genealogical method]]: in the Second Essay, Dühring illustrates how genealogy inverts the claims of moral philosophy (justice is not born from revenge); in the Third Essay (§14), he illustrates how genealogy diagnoses the psychology behind moral postures (righteousness as disguised [[will-to-power|will to power]]); and in the Third Essay (§26), he illustrates how genealogy distinguishes between types of intellectual life (the committed error vs. the detached observation). Dühring is, in this sense, the perfect genealogical specimen: his own self-understanding contradicts what his words and deeds actually reveal, and genealogy's task is precisely to expose this contradiction.
