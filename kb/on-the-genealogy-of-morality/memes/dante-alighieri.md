## Overview

Dante Alighieri (c. 1265–1321) was an Italian poet, philosopher, and political thinker, author of the *Divine Comedy* — one of the supreme achievements of medieval Christian literature.

In Essay 1 of the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] selects Dante for a sharp polemical thrust: the poet's famous inscription over Hell's gate — "Me too made eternal love" — is, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, a lie. What it actually masks is the eternal *hatred* that [[christianity|Christianity]]'s doctrine of eternal [[punishment]] encodes into the structure of its cosmos.

## Role in the Genealogy

Dante appears in Essay 1, §14 (PN 014-001), within [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s extended dissection of the Christian idea of eternal blessedness. The argumentative context is crucial: [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] has been tracing how Christian "love" is a transvaluation of Jewish hatred, how the "kingdom of God" is a fantasy of revenge deferred to eternity, and how the blessedness of Paradise is logically dependent on witnessing the suffering of the damned.

Dante's inscription becomes the literary symbol of this entire theological apparatus. By placing "eternal love" over the entrance to Hell, Dante — "with awe-inspiring ingenuity," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] sardonically notes — gives aesthetic form to what [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] considers [[christianity|Christianity]]'s foundational dishonesty.

## Nietzsche's Critique

**The gate inscription as theological fraud.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s argument is direct: if the Christian Paradise requires eternal [[punishment]] as its counterpoint — if the blessed are made happier by watching the damned suffer (as [[thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]] explicitly states) — then the honest inscription over Paradise's gate would read "Me too made eternal *hate*." The love Dante proclaims is structurally indistinguishable from hatred.

**[[thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]] as corroborating witness.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] buttresses his reading of Dante by citing [[thomas-aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]] directly: "*Beati in regno celesti videbunt pœnas damnatorum, ut beatitudo illis magis complaceat*" — "The blessed in the kingdom of heaven will see the [[punishment]]s of the damned, that their blessedness may be more pleasing to them" (014-001). This is not [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s polemical invention but the explicit doctrine of [[church|the Church]]'s greatest theologian.

**The [[ressentiment]] structure.** What [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] exposes in Dante is the [[ressentiment]] mechanism operating at cosmic scale: the weak, denied revenge in this life, project their [[revenge|vengeance]] into eternity, then call it "love." The poet who gave this structure its most magnificent expression is thus the unwitting architect of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s self-deception.

## Key Passages

> Dante, as it seems to me, made a crass mistake when with awe-inspiring ingenuity he placed that inscription over the gate of his hell, "Me too made eternal love": at any rate the following inscription would have a much better right to stand over the gate of the Christian Paradise and its "eternal blessedness" — "Me too made eternal *hate*" — granted of course that a truth may rightly stand over the gate to a lie! (014-001)

> *Beati in regno celesti* ... *videbunt pœnas damnatorum, ut beatitudo illis magis complaceat.* — Thomas Aquinas, quoted in (014-001)

> For what is the blessedness of that Paradise? Possibly we could quickly surmise it; but it is better that it should be explicitly attested by an authority who in such matters is not to be disparaged, Thomas of Aquinas, the great teacher and saint. (014-001)
