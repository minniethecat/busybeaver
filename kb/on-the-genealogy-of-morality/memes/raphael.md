## Overview

Raphael (Raffaello Sanzio da Urbino, 1483–1520) was an Italian High [[renaissance|Renaissance]] painter and architect, celebrated for his harmonious compositions and serene Madonnas. Together with [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]] and [[leonardo-da-vinci|Leonardo]], he forms the traditional trinity of great [[aristocracy|masters]] of the period.

In the appendix "Peoples and Countries," [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] invokes Raphael as the counterpoint to [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]]: the [[artist|art]]ist who does not transcend his age but [[religion|faith]]fully glorifies its received values. This contrast serves [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s broader genealogical argument about the relationship between [[artist|art]], culture, and the creation of new values.

## Role in the Genealogy

Raphael appears in Peoples and Countries §1 (072-001), within a sustained comparison between [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]] and Raphael that extends from the immediately preceding discussion of [[renaissance|Renaissance]] honesty (071-004). [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s argument moves from the [[renaissance|Renaissance]] attempt at intellectual honesty to the specific case of [[artist|artistic creation]], where the question becomes: does the [[artist|art]]ist merely reflect the values of his time, or does he forge new ones?

The passage positions Raphael as an [[artist|art]]ist of the second type — one who "truly and modestly glorified only the values handed down to him." In [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s typology, Raphael represents [[artist|art]]istic fidelity to tradition rather than the law-giving, value-creating impulse he attributes to [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]].

## Nietzsche's Critique

**The Christo-Raphaelian [[ideals|ideal]].** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] coins the compound "Christo-Raphaelian" to designate the fusion of Christian piety with [[renaissance|Renaissance]] harmony that Raphael perfected. This [[ideals|ideal]], [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] argues, is the one [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]] had to overcome — "through all the Christian clouds and prejudices of his time" — in order to glimpse a "nobler" culture beyond [[christianity|Christianity]].

**Modesty as limitation.** [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s description of Raphael as "modest" is not a compliment but a diagnosis. Raphael's modesty is the sign of an [[artist|art]]ist who did not "carry within himself any inquiring, yearning instincts" — who accepted the framework of Christian value rather than questioning or surpassing it. In the vocabulary of the *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*, Raphael lacks the active, aggressive, self-overcoming impulse that defines the highest type of creative spirit.

**The law-giver contrast.** [[michael-angelo|Michelangelo]] is presented as "the problem of the law-giver of new values," the "conqueror made perfect." Raphael, by implication, is what the law-giver must overcome — the seduction of beautiful accommodation to existing norms. The contrast between the two [[renaissance|Renaissance]] [[aristocracy|masters]] thus replays, in the aesthetic domain, the fundamental opposition that structures the entire *[[genealogy|Genealogy]]*: active versus reactive, value-creating versus value-inheriting.

## Key Passages

> I rate Michael Angelo higher than Raphael, because, through all the Christian clouds and prejudices of his time, he saw the ideal of a culture _nobler_ than the Christo-Raphaelian: whilst Raphael truly and modestly glorified only the values handed down to him, and did not carry within himself any inquiring, yearning instincts. (072-001)

> Michael Angelo, on the other hand, saw and felt the problem of the law-giver of new values: the problem of the conqueror made perfect, who first had to subdue the "hero within himself," the man exalted to his highest pedestal, master even of his pity, who mercilessly shatters and annihilates everything that does not bear his own stamp, shining in Olympian divinity. (072-001)

> Attempts towards honesty at the Renaissance, for example: always for the benefit of the arts. Michael Angelo's conception of God as the "Tyrant of the World" was an honest one. (071-004)
