## Overview

The Accademia di Belle Arti (now the Gallerie dell'Accademia) is [[venice|Venice]]'s preeminent art museum, housed in the former Santa Maria della Carità complex on the south bank of [[canal-grande|the Grand Canal]]. Its collection is the finest assembly of Venetian painting in the world, spanning from the Byzantine-influenced fourteenth century through the Renaissance to the eighteenth century.

## Elena and Insarov's Visit

During their day of [[happiness]] in [[venice|Venice]], [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] and [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] visited the Accademia between their gondola ride along [[canal-grande|the Grand Canal]] and their evening at the [[la-traviata-performance-in-venice|opera]]. The visit is described as one of unselfconscious delight — neither connoisseurs nor dilettantes, they simply en[[happiness|joy]]ed themselves (033-021):

> Elena was afraid of the chill of the churches for Insarov; but she remembered the Accademia di Belle Arti, and told the gondolier to go towards it. They quickly walked through all the rooms of that little museum. Being neither connoisseurs nor dilettantes, they did not stop before every picture; they put no constraint on themselves; a spirit of lighthearted gaiety came over them. Everything seemed suddenly very entertaining.
>
> (033-021)

They laughed at Tintoretto's *St. Mark Freeing the Slave* ([[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] "laughed till she cried over the St. Mark of Tintoretto, skipping down from the sky like a frog into the water") and admired the figures in Titian's *Assumption of the Virgin*, particularly the Madonna — "a splendid, powerful woman, calmly and majestically making her way towards the bosom of God the Father." They also appreciated "the austere and reverent painting of the elder Cima da Conegliano."

The visit concludes with them laughing at everything — the English tourists with their "long rabbit-like teeth," the gondolier, an old-clothes seller — and finally at each other, in a moment of pure, shared [[happiness|joy]].

This mirthful mood carried over into the evening:

> The mirthful mood which had come upon them in the Accademia di Belle Arti had not yet passed off.
>
> (033-022)

> No one who has not seen Venice in April knows all the unutterable fascinations of that magic town. The softness and mildness of spring harmonise with Venice, just as the glaring sun of summer suits the magnificence of Genoa, and as the gold and purple of autumn suits the grand antiquity of Rome.
>
> (033-020)

## The Collection

Among the works specifically mentioned in the novel:
- **Tintoretto, *St. Mark Freeing the Slave*** — depicts St. Mark descending from the sky to rescue a tortured slave
- **Titian, *Assumption of the Virgin*** (**Assunta**) — the celebrated altarpiece with the Madonna ascending toward God the Father
- **Cima da Conegliano** — an unspecified work of "austere and reverent" character by the elder Venetian master

## Significance in the Novel

The Accademia visit is the symbolic heart of [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] and [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s day of [[happiness]] — their last before the emotional crisis of the opera and [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s final illness. Within these museum rooms, the weight of politics, illness, and [[fate|destiny]] is temporarily lifted. Their irreverent laughter — at great art, at tourists, at each other — represents the novel's most sustained moment of pure, unburdened [[happiness|joy]]. That this [[happiness|joy]] takes place in a museum, among the masterpieces of a civilization that [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] will soon leave forever, gives it a particular poignancy.
