# Annushka

Annushka is a young maid in the [[Stahov-Household|Stahov house]]hold in Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve*. She appears only in passing — a figure of the servant class whose brief presence in the narrative contributes to the texture of the domestic world that [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] will ultimately reject.

## Appearances

Annushka is first mentioned in Chapter V, when [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]], in the midst of emotional turmoil, "stopped short, and cried 'Annushka!'" (005-045). "The girl turned round quickly" — the detail suggests youth, alertness, and the reflexive obedience of a servant summoned by a social superior.

In Chapter IX, [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] mentions Annushka in conversation, only to deflect: "I suppose you want to remind me of Annushka. What of it? I don't deny it" (009-036). The reference suggests some familiarity — perhaps flirtation — between the sculptor and the maid, a dynamic typical of the gentry [[family|household]] in which servants exist in ambiguous proximity to their employers' private lives.

In Chapter XII, [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]], leaving a room in emotional distress, "felt an impulse to" call out to Annushka (012-031) — another fleeting reference that places her in the background of the [[family|household]]'s daily rhythms. In Chapter XX, [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] and Annushka are glimpsed together in passing (020-016).

> Shubin abruptly left the window. "Annushka!" Bersenyev felt an impulse to shout after him, but he restrained himself; Shubin had really been white with emotion. Two minutes later, Bersenyev even caught the sound of sobbing; he got up and opened the window; everything was still, only somewhere in the distance someone—a passing peasant, probably—was humming "The Plain of Mozdok."
>
> (012-031)

In Chapter XX, [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s clay [[art|sculpture]] of Annushka reveals the casual cruelty with which the gentry artist treats his servant-subject:

> The cloth was lifted and Bersenyev saw two heads, modelled side by side and close as though growing together... he recognised in one of them Annushka, in the other Shubin himself. They were, however, rather caricatures than portraits. Annushka was represented as a handsome fat girl with a low forehead, eyes lost in layers of fat.
>
> (020-016)

Annushka never speaks in the novel, never drives a plot development, and remains entirely in the margins. But her presence — like the presence of the other servants, the furniture, the rooms themselves — is part of the Stahov world that Turgenev renders with quiet fidelity before [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] leaves it all behind.

## Narrative and Symbolic Role

Annushka's narrative function extends beyond the incidental. She embodies the servant class that forms the invisible architecture of the [[Stahov-Household|Stahov house]]hold — present in every room, attending to every need, yet never granted interiority or voice. Her four appearances in the novel (Chapters V, IX, XII, XX) are all mediated through the perspective of her social superiors.

The clay [[art|sculpture]] in Chapter XX is particularly revealing. [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s paired heads of himself and Annushka, "modelled side by side and close as though growing together" (020-016), literalize the asymmetrical intimacy of the gentry house: master and servant are inseparable yet unequal, the one free to represent the other as a "handsome fat girl with a low forehead, eyes lost in layers of fat" (020-016).

Her silence across the entire novel is itself a statement. In a narrative filled with characters who debate philosophy, declare [[love]], and pledge themselves to causes, Annushka's voicelessness marks the boundary of whose lives count as fully human in Turgenev's world. She is present at the novel's beginning, present through its middle, and simply absent at its end — [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] leaves for [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]], the [[family|household]] dissolves, and no one remarks on what became of the maid.
