# Nature

Nature is the narrative and symbolic frame of *On the Eve*. The novel opens with two young men lying in the grass on a summer day on the banks of the [[moscow|Moskva]] River at [[kuntsovo|Kuntsovo]], and throughout the work, natural description serves not merely as backdrop but as an expressive language — a way of rendering emotional states that the characters cannot always articulate directly. Turgenev, one of the great landscape writers in [[russia|Russia]]n literature, uses nature as both setting and symbol, a mute presence against which human longing measures itself.

## Definition and Scope

- **General Definition**: The physical world and its phenomena, especially as contrasted with human society and artifice.
- **In the Novel's Context**: Nature functions on multiple levels: as the literal setting of key scenes, as the subject of [[philosophical-debate|philosophical debate]] between [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] and [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]], and as a symbolic register through which Turgenev conveys the inner lives of his characters. The novel's geographical arc — from the green [[russia|Russia]]n countryside to the watery decay of [[venice|Venice]] — traces a descent that is simultaneously physical and existential.
- **Frequency**: Nature imagery is pervasive, with the opening chapters (I–V) especially dense in landscape description and philosophical reflection on the natural world. Nature recedes as the novel's focus shifts to political action and urban [[All-Places|settings]].

## Expression in the Novel

### Through Characters

- **[[andrei-bersenyev|Andrei Bersenyev]]** is nature's contemplative observer. The novel introduces him "gazing meditatively into the distance" (001-001), and he articulates the effect of landscape on the soul: "what a strange feeling nature produces in us" (001-032). For [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]], nature is an object of philosophical wonder — beautiful but mute, complete in itself but offering no guidance for human conduct.

- **[[pavel-shubin|Pavel Shubin]]** responds to [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]]'s nature mysticism with characteristic irreverence. "However much you knock at nature's door, she will never answer you in comprehensible words, because she is dumb" (001-033). For [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]], [[art|the artist]], nature's silence is not mystical but simply evidence that human connection — [[love]] — is the only real answer. Nature inspires art but cannot replace it.

	Shubin's fullest expression of this idea comes in the same exchange:

	> However much you knock at nature's door, she will never answer you in comprehensible words, because she is dumb. She will utter a musical sound, or a moan, like a harp string, but don't expect a song from her. (001-033)

### Through Plot

The novel's opening scene — the [[moscow|Moskva]] River, the lime-tree, the summer heat — establishes a pastoral mood that the subsequent narrative will complicate and ultimately darken. As the story moves from the [[russia|Russia]]n countryside to [[venice|Venice]], the natural setting shifts from expansive vitality to the "damp breath of the lagoon," mirroring [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s physical decline and the novel's movement toward tragedy. The yellow flowers in Chapter I, hanging "still as [[death]]" (001-031), prefigure this arc from the very first pages.

### Through Symbolism

Turgenev's natural descriptions are never merely decorative. When [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] argues that nature "arouses in us a need for [[love]], and is not capable of satisfying it" (001-037), he articulates the novel's central symbolic logic: nature is a language of longing, a visible expression of invisible desires. The [[russia|Russia]]n landscape — expansive, beautiful, indifferent — becomes a metaphor for the condition the characters must transcend: a world of feeling without action, of contemplation without commitment.

## Key Quotations

> "What a strange feeling nature produces in us? Everything in it is so complete." — Andrei Bersenyev (001-032)

> "However much you knock at nature's door, she will never answer you in comprehensible words, because she is dumb." — Pavel Shubin (001-033)

> "Nature is gently driving us to other living embraces, but we don't understand, and expect something from nature herself." — Pavel Shubin (001-037)
