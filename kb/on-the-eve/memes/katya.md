## Identity and Appearance

Katya is a poor beggar-girl living near the Stahov estate, about ten years old when [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] first befriends her. She has "quick black, almost animal eyes," sunburnt hands, a hoarse voice, and ragged clothes (006-003). She wears a wreath of cornflowers at least once, and [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is struck by how everything about her seems "particular and distinguished, almost holy" (006-003).

## Friendship with Elena

At the age of ten, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna Stahov]], already distinguished by her intense com[[love|passion]] for the suffering, secretly befriends Katya. [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] meets her in the garden of the Stahov estate, behind a thick growth of nettles, bringing her "nice things to eat," handkerchiefs, and pennies — though Katya refuses to accept playthings (006-003). The two sit together on the dry earth, and with "a feeling of delicious humility" [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] eats Katya's stale bread and listens to her stories (006-003).

Katya lives with an ill-[[nature]]d aunt who often beats her, and she speaks constantly of running away to live in what she calls "God's full freedom" — words that [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] drinks in with "secret respect and awe" (006-003). Katya also teaches [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] a "half-brutal soldier's song," which [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] later sings at home, alarming her mother [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]] (006-003). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] refuses to reveal where she learned it, feeling "that she would let them tear her to pieces sooner than betray her secret" (006-005).

Through Katya, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] first encounters the world beyond her privileged upbringing. She dreams of cutting herself a hazel stick, putting on a wallet, and running away with Katya to wander the roads — fantasies that mingle terror and rapture (006-003).

## Death

The friendship does not last long. Katya falls sick with fever and dies within a few days (006-005). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is "greatly distressed" and spends sleepless nights for long afterward; the last words of the little beggar-girl ring constantly in her ears, and she fancies that she is being called (006-006). The loss coincides with [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s passage into a solitary adolescence, her youth gliding by "swiftly and noiselessly, like waters running under the snow" (006-007).

## Dream Vision in Venice

Years later, on the eve of [[dmitri-insarov|Dmitri Insarov]]'s [[death]] in [[venice|Venice]], Katya reappears in one of the novel's most haunting passages. As [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] falls asleep in their hotel room on the [[riva-dei-schiavoni|Riva dei Schiavoni]], she has a strange dream: she is travelling in a sledge through boundless plains of snow, and beside her sits "a little creature muffled in an old cloak" — Katya, her poor little friend (034-024). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is seized with terror: "Why, isn't she dead?" she thinks (034-024).

Katya does not answer, only nestles closer in her cloak, freezing. Ahead, through the drifting snow, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] sees high white towers with silvery cupolas — not [[moscow|Moscow]], but the [[solovetsky-monastery|Solovetsky Monastery]], "full of little narrow cells like a beehive" where she feels Dmitri is imprisoned (034-025). As a grey abyss opens before them and the sledge begins to fall, Katya laughs — and then [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s voice calls [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s name from the abyss, waking her to find him [[death|dying]] (034-025)(034-026).

The dream links Katya with the [[All-Concepts|themes]] of innocence, [[death]], and the inescapable pull of fate that runs through the novel. Katya, who first taught [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] about "God's full freedom," returns at the moment when [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] faces the annihilation of everything she has chosen.

## Significance

Katya is a minor character in terms of narrative duration but a major one in psychological weight. She represents [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s earliest experience of [[love]] that crosses class boundaries, her instinctive [[sacrifice|self-sacrifice]], and her longing for a world beyond the confines of the [[stahov-household|Stahov household]]. The friendship prefigures [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s later [[sacrifice|devotion]] to [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] and the [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n cause — in both [[character-relationships|relationships]], [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is drawn to those who suffer and who promise a life of meaningful action. Katya's dream reappearance at the moment of [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[death]] closes a circle: the first great loss of [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s life returns to companion her through the last.
