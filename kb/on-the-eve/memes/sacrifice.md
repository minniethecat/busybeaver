# Sacrifice

In Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve* (1860), [[duty|self-sacrifice]] represents the novel's highest moral value — the willingness to subordinate personal [[happiness]] to a cause or a person beyond oneself. Introduced in the opening [[philosophical-debate|philosophical debate]] between [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] and [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] and culminating in [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s decision to follow the [[death|dying]] [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] toward [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]], the theme of sacrifice provides the novel's ethical architecture.

## Definition and Scope

- **General Definition**: The act of giving up something valued for the sake of something else regarded as more important or worthy; in the novel's idiom, "the [[love]] which is not en[[happiness|joy]]ment, the [[love]] which is [[duty|self-sacrifice]]" (001-052).
- **In the Novel's Context**: Turgenev presents [[duty|self-sacrifice]] as the criterion by which [[love]] is judged. [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s self-assertive [[love|passion]] — "I want to [[love]] for myself" — is contrasted with [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]]'s ideal of [[love|self-sacrificing love]], and the novel's plot tests which vision of [[love]] can sustain a life. [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s trajectory answers the question: she chooses [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] not despite the sacrifice his cause demands, but because of it.
- **Frequency**: Approximately 3 direct mentions, but the concept structures the novel's entire moral argument.

> "Is there nothing higher than happiness?" — Andrei Bersenyev (001-044)

## Expression in the Novel

### Through Characters

The novel's characters are differentiated by their capacity for [[duty|self-sacrifice]]:

- **[[andrei-bersenyev|Andrei Bersenyev]]** is the novel's first spokesman for [[love|self-sacrificing love]]. In the opening chapter, he tells [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]: "the [[love]] which is not en[[happiness|joy]]ment, the [[love]] which is [[duty|self-sacrifice]]" (001-052). [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] articulates the ideal but, crucially, does not embody it — he remains a spectator to the [[love]] story rather than a participant in it.

- **[[dmitri-insarov|Dmitri Insarov]]** embodies [[duty|self-sacrifice]] as a lived reality. His entire existence — orphaned, self-educated, committed to the [[freedom|liberation]] of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] — is an ongoing act of sacrifice. [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s assessment captures this: "hardworking, capable of [[duty|self-sacrifice]] (you see, I am impartial)" (022-034). The parenthetical is telling — [[duty|self-sacrifice]] is presented as a objective moral quality, not a subjective impression.

- **[[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]]** undergoes the novel's central transformation: from a young woman restless with her own unfocused longings to someone capable of sacrificing everything — family, country, safety — for [[love]] and the cause it has become entangled with. Her final decision to continue toward [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] after [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[death]] is the novel's ultimate act of [[duty|self-sacrifice]].

### Through Plot

The novel's plot is structured around a progressive raising of the stakes of sacrifice. The early chapters pose the question theoretically ([[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] vs. [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] on [[love]]); the middle chapters test it practically ([[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s choice of [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]); the final chapters demand its ultimate expression (the [[insarov-journey-to-bulgaria|journey to Bulgaria]], [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[death]], [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s continuation). The novel's famous inconclusiveness — we never learn [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s fate — is itself a formal acknowledgment that the cost of sacrifice cannot be fully narrated.

### Through Symbolism

Turgenev associates sacrifice with the image of the Dalmatian fishermen who "have sacrificed their dredging weights" (033-009) — a minor detail that encapsulates the novel's moral vision. The fishermen give up the tools of their livelihood — their "dredging weights," their lead sinkers — to the sea, in an act of self-depletion that enables their survival. The image is Turgenev's quiet metaphor for the economy of sacrifice: you must lose something essential to gain something greater.

## Connection to Russian Literary Tradition

The theme of [[duty|self-sacrifice]] places *On the Eve* within the deep [[russia|Russia]]n literary and religious tradition of *samootverzhenie* (self-renunciation). Turgenev's treatment is secular — there is no explicitly Christian framework — but the novel draws on the cultural vocabulary of [[russia|Russia]]n Orthodoxy, in which [[duty|self-sacrifice]] is the highest form of [[love]]. The novel's contribution is to translate this religious value into the secular idiom of political commitment, anticipating the cult of revolutionary [[duty|self-sacrifice]] that would dominate [[russia|Russia]]n radical culture in the decades to come.
