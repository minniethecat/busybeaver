# Renditch

A Dalmatian sailor and old acquaintance of [[Dmitri Insarov]] who appears in the novel's final chapters in [[venice|Venice]], promising to arrange passage for [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] and [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] to [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] and becoming the object of [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s fevered anticipation as his health fails.

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full Name**: [[renditch|Renditch]] (surname only given)
- **Identity**: Dalmatian sailor, old acquaintance of [[dmitri-insarov|Dmitri Insarov]]
- **[[family|Family]]**: Not specified
- **Social Circle**: [[Dmitri Insarov]] (old acquaintance), [[Elena Nikolaevna Stahov]] (meets for the first time in [[venice|Venice]])
- **First Appearance**: Chapter 33, mentioned by [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]: "[[renditch|Renditch]] promised to arrange everything" (033-009)

## Role in the Novel

### Introduction

[[renditch|Renditch]] is first mentioned by [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] during his illness in [[venice|Venice]], when he tells [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]: "[[renditch|Renditch]] promised to arrange everything — we shall have no difficulty" (033-009). The name becomes a refrain of [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s delirium: he mutters "[[renditch|Renditch]]!" in his sleep, and on waking his first question is always "[[renditch|Renditch]] has not come?" (034-002).

### Key Plot Arc

The anticipation of [[renditch|Renditch]]'s arrival becomes the novel's central dramatic mechanism in the [[venice|Venice]] chapters. [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]], growing weaker, repeatedly asks whether [[renditch|Renditch]] has come — the sailor's arrival representing the last hope of reaching [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] before [[death]] claims him (034-013, 034-018). When [[renditch|Renditch]] finally does arrive in Chapter 34, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] cries out: "[[renditch|Renditch]]! It's you! Look, for God's sake, he's ill!" (034-032). [[renditch|Renditch]] says nothing and only moves aside as the doctor slips past him (034-033). His arrival, coming moments before [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[death]], is too late. The narrative provides his background in the following chapter: "[[renditch|Renditch]] was a Dalmatian, a sailor, with whom [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] had become acquainted during his wanderings in his own country, and in whom he had the fullest confidence" (035-002).

### Fate

[[renditch|Renditch]] is present at [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[death]]bed. In the novel's final chapter, he stands at the window of the hotel room while [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] lies [[death|dying]], a silent witness to the tragedy he could not prevent (035-001).

## Character and Themes

- **Traits**: Reliable, taciturn, loyal, practical
- **Representing**: The world of action beyond the novel's domestic interiors — [[renditch|Renditch]] is the only character who embodies the practical, maritime, cross-border world [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] belongs to
- **Narrative Function**: Dramatic catalyst and tragic irony — his delayed arrival heightens the tension of [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s illness, and his appearance, too late to help, underscores the novel's theme of missed timing

## Key Quotations

> "Renditch!" muttered Insarov in his sleep. "Renditch has not come?" was his first question on waking. (033-034, 034-002)

> "Renditch!" cried Elena, "it's you! Look, for God's sake, he's ill!" Renditch said nothing and only moved on one side. (034-032, 034-033)

> Renditch was a Dalmatian, a sailor, with whom Insarov had become acquainted during his wanderings in his own country, and in whom he had the fullest confidence. (035-002)
