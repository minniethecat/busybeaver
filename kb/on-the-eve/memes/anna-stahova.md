# Anna Vassilyevna Stahov

Anna Vassilyevna is [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]]'s mother and a representative of the older generation of [[russia|Russia]]n gentry women in Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve*. Kind, sentimental, and fundamentally passive, she embodies the domestic virtues that her daughter will ultimately reject in favor of a life of purpose and action. [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]], her distant relative, describes her with fond exasperation: she is good-hearted but incapable of understanding her daughter's deeper [[nature]].

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full Name**: Anna Vassilyevna Stahov (née [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]])
- **Role**: Wife of [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevich Stahov]], mother of [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]
- **Husband**: Nikolai [[art|Art]]emyevich Stahov — a retired cavalry officer who conducts an open affair with a German widow, humiliating Anna Vassilyevna in her own [[family|household]]
- **Daughter**: [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna Stahov]] — the novel's protagonist, whose restless spirit her mother cannot comprehend
- **Distant Relative**: [[pavel-shubin|Pavel Shubin]] — a young sculptor who lives with the Stahovs and treats Anna Vassilyevna with affectionate irreverence

## Role in the Novel

### Background

Anna Vassilyevna was born Anna Vassilyevna [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] (003-001). She was married at sixteen to [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Stahov]], a young cavalry officer who "hooked" her for her modest fortune (003-002). The marriage was a transaction rather than a union: Stahov soon abandoned his military career for a life of idle self-regard, and Anna Vassilyevna has since endured his infidelities — particularly his long-term affair with a German widow — with quiet, tearful resignation. Her husband's betrayals have wounded but not embittered her; she responds to disappointment with headaches rather than action, with "a wet bandage on her head" rather than protest.

### Narrative Function

Anna Vassilyevna's primary narrative function is as a foil to [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]. Where [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is restless, serious, and driven by moral purpose, her mother is settled, sentimental, and governed by affection. [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] notes the physical resemblance between mother and daughter while acknowledging their fundamental difference: [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] possesses a drive that Anna Vassilyevna never had (001-029). The contrast is Turgenev's way of showing what [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is rebelling against: not cruelty but emptiness, not oppression but purposelessness.

The tragedy of Anna Vassilyevna is not that she is wicked but that she is insufficient. Her kindness is genuine, her [[love]] for [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is sincere — but kindness and [[love]], in Turgenev's moral uni[[poetry|verse]], are not enough. The novel does not condemn her; it simply leaves her behind, as [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] does, as history itself will.

### Fate

Anna Vassilyevna remains in [[russia|Russia]] after [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] leaves with [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]. She belongs to the world [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] abandons — not villainous, merely inadequate for a soul that demands more than domestic comfort. Her final appearance in the novel is one of bewildered grief: she cannot understand why her daughter had to go, and her inability to understand is itself the point.

## Character and Themes

- **Traits**: Kind, sentimental, passive, long-suffering, domestically competent, emotionally dependent (003-001)(002-006)
- **Representing**: The older generation, domestic femininity, the insufficiency of mere kindness, the personal cost of social convention
- **Narrative Function**: Foil to [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]; embodiment of the life [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] rejects; a sympathetic portrait of what the "woman question" meant for an earlier generation

## Key Quotations

> Shubin's description of Elena captures the contrast with Anna Vassilyevna: "She's a wonderful creature... She has a passion of a sort. But it's not the same as her mother's. Anna Vassilyevna is an excellent woman, I respect her from the bottom of my heart — but she never had a passion of that sort" (001-029)

> Anna Vassilyevna's biography in Chapter III establishes her as a woman shaped by forces beyond her control: married at sixteen, betrayed by her husband, sustained by sentiment rather than conviction (003-001, 003-002). Turgenev's tone is not satirical — it is elegiac.

> After Elena's departure, Anna Vassilyevna's grief is the novel's acknowledgment that liberation, however necessary, carries a human cost that cannot be wished away. She represents the world of feeling that Elena must transcend — but the novel does not allow us to forget that this transcendence leaves real people behind.
