# Augustina Christianovna

A German woman living in [[moscow|Moscow]] who becomes the mistress of [[Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov|Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov]], revealing his hypocrisy through her correspondences and his dependence on her, and ultimately fading from his life after [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s [[the-journey-to-venice|departure]].

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full Name**: [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]] (patronymic only known)
- **Identity**: German expatriate woman in [[moscow|Moscow]], mistress of [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Stahov]]
- **[[family|Family]]**: A cousin, Thèche (confidante in correspondence)
- **Social Circle**: [[Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov]] ([[love]]r), the [[moscow|Moscow]] German community
- **First Appearance**: Mentioned in Chapter 1 as the place where Nikolai spends his days (001-025)

## Role in the Novel

### Introduction

[[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]] is introduced indirectly in the novel's opening chapter, when [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] remarks sarcastically that [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]] "sits for whole days together at his [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]]'s" and is "bored to [[death]]" there (001-025). She never appears directly in a scene; her presence is felt entirely through others' comments and Nikolai's behavior.

### Key Plot Arc

Her significance comes from what her correspondence reveals: in letters to her cousin Thèche, she mockingly described Nikolai, and through a "martyred" friend of [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]]'s, these letters found their way back to the [[Stahov-Household|Stahov house]]hold (003-002). [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]] is not as wounded by the infidelity itself as by the "mortification" of [[education|learning]] that [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]] presented the affair to her friend as "a piece of good news," treating Anna's misfortune as a source of amusement (003-003). Nikolai remains "sulky with [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]]" for a time after his plans for [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s marriage are presented to him (015-001). She briefly leaves [[moscow|Moscow]] to visit friends in Revel, during which time Nikolai begins "to want his wife" again (023-001). After [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s [[the-journey-to-venice|departure]], [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]] returns "in sober earnest," but Nikolai, aged and greyer, has already "parted from [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]]" and taken instead to "abusing foreigners of every nationality" (035-025).

### Fate

In the novel's epilogue, Nikolai has ended the affair. [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]], a figure of Nikolai's middle-aged vanity, is discarded when his world collapses around him — [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s [[elenas-elopement|elopement with Insarov]] having shattered his pretensions to patriarchal authority.

## Character and Themes

- **Traits**: Mercenary, mocking, clever, unscrupulous
- **Representing**: Moral hypocrisy of the older generation, the currency of reputation in [[moscow|Moscow]] society
- **Narrative Function**: Offstage antagonist — her existence exposes [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Stahov]]'s moral vacuity without her ever appearing in person. She is less a character than a mirror held up to Nikolai's self-deception.

## Key Quotations

> Nikolai Artemyevitch never suspected that Augustina Christianovna, in letters to her cousin, Thèche, described him in the most unflattering and ridiculous terms. (003-002)

> Her husband's infidelity would not have wounded Anna Vassilyevna so deeply if only Augustina Christianovna had not seen fit to present the whole affair to her friend as a piece of good news. (003-003)

> He has grown fatter and greyer, and has parted from Augustina Christianovna. He has taken now to abusing foreigners of every nationality. (035-025)
