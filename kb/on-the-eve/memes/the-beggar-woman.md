## Identity and Appearance

The beggar-woman is "a little old" woman with a face "yellow and wrinkled now, though once handsome" (018-001). She moves with sighs and groans and speaks in the idiom of [[russia|Russia]]n folk piety, crossing herself and murmuring "Lord Jesus Christ" at the thunder (018-006). She identifies herself as a fortune-teller: "I'm a fortune-teller" (018-011). Her manner blends folk wisdom, religious [[sacrifice|devotion]], and an uncanny perceptiveness that sets her apart from the novel's other minor characters.

## First Encounter in Moscow

The beggar-woman first appears in [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]]'s diary, where [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] records giving her a penny (016-004). The woman asks [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] a single, penetrating question: "Why are you so sorrowful?" (016-004). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]], who "never suspected I looked sorrowful," is startled by the observation — it is the period when she is falling in [[love]] with [[dmitri-insarov|Dmitri Insarov]] but has not yet acknowledged it to herself (016-004).

## The Chapel Scene at Kuntsovo

The beggar-woman's most significant appearance occurs in the chapel near [[kuntsovo|Kuntsovo]] during a thunderstorm (018-001). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]], walking to meet [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]], is caught in the storm and takes shelter in a "little old broken-down chapel that had been built over a disused well" (018-001). The beggar-woman enters, shaking off the rain, and sits down on a ledge near the well (018-001).

When [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] reaches into her pocket for alms and finds no purse, she gives the woman her handkerchief instead (018-003). The beggar-woman blesses her: "God reward you for your goodness!" and jokes that the handkerchief might serve as "a wedding-present to my grandchild when she's married" (018-004) — an unconscious foreshadowing of [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s approaching marriage.

After a peal of thunder, the old woman crosses herself and suddenly recognizes [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] (018-006): "Didn't you give me alms in Christ's name?" (018-006). She then recalls their first meeting: "wasn't it you asked me why I was so sorrowful?" [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] replies (018-008). The woman observes that [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s handkerchief is "wet from tears" and tells her: "you young people, you all have the same sorrow, a terrible woe it is!" (018-009).

## The Prophecy

The beggar-woman then delivers her prophecy — one of the novel's most memorable passages:

> "Ah, my good young lady, you can't deceive an old woman like me. I know what your heart is heavy over; your sorrow's not an uncommon one. Sure, I have been young too, darling. I have been through that trouble too. Yes. And I'll tell you something, for your goodness to me; you've won a good man, not a light of love, you cling to him alone; cling to him stronger than death. If it comes off, it comes off — if not, it's in God's hands."
>
> (018-011)

The prophecy is remarkable for its accuracy: [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] has indeed "won a good man," and the admonition to "cling to him stronger than [[death]]" proves tragically prescient. The phrase "stronger than [[death]]" echoes through the novel's finale, where [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s [[sacrifice|devotion]] persists beyond [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[death]] and she carries his body toward [[zara|Zara]].

The beggar-woman then performs a symbolic ritual: "I'll carry away your sorrow with your handkerchief. I'll carry it away, and it's over" (018-011). She leaves the chapel, and almost immediately afterward, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] sees [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] passing on the road — the meeting that leads to their declaration of [[love]] (018-013).

## Later Recurrence

After the chapel scene, the beggar-woman recurs in [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s mind. In "the first languor of [[happiness]]" after her [[love]] is confirmed, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] remembers the old woman: "She did carry away my sorrow" (019-008). The handkerchief ritual, a piece of folk magic, has proven effective — or at least [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] believes it has.

> A little old beggar-woman came into the chapel, shook herself, said with a curtsy: "Out of the rain, good lady," and with many sighs and groans sat down on a ledge near the well. Elena put her hand into her pocket; the old woman noticed this action and a light came into her face, yellow and wrinkled now, though once handsome. There happened to be no purse in Elena's pocket, but the old woman was still holding out her hand. "I have no money, grannie," said Elena, "but here, take this, it will be of use for something." She gave her her handkerchief. "O-oh, my pretty lady," said the beggar, "what do you give your handkerchief to me for? For a wedding-present to my grandchild when she's married? God reward you for your goodness!"
>
> (018-001)(018-003)(018-004)

## Significance

The beggar-woman belongs to Turgenev's gallery of symbolic folk figures who possess an intuitive wisdom that educated characters lack. Like the peasant characters in *A Sportsman's Sketches*, she speaks with an authority drawn from experience and tradition rather than book-[[education|learning]]. Her prophecy serves as a narrative hinge: immediately after she "carries away" [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s sorrow, [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] appears, and the [[love]] plot moves decisively forward.

The figure also embodies the novel's preoccupation with [[fate]]. The beggar-woman's fatalism — "If it comes off, it comes off — if not, it's in God's hands" — mirrors the novel's larger meditation on whether human beings can shape their [[fate|destiny]] or must submit to forces beyond their control. Her parting blessing, "Good Lord, have mercy on us!" (018-011), resonates with the religious dimension of [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s character and the novel's final, ambiguous movement toward [[sacrifice|self-sacrifice]] and disappearance.
