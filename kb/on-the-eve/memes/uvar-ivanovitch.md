# Uvar Ivanovitch Stahov

Uvar Ivanovitch Stahov is a distant cousin of [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]] who lives with the [[family|Stahov family]] in Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve*. A retired cavalry cornet of about sixty years old, he occupies a peculiar position in the [[family|household]]: always present, rarely speaking, yet somehow serving as a kind of human barometer for the family's internal weather.

## Character

[[Uvar-Ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch]] is introduced in Chapter VIII as a fixture of the Stahov drawing-room: "a certain Uvar Ivanovitch Stahov, a distant cousin of [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]], a retired cornet of sixty years" (008-001). He sits in an easy chair, speaks in a drawling voice ("[[Uvar-Ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch]]'s drawling voice," 008-037), and communicates as much through gesture as through words — his fingers drumming, his eyes opening and closing with ponderous significance.

His defining trait is a kind of monumental passivity. When [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] needles him for an opinion, [[Uvar-Ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch]] can "only look at him and dumbly open his eyes" (008-004). When pressed on whether the devil tempts him, he replies with a single word — "You, of course" (008-047) — that carries the weight of an entire worldview. His minimal responses to [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s provocations form a comic counterpoint to the novel's more earnest [[philosophical-debate|philosophical debate]]s.

Uvar’s characteristic gesture — his fingers drumming or working — conveys his wordless opinions with unmistakable force.

> Uvar Ivanovitch’s fingers began to work. “There, there, my boy, don’t provoke me.”
>
> (008-045)

> Shubin laughed and ran away. "Hi," shouted Uvar Ivanovitch a quarter of an hour later, "you there … a glass of spirits."
>
> (008-050)

## Narrative Function

[[Uvar-Ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch]] functions as a comic chorus — a figure whose immobility and silence comment obliquely on the turbulent romantic and philosophical currents swirling around him. When [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] flees the room, [[Uvar-Ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch]] shouts after him — "Hi" — "a quarter of an hour" later (008-050), a timing that captures the character's essence: he is always just slightly out of sync with the world, a human afterthought.

He belongs to the older generation that [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] will leave behind, but unlike [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]], he inspires affection rather than contempt. He is harmless, comic, and strangely dignified in his refusal to be anything other than what he is: an old man in an easy chair, watching the young people make their mistakes.
