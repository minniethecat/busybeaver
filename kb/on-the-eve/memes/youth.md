# Youth

Youth is a defining thematic condition of Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve* (1860). The novel's protagonist, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]], is twenty years old; her suitors — [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]], [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]], [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] — are all young men at the threshold of their adult lives. Youth in the novel is not merely a chronological stage but a moral category: it is the condition of being unfinished, of having one's character still in formation, of standing at the point where choices become fate.

## Definition and Scope

Youth in *On the Eve* is characterized by intensity rather than innocence. [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is "not a child; she had long been a woman in feeling" (006-007), and the novel treats her seriousness as native to her youth, not an escape from it. The novel's young characters are intellectually and emotionally adult — what distinguishes them is their openness to transformation, a openness that the older generation ([[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]], [[Uvar-Ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch]]) has long since lost.

## Expression in the Novel

### Elena's Coming of Age

[[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s youth is the novel's central narrative material. The opening scene at [[kuntsovo|Kuntsovo]], with [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] and [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] lying on the grass in "the carelessness, conceit, self-admiration... of perfect health and youth" (001-001), establishes a baseline of youthful ease that [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s intensity immediately disrupts. Her youth is restless, searching, dissatisfied — "she would go to [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]], to [[serbia|Serbia]]" (006-007) — in a way that [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s and [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]]'s are not.

### Generational Contrast

The novel measures its young characters against their elders. [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]] "in his youth he had played no games of any kind" (003-002) — a detail that suggests a youth without [[happiness|joy]], leading to an adulthood without substance. [[Uvar-Ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch]], the elderly cornet who "did nothing" and "hardly spoke" (008-001), represents the terminus of a certain kind of [[russia|Russia]]n youth: energy without purpose, settling into torpor.

### Youth's Test

The novel tests whether youthful idealism can survive. [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s trajectory — from restless dreaming to decisive action to grief and renewed commitment — suggests that youth is not a phase to be outgrown but a quality to be tested and refined. [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[death]] cuts short one kind of youth; [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s continuation of his cause extends another.

## Key Quotations

> "The carelessness, conceit, self-admiration... of perfect health and youth" — the narrator describing Shubin and Bersenyev at the novel's opening (001-001)

> "In his youth he had played no games of any kind" — on Nikolai Artemyevitch's sterile early years (003-002)

> "Elena's youth glided by, outwardly uneventful, inwardly full of struggle and thought" — on Elena's formative years (006-007)
