# Freedom

In Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve* (1860), freedom operates on two distinct but intertwined registers: the personal freedom sought by [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]], a young woman trapped in the conventions of gentry [[family|domestic life]], and the political freedom for which [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] dedicates his life — the liberation of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] from Ottoman rule. The novel's achievement is to show how these two freedoms converge in [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s decision to follow [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]], transforming personal desire into political commitment.

## Definition and Scope

- **General Definition**: The power or right to act, speak, or think as one wants without hindrance or restraint; also, the state of not being imprisoned or enslaved.
- **In the Novel's Context**: Turgenev presents freedom as the novel's deepest moral value, but he complicates it by showing that freedom is never merely personal — it implicates others, demands sacrifice, and ultimately requires the subordination of individual desire to collective purpose.
- **Frequency**: Approximately 3 direct mentions, with the concept permeating the novel's entire narrative arc.

## Expression in the Novel

### Through Characters

Freedom is the quality that distinguishes the novel's central characters:

- **[[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]]** longs for freedom from the stifling conventions of her family. The narrator describes her desire to live in "God's full freedom" (006-003) — a phrase that captures the spiritual dimension of her restlessness. [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s freedom is not libertinism but the right to shape her own life according to her own conscience. Her choice of [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] over [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] and [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] is, fundamentally, a choice for a life in which freedom is earned through commitment rather than inherited through class.

- **[[dmitri-insarov|Dmitri Insarov]]** embodies freedom as political purpose. His entire life is directed toward the liberation of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]], and his attraction for [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] lies partly in the fact that he represents a freedom beyond the personal — a cause large enough to absorb and transform her own restless longing. [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[death|dying]] words invoke "[[love]], freedom, country" (030-057) as a trinity of values for which a life may be given.

- **[[pavel-shubin|Pavel Shubin]]**, by contrast, represents a shallow version of freedom — the freedom of [[art|the artist]] to feel and express without commitment. His demand "why do you encroach on the freedom of others?" (004-038) is, in context, a defense of his own ir[[duty|responsibility]] rather than a genuine commitment to liberty.

### Through Plot

The novel's plot is structured as a progression from confinement to freedom. The opening scenes at the Stahov dacha establish [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s domestic imprisonment; the [[moscow|Moscow]] chapters show her awakening to the possibility of a larger life through [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]; the [[venice|Venice]] chapters represent a liminal space of freedom between the old life and the new; and the novel's final movement toward the [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n border represents the ultimate claim of political freedom over personal safety.

### Through Symbolism

Turgenev associates freedom with spatial openness — the broad lagoon of [[venice|Venice]], the open sea, the [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n frontier — in contrast with the enclosed domestic spaces of [[moscow|Moscow]] drawing-rooms and the Stahov dacha. The novel's geography is a map of liberation, moving steadily from the most enclosed spaces (the dacha, the [[family|family circle]]) toward the most open (the sea, the border).

## Connection to Russian Literary Tradition

The theme of freedom places *On the Eve* at the threshold of the 1860s, the decade in which [[russia|Russia]]n literature would be transformed by the emancipation of the serfs and the rise of radical politics. Turgenev's treatment differs from the polemical radicalism that would follow — his freedom is never merely political slogan — but the novel's insistence that personal freedom must be completed by political commitment anticipates the central debates of the [[russia|Russia]]n 1860s.

## Key Quotations

> "Why do you encroach on the freedom of others? And allow me to inform you, that is not the only point on which you encroach." — Pavel Shubin (004-038)

> "She would go away to her aunt and live in 'God's full freedom'; with secret respect and awe she recalled her aunt's words." — Narrative description of Elena (006-003)

> "To liberate one's country!" she said. "It is terrible even to utter those words, they are so grand." — Elena Nikolaevna (010-049)

> "Conflict, defeat, triumph, love, freedom, country. … Good God, grant a blessing!" — Insarov's dying words (030-057)
