# Yegor Kurnatovsky

A young upper-secretary to the Senate whom [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]] introduces as a prospective husband for his daughter [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]], embo[[death|dying]] the ideal of the practical, principled bureaucratic man that stands in direct opposition to [[art|the artist]]ic and intellectual temperaments of [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] and [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]].

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full Name**: Yegor Andreyevitch Kurnatovsky
- **Identity**: Upper-secretary to the Senate, rising young bureaucrat
- **[[family|Family]]**: Not specified in the novel
- **Social Circle**: [[Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov]] (patron, eager matchmaker), [[Elena Nikolaevna Stahov]] (prospective bride, indifferent), [[Pavel Shubin]] (rival and critic), [[Andrei Bersenyev]] (rival)
- **First Appearance**: Chapter 22, arriving at the [[Stahov-Household|Stahov house]] after Nikolai's enthusiastic introduction (022-032)

## Role in the Novel

### Introduction

Kurnatovsky is first mentioned by [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]] to his daughter [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] with evident pride: "Kurnatovsky, [[yegor-kurnatovsky|Yegor Andreyevitch]]. You don't know him?" (022-007). Nikolai describes him as "upper-secretary to the Senate" and "a man of the highest principle" — his highest term of approbation — hoping to secure him as [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s husband (022-034, 022-023).

### Key Plot Arc

When Kurnatovsky actually appears in Chapter 22, the narrative description undercuts Nikolai's enthusiasm: "He was a man of about thirty, of medium height, with a large head, a broad, flat face, and small, close-set eyes" (022-032). [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] immediately baits him with a question about "principle," and Kurnatovsky's irritated, bureaucratic response reveals a man uncomfortable with play or irony (022-038). Three weeks after his first visit, [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]] notes with satisfaction that [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] "does not seem to dislike him," but this proves illusory (023-001). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s true feelings emerge in Chapter 24 when she simply leaves the room upon his arrival — a silent verdict that speaks louder than any argument (024-009). By Chapter 26, "[[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]]'s cherished projects in regard to Kurnatovsky were making no way," and the practical suitor fades from the narrative (026-001).

### Fate

In the epilogue's summary of [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Stahov]]'s later years, Kurnatovsky receives a brief, ironic coda: "Kurnatovsky, like every man of ardent temperament, could not long remain a widower, and has married a merchant's daughter of Kolomna" (035-025). The "man of principle" ends as a figure of provincial domesticity.

## Character and Themes

- **Traits**: Practical, principled (in the bureaucratic sense), unimaginative, self-satisfied
- **Representing**: The new bureaucratic class, the limits of "principle" without [[love|passion]]
- **Narrative Function**: Antithesis to [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] — Kurnatovsky represents the "establishment" alternative [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] rejects. Where [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s [[sacrifice|devotion]] to a cause is rooted in national [[freedom|liberation]] and personal [[love|passion]], Kurnatovsky's "principle" is bureaucratic abstraction. He is a test [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] must pass, and her indifference to him proves her worthiness for a more meaningful commitment.

## Key Quotations

> "Kurnatovsky, Yegor Andreyevitch. You don't know him? He is upper-secretary to the Senate... a man of the highest principle." — Nikolai Artemyevitch (022-007, 022-034)

> He was a man of about thirty, of medium height, with a large head, a broad, flat face, and small, close-set eyes. He was dressed with the most fastidious neatness. (022-032)

> "And what is that principle?" asked Shubin. Kurnatovsky seemed annoyed or surprised, and looked round at the speaker as though asking him what had provoked the question. (022-038)
