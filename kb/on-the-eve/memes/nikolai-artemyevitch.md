# Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov

Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov is a supporting character in Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve* (1860). As [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s father and the nominal head of the [[Stahov-Household|Stahov house]]hold, he is a retired cavalry officer who married into wealth, abandoned his estate for [[moscow|Moscow]] society life, and conducts a long-running extramarital affair with a German widow — all while maintaining the posture of an aggrieved patriarch. He embodies the "superfluous man" in its most hollow form: not the tortured intellectual of Turgenev's earlier fiction, but a man of petty grievances whose authority is everywhere asserted and nowhere respected.

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full Name**: Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov
- **Identity**: Retired cavalry officer; husband of [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]]; father of [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]]
- **Marriage**: "Hooked" (the narrator's word) the wealthy orphan [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]] [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] at age twenty-five; the marriage brought him a "pretty considerable property" (003-001)
- **Affair**: Maintains a long-standing relationship with [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina Christianovna]], a German widow — a liaison so open that [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]] is "greatly upset" when [[augustina-christianovna|Augustina]] sends her a letter demanding money (008-014)
- **Distant Relative**: [[uvar-ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch Stahov]], a retired cornet who lives with the family, is a distant cousin
- **Children**: [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] (the protagonist)

## Role in the Novel

### Background

The narrator introduces Nikolai Artemyevitch with careful irony: at twenty-five, he "retired from the service and went into the country to manage the property" — but "was soon tired of country life" and settled in [[moscow|Moscow]] (003-002). This pattern — restless acquisition followed by boredom, followed by abandonment — defines his character. He is a man who owns much and values nothing.

### A Father in Absentia

Nikolai Artemyevitch's relationship with [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] is one of estrangement disguised as concern. In Chapter VIII, he complains that "[[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]] I don't pretend to understand" (008-005), then busies himself with his own grievances — his liver, his servants, his mistress. When [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] leaves for [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] with [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]], her father's response is never dramatized; he is, by that point, narratively irrelevant.

### Comic Irritability

Turgenev renders Nikolai Artemyevitch with a comic touch that borders on contempt. He is perpetually affronted by imagined slights, complains about his liver when things go badly (008-020), and insists on his dignity even as everyone around him — his wife, his daughter, his servants — quietly circumvents his authority. [[Uvar-Ivanovitch|Uvar Ivanovitch]]'s noncommittal bass rumble serves as the novel's running commentary on Nikolai Artemyevitch's pronouncements.

## Character and Themes

- **Traits**: Vain, irritable, self-important, ineffectual, unfaithful
- **Representing**: The hollow patriarch; the empty authority of the [[russia|Russia]]n gentry; a generation whose moral capital is exhausted
- **Narrative Function**: Background figure whose inadequacy motivates [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s search for a different kind of man — and a different kind of life

Nikolai Artemyevitch is the novel's quietest indictment. He is not a villain — he is merely useless, a man whose position in the social order is wholly unearned. [[elenas-elopement|Elena's flight]] from [[russia|Russia]] is, in part, a flight from the world her father represents: a world of inherited status, performative dignity, and moral drift.

## Key Quotations
