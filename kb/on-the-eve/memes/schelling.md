Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling (1775–1854) was a German idealist philosopher, a central figure in the development of German Idealism alongside Fichte and Hegel. In Turgenev's *On the Eve*, [[philosophy|Schelling]] functions as a marker of the intellectual atmosphere of the 1840s [[russia|Russia]]n educated class — the generation of [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s father and [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]]'s father, whose worldviews were shaped by [[philosophy|German philosophy]] (004-025)(010-002).

## In the Novel

### Elena's Father, the Schellingist

[[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s father, [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov]], is described as having been influenced by [[philosophy|Schelling]]ian [[All-Concepts|ideas]]. When [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] attempts to explain his father's intellectual background, he says: "My father was a learned man, a Schellingist; he used terms which were not always very clear" (004-025). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]], unfamiliar with the term, asks: "what does that mean, a Schellingist?" [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] explains: "A Schellingist means a follower of [[philosophy|Schelling]], a German philosopher" (004-026)(004-028).

[[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] immediately protests: "for mercy's sake! Surely you don't mean to give [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]] a lecture on [[philosophy|Schelling]]? Have pity on her!" — a remark that highlights the gulf between the serious, academic [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] and the irreverent artist [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] (004-029).

### Intellectual Debates

[[philosophy|Schelling]] reappears in the novel's intellectual [[nature|landscape]]. [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]], "always on the lookout for remarkable men," is drawn to [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]]'s earnest discussion of Schiller and [[philosophy|Schelling]] (005-035). Later, [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] teases [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]]: "at home, [[philosophy|Schelling]] will keep. It's true he did you good service today; but you need not hurry for all that" (005-044).

### Bersenyev's Father

The most extensive treatment of [[philosophy|Schelling]]'s influence comes through the portrait of [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]]'s father — an old Göttingen student, a disciple of the Illuminati, and author of a manuscript "in which [[philosophy|Schelling]]'s philosophy, Swedenborgianism and republicanism were mingled in the most original fashion" (010-002). This fusion of German idealism with mysticism and political thought was characteristic of the [[russia|Russia]]n intelligentsia of the 1830s–1840s.

## Significance

[[philosophy|Schelling]] in *On the Eve* represents a generation already passing — the philosophical idealism of the fathers, against which the younger generation ([[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]], [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]) define themselves through action rather than abstract speculation. The Schellingist fathers — [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch]] and the elder [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] — are ineffectual dreamers; their philosophical systems cannot address the urgent questions of [[love]], [[duty]], and national [[freedom|liberation]] that drive the novel's central characters.
