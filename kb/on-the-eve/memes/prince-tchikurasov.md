# Prince Tchikurasov

Prince Ardalion Tchikurasov is a minor figure in Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve* — a wealthy patron of the arts ("a Maecenas of a Kazan-Tartar stock") who moves through [[moscow|Moscow]] society hosting parties and serving as guardian to the orphaned [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]].

## In the Novel

Tchikurasov first appears in the novel's genealogy: after [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]] [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] was orphaned at seven, "Prince Ardalion Tchikurasov... was appointed her guardian, placed her in the best [[moscow|Moscow]] boarding-school, and when she left school, took her into his own home" (003-001).

It was at one of Tchikurasov's balls that [[anna-stahova|Anna Vassilyevna]] met her future husband, [[nikolai-artemyevitch|Nikolai Artemyevitch Stahov]].

In Chapter IX, [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] attends one of Tchikurasov's gatherings and, with characteristic irreverence, spends the evening insulting his host: "[[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] did really go to Prince Tchikurasov, to whom with the most cordial air he began saying the most insulting things" (009-037).

## Shubin's Party

In Chapter IX, [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] attends one of Prince Tchikurasov's [[moscow|Moscow]] gatherings, but rather than behaving as a grateful guest, he spends the evening insulting his host with calculated charm.

Turgenev's narration is withering in its understatement: "[[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] did really go to Prince Tchikurasov, to whom with the most cordial air he began saying the most insulting things" (009-037). The Maecenas chuckled, his guests laughed, "but no one felt merry, and everyone was in a bad temper when the party broke up" (009-037).

The scene reveals much about both men — [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s irreverence toward the aristocracy whose patronage he depends upon, and Tchikurasov's imperviousness as a host who absorbs insults as easily as he dispenses hospitality.

[[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s own sardonic description of his host, delivered to [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] before departing for the party, captures Tchikurasov's defining quality:

> He is a Maecenas of a Kazan-Tartar stock, after the style of Volgin. Do you see this note of invitation, these letters, R.S.V.P.? Even in the country there's no peace for me. *Addio!*
>
> (009-036)

Bersenyev's silent response to Shubin's tirade precedes an account of Shubin's conduct once he reaches the party:

> Bersenyev listened to Shubin's tirade in silence, looking as though he were just a little ashamed of him. Then he went into the courtyard of the Stahovs' house. And Shubin did really go to Prince Tchikurasov, to whom with the most cordial air he began saying the most insulting things. The Maecenas of the Tartars of Kazan chuckled; the Maecenas's guests laughed, but no one felt merry, and everyone was in a bad temper when the party broke up.
>
> (009-037)
