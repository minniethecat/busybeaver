# Love

A central thematic concern of Ivan Turgenev's *On the Eve* (1860), explored through the [[philosophical-debate|philosophical debate]]s of its characters, the romantic triangulation of its plot, and the novel's broader meditation on the relationship between personal [[happiness]] and [[duty|self-sacrifice]].

## Definition and Scope

- **General Definition**: An intense feeling of deep affection, romantic attraction, or [[sacrifice|devotion]] toward a person or ideal.
- **In the Novel's Context**: Turgenev presents love not as a single emotion but as a spectrum — from the passionate, self-assertive love championed by the sculptor [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] to the self-sacrificing [[sacrifice|devotion]] embodied by [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s eventual choice of [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]. Love is simultaneously a personal yearning and a force that drives characters toward larger commitments beyond themselves.
- **Frequency**: Love is one of the most frequently discussed abstract concepts in the novel, appearing in [[philosophical-debate|philosophical debate]]s, narrative descriptions, and plot-driving confessions across all 35 chapters.

## Expression in the Novel

### Through Characters

The novel's central characters each embody distinct attitudes toward love:

- **[[pavel-shubin|Pavel Shubin]]**, the young sculptor, advocates for passionate, self-assertive love. He declares "I want to love for myself; I want to be first" (001-054). His confession to [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] — "I'm in love with [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]" (005-021) — sets the romantic plot in motion. For [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]], love is inseparable from art, [[beauty]], and the vitality of youth.

- **[[andrei-bersenyev|Andrei Bersenyev]]**, the philosopher and scholar, represents a more contemplative stance. He questions [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s framework: "what sort of love do you mean?" (001-034), suggesting that love may take higher forms than mere self-gratification. [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] later tells him "she loves you" (005-029), casting [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] as an unwitting rival in the emerging romantic triangulation.

- **[[elena-nikolaevna|Elena Nikolaevna]]** is the object of romantic attention throughout the novel's early chapters. Her eventual choice — neither [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] nor [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]], but the [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n revolutionary [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] — redefines love as a force that merges personal [[sacrifice|devotion]] with commitment to a higher political and moral cause.

### Through Plot

The love plot unfolds as a romantic triangulation among [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]], [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]], and [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]. [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s confession in Chapter V (005-021) reveals his unrequited love and his suspicion that [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] loves [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]] instead. The narrative tension between these competing claims — [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s passionate self-assertion versus [[andrei-bersenyev|Bersenyev]]'s quiet, scholarly [[sacrifice|devotion]] — dramatizes Turgenev's central question: what kind of love is worthy of a life?

### Through Symbolism

Turgenev embeds the love theme in the natural [[nature|landscape]] of the novel's opening. [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]] argues that [[nature]] "arouses in us a need for love, and is not capable of satisfying it" (001-037), making the summer [[nature|landscape]] on the banks of the [[moscow|Moskva]] River a symbolic expression of longing. The moonlit night scene in Chapter V — "How sweet it must be tonight for men who are loved!" (005-042) — uses the [[nature|natural world]] as a mirror for romantic emotion, contrasting [[pavel-shubin|Shubin]]'s unrequited state with the imagined [[happiness|bliss]] of the beloved.

Shubin elaborates on this theme in a key passage:

> "Nature is gently driving us to other living embraces, but we don't understand, and expect something from nature herself. Ah, Andrei, Andrei, this sun, this sky is beautiful, everything around us is beautiful, still you are sad; but if, at this instant, you were holding the hand of a woman you loved, if that hand and the whole woman were yours, if you were even seeing with her eyes, feeling not your own isolated emotion, but her emotion—nature would not make you melancholy or restless then, and you would not be observing nature's beauty; nature herself would be full of joy and praise; she would be reechoing your hymn, because then you would have given her—dumb nature—speech!"
>
> (001-037)

## Connection to Russian Literary Tradition

Turgenev's treatment of love in *On the Eve* occupies a distinctive position in nineteenth-century [[russia|Russia]]n literature. Unlike Dostoevsky's exploration of love as a site of spiritual torment and moral extremism, or Tolstoy's anatomization of love within the structures of family and society, Turgenev presents love as a test of character — a force that reveals whether an individual is capable of transcending mere personal [[happiness]] in favor of something larger. This conception places *On the Eve* within the broader Turgenevian theme of the "superfluous man" and his counterpoint: the person of action, for whom love becomes a gateway to commitment rather than a self-enclosed emotional world.
