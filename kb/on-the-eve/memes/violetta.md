Violetta Valéry is the heroine of Giuseppe Verdi's opera *La Traviata* (1853), based on Alexandre Dumas *fils*'s novel *La Dame aux Camélias*. In Turgenev's *On the Eve*, the opera is performed during [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] and [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s stay in [[venice|Venice]], and Violetta's story of [[love]], sacrifice, and untimely [[death]] creates a powerful parallel to the novel's central narrative (033-022).

## In the Novel

### The Opera Performance (Chapter XXXIII)

[[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] and [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] attend a performance of *La Traviata* at a [[venice|Venice]] theatre. Turgenev notes that the opera, "though, honestly speaking, rather vulgar, has already succeeded in making the round of all the European theatres" (033-022). The part of Violetta is sung by an artist of no renown, initially received coolly by the public (033-022).

[[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] watches Violetta attentively from his box (033-024). As the performance progresses, the singer transcends her limitations: "She had thrown aside everything subsidiary, everything superfluous, and *found herself*; a rare, a lofty delight for an artist!" The audience, initially indifferent, is "thrilled and astonished" as the singer transforms before them (033-028).

### Parallel to Elena

The choice of *La Traviata* is pointed: Violetta is a woman who sacrifices herself for [[love]] and dies young, just as [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] will soon lose [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] to illness. The singer's transformation from mediocrity to transcendence mirrors the novel's theme of finding authentic selfhood through [[love]] and suffering. [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s absorption in watching Violetta (033-024) suggests his unspoken recognition of the parallel to his own situation — a man conscious of his [[death|mortality]] watching a story of [[love]] cut short by [[death]].
