## Context

In April 1854, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] and [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] are staying at a hotel on the [[riva-dei-schiavoni|Riva dei Schiavoni]] in [[venice|Venice]], waiting to continue their journey through [[zara|Zara]] to [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]. [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] is visibly declining — thin, pale, and coughing — but on this day he feels better, and [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] proposes they spend the day together: "we will forget politics and war and everything" (033-016). They visit the [[accademia-di-belle-arti|Accademia di Belle Arti]] in high spirits, laughing through the galleries, and after dinner set off for the theatre.

## The Performance

The opera is Verdi's *La Traviata*, described by Turgenev as "though, honestly speaking, rather vulgar, has already succeeded in making the round of all the European theatres, an opera, well-known among [[russia|Russia]]ns" (033-022). The season in [[venice|Venice]] is over, and the cast is undistinguished — every singer "shouted to the best of their abilities" (033-022).

The singer playing [[violetta|Violetta]] is a young, not very pretty, black-eyed girl with "an unequal and already overstrained voice," dressed in a naively gaudy costume (033-022). She is the daughter of some Bergamese shepherd, with no knowledge of how [[Paris|Parisian]] *dames aux camélias* dress. Yet [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] immediately perceives her authenticity:

> "They hardly clap that poor girl at all," said Elena, "but I like her a thousand times better than some conceited second-rate celebrity who would grimace and attitudinise all the while for effect. This girl seems as though it were all in earnest; look, she pays no attention to the public."
>
> (033-023)

[[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s reply carries a darker insight: "she is in earnest; she's on the brink of the grave herself" (033-025). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] falls silent.

## The Mirroring of Life and Art

The third act of *La Traviata* opens on the image of a [[death]]bed — the drawn curtains, the glass of medicine, the shaded lamps — and [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] shudders: "She recalled the near past. 'What of the future? What of the present?' flashed across her mind" (033-027). At that moment, the actress's mimic cough on stage is answered by "the hoarse, terribly real cough of [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]" in their box (033-027). [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] forces calmness onto her face; [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] understands and begins softly humming the tune.

As the performance builds toward the duet, [[violetta|Violetta]] transcends her limitations. She "had suddenly crossed the limit, which it is impossible to define, beyond which is the abiding place of [[beauty]]" (033-028). The aria "Lascia mi vivero — morir si giovane" (Let me live — to die so young!) bursts from her "in such a tempest of prayer rising to heaven, that the whole theatre shook with frenzied applause and shouts of delight" (033-028).

[[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] feels cold all over. Her hand seeks [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s and clasps it tightly. He responds — but Turgenev marks the difference: "Very different was the clasp of hands with which they had greeted each other in the gondola a few hours before" (033-029).

## Aftermath

After the performance, the couple walk through the Square of St. Mark, but [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s heart "could not regain its calm" (033-030). Back in their hotel room, [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] sits at the window while [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] sleeps, and the aria echoes through her thoughts:

> "O God," thought Elena, "why must there be death, why is there separation, and disease and tears? or else, why this beauty, this sweet feeling of hope... Can it be that all that is only in us, and that outside us is eternal cold and silence?"
>
> (033-031)

The phrase *morir si giovane* repeats in her heart like a dark omen. She prays: "grant that he, O God, grant that we both, may die at least a noble, glorious [[death]] — there, on the plains of his country, not here in this dark room" (033-032). The seagull omen that follows — a white bird that drops "as though it had been shot" — confirms the premonition (033-037). [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]] will be dead within hours.

## Significance

The La Traviata scene is one of Turgenev's most celebrated set pieces and a masterpiece of intertextual construction. The opera's story of [[violetta|Violetta]] — a woman [[death|dying]] of consumption, sacrificing herself for [[love]] — mirrors [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]] and [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s situation with devastating precision. But where [[violetta|Violetta]]'s story is fictional and aestheticized, [[dmitri-insarov|Insarov]]'s cough is "terribly real." The scene dramatises the novel's central question: can [[art]] and [[beauty]] console us in the face of [[death]], or are they merely a "vulgar" distraction from the one reality that matters?

The scene also marks the turning point from [[happiness]] to tragedy. The "mirthful mood" of [[the-accademia-di-belle-arti|the Accademia]] visit and the gondola ride gives way, through the opera, to the terror of the [[death]]bed and [[elena-nikolaevna|Elena]]'s desperate prayer. Turgenev uses the theatre as a hinge: before it, [[love]] and laughter; after it, illness and loss.
