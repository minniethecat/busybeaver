## Definition

The Twentieth Century Treatises are the series of instructional books written by [[Mrs. Clandon]] (under her professional name, Mrs. Lanfrey Clandon) that have made her "an authoress of great repute—in Madeira" (001-100). The series, rattled off by the twins in Act I, includes *Twentieth Century Cooking*, *Twentieth Century Creeds*, *Twentieth Century Clothing*, *Twentieth Century Conduct*, *Twentieth Century Children*, and *Twentieth Century Parents* (001-101-106). The treatises represent Mrs. Clandon's life's work: the application of rational, progressive principles to every domain of domestic life. They are also a target of Shaw's satire on the limits of rationalism.

## Context

The treatises belong to the tradition of Victorian and Edwardian conduct literature—books that instructed middle-class readers on how to live properly. Mrs. Clandon's innovation was to apply advanced rationalist and feminist principles to this genre. Her books championed the causes she had advocated in her youth: married women's property rights, Darwinism, John Stuart Mill's liberty, access to education and professions for women (001-427-428). They represent the intellectual armory of the 1860s-80s women's movement, preserved in Madeira while England moved on to Socialism.

## In the Play

The treatises are introduced in Act I through the twins' rapid-fire recitation, which is both admiring and mocking. "No household is complete without them," [[Philip Clandon]] intones. "Cloth limp, half a dollar" (001-107-108). The commercial details undercut the high-mindedness, and the twins' evident desire to escape the books—"We came to England to get away from them" (001-100)—suggests the suffocating effect of being raised by an author of conduct manuals.

The treatises reappear throughout the play as a source of both authority and comedy. [[Dolly Clandon]] quotes them to her mother: "Answer all your children's questions, and answer them truthfully, as soon as they are old enough to ask them. See *Twentieth Century Motherhood*" (001-243). But when Mrs. Clandon is challenged on her own principles, she retreats: "I did not say that you were too young to know. I said you were too young to be taken into my confidence" (001-247). The gap between the treatises' prescriptions and their author's practice is one of the play's central ironies.

In Act III, [[Gloria Clandon]] observes that the new edition of *Twentieth Century Women* has "a chapter missing. I mean an unwritten one. Perhaps I shall write it for you—when I know the end of it" (001-851-853). This is a poignant acknowledgment that the treatises' rational program cannot account for love, desire, and the irrational forces that have just overwhelmed Gloria on the terrace.

## Shaw's Commentary

The treatises embody both Shaw's respect for the nineteenth-century women's movement and his critique of its limitations. Mrs. Clandon's principles are admirable—she truly believes in liberty, reason, and equality—but they prove inadequate to the complexities of actual human relationships. The books can teach children about conduct but cannot prepare them for the "chemical action" of love (001-798). They can advocate truthful answers but cannot give their author the courage to tell her children about their father. Shaw's satire is affectionate: Mrs. Clandon is not a hypocrite but a person of genuine principle who discovers that principles are insufficient. The unwritten chapter Gloria identifies is the one that would have to grapple with the very forces the rationalist program cannot master.
