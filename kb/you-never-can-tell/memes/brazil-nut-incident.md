# Brazil Nut Incident

## Definition

The Brazil nut incident is the play's primary comic catalyst -- the chance event that brings Fergus Crampton into Valentine's dental surgery and, through a chain of accidents and invitations, reunites him with the family he has not seen for eighteen years. The nut embodies Shaw's theme of contingency: a hard-shelled seed, cracked by teeth formed on "kitchen soap," sets in motion the entire comic resolution. It is a plot device that draws attention to its own absurdity, in keeping with the play's title philosophy.

## Context

Brazil nuts were a common Victorian snack, often served at Christmas and in mixed nut bowls. Their hard shells require significant force to crack, making them a plausible agent of dental damage. Within the play's symbolic economy, the nut connects to Crampton's philosophy of dental self-reliance: "I've always cracked nuts with them: what else are they for?" (001-340). The nut that breaks his tooth is thus a direct consequence of his own principles -- his philosophy carries the seed of its own defeat.

## In the Play

The nut incident arrives indirectly, reported by Valentine rather than shown on stage. Valentine returns from his interview with the landlord: "He's broken his tooth on a Brazil nut; and he wants me to look at it and to lunch with him afterwards" (001-264). Dolly immediately seizes the opportunity: "Then have him up and pull his tooth out at once; and we'll bring him to lunch, too" (001-265).

The nut is never named again, but its consequences drive the entire plot. Crampton's dental emergency brings him to Valentine at the precise moment the Clandons are visiting; Dolly's impulsive invitation brings him to the same lunch as his unrecognized family; the gas that Valentine administers disorients Crampton enough that he fails to recognize his wife in advance. The nut is the absurdist hinge on which the whole improbable reunion turns.

## Key Passages

> [001-264] Valentine announces the nut incident: "He's broken his tooth on a Brazil nut; and he wants me to look at it and to lunch with him afterwards." -- The comic catalyst delivered in a single line.

> [001-340] Crampton's dental philosophy, which produced the vulnerability the nut exploits: "I've always cracked nuts with them: what else are they for? ... The proper way to keep teeth good is to give them plenty of use on bones and nuts, and wash them every day with soap -- plain yellow soap."

> [001-265] Dolly's transformative intervention: "Then have him up and pull his tooth out at once; and we'll bring him to lunch, too." -- Chance plus impulse equals plot.

## Shaw's Commentary

The Brazil nut is Shaw's acknowledgment that comedy depends on coincidence. Rather than disguise the improbability, he foregrounds it -- the nut is so arbitrary a cause that it becomes a joke about plot itself. The deeper point is philosophical: Crampton's tooth cracks not despite his "proper" dental care but because of it. The methods that supposedly strengthen character actually create brittleness. The nut is nature's reply to the soap -- a gentle, almost humorous correction that demonstrates the superior power of the unexpected over rigid systems.
