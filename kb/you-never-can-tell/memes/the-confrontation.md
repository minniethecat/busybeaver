## Definition

The Confrontation is the pivotal scene in Act II of *You Never Can Tell*, set on the terrace of the [[Marine Hotel]], where [[Fergus Crampton]] is brought to lunch by [[Valentine]] and discovers—along with everyone else—that his luncheon hosts are the wife and children who abandoned him eighteen years earlier. The scene is a masterpiece of comic horror, combining farcical coincidence with genuine emotional violence as a broken family is forced to break bread together.

## Context

The confrontation is made possible by a chain of accidents and misrecognitions. The [[Clandon children]] have grown up in Madeira knowing nothing of their father. When they encounter Crampton in Valentine's dental surgery, [[Dolly Clandon]] impulsively invites him to lunch, noting only that his name "sounds rheumaticky" (001-004). The children do not connect "Mr. Crampton" with their unknown father until [[Finch McComas]] reveals that their father lives in this very town and is fifty-seven years old (002-005)—the exact age and location of their luncheon guest.

## In the Play

The recognition unfolds in stages. First, McComas reveals the identity to the children on the terrace (002-005). [[Philip Clandon]]'s immediate response is rejection: "he is entirely unfit to be my father" (002-005). The family then faces the question of who will break the news to Crampton, and in an inspired piece of comic plotting, the task falls to [[Walter the Waiter]] (002-005), who botches it by treating it as another of Philip's jokes (002-005).

When Crampton arrives and realizes the truth, his reaction is fury: "This is a plant, a conspiracy. This is my family—my children—my infernal wife" (002-005). The lunch itself becomes an ordeal of mutual recrimination. Crampton demands a blessing (002-005), complains about his children's manners, and is mortified by [[Dolly]]'s smoking (002-005). [[Mrs. Clandon]] greets him with "Fergus: you are greatly changed" (002-005), to which he bitterly replies, "A man does change in eighteen years" (002-005). The meal collapses when Dolly, stung by Crampton's criticism, walks off to smoke on the beach (002-005), and the party disintegrates.

The emotional climax belongs to [[Gloria Clandon]], who delivers the devastating "Goodbye, father" (002-005)—a line that wounds Crampton precisely because it acknowledges the relationship while refusing any warmth. Crampton's anguished response reveals the depth of his pain: "Father! Father!!" (002-005).

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw constructs the confrontation as a demonstration that good intentions and intelligence are no guarantee against disaster when fundamental grievances remain unaddressed. Every character enters the lunch hoping to be civil; all fail. The scene is Shaw's argument that Victorian family life, based on patriarchal authority and wifely submission, produces not harmony but festering wounds that eighteen years of separation cannot heal. The children, raised on "Twentieth Century" principles, prove as incapable of generosity as their father is of flexibility. Only the waiter, with his professional diplomacy and humane wisdom, navigates the scene without causing pain.
