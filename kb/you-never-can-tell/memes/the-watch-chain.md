# The Watch Chain

## Definition

The watch chain appears only once in the play's dialogue, in Dolly's suggestion that Valentine drill her crown piece and "wear it on your watch-chain" (001-069). Though physically minimal, the image reverberates through the drama: the watch chain would display the drilled coin against Valentine's body, converting a professional tool (the dental drill) and a monetary token into a personal ornament. The chain thus bridges the play's central oppositions -- profession and passion, transaction and gift, surface and depth.

## Context

In the 1890s, a gentleman's watch chain was a standard part of male dress, typically worn across the waistcoat. Chains often carried decorative fobs, seals, or sentimental tokens. Dolly's suggestion that Valentine wear a drilled coin on his chain is simultaneously playful and intimate -- it would mark him publicly with her gift. In the class-coding of the period, a dentist drilling coins for jewelry would be a slight breach of professional dignity, which Dolly characteristically ignores.

## In the Play

The watch chain is invoked once and never mentioned again, yet its symbolic work persists. Valentine never drills the coin (the play offers no scene of this happening), and the crown piece passes to the Waiter instead. The undrilled coin and unworn chain mark the space between Dolly's impulsive generosity and the romantic resolution the play ultimately delivers between Gloria and Valentine.

The chain's absence is meaningful. Dolly's suggestion creates an image of what Valentine might become -- a man who wears his affections visibly -- that he achieves not through Dolly's coin but through his pursuit of Gloria. The watch chain symbolizes a transformation that happens offstage: Valentine ceases to be the careful, respectability-obsessed dentist and becomes the reckless lover who "can't be unhappy about anything" (003-1004).

## Key Passages

> [001-069] Dolly offers the crown piece: "Make a hole in it with the thing you drill people's teeth with and wear it on your watch-chain." -- The single reference that establishes the watch chain as a symbol of transformed professional identity.

> [002-712] Valentine gives the crown piece to the Waiter instead of keeping it -- the coin that was to adorn his watch chain is given away, marking his shift from prudent accumulation to extravagant generosity.

> [003-1004] "I can't be unhappy about anything. I shall never be unhappy again, never, never, never, while grass grows or water runs." -- Valentine achieves the transformation the watch chain symbolized, not through the coin but through love.

## Shaw's Commentary

The unworn coin on the absent watch chain is a characteristically Shavian symbol -- mentioned once, never literalized, working through implication rather than statement. Shaw avoids the sentimentality that would come from having Valentine actually drill and wear the coin. Instead, he shows Valentine achieving the transformation the coin represented through a different path: not by keeping Dolly's token but by giving it away, and not by wearing a symbol of love but by falling in love. The watch chain reminds us that the play's real subject is how people change each other -- not through objects, but through presence.
