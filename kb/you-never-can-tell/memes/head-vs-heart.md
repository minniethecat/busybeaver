## Definition

Head versus heart describes the internal psychological conflict experienced by characters in *You Never Can Tell* who find their intellectual convictions at war with their emotional needs. This conflict is most acute in [[Gloria Clandon]], who has been educated to despise romantic love as a form of female weakness, but is structurally present in nearly every character, including [[Valentine]] (who intellectualizes love to avoid feeling it) and [[Fergus Crampton]] (whose anger masks desperate need for affection).

## Context

The head-versus-heart dichotomy has a long history in Western thought, but Shaw gives it a distinctively modern, post-Darwinian inflection. The "head" is not pure reason but the internalized voice of social convention and educational conditioning. The "heart" is not divine inspiration but the biological imperative of the Life Force—Shaw's term for the evolutionary drive toward fuller life and consciousness. The conflict is thus between socially constructed identity and biological reality, both of which are within the individual.

## In the Play

Gloria's head-versus-heart crisis unfolds across Acts II and III. Her head tells her that marriage is an institution "such as no self-respecting woman can accept" (002-005); that love is a sentimental weakness; that Valentine is unworthy of her respect. Her heart—or, in Valentine's term, her "depths" (003-002)—tells her something entirely different. The crisis peaks in Act III when she learns of Valentine's previous affairs. Her instinct is violence: "Gloria raises her clenched hands" (003-002). The rational daughter of a rationalist mother nearly strikes a man in jealous rage.

What distinguishes Gloria's journey is her eventual acceptance of the heart's legitimacy. In Act IV, she confesses: "My feelings—my miserable, cowardly, womanly feelings—may be on your side; but my conscience is on hers" (004-002). The language reveals her continuing shame about emotion ("miserable, cowardly, womanly"), but the admission itself is progress. When she proposes to Valentine, she acts on feeling while retaining rational control of the terms.

Valentine's version of the conflict is the inverse of Gloria's. He presents himself as a cynic, a "Duellist of Sex" (003-002) who has mastered the art of seduction through rational understanding. But his head's cynicism is a defense against his heart's vulnerability. His confession in Act III—"I'm honestly trying to be sensible—scientific—everything that you wish me to be. But—but—oh, don't you see what you have set to work in my imagination?" (002-005)—reveals the intellectual armor cracking.

Even [[Mrs. Clandon]] experiences this conflict, though she has suppressed it so thoroughly that she barely recognizes it. Her admission that she has never been in love (003-002) is presented as a point of pride, but the play subtly suggests it is a wound. Her tenderness toward [[Dolly Clandon]]—"the tenderness in her voice is unmistakable" (001-004)—reveals a heart that her head cannot entirely discipline.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of the head-heart conflict rejects both the Romantic elevation of emotion over reason and the Rationalist dismissal of emotion as mere weakness. The play's movement toward integration—Gloria and Valentine's marriage, the family's partial reconciliation—suggests that psychological health requires acknowledgment of both faculties. What Shaw condemns is not emotion or reason but the use of one to deny the other. Gloria's "shame" (002-005) at being kissed is not a sign of virtue but of psychological damage: she has been taught to despise a fundamental part of herself. Her recovery involves learning to trust her feelings without abandoning her intelligence—the settlement Bohun insists upon (004-002) is the rational framework within which her emotional commitment can flourish.
