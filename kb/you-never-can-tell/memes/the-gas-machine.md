# The Gas Machine

## Definition

The gas machine -- the pump and cylinder beside Valentine's operating chair for administering nitrous oxide -- is a significant piece of stage furniture that embodies the play's theme of modernity versus Victorian endurance. It appears in the opening stage direction ("an operating chair, with a gas pump and cylinder beside it," 001-033) and serves as the instrument of Crampton's comic defeat. The machine represents the new world of comfort, anesthesia, and the rejection of unnecessary suffering -- the ethical position the play ultimately endorses.

## Context

Nitrous oxide (laughing gas) had been used in dentistry since the 1840s, but its routine use was still relatively modern in the 1890s. The extra charge of five shillings for gas (001-045) made it a class marker -- available to those who could afford comfort. The gas machine in Valentine's surgery is part of his "professional plant" on the "hire-purchase system" (001-054), marking his practice as modern but financially precarious. The machine occupies the ambiguous territory between medical progress and commercial transaction.

## In the Play

The gas machine first appears in the opening stage description, alongside the dental drill described as "a slender machine like a whip provided with a stand, a pedal, and an exaggerated winch" (001-033). Dolly has refused gas: "Because you said it would be five shillings extra" (001-045), establishing the economic barrier to comfort.

The machine's dramatic moment comes at the end of Act I, when Valentine administers gas to Crampton against his will. Crampton's defiance -- "Rubbish, man: I want none of your gas. Out with it. People were taught to bear necessary pain in my day" (001-352) -- is overwhelmed by Valentine's deft deception. The stage direction describes Valentine "pressing the mouthpiece over Crampton's mouth and nose, leaning over his chest so as to hold his head and shoulders well down on the chair" (001-383). Crampton's arms "wave aimlessly, then subside and drop. He is quite insensible."

By Act II, Crampton complains: "Those steps make me giddy. I have not got over that infernal gas yet" (002-547). The gas has literally disoriented the patriarch, softening him for the family reunion he cannot escape.

## Key Passages

> [001-033] Stage direction: "The operating chair, with a gas pump and cylinder beside it, is halfway between the centre of the room and one of the corners." -- The gas machine established as a key scenic element.

> [001-352-354] Crampton refuses gas: "Rubbish, man: I want none of your gas. Out with it. People were taught to bear necessary pain in my day." Valentine replies: "Oh, if you like being hurt, all right. I'll hurt you as much as you like, without any extra charge for the beneficial effect on your character."

> [001-383] Valentine administers the gas: "He presses the mouthpiece over Crampton's mouth and nose ... Crampton makes an inarticulate sound ... After a moment his arms wave aimlessly, then subside and drop. He is quite insensible." -- The patriarch rendered helpless by modern comfort.

## Shaw's Commentary

The gas machine is modernity's answer to the soap. Crampton's philosophy values endurance of pain; Valentine's practice eliminates pain. The gas costs five shillings extra, and the question of whether comfort is worth paying for runs through the play. Shaw's position is clear: unnecessary suffering is not virtuous, and those who insist on it (like Crampton) are not strong but brittle. The gas machine's triumph over Crampton's will -- he is rendered unconscious despite his protests -- is a comic parable: the modern world will anesthetize the Victorian patriarch whether he likes it or not.
