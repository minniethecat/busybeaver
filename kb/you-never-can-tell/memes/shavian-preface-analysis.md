# Shavian Preface Analysis

## Definition

Shaw's practice of prefacing his published plays with extended essays is one of the most distinctive features of his dramaturgy. While *You Never Can Tell* was first published in *Plays Pleasant* (1898) without an individual preface, Shaw later provided context in the volume's general preface, and the play itself internalizes its argumentative content through its dialogue. The Shavian preface functions as a parallel text -- an essay that can be read independently but that illuminates the play's intellectual architecture.

## Context

Shaw developed the preface as a genre in its own right. His prefaces range from a few pages to book-length works (the preface to *Major Barbara* rivals the play in length). They served multiple purposes: reaching readers who would never see the play performed, shaping critical interpretation, and providing Shaw a platform for his political and philosophical views that the play's form might constrain. The prefaces were also a commercial strategy -- making the published plays saleable as reading matter in an era when play-texts rarely sold well.

## In the Play

*You Never Can Tell* belongs to *Plays Pleasant*, the 1898 volume that Shaw described as "pleasant" in contrast to the "unpleasant" plays of social criticism in his earlier volume. The volume's general preface establishes the framework: these are comedies that entertain while advancing ideas. The play itself embeds its "prefatory" content in explicit intellectual debate: Mrs. Clandon's *Twentieth Century Treatises* (001-004) parody Shaw's own didactic impulse; McComas's catalogue of Darwin, Mill, Huxley, Tyndall and George Eliot (002-005) situates the play's intellectual genealogy; Valentine's theory of the "duel of sex" (003-002) is a miniature Shavian essay delivered in dialogue.

The absence of a separate preface for *You Never Can Tell* may reflect Shaw's judgment that this play's ideas are sufficiently integrated into its comedy -- unlike the "unpleasant" plays, which required discursive framing.

## Key Passages

> (002-005) McComas catalogs Mrs. Clandon's intellectual positions: "to insist on a married woman's right to her own separate property ... to champion Darwin's view of the origin of species and John Stuart Mill's essay on Liberty ... to read Huxley, Tyndall and George Eliot ... and to demand University degrees, the opening of the professions, and the parliamentary franchise for women as well as men?" -- A compressed intellectual history of the period.

> (003-002) Valentine's "duel of sex" speech: "During this whole century, my dear Mrs. Clandon, the progress of artillery has been a duel between the maker of cannons and the maker of armor plates to keep the cannon balls out. ... The duel of sex is just like that." -- Shavian argument embedded in comic dialogue.

> (001-004) The twins recite Mrs. Clandon's *Twentieth Century* titles: "Twentieth Century Cooking ... Twentieth Century Creeds ... Twentieth Century Clothing ... Twentieth Century Conduct ... Twentieth Century Children ... Twentieth Century Parents." -- A parody of the Shavian preface as totalizing intellectual project.

## Shaw's Commentary

The play's self-conscious intellectualism is itself a comment on the preface as form. Mrs. Clandon has written her own "prefaces" -- the *Twentieth Century Treatises* -- and they have driven her children to flee Madeira. The treatises' comprehensive ambition (cooking, creeds, clothing, conduct, children, parents) mirrors Shaw's own encyclopedic reach. Yet the play's resolution suggests that no preface, no treatise, no intellectual system can account for what the Waiter calls "the unexpected that always happens" (002-005). The preface may explain the play, but the play exceeds the preface -- as the Waiter adds, "You never can tell, sir: you never can tell."
