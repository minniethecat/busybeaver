# The Reconciliation

## Definition

The climactic reconciliation between Crampton and his family — achieved not through sentimental speeches but through Bohun's cross-examination, which breaks through Crampton's defenses to reach the wounded man beneath.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

The reconciliation scene is the play's emotional climax. Bohun, operating in full professional mode, cross-examines Crampton about his feelings, his resentments, and his desires. The process is brutal but therapeutic — Crampton is reduced to tears, and in that moment of vulnerability, his family sees him clearly for the first time. The reconciliation that follows is not sentimental but structural: the settlements provide the framework, and the emotions follow. Shaw's point is that feeling needs form to be sustainable.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
