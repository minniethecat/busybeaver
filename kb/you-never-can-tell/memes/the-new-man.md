## Definition

Valentine, the five-shilling dentist, represents Shaw's conception of the "New Man" — a male counterpart to the "New Woman" who defines masculinity through professional competence, emotional honesty, and respect for female autonomy rather than through dominance, wealth, or inherited status. The New Man is, for Shaw, the necessary complement to the New Woman: a partner capable of meeting her on equal terms.

## In the Play

Valentine's modernity is established through contrast with the other male characters. Against Crampton's outdated paternalism and threatened violence, Valentine offers candor and vulnerability. Against Bohun's professional brusqueness, Valentine offers warmth and humor. His "five-shilling dentist" system — honest pricing, no class distinctions among patients — embodies his professional ethics. His willingness to acknowledge his love for Gloria without demanding her submission marks him as a man who has abandoned the old scripts of masculine conquest.
