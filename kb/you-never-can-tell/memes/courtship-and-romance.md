## Definition

Courtship and romance in *You Never Can Tell* follow an unconventional trajectory that subverts Victorian expectations. Valentine falls in love with Gloria at first sight, but their path to engagement is mediated not by romantic gestures but by rational negotiation, legal intervention, and the gradual dismantling of Gloria's ideological resistance to love. Shaw presents romance as a natural force that must be acknowledged rather than a social performance to be enacted.

## In the Play

Valentine's instant attraction to Gloria is signaled physically — he "falls in love at first sight and is miserably confused" [001-165] the moment she enters the dentist's office. But Gloria, raised on her mother's rationalist principles, resists both his advances and her own feelings. Their courtship unfolds through intellectual debate rather than sentimental exchange, with Valentine arguing that love is a biological imperative that reason cannot override.

The courtship reaches its crisis in Act III, when Gloria challenges Valentine: does he truly love her, or is he merely playing the romantic suitor? Valentine's honest reply — that his love is genuine but his conscience would not permit him to marry without means — transforms the conventional proposal scene into a Shavian examination of love's material conditions. Their eventual engagement, facilitated by Bohun's legal machinery, represents a synthesis of romance and practicality.
