# Love and Marriage

## Definition

In *You Never Can Tell*, love is not a sentiment but a force — biological, psychological, and social — that operates independently of the intellect and will. Marriage is the institution through which that force must be channeled, and the play's comic structure is built around the collision between romantic attraction and the various systems ([[social-class|class]], money, parenting philosophy, legal authority) designed to regulate it. Shaw's treatment is characteristically dialectical: he simultaneously celebrates the Life Force that draws [[valentine|Valentine]] and [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] together and ruthlessly exposes the economic and ideological machinery that makes their union possible. [001-001]

The play offers not one marriage plot but three: the young lovers ([[valentine|Valentine]] and [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]), the estranged spouses ([[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] and [[crampton|Crampton]]), and the implied companionate union of the future (a companionate union of equals implied by the play's critique of the Victorian model). Together, these three models constitute Shaw's comprehensive argument about what marriage has been, what it is, and what it might become.

## Context

*You Never Can Tell* was written in 1896–97, contemporaneous with Shaw's *The [[philip-clandon|Phil]]anderer* and *Mrs. Warren's Profession*, both of which treat marriage as a social institution requiring radical reform. The late Victorian marriage market was governed by contradictory imperatives: romantic love was idealized in fiction but subordinated to economic calculation in practice; women were expected to marry but denied the education and autonomy that would make marriage a choice rather than a necessity.

Shaw's own marriage to Charlotte Payne-Townshend in 1898 — a companionate, possibly celibate union — reflected his conviction that marriage should be a partnership of equals rather than a transfer of [[money-and-status|property]]. His plays consistently reject the Victorian idealization of romantic love while affirming the vitality of the sex instinct — what he would later theorize as the "Life Force" driving human evolution toward higher consciousness. In *You Never Can Tell*, this philosophy finds its sunniest expression: love is a force of nature, comic rather than tragic, and marriage, properly understood, is the social form that accommodates it.

The play's 1890s context also explains its preoccupation with the "duel of sex" (003-124). Shaw was writing in the wake of the 1882 Married Women's Property Act and the ongoing campaign for women's suffrage — a moment when the legal architecture of marriage was being actively contested. [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s flight from her husband, and her children's determination to understand their father's identity, reflect the real legal and emotional consequences of marriage reform.

## In the Play

### Valentine and Gloria: The Life Force in Action

[[valentine|Valentine]] and [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s romance occupies the play's emotional center and provides its most sustained philosophical argument. Their relationship unfolds in four movements:

**Act I — First Contact (001-039–001-082).** [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s dental consultation is a courtship in miniature, conducted in the language of professional transaction. Her delighted response to the five-shilling fee — "How nice! Well, here! a nice new five shilling piece! your first fee!" (001-069) — transforms a clinical encounter into a gift exchange. Neither character recognizes what is happening, but the audience, trained by Shaw's stage directions, can see the chemistry taking hold.

**Act II — Falling (002-849–003-175).** [[valentine|Valentine]]'s monologue of comic self-diagnosis at [[marine-hotel|the Marine]] Hotel is one of Shaw's great set pieces. He tries to reason himself out of love by cataloging the mundane facts of his situation: "What's this place? it's not heaven: it's [[marine-hotel|the Marine]] Hotel. What's the time? it's not eternity: it's about half past one in the afternoon. What am I? a dentist — a [[five-shilling-dentist|five shilling dentist]]!" (002-849). The attempt fails because love operates outside rational calculation — a point [[valentine|Valentine]], the man of science, must learn to accept.

> (002-849) Valentine's "What am I? a dentist — a five shilling dentist!" — The comic heart of the play: a rational man discovering that reason has no jurisdiction over attraction.

**Act III — The Duel of Sex (003-124–003-145).** [[valentine|Valentine]] articulates his evolutionary theory of courtship to [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]: "What happens in the duel of sex? The old fashioned mother received an old fashioned education to protect her against the wiles of man. … So she gave her daughter a scientific education — your plan" (003-124). This is Shaw's meta-commentary on his own plot: each generation's romantic strategy provokes a counter-strategy in the next, producing an arms race of courtship techniques. [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s scientific education was meant to protect her from the "old fashioned man"; [[valentine|Valentine]], the new-fashioned man, is equipped with irony and self-awareness — weapons the previous generation could not have anticipated.

**Act II — Chemistry (002).** The play's philosophical center comes when [[valentine|Valentine]], attempting to be rational, calls their attraction "chemistry" — "the most irresistible of all natural forces" (002-849). [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s response — "I hope you are not going to be so foolish — so vulgar — as to say love" (002-849) — is both defensive and revealing. She has been trained to dismiss romantic passion as irrational; [[valentine|Valentine]]'s redefinition of love as chemical action offers her a vocabulary her education can accept, even as the scene collapses into a quarrel: he calls her "a feminine prig" and she answers with "elaborate calm" that "shows how very little you understand my real character" (002-849). The attraction is acknowledged not through a graceful acceptance but through the very friction of the exchange.

### Mrs. Clandon and Crampton: The Failed Marriage as Cautionary Tale

The elder Clandons' failed marriage provides the play's structural counterpoint. Their union was contracted under Victorian assumptions of male authority and female submission — the very "sort of family life" [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] describes with "biting acrimony" as one "in which husbands open their wives' letters, and call on them to account for every farthing of their expenditure and every moment of their time" (001-004). [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s flight to [[madeira|Madeira]] with her children was an act of radical self-preservation — a refusal to participate in the patriarchal marriage she had unwittingly entered.

Their reconciliation at the play's end is deliberately incomplete. [[crampton|Crampton]] is restored to his children's acquaintance but not to his wife's affection; the marriage is acknowledged rather than resumed. Shaw's point is that some marital damage is irreparable, and that the best the law can do — [[bohun|Bohun]]'s judgment — is to establish the material terms under which estranged spouses can coexist in civil peace.

### The Waiter's Wisdom

[[walter-the-waiter|Walter]] the waiter provides the play's most subversive commentary on marriage. His deference to "one's station in life, sir" — voiced even as his own son rises to be a Q.C. — quietly implies that social roles, including husband and wife, are provisional and performable rather than fixed. The waiter who is also the father of a Q.C. embodies the possibility of multiple identities coexisting in a single person, just as the play suggests that marriage can accommodate multiple kinds of relationship.

## Key Passages

> (001-069) "How nice! Well, here! a nice new five shilling piece! your first fee! Make a hole in it with the thing you drill people's teeth with and wear it on your watch-chain." — Gloria, transforming a dental fee into the play's central romantic symbol.

> (002-849) "What's this place? it's not heaven: it's the Marine Hotel. What's the time? it's not eternity: it's about half past one in the afternoon. What am I? a dentist — a five shilling dentist!" — Valentine's failed attempt to reason himself out of love, the play's comic credo.

> (003-124) "The old fashioned mother received an old fashioned education to protect her against the wiles of man. … So she gave her daughter a scientific education — your plan." — Valentine's evolutionary theory of courtship as intergenerational arms race.

> (001-004) "There is another sort of family life: a life in which husbands open their wives' letters … in which duty, obedience, affection, home, morality and religion are detestable tyrannies." — Mrs. Clandon's indictment of patriarchal marriage, the play's feminist manifesto.

> (002-849) "Let's call it chemistry. You can't deny that there is such a thing as chemical action, chemical affinity, chemical combination — the most irresistible of all natural forces." — Valentine's redefinition of love as natural law, enabling Gloria to accept it on rational terms.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of love is resolutely anti-sentimental but not cynical. [[valentine|Valentine]] does not fall in love because [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] is beautiful or virtuous — he falls in love because the Life Force compels him to, and his resistance is both comic and futile. The play's resolution is a Shavian paradox: love triumphs, but only through the intervention of money ([[crampton|Crampton]]'s inheritance), law ([[bohun|Bohun]]'s authority), and social convention (the luncheon party). Shaw wants his audience to enjoy the happy ending while recognizing its impossibility under normal circumstances.

This is the deeper point: love is natural, but marriage is social. The "chemistry" that draws [[valentine|Valentine]] and [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] together is real and irresistible; the institution that accommodates it is artificial and contingent. The play's comic structure — mistaken identities, improbable coincidences, a waiter who is a lawyer — mirrors the artificiality of the marital institution itself. By making the machinery of resolution visible, Shaw insists that marriage, like comedy, is a human construction, and that its forms can and should evolve to accommodate the human beings it is meant to serve.

The title — *You Never Can Tell* — is the play's final word on love. The most rigorously educated young woman in English drama cannot predict her own romantic fate; the most self-aware dentist in English comedy cannot reason his way out of attraction. Love operates in the gap between knowledge and experience, and "you never can tell" is the comic wisdom that acknowledges that gap and celebrates it.
