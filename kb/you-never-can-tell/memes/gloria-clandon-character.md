## Definition

Gloria Clandon is Shaw's most fully realized portrait of the "New Woman" — a young woman raised on rationalist principles who must reconcile her intellectual commitments with her emotional life. Unlike the twins, whose freedom takes the form of irreverent play, Gloria's freedom takes the form of principled intensity. She is "all passion" constrained by "obstinate pride and intense fastidiousness" (003-164), a combination that makes her both formidable and vulnerable.

## In the Play

Gloria's dramatic journey is from ideological certainty to emotional openness. At the outset, she is her mother's daughter — convinced that independence requires resistance to love, that reason must govern feeling. Valentine's persistent honesty, and her own undeniable attraction to him, gradually erode this position. Her acceptance of his proposal is not a surrender but a synthesis — she chooses love without abandoning principle, recognizing that the two need not be opposed. Her character arc embodies Shaw's argument that feminism and love can coexist.
