# Shaw on Marriage

## Definition

Shaw's critique of marriage in *You Never Can Tell* operates on multiple fronts. The play examines marriage as a failed institution through the Cramptons' disastrous union, proposes an alternative model of voluntary association through the Clandon family's life in Madeira, and ultimately suggests that the rational rejection of marriage is powerless against the irrational force of love. Shaw's position is neither simply pro- nor anti-marriage but dialectical: the institution as constituted is indefensible, yet the human impulse toward union cannot be legislated away.

## Context

Shaw was writing in the aftermath of the Married Women's Property Acts (1870-1882) and during the campaign for women's suffrage. The "marriage question" was central to late-Victorian social debate. Shaw himself married Charlotte Payne-Townshend in 1898, a year after completing *You Never Can Tell*, in what both partners described as a practical arrangement. His critiques of marriage were thus grounded in personal ambivalence as well as political conviction. The Fabian Society, of which Shaw was a leading member, advocated for marriage reform as part of a broader socialist program.

## In the Play

The Crampton marriage provides the negative case study. Mrs. Clandon's account of marriage is devastating: "a life in which husbands open their wives' letters, and call on them to account for every farthing of their expenditure and every moment of their time" (001-195). She fled with the children to Madeira, creating a family based on "mutual respect, on recognition of the right of every member of the household to independence and privacy" (001-195).

Gloria provides the play's intellectual critique: "I do not think the conditions of marriage at present are such as any self-respecting woman can accept" (002-769). Valentine agrees: "the conditions are most unfair" (002-770). Yet by Act IV, Gloria has proposed to Valentine, and the Waiter has delivered the play's final word on marriage: "every man is frightened of marriage when it comes to the point; but it often turns out very comfortable, very enjoyable and happy indeed, sir -- from time to time. ... You never can tell, sir: you never can tell" (004-1406).

## Key Passages

> [001-195] Mrs. Clandon's indictment of Victorian marriage: "a life in which husbands open their wives' letters, and call on them to account for every farthing of their expenditure ... in which no room is private and no hour sacred; in which duty, obedience, affection, home, morality and religion are detestable tyrannies."

> [002-769] Gloria: "I do not think the conditions of marriage at present are such as any self-respecting woman can accept." Valentine: "I quite agree with you: the conditions are most unfair." -- A Shavian duet of rational marriage critique.

> [004-1406] The Waiter: "every man is frightened of marriage when it comes to the point; but it often turns out very comfortable, very enjoyable and happy indeed, sir -- from time to time. ... You never can tell, sir: you never can tell." -- The final, anti-dogmatic resolution.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's argument is dialectical rather than dogmatic. Marriage as a legal institution is indefensible; marriage as a human relationship is inevitable. The play's comic resolution -- Gloria and Valentine engaged, Crampton and Mrs. Clandon not reconciled -- suggests that some marriages should happen and others should not. The criterion is not law or tradition but mutual voluntariness. Bohun's "all matches are unwise" (004-1405) is the lawyer's truth, but the Waiter's "so much the worse for wisdom" (004-1406) is the play's.
