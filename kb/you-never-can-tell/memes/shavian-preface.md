## Definition

George Bernard Shaw's practice of furnishing his published plays with substantial prefaces is one of the distinctive features of his dramaturgy. The prefaces allowed Shaw to address his readers directly, articulating the philosophical and social arguments that his plays embodied dramatically. *You Never Can Tell*, first published in 1898 in *Plays Pleasant*, appeared with a preface that situated it within Shaw's larger project of reforming English drama and English society.

## In the Play

While the preface is external to the performed play, it shapes the interpretive frame through which readers encounter the text. Shaw used the *Plays Pleasant* preface to distinguish these comedies from the "unpleasant" plays (*Widowers' Houses*, *Mrs. Warren's Profession*) and to argue that comedy could be as intellectually serious as tragedy. The preface tradition reflects Shaw's conviction that theater was a medium of ideas, and that those ideas deserved explicit articulation alongside their dramatic embodiment.
