## Definition

Madeira, the Portuguese island where Mrs. Clandon raised her children, functions in *You Never Can Tell* as an ambiguous space — both refuge from English social tyranny and exile from the children's cultural birthright. The eighteen years the Clandons spent there have produced children who are "not in the least" English in manner, whose complexions have been "burnt biscuit color by some warmer sun than England's" [001-045]. Madeira represents the possibility of alternative social arrangements, yet also the cost of separation from one's origins.

## In the Play

The twins' unfamiliarity with English social codes — their ignorance that a father is required, their impulsive lunch invitations, their frank questions — is attributed to their Madeiran upbringing. They are simultaneously more polished (their dress and manners are impeccable) and more natural (their curiosity is unconstrained by English reserve) than their English counterparts. Their return to England is thus a kind of coming-of-age: they must learn to navigate a society whose rules they have not internalized.
