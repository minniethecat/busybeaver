# The Happy Ending as Critique

## Definition

Shaw's deliberately artificial happy ending — marriages, reconciliations, and financial settlements all achieved through Bohun's legal machinery rather than organic resolution — as a meta-theatrical commentary on the conventions of comedy.

## Context

The late Victorian period (the 1890s) was a time of social transition when traditional Victorian certainties were being challenged by new ideas — Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emerging professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* is set at a seaside resort in this transitional moment, and the play's characters embody different responses to the collision between old and new values.

## In the Play

This concept manifests through the play's action and characters. The Clandon family, returning from eighteen years of exile in Madeira, encounter English society and its rigid conventions with fresh eyes. Their unconventional behavior — asking direct questions, ignoring social hierarchies, treating servants as equals — constantly disrupts the expected social script. The comic tension of the play derives largely from this collision between Madeiran freedom and English constraint.

## Key Passages

> (002-427) McComas's summary of Mrs. Clandon's intellectual positions provides a catalog of the progressive ideas that shaped the play's worldview.

> (001-298) Shaw's stage directions for Crampton — "hard and stringy, with an atrociously obstinate … mouth" yet with "wistful, wounded eyes" — exemplify the play's method of presenting surface harshness concealing inner vulnerability.

> (002-393) Walter the Waiter's description — "so cheerful and contented that in his encouraging presence ambition stands rebuked as vulgarity" — illustrates the play's philosophical elevation of the ordinary.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort setting — a liminal space between metropolitan sophistication and provincial convention — to stage his argument about the need for social change. The characters who represent the future (the Clandons, Valentine) are attractive, witty, and life-affirming; those who represent the past (Crampton before his transformation) are rigid, unhappy, and isolated. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter is the solvent of outdated convention.
