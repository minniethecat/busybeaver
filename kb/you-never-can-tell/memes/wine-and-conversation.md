# Wine and Conversation

## Definition

Wine, drink, and the rituals surrounding their consumption function throughout *You Never Can Tell* as markers of class identity, instruments of social negotiation, and indices of emotional state. From the luncheon table's elaborate drink orders to the Act IV claret cup, what characters drink, how they order it, and who orders for whom reveal the play's underlying social dynamics more precisely than any direct statement.

## Context

Victorian drinking was heavily coded by class, gender, and occasion. Wine (especially port and claret) signaled middle-class prosperity. Beer and spirits were associated with working-class and masculine sociability. Abstinence (the Apollinaris mineral water McComas drinks) could signal religious conviction, health consciousness, or (in McComas's case) a solicitor's professional caution. Women's drinking was particularly scrutinized: a woman ordering anything stronger than wine or sherry risked her respectability. The seaside hotel bar, where Crampton is known by the Waiter, occupied an ambiguous space between respectability and license.

## In the Play

The luncheon drink orders are a social map in miniature. Philip orders "two small Lagers for the children as usual, William; and one large for this gentleman" (002-606) -- Lager is the modern, slightly vulgar choice, appropriate for the irreverent twins. "Large Apollinaris for Mr. McComas" (002-606) -- the teetotal solicitor. "Number 413 for my mother and Miss Gloria as before" (002-610) -- wine coded by number rather than name, suggesting the Clandon ladies' detachment from the masculine rituals of wine knowledge.

Crampton's drink is the most revealing. The Waiter intervenes: "We know what Mr. Crampton likes here, sir" (002-612), and later specifies "Seltzer and Irish, sir" (002-619). Irish whiskey with seltzer is a bar drink, not a dining-room wine. It marks Crampton as a man who drinks in public houses rather than private dining rooms -- a class marker he would prefer to suppress.

The Waiter's ministrations extend to flavor: "A small lump of sugar, sir, will take the flatness out of the seltzer without noticeably sweetening the drink, sir" (002-717). The gesture is gratuitously kind -- the Waiter improving Crampton's drink as he would like to improve his character.

In Act IV, the drinks return: "Claret cup, ma'am! ... Put some cucumber in it" (004-1187-1189). Claret cup is a summer party drink, lighter and more festive than the luncheon wines. The cucumber marks the transition from formal dining to informal celebration.

## Key Passages

> [002-606-613] The drink orders: "Two small Lagers ... Large Apollinaris ... Number 413 ... We know what Mr. Crampton likes here, sir." -- A compressed social taxonomy in drink form.

> [002-717] The Waiter's sugar intervention: "A small lump of sugar, sir, will take the flatness out of the seltzer without noticeably sweetening the drink, sir. Allow me, sir." -- Kindness expressed through drink craft.

> [004-1187-1194] The Act IV orders: "Claret cup, ma'am! ... Put some cucumber in it. ... Syphon -- Scotch. ... Irish for you, sir." -- Drink as emotional restoration, the Waiter returning to his element.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses drink to demonstrate that taste is never innocent. Every beverage choice is a social statement, and mastery of the drink code is a form of class competence that the Clandon children (with their Lager and their ignorance of wine numbers) conspicuously lack. But Shaw's deeper point is that the Waiter, who knows the drink code better than anyone, is also the character least constrained by it. He can add sugar to Crampton's seltzer because he understands both the chemistry of the drink and the psychology of the drinker. Wisdom, Shaw suggests, is not transcending social codes but using them skillfully in the service of kindness.
