## Definition

Gas and anesthesia in *You Never Can Tell* refers to [[Valentine]]'s use of nitrous oxide (laughing gas) in his dental practice—"a gas pump and cylinder beside" the operating chair (001-001). The gas serves multiple functions: a comic device ([[Dolly Clandon]] refuses it to save five shillings), a plot mechanism (Valentine uses it to knock out [[Fergus Crampton]] and bring him to lunch), and a symbol of the modern professional's approach to pain—manage it rather than endure it.

## Context

Nitrous oxide was discovered as an anesthetic in the 1840s and was widely used in dentistry by the 1890s. Its nickname, "laughing gas," came from the euphoria it could induce. The availability of anesthesia transformed dentistry from a torture into a tolerable medical procedure, but it was also a class marker: those who could afford gas avoided pain; those who could not endured it. Dolly's refusal of gas to save five shillings (001-004) is a small but telling detail about the Clandon family's economic circumstances.

## In the Play

The gas is introduced in the opening stage direction (001-001) and immediately becomes a comic subject. Dolly's refusal—"Because you said it would be five shillings extra" (001-004)—establishes her practicality and Valentine's system. Valentine's response—"It makes me feel as if I had hurt you for the sake of five shillings" (001-004)—introduces the moral ambiguity of professional pain-for-pay.

The gas becomes a plot mechanism at the end of Act I. Valentine, having bet Crampton his six weeks' rent that he can extract the tooth painlessly, uses the gas to render him unconscious (001-004). The stage direction is precise and comic: "Crampton makes an inarticulate sound in the mouthpiece and tries to lay hands on Valentine, whom he supposes to be in front of him. After a moment his arms wave aimlessly, then subside and drop" (001-004).

This "drugging" becomes a legal issue in Act III when Crampton accuses Valentine of being part of a conspiracy: "He alleges that you drugged him, Mr. Valentine" (003-002). Valentine's response—"So I did" (003-002)—is a moment of pure Shavian honesty that collapses the legal accusation by refusing to treat the gas as anything other than standard professional practice.

The gas also participates in the play's broader argument about pain and character. Crampton initially refuses gas: "People were taught to bear necessary pain in my day" (001-004). Valentine counters: "Oh, if you like being hurt, all right. I'll hurt you as much as you like, without any extra charge for the beneficial effect on your character" (001-004). This exchange captures the generational divide: Crampton's stoicism versus Valentine's modern pragmatism, the ethic of endurance versus the ethic of comfort.

## Shaw's Commentary

The gas functions as a symbol of modernity's approach to pain: manage it, avoid it, anesthetize it. Crampton's stoicism—washing his teeth with yellow soap (001-004), refusing anesthesia—represents a Victorian ethic of character formation through suffering. Valentine's pragmatism represents the modern alternative: pain is not character-building but unnecessary, and the professional's job is to eliminate it. Shaw's own position, as usual, is dialectical. He clearly prefers Valentine's approach—the play mocks Crampton's stoicism—but he also suggests that the avoidance of pain has costs. The family's eighteen-year estrangement is itself a form of anesthesia: Mrs. Clandon fled the pain of her marriage, and the result was a different kind of damage. The gas that puts Crampton to sleep is a comic version of the forces that put the family to sleep for eighteen years. The play's resolution requires the characters to wake up—from gas, from exile, from denial—and face one another.

## Historical Provenance

The anesthetic history behind the "gas" is older than the play's 1896 setting. Nitrous oxide was first synthesized by Joseph Priestley in 1772, and Humphry Davy demonstrated its intoxicating and pain-dulling effects in 1799, coining the name "laughing gas." The American dentist Horace Wells first used it for a tooth extraction in 1844. By the 1890s the apparatus—Shaw's "gas pump and cylinder" (001-001)—was a recognizable signifier of a modern, scientifically equipped practice, though still novel enough that a patient like [[Fergus Crampton]] could refuse it as newfangled ("Rubbish, man: I want none of your gas," 001-004) without seeming eccentric to his own generation.
