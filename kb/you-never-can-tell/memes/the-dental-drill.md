# The Dental Drill

## Definition

The "slender machine like a whip" (001-001) in Valentine's office — symbol of modernity, professional skill, and the anxiety provoked by new technology.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Shaw's description of the dental drill is carefully menacing: "a slender machine like a whip provided with a stand, a pedal, and an exaggerated winch" (001-001). The comparison to a whip is not accidental — it links the dental drill to the whip that Crampton bought to hurt his wife, establishing a thematic connection between professional technology and domestic violence. Both are instruments of power, but one is used to heal and the other to harm.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
