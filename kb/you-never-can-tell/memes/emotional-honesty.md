## Definition

Emotional honesty in *You Never Can Tell* is presented as a moral imperative and a practical necessity. Valentine's defining characteristic is his inability to sustain deception about his feelings — his "conscience," which he repeatedly identifies as his "ruin," compels him to tell the truth even when it damages his interests. Shaw argues that emotional honesty, though costly in the short term, is the only foundation on which genuine relationships can be built. [001-004]

## In the Play

Valentine's candor operates on multiple levels. Professionally, his five-shilling system is an exercise in pricing honesty. Socially, he cannot maintain the pretense of prosperity when his landlord is demanding rent. Romantically, he refuses to disguise his love for Gloria behind conventional courtship rituals. His honesty is contrasted with the secrecy that has governed the Clandon family for eighteen years — Mrs. Clandon's concealment of the children's father, Crampton's absence, the unspoken pact of silence among the adults. The play's resolution validates honesty over secrecy: only when the truth is spoken can reconciliation begin.

## Rewarded and Forced Honesty

Shaw sorts his characters by their relationship to emotional truth. Those who can acknowledge their feelings tend to be rewarded: Valentine wins Gloria by confessing his professional failures rather than concealing them, and Dolly earns affection precisely by saying exactly what she thinks, however socially inappropriate. Those who repress their feelings must be forced into honesty by circumstance rather than choosing it — Crampton, cornered at the Marine Hotel, is driven to admit how much he wants his estranged children ("Are those my children?"), and Mrs. Clandon, whose habitual reserve keeps her from sharing her pain, is compelled by the family reunion to explain the buried past. The play's comic machinery does this work: it manufactures the situations that make concealment untenable, so that honesty, once forced into the open, can become the ground of reconciliation.

## Key Passages

> "ambition stands rebuked as vulgarity, and imagination as treason to the abounding sufficiency and interest of the actual" (002-003)

Shaw's stage direction on Walter the Waiter frames emotional honesty not merely as candor but as contentment — a settled truthfulness about what one actually is, against which restless ambition looks like a kind of dishonesty.
