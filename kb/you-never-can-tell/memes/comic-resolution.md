## Definition

The comic resolution of *You Never Can Tell* is the mechanism by which Shaw transforms the play's serious conflicts—eighteen years of marital estrangement, threatened litigation, romantic crisis—into the joyful dance that concludes Act IV. Unlike realistic drama, which might require characters to work through their psychological issues, Shaw resolves the plot through deliberately artificial comic devices: the fancy dress ball, [[Bohun]]'s theatrical unmasking, the substitution of dance for dialogue, and the waiter's philosophical refrain.

## Context

Shaw classified *You Never Can Tell* as one of his *Plays Pleasant*, a category he distinguished from the *Plays Unpleasant* (*Widowers' Houses*, *Mrs. Warren's Profession*, *The Philanderer*) which addressed social problems with uncompromising seriousness. The "pleasant" plays were designed to engage audiences with serious ideas through the vehicle of comedy. The comic resolution is thus not an evasion of the play's themes but their dramatization through a different mode: the point is precisely that problems which cannot be solved through argument can sometimes be dissolved through festivity.

## In the Play

The resolution is prepared throughout Act IV by the simultaneous presence of the family conference and the fancy dress ball. The stage directions describe "Chinese lanterns glowing among the trees outside, with the starry sky beyond" and "the band playing dance-music in the garden, drowning the sound of the sea". The ball provides an alternative reality—carnival, anonymity, collective joy—that gradually invades the room and transforms the proceedings.

Bohun himself enters in carnival disguise, wearing a domino and false nose. His removal of the mask is the play's central theatrical gesture: the formidable Q.C. strips away illusion to reveal himself as both a legal genius and the waiter's son. This dual identity—the highest professional eminence and the humblest parentage—encapsulates the play's democratic vision.

The resolution proceeds through the progressive capitulation of every character to the dance. [[Dolly]] demands a dance with Bohun; Bohun dances her off "in a most powerful manner, but with studied propriety and grace" (004-002). [[Crampton]] and [[McComas]] resist but are persuaded to don false noses (004-1327-1330). Mrs. Clandon is whirled away by [[Philip]]. Gloria is claimed by Bohun as "counsel's fee", leaving Valentine gasping on the ottoman with the waiter's benediction: "You never can tell, sir: you never can tell".

The final stage direction captures the transformation: Crampton exits "with senile glee. Ho! ho! He! he! he!" (004-002). The man who entered the play as a bitter, frightening figure exits chuckling like a delighted child.

## Shaw's Commentary

The comic resolution is Shaw's alternative to both the sentimental happy ending and the tragic catastrophe. He argues, through the play's structure, that problems of personality and history cannot be resolved through rational argument alone—hence Bohun's analysis, while necessary, is not sufficient. The resolution requires the suspension of ordinary rules through carnival, the acceptance of absurdity through the false noses, and the physical communion of dance. The play's final wisdom, voiced by the waiter, is epistemological modesty: "You never can tell." We cannot predict outcomes, and the attempt to control them through law or principle often worsens the conflicts it means to resolve.

## Dramatic Technique

Act IV's resolution is structurally *retrospective*: much of the decisive action—[[Bohun]]'s legal negotiation of the family settlement—occurs offstage and is reported by characters who enter with news of terms reached. This technique, borrowed from classical drama, lets Shaw advance the plot efficiently while preserving the play's conversational texture. The result is an anti-sentimental happy ending achieved through contracts and settlements rather than grand gestures or sudden conversions. The closing configuration deliberately mirrors the opening: a family lunch, transformed from an awkward gathering of strangers into a genuine family occasion.
