# Philip as Arbiter

## Definition

Philip Clandon repeatedly positions himself as the family's authority on character, invoking his "knowledge of human nature" as a credential. This self-appointed role is simultaneously a running comic gag and a satiric commentary on the pretensions of youth. Philip's pronouncements are delivered with gravity and impeccable manners, but their content reveals a young man who has absorbed the language of authority without its substance -- a parody of patriarchal judgment.

## Context

The phrase "knowledge of human nature" was a nineteenth-century cliche of middle-class self-assurance, particularly associated with businessmen and men of practical affairs who distrusted book-learning. Shaw, the Fabian socialist and Ibsenite, had little patience for such empiricist posturing. Philip's deployment of the phrase parodies the Victorian paterfamilias -- the very role his absent father failed to fill -- while simultaneously showing how easily the younger generation can adopt the rhetorical habits of the old.

## In the Play

Philip first deploys his signature phrase in Act I: "my knowledge of human nature confirms Mr. Valentine's judgment" (001-086), when in fact Philip knows nothing of Valentine's circumstances. His pronouncements grow more elaborate: "My knowledge of human nature is fairly extensive, Mr. McComas; but I find it impossible to take the inhabitants of this island seriously" (002-452). When Crampton is revealed as the father, Philip's arbitration becomes explicit: "I desire to consider your feelings in every possible way: but I warn you that if you stretch the long arm of coincidence to the length of telling me that Mr. Crampton of this town is my father, I shall decline to entertain the information" (002-484-486). His verdict is swift and wrong.

The running joke peaks in the family conference when Philip plants himself "in his best head-of-the-family attitude" (003-1021). Yet his arbitration softens: he is the first to propose giving Crampton another chance. By Act IV, his "Coming, dad, coming" (004-1336) marks the transition from arbiter to son.

## Key Passages

> [001-191] Philip opens his case to Mrs. Clandon: "Dolly and I have been talking over things a good deal lately; and I don't think, judging from my knowledge of human nature -- we don't think that you quite appreciate the fact --" -- Dolly completes: "That we've grown up."

> [002-452] "My knowledge of human nature is fairly extensive, Mr. McComas; but I find it impossible to take the inhabitants of this island seriously." -- Philip's comic provincialism, judging England from a Madeiran perspective.

> [002-484-486] "I have seen the gentleman; and he is entirely unfit to be my father, or Dolly's father, or Gloria's father, or my mother's husband." -- Philip pronouncing judgment on Crampton before knowing the facts.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses Philip's "knowledge of human nature" as a satire on masculine authority claims. Philip, at perhaps eighteen, adopts the same rhetorical posture as the Victorian fathers Shaw despised -- yet unlike them, Philip is self-aware enough to be funny. The irony is that Philip's actual observations are often correct (Crampton is unfit; the lunch is a disaster), but his reasoning is absurd. Shaw suggests that "knowledge of human nature" is always a mask for prejudice; what matters is the flexibility to change one's mind, which Philip demonstrates.
