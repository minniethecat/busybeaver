## Definition

The "well-made play" (*pièce bien faite*), the dominant form of nineteenth-century French and English drama, provided Shaw with a structural template that he both used and transformed. The form's characteristic elements — a secret known to the audience but not the characters, a series of revelations, a climactic confrontation, a logical resolution — are all present in *You Never Can Tell*. But Shaw subverts the form by making the secret's revelation an occasion for moral inquiry rather than melodramatic sensation, and by replacing the conventional romantic climax with rational negotiation.

## In the Play

The family secret functions as the well-made play's central mechanism: the audience learns early that Crampton is the children's father, and the dramatic interest lies in watching the characters discover what the audience already knows. But Shaw's treatment differs from the Scribean model in his refusal to exploit the secret for suspense or sentiment. The revelation is gradual, mediated, and ultimately redemptive rather than destructive. The well-made play's machinery is present but repurposed for Shaw's intellectual and moral ends.
