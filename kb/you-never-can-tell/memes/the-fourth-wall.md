# Breaking the Fourth Wall

## Definition

The play's implicit acknowledgment of its own theatricality — including Shaw's stage directions and the deliberately artificial resolution — as a meta-theatrical strategy that engages the audience's critical intelligence.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Shaw never lets the audience forget they are watching a play. The stage directions, written to be read as much as performed, address the reader directly. The resolution is explicitly artificial — Bohun as deus ex machina, the marriages as legal constructs. This meta-theatricality is Shaw's democratic gesture: the audience is invited to think critically about what they are watching rather than being manipulated into emotional response. The play is an argument, and the audience is expected to engage with it intellectually.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
