# Mrs. Clandon

## Role

Mrs. Clandon is the matriarchal center of *You Never Can Tell* — the intellectual architect whose decisions eighteen years before the play's action set every subsequent event in motion. She is a first-generation [[new-woman|New Woman]]: author, lecturer, rationalist, and mother by choice rather than by convention. Her role is both dramatic (the estranged wife who must be reconciled) and philosophical (the progressive who has proven that women can raise children without patriarchal authority).

## Biography

Eighteen years before the play opens, Mrs. Clandon left her husband, taking their three children to [[madeira|Madeira]]. Whether [[crampton|Crampton]] was actively cruel or merely conventionally patriarchal, she determined that her children would not be raised under his influence. In [[madeira|Madeira]] she wrote the *Twentieth Century* series of treatises — *Twentieth Century Cooking*, *Creeds*, *Clothing*, *Conduct*, *Children*, *Parents*, and *Women* — and became "an authoress of great repute" (001-004). She supported her family through her writing and lecturing, proving that a single mother could be economically independent. She has now returned to England, feeling "I had no right to bury [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] alive in [[madeira|Madeira]] — my St. Helena" (002-005).

## Key Scenes

> Her encounter with McComas at the Marine Hotel after eighteen years. She recognizes him only after scrutinizing his face: "Where's your beard?" The exchange reveals what has and hasn't changed — she is as radical as ever, he has become "respectable."
> (002-005)

> McComas's catalog of her intellectual commitments — Darwin, Mill, Huxley, George Eliot, women's suffrage, university degrees, the opening of professions — to each of which she nods, confirming she has "not gone back one inch."
> (002-005)

> The climactic confrontation with Crampton, in which the terms of their reconciliation are negotiated through the combined efforts of McComas, Bohun, and the children.
> (004-002)

## Relationships

- **[[crampton|Crampton]]** — estranged husband. She left him eighteen years ago and has raised their children without him. The play's resolution requires her to accept his return on terms of equality.
- **[[gloria-clandon|Gloria Clandon]]** — eldest daughter, the "[[new-woman|Woman of the Twentieth Century]]." Mrs. Clandon has educated her to carry the progressive project forward.
- **[[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] and [[philip-clandon|Philip Clandon]]** — younger children. They treat her principles with affectionate irreverence, quoting the *[[twentieth-century-women|Twentieth Century Treatises]]* as conversational weapons.
- **[[mc-comas|Finch McComas]]** — old friend and family solicitor, one of the few people who remembers her before she became "Mrs. Clandon."

## Themes

Mrs. Clandon embodies the play's argument that women can be the intellectual and moral equals of men — and that this equality requires institutional as well as personal transformation. Her life is proof that the [[new-woman|New Woman]] is viable, but the play also examines the costs of her experiment: three children raised without knowledge of their father, a daughter "buried alive in [[madeira|Madeira]]."
