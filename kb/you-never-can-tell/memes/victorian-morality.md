## Definition

Victorian morality in *You Never Can Tell* is the system of values against which Shaw's characters define themselves. The play subjects conventional Victorian pieties — respectability, filial duty, feminine modesty, paternal authority — to sustained comic scrutiny, revealing them as social constructs that serve particular interests rather than universal truths. Shaw's alternative is not amorality but a reconstructed morality based on honesty, mutual respect, and the acknowledgment of natural impulse.

## In the Play

The play's central moral confrontation occurs when Mrs. Clandon reveals that she left her husband because he beat her. Victorian morality would condemn a wife who leaves her husband; Mrs. Clandon's action, and Shaw's endorsement of it, inverts this judgment. Similarly, Gloria's willingness to consider Valentine's proposal despite her principles reflects Shaw's argument that moral codes must accommodate human nature — a morality that demands the suppression of love is, for Shaw, no morality at all.
