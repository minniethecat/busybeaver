# Fabian Society Context

## Definition

The Fabian Society — the gradualist socialist organization of which Shaw was a leading member — as the political context for the play's ideas about social reform through reason and professional expertise.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Shaw joined the Fabian Society in 1884 and remained committed throughout his life. Fabianism advocated gradual, rational reform rather than revolution — transformation through argument, legislation, and professional expertise. This is dramatized in *You Never Can Tell* through Bohun's legal mediation, which achieves through reasoned process what sentiment could not. The play is a Fabian comedy: social problems solved not by revolution but by professional intelligence. Bohun is the Fabian ideal — the expert whose competence transforms chaos into order.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
