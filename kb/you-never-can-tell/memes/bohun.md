# Bohun

## Role

Bohun is the play's deus ex machina — a Queen's Counsel whose forensic ferocity is so overwhelming that it becomes a form of [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]]. Summoned to resolve the Clandon family's legal and emotional impasse, he takes command of the proceedings, cross-examines every party in turn, and delivers a judgment that no court could issue but that everyone accepts. He is Shaw's demonstration that authority, properly deployed, can serve liberation rather than oppression. His entrance in Act IV produces "general awe," and he places himself "magisterially in the centre of the group" before subduing the whole company. (004-002)

## Character

Shaw presents Bohun as a man whose personality has been consumed by his profession. He does not converse; he pounces. He silences interruptions by "annihilating him with a vocal thunderbolt" and "drowning them in two thunderous words," and he throws himself back in his chair "with an air of being prepared to listen tolerantly to their grievances." His self-description is exact: "My specialty is being right when other people are wrong. If you agreed with me I should be of no use here." (004-002)

Yet within this forensic carapace he delivers the play's most humane judgments — that the Clandons are a family, that [[crampton|Crampton]] must be acknowledged, and that love should not be obstructed by legal technicality. Even his cynicism is oddly liberating: "all matches are unwise. It's unwise to be born; it's unwise to be married; it's unwise to live; and it's unwise to die." (004-002) The only person who is not afraid of him is [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]], who reads him instantly — "You're going to bully us, Mr. Bohun… I know by your eyebrows" — and whose "method," he concedes, "is right." (004-002)

## Key Scenes

> (004-002) Bohun's entrance and command of the family conference. He cross-examines each party in turn, reducing their tangled emotional grievances to crisp questions of fact — pouncing on Crampton's inconsistencies, waving down McComas, and pressing his questions until the real issues emerge from beneath the sentiment.

> (004-002) The resolution: after settling the "trifles that will wreck you at the harbor mouth," Bohun turns to the engagement, advises Valentine to "insist on a settlement," and dances Gloria off "in a most powerful manner." His authority transforms emotional chaos into practical order.

## Relationships

- **[[mc-comas|McComas]]** — the solicitor who defers to him; Bohun overrides him at every turn ("McComas: you're making yourself ridiculous. Sit down").
- **[[walter-the-waiter|Walter the Waiter]]** — revealed to be Bohun's father. The recognition scene — the great Q.C. and the humble waiter — is the play's central surprise, and Walter watches "the working of his trained and powerful mind" with paternal pride.
- **The Clandons** — the family he reassembles through legal alchemy.

## Themes

Bohun represents Shaw's argument that professional competence, genuinely practiced, can be a force for good rather than oppression. His terrifying manner is not cruelty but rigor — the refusal to let sentiment obstruct clarity. In a play about the failure of patriarchal authority, Bohun demonstrates that authority can be reconstructed on professional rather than hereditary grounds — and, in the revelation that this Q.C. is a waiter's son, that it need not be inherited at all.
