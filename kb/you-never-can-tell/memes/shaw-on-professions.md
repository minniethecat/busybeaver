# Shaw on Professions

## Definition

*You Never Can Tell* stages a sustained critique of professional identity as social performance. Every character with a formal profession -- Valentine the dentist, McComas the solicitor, Bohun the Q.C., even Crampton the yacht builder -- is shown to be performing a role that conceals as much as it reveals. The Waiter, whose profession explicitly involves performance ("putting on a dress coat"), is paradoxically the most authentic character on stage. Shaw's argument is that professional identity is always a costume, and wisdom consists in recognizing this rather than mistaking the costume for the self.

## Context

The late Victorian period saw the consolidation of professions as markers of middle-class respectability. The professional classes -- lawyers, doctors, dentists, clergymen -- derived status from their perceived disinterested expertise. Shaw, who had worked as a clerk and a critic before becoming a playwright, was skeptical of professional mystique. His Fabian socialism inclined him to see professions as guilds that protected their members' interests while claiming to serve the public. The figure of the "gentleman professional" was a particular target: Shaw saw the performance of gentlemanliness as a class strategy.

## In the Play

Valentine is introduced as a performer of professionalism whose heart isn't in it: "his professional manner evidently strikes him as being a joke" (001-037). He has abandoned a medical career because he "acted conscientiously, and told my patients the brute truth instead of what they wanted to be told" (001-150). Dentistry is his last chance at respectability -- and he fails at it, making five shillings in six weeks.

McComas's professional identity is literally his disguise: "Would you employ a solicitor with a beard? ... Would you employ a solicitor with a sombrero?" (002-417-419). He has sacrificed his bohemian past for professional standing.

Bohun is the play's most formidable professional, and the most explicitly performative -- he enters in a domino and false nose, and his legal expertise is indistinguishable from theatrical intimidation.

The Waiter's analysis is definitive: "If I must put on a dress coat to show what I am, sir, he must put on a wig and gown to show what he is. If my income is mostly tips, and there's a pretence that I don't get them, why, his income is mostly fees, sir; and I understand there's a pretence that he don't get them!" (002-717).

## Key Passages

> [001-037] Stage direction on Valentine: "his professional manner evidently strikes him as being a joke, and is underlain by a thoughtless pleasantry which betrays the young gentleman still unsettled and in search of amusing adventures, behind the newly set up dentist in search of patients."

> [002-717] The Waiter's comparison of his profession with his son's: "If I must put on a dress coat to show what I am, sir, he must put on a wig and gown to show what he is." -- The levelling insight that all professions are costumes.

> [002-417-419] McComas on his professional appearance: "Would you employ a solicitor with a beard? ... Would you employ a solicitor with a sombrero?" -- Professional identity as calculated self-suppression.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's target is not the professions themselves but the pretence that professional status confers moral authority. Valentine is a better man when he abandons professional prudence for love. The Waiter, who makes no claims to professional dignity, has more dignity than anyone on stage. Bohun's professional brilliance is real but irrelevant to the human needs of the situation -- it is the Waiter, not the Q.C., who resolves the emotional impasse. Shaw's radical proposition is that professional competence and human wisdom are unrelated, and that the hierarchy that ranks the Q.C. above the waiter is a social fiction.
