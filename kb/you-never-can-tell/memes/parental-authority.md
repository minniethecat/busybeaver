## Definition

Parental authority in *You Never Can Tell* is subjected to the same critical scrutiny Shaw applies to all forms of unearned power. Paternal authority — Crampton's claim to his children's obedience by virtue of biology — is exposed as morally empty, a relic of patriarchal assumptions the play works to dismantle. But maternal authority — Mrs. Clandon's control over her children's knowledge of their origins — is also questioned, suggesting that any parental power exercised without accountability can become a form of tyranny.

## In the Play

The play stages a negotiation between competing models of parental authority. Crampton's model is traditional and authoritarian — he expects deference from children he has never raised. Mrs. Clandon's model is progressive and protective — she has shielded her children from a violent father but also from knowledge of their own history. The resolution rejects both models in their pure form: Crampton must earn his children's respect through genuine repentance, while Mrs. Clandon must accept that her children have a right to know and judge their father for themselves.
