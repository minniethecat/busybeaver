# New Woman

## Definition

The "New Woman" was a feminist ideal that emerged in the late nineteenth century — a woman who claimed intellectual, professional, and personal autonomy in defiance of Victorian domestic ideology. She sought education, economic independence, and the right to define her own life rather than being defined by her relationships to men. In *You Never Can Tell*, Shaw presents not one but a spectrum of New Women across three generations: [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]], the pioneering author and lecturer; [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]], the "Woman of the Twentieth Century"; and [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]], whose irreverent intelligence suggests the next evolution of female independence. [001-001]

## Context

The New Woman movement gained prominence in the 1890s, fueled by debates over female education, married women's [[money-and-status|property]] rights, and the suffrage campaign. Writers like Sarah Grand, Mona Caird, and Olive Schreiner articulated the new ideal in fiction, while public figures like George Eliot and Annie Besant demonstrated its viability. Shaw was a committed supporter of women's rights — a Fabian socialist who served on the executive of the London Society for Women's Suffrage. His plays systematically dismantle the Victorian ideology of separate spheres, and *You Never Can Tell* is among his most sustained explorations of what happens when women are raised without patriarchal assumptions.

## In the Play

[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] embodies the first-generation New Woman: an author of progressive treatises who fled an unhappy marriage, raised three children alone, and built a career through public lectures. Her *Twentieth Century* series — *Twentieth Century Cooking*, *Twentieth Century Creeds*, *Twentieth Century Clothing*, *Twentieth Century Conduct*, *Twentieth Century Children*, and *Twentieth Century Parents* — constitutes a complete program for remaking domestic life on rational principles (001-004). [[mc-comas|McComas]]'s summary of her achievements is an inventory of New Woman commitments: "to make speeches in public, in spite of your sex … to insist on a married woman's right to her own separate [[money-and-status|property]] … to champion Darwin's view … and John Stuart Mill's essay on [[liberty|Liberty]] … to read Huxley, Tyndall and George Eliot … and to demand University degrees, the opening of the professions, and the parliamentary franchise for women" (002-005).

[[gloria-clandon|Gloria]], the next generation, is the pure product of her mother's experiment. Raised without fathers, never taught to defer to male authority, she is [[valentine|Valentine]]'s equal in intellect and his superior in self-possession. The play's romantic plot is driven by whether she can integrate love into her rationally-ordered identity without surrendering the independence her mother fought for.

[[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] represents a third generation — the irreverent inheritor who takes female autonomy for granted. She treats the *[[twentieth-century-women|Twentieth Century Treatises]]* as household jokes ("Inaccessible to rhetoric," she quips, when [[philip-clandon|Philip]] cites them (001-004)) and navigates the world with an insouciance that suggests the New Woman has become her natural habitat rather than a political project.

## Key Passages

> (001-004) "We are the children of the celebrated Mrs. Lanfrey Clandon, an authoress of great repute — in Madeira. No household is complete without her works. … They are called the Twentieth Century Treatises." — **Philip**

[[philip-clandon|Philip]]'s introduction of the family background, revealing both the scope of [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s intellectual project and the children's playful detachment from it.

> (002-005) "Bless me! And you are still ready to make speeches in public, in spite of your sex … to champion Darwin's view of the origin of species and John Stuart Mill's essay on Liberty … and to demand University degrees, the opening of the professions, and the parliamentary franchise for women as well as men?" — **McComas**

[[mc-comas|McComas]]'s astonished summary of [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s progressive commitments catalogs the New Woman agenda in full.

> (003-002) "What happens in the duel of sex? The old fashioned mother received an old fashioned education to protect her against the wiles of man. … So she gave her daughter a scientific education — your plan. That was a corker for the old fashioned man." — **Valentine**

[[valentine|Valentine]]'s analysis of the New Woman's strategic advantage, recognizing that scientific education is armor against patriarchal seduction.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of the New Woman is characteristically dialectical: he presents the ideal as clearly superior to its Victorian predecessor, but he does not sentimentalize it. [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s experiment in rational motherhood has real costs — her children don't know their father, and [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] has been raised without the emotional education that would prepare her for love. The play's resolution requires the New Woman to claim her right to [[love-and-marriage|romance]] without sacrificing her intellectual autonomy.

Shaw's deeper argument is that the New Woman is not a threat to men but a liberation for both sexes. [[valentine|Valentine]] thrives precisely because he is willing to meet [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] on terms of equality. The "duel of sex" resolves not through domination but through mutual recognition — an outcome impossible under the old dispensation.
