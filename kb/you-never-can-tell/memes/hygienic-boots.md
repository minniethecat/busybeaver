## Definition

Hygienic boots were a product of the late-Victorian health reform movement: flat-heeled, wide-toed boots designed to accommodate the natural shape of the foot rather than the pointed, heeled boots of fashion. In *You Never Can Tell*, they appear as a comic detail in [[Valentine]]'s orientation of the [[Clandon children]] to English seaside society: "the rector's wife advocates dress reform and wears hygienic boots" (001-145). [[Dolly Clandon]]'s immediate response—furtively looking at her own shoe—triggers Valentine's deft clarification: "No, that's not the sort of boot I mean. Dolly's shoe vanishes" (001-145).

## Context

The hygienic boot was part of the broader rational dress movement that sought to reform women's clothing on health grounds. Conventional Victorian women's boots were constructed on a straight last (meaning the left and right boots were identical), had elevated heels, and compressed the toes into a narrow point—all of which caused foot deformities and back problems. The hygienic boot, by contrast, was built on an anatomical last (different for left and right feet), had a low or flat heel, and allowed the toes to spread naturally. It was associated with progressive politics, the women's movement, and the general revolt against fashion's tyranny over the female body.

## In the Play

The hygienic boot reference serves several functions. First, it establishes the seaside resort as a space of relative social freedom where advanced ideas have achieved a degree of middle-class respectability—the rector's *wife*, not some bohemian outsider, wears them. Second, it introduces the theme of dress as social signification: what people wear tells observers about their political and intellectual allegiances. Third, it provides a characteristically Shavian comic moment: Dolly's reflexive shoe-check and Valentine's gentle correction reveal her insecurity about English social codes and his practiced skill in managing social situations.

The detail also participates in the play's broader interest in feet and footwear as markers of modernity. [[Lord de Cresci]]'s sister bicycles in knickerbockers (001-145), another reform garment associated with foot freedom. The beach setting itself is a place where shoes are removed and feet are liberated—a physical correlate of the social loosening the play dramatizes.

The hygienic boot belongs to a cluster of references that position the play's 1896 present at a specific moment in the history of bodily reform. The rector's wife's boots are advanced by the standards of the town but modest by the standards of the movement. They represent incremental rather than revolutionary change—a foot in both worlds, so to speak.

## Shaw's Commentary

The hygienic boot is a minor detail that carries major thematic weight. It exemplifies Shaw's technique of using material culture to index social change: the reform of the foot stands for the reform of society. The gentle comedy of the reference—Dolly's anxious self-inspection, Valentine's smooth correction—prevents the detail from becoming didactic while still communicating the play's progressive sympathies. The hygienic boot is also a marker of the play's historical moment: by 1896, dress reform had progressed far enough to be adopted by a rector's wife but not so far as to be unremarkable. The detail thus precisely locates the play at the intersection of Victorian convention and modern emancipation—the terrain it occupies throughout.
