## Definition

Dentistry in the 1890s, when *You Never Can Tell* is set, was a profession in transition — moving from the barber-surgeon tradition toward modern medical respectability. Valentine's position as a "five-shilling dentist" reflects this transitional status: he is a trained professional (his M.R.C.S. diploma hangs on the wall) but his practice is precarious, his equipment is hired, and his patients are few. Shaw uses dentistry as a lens through which to examine the broader condition of the modern professional class.

## In the Play

Valentine's dental office is described in elaborate detail, with its "operating chair, with a gas pump and cylinder beside it," its "cabinet of instruments," and its "dental drill" that makes the audience "shudder and look away" [001-040]. The professional apparatus is both impressive and slightly menacing — a fitting emblem for a profession that extracts (teeth, truths) for a living. Valentine's five-shilling flat fee represents a democratic approach to professional service, refusing to calibrate treatment to the patient's social rank.
