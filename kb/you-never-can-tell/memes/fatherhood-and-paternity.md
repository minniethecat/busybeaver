## Definition

Fatherhood and paternity is a central thematic concern of *You Never Can Tell*, raised in the play's opening scene when [[Valentine]] tells the twins that "in a seaside resort there's one thing you must have before anybody can afford to be seen going about with you; and that's a father, alive or dead" (001-145). The play explores fatherhood as biological fact, social role, legal status, and emotional relationship—often in tension with one another—through the figure of [[Fergus Crampton]] and his children's search for identity.

## Context

The late Victorian period saw significant anxiety about fatherhood. The patriarchal authority that had defined Victorian family life was being challenged by women's rights movements, while the rise of the "New Woman" and changing divorce laws made paternity more legally contestable. Shaw's own father, George Carr Shaw, was an unsuccessful businessman and alcoholic, giving Shaw a personal stake in thinking about what made a father worthy of the name. The play's treatment of fatherhood is characteristically Shavian in refusing either to idealize or to dismiss the paternal role.

## In the Play

The father question is raised immediately through the twins' predicament. [[Dolly Clandon]] and [[Philip Clandon]] do not know their father's identity, and this ignorance makes them socially vulnerable in England (001-145). The children demand that [[Mrs. Clandon]] tell them, triggering her anguished revelation about the whip: "That would have been your last recollection of your father, Gloria, if I had not taken you away from him" (001-259). This statement defines biological paternity as potential violence, setting the terms for the play's subsequent rehabilitation of Crampton.

The play then systematically tests different models of fatherhood. [[Valentine]] briefly becomes a paternal figure to the twins. [[Walter the Waiter]] is recruited by [[Philip Clandon]] as a surrogate father: "William: you remember my request to you to regard me as your son?" Even the waiter's own fatherhood is examined through his son [[Bohun]]'s remarkable rise from waiter's son to Queen's Counsel.

The rehabilitation of Crampton as father proceeds through the recognition that his paternal feelings, however badly expressed, are genuine. [[McComas]] pleads that Crampton, for all his abominable temper, "need[s] affection as much as the rest of us." Gloria's recognition of her paternal inheritance acknowledges the inheritance of temperament. And Crampton's final transformation—donning a false nose and dancing into the garden—shows a father learning to be absurd for his children's sake.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of fatherhood rejects both patriarchal absolutism and the radical feminist erasure of paternity. Crampton's claim to his children is real but not absolute: he must earn the relationship he demands as a right. The play endorses neither Mrs. Clandon's view (father as threat to be excluded) nor Crampton's view (father as authority to be obeyed), but a third position in which paternity is a relationship negotiated among flawed individuals. The waiter and Bohun demonstrate that fatherhood can be successful across vast class distances through mutual respect and affection. The Clandons and Crampton demonstrate that even badly damaged paternity can be restored if all parties accept imperfection.
