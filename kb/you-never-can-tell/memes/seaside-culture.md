## Definition

The English seaside resort in *You Never Can Tell* provides more than a picturesque backdrop — it constitutes a distinctive social environment with its own codes, rhythms, and possibilities. Shaw chose a "fashionable watering place" on the Devon coast as his setting in part because the seaside resort was, in the 1890s, a liminal space where social conventions were simultaneously heightened and relaxed. Strangers mingled, holidays suspended routine, and the usual markers of status (home, neighborhood, parish) were replaced by the more fluid currency of hotel society.

## In the Play

Valentine's warning to the twins — "have you ever been in an English seaside resort before? ... it's not a question of manners and appearance" — introduces the resort as a social space with its own demands [001-145]. The Marine Hotel, with its terraces, dining room, and promenading guests, functions as a stage on which the characters perform their social identities. The seaside setting also enables the play's compressed timeline: the events unfold over a single day, a compression made plausible by the holiday atmosphere.
