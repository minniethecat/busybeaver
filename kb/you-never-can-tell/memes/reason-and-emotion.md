## Definition

Reason and emotion in *You Never Can Tell* are presented not as opposites but as forces that must achieve equilibrium. Mrs. Clandon's rationalist philosophy, embodied in her *Twentieth Century Treatises*, has taught her children to privilege reason over sentiment — yet the play consistently demonstrates that emotional truth will not be suppressed. Valentine, the play's clearest spokesman for this view, argues that love is a biological imperative that reason can acknowledge but never override.

## In the Play

The conflict between reason and emotion is most sharply dramatized in Gloria's internal struggle. Raised to value intellect and independence, she finds herself drawn to Valentine despite her principles. Her resistance to love is not prudishness but a genuine philosophical position — she has been taught that romantic attachment compromises female autonomy. Valentine's counterargument, that love is natural and reason must accommodate nature, represents Shaw's own position: rationalism is valuable, but it must recognize the limits of its jurisdiction.
