## Definition

Forgiveness in *You Never Can Tell* is the mechanism by which the play achieves its comic resolution. Fergus Crampton's past violence toward his wife and his eighteen-year absence from his children's lives constitute real harms — not the trivial misunderstandings of conventional comedy. Shaw's achievement is to make forgiveness plausible without minimizing the offense, by showing that Crampton's remorse is genuine and that his children's willingness to forgive is an act of strength, not weakness. [004-004]

## In the Play

The question of whether Crampton can be forgiven structures the play's emotional arc. Mrs. Clandon, who suffered the original injury, has achieved a kind of negative peace — she has moved on, but she has not forgiven. The children, who did not suffer directly, are free to approach their father with curiosity rather than resentment. Gloria's capacity for forgiveness is particularly significant: as the most principled character, her acceptance of her father signals that forgiveness is not a betrayal of principle but a higher form of it.
