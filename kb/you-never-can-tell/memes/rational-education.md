## Definition

Rational education, as practiced by Mrs. Clandon and articulated in her *Twentieth Century Treatises*, is the philosophy that children should be raised through reason rather than authority, through mutual respect rather than coercion. This approach, radical for the 1890s, produces the Clandon children — spirited, intelligent, independent, and refreshingly free from Victorian inhibitions. Shaw presents rational education as a genuine advance over traditional methods, while also exploring its limitations and unintended consequences. [004-004]

## In the Play

The results of Mrs. Clandon's educational experiment are on display in the twins and Gloria. The twins are the play's most vivid testimony to rational education's success — they are charming, curious, and fundamentally decent. But Gloria's struggle with love suggests a limitation: rational education has not prepared her for the experience of emotion that resists rational analysis. Shaw's point is not to reject rational education but to suggest that it must make room for the non-rational dimensions of human experience.
