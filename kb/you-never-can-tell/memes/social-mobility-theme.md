# Social Mobility

## Definition

The play's exploration of social mobility through professional achievement — Valentine rising through dentistry, the Clandons through authorship, Bohun through law — as Shaw's argument for meritocracy.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Every major character in *You Never Can Tell* is engaged in some form of social mobility. Valentine is trying to establish himself as a professional despite his poverty. Mrs. Clandon has built a career as an authoress in exile. Bohun has risen to the top of the legal profession through sheer competence. Even Walter the waiter has achieved a form of professional apotheosis — so good at his job that it becomes philosophy. Shaw's point is that professional achievement, rather than inherited status, should determine social position.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
