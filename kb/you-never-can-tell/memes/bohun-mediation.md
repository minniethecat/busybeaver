## Definition

Bohun's mediation is the legal and theatrical centerpiece of Act IV, in which the eminent Queen's Counsel [[Bohun]]—revealed to be the son of [[Walter the Waiter]]—presides over an informal family conference at the [[Marine Hotel]] that resolves the eighteen-year estrangement between [[Fergus Crampton]] and his family. The mediation is extraordinary because it achieves in a single evening what eighteen years of separation and threatened litigation could not: a workable family reconciliation.

## Context

The mediation arises from the crisis created by the Clandon family's accidental reunion. [[Fergus Crampton]], having been brought to lunch by [[Valentine]] (who did not know the parties were related), finds himself confronting the wife and children who left him eighteen years ago. By Act III, Crampton is threatening legal action, demanding custody of his two younger children (003-002). [[Finch McComas]], the family solicitor, proposes an informal conference with counsel's opinion, and by chance encounters Bohun Q.C.—his own son's former pupil-master—who happens to be visiting the town for the sea air (003-002).

## In the Play

Bohun's entrance is deliberately theatrical: he arrives in a domino and false nose from the fancy dress ball (004-002). His removal of the disguise announces the stripping away of pretense that will characterize his approach. He immediately establishes absolute authority—"My name is Bohun"—and proceeds to dominate the room with his "powerful, menacing voice, impressively articulated speech, strong inexorable manner" (004-002).

Bohun's method is to dismantle every participant's pretensions. He silences [[Valentine]]'s interruptions (004-002), calls out [[Crampton]]'s evasions about the children's dress (004-002), and endorses [[Dolly Clandon]]'s Socratic questioning of her father (004-002). He identifies the core issues with surgical precision: the name dispute between Clandon and Crampton (004-002); the unworkability of Crampton living with his children (004-002); the inevitability that the daughters will marry (004-002); and the actual power balance—their personal charm versus his income (004-002).

The most remarkable disclosure is that Bohun is the waiter's son (004-002). [[Walter]] collapses emotionally: "A waiter for your father on the top of a false nose!" (004-002). This revelation transforms the social dynamics of the scene, demonstrating that status is accidental and that wisdom and dignity are matters of character—a theme the waiter himself has embodied throughout the play.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the mediation to advance several arguments simultaneously. First, the law as conventionally practiced is a blunt instrument—Bohun dismisses litigation as "all nonsense" because "your children will be of age before you could get the point decided" (004-002). Second, genuine resolution requires facing what Bohun calls "the trifles" rather than grand legal principles. Third, the mediator's effectiveness depends on his ability to see through self-deception—Bohun's relentless interruption of every speaker's opening platitudes serves this function. Finally, the revelation that the formidable Q.C. is the waiter's son reinforces the play's democratic thesis that competence and character transcend class.
