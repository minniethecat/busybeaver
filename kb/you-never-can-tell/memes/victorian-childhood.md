## Definition

Childhood in *You Never Can Tell* is presented as a site of ideological contest between Victorian authoritarianism and progressive reform. The Clandon children represent the reform model: raised with "mutual respect" and "independence and privacy," they are confident, articulate, and free from the fear and inhibition that characterized traditional Victorian child-rearing. The play implicitly asks: what kind of adults do different models of childhood produce?

## In the Play

The contrast between the Clandon children and what they might have been — had they been raised by Crampton under conventional Victorian discipline — is the play's implicit argument for progressive child-rearing. The twins' freedom, curiosity, and fundamental decency testify to their mother's methods. Yet Shaw also shows the limits of the progressive model: the children's ignorance of their own history is itself a form of constraint, suggesting that even the best-intentioned rearing cannot fully protect children from the complexities of adult life.
