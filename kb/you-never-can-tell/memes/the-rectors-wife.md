## Definition

The rector's wife appears in *You Never Can Tell* only as a brief reference in Valentine's description of English seaside freedom: "the rector's wife advocates dress reform and wears hygienic boots" [001-145]. This single line connects the play to the dress reform movement of the 1890s, in which progressive women challenged the physical constraints and health hazards of Victorian fashion. The rector's wife, by combining clerical respectability with reformist practice, embodies the diffusion of progressive ideas into the mainstream of English society.

## In the Play

The reference serves Valentine's argument that English society is more tolerant of innovation than the Clandons suppose. A rector's wife in hygienic boots represents the respectable face of reform — change endorsed by the establishment rather than threatening it. Shaw's choice of this figure to make his point is characteristic: social progress, he suggests, advances not through revolution but through the gradual adoption of sensible practices by sensible people.
