# The Legal Settlement

## Definition

The marriage settlements drafted by Bohun in Act IV — Shaw's dramatization of law as creative rather than restrictive, capable of establishing conditions for human flourishing.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

The legal settlement scene is unique in dramatic literature: a happy ending achieved through contract drafting. Bohun writes settlements specifying financial arrangements, property rights, and mutual obligations with conveyancing precision. For Shaw, this is genuinely romantic — a marriage built on clear terms is more likely to succeed than one built on illusion. The law becomes a tool for flourishing rather than merely a system of constraints. It is the Fabian vision in miniature.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
