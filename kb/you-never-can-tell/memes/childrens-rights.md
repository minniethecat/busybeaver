## Definition

Children's rights in *You Never Can Tell* refers to the play's sustained argument that children have legitimate claims against their parents—particularly the right to know their origins and the right to autonomy in their personal affairs. The issue is raised in Act I when the twins demand to know their father's identity and is developed throughout the play through the children's assertion of independence from both parents. The play neither endorses absolute parental authority nor absolute filial autonomy but negotiates a middle ground in which children's claims are recognized without denying parents' legitimate interests.

## Context

The concept of children's rights was embryonic in the 1890s. The Victorian family was organized around paternal authority, with children legally subordinate to their fathers until the age of majority (twenty-one). The custody reforms of the late nineteenth century had begun to recognize mothers' claims, but children were still treated as objects of parental rights rather than as rights-holders themselves. Shaw, drawing on his own experience of an unhappy childhood with an alcoholic father, was an early advocate for treating children as individuals with legitimate interests distinct from their parents'.

## In the Play

The children's rights claim is asserted most forcefully by [[Gloria Clandon]] when she declares: "Mother: we have a right to know" (001-004). This assertion, supported by the twins, represents a direct challenge to [[Mrs. Clandon]]'s authority. The mother's response—"Gloria! 'We!' Who is 'we'?"—registers her shock at being opposed by the daughter who had always been her ally. The children ground their demand in their mother's own principles: "Answer all your children's questions, and answer them truthfully", quoting *Twentieth Century Motherhood*.

Gloria provides the most compelling rationale for the right to know when she explains why she refused the steamship officer's proposal: "A woman who does not know who her father was cannot accept such an offer" (001-004). The ignorance imposed by her mother is not merely an inconvenience but an existential disability that prevents her from functioning as an adult in society.

The twins assert a different kind of children's right: the right to autonomy in dress and behavior. [[Dolly Clandon]]'s spirited defense of "dressing prettily" against her father's objections (004-002) and [[Philip Clandon]]'s resistance to being called "boy" both embody the claim that children, particularly near-adult children, have legitimate control over their own self-presentation.

The play's resolution acknowledges children's rights while contextualizing them. The children learn their father's identity—their right to know is vindicated. But they also learn that the knowledge brings responsibilities as well as rights. Gloria's recognition that she is "my father's daughter" (004-002) is an acceptance of an inheritance she did not choose, and the family's partial reconciliation requires concessions from all sides.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of children's rights is characteristically dialectical. He affirms the children's claim to know their origins and to exercise autonomy in personal matters, but he does not endorse the radical position that children are entirely independent of their parents. The play suggests that children's rights, like all rights, are relational: they exist within a web of mutual obligations that includes duties to parents as well as claims against them. The children's demand to know their father is legitimate, but so is Mrs. Clandon's need for privacy about a traumatic experience. The resolution—the children learn who their father is, Mrs. Clandon does not have to relive her trauma—is a compromise that acknowledges both sets of claims. Shaw's implicit argument is that children's rights, properly understood, are not a weapon against parents but a framework for negotiating the transition from childhood dependence to adult autonomy.
