# Twentieth Century Women

## Definition

*Twentieth Century Women* is the culminating volume of [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s series of progressive treatises, collectively known as the *Twentieth Century* series. The individual treatises — *Twentieth Century Cooking*, *Twentieth Century Creeds*, *Twentieth Century Clothing*, *Twentieth Century Conduct*, *Twentieth Century Children*, *Twentieth Century Parents*, and *Twentieth Century Women* — constitute a complete rationalist program for modernizing domestic life. Within the world of the play, these books are both [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s professional legacy and her children's inheritance of radical ideas, quoted and parodied in equal measure.

## Context

The *Twentieth Century* series belongs to the tradition of Victorian and Edwardian conduct literature — the genre of advice books that told middle-[[social-class|class]] readers how to run their homes, raise their children, and conduct their social lives. [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s innovation is to invert the genre's traditional premises: instead of reinforcing patriarchal and religious norms, her treatises advocate Darwinism, free thought, and female autonomy. Shaw's invention of this series was a nod to actual progressive publishing of the era, including works by the Fabian Women's Group and the Rationalist Press Association.

## In the Play

The treatises are introduced in Act I through [[philip-clandon|Philip]] and [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]]'s playful recitation of the titles: *Twentieth Century Cooking*, *Twentieth Century Creeds*, *Twentieth Century Clothing*, *Twentieth Century Conduct*, *Twentieth Century Children*, and *Twentieth Century Parents* (001-004). The list is capped by Philip's introduction of "the other member of the family: the Woman of the Twentieth Century; our sister Gloria!" — the treatises made flesh.

The treatises function as comic props throughout the play: [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] invokes *Twentieth Century Parents* ("Inaccessible to rhetoric. See *Twentieth Century Parents*, chapter on [[liberty|Liberty]], passim." (001-004)) to deflect [[philip-clandon|Philip]]'s arguments; and [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] herself is in Act III correcting the proofs of a new edition of *Twentieth Century Women* (003-002). The books are simultaneously a genuine intellectual achievement and a running family joke — the children have absorbed their principles so thoroughly that they can deploy them as conversational weapons.

## Key Passages

> (001-004) "We are the children of the celebrated Mrs. Lanfrey Clandon, an authoress of great repute — in Madeira. No household is complete without her works. We came to England to get away from them. They are called the Twentieth Century Treatises." — **Philip**

[[philip-clandon|Philip]]'s introduction reveals both the scope of [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s literary output and the children's affectionate irreverence toward it.

> (001-004) "*Twentieth Century Cooking*. … *Twentieth Century Creeds*. … *Twentieth Century Clothing*. … *Twentieth Century Conduct*. … *Twentieth Century Children*. … *Twentieth Century Parents*." — **Dolly and Philip, alternating**

The catalogue of titles demonstrates the series' ambition to reform every aspect of domestic life from a rational, progressive perspective.

> (003-002) "The new edition of *Twentieth Century Women*." — **Mrs. Clandon**

[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s answer, in Act III, when [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] asks what proofs she has been correcting — a quiet but confident assertion of her ongoing intellectual productivity, to which Gloria replies with a bitter smile that "There's a chapter missing."

## Shaw's Commentary

The *Twentieth Century* series is Shaw's affectionate satire of progressive publishing and the Fabian impulse to reform everything through rational instruction. The treatises' comprehensive ambition — from cooking to creeds — mirrors the socialist conviction that no aspect of life is too small for systematic improvement. Yet Shaw also shows the limits of the approach: [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s children have absorbed her principles but treat them as intellectual toys, and [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s [[scientific-education|rational education]] has left her unprepared for the emotional reality of love.

Shaw's title joke — that [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]], living in [[madeira|Madeira]], is writing the script for England's twentieth century — suggests the Fabian paradox: progressive intellectuals often had more influence over the future than over their present circumstances.
