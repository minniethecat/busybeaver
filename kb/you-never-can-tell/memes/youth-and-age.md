## Definition

Youth and age in *You Never Can Tell* structure the play's generational conflicts and alliances. The Clandon twins and Gloria represent youth in its various forms — irreverent, idealistic, passionate — while Mrs. Clandon, Crampton, McComas, and Bohun embody different models of age and authority. Shaw's sympathies are clearly with youth, but he refuses to sentimentalize it: the young characters are shown to be as capable of error as their elders, and the resolution depends on both generations finding common ground.

## In the Play

The twins' youthful energy disorients every adult they encounter. Valentine, at thirty-one, occupies an intermediate position — old enough to be conscious of his professional responsibilities, young enough to be drawn into the twins' orbit. His romance with Gloria bridges the generational divide: he must persuade her that love is not a concession to convention but a natural fulfillment. The play's resolution, in which the older generation (Crampton, Mrs. Clandon, Bohun) arranges the terms under which the young couple may marry, suggests that age and youth need each other — age provides structure, youth provides vitality.
