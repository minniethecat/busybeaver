## Definition

Philip Clandon, the twin brother of Dolly, is one of Shaw's most charming creations — a young man whose precocious self-possession, intellectual confidence, and instinctive courtesy make him "staggering to his elders" [001-100]. Philip embodies Shaw's ideal of the young person raised free from Victorian repression: he is curious without being impertinent, confident without being arrogant, and capable of serious moral inquiry beneath his playful surface.

## In the Play

Philip's role in the play's action is initiatory — it is he who demands that Mrs. Clandon reveal their father's identity, framing the question in terms of "mutual respect" that echo his mother's own principles. His "knowledge of human nature," a phrase he deploys with comic frequency, is both a genuine insight and a running joke — Philip understands more than his elders expect, but less than he thinks. His relationship with Dolly is the play's most consistently delightful element, their verbal duets creating a space of freedom and play within the more constrained adult drama.
