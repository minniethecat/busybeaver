# Waiter as Chorus

## Definition

The Waiter -- actually named Walter, nicknamed William by Dolly after the Shakespeare bust, and known locally as "Balmy Walters" -- functions as the play's deepest choric voice. Unlike Dolly's comic chorus or Philip's mock-arbitration, the Waiter's commentary carries genuine philosophical weight. He is the only character who articulates the play's title theme ("you never can tell") as a principle of life, and his serene acceptance of the world's contradictions makes him the moral compass against which all other characters are measured.

## Context

Shaw alludes to Shakespeare through Dolly's observation that the Waiter resembles "the bust of Shakespeare in Stratford Church" (002-410). The nickname "William" explicitly connects the Waiter to Shakespearean fools and wise servants -- figures like Feste in *Twelfth Night* or the Fool in *King Lear*, whose low station masks profound insight. The Waiter also embodies a distinctly Shavian ideal: the dignity of work well done, the philosophy that competence and kindness are more valuable than social status. His son is a Q.C. (Bohun), and his reflections on their reversed stations constitute some of the play's sharpest social commentary.

## In the Play

The Waiter appears in Acts II, III, and IV, always at the center of the family's unfolding drama. In Act II, he delivers the play's thematic statement: "You never can tell. That's a principle in life with me, sir" (002-715). His monologue comparing his own profession to his son's -- "If I must put on a dress coat to show what I am, sir, he must put on a wig and gown to show what he is" (002-717) -- is a miniature essay on the performativity of class.

The Waiter's reconciliation scene with his son Bohun in Act IV is the emotional climax of the subplot: "You in a false nose, Walter!" (004-1173). His resilience -- bouncing from humiliation to professional pride within minutes -- demonstrates the philosophy he preaches. His final speech to Valentine: "Every man is frightened of marriage when it comes to the point; but it often turns out very comfortable ... You never can tell, sir: you never can tell" (004-1406) closes the play's thematic arc.

## Key Passages

> [002-715] "Well, sir, you never can tell. That's a principle in life with me, sir, if you'll excuse my having such a thing, sir." -- The Waiter articulates the play's governing philosophy, grounded in the unexpected trajectory of his own son's career.

> [002-717] "If I must put on a dress coat to show what I am, sir, he must put on a wig and gown to show what he is. If my income is mostly tips, and there's a pretence that I don't get them, why, his income is mostly fees, sir; and I understand there's a pretence that he don't get them!" -- A brilliant Shavian analysis of class as performance.

> [004-1406] "Cheer up, sir, cheer up: every man is frightened of marriage when it comes to the point; but it often turns out very comfortable, very enjoyable and happy indeed, sir -- from time to time. ... You never can tell, sir: you never can tell." -- The Waiter's final benediction, embodying the play's comic wisdom.

## Shaw's Commentary

The Waiter is Shaw's answer to the problem of where wisdom resides. It does not reside in rank (Bohun), in money (Crampton), in intellect (Mrs. Clandon), or in education (Gloria). It resides in experience, kindness, and the humility to accept that "you never can tell." The Waiter's philosophy is not passivity -- he intervenes constantly, tactfully, to keep the peace -- but a recognition that outcomes cannot be controlled, only met with grace. Shaw's radical gesture is to make a servant the wisest character on stage, and to give him a son who is a Q.C. without this destroying their mutual affection.
