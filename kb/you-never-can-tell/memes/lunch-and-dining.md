## Definition

Lunch and dining in *You Never Can Tell* function as more than incidental meals — they are the play's central social ritual, the framework within which characters negotiate status, relationships, and reconciliation. The Marine Hotel's dining room serves as the play's primary setting after Act I, and the shared meal becomes a vehicle for Shaw's critique of social convention, class display, and the performance of civility.

## In the Play

The invitation to lunch structures the opening scenes: Valentine's acceptance depends entirely on whether the Clandons pass the social test of having a father [001-150]. The meal itself becomes a status performance — who pays, who hosts, who serves. Walter the Waiter, as the guardian of dining ritual, elevates service to an art form, and his running commentary on the lunch arrangements provides a chorus-like perspective on the social drama unfolding around the table.

The dining scenes at the Marine Hotel also stage the play's central confrontations. It is over lunch that Crampton first encounters his children; it is at the hotel that Bohun orchestrates the family settlement. The meal provides a veneer of civility under which profound emotional and social conflicts can be negotiated. Shaw's choice of the dining room as dramatic setting reflects his understanding that social rituals contain and channel the passions they pretend to exclude.

## Key Passages

> "Oh, do, Mr. Valentine. At the Marine Hotel — half past one." [001-145]

Dolly's impulsive lunch invitation sets the play's social machinery in motion.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's use of dining as dramatic framework draws on the comedy of manners tradition — from Congreve to Wilde — in which the meal is the arena of social performance. But Shaw inflects the convention with his own reformist sensibility: the meal is not merely an occasion for wit but a genuine site of social negotiation, where old grievances can be aired and new relationships forged.
