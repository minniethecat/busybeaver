# Walter the Waiter

## Role

Walter is the waiter at [[marine-hotel|the Marine]] Hotel and, by Shaw's design, the play's moral center. In a drama about professional identity, [[social-class|class]] anxiety, and the performance of [[victorian-respectability|respectability]], Walter is the one character whose professional performance is entirely authentic — his service is not a disguise but a vocation. Every other character, regardless of [[social-class|class]], defers to his judgment by the play's end. Shaw's point is characteristically paradoxical: the servant is the only character who is genuinely free. [001-004]

## Character

[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] calls him "William" because she thinks he resembles the bust of Shakespeare in Stratford Church (002-003). His real name is Walter. The double naming captures something essential about his role: he is simultaneously a generic servant (William) and a specific, irreplaceable individual (Walter). His [[professional-identity|professional manner]] is flawless — deferential without being subservient, knowing without being intrusive — and it earns him the respect of every character in the play, including the terrifying [[bohun|Bohun]].

## Key Scenes

> (002-003) Walter's introduction: "A silky old man, white-haired and delicate looking" who navigates the social complexities of the Marine Hotel with a grace that makes class boundaries seem irrelevant. Mrs. Clandon's affection for him is genuine and uncondescending.

> (004-002) Walter's interaction with Bohun: the Q.C. is revealed to be his son ("You will excuse him, Mrs. Clandon, when I inform you that he is my father"), and Walter meets even this formidable presence with recovered composure — recognizing a fellow professional across the gulf of station.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]** — calls him William, treats him with genuine warmth
- **[[bohun|Bohun]]** — the Q.C. who is, improbably, Walter's own son
- **The Clandon children** — treat him as a co-conspirator in the luncheon's [[comedy-of-manners|social comedy]]

## Themes

Walter embodies the Shavian argument that dignity is a function of competence, not [[social-class|class]]. He is the play's most secure character — free from the anxieties that plague [[valentine|Valentine]] (money), [[crampton|Crampton]] (recognition), [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] (legacy), and [[bohun|Bohun]] (the loneliness of terror). His service is his [[liberty|freedom]].

## Quotes

> (004-002) "That depends a good deal on one's station in life, sir. If you were a waiter, sir, you'd find that simple faith would leave you just as short as Norman blood. I find it best to spell myself *B*-double-*O*-*N*, and to keep my wits pretty sharp about me." — **Walter**

Walter's philosophy of service, delivered to Crampton: professional identity as clear-eyed moral realism rather than deference.

> (002-003) "She thought me like the bust of Shakespeare in Stratford Church, sir. That is why she calls me William, sir. My real name is Walter, sir." — **Walter**

The waiter's serene account of his double name — the generic "William" bestowed by others, the specific "Walter" he quietly keeps for himself.
