## Definition

The Life Force is Shaw's term for the evolutionary impetus that drives living beings toward greater complexity, consciousness, and creative achievement. Though the term is not used explicitly in *You Never Can Tell*, the concept pervades the play's treatment of love, attraction, and family. It is most clearly articulated in [[Valentine]]'s description of Nature seizing two people "by the scruffs of our little necks, and use us, in spite of ourselves, for her own purposes, in her own way" (002-005). The Life Force is the philosophical underpinning of the play's insistence that reason cannot finally resist passion.

## Context

Shaw developed the concept of the Life Force most fully in *Man and Superman* (1903), but its elements are already present in *You Never Can Tell* (1897). Drawing on Schopenhauer's concept of the Will, Bergson's elan vital, and Lamarckian evolutionary theory, Shaw posited a purposeful drive in nature that uses individual organisms—particularly through sexual attraction—to achieve its ends. The concept allowed Shaw to reconcile his rationalist commitments with his recognition of the power of irrational forces in human life.

## In the Play

The Life Force operates most visibly through [[Gloria Clandon]] and [[Valentine]]. Both enter the play committed to rational control of their lives: Gloria through her mother's advanced principles, Valentine through his cynical mastery of the "duel of sex" (003-002). Both are overwhelmed by a force they cannot rationalize. Valentine describes it as a physical transformation: "my blood got—oxygenated: my muscles braced; my mind cleared; my courage rose" (002-005). His "chemistry" metaphor (002-005) is a rationalist's attempt to name what is happening, but the experience transcends the metaphor.

The same force drives the family plot. The accidental reunion of the Cramptons after eighteen years, the children's demand to know their father, the reconciliation at the ball—all can be understood as the Life Force working through individuals to restore a broken family unit. [[Mrs. Clandon]]'s own description of her marriage—"I married before I was old enough to know what I was doing" (003-002)—is an account of the Life Force operating without the guidance of consciousness.

The play's resolution suggests that the proper response to the Life Force is neither resistance nor blind submission but conscious cooperation. Gloria and Valentine's engagement, with its legal settlement and public acknowledgment, represents the Life Force channeled through reason rather than suppressed by it. The dance that concludes the play—the entire company whirling into the garden—is a physical image of the Life Force in joyful operation.

## Shaw's Commentary

The Life Force concept allows Shaw to maintain a materialist, evolutionary worldview while acknowledging experiences that seem to transcend materialism—love, creative inspiration, the drive toward justice. In *You Never Can Tell*, the concept functions primarily as a critique of Mrs. Clandon's rationalism. Her attempt to educate passion out of her daughters is doomed because passion is not a social convention but a biological imperative. The play's happy ending is not a sentimental concession but a philosophical statement: the Life Force achieves its purposes through comedy as readily as through tragedy. The dance that replaces the duel is the Life Force's victory over the conventions that would deny it.
