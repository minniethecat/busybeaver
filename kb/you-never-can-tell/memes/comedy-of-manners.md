# Comedy of Manners

## Definition

Comedy of manners is a dramatic genre, originating in Restoration England, that satirizes the social conventions of a particular [[social-class|class]] through witty dialogue, sexual intrigue, and the exposure of hypocrisy. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* is a late-Victorian revival and transformation of the form: it retains the witty repartee, the seaside setting, the romantic entanglements, and the climactic revelation of identity, but it replaces the genre's cynicism with Shavian moral seriousness. The play is a comedy of manners in which the manners under scrutiny are those of the [[new-woman|New Woman]], the professional [[social-class|class]], and the Victorian family. [001-004]

## Context

The comedy of manners flourished in Restoration England (1660-1700) with playwrights like [[walter-the-waiter|William]] Congreve and [[walter-the-waiter|William]] Wycherley, who used the form to satirize the affected speech and sexual hypocrisy of the aristocracy. The genre was revived in the late nineteenth century by Oscar Wilde (*The Importance of Being Earnest*, 1895) and Arthur Wing Pinero. Shaw admired Wilde's wit but found the genre's moral emptiness unsatisfying; *You Never Can Tell* is his attempt to write a comedy of manners with philosophical content — a play as witty as Wilde's but with ideas as serious as Ibsen's.

## In the Play

The [[marine-hotel|Marine Hotel]] functions as the genre's traditional liminal space — a place where normal social rules are suspended and characters can encounter each other outside their usual contexts. The luncheon party in Act II is a [[social-class|class]]ic comedy-of-manners set piece: characters from incompatible social worlds (the intellectual Clandons, the professional [[valentine|Valentine]], the estranged father [[crampton|Crampton]]) forced into shared social ritual, generating friction and revelation.

Shaw's subversion of the genre lies in his treatment of the conventional romantic resolution. A traditional comedy of manners ends with the lovers united through a contrivance that preserves social appearances. Shaw ends with a genuine moral reconciliation — the estranged family reconstituted on terms of equality — that requires the dismantling of the very conventions the genre traditionally celebrates.

## Key Passages

> (002-005) "Allow me to discharge my first duty as host by ordering your wine." — **Philip**

A line that Congreve or Wilde might have written — the son performing host to his unrecognized father — but carrying philosophical weight about what makes a family.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's relationship to the comedy of manners is dialectical: he appropriates its pleasures while subverting its premises. The genre traditionally reinforced [[social-class|class]] hierarchy through laughter — we laugh at those who breach decorum, affirming the code. Shaw inverts this: we laugh at the code, not at those who breach it. The play's most sympathetic characters are those who treat social convention as optional, and its resolution depends on convention being overridden by genuine moral repair. Shaw takes the form's delight in verbal wit and channels it into philosophical argument — the play entertains like Wilde and persuades like Ibsen.
