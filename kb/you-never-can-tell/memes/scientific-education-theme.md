# Scientific Education

## Definition

The Clandon children's rational education based on the *Twentieth Century Treatises* — Shaw's exploration of what happens when children are raised according to progressive principles rather than traditional authority.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

The *Twentieth Century Treatises* — *Twentieth Century Cooking*, *Twentieth Century Creeds*, *Twentieth Century Clothing*, *Twentieth Century Conduct*, *Twentieth Century Children*, *Twentieth Century Parents* — are Mrs. Clandon's attempt to systematize progressive parenting into a commercial product. The twins quote them like scripture: "Answer all your children's questions, and answer them truthfully, as soon as they are old enough to ask them" (001-004). Shaw's treatment is characteristically double-edged: the principles are admirable but their application produces unexpected results. Progressive parenting, like all human endeavors, is subject to the law of unintended consequences.

## Key Passages

> (002-005) McComas's catalog of Mrs. Clandon's intellectual commitments maps the radical ideas shaping the play's worldview.

> (001-001) Shaw's detailed stage directions establish character with a precision that is itself a form of social analysis.

> (002-003) Walter the Waiter's philosophical contentment rebukes ambition as "vulgarity" and represents wisdom found in unexpected places.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
