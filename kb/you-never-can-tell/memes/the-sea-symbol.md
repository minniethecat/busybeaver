# The Sea as Symbol

## Definition

The sea as constant backdrop — visible from Valentine's office and audible from the Marine Hotel terrace — representing nature's indifference and the larger forces that Shaw believed shaped human destiny.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

The sea is a constant presence — visible from Valentine's dental office ("You have a good view of the sea from these rooms!" — 001-059), framing every terrace scene. Shaw uses it to suggest nature's larger rhythms: evolution, the Life Force, biological imperatives that underlie human reason. The characters debate and negotiate, but the sea is always there, suggesting something larger than intellectual argument is at work. It is the visual correlative of Shaw's philosophical naturalism.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
