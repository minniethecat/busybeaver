## Role

The cook at the Marine Hotel is an entirely offstage character, mentioned in passing but never appearing. She represents the invisible labor that sustains the social world of the play. While the Clandons, Valentine, and Crampton negotiate family reconciliation over lunch, the cook's work in the kitchen makes that negotiation possible. Her absence from the stage is itself significant — Shaw reminds us that every social ritual depends on work performed by people who go unseen and unnamed. [001-004]

## Biography

No biographical details are given; the cook exists only as an occupational reference. She is presumably a woman (domestic cooking in Victorian hotels was typically women's work) and one of several kitchen staff at the Marine Hotel.

## Key Scenes

The cook is referenced in Act II during the luncheon preparations. Walter the Waiter mentions her in connection with the meal service.
