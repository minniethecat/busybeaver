## Definition

The Gloria-and-Valentine romance is the central love plot of *You Never Can Tell*, tracing the courtship between [[gloria-clandon|Gloria Clandon]], the fiercely intellectual eldest daughter raised on advanced principles, and [[valentine|Valentine]], the charming but penniless "five-shilling dentist." Their romance is not a conventional love story but a Shavian dramatization of the "duel of sex"—a contest between rational principle and natural passion in which both participants are profoundly transformed.

## Context

The romance unfolds against the backdrop of late-Victorian debates about marriage, women's rights, and the proper relations between the sexes. Gloria has been educated by her mother to be a "Woman of the Twentieth Century"—rational, independent, and intellectually formidable. Valentine, by his own admission, is a ladies' man who "learnt how to circumvent the Women's Rights woman before I was twenty-three" (003-002). Their encounter thus becomes a test case for whether modern education can truly armor a woman against love.

## In the Play

Their first meeting in [[dentist-office|Valentine's dental office]] produces immediate chemistry—[[philip-clandon|Philip]] observes "Love at first sight" (001-004)—but the romance proper develops across Acts II and III. On the terrace at the [[marine-hotel|Marine Hotel]], Valentine and Gloria engage in an extraordinary dialogue that moves from intellectual banter about marriage conditions to Valentine's passionate declaration that she has changed his world (002-005). The key turning point occurs in the final act when Valentine, having deployed all his modern seduction techniques, finds himself genuinely overcome: "When the great moment came, who was awakened? who was stirred? in whom did the depths break up? In myself—myself" (004-002).

The romance reaches its crisis in Act III when Gloria discovers Valentine's previous love affairs. The confrontation in the hotel sitting room (003-002) tests whether their connection can survive the collision between her principles and his past. Their ultimate reconciliation in Act IV—when Gloria takes the initiative and proposes the engagement (004-002)—inverts conventional gender roles and demonstrates the Shavian principle that honesty about desire is the foundation of genuine partnership.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses this romance to dismantle both Victorian sentimentality and the rationalist feminism of the 1860s-80s. Valentine's "artillery" metaphor (003-002)—comparing courtship to the arms race between cannons and armor plate—is a devastating critique of Mrs. Clandon's educational project. The higher education of women, Valentine argues, only produced a more sophisticated kind of male seducer: "the Higher Education of Women delivered Gloria into my hands" (003-002). Yet Shaw also subverts Valentine's cynicism by making him genuinely fall in love, suggesting that Life Force transcends both rationalism and libertinism. Gloria's final assertion of agency—she proposes the marriage, she insists on the settlement—reclaims the romance on terms that honor her mother's principles even as her heart overrules her head.
