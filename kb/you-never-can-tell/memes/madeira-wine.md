## Definition

Madeira, the Portuguese island where the Clandon family lived for eighteen years, was known to Shaw's audience primarily for its fortified wine — a product of the island's position on Atlantic trade routes and its historical connection to England. The association of Madeira with wine culture adds a layer of cosmopolitan sophistication to the Clandons' background, distinguishing them from provincial English characters while also marking them as slightly exotic, slightly foreign.

## In the Play

Madeira is referenced throughout the play as the site of the Clandons' exile and the source of their distinctive character. The twins' "delicate biscuit complexion" is attributed to "some warmer sun than England's" [001-045], and their freedom from English inhibitions is explained by their Madeiran upbringing. The island thus functions as a counter-England — a place where alternative social arrangements are possible, where a woman can raise children alone and support them through authorship without English society's constant demand for a father.
