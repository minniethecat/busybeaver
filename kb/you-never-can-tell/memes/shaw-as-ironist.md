## Definition

Shaw's irony, as practiced in *You Never Can Tell*, is a method of intellectual exposure rather than mere verbal cleverness. It operates by setting incompatible perspectives side by side and allowing the audience to register the gap — between social pretension and human reality, between stated principle and actual behavior, between conventional wisdom and observed truth. Unlike the destructive irony of some satirists, Shaw's irony is fundamentally constructive: it clears away falsehood to make room for more honest understanding.

## In the Play

The play's ironic method is most visible in its treatment of social convention. Valentine's "respectability" — the quality that supposedly qualifies him to lunch with the Clandons — is exposed as a performance sustained by luck and dissimulation. The family's "privacy" — Mrs. Clandon's governing principle — is exposed as a form of concealment that damages the children it was meant to protect. Each irony functions as a small revelation, contributing to the play's larger movement from illusion to clarity.
