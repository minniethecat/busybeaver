# The Duel of Sex

## Definition

"The duel of sex" is a phrase Shaw puts directly into the mouth of [[valentine|Valentine]], who uses it to describe courtship not as romance but as combat — an arms race between men and women in which each advance in one sex's defenses provokes a countering advance in the other's attack. It is the play's adversarial theory of love, standing in deliberate tension with the cooperative pull of [[the-life-force|the Life Force]]: where the Life Force is Nature seizing lovers "for her own purposes," the duel of sex is the conscious, tactical struggle the lovers wage on the surface while that deeper current runs beneath. Valentine names himself its practitioner — "the Duellist of Sex" (003-002) — and the play ends with him its casualty.

## The Artillery Metaphor

Valentine expounds the concept to [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] through an image drawn from modern warfare: "During this whole century, the progress of artillery has been a duel between the maker of cannons and the maker of armor plates... Well, the duel of sex is just like that" (003-002). Each generation's defensive innovation is answered by a fresh offensive one. The "old fashioned" mother was educated to resist "the wiles of man," so man learned to get round her; she therefore armored her daughter with a [[scientific-education|scientific education]] — [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s own "plan" for [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] — which forced man to abandon the kneeling proposal for a new line of attack. The metaphor casts the [[new-woman|New Woman]]'s emancipation itself as a move in an unending campaign.

## Gloria and Valentine

The theory is not merely expounded but enacted. [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]], armored by reason and her mother's training, is precisely the "new" woman the duel has produced; [[valentine|Valentine]], the improvident [[five-shilling-dentist|five-shilling dentist]], is the man who has discarded the old tactics for the new. [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] exposes his method with scorn, demanding Gloria ask him "how often he has laid the trap in which he has caught you; how often he has baited it with the same speeches; how much practice it has taken to make him perfect in his chosen part in life as the Duellist of Sex" (003-002). Yet the duel's irony is that the practised attacker is himself captured: Valentine wins Gloria and loses his freedom in the same stroke.

## The Captured Duellist

Shaw completes the figure at the play's close. Having "won," Valentine collapses in dismay — "I might as well be a married man already" — while the [[walter-the-waiter|Waiter]] "contemplates the captured Duellist of Sex with affectionate commiseration, shaking his head slowly" (004-002). The predator is the prey; the duel of sex, fought with wit and tactics, resolves into the very marriage its combatants thought they were manoeuvring to avoid. The adversarial surface has been overruled by the [[the-life-force|Life Force]] all along.

## Shaw's Argument

The duel of sex lets Shaw have his comedy and his philosophy at once. On the surface the play is a battle of wits between an emancipated woman and a charming opportunist; underneath, Shaw argues that all such tactics are futile against biological purpose. The "duel" is real as social performance and illusory as metaphysics — a distinction that turns [[courtship-and-romance|courtship]] into both sport and destiny. It is the comic mechanism by which Shaw dramatizes his conviction that reason, education, and even seduction are instruments Nature bends to ends the duellists never chose.
