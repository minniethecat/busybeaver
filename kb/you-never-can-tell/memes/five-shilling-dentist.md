# Five-Shilling Dentist

## Definition

The "five-shilling dentist" is [[valentine|Valentine]]'s self-described professional identity — a dental practice that charges a uniform flat fee of five shillings for every procedure, regardless of complexity. The pricing model is both a genuine business innovation and a moral position: accessible dentistry for everyone, [[conscience-and-truth|conscience]] money for the dentist who has "sold himself" to fashionable practice. In *You Never Can Tell*, it serves as Shaw's vehicle for exploring the impossible tension between professional integrity, commercial survival, and the performative [[victorian-respectability|respectability]] demanded by late Victorian society.

The coin itself — [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s "nice new five shilling piece" (001-004) — becomes a symbol that threads through the play. She suggests [[valentine|Valentine]] drill a hole in it and wear it on his watch-chain, transforming a commercial token into a personal memento. The five shillings thus operates on three levels: as an economic model (flat-fee pricing), as a moral position ([[conscience-and-truth|conscience]] money), and as a romantic symbol (the gift that inaugurates [[valentine|Valentine]] and [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s relationship).

## Context

The five-shilling dentist emerges from the specific conditions of the late Victorian medical marketplace. Dentistry was undergoing professionalization — the Dentists Act of 1878 had established a register of qualified [[professional-identity|practitioner]]s — but the field remained commercially competitive, with [[professional-identity|practitioner]]s vying for patients in an unregulated fee market. A dentist charging a uniform five shillings positioned himself outside the traditional hierarchy: accessible to the middle [[social-class|class]]es but suspect to those who equated price with quality.

Shaw, a Fabian socialist and the son of an unsuccessful merchant, was deeply interested in how economic structures shaped human behavior and moral choices. His essays and plays consistently argue that poverty and economic precarity are the primary obstacles to ethical conduct — that it is impossible to be honest when [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]] means starvation. The five-shilling dentist is a microcosm of this critique: [[valentine|Valentine]]'s pricing model is a form of integrity made necessary by the commercial pressures that punish integrity.

The concept also reflects Shaw's theatrical strategy of building comedy around "modern" professions — the dentist, the solicitor, the secretary — whose [[professional-identity|practitioner]]s must navigate the collision between professional duty and commercial necessity. [[valentine|Valentine]]'s dental office, with its rented furniture and hired plant, is a stage set for this collision, and the five-shilling system is its script.

## In the Play

### Act I: The System Introduced (001-004)

[[valentine|Valentine]] introduces the five-shilling model casually, almost offhandedly, to the Young Lady ([[gloria-clandon|Gloria Clandon]]): "I'm what's called a five shilling dentist" (001-004). The phrase "what's called" betrays his characteristic ironic distance from his own professional identity — he is simultaneously the five-shilling dentist and the observer of the five-shilling dentist, performing a role he finds slightly absurd.

[[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s response transforms the encounter. She produces a crown piece and says: "How nice! Well, here! a nice new five shilling piece! your first fee! Make a hole in it with the thing you drill people's teeth with and wear it on your watch-chain" (001-004). The suggestion reframes the transaction as a gift, the coin as a memento, the dentist as a person rather than a functionary. In this exchange, the five-shilling system reveals its unintended genius: by fixing the price at an impersonal, egalitarian level, it creates space for personal connection to flourish around the commercial core.

### Act I: The Confession (001-004)

[[valentine|Valentine]]'s confession to the twins reveals the moral stakes behind the pricing model:

> (001-004) "My conscience! My conscience has been my ruin. … Now I've set up as a dentist, a five shilling dentist; and I've done with conscience forever. This is my last chance. I spent my last sovereign on moving in; and I haven't paid a shilling of rent yet."

The five-shilling system is presented as [[valentine|Valentine]]'s final compromise with a world that punishes [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]]. His previous attempts at medical practice failed because he told patients "the brute truth" (001-004) — that their ailments were psychosomatic, that their expensive specialists were frauds. The flat fee is his attempt to preserve integrity in the one area where integrity is commercially possible: transparent pricing. But even this modest [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]] is precarious — he has not paid his rent, and his first six weeks have yielded exactly one patient.

### Act II: The Self-Deprecation (002-005)

When love strikes at [[marine-hotel|the Marine]] Hotel, [[valentine|Valentine]]'s professional identity becomes his shield against romantic feeling. His monologue of self-deflation culminates in the cry: "What am I? a dentist — a five shilling dentist!" (002-005). The phrase is both genuine [[social-class|class]] anxiety and Shavian irony: the five-shilling dentist is, in fact, the moral center of the play, the character whose professional modesty and ethical transparency make him the only suitable partner for the [[new-woman|Woman of the Twentieth Century]].

> (002-005) "What am I? a dentist — a five shilling dentist!" — Self-deprecation that the play's structure refutes: the five-shilling dentist is the hero, not the clown.

### The Coin as Comic Motif

Throughout the play, [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] repeatedly invokes the "five shillings extra" for gas as a comic refrain (001-004). Her persistence transforms the pricing model into a running joke that binds the Clandon twins to [[valentine|Valentine]] in shared absurdity. The coin — mentioned, joked about, worn on a watch chain — operates as the play's comic talisman, a small object that accumulates meaning across four acts until it stands for everything the play values: [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]], equality, and the possibility of authentic connection in a commercial world.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the five-shilling dentist as a satirical lens on Victorian professional culture, but the satire operates on multiple levels that reward close attention.

**First level — economic satire.** The flat fee exposes the absurdity of stratified medical pricing. If a tooth extraction costs the dentist the same effort regardless of the patient's income, why should the fee vary? [[valentine|Valentine]]'s system is economically rational but socially transgressive — it refuses to perform the [[social-class|class]] distinctions that Victorian commerce was designed to encode.

**Second level — moral paradox.** The five-shilling system is a form of integrity (transparent, egalitarian, honest) that is itself a compromise with dis[[conscience-and-truth|honesty]] ([[valentine|Valentine]] has "done with [[conscience-and-truth|conscience]] forever"). Shaw's point is that under capitalism, even integrity must be sold — the honest dentist must market his [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]] as a "system," commodifying the virtue he practices. The five-shilling fee is [[conscience-and-truth|conscience]] translated into commercial terms, which is both admirable and absurd.

**Third level — theatrical self-reference.** The five-shilling dentist is also Shaw's meta-commentary on his own position as a popular playwright with serious intentions. Like [[valentine|Valentine]], Shaw charged admission to convey uncomfortable truths; like the five-shilling fee, his comedies made radical ideas accessible by pricing them for the middle-[[social-class|class]] audience. The dentist who tells "the brute truth" and fails is the artist who refuses to entertain and goes unproduced; the five-shilling dentist who packages truth as comedy is the Shavian dramatist, successful but self-mocking.

**Resolution.** The play's ending is a deliberately artificial resolution of the economic problem the five-shilling system represents. [[valentine|Valentine]] cannot survive on five shillings per patient — the math simply does not work. His rescue comes not through dentistry but through marriage, not through the market but through the Life Force. Shaw offers the happy ending with a wink, knowing that his audience understands the real-world economics are unsustainable. The five-shilling dentist is a beautiful idea that requires a [[money-and-status|wealth]]y father-in-law to survive — and that, Shaw implies, is the problem with beautiful ideas under capitalism.
