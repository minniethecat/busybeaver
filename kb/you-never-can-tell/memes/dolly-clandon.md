# Dolly Clandon

## Role

Dolly is the play's comic engine and its sharpest social observer. As the younger Clandon twin, she combines childlike directness with sophisticated irony — making her simultaneously the most innocent and the most knowing character on stage. Her function is to puncture pretension, and she does so with an efficiency that makes her the play's most reliable truth-teller. While other characters struggle with [[conscience-and-truth|conscience]], convention, and [[love-and-marriage|romance]], Dolly simply refuses to take any of them seriously. [001-001]

## Biography

Dolly is one of [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s twin children, raised in [[madeira|Madeira]] without knowledge of her father. She has absorbed her mother's progressive principles so thoroughly that she can deploy them as weapons — quoting *Twentieth Century Children*, *Twentieth Century Parents*, and *Twentieth Century Conduct* in the same breath as she subverts them. Her running joke about the "five shillings extra" for gas becomes the play's most reliable comic motif.

## Key Scenes

> Dolly's introduction: she and Philip recite the *Twentieth Century* titles in alternating sequence, and she delivers the immortal line "Inaccessible to rhetoric. See *Twentieth Century Parents*, chapter on Liberty, passim."

> "Make him give you gas. It's five shillings extra: but it's worth it." Dolly's advice to Crampton — a joke that she repeats throughout the play until it becomes a structural motif.
> (001-573)

> "It's all right, mamma. The dentist is coming; and he's bringing his old man." Dolly's characteristic compression — the entire family-reconciliation plot summarized in one breathless sentence.

## Relationships

- **[[philip-clandon|Philip Clandon]]** — her twin, partner in verbal mischief. Their rapid-fire dialogue is the play's most distinctive stylistic feature.
- **[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]** — mother and unwitting source of Dolly's best lines (the *[[twentieth-century-women|Twentieth Century Treatises]]*).
- **[[gloria-clandon|Gloria Clandon]]** — older sister. Dolly treats [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s romantic drama with affectionate amusement.
- **[[crampton|Crampton]]** — father. Dolly approaches him with the same cheerful curiosity she brings to everything, which wounds him precisely because it lacks deference.

## Themes

Dolly represents Shaw's ideal of the educated woman freed from anxiety — the generation after the [[new-woman|New Woman]]'s struggle, for whom independence is a birthright rather than a cause. Her irreverence is the play's moral compass: she laughs at what deserves to be laughed at, and she refuses to be impressed by authority, [[money-and-status|wealth]], or convention.
