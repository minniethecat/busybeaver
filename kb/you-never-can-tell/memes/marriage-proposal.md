## Definition

The marriage proposal in *You Never Can Tell* is remarkable for reversing the conventional Victorian script: [[Gloria Clandon]] proposes to [[Valentine]], not the other way around. After a day of mutual attraction, conflict, and reconciliation, Gloria announces to the assembled company: "tell my mother that we have agreed to marry one another" (004-002). This inversion of gender roles is the culmination of Shaw's critique of conventional courtship and his dramatization of marriage as a partnership between equals.

## Context

In Victorian society, the marriage proposal was the defining ritual of the courtship narrative: the man knelt, declared his love, and the woman modestly accepted. This script encoded deep assumptions about male agency and female passivity. Shaw, as a Fabian socialist and advocate for women's rights, found this ritual both dishonest and degrading. In the "duel of sex" framework (003-002), the conventional proposal was simply the man's final tactical move. Shaw's innovation was to let the woman seize the initiative when the moment of genuine feeling arrived.

## In the Play

The proposal scene in Act IV (004-002) follows a complex day of emotional turmoil. Earlier, Valentine had declared his love and kissed Gloria on the terrace (002-005), but their courtship almost collapsed in Act III when Gloria learned of his previous affairs. The Act IV reconciliation is achieved through radical honesty: Valentine admits, "I'm frightened, I'm positively frightened; and that's the plain truth" (004-002), and Gloria, moved by his vulnerability, takes command. She proposes, kisses him, and dismisses his financial objections with "Can't you earn one? Other people do" (004-002).

Even after Gloria has proposed, the couple must navigate practical reality. [[Bohun]] intervenes to insist on a marriage settlement (004-002), and Valentine's poverty threatens to derail the match. [[Walter the Waiter]] saves the moment by offering to put the ball ticket "down to you" (004-002), and his quiet wisdom—"You never can tell, sir: you never can tell" (004-002)—provides the benediction.

The proposal also includes what [[Dolly Clandon]] dubs a numbered sequence: "Number Six!" (004-002), a running joke about Gloria's previous admirers. This comic deflation prevents the scene from becoming sentimental.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of the proposal scene serves multiple purposes. First, it demonstrates that genuine partnership can only emerge when convention is abandoned—Gloria proposes only after Valentine abandons all pretense of mastery. Second, it shows that practical arrangements (the settlement) are not antithetical to love but its necessary foundation. Third, the inclusion of the entire family in the moment transforms the proposal from a private transaction into a public commitment. Shaw is arguing that marriage, properly understood, is neither a romantic fairy tale nor a commercial contract but a social arrangement that requires honesty, community, and legal structure.
