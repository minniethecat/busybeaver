## Definition

The sea in *You Never Can Tell* is simultaneously a physical presence—the waters visible from the [[Marine Hotel]] terrace, the element in which the twins boat—and a symbolic force representing nature, change, and the elemental depths that rationalism cannot master. The sea is present from the play's first scene ([[Valentine]]'s rooms have "a good view of the sea" at 001-047) to its last (the band's dance music "drowning the sound of the sea" at 001-1112). The arc from visibility to audibility to submersion traces the play's movement from nature observed to nature integrated into human festivity.

## Context

The sea has a long history in English literature as a symbol of nature's power, change, and the unconscious. For the Victorians, the sea was also a site of holiday, health, and escape from urban industrial life. Shaw draws on both traditions: the sea is both the picturesque backdrop of the seaside holiday and the symbol of forces that rationalism cannot domesticate. The play's coastal setting on a terrace "which stands up over the sea like a cliff" (002-001) positions the characters literally on the edge between civilization and nature.

## In the Play

The sea is first mentioned when [[Dolly Clandon]] observes the view from Valentine's window: "You have a good view of the sea from these rooms!" (001-004). This casual remark establishes the sea as a marker of desirability—rooms with sea views are more expensive—and as a constant presence in the characters' world.

The Act II terrace stage direction emphasizes the sea's proximity and power: the terrace's parapet "stands up over the sea like a cliff" (002-001). The image of the cliff suggests both stability (the hotel is built on solid ground) and precariousness (the sea is eroding the cliff below). This precariousness is the play's condition throughout: civilized arrangements are always threatened by the elemental forces beneath them.

The sea is the space of the twins' freedom. They go boating, return exhilarated, and report Valentine's seasickness—"He is unaccustomed to navigation" (003-002). The sea here is both playground and test, revealing who is at home in the elemental and who is not. [[Philip Clandon]] and [[Dolly Clandon]], the creatures of Madeira, are at home on the water; Valentine, the creature of London consulting rooms, is not.

The crucial symbolic moment comes in the Act IV stage direction. The band is "playing dance-music in the garden, drowning the sound of the sea" (004-001). At the play's climax, human artifice—music, dance, festivity—literally drowns out the sound of nature. This is not a conquest but a temporary reprieve: the sea is still there, and when the music stops, its sound will return. Shaw's point is that civilization is not the elimination of nature but its temporary orchestration into forms that humans can bear.

## Shaw's Commentary

The sea in Shaw's play is not a romantic symbol of the sublime but a materialist symbol of the forces that rationalism cannot master. When Valentine describes Nature "suddenly lifting her great hand to take us—her two little children—by the scruffs of our little necks" (002-005), he is describing the sea within—the emotional depths that Gloria's education has denied and that his cynicism has armored against. The sea that drowns in dance music is the same sea that threatens to drown the characters in feeling. The play's resolution does not conquer the sea but sets it to music—transforms its dangerous power into the rhythm of dance, the structure of marriage, the comedy of family life. This is Shaw's alternative to both romantic immersion and rationalist denial: the domestication of elemental forces through form, humor, and collective ritual.
