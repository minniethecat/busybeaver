# The Seaside Resort

## Definition

The seaside resort as liminal space — a place of holiday license, social mixing, and temporary suspension of normal rules, where the estranged family can encounter each other as strangers.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Shaw's choice of a seaside resort is strategically brilliant. Resorts in the 1890s were socially ambiguous — places where class boundaries relaxed, metropolitan sophistication mingled with provincial convention, and normal rules were suspended. It is no accident that the Clandons can lunch with a dentist, a landlord can become a son-in-law, and a Q.C. can become a family therapist. The resort setting makes all social transgressions plausible while maintaining the comic frame.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
