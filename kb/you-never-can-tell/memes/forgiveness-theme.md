# Forgiveness as Process

## Definition

The play's treatment of forgiveness — not as emotional act but as rational process, achieved through Bohun's legal machinery rather than sentimental reconciliation.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Forgiveness in *You Never Can Tell* is procedural rather than emotional. Crampton does not apologize conventionally; he is brought to tears by Bohun's cross-examination and then, through legal settlements, restored to his family. Mrs. Clandon does not forgive in words; she agrees to the settlements, implicitly accepting reconciliation. Shaw's point is unsentimental: forgiveness works better when structured by clear agreements than left to unreliable emotion. The law, paradoxically, enables the heart.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
