## Definition

Shaw's relationship to feminism, as dramatized in *You Never Can Tell*, was both sympathetic and complex. He was a committed supporter of women's rights — suffrage, economic independence, professional opportunity — and his female characters consistently embody these principles. Yet Shaw also subjected feminist positions to the same critical scrutiny he applied to all ideologies, questioning whether rationalist feminism adequately accounted for emotional life. The play's treatment of Mrs. Clandon and Gloria reflects this dual commitment: admiration for their independence, and gentle critique of its limits.

## In the Play

Mrs. Clandon represents first-wave feminism in its accomplished form — she has achieved economic independence through authorship, raised her children outside patriarchal structures, and maintained her principles across eighteen years of exile. Gloria represents the next generation's challenge: how to reconcile feminist principles with the experience of love, which her mother's philosophy has taught her to mistrust. Valentine's argument — that love is natural, not patriarchal — represents Shaw's own attempt to reconcile feminism with romantic feeling.
