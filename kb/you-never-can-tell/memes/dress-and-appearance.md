## Definition

Dress and appearance in *You Never Can Tell* function as a visible index of character, class, and social allegiance. Shaw's detailed stage directions describe each character's clothing with novelistic precision — from Dolly's "recklessly pretty" frock to Gloria's "tailor-made skirt-and-jacket dress of saffron brown cloth" with its "blouse of sea-green silk which upsets its conventionality with one stroke" [001-045]. These descriptions are not mere ornament but dramatic argument: clothing reveals the wearer's relationship to social convention.

## In the Play

The Clandon twins' "terra-cotta cashmere" suits and "tan gloves" signal their Madeiran upbringing — they are dressed for an English seaside resort with a precision that is itself slightly foreign, slightly too perfect. Gloria's blouse, which "upsets its conventionality with one stroke," mirrors her character: apparently conforming to social expectation while quietly subverting it. The play's attention to dress extends to the minor characters — Walter the Waiter's professional uniform, Crampton's outdated gentleman's attire — each costume telling a story about its wearer's place in the social order.
