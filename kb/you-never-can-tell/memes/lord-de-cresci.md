## Definition

Lord de Cresci appears in *You Never Can Tell* only as a name — an example Valentine offers of the social freedom of English seaside resorts: "Lord de Cresci's sister bicycles in knickerbockers" [001-145]. This single reference opens a window onto the changing social mores of the 1890s, when aristocratic women were among the first to adopt the bicycle — a technology that required practical clothing (knickerbockers rather than skirts) and enabled independent mobility.

## In the Play

The reference serves Valentine's argument that English society, despite its reputation for rigid convention, is more flexible than the Clandon children suppose. An aristocrat's sister cycling in rational dress suggests that even the upper classes are subject to the forces of modernization. Shaw's choice of an offstage aristocrat to make this point is characteristically ironic — social change is validated by its adoption among those with the most social capital to risk.
