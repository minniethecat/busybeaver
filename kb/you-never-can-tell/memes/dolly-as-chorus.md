# Dolly as Chorus

## Definition

Dolly Clandon operates as the play's primary comic chorus -- a character who observes, comments upon, and catalyzes the action while remaining largely outside its emotional stakes. Her function is threefold: she speaks truths others evade, she serves as the emotional barometer of any scene, and her irreverence punctures pretension. Unlike a Greek chorus, her commentary is embedded in dialogue rather than set apart, making her simultaneously participant and observer.

## Context

Shaw inherited the choric tradition from Aristophanic comedy and Shakespearean fools but modernized it. The Shavian chorus figure is integrated into the action rather than standing outside it. In the 1890s, Shaw was developing his "theatre of ideas," where characters function as intellectual positions as much as psychological beings. Dolly represents the unfiltered perspective of youth raised without Victorian repression -- the voice of natural candor.

## In the Play

Dolly's choric role begins in Act I when she extracts information from Valentine through relentless questioning -- deducing his bachelor status, his six-week practice, his hired furniture (001-057-061). She names things directly: the landlord is "Chalkstones", her father is "Mr. Crampstones" (001-004). Her comic misinterpretations often contain deeper truths. At the luncheon, it is Dolly who breaks the tension by observing, "It's embarrassing, isn't it? It's just as bad for us, you know".

Throughout Acts II and III, Dolly's interjections -- "What cheer, Finch?" (003-002), "The whisky's on the last page but one" -- deflate solemnity and keep the action from becoming melodramatic. In Act IV, her challenge to Crampton's objection to the twins' dress -- "Never mind my age. Is it pretty?" (004-002) -- is logically unassailable and reveals the superficiality of his complaint.

## Key Passages

> [001-057-061] Dolly's first interrogation of Valentine demonstrates her choric function: "Any family? ... Of course not: anybody can see that. I meant sisters and mother and that sort of thing. ... If you've been here six weeks, and mine was your first tooth, the practice can't be very large, can it?" -- revealing the truth behind Valentine's professional facade through comedy.

> [001-283-287] "Sounds rheumaticky, doesn't it? ... Show Mr. Crampstones up. ... Crampton, Crampton, Crampton, Crampton, Crampton. I must get that name right, or Heaven knows what I shall call him." -- Dolly's comic mangling presages the genuine confusion about identity that structures the entire play.

> [004-1276-1278] "Never mind my age. Is it pretty? ... How can you think it pretty and not like it?" -- Dolly presses the logical contradiction in Crampton's position, earning Bohun's approval: "The young lady's method is right" (004-002).

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses Dolly to demonstrate that what Victorian society labels "indiscretion" is often simple honesty. Her choric function embodies a key Shavian argument: that children raised in freedom -- without the coercive discipline Crampton represents -- develop into truthful, emotionally healthy adults. Dolly's choruses are not cynical but clarifying; she is never cruel, only candid. Her famous "you never can tell" spirit inverts the cautionary proverb into an embrace of life's unexpectedness.
