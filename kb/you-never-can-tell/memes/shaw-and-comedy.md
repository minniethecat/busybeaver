## Definition

Shaw's conception of comedy, as demonstrated in *You Never Can Tell*, differs significantly from both the Restoration comedy of manners and the Victorian farce that preceded him. For Shaw, comedy was not escape from ideas but a vehicle for them — the most effective medium for social criticism because it disarmed resistance through laughter. His comedies are intellectually demanding without being solemn, entertaining without being frivolous. *You Never Can Tell*, as one of the *Plays Pleasant*, represents Shaw's mature comic mode: serious ideas explored through sparkling dialogue and resolved through rational negotiation rather than sentimental conversion.

## In the Play

The play's comedy operates at multiple levels — verbal wit (the twins' repartee), situation comedy (the series of recognitions and misunderstandings at the hotel), and intellectual comedy (the collision of incompatible worldviews). Shaw's innovation is to make the intellectual comedy the deepest source of pleasure: the audience laughs not only at what characters say but at the ideas their words embody. The resolution, achieved through Bohun's legal machinery, is a comic ending in the deepest sense — order restored, relationships healed, the future opened — but achieved through reason rather than magic.
