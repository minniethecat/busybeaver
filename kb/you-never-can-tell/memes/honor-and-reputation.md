## Definition

Honor and reputation in *You Never Can Tell* represent the social currency of late-Victorian England — the intangible asset that determines whether one may lunch at a hotel, practice a profession, or marry. Shaw treats these codes with characteristic irony, exposing them as arbitrary social constructs while acknowledging their real power over individual lives. The play contrasts traditional honor (duelling, family name) with modern reputation (professional standing, personal integrity).

## In the Play

The social machinery of reputation operates from the play's opening scene. Valentine's status as a "respectable" dentist is undermined by his lack of patients and unpaid rent; his professional reputation is a performance sustained by appearances. When Dolly and Philip's lack of a known father threatens their social standing, the revelation of a clerical grandfather — "a canon of Lincoln Cathedral" — immediately restores it [001-150]. Shaw's point is unmistakable: reputation is inherited, not earned.

The character of Bohun Q.C. embodies the professional apex of reputation. His legal eminence gives him authority over every situation, even family reconciliation. Yet Shaw subtly undermines this authority by showing Bohun's professional manner as a performative role, no more essential than Valentine's "professional gravity." The play's resolution — Bohun arranging the family settlement — suggests that reputation, however artificial, can serve constructive social purposes when wielded by those who recognize its constructed nature.

> "If I swerve by a hair's breadth from the straight line of the most rigid respectability, I'm done for." [001-150]
