# Gloria Clandon

## Role

Gloria Clandon is the female protagonist of *You Never Can Tell* — the purest product of her mother's [[scientific-education|progressive education]]al experiment and the character whose emotional awakening drives the play's romantic plot. She is introduced in Act I as "The Young Lady," a dental patient whose composure, directness, and "cool insolence" immediately signal that she is no conventional Victorian heroine. Where her younger siblings [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] and [[philip-clandon|Philip]] supply the play's wit, and [[valentine|Valentine]] its intellectual energy, Gloria provides its emotional and philosophical gravity. Her arc — from rational self-containment to the bewildering discovery that love operates outside the laws her education taught her — is the play's central psychological drama. (001-004)

Shaw's stage directions introduce her as "a much more formidable person than her mother" (001-004), "the incarnation of haughty highmindedness, raging with the impatience of an impetuous, dominative character paralyzed by the impotence of her youth" (001-004). In an era when female protagonists were expected to be either ingenues or schemers, Gloria is neither: she is intellectually serious, morally exacting, and possessed of a freezing dignity that collapses only when she confronts the one force her [[scientific-education|rational education]] cannot explain.

## Biography

Gloria is the eldest of [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s three children, raised from infancy in [[madeira|Madeira]] without any knowledge of her father, [[crampton|Fergus Crampton]]. Her mother designed her education as a deliberate experiment: raise a daughter entirely on rational principles — Darwin, Mill, Huxley, George Eliot — without patriarchal authority, religious indoctrination, or the social conventions that trained Victorian women for submission. In her own words, [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] intended Gloria to "take up my work where I left it" (002-005), to carry forward the emancipatory project into the next generation.

The experiment succeeded in producing a young woman who is intellectually formidable, socially fearless, and emotionally armored. Gloria has never been taught to defer to male authority, to value conventional femininity, or to treat marriage as a career. She enters Act I as a dental patient who interrogates her dentist with the confidence of an equal, and within minutes has extracted his life story and invited him to lunch — not as flirtation but as the natural behavior of someone who has never learned to be coy.

Her fault line, however, is the emotional domain her education treated as irrelevant. Raised to value "social questions and principles" over "persons" — a trait she shares with her mother, who feels "strongly about social questions and principles, not about persons" (001-004) — Gloria has never developed the language or emotional repertoire for romantic love. When [[valentine|Valentine]] falls in love with her at first sight, she is utterly unprepared.

## Key Scenes

### Act I: The Dental Consultation (001-004)

Gloria's first appearance, unnamed and identified only as "The Young Lady," establishes her character in miniature. She enters [[valentine|Valentine]]'s [[dentist-office|consulting room]] expecting competent professional treatment, and when she discovers she was his first patient, she does not panic but interrogates: "Your first! Do you mean to say that you began practising on me?" (001-004). When [[valentine|Valentine]] explains his five-shilling fee, she produces a crown piece and says: "How nice! Well, here! a nice new five shilling piece! your first fee! Make a hole in it with the thing you drill people's teeth with and wear it on your watch-chain" (001-004). The gesture is spontaneous, generous, and entirely without self-consciousness — the [[new-woman|New Woman]]'s natural grace. Within minutes she has gathered intelligence about [[valentine|Valentine]]'s practice, his landlord, his furniture, and his marital status, and has invited him to lunch.

> "Well, so you have! Why shouldn't you? it's your business to hurt people." — Gloria accepts pain as a professional fact, not a personal affront. The line captures the rational, unflinching quality that distinguishes her from sentimental heroines.
> (001-004)

### Act II: Love at First Sight (001-004)

When [[valentine|Valentine]] returns for lunch, Gloria is introduced by name. The stage direction describes her with exceptional care: she is "hardly past twenty" but "a much more formidable person than her mother" (001-004). Her beauty — "deep chestnut hair, olive brown skin, long eyelashes, shaded grey eyes that often flash like stars" — is presented as dangerous rather than decorative: "A very dangerous girl, one would say, if the moral passions were not also marked, and even nobly marked, in a fine brow" (001-004).

The critical moment is [[philip-clandon|Philip]]'s announcement: "Love at first sight. You can add his scalp to your collection, Gloria" (001-004). This line, delivered with the twins' characteristic irreverence, frames Gloria as a collector of masculine admiration — a woman so armored by intellect that suitors are trophies rather than threats. The framing is [[philip-clandon|Philip]]'s, not Gloria's, but it captures something truthful about her emotional posture in Act II: watchful, unyielding, refusing to acknowledge what everyone else can see.

### Act II: The Father Confrontation (001-004)

When [[philip-clandon|Philip]] and [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] demand to know their father's identity, and [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] refuses, Gloria intervenes decisively. "Mother: we have a right to know" (001-004). When her mother pushes back — "In your mouth 'we' used to mean you and I, Gloria" (001-004) — Gloria does not retreat. This is the moment she steps out from her mother's shadow, asserting herself not as [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s lieutenant but as an independent adult with loyalties to her siblings as well as her mother. The twins "instantly go over to the enemy" (001-004), and the family's power structure shifts permanently.

### Act IV: The Chemistry of Love (002-005)

The play's philosophical climax is Gloria and [[valentine|Valentine]]'s debate about whether romantic attraction is love or "[[love-and-marriage|chemistry]]." [[valentine|Valentine]], trying to be rational, says: "Let's call it [[love-and-marriage|chemistry]]. You can't deny that there is such a thing as chemical action, chemical affinity, chemical combination — the most irresistible of all natural forces. Well, you're attracting me irresistibly — chemically" (002-005). Gloria's response — "Nonsense!" — is half conviction and half self-defense. She has been trained to dismiss romantic passion as irrational, even "vulgar" (002-005), yet she is not immune to it. The scene dramatizes the central Shavian idea that "[[love-and-marriage|chemistry]]" is nature's honest word for what convention calls "love," and that acknowledging nature is not surrender but wisdom.

### Act IV: The Acceptance (004-002)

Gloria's final acceptance of [[valentine|Valentine]] is framed not as sentimental surrender but as an imperious act of will. Cutting through his abjection, she commands: "Now let us have no false delicacy. Tell my mother that we have agreed to marry one another" (004-002) — and when a settlement is proposed, insists "Proudly" that "He shall have a settlement" (004-002). She accepts what her education did not prepare her for — partnership with a man — without abandoning the dominative pride that education gave her. It is a Shavian resolution: love accommodated, not denied; intellect and command preserved, not abandoned.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]** — mother and mentor. Gloria is both her mother's greatest achievement and the living test of whether a purely [[scientific-education|rational education]] produces whole human beings. Their relationship fractures in Act II when Gloria insists on knowing about her father, marking her transition from disciple to independent agent.

- **[[valentine|Valentine]]** — romantic partner and intellectual equal. Their [[love-and-marriage|courtship]] is a meeting of the five-shilling dentist and the [[new-woman|Woman of the Twentieth Century]] — two people equally committed to rational modernity, equally unprepared for the irrational force of desire. The [[love-and-marriage|chemistry]] metaphor [[valentine|Valentine]] deploys is Shaw's way of dignifying love as natural law.

- **[[crampton|Fergus Crampton]]** — father and antagonist. Gloria's first encounter with her father in Act III is the play's rawest emotional scene. When [[crampton|Crampton]] curses her mother, Gloria springs up: "You wretch! How I hate the name! How I love the name of mother! You had better go" (002-005). The intensity of her reaction reveals the emotional landscape her education was designed to suppress — loyalty, fury, the primal attachment to the parent who raised her.

- **[[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] and [[philip-clandon|Philip]]** — younger siblings. Gloria is the serious older sister to their irreverent younger antics. The twins treat her romantic life as entertainment, but their support of her in the father confrontation shows the Clandon children's united front.

## Themes

Gloria embodies the central Shavian question of *You Never Can Tell*: can the [[new-woman|New Woman]] have it all — intellectual independence, professional purpose, *and* romantic fulfillment? The play's answer is a qualified yes, but on terms that neither Victorian convention nor her mother's rationalism can supply.

[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s feminism, forged in the 1860s–1880s struggle for women's rights, treated love and marriage as dangers to be managed or avoided. Her emphasis on "privacy" as the supreme domestic value (001-004) is a response to the patriarchal marriage she fled — a defensive architecture designed to prevent emotional coercion. Gloria inherits this architecture and discovers its limitations. She is "all passion" (001-004) — more passionate than her mother, more serious than her siblings — yet her passion has been channeled entirely into moral and intellectual commitments, leaving no vocabulary for romantic feeling.

Shaw's innovation is to treat this not as a failure of feminism but as a developmental stage. Gloria's education gave her the strength to resist [[crampton|Crampton]]'s patriarchal authority; what it could not give her was the emotional repertoire for intimacy with an equal. [[valentine|Valentine]] supplies the missing piece — not by dominating her but by matching her. Their love is presented as a "chemical" fact, a natural force that education cannot abolish and should not try to. The resolution is integration, not substitution: Gloria marries, but on the terms her education taught her to insist on — [[liberty|freedom]], [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]], and mutual respect.

The title's echo — "You Never Can Tell" — applies nowhere more than to Gloria's romantic fate. The most rigorously educated young woman in English drama discovers that the heart has its own curriculum.
