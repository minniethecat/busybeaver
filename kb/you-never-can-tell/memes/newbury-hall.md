## Definition

Newbury Hall, mentioned in Act I when Valentine asks Dolly whether she is "a daughter of Mr. Densmore Clandon of Newbury Hall," functions as a symbol of the landed gentry — the traditional English upper class based on property and lineage rather than profession or achievement. Valentine's question reveals his assumption that "Clandon" must signify this kind of status, and Dolly's non-recognition of the name confirms her detachment from English social categories.

## In the Play

The reference to Newbury Hall is brief but thematically significant. It establishes the gap between the Clandon name (which sounds like a gentry name) and the Clandon reality (a family of authors and professionals with no landed estate). The hall represents the old England of inherited status, against which Shaw sets the new England of achieved status — Valentine's diploma on the wall rather than a portrait of ancestors.
