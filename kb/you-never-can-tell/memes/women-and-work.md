## Definition

Women and work in *You Never Can Tell* is explored through multiple characters whose economic roles challenge Victorian assumptions about female dependency. Mrs. Clandon's career as an author supports her family and enables her independence from an abusive husband. The parlor maid and the cook represent women's work in the service economy. Even Dolly and Gloria, though not yet employed, are being raised with the expectation of intellectual and economic autonomy. Shaw presents women's work as both an economic necessity and a moral good — the foundation of genuine independence.

## In the Play

Mrs. Clandon's authorship is the play's most significant instance of women's work. Her *Twentieth Century Treatises* have supported three children for eighteen years — a fact that refutes any claim that women require male economic support. The parlor maid, though a minor character, represents the vast population of working women whose labor sustained Victorian domestic life. The play's attention to women's work, across class lines, reflects Shaw's engagement with the feminist economics of his time.
