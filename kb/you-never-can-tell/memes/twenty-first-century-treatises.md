## Definition

The *Twentieth Century Treatises*, Mrs. Clandon's series of instructional books, function as both a running joke and a serious thematic element in *You Never Can Tell*. The titles — *Twentieth Century Cooking*, *Twentieth Century Creeds*, *Twentieth Century Clothing*, *Twentieth Century Conduct*, *Twentieth Century Children*, and *Twentieth Century Parents* — cover the full range of domestic and moral life, proposing that every traditional practice can be reformed through rational inquiry. The treatises embody the progressive spirit of the 1890s while also being the object of Shaw's gentle satire of systematic rationalism.

## In the Play

The twins recite the treatise titles with practiced fluency, their delivery balanced between pride in their mother's achievement and affectionate mockery of its comprehensive ambition: "Cloth limp, half a dollar. Or mounted on linen for hard family use, two dollars. No family should be without them" [001-130]. The treatises represent Mrs. Clandon's solution to the problems of family life — if traditional practices produce misery, replace them with rational ones. Shaw's implicit critique is not of the treatises' content but of their assumption that all of life can be systematized.
