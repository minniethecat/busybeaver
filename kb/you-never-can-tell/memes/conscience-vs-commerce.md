# Conscience vs. Commerce

## Definition

The conflict between conscience and commerce is the ethical engine of *You Never Can Tell*. Valentine articulates this dilemma directly: his previous medical practices failed because he "acted conscientiously, and told my patients the brute truth instead of what they wanted to be told. Result, ruin" (001-004). The five-shilling dentist system is his compromise — a way to preserve a kernel of integrity (transparent pricing) within a commercial framework that otherwise punishes honesty. Shaw uses this personal dilemma to argue that capitalism makes ethical conduct structurally impossible.

## Context

Shaw was a committed socialist who believed that economic structures, not individual virtue, determined moral behavior. In his essays, he argued that poverty and economic insecurity are the primary enemies of ethical conduct — that it is impossible to be honest when honesty means starvation. This conviction was rooted in Shaw's own experience. His father was an unsuccessful merchant whose financial failures shaped the family's precarious existence. Shaw knew firsthand what it meant to choose between principle and survival, and his plays repeatedly stage this choice.

## In the Play

Valentine's confession to the twins in Act I is the play's most direct statement of the conscience-versus-commerce dilemma. "Twice before I have set up as a respectable medical practitioner in various parts of England. On both occasions I acted conscientiously, and told my patients the brute truth instead of what they wanted to be told. Result, ruin" (001-004).

The irony is that Valentine has not truly "done with conscience" — the five-shilling system is itself an act of conscience, a refusal to participate in the stratified pricing that encodes class hierarchy into medical care. He has simply found a form of integrity that is commercially semi-viable. The play's resolution confirms the structural nature of the problem: Valentine cannot survive on five shillings per patient, and his rescue comes through marriage to a wealthy woman, not through the market.

## Key Passages

> (001-004) "Now I've set up as a dentist, a five shilling dentist; and I've done with conscience forever. This is my last chance." — Valentine's confession: integrity as commercial suicide.

> (001-004) "On both occasions I acted conscientiously, and told my patients the brute truth instead of what they wanted to be told. Result, ruin." — The structural impossibility of honest professional practice under capitalism.

> (002-005) "What am I? a dentist — a five shilling dentist!" — Valentine's self-deprecation reveals the social cost of ethical commerce.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses Valentine's dilemma to make a structural argument about capitalism. Individual conscience cannot survive in a market system — the person who tells the truth goes bankrupt. The five-shilling system is a beautiful ethical idea that proves commercially unsustainable. Shaw's point is not that Valentine should be more principled but that the system should be changed so that principled behavior is not punished. The play's deliberately artificial happy ending — rescue by wealthy father-in-law — is Shaw's wink to the audience: this is how integrity survives under capitalism, not through the market but through luck, inheritance, and the Life Force.
