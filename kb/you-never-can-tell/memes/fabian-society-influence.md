# Fabian Society Influence

## Definition

The Fabian Society, founded in 1884, was the intellectual engine of British gradualism -- a socialism committed to reform through persuasion rather than revolution. Shaw joined in 1884 and quickly became one of its leading propagandists, writing tracts, delivering lectures, and editing *Fabian Essays in Socialism* (1889). The Fabian influence on *You Never Can Tell* is everywhere implicit: in the play's critique of marriage as property, its analysis of class as performance, its vision of gradual social evolution through education, and its fundamental optimism about the possibility of human improvement.

## Context

Fabianism took its name from the Roman general Fabius Maximus, who defeated Hannibal through attrition rather than decisive battle. The Society's strategy was "permeation" -- influencing existing institutions rather than overthrowing them. By the mid-1890s, when Shaw was writing *You Never Can Tell*, Fabian ideas had permeated the Liberal Party and were shaping municipal reform across Britain. The Society's program included municipal ownership of utilities, progressive taxation, women's suffrage, secular education, and marriage law reform. Shaw's plays were a crucial vehicle for this permeation -- a way of smuggling socialist ideas into middle-class drawing rooms under cover of entertainment.

## In the Play

Fabian themes surface explicitly when McComas warns Mrs. Clandon about the intellectual climate awaiting Gloria: "Socialism. That's what Miss Gloria will be up to her ears in before the end of the month if you let her loose here" (002-431). Mrs. Clandon's reply -- "I can prove to her that Socialism is a fallacy" (002-432) -- is ironic, since her own positions on women's rights, secularism, and family reform are essentially Fabian.

The play's class analysis is thoroughly Fabian. The Waiter's comparison of his profession with his son's (002-717) is a compressed lesson in the performativity of class. McComas's admission that the Clandons are "out of everything, because I've refused to bow the knee to Socialism" (002-429) marks the divide between old radicalism and the new socialist politics. The play's gradualist optimism -- Crampton is not excluded but incorporated; Gloria does not reject marriage but reforms it -- reflects Fabian faith in evolutionary progress.

## Key Passages

> [002-429-432] McComas describes the intellectual shift: "I'm out of everything, because I've refused to bow the knee to Socialism." Mrs. Clandon is shocked: "Socialism!" > [002-717] The Waiter's analysis of class: "If I must put on a dress coat to show what I am, sir, he must put on a wig and gown to show what he is." -- Fabian class analysis in comic form.

> [001-195] Mrs. Clandon's critique of patriarchal marriage: "a life in which husbands open their wives' letters, and call on them to account for every farthing of their expenditure." -- Fabian feminism articulated as personal testimony.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw does not preach Fabianism directly; he dramatizes its conclusions. The play shows that the Clandon family, organized on feminist and rationalist principles, produces healthier children than the Victorian patriarchal model. It shows that class is performance, not essence. It shows that gradual accommodation -- between Crampton and his children, between Gloria and Valentine, between the generations -- is possible without revolutionary rupture. These are Fabian positions rendered as comedy.
