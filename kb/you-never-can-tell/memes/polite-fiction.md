## Definition

Polite fiction in *You Never Can Tell* refers to the tacit agreements by which society maintains its surface of civility while concealing uncomfortable truths. The Clandon family's silence about their father is the play's central polite fiction — a truth known to all the adults but withheld from the children "for their own good." Shaw exposes the corrosive effects of such fictions while acknowledging their social function: without some polite fictions, social life might become intolerably abrasive.

## In the Play

The play traces the dismantling of the central polite fiction — the concealment of Crampton's identity — and its replacement by a more honest, if more uncomfortable, acknowledgment of truth. Valentine's resistance to polite fiction (his "conscience" makes him tell the truth) positions him as the agent of this dismantling. Yet Shaw is not absolutist about honesty: Bohun's legal negotiations involve their own polite fictions (the pretense that financial arrangements are not personal), and these are presented as socially useful. The play's wisdom lies in distinguishing between fictions that enable life and fictions that deform it.
