## Definition

The play's valuation of emotional transparency — characters who can acknowledge their feelings (Valentine, Dolly) are rewarded, while those who repress them (Crampton, Mrs. Clandon) must be forced to honesty by circumstances.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Emotional honesty is the play's governing virtue. Valentine wins Gloria by confessing his professional failures rather than concealing them. Dolly earns affection by saying exactly what she thinks. Crampton's redemption begins when he is driven to admit that he wants his family back. Mrs. Clandon's limitation is her inability to share her pain with her children. Shaw's argument is that honesty — even uncomfortable, socially inappropriate honesty — is the foundation of authentic relationship. The play's comedy is the vehicle for this argument: laughter makes honesty bearable.

## Key Passages

> "ambition stands rebuked as vulgarity, and imagination as treason to the abounding sufficiency and interest of the actual" (002-003)

Walter the Waiter's serene contentment rebukes ambition and represents a settled, honest acceptance of what one actually is — wisdom found in an unexpected place.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
