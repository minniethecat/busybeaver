# The Crown Piece

## Definition

The crown piece -- a five-shilling coin -- is the play's central material symbol, introduced in Act I when Dolly pays Valentine for her tooth extraction and transformed through the action into a love token, an ironic commentary on professional identity, and ultimately a measure of human connection that transcends its monetary value. The coin circulates through the play, passing from Dolly to Valentine to the Waiter, accumulating meanings at each exchange.

## Context

A crown was five shillings, a substantial coin in 1896 (roughly equivalent to thirty pounds today). Valentine's "system" as a "five shilling dentist" positions him at the cheap end of the professional spectrum -- accessible to working people but beneath the notice of the middle class. The crown piece thus marks Valentine's social position even as Dolly intends it as a gift. The coin's value also connects to the play's running five-shilling motif: the fancy ball tickets cost five shillings, gas costs five shillings extra, and Valentine cannot afford the ball ticket in Act IV.

## In the Play

Dolly produces the crown piece in Act I: "a nice new five shilling piece! your first fee! Make a hole in it with the thing you drill people's teeth with and wear it on your watch-chain" (001-004). The gesture is simultaneously payment and gift -- Dolly is paying for a service while treating the money as a souvenir. Her suggestion to drill it and wear it transforms currency into ornament, transaction into relationship.

In Act II, Valentine offers the crown piece to the Waiter as a tip: "if you will allow me to present you with a professional man's earnings for six weeks --". Valentine gives away his entire income, enacting his own transformation from careful professional to reckless lover. The Waiter's gracious acceptance ("as if the sum were beyond his utmost expectations") dignifies the exchange.

In Act IV, the Waiter offers to cover Valentine's five-shilling ball ticket on credit: "do not let a matter of five shillings stand between you and your happiness, sir" (004-002). The crown piece's value has been fully spiritualized -- money is no obstacle.

## Key Passages

> [001-069] Dolly holds up the crown piece: "a nice new five shilling piece! your first fee! Make a hole in it with the thing you drill people's teeth with and wear it on your watch-chain." -- The coin's first transformation from payment to gift.

> [002-712] Valentine offers the crown piece to the Waiter: "if you will allow me to present you with a professional man's earnings for six weeks --" -- Valentine divests himself of money and professional identity simultaneously.

> [004-1394] The Waiter to Valentine: "do not let a matter of five shillings stand between you and your happiness, sir. We shall be only too pleased to put the ticket down to you." -- The Waiter completes the crown piece's symbolic journey from transaction to gift to grace.

## Shaw's Commentary

The crown piece embodies Shaw's critique of the boundary between economic and emotional exchange. Victorian propriety insisted these realms were separate; Shaw shows they are inseparable. Dolly's gift is also a payment; Valentine's tip is also self-liberation; the Waiter's credit is also kindness. The coin's proposed transformation -- drilled and worn on a watch-chain -- symbolizes the possibility that money can be made into something beautiful through human relationship. That the drilling never happens on stage suggests the symbolism is enough.
