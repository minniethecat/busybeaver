# Conscience and Truth

## Definition

In *You Never Can Tell*, conscience is presented not as a moral compass but as a social liability — the impulse to speak truthfully that proves professionally ruinous in a society organized around strategic deception. Shaw uses [[valentine|Valentine]]'s career trajectory to dramatize the conflict: a medical [[professional-identity|practitioner]] who tells "the brute truth" fails twice, while the "five-shilling dentist" who abandons conscience may yet survive. The play's comedy arises from the tension between the moral imperative to be truthful and the social imperative to be agreeable. [001-004]

## Context

Shaw wrote *You Never Can Tell* during the period of his early critical essays, when he was developing the theory of the "worldly wisdom" of the moral revolutionist — the idea that progressive ethics must operate strategically within corrupt institutions. This was also the era of Ibsen's *An Enemy of the People*, which explores the same theme: the truth-teller destroyed by the community he tries to serve. Shaw, an Ibsen devotee, reframes the tragedy as comedy: [[valentine|Valentine]] is not destroyed but forced to negotiate between his conscience and his appetite.

## In the Play

[[valentine|Valentine]]'s confession in Act I provides the concept's central statement: "My conscience! My conscience has been my ruin. Listen to me. Twice before I have set up as a respectable medical [[professional-identity|practitioner]] in various parts of England. On both occasions I acted conscientiously, and told my patients the brute truth instead of what they wanted to be told. Result, ruin" (001-243). The speech establishes the play's moral economy: truth costs money, and integrity is a luxury the economically precarious cannot afford.

The Clandon family operates on the opposite principle. [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] has raised her children on *Twentieth Century Conduct*, which exhorts them "to curtail the number of unnecessary lies we tell" (001-136). [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] and [[philip-clandon|Philip]] take this literally, cheerfully telling strangers their mother is a famous authoress rather than maintaining polite fictions. The collision between [[valentine|Valentine]]'s compromised conscience and the Clandons' principled candor generates much of the play's wit.

In Act II, [[valentine|Valentine]] deploys truth as a weapon of self-abnegation: "What am I? a dentist — a [[five-shilling-dentist|five shilling dentist]]!" (002-849). Here truth-telling becomes a strategy for suppressing romantic feeling rather than a moral practice.

## Key Passages

> (001-243) "My conscience! My conscience has been my ruin. … Twice before I have set up as a respectable medical practitioner in various parts of England. On both occasions I acted conscientiously, and told my patients the brute truth instead of what they wanted to be told. Result, ruin. Now I've set up as a dentist, a five shilling dentist; and I've done with conscience forever." — **Valentine**

The play's definitive statement on the economic impossibility of professional truth-telling in Victorian England.

> (001-136) "[Mother's] repeated personal exhortations to us to curtail the number of unnecessary lies we tell" — **Philip**

The Clandon family's commitment to radical honesty, revealing how truth-telling can become a family ideology.

> (002-849) "What am I? a dentist — a five shilling dentist!" — **Valentine**

Truth used as self-diminishment rather than moral practice — [[valentine|Valentine]]'s conscience prevents him from overreaching socially, even as it prevents him from loving.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of conscience is dialectical, not dogmatic. He does not argue that truth-telling is always virtuous or that strategic deception is always corrupt. Instead, he shows that conscience operates within economic and social structures that determine its viability. [[valentine|Valentine]]'s conscience is real and admirable, but it is also a luxury he cannot afford — and the play's comic resolution, in which the Clandon family's [[money-and-status|wealth]] resolves the tension between integrity and survival, is deliberately artificial. Shaw knew perfectly well that most truth-tellers are not rescued by rich future in-laws.
