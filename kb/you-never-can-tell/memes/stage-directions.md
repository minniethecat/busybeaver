## Definition

Shaw's stage directions in *You Never Can Tell* are among the most elaborate in English drama — miniature character sketches, social analyses, and physical descriptions that rival the dialogue in literary interest. Unlike the minimalist directions of earlier dramatists, Shaw's directions read like passages from a novel, offering interpretations of character and motive that the dialogue alone could not convey. This practice reflects Shaw's conviction that the printed play was a literary work in its own right, not merely a performance script.

## In the Play

The stage directions for the principal characters are particularly rich. Gloria's description — "the incarnation of haughty highmindedness, raging with the impatience of an impetuous, dominative character paralyzed by the impotence of her youth" — conveys more about her psychology than any single speech [001-155]. The dental office's detailed set description, with its "wall paper, designed, with the taste of an undertaker, in festoons and urns," functions as social criticism, using physical detail to indict Victorian taste and the values it expresses.
