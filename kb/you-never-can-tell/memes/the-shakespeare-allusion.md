# Shakespeare Allusion

## Definition

Dolly's recognition of Walter as resembling "the bust of Shakespeare in Stratford Church" (002-410) — a comic self-reference that positions Shaw's comedy in relation to Shakespearean tradition.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Dolly's comparison of Walter to the Shakespeare bust is a joke that carries serious weight. By aligning the waiter with Shakespeare — calling him "William," seeing the resemblance to the Stratford bust — Shaw is making a claim about the relationship between his comedy and Shakespeare's. The waiter, like Shakespeare's fools, speaks wisdom through apparent simplicity. His professional contentment is a form of philosophy, just as Shakespeare's clowns and servants often articulate truths that elude their social superiors.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
