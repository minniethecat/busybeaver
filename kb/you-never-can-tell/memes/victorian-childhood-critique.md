# Victorian Childhood Critique

## Definition

*You Never Can Tell* conducts a systematic critique of Victorian child-rearing through the contrast between two models: Crampton's philosophy of hardening through discipline and punishment (the soap, the whip) and Mrs. Clandon's philosophy of rational liberty and mutual respect. The Clandon children are presented as living evidence for the superiority of the latter approach -- they are intelligent, affectionate, and honest -- while Crampton's own character is presented as the damaged product of the former.

## Context

Victorian child-rearing was dominated by the evangelical doctrine of original sin and the utilitarian doctrine of character formation through discipline. Children were believed to require "breaking" -- the subduing of natural willfulness through punishment and privation. This was the era of the strict nursery, the caning schoolmaster, and moral tracts emphasizing obedience. By the 1890s, these practices were under attack from progressive educators, child psychologists, and feminist critics of patriarchal authority. Shaw, heavily influenced by Samuel Butler's critique of Victorian family life in *The Way of All Flesh*, was among the most vocal critics.

## In the Play

The two models are established in Act I. Mrs. Clandon's method is described by Philip quoting her own writings: "Answer all your children's questions, and answer them truthfully, as soon as they are old enough to ask them" (001-243, quoting *Twentieth Century Motherhood*). Crampton's method is described by Mrs. Clandon: the whip "bought to beat me with" (001-259), and by Crampton himself: "When I was her age, I had many a good hiding fresh in my memory to teach me manners" (001-336).

The results are visible. The Clandon children are socially unconventional but emotionally healthy. They speak their minds, challenge authority, and show genuine affection for each other and their mother. Crampton is socially conventional but emotionally crippled -- unable to express affection, prone to rage, isolated. The soap-for-teeth (see [[soap-for-teeth]]) is the concentrated symbol of his upbringing: effective at preserving teeth, destructive of character.

Dolly's indulgence -- smoking, speaking freely, wearing fancy dress -- is the test case. Crampton objects; Bohun approves. The play's verdict is that a "spoiled" child (in Crampton's terms) may be a healthier human being than a "properly hardened" one.

## Key Passages

> [001-195] Mrs. Clandon on the alternative: "The sort you know is based on mutual respect, on recognition of the right of every member of the household to independence and privacy in their personal concerns."

> [001-243] The twins quote Mrs. Clandon's child-rearing manual: "Answer all your children's questions, and answer them truthfully, as soon as they are old enough to ask them. See *Twentieth Century Motherhood* -- Page one -- Chapter one -- Sentence one."

> [001-336] Crampton on his own upbringing: "When I was her age, I had many a good hiding fresh in my memory to teach me manners." -- The punitive model stated by its product.

> [001-259-260] Mrs. Clandon on the whip: "Gloria: if I had ever struck you -- struck you purposely, deliberately, with the intention of hurting you, with a whip bought for the purpose! Would you remember that, do you think? ... That would have been your last recollection of your father, Gloria, if I had not taken you away from him."

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's argument is that character is formed by the method of its formation. Crampton was hardened, and he became hard -- brittle, isolated, incapable of intimacy. The Clandon children were trusted, and they became trustworthy. This is not sentimentalism; Shaw acknowledges that the Clandon children are difficult, tactless, and sometimes cruel. But their faults are the faults of freedom -- they can be corrected through experience and relationship. Crampton's faults are structural -- he cannot change because his character was built on resistance to change. The play's optimism lies in its suggestion that even Crampton can be softened, through the patient work of people who were raised differently.
