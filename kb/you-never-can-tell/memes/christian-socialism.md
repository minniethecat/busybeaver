## Definition

Religion enters *You Never Can Tell* primarily through the offstage figure of the Clandon children's grandfather — "a canon of Lincoln Cathedral." The canon's social respectability is invoked as a credential, but his religious function is notably absent from the play's moral universe. Shaw, a committed secularist and critic of organized religion, allows the canon to serve as a social reference point while the play's actual moral work is done by secular agents — professionals, authors, reformers. [001-004]

## In the Play

The canon of Lincoln is mentioned only as a social credential — his religious office matters less than the institutional respectability it confers. This treatment reflects Shaw's broader analysis of religion in English society: the Church of England had become, in his view, a branch of the social establishment rather than a source of moral challenge. The play's own moral framework — honesty, mutual respect, rational negotiation — is entirely secular, suggesting that genuine ethical progress comes from human reason rather than divine authority.
