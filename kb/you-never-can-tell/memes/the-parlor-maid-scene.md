# The Parlor Maid

## Definition

The parlor maid who announces visitors at Valentine's office — a minor character whose professional demeanor contrasts with the Clandons' social unconventionality.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

The parlor maid appears briefly but significantly in Act I, announcing visitors and delivering the landlord's message. Her professional correctness — the formal language, the proper forms of address — contrasts with the Clandons' cheerful informality. She represents the world of convention that the Clandons are disrupting, and her composure in the face of their unconventionality is a small triumph of professional training over personal inclination.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
