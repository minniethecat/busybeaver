# Walter the Waiter as Chorus

## Definition

Walter the head waiter — called "William" by the Clandons for his resemblance to the Shakespeare bust — a man "so cheerful and contented that in his encouraging presence ambition stands rebuked as vulgarity" (002-393).

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

Walter the waiter is the play's philosophical center of gravity. His professional contentment is so complete that it becomes a form of wisdom: "in his encouraging presence ambition stands rebuked as vulgarity, and imagination as treason to the abounding sufficiency and interest of the actual" (002-393). The Clandons call him William because Dolly thinks he resembles the Shakespeare bust in Stratford Church (002-410) — a joke that contains a serious point: the waiter, like Shakespeare's fools, speaks wisdom through apparent simplicity.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
