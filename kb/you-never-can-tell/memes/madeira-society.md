# Madeira Society

## Definition

Madeira, the Portuguese Atlantic island where Mrs. Clandon raised her children after fleeing England, functions in the play as a counter-space to English society. It is described as a place of freedom, sunshine, and unconventionality, where the Clandons' intellectual and sartorial experiments went unchallenged. It is also, Mrs. Clandon admits, a kind of exile -- "my St. Helena" (002-005) -- and the play acknowledges that the Madeiran alternative, while liberating, has left the children unprepared for English social reality.

## Context

Madeira was a popular destination for British expatriates in the nineteenth century, particularly those seeking a warm climate for health reasons. The island had a substantial English community, with its own churches, hotels, and social life. It was also a stopping point for ships traveling between Europe, Africa, and the Americas. For Mrs. Clandon, Madeira offered distance from Crampton and English law, a milder climate, and an environment where her advanced ideas attracted less hostility than they would have in England. The Portuguese colonial context of Madeira is not explicitly addressed in the play, though Dolly's reference to "a Portuguese" who "used to thrash his wife and daughters with a cartwhip" (001-004) gestures toward the limits of the Clandons' cosmopolitanism.

## In the Play

Madeira is established as the Clandons' formative environment in the opening dialogue. The stage direction notes Dolly's "biscuit color" complexion "by some warmer sun than England's" (001-004). Philip announces: "We're from Madeira, but perfectly respectable, so far". The *Twentieth Century Treatises* were published there: "No household is complete without her works. We came to England to get away from them" (001-004).

Mrs. Clandon describes Madeira as both refuge and prison: "I felt that I had no right to bury her alive in Madeira -- my St. Helena, Finch" (002-005). The Napoleon reference is telling -- Madeira preserved her freedom at the cost of her engagement with the world.

The children's foreignness is repeatedly noted. They "have never been in England before"; they find English customs absurd; their manners are described as "barbarian". Yet they are also praised by the Waiter: "They have a free way with them that is very taking, sir" (002-005).

## Shaw's Commentary

Madeira enables the experiment in alternative family life, but Shaw does not sentimentalize it. The island's distance from England is both liberation and limitation -- the children arrive in England charming but dangerously naive. Shaw's point is that freedom requires engagement with the world, not withdrawal from it. The Clandons' return to England is necessary for their completion: Gloria must meet Valentine, Dolly and Philip must meet their father, and the family must test its principles against English reality.
