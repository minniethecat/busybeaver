# Gas Anesthesia in Dentistry

## Definition

Gas anesthesia in *You Never Can Tell* is nitrous oxide (laughing gas), offered by Valentine to his dental patients for the additional fee of five shillings. It functions simultaneously as a realistic detail of 1890s dental practice and as a comic motif that runs through the play, symbolizing the modern willingness to surrender control, accept comfort, and embrace the new. The choice between "gas" and "bearing the pain" becomes a Shavian litmus test: those who refuse gas (like Crampton) are revealed as emotionally constipated, while those who accept it demonstrate the enlightened self-interest Shaw championed.

## Context

Nitrous oxide was first synthesized by Joseph Priestley in 1772, and its anesthetic properties were demonstrated by Humphry Davy in 1799. Horace Wells, an American dentist, first used it for tooth extraction in 1844. By the 1890s, gas anesthesia was widely available in dental practices but still regarded with suspicion by many patients who equated pain endurance with moral virtue.

The gas apparatus described in Shaw's stage directions — "a gas pump and cylinder beside" the operating chair (001-001) — would have been a recognizable signifier of a modern, scientifically equipped practice. Yet the technology was still novel enough that Valentine must persuade his patients to accept it, and Crampton's refusal ("Rubbish, man: I want none of your gas" — 001-004) represents the older generation's resistance to medical and social progress.

## In the Play

Dolly immediately grasps the comic potential of gas anesthesia. Her refrain — "five shillings extra" (001-004) — transforms a medical option into a running joke that binds the Clandon twins to Valentine. She advises Crampton: "Make him give you gas. It's five shillings extra: but it's worth it" (001-004). The repetition of "five shillings extra" becomes the play's comic heartbeat, a small phrase that accumulates meaning across four acts.

Crampton's refusal of gas is character-revealing: "people were taught to bear necessary pain in my day" (001-004). His equation of suffering with virtue, and comfort with moral weakness, places him firmly in the Victorian past that the play is moving beyond. Valentine's response — "Oh, if you like being hurt, all right. I'll hurt you as much as you like, without any extra charge for the beneficial effect on your character" (001-004) — is Shavian wit at its sharpest: the offer to provide pain free of charge makes visible the absurdity of choosing suffering.

Valentine's bet with Crampton — "What do you bet that I don't get that tooth out without your feeling it?" (001-004) — culminates in Crampton being rendered unconscious by the very gas he refused. The self-proclaimed hardened man is reduced to helplessness, and the tooth is extracted painlessly. The gas triumphs over character, comfort over discipline, the modern over the Victorian.

## Key Passages

> (001-004) "Because you said it would be five shillings extra." — Dolly's reason for refusing gas (spoken as the "Young Lady" in the opening dental scene), the first appearance of the phrase that becomes the play's comic refrain.

> (001-004) "Make him give you gas. It's five shillings extra: but it's worth it." — Dolly's advice to Crampton, practical and subversive.

> (001-004) "What do you bet that I don't get that tooth out without your feeling it?" — Valentine's bet, which Crampton loses when the gas renders him unconscious.
