## Definition

The opposition between the natural and the artificial runs throughout *You Never Can Tell*, structuring its treatment of love, family, and social behavior. Shaw consistently privileges natural impulse — love, curiosity, honesty — over artificial convention — respectability, deference, social form. But his position is more nuanced than simple Romantic primitivism: he recognizes that social forms are necessary, and that the goal is not to abolish convention but to align it with natural human needs. [001-004]

## In the Play

Valentine articulates the play's central thesis when he argues that love is a natural force that reason must accommodate. The twins' spontaneity is presented as natural and admirable, yet Shaw also shows its limits — their wit cannot resolve the family crisis that only adult negotiation can address. Gloria's struggle between her rational principles and her emotional impulses embodies the play's central dialectic. The resolution — achieved through Bohun's legal machinery — represents a synthesis: natural bonds recognized and formalized through artificial structures.
