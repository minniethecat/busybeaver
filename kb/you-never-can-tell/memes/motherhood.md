## Definition

Motherhood in *You Never Can Tell* is embodied in Mrs. Lanfrey Clandon, who has raised three children alone in Madeira while sustaining a career as an author. Her model of motherhood — based on independence, privacy, and rational respect — stands in deliberate contrast to the Victorian ideal of dependent, domestic maternity. Shaw presents her as both admirable and limited, a feminist pioneer whose principles sometimes fail to meet her children's emotional needs.

## In the Play

Mrs. Clandon has built her identity around a radical conception of maternal duty. She removed her children from an abusive husband, supported them through her writing, and gave them an upbringing based on "mutual respect, on recognition of the right of every member of the household to independence and privacy in their personal concerns" [001-255]. This philosophy, drawn from her *Twentieth Century Treatises*, produces children who are spirited, intelligent, and refreshingly free of Victorian inhibitions — yet also children who lack knowledge of their own father.

The play tests Mrs. Clandon's maternal philosophy against reality. When Dolly and Philip demand to know their father's identity, Mrs. Clandon is forced to reveal the painful history she has suppressed. Her confession that Crampton beat her exposes the cost of her maternal protectiveness — she sacrificed openness for safety. The play neither condemns nor fully endorses her choices, instead showing that even the most principled motherhood operates under constraints.

## Key Passages

> "There are two sorts of family life, Phil; and your experience of human nature only extends, so far, to one of them. The sort you know is based on mutual respect, on recognition of the right of every member of the household to independence and privacy in their personal concerns." [001-255]

Mrs. Clandon's manifesto for a new model of family life, rooted in the Women's Rights movement.

> "I have kept him out of your life: keep him now out of mine by never mentioning him to me again." [002-259]

The emotional boundary Mrs. Clandon draws, revealing the unresolved pain beneath her rational exterior.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's portrait of Mrs. Clandon reflects his engagement with the "New Woman" debate of the 1890s. She embodies the feminist argument that motherhood need not entail domestic subordination. Yet Shaw also shows the limits of her approach — her emphasis on privacy becomes a form of silence that harms her children. In this, Shaw anticipates later feminist critiques that would distinguish between protective and repressive forms of maternal authority.
