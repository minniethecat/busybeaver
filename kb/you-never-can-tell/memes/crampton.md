# Crampton

## Role

Crampton is the estranged husband of [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] and the biological father of her three children — a man whose authority has been replaced by absence and whose [[money-and-status|wealth]] cannot buy the recognition he craves. He is the play's structural antagonist: the obstacle that must be overcome for the family to be reconstituted. But he is also its most wounded character, experiencing his children's indifference as physical pain. [002-005]

## Biography

Eighteen years before the play opens, [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] left Crampton, taking their three children to [[madeira|Madeira]]. The play never specifies exactly what he did to drive her away — whether he was actively cruel or merely conventionally patriarchal. What matters dramatically is the result: a man of [[money-and-status|property]] (Crampton owns houses, investments, "Newbury Hall") who has been denied the one thing his [[money-and-status|wealth]] cannot purchase — his children's recognition. He encounters them accidentally, through [[valentine|Valentine]]'s dental practice, and the [[family-reconciliation|reunion]] is from his perspective a catastrophe of indifference.

## Key Scenes

> The encounter at the dentist's: Crampton meets Dolly and Philip without knowing who they are, and their cheerful frankness about their family history — told to "curtail the number of unnecessary lies" — wounds him before he understands why.
> (001-243)

> The luncheon at the Marine Hotel: Philip orders Crampton's wine, reducing him to "the casual acquaintance picked up that morning at the dentist's." Shaw's stage direction records the "keen pang" that "goes through the father with so keen a pang that he trembles all over; his brow becomes wet; and he stares dumbly at his son."
> (002-385)

> The reconciliation: Bohun orchestrates the terms under which Crampton is restored to his family — not as patriarchal authority but as acknowledged father on terms of equality with his wife.
> (004-426)

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]** — estranged wife. She left him, and her return to England after eighteen years forces the confrontation he has avoided.
- **[[dolly-clandon|Dolly]], [[philip-clandon|Philip]], [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]** — his children, who have been raised without knowledge of him. Their treatment of him ranges from cheerful curiosity ([[dolly-clandon|Dolly]]) to polite composure ([[philip-clandon|Philip]]) to emotional complexity ([[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]).
- **[[mc-comas|McComas]]** — family solicitor who attempts to mediate the reconciliation.
- **[[bohun|Bohun]]** — the Q.C. who legally formalizes Crampton's restoration to the family.

## Themes

Crampton embodies the collapse of patriarchal authority in the face of the [[new-woman|New Woman]]'s independence. His [[money-and-status|property]], his legal rights, his [[social-class|social position]] — all the traditional instruments of masculine power — are worthless against the fact that his children do not know him and his wife does not need him. The play's resolution restores him not as master but as member: he must accept equality or remain alone.
