## Definition

The duelling motif in *You Never Can Tell* serves as Shaw's satirical commentary on the persistence of aristocratic honor codes in modern commercial society. When Fergus Crampton threatens to challenge Valentine to a duel, the anachronism of the gesture exposes the gap between Crampton's outdated conception of manhood and the rational, professional world that the younger generation inhabits. [001-004]

## In the Play

Crampton, enraged by Valentine's intimacy with his daughter, reverts to the gentleman's code of honor he learned in his youth. The threatened duel is immediately deflated by the commonsense responses of the other characters — Bohun treats it as a legal nuisance, Valentine as absurd. The episode reveals Crampton's inability to navigate the modern social world: he reaches for a ritual of aristocratic masculinity in a context where it has no purchase.

The duelling motif also illuminates Shaw's broader critique of masculine honor culture. Valentine, the modern professional, has no interest in proving his manhood through violence. His masculinity is expressed through work, honesty, and emotional vulnerability — qualities that the duelling code cannot accommodate.

## Key Passages

Crampton's threat of a duel occurs in Act III, framed by Bohun's legalistic dismissal and Walter the Waiter's philosophical commentary on gentlemen's quarrels.
