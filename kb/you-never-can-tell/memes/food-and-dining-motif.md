# Food and Dining Motif

## Definition

Food and eating constitute one of *You Never Can Tell*'s most pervasive motifs, extending well beyond the central luncheon party. The motif appears in the play's opening image (a dental surgery -- the site where eating becomes impossible), in Crampton's philosophy of dental self-reliance (nuts, bones, soap), in the hospitality economy of the hotel, in the tea service of Act III, and in the claret cup and refreshments of Act IV. Food mediates every major social encounter, and the characters' relationships to eating reveal their relationships to each other and to the world.

## Context

Shaw was a vegetarian and a health reformer who thought seriously about food as a moral and political issue. His plays frequently use eating as an index of character. The Victorian novel had established food as a marker of class and moral worth -- from Dickens's Christmas feasts to the thin gruel of the workhouse -- and Shaw inherits this symbolic vocabulary while giving it his own inflection. In *You Never Can Tell*, food is not about nourishment but about relationship: who feeds whom, who accepts hospitality, and who refuses it.

## In the Play

The motif opens in the dental surgery, a space dedicated to the repair of eating apparatus. Valentine has been "eating and drinking on credit" (001-004); the twins invite him to lunch partly out of pity for his evident hunger. Dolly's refrain -- "We're hungry" (001-004) -- establishes appetite as a motive force.

The luncheon is the motif's peak, but it extends into Act III (tea, with Gloria too agitated to drink) and Act IV, where the Waiter's taking of drink orders (Scotch, Irish, claret cup with cucumber, 004-002) restores him from humiliation to professional equilibrium. The claret cup -- a light, summery drink associated with garden parties -- marks the tonal shift from the tense Act II luncheon to the festive Act IV resolution.

The motif even extends to language: Philip calls Valentine "the ivory snatcher" and "the gum architect" (001-004), metaphors that reduce dentistry to its alimentary function. The final action of the play is dancing, not eating, but the Waiter's offer to cover Valentine's ticket signals the transformation of the hospitality economy from commercial transaction to gift.

## Key Passages

> (001-004) Valentine: "I'm eating and drinking on credit; my landlord is as rich as a Jew and as hard as nails; and I've made five shillings in six weeks." -- Food insecurity as motive for the plot.

> (001-004) Dolly: "Don't be long. We're hungry." -- Appetite as comic drive.

> (002-005) The drink orders at lunch: Lager, Apollinaris, "Number 413," the Waiter's intervention -- a menu that maps social identity.

> (004-002) The Act IV drink orders: "Coffee, miss? ... Claret cup, ma'am! ... Syphon -- Scotch. ... Irish for you, sir." -- The Waiter restored to professional grace through the ritual of service.

## Shaw's Commentary

Food in Shaw is never just food. It is a medium of social relation, a test of character, and a register of emotional states. Valentine's hunger is real but also symbolic -- he is hungry for patients, for income, for love. Crampton's dental self-reliance (nuts cracked with teeth) is the alimentary equivalent of his emotional isolation. The Waiter's genius is his ability to use food and drink as instruments of peace -- the cucumber in the claret cup, the timely cup of tea (002-005). Shaw suggests that the way we feed each other is the way we love each other.
