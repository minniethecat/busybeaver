# Finch McComas

## Role

McComas is the Clandon family solicitor and [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s old friend from the days of the Dialectical Society — a man who once shared her radical convictions but has since "become respectable" (002-005). His role is to mediate between the estranged couple, a task for which his legal training is insufficient and his old affection is essential. He summons [[bohun|Bohun]] as the heavy artillery when his own efforts prove inadequate. [002-005]

## Biography

McComas knew [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] before her marriage, when they attended the Dialectical Society together and shared progressive convictions. Eighteen years have made him a respectable family solicitor, complete with silk hat, and [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s first words to him — "Where's your beard?" — measure the distance between the young radical she remembers and the established professional he has become. His beard and sombrero have been replaced by clean-shaven conformity, but his loyalty to her has survived.

## Key Scenes

> (002-005) The reunion with Mrs. Clandon at the Marine Hotel: "Where's your beard?" Her recognition of him — and of what he has lost — in a single question.

> (002-005) His catalog of her progressive commitments, delivered with a mixture of astonishment and admiration: "Bless me! And you are still ready to make speeches in public, in spite of your sex."

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]** — old friend and client. His affection for her complicates his professional objectivity.
- **[[bohun|Bohun]]** — professional colleague. McComas summons [[bohun|Bohun]] when his own mediation fails.
- **[[crampton|Crampton]]** — the opposing party, whom McComas must represent without alienating.

## Themes

McComas represents the tension between youthful radicalism and middle-aged [[victorian-respectability|respectability]] — the path the [[new-woman|New Woman]]'s male allies often took. He has compromised where she has not, and the play treats his compromise with affectionate satire rather than contempt. He is a good man who has become comfortable, and his discomfort at being reminded of his former convictions is one of the play's gentler jokes.
