## Definition

Self-knowledge in *You Never Can Tell* is the quality that distinguishes characters who grow from those who remain static. Valentine's self-awareness — his understanding of his own conscience, his professional failings, his romantic feelings — is the foundation of his moral authority in the play. Gloria's journey is toward self-knowledge: she must recognize that her principled resistance to love is partly a fear of vulnerability. Crampton's redemption depends on his ability to acknowledge his past failures. Shaw presents self-knowledge as the prerequisite for moral action.

## In the Play

Valentine's self-knowledge is established from his first scene, where he frankly acknowledges his professional failures and his conscience's role in them. This self-awareness, while costly, distinguishes him from characters who lack it — Crampton's bluster, for instance, is a defense against self-knowledge. Gloria's dramatic arc is the play's central journey toward self-knowledge: her acceptance of love requires her to revise her understanding of herself. The play's resolution is made possible by characters coming to know themselves more honestly.
