# Comic Chorus Function

## Definition

The comic chorus function in Shavian drama refers to Shaw's modernization of the classical Greek chorus -- not as a separate, commenting body, but as characters embedded within the action who simultaneously observe, interpret, and influence events. In *You Never Can Tell*, three characters perform distinct choric roles: Dolly as the irreverent truth-teller, Philip as the mock-authoritative arbiter, and the Waiter as the philosophical sage. Together they form a distributed chorus that provides the audience with multiple interpretive lenses on the central conflict.

## Context

The chorus in Greek tragedy provided commentary, context, and moral judgment from outside the action. Shakespeare internalized this function in characters like fools, rustics, and servants. Shaw went further, dissolving the chorus entirely into the dramatis personae so that choric commentary emerges naturally from character. This technique serves Shaw's didactic purposes: rather than preaching, he lets characters embody different philosophical positions and allows the audience to triangulate truth from their interaction. The technique also reflects Shaw's debt to Ibsen's naturalistic dialogue, where ideas emerge through conversation rather than declamation.

## In the Play

The distributed chorus operates at three levels in *You Never Can Tell*. Dolly's chorus is comic and truth-seeking -- she names what others evade, from Crampton's identity to the absurdity of English social conventions. Philip's chorus is parodic -- his "knowledge of human nature" mimics patriarchal authority while exposing its emptiness. The Waiter's chorus is philosophical -- he articulates the play's deepest themes ("you never can tell") and models resilience and kindness.

These three voices interact dynamically. When Philip and Dolly delegate the task of "breaking the news" to Crampton to the Waiter (002-510-534), the choric functions converge: the twins recognize wisdom when they see it, and the Waiter proves more capable than the solicitor McComas. In Act IV, Bohun's entrance as a "grotesquely majestic stranger in a domino and false nose" (004-1163-1165) briefly adds a fourth, carnivalesque choric mode.

## Key Passages

> [001-057-063] Dolly's systematic interrogation of Valentine: "Any family? ... I suppose you haven't been here long? ... If you've been here six weeks, and mine was your first tooth, the practice can't be very large, can it?" -- A choric exposure of social reality through comic questioning.

> [002-715-717] The Waiter's philosophical monologue: "Well, sir, you never can tell. That's a principle in life with me, sir ... If I must put on a dress coat to show what I am, sir, he must put on a wig and gown to show what he is." -- The choric voice achieving full philosophical expression.

> [002-510-534] The twins assign the Waiter the choric task of breaking the family news: "William: your sagacity puts us all to shame." Dolly adds: "You really are like Shakespeare, William." -- Explicit acknowledgment of the Waiter's choric status.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's choric technique is fundamentally democratic. Wisdom in his plays does not reside in a single authoritative voice but emerges from the collision of perspectives. The Waiter knows more than the Q.C.; the eighteen-year-old twins see more clearly than their solicitor. By distributing the chorus across characters of different ages, classes, and genders, Shaw argues that truth is accessible to anyone who looks clearly and speaks honestly -- not a function of expertise but of character.
