# Valentine

## Role

Valentine is the male protagonist of *You Never Can Tell* — a [[five-shilling-dentist|dentist]] whose radical [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]] is both his defining virtue and his professional liability. He functions as Shaw's surrogate, articulating the play's philosophical arguments while simultaneously being entangled in its romantic plot. His role is unusually complex for a romantic lead: he is at once the lover, the truth-teller, the economic failure, and the comic analyst of his own predicament. Unlike the conventional romantic hero, Valentine's charm lies not in [[money-and-status|wealth]] or status but in his complete inability to dissemble — a quality that both attracts [[gloria-clandon|Gloria Clandon]] and renders him professionally precarious. [001-001]

## Biography

Valentine is "a young man of thirty or thereabouts" (001-003), a dentist who has recently set up practice in a seaside resort. His professional history is one of principled failure: twice before he attempted to establish himself as a medical [[professional-identity|practitioner]] and twice was ruined by telling "the brute truth instead of what [patients] wanted to be told" (001-243). His current practice — the "five-shilling dentist" system — represents his last chance. He spent his last sovereign on moving in, has not paid rent, and has earned five shillings in six weeks. His professional equipment is hired, his furniture is the landlord's, and his M.R.C.S. diploma is decorative rather than practical.

Valentine's biography is shaped by the tension between [[professional-identity|professional competence]] and commercial necessity. He is a trained medical man operating with the financial desperation of a tradesman — a contradiction that Shaw uses to expose the [[social-class|class]] hypocrisy of Victorian professional culture. His relocation to a seaside resort was itself an act of retreat after his London failures, placing him in the liminal world of the [[marine-hotel|Marine Hotel]] where social boundaries are temporarily suspended.

## Key Scenes

> Valentine's introduction: the stage direction describes his "professional manner" as "a joke," underlain by "thoughtless pleasantry" that reveals "the young gentleman still unsettled and in search of amusing adventures, behind the newly set up dentist in search of patients."
> (001-003)

> Valentine's confession to Gloria: "My conscience! My conscience has been my ruin. … Now I've set up as a dentist, a five shilling dentist; and I've done with conscience forever. This is my last chance." The play's central moral statement.
> (001-243)

> Valentine's love monologue: "What am I? a dentist — a five shilling dentist!" His attempt to reason himself out of love by cataloging his mundane reality — the [[marine-hotel|Marine Hotel]], the afternoon hour, his profession — only to discover that reason is powerless against emotion.
> (002-849)

> Valentine proposes to [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]] — and is accepted on terms of freedom and honor rather than submission, in a scene that inverts every convention of Victorian courtship.
> (004-436)

## Themes

Valentine embodies the play's central tension between [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]] and survival, [[conscience-and-truth|conscience and commerce]]. His career trajectory — ruined twice by [[conscience-and-truth|truth-telling]] — is Shaw's argument that Victorian professional culture was structurally incompatible with integrity. Yet Valentine is not a tragic figure but a comic one: his self-awareness, his capacity for self-mockery, and his willingness to be saved by [[love-and-marriage|love]] make him the play's most sympathetic character.

The figure of the "five-shilling dentist" is also Shaw's commentary on [[social-class|social class]]: a [[professional-identity|professional man]] whose fee places him below the gentry but whose education places him above the tradesman — a man suspended between worlds, like the play itself suspended between comedy of manners and Shavian argument.
