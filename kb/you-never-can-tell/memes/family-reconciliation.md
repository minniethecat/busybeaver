# Family Reconciliation

## Definition

Family reconciliation is the central plot mechanism of *You Never Can Tell*: the reassembly of the Clandon family — mother, father, and three children — after eighteen years of separation. The reunion is not spontaneous but engineered through the combined efforts of a solicitor ([[mc-comas|McComas]]), a Queen's Counsel ([[bohun|Bohun]]), a waiter ([[walter-the-waiter|Walter]]), and the children themselves. Shaw presents reconciliation not as a sentimental return to natural harmony but as a complex social construction requiring institutional infrastructure: legal recognition, economic settlement, and the careful management of pride. [001-004]

## Context

Victorian family law gave fathers near-absolute authority over children and marital [[money-and-status|property]]. A woman who left her husband — as [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] did — forfeited her children's legal status and her own economic security. The Married Women's Property Acts (1870, 1882) had begun to reform this regime, but the ideological framework of paternal authority remained largely intact. Shaw's play is in part a brief for the proposition that families can be reconstituted on terms of equality rather than hierarchy.

## In the Play

The reconciliation plot unfolds in three concurrent strands. The children's reunion with their unknown father — met accidentally, through [[valentine|Valentine]]'s dental chair — is the most innocent strand: [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] and [[philip-clandon|Philip]] treat [[crampton|Crampton]] with cheerful curiosity rather than filial deference, which both wounds him and opens the possibility of relationship on new terms. [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s encounter with her estranged husband is the most difficult strand, requiring [[mc-comas|McComas]]'s mediation and [[bohun|Bohun]]'s authority to resolve. [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s parallel discovery — that she can love a man without surrendering her independence — provides the emotional template for the family's reconstitution.

The play's comic resolution assembles the entire company at [[marine-hotel|the Marine]] Hotel for a luncheon that functions as a ritual of incorporation: [[crampton|Crampton]] is acknowledged as father, [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] as mother, and the children as legitimate members of a reconstituted family.

## Key Passages

> (002-005) "Allow me to discharge my first duty as host by ordering your wine. … The consciousness of it goes through the father with so keen a pang that he trembles all over; his brow becomes wet; and he stares dumbly at his son" — **Philip / Stage Direction**

The violent emotional cost of estrangement, experienced by [[crampton|Crampton]] as physical pain even as [[philip-clandon|Philip]] treats him with the studied courtesy owed to a casual acquaintance.

> (004-002) "In this family, it appears, the husband's name is Crampton: the wife's Clandon. Thus we have on the very threshold of the case an element of confusion." — **Bohun**

[[bohun|Bohun]]'s legal framing of the family problem — reconciliation must resolve formal and procedural questions of name, status, and settlement, not merely emotional ones.

> (004-002) "If this family difference is to be smoothed over as we all hope it may be, Mrs. Clandon, as a matter of social convenience and decency, will have to resume her husband's name … Or else Mr. Crampton will have to call himself Mr. Clandon." — **Bohun**

The barrister reduces reconciliation to a question of social form — a comic deflation of sentiment that nonetheless names the real obstacle: the pride of both estranged spouses.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of family reconciliation rejects sentimentality without rejecting hope. The Clandon family is restored not because love conquers all but because a network of professionals — solicitor, barrister, waiter — constructs the conditions under which love can operate. The play is a comedy about the institutional infrastructure of intimacy: before people can love each other, they need legal recognition, economic security, and a good lunch.

The deeper Shavian argument is that the Victorian patriarchal family was an institution designed for control rather than love, and that genuine family feeling can only emerge when the institutional framework is rebuilt on terms of equality.
