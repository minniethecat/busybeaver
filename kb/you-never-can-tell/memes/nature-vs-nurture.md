# Nature versus Nurture

## Definition

Nature versus nurture — the question of whether human identity is determined by biological inheritance or by environmental formation — is the philosophical spine of *You Never Can Tell*. Shaw stages it through the Clandon family: three children who share their father's DNA but have been raised exclusively within their mother's rationalist pedagogy. The play's comic resolution affirms nurture's primacy while acknowledging nature's persistence — education can remake character, but biology cannot be entirely reasoned away. [001-004]

## Context

The 1890s debate over heredity versus environment was shaped by Darwin's theory of evolution and Francis Galton's eugenic arguments for inherited intelligence. Shaw, a committed Lamarckian, believed that acquired characteristics could be inherited and that conscious will — the Life Force — drove evolutionary progress. The Fabian socialist program of institutional reform was, at bottom, an environmentalist argument: change the conditions and you change the people.

## In the Play

The experiment is [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s: three children raised without a father, on a curriculum of Darwin, Mill, Huxley, and George Eliot. The results — [[philip-clandon|Philip]]'s composure, [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]]'s irreverence, [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s intellectual independence — are manifestly superior to what Victorian convention would have produced. But when [[crampton|Crampton]] encounters his unknown children (002-005), the wound is nature's revenge: biology asserts a claim that education cannot satisfy. The reconciliation requires both parties to acknowledge what nature gave and what nurture shaped.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Lamarckian convictions make this more than a philosophical puzzle. The play argues that character *is* malleable — that the right education can produce a new kind of person — but also that the biological substrate cannot be denied. [[gloria-clandon|Gloria]]'s love for [[valentine|Valentine]] is not a failure of her education but a demonstration that education must accommodate the whole person, including the emotional life her rational curriculum neglected. The synthesis Shaw offers is nurture working *with* nature rather than against it.
