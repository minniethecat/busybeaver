## Definition

The categories of "gentleman" and "lady" in *You Never Can Tell* are subject to Shaw's characteristic demystification. Throughout the play, characters invoke these terms to claim social status, exclude others, or assert moral superiority — and Shaw repeatedly exposes the gap between the label and the reality. A true gentleman, the play suggests, is defined not by birth or wealth but by conduct; a true lady, not by refinement but by integrity.

## In the Play

Valentine's claim to gentility is precarious — he is a tradesman who has "set up as a dentist" and lives on credit. When he declares he must maintain "the most rigid respectability," he acknowledges that gentlemanly status is a performance sustained by appearances [001-150]. Conversely, Bohun Q.C., whose professional eminence places him beyond question, behaves with a brusqueness that violates every convention of gentlemanly conduct — yet no one denies him the title.

The Clandon children, raised in Madeira with no knowledge of English social codes, instinctively perform the roles of "gentleman" and "lady" with a polish that shames their English counterparts. Shaw's implication is clear: the qualities that define a gentleman — courtesy, self-possession, consideration — are products of character, not breeding.
