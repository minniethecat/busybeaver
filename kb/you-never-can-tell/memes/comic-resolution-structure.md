# Comic Resolution as Critique

## Definition

The deliberately artificial happy ending achieved through Bohun's legal machinery rather than emotional resolution — Shaw's meta-theatrical commentary on the conventions of stage comedy.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

The resolution of *You Never Can Tell* is self-consciously artificial. Three marriages, a family reunion, and a fortune settled — all within minutes of Bohun's arrival. Shaw makes no attempt to disguise the machinery: Bohun drafts settlements on the spot, functioning as a theatrical device rather than a realistic character. The audience is meant to recognize the artifice and appreciate the joke: in Shavian comedy, happy endings are achieved not by fate but by professional competence and legal reason.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
