# Money and Status

## Definition

Money, in *You Never Can Tell*, is the hidden infrastructure of every social relation. [[valentine|Valentine]] cannot afford to be honest because [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]] costs patients; [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] could afford to leave her husband because she earned her own living as an author; [[crampton|Crampton]]'s claim to paternal recognition is inseparable from his property; and the play's resolution depends on an inheritance that eliminates the economic anxiety driving every character's behavior. Shaw, the Fabian economist, constructs a comedy in which love triumphs only after the balance sheet is settled. [001-001]

## Context

Shaw was simultaneously a playwright and an economic polemicist. His Fabian essays and lectures argued that capitalism distorted every human relationship — that inequality made [[conscience-and-truth|honesty]], love, and family life impossible for those without independent means. *You Never Can Tell* is the dramatic expression of this analysis: a play in which money is never the ostensible subject but always the determining condition. Shaw had been an impoverished young man himself, working as a clerk before his writing career, and [[valentine|Valentine]]'s financial precarity — "five shillings in six weeks" (001-243) — has the texture of lived experience.

## In the Play

[[valentine|Valentine]]'s economic anxiety is the play's most immediate expression of money's power. His professional equipment is hired, his furniture is the landlord's, and he has not paid a shilling of rent. His "five-shilling dentist" system is both a marketing innovation and a desperate survival strategy — flat-rate pricing eliminates the need for the professional dis[[conscience-and-truth|honesty]] that ruined him before, but it has produced only five shillings in six weeks.

[[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] represents the counter-case: a woman whose professional income as an author gave her the economic independence to leave an unhappy marriage and raise three children on her own terms. Her [[liberty|freedom]] is real, but it is purchased — as all [[liberty|freedom]] is.

[[crampton|Crampton]]'s wealth is the key that unlocks the resolution. His property — the houses, the investments, the income — is the material basis of his claim to family recognition. The children do not need his money (they have their mother's), but the reconciliation requires his economic acknowledgment.

## Key Passages

> (001-243) "I spent my last sovereign on moving in; and I haven't paid a shilling of rent yet. I'm eating and drinking on credit … and I've made five shillings in six weeks." — **Valentine**

The arithmetic of economic precarity — [[valentine|Valentine]]'s confession is a balance sheet as much as a moral disclosure.

> (001-079) "I'm what's called a five shilling dentist." — **Valentine**

Economic identity as professional identity — the flat fee is both pricing strategy and moral statement.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's economic analysis pervades the play without ever becoming didactic. Money is never discussed in the abstract — it is always specific: five shillings for gas, a crown piece for a first fee, a sovereign for moving in. This concreteness is the mark of Shaw the Fabian, who believed that political economy was about actual people making actual choices under actual constraints. The play's resolution — an inheritance that solves everyone's problems — is deliberately artificial, a comic contrivance that Shaw offers his audience knowing they recognize it as fantasy. In the real world, most five-shilling dentists do not marry into money.
