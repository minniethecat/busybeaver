## Definition

Social respectability in *You Never Can Tell* refers to the unwritten code of propriety that governs behavior in English society, particularly in the seaside resort where the play is set. This code is invoked by [[Valentine]] in Act I when he explains that "in a seaside resort there's one thing you must have before anybody can afford to be seen going about with you; and that's a father, alive or dead" (001-145). Respectability is the invisible force that structures social life, determining who may associate with whom, what may be discussed, and how people must present themselves.

## Context

Victorian respectability was a complex social phenomenon combining genuine moral conviction with class anxiety and social control. It governed everything from dress and manners to family structure and public behavior. For the middle classes particularly, respectability was both an achievement and a burden—the marker of social standing and the constraint on personal freedom. Shaw, as a socialist and iconoclast, regarded respectability as a form of social tyranny that enforced conformity and punished honesty.

## In the Play

Respectability is introduced as a practical problem for the [[Clandon children]]. Having grown up in Madeira without a father, they lack the essential credential for English social life. Valentine's frank explanation—he cannot lunch with them because his precarious professional position requires "the straight line of the most rigid respectability" (001-150)—reveals respectability as both arbitrary and powerful.

The play then systematically exposes respectability's contradictions. The rector's wife wears hygienic boots and advocates dress reform (001-145)—respectability can accommodate advanced views when held by the right people. [[Fergus Crampton]]'s obsession with his children's dress and manners (001-1242) is a parody of respectability's fixation on surfaces. His horror at [[Dolly Clandon]]'s smoking (001-665) and the twins' fancy dress (001-1273) reveals respectability as a defense against genuine engagement with difference.

The play's most devastating critique of respectability comes through [[Bohun]]. The most formidable figure in the play—a Queen's Counsel who dominates every room he enters—is revealed to be the son of a waiter. When [[Walter the Waiter]] collapses in shame at his son appearing in a false nose (001-1175), the scene exposes respectability as a system that makes people ashamed of their own parents. Bohun's response—calm acknowledgment of his father and continuation of his professional work—models a healthier relationship to social status.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of respectability is both satirical and analytical. He exposes its absurdities—the father requirement, the dress code, the horror of scandal—while acknowledging its social power. Valentine's predicament is treated sympathetically: he knows the rules are absurd, but he must follow them to survive. The play's resolution suggests that respectability can be overcome not through rebellion but through transcendence. Bohun does not attack respectability; he simply ignores it, and his competence makes his origins irrelevant. The family's final transformation—Crampton donning a false nose, Mrs. Clandon dancing—suggests that respectability dissolves in the presence of genuine joy. The seaside setting, with its holiday license and social mixing, is the condition of this dissolution.
