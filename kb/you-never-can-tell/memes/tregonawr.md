## Setting

Tregonawr is Fergus Crampton's country estate, mentioned in the play but never seen on stage. It represents the landed property that establishes Crampton's status as a gentleman — the material foundation of his social identity. Like Newbury Hall, Tregonawr functions as an offstage symbol of the traditional English social order based on land ownership.

## Appears In

Tregonawr is referenced in Act III during the discussions of Crampton's circumstances and the marriage settlement. Its existence as property is significant to the financial negotiations that Bohun arranges.

## Significance

Tregonawr represents the old economic order of landed wealth against which Shaw sets the new economy of professional expertise. Crampton's estate, like his outdated conceptions of paternal authority and masculine honor, belongs to a world that is passing. The play's resolution, in which Crampton's wealth is redirected toward supporting the new generation's professional and domestic arrangements, symbolizes the transfer of resources from the old order to the new.
