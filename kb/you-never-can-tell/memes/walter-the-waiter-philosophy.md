## Definition

Walter the Waiter's philosophy is the humane, pragmatic wisdom that the play's most observant character dispenses throughout *You Never Can Tell*. Its core tenet is epistemological modesty: "You never can tell, sir: you never can tell" (004-002). This refrain, repeated at crucial moments, is not a counsel of despair but an acknowledgment that human outcomes are unpredictable and that rigid principles—whether patriarchal authority or feminist rationalism—fail precisely because they claim to know what cannot be known.

## Context

[[Walter the Waiter]] (also known as William, though his real name is Walter, 002-005) is described in the Act II stage direction as "a silky old man, white-haired and delicate looking, but so cheerful and contented that in his encouraging presence ambition stands rebuked as vulgarity, and imagination as treason to the abounding sufficiency and interest of the actual" (002-005). This description establishes Walter not as a comic servant but as a philosophical presence—a man whose contentment with the actual world provides an implicit critique of those who seek to transcend or reform it.

## In the Play

Walter's philosophy is articulated through his actions as much as his words. His management of the disastrous lunch in Act II (002-005) demonstrates practical wisdom: knowing when to intervene, when to withdraw, when to console. He navigates the class dynamics of the situation with extraordinary skill—serving, advising, and finally comforting [[Fergus Crampton]] with a glass of neglected seltzer (002-005).

His account of his relationship with his son, [[Bohun]], is a model of cross-class family relations. "We get on together very well, very well indeed, sir, considering the difference in our stations" (002-005). He refuses to be ashamed of being a waiter or to resent his son's success. His analysis of the symmetry between their professions—both wear uniforms, both rely on tips/fees, both serve all ranks—is a miniature sociology of class that is more penetrating than any of the play's explicit arguments.

The refrain "You never can tell" first appears when [[Valentine]] rushes off to pursue [[Gloria Clandon]]: "Well, sir, you never can tell. That's a principle in life with me, sir" (002-005). It returns at the play's conclusion, as benediction on the engagement: "You never can tell, sir: you never can tell" (004-002). Between these appearances, the phrase operates as a running commentary on the play's improbable events—the accidental reunion, the whirlwind romance, the fancy dress resolution—all of which confirm that outcomes cannot be predicted.

Walter also embodies the philosophy of service. "I have always said that the great advantage of a hotel is that it's a refuge from home life, sir" (002-005). This aphorism, delivered to Crampton in his moment of defeat, is both consolation and wisdom: the hotel, like life, is a temporary arrangement in which imperfect people must manage to coexist.

## Shaw's Commentary

Walter's philosophy represents Shaw's alternative to both the rationalist certainties of Mrs. Clandon and the patriarchal certainties of Crampton. Walter does not claim to know how things will turn out; he observes, adapts, and serves. His wisdom is experiential rather than theoretical, practical rather than ideological. "You never can tell" is not a refusal to think but a recognition that thinking has limits—that life exceeds our categories and that humility before the actual is the beginning of wisdom. Shaw, the lifelong controversialist and opinion-monger, here gives the play's wisest lines to a character who makes no claims to intellect, embodying the paradox that the most profound insight may come from the least expected source.
