## Definition

Quarrel and reconciliation is the structural pattern that organizes the dramatic action of *You Never Can Tell*. The play proceeds through a series of escalating confrontations—the children's demand to know their father (Act I), the disastrous lunch (Act II), the legal threat and romantic crisis (Act III)—before achieving a comprehensive reconciliation in Act IV through the combined agencies of [[Bohun]], the fancy dress ball, and the characters' exhaustion with anger.

## Context

Shaw wrote *You Never Can Tell* as a "pleasant play"—one of the *Plays Pleasant* (1898)—which means that despite its serious themes, it must end happily. The challenge Shaw set himself was to achieve a happy ending without falsifying the psychological truth of the characters' grievances. The quarrel-and-reconciliation pattern is his solution: the conflicts are real and the wounds are deep, but the resolution, while sudden and theatrical, is psychologically credible because all parties are genuinely exhausted by their own anger.

## In the Play

The pattern operates at multiple levels. At the family level, the primary quarrel is between [[Fergus Crampton]] and [[Mrs. Clandon]], whose incompatible temperaments destroyed their marriage eighteen years ago. Their conflict is reignited by the accidental reunion and threatens to escalate into legal warfare. At the romantic level, [[Gloria]] and [[Valentine]] embody the quarrel pattern in miniature—their courtship proceeds through fierce argument, rejection ("Never! While grass grows or water runs, never, never, never!!!" at 001-1003), and eventual reconciliation. Even the twins' interactions with their father follow this rhythm: [[Dolly Clandon]] storms off (003-002), returns in fancy dress, and winds up kissing her father.

The reconciliation in Act IV is achieved through several mechanisms: Bohun's masterful mediation strips away pretense and identifies the real issues; the fancy dress ball provides a carnival atmosphere that dissolves social stiffness; the waiter's humane wisdom offers a model of forbearance; and Valentine and Gloria's engagement creates momentum toward a new family configuration. The final image—everyone dancing off into the lantern-lit garden (001-1410-1417)—transforms conflict into choreography.

Crucially, the reconciliation does not pretend to resolve everything. Mrs. Clandon never warms to Crampton; the name question is never settled; Crampton's gruffness is not magically cured. The achievement is more modest but more honest: the family agrees to coexist, the engagement provides a forward-looking structure, and the "You never can tell" philosophy acknowledges that outcomes cannot be predicted.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of quarrel and reconciliation reflects his conviction that human relationships are fundamentally adversarial but potentially generative. The "duel of sex" (003-002) is not a flaw to be eliminated but a dynamic to be acknowledged and managed. Reconciliation in Shaw is not the suppression of conflict but its transformation into productive engagement. The family that dances off together at the end has not become harmonious; it has learned to quarrel constructively, with Bohun's legal framework and the waiter's personal warmth providing the structure and spirit within which ongoing conflict can be sustained without destruction.
