# Aside and Monologue

## Definition

While Shaw largely eschews formal asides and soliloquies in *You Never Can Tell*, the play is saturated with theatrical self-consciousness -- moments where characters perform for each other and for the audience, where dialogue functions as commentary on the action, and where the play knowingly acknowledges its own artifice. The twins in particular treat life as performance; the Waiter's philosophical interludes function as miniature monologues; and the entire structure of the luncheon party and the Act IV family conference are self-consciously staged social theater.

## Context

By the 1890s, the theatrical aside and soliloquy were falling out of fashion in serious drama under the influence of naturalism and Ibsenite realism. Shaw rarely used formal asides in his major plays, preferring to embed metatheatrical awareness within naturalistic-seeming dialogue. His characters comment on their own behavior as if observing themselves from outside. This technique owes something to Wildean epigram (Shaw was writing *You Never Can Tell* in 1896-97, in the wake of Wilde's success) but is turned to philosophical rather than purely comic ends.

## In the Play

The most explicit theatrical moment comes in Act I when the twins, caught off-guard without an audience, "droop and become commonplace at once" (001-004). When the parlor maid enters, they "instantly shine out again with their former brilliancy" (001-004) -- Shaw stage-manages their self-conscious performance. Philip's formal pronouncements are delivered "with deadly politeness" (002-005), as if he is playing a part in a courtroom drama of his own devising.

The Waiter's extended speech about his son's profession is effectively a monologue, delivered while his listeners sit stunned. Dolly's aside to the Waiter -- "Keep your wits about you, William. There will be fireworks" (002-005) -- is one of the few moments approaching a formal stage aside, a conspiratorial bridge between character and audience.

Bohun's Act IV entrance in a domino and false nose, theatrically removing them with the gesture of "a champion throwing down his glove" (004-002), is the play's most overt metatheatrical moment -- the great Q.C. literally unmasking himself.

## Key Passages

> [001-155] "Missing their audience, they droop and become commonplace at once." -- Shaw's stage direction explicitly frames the twins as performers who require an audience to sustain their brilliance.

> [002-544] Dolly to the Waiter: "Keep your wits about you, William. There will be fireworks." -- A choric aside that frames the coming action as theatrical spectacle.

> [004-1165] Bohun removing his false nose and domino "like a champion throwing down his glove" -- the play's climactic metatheatrical gesture, transforming carnival disguise into legal performance.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's theatrical self-consciousness serves his argument that all social life is performance. The Clandon children have been raised without Victorian "manners" -- yet they are the most self-consciously performative characters on stage. The paradox is deliberate: Shaw suggests that the most honest performance is one that acknowledges its own performativity. The Victorian characters (Crampton, McComas) who believe themselves to be authentic are the most artificial because their performance is unconscious. The twins' self-awareness liberates rather than constrains them.
