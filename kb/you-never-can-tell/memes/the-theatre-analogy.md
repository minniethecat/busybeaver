# The Theatre Analogy

## Definition

When McComas tells Mrs. Clandon that "There is only one place in all England where your opinions would still pass as advanced" and she guesses "The Church, perhaps?" — his reply is "No, the theatre" (002-434). This exchange is Shaw's meta-theatrical self-justification: the playwright claiming that his medium, not the pulpit or the lecture hall, is the true home of radical ideas in modern society.

## Context

By the 1890s, the theatre had become a venue for social criticism in ways that other institutions could not match. The Church of England was an establishment institution, its radical energies channeled into Christian Socialism. The universities excluded women and dissenters. Parliament was inaccessible to most. But the theatre — particularly the new "problem play" pioneered by Ibsen and championed by Shaw — could reach a broad middle-class audience with ideas that would be censored or ignored elsewhere.

Shaw's own career as a drama critic (writing for the *Saturday Review* from 1895 to 1898) was built on the conviction that theatre mattered — that what happened on stage had real consequences for how people thought about marriage, property, class, and gender. McComas's throwaway line is Shaw's manifesto in miniature.

## In the Play

McComas's observation occurs in Act II, during his reunion with Mrs. Clandon. She has been listing her unchanged opinions — Darwin, Mill, Huxley, George Eliot, women's suffrage, university degrees for women — and McComas has been gently disabusing her of the notion that these are still radical. "I'm out of everything," he admits, "because I've refused to bow the knee to Socialism" (002-429).

The theatre reference is double-edged. On one level, it is a joke at the expense of the theatre — the place where outdated radicals go to feel relevant. On another level, it is Shaw's serious claim: the stage, not the church or the university, is where society's most important arguments now take place. *You Never Can Tell* itself is the proof — a commercial comedy that smuggles feminist, socialist, and Darwinian ideas past the audience's defenses by making them laugh.

## Key Passages

> (002-434) "There is only one place in all England where your opinions would still pass as advanced." / "The Church, perhaps?" / "No, the theatre." — McComas and Mrs. Clandon, Shaw's meta-theatrical self-positioning.

> (002-427) The catalog of Mrs. Clandon's opinions — Darwin, Mill, Huxley, Tyndall, George Eliot, women's suffrage — establishes the intellectual credentials that the theatre now inherits.

> (002-429) "I'm out of everything, because I've refused to bow the knee to Socialism." — McComas's confession that Spencerian individualism has been overtaken, clearing the ground for Shaw's Fabian alternative.

## Shaw's Commentary

The theatre analogy is Shaw's most direct act of professional self-assertion in the play. By having McComas identify the stage as the home of advanced thought, Shaw is claiming for his own art form the cultural authority that Victorian society reserved for the church, the university, and Parliament. It is a characteristically Shavian move — at once self-promoting and self-mocking, making a serious claim in the form of a joke. The play that contains this line is itself the evidence for its truth.
