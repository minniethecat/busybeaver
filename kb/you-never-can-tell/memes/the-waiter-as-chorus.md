## Definition

The waiter as chorus describes [[Walter the Waiter]]'s structural function in *You Never Can Tell* as a choric figure: a character who stands partly outside the action, comments on it for the audience's benefit, and provides a moral and philosophical perspective that frames the play's events. This function is distinct from his role as a character in the plot; it is a dramatic technique through which Shaw embeds commentary within the action without resorting to authorial intrusion.

## Context

The chorus is an ancient dramatic device, originating in Greek tragedy, where a group of performers commented on the action and provided the audience with moral and narrative context. Shaw adapts this device to the realistic conventions of modern drama by embodying the choric function in a single, fully realized character. Walter is both a believable waiter—with a backstory, motivations, and relationships—and a chorus whose observations guide the audience's interpretation of events.

## In the Play

Walter's choric function is established in the Act II stage direction, which describes him in terms that transcend naturalistic characterization: "so cheerful and contented that in his encouraging presence ambition stands rebuked as vulgarity, and imagination as treason to the abounding sufficiency and interest of the actual" (002-005). This is not the description of a character but of a moral perspective made flesh.

Walter exercises his choric function through several techniques. He provides narrative exposition: informing [[Valentine]] and [[Crampton]] about the family's conversations, summarizing the twins' jokes, and reporting on the fancy dress ball arrangements. He offers moral commentary: his assessment of the children's dress (001-1264) resolves the dispute between Crampton and the family by providing expert, disinterested judgment. He speaks the play's thematic refrain: "You never can tell" (001-715, 001-1406) encapsulates the play's wisdom in four words.

Crucially, Walter's choric function is integrated with his character. He is not a disembodied narrator but a man with a son, a career, and a personal history. His choric observations emerge from his experience as a waiter—a professional observer of human behavior. This integration is what makes the device work: Walter's wisdom is earned, not imposed, and his authority derives from his intimate knowledge of the world he comments on.

Walter's most remarkable choric moment is his speech about the symmetry between his profession and his son's: "If I must put on a dress coat to show what I am, sir, he must put on a wig and gown to show what he is" (002-005). This is social analysis of the highest order, delivered in the voice of a waiter, and it reframes the play's class dynamics more effectively than any of the explicit arguments.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's use of the waiter as chorus is a technical innovation that solves a persistent problem of didactic drama: how to convey the author's perspective without breaking the dramatic illusion. By embodying his commentary in a character whose profession is observation and service, Shaw makes the choric function naturalistic. Walter's wisdom is not authorial intrusion but professional expertise—he knows human nature because he has served it for decades. The device also serves Shaw's democratic instincts: the play's wisest character is not the Q.C., the solicitor, or the authoress, but the waiter. The chorus speaks from below, and its perspective is the more penetrating for that.
