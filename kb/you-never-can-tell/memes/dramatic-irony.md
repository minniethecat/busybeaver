## Definition

Dramatic irony in *You Never Can Tell* operates through the audience's early knowledge of the family secret. We know, well before the Clandon children do, that the gentleman at the Marine Hotel is their father; we watch their innocent interactions with Crampton through the lens of this knowledge. Shaw's deployment of dramatic irony differs from the melodramatic tradition, however, in that the irony serves intellectual rather than sensational purposes — it invites the audience to reflect on the nature of knowledge, concealment, and recognition.

## In the Play

The dramatic irony is established early, when McComas reveals Crampton's identity to Mrs. Clandon in Act I, and sustained through the luncheon scenes in Act II where the children banter with a father they do not recognize. Shaw handles the irony with restraint — he does not milk it for suspense but allows it to function as a structural principle, organizing the audience's experience of the gradual movement toward recognition and reconciliation.
