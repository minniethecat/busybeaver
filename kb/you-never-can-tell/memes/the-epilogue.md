# The Play's Epilogue

## Definition

The brief closing moment after the settlements are signed — the characters dispersed, the lunch concluded, the waiter philosophizing — as Shaw's final comment on the artificiality of happy endings.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

The play ends not with the marriages but with their aftermath — the characters dispersed, the waiter reflecting on what has occurred. This brief epilogue functions as a Shavian wink: the happy ending has been achieved, but the audience is reminded that it was achieved through theatrical machinery, not realistic probability. The waiter, as always, has the last word — or rather, the last philosophical observation. The play closes not on a declaration of love but on a note of professional contentment, which is, for Shaw, the truest form of happiness.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
