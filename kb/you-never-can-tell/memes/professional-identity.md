# Professional Identity

## Definition

Professional identity in *You Never Can Tell* is a performed social role — a costume of competence, [[victorian-respectability|respectability]], and authority that characters don to navigate a society organized around occupational prestige. Shaw systematically exposes the gap between professional performance and personal reality: [[valentine|Valentine]]'s "professional manner" is "a joke" (001-003), his diploma is cosmetic, and his equipment is hired. The play suggests that all professional identity is, to some degree, theatrical — and that the real measure of a person lies in how they behave when the performance is suspended. [001-001]

## Context

The late nineteenth century saw the consolidation of the modern professions — medicine, law, dentistry — with formal credentials, professional associations, and standardized fee structures. The M.R.C.S. diploma on [[valentine|Valentine]]'s wall and [[bohun|Bohun]]'s Q.C. designation are markers of this new occupational order. Shaw, who worked as a clerk before becoming a critic and playwright, was acutely attuned to the ways professional identity functioned as a [[social-class|class]] marker, separating gentlemen from tradesmen and conferring social authority independent of personal merit.

## In the Play

[[valentine|Valentine]] is the play's central exploration of professional identity in crisis. The stage direction describes him as a man whose "professional manner evidently strikes him as being a joke" (001-003), and his confession reveals a history of professional failure driven by an excess of integrity. His "five-shilling dentist" system is both a professional innovation and a surrender — a way of practicing that eliminates the need for the professional judgment (and deception) that ruined him before.

The contrast figure is [[bohun|Bohun]], the Q.C., whose professional identity is his entire being. Where [[valentine|Valentine]]'s professional manner is a transparent performance, [[bohun|Bohun]]'s is internalized to the point of becoming character: he cannot stop being a barrister even at a family luncheon. The waiter [[walter-the-waiter|Walter]] provides a third model — professional service performed with such grace that it transcends its station, earning him the respect of every character in the play.

## Key Passages

> (001-003) "He does not give the impression of being much of a workman: his professional manner evidently strikes him as being a joke, and is underlain by a thoughtless pleasantry which betrays the young gentleman still unsettled and in search of amusing adventures, behind the newly set up dentist in search of patients." — **Stage Direction**

Shaw's description of [[valentine|Valentine]]'s divided professional self — the role and the man behind it in unresolved tension.

> (001-243) "On both occasions I acted conscientiously, and told my patients the brute truth instead of what they wanted to be told. Result, ruin. Now I've set up as a dentist, a five shilling dentist; and I've done with conscience forever." — **Valentine**

Professional identity as a moral compromise — integrity is incompatible with commercial survival.

> (002-849) "What am I? a dentist — a five shilling dentist!" — **Valentine**

Professional identity deployed as self-abasement — [[valentine|Valentine]] uses his occupation to define himself downward, away from the possibility of love.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's critique is that professional identity, as constructed in Victorian society, is a form of licensed hypocrisy. The doctor must tell comforting lies; the dentist must pretend his rented furniture is his own; the barrister must advocate positions he does not believe. Only the waiter, whose profession requires no pretense of moral authority, can achieve an authentic professional grace. Shaw's solution is not to abolish professions but to make them transparent — to create a society in which professional competence does not require moral compromise.
