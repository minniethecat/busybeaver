## Definition

Fatherhood in *You Never Can Tell* operates as both an absence and a social imperative. The Clandon children have been raised without knowledge of their father, Fergus Crampton; yet the first social test they encounter in England is whether they possess one. Shaw uses this paradox to critique Victorian society's conflation of paternal lineage with social legitimacy.

## In the Play

The question of fatherhood drives the play's plot from its opening scene. Valentine, the five-shilling dentist, initially refuses to lunch with Dolly and Philip because they lack a father — "in a seaside resort there's one thing you must have before anybody can afford to be seen going about with you; and that's a father, alive or dead" [001-145]. When the twins reveal they have a grandfather who is a canon of Lincoln Cathedral, Valentine immediately reverses his position, exposing the absurdity of the social rule [001-150].

Mrs. Clandon's revelation that she fled her husband because he beat her with a whip reframes fatherhood as a threat rather than a benefit [001-259]. Yet the play ultimately moves toward reconciliation rather than condemnation, with the children seeking to know and forgive their father. The final scenes at the Marine Hotel bring about a cautious family reunion, with Crampton's genuine remorse and his children's willingness to accept him suggesting that fatherhood, however flawed, retains value.

## Key Passages

> "in a seaside resort there's one thing you must have before anybody can afford to be seen going about with you; and that's a father, alive or dead" [001-145]

Valentine articulates the social demand for paternity as a credential of respectability.

> "No man alive shall father me." [001-159]

Philip's defiant rejection of paternal authority reflects Shaw's critique of patriarchal power, even as the play ultimately softens this stance.

> "Gloria: if I had ever struck you — struck you purposely, deliberately, with the intention of hurting you, with a whip bought for the purpose! Would you remember that, do you think? That would have been your last recollection of your father, Gloria, if I had not taken you away from him." [001-259]

Mrs. Clandon's justification for separating her children from their father reveals the moral complexity of her decision.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses fatherhood as a lens through which to examine Victorian social hypocrisy. The legal and social authority vested in fathers is exposed as arbitrary — Crampton's biological paternity gives him no moral claim to his children's affection. Yet Shaw, ever the reformer rather than the nihilist, allows for redemption: the family's tentative reconciliation at the play's end suggests that fatherhood, properly understood as earned rather than automatic, can be a positive force.

## Fatherhood Across Class

Shaw sets the Clandons' damaged paternity against a contrasting model drawn from a lower social rank. The waiter — whose real name is Walter, though the Clandons call him William — has a son who has risen far above his father's station: in Act III the waiter reveals, "with a touch of melancholy," that his boy went not to the drinking bar but to the legal one — "your profession, sir. A Q.C., sir." That son is Bohun, the eminent counsel eventually brought in to arbitrate the Clandon family quarrel. The waiter's quiet pride in a child who has outstripped him — and Bohun's evident respect for his father across that class gulf — offers the play's one image of paternity that simply *works*, requiring neither rehabilitation nor forgiveness.

The twins improvise surrogate fathers in the same spirit. Fatherless in a society that demands a father, Philip playfully recruits the waiter himself, asking to be regarded as his son ("William: you remember my request to you to regard me as your son?") — a joke that nonetheless dramatizes how readily affection can be extended across the lines of blood and class that Victorian respectability treated as fixed. Against these freely chosen and freely working bonds, Crampton's insistence on paternity as a *right* looks all the more hollow: the play repeatedly shows fatherhood succeeding precisely where it is offered and accepted rather than demanded.
