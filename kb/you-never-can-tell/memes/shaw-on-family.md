# Shaw on Family

## Definition

In *You Never Can Tell*, Shaw presents a radical vision of the family as a voluntary association founded on mutual respect rather than biological imperative. The Clandon household -- mother and three children living in Madeira without a father -- represents an alternative family structure that has successfully raised children to be confident, honest, and intellectually independent. The play tests this model by reintroducing the biological father and asking whether blood relationship confers any claim that freely chosen affection does not.

## Context

Shaw's ideas about family drew from multiple sources: the feminist critique of patriarchal marriage, the socialist vision of collective child-rearing, and his own experience of an unconventional Dublin upbringing. The Fabian Society debated family reform extensively. Shaw was influenced by Ibsen's *A Doll's House* (1879), which dramatized a wife's departure from an oppressive marriage, and by Samuel Butler's *The Way of All Flesh* (published 1903 but known to Shaw earlier), which savaged Victorian family piety. The idea that family should be chosen rather than inherited was central to progressive thought in the 1890s.

## In the Play

Mrs. Clandon's statement of principle establishes the alternative family model: "There are two sorts of family life, Phil ... The sort you know is based on mutual respect, on recognition of the right of every member of the household to independence and privacy in their personal concerns" (001-195). This is contrasted with the Victorian model of "duty, obedience, affection, home, morality and religion" as "detestable tyrannies."

The Clandon children embody the success of this model: they are articulate, affectionate, and unfrightened by authority. Philip's declaration is Shaw's thesis made explicit: "Here you find a family enjoying the unspeakable peace and freedom of being orphans. We have never seen the face of a relative -- never known a claim except the claim of freely chosen friendship" (002-489).

The play's resolution is nuanced. Crampton is not excluded; he is allowed a relationship with his children, but on terms of mutual consent rather than patriarchal right. Philip's "Coming, dad, coming" (004-1336) marks the transition from biological assertion to voluntary affiliation.

## Key Passages

> [001-195] Mrs. Clandon: "The sort you know is based on mutual respect, on recognition of the right of every member of the household to independence and privacy in their personal concerns." -- The positive model of family as voluntary association.

> [002-489] Philip: "Here you find a family enjoying the unspeakable peace and freedom of being orphans. We have never seen the face of a relative -- never known a claim except the claim of freely chosen friendship." -- The Shavian thesis in its most explicit form.

> [004-1336] Philip, turning after Crampton exits: "Did you feel the pathos of that?" -- The comic acknowledgment that even voluntary families can expand to include the previously excluded.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw does not argue for the abolition of family but for its reconstitution on voluntary principles. The Clandons are a family -- they love each other, protect each other, and function as a unit -- but their bonds are chosen, not coerced. Crampton's return constitutes a test: can the voluntary family absorb the biological father without reverting to patriarchal patterns? The play's answer is an optimistic yes, but only after Crampton has been disarmed (by gas), humbled (by his children's indifference), and forced to seek connection on their terms rather than his.
