## Definition

The whip with which Fergus Crampton beat his wife is the play's most potent symbol — an object never seen on stage but vividly described in Mrs. Clandon's confession. It represents patriarchal violence in its most literal form: the legal right of a Victorian husband to physically discipline his wife, enforced through an instrument designed for the domination of animals. The whip transforms Crampton from a figure of comic bluster into a figure with a genuinely dark past, deepening the play's moral stakes.

## In the Play

Mrs. Clandon's revelation of the whip occurs in Act I, in response to Gloria's defense of their unknown father. The detail is devastating in its specificity: "struck you purposely, deliberately, with the intention of hurting you, with a whip bought for the purpose" [001-259]. The whip functions as the play's moral anchor — it prevents the audience from dismissing Mrs. Clandon's flight as merely eccentric or ideological, and it makes Crampton's redemption contingent on genuine acknowledgment of the harm he has done.
