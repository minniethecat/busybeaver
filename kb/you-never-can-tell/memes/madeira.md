# Madeira

## Setting

Madeira is a Portuguese archipelago in the North Atlantic, approximately 400 kilometers north of the Canary Islands. In the late nineteenth century, it was a popular winter resort for [[money-and-status|wealth]]y Europeans, known for its mild climate and wine. In *You Never Can Tell*, it functions as an offstage space — never shown but constantly referenced — where [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] spent eighteen years raising her children and writing the *[[twentieth-century-women|Twentieth Century Treatises]]*.

## Appears In

Madeira is referenced throughout the play but never appears on stage. [[philip-clandon|Philip]] mentions it in Act I when introducing the family ("an authoress of great repute — in Madeira"); [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] discusses it with [[mc-comas|McComas]] in Act II.

## Key Scenes

> (001-136) Philip's introduction: "We are the children of the celebrated Mrs. Lanfrey Clandon, an authoress of great repute — in Madeira. No household is complete without her works. We came to England to get away from them."

> (003-124) Mrs. Clandon's reflection on her exile: "I felt that I had no right to bury her alive in Madeira — my St. Helena, Finch." The Napoleon reference is precise: Madeira was her prison, but also the place where she produced her life's work.

## Atmosphere

Madeira is doubly coded in the play: as paradise (the warm island where the children grew up free) and as prison (the exile where [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] was cut off from the intellectual life of England). It represents both escape from patriarchal authority and estrangement from home — the costs and benefits of the [[new-woman|New Woman]]'s independence are compressed into a single geographical reference.

## Thematic Significance

Madeira functions as the symbolic counterweight to [[marine-hotel|the Marine Hotel]]. Both are liminal seaside spaces where the [[victorian-respectability|rules of English society]] are suspended — but they work in opposite directions. Madeira is exile from England; the Marine Hotel is return to it. Madeira is where [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]] wrote the *[[twentieth-century-women|Twentieth Century Treatises]]* in isolation; the Marine Hotel is where those ideas are tested against living Englishmen. The eighteen-year Madeiran interlude is the precondition for everything that happens in the play — without it, there are no "un-English" Clandon children, no *Twentieth Century Women*, no [[new-woman|New Woman]] confronting old convention.
