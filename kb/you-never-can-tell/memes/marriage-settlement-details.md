## Definition

The marriage settlement details refer to the legal and financial arrangements that [[Bohun]] insists upon as a condition of approving [[Gloria Clandon]]'s engagement to the penniless [[Valentine]] in Act IV. When Valentine protests that "the gentleman hasn't a rap" (004-002), Bohun responds with his characteristic directness: "Then insist on a settlement. That shocks your delicacy: most sensible precautions do" (004-002). This insistence on practical arrangements amid the romantic climax is Shaw's way of arguing that sound marriages require sound finances.

## Context

Marriage settlements were a standard feature of Victorian upper- and middle-class life, designed primarily to protect the wife's property from her husband's control and to ensure provision for children. Under the Married Women's Property Acts (1870, 1882), married women gradually gained the right to hold property independently—a cause [[Mrs. Clandon]] had championed in her youth (002-005). The settlement in the play thus operates at two levels: as a practical legal instrument and as a symbol of the reformed marriage Shaw advocated.

## In the Play

The settlement discussion takes place in the final moments of Act IV, after Gloria has proposed and the engagement is announced. [[Finch McComas]], ever the solicitor, frames the issue: "The lady has some property, and will probably have a good deal more" (004-002). [[Fergus Crampton]] confirms this: "Possibly. I hope so" (004-002). Valentine immediately protests his poverty, and Bohun intervenes with the blunt demand for a settlement.

Gloria's response—"He shall have a settlement" (004-002)—is significant. She, not her father or her fiance, commits to the financial arrangement. This inverts the conventional Victorian pattern where the bride's father negotiated the settlement with the groom. Shaw is demonstrating that the reformed woman takes active responsibility for the economic dimension of marriage rather than being its passive object.

Bohun then delivers his characteristically paradoxical wisdom: "When you're married, she won't take yours either—oh, no, you won't: you think you will; but you won't. He'll set to work and earn his living—oh, yes, you will: you think you won't; but you will. She'll make you" (004-002). This prediction caps the settlement discussion by asserting that the real economic outcome of the marriage will be determined by character, not contract.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of the marriage settlement reflects his conviction that romantic love and economic realism are not opposed but complementary. The settlement is not a cold intrusion of commerce into romance but the necessary foundation on which genuine partnership can be built. By having Gloria, not her father, take the lead on the settlement, Shaw demonstrates that economic agency is an essential component of women's emancipation. And by having Bohun predict that the settlement's terms will matter less than the couple's ongoing negotiation of power, Shaw acknowledges that no legal instrument can fully determine the course of a marriage—a realism that complements rather than contradicts the play's romantic ending.
