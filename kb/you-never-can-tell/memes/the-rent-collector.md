## Definition

The economic dimension of *You Never Can Tell* — rent, credit, fees, settlements — is unusually explicit for a comedy of the period. Valentine's financial precarity is established in his first scene: he owes six weeks' rent, eats on credit, and has earned exactly five shillings. The landlord, though never appearing on stage, exerts constant pressure, his demands shaping Valentine's behavior and reminding the audience that the comedy of manners unfolds on a material foundation of economic necessity.

## In the Play

Valentine's economic situation is both a source of comedy and a serious commentary on the condition of the modern professional. His relief when the Clandons' presence creates "the apparent boom in my business" is comic, but the desperation underlying it is real [001-165]. The play's resolution addresses economic reality directly — Bohun's settlement includes financial provisions that make the marriage possible. Shaw refuses to separate romantic resolution from economic arrangement, insisting that love, like dentistry, has its material conditions.
