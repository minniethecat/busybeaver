## Definition

The gas pump and cylinder beside Valentine's operating chair in Act I function as more than period medical equipment — they are a visual metaphor for the play's central action. Just as the dental gas would render a patient insensible to the pain of extraction, so the social conventions of the seaside resort — lunch invitations, hotel dining, professional courtesy — provide a kind of social anaesthesia under which the painful truths of the Clandon family history can be extracted.

## In the Play

The gas pump is explicitly referenced when Dolly declines anaesthesia to save five shillings — a choice that Valentine finds shocking: "Why didn't you let me give you gas?" [001-050]. Dolly's willingness to endure pain rather than pay for relief establishes her character's toughness and foreshadows the family's capacity to face difficult truths directly. The gas pump remains present throughout Act I as a visual reminder that extraction — of teeth and of truths — is the business at hand.
