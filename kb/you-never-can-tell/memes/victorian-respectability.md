# Victorian Respectability

## Definition

Victorian respectability was the dominant social code of the English middle [[social-class|class]]es in the nineteenth century — a system of manners, morals, and material display that signaled one's fitness for social credit. In *You Never Can Tell*, Shaw treats respectability not as genuine morality but as defensive camouflage: a performance designed to secure commercial trust and [[social-class|social standing]] while concealing the fear, desire, and economic precarity that actually drive human behavior. The play's setting — a [[marine-hotel|seaside hotel]] whose decor "suggests early Victorian commercial respectability" — is itself a character in this satire. [001-001]

## Context

The Victorian cult of respectability reached its zenith in the 1880s and 1890s, when it functioned as a gatekeeping mechanism for the expanding middle [[social-class|class]]. Proper dress, correct speech, church attendance, and sexual propriety were mandatory performances; failure at any of them could mean social and economic ruin. Shaw, as a Fabian socialist and critic, saw respectability as a system of ideological control — what he called "Bible fetishism" and "fear of hell always at war with fear of poverty." His plays consistently attack respectability as the enemy of both individual vitality and social progress.

## In the Play

The detailed stage direction that opens Act I is a sustained satirical inventory of Victorian respectability in material form: the M.R.C.S. diploma framed on the chimneypiece, the "black marble which gives the fireplace the air of a miniature family vault," the "ormolu clock under a glass cover … its uselessness emphasized by a cheap American clock" (001-001). Every object signals aspiration to respectability while simultaneously revealing its hollowness — the decor is the landlord's, the professional plant is hired, the diploma is decorative rather than substantive.

The play's plot is driven by respectability anxieties: [[crampton|Crampton]]'s horror at discovering his estranged family living in apparent impropriety; [[mrs-clandon|Mrs. Clandon]]'s fear that the children's ignorance of their father will become a social scandal; the waiter [[walter-the-waiter|Walter]]'s exquisite negotiation of [[social-class|class]] boundaries through professional deportment. Each character is defined by their relationship to the respectability code — whether they perform it, rebel against it, or, in the case of [[dolly-clandon|Dolly]] and [[philip-clandon|Philip]], treat it as an absurd game.

## Key Passages

> (001-001) "…all combine with the black marble which gives the fireplace the air of a miniature family vault, to suggest early Victorian commercial respectability, belief in money, Bible fetishism, fear of hell always at war with fear of poverty, instinctive horror of the passionate character of art, love and Roman Catholic religion, and all the first fruits of plutocracy in the early generations of the industrial revolution."

Shaw's stage direction is a compact critical essay on the psychological foundations of Victorian respectability — fear, money, and the repression of passion.

> (001-004) "I'm eating and drinking on credit; my landlord is as rich as a Jew and as hard as nails; and I've made five shillings in six weeks. If I swerve by a hair's breadth from the straight line of the most rigid respectability, I'm done for." — **Valentine**

[[valentine|Valentine]] articulates the economic reality beneath the performance: respectability is survival.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's satire of Victorian respectability is not mere mockery — it is a structural analysis. The passage in the opening stage direction functions like a Marxian reading of interior design: the furniture, the clock, the diploma, the wallpaper are all ideological objects, material expressions of a [[social-class|class]] that knows its ascendancy is recent and precarious. By setting the play in a rented room furnished with the landlord's taste, Shaw makes visible what respectability conceals: that the performance of solidity is itself a commodity, rented by the month.

The play's resolution — an inheritance that removes economic anxiety and enables the characters to love freely — is Shaw's comic solution to the problem respectability poses. The happy ending is possible only when money ceases to be a concern, which is to say: when the economic basis of respectability is eliminated.
