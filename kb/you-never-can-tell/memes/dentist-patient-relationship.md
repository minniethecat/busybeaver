# The Dentist-Patient Relationship

## Definition

Valentine's interactions with his patients — Dolly, Crampton — as microcosms of professional ethics, where the power imbalance of the dental chair becomes a stage for negotiating trust, authority, and humanity.

## Context

The late Victorian period of the 1890s was an era of rapid social and intellectual change. Victorian certainties were being challenged by Darwinism, socialism, feminism, and the emergence of new professional classes. Shaw's *You Never Can Tell* captures this transitional moment at an English seaside resort in August 1896.

## In the Play

The dental chair creates an inherently unequal relationship: the patient is physically vulnerable, reclining, unable to speak, while the dentist stands above with instruments. Shaw exploits this inequality for both comedy and commentary. Crampton, the hardened man, is rendered unconscious by gas despite his protests. Dolly, the seemingly frivolous girl, manages the encounter with complete poise. The dentist-patient relationship becomes a laboratory for exploring power, trust, and the possibility of humane professional practice.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses the seaside resort as a liminal space where social conventions are suspended. The characters who represent the future are attractive and life-affirming; those representing the past are rigid and isolated until transformed. The play's comic form is itself the argument: laughter dissolves outdated convention.
