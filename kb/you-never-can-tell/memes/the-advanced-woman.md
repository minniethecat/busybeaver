## Definition

The advanced woman in *You Never Can Tell* refers specifically to the generation of feminist intellectuals who came of age in the 1860s-80s and who championed causes including married women's property rights, university education for women, Darwinian science, and John Stuart Mill's liberalism. [[Mrs. Clandon]] is the play's exemplar of this type: "an advanced woman, accustomed to defy public opinion, and with no regard for what the world might say of you" (003-002). The play treats this figure with a mixture of respect for her courage and irony about her historical obsolescence.

## Context

The advanced women of the mid-Victorian period were pioneers who faced genuine hostility—"howled at," as Mrs. Clandon recalls (002-005). They fought for access to education, professions, and political rights in a society that regarded such ambitions as unnatural. By the 1890s, however, the intellectual landscape had shifted. Socialism had emerged as the new radicalism, and the liberal individualism of the earlier movement seemed dated. McComas's confession—"I'm out of everything, because I've refused to bow the knee to Socialism" (002-005)—captures the generational shift.

## In the Play

Mrs. Clandon's advanced principles structure her entire life. She left her abusive husband, supported herself through writing, raised her children on rational principles, and educated Gloria to "take up my work where I left it" (002-005). Her intellectual commitments are genuine and her courage is real. When McComas asks whether she still holds to her old opinions, she replies firmly: "As firmly as ever" (002-005).

Yet the play exposes the limitations of advanced thinking. Mrs. Clandon's rationalism has left her emotionally impoverished: she confesses "I have never been in love" (003-002). Her principles, when challenged by her children's demand to know their father, prove to be aspirational rather than practiced (001-004). And her assumption that rational education can protect women from the "duel of sex" is demonstrated to be tragically naive.

McComas provides the external perspective on the advanced woman's fate. His account of becoming respectable—"Would you employ a solicitor with a beard?" (002-005)—and losing his young disciples to Socialism registers the melancholy of a movement that succeeded in changing laws but failed to change hearts. The theatre, not the church, is now the only place where advanced opinions "would still pass as advanced" (002-005)—a characteristically Shavian observation about the displacement of politics by culture.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of the advanced woman is a study in historical irony. Mrs. Clandon's principles were genuinely radical in their time, and her courage was real. But history has moved on, and the very success of the advanced woman's program—women's education, professional opportunities, legal reforms—has created a younger generation for whom those achievements are taken for granted. Gloria does not need to fight for access to ideas; she needs to learn how to integrate ideas with feelings. The play suggests that the advanced woman's greatest achievement—the education of her daughters—has produced women who must move beyond their mother's rationalism toward a more comprehensive understanding of human life. This is not a repudiation of the advanced woman but an acknowledgment that each generation must solve its own problems with its own resources.
