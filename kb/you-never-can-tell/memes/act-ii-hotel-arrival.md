## Definition

Act II of *You Never Can Tell* shifts the action to the Marine Hotel, where the separate threads established in Act I converge. The act introduces Walter the Waiter as a presiding presence, brings Crampton into contact with his unrecognized children, and stages the first explicit confrontation between the estranged spouses. Shaw manages the tonal shift from the intimacy of the dental office to the public space of the hotel with careful attention to the different social dynamics each setting enables. [002-001]

## In the Play

The act opens with Walter the Waiter's philosophical reflections on his profession, establishing the hotel as a space governed by service rather than domestic authority. The Clandon children arrive for lunch, followed by Valentine, McComas, and finally Crampton. The recognition scene — in which Crampton gradually realizes that these charming young people are his own children — is handled with Shaw's characteristic restraint, avoiding sentimentality while preserving emotional weight. The act closes with the family's agreement to meet again, the first tentative step toward reconciliation.
