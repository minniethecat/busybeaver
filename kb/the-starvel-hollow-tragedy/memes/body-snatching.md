## Definition

Body-snatching, in its strict legal sense, is the unlawful removal of a corpse from its grave. Historically associated with the 18th and early 19th centuries — when medical schools paid for cadavers for anatomical dissection, leading to the infamous "resurrection men" — the crime had become rare by the 1920s. In the context of criminal investigation, body-snatching takes on a forensic dimension: the removal of a body to prevent its identification, to substitute it for another, or to conceal the true circumstances of death.

## Role in the Novel

Body-snatching emerges as one of the most shocking elements of the Starvel case. Inspector French, having reconstructed the crime through "analysis and deduction" [016-012], reaches a conclusion that requires verification through exhumation: the body of Markham Giles, a man who died shortly before the Starvel fire, may not be in his coffin at all.

French obtains an exhumation order and assembles a party at the Thirsby cemetery shortly after midnight. The group includes Dr. Laming (the Home Office representative), Colonel Followes (the local magistrate), Sergeant Kent, and two gravediggers [016-008]. After an hour of digging by acetylene lamplight, the coffin is raised. The brass plate reads: "Markham Giles, died 14th September, 1926. Aged 36" [016-011].

The lid is unscrewed and raised — and the onlookers, prepared for a gruesome sight, are instead met with incredulous amazement. "There was no sign of Markham Giles' body or any other! Instead, the coffin was half-full of dark, peaty earth" [016-012]. The body has been stolen.

## Significance in the Plot

This revelation is the novel's decisive turning point. French had theorised that Roper murdered Giles, stole his body, and placed it in Starvel before the fire — so that the charred remains found in the ruins would be identified as four victims (Averill, the two Ropers, and Giles) rather than three. The missing Giles body also enabled Roper to assume Giles's identity as part of his escape.

The discovery vindicates French's entire chain of reasoning: "The conclusion which French had reached by analysis and deduction had been tested and had proved true, and that proof established at one and the same time the whole of the steps of his line of reasoning" [016-012]. It confirms that "Roper was guilty of one of the most diabolical plots ever conceived in the mind of a criminal" — a scheme involving the sacrifice of three lives to facilitate the theft of Averill's fortune [016-012].

## The Exhumation as Procedural Set-Piece

Chapter 12 ("A Somewhat Gruesome Chapter") is one of the novel's most atmospheric sequences. Crofts renders the exhumation in precise procedural detail — the tarpaulin laid beside the grave, the removal of sods, the hour of digging through "dark soil and then through yellow" [016-009], the dull thud of spade striking coffin, the slow withdrawal of screws — building suspense through methodical realism rather than melodrama. The cemetery at night, with "shadowy and menacing forms" of shrubs and bushes moving in the wind, provides the novel's most Gothic moment [016-009].

The term "body-snatching" is explicitly used in the novel's summary: French tells his colleague Willis that the case involves "Murder — quadruple murder — theft, arson and body-snatching," to which Willis responds "Body-snatching? Good Lord! You don't often hear of that nowadays" [020-003].
