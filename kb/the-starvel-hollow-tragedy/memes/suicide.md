# Suicide

**Suicide** appears in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy* both as a tragic element of character backstory and as an investigative hypothesis that French must consider and ultimately rule out.

## Ruth's Mother

The most significant suicide in the novel occurs in the backstory of Ruth Averill. After her husband Theodore disappeared following a gambling-room killing, Ruth's mother "collapsed altogether. She brought Ruth, a child of four, to Simon, begged him to look after her, and then committed suicide." [1727-1727] This event shapes Ruth's entire life trajectory, placing her in Simon Averill's household at Starvel and ultimately positioning her as heiress to the Averill estate.

## The Giles Hypothesis

When French investigates the death of Dr. Giles, he must consider the possibility raised by others: "You mean that Mr. Giles might have committed suicide?" [2693-2693] The question reflects the investigative necessity of examining all possible causes of death before concluding foul play.

French ultimately rejects suicide as inconsistent with the physical evidence. His analysis of the body disposal at the canal bridge reinforces this conclusion: "A suicide might have been feared. Some officious person might have come forward. No, the unknown would simply chuck his parcel over without even turning his head, secure in the belief that even if by some miracle it was found, the contents would never be traced to him." [2979-2979] The killer's calculated method argues against any self-destructive intent.

## Suicide as Contrast

The novel uses suicide as a counterpoint to murder — one representing an internal collapse, the other an external violation. Ruth's mother's suicide arises from despair; the novel's murders arise from greed. This distinction sharpens the moral architecture of the story, where those who take their own lives are victims of circumstance, while those who take others' lives are architects of crime.
