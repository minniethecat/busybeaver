# Duty

**Duty** is a recurring ethical concept in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*, encompassing the professional obligations of investigators, the civic responsibilities of witnesses, and the moral imperatives that drive characters to act — or to conceal.

## The Coroner's Instruction

At the initial inquest, Coroner Standish defines the jury's threefold duty: "first, to state the identity of the deceased if they were reasonably convinced by the evidence on this point; second, to find the cause of death in each case, and third, to state whether, in their opinion, blame attached to any person or persons." [306-306] He further cautions against casting undeserved blame on Roper's memory, noting that the suggestion of Roper's drunkenness causing the fire lacked direct evidence.

## Duty and Disclosure

Constable Sheldrake's decision to come forward illustrates the tension between personal embarrassment and civic duty. After initially concealing his sighting of Whymper near Starvel on the night of the fire to avoid "being chaffed about the girl," Sheldrake ultimately reports: "now he believed it to be his duty to come forward with his report." [898-898] This testimony becomes a pivotal moment in French's investigation.

## Whymper's Conflicted Duty

Pierce Whymper faces a moral dilemma after Averill's death. Though his employer is dead, Whymper determines: "I saw that my duty would be to go just as if Mr. Averill was still alive." [1026-1026] He proceeds to France with Averill's £500, though unable to complete the mission. His retention of the remaining £480 complicates the question of whether duty or self-interest ultimately prevailed.

## French's Investigative Duty

Inspector French frames his own actions in terms of professional obligation. When weighing the Philpot case, he concludes: "from two points of view it was his duty to carry on" — both to pursue justice for a possible murder and because it might illuminate the Starvel tragedy. [1157-1157] This dual imperative exemplifies French's methodical approach to duty.

At a moment of investigative impasse, French reflects that "for once duty and inclination coincided" [1419-1419] when he decides to combine his investigative contemplation with a long tramp through the moorland — a rare moment where obligation and personal desire align.
