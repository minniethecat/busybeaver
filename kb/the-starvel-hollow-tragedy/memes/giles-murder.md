## Summary

The murder of Markham [[markham-giles|Giles]] was a calculated killing carried out by [[john-roper|John Roper]] to obtain a substitute body for his own supposed death in the [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] fire. [[markham-giles|Giles]], a semi-invalid former soldier and amateur entomologist, lived in a cottage on the Starvel estate, half a mile from the main house. His physical description — a man of middle height and middle age — closely matched [[john-roper|Roper]], making him an ideal victim for the substitution. [[markham-giles|Giles]] was smothered or otherwise killed on the night of 13–14 September 1926, and his body was placed in the Starvel house before [[john-roper|Roper]] set the fire. The crime was discovered by Inspector [[joseph-french|French]] during his investigation of the [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] tragedy [1953-PN].

## Detailed Account

### Background

Markham [[markham-giles|Giles]] was the son of an old friend of [[simon-averill|Simon Averill]]. Physically rejected for early army service in the Great War, he was later accepted when recruitment standards were lowered. He served in Flanders, was badly gassed and wounded, and left hospital "the shadow of his former self." Penniless save for his pension, he came north to his father's old friend, and Averill offered him a vacant cottage on the Starvel estate at a nominal rent [106-PN].

[[markham-giles|Giles]] occupied himself keeping bees and studying the insect life of the moor. He became something of an authority on entomology, writing articles that attracted attention in scientific circles. He and [[ruth-averill|Ruth Averill]] became friends, and she helped him capture and arrange specimens [106-PN].

By September 1926, [[markham-giles|Giles]] was in declining health. [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] visited him on the Thursday before the fire and found him "looking worse than when she had last seen him. He lay with half-closed eyes and seemed too dull and listless to more than notice his visitors" [110-PN].

### The Murder

[[joseph-french|French]]'s reconstruction established that [[john-roper|Roper]] — or more probably his wife Flora, acting on his instructions — visited [[markham-giles|Giles]] on the night of Tuesday, 13 September. They told neighbours that Mrs. [[john-roper|Roper]] had called on [[markham-giles|Giles]] about eight o'clock that evening and found him "weak, but fairly easy and showing no sign of any early collapse." The following morning, Wednesday 14 September, she allegedly returned to find him dead [2615-PN].

In reality, [[john-roper|Roper]] murdered [[markham-giles|Giles]] — most likely by suffocation, which would leave no external marks — on the Tuesday night or early Wednesday. His body was then concealed until the night of the fire, when it was carried into Starvel and placed in the southern wing, where the body identified as "John [[john-roper|Roper]]" was later found among the ruins [1953-PN].

Dr. Emerson, who had been treating [[markham-giles|Giles]], was expecting the man to die from his heart condition (myocarditis). When [[markham-giles|Giles]] did die, Emerson "assumed that the disease was the cause" and issued a death certificate without an autopsy. [[joseph-french|French]] noted that Emerson had "jumped to conclusions and had signed the death certificate without having really taken any trouble to ascertain the cause of death" [2069-PN] [2077-PN].

### Roper's Cover-up

[[john-roper|Roper]] handled all the funeral arrangements himself — reporting the death to Dr. Emerson, purchasing a grave at the Town Hall, visiting the new cemetery to select the site, and arranging details with the undertaker [2615-PN]. By managing every aspect personally, he ensured that no questions would be asked about the body inside the coffin.

## Significance

The Giles murder was the linchpin of [[john-roper|Roper]]'s entire scheme. Without a substitute body matching his own description, the fire would have yielded only two sets of remains (Averill and Mrs. [[john-roper|Roper]]), immediately signalling that [[john-roper|Roper]] had survived. The Giles murder allowed [[john-roper|Roper]] to:

1. **Fake his own death** — Dr. Emerson testified at [[the-inquest|the inquest]] that the third body was "that of a man of middle height and middle age," which "would correspond" to [[john-roper|Roper]] — but equally matched [[markham-giles|Giles]] [284-PN] [1953-PN].

2. **Escape with the money** — With everyone believing him dead, [[john-roper|Roper]] could vanish with Averill's stolen banknotes and begin a new life under a new identity.

3. **Exploit medical complacency** — [[john-roper|Roper]] knew that Dr. Emerson expected [[markham-giles|Giles]] to die, and counted on the doctor issuing a death certificate without thorough examination — which is exactly what happened [2069-PN].

[[joseph-french|French]]'s discovery of the [[markham-giles|Giles]] connection was fortuitous: while questioning [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] Averill about Starvel neighbours, she happened to mention [[markham-giles|Giles]], and [[joseph-french|French]] "almost leaped off his seat" as he realised the significance [1935-PN]. The empty coffin — when exhumed — became the definitive [[evidence|proof]] that [[john-roper|Roper]] had faked his death [2149-PN].

## Investigation Notes

[[joseph-french|French]]'s breakthrough came from an apparently casual conversation with [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] Averill. When she mentioned [[markham-giles|Giles]]' death and funeral, [[joseph-french|French]] immediately connected the dates: [[markham-giles|Giles]] died just before the fire, his body was of similar build to [[john-roper|Roper]], and [[john-roper|Roper]] himself had managed all the funeral arrangements [1935-PN] [1953-PN].

The conclusive [[evidence|proof]] came from the exhumation of [[markham-giles|Giles]]' coffin on the night of 13 November 1926. When the lid was raised, the coffin contained "no sign of Markham [[markham-giles|Giles]]' body or any other! Instead, the coffin was half-full of dark, peaty earth" [2149-PN]. This confirmed beyond doubt that [[markham-giles|Giles]]' body had been stolen and used for the Starvel fraud.

[[joseph-french|French]] also investigated the possibility that [[john-roper|Roper]] had robbed a grave rather than committed murder. He examined both the old churchyard at Thirsby (surrounded by houses, gates locked at night, "an absolute impossibility" for grave robbing) and a village cemetery three miles northeast of Starvel — both with negative results [1901-PN] [1905-PN]. This eliminated the grave-robbing hypothesis and confirmed murder.
