## Summary

The inquest into the deaths of Simon Averill, John [[john-roper|Roper]], and Flora [[john-roper|Roper]] was held at the Thirsby courthouse on the afternoon of 17 September, two days after the discovery of the Starvel fire. Presided over by [[dr-lonsdale|the coroner]], the inquest heard testimony from the baker Peter Spence, Sergeant Kent, [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] Averill, the farmer George Mellowes, and medical witnesses. The coroner directed the jury to return a verdict of accidental death, explicitly cautioning against casting "a slur on the memory of Mr. [[john-roper|Roper]]" despite [[evidence]] that [[john-roper|Roper]] had been drinking on the evening of the fire [002-014]. This verdict, though reasonable on the [[evidence]] then available, would later be exposed as fundamentally mistaken — a dramatic irony that structures the novel's subsequent investigation.

## Detailed Account

The inquest was convened at three o'clock in the afternoon at the Thirsby courthouse. [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] Averill, staying with the Oxleys after her return from York, was "naturally nervous at the prospect of giving [[evidence]], which she had always heard was a terrible ordeal" [002-003]. Mrs. Oxley reassured her, explaining that she need only answer the questions put to her, while Mr. Oxley promised to ensure nothing untoward occurred [002-003].

The testimony proceeded through several witnesses:

**Peter Spence (the baker)**: Described his discovery of the fire during his regular Thursday delivery to Starvel. His testimony established the timeline: the fire had burned through the night and was still smouldering when he arrived at approximately eleven o'clock on Thursday morning [002-005].

**Sergeant Kent**: Corroborated the preceding testimony and added that an expert from Hellifield had opened the safe, finding £1,952 in sovereigns and "a mass of burnt papers" [002-007]. The presence of the sovereigns — gold coins that would survive a fire — was noted; the burnt papers were not, at this stage, regarded as suspicious.

**[[ruth-averill|Ruth]] Averill**: Testified about the household members, confirming the identities of the three deceased, and establishing her own whereabouts in York at the time of the fire. The coroner treated her with kindness, promising "I shall not keep you longer than I can help" [002-010]. Her [[evidence]] was read back to her, she signed the deposition, and was permitted to return to her seat with the Oxleys [002-012].

**George Mellowes (farmer)**: Testified that he had driven John [[john-roper|Roper]] home from Thirsby on the evening before the fire — the last confirmed sighting of any of the victims alive. His testimony included the significant detail that [[john-roper|Roper]] had been drinking, an observation that raised the possibility of accidental fire causation [002-013].

After Mellowes's testimony, [[dr-lonsdale|the coroner]] delivered his summation to the jury. He acknowledged the tragedy's magnitude and expressed sympathy for Miss Averill. He then addressed the question of [[john-roper|Roper]]'s drinking directly, making a statement that would prove profoundly ironic:

> "It had been shown that the manservant, John Roper, had been to some extent under the influence of drink on the evening in question. The suggestion, of course, was that some careless act of Roper's might have caused the fire. Now, while he approved the action of the police in bringing out this matter — they could not have done anything else — he must point out to the jury that there was no evidence that Mr. Roper's condition had had anything to do with the fire. If anything, the evidence tended in the opposite direction." [002-014]

The coroner further argued that the position of the remains suggested the victims had been "burnt in their beds," implying they had been "suffocated by the smoke while asleep" — a scenario that would rule out "any accident with lamps, or by falling in the fire or by igniting petrol or paraffin oil" [002-014]. The jury returned a verdict of death by misadventure: that the three deceased had "lost their lives in a fire at Starvel on the night of the fifteenth of September, the cause of which there was no [[evidence]] to show" [002-014].

## Significance

The inquest is a pivotal narrative event that serves multiple functions:

**As exposition**: The testimony systematically establishes the known facts of the case — timeline, identities, locations — providing both the reader and future investigators with a baseline of established knowledge.

**As misdirection**: The coroner's careful exoneration of [[john-roper|Roper]], though professionally proper on the available [[evidence]], creates the conditions for the novel's central twist. A reader who accepts [[dr-lonsdale|the coroner]]'s conclusion will be as surprised as the Thirsby community when [[joseph-french|French]]'s investigation reveals [[john-roper|Roper]]'s guilt.

**As legal framework**: The inquest represents the official, institutional response to death in 1920s England. Its procedures, its limitations (it cannot assign criminal guilt, only establish facts), and its susceptibility to manipulation ([[john-roper|Roper]]'s drinking is framed as a possible accident cause rather than [[evidence]] of premeditated incapacitation) are all accurate to the period.

**As character moment**: [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s nervousness, Mrs. Oxley's reassurance, Mr. Oxley's protective presence — these details deepen the character relationships established in the opening chapters and ground the legal proceedings in emotional reality.

## Participants

- **[[ruth-averill]]**: Witness. Her testimony establishes the identities of the deceased and confirms her own [[alibi]] in York. The coroner treats her with notable gentleness [002-010].
- **Peter Spence (baker)**: Discoverer of the fire. His Thursday delivery route makes him a routine witness whose testimony establishes the basic timeline [002-005].
- **Sergeant Kent**: Investigating officer from Thirsby police. Presents the initial findings including the safe contents [002-007].
- **George Mellowes (farmer)**: Last person to see [[john-roper|Roper]] alive. His testimony about [[john-roper|Roper]]'s drinking is the only [[evidence]] that even hints at a possible cause for the fire — and [[dr-lonsdale|the coroner]] explicitly dismisses it [002-013].
- **Dr. Emerson**: Medical witness, presumably testifying about the condition of the recovered bodies (though his specific testimony is not detailed in the narrative).
- **The Coroner**: Presiding officer who directs the jury toward a verdict of accidental death. His careful, compassionate handling of the proceedings ironically enables a miscarriage of justice [002-014].

## Investigation Notes

[[joseph-french|French]], reviewing the inquest depositions after his arrival from [[london|London]], notes several points that [[dr-lonsdale|the coroner]]'s court had no reason to question:

- The "mass of burnt papers" in the safe was accepted as consistent with Averill's known habit of keeping documents and bank notes. No one at the inquest examined the ash closely enough to discover that the notes from 13–15 September had been burned — and that this burning contradicted the safe's fire[[evidence|proof]] rating [005-003].
- [[john-roper|Roper]]'s drinking was treated as a possible accident cause or as irrelevant; no one considered that it might have been a deliberate stratagem — [[john-roper|Roper]] ensuring he would not wake during the fire.
- [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s departure for York was accepted as a fortunate coincidence. No one connected Averill's uncharacteristic generosity (funding the trip) with the fire that followed almost immediately.

The inquest thus represents both the necessary first step in the official response to the tragedy and the first of several official conclusions that [[joseph-french|French]]'s investigation will overturn.
