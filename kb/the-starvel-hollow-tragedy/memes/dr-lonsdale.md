## Profile

Dr. Lonsdale serves as the coroner presiding over [[the-inquest|the inquest]] into the [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] fire deaths. He is described as "a stout thickset man with a square face and a walrus moustache" [202-202]. His manner throughout [[the-inquest|the inquest]] is methodical and deliberate: he opens his bag, arranges his papers, and writes steadily as each witness gives [[evidence]] [204-204]. He is portrayed as a careful, fair-minded official who takes his responsibilities seriously.

## Role in the Narrative

Dr. Lonsdale's sole appearance in the novel is at [[the-inquest|the inquest]], but his role there is pivotal. He conducts the examination of each witness with methodical precision, "after each question a pause ensued while the coroner wrote a précis of the man's reply" [204-204]. His careful documentation creates the official record of the tragedy.

The coroner demonstrates independent judgment and fairness throughout the proceedings. Most notably, he expresses skepticism about several lines of questioning. When asked about whether the fire might have been visible, he remarks: "It seems to me an extraordinary thing that a fire of such magnitude could take place without being seen" [206-206]. He also pushes back against speculative theories about how the fire started.

His most significant contribution comes in his summation to the jury. He explicitly warns against casting blame on [[john-roper|John Roper]], despite [[evidence]] that [[john-roper|Roper]] had been drinking on the night of the fire: "he should be sorry that a slur should be cast on the memory of Mr. [[john-roper|Roper]], to whose zeal and efficiency different witnesses had testified, unless that slur were really deserved" [206-206]. He guides the jury toward the finding that "Simon Ralph Averill, John [[john-roper|Roper]] and Flora [[john-roper|Roper]] had lost their lives in a fire at Starvel on the night of the fifteenth of September, the cause of which there was no [[evidence]] to show" [206-206] — an open verdict that leaves room for the criminal investigation to follow.

## Relationships

- **[[sergeant-kent|Sergeant Kent]]**: Kent serves as the coroner's officer during [[the-inquest|the inquest]], managing procedural matters.
- **[[ruth-averill|Ruth Averill]]**: Dr. Lonsdale examines [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] as a witness and conveys "respectful sympathy in her loss" on behalf of the court [206-206].
- **[[john-roper|John Roper]]**: Though [[john-roper|Roper]] is deceased, Dr. Lonsdale's jury direction regarding [[john-roper|Roper]]'s character is a crucial narrative moment.
