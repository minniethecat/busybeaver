# Receipt

The **receipt** functions as a crucial form of documentary evidence in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*, appearing in financial transactions, French's investigative methodology, and the tracing of suspects through paper trails.

## Receipts in the Averill Financial System

Averill maintained a meticulous system of receipts for the cash transferred to Starvel. His solicitor Oxley explained that when £320 in twenty-pound notes was sent to Starvel, "Bloxham saw Averill lock these notes away in his safe and brought me the old man's receipt." [003-039] This receipt served as the only independent verification that the cash had reached its destination — a fact that became critically important after the fire destroyed the safe's contents.

Averill kept a sum of £500 "on deposit receipt" in addition to his current account. [003-035] Oxley noted this was unusual: "I don't know exactly why he did so, but I presume it was as a reserve against emergencies." [003-035] The deposit receipt represented a separate financial instrument from the cash hoard kept in the Starvel safe.

## Receipt Practices at Thomas Cook & Son

French's investigation at Thomas Cook & Son reveals the limitations of receipts as investigative tools. When French asks, "Do you give receipts for all monies received?", the manager replies: "For most transactions. But not all. If a man came in for a ticket to Harrogate, for example, we should hand him the ticket, and the ticket would be his receipt." [005-024] [005-025]

The manager explains the structural gap in receipt records: "The balance of the payments in notes would not, in the nature of things, require receipts, and conversely most of the accounts requiring receipts are paid by cheque." [005-028] This asymmetry between cash and cheque transactions limits French's ability to trace the twenty-pound notes through the financial system.

## Receipts as an Investigative Trail

Despite their limitations, receipts become central to French's methodology. He presses the Cook's manager: "If I could look over your receipts for some time prior to yesterday week I might find a name and address which would suggest a line of inquiry." [005-027] The receipt for Whymper's travel arrangements proves pivotal: "on that date a receipt had been made out to him for the £16 8s. 4d." [005-039] This documentary trail connects Whymper to the Talloires travel that French uses to reconstruct Roper's movements.

## Receipt as Leverage

In his confrontation with Whymper, French weaponises the receipt: "You had better hand over the notes also, for which, of course, I'll give you a receipt. I shall also want a photograph of yourself and a sample of your handwriting." [006-060] Here the receipt serves a dual purpose — it legitimises the seizure of evidence while creating a paper trail that protects French against later accusations of impropriety.
