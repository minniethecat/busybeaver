## Description

Braeside is a small detached house situated about half a mile from the town of Kintilloch, Fifeshire, and "a few hundred yards from the gate of the Ransome Institute" [006-012]. The house is built on sloping ground, a detail of some importance: the front hall door is level with the road, while the basement kitchen has an independent entrance to the lower ground at the rear [006-013].

The house is modest in scale — suitable for a professional couple with no children and a single live-in servant — and its address, printed in "small embossed letters" on grey note paper, reads "Braeside, Kintilloch, Fife" [006-001]. The layout includes a study where Dr. Philpot worked, a hall at the foot of the stairs where Edna Philpot's body was found, and a back staircase connecting the basement kitchen to the main floor [006-013].

The house's name — "Braeside," suggesting "brae" (Scots for hillside or slope) — is geographically apt, given its position on sloping ground. Like many Scottish house names of the period, it combines topographic description with a modest domestic dignity.

## Significance in the Novel

Braeside is the scene of the novel's central hidden crime — the death of Edna Philpot — and its physical layout is integral to understanding how that death occurred and how it was misrepresented. The house's architectural features become elements of the plot:

**The staircase**: Edna Philpot's death was reported as an accidental fall down the stairs, with the body found "at the foot of the stairs" in the hall [006-013]. The physical layout — the stairs descending into the hall, the proximity of the telephone from which Philpot summoned help — is consistent with the official account, but also with the alternative explanation that Philpot murdered his wife and staged the scene.

**The basement entrance**: Flora Macfarlane's use of the basement entrance on the afternoon of the death is a critical circumstantial detail. She left through the basement, sheltered from rain under a tree, and returned earlier than expected through the same entrance — hearing Dr. Philpot speaking "in a loud and agitated voice" before discovering Mrs. Philpot's body [006-013]. Her early return, the result of weather rather than suspicion, made her an accidental witness who constrained Philpot's freedom of action.

**The study**: Philpot's study, where he claimed to have been working when his wife fell, is the symbolic centre of the doctor's constructed [[alibi]]. It is from here that he "rushed out" upon hearing his wife's cry [006-013] — a sequence of movements that [[joseph-french|French]], with his methodical approach to physical [[evidence]], would scrutinise for consistency.

The house thus functions as both domestic setting and crime scene, its ordinary features — stairs, basement, study — transformed by the events of 15 May 1921 into the architecture of a possible murder.

## Real-World Context

Braeside's name and description place it within the tradition of Scottish suburban villa architecture of the early twentieth century. The detached house on the edge of a small town, occupied by a professional family with a single servant, was a common domestic arrangement for the period — reflecting the social expectations and economic realities of the professional middle class.

The name "Braeside" is authentically Scottish. "Brae" is the Scots word for a hillside or slope, and house names incorporating topographic features were common throughout Scotland in the period. The name is one of several details — along with "Kintilloch," the *Weekly Argus*, and the train connection via Cupar — that give Crofts's fictional Fifeshire its texture of authenticity.

The house's role as the scene of a domestic crime investigated years later places it within a long tradition of detective fiction in which ordinary suburban or rural homes become sites of concealed violence — a convention that Crofts, with his preference for the mundane over the melodramatic, handles with characteristic restraint.
