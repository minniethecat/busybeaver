## Definition

In *The [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] Tragedy*, robbery is the primary criminal motive underlying the Starvel fire. The theft of [[simon-averill|Simon Averill]]'s fortune — several thousand pounds in gold sovereigns and bank notes stored in his bedroom safe — is the objective that drives [[john-roper|John Roper]] to commit the series of crimes that culminate in murder and arson. Robbery in the novel is not a simple act of taking but a carefully planned operation involving deception, substitution, and the destruction of evidence.

The robbery is discovered not by the theft itself but by its traces: the absence of bank-note fragments in the supposedly fireproof safe. When [[joseph-french|Inspector French]] examines the safe and finds only newspaper ash where bank notes should have been, he immediately draws the inference: "What, he asked himself, could this portend? What other than robbery? And if robbery, then murder! Murder and arson!" [008-005]. The robbery is the logical hinge on which the entire criminal scheme turns.

## Role in the Novel

Robbery functions as the motive that explains all other crimes in the novel. The narrative traces a chain of criminal reasoning: [[john-roper|Roper]], knowing of [[simon-averill|Averill]]'s wealth through local gossip, takes the position of handyman at Starvel specifically to gain access to the safe. "The belief in Averill's wealth was universal and Roper could scarcely have failed to hear of it when inquiring about the place. The feebleness of the old man and the isolation of the house were, of course, patent. Was it too much to conclude that the idea of robbery had been in Roper's mind from the first?" [014-001].

The robbery plot involves multiple layers of criminal sophistication. [[john-roper|Roper]] must first ensure the stolen bank notes can be passed without detection — he tests this by giving [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] £500 in the notes and sending him abroad [014-002]. He must then dispose of the gold sovereigns, which cannot be burned, by some other method. And he must arrange for the fire to appear accidental so that the missing money is attributed to the blaze rather than to theft.

The novel explores the speculative nature of robbery as a working theory. [[tarkington|Tarkington]] first suggests it — "There have been several burglaries lately... Is it impossible that some of these burglars might have decided to make an attempt on Starvel?" [007-003] — and [[oxley|Oxley]] finds the idea "chimerical, fantastic, absurd, and yet — it was certainly possible" [007-003]. This tension between the plausibility of the theory and the absence of direct evidence characterises much of the investigation.

The stolen money itself becomes a key piece of [[evidence|evidence]]. [[joseph-french|French]]'s tracing of a single twenty-pound note through the banking system provides the documentary thread that leads to [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] and, ultimately, to the truth [009-003]. The robbery is thus both the motive for the crime and the mechanism of its detection.

## The Target: Averill's Hoard

The novel establishes [[simon-averill|Averill]]'s wealth through the independent testimony of multiple witnesses, each with partial knowledge. [[tarkington|Tarkington]], the bank manager, estimates that [[simon-averill|Averill]]'s annual income was "between three and four thousand a year" and that, living on "about £800 a year", he must have saved "something like thirty thousand pounds" [007-002]. The hotel landlord adds the local rumour: "the old man always kept a lot of money in the house" [008-002].

This convergence of estimates from different social positions — the professional banker, the gossipy publican — serves a dual narrative purpose. It establishes the magnitude of the target with documentary precision while also demonstrating how readily such information circulated in the small community of [[thirsby|Thirsby]]. The very visibility of [[simon-averill|Averill]]'s wealth is what made him vulnerable: "the belief in Averill's wealth was universal" [014-001].

The specific contents of the safe are disclosed only through the investigation's reconstruction. The safe contained approximately £8,000 in twenty-pound Bank of England notes and a substantial quantity of gold sovereigns — the notes being the key to the detection because each carried a traceable serial number [008-004].

## Mechanics of the Theft

The robbery, as [[joseph-french|French]] reconstructs it, unfolds in three stages:

**Stage 1 — Access**: [[john-roper|Roper]], as handyman, had physical access to [[simon-averill|Averill]]'s room and, after the murder, to the keys kept beneath [[simon-averill|Averill]]'s pillow. The safe, made by [[carter-and-stephenson|Carter & Stephenson]] of Leeds, was advertised as fireproof — a claim that proved pivotal because it established that any documents inside should have survived the blaze [008-002].

**Stage 2 — Substitution**: [[john-roper|Roper]] removed the bank notes and gold from the safe and replaced them with newspapers. When the fire destroyed the house, the newspapers burned to ash, creating the illusion that the safe's entire contents — including the notes — had been consumed by fire. Only the gold sovereigns, which do not burn, remained in the bottom of the safe as anomalous survivors [008-004].

**Stage 3 — Laundering**: The stolen notes had to be converted into usable currency without attracting attention. [[john-roper|Roper]]'s solution was to send [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] abroad with £500 in the notes, instructing him to pass them through a Cook's travel office in France. This tested whether the serial numbers would trigger any banking alert while simultaneously removing the most traceable notes from England [014-002].

## The Concealed Hoard

The novel's final twist on the robbery theme is the discovery that the stolen money was never recovered by [[john-roper|Roper]] at all. [[joseph-french|French]]'s search of Starvel Moor uncovers a buried cache containing what he believes to be the stolen notes and gold, hidden in a tin box wrapped in yellow clay — the "piece of yellow clay" from which Chapter 13 takes its name [017-005].

This discovery introduces an additional layer of criminal failure: [[john-roper|Roper]] committed the perfect robbery only to be unable to retrieve its proceeds. The money was buried, the location described in a code, and before [[john-roper|Roper]] could recover it, the investigation had closed in. The robbery that was designed to enrich its perpetrator ended by enriching no one — the notes were returned to the bank, the gold to the estate.

## Key Instances

- **The robbery hypothesis (Chapter 8)**: [[joseph-french|French]]'s examination of the safe yields the crucial discovery — no bank-note fragments, only newspaper ash. The inference of robbery triggers the full criminal investigation [008-005].

- **The stolen twenty-pound note (Chapter 9)**: A single note, traced from Tarkington's branch through Thomas Cook to [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] in France, provides the first concrete evidence of the theft [009-003].

- **Roper's plan (Chapter 14)**: The reconstruction of the theft — [[john-roper|Roper]] taking the keys from the dead [[simon-averill|Averill]]'s pillow, opening the safe, packing the notes in a suitcase, and substituting newspapers to be burned — reveals the calculated nature of the robbery [014-002].

- **The hidden money (Chapter 17)**: [[joseph-french|French]]'s discovery of a cache containing what he believes to be the stolen money buried on Starvel Moor provides the climactic recovery of the proceeds of the robbery [017-005].

- **The universal belief in Averill's wealth**: The novel establishes that "the belief in Averill's wealth was universal" — the very openness of Averill's financial position makes the robbery inevitable [014-001].
