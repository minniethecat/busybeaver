## Summary

The joint funeral of Simon Averill, John Roper, and Flora Roper was held in [[thirsby|Thirsby]] in September 1926, following the [[starvel-hollow-fire|Starvel Hollow fire]]. The funeral marked the public culmination of the tragedy, with [[ruth-averill|Ruth Averill]] attending alongside [[dr-emerson|Dr. Emerson]] and [[vicar-stackpool|the vicar]]. For [[ruth-averill|Ruth]], who had lost her uncle, her companion Flora, and a family servant in a single night, the funeral was the final, most harrowing episode after [[the-inquest|the inquest]] [318-PN].

## Detailed Account

### The Procession

On the second day after the fire, [[ruth-averill|Ruth Averill]] went out to see Starvel, driven by Mrs. Oxley. As they approached the point where the Starvel road branched off, they encountered the funeral procession. "As they mounted the rim of the Hollow they saw a funeral approaching along a converging road. It was a sorry procession; only the hearse, and the vicar and Dr. Emerson in the former's car" [168-PN].

[[vicar-stackpool|The vicar]] and [[dr-emerson|Dr. Emerson]] stopped to express their sympathy, and [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] and Mrs. Oxley joined the procession. The stark simplicity of the funeral — a single hearse carrying three coffins, followed by only two vehicles — underscored the isolation of Starvel Hollow and the completeness of the destruction.

### Ruth's Reaction

For [[ruth-averill|Ruth]], the funeral followed the inquest and marked the final stage of the public ceremonies surrounding the tragedy. "Now with the ending of the inquest, it seemed to her that the terrible affair was all but over. The final episode, the funerals, would not be anything like so harrowing" [318-PN]. Yet the funeral brought its own weight of grief — three lives extinguished, the household she had known utterly destroyed.

## Significance

The Averill funerals represent the public closure of the tragedy's first phase — before [[joseph-french|Inspector French]]'s investigation revealed that the fire was not an accident but a calculated triple murder. The funeral was conducted in good faith, with the community believing the deaths were the result of a tragic house fire.

The funeral also highlights the social dimension of the crime: the destruction of an entire household. Simon Averill was not merely a victim of theft; he was murdered in his own home, along with his companion Flora Roper and the servant John Roper (whose body was, unbeknownst to all, that of the murdered [[markham-giles|Markham Giles]]).
