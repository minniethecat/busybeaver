## Definition

A miser is a person who hoards wealth and spends as little money as possible, often to the point of self-deprivation and neglect of others. Miserliness is characterized by the compulsive accumulation and retention of money, typically in physical form (coins, banknotes), and an extreme reluctance to part with it for any purpose. In literature, the miser is a longstanding character type, from Molière's Harpagon to Dickens's Ebenezer Scrooge.

## Role in the Novel

Simon Averill is explicitly and repeatedly characterized as a miser. The narrator states this directly: "Simon Averill was a miser" (001-007). His miserliness is the defining trait of his character and a central plot element.

The manifestations of his miserliness include:
- Hoarding coins and banknotes in his safe rather than depositing them in a bank (001-010)
- Maintaining Starvel in a dilapidated state: threadbare furnishings, broken fencing, minimal staff
- Clothing his niece in out-of-fashion, mended garments despite his wealth (001-006)
- His general reclusiveness and lack of social expenditure

His unexpected gift of ten pounds to [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] for her York trip is therefore startlingly out of character — "an absolute miracle" (001-026). This anomaly raises suspicion: was Averill acting freely, or was he manipulated? Was the gift a genuine gesture, or did someone else arrange it to ensure [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s absence?

The fate of Averill's hoard after the fire is a major investigative question. If the money burned with him, the fire is tragic but not criminal (except possibly as arson-murder). If the money was removed before the fire, theft becomes a [[motive]] for murder.

## Averill's Financial System

The precise mechanism of Averill's hoarding is revealed through his solicitor, Mr. Tarkington. Averill's entire income passed through his bank account in the form of cheques and drafts. Tarkington's instructions were to maintain the current account at between £40 and £60 — "whatever would leave an even £20 over" — and to "send the surplus cash in £20 notes out to Starvel" (010-006). Any monies remaining after raising the current account to £60 were considered capital and "went out to swell the hoard in the safe at Starvel" (010-006).

In addition, Averill maintained a separate deposit of £500 — which Tarkington believed functioned "as a sort of nestegg in the event of his safe being burgled" (010-006). Averill insisted that his money be in £20 notes, which, as Tarkington notes, "made it easier to hoard and count" (010-007).

Averill's miserly system was well-known in the district. As Tarkington later testifies, "Averill was universally believed to be wealthy — the legend of the safe was common property" (003-012). This universal belief becomes a crucial point in the investigation: it provides the [[motive]] for anyone who knew of the hoard.

## The Hoard and the Crime

When Sergeant Kent opened the fire-damaged safe, he found "£1,952 in sovereigns and a mass of burnt papers" (002-007). But Tarkington's records showed that Averill had recently received £320 in twenty-pound notes whose serial numbers had been recorded. The discovery that the notes had been destroyed in the fire — and that the safe was fireproof — transforms the case from accident to murder.

The financial dimension of the crime is staggering. Tarkington estimates that "Averill must have been worth between thirty and forty thousand pounds when he died" (003-003), and "practically the whole sum was in Mr. Averill's safe" (003-003). Only "some five hundred pounds" remained in the bank; "the rest was in Mr. Averill's safe in notes and gold" (003-004). The fire destroyed not just lives, but the entirety of the Averill fortune — leaving [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] penniless.

The theory that the crime was a robbery gone wrong is explicitly raised: "Averill was universally believed to be wealthy — the legend of the safe was common property. Is it impossible that some of these burglars might have decided to make an attempt on Starvel?" (003-012). The isolation of the house, combined with the universal belief in the miser's hoard, made it a compelling target.

## The Fireproof Safe — Evidence of Theft

The critical turning point in the investigation comes when Tarkington examines the safe at Starvel and makes a startling observation: "Oxley, that safe was fireproof!" (003-016). The manufacturers, Carter & Stephenson of Leeds, confirm that the safe was "twenty years old, but it was the best fireproof safe of its day" and that "the notes wouldn't scarcely be browned, not no matter how fierce the fire might be" (004-009). This finding — that the burned papers in the safe could only have been deliberately placed there — is the first piece of hard evidence that the fire was not an accident but a crime designed to conceal theft.

## The Miser and the Narrative

Averill's miserliness functions as more than a character trait — it is the condition that makes the entire Starvel crime possible. His insistence on keeping his fortune in physical form inside a house safe, rather than in a bank, creates the concentrated target that attracts Roper's criminal ambition. His refusal to maintain adequate household staff leaves the house vulnerable. His suspicious, reclusive temperament prevents the kind of social scrutiny that might have deterred the conspirators.

In this sense, the miser is not merely a victim of crime but, paradoxically, its necessary precondition. The novel suggests a subtle moral economy: Averill's pathological hoarding, his neglect of his niece's welfare, and his withdrawal from the social world create the very conditions under which his destruction becomes possible. The crime exploits not just the physical hoard of cash but the social deficit created by a lifetime of miserly withdrawal.

This reading is reinforced by the contrast with the novel's other financial figures. Tarkington, the methodical solicitor who keeps careful records, represents the institutional banking system that Averill rejected. The £20 notes pass through Cook's, the Northern Shires Bank, and Tarkington's ledgers — leaving a documentary trail that ultimately exposes the crime. Averill's cash, hoarded in isolation, leaves no trail and no defense.

## Historical Context

In 1920s England, misers who hoarded physical currency rather than using banks were increasingly anachronistic but still recognized as a social type. The post-World War I economic adjustments and the gradual spread of banking made physical hoarding seem old-fashioned, reinforcing Averill's characterization as a man living in the past. In detective fiction, the miser-victim is a common figure: their wealth creates [[motive]], and their secretive habits around money create ambiguity about what was actually taken. Crofts's innovation is to use the mechanics of the miser's financial system — the solicitor's records, the recorded serial numbers, the fireproof safe — as the very evidence that exposes the crime.
