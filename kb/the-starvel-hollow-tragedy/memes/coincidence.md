# Coincidence

**Coincidence** plays a structural role in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*, where events that initially appear to be chance occurrences gradually reveal themselves as elements of a calculated criminal design. Inspector French's investigation centers on distinguishing genuine coincidence from orchestrated circumstance.

## Ruth's Timely Absence

French notes as a "curious coincidence that she should have left the house on the day before the tragedy." [1449-1449] Ruth Averill's visit to York on the day of the fire initially appears fortuitous, but French re-examines this fact: "Had her absence been arranged; arranged by Roper?" This reconsideration transforms an apparent coincidence into a potential element of the murderer's plan.

## The Four Deaths

The concentration of deaths surrounding the Averill case strikes characters as improbable. French observes to Dr. Emerson: "What a curious coincidence it was that this man should pass away at the very time of the fire! That all four of Miss Averill's closest acquaintances should die at practically the same time is, you must admit, as strange as it is tragic." [1971-1971]

Dr. Emerson responds with professional skepticism: "Strange enough and tragic enough, I admit, but such coincidences are not infrequent. It is my experience that coincidences which would be deemed too remarkable for a novel constantly occur in real life." [1975-1975] This exchange crystallizes the novel's central epistemological tension between suspicion and statistical probability.

## Orchestrated Encounters

Coincidence also operates at the level of investigative method. Tarkington's clerk, Bloxham, repeatedly arranges to "coincidentally" encounter French: "Coincidence ordained that French should meet at the station the one person whose curiosity as to the progress of the investigation was even keener than Oxley's — Tarkington's clerk, Bloxham." [2123-2123] French recognizes these "frequent 'unexpected' meetings" as "carefully prearranged," demonstrating how apparent coincidence can mask deliberate intent.
