## Overview

Simon Averill was the elderly owner of Starvel Hollow and uncle of Ruth Averill. A notorious miser, he lived in near-isolation in the remote Hollow, hoarding wealth while his niece depended entirely on his bounty. His death in the Starvel Hollow fire of September 1926 was later revealed to be murder at the hands of John Roper, who killed him to gain access to his money and securities.

## Life and Character

Simon Averill was the brother of Theodore Averill, who had enjoyed a promising career as the French representative of a London firm. Theodore married a French woman and had a daughter, Ruth. After both Theodore and his wife died, the four-year-old Ruth came to live with Simon at Starvel. Simon sent her to school in Leeds and provided for her financially, but his miserly nature meant every penny was grudgingly given.

Mr. Oxley of Oxley & Co., the local solicitors in Thirsby, managed all of Simon Averill's business affairs. The bank manager in Thirsby was also familiar with Simon's finances and "willingly repeated all he knew about old Simon Averill and his finances" to Inspector French during the investigation.

## The Murder

French's investigation revealed that John Roper was "the evil genius behind all these involved happenings." Roper murdered Simon Averill as part of his scheme to steal Averill's money and securities. He also "got Ruth away from the doomed house" on the night of the fire, sent Whymper to pass stolen banknotes to test if their numbers were known, and stole Averill's valuables before setting the fire.

## Ruth's Orphanhood

Ruth Averill was "an orphan, dependent on the bounty of her uncle, Simon Averill, for every penny." Her thoughts after the fire were "all for old Simon Averill" — she worried whether the poor old man was badly injured and suffering. Mrs. Oxley, wife of the solicitor, invited Ruth to stay the night after the tragedy.

## Significance

Simon Averill's miserly character and hoarded wealth provided the motive for the crime. His isolation at Starvel Hollow made him a vulnerable target, and his murder was the central event of a conspiracy that claimed three lives and almost went undetected as an accident.
