# Greed

**Greed** is a primary criminal motive in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*, driving both the Starvel Hollow arson-murder and the systematic financial exploitation that precedes it.

## Simon Averill's Miserliness

The novel opens with the revelation of Simon Averill's pathological relationship with money. Mr. Oxley explains: "The poor fellow was a miser, a miser of the most primitive kind. He loved money for itself — just to handle and to look at and to count. His safe was just packed full of money." [376-376]

Mr. Tarkington confirms Averill's habits: "I am acquainted with Averill's habits; he made no secret of them. Monies he paid out he paid by cheque on the current account — everything that one can think of went through it, even the Ropers' salaries. The cash sent out to Starvel went into the hoard." [402-402]

This hoard — thirty to forty thousand pounds kept in notes and gold within the Starvel safe — becomes the irresistible lure that sets the criminal plot in motion.

## Roper's Financial Avarice

John Roper's greed drives the entire conspiracy. He systematically extorts money from Philpot: first ten shillings a week for silence about the nurse, escalating to "two pounds a week, he demanded, until further notice." [1369-1369] Even after Philpot destroys what he believes to be the only copy of his confession, Roper has "made a copy of his forgery and kept one." [1383-1383]

Roper's greed culminates in the Starvel crime: "The whole of Averill's wealth might be his." [3502-3502] French deduces that Roper "must have packed the notes in a suitcase" from the safe but "it must have gone to Roper's heart to leave the gold." [1615-1615]

## Fear and Greed Combined

The novel's climactic revelation explicitly links the two motives: "fear and greed had by this time rendered Philpot almost inhuman and he cared for nothing but his own welfare." [3502-3502] The combination of terror at exposure and hunger for wealth transforms both men into murderers.

## The Destruction Wrought by Greed

The ultimate cost of greed is enumerated: three murders — Averill, Giles, and Mrs. Roper — all sacrificed so Roper "might with the greater security steal his employer's money." [2153-2153] French traces the stolen notes through Roper's ring purchases, the criminal "getting rid of his tainted money." [3420-3420]
