## Description

Starvel is an old, dilapidated house situated at the bottom of Starvel Hollow, a saucer-shaped depression in the moorland of Yorkshire. The house is approached across open moorland described as "wild and lonely and forbidding," with dun-coloured sedgy grass and darker patches where rock outcrops [001-003]. The name itself — "Starvel" — connotes starvation and deprivation, thematically appropriate for the home of a [[miser]] who hoarded wealth while his household lived in near-poverty.

The grounds feature Scotch firs, a grass-grown drive, and a broken-down paling of posts and wire [001-003]. The house itself is a long, slate-roofed building of two storeys, "L" shaped, with a single-storey porch in the angle of the two wings [004-018]. It is surrounded by thin, stunted pines that lean "one way from the prevailing wind, melancholy and depressing" [004-017].

The Hollow's geography is central to the plot. The house is hidden from the surrounding country by the rim of the natural depression, making it invisible from the road. This isolation is why the baker only notices the fire when he sees smoke rising above the hollow's rim — the house itself cannot be seen until one crosses the shoulder of the depression [ch01]. [[joseph-french|French]], on his first approach, is struck by "the isolation of the ruins and the morbid, not to say sinister atmosphere which seemed to brood over the entire place" [004-017].

After the fire, only "blackened windowless walls surrounding a still smouldering interior" remain. The walls, built of stone, survive the blaze, but everything else — floors, roof, staircase, window sashes — is consumed [004-018]. The safe, manufactured by Tarkington's firm, is one of the few objects that survives intact, though its contents are reduced to ash.

## Significance in the Novel

Starvel is simultaneously setting, symbol, and crime scene.

**As setting**: The house's physical isolation in the hollow creates the conditions for the crime. No one can see the house from the road; no neighbour is close enough to notice unusual activity; the fire burns for hours before discovery. This geography is not incidental but instrumental — the killer chose Starvel precisely because its isolation made detection unlikely.

**As symbol**: The house embodies Simon Averill's character with a precision that borders on allegory. Old, neglected, isolated, and concealing a hoard of wealth within its walls — Starvel is Averill made architecture. [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s hatred of the place mirrors her hatred of her dependent life there. The fire that destroys the house is, in a symbolic sense, the liberation she could not achieve through her own agency.

**As crime scene**: After the fire, Starvel becomes the focus of the investigation. The smouldering ruins, the positions of the three bodies, the intact but ash-filled safe — each detail is scrutinised by [[joseph-french|French]] for what it reveals about the fire's origin and purpose. The outhouse that survived the fire (being separate from the main building) provides the only intact structure for investigators to examine.

## Real-World Context

Starvel Hollow is a fictional location, but its description draws on the real geography of the Yorkshire moors. The "saucer-like depression" with its rim of moorland, the stunted wind-leaning pines, the isolation from neighbouring settlements — these are features of the Yorkshire landscape that Crofts, who travelled extensively in England, renders with observational precision.

The house itself — an old stone-built country house fallen into neglect — represents a familiar type in English fiction: the decaying manor that embodies the moral or financial decline of its inhabitants. In the Gothic tradition, such houses are haunted; in the detective tradition that Crofts helped establish, they are crime scenes. Starvel belongs to this lineage while serving the specific demands of the detective plot.

The name "Starvel" (suggesting "starve" + "dell/hollow") is an invention that functions as both place name and moral commentary. It is one of several names in the novel — "Thirsby," "Thirsdale Arms" — that evoke Yorkshire without corresponding to real locations.
