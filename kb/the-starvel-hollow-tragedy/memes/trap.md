# Trap

The **trap** is Inspector French's signature investigative stratagem in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*. He employs carefully baited psychological traps to draw out suspects, while the criminals live in perpetual fear of walking into one.

## French Baits His Trap

Chapter 15 is titled "French Baits His Trap," [015-001] signaling the centrality of this tactic to the novel's climactic sequence. French's method involves the deliberate release of misleading information at the inquest, designed to lure the criminal into revealing himself. He reflects on his success with satisfaction: "He had devised and skilfully baited a trap for his victim, and lo! the victim had walked into it with the most commendable promptness." [018-003]

French attributes the entire dénouement to this trap: "Was it not his, French's, foresight and ingenuity at the inquest?" [018-003] The trap represents the culmination of his investigative methodology — not the passive collection of evidence, but the active manipulation of the criminal's expectations.

## The Trap's Mechanism

French's trap exploits the criminal's financial anxiety. By publicly announcing that the stolen note numbers are known and traceable, he creates a situation where the criminal must either abandon his money or risk exposure by attempting to access it. French calculates: "Unless the man was extraordinarily well up in Brazilian politics, of which the chances were negligible, he would suspect nothing amiss. And if he did not suspect a trap he would almost certainly turn up." [019-017]

The psychology is precise: "Not only would he really be anxious about his money, but he would see that it would be suspicious not to show such anxiety." [019-017] French's trap works because it exploits a genuine need — the criminal cannot afford to ignore his trapped assets.

## The Criminal's Fear of Traps

The criminals, for their part, are acutely aware of the danger of traps. Philpot "did not fully believe" French's inquest announcement, "fearing a trap." [020-031] When French's letter arrives through the Edinburgh tobacconist, Roper "half-suspected a trap, and of course the plan became apparent when French's letter to himself arrived." [020-033]

This creates a double bind: "He saw, however, that he was either quite safe or irretrievably lost. If French had no inkling of the truth it was evident that he must keep the appointment and continue to play his game." [020-033] The trap forces the criminal into a binary choice — ignore it and lose everything, or walk into it and risk capture.

## The Trap Turned Against Its Maker

French also contemplates whether Roper might himself have been caught in his own trap. He puzzles over Roper's death: "Could so able a man as Roper have bungled so hideously as to have allowed himself and his wife to be caught in the trap he had arranged for Averill?" [009-017] This ironic reversal — the trapper trapped — adds a layer of moral complexity to the novel's resolution.
