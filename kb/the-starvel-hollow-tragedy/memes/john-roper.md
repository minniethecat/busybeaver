## Profile

John Roper is the male attendant at Starvel, employed along with his wife Flora as live-in servants for Simon Averill. The couple replaced the previous elderly women servants and gardener approximately fourteen months before the novel's opening [001-011]. Roper is described as having a "pleasant Scotch voice" and coming from "somewhere in Fife" [001-029].

On the surface, Roper appears competent and efficient. He performs his duties with what [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] perceives as "sleek civility" — a phrase that captures her ambivalent feelings toward him [001-033]. The landlord of the Thirsdale Arms describes him as "quiet enough and civil spoken" but "close — very close. Sly, the landlord would call it" [004-012]. These early impressions of Roper as a man who conceals more than he reveals prove prophetic: by the novel's conclusion, Roper emerges as the orchestrator of the entire Starvel tragedy.

## Role in the Narrative

Roper functions as the novel's hidden antagonist — a character whose true nature is revealed only gradually through the accumulation of [[evidence]]. His role shifts across the narrative in three distinct phases:

**Phase 1: The Functionary (Chapters 1–3).** Roper appears as a competent servant delivering messages between Simon Averill and [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]. He brings [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] the news of her uncle's permission to visit York and the ten-pound gift — a message that sets the plot in motion [001-016]. His practical competence is shown when he arranges [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s transport and suggests waiting for Dr. Philpot rather than the less competent Dr. Emerson. At this stage, he is simply part of the Starvel household machinery, noted but not suspected.

**Phase 2: The Victim (Chapters 4–6).** Roper's death in the fire, alongside his wife and employer, initially positions him as one of three tragic victims. The coroner explicitly warns against casting "a slur on the memory of Mr. Roper, to whose zeal and efficiency different witnesses had testified" [002-014]. This official exoneration creates dramatic irony for the reader who later learns the truth.

**Phase 3: The Perpetrator (Chapters 15–20).** The investigation gradually peels back Roper's facade. [[joseph-french|French]] discovers that Roper was a systematic blackmailer who had been extorting money from Dr. Philpot for years over the circumstances of Mrs. Philpot's death [008-007]. More significantly, Roper had manipulated Pierce [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] into passing the stolen twenty-pound note — a calculated move designed to test whether the serial numbers of Averill's notes were known to the authorities [019-003]. The revelation that Roper stole Averill's hoard of bank notes, replaced them with burnt newspaper, and set the fire to destroy the [[evidence]] of both the theft and Averill's murder transforms the seemingly minor character into the novel's central villain.

## Relationships

- **[[simon-averill]]**: Employer. Roper served Averill for fourteen months, during which time he presumably planned and executed the systematic theft of the old [[miser]]'s hoard.
- **[[ruth-averill]]**: Served as attendant. [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s instinctive dislike of Roper — her sense of his "sleek civility" — is retrospectively vindicated [001-033].
- **Flora Roper**: Wife and fellow servant. The extent of Flora's knowledge of or complicity in Roper's crimes is never fully established; she dies alongside him in the fire.
- **[[pierce-whymper]]**: Target of manipulation. Roper exploited [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]]'s romantic interest in [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] to trick the young architect into passing a stolen bank note, thereby testing whether the authorities could trace the serial numbers [019-003].
- **[[dr-philpot]]**: Blackmail victim. Roper systematically extorted money from Philpot over a period of years, using his knowledge of the suspicious circumstances surrounding Mrs. Philpot's death [008-007].

## Character Analysis

Roper is the most psychologically complex character in the novel, precisely because his complexity is concealed behind a facade of ordinariness. He is the servant who is more intelligent than his masters, the "quiet" man whose silences conceal calculation, the "civil spoken" employee whose courtesy masks contempt. The landlord's description — "close" and "sly" — captures the surface, but [[joseph-french|French]]'s investigation reveals depths of cunning that no one in Thirsby suspected.

Roper's criminal genius lies not in the violence of his acts — though the fire that kills three people is undeniably violent — but in his understanding of systems. He comprehends how bank notes are tracked by serial number, how coroners' inquests reach their conclusions based on available [[evidence]], how young men in love can be made into unwitting accomplices. His manipulation of [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] is particularly ingenious: by having someone else pass the stolen note, Roper created a test case that would reveal whether the authorities knew the note numbers, while simultaneously providing a plausible suspect should the test fail [019-003].

The character also embodies a dark inversion of the servant-master relationship. Simon Averill, the [[miser]] who hoarded wealth he never spent, is served by a man who systematically steals that wealth over fourteen months and ultimately destroys his employer to cover the theft. The power dynamic of the household — with Averill as the reclusive, tyrannical master and Roper as the obedient attendant — is revealed to be an illusion. Roper was never the subordinate; he was always the predator.
