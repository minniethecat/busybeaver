## Summary

The discovery of the Starvel safe's true contents was the turning point in Inspector [[joseph-french|French]]'s investigation. When [[joseph-french|French]] examined the safe at the burnt ruins of Starvel, he found that the supposed remains of [[simon-averill|Simon Averill]]'s fortune — £30,000–40,000 in bank notes — were not bank notes at all, but newspaper ash. The safe was fire[[evidence|proof]]; its contents should have survived any fire intact. This discovery proved that the safe had been opened before the fire, its contents removed and replaced with burning newspapers — transforming what appeared to be a tragic accident into a case of robbery, arson, and triple murder [638-PN] [640-PN] [692-694-PN].

## Detailed Account

### Background

At [[the-inquest|the inquest]] into the [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] fire, Sergeant Kent testified that an expert from Hellifield had opened the safe found in the ruins. Inside were "£1,952 in sovereigns and a mass of burnt papers" [222-PN]. The assumption — accepted by [[dr-lonsdale|the coroner]] and jury — was that the burnt papers were the remains of Averill's bank notes, destroyed in the fire.

But [[tarkington|Mr. Tarkington]], the Thirsby bank manager, was not satisfied. He knew the safe was fire[[evidence|proof]]: "that safe was fire[[evidence|proof]]!" he insisted to [[oxley|Mr. Oxley]] [468-PN]. His suspicion led to [[major-valentine|Chief Constable Valentine]] requesting assistance from Scotland Yard [516-PN].

### French's Examination

When [[joseph-french|French]] first heard the details, he was sceptical: "The only shred of [[evidence]] for foul play was the safe-builders' statement that their safe would protect papers even in the fiercest fire, and that statement left him cold" [574-PN].

Nevertheless, [[joseph-french|French]] visited the Starvel ruins to examine the safe personally. The safe had been "lifted upright and fixed on a roughly built pile of stones" by the town officer. [[joseph-french|French]] swung open the doors and found inside "a mass of paper ash" [632-PN].

Using forceps and a lens, [[joseph-french|French]] began turning over the fragments. He was "delighted to find that on nearly all he could distinguish marks of printing." But as he examined piece after piece, he became "conscious of growing astonishment." The printing was not that of bank notes but of newspaper type [638-PN].

His interest "keenly aroused," [[joseph-french|French]] checked every fragment he could find. The conclusion was inescapable: "There were no fragments of notes! Every single piece that bore any marking was newspaper!" [640-PN].

### Identifying the Newspapers

[[joseph-french|French]] mounted the best-preserved fragments on gummed cards. One fragment, "about the size of half a crown," bore the partial headline: "**Rat-Catcher's F——**" and the words "—ing as we—" [644-PN].

At the *Thirsby Mercury* office, [[joseph-french|French]] searched back issues and found his match: "Tucked in among a number of small news items in the paper of Tuesday, 14th September, he read: '**Rat-Catcher's Fatal Fall**'" [658-PN]. He went on to identify eight of the eleven fragments, proving the burnt papers came from the issues of 13, 14, and 15 September — the three days immediately before the fire [660-PN].

### Confirming the Safe Was Fireproof

[[joseph-french|French]] then visited Messrs. Carter & Stephenson in [[leeds|Leeds]], the makers of the safe. Mr. Stephenson was emphatic: "Not under any conceivable circumstances... that is, of course, unless the door had been left open. With the door shut it's absolutely impossible. And I'll be prepared to stand by that in any court of law" [676-PN].

[[joseph-french|French]] showed Stephenson the burnt fragments: "Suppose there were newspapers in that safe before the fire, could they have come out like that after it?" Stephenson confirmed they could not. The newspapers had been burned while the safe door was open — but the door was locked when Kent had it opened after the fire [678-PN].

### The Reconstruction

[[joseph-french|French]]'s logical deduction from the [[evidence]]:

1. The fire[[evidence|proof]] safe would have protected any paper contents from fire damage [676-PN].
2. The paper fragments inside were all newspaper, with no trace of bank notes [640-PN].
3. The newspapers were dated the three days immediately before the fire [660-PN].
4. The newspapers were burned before the safe door was closed [678-PN].

The only possible conclusion: "After the notes were taken out of the safe, newspapers were put in to replace them. The newspapers were set on fire and allowed to burn to ashes while the safe door was open" [692-694-PN].

This meant the fire was not an accident — it was arson, committed to cover up robbery and murder.

## Significance

The safe discovery was the pivotal event in the entire investigation. Before it, [[joseph-french|French]] believed the case was "the thinnest he had listened to for many a day" and expected to take "a return train to St. Pancras before many hours had passed" [574-PN]. After it, he knew he was investigating a calculated triple murder.

The discovery established the method and [[motive]] of the crime:
- **Method**: The murderer killed the three inhabitants, stole the bank notes, replaced them with newspapers, set the newspapers alight, locked the safe, and set fire to the house.
- **Motive**: Robbery — Averill kept his entire fortune (£30,000–40,000) in the safe in cash [328-PN].

[[joseph-french|French]] asked himself: "What, he asked, himself, could this portend? What other than robbery? And if robbery, then murder! Murder and arson!" [642-PN].
