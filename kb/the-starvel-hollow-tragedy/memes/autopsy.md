# Autopsy

An **autopsy** is a post-mortem medical examination that becomes a key investigative issue in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*. Inspector French repeatedly presses for autopsy procedures to transform clinical assumptions into forensic certainty, particularly in the case of Dr. Giles's death.

## French's Medical Inquiry

French challenges Dr. Emerson's certificate of death from myocarditis by questioning whether clinical diagnosis alone is sufficient. He states: "I understood, please correct me if I am wrong, but I understood that only an autopsy could really establish the point beyond question." [1991-1991] This insistence on pathological verification exemplifies French's investigative philosophy of refusing to accept surface explanations.

Dr. Emerson acknowledges the point but defends his clinical judgment: "You are perfectly correct on the other point. Only an autopsy can establish beyond question the fact of a death from myocarditis. If I had had the slightest doubt in Mr. Giles' case I should have required one before giving a certificate. But I had no doubt, and with all due respect to you I have none now." [2011-2011]

## The Investigative Imperative

French's pursuit of autopsy extends to the exhumation of remains. When arranging for the recovery of Philpot's body from the canal, French confirms: "We shall want an autopsy also." [2373-2373] The procedure is part of his systematic approach to building an evidentiary foundation that can withstand legal scrutiny.

The contrast between Dr. Emerson's clinical confidence and French's demand for pathological evidence reflects the novel's broader tension between professional judgment and the higher standard of proof required in criminal investigation.
