## Definition

In *The [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] Tragedy*, disguise refers to the deliberate alteration of one's physical appearance to conceal identity. The concept operates on two distinct levels: the literal use of physical disguises by criminals to evade detection, and the investigative method by which [[joseph-french|Inspector French]] operates under a carefully constructed cover identity.

Roper's disguise — a dark moustache and tinted glasses — is the novel's most prominent example, used to purchase wedding rings without revealing his true identity [017-073]. The jeweller's witness who served him described "a slight, dark man" with "a thick, dark moustache and dark glasses" [017-103]. [[joseph-french|French]]'s deductive breakthrough comes when he realises "the fact that the elusive purchaser had a moustache and glasses while Roper wore neither by no means cleared Roper of the purchase" [017-085]. This insight — that concealment is the point of disguise — transforms the investigation.

[[joseph-french|French]] himself employs a form of professional disguise, presenting himself at Thirsby not as a police officer but as an insurance investigator. "Following out his theory that where absolute truth is inadmissible, deviations therefrom should be as slight as possible" [008-002], he crafts a persona just different enough from reality to be maintained under scrutiny. This act of professional disguise allows him to gather intelligence from local informants without alerting the conspirators.

## Role in the Novel

Disguise serves as both a criminal technique and an investigative counter-move, creating the novel's cat-and-mouse dynamic. Roper uses physical disguise to commit the wedding-ring purchases — a critical piece of evidence that links him to the substitution of the tramp's body for the Starvel victims. Without this disguise, he could not have acquired rings in the names of the dead without being identified.

However, the disguise also provides the thread that unravels Roper's conspiracy. The jeweller's detailed description of the "dark-haired man with moustache and glasses" [017-084] becomes a key piece of [[circumstantial-evidence|circumstantial evidence]]. [[joseph-french|French]]'s method of showing witnesses a photograph of Roper with covered forehead and eyes — recreating what the jeweller would have seen — is a clever inversion of the disguise: where Roper used concealment to hide, [[joseph-french|French]] uses concealment to reveal [017-096].

## Key Instances

- **Roper's ring-buying disguise**: A dark moustache and tinted glasses allow Roper to purchase wedding rings without being identified, a crucial step in the body-substitution scheme [017-073].

- **The jeweller's uncertain identification**: Shown a photograph of Roper, the jeweller Stanley hesitates — "I couldn't say for sure. You see that photograph shows his hair and his forehead and his eyes. Well, I didn't see any of those things" [017-096].

- **French's deductive reasoning**: [[joseph-french|French]] realises that the very fact Roper wore no moustache or glasses normally makes him *more* likely, not less, to be the disguised purchaser — a man who needs to buy rings without being identified would naturally alter his appearance [017-085].

- **French's own professional disguise**: Posing as an insurance investigator, [[joseph-french|French]] uses a cover identity that parallels the criminal's method, demonstrating that detection and crime operate on the same principle of controlled appearance.
