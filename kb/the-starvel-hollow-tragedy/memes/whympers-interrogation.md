## Summary

The interrogation of [[pierce-whymper|Pierce Whymper]] by Inspector [[joseph-french|French]] in the vestry of Thirsby church was a pivotal moment in the [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] investigation. [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]], a young architect working on the church restoration, had been seen near Starvel on the night of the fire and was found in possession of one of the stolen £20 notes from Averill's safe. [[joseph-french|French]] questioned him twice — first obtaining evasive answers, then confronting him with evidence that made him the prime suspect [982-PN].

## Detailed Account

### The First Interview

[[joseph-french|French]] first questioned [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] at the church, where the young man's behaviour was immediately suspicious. "At this question Whymper seemed to crumple up. He stared at his questioner with an expression of something very like horror. When he answered it was almost in a whisper" [838-PN]. [[joseph-french|French]] pressed him on his possession of a £20 note that had been in Averill's safe five days before the fire [888-PN].

### The Vestry Confrontation

The second and more dramatic interview took place in the church vestry, which [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] used as an office. "Of all the sights which the groined roof of the old vestry had looked down on during the three centuries of its existence, none perhaps was so out of keeping with the character of the place as this interview between a detective of the C.I.D. and the man whom he half suspected of murder, arson, and burglary" [980-PN].

[[joseph-french|French]] warned [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] that "if your answers are unsatisfactory I must arrest you, and then anything you have said may be used in evidence against you" [982-PN]. [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] "paled slightly" but agreed to answer [984-PN].

### Whymper's Defence

When confronted with evidence, [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] admitted that he had been at Starvel on the night of the fire — but claimed he had gone there at [[john-roper|Roper]]'s suggestion to deliver a message and had fled when he saw the blaze. He also admitted taking the £20 note from the safe, but claimed he had done so at [[john-roper|Roper]]'s direction and only after the fire.

[[joseph-french|French]] was not convinced. "It was obvious in the first place that the evidence which he had obtained against Pierce Whymper would have been considered by most police officers sufficient to justify an arrest" [956-PN].

## Significance

The interrogation of [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] illustrates [[joseph-french|French]]'s investigative method: gathering circumstantial evidence, confronting the suspect, and carefully evaluating the response. Although [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] appeared guilty, [[joseph-french|French]] sensed that the case against him was too neat. His eventual conclusion that [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] was innocent — framed by [[john-roper|Roper]] as a decoy — demonstrated French's refusal to accept the obvious answer when the evidence did not fully cohere.

The interrogation also revealed key elements of [[john-roper|Roper]]'s plan: using [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]] as a diversion, manipulating the architect's connections to the Averill household, and creating a trail of false evidence pointing away from himself.
