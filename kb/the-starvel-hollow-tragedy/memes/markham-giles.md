## Profile

Markham Giles is a semi-invalid entomologist who lives alone in a small cottage half a mile from Starvel, across the moor [001-012]. He is the son of an old friend of Simon Averill, and Averill offered him the cottage — which belongs to the Starvel estate — at a nominal rent.

Giles is described as a "pathetic instance of the wreckage left by the War" [ch01]. Though never physically strong, he volunteered for military service after recruitment standards were lowered, served in Flanders, and was badly gassed and wounded. He left the hospitals six months later as "the shadow of his former self." Penniless apart from his pension, he came north to his father's old friend for shelter.

Despite his disabilities, Giles has built a meaningful life studying the insect life of the moor. He keeps bees and has become "somewhat of an authority" on entomology, writing articles that attract attention in scientific circles [ch01]. [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] Averill helps him capture and arrange specimens, and their shared interest in natural history forms the basis of their friendship.

## Role in the Narrative

Giles's death from influenza, occurring during [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s absence in York, compounds the tragedy of the Starvel fire. His death is the "second blow" [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] receives upon her return — she learns of it as she passes his funeral procession on the road [ch01]. Coming so quickly after the news of the fire, it leaves her "weak and trembling."

His death removes one of the few sympathetic characters from the novel's world and deepens [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s isolation. Where she had four people she knew well — her uncle, the [[john-roper|Roper]]s, and Giles — she now has none. The vicar's comment that Giles's death at thirty-six is "truly, another tragedy of the War" [ch01] links his fate to the broader historical trauma that forms the novel's backdrop.

Giles's cottage, lying just outside the lip of [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]], may also be geographically relevant: as the nearest dwelling to Starvel, it is a potential vantage point from which the fire or its precursor events might have been observed — though Giles's illness confined him to bed.
