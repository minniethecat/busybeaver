# Deduction

**Deduction** is the logical engine of [[joseph-french|Inspector French]]'s investigative method. Coupled with analysis, it forms the intellectual process by which French transforms scattered observations into a coherent theory of the crime — a theory that, in the [[starvel-hollow-fire|Starvel Hollow]] case, proves devastatingly accurate.

## Analysis and Deduction

The novel characterises French's method as a dual process of analysis (breaking down the evidence into its components) followed by deduction (drawing logical inferences from those components):

> The conclusion which French had reached by analysis and deduction had been tested and had proved true, and that proof established at one and the same time the whole of the steps of his line of reasoning. [2153-PN]

This passage marks the moment of vindication — French's deductive chain, built link by link from [[circumstantial-evidence]], is confirmed in its entirety when [[john-roper|Roper]]'s guilt is established [2153-PN].

## Deduction vs. Circumstantial Evidence

French's deductive method is inseparable from his reliance on [[cumulative-evidence|circumstantial evidence]]. Unlike detectives who seek a single decisive clue, French constructs a chain of deductions, each link supported by indirect but mutually reinforcing facts. The methodology is distinctly inductive in practice — building from particulars to general conclusions — though the novel uses "deduction" in its broader sense of logical reasoning from evidence to conclusion.

## The Limits of Deduction

Even French's formidable deductive powers have limits. After reaching [[certainty]] about Roper's guilt, he is frustrated by the one point his deductions cannot resolve — Roper's own fate and the whereabouts of the stolen money [1785-PN]. This gap illustrates that deduction, however powerful, remains dependent on available evidence.
