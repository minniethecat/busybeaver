# Jury

The **jury** is a recurring institutional presence in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*, appearing primarily in the context of the coroner's inquest into the Starvel fire deaths. Inspector French also invokes the jury as a benchmark of legal proof when evaluating the strength of his cases.

## The Coroner's Jury at the Inquest

The inquest into the Starvel Hollow fire opens with the assembly of a jury under Coroner Dr. Lonsdale. The proceedings begin with the calling of jurors: Sergeant Kent "shouted out 'Robert Judd!' so suddenly and loudly that Ruth jumped," and the man "answered 'Here!' and was promptly ordered to come forward and enter the jury box." [002-008]

Before testimony begins, the jury is taken to view the remains of the deceased: "'Have the jury viewed the remains?' asked the coroner, and Kent, answering, 'They're going to do it now, sir,' shepherded his charges out of the box." [002-009] Only after the jury has seen the physical evidence do the proceedings begin "in real earnest." [002-009]

## The Jury's Role in Testimony

During witness examination, the coroner actively invites the jury to participate in questioning. After Peter Spence's testimony, "Dr. Lonsdale, after vainly inviting the jury to ask the witness any questions, read over what he had written." [002-013] The jury's silence throughout the inquest suggests their passive acceptance of the coroner's direction.

## The Coroner's Direction and Verdict

At the conclusion of the evidence, "the coroner made a little speech to the jury," expressing that "everyone must feel appalled at the terrible tragedy which had happened so near to them all," and reminding the jury that "their duty on this occasion was threefold." [002-059] The coroner's speech effectively directs the jury's finding: "Without leaving the box the jury found as the coroner directed, the verdict was entered on the records and signed, and the inquest was over." [002-060] The phrase "as the coroner directed" emphasizes the jury's subordinate role in the coroner's court.

## French's Strategic Use of Jury Standards

Outside the inquest context, French regularly measures his cases against the standard of what a jury would believe. When questioning Pierce Whymper, French challenges his story bluntly: "I am not saying whether I myself believe it or not, but I will say this, that no jury on the face of this earth would believe it." [006-047] This rhetorical appeal to a hypothetical jury's judgment becomes a tool for extracting more complete testimony.

Similarly, when weighing Philpot's claim of innocence, French's assessment is framed in terms of jury conviction: "Whatever the truth, he saw that he had no case to bring into court. No jury would convict on such evidence." [008-077] The jury serves as French's mental model for the threshold of legal proof — a case that cannot convince a jury is not worth bringing to trial.
