# Proof

**Proof** is the destination of Inspector French's investigative method in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*. The novel traces the arc from suspicion — a "mare's nest" of unverified possibilities — through the accumulation of circumstantial evidence, to the conclusive demonstration that resolves the case.

## The Threshold of Proof

French's professional discipline imposes a clear distinction between belief and proof. Even when conviction has crystallised, he restrains himself: "In spite of this French was certain that he had discovered the truth. But he felt that before acting on his theory he must put it to the proof." [017-101] The verification step is not optional — it is the procedural obligation that separates the professional detective from the amateur speculator. "Fortunately there was a very obvious way of doing so" [017-101]: French's method requires that every theory be testable.

## Analysis, Deduction, Verification

The novel's resolution is structured as a three-stage epistemological sequence. French's conclusion, "reached by analysis and deduction," is tested against reality, and "that proof established at one and the same time the whole theory of the crime." [012-096] The interlocking nature of the proof — each verified element confirming the others — exemplifies Crofts's characteristic technique of cumulative verification. "Each new fact which he learned tended to strengthen his theory" [013-034]: proof is accretive, not singular.

## Proof and Its Absence

Much of the novel's middle section is occupied by the frustrating gap between French's certainty and his capacity for proof. The Whymper episode illustrates this: "The facts looked as bad as possible, and yet if Whymper's tale were true, he might be absolutely innocent." [006-056] French's instinct — "his inclination was to believe the story so far as it went" [006-056] — must await verification. The evidence against Whymper "would have been considered by most police officers sufficient to justify an arrest" [006-010], but French's higher standard of proof prevents a premature and potentially wrongful accusation.

## The Roper Proof

The breakthrough of the investigation is French's "proof that the Whymper incident had been engineered by Roper." [011-003] This proof is significant not only for what it establishes — Roper's criminal agency — but for the investigative method it validates. French had "been unable to sleep" on the night he completed this proof [011-003]: the intellectual satisfaction of a demonstration achieved is inseparable from the professional imperative to act on it.

## Proof and the Body

The most macabre proof in the novel is the exhumation. French reasons that Roper's body substitution — "the secret acquisition of a dead body in a country like England" [011-011] — was "an act which, French felt, could not have been done without leaving traces." [011-011] The material proof of Giles's body, with its tell-tale cocaine injection and heart disease, transforms the case from a plausible theory into an empirically established fact.
