## Definition

A false identity is a fabricated persona adopted to conceal one's true identity, typically for criminal purposes or self-protection. In *The [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] Tragedy*, false identities operate at multiple levels — from assumed names and physical disguises to the deeper deception of presenting a false character to the world.

## Role in the Novel

False identity functions in the novel on two distinct planes: as a criminal tool used by [[roper|Roper]] to execute and escape from his crimes, and as a thematic concern about the gap between appearance and reality.

**[[john-roper|Roper]]'s Disguises**: When Roper attempts to liquidate the stolen bonds after the [[starvel-hollow-fire|Starvel fire]], he adopts physical disguise — "a moustache and tinted glasses." French's reasoning about this is telling: "To a person of Roper's mental calibre a moustache would appear one of the best of disguises, while a man with a squint had practically no option but to wear tinted glasses if he wished to preserve his incognito" [ch22]. Roper's disguise is simultaneously crude and effective — crude in conception, but sufficient to baffle casual identification.

**The "Arthur Lisle Whitman" Alias**: For the bond transactions, Roper assumes the name "Arthur Lisle Whitman." French later analyzes the signatures at the [[peveril-hotel|Peveril Hotel]] register and the [[dashwood-and-munce|Dashwood]] & Munce forms, noting that "at first sight the signatures were not so obviously Roper's... but as French examined them he felt more and more satisfied that the man had indeed written them, though he had obviously made some attempt at disguise" [ch23].

**The Deeper False Identity**: Beyond physical disguises, the novel dramatizes a more fundamental false identity: Roper's presentation of himself as a reformed man to [[philpot|[[dr-philpot|Dr. [[dr-philpot|Philpot]]]]]]. By "ceasing" the blackmail, handing over the forged confession to be burned, and adopting a civil manner, Roper created a false impression of reformation. Philpot "began to believe his reformation was genuine" [ch13]. But this was itself a deception — Roper had secretly kept a copy of the confession. This false identity — the "reformed Roper" — was strategic cover for the Starvel robbery.

## Historical Context

The use of aliases and disguises was a common trope in Golden Age detective fiction, and Crofts grounded his treatment in the practical realities of 1920s identity verification — an era before photo ID was universal, when a forged letter or a confident manner could open doors. The *Police Gazette* circulation described in the novel (French sends Roper's description and photograph to be "published in the next issue... this meant that within three or four days every police officer in the land would be applying it") [ch20] reflects the real pre-digital methods of criminal identification and capture.
