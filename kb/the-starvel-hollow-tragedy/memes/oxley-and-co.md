## Overview

Oxley & Co. is the legal practice of Mr. Oxley, a solicitor based in Thirsby, Yorkshire. The firm handles all of Simon Averill's legal business, including the drafting and safekeeping of his will. As the Averill family solicitor, Mr. Oxley occupies a position of unusual knowledge and trust: he knows the disposition of Averill's estate, understands the family's financial affairs, and becomes [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] Averill's de facto guardian after the tragedy [ch01].

Mr. Oxley is described as "pleasant" and "cheery" in manner. He has known [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] for years and makes a point of chatting with her during his visits to Starvel [ch01]. Mrs. Oxley complements her husband's professional role with practical kindness, hosting [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] in their home and providing the emotional support that the young woman desperately needs after the fire [ch02].

## Role in the Investigation

Oxley & Co. functions as an institutional nexus in the investigation, connecting several key threads:

**As first responder**: Mr. Oxley is among the first at the scene of the Starvel fire, alongside Sergeant Kent and Dr. Emerson [ch01]. His presence reflects both his professional responsibility (as Averill's solicitor) and his personal concern (as [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s family friend).

**As guardian**: After the fire, Mr. Oxley assumes responsibility for [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s welfare. He sends the telegram informing her of the tragedy, hosts her at his home, shields her from intrusive inquiries, and advises her on financial matters. His revelation that he holds Averill's will and that [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] "was not to worry as to her future" provides the first concrete information about her changed circumstances [ch02].

**As source of information**: The firm's knowledge of Averill's will makes it a repository of information relevant to the question of [[motive]]. Oxley can confirm who stood to benefit from Averill's death and by how much — information that is crucial to [[joseph-french|French]]'s investigation.

**As intermediary**: Oxley serves as a bridge between the official investigation (police, inquest) and the private world of the Averill family. His presence at [[the-inquest|the inquest]], his communications with the police, and his role in managing [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s affairs place him at the intersection of public and private responses to the tragedy.

## Key Members

- **Mr. Oxley**: Principal solicitor. A capable professional whose kindness is balanced by shrewd judgment. He provides [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] with both practical support (lending her twenty pounds for mourning clothes) and legal guidance [ch02]. His relationship with [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] is avuncular — protective without being patronising.
- **Mrs. Oxley**: Wife of Mr. Oxley. Though not formally part of the firm, she provides crucial emotional support to [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]. Her "kindly way" reassures [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] before the ordeal of giving [[evidence]] at [[the-inquest|the inquest]], and her motherly care offers comfort in the immediate aftermath of the tragedy [ch02]. She also plays a small but significant role in facilitating [[ruth-averill|Ruth]]'s encounters with Pierce [[pierce-whymper|Whymper]], orchestrating the "accidental" meeting at the parish church [001-013].

## Real-World Context

The depiction of a provincial solicitor's practice in 1920s Yorkshire is grounded in the professional realities of the period. Solicitors in market towns like Thirsby served multiple functions: legal adviser, estate manager, will drafter, and often family confidant. The Oxley depicted in the novel — handling Averill's will, managing the legal aftermath of the deaths, advising [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] on her inheritance — performs exactly the roles that a real solicitor of the period would have fulfilled.

The firm's name, "Oxley & Co.," suggests a practice of some standing, though no partners other than Mr. Oxley himself are mentioned in the novel. The "& Co." may indicate a tradition of partnership that has since contracted, or simply the customary naming convention of provincial legal practices of the era.

The relationship between solicitor and client in the novel also reflects period norms. Oxley's assumption of quasi-guardianship over [[ruth-averill|Ruth]] — advising her on financial matters, hosting her in his home, managing her affairs — would have been considered appropriate professional conduct for a family solicitor in the 1920s, particularly when dealing with a young, unmarried female client with no other family.
