# Chance

**Chance** operates throughout *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy* as both a genuine narrative force and a deceptive surface beneath which criminal design conceals itself. Inspector French's investigation repeatedly turns on his ability to distinguish coincidence from contrivance.

## The Banknote Gamble

French's investigation opens with a calculated wager on chance. The £20 banknote passed by the murderer represents "a channel which offered a very poor chance of success" [005-003], yet French commits to tracing it because no alternative presents itself: "The bank note, then, appeared to be the only chance, and French decided that he would begin operations by trying to trace the passer." [005-006] When the bank manager offers "no certainty, but a good sporting chance" [005-021] of identifying the note through branch records, French seizes the opening — his method accepts that probability, not certainty, is the starting point of investigation.

## Chance Encounters

The novel is structured around apparently fortuitous meetings. Whymper's encounter with Roper outside the church gate — "We met by chance and merely wished each other good evening" [005-083] — is exposed as a calculated rendezvous concealing the transmission of Averill's £500. The encounter that is the most consequential for Roper's plan, however, is genuine: "Of course it was a lucky chance for him that George Mellowes should overtake him on the road" [020-024], providing an unwitting witness to Roper's simulated drunkenness and thereby reinforcing the accident theory.

## Chance and Concealment

Roper's criminal strategy depends on exploiting locations invisible to chance observation. French reconstructs the logic: "Not near the road where the disturbed earth would be visible to a chance passerby. Not near the house in case some of the crowds attracted by the fire should stumble upon it." [013-058] The cache of stolen notes is hidden precisely where chance will not discover it — a deliberate negation of randomness.

## Seizing the Chance

French's tactical instinct is to recognise and exploit opportunities. At the second inquest, he proposes: "This inquest gives us a chance that perhaps we should take advantage of." [014-101] His proposal to suppress the Giles murder finding temporarily — to keep the criminal unaware that the body substitution had been detected — transforms a procedural event into a strategic opportunity. French's professional genius lies in converting the given circumstances of an investigation into tactical chances.

## The Pattern Beneath Chance

The novel's epistemological arc moves from seeing events as chance occurrences to recognising them as elements of design. Whymper's willingness to "chance a murder charge rather than reveal" the Averill secret [006-077] reflects his perception that the criminal charge is a random misfortune; French's investigation demonstrates that every apparent accident — the fire, the deaths, the missing body — was deliberately engineered. What appears as chance is, in Crofts's design, concealment awaiting exposure.
