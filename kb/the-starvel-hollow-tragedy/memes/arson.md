## Definition

Arson is the criminal act of deliberately and maliciously setting fire to property — buildings, vehicles, or land. In English law, arson was historically a capital offence (codified in the Malicious Damage Act 1861) and treated with particular severity because fire, once started, endangers not only the targeted property but also lives and surrounding structures. In the context of detective fiction, arson serves a dual function: as a method of murder (destroying evidence along with victims) and as a concealment technique (obscuring the true nature of a crime behind the devastation of fire).

## Role in the Novel

The Starvel fire is at the centre of the entire narrative. Three people — Simon Averill and the Ropers — die in the blaze, and the house is reduced to "the most complete destruction" the Town Officer had ever seen [006-002]. For a significant portion of the early novel, the fire is treated as a tragic accident: an oil lamp overturned, a petrol lamp malfunctioning, or some domestic mishap in a house lit by oil and petrol in a remote location [006-002].

The transformation of the fire from "accident" to "crime" is the novel's first major investigative pivot. Mr. Tarkington, the bank manager, is the first to voice suspicion. He suggests to Mr. Oxley that the fire might have been deliberately set — asking explicitly "Do you mean arson?" [003-007]. Tarkington's theory, which the solicitor initially dismisses as "chimerical, fantastic, absurd" [003-007], proposes that burglars broke into Starvel, killed the occupants, and set fire to the house to conceal their crime.

Inspector French's investigation transforms this speculation into certainty. His discovery that the safe contained only burnt newspaper — no fragments of bank notes — leads him to conclude "robbery! And if robbery, then murder! Murder and arson!" [008-004]. The arson is thus established as the secondary crime, designed to conceal the primary crimes of theft and murder by destroying physical evidence and creating the appearance of an accident.

French later confronts Pierce Whymper directly with the allegation: he is suspected of "murder, arson, and burglary" [010-017]. The novel's final summary lists the crimes definitively as "Murder — quadruple murder — theft, arson and body-snatching" [020-003].

## The Arson as Cover

The arson in *Starvel Hollow* exemplifies the classic crime-fiction function of fire as a destroyer of evidence. The complete devastation of the house — "Nothing that could be burnt was left. Between the walls the debris was still a red-hot glowing mass" [006-002] — is designed to:
- Destroy the bodies beyond recognition (making identification and cause-of-death determination impossible)
- Incinerate the safe's contents (creating the illusion that the bank notes burned with the money)
- Eliminate any forensic traces of the crime (footprints, bloodstains, signs of struggle)
- Mask the true number of victims (the substitution of bodies is concealed by charring)

The fire is ultimately undone not by conventional arson investigation but by the one object it failed to destroy: the fireproof safe and the serial numbers of the bank notes that should have been within it.
