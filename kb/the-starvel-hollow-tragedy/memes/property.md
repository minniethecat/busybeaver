# Property

**Property** — both real estate and movable assets — functions as a central economic driver in *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*, shaping Averill's wealth, the criminals' motive, and the investigative trail that French follows.

## Averill's Leeds Property

The bulk of Averill's wealth derived from land on the outskirts of Leeds: "land on the outskirts of Leeds which had been built over and which had become a very valuable property." [003-033] This property generated the majority of his income of "between sixteen and seventeen hundred a year." [003-033]

Oxley confirms the property's provenance: "The old man got it through his wife and it went back to her family at his death." [003-034] Critically, Averill "had only a life interest" in this property, meaning it would not pass to his heirs — a detail that shapes the inheritance structure of the novel. [003-033]

The property generated significant periodic income: "a cheque for £346 came in from the Leeds property" shortly before the tragedy, of which £320 was sent to Starvel in twenty-pound notes. [003-039] This cash transfer placed a large sum in the Starvel safe immediately before the fire, creating both the motive and the means for the crime.

## The Safe as Common Property

The legend of Averill's wealth was widely known: "Averill was universally believed to be wealthy — the legend of the safe was common property." [003-057] This public knowledge made Starvel a target: "Is it impossible that some of these burglars might have decided to make an attempt on Starvel?" [003-057] The phrase "common property" is telling — the knowledge of Averill's hoard was so widespread it had become a community asset, accessible to anyone with criminal intent.

## French's Discoveries Become Common Property

By the conclusion of the investigation, French's findings had themselves become widely shared knowledge: "The story of French's discoveries, by this time common property, had created an absolute furore in the little town." [015-002] The transformation of investigative knowledge into community property parallels the earlier transformation of Averill's wealth into public knowledge — both forms of "property" circulate beyond their original owners' control.

## Property as Evidence of Guilt

French invokes possession of stolen property as the archetypal standard of criminal proof: "To find a man suspected of the theft with the stolen property in his possession was usually reckoned an overwhelming proof of his guilt." [006-010] This legal principle guides French's investigation as he seeks to connect suspects to the missing notes and valuables.

## Whymper's Private Property

When French searches Whymper's rooms, he immediately identifies the desk as "Whymper's private property, and in its locked drawers would lie any secret documents the young man might possess." [005-111] French's use of "skeleton keys and a little apparatus of steel wire" to open this private property demonstrates the tension between property rights and investigative necessity. [005-111]
