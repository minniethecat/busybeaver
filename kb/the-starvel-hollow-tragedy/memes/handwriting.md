## Definition

Handwriting analysis, in the forensic context, is the examination and comparison of handwritten documents to determine authorship, detect forgery, or verify authenticity. In the 1920s, forensic handwriting examination was an established but still-developing discipline — Scotland Yard maintained a cadre of document examiners who could be called upon to provide expert testimony. In detective fiction, handwriting serves as a versatile plot device: it can incriminate (a forged letter), exonerate (a verified signature), or mislead (a clever imitation).

## Role in the Novel

Handwriting emerges as a critical forensic tool at a pivotal moment in French's investigation. The question is whether a letter purportedly written by Simon Averill — the letter that lured Pierce Whymper to Starvel on the night of the fire — is genuine or a forgery.

French initially accepts the letter as authentic. But as his investigation deepens and his suspicion shifts from Whymper to Roper, he revisits the document: "French remembered that the handwriting was extremely like Averill's, but in the absence of any reason for suspecting its authenticity he had not given it the careful scrutiny which he might have done. That was an error he must repair at once." [013-009]

The key to unlocking the case lies in Dr. Philpot's revelation about Roper's background. Philpot tells French that Roper possessed skill as a forger — a detail that transforms French's understanding of every document in the case. French realises that "though Averill was represented as the moving spirit of the affair, his connection with it had never been directly proved. Roper, and Roper alone, had appeared." [013-009]

## The Forgery Investigation

French's re-examination of the Averill letter proceeds methodically. He compares the Whymper letter against samples of Averill's genuine handwriting obtained from Tarkington, the bank manager. His initial comparison leaves him uncertain — "by no means positive" — but increasingly "inclined to believe Whymper's was a forgery." [013-010] He then obtains four additional genuine letters from Oxley, the solicitor, and after "critical examination" finds his "suspicions strengthened." [013-010]

Recognising the limits of his own expertise, French sends the documents to Scotland Yard "asking for an expert opinion to be wired him." [013-010] This professional humility — the willingness to defer to specialist expertise — is characteristic of French's investigative method.

The handwriting investigation extends beyond the Averill letter. French also examines the letter of invitation from Mrs. Palmer-Gore to Ruth Averill — the invitation that conveniently removed Ruth from Starvel before the fire. He travels to York to interview Mrs. Palmer-Gore, asking her directly: "Did you ever suspect that that letter might be a forgery?" [013-013] The question reflects French's growing conviction that Roper systematically used forged documents to manipulate everyone around him.

## The Forged Confession

The most significant document requiring handwriting analysis is the confession supposedly written by Dr. Philpot and found in Roper's safe deposit box. French's examination yields a definitive conclusion:

> "The confession was a forgery. The lens revealed a shakiness in the writing due to slow and careful formation of the letters which would not have been there had it been written at an ordinary speed." [013-013]

The Yard experts confirm his finding: "an enlarged photograph proved it conclusively." The forged confession becomes central to understanding Roper's blackmail scheme — Roper had created a document so skillfully imitating Philpot's handwriting that Philpot himself could not be certain it was not genuine, giving Roper perpetual leverage.

> "Anxious to see whether Roper's handwriting contained any idiosyncrasies which had been reproduced in the forged documents, French with some difficulty obtained some old forms which he had filled up. These he put in his pocket for future study." [013-014]

## Handwriting as Plot Mechanism

In Crofts's construction, the forged letter serves a precise narrative function. It is the mechanism by which:

- Whymper is drawn to Starvel on the fatal night
- Suspicion is initially directed away from Roper (since "Averill" appears to have summoned Whymper)
- French's eventual discovery of the forgery marks his intellectual breakthrough — the moment he understands that Roper, not Averill or Whymper, is the active criminal intelligence

The handwriting evidence is thus both a forensic detail and a structural pillar of the plot. Its function is not to provide a dramatic courtroom revelation (there is no trial scene) but to serve as one link in French's chain of cumulative evidence — a link that, once verified, enables every other link to fall into place.
