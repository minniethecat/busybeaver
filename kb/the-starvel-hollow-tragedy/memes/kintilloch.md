## Description

Kintilloch is a small town in Fifeshire, Scotland, situated about a mile from the Ransome Institute, the large mental hospital that dominates the local economy and community [006-010]. The town is small enough that its social life is organised around a tight network of personal relationships — the Philpots are described as taking "a prominent part in the social life of the town" [006-005] — yet large enough to support a weekly newspaper, the *Kintilloch Weekly Argus*, which reported in detail on the death of Edna Philpot [006-008].

The town has its own police station, staffed by Sergeant MacGregor, whom the district superintendent describes as "a shrewd gossip" who knows everything that happens in his district [006-014]. The local railway station connects Kintilloch to Cupar (the district headquarters), [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], and the main line to [[london|London]], making it accessible to [[joseph-french|French]] when he travels from [[london|London]] to investigate Philpot's background [006-012].

The town is sufficiently self-contained that its residents know each other's histories in detail. The community's collective memory — preserved in police records, newspaper files, and the recollections of long-serving officials like MacGregor — provides [[joseph-french|French]] with a ready-made archive of information about the principal figures in the case.

## Significance in the Novel

Kintilloch functions as the novel's secondary location — the place where the tragedy's prehistory unfolded and where [[joseph-french|French]] must travel to uncover the truth behind the events at Starvel. Its role is threefold:

**As the past made present**: Every significant element of the Starvel conspiracy has roots in Kintilloch. Philpot's marriage, his wife's suspicious death, [[john-roper|Roper]]'s blackmail, the forged confession, [[john-roper|Roper]]'s dismissal from [[ransome-institute|the Institute]] — all occurred in and around this small Scottish town [006-003 through 006-017]. When [[joseph-french|French]] needs to verify Philpot's story, he must travel to Kintilloch to consult the people and records that can confirm or refute it.

**As investigative counterpoint**: The contrast between Thirsby (Yorkshire, English, where the crime occurred) and Kintilloch (Fife, Scottish, where the conspiracy originated) structures the investigation geographically. [[joseph-french|French]]'s journey north represents the investigation's movement from the present crime to its past causes, from the physical [[evidence]] of the fire to the buried history of the people involved.

**As community as witness**: Kintilloch's tight-knit social fabric means that no significant event goes unobserved or unrecorded. Sergeant MacGregor's detailed knowledge, the *Weekly Argus*'s thorough reporting, and the institutional records of the Ransome Institute together create a documentary record that allows [[joseph-french|French]] to reconstruct events years after they occurred.

## Key Events

- Dr. Philpot marries Edna Menzies in May 1914 and sets up house at Braeside [006-010]
- Edna Philpot dies on 15 May 1921, ostensibly from a fall downstairs at Braeside, and is reported in the *Kintilloch Weekly Argus* [006-005]
- The local superintendent, dissatisfied with the circumstances, conducts a confidential inquiry but finds no grounds for action [006-009]
- [[john-roper|Roper]] is dismissed from the Ransome Institute for drugging patients [006-017]
- [[joseph-french|French]] travels to Kintilloch for the first time, interviewing Sergeant MacGregor and others [006-012 through 006-017]
- [[joseph-french|French]] returns to Kintilloch later in the investigation, seeking anyone who might recognise [[john-roper|Roper]] in disguise [015-004 through 015-006]

## Real-World Context

Kintilloch is a fictional name, but Crofts was familiar with Scotland — he was born in Dublin and travelled throughout the British Isles — and the town is drawn with sufficient geographical and social specificity to be plausible. The detail that the local train connects to Cupar (a real town in Fife) before reaching [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] anchors Kintilloch in actual Scottish geography.

The town's name follows Scottish naming conventions: "Kintilloch" combines the Gaelic-derived prefix "Kin-" (head, chief) with "-tilloch," possibly suggesting a location at the head of a loch or water feature. This is consistent with Fifeshire place names and with Crofts's practice of creating fictional names that sound authentic within their regional context.

The *Kintilloch Weekly Argus* represents the kind of local newspaper that was ubiquitous in British towns of the period — a source of detailed community reporting that, as the novel demonstrates, could serve as an informal archive for investigators reconstructing past events.
