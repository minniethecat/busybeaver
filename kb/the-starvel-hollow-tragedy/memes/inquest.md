## Definition

An inquest is a formal judicial inquiry conducted by a coroner to determine the cause of death in cases where the death was sudden, violent, or of unknown cause. In English law, inquests are held before a coroner and a jury, who hear witness testimony and examine evidence to establish the identity of the deceased and the manner of death. The inquest does not determine criminal guilt — it establishes facts. In Golden Age detective fiction, the inquest frequently serves as the narrative mechanism for presenting the known facts of a case to the reader before the detective proper begins work.

## Role in the Novel

The inquest into the Starvel deaths is the subject of Chapter 2 ("The Inquest") and represents the novel's first formal presentation of evidence. It is held at the Thirsby courthouse at three o'clock in the afternoon, two days after the fire, with Dr. Lonsdale presiding as coroner [006-002].

The inquest serves multiple narrative functions:

First, it establishes the known facts. Witness testimony from Peter Spence (the baker who discovered the fire), Abel Hesketh (Town Officer and fire brigade chief), Sergeant Kent, and the tramp James Stokes collectively reconstructs the physical facts: the fire was discovered around eleven in the morning after having burned through the night, the house was completely destroyed, three charred bodies were found, and the glow had been visible from at least three locations during the night [006-002].

Second, it introduces the characters and their relationships. Ruth Averill testifies about her uncle's health, the household arrangements, her trip to York, and crucially, denies ever having seen either of the Ropers intoxicated — a denial that carries significance given later revelations about Roper's drinking [006-002].

Third, it is the site of French's most ingenious investigative gambit. Though French attends the inquest, he does so covertly — posing as an insurance investigator rather than a Scotland Yard detective. His presence at the inquest is not merely observational; he later reveals that he "had devised and skilfully baited a trap for his victim" at the inquest, and the trap succeeded [018-001].

## The Inquest as Narrative Device

Crofts uses the inquest to demonstrate the gap between surface appearances and underlying truth. The coroner and jury hear testimony, examine physical evidence (the safe with £1,952 in sovereigns and "a mass of burnt papers" [006-002]), and reach conclusions — yet the inquest's official finding of "accidental death" is fundamentally wrong. The inquest thus illustrates a recurring Crofts theme: that official procedures, however conscientiously conducted, can fail when the facts presented are themselves a deception.

## The First Verdict

The coroner's direction to the jury is decisive in shaping the outcome. After summarizing the evidence, Dr. Lonsdale makes a speech that effectively guides the jury away from any finding of blame:

> "He would remind the jury that their duty on this occasion was threefold: first, to state the identity of the deceased if they were reasonably convinced by the evidence on this point; second, to find the cause of death in each case, and third, to state whether, in their opinion, blame attached to any person or persons, and if so, to whom." [006-002]

The coroner specifically cautions against blaming Roper, noting "there was no evidence that Mr. Roper's condition had had anything to do with the fire." He suggests the victims "had been suffocated by the smoke while asleep," and that "they might find that Simon Ralph Averill, John Roper and Flora Roper had lost their lives in a fire at Starvel on the night of the fifteenth of September, the cause of which there was no evidence to show."

> "Without leaving the box the jury found as the coroner directed, the verdict was entered on the records and signed, and the inquest was over." [006-002]

The speed and compliance of this verdict — rendered without the jury even leaving the box — underscores how readily official processes can be satisfied with surface explanations.

## Key Details

- The coroner questions why the fire was not more widely observed, noting that "there must have been a tremendous glare reflected from the sky which would have been visible for miles round" [006-002]
- Three people did see the glow: James Stokes (a tramp sleeping in an outhouse), Mrs. Eliza Steele (attending her sick husband), and the Misses Locke (elderly sisters)
- The jury is required to view the remains before the proceedings begin
- Every witness's testimony is transcribed, read back, and signed as a formal deposition
- Ruth experiences emotional relief after the inquest: "Now with the ending of the inquest, it seemed to her that the terrible affair was all but over." [006-002]
