# Certainty

**Certainty** is the middle stage in [[joseph-french|Inspector French]]'s investigative epistemology, positioned between [[suspicion]] and [[proof]]. It represents the point at which circumstantial evidence has accumulated to the degree that the investigator no longer harbours reasonable doubt, even though formal legal proof has not yet been established.

## French's Epistemological Method

The novel articulates French's method through a passage that defines his entire approach to criminal investigation:

> By this method of cumulative circumstantial evidence was he accustomed to find suspicion grow to certainty and certainty to proof. [2255-PN]

This progression — suspicion → certainty → proof — is the backbone of French's detective work throughout the Starvel Hollow case. Each new fact strengthens the chain of reasoning until certainty is reached [2255-PN].

## Certainty vs. Proof

French distinguishes clearly between subjective certainty and objective proof. When he identifies [[john-roper|Roper]] as the criminal mastermind, he reaches certainty before he can prove it:

> So that was certainty at last. Roper was the evil genius behind all these involved happenings. He it was who had got Ruth away from the doomed house; he had sent Whymper off to pass the stolen notes so that he might learn if their numbers were known; he had murdered Simon Averill; he had stolen the notes from the safe; he had murdered his wife; he had burned the house. [1785-PN]

Yet even at this moment of certainty, French is frustrated by the remaining unknown — what happened to Roper himself and where the money was hidden [1785-PN].

## The Certainty of Identification

The novel also explores the limits of certainty in forensic contexts. Dr. Emerson testifies that sex and approximate age can be determined from skeletal remains "with certainty," but this is distinct from positive identification of a specific individual [1833-PN]. This forensic nuance plays a critical role in the body substitution plot.
