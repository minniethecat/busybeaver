# Reputation

**Reputation** operates as a form of social currency throughout *The Starvel Hollow Tragedy*, influencing characters' credibility, professional standing, and the criminals' strategy of concealment.

## Professional Reputation

Several characters are introduced through the lens of their professional reputations. Dr. Philpot, Averill's physician, is described as "a youngish man who had come to the place some three or four years earlier, and who had already built up a reputation for care and skill." [001-038] This reputation for competence contrasts with his older colleague, "Dr. Emerson, who was old and past his work, and had retired in all but name." [001-038]

French himself values reputation as a professional asset. He explains his thorough documentation: "I like to do things thoroughly. Gets you a reputation for efficiency, you know. One can't afford to sneeze at it." [016-028] For French, reputation is not vanity but a practical tool — it opens doors, earns cooperation from witnesses, and lends authority to his conclusions.

## Reputation as Investigative Asset

French leverages the reputations of others to advance his investigation. He seeks out "Colonel Followes, a prominent magistrate with a reputation for discretion, whose name had been given him by Sergeant Kent." [006-019] A magistrate known for discretion is essential when French needs to obtain an arrest warrant without the details becoming public. Reputation here functions as a vetting mechanism — Followes's standing guarantees his reliability.

## French's Reputation at Stake

At the exhumation, French experiences a moment of personal vulnerability tied to reputation. Unlike the other observers who feel morbid curiosity, "with French it was different. He was moved because his reputation was at stake. So much depended for him on what that raised lid would reveal." [012-093] The exhumation is a professional gamble — if the body yields no evidence, French's reputation as an investigator would be damaged.

## Reputation as Criminal Camouflage

The criminals deliberately construct a false reputation to deflect suspicion. After settling at Starvel, Roper and his wife begin "living exemplary lives" and "sought to establish a reputation for integrity which would tend to support the accident theory to be put forward later." [020-018] Philpot insists on patience: "for at least a year they were to carry out their duties quietly, so that no one would think the 'accident' came suspiciously soon after their advent." [020-018]

This calculated cultivation of reputation reveals its dark side — reputation is not just earned but can be fabricated, making it an unreliable proxy for character.

## Shady Reputation as Clue

French's investigation also exploits the shadow economy of disreputable contacts. The intermediary "Mr. Andrews Macdonald" is "a small tobacconist with a rather shady reputation," making him the ideal conduit for Roper's anonymous correspondence: "Roper had adopted a time-honoured expedient to obtain his correspondence secretly. Letters could be addressed to Macdonald and for a consideration they would either be re-addressed to Roper or be kept till called for." [018-076] A shady reputation, in this context, becomes a functional asset for criminal communication.
