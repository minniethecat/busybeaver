## Profile

Ruth Averill is the protagonist of *The [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]] Tragedy*. She is a young woman of about twenty years, an orphan dependent on the charity of her uncle, Simon Averill. Born in Southern France, she was raised in the gloomy old house of Starvel on the Yorkshire moors after losing her parents.

Physically, she is described as under medium height with a charming figure, dark hair, and delicate features that suggest Spanish or Italian heritage. Her firmly rounded chin gives "promise of character" [001-005]. She has the fresh vitality of youth but dresses in threadbare, out-of-fashion clothes, reflecting her dependent financial position [001-006].

## Life at Starvel

Ruth's existence at Starvel is one of "dull loneliness" [001-007]. The house is gloomy and isolated on the Yorkshire moors. Her uncle [[simon-averill|Simon]] is a [[miser]] who hoards coins and banknotes in his safe, requiring only that Ruth read to him once daily while otherwise neglecting her [001-010]. The household servants, [[john-roper|John]] and [[flora-roper|Flora Roper]], are hostile to her — she suspects them of reading her letters and resents their petty surveillance [001-007] [001-011].

Her social circle is painfully narrow: apart from the inhabitants of Starvel, she knows only [[markham-giles|Markham Giles]], a semi-invalid war veteran and amateur entomologist who lives in a nearby cottage [001-012], and the butcher and bootmaker in Thirsby.

## Role in the Narrative

### Entry Point and Audience Surrogate

Ruth serves as the reader's entry point into the world of [[starvel|Starvel Hollow]]. Her perspective opens the novel, and the tragedy is first filtered through her experience. The reader meets the household, learns of its tensions, and feels the weight of its isolation through her eyes.

Her departure for York — funded by her uncle's uncharacteristic generosity of ten pounds [001-026] — places her away from Starvel at the moment of the catastrophic fire. This is a crucial narrative device: it removes her from the suspect pool while denying her (and the reader) direct witness to the central event. Everything she knows about the tragedy comes secondhand, positioning her as an audience surrogate for the investigative revelations that follow [001-033].

### The Inquest Witness

At the inquest (Chapter 2), Ruth testifies as one of the most important witnesses [006-019]. She describes the household's lighting arrangements — oil lamps and a petrol lamp for her uncle's poor eyesight — and is questioned about whether an accident might have caused the fire. She rejects suggestions that her uncle might have knocked over his lamp or fallen in the fire, noting that he was "a strict teetotaller" [006-097].

Her testimony is notable for what she cannot say: she was in York during the fire and has no direct knowledge of what occurred at Starvel that night.

### Emotional Arc

Ruth undergoes a significant emotional journey through the novel. Her initial hatred of Starvel and its inhabitants is palpable and credible — she "hated both the place and its inhabitants" [001-007]. After the tragedy, she experiences overwhelming guilt over these feelings:

> When Ruth Averill awoke next morning she found that the overwhelming sense of sick horror which had weighed her down on the previous evening had lightened. She had been worn out in body from the shock and the nervous strain, but sleep had restored her physical well-being, and her mind reacted to her body. She was young, she was in perfect health, and — she was in love. [006-009]

This passage, opening Chapter 2, captures the central tension of her character: guilt and grief contend with youth, health, and romantic hope. Mrs. Oxley's kindness — bringing tea and the "thinnest of bread and butter" — represents the first nurturing attention Ruth has received in years [006-013].

### Whymper and the Secret of Her Parentage

The romantic subplot with [[pierce-whymper|Pierce Whymper]] takes on darker dimensions as the plot unfolds. [[john-roper|Roper]]'s scheme exploited Whymper by fabricating a story about Ruth's parentage — claiming her mother was Madeleine Blancquart, a woman of questionable character in [[talloires|Talloires]] [014-131]. This cruel invention was designed to manipulate Whymper into an unwitting role in the fraud.

Ruth herself may never learn of this aspect of the scheme — but Whymper's willingness to risk arrest rather than expose her to scandal testifies to the depth of his devotion and the vulnerability of her social position.

## Relationships

- **[[simon-averill]]**: Her uncle and guardian. A [[miser]] who hoards coins and banknotes in his safe [001-010]. Ruth dislikes him intensely, though she later feels guilty about this after his death. He requires her to read to him once daily but otherwise neglects her. His uncharacteristic gift of ten pounds for her York trip — described as "a minor miracle" — later proves to be part of Roper's scheme [001-026].

- **[[pierce-whymper]]**: Junior assistant to an ecclesiastical architect in [[leeds|Leeds]], serving as clerk of works during renovations to Thirsby parish church [001-013]. Ruth meets him during a church visit and they develop a mutual romantic attraction. By the time she leaves for York, she is certain she loves him and that he reciprocates. His later concealment of the fabricated parentage story, intended to protect her, becomes a key element of the investigation.

- **[[markham-giles]]**: A semi-invalid entomologist who lives in a cottage near Starvel [001-012]. A war veteran who was gassed and wounded in Flanders, he is one of Ruth's few friends. She helps him with his insect specimens. He dies of influenza while Ruth is in York — compounding her loss: within two days, all four people she knew best are dead.

- **[[helen-palmer-gore]]**: An old friend of Ruth's father who nursed Ruth as a baby [001-024]. Her invitation to visit York provides Ruth's [[alibi]] during the fire and represents Ruth's sole connection to her parents' past.

- **[[john-roper]]**: The servant at Starvel who murdered Simon Averill and orchestrated the fire. Ruth's instinctive dislike of the Ropers proves prescient. Roper's scheme included the fabrication about Ruth's parentage used to manipulate Whymper.

## Character Analysis

Ruth Averill fits the archetype of the vulnerable young woman whose fate hangs on the resolution of a mystery — a common figure in Golden Age detective fiction. However, Crofts gives her more interiority than is typical: her hatred of Starvel is palpable and credible, her guilt after the deaths adds psychological depth, and her narrative position as both insider (to the household) and outsider (absent during the crime) makes her a uniquely effective perspective character.

Her position as orphan-dependent creates both sympathy and narrative tension. The question of inheritance — whether any of Simon Averill's hoarded money will come to her — introduces a material stake in the resolution. Mrs. Oxley's revelation that Mr. Oxley had drawn up the will and "there would be some money" provides the first hint of Ruth's financial future [006-013]. The romantic subplot with Whymper provides emotional stakes and a potential future beyond the tragedy.

Ruth's evolution from passive sufferer to engaged participant mirrors the novel's larger movement from catastrophe to resolution. Her initially bleak worldview — shaped by the loveless household at Starvel — gradually gives way to the possibility of happiness through Whymper and financial independence, contingent on the exposure of Roper's crimes.
