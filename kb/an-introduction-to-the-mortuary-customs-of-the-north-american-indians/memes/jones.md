# Charles C. Jones, Jr.

## Overview

Charles Colcock Jones, Jr. (1831–1893) was an American antiquarian, historian, and attorney whose two major archaeological monographs — *Antiquities of the Southern Indians* (1873) and *Antiquities of [[tennessee|Tennessee]]* (1876, published by the Smithsonian Institution) — constituted some of the most thorough Southern archaeological surveys available to Yarrow at the time of writing. Jones's work bridged the gap between the antiquarian tradition of the early nineteenth century and the professionalizing ethnology represented by the Smithsonian's Contributions to Knowledge series, and his detailed descriptions of burial sites across [[georgia|Georgia]], the [[carolina|Carolinas]], and [[tennessee|Tennessee]] supplied Yarrow with a wealth of primary archaeological data (001-004)(015-005).

## Contributions

Jones provided Yarrow with two distinct categories of evidence: historical accounts of living burial customs drawn from earlier observers, and archaeological descriptions of prehistoric burial sites derived from his own fieldwork. In the first category, Jones quoted at length from the colonial traveler [[lawson-john|John Lawson]], who recorded the burial customs of the [[carolina|Carolina]] tribes around 1700. Lawson's account, as transmitted by Jones, described a stratified funerary ritual in which the corpse was first placed in a cane bundle, guarded and mourned for a day and night, then wrapped in blankets stripped from young men of the town, encased in a woven-reed coffin, and finally carried to a peach orchard where a medicine-man delivered a funeral oration recounting the deceased's exploits and counselling the living to follow his example (001-004).

In the second category, Jones's archaeological work on the stone graves of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] represented a major contribution to the Smithsonian's Contributions to Knowledge series. Yarrow referred his readers to this volume for a comprehensive treatment of stone-grave burial, noting that Jones had documented the characteristic slab-lined cists found throughout the Cumberland and [[tennessee|Tennessee]] River valleys with an attention to construction detail and associated grave goods that set a new standard for Southern archaeology (003-007). Jones was among the first to systematically distinguish between prehistoric stone-grave complexes and the burial practices of historically documented tribes, a methodological contribution that Yarrow implicitly acknowledged by citing Jones as his principal authority on the subject.

Jones's service as an archaeological field observer also reached into [[california|California]], where his examination of the Calaveras County cave on the Stanislaus River — a site that had yielded human skulls to the Smithsonian collections — provided Yarrow with evidence relevant to the discussion of cave burial. Jones noted the presence of bows, arrows, and charcoal alongside the skulls, and concluded that the cave had served as a burial place for the region's historic Indian population rather than representing an earlier geological horizon (005-010).

## Accounts Provided

Through his published works, Jones contributed to Yarrow: the Lawson-derived account of [[carolina|Carolina]] tribal burial ritual (001-004); the Smithsonian monograph on [[tennessee|Tennessee]] stone graves (003-007); field observations from Calaveras County cave, [[california|California]], including artifact associations (005-010); and references to [[natchez|Natchez]] burial customs drawn from earlier historical sources (015-005).
