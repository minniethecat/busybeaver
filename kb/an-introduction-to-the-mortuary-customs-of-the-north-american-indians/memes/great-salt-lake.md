# Great Salt Lake

## Overview

The Great Salt Lake region of present-day [[utah|Utah]] appears in Yarrow (1880) through the accounts of nineteenth-century military explorers who documented [[gosiute|Goshute]] burial practices in the surrounding valleys and deserts. The area's arid environment and distinctive geography — including Skull Valley and the salt flats — made it a notable locus for aboriginal mortuary customs (017-002)(012-001).

## Skull Valley and the Goshute Ossuary

The most significant burial-related site in the region is Skull Valley, "which is a part of the Great Salt Lake Desert." The name derives, according to guide George W. Bean, "from the number of skulls which have been found in it, and which have arisen from the custom of the Goshute Indians burying their dead in springs, which they sink with stones or keep down with sticks" (017-003). Bean stated that he had actually seen the Indians bury their dead in this way near the town of Provo, where he resided (017-003).

The Goshute burial customs in the area were such that human remains became a visible feature of the landscape. The concentration of skulls and bones in Skull Valley suggests that it served as a recurring burial ground over an extended period, with the dry climate contributing to the preservation of osseous remains on the surface (017-003).

## Aquatic Burial: A Unique Mode of Sepulture

Yarrow, after a careful search for well-authenticated instances of burial both aquatic and semi-aquatic, found only two, one of which was the Gosh-Ute practice documented by Captain J. H. Simpson (017-002). Yarrow described this peculiar mode of burial as "entirely unique, so far as known, and but from the well-known probity of the relator might well be questioned, especially when it is remembered that in the country spoken of water is quite scarce and Indians are careful not to pollute the streams or springs near which they live" (017-005).

As corroboration of the aquatic burial claim, Captain Simpson mentioned that, arriving at a spring one evening during his 1858–1859 exploration of the Great Salt Lake Valley, the party was "obliged to dig out the skeleton of an Indian from the mud at the bottom before using the water" (017-004). This firsthand encounter with the material remains of the practice lent considerable weight to Bean's account.

Yarrow concluded that "conjecture seems useless to establish a reason for this disposition of the dead" (017-005), acknowledging that the practice defied the usual explanatory frameworks applied to other mortuary customs.

## Exploration Accounts

The valley was surveyed by Captain Howard Stansbury during his 1849–1850 expedition, published as *Exploration of the Valley of the Great Salt Lake of Utah* (1852). Stansbury's account of [[sioux|Sioux]] burial practices, while documenting observations made elsewhere, was one of the important primary sources Yarrow drew upon for his monograph (012-001).

Captain J. H. Simpson's 1858–1859 exploration of the Great Salt Lake Valley, published in 1859, provided documentation of Goshute burial customs specifically within the Salt Lake region. Simpson's route through Skull Valley yielded the description of the ossuary and the origin of the valley's name that Yarrow incorporated into his work (017-002)(017-003). The Goshute themselves, a Shoshonean-speaking people of the Great Basin, inhabited the desert region surrounding the lake and developed mortuary practices adapted to the extreme aridity and sparse resources of their environment.
