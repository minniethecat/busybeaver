## Overview

New Mexico, in the American Southwest, is documented in Yarrow's 1880 monograph primarily through accounts of [[navajo|Navajo]] mortuary customs and the region's distinctive ceramic burial urns. The arid climate of New Mexico influenced both burial methods and the preservation of archaeological remains.

## Burial Sites & Practices

The [[navajo|Navajos]] of New Mexico and neighboring [[arizona|Arizona]] received particular attention in Yarrow's survey. The [[navajo|Navajo]] people, "a tribe living a considerable distance" from Anglo-American settlements, practiced burial customs that differed markedly from Plains and Eastern tribes (002-005). Their mortuary rites involved careful treatment of the deceased's possessions and specific protocols for [[inhumation|interment]].

Ceramic burial urns are a distinctive archaeological feature of New Mexico. [[barber-ea|E. A. Barber]] described "the burial-urns of New Mexico," noting their characteristic form and decoration, which distinguished them from the pottery of [[california|California]] and other Southwestern regions (007-007). Yarrow also notes that "urns or ollas in the graves of New Mexico and [[california|California]]" were commonly found, often containing cremated remains or serving as grave markers (007-004). The practice of [[urn-burial|urn burial]] in New Mexico represents a regional variant of this widespread mortuary custom, with the vessels often being of considerable size and decorated with geometric patterns.
