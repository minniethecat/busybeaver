# Coyotero Apache

## Overview

The Coyotero Apaches constituted a division of the [[apache|Western Apache]] people inhabiting the rugged terrain of present-day eastern [[arizona|Arizona]] and western [[new-mexico|New Mexico]]. As a mobile, semi-nomadic people adapted to the arid Southwestern environment, the [[apache|Coyoteros]] developed mortuary customs that were notable — in the eyes of nineteenth-century observers — for their utilitarian minimalism. Yarrow (1880) preserves the detailed account of [[hoffman-wj|Dr. W. J. Hoffman]], whose field observations among the [[apache|Coyoteros]] provide one of the most vivid portraits of [[apache|Apache]] burial practice in the ethnographic literature of the period.

## Mortuary Customs

### Natural Cavity Interment

The defining feature of Coyotero Apache burial was the use of pre-existing natural cavities — the hollow left by the removal of a rock or a tree stump — as ready-made graves. According to Dr. [[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]]:

> The Coyotero Apaches, according to Dr. W. J. Hoffman, in disposing of their dead, seem to be actuated by the desire to spare themselves any needless trouble, and prepare the defunct and the grave in this manner (001-026).

This characterization — "spare themselves any needless trouble" — reflects the nineteenth-century observer's cultural bias but inadvertently captures an essential feature of Coyotero mortuary logic: the integration of the dead into the existing landscape rather than the construction of elaborate, permanent burial structures. The natural world itself provided the grave.

### The Burial Process

[[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]]'s account describes the burial procedure in detail:

> The Coyoteros, upon the death of a member of the tribe, partially wrap up the corpse and deposit it into the cavity left by the removal of a small rock or the stump of a tree. After the body has been crammed into the smallest possible space the rock or stump is again rolled into its former position, when a number of stones are placed around the base to keep out the coyotes (001-027).

The practice of "cramming" the body into the smallest possible cavity — folding or flexing the corpse into a tightly compacted position — is consistent with the flexed burial position documented across many indigenous North American cultures. The name "Coyotero" itself may derive from the Spanish *coyotero* (one who deals with coyotes), and the ritual precaution of placing stones around the grave base to exclude these scavengers reflects the practical realities of burial in a landscape shared with predators.

### The Thirty-Day Mourning Period

Coyotero [[mourning]] observances extended for a fixed period of one month:

> The nearest of kin usually mourn for the period of one month, during that time giving utterance at intervals to the most dismal lamentations, which are apparently sincere. During the day this obligation is frequently neglected or forgotten, but when the mourner is reminded of his duty he renews his howling with evident interest (001-027).

[[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]]'s dry observation that the mourner's daytime lapses were remedied "with evident interest" when reminded captures the performative dimension of [[mourning]] — a social obligation as much as an emotional expression. Yarrow notes that this custom of [[mourning]] for thirty days "corresponds to that formerly observed by the [[natchez|Natchez]]" (001-027), drawing a cross-cultural parallel between the Southwestern [[apache|Apache]] and the Lower [[mississippi|Mississippi]] Valley [[natchez|Natchez]], two peoples separated by vast geographic and cultural distance.

## Historical Sources

The principal source for Coyotero Apache burial customs is [[hoffman-wj|Dr. W. J. Hoffman]], whose observations were published in the U.S. Geological Survey of the Territories for 1876. [[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]]'s account is notable for its detailed, first-hand description of the burial process and for its candid — if culturally conditioned — assessment of Coyotero motivations. Yarrow's index preserves three specific cross-references to Coyotero burial: "Coyotero Apaches" (024-220), "Coyotero Apaches, inhumation of" (024-152), and an additional entry at 024-220.
