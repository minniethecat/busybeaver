# Congaree and Santee Indians

## Overview

The Congaree and Santee Indians inhabited the river valleys of present-day [[carolina|South Carolina]], along the watercourses that bear their names — the Congaree River and the [[santee|Santee]] River. Yarrow (1880) recorded their distinctive mortuary practice through the observations of [[lawson-john|John Lawson]], an early eighteenth-century explorer and naturalist whose *A New Voyage to [[carolina|Carolina]]* (1709) provided one of the first systematic ethnographic accounts of the Native peoples of the [[carolina|Carolina]] colony. The Congaree and [[santee|Santee]] were noted for a mortuary complex that combined partial embalmment of the dead with an unusual mode of above-ground [[inhumation|interment]] in boxes supported by forked posts.

## Mortuary Customs

### Partial Embalmment

The Congaree and Santee Indians practiced a process of partial embalmment, a technique that set them apart from most Eastern Woodlands tribes who practiced simple inhumation or scaffold exposure. According to the account preserved by [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]] and cited in Yarrow:

> The Congaree or Santee Indians of South Carolina, according to Lawson, used a process of partial embalmment, as will be seen from the subjoined extract from Schoolcraft; but instead of laying away the remains in caves, placed them in boxes supported above the ground by crotched sticks (006-014).

The technique of partial embalmment involved the removal of the viscera and the application of preservative substances to the external tissues — a practice more commonly associated with the cultures of the Pacific coast and the Gulf of Mexico than with the Southeastern interior. Yarrow's index provides two further references to this distinctive custom: "Congaree and Santee Indians, partial embalmment of" (024-149) and "embalmment, Congaree and Santee Indians" (024-323), confirming that this regional variant of embalmment was considered a significant datum in the comparative framework of the 1880 survey.

### Above-Ground Box Burial

After the partial embalmment was completed, the Congaree and [[santee|Santee]] placed the prepared remains in boxes that were elevated above the ground on crotched sticks — forked poles driven into the earth — rather than deposited in subterranean graves. This mode of disposal represents a hybrid between scaffold burial (in which the body is exposed on an elevated platform) and the [[ossuary|charnel-house]] [[ossuary]] traditions of the Southeast. The box provided protection from scavengers while the elevation kept the remains above the damp soil, which may have facilitated the preservation process begun by the embalmment.

## Historical Sources

The principal source for Congaree and [[santee|Santee]] burial customs is [[lawson-john|John Lawson]], whose *A New Voyage to [[carolina|Carolina]]* documented the indigenous peoples of the [[carolina|Carolina]] colony during the early years of the eighteenth century, prior to their substantial demographic decline from introduced disease and colonial conflict. Lawson's account was transmitted to Yarrow through [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]]'s *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1854, Part IV, p. 155 et seq.), the monumental six-volume compilation that served as the foundation for much of nineteenth-century American ethnography.
