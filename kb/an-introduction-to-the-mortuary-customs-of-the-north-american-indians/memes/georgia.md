## Overview

Georgia, on the southeastern coast of the United States, appears in Yarrow's 1880 survey through archaeological accounts of aboriginal burial urns discovered on its coastal islands. The state's coastal and inland regions preserve evidence of several distinct mortuary traditions.

## Burial Sites & Practices

St. Catherine's Island, on the coast of Georgia, yielded significant archaeological evidence of aboriginal burial practices. Professor Swallow reported the discovery of burial urns "on St. Catherine's Island, on the coast of Georgia," describing ceramic vessels containing cremated human remains and associated grave goods (007-002). These coastal Georgia burial urns represent a mortuary tradition shared with other southeastern coastal regions, where sand and shell middens frequently contain [[urn-burial|pottery burial]] vessels.

Yarrow's index of burial types includes specific entries for "Georgia" and "Georgia burial urns," indicating the state was recognized as a distinct archaeological province for urn-burial practices (024-101, 024-191). The [[urn-burial|urn burial]] tradition of coastal Georgia shares affinities with similar practices documented in [[florida|Florida]] and the [[carolina|Carolinas]], suggesting a broader southeastern coastal mortuary complex.
