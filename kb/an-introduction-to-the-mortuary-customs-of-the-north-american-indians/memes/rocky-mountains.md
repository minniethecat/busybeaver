## Overview

The Rocky Mountains, the principal mountain range of western North America, formed a major geographical and cultural boundary for aboriginal mortuary practices documented in Yarrow's 1880 survey. The tribes inhabiting the mountain slopes and intermontane valleys practiced distinctive forms of aerial and scaffold burial.

## Burial Sites & Practices

The tribes "living in the Rocky Mountains and the Sierra Nevadas" practiced mortuary customs adapted to the mountainous terrain (009-001). Scaffold burial and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]] were the predominant methods, with the dead placed on elevated platforms or in the branches of trees to protect remains from scavengers while facilitating the natural process of decomposition.

On the "western slope of the Rocky Mountains," Yarrow notes that "although we have undoubted evidence" of burial practices, the remoteness of the region meant fewer detailed eyewitness accounts compared to the Plains or Pacific Coast (010-001). One notable account describes an Indian burial "on this side of the Rocky Mountains" where "the body had been elaborately" prepared — wrapped in multiple layers of hide and cloth, adorned with personal ornaments, and placed in a carefully constructed scaffold (014-017).
