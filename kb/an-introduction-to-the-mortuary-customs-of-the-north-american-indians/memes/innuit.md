# Innuit

## Overview

The Innuit — the continental Eskimo peoples of [[alaska|Alaska]] — practiced a distinctive form of cave burial documented in Yarrow (1880) through the archaeological investigations of [[dall-wh|W. H. Dall]], whose work in the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands and adjacent [[alaska|Alaska]]n territories uncovered burial customs of considerable antiquity. [[dall-wh|Dall]]'s excavations at Amaknak Cave and Adakh revealed a burial tradition in which the deceased was tightly bound, placed in a rectangular sarcophagus constructed from whalebone, and deposited in caves — a practice that [[dall-wh|Dall]] explicitly distinguished from the later [[aleut|Aleut]] burial fashions.

## Mortuary Customs

### Whalebone Sarcophagus Burial

At Amaknak Cave, [[dall-wh|Dall]] discovered burial enclosures constructed from "the very much decayed supra-maxillary bones of some large cetacean." These whalebones were arranged to form a rectangular sarcophagus measuring somewhat less than four feet long, two feet wide, and eighteen inches deep, with a floor of flat stone slabs. Three such enclosures were found close together, each containing a skeleton in the last stages of decay (005-012).

[[dall-wh|Dall]] observed that the body "had evidently been tied up in the Innuit fashion to get it into its narrow house" — a compact, flexed position achieved by binding the limbs tightly to the torso. This binding practice was "precisely similar to that adopted by most of the continental Innuit, but equally different from the modern [[aleut|Aleut]] fashion," indicating a distinct Innuit mortuary tradition of considerable time depth (005-012).

At Adakh, [[dall-wh|Dall]] excavated a small knoll near an ancient shell-heap and discovered a skeleton in a precisely similar whalebone sarcophagus, this one filled with a dense peaty mass — the result of "centuries of sphagnous growth, which had reached a thickness of nearly 2 feet above the remains." [[dall-wh|Dall]] noted that "when we reflect upon the well-known slowness of this kind of growth in these northern regions, attested by numerous Arctic travelers, the antiquity of the remains becomes evident" (005-012).

### Whaling Class Preservations

[[dall-wh|Dall]] distinguished the general Innuit burial custom from a specialized practice associated with the whaling class among the Kadiak Innuit. The whalers preserved only the bodies of males, using "stone weapons and actual utensils instead of effigies, and with the meanest apparel, and no carvings of consequence" — a markedly different treatment from the honored burials that included women as well as men and were accompanied by elaborate grave goods (006-039).

## Historical Sources

The Innuit burial customs in Yarrow derive entirely from [[dall-wh|W. H. Dall]]'s *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1877), which reported the results of his [[alaska|Alaska]]n archaeological investigations. [[dall-wh|Dall]]'s careful distinction between Innuit and [[aleut|Aleut]] burial practices, his attention to the construction details of the whalebone sarcophagi, and his use of sphagnum growth rates to estimate the antiquity of the remains make his account one of the more archaeologically sophisticated contributions to the Yarrow monograph.
