# Stone Graves

## Overview

Stone graves, also referred to as cist graves or stone cists, constituted a distinctive mode of inhumation practiced extensively in the eastern United States, particularly in [[tennessee|Tennessee]] and the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]]. Unlike simple earth burials, stone graves featured carefully constructed chambers lined with flat stone slabs forming the bottom, sides, and covering of the burial receptacle. Yarrow (1880) classified this method under inhumation, noting that it represented a significant investment of labor by the survivors and testified to "the manifest care taken by the survivors to provide for the dead what they considered a suitable resting-place" (003-001). The practice was associated primarily with the mound-building cultures of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] drainage system, where thousands of such graves have been documented.

## Construction and Description

The earliest detailed account of stone grave construction was provided by [[fiske-moses|Moses Fiske]], published in the *Transactions of the American Antiquarian Society* (1820). Fiske described how the graves were formed by setting stone slabs on edge to create the walls of a rectangular chamber, covering the bottom with flat stones, placing the body within, and then capping the structure with additional stone slabs serving as a roof. The entire construction was then covered with earth, forming either an individual grave or contributing to a larger mound structure (003-001).

[[powell-jw|Powell]] noted that in the crowded cemeteries of [[tennessee|Tennessee]], the builders sometimes economized on materials: "Sometimes, in the subsequent burials, the side slab of a previous burial was used as a portion of the second cist. All of the cists were covered with slabs" (003-006). This detail reveals both the practical resourcefulness of the grave builders and the communal, accretive nature of the mound cemeteries, which grew over long periods as new graves were added around and above earlier [[inhumation|interment]]s.

## Geographic Distribution

### Tennessee and the Cumberland Valley

Stone graves were most abundantly concentrated in [[tennessee|Tennessee]], where, according to [[powell-jw|Maj. J. W. Powell]], "these ancient cemeteries are exceedingly abundant throughout the State, often hundreds of graves may be found on a single hillside." [[powell-jw|Powell]] observed that in some locations the graves were scattered, while in others they were collected in mounds, "each mound being composed of a large number of cist graves." He noted a characteristic concentric arrangement in the earliest burials at the center of mounds, with the feet oriented inward, and increasingly irregular patterns in subsequent additions as the mound grew outward and upward (003-004).

### The Ohio Valley

[[putnam-fw|Prof. F. W. Putnam]], curator of the Peabody Museum of Archaeology at Cambridge, conducted extensive explorations of stone grave sites in the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]] over a period of two years, assisted by Mr. [[curtiss-edwin|Edwin Curtiss]]. During this investigation, "several thousand of the singular stone graves of the mound builders of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] had been carefully opened," yielding hundreds of artifacts including pottery vessels, chipped flint implements, and shell beads. [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] documented a walled town site on the Lindsley estate in a bend of Spring [[creek|Creek]], where a burial mound approximately fifty feet in diameter contained sixty human skeletons, each interred in a carefully constructed stone grave arranged in two rows forming the four sides of a square, stacked in three layers (004-009).

## Archaeological Accounts

### Moses Fiske (1820)

Fiske, writing in the *Transactions of the American Antiquarian Society*, provided the foundational description of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] cist graves, documenting the stone-slab construction technique and the careful arrangement of the dead within their stone chambers. His account established the typology that later archaeologists would elaborate upon (003-001).

### J. W. Powell in Tennessee

Maj. [[powell-jw|Powell]]'s observations, made during his survey work in [[tennessee|Tennessee]], provided the most systematic description of the distribution and arrangement of stone grave cemeteries. He emphasized the gradual, long-term accumulation of graves within mounds and the increasingly irregular burial patterns as cemeteries expanded. [[powell-jw|Powell]] also noted the absence of European trade goods in the deepest and most centrally located graves, suggesting considerable antiquity for the earliest [[inhumation|interment]]s (003-004).

### Putnam's Ohio Valley Excavations (1878)

Prof. [[putnam-fw|Putnam]]'s excavations, reported to the Boston Society of Natural History in October 1878, yielded the most extensive archaeological data on stone graves. Among the most significant discoveries was the practice of burying children beneath the clay floors of houses rather than in the communal burial mound: "As only the bodies of adults had been placed in the one mound devoted to burial, and as nearly every site of a house he explored had from one to four graves of children under the clay floor, he was convinced that it was a regular custom to bury the children in that way." [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] noted that the children's graves contained many of the finest pottery pieces, shell beads, and pearls recovered from the site — objects he believed "were probably the playthings of the little ones while living" (004-009).

## Comparative Evidence

### France: Reindeer Period Parallels

Yarrow drew a notable cross-cultural parallel between the [[tennessee|Tennessee]] cist graves and prehistoric burials of the reindeer period near Solutre, France, which he personally assisted in excavating in 1873. He observed that the French graves "were almost identical in construction with those described by Mr. Fiske, with the exception that the latter were deeper." Yarrow attributed this difference to the greater accumulation of earth over the centuries since the [[tennessee|Tennessee]] burials were made (003-003)(024-365).

### Santa Barbara, California

During excavations at Santa Barbara in 1875, Yarrow encountered burials that "resembled somewhat cist graves, the bottom and sides of the pit being lined with large flat stones, but there were none directly over the skeletons." This variant, in which the stone lining was partial rather than fully enclosing the body, represented a regional adaptation of the cist burial concept in a context far removed from the mound-building cultures of the East (003-003).

## Cultural Significance

The construction of stone graves reflected a significant cultural investment in mortuary practice. The labor required to quarry, transport, and position stone slabs — often weighing tens of pounds each — indicates that these were not expedient burials but deliberately constructed [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]] intended to endure. The inclusion of pottery, implements, shell beads, and pearls as grave goods, particularly in the burials of children, suggests belief in an [[spirit-land|afterlife]] in which the deceased would have use for these objects.

The concentric arrangement of graves within mounds, with the earliest burials at the center, implies a hierarchical or genealogical organization of burial space — possibly reflecting clan or lineage affiliations that persisted across generations. Dr. Joseph [[jones|Jones]], in his Smithsonian Institution monograph *Antiquities of [[tennessee|Tennessee]]* (1876), provided the most detailed study of these burial patterns, which Yarrow recommended to readers seeking a more exhaustive account (003-007).
