# Makah

## Overview

The Makah people inhabit the extreme northwestern tip of the Olympic Peninsula at Cape Flattery, Washington — the northwesternmost point of the contiguous United States. As a maritime people whose livelihood depended on whaling, fishing, and sea-mammal hunting, the Makah developed mortuary customs that reflected their intimate relationship with the coastal environment. Yarrow (1880) documented their burial practices as part of a broader survey of Puget Sound and coastal Washington funerary traditions, noting the co-existence of multiple burial modes within a single cultural group.

## Mortuary Customs

### Box-Covered Graves

The most distinctive Makah burial form was the box-covered grave. Yarrow records that among the Makah of Cape Flattery:

> The graves are covered with a sort of box, rudely constructed of boards (016-009).

This practice involved constructing a wooden box-like covering over the grave site, providing a protective enclosure that distinguished Makah burials from the simpler earth [[inhumation|interment]]s found elsewhere in the region. The reference to "Makah burial boxes" in Yarrow's index (024-261) confirms that this practice was considered a defining trait of Makah mortuary custom.

### Regional Variation on Puget Sound

Yarrow's account emphasizes that on Puget Sound, "all the forms obtain in different localities" (016-009). The Makah practiced box-covered graves, while elsewhere on the Sound the same box method might be adopted in some cases, while in other locations bodies were placed on elevated scaffolds. As a general principle, Indians upon the water — those living directly on the coastline — placed their dead in [[canoe-burial|canoes]], while those at a distance from the shore buried them in the earth.

### Canoe Burial

Like other coastal tribes of the Pacific Northwest, the Makah practiced canoe burial for certain individuals, particularly those of high status. The canoe — the central technology of Makah life, essential for whaling and seaborne subsistence — was transformed in death into a vessel for the deceased, placed in trees, on scaffolds, or left on isolated points of land overlooking the sea.

### Grave Goods and Surroundings

Yarrow notes that most Makah graves were "surrounded with strips of cloth, blankets, and other articles of property" (016-009), indicating the persistence of the practice of placing grave goods with the deceased. These offerings — textiles, blankets, and personal possessions — marked the grave site within the landscape and signaled the continuing social identity of the deceased within the community.

## Historical Sources

Yarrow's description of Makah burial customs draws on observations from the 1870s by multiple correspondents familiar with the Washington Territory coast. The integration of Makah practices into the broader Puget Sound survey allowed Yarrow to position this relatively small tribal group within a regional pattern while still recognizing the distinctive features — particularly the box-covered graves — that set Makah burial apart.
