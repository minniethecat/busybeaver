# Choctaw

## Overview

The Choctaw people, originally inhabiting the region of present-day [[mississippi|Mississippi]], Alabama, and Louisiana, were among the most populous and politically sophisticated tribes of the American Southeast. By the time of Yarrow's 1880 monograph, the Choctaw had already endured the trauma of the [[indian-removal|Indian Removal]] of the 1830s, which relocated the majority of the tribe to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma). Despite this disruption, or perhaps because of the urgency it created for ethnographic documentation, the Choctaw received extensive attention from Yarrow's correspondents, who preserved detailed records of their distinctive mortuary practices.

## Mortuary Customs

### The Bone-Picking Ceremony

The most distinctive Choctaw mortuary practice was the "bone-picking" ceremony, a secondary burial ritual performed by designated ritual specialists. After initial scaffold exposure or temporary earth burial — during which the body decomposed naturally — the bone-pickers, individuals of a special social caste, would gather the skeletal remains and clean them meticulously. Yarrow cites multiple accounts of this practice, which was "spoken of" alongside the customs of the [[creek|Creeks]] and [[cherokee|Cherokees]] as sharing common elements (013-002).

### Ossuary Burial

Following the bone-picking ceremony, the cleaned remains were deposited in a communal [[ossuary]] or [[bone-house]]. Yarrow's index contains multiple entries for this practice: "Choctaw bone houses" and the "[[ossuary]] of Choctaws" (024-138)(024-318). These structures served as repositories for the accumulated remains of the community, representing a form of collective ancestor veneration.

### Scaffold Exposure and Box Burial

Yarrow (1880) documents that the Choctaws practiced "burial in boxes of" a particular design (024-139). The initial disposition of the body often involved placement on an elevated scaffold or platform, where it remained until decomposition was sufficiently advanced for the bone-picking ritual to be performed.

[[bartram-william|Bartram]], the celebrated naturalist and traveler, provided one of the earliest and most detailed accounts:

> Writing of the Choctaws, Bartram describes the process of scaffold exposure followed by the bone-picking ceremony, after which the cleaned bones were placed in a specially constructed ossuary (004-031).

### Carolina Choctaws

A distinct branch of the Choctaw people residing in the [[carolina|Carolina]] region practiced burial customs that blended Southeastern traditions with local variations. Yarrow notes that [[roman|Roman]]s provided an account "of burial among the Choctaws of [[carolina|Carolina]]," suggesting regional diversity within the broader Choctaw cultural sphere (015-007).

One contemporary observer recorded:

> The Choctaws pay their last duties and respect to the deceased with great solemnity, the ceremony extending over several days and involving the entire community in acts of ritual mourning and feasting (015-008).

## Related Tribes

The Choctaw shared many mortuary practices with their Muskogean neighbors and Southeastern cultural relatives. The [[creek|Creek]] and [[cherokee|Cherokee]] practiced similar forms of scaffold burial and secondary bone deposition (013-002). The [[chickasaw|Chickasaw]], linguistically and culturally the closest relatives of the Choctaw, likely observed nearly identical customs, though Yarrow's sources provide less detail for this group.

The bone-picking ceremony represented a distinctly Southeastern pattern that contrasted sharply with the [[cremation]] practices of [[california|California]] tribes, the tree and scaffold burials of the Plains, and the canoe burials of the Pacific Northwest.
