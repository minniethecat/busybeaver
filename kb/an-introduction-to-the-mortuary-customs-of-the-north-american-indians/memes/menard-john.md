# John Menard

## Overview

Dr. John Menard served as physician to the [[navajo|Navajo]] Agency in [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] and [[arizona|Arizona]] during the nineteenth century. His clinical posting placed him in sustained contact with [[navajo|Navajo]] communities, enabling him to observe and document their mortuary practices with a level of detail uncommon in the reports of transient military or exploratory observers. Yarrow incorporated Menard's account into the 1880 monograph as a key primary source on the burial customs of the [[navajo|Navajos]], whose practices he noted shared certain features with those of the geographically distant [[chickasaw|Chickasaws]] (002-005).

## Contributions

Menard's description of [[navajo|Navajo]] mortuary practice, as transmitted by Yarrow, records a suite of distinctive customs centered on the avoidance of corpse contact and the abandonment of places associated with death. According to Menard, the conventional [[navajo|Navajo]] practice was to leave the body where death occurred, either closing up the dwelling (hogan) or covering the corpse with stones or brush. When a body was moved, it was taken to a cleft in the rocks, thrown in, and buried under a cairn of stones. Any person who touched or carried the body was required first to remove all clothing and then to wash thoroughly before dressing again or rejoining the living (002-005).

Menard further reported that the hogan in which death had occurred was invariably burned down and the site permanently abandoned, as the [[navajo|Navajo]] believed that a malevolent spirit came to and remained at the place of death. He observed that wild animals frequently accessed the exposed bodies, making it common to find scattered skulls and bones around old camping grounds. In cases where abandoning a dwelling was undesirable — for instance, during winter encampments — the moribund person was moved to an isolated spot and sheltered with brush. Menard recorded one striking instance of a person so left who revived after being given coffee from his own household and was, at the time of his writing, still alive and well (002-005).

## Accounts Provided

Menard's communication to Yarrow provides a first-hand description of the complete [[navajo|Navajo]] mortuary sequence: in-situ abandonment or rock-crevice deposition, post-contact purification rituals for corpse-handlers, the [[cremation|burning]] and permanent abandonment of death-sites, the indirect disposal of the dying through temporary brush shelters, and the frequent scattering of skeletal remains by wild animals.
