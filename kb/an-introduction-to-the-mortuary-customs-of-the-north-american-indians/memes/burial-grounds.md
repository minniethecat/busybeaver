# Burial Grounds

## Overview

Burial grounds among North American Indian tribes took diverse forms, from expansive mound complexes to communal sepulchers and marked grave sites. Yarrow (1880) documented numerous burial places through the accounts of explorers, military officers, and archaeologists who investigated these sites in the nineteenth century, revealing a landscape rich with mortuary architecture and ritual geography.

## Mound Burial Complexes

The most prominent burial grounds in eastern North America were the large earthen mounds that served as communal cemeteries. These structures, built over generations, held the remains of hundreds or even thousands of individuals and represented one of the most significant forms of monumental architecture in pre-contact North America.

### Cumberland Valley Mounds

In the Cumberland Valley of [[tennessee|Tennessee]], F. W. Putnam explored ancient mounds and burial places that contained a wealth of archaeological material. The scale of these structures was remarkable: one tumulus, rising more than ninety feet and standing on an artificial hill, contained an immense number of human skeletons.

> One tumulus, more than 90 feet in height and standing on an artificial hill, must have been the common cemetery, as it contains an immense number of human skeletons. (004-021)

These mounds served not merely as disposal sites but as enduring monuments that marked the landscape and maintained a visible connection between the living community and its ancestors.

### Little Tennessee River Burials

Along the Little Tennessee River, early nineteenth-century farmers made a striking discovery while plowing their fields. A certain portion of the field produced a hollow, rumbling sound under the plowshare, prompting investigation that revealed large subterranean burial chambers.

> The plowshare, in passing over a certain portion of the field, produced a hollow rumbling sound. Upon investigation, large burial chambers were discovered, containing multiple skeletons interred in a sitting posture. (011-002)

The sitting posture of the interred skeletons is characteristic of the Stone Grave people of Tennessee, whose distinctive burial practices distinguish them from neighboring cultural groups.

## Ossuary and Communal Burial

One of the most widespread and distinctive burial customs east of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] involved periodic communal reburial. At intervals of eight to ten years, tribes would gather the remains of all who had died during the intervening period, clean the bones, and deposit them in a common sepulcher.

> Every eight or ten years, the tribes would collect and clean the osseous remains of those of its number who had died in the intervening time, and inter them in one common sepulcher, lined with choice furs, and marked with a mound of wood, stone, or earth. (015-013)

This practice transformed individual grave sites into collective sacred spaces, reinforcing tribal identity and creating physical monuments that endured across generations. The Huron Feast of the Dead, described in detail by early French missionaries, represents the most famous documented example of this widespread tradition.

## Pacific Northwest Burial Places

### Columbia River Sepulchers

The [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] region featured distinctive burial places that differed markedly from the mound traditions of the East. Captain Belcher of the British ship *Sulphur*, visiting in 1839, encountered an island so densely populated with burial structures that he named it Sepulcher Island.

> Captain Belcher, in 1839, gave an island in the Columbia River the name of Sepulcher Island, on account of the many burial places of the Watlala, a tribe of the Upper Tsinuk, which he found there. (016-008)

### Penn Cove Burial Structures

At Penn Cove, Mr. Whidbey of Vancouver's expedition documented several distinctive sepulchers — structures formed exactly like sentry-boxes, some containing the skeletons of young children tied up in baskets. A separate cemetery area revealed multiple disposal methods for children, including suspension from trees and placement within hollow trunks.

> At Penn Cove, Mr. Whidbey observed several sepulchers formed exactly like a sentry-box. Some contained the skeletons of young children tied up in baskets. (016-012)

> The corpses of children were variously disposed of; sometimes by suspending them, at others by placing in the hollows of trees. (016-013)

The diversity of burial forms within a single location — sentry-box structures, suspended remains, and tree-hollow placements — illustrates how a single community might employ multiple burial methods depending on the age and status of the deceased.

## Stone-Covered Graves

In [[north-carolina|North Carolina]], an unusual regional burial tradition involved covering the deceased with a coating of plastic clay that hardened into a durable encasement. Documented by Mr. Wilcox, these stone-covered graves featured bodies placed face-up beneath their clay shells.

> Mr. Wilcox documented an unusual method of burial by an ancient race of Indians. The bodies were placed face-up and covered with a coating of plastic clay, which formed a hard, durable covering. (011-008)

This practice represents a distinctive regional variant — neither fully inhumation nor stone-box burial, but rather a unique synthesis of materials and techniques adapted to local conditions.

## Significance

Burial grounds were more than mere repositories for the dead. They were sacred landscapes that mapped tribal territory, recorded genealogical relationships, and served as focal points for periodic ceremonies. The diversity of burial ground forms across North America — from the monumental mounds of the Southeast to the sentry-box sepulchers of the Northwest Coast — reflects the equally diverse cosmologies and social structures of the peoples who built them.
