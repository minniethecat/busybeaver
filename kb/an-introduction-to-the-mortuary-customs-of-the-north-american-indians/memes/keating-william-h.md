# William H. Keating

## Overview

William Hypolitus Keating (1799–1840) was an American geologist and naturalist who served as the chronicler of Major Stephen H. Long's expedition to the St. Peter's (Minnesota) River in 1823. His published narrative of the expedition (1824) contained some of the earliest detailed descriptions of [[sioux|Sioux]] scaffold burial and [[chippewa|Chippewa]] ([[chippewa|Ojibwe]]) [[soul-beliefs]] — ethnological observations that, though incidental to the expedition's primary geological mission, were valued by Yarrow for their precision and for their status as early nineteenth-century eyewitness records predating the more systematic ethnological work of the Bureau of American Ethnology era (014-007)(021-032).

## Contributions

Keating provided Yarrow with a notably precise description of [[dakota|Dakota]] scaffold burial. He recorded that scaffolds were constructed eight to ten feet high, with the corpse deposited in a box made from a portion of a broken canoe. Locks of hair, torn from their heads by relatives to testify their grief, were suspended from the scaffold. In the center, between the four supporting posts, a stake about six feet high was planted, bearing carved human figures: five with petticoat designs indicating females, and seven naked male figures, of which four were headless — representing warriors who had been slain — while three held staffs designating slaves. Keating noted that this post did not represent the achievements of the deceased but rather those of the warriors who gathered near the remains to dance the dance of the post and relate their martial exploits. He further recorded that the boxes were too short for a body to lie extended, and that after a period of exposure the corpses were taken down and buried (014-007).

Keating's narrative also preserved a remarkable firsthand observation related to him by his guide, Renville: an Indian who had traveled up the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] to retrieve his son's remains found the body already decomposing, and undertook with friends to clean the bones — scraping the flesh into the stream, collecting the bones into his canoe, and conveying them downriver for proper burial. This account is among the earliest recorded instances of secondary burial practice on the Upper [[mississippi|Mississippi]], bridging the scaffold-exposure and final-inhumation stages of the mortuary sequence (014-007).

Keating further contributed a valuable account of [[chippewa|Chippewa]] eschatology, recording the belief in the *Ochechag* — an essence distinct from the body, akin to the soul — which departed at death for *Chekechekchekawe*, a region situated southward on the shores of the great ocean. Before arrival, souls were obliged to cross a stream on a great serpent that served as a bridge; souls of those who drowned were thrown into the stream and remained there forever, while souls of persons in lethargy or trance were refused passage and returned to reanimate their bodies. Keating recorded the [[chippewa|Chippewa]] belief that animals and even inorganic substances — kettles and the like — possessed a similar animating essence. In the land of souls, good persons spent their time dancing, singing, and feeding upon abundant mushrooms, while the wicked were haunted by the phantoms of all they had injured (021-032).

## Accounts Provided

Keating's published narrative (*Narrative of an Expedition to the Source of St. Peter's River*, 1824, vol. i, p. 392, and vol. ii, p. 158) covers [[dakota|Dakota]] scaffold burial, the Renville [[secondary-burial]] episode, and [[chippewa|Chippewa]] soul-journey and serpent-bridge eschatology. As an observer trained in the natural sciences rather than in ethnology, Keating's accounts are valuably free of speculative cultural theorizing and are restricted to what he directly observed or was told by his guides.

## Reception and Scholarly Assessment

Keating's narrative of the Long Expedition occupies a chronologically privileged position within the Yarrow corpus. Published in 1824, it predates not only the founding of the Bureau of American Ethnology (1879) but the entire development of professional American ethnology as a distinct discipline. Yarrow's decision to incorporate Keating's observations alongside those of Smithsonian field agents and Army medical officers reflects the monograph's inclusive approach to source authority — an approach that valued specificity of observation over institutional affiliation (014-007).

The precision of Keating's [[sioux|Sioux]] scaffold description — recording the dimensions of the structure, the material of the burial box, the arrangement of the commemorative post, and the iconographic program of its carved figures — is remarkable for a geologist whose expedition was tasked primarily with mapping and mineral surveys. The detail that four of the seven male figures on the commemorative post were headless, representing slain warriors, while three held staffs indicating slaves, provides a glimpse of the social hierarchies encoded in [[sioux|Dakota]] mortuary display. Keating's willingness to record these distinctions without attempting to reconcile them into a unified cultural interpretation exemplifies the descriptive restraint characteristic of early nineteenth-century scientific travelogues.

His account of [[chippewa|Chippewa]] eschatology, transmitted through the guide Renville, is among the earliest recorded versions of the soul-journey narrative that would later be documented in greater detail by [[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]], [[jenks-albert-ernest|Jenks]], and other Bureau of American Ethnology researchers. The serpent-bridge motif — in which souls must cross a great serpent spanning a stream to reach the land of the dead, with the unworthy being thrown into the water — recurs across Algonquian-speaking peoples from the Great Lakes to the Atlantic seaboard. Keating's 1823 transcription of this motif, coming before the extensive missionary and reservation-era documentation that would follow, preserves a version of the narrative relatively unmediated by Christian eschatological concepts.
