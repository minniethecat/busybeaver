## Overview

Mourning mutilation refers to the deliberate infliction of bodily injury upon oneself as a ritual expression of grief following a death. Among the North American tribes surveyed by Yarrow (1880), practices ranged from cutting off finger joints to slashing the body, cropping hair, and gashing the face and limbs. These acts served as visible, often permanent, markers of bereavement (P01-022).

## Tribal Distribution

**[[california|California]] Tribes** — Among several [[california|California]] groups, "mutilations were ghastly," with mourners cutting and gashing themselves in displays of grief that shocked outside observers (019-003). The extent of self-wounding varied by the status of the deceased and the closeness of kinship.

**Crow** — Yarrow recorded a remarkable assembly of over ten thousand [[crows|Crows]] gathering at the Rose Bud for a period of general [[mourning]] following the death of a head chief. The scene was described as "such a scene of disorderly vociferous [[mourning]] no imagination can conceive nor any pen portray." Long Hair, a prominent leader, "cut off a large roll of his hair, a thing he was never known to do before" (019-003). This account illustrates how [[mourning]] mutilation could be performed even by those for whom it represented an exceptional departure from normal practice.

**Northwestern Tribes** — Mutilation was "frequently practiced as a mode of [[mourning]]," including "wild scenes of maiming" that accompanied funeral rites (P01-022). The cutting off of finger joints was particularly common, with some individuals losing multiple joints over a lifetime of [[mourning]] successive relatives.

**Plains Tribes** — Yarrow personally observed and "reasoned with" practitioners of [[mourning]] mutilation among Plains tribes. He was "pained by often coming on skeletons in trees and the stench of half-consumed remains in the brush, and shocked by the frequent mutilations visible" (014-006). Mutilation of the person persisted "to this day among some tribes" as a visible mark, practiced particularly by members of "an inferior clan" or as an enduring [[mourning]] custom (014-022).

**[[pima|Pima]]** — Among the [[pima|Pima]], the nearest relatives of the deceased remained in the village for weeks or sometimes months as a sign of their sorrow. "The men cut off about six inches of their long hair, while the women cut their hair quite short" (001-025). This differential by gender — men removing a measured length, women cropping close — was a common pattern across multiple tribes.

## Variations

The specific forms of [[mourning]] mutilation included:

- **Finger amputation** — Removal of one or more finger joints, most commonly among Northwestern and Plains tribes (P01-022)
- **Hair cutting** — Among the [[pima|Pima]] and others, relatives cut their hair short, with men removing "about six inches" and women cutting "quite short" (001-025)
- **Face and body laceration** — Gashing of the face, arms, and torso with sharp implements, leaving permanent scars. The women relatives of the deceased would get "on some elevated spot near where the body rests, and keeping up a dismal wail, frequently even in extreme cold weather, the greater part of the night, and this is kept up often for a month" (014-006)
- **Flesh cutting** — Among the [[sioux|Sioux]] and other Plains tribes, slashing the arms and legs to draw blood as a visible sign of grief

The Chiricahua and Mescalero [[apache|Apache]] shaved the hair and bathed the body at the end of the [[mourning]] period to signify the conclusion of formal grief (022-006).
