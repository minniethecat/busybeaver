# Aquatic Burial

## Overview

Aquatic burial — the disposal of the dead by submersion in water — occupies a marginal but intriguing position in the mortuary customs of North America. Yarrow (1880) opened his chapter on the subject with the unequivocal observation that "as a confirmed rite or ceremony, this mode of disposing of the dead has never been followed by any of our North American Indians" (017-001). Nevertheless, he documented scattered instances in which bodies were sunk in springs, thrown into rivers, cast into the sea, or set adrift in canoes — practices that were largely expedient, incidental, or confined to the disposal of slaves and enemies rather than constituting systematic mortuary ritual.

## Historical and Comparative Context

Yarrow situated North American aquatic burial within a broader cross-cultural framework, noting that among the nations of antiquity the practice was far from uncommon. The Ichthyophagi, or "fish-eaters" mentioned by Ptolemy, who inhabited a region bordering the Persian Gulf, "invariably committed their dead to the sea, thus repaying the obligations they had incurred to its inhabitants." The Lotophagians followed the same custom, and the Hyperboreans, in what Yarrow described as an act of "commendable forethought for the survivors," voluntarily threw themselves into the sea when ill or approaching death (017-001).

The most celebrated example from Northern European tradition was the burial of Baldor "the beautiful," whose body was placed in a highly decorated ship, pushed down to the sea, set ablaze, and committed to the waves — a scene familiar from Norse mythology. In the Americas, [[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]] reported that the [[itzas|Itzas]] of Guatemala, dwelling on the islands of Lake Peter, "threw their dead into the lake for want of room" on their confined island territory (017-001).

## North American Instances

### Gosh-Utes of Utah — Spring Burial

The most thoroughly authenticated North American instance of aquatic burial comes from Capt. [[simpson-jh|J. H. Simpson]]'s exploration of the [[utah|Great Salt Lake Valley]] in 1859. [[simpson-jh|Simpson]]'s guide, [[catlin-george|George]] [[gardner-w|W.]] Bean, reported that Skull Valley — a part of the Great Salt Lake Desert — derived its name "from the number of skulls which have been found in it, and which have arisen from the custom of the [[gosh-ute|Goshute]] Indians burying their dead in springs, which they sink with stones or keep down with sticks." Bean testified that he had personally witnessed this practice near the town of Provo, [[utah|Utah]] (017-002).

As corroboration, [[simpson-jh|Simpson]] recorded that upon arriving at a spring one evening during the expedition, his party "were obliged to dig out the skeleton of an Indian from the mud at the bottom before using the water" (017-003). Yarrow noted the peculiarity of this practice given the scarcity of water in the region and the general care Indians exercised to avoid polluting springs and streams near their dwelling places, concluding that "conjecture seems useless to establish a reason for this disposition of the dead" (017-004).

### Chinook of the Columbia River — Infant Canoe Burial

The second documented instance, recorded by the artist and ethnographer [[catlin-george|George Catlin]] in his *History of the North American Indians* (1844), concerned the [[chinook|Chinook]] people of the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]]. [[catlin-george|Catlin]] described the practice of placing deceased infants in their cradle-boards, which then served as miniature canoes:

"The little cradle has a strap which passes over the woman's forehead whilst the cradle rides on her back, and if the child dies during its subjection to this rigid mode its cradle becomes its coffin, forming a little canoe, in which it lie floating on the water in some sacred pool." [[catlin-george|Catlin]] added that the [[chinook|Chinook]] were "often in the habit of fastening their canoes containing the dead bodies of the old and young" in such pools, provisioning them with paddles, ladles, provisions, and pipes for the long journey to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] (017-005).

### Cherokee River Disposal

According to Timberlake, cited by Yarrow, the [[cherokee|Cherokees]] of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] "seldom bury the dead, but threw them into the river" (017-001). This practice, however, may have been a misinterpretation by early observers, as the [[cherokee|Cherokees]] are known from other sources to have practiced a range of burial methods including scaffold exposure and earth inhumation.

### Nootka Sound and Chinook — Slave Disposal

The Indians of [[nootka|Nootka]] Sound on the Pacific Northwest coast, as well as the [[chinook|Chinook]], were reported to dispose of deceased slaves by throwing their bodies into the water — a practice that underscored the differential treatment of free persons and slaves in mortuary rites. This utilitarian mode of disposal for those of low social status contrasted sharply with the elaborate canoe burials and scaffold elevations reserved for free members of the tribe (017-001).

## Interpretation

The paucity of well-authenticated instances — Yarrow could locate only two after "a careful search" — suggests that true aquatic burial was never a normative mortuary practice among North American Indians. The isolated cases that do appear in the ethnographic record are better understood as occasional, circumstantial, or class-specific practices. The [[gosh-ute|Gosh-Ute]] spring burial, unique in its method, remains an ethnographic anomaly for which no satisfactory explanation has been offered.

The cultural logic of aquatic disposal differed markedly between the Old World and the New. Whereas ancient Mediterranean and Northern European peoples developed elaborate ship-burial traditions imbued with cosmological significance, North American instances appear to have been motivated by practicality (the [[itzas|Itzas]]' shortage of burial space on their island), social hierarchy (the disposal of slaves), or idiosyncratic local custom (the [[gosh-ute|Gosh-Utes]]' spring burial).
