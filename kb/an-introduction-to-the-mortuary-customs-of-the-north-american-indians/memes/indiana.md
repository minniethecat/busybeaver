## Overview

Indiana, situated between the Ohio River and Lake Michigan, was home to the Miami, Potawatomi, and other Algonquian peoples whose territories encompassed the forests and prairies of the Old Northwest. Yarrow's 1880 monograph references the state primarily through archaeological evidence of prehistoric burial practices, particularly its cave mummies and the mound-building cultures of the Ohio Valley. The caverns of Indiana, together with those of [[ohio|Ohio]] and [[kentucky|Kentucky]], formed a regional burial complex in which limestone caves impregnated with nitre (saltpeter) created natural desiccation conditions that preserved human remains without deliberate embalming (005-013).

## Burial Sites and Practices

### Cave Mummies and the Embalming Question

Yarrow records that the caverns of Indiana, together with those of [[ohio|Ohio]] and [[kentucky|Kentucky]], contained many mummified human remains. The cave environment's cool, dry, and mineral-rich atmosphere — particularly in limestone caverns impregnated with nitre (saltpeter) — created natural conditions for desiccation, arresting decomposition without the deliberate embalming processes used by the [[aleutian-islands|Aleutian Islanders]] or the ancient Egyptians. Yarrow treats these cave mummies as primary interments: "in the majority of cases, especially as regards the caves of the Western States and Territories, the interments were primary ones, and this is likewise true of many of the caverns of Ohio, Indiana, and Kentucky" (005-013). However, he acknowledged that such receptacles were also "largely used as places of secondary deposits," noting that "the many fragmentary skeletons and loose bones found seem to strengthen this view" (005-013).

An important scientific question surrounded these cave mummies: were they embalmed deliberately, or did the cave environment alone account for their preservation? Yarrow noted that "it is still a matter of doubt with archaeologists whether any special pains were taken to preserve these bodies, many believing that the impregnation of the soil with certain minerals would account for the condition in which the specimens were found" (005-013). This contrasts sharply with the deliberate Aleut embalming process using moss and grass — the Indiana-Oho-Kentucky cave mummies represent natural mummification rather than artificial preservation.

### The Kentucky Mummy: A Regional Comparison

Charles Wilkins, in his account published in the *Transactions of the American Antiquarian Society* (1820), described a cave mummy discovered in Kentucky that serves as a point of comparison for the Indiana cave remains. The specimen was found wrapped in deerskins and a coarse cloth, deposited in a sitting posture within a limestone cave. A critical observation was that "there is nothing bituminous or aromatic in or about the body, like the Egyptian mummies, nor are there bandages around any part. Except the several wrappers, the body is totally naked. There is no sign of a suture or incision about the belly whence it seems that the viscera were not removed" (005-013). This absence of evisceration — the defining feature of Egyptian mummification — confirms that the cave mummies of the Ohio Valley states, including Indiana, owed their preservation to environmental conditions rather than to any artificial embalming process comparable to that of the Old World.

Yarrow concluded the discussion of mummification with the practical advice that "particular care should be taken, in case mummies are discovered, to ascertain whether the bodies have been submitted to a regular preservative process, or owe their protection to ingredients in the soil of their graves or to desiccation in arid districts" (005-013).

### George Catlin's Indiana Accounts

The celebrated artist and ethnographer [[catlin-george|George Catlin]], whose *Manners, Customs, and Condition of the North American Indians* (1844) is extensively cited throughout Yarrow's monograph, documented the mortuary practices of Indiana tribes during his travels in the 1830s. Catlin's account includes descriptions of scaffold burial and [[mourning]] ceremonies among the indigenous peoples of the region, captured in both narrative and visual form (008-004). Catlin's work represents one of the most valuable ethnographic records of pre-removal Native American life in the Old Northwest.

### Archaeological Evidence

The Rev. Charles Jones, in his *Antiquities of the Southern Indians* (1873), drew on evidence from Indiana mounds and burial sites in his comprehensive survey of Southeastern archaeology. Jones, quoted by Yarrow, cites "one of the older writers" regarding the [[natchez|Natchez]] tribe's elaborate mortuary rituals — customs that had parallels among the mound-building cultures whose works dotted the Indiana landscape (015-005). The archaeological record of Indiana, spanning from the Adena and Hopewell traditions through the historic period, demonstrates a continuous tradition of mound and earthwork construction for funerary purposes.
