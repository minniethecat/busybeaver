# California

## Overview

California, with its extraordinary diversity of indigenous peoples and ecosystems, presented Yarrow with a corresponding diversity of mortuary customs. From the [[cremation]] practices of the [[Nishinam]] in the north to the cave burials and urn depositions of the southern tribes, California offered perhaps the widest range of burial modes of any region surveyed in the 1880 monograph (005-004)(007-006)(010-001).

## Burial Sites & Practices

### Cremation
The [[Nishinam]] and other northern California tribes practiced [[cremation]] as their primary mode of burial. Yarrow documented varying accounts of the ceremony, with correspondents differing on details of the ritual. The [[Indians of Clear Lake]] also practiced [[cremation]], and the [[achomawi|Achomawi]] of northeastern California combined burial and [[cremation]] (010-001)(010-003).

### Cave Burial
California's numerous natural caves served as burial repositories. Yarrow noted that cave burial was practiced across the state, with bodies placed in dry caves and rock shelters. The practice often resulted in natural [[mummification|desiccation]], preserving remains for study (005-004).

### Urn Deposition
In the graves of ancient California peoples, Yarrow himself found bones in ollas (earthen jars). He interpreted these as secondary [[inhumation|interment]]s — bones dug up during successive burials in long-used cemeteries and placed in pots as convenient receptacles. This practice was also observed in [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] (007-005)(007-006).

### Burial in Cabins
Some California tribes buried their dead within or adjacent to dwellings, a practice that connected the living and dead within the same domestic space (002-008).

## Associated Tribes

- [[Achomawi]] — Northeastern California, combined burial and [[cremation]]
- [[Indians of Clear Lake]] — [[cremation|Cremation]] practitioners
- [[Senel Indians]] — California tribe documented in Yarrow
