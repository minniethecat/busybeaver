# The Reservation System

## Overview

The reservation system — the policy of confining Native American tribes to federally designated tracts of land — emerged in the 1850s as the United States expanded westward and the demand for Native lands intensified. Unlike the [[indian-removal|Indian Removal]] policy, which relocated entire tribes across the [[mississippi|Mississippi]], the reservation system sought to concentrate tribes within reduced territories inside their ancestral homelands or, increasingly, in locations chosen for their remoteness rather than their cultural significance. By the time Yarrow collected his data in the late 1870s, the reservation system had become the dominant framework governing Native American life — including the most intimate of cultural practices: the burial of the dead.

## Impact on Mortuary Customs

### Restricted Access to Traditional Burial Sites

The confinement of tribes to reservation boundaries severed access to ancestral burial grounds that lay outside the designated tracts. Caves, rock shelters, mountain peaks, and river bluffs that had served as burial sites for generations became inaccessible. Among the [[ute|Ute]] of [[utah|Utah]], cave burial sites documented by Yarrow's correspondents — including the rock cemeteries of central [[utah|Utah]] (009-002) — were increasingly abandoned as the tribe was restricted to reservation lands that did not encompass the traditional burial locations.

### Concentration and Cultural Compression

On reservations, multiple bands and sometimes multiple tribes were concentrated in close proximity, creating novel cultural dynamics. Burial customs that had evolved in relative isolation now coexisted in shared spaces. Yarrow's correspondents stationed at agencies observed this compression first-hand: the [[navajo|Navajo]] practice of dwelling abandonment coexisting uneasily with the fixed-location cemeteries required by reservation administrators; the Plains scaffold burial tradition colliding with agency regulations that mandated earth [[inhumation|interment]].

### The Role of Indian Agents as Ethnographic Observers

The very personnel who enforced reservation policies — Indian agents — were among Yarrow's most prolific correspondents. Their reports, submitted from agencies across the West, provide a uniquely positioned record of mortuary customs in transition. Yarrow's questionnaire circulated through the agency system, and the responses he received reflect the perspective of men who were simultaneously agents of cultural disruption and the principal recorders of the customs they were helping to transform.

### Mission Influence

The [[mission]] system, operating in conjunction with reservations, introduced Christian burial practices that competed with and often suppressed traditional customs. Missionaries encouraged or required Christian [[inhumation|interment]], discouraged "pagan" rituals of [[mourning]], and in some cases actively prevented practices such as [[sacrifice]], self-mutilation, and scaffold exposure. The accounts preserved by Yarrow and his correspondents thus capture customs already under active pressure from multiple directions.

### Preservation and Transformation

Despite these pressures, many tribes adapted their mortuary customs to reservation conditions rather than abandoning them entirely. The persistence of traditional practices — modified but recognizable — is evident throughout Yarrow's documentation. The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] continued forms of the bone-picking ceremony in Indian Territory. [[california|California]] tribes maintained elements of the [[mourning]] "last cry" ritual even when confined to rancherias. The distinction between full preservation and adaptive transformation was often subtle, and Yarrow's correspondents were not always equipped to recognize it.

## The Yarrow Survey as a Reservation-Era Document

Yarrow's 1880 monograph must be understood as a document of the reservation era. The customs it describes were not timeless traditions observed in a state of cultural equilibrium but practices under siege, documented at a moment when the reservation system had already begun to fundamentally reshape Native American life. The questionnaire itself — with its systematic, categorizing approach to mortuary customs — reflected the bureaucratic mentality of the agency system through which it was distributed.

The military officers who provided many of Yarrow's accounts were stationed at forts established to support the reservation system. The physicians who described burial practices often served at agencies where they treated Native patients while simultaneously documenting their customs. This entanglement of observation and administration created the conditions of possibility for Yarrow's survey while also shaping — and limiting — the nature of the evidence it produced.
