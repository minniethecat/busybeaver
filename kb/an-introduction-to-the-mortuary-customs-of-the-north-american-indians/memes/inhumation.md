# Inhumation

## Overview

Inhumation — the interment of the dead in the ground — was the commonest mode of burial among the North American Indians, documented across virtually every region and tribe surveyed by Yarrow (1880). The practice took many forms: from simple pit graves to elaborate mound structures, from seated burials to extended positions, each reflecting the particular customs and beliefs of the tribe.

## Tribal Distribution

### Northeastern Tribes
The [[Mohawks]] of [[new-york|New York]] placed the body upright or upon its haunches in a large round hole, covering it with timber to support the earth heaped above. The corpse was dressed in all its finery, with wampum and other grave goods deposited alongside. Relatives maintained the grave mound, preventing grass or weeds from growing upon it (001-001)(001-002).

### Southeastern Tribes
Among the [[carolina|Carolina]] tribes, inhumation was accompanied by elaborate ceremonies. The corpse was first placed in a cane bundle and deposited in an outhouse for a day and night of [[mourning]]. The deceased was wrapped in blankets and mats, placed in a coffin of woven reeds, then carried to the grave by four young men. A medicine man delivered a funeral oration recounting the exploits of the deceased before interment in the peach orchard (001-004)(001-005).

### Plains and Western Tribes
Many Plains tribes practiced simple earth burial, often placing the body in a seated position facing the rising sun. Grave goods — weapons, pipes, personal ornaments — were commonly included. Among some bands, the burial site was marked with poles or stones, while others left graves unmarked to prevent disturbance.

## Variations

Inhumation practices varied considerably in depth, orientation, and accompanying structures. Shallow graves were common in warmer regions, while deeper interments were practiced where frozen ground permitted. The [[Sioux]] sometimes used scaffold burial as a primary mode and inhumation as secondary. The [[Coyotero Apaches]] practiced inhumation with specific ceremonial protocols distinct from neighboring tribes (001-010)(001-015).

## Historical Accounts

[[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s *Historical and Statistical Information Respecting the History, Condition and Prospects of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1853) provided some of the earliest systematic descriptions of [[mohawk|Mohawk]] burial practices. [[jones|Jones]]'s *Antiquities of the Southern Indians* (1873) documented [[carolina|Carolina]] tribal customs through Lawson's eyewitness accounts, offering rare detail on the ceremonial aspects of pre-contact inhumation (001-003)(001-004).
