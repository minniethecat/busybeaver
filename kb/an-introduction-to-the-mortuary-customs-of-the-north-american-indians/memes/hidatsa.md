# Hidatsa

## Overview

The Hidatsa people, also known as the Minitari or Gros Ventres of the Missouri, inhabited the upper [[missouri|Missouri]] River region in present-day North [[dakota|Dakota]]. An agricultural people who lived in permanent earth-lodge villages along the river bluffs, the Hidatsa developed a rich cosmology of death and the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] that was documented with unusual ethnographic precision by [[gardner-w|Dr. W.]] Matthews, U.S. Army surgeon, whose account Yarrow (1880) preserved as a principal source for understanding Northern Plains mortuary beliefs.

## Mortuary Customs

### The Four-Night Lingering of the Shade

Central to Hidatsa mortuary belief was the concept that the shade — the spiritual essence of the deceased — did not depart immediately upon death. According to Dr. Matthews:

> When a Hidatsa dies his shade lingers four nights around the camp or village in which he died, and then goes to the lodge of his departed kindred in the "village of the dead" (021-030).

This four-day transitional period between death and final departure represents a near-universal temporal structure in Native American eschatology. Yarrow notes that the Hidatsa shared this belief with the Algonkins and the Mexicans — cultures separated by vast distances yet converging on the conviction that four days were required before the spirit could finally leave the earth (021-031).

### The Village of the Dead

In the [[spirit-land|afterlife]], Hidatsa shades inhabited a "village of the dead" structured as a mirror of earthly society. There, as in life, "the brave man is honored and the coward despised." The shades of those who committed suicide were said to occupy a separate part of the village, though their condition differed in no other respect from that of the naturally deceased. Human shades hunted the shades of buffalo and other animals that had died in the terrestrial world — the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] as a continuation of the Plains subsistence cycle. Notably, the four seasons in the village of the dead arrived in inverse order to the terrestrial seasons, a cosmological inversion that marked the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] as a parallel but distinct realm (021-030).

### The Scorched Moccasin Ritual

The four-night lingering of the shade created both an opportunity for farewell and a source of apprehension. Those who had disliked or feared the deceased — and who did not wish to receive a visit from the lingering shade — employed a protective ritual:

> Those who disliked or feared the deceased, and do not wish a visit from the shade, scorch with red coals a pair of moccasins which they leave at the door of the lodge. The smell of the burning leather they claim keeps the ghost out (021-030).

This apotropaic practice — using the offensive odor of scorched leather to repel the disembodied spirit — represents a distinctive Hidatsa contribution to the broader Plains pattern of ghost-avoidance customs. Significantly, the true friends of the deceased took no such precautions, welcoming rather than fearing the shade's presence during its four-night earthly sojourn.

## Historical Sources

The principal ethnographic source for Hidatsa mortuary beliefs is [[gardner-w|Dr. W.]] Matthews, U.S. Army, whose study of Hidatsa ethnology and philology was published by the U.S. Geological Survey of the Territories in 1877. Yarrow incorporated this material into his comparative framework, indexing it under "Hidatsa burial superstitions" (024-201). The Matthews account is notable among Yarrow's sources for its attention to belief and cosmology rather than material burial practice alone.
