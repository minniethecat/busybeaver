## Overview

Walter James Hoffman (1846–1899) was a United States Army surgeon who served at military posts across the American West during the 1870s. His medical duties brought him into contact with numerous Native American communities, and his published observations of their mortuary practices — notably those of the [[coyotero-apache|Coyotero Apache]] of [[arizona|Arizona]] and the [[pima|Pima]] of the Gila River region — provided Yarrow with some of the most frank and detailed accounts of Southwestern burial customs in the 1880 monograph. Hoffman later became a prominent ethnologist with the Bureau of American Ethnology (001-026)(005-003).

## Contributions

### Coyotero Apache Burial

Hoffman's account of [[coyotero-apache|Coyotero Apache]] burial practices is notable for its unusual candor. He observed that the [[apache|Coyoteros]], in disposing of their dead, appeared to be actuated primarily by the desire to spare themselves needless trouble. The corpse was partially wrapped and deposited into the cavity left by the removal of a small rock or the stump of a tree; after the body had been crammed into the smallest possible space, the rock or stump was rolled back into position and stones were placed around the base — a precaution directed not at ritual purposes but at the practical necessity of excluding coyotes. Hoffman's blunt characterization of this practice as motivated by convenience rather than by cosmology — a rare instance of an observer explicitly attributing utilitarian rather than religious motivation to mortuary behavior — makes his account a valuable counterpoint to the more reverential descriptions provided by other correspondents (001-026)(001-027).

Hoffman further documented the Coyotero [[mourning]] observances, noting that the nearest of kin mourned for a period of one month, during which they uttered what he described as the most dismal [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s, which he judged to be apparently sincere. However, he also recorded the intermittent character of this [[mourning]]: during daylight hours the obligation was frequently neglected or forgotten, but when the mourner was reminded of his duty, he renewed his howling with what Hoffman characterized as evident interest. This observation of the socially regulated but imperfectly performed nature of [[mourning]] ritual provides a rare glimpse into the quotidian reality of nineteenth-century [[apache|Apache]] grief practice (001-027).

### Pima Burial

Hoffman also contributed significant observations of [[pima|Pima]] burial customs, recording the distinctive practice of binding the deceased's body with ropes passed around the neck and under the knees, drawing them tight until the body was doubled into a sitting position. The grave itself was dug four to five feet deep and perfectly round, with a side vault hollowed out at the bottom to receive the body. Hoffman noted that [[pima|Pima]] graves were frequently prepared before the patient actually died, and that open graves awaiting their intended occupants could be seen in [[pima|Pima]] burial-grounds — a practice that, as he dryly observed, sometimes resulted in empty graves when the anticipated deceased unexpectedly recovered (005-003).

Hoffman's accounts, published in the *United States Geological Survey of the Territories* for 1876 (p. 473), cover [[coyotero-apache|Coyotero Apache]] rock-cavity inhumation, Coyotero [[mourning]] observances, and [[pima|Pima]] seated-position burial. His medical training is evident in his attention to the physical details of corpse preparation and grave construction, while his ethnographic instincts — which would later lead to his appointment at the Bureau of American Ethnology — are apparent in his willingness to record both the stated beliefs and the observed behaviors of his informants, even when these diverged.

## Reception and Scholarly Assessment

Hoffman's accounts occupy a distinctive position within the Yarrow corpus, valued not only for their ethnographic detail but for their unusual authorial frankness. His characterization of Coyotero [[apache|Apache]] burial as motivated primarily by convenience — to spare the survivors needless trouble — represents one of the few instances in the 1880 monograph in which a firsthand observer explicitly attributed utilitarian rather than religious motivation to mortuary behavior. Yarrow appears to have recognized the exceptional candor of Hoffman's reporting, reproducing his account in full without editorial softening (001-026).

The transition in Hoffman's career from Army surgeon to Bureau of American Ethnology ethnologist is reflected in the dual character of his observations. His medical training is evident in the precision with which he described the physical manipulation of the deceased: the binding of ropes around the neck and knees of [[pima|Pima]] corpses, the depth and diameter of graves, and the practical details of rock-cavity disposal. Yet his ethnographic instincts — his willingness to record both stated belief and observed behavior, and his attention to the gap between them — anticipate the professional standards that would come to define the Bureau's work in the following decade. His note that Coyotero mourners frequently neglected their howling during daylight hours but renewed it "with evident interest" when reminded of their duty is an early instance of what modern ethnographers would recognize as the documentation of practice as distinct from prescription.

Hoffman's influence extended beyond the Yarrow monograph. His subsequent appointment at the Bureau of American Ethnology placed him among the first generation of professionally trained ethnologists working within the federal scientific establishment, and his field methods — combining direct observation with the collection of material culture — helped establish the documentary standards that would characterize the Bureau's extensive publication series. His [[coyotero-apache|Coyotero]] and [[pima|Pima]] accounts, though brief by the standards of later Bureau monographs, remain among the earliest systematic descriptions of Southwestern mortuary customs by a medically trained observer.
