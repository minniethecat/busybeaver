# Mummification

## Overview

Mummification — the intentional or natural preservation of the dead from decomposition — was practiced by several Native American peoples, with the best-known examples found in cave burials. Unlike the elaborate Egyptian processes, American mummification typically involved desiccation through fire, smoke, or natural cave conditions. Yarrow (1880) noted the subject deserved deeper investigation but provided a preliminary survey of documented cases (006-001)(006-002).

## Tribal Distribution

### Aleutian Islands
The [[Aleutian mummies]], among the most celebrated examples of American mummification, were found in caves of the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands. Bodies were carefully wrapped and placed in elevated positions within dry volcanic caves, where natural conditions preserved them for extended periods. Early explorers and traders reported finding remarkably intact remains (006-005)(006-010).

### Southwestern Tribes
In the arid regions of the American Southwest, natural desiccation preserved bodies placed in caves and rock shelters. Some tribes of present-day [[arizona|Arizona]] and [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] practiced intentional preparation of the dead, using fire to dry bodies before wrapping them in mats or skins for deposition in caves or cliff dwellings (006-008).

### Southeastern Tribes
The [[Congaree and Santee Indians]] of the [[carolina|Carolinas]] practiced partial embalmment, applying herbal preparations and pigments to the body before [[inhumation|interment]]. This practice differed from full mummification but represented a deliberate effort at preservation (006-012).

## Variations

The motivations for mummification varied. Yarrow surveyed theories drawn from Egyptian scholarship: Cassien attributed [[embalming]] to the practical need during Nile inundation seasons; Herodotus believed it prevented bodies from becoming prey to animals; Diodorus of Sicily credited filial piety; and De Maillet argued for a religious belief in the soul's return to the preserved body after vast cosmic cycles. Which of these motivations, if any, animated American Indians remained, in Yarrow's view, an open question (006-001)(006-003).

## Historical Accounts

The literature on American mummification was sparse at the time of Yarrow's writing. The [[aleut|Aleut]]ian specimens had been noted by Russian explorers and traders, while cave burials in [[kentucky|Kentucky]] and [[tennessee|Tennessee]] sometimes yielded naturally mummified remains. Yarrow indicated that a more comprehensive treatment would appear in a planned subsequent volume (006-001)(006-015).
