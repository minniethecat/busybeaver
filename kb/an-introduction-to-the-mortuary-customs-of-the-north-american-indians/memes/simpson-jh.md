# J. H. Simpson

## Overview

James Hervey Simpson (1813–1883) was a captain in the United States Army Corps of Topographical Engineers whose explorations of the Great Basin region in the 1850s produced some of the earliest systematic descriptions of the indigenous peoples of present-day [[utah|Utah]] and Nevada. His report *Exploration of the [[utah|Great Salt Lake Valley]], [[utah|Utah]]* (1859) included an account of [[gosh-ute|Gosh-Ute]] mortuary practice so unusual — the submersion of corpses in desert springs — that Yarrow characterized it as entirely unique among the burial customs surveyed in his 1880 monograph (017-001).

## Contributions

Simpson's contribution to Yarrow's monograph concerns the [[gosh-ute|Gosh-Ute]] Indians of the Great Salt Lake Desert and their practice of aquatic burial. According to Simpson, Skull Valley — a part of the Great Salt Lake Desert — derived its name from the number of skulls found there, a consequence of the [[gosh-ute|Gosh-Ute]] custom of burying their dead in springs. The bodies were sunk with stones or held down with sticks, the spring waters serving as the repository for the deceased. Simpson's guide, [[catlin-george|George]] [[gardner-w|W.]] Bean, reported having personally witnessed [[gosh-ute|Gosh-Ute]] Indians burying their dead in this manner near the town of Provo (017-001).

As corroboration, Simpson noted in another passage of his report that the expedition, arriving at a spring one evening, was obliged to dig out the skeleton of an Indian from the mud at the bottom before the water could be used. This grim discovery — a direct consequence of the burial practice the expedition was simultaneously documenting — underscores the intimate and often uncomfortable proximity between ethnographic observation and the practical exigencies of desert travel. Yarrow remarked that the peculiar mode of burial was entirely unique, so far as was known, and that only the well-known probity of the relator prevented the account from being questioned — particularly given the scarcity of water in the region and the customary care Indians took not to pollute streams or springs near which they lived (017-001).

Simpson's account raises a question that Yarrow acknowledged but did not resolve: why a desert people, for whom water was among the scarcest and most precious of resources, would choose springs as the repository for their dead. The practice stands in marked contrast to the [[cremation]], inhumation, and scaffold burial that predominated across the Great Basin and adjacent regions, and it remains one of the most enigmatic mortuary customs in the entire Yarrow corpus (017-001).

## Accounts Provided

Simpson's single but significant contribution to Yarrow's monograph is the documentation of [[gosh-ute|Gosh-Ute]] spring burial, published in his *Exploration of the [[utah|Great Salt Lake Valley]], [[utah|Utah]]* (1859, p. 48). His topographical training is reflected in the precision of his geographic references — Skull Valley, the Great Salt Lake Desert, the town of Provo — while his reliance on the testimony of an experienced guide ([[catlin-george|George]] [[gardner-w|W.]] Bean) for eyewitness corroboration reflects the hybrid character of nineteenth-century exploration reportage, in which the observations of locally knowledgeable informants were integrated into the official record.
