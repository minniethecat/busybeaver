# E. Foreman

## Overview

Dr. E. Foreman (Edward Foreman) was a physician affiliated with the Smithsonian Institution during the 1870s who contributed two distinct and valuable accounts to Yarrow's monograph. His description of a [[creek|Muscogee]] burial urn excavated from the Oconee River bottomlands in [[georgia|Georgia]] provided important comparative evidence for the urn-burial chapter, while his account of a [[utah|Utah]] [[cremation]] mound — in which not only the body but all personal possessions and even the dwelling of the deceased were destroyed by fire — documented one of the most extreme forms of mortuary property destruction recorded in the Yarrow corpus. His contributions to the *American Naturalist* in 1876 were forwarded to Yarrow through the Smithsonian's institutional network (007-004)(010-028).

## Contributions

Foreman's most significant contribution to Yarrow's work was his detailed description of a Southern burial urn and cover (National Museum catalog nos. 27976 and 27977), received from [[bartram-william|William]] McKinley of Milledgeville, [[georgia|Georgia]]. The urn, exhumed from the bottomlands of the Oconee River — then covered with "almost impassable canebrakes, tall grasses, and briers" — was made of baked clay, shaped with a sharp-apex bottom rather than the rounded base typical of [[california|California]] steatite ollas, and fitted with a cover that also terminated in an apex. Around the border of the cover, near where it rested on the rim of the vessel, were indented scroll ornamentations. McKinley attributed these urns to the [[creek|Muscogee]]s, a branch of the [[creek|Creek]] Nation — an attribution that Yarrow recorded without editorial challenge, suggesting he found it plausible (007-004).

Foreman also contributed an account of [[utah|Utah]] [[cremation]] practices of particular violence: at the death of a person among the Indians of southern [[utah|Utah]], not only were the remains consumed by fire but all articles of personal property, "even the very habitation which had served as a home," were likewise destroyed. After the conflagration, what remained unburned was covered with earth and a mound formed. Yarrow noted that this degree of wholesale property destruction — exceeding the more common practice of [[cremation|burning]] selected belongings — was without parallel in his survey of North American mortuary customs (010-028).

## Accounts Provided

Foreman's two published accounts cover [[creek|Muscogee]] ([[creek|Creek]]) [[urn-burial|urn burial]] with ceramic typology and provenience from the Oconee River, [[georgia|Georgia]], and an extreme form of [[utah|Utah]] Indian [[cremation]] involving total destruction of both personal property and dwelling. Both were published in the *American Naturalist* (1876).
