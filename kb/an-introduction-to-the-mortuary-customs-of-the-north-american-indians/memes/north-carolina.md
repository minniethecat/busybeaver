## Overview

North Carolina, encompassing both the Appalachian highlands and the Atlantic coastal plain, was home to the [[cherokee|Cherokee]] and other indigenous peoples whose mortuary customs were documented through both archaeological excavation and eyewitness observation in the nineteenth century. Yarrow's 1880 monograph preserves several detailed accounts of North Carolina burial practices, drawing on the fieldwork of local antiquarians and the observations of travelers.

## Burial Sites and Practices

### Caldwell County Mound Excavation

One of the most meticulously recorded excavations in Yarrow's monograph is that of J. Mason Spainhour, a dentist from Lenoir, Caldwell County, North Carolina. On March 11, 1871, Spainhour excavated an Indian mound on the farm of R. V. Michaux, near John's River in Burke County. Yarrow vouches for Spainhour's credibility as "an observer of undoubted integrity, whose facts as given may not be doubted" (004-035). The excavation revealed a carefully constructed burial mound with stratigraphic layers of interment, providing archaeological evidence of the region's prehistoric burial practices (004-036).

### Clay-Coated Burial

A distinctive and unusual method of burial was reported from North Carolina by a Mr. Wilcox. In numerous instances, burial places were discovered "where the bodies had been placed with the face up and covered with a coating of plastic clay about an inch thick." This clay casing appears to have served as a form of primitive embalmment, sealing the body from air and moisture. A pile of wood was placed over the clay-covered body, suggesting either a ritual fire component or a practical means of marking and protecting the grave (011-008). This practice, allied somewhat to [[cremation]] in its use of fire, is described as peculiar to the [[cherokee|Cherokees]] or some other tribe of North Carolina (011-001).

### Moravian Settlement Mourning Customs

Yarrow records that at the Moravian settlement of Salem, North Carolina, traditional funeral observances persisted well into the nineteenth century. The custom of relatives gathering at the graveside with loud lamentation was noted as late as the mid-nineteenth century, described by John Henry Boner as a "remnant of the ancient funeral observances" rather than a practical measure to prevent premature burial (014-026). This continuity of indigenous mourning practice within a Christian settlement context illustrates the resilience and syncretism of Native [[mourning]] traditions in the post-contact period.
