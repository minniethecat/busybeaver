## Overview

A sepulchre, in the context of North American Indian mortuary customs, denotes a permanent or semi-permanent structure designed to house human remains — ranging from elaborate chambered mounds and dolmens to natural rock cavities used as burial vaults. Unlike simple earth graves, sepulchres represented a greater investment of labor and materials, often serving as repositories for multiple individuals, families, or persons of high status. As one early observer noted of a tribal chief's burial, "By him is commonly the sepulchre of their Kings" (006-009). The concept of the sepulchre as a "repository of greatest riches" was recognized by early European commentators on indigenous burial customs, who drew parallels between Native American burial structures and the sepulchres of the Old World (P01-031).

## Chambered Mounds

Among the most elaborate sepulchral structures were the chambered mounds of the eastern United States. These "chambered mounds" contained internal vaults constructed with stone or timber — described in some accounts as "rude dolmens (or chambered barrows, as they would be called in England)" (004-003). The chambers served as burial vaults, often accessed through a passage, and were designed to receive multiple burials over time. Archaeological investigation revealed that "a large mound near the chambered mounds was also opened, but it contained only detached bones" (004-005), suggesting varying burial practices even within the same mound complex. Additional accounts describe "other chambered mounds which are, like the preceding, constructed with stone chambers and passages" (004-007), indicating that this was a regional architectural tradition rather than an isolated practice.

## Rock Sepulchres

In the western regions, natural rock formations were adapted as sepulchres. Among the Ute, it was "customary to select sepulchres of this character" — natural rock cavities and niches that provided protection from the elements and scavengers (005-006). These rock sepulchres offered durable, low-maintenance burial sites particularly suited to the arid and mountainous terrain of the Great Basin and surrounding regions. The use of such natural features as permanent burial repositories distinguishes them from the constructed chambers of the mound-building cultures of the East.

## Deposition Practices

The term "sepulchre" in historical accounts often encompassed charnel-houses and bone repositories as well as individual tombs. Once deposited, bodies in sepulchres were thought by some tribes to enter "a sort of life in their living sepulchres" (001-018), reflecting the belief that the dead continued to exist within their burial chambers. Among some groups, the "sepulchre" was described as "about six feet deep and much longer than ordinary graves" (001-007), indicating substantial construction effort beyond simple inhumation. The Choctaw and neighboring southeastern tribes constructed sepulchres that "are generally sepulchres" associated with major ceremonial occasions (015-008).

## Spiritual and Social Dimensions

Sepulchres served functions beyond mere physical containment of remains. As permanent, visible structures, they marked enduring claims to territory and ancestorhood. The Quiogozon of the South Carolina tribes — "the royal tomb or burial place of their kings and war captains, being a more magnificent cabin reared at the public expense" — was "an object of veneration" where leaders "spend several days with their idols and dead kings" (006-014). This fusion of tomb, temple, and political monument illustrates how the sepulchre transcended its practical function to become a center of ritual, governance, and communal identity.
