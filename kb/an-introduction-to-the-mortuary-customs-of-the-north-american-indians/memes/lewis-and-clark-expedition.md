The Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–1806), officially the Corps of Discovery Expedition, was the United States' first overland expedition to the Pacific Coast and back, commissioned by President Thomas Jefferson following the Louisiana Purchase. Led by Captain Meriwether Lewis and Lieutenant William [[clarke-william|Clark]], the expedition traversed the continental divide and descended the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] to its mouth, recording — among a vast body of geographic, botanical, zoological, and ethnographic observations — some of the earliest American descriptions of mortuary customs among the Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest.

## Ethnological Contributions to Mortuary Customs

**Columbia River Canoe Burial.** In their journals, Lewis and Clark documented the characteristic mortuary practice of the fishing tribes of the lower [[columbia-river|Columbia River]]: [[canoe-burial|canoe burial]]. The deceased were placed in canoes which were then drawn into the woods at prominent points a short distance from the village, sometimes wedged between the forks of trees or raised from the ground on posts. This practice was most notably observed among the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] (Chinook) people (016-005).

**Mount Coffin and Coffin Rock.** The expedition recorded the existence of Mount Coffin, a high isolated bluff about three miles below the mouth of the Cowlitz River, which served as one of the principal burial grounds of the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]]. Significantly, Lewis and Clark noted the mount's existence but did not describe it as containing a large number of canoes — an observation that provides a valuable temporal benchmark. Lieutenant Broughton of Vancouver's expedition, exploring the river a decade earlier, had noted only *several* canoes at the site. By the time of Captain Charles Wilkes's United States Exploring Expedition (1838–1842), however, it was conjectured that no fewer than three thousand canoes containing human remains were deposited there. Lewis and Clark's sparse record thus captures the site at an intermediate stage in its development as a major indigenous cemetery (016-005).

**Tsinuk Vault Sepulchers.** William [[clarke-william|Clark]] also described the burial vaults of the Tsinuk at the Cascades of the Columbia River — box-like structures raised above ground that contained the bodies of the dead, with carved wooden images placed against the walls. Clark's interpretive caution in describing these images — "most probably intended as resemblances of those whose decease they indicated" rather than as objects of worship — was noted by subsequent scholars as methodologically unusual for early American exploration literature, which frequently misidentified indigenous iconography as idol worship.

## Documentary Significance

The Lewis and Clark journals, published in various editions throughout the nineteenth century, provided a chronological baseline for the study of lower Columbia River mortuary practices. The contrast between Lewis and Clark's relatively sparse mention of Mount Coffin and Wilkes's estimate of three thousand canoes four decades later documents the intensification of the site's use during the early nineteenth century — a period of massive demographic disruption among the indigenous peoples of the Columbia basin.

Yarrow (1880) drew upon the Lewis and Clark journals as primary sources for the Tsinuk material, integrating them with the later and more detailed accounts of [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]] and Captain Wilkes. The expedition's observations, though incidental to its primary mission of geographic discovery, became part of the documentary foundation of American mortuary ethnology (016-005).
