# Hair Offering

The cutting or tearing of **hair** as a [[mourning]] practice was widespread among North American Indian tribes. Unlike the temporary application of paint or clay, the severing of hair represented a permanent and highly visible transformation of the mourner's body — a [[sacrifice]] of personal adornment that signaled the depth of loss.

## Forms of Hair Offering

The most dramatic form involved cropping the hair close to the scalp. Among the [[dakota|Dakota]], "the hair, never cut under other circumstances, is cropped off even with the neck" upon a death [014-020]. The same account notes that the top of the head and forehead were then smeared with white clay — the hair cutting and the clay application together constituting the full [[mourning]] costume.

On one scaffold burial, observers noted "some hair was suspended, which we at first mistook for a scalp, but our guide informed us that these were locks of hair torn from their heads by the relatives to testify their grief" [014-008]. This practice — tearing hair from the living to suspend near the dead — blurred the line between self-mutilation and offering. The hair, a part of the mourner's own body, was left with the deceased as a permanent token.

The [[pima|Pima]] of the Southwest practiced a distinctive form of hair cutting tied to the burial itself:

> "The Pimas tie the bodies of their dead with ropes, passing the latter around the neck and under the knees and then drawing them tight until the body is doubled up and forced into a sitting position. They dig the grave from four to five feet deep and perfectly round (about two feet in diameter), then hollow out to one side of the bottom of this grave a sort of vault large enough to contain the body. Here the body is deposited, the grave is filled up level with the ground, and poles, trees, or pieces of timber placed upon it to protect it from coyotes." After the burial, the male relatives "cut off about six inches of their long hair" [001-025].

## Hair Offering at Cremations

At [[cremation]] ceremonies, the offering of hair took on an especially dramatic character. At a [[florida|Florida]] [[cremation]] witnessed by one observer, the participants were "screaming, [[mourning|wailing]], tearing their hair, beating their breasts in the wildest excitement" as the flames consumed the body and the grave goods [010-018]. The act of tearing hair — spontaneous, violent, and public — contrasted with the more formal cropping practised after scaffold and earth burials.

## Cross-Tribal Distribution

The practice was not confined to any single culture area. Among the tribes of the Plains, "the hair, never cut under other circumstances, is cropped off even with the neck, and the top of the head and forehead, and sometimes nearly the whole body, are smeared with a species of white earth resembling chalk, moistened with water" [014-020]. In [[california|California]], among the [[achomawi|Pitt River Indians]], it was recorded that "relations of the deceased (particularly those of the female sex who were of the nearest relation to the man of distinction) had their hair cropped short" [011-010]. The near-universal distribution of hair offering — from the Atlantic coast to the Pacific — suggests it was among the most deeply rooted of all [[mourning]] customs.

The cutting of hair was recognized by Yarrow as an established [[mourning]] observance across multiple tribes. His questionnaire specifically inquired about the practice, and his informants consistently reported it. "The cutting of hair as a [[mourning]] observance" appears alongside other bodily mutilations — the cutting off of fingers, the lacerating of flesh — as acts by which the living demonstrated the sincerity of their grief [014-022].

## Gender and Hair Offering

Women appear to have been the primary practitioners of hair offering, consistent with their broader role as the principal mourners. The [[dakota|Dakota]] account describes women relatives "getting on some elevated spot near where the body rests, and keeping up a dismal wail" — the hair cutting was likely performed in conjunction with these vigils [014-006].

## Symbolic Significance

The offering of hair carried multiple layers of meaning. Hair was a marker of personal identity and beauty; its [[sacrifice]] represented the mourner's temporary renunciation of normal social life. The practice of suspending hair near the corpse or scaffold may also have served as a material link between the living and the dead — a part of the mourner left to accompany the deceased on the journey to the spirit world.
