## Overview

The Aleutian Islands, extending westward from the Alaska Peninsula into the North Pacific, were home to the Aleut (Unangan) people whose sophisticated mortuary practices — including embalmment, mummification, and elaborate cave deposition — were documented by Russian and American observers from the early nineteenth century onward. Yarrow's 1880 monograph preserves several accounts of Aleutian burial customs that represent the most northerly examples of deliberate corpse preservation in aboriginal North America. The islands' volcanic geology, with its dry, sulphurous caves, created natural preservation conditions that, combined with deliberate embalming techniques, produced some of the best-preserved mummies in the Western Hemisphere outside of Peru.

## Mortuary Customs

### Embalmment and Seated Burial

The earliest detailed account of Aleutian Islanders' burial practices comes from Martin Sauer, secretary to the Billings Expedition of 1802. Sauer recorded that the Aleut "embalm the bodies of the men with dried moss and grass; bury them in their best attire, in a sitting posture, in a strong box, with their darts and instruments." The tomb was then "decorated with various coloured mats, embroidery, and paintings." Women received less elaborate treatment, and a mother would sometimes keep a deceased child embalmed in the hut "for some months, constantly wiping it dry," only burying it "when it begins to smell, or when they get reconciled to parting with it" (006-041). The seated burial posture — with the body doubled up by "severing some of the muscles at the hip and knee joints and bending the limbs downward horizontally upon the trunk" (006-045) — was a distinctive feature of Aleutian funerary practice.

### Kagamale Cave Discovery

One of the most remarkable archaeological discoveries documented by Yarrow was the 1874 recovery of mummified remains from Kagamale Island, north of Ounalaska (Unalaska). Captain Henning, an agent of the Alaska Commercial Company, learned from Aleut oral tradition that Kagamale was "the last resting-place of a great chief, known as Karkhayahouchak." After three days of attempting to land at the island, Henning gained entrance to a volcanic cave that "smelt strongly of hot sulphurous vapors" and discovered the mummified remains of the chief, his family, and relatives — eleven packages of bodies in total (006-043, 006-044).

The chief's body was enclosed in "a large basket-like structure, about four feet in height," wrapped in "finely-wrought sea-grass matting, exquisitely close in texture, and skins." A broad hoop of thinly-cut wood formed the base, and the body was covered with "the fine skin of the sea-otter, always a mark of distinction in the interments of the Aleuts" (006-045). The packages were stretched with fish-nets made of sea-lion sinew and bird-nets, and the collection included carved ivory sea-lion figurines, a grass-plaited cap, and a necklet of birds' claws (006-045).

### Ancient Whale-Bone Sarcophagi

The earliest archaeological evidence from the Aleutian region comes from the Amaknak Cave, where investigators found rectangular enclosures constructed from "the very much decayed supra-maxillary bones of some large cetacean." These whale-bone sarcophagi, measuring less than four feet in length, two feet in width, and eighteen inches in depth, contained skeletons "in the last stages of decay," tied up "in the Innuit fashion" to fit into the narrow enclosures (005-012). Yarrow's source notes that this mode of deposition was "precisely similar to that adopted by most of the continental Innuit, but equally different from the modern Aleut fashion," suggesting cultural change or succession in the region's mortuary practices over time (005-012).

### Grave Goods and Status Markers

The Aleutian burial tradition placed strong emphasis on grave goods as markers of social status. The chief of Kagamale was interred with "wooden vessels scooped out smoothly; a piece of dark, greenish, flat stone, harder than the emerald, which the Indians use to tan skins; a scalp-lock of jet-black hair; a small rude figure, which may have been a very ugly doll or an idol; two or three tiny carvings in ivory of the sea-lion, very neatly executed, a comb, a necklet made of birds' claws inserted into one another, and several specimens of little bags, and a cap plaited out of sea-grass and almost water-tight" (006-045). The sea-otter skin wrapping was specifically "a mark of distinction in the interments of the Aleuts," reserved for high-status individuals, while commoners received simpler treatments (006-045). Among the recovered relics were also items of body armor "composed of reeds bound together," reinforcing the warrior identity of the chiefly deceased (006-045).

## Cultural Significance

The Aleutian Island burial practices represent a unique synthesis of Arctic and Pacific maritime funerary traditions. The use of sea-mammal products — whale bone for sarcophagi, sea-otter skin as a mark of chiefly distinction, sea-lion sinew for netting — reflects the thoroughly marine orientation of Aleut culture. The sulphurous volcanic caves that preserved the mummies for over a century functioned as natural embalming chambers, their dry, mineral-rich atmosphere arresting decomposition without the artificial preservatives used in Egyptian mummification. The distinction between the ancient whale-bone burials at Amaknak and the later, elaborated practices documented by Sauer and Henning suggests an evolution from simpler Arctic-style interment to the more stratified, status-marked funerary complex of the historic Aleut, reflecting broader patterns of social complexity in the North Pacific region.
