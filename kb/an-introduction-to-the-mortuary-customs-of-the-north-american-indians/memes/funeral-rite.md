# Funeral Rites

## Overview

Funeral rites among North American Indian tribes varied considerably in their specific forms, but typically involved a multi-day sequence of ceremonies centered on preparing the deceased, communal mourning, and final disposition of the body. Yarrow (1880) documented these rites across numerous tribes, collecting accounts from military officers, physicians, and explorers to produce one of the earliest systematic surveys of Indigenous American mortuary ceremonialism.

> The accounts here gathered have been collected from a great variety of sources — military officers, physicians, and explorers — and though fragmentary in character, represent the most complete assemblage of information on the subject yet published. (001-020)

## Ceremonial Practices

### Multi-Day Rites

The funeral ceremonies of many tribes occupied three days, during which the soul of the deceased was believed to be in danger from malevolent spirits. Among the [[gosiute|Gosi-Ute]], the body was placed in a cave, and the funeral rites included specific observances described by a tribal member who had himself participated in placing dead members of his tribe in such locations.

> The Gosi-Ute, according to the description given by a member of the tribe who was questioned, placed their dead in caves. The informant stated that he had himself assisted in placing dead members of his tribe in such repositories. (005-003)

### Choctaw Ceremonies

Among the [[choctaw]], funeral ceremonies embraced three principal elements: the disposition of the body, a mourning feast, and a ceremonial dance. These were described in detail by Benson in his 1860 account *Life among the Choctaws*, which remains one of the most complete contemporaneous records of Southeastern tribal funeral practices.

> Benson, in his *Life among the Choctaws*, 1860, described the funeral ceremonies of this tribe as embracing the disposition of the body, a mourning feast, and a ceremonial dance. (019-008)

The sequence of rites — body preparation, communal feast, and dance — reflected the tribe's cosmology, in which the deceased transitioned through stages requiring different forms of ritual attention from the living community.

## Offerings and Sacrifices

Animal sacrifice formed a significant component of funeral ceremonies, particularly among Plains tribes. Horses were the most prominent sacrificial animals, their remains left scattered near grave sites as testimony to the importance of the deceased.

> Near the graves of deceased individuals were scattered the osseous remains of a number of horses which had been sacrificed no doubt during the funeral ceremonies. (009-002)

This practice reflected the belief that the deceased required the same resources and companions in the afterlife as in life. The scale of sacrifice — multiple horses at a single funeral — indicates the considerable economic investment that funeral ceremonies could represent for the deceased's family and community.

## Feasting and Communal Aspects

The funeral feast was a central and nearly universal component of tribal mortuary rites. These feasts served multiple functions: honoring the deceased, reinforcing social bonds among the living, and in many traditions, providing sustenance for the spirit of the departed during its journey.

> Beltrami, in his *Pilgrimage* of 1828, gave an account of funeral ceremonies among the western tribes, including a feast which was held before the body was consigned to its final resting place. (020-001)

At ceremonies for members of the *Grand Medicine* society, the feast included a powerful symbolic gesture: an allowance of food was served up for the dead alongside the portions for the living.

> At a funeral ceremony for a member of the *Grand Medicine* society, an allowance was served up for the dead. This reflected the belief that the deceased continued to participate in communal meals even after death. (020-003)

### Communal Singing

Funeral gatherings often featured songs for the dead, with participants moving rhythmically around the center of the ceremonial space. These songs could involve multiple singers performing simultaneously while each maintained an individual expression — a form of collective yet personalized mourning.

> At funeral gatherings, songs for the dead were sung while the participants moved around the center of the room. (021-006)

> It has been customary among several tribes to sing songs not only at the funeral, but for extended periods thereafter. Several persons may sing the same song simultaneously, yet each maintains their own individual expression. (021-017)(021-018)

## Variability

The specific form of funeral rites depended on multiple factors: the age, sex, and social standing of the deceased; the cause of death; and the particular customs of each tribe or band. Yarrow emphasized that a complete understanding of any tribe's mortuary customs required witnessing many funerals, as the rites varied significantly even within a single community.

> A complete account of these customs in any tribe will necessitate the witnessing of many funeral rites, as the custom will differ at the death of different persons according to the age, sex, and social standing of the individual. (P01-021)

This variability means that any single description of a tribe's funeral rites is necessarily incomplete — capturing one variant among many possible forms.
