# Chippewa

## Overview

The Chippewa, also known as the Ojibwe or Ojibwa, were one of the largest and most widely distributed Algonquian-speaking peoples of North America, occupying territories around Lake Superior, Lake [[huron|Huron]], and westward into present-day Minnesota and the Canadian plains. By the 1870s, when Yarrow's correspondents were actively gathering information, the Chippewa of Lake Superior were under the supervision of United States Indian Agent I. L. Mahan at Red Cliff, Wisconsin, who provided some of the most detailed accounts of Chippewa [[mourning]] customs preserved in the monograph.

## Mortuary Customs

### Widow Mourning and Grave Devotion

The intensity of Chippewa [[mourning]], particularly that of widows, received detailed attention from Agent Mahan. Yarrow records:

> There is probably no people that exhibit more sorrow and grief for their dead than they. The young widow mourns the loss of her husband; by day as by night she is heard silently sobbing; she is a constant visitor to the place of rest; with the greatest reluctance will she follow the raised camp (019-004).

The widow's grief involved self-deprivation. Friends and relatives would "incessantly devise methods to distract her mind from the thought of her lost husband," yet she would refuse nourishment. When exhaustion compelled her to eat, "the supply is scant, but on every occasion the best and largest proportion is deposited upon the grave of her husband" (019-004). The offering of food at the gravesite — giving the finest portion to the deceased rather than consuming it herself — constituted a continuing ritual of care that extended the domestic bond beyond death.

### The Substitution Effigy

A custom documented among the Chippewa involved the creation of a surrogate figure representing the deceased husband. Yarrow notes:

> The substitution of a reminder for the dead husband, made from rags, furs, and other articles, is not confined alone to the Chippewas, other tribes having the same custom (019-007).

This practice of fabricating an effigy from available materials — rags and furs assembled into a human simulacrum — served as a transitional object during the [[mourning]] period. In some instances among related groups, the widow was obliged to carry around a bundle containing the actual bones of the deceased consort for a variable period, a custom that literalized the continuing physical connection between the mourner and the dead (019-007).

### Soul Journey Beliefs

The Chippewa held a well-developed conception of the soul's postmortem journey, recorded by the explorer and geologist [[keating-william-h|William H. Keating]] during Long's Expedition of 1823:

> The Chippewas believe that there is in man an essence entirely distinct from the body; they call it *Ochechag*, and appear to supply to it the qualities which we refer to the soul. They believe that it quits the body at the time of death and repairs to what they term *Chekechekchekawe*; this region is supposed to be situated to the south and on the shores of the great ocean (021-033).

The soul's path to this southern [[spirit-land|paradise]] was perilous. Before arriving there, the dead "meet with a stream which they are obliged to cross upon a large snake that answers the purpose of a bridge" (021-033). This serpent-bridge served as a discriminating mechanism: those who died from drowning were permanently barred from crossing, cast into the stream to remain there forever. Souls of persons in a state of lethargy or trance — suspended between life and death — might reach the stream's edge only to be prevented from crossing by the snake, which "threatens to devour them" (021-033).

### Burial Superstitions

Yarrow's index records the Chippewa under the headings "Burial superstitions, Chippewa" (024-090) and "Chippewa burial superstitions" (024-136), as well as a general entry for "Chippewas" (024-288). While the full corpus of these superstitions is not developed in Yarrow's surviving text, the multiple index entries suggest that Chippewa mortuary belief was a recognized subject within the ethnographic literature of the period.
