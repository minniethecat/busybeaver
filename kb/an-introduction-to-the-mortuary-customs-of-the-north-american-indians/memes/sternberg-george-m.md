# George M. Sternberg

## Overview

George Miller Sternberg (1838–1915) was a United States Army surgeon who served at frontier posts in the American West during the 1870s, a period in which his medical duties brought him into contact with surviving Native American burial sites on the Great Plains. His published account of an elevated scaffold burial discovered on the banks of Walnut [[creek|Creek]], [[kansas|Kansas]] — notable for the exceptional care in its construction and the richness of its grave goods — was cited by Yarrow in the chapter on superterrene and aerial burial. Sternberg later achieved distinction as U.S. Surgeon General (1893–1902) and as a pioneering bacteriologist, and the 1880 monograph acknowledges his contribution among the Army medical officers who assisted Yarrow's research (004-027).

## Contributions

Sternberg's most notable contribution to the monograph was his discovery and documentation of an elaborate scaffold burial on the banks of Walnut [[creek|Creek]], [[kansas|Kansas]]. The burial case, elevated approximately eight feet from the ground by four notched poles firmly planted in the earth, contained a cradle of interlaced white willow branches, approximately six feet long by three feet broad and three feet high, with a flooring of buffalo-hide thongs arranged as net-work. The cradle was securely fastened by strips of buffalo hide to four poles of ironwood and cottonwood, about twelve feet in length, which rested upon the forked extremities of the vertical support poles. The unusual care manifested in the construction of the burial case led Sternberg to infer "that some important chief was inclosed in it." Rather than open the case in the field, he determined to send it unopened to the Army Medical Museum in Washington, believing that the scientific value of the articles within "would be enhanced if they were received at the Museum as left by the Indians" (014-012)(014-015).

When the case was later opened under Dr. [[catlin-george|George]] A. Otis's supervision at the Army Medical Museum, its contents were found to be the remains of a child — a discovery that surprised the examiners, given the elaborate construction that had suggested an adult chief. The burial wrappings consisted of seven buffalo robes, five new blankets in scarlet, blue, and white, a striped sack, a United States Infantry overcoat, and three richly bead-worked buffalo-calf skin robes with hoods, each ornamented in a different color scheme of bead-work stripes and fringed with spherical brass bells. This lavish [[inhumation|interment]] of an infant or young child provided striking evidence of the high social status that could be conferred at death regardless of age, and of the material wealth that Plains communities were willing to commit to mortuary display (014-016).

## Accounts Provided

Sternberg's account, published in the *Proceedings of the American Association for the Advancement of Science* (1875, p. 288) and furnished to Yarrow by Dr. [[catlin-george|George]] A. Otis of the Army Medical Museum, is the most detailed single description of a Plains scaffold burial in the monograph, documenting the construction materials, dimensions, fastening methods, and layered wrappings with a precision reflecting Sternberg's medical training and his recognition of the specimen's scientific importance.
