## Overview

Nevada, encompassing the arid Great Basin between the [[rocky-mountains|Rocky Mountains]] and the Sierra Nevada, was home to [[shoshone|Shoshonean]], [[paiute|Paiute]], and [[washoe|Washoe]] peoples whose mortuary customs were shaped by the harsh desert environment. Yarrow's 1880 monograph documents burial practices in the territory through the accounts of U.S. Army exploration parties and geological survey expeditions.

## Burial Practices

### Cave Interment

Yarrow records an attempt to explore a cave burial site "near the Nevada border" in Utah Territory. An Indian guide had reported the existence of a cave used for aboriginal [[inhumation|interment]], and "an attempt made to explore it, which failed for reasons to be subsequently given" (005-003). The difficulty of accessing these remote desert cave sites — combined with the arid climate that naturally preserved interred remains — made Nevada's cave burials among the least-documented in Yarrow's survey, though their existence was well-attested by local knowledge.

### Cairn and Rock Burial

The wider Great Basin and Sierra Nevada region was characterized by cairn or rock burial, a practice that "has prevailed and is still common to a considerable extent among the tribes living in the Rocky Mountains and the Sierra Nevadas" (009-001). This mode of [[inhumation|interment]] involved covering the body with carefully piled stones — a practice well-suited to the rocky, arid terrain of Nevada where digging graves in the hard desert soil was laborious and timber for scaffold construction was scarce. The stone cairns served the dual purpose of marking the grave for future visitation and protecting the body from scavenging animals.

## Associated Tribes

- [[paiute]] — Southern Numic people of the Great Basin and eastern Sierra
- [[gosh-ute]] — Western Shoshonean people of the Utah-Nevada borderlands
