## Overview

New Jersey, situated between the Delaware River and the Atlantic coast, is documented in Yarrow's 1880 monograph for a distinctive burial site combining both inhumation and [[cremation]] practices, recorded on the banks of the Delaware River. The state is also notable for one of the most singular aboriginal burials recorded in the entire monograph — a standing-position interment suggesting ritual execution.

## Burial Sites & Practices

A notable burial site was documented "on the New Jersey bank of the Delaware River," where aboriginal remains were discovered in a stratified deposit showing evidence of both burial and [[cremation]] (011-012). The site, reported by Dr. Leibel of Philadelphia, contained multiple [[inhumation|interment]]s at varying depths, with some remains showing evidence of fire exposure while others were intact inhumations.

Yarrow's index specifically records "New Jersey, burial and [[cremation]] in" as a recognized entry, indicating the state was considered a distinct mortuary province where both practices coexisted (024-305). This combination of burial and [[cremation]] within a single site is relatively uncommon in the eastern United States and suggests either temporal succession of different cultural groups or the coexistence of multiple mortuary traditions within a single community.

### The Standing Burial of Gloucester City

The most remarkable aboriginal burial documented in New Jersey was reported by [[klingbeil-william|William Klingbeil]] of Philadelphia, who discovered a human skeleton buried in a standing position in a high, red, sandy-clay bluff on the New Jersey bank of the Delaware River, a short distance below Gloucester City. Yarrow presents Klingbeil's account in full:

> "A few inches below the surface the neck bones were found, and below these the remainder of the skeleton, with the exception of the bones of the hands and feet. The skull being wanting, it could not be determined whether the remains were those of an Indian or of a white man, but in either case the sepulture was peculiarly aboriginal. A careful exhumation and critical examination by Mr. Klingbeil disclosed the fact that around the lower extremities of the body had been placed a number of large stones, which revealed traces of fire, in conjunction with charred wood, and the bones of the feet had undoubtedly been consumed. This fact makes it appear reasonably certain that the subject had been executed, probably as a prisoner of war. A pit had been dug, in which he was placed erect, and a fire kindled around him. Then he had been buried alive, or, at least, if he did not survive the fiery ordeal, his body was imbedded in the earth, with the exception of his head, which was left protruding above the surface. As no trace of the cranium could be found, it seems probable that the head had either been burned or severed from the body and removed, or else left a prey to ravenous birds. The skeleton, which would have measured fully six feet in height, was undoubtedly that of a man." (011-012)

The standing burial represents a mode of sepulture apparently unique in Yarrow's survey of North American mortuary customs. Several features distinguish it: the vertical posture (unlike the flexed, extended, or sitting positions typical of aboriginal burials), the evidence of fire confined to the lower extremities (suggesting a targeted rather than comprehensive burning), the absence of the cranium (implying post-mortem removal or destruction), and the apparent live interment combined with fire — elements that together point toward a ritualized execution rather than a conventional burial. Klingbeil's conclusion that the subject "had been executed, probably as a prisoner of war" is consistent with the pattern of differential mortuary treatment accorded to captives in many Native American societies.
