## Overview

The log coffin — a burial receptacle formed from a hollowed-out tree trunk or log — was employed by several North American tribes as an alternative to ground [[inhumation|interment]] or scaffold exposure. Yarrow (1880) classified this as one of the principal modes of surface burial, noting that "the surface burial in hollow logs" constituted a distinct type of [[inhumation|interment]] practiced in heavily forested regions where suitable timber was abundant (008-002).

## Tribal Distribution

**Southeastern Tribes** — Surface burials in hollow logs were documented among tribes of the Southeast. These burials were placed above ground, the body enclosed within the hollowed section of a large tree trunk. "The surface burial in hollow logs" constituted a distinct type, with examples found in the [[carolina|Carolinas]] and [[georgia|Georgia]]. In some cases, "a tree has been split and the two halves hollowed out to receive the body, when it was either closed with withes or confined to the ground with crossed stakes," while in other instances "a hollow tree is used by closing the ends" (008-002).

**[[seminoles|Seminole]] of [[florida|Florida]]** — The [[seminoles|Seminoles]] and other [[florida|Florida]] natives "are said to have buried in hollow trees, the bodies being placed in an upright position, occasionally the dead being crammed into a hollow log lying on the ground" (008-001). Yarrow notes that this mode of [[inhumation|interment]] was "practiced to only a limited extent, so far as can be discovered, and it is quite probable that in most cases it was employed as a temporary expedient when the survivors were pressed for time" (008-001).

**Eastern Tribes** — Among some Eastern tribes, "a log was split in half and hollowed out sufficiently large" to contain the body, with the two halves then rejoined and secured. This split-log method differed from the hollow-tree variant in that it required deliberate manufacture of the receptacle rather than simply utilizing an existing cavity (008-001).

**Northwest Tribes** — Tribes of the Pacific Northwest practiced a related method, placing the deceased "in a neatly-hollowed piece of wood" secured within a burial structure (014-028). The use of "hollows of trees" as burial depositories was also documented at cemeteries where branches of trees were employed for [[inhumation|interment]] (016-013).

**[[choctaw|Choctaw]]** — The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] employed a scaffold-and-log method in which bones remaining after scaffold exposure were collected and deposited within hollow logs or similar receptacles for secondary [[inhumation|interment]].

## Construction and Placement

Log coffins were constructed in two principal ways. In the first, a standing dead tree was selected, the interior hollowed or enlarged, and the body inserted through an opening that was subsequently closed (008-002). In the second, a living tree was felled and split lengthwise, each half hollowed to the desired dimensions, and the halves rejoined around the body. The latter method, described as "surface burial where the body was covered by a small pen of logs laid up as we build a cabin, but drawing in every course until they meet in a single log at the top," represents a hybrid between log coffin and log-pen burial (008-003).

Placement varied by region: Southeastern log coffins rested directly on the forest floor, while [[florida|Florida]] upright hollow-[[scaffold-burial|tree burial]]s stood vertically with the base set into the ground (008-001). The Northwest variant involved securing the hollowed wood section within a larger burial enclosure or platform (014-028).

## Variations

The upright log burial of [[florida|Florida]] (008-001) differs markedly from the horizontal surface log burial of the Southeast (008-002) and the hollowed-wood containers of the Northwest (014-028). While the Southeastern method placed the log directly on the ground surface, the [[florida|Florida]] variant involved standing the hollowed tree section vertically. The split-log technique of the Eastern tribes required substantially more labor but produced a more secure enclosure.
