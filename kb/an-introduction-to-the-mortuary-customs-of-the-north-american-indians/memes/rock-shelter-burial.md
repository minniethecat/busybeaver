## Overview

Rock shelter burial is a mode of [[inhumation|interment]] in which the deceased is placed beneath a natural rock overhang or within a shallow cave rather than in an excavated grave. Yarrow (1880) documented this practice among tribes of the western United States, where the arid climate and prevalence of suitable geological formations made rock shelters convenient and protective mortuary sites (006-037).

## Tribal Distribution

**Western Tribes** — Tribes of the Great Basin and Rocky Mountain regions favored rock shelters, placing bodies "on straw and moss" beneath protective stone overhangs. The bodies were "covered by matting, and rarely have either implements, weapons, or carvings associated with them." Yarrow examined "a great number" of these rock shelter burials and found "only three or four specimens in all" that contained grave goods, suggesting this was the more ancient and austere form of the practice (006-037).

**[[california|California]] and [[oregon|Oregon]] Tribes** — Some tribes placed the deceased "on the ground in some convenient rock shelter," though occasionally "the preparation is such that the body is entirely exposed to the weather" (006-038). In these cases, the rock overhang served more as a designated burial location than as environmental protection.

**[[ute|Ute]]** — The [[ute|Ute]] of [[utah|Utah]] and Colorado selected rock clefts as natural sepulchres, with "the space accommodation of the sepulchre probably regulating" how the body was positioned. After the body was placed within the cleft, "it is well covered with pieces of rock, to protect it against the ravages of wild animals" (387-001). [[ute|Ute]] burial sites were carefully chosen for concealment on "almost inaccessible" mountainsides.

## Placement and Protection

The rock-shelter burial sites examined by Yarrow's sources displayed several consistent features. Bodies were typically laid on a "rude platform of drift-wood or stones" constructed within the shelter (006-037). Straw, moss, and matting served as bedding materials. Stone cairns or flat slabs were heaped atop the remains to deter scavengers, though the overhead rock provided the primary barrier against weather.

Unlike cave burial, which utilized deeper subterranean chambers, rock-shelter burial occupied the transitional zone between open-air and underground [[inhumation|interment]]. The shelters selected were typically shallow overhangs — deep enough to deflect rain and snow but not so deep as to constitute true caves. This intermediate depth may explain the relative scarcity of grave goods: the accessible location made these sites more vulnerable to disturbance than sealed cave burials (006-037).

## Variations

Rock shelter burial ranged from simple deposition beneath an overhang with minimal covering (006-038) to more elaborate arrangements with stone cairns built atop the remains (006-037). The [[ute|Ute]] practice of selecting hidden clefts high on mountainsides represents the most deliberately concealed variant (387-001).

A later variant involved a more elaborate preparation of the body. "In comparatively modern times, probably within a few centuries, and up to the historic period (1740), another mode was adopted for the wealthy, popular, or more distinguished class. The bodies were eviscerated, cleansed from fatty matters in running water, dried, and usually placed in suitable cases in wrappings of fur and fine grass matting" (006-038). This mummification variant, practiced alongside simpler rock-shelter deposition, suggests social stratification in mortuary treatment.

The natural protection afforded by rock shelters often resulted in better preservation of remains than ground burial, particularly in the arid regions where this practice was most common.
