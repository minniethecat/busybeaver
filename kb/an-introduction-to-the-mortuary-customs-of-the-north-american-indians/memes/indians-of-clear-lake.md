# Indians of Clear Lake

## Overview

The Indians of Clear Lake inhabited the shores and hinterlands of Clear Lake, the largest natural freshwater lake wholly within the boundaries of present-day [[california|California]], located in the Coast Range north of San Francisco Bay. This region has been home to indigenous communities — predominantly of Pomoan and neighboring linguistic stocks — for millennia. Yarrow (1880) preserved a concise but vivid account of their mortuary customs as recorded by [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], the prominent nineteenth-century ethnologist and geologist who contributed substantially to [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s monumental *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1853).

## Mortuary Customs

### Scaffold Cremation

The defining mortuary practice of the Clear Lake Indians was a distinctive form of scaffold [[cremation]] in which the body was consumed by fire on an elevated platform constructed directly above a prepared pit. According to [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]]:

> "the body is consumed upon a scaffold built over a hole, into which the ashes are thrown and covered" (010-016).

This method represents a hybrid of two distinct mortuary patterns: the scaffold exposure typical of many North American tribes (in which the body was placed on an elevated platform to decompose naturally) and [[cremation]] (in which the body was reduced to ashes by fire). By combining the scaffold — which elevated the deceased above the ground — with the application of fire, the Clear Lake practice ensured that the physical remains were both transformed by flame and collected for final disposition.

### The Collection and Covering of Ashes

A notable feature of the Clear Lake ceremony was the careful management of the cremated remains. The ashes were not scattered or abandoned but were thrown into the prepared hole beneath the scaffold and subsequently covered with earth. This final act of covering the ashes — returning the transformed dead to the ground from which the scaffold had temporarily elevated them — completed the mortuary cycle. The index entries "Indians of Clear Lake" (024-159) and "Indians of Clear Lake, [[cremation]]" (024-213) confirm that Yarrow recognized this practice as a distinct and catalogued variant within the broader category of [[cremation]] customs.

## Cultural Context

[[cremation]] was the dominant mode of mortuary disposal across much of aboriginal [[california|California]], practiced by tribes from the Pacific coast to the Sierra Nevada foothills. The scaffold variant documented at Clear Lake may reflect an adaptation to the particular environmental conditions of the lake basin — the elevated platform would have facilitated the complete combustion of the body by improving airflow, while the pit beneath ensured that the ashes remained in a single location for final [[inhumation|interment]].

## Historical Sources

The account preserved by Yarrow derives from [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], whose observations were published in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1853, Part III, p. 112). [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] was a careful observer of [[california|California]] ethnography, and his field notes from the Clear Lake region represent one of the earliest written records of the indigenous communities of the northern [[california|California]] lake country — peoples whose traditional lifeways were undergoing rapid transformation by the time of [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]]'s visits in the mid-nineteenth century.
