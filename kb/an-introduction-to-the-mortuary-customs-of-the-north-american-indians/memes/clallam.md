# Clallam

## Overview

The Clallam people inhabited the southern shore of the Strait of Juan de Fuca in Washington Territory. Their mortuary customs, documented in Yarrow (1880) through the detailed account of the Reverend M. Eells, a missionary to the Skokomish Agency, centered on canoe burial — a form of [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]] in which the deceased was placed in a canoe elevated on posts overlooking the water. The Clallam variant of this widespread Northwest Coast practice is distinguished by the exceptional ethnographic detail preserved in Eells' firsthand narrative.

## Mortuary Customs

### Canoe Burial

The Rev. M. Eells provided Yarrow with a complete account of a Clallam woman's funeral. The deceased, about thirty-five years of age and dead of consumption, was placed in a Hudson's Bay Company box serving as a coffin — "about 3 1/2 feet long, 1 3/4 wide, and 1 1/2 high." Before the burial, a fire consumed many of her possessions, while the remainder were packed in three boxes placed near the coffin. Her mother sang the [[mourning]] song, repeating the refrain: "My daughter, my daughter, why did you die?" (016-001).

The burial took place the following day. A canoe approximately twenty-five feet long was prepared for the aerial [[inhumation|interment]]. Posts of hewn boards, each about a foot wide, were set into the ground, with holes cut to receive cross-boards upon which the canoe rested. Eells noted a curious detail: "As fast as the holes were cut in the posts green leaves were gathered and placed over the holes until the posts were put in the ground" — a ritual gesture whose significance he could not determine (016-001).

The coffin and the three boxes of possessions were placed in the canoe, a roof of boards was constructed over the central portion and covered with white cloth, and the headboard and footboard of the deceased's bedstead were nailed to the posts facing the water, each adorned with a dress. After a benediction, the party withdrew to the beach, leaving the father, mother, and brother to remain "ten or fifteen minutes, pounding on the canoe and [[mourning]]" (016-001).

### Gift-Giving and Secondary Burial

Following the canoe burial, the family distributed presents to the approximately fifteen attendees — a gun to Eells, blankets to several others, and money to the remainder. Short speeches concluded the ceremony. Eells recorded that this was an interim burial: "In about nine months it is expected that there will be a 'pot-latch' or distribution of money near this place," after which delegations from neighboring tribes would leave presents at the grave, and "soon after that shall be done she will be buried in the ground." Both parents cut off their hair as a sign of grief following the death (016-001).

## Historical Sources

The Clallam mortuary account in Yarrow derives from the Rev. M. Eells, whose firsthand participation in the funeral he described provides an exceptionally complete record of a single burial event — from the initial preparation of the body through the canoe elevation, the gift distribution, and the anticipated secondary ground burial. Eells' account is supplemented by [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]]' broader survey of [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington Territory burial ceremonies (016-004).
