## Overview

Florida, the southeastern peninsula of the United States, appears in Yarrow's 1880 monograph primarily through accounts of [[seminoles|Seminole]] burial practices and archaeological observations of aboriginal sand mounds. The region's mortuary customs combine elements of mound burial and scaffold exposure.

## Burial Sites & Practices

The [[seminoles|Seminoles]] of Florida practiced distinctive burial customs. Yarrow notes that "the [[seminoles|Seminoles]] of Florida are said to have buried in hollowed-out logs" or placed the dead in shallow graves covered with earth (008-001). This practice contrasts with the scaffold burial more common among Plains tribes.

Sand and earth mounds were common [[inhumation|interment]] sites. Yarrow records "Florida burial mounds" as a distinct category, noting their construction from sand and shell deposits (024-184, 024-278). Near Santa Fe Lake in northern Florida, the writer documented "two instances of burial in sand mounds," where remains were placed in flexed positions and covered with layers of sand and soil (010-026).

The funerals of Florida chiefs were elaborate affairs. A Spanish account cited by Yarrow describes how "the funerals of the chiefs of the Florida and [[carolina|Carolina]] Indians" involved the [[sacrifice]] of possessions, with "all the vessels and utensils" of the deceased being destroyed at the grave site (014-022). [[roman|Roman]]'s *History of Florida* (1775) provides additional details on chiefly burial ceremonies (015-001).
