## Overview

Death lament — also termed death wailing or funeral lamentation — refers to the ritualized vocal expression of grief practiced by numerous Native American tribes at the time of death and during mourning periods. These lamentations ranged from subdued, periodic cries to "the most dismal" and "wild and weird" outbursts, serving both as personal expressions of sorrow and as prescribed ceremonial obligations (001-027, 014-019). The practice was nearly universal across the North American continent, though its specific forms varied considerably by tribe and region. In Yarrow's 1880 survey questionnaire, the inquiry "How are the dead lamented?" was included as a core question for field observers, underscoring the centrality of lamentation to the study of indigenous mortuary customs (022-006).

## Tribal Distribution

The custom of death lamentation is documented among tribes from the Atlantic seaboard to the Pacific coast. The Choctaw, for instance, mourned common dead "for a period of three days" with ritual lamentation (015-006). Among the tribes of the Northwest Coast, the Chinook filled the air "with their lamentations" during funeral rites (014-004). The Sioux (Dakota) engaged in screaming, wailing, and tearing of hair as expressions of grief (010-018). On the southeastern coast, the Catawba and Congaree gathered around graves "wailing most pitifully, tearing their hair" (002-008). The practice extended to the Caribbean region, where the Caribs "lament loudly, their wailings being interspersed with comic remarks" — a striking blend of grief and humor that distinguished Carib lamentation from the more uniformly somber practices of mainland tribes (014-025).

## Historical Accounts

Several early observers recorded detailed accounts of lamentation practices. One account describes how mourners gathered "around the grave wailing most pitifully, tearing their hair" (002-008). Another notes that women would remain "fasting, smoking, and wailing out their lamentations for two or three days" (014-020). In Cherokee ceremonies, the widow "gave vent to her grief in the most piteous cries and wailings" during the preparation of funeral feasts (019-011). Among the tribes of the Plains, mourners "burst forth into a bitter wailing, some screaming as if in agony" (021-011). The Choctaw mourned for "a period of three days" with formalized lamentation rituals (015-006). In California, the Yo-kai-a widow "mingles the ashes of her dead husband with pitch making a white tar or unguent, with which she smears a band about two inches wide all around the edge of the hair," combining physical self-marking with vocal lamentation (021-011). These accounts consistently portray lamentation as a highly visible, audibly intense social ritual rather than a private expression of grief.

## Variations

Lamentation forms varied significantly across regions. Some tribes practiced loud, public wailing characterized by "terrific lamentations and yells" (020-002). Others employed a more rhythmic pattern of "alternate allelujah and lamentation, slowly proceeding on" (015-008). The duration also differed — from the Choctaw three-day period (015-006) to longer observances among other groups. The funeral lamentations of the Northwest Coast were described as "so wild and weird that they beggar description" (014-019). Gender roles were often prescribed: women typically led the lamentations, as recorded among the Cherokee where the widow's "bitter wailings and lamentations" formed a central part of the ceremony (019-011). Among some tribes, the lament was renewed periodically: "each year at the anniversary of their deceased friends this lamentation is renewed" (016-016), creating a recurring calendar of grief that bound the living to the dead across generations.

## Spiritual Significance

The lament served purposes beyond emotional expression. In many indigenous theologies, the vocalization of grief was believed to aid the deceased's spirit in its journey to the afterlife. The loud, public nature of the wailing — "filling the air with their lamentations" (014-004) — was not merely cathartic but cosmologically functional, announcing the death to the spirit world and guiding the soul on its path. The practice was sufficiently important that failure to properly lament the dead could be seen as a breach of both social and spiritual obligation.

## Related Practices

Death lamentation was closely intertwined with other mortuary customs, including [[mourning]], [[funeral-dance]], and [[feast-of-the-dead]]. Among many tribes, lamentation accompanied the preparation of the body and the funeral feast, forming part of a larger ceremonial complex rather than an isolated practice. The lament served both a ritual function — guiding the spirit of the deceased — and a social function — publicly demonstrating the depth of loss and the status of the deceased.
