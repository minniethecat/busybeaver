Cremation was practiced by numerous Native American tribes across the continent, from the Pacific Northwest to the Southeast. Yarrow documents multiple variants: partial cremation where only flesh was burned from bones, scaffold cremation where the body was placed on a pyre of logs, and pit cremation where remains were burned in excavated depressions. This page aggregates all pages that document cremation or cremation-related burial practices.

## Cremation in Context

Cremation among Native American tribes was not a single uniform practice. The Chinook of the Columbia River placed bodies in canoes and burned them with personal property. California tribes practiced pit cremation with elaborate mourning rituals. Southeastern groups combined cremation with mound burial, first reducing bodies to ashes before interring them in earthen structures. Yarrow's sources suggest that cremation was often reserved for individuals of high status, with commoners receiving earth or scaffold burial instead.
