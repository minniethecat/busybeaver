# Yuki

## Overview

The Yuki, known in Yarrow (1880) as the Yo-kai-a, inhabited the region of present-day Mendocino County, [[california|California]], near the Russian River drainage around the town of Ukiah — a place name derived from their own designation. Yarrow's account of Yuki mortuary customs, drawn from the fieldwork of [[powers-stephen|Stephen Powers]], provides one of the most detailed records of central [[california|California]] burial ceremonialism, documenting an elaborate assembly-house ritual, a three-day condolence dance, and [[mourning]] practices of unusual specificity.

## Mortuary Customs

### The Assembly-House Burial

The central institution of Yuki mortuary practice was the assembly-house, a semi-subterranean structure that served both civic and funerary functions. [[powers-stephen|Powers]] described the building as a pit approximately fifty feet in diameter and four or five feet deep, so heavily roofed with earth that the interior was damp and somber as a tomb. Entry was through a tunnel-like passage about ten feet long and four feet high, leading down to the level of the floor. The mouth of the tunnel was closed with brush, and the venerable sexton would not remove the barrier until he had slowly and devoutly paced several times to and fro before the entrance (021-007).

Inside, the massive roof was supported by peeled poles painted white and ringed with black, ornamented with rude devices. The floor was covered thick and green with sprouting wheat, which had been scattered to feed the spirit of the recently deceased captain of the tribe. This practice of providing spiritual sustenance to the dead chief through the medium of growing grain is a distinctive feature of Yuki funerary ritual not widely paralleled elsewhere in the Yarrow survey (021-009).

### The Condolence Dance

#### Ceremonial Setting and Officials

Upon the death of a Yuki chief, neighboring tribes sent deputations to offer condolence — a diplomatic as well as ritual obligation. [[powers-stephen|Powers]]' account describes a three-day dance held when the [[senel|Senel]] came to condole with the Yo-kai-a on the loss of their chief, the visitors being hosted at considerable expense to the bereaved tribe (021-009).

The ceremonial proceedings were overseen by four officials of the assembly-chamber, who wore black vests trimmed with red flannel and shell ornaments, together with an old man and a young woman who appeared to function as priest and priestess. The priestess wore a white dress covered with spots of red flannel cut in neat figures and ornamented with shells — a costume denoting ceremonial office distinct from the plain calico dresses of other women present (021-009).

Before the visitors entered, the older men reclined around the fire smoking and chatting. When the ceremonies commenced, the priest and priestess stood at the end opposite the entrance and inaugurated the exercises with a brief service of dedication, each speaking a few words and joining in a brief chant. The house was then thrown open to the visitors, who were seated along one side of the room with approximately two hundred persons assembled (021-009).

#### The Condolence Address and Communal Wailing

The chief of the visiting [[senel|Senel]] then made a brief speech referring to the death of the Yo-kai-a chief and offering the sympathy of his tribe. As he spoke, the women of the assembly scarcely refrained from crying out, and when he concluded the whole assemblage burst forth into a bitter [[mourning|wailing]] — some screaming as if in agony — a din so piercing that [[powers-stephen|Powers]] was compelled to stop his ears. This communal [[death-wailing|lamentation]] lasted three to five minutes, then ceased at a given signal, after which the participants wiped their eyes and quieted (021-011).

#### The Three-Day Dance Progression

The dance itself involved five principal male dancers, muscular and agile men profusely decorated with paint and feathers, their bodies marked with white and dark stripes. They wore feather mantles reaching below the knee, strings of shell ornaments about the neck, and crowns of eagle feathers. Each dancer held a whistle in his mouth as he performed — swaying, bending, and whirling with an agility and grace that [[powers-stephen|Powers]] described in vivid detail. They were assisted by a semicircle of twenty women who marked time with short steps. The women's dresses — white trimmed heavily with black velvet in stripes about three inches wide — indicated their [[mourning]] for the dead chief (021-011).

The three-day progression of the dance followed a deliberate emotional arc. The first day was slow and funereal, accompanied by a monotonous chant of only two tones, with the rattling of split sticks and stamping on a hollow slab. The second day saw livelier music employing airs of greater tonal range, with the women joining in the chorus. The third day, in accordance with Indian custom, was the most animated of all — an emotional trajectory [[powers-stephen|Powers]] compared to the return from a Christian funeral, which is apt to be much more jolly than the going out (021-011).

## Mourning Observances

### The Widow's Chaplet

Yuki widow's [[mourning]] was distinctive among all tribes documented by Yarrow. The widow mingled the ashes of her deceased husband with pitch, creating a white tar or unguent. With this substance she smeared a band approximately two inches wide all around the edge of her hair, which had been cut off close to the head. The effect, viewed from a little distance, was that of wearing a white chaplet — a visible token of bereavement worn upon the very body of the mourner (021-015).

### Feeding the Dead

The Yuki maintained a practice of feeding the spirits of the dead for the space of one full year, going daily to the places the deceased had frequented while living and sprinkling pinole upon the ground. A Yo-kai-a mother who had lost her infant performed an especially poignant ritual: every day for a year she visited the places where her child had played, or the spot where the body had been burned, and milked her breasts into the air. This act was accompanied by plaintive [[mourning]], weeping, and piteous calling upon the child to return, and sometimes by a hoarse and melancholy chant accompanied by a wild ecstatic swaying of the body (021-016).

## Historical Sources

The principal source on Yuki mortuary customs is [[powers-stephen|Stephen Powers]], whose fieldwork among the [[california|California]] tribes was published in *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1878, vol. iv, p. 164) and incorporated by Yarrow into his survey. The eyewitness account of the three-day dance is credited to Mr. [[menard-john|John]] Tenney, whose description [[powers-stephen|Powers]] reproduced with minor changes. Together these sources provide a remarkably detailed ethnographic record of central [[california|California]] funerary ceremonialism in the late nineteenth century.
