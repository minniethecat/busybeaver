# Santee

## Overview

The [[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree and Santee Indians]] inhabited the coastal plain and river valleys of present-day [[carolina|South Carolina]]. Although relatively small in population compared to the great confederacies of the interior Southeast, the Santee developed one of the most elaborate mortuary complexes documented in Yarrow (1880). Drawing on the early eighteenth-century observations of [[lawson-john|John Lawson]], preserved and transmitted through [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]]'s comprehensive *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States*, the Santee account provides an exceptionally detailed portrait of secondary burial, bone preservation, and the veneration of ancestral remains over multiple generations.

## Mortuary Customs

### Partial Embalmment and Sun Exposure

Upon death, the Santee laid the corpse upon a piece of bark in the sun, seasoning or [[embalming]] it with a small root beaten to powder "which looks as red as vermilion," mixed with bear's oil to beautify the hair. After a day or two of sun exposure, the body was removed and laid upon crotched sticks elevated from the earth, then anointed all over with the powdered root and bear's oil mixture. The body was then covered "very exactly with the bark of the pine or cypress tree to prevent any rain to fall upon it," and the ground around it was swept clean (Lawson, in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]).

### The Chief Mourner

The nearest of kin served as chief mourner, clad in moss, with a stick in his hand. For three or four days he kept a mournful dirge, his face blackened with the smoke of pitch pine mixed with bear's oil. Throughout this period, he recounted to the assembled relatives and spectators the identity of the deceased and the great feats performed in life — "all that he speaks tending to the praise of the defunct" (Lawson, in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]).

### Secondary Burial and Bone Preservation

Once the flesh grew mellow and would cleave from the bone — a process accelerated by the sun exposure and the [[embalming]] root — the soft tissue was removed and burned. The bones were then meticulously cleaned, anointed once more with the preserving root-and-oil mixture, and the skull was wrapped very carefully in a cloth "artificially woven of opossum's hair." The cleaned bones were preserved in a wooden box, and every year they were taken out, oiled, and cleansed anew. By these means, the Santee preserved ancestral remains for many ages — Lawson records that one might "see an Indian in possession of the bones of his grandfather or some of his relations of a longer antiquity" (Lawson, in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]).

### The Quiogozon — Royal Tomb

For persons of distinction, a more elaborate [[inhumation|interment]] was practiced. A roof of light wood or pitch pine was constructed over the grave, covered with bark and then with earth, leaving the body in a subterranean vault until the flesh separated from the bones. The bones were then taken up, cleaned, jointed, clad in white dressed deer skins, and laid away in the *Quiogozon* — "the royal tomb or burial place of their kings and war captains, being a more magnificent cabin reared at the public expense" (Lawson, in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]).

The Quiogozon was an object of profound veneration. Lawson notes that the king, old men, and conjurers would spend several days within it, communing with their idols and dead kings — a sanctuary into which outsiders could never gain admittance.

### Heroic Cairns

For warriors slain in battle, the Santee practiced a distinct form of commemoration. At the very place where an Indian fell, a heap of stones — or sticks where stones were unavailable — was raised. Every Indian who passed by added a stone to augment the heap, "in respect to the deceased hero" (Lawson, in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]). These way-side cairns, accumulative and collective, represented a form of memorial entirely distinct from the curated bone preservation of the settled burial grounds.

## Historical Sources

The primary source for Santee mortuary customs is [[lawson-john|John Lawson]], an early eighteenth-century explorer and naturalist whose detailed observations of [[carolina|Carolina]] Indian life were transmitted through [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]]'s *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1854). Yarrow preserved these accounts both in the body of his text and in his index under the entries "[[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree and Santee Indians]], partial embalmment of" and "embalmment, [[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree and Santee Indians]]" (024-133).
