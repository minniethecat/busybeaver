# Yanktonais

## Overview

The Yanktonais (also recorded as Yanktonias in Yarrow's index) constituted one of the principal divisions of the [[sioux|Sioux]] or [[dakota|Dakota]] people, inhabiting the northern Great Plains in the region of present-day North and South [[dakota|Dakota]]. As a Plains tribe whose territory lay at the heart of the bison range, the Yanktonais developed mortuary customs centered on [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]] — the practice of elevating the dead above the earth on scaffolds, placing them closer to the sky and out of reach of prairie scavengers. Yarrow (1880) used the Yanktonais as his primary example of scaffold burial construction, citing the detailed narrative of Belden, the White Chief.

## Mortuary Customs

### Scaffold Burial

The Yanktonais practiced scaffold burial as their principal mortuary mode, a custom they shared with many other Plains tribes. Scaffold burial belongs to the broader category of [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]] — burial above the ground — which Yarrow distinguishes from both surface burial and inhumation. As he notes in introducing the subject:

> From what can be learned, the choice of this mode depends greatly on the facilities present; where timber abounds, trees being used; if absent, scaffolds being employed (014-001).

The availability of timber in Yanktonais territory — the cottonwood groves along the [[missouri|Missouri]] River and its tributaries — provided the necessary materials for scaffold construction, making this the preferred method over [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]], which required standing timber of sufficient size.

### Scaffold Construction

The construction of the Yanktonais burial scaffold is described in detail by Belden:

> The construction of which among the Yanktonais is related as follows (014-001).

The scaffold typically consisted of four upright poles supporting a platform of horizontal poles or planks, elevated several feet above the ground. The deceased, wrapped in robes or blankets, was placed upon this platform along with personal possessions — weapons, pipes, and implements. The elevation served the practical function of protecting the body from wolves and other scavengers, while also carrying symbolic significance as a form of sky burial, returning the deceased to the open air and the elements that defined Plains life.

Yarrow's index contains two additional references: "Yanktonias" (024-353) and "Yanktonias, scaffold burial of" (024-409), confirming that scaffold burial was the defining mortuary trait of this [[sioux|Sioux]] division.

### Relationship to Sioux Mortuary Patterns

The Yanktonais shared the broader [[sioux|Sioux]] pattern of [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]], with variations among the different divisions. The [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[dakota|Santee Sioux]]) practiced both scaffold burial and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]], while the [[sioux]] in general employed a range of above-ground methods. The Yanktonais' preference for scaffolds over trees reflected the specific environmental conditions of their territory — the open prairie with its scattered river-bottom timber stands.

## Historical Sources

The principal source for Yanktonais scaffold burial is the autobiographical narrative of Belden, the White Chief, published in 1871. Belden's account, based on his extended residence among the Plains tribes, provides one of the most detailed contemporary descriptions of scaffold construction and the ritual context of Yanktonais burial. Yarrow incorporated this source into his comparative framework, positioning the Yanktonais as the representative example of Plains scaffold burial within the broader typology of North American mortuary customs.
