## Overview

Edwin Curtiss served as field assistant to [[putnam-fw|Prof. F. W. Putnam]], curator of the Peabody Museum of Archaeology and Ethnology at Cambridge, during the late 1870s. Working under [[putnam-fw|Putnam]]'s direction, Curtiss explored chambered mounds in Clay County, [[missouri|Missouri]], and spent over two years excavating stone graves and mounds in the [[tennessee|Cumberland Valley]] of [[tennessee|Tennessee]]. His careful field observations of [[cremation]] burials within stone chambers provided Yarrow with a key example of secondary burial in mound-building cultures, connecting the evidence of burnt skeletal remains to the architectural features of the burial structures themselves (004-003). Curtiss subsequently participated in excavations of the [[tennessee|Cumberland Valley]] mounds and stone graves, a campaign that extended for over two years and yielded several thousand carefully opened graves (004-009).

## Contributions

### Missouri Excavations

Curtiss's principal contribution to the Yarrow corpus comes through his [[missouri|Missouri]] excavations, where he opened three chambered mounds in a large group situated on both sides of the [[missouri|Missouri]] River in eastern Clay County. The chambers, each approximately eight feet square and four-and-a-half to five feet high, had passageways several feet in length leading from the southern side and opening onto the mound edge. Their walls, about two feet thick, were carefully constructed of stones laid evenly without clay or mortar. One chamber had a roof of large flat rocks; the others appeared to have been closed with timber. Crucially, each chamber was filled with burnt clay, and the inside walls bore signs of fire exposure. Beneath the burnt clay, Curtiss found fragmentary remains of multiple human skeletons — he estimated five in one chamber and thirteen in another — all burnt to such an extent that only small bone fragments remained, mixed with ash and charcoal. A few flint implements and minute pottery fragments accompanied the skeletal material (004-004).

In addition to the chambered mounds, Curtiss opened a nearby large mound that lacked chambers entirely. This mound contained no burnt bodies but proved remarkably rich in large flint implements, well-made pottery, and a distinctive red-stone gorget. The contrasting burial treatments — cremated remains in stone chambers versus unburnt [[inhumation|interment]]s in earth mounds — raised the question of whether the chamber burials represented secondary deposition following primary [[cremation]] elsewhere. Yarrow recorded that a nearby large mound, though lacking chambers and [[cremation]]s, proved rich in flint implements, pottery, and a distinctive red-stone gorget, suggesting distinct cultural practices at the same site (004-005).

### Tennessee Excavations

Curtiss's [[tennessee|Tennessee]] work, conducted over more than two years, encompassed the thorough exploration of numerous mound varieties and several thousand stone graves of the region's mound builders. Under [[putnam-fw|Putnam]]'s direction, this fieldwork also documented a walled town on the Lindsley estate, where a burial mound containing sixty skeletons — each in a carefully made stone grave arranged in two rows forming a square — was accompanied by the discovery of approximately seventy house sites, under whose clay floors children had been interred, a practice [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] interpreted as a regular custom distinct from the communal adult burial mound (004-009).

Curtiss's field records, communicated by [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] to the Boston Society of Natural History (1878), provide the primary documentation of [[mound-burial|chambered-mound]] [[cremation]] burial in Clay County, [[missouri|Missouri]], and of the extensive stone-grave complex of the [[tennessee|Cumberland Valley]]. His observations of burnt-clay chamber fill, differential treatment of adults and children in the [[tennessee|Tennessee]] town site, and the architectural details of stone-lined passage-graves constitute the evidentiary core for Yarrow's discussion of mound burial in the central United States.

## Reception and Scholarly Assessment

Curtiss's field observations, communicated to the scholarly community through [[putnam-fw|Prof. F. W. Putnam]]'s presentation to the Boston Society of Natural History on October 15, 1878, were received as an important contribution to the then-emerging body of evidence for cremation practices among the mound-building cultures of the central United States. The discovery that chambers were filled with burnt clay — with walls showing fire exposure and fragmentary skeletal remains mixed with ash beneath the fill — provided a physical context for a practice that earlier observers had only inferred from scattered reports (004-003).

Yarrow incorporated Curtiss's [[missouri|Missouri]] findings into his broader synthesis as the principal exemplar of secondary [[cremation]] deposition within stone chambers, placing it alongside the [[cremation]] practices documented among the [[takouli|Takouli]] and other western tribes to argue for the wide geographic distribution of the custom. The detail of Curtiss's reporting — specifying chamber dimensions, wall thickness, orientation of passageways, and the distinction between chambered mounds with [[cremation]]s and unchambered mounds without — was precisely the type of archaeologically rigorous evidence that distinguished the 1880 monograph from earlier compendiums of traveler's tales.

The subsequent two-year excavation campaign in the [[tennessee|Cumberland Valley]], conducted under [[putnam-fw|Putnam]]'s direction, marked a significant expansion of scope: from the investigation of isolated mound features to the systematic exploration of an entire settlement complex, including burial mounds, house sites, and defensive earthworks. The discovery that children were interred beneath house floors while adults were consigned to communal burial mounds provided one of the earliest documented instances of age-graded mortuary treatment in North American archaeology, a finding that anticipated later studies of social stratification in Mississippian-period societies. Curtiss's field records, though filtered through [[putnam-fw|Putnam]]'s presentations, thus constitute a foundational dataset for understanding the variability of mortuary practice within a single mound-builder community.
