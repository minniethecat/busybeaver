# Secondary Burial

## Overview

Secondary burial — the practice of exhuming remains after initial decomposition and redepositing the cleaned bones in a communal repository — was among the most distinctive mortuary customs of the mound-building tribes of eastern North America (004-006). It represented a two-stage relationship with the dead: an initial temporary disposition followed, often years later, by a ceremonial collective reburial.

This custom was intimately connected with the construction of burial mounds. Yarrow noted that mounds "were for the most part used for secondary burial, although intrusions were doubtless common" (004-011). The practice served both practical purposes — consolidating remains as villages relocated — and ritual functions, marking the final liberation of the soul.

[[brinton-dg|Brinton]] observed the geographic extent of this custom: "East of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] nearly every nation was accustomed at stated periods — usually once in eight or ten years — to collect and clean the osseous remains of those of its number who had died in the intervening time, and inter them in one common sepulcher, lined with choice furs, and marked with a mound of wood, stone, or earth" (015-013). This periodicity gave secondary burial the character of a calendrical ritual, uniting dispersed bands in a shared obligation to the dead.

## The Two-Stage Process

The secondary burial process proceeded in two distinct stages. In the first stage, the body was placed on an elevated scaffold, buried temporarily in the ground, or deposited in a [[ossuary|charnel-house]], where it remained until decomposition had reduced the flesh sufficiently for bone cleaning (015-001). The duration of this exposure varied by tribe: among the [[choctaw|Choctaw]], the scaffold period ranged from a few weeks to three or four months, typically half that time (015-003).

In the second stage, designated undertakers — described among the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] as "a certain set of venerable old Gentlemen who wear very long nails as a distinguishing badge on the thumb, fore and middle finger of each hand" (015-003) — stripped the remaining flesh from the bones. These bone-cleaners scraped the bones clean with their fingernails, burned the scrapings, painted the skull with red vermillion, and placed the cleaned remains in a specially prepared chest or coffin (015-003).

The [[natchez|Natchez]] practiced a variant in which the body was placed in an elevated tomb near the temple. After the flesh had rotted away, "the bones were taken out, placed in a box made of canes, and then deposited in the temple" (015-006). The [[burial-vaults|tombs]] themselves were "about eight feet long and a foot and a half wide," constructed on four forked sticks, with a basket-work of twigs covered in mud woven around the corpse (015-006).

## Tribal Distribution

### Choctaw

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] practiced a clearly documented form of secondary burial. [[bartram-william|Bartram]] recorded that when the [[ossuary|charnel-house]] was full, a general inhumation occurred: "the respective coffins are borne by the nearest relatives of the deceased to the place of [[inhumation|interment]], where they are all piled one upon another in the form of a pyramid, and the conical hill of earth heaped above" (004-032). The ceremony concluded with a "feast of the dead" (004-032).

A more detailed account describes the scaffold exposure of the corpse until the flesh could be stripped from the bones, after which the cleaned bones were placed in "a curiously-wrought chest or coffin, fabricated of bones and splints" and deposited in the [[bone-house]] (015-008). When the [[bone-house]] was full, the coffins were borne in procession to the place of general [[inhumation|interment]], where they were piled in a pyramid and covered with earth — producing the conical mounds that [[bartram-william|Bartram]] and other early observers described (015-008).

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] also maintained a second, more intimate ritual: after one year in the [[bone-house]], the chest of a notable person would be taken down, the head "refreshed" with red paint and the box repainted, before final deposition "to lasting oblivion" (015-003).

### Iroquois

The [[iroquois|Iroquois]] exposed their dead on bark scaffolds until only skeletons remained, then preserved them in family bark-houses. [[morgan-lh|Morgan]] reported that "after the lapse of a number of years, or in a season of public insecurity, or on the eve of abandoning a settlement, it was customary to collect these skeletons from the whole community around and consign them to a common resting place" (015-010).

[[morgan-lh|Morgan]] further noted the archaeological correlate: "To this custom, which is not confined to the [[iroquois|Iroquois]], is doubtless to be ascribed the barrows and bone-mounds which have been found in such numbers in various parts of the country. On opening these mounds the skeletons are usually found arranged in horizontal layers, a conical pyramid, those in each layer radiating from a common center" (015-011). This systematic arrangement within the mounds distinguished secondary from primary burial deposits.

### Huron

The [[huron|Huron]] "Feast of the Dead" was witnessed by the Jesuit missionary [[brebeuf-p-de|De Brebeuf]] at the village of Ossosane, where "the people belonging to five large villages deposited the bones of their dead in a gigantic shroud, composed of forty-eight robes, each robe being made of ten beaver skins" (020-005). The ceremony took place before two thousand Indians, who offered twelve hundred presents as tokens of grief (020-005). According to [[huron|Huron]] belief, "the souls of the dead remain near the bodies until the 'feast of the dead'; after which ceremony they become free, and can at once depart for the land of spirits, which they believe to be situated in the regions of the setting sun" (020-005). A wall of stones was built around the vast [[ossuary]] to protect it, and before covering the bones with earth, women threw grains of Indian corn upon the sacred relics (020-005).

### Natchez

The [[natchez|Natchez]] maintained a distinctive form of secondary burial centered on temple storage. After initial decomposition in an elevated tomb, the cleaned bones were deposited in the temple in cane boxes (015-006). This practice kept the remains of the dead within the sacred precinct of the community, unlike the [[mound-burial]] tradition that removed them to a separate burial ground.

### Mound-Builder Region

In the [[tennessee|Cumberland Valley]] of [[tennessee|Tennessee]], chambered mounds contained remains that "were used for secondary burials, the bodies having first been cremated" (004-006). This [[cremation]]-first variant produced charred bone fragments mixed with ashes and charcoal, representing a distinct regional expression of the secondary-burial tradition (004-006).

## Archaeological Evidence

The archaeological signature of secondary burial was distinctive. [[morgan-lh|Morgan]] noted that skeletons in bone-mounds were "usually found arranged in horizontal layers, a conical pyramid, those in each layer radiating from a common center," while in other cases "they are found placed promiscuously" (015-011). The layered, radial arrangement suggested deliberate placement by mourners rather than casual deposition, consistent with the ceremonial nature of the secondary burial event.

The chambered mounds of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] revealed cremated bone fragments — evidence that fire-based initial disposal preceded the collection and mound deposition of remains (004-006). This variant, in which [[cremation|burning]] replaced scaffold-exposure as the first stage, produced a different archaeological and osteological profile than the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] and [[iroquois|Iroquois]] pattern.

## Significance

Secondary burial served as a unifying social ritual, periodically bringing together dispersed bands to honor their collective dead. The communal [[ossuary]] and the mound erected over it became visible monuments of tribal identity across generations. The practice also marked a theological transition: as the [[huron|Huron]] believed, the soul remained bound to the body until the communal reburial ceremony liberated it for the journey to the land of spirits (020-005).
