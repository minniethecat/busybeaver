# Burial Superstition

## Overview

The tribes of North America maintained an elaborate system of superstitions regarding death and burial — beliefs that governed everything from the treatment of corpses to the utterance of the names of the deceased. Yarrow observed that "an entire volume might well be written which should embrace only an account of the superstitions regarding death and burial among the Indians" (021-029).

These superstitions fell into several categories: taboos on naming the dead, beliefs about the soul's journey to the spirit land, practices to drive away hostile spirits, and consequences for those who violated burial customs.

## Tribal Distribution

Burial superstitions were documented across the continent, with particularly detailed accounts from the tribes of [[california|California]] and the Great Lakes region. [[chippewa|Chippewa]], [[hidatsa|Hidatsa]], Karok, Kelta, Mat-toal, Tolowa, Yurok, and Mosquito tribes all maintained distinctive bodies of mortuary superstition recorded by nineteenth-century observers (024-136)(024-201)(024-237)(024-239)(024-266)(024-387)(024-413)(021-038). The [[california|California]] tribes — Karok, Yurok, Tolowa, Kelta, and Mat-toal — shared particularly strong [[name-taboo]]s and beliefs about the soul's transmigration (021-036)(021-037). The Algonkin and [[chippewa|Chippewa]] traditions emphasized the four-day journey and the serpent-bridge crossing to the spirit land (021-027)(021-033).

## Name Taboo

One of the most widespread superstitions was the prohibition against speaking the name of the deceased. Among the Karok of [[california|California]], "the highest crime one can commit is the pet-chi-e-ri, the mere mention of the dead relative's name. It is a deadly insult to the survivors and can be atoned for only by the same amount of blood money paid for willful murder" (021-036). The Karok believed that "at the mention of his name the moldering skeleton turns in his grave and groans" (021-036).

The Tolowa shared this observance. When [[powers-stephen|Powers]] asked the chief Tahhokolli for certain native words, "he shook his head mournfully and said 'all dead,' 'all dead,' 'no good'" (021-037). The Mosquito Indians of Central America "studiously and superstitiously avoid mentioning the name of the dead" (021-038), indicating the wide geographic spread of this belief.

## The Soul's Journey

### The Slippery Pole

The Yurok of [[california|California]] believed that after death, spirits "are compelled to cross an extremely attenuated greasy pole, which bridges over the chasm of the debatable land, and that they require the fire to light them on their darksome journey" (021-028). A righteous soul traversed the pole faster than a wicked one, and the number of nights a fire was kept [[cremation|burning]] at the grave was regulated accordingly (021-028).

### The Serpent Bridge

The [[chippewa|Chippewas]] believed the soul must cross a stream "upon a large snake that answers the purpose of a bridge." Those who died by drowning were thrown into the stream and remained there forever. Souls in a trance or lethargy who were refused passage "return to their bodies and reanimate them" (021-033).

In the [[chippewa|Chippewa]] land of souls, the good "are free from pain, they have no duties to perform, their time is spent in dancing and singing and they feed upon mushrooms which are very abundant," while bad souls are haunted by the phantoms of those they injured in life (021-034).

### Four-Night Vigil

The Algonkins and Mexicans shared a belief that the soul required four days to complete its journey to the spirit land. During each of these four nights, a fire was kindled on the grave "to light the spirit on its journey" (021-027). The [[hidatsa|Hidatsa]] likewise held that "when a [[hidatsa|Hidatsa]] dies his shade lingers four nights around the camp or village in which he died" before departing for the "village of the dead" (021-030).

## Transmigration and Spirit Birds

The Mat-toal held that "the good depart to a happy region somewhere southward in the great ocean, but the soul of a bad Indian transmigrates into a grizzly bear, which they consider of all animals the cousin-german of sin" (021-037). The Kelta believed that at death "a little bird flies away with his soul to the spirit land. If he was a bad Indian a hawk will catch the little bird and eat him up soul and feathers" (021-037).
