# Ghost Beliefs

Across North American Indian tribes, the **ghost** — the restless or returning spirit of the dead — occupied a prominent place in mortuary belief. Unlike the soul that peacefully journeyed to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]], the ghost was regarded with fear and was the object of elaborate propitiatory and apotropaic rituals.

## The Ghost Gamble

Among the [[sioux|Sioux]], the "ghost gamble" or spirit-lodge ceremony was a formal ritual for communicating with the recently dead. One account from an observer stationed among the Wahpeton and Sisseton [[sioux|Sioux]] "furnishes a detailed and interesting account of what is called the 'ghost gamble'" [021-019]. At this ceremony, held "at the time of the first feast held over the remains of the deceased," the near relatives took charge of the effects and organized a ritual gathering [021-020]. Seeds were used in the gamble — "two seeds are simply blackened on one side the reverse containing nothing" — and the game was understood as a vehicle for the ghost to communicate with the living [021-022].

## Fear of the Returning Dead

Ghost beliefs were not limited to formal ceremonies. The widespread practice of destroying or removing the deceased's property stemmed partly from the fear that possessions left behind would attract the ghost's return. Among the [[ute|Ute]], the destruction of weapons and effects at a male burial was explicitly linked to severing the connection between the dead and the living world [005-008].

The practice of avoiding the name of the dead, documented among [[california|California]] and Central American tribes, served a similar function: to speak the name was to risk summoning the ghost. "The Mosquito Indians of Central America studiously and superstitiously avoid mentioning the name of the dead" [021-038]. [[powers-stephen|Powers]] observed of the Tolowa that they "share in the superstitious observance for the memory of the dead which is common to the Northern [[california|California]]n tribes" — including name avoidance [021-037].

## Locks of Hair as Tokens

Hair torn from the heads of mourners was sometimes suspended near burial sites. On one scaffold burial, "some hair was suspended, which we at first mistook for a scalp, but our guide informed us that these were locks of hair torn from their heads by the relatives to testify their grief" [014-008]. This practice, while primarily an expression of [[mourning]], also carried connotations of ghost-appeasement — the offering of a part of oneself to the spirit of the deceased.

## Regional Variation

Ghost beliefs varied in intensity and elaboration. Southeastern tribes, with their elaborate secondary burial ceremonies, conceived of the ghost as a transitional entity that required proper ritual treatment before the soul could be released to its final destination [015-013]. Plains tribes focused more on the immediate danger posed by the ghost during the days following death, when "the soul of the deceased is in danger from _O-mah-u_ or the devil" [001-020].
