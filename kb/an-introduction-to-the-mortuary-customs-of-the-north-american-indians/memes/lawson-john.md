## Overview

John Lawson was an early eighteenth-century explorer and naturalist who traveled through the [[carolina|Carolinas]] and documented the customs of the indigenous peoples of the region. His observations of [[carolina|Carolina]] Indian burial practices, preserved in [[jones|Jones]]'s *Antiquities of the Southern Indians* (1873) and [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States*, provided Yarrow with some of the most detailed accounts of Eastern Woodland mortuary customs in the 1880 monograph.

## Contributions

Lawson's accounts contributed to Yarrow's descriptions of two distinct burial customs. First, Lawson documented the elaborate funeral ceremonies of the [[carolina|Carolina]] tribes, in which the corpse was initially placed in a cane bundle and deposited in an outhouse, and later carried to the grave by four young men attended by relatives and the entire community (001-004). The grave, approximately six feet deep and eight feet long, featured pitch-pine forks driven into the ground at each end to support poles that received the corpse (001-007). Second, Lawson described a process of partial embalmment practiced by the [[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree]] or [[congaree-santee-indians|Santee Indians]] of [[carolina|South Carolina]], in which the remains were placed in boxes supported above ground by crotched sticks rather than interred in caves (006-014).

## Accounts Provided

- **[[carolina|Carolina]] funeral ceremonies**: Detailed account of the multi-stage burial process among the [[carolina|Carolina]] tribes, from initial cane-bundle deposition to the communal grave ceremony (001-004, 001-007).

- **[[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree]]-[[santee|Santee]] partial embalmment**: Description of above-ground box burial with partial embalmment among the [[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree]] and [[santee|Santee]] peoples of [[carolina|South Carolina]] (006-014).
