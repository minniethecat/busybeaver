# Indian Removal

## Overview

The Indian Removal Act of 1830, signed into law by President Andrew Jackson, authorized the federal government to negotiate treaties exchanging Native American lands in the Southeastern United States for territory west of the [[mississippi|Mississippi River]] — a region designated as Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma). What began as a policy of "voluntary" relocation quickly became a program of forced displacement, affecting the [[choctaw|Choctaw]], [[cherokee|Cherokee]], [[creek|Creek]], [[chickasaw|Chickasaw]], and [[seminoles|Seminole]] peoples. The deaths of thousands along the "Trail of Tears" — and the severing of ancestral ties to burial grounds maintained for countless generations — represented one of the most profound disruptions of Native American mortuary traditions in history.

## Impact on Mortuary Customs

### Severance from Ancestral Burial Grounds

The forced relocation severed tribal communities from their ancestral burial grounds — the ossuaries, mounds, and cemeteries where generations of ancestors had been interred with careful ceremony. The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] [[burial-vaults|bone-houses]], the [[cherokee|Cherokee]] burial mounds, and the [[creek|Creek]] ceremonial grounds were abandoned, never to be revisited by the descendants who had maintained them for centuries. This rupture in the physical continuity of burial practice had profound spiritual and cultural consequences.

### Adaptation in Indian Territory

In Indian Territory, the relocated tribes faced the challenge of reconstructing their mortuary traditions in an unfamiliar landscape. Traditional burial structures — the scaffolds, [[burial-vaults|bone-houses]], and charnel platforms of the Southeast — were adapted to the new environment. The proximity of different tribal groups in the confined space of Indian Territory also led to cultural exchange and hybridization of mortuary practices. Yarrow's correspondents, writing from posts in and around Indian Territory, documented customs that had already undergone significant transformation since the Removal era.

### The Role of Military Correspondents

Many of the military officers and Indian agents who served as Yarrow's correspondents were stationed at forts and agencies established in the wake of Removal. Their observations of mortuary customs thus captured practices at a specific historical moment: after the trauma of dislocation but before the full assimilation pressures of the [[reservation-system|reservation era]]. The accounts of burial customs among the relocated [[choctaw|Choctaw]] (015-001)(015-007), for instance, reflect traditions maintained in exile, preserved through the deliberate effort of elders and ritual specialists who carried sacred knowledge across the [[mississippi|Mississippi]].

## The Yarrow Survey in Historical Context

Yarrow conducted his mortuary customs survey in the late 1870s, roughly forty years after the peak of the Removal era. By this time, the generation that had experienced Removal firsthand was passing away, and with them, direct knowledge of pre-Removal burial customs was being lost. Yarrow's questionnaire (022-001) was thus not merely an academic exercise but an urgent salvage operation — an attempt to document customs before they disappeared entirely.

The military officers and physicians who responded to Yarrow's circular were often the same personnel responsible for managing the practical consequences of Removal: overseeing reservation boundaries, administering annuities, and mediating conflicts between relocated tribes and the Plains groups whose territories they now shared. Their dual role — as agents of the policy that disrupted traditional customs and as the recorders of those same customs — created a complex documentary legacy that scholars continue to unpack.

## Related Events and Policies

The Indian Removal policy was not an isolated event but part of a broader pattern of federal action that reshaped Native American life in the nineteenth century. The establishment of the [[reservation-system|reservation system]] in the 1850s extended the logic of Removal to tribes across the West. The allotment policies of the 1880s (Dawes Act) would further erode tribal land bases and, with them, traditional burial access. Yarrow's 1880 monograph thus appeared at a pivotal moment — after Removal but before allotment — capturing mortuary customs in a state of traumatic transition.
