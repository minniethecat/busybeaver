# Dance Rituals

## Overview

Ceremonial dances formed an integral part of funeral and mourning practices among numerous North American Indian tribes. These dances served multiple purposes: honoring the deceased, expressing communal grief, and, in some traditions, facilitating the soul's passage to the afterlife. Yarrow (1880) documented dance rituals across a range of tribal groups, from the Eastern Woodlands to the Pacific Coast.

## Choctaw Funeral Dance

Among the [[choctaw|Choctaw]], the funeral ceremony described by Benson in 1860 included "the disposition of the body, mourning feast and dance" as an integrated sequence. The dance component was not merely expressive but constituted a formal element of the obsequies, performed in conjunction with feasting and the burial itself (019-008).

## Omaha and Plains Traditions

The funeral ceremonies of the [[omaha|Omaha]] chief Blackbird exemplified the elaborate nature of high-status mortuary rites on the Plains. The obsequies were "romantically conceived, and carried out to the fullest possible extent in accordance with the *ante mortem* wishes of the dead," involving a series of ritual performances in which dance likely played a part alongside the construction of an elevated burial site (008-004).

## Scaffold Burial Dances

In the context of scaffold burial, mourners performed ritual acts that included dance-like movements. When scaffolds bearing the dead were visited, mourners would tear locks of hair from their heads and suspend them from the scaffold — actions accompanied by formalized movements and vocalizations that constituted a form of ritual dance. These observances, witnessed along the upper [[missouri|Missouri River]], demonstrated the integration of dance into the broader mortuary complex (014-008).

## California Tribal Practices

Among the [[california|California]] tribes, dance and ritual performance were central to funeral observances. The Se-nel people held beliefs about the afterlife in which "bad Indians return into coyotes" while "others fall off a bridge which all souls must traverse." Funeral dances enacted aspects of these beliefs, reinforcing communal understanding of the soul's journey after death (010-020).

## Ceremonial Context

Dance rituals typically occurred as part of larger funeral complexes that also included feasting, gift-giving, and the construction of burial structures. The dances were performed "as a secondary or adjunct part of the funeral ceremony," accompanying rather than replacing the primary rites of body disposition (021-019).
