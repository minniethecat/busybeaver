# Bone House

A bone house was a specialized structure used by certain Native American tribes for the temporary or permanent storage of human skeletal remains after preliminary above-ground decomposition. The practice was most notably documented among the [[choctaw|Choctaws]] and [[iroquois|Iroquois]]. [015-001]

## Choctaw Bone Houses

According to an early account by [[roman|Roman]] in his _History of [[florida|Florida]]_ (1775), the [[choctaw|Choctaws]] practiced a distinctive two-stage burial process. Upon death, a stage or scaffold was erected and the corpse was laid upon it, covered with a bear skin. If the deceased was a man of note, the stage was decorated and the poles painted red with vermillion and bear's oil. If the deceased was a child, it was put upon stakes set across the stage. At this stage, relations would come to weep and mourn. [015-001] [015-002]

The stage was fenced round with poles and remained for a variable period — sometimes extended to three or four months, but seldom more than half that time. A particular set of venerable old men, who wore very long nails as a distinguishing badge on the thumb, fore, and middle finger of each hand, constantly traveled through the nation. When the flesh became putrid and easily separated from the bones, these men would clean the bones and deposit them in a hut built for that purpose, called a bone house. Each town had one of these structures. After remaining there for about one year, the bones were buried. Enemies and those who committed suicide were buried under the earth directly, being considered unworthy of the ceremonial obsequies and [[mourning]]. [015-002] [015-003] [015-004]

[[bartram-william|Bartram]], in his _Travels_ (1791), provided a similar account: the [[choctaw|Choctaws]] erected a scaffold 18 or 20 feet high in a grove adjacent to the town, where the corpse was laid lightly covered with a mantle and left to decompose, visited and protected by friends and relatives. After the bones were cleaned, they were placed in a neatly made chest and carried to the bone house. [015-007] [015-008]

## Iroquois Practice

[[morgan-lh|Morgan]], in his _League of the [[iroquois|Iroquois]]_ (1851), described how the body of the deceased was exposed upon a bark scaffolding erected upon poles, or secured upon the limbs of trees, where it was left to waste to a skeleton. After decomposition in the open air, the bones were removed either to the former house of the deceased or to a small bark house by its side, prepared for their reception. In this manner, the skeletons of entire families were preserved for many years. [015-010] [015-011]

[[morgan-lh|Morgan]] further noted that this custom was the probable origin of the barrows and bone mounds found throughout the country. On opening these mounds, skeletons were usually found arranged in horizontal layers forming a conical pyramid, with those in each layer radiating from a common center. [015-011]

## Ossuary Tradition

[[brinton-dg|Brinton]], in _Myths of the New World_ (1868), recorded that east of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] nearly every nation was accustomed at stated periods — usually once every eight or ten years — to collect and clean the osseous remains of those who had died in the intervening time, and inter them in one common sepulcher lined with choice furs and marked with a mound of wood, stone, or earth. These collective [[inhumation|interment]]s produced the immense tumuli found throughout the Eastern United States. [015-013]

## Regional Distribution

The bone house custom and the associated two-stage burial practice were widespread east of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]], though they do not appear to have prevailed among the Indians of the northwest coast. The practice reflects a cultural emphasis on collective ancestral memorialization distinct from the individual grave burial more common among other tribes. [015-019]
