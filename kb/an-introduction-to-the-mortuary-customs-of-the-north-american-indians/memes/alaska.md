# Alaska

## Overview

Alaska, the vast northwestern territory of North America, was home to numerous indigenous peoples including the [[Aleut]], [[Innuit]] ([[innuit|Inuit]]), and various Athabascan groups. Yarrow (1880) documented several distinctive Alaskan mortuary customs, most notably the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian practice of cave burial with mummification (005-003)(006-005).

## Burial Sites & Practices

### Aleutian Cave Burial and Mummies
The [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands produced some of the most celebrated examples of American mummification. Bodies were carefully wrapped and placed in elevated positions within dry volcanic caves, where natural conditions — low humidity and constant cool temperatures — preserved them for extended periods. Early Russian explorers and traders reported finding remarkably intact remains, some still wrapped in sea-otter furs and accompanied by hunting implements (006-005)(006-010).

### Cave Burial
Beyond the [[aleut|Aleut]]ians, cave burial was practiced by other Alaskan native groups. The dry caves of the Alaskan interior and coastal regions provided natural [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]], protecting bodies from scavengers and the elements. This practice, also documented in [[utah|Utah]] and [[california|California]], represented one of the major burial modes of western North America (005-003).

## Associated Tribes

- [[Aleut]] — Island people known for mummification in caves
- [[Innuit]] — Arctic peoples of northern Alaska
