## Overview

The Cascade Range, extending from northern [[california|California]] through [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington into British Columbia, forms the great divide between the humid Pacific coastal region and the arid interior plateau. The [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] cuts through the range at the Columbia River Gorge, creating a corridor of indigenous settlement and trade that Yarrow's 1880 monograph documents through the accounts of early nineteenth-century maritime explorers. At the Cascades themselves, a distinctive form of canoe burial was practiced that differed from the method used on the lower river, illustrating the variability of mortuary custom even within a single river system.

## Burial Sites and Practices

### Corpse Island and Coffin Mount

Captain Belcher of the British ship *Sulphur*, who visited the Columbia River in 1839, observed that following a smallpox epidemic in 1826 and a subsequent ague outbreak, "Corpse Island and Coffin Mount, as well as the adjacent shores, were studded" with indigenous burials. The epidemic had so devastated the local population that the river islands became mass burial grounds — canoe cemeteries where the dead were deposited in their vessels according to the regional [[canoe-burial]] tradition (016-006).

### Cascade Burial: A Different Form

Farther up the Columbia, at the Cascades themselves, a different burial method was adopted. Captain Clarke described the practice as involving burial places constructed in a form distinct from the canoe interments of the lower river (016-008). The position of the body was of universal observance among the Columbia River tribes: the head was always placed to the west (016-008). The reason assigned to Clarke was "that the road to the *me-mel-us-illa-hee*, the country of the dead, is toward the west, and if they place them otherwise they would be confused" (016-008). This westward orientation of the deceased is one of the most consistently observed features of Chinookan mortuary practice across the entire Columbia River system.

### Sepulcher Island and the Watlala

A notable burial site documented by Belcher was Sepulcher Island in the Columbia River, located a few miles above the Cascades. The island served as the principal cemetery for the Watlala, a tribe of the Upper [[tsinuk|Chinook]] who were described as "now nearly extinct" by the time of Belcher's visit. Despite the tribe's catastrophic demographic decline, "a number of the sepulchers still remain in different states of preservation" on the island, serving as mute testimony to the former population of the Cascade-Columbia corridor (016-008). The Watlala's canoe burials, elevated on islands rather than in forests, represent a distinctive variant of the Northwest Coast canoe-interment tradition.

### Grave Secrecy and Sacred Protection

Captain Belcher recorded that "great secrecy is observed in all their burial ceremonies, partly from fear of Europeans," and that among the Columbia River tribes, violation of a tomb was regarded as an offense of the first magnitude (016-011). The tribes would "instantly punish by death any violation of the tomb or wage war if perpetrated by another tribe," and they were "inveterate and tenaceously bent on revenge should they discover that any act of the kind has been perpetrated by a white man" (016-011). A documented instance involved "part of the crew of a vessel on her return to this port" suffering consequences because a person formerly associated with the vessel had taken a skull as an object of curiosity (016-011). For chiefs, the canoes were "repainted, decorated, and greater care taken by placing them in sequestered spots" (016-011), while for common persons, family pride was satisfied with gathering the bones after decay and wrapping them in a new mat (016-011).

### Child Burial Practices

Belcher also noted the varied disposal methods for children among the Columbia River tribes, recording that "the corpses of children are variously disposed of; sometimes by suspending them, at others by placing in the hollows of trees." A cemetery on the river shore was observed with "canoes raised from the ground on stages ... containing skeletons of children only" (016-013). The separate treatment of child remains — neither accorded the full canoe burial of adults nor simply abandoned — suggests a graded mortuary system that differentiated by both age and status.

### East of the Cascades: Plains Influence

East of the Cascade Mountains, the tribes "whose habits are equestrian, and who use canoes only for ferriage or transportation purposes, bury their dead, usually heaping over them piles of stones, either to mark the spot or to prevent the bodies from being exhumed by the prairie wolf" (016-008). Among the Yakamas, graves were placed "in conspicuous points of the basaltic walls which line the lower valleys, and designated by a clump of poles planted over them, from which fluttered various articles of dress" (016-008). Formerly, these prairie tribes "killed horses over the graves — a custom now falling into disuse in consequence of the teachings of the whites" (016-008). The sharp divide between the canoe-burial peoples of the western Cascades and the stone-heap burial peoples of the eastern plateau marks the Cascade Range as not merely a geographic divide but a cultural boundary between distinct mortuary provinces.
