# Cheyenne

## Overview

The Cheyenne (Tsitsistas) are an Algonquian-speaking people of the Great Plains who, by the nineteenth century, had migrated from the western Great Lakes region to the central Plains, where they adopted the equestrian bison-hunting culture characteristic of the region. Yarrow's 1880 monograph includes a detailed account of a Cheyenne burial case documented by Brevet Major [[sternberg-george-m|George M. Sternberg]], a U.S. Army surgeon, and transmitted to the Army Medical Museum in Washington, D.C., by Dr. [[catlin-george|George]] A. Otis. The case provides one of the most meticulously recorded examples of Plains scaffold burial, notable for the extraordinary care and material wealth devoted to the internment of what proved to be a child (024-303).

## Mortuary Customs

The Cheyenne practiced scaffold burial as their primary mode of [[inhumation|interment]]. The documented burial case was found on the banks of Walnut [[creek|Creek]], [[kansas|Kansas]], elevated approximately eight feet from the ground by four notched poles firmly planted in the earth. The elevation served the practical purpose of protecting the remains from scavenging animals while also expressing the Plains belief that the dead should be raised toward the sky — the domain of the Great Spirit (024-303).

The burial cradle itself was constructed of interlaced branches of white willow, measuring approximately six feet in length, three feet in breadth, and three feet in height, with a flooring of buffalo thongs arranged as net-work. This cradle was securely fastened by strips of buffalo hide to four poles of ironwood and cottonwood, each approximately twelve feet in length, which rested upon the forked extremities of the vertical support poles. The substantial engineering of this elevated platform — using heavy hardwood poles capable of bearing considerable weight — reflects the importance the Cheyenne attached to the proper treatment of their dead (024-303).

The most remarkable aspect of the Cheyenne burial was the elaborate system of wrappings enveloping the deceased. Upon opening the burial case at the Army Medical Museum, investigators found an aperture eighteen inches square at the middle of the right side of the cradle, through which the inner wrappings were accessed. Seven buffalo robes of large size were the first layer — an extraordinary quantity of material, each robe representing significant labor in hunting, skinning, and tanning. Beneath these were five blankets: two scarlet, two blue, and one white, all nearly new. A striped white and gray sack and a United States Infantry overcoat formed the next layer. Finally, the immediate envelopes of the remains were three buffalo-calf skin robes with hoods, each approximately four feet in length and elaborately ornamented with bead-work in stripes — the outer robe with rows of blue and white bead-work, the second with green and yellow, and the third with blue and red (024-303).

The lavishness of this burial — involving seven buffalo robes, five trade blankets, military clothing, and three beaded ceremonial robes — for a child indicates both the high status of the deceased and the Cheyenne belief that material goods accompanied the spirit into the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. The spherical brass bells attached about the borders of the beaded robes by strings of beads further attest to the incorporation of trade goods into traditional funerary ritual. The total value of the burial goods, by nineteenth-century Plains standards, represented a substantial portion of a family's wealth, voluntarily surrendered in honor of the dead (024-303).

## Comparative Plains Practice

Cheyenne scaffold burial belongs to a broader Plains tradition of elevated [[inhumation|interment]] shared by the [[sioux|Sioux]], Arapaho, Kiowa, and Comanche. The Cheyenne practice is distinguished, however, by the exceptional number of wrapping layers and the incorporation of beaded ceremonial robes at the innermost level — a feature not commonly recorded among other Plains tribes. The use of both traditional materials (buffalo robes, willow cradle, rawhide thongs) and Euro-American trade goods (woolen blankets, military overcoat, brass bells) illustrates the adaptive character of Cheyenne mortuary ritual in the contact period (024-303).

## Historical Sources

The Cheyenne burial case was documented by Brevet Major [[sternberg-george-m|George M. Sternberg]], U.S. Army, and transmitted to the Army Medical Museum by Dr. [[catlin-george|George]] A. Otis. [[sternberg-george-m|Sternberg]]'s decision to send the case unopened to the Museum — believing that the value of any enclosed articles would be enhanced if received in their original state — proved prescient, as the undisturbed stratigraphy of wrappings provided an unparalleled record of Cheyenne burial material culture.
