# Food Offerings

## Overview

The custom of providing food to the dead was a widespread practice among North American Indian tribes, reflecting beliefs that the deceased required sustenance for their journey to the afterlife. Yarrow (1880) documented numerous instances of this practice, drawing on accounts from across the continent. The custom paralleled similar practices in other ancient cultures, including the Romans, where food offerings "usually accompanied cremation, and was not confined to food alone" (014-022).

## Forms of Offering

Food offerings took several distinct forms across tribes. On the Great Plains, scaffolds holding the dead were equipped with "small buckets and baskets containing food and drink for the dead hung on the scaffolds for use in the future life," reflecting the belief that the soul required physical sustenance on its journey (014-002). Along the [[oregon|Oregon Trail]], travelers noted that Indians "plant a stake on the right side of the head of the deceased and bury them in a bark canoe. Their children come every year to bring provisions to the place where their fathers are buried," indicating an ongoing practice of food renewal at grave sites (001-031).

## Annual Offerings

Some tribes maintained food offerings at burial sites on a recurring basis. Among certain groups, the body "is preserved for three years, food being supplied, and on each anniversary of the death it is redressed and attended to amid certain ceremonies," combining food offering with periodic ceremonial attention to the remains (012-007).

## Regional Practices

Among the [[choctaw|Choctaw]], funeral ceremonies included the deposition of the deceased's personal items with the body: "The gun, bow and arrows, hatchet and knife are deposited in the grave," alongside poles planted at the head and foot upon which flags were displayed. Food items formed part of the grave assemblage (019-010).

The [[achomawi|Pitt River (Achomawi)]] Indians of [[california|California]] practiced a distinctive form of burial that included offerings. The body was buried standing upright in a grave, with a small circular space hollowed out around it. "Articles of personal property and food for the spirit" were included in the burial (011-010).

## Antiquity of the Custom

Yarrow observed that "supplying food to the dead is a custom which is known to be of great antiquity." The practice appeared across numerous distinct cultural regions, indicating it was a deeply rooted element of Native American mortuary tradition rather than a localized innovation (014-022).
