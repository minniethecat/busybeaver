# Mound Builders

## Overview

The term "mound builders" designates the prehistoric Native American peoples of the Eastern Woodlands — particularly of the [[ohio|Ohio]] and [[mississippi|Mississippi]] Valleys — who constructed extensive earthwork burial mounds, ceremonial tumuli, and stone grave cemeteries over many centuries. Yarrow (1880) drew upon the archaeological investigations of [[powell-jw|Powell]], [[putnam-fw|Putnam]], [[atwater-caleb|Atwater]], and other fieldworkers to document the mortuary practices of these cultures, which included cist-grave inhumation, [[cremation]], urn-burial, and the periodic collective [[inhumation|interment]] of skeletal remains. The mound builders' funerary architecture represents the most elaborate mortuary tradition documented in the Yarrow monograph, distinguished by its scale, longevity, and technical sophistication.

## Geographic Extent

The mound-building cultures occupied a vast territory centered on the [[ohio|Ohio]] and [[mississippi|Mississippi River]] drainages. In [[tennessee|Tennessee]], [[powell-jw|Maj. J. W. Powell]] reported that ancient cemeteries were "exceedingly abundant throughout the State, often hundreds of graves may be found on a single hillside," with the graves arranged either as scattered individual burials or collected into mounds, "each mound being composed of a large number of cist graves" (004-004). In the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]], [[putnam-fw|Prof. F. W. Putnam]]'s two-year excavation campaign for the Peabody Museum opened "several thousand of the singular stone graves of the mound builders of [[tennessee|Tennessee]]" and revealed walled town sites, domestic structures, and evidence of systematic burial practices differentiated by age and status (004-009).

## Mortuary Practices

### Stone Grave Construction

The most distinctive burial practice of the mound builders was the construction of stone-lined cist graves. These were rectangular chambers formed by setting flat stone slabs on edge for walls, lining the bottom with additional stones, and capping the structure with slab roofing, the whole then covered with earth. The graves were often arranged in concentric patterns within mounds, with the earliest burials at the center featuring feet oriented inward, and progressively irregular arrangements in later additions as the mound expanded (004-004).

### Cremation

The mound builders of the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]] frequently employed [[cremation]] as a mode of disposal. Yarrow noted that they "often placed a layer of clay over the dead, but not in immediate contact, upon which they builded fires; and the evidence that [[cremation]] was often resorted to in their disposition are too abundant to be gainsaid" (011-005). Archaeological evidence included charred human remains resting on clay floors, with the combustion appearing to have been complete — the porous portions of bones reduced to "pulverized bone-black" (011-005).

### Urn-Burial

"Urn-burial appears to have been practiced to some extent by the mound-builders, particularly in some of the Southern States," Yarrow recorded. In the mounds on the Wateree River near Camden, [[carolina|South Carolina]], Dr. Blanding discovered "ranges of vases, one above the other, filled with human remains." In some instances, when the mouth of the vase was too small to admit a full skull, the cranium was placed face downward as a cover. At New Madrid, [[missouri|Missouri]], Prof. Swallow recovered a human skull enclosed in an earthen jar whose opening was too narrow to permit extraction, indicating that the vessel "must have been molded on the head after death" (007-002).

### Collective Ossuary Mounds

East of the [[mississippi|Mississippi River]], a widespread custom involved the periodic collection and communal [[inhumation|interment]] of skeletal remains. "Nearly every nation was accustomed at stated periods — usually once in eight or ten years — to collect and clean the osseous remains of those of its number who had died in the intervening time, and inter them in one common sepulcher, lined with choice furs, and marked with a mound of wood, stone, or earth." Yarrow identified this practice as "the origin of those immense tumuli filled with the mortal remains of nations and generations" that antiquaries so frequently encountered throughout the territory (014-022).

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] provided a well-documented example of this custom. Upon death, the deceased was placed on a scaffold until the flesh decayed; specialist undertakers then stripped and cleaned the bones, which were deposited in "a curiously-wrought chest or coffin, fabricated of bones and splints" and stored in a [[bone-house]]. When the [[bone-house]] became full, a general funeral was held: the coffins were carried in procession to the place of [[inhumation|interment]] and arranged "forming a pyramid," which was then covered with earth to create a conical mound — followed by a communal feast (014-020).

## Archaeological Documentation

The mound builder sites attracted systematic archaeological attention in the late nineteenth century. [[atwater-caleb|Caleb Atwater]]'s survey of the burial mounds of [[ohio|Ohio]], published in the *Transactions of the American Antiquarian Society* (1820), represented the earliest comprehensive documentation of these earthworks. [[putnam-fw|Putnam]]'s Peabody Museum campaign (1876–1878) brought modern archaeological methods to bear, mapping an entire walled town on the Lindsley estate in [[tennessee|Tennessee]], where approximately seventy house sites were traced, each with child burials beneath the clay floors — a practice reserved for children, as adult remains were consigned to a dedicated burial mound (004-009).

## Cultural Significance

The mound builders' mortuary architecture embodied a communal approach to death that contrasted with the individualistic burial practices of many historic tribes. The accretion of graves within mounds over long periods, the periodic collective [[secondary-burial|reburial]] ceremonies, and the differentiation of burial treatment by age (children beneath houses, adults in mounds) all point to a society in which mortuary ritual served to reinforce collective identity across generations. The scale of construction — mounds fifteen feet high containing sixty or more carefully arranged stone graves — required coordinated labor and sustained cultural commitment to the veneration of the dead.
