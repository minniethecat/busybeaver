## Overview

Indian Territory, established in the 1830s in what is now Oklahoma, became the destination for dozens of Eastern tribes forcibly removed from their ancestral homelands during the era of Indian Removal. Yarrow's 1880 monograph preserves accounts of mortuary customs in the territory from U.S. Indian Agency physicians and observers, documenting both the persistence of traditional practices and their adaptation to the new environment of the Southern Plains.

## Burial Practices

### Wichita Earth Burial

Dr. Fordyce Grinnell, physician to the Wichita Agency in Indian Territory, provided Yarrow with a detailed description of the burial ceremonies of the [[wichita|Wichita]] Indians, who called themselves "Kitty-la-tats" or "those of the tattooed eyelids" (001-008). The Wichita burial procedure began with the town-crier, who "goes up and down through the village and announces the fact" of a death (001-008). The body was taken without delay to the grave, and if the grave was some distance from the village, the body was "carried thither on the back of a pony, being first wrapped in blankets and then laid prone across the saddle, one walking on either side to support it" (001-008).

The grave itself was "dug from 3 to 4 feet deep and of sufficient length for the extended body" (001-008). First blankets and buffalo robes were laid in the bottom; then the body, being taken from the horse and unwrapped, "is dressed in its best apparel and with ornaments is placed upon a couch of blankets and robes, with the head towards the west and the feet to the east" (001-008). The valuables belonging to the deceased were placed with the body — "with the man are deposited his bows and arrows or gun, and with the woman her cooking utensils and other implements of her toil" (001-008). Over the body, sticks were placed six or eight inches deep with grass over them, so that when the earth was filled in it need not come in contact with the body. A pen of poles was built around the filled grave, or stakes were driven so that they crossed each other over the grave, "thus forming a complete protection from the invasion of wild animals" (001-008). The ground around the grave was carefully scraped clean — "the grass or other *débris* is carefully scraped from about the grave for several feet, so that the ground is left smooth and clean" (001-008). It was seldom that relatives accompanied the remains to the grave; they more often employed others to bury the body, usually women. Mourning was similar to other tribes and "consists in cutting off the hair, fasting, &c. Horses are also killed at the grave" (001-008).

### Caddo Burial Practices

The Caddoes, *Ascena* or Timber Indians as they called themselves, followed nearly the same mode of burial as the Wichitas, but with one distinctive custom: they maintained specific burial practices that reflected their identity as a Caddoan-speaking people of the Southern Plains, related to but distinct from the Wichita (001-009).

### Creek Burial Practices

The [[creek|Creek]] (Muscogee) people, removed from Alabama and Georgia to Indian Territory in the 1830s, continued their traditional burial customs in their new homeland. Yarrow records that among the Creeks in Indian Territory, the dead "are buried on the surface, in a box or a substitute made of branches of trees, covered with small branches, leaves, and earth" (013-004). Yarrow noted that some graves "after a few weeks had become uncovered and the remains exposed to view" (013-004), suggesting the fragility of these surface-level interments. Grave goods were consistently present: "I saw in one Creek grave (a child's) a small sum of silver, in another (adult male) some implements of warfare, bow and arrows" (013-004). The Creeks were "all interred with the feet of the corpse to the east" (013-004) — a complete inversion of the Wichita practice of head-to-west orientation. In the mourning ceremonies of the Creeks, "the nearer relatives smeared their hair and faces with a composition made of grease and wood ashes, and would remain in that condition for several days, and probably a month" (013-004).

### Cultural Convergence

Indian Territory's unique status as a multi-tribal relocation zone created conditions for cultural convergence in mortuary practice. Tribes from the Southeast ([[choctaw|Choctaw]], [[chickasaw|Chickasaw]], [[creek|Creek]], [[cherokee|Cherokee]]), the Northeast ([[shawnee|Shawnee]], [[ottawa|Ottawa]], [[potawatomi|Potawatomi]]), and the Plains ([[wichita|Wichita]], [[caddo|Caddo]]) were concentrated in a single territory, bringing their diverse burial traditions into proximity. The Wichita buried head-to-west in deep graves with elaborate pole protections, while the Creeks buried on the surface feet-to-east in boxes of branches — two fundamentally different approaches to interment practiced within the same territory. The resulting mortuary landscape of Indian Territory was one of the most heterogeneous in nineteenth-century North America.
