# Food Offerings

The deposition of **food offerings** with the dead was a widespread custom among the indigenous peoples of North America. These offerings — ranging from simple provisions of water and pinole to elaborate feasts left at the grave — were intended to sustain the spirit on its journey to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]], to provide comfort in the intermediate state before final disposal, or to honor the deceased through continued provision.

## Food Placed in the Grave

The most direct form of food offering was the placement of provisions directly in or on the grave. Among the [[california|California]] tribes, a squaw would take "pinole in her blanket" and "repair to the scene of the mortuary rites" to deposit the offering [010-020]. At a [[sioux|Sioux]] burial witnessed on the Plains, "saddles, spears, camp-kettles, and all their accoutrements" were piled around the bodies, including vessels that had contained food and water [012-002].

The tribes of the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] region included food vessels as a standard part of the grave assemblage. When the dead were placed in canoe burials or on scaffolds, "a kettle, a bowl, and a spoon" were regularly placed beside the body, along with provisions for the journey [014-002]. The persistence of this custom reflected a deeply held belief that the dead required physical sustenance in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

## The Funeral Feast

Beyond individual food offerings, the funeral feast was a communal event that served both ritual and social functions. The first feast held over the remains of the deceased, documented among the [[sioux|Sioux]], involved the gathering of near relatives who "take charge of the effects" and participate in ceremonial gambling and feasting [021-020]. The food consumed at these feasts was understood as being shared with the spirit of the deceased.

Among the [[huron|Huron]], the "Feast of the Dead" described by the Jesuit missionary [[brebeuf-p-de|P. de Brebeuf]] was an elaborate ceremony involving 2,000 participants who "offered 1,200 presents at the common tomb, in testimony of their grief" [020-005]. These presents included substantial quantities of food, which accompanied the communal [[secondary-burial|reburial]] of bones collected from five villages. The scale of the offerings indicated that food provision for the dead was not merely symbolic but represented a significant economic commitment.

## Food for the Spirit's Journey

The belief that the soul required sustenance for its journey to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] was widespread. Among the [[carolina|Carolina]] tribes, the "dead body is wrapped and then covered with two or three mats" and provisioned for its passage [001-005]. Food and drink were among the items most consistently included in grave deposits precisely because they addressed the most immediate imagined need of the traveling spirit.

An observer among the Central [[california|California]] tribes recorded that "the friends of the deceased place food and drink at the head of the grave for several days after [[inhumation|interment]]," refreshing the offerings as they were consumed — or, as the Indians believed, as they were taken by the spirit [010-020]. This practice of ongoing offering blurred the line between the initial burial deposit and a continuing relationship with the dead maintained through food.

## Regional Variation

The types and quantities of food offerings varied with ecology and social complexity. Hunting tribes of the Plains and Plateau regions tended to deposit dried meat and pemmican, while agricultural tribes of the Southeast and Southwest included maize, beans, and squash. Coastal and riverine tribes added fish and shellfish. The common thread across all regions was the conviction that "the wants of the dead are the same as the wants of the living" — a principle that extended to the most basic need of all: sustenance.
