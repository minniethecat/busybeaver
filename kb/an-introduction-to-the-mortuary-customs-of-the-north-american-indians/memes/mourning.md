# Mourning Customs

## Overview

Mourning customs constituted a central and elaborate component of mortuary practices among the North American Indian tribes surveyed by H. C. Yarrow in his 1880 monograph. Far from being spontaneous expressions of grief, these were highly formalized rituals governed by tribal custom, encompassing prescribed periods of [[death-wailing|lamentation]], specific bodily adornments (or disfigurements), and communal ceremonies that reinforced social bonds. Yarrow's correspondents — military officers, physicians, and Indian agents stationed across the continent — documented mourning practices that ranged from the austere to the spectacular, revealing a remarkable diversity of cultural responses to death.

## Elements of Mourning

### Ritual Lamentation and Wailing

The most widespread element of mourning was ritualized crying, known variously as the "death wail" or "last cry." Yarrow (1880) describes how mourners would gather and "for twenty-four hours in the houses mourning and lamenting by turns" (006-010). This was not merely individual expression but a structured communal activity — the "last cry" marked the formal conclusion of the mourning period, after which "the days of mourning are ended, and the widow is now free to marry again" (019-011).

An observer among a [[california|California]] tribe provided this account:

> The widow 'goes into mourning' by smearing her face with a black pigment, packing her household goods, and retiring to her mother's house, where she remains for a prescribed period, wailing each morning at daylight (005-007).

### The Mourning Song

Music formed an integral part of mourning ritual. Yarrow notes that "her mother sang the mourning song, sometimes with others" accompanying the widow's [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s (016-002). These songs were not improvised but followed traditional patterns, passed down through generations as part of the tribe's cultural inheritance.

### Self-Mutilation and Bodily Sacrifice

One of the most dramatic mourning customs documented by Yarrow was the practice of self-mutilation. The author remarks on how extensively "practiced as a mode of mourning find wild scenes of maiming and blood-letting" across numerous tribes (P01-022). Fingers were amputated, flesh was cut, and blood was shed as tangible demonstrations of grief.

This practice merged with the concept of [[sacrifice]], as mourners offered their own bodies as testimony to their loss:

> The practice of absolute personal sacrifice... In later days, among our Indians, the sacrifice took the form of cutting off fingers, slashing limbs, and other forms of mutilation (014-022).

### Hair Cutting

The cutting of hair as a mourning observance was widespread and of "very great antiquity" according to Yarrow (014-022). Both men and women would crop or shave their hair upon the death of a close relative, with the length and style of cutting varying by tribe and by the mourner's relationship to the deceased. The period during which hair remained uncut or unkempt marked the duration of formal mourning.

### Face Blackening and Mourning Garb

Visual markers of mourning were nearly universal. Among the tribes described, mourners painted their faces with charcoal, ashes, or specific pigments. One account describes how "pigments are used as signs of mourning" — a practice Yarrow notes was also observed among "the natives of Guinea," suggesting parallels beyond North America (011-013).

A detailed description of mourning attire among one Plains group records:

> Black paint, mixed with bear's grease, was smeared over the left cheek. This constituted the mourning garb, the period of which lasted for one year (011-010).

The mourning period was marked by a distinctive costume that set the bereaved apart from the community, signaling their status and invoking social obligations of respect and support.

### The Widow's Mourning

Widows bore the most visible and prolonged burden of mourning across many tribes. The period of widow's mourning typically lasted from several months to a full year, during which the widow was subject to strict behavioral codes. Yarrow documents that after the prescribed mourning period, a ceremonial release was performed — "to relieve her from her painful mourning," a ceremony "of much ceremony and significance" (010-010).

A [[yuki|Yo-kai-a]] widow's mourning was described as "peculiar":

> In addition to the face-blackening and wailing, she was required to carry a bundle representing her deceased husband for a full year, never setting it down in public view (021-015).

The mourning was often accompanied by "plaintive mourning and weeping and piteous calling upon the name of the deceased" (021-016).

### The Mourning Feast

The conclusion of mourning was typically marked by a feast and dance, bringing the community together in a ritual that simultaneously honored the dead and restored the living to normal social relations. Yarrow describes one such gathering where the formal mourning concluded with "mourning feast and dance," after which normal life resumed (019-008).

In another account, a chief's mourning ceremony involved the distribution of his possessions — "to supply the necessary clothing and other articles required by the deceased in the spirit world" — a practice that served both ritual and social functions by redistributing wealth within the community (014-005).

## Distribution and Variation

Mourning customs were documented by Yarrow's correspondents across nearly every major tribal group discussed in the monograph. The [[dakota|Dakota]] people observed elaborate mourning ceremonies, as did the [[choctaw|Choctaw]], [[navajo|Navajo]], and numerous [[california|California]] tribes (014-020)(014-018). Among the [[creek|Creek]] people, mourning ceremonies involved specific spatial orientations — the corpse was placed facing east, and mourners arranged themselves accordingly (013-005). The [[chinook|Chinook]] of the Pacific Northwest practiced distinctive mourning rituals, while the [[yuki|Yo-kai-a]] widow's mourning style was noted as "peculiar" by contemporary observers (021-015).

The geographic distribution of mourning customs reveals both common patterns and striking regional variations. Tribes of the Plains, the Pacific Coast, the Southwest, and the Eastern Woodlands each developed mourning traditions adapted to their environments and social structures.

## Historical Significance

Yarrow's systematic collection of mourning customs represents one of the earliest comparative ethnographic surveys of the subject in North America. By soliciting standardized observations from correspondents across the continent, he created a dataset that revealed both the diversity and the common threads of mourning practices. The timing of his survey — in the late 1870s — captured these traditions at a critical moment, as many tribes were being forced onto [[reservation-system|reservations]] and traditional practices were undergoing rapid transformation under the pressures of the [[indian-removal|Indian Removal]] policy and the [[reservation-system|reservation system]].

Yarrow himself noted that his "research relates to the customs of mourning, embracing the time of mourning, manner of its observance" as one of the principal subjects requiring investigation (P01-019). The questionnaire he circulated to correspondents specifically asked about "the period of mourning, manner of its observance, &c." (P03-019), demonstrating the importance he placed on this aspect of mortuary study.
