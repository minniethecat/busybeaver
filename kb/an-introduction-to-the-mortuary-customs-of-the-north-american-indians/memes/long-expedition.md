The Long Expedition of 1823 was a United States military-scientific expedition led by Major Stephen H. Long of the U.S. Army Topographical Engineers to explore the St. Peter's (now Minnesota) River and the northern boundary region between the United States and British North America. While the expedition's primary mission was geographical and geological survey, its chronicler, the geologist [[keating-william-h|William Hypolitus Keating]], produced a narrative (published 1824) that included detailed ethnological observations of the Indigenous peoples encountered — most notably the [[sioux|Sioux]] (Dakota) and the [[chippewa|Chippewa]] (Ojibwe).

## Ethnological Contributions

Keating's narrative of the Long Expedition preserved descriptions of two mortuary practices that Yarrow (1880) incorporated into his monograph as early nineteenth-century primary sources:

**Sioux Scaffold Burial.** Keating described the scaffold burial method practiced by the Sioux of the Minnesota River region, wherein the deceased was placed upon an elevated platform constructed of poles and left exposed to the elements. This account, together with those of later observers, allowed Yarrow to trace the geographic distribution and cultural variation of scaffold burial across the Plains tribes (014-007).

**Chippewa Soul-Beliefs.** Keating recorded the Chippewa belief that the soul of the deceased must cross a slippery pole or log bridging a great river to reach the land of the dead — a tradition that showed "slight analogy" to the bridge-of-the-dead motif found in other North American belief systems. Yarrow noted this parallel in his survey of mortuary-related beliefs (021-032).

## Historical Significance

Published in 1824, Keating's narrative predates not only the founding of the Bureau of American Ethnology (1879) but the entire emergence of professional American ethnology as a distinct scientific discipline. Yarrow's decision to incorporate Keating's observations alongside those of Smithsonian field agents and Army medical officers reflects the monograph's methodologically inclusive approach to source authority — one that valued specificity of eyewitness observation over institutional affiliation (014-007).
