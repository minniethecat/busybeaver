## Overview

Sitting burial is a distinctive mode of [[inhumation|interment]] in which the corpse is placed in a sitting or flexed posture within the grave rather than extended horizontally. Yarrow (1880) documented this practice across multiple tribal groups, from the [[pima|Pima]] of the Southwest to the [[mohawk|Mohawks]] of the Northeast and the ancient [[mound-builders]] of the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]].

## Tribal Distribution

**[[pima|Pima]]** — The most detailed account of sitting burial comes from the [[pima|Pima]], who "tie the bodies of their dead with ropes, passing the latter around the neck and under the knees and then drawing them tight until the body is doubled up and forced into a sitting position." A circular grave four to five feet deep was dug, with a vault hollowed out to one side to receive the body. The grave was "perfectly round (about two feet in diameter)," and the body deposited in the side vault. After the grave was filled level with the ground, "poles, trees, or pieces of timber" were placed upon it to protect the remains from coyotes. Burials usually took place at night without much ceremony, though mourners chanted during the burial (001-025).

**[[creek|Muscogulges]] ([[creek|Creek]])** — The [[creek|Muscogulges]] buried their deceased in the earth, digging "a four-foot, square, deep pit under the cabin or couch which the deceased laid on in his house." The grave was lined with cypress bark, and the corpse was placed "in a sitting posture, as if it were alive," accompanied by his gun, tomahawk, pipe, and other valued possessions. His eldest wife had second choice of his remaining possessions, with the rest divided among other wives and children (002-003).

**[[mohawk|Mohawk]]** — A variant form placed the deceased "in a sitting posture, as if it were alive," with the body dressed in its finest attire and enclosed in a strong box (002-003)(006-041). The [[mohawk|Mohawks]] of [[new-york|New York]] made "a large round hole in which the body was placed upright or upon its haunches," covered with timber to support the earth. The corpse was "always dressed in all its finery" and wampum placed in the grave (001-002).

**Ancient Mound-Builders** — Excavations of burial mounds in the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]] revealed multiple skeletons in sitting postures. One mound contained "a central corpse in a sitting posture, and over thirty skeletons" arranged in a circle around it, also in a sitting posture "but leaning against one another, tipped over towards the right facing inwards" (004-034). Another excavation found remains "in a sitting posture, facing the east" with a tomahawk resting on a rock beside the right hand, surrounded by beads and red paint (004-040).

**[[california|California]] Tribes** — Several accounts document sitting burial in [[california|California]]. A stone-coffin grave contained a skeleton that had been "placed in a sitting posture, incased in broad stones standing on their edges, with a flat stone covering the whole" (006-017). Another account describes the body "placed in it in a sitting posture, resembling that in which they crouched round the fire or the table when alive, with the elbows on the knees and the palms of the hands against the cheeks" (010-013).

## Variations and Associated Customs

The flexed or contracted position — with knees drawn up to the chest — represents a related practice employed when space constraints or cultural preference dictated a compact burial configuration. Among the [[mohawk|Mohawks]], the seated posture was combined with rich grave goods, the body "dressed in all its finery" with wampum deposited alongside (002-003). The [[pima|Pima]] method of binding the body with ropes before rigor mortis represents a deliberate effort to achieve the seated configuration (001-025).

The Caraib custom described by Yarrow involved painting the deceased with *roucou* (a preservative pigment) and placing the body in the grave in a crouching position, with sand filled to the knees until all relatives had examined the body — a process that could extend for months while the *roucou* preserved the remains (010-013).
