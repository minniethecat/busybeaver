# Frederic Ward Putnam

## Overview

Frederic Ward Putnam (1839–1915) was an American anthropologist and archaeologist who served as curator of the Peabody Museum of Archaeology at Harvard University (then at Cambridge) and was a foundational figure in the professionalization of American archaeology. His two-year excavation campaign in the [[tennessee|Cumberland Valley]] of [[tennessee|Tennessee]], assisted by [[curtiss-edwin|Edwin Curtiss]] and presented to the Boston Society of Natural History on October 15, 1878, systematically documented the stone graves, burial mounds, and settlement patterns of the mound-building peoples. Yarrow selected Putnam's communication — notable for its meticulous method and for the discovery of child burials beneath house floors — as the opening account in his chapter on burial in stone graves (004-002)(004-008).

## Contributions

Putnam's excavations in the [[tennessee|Cumberland Valley]] involved the thorough exploration of many mounds of various kinds and the careful opening of "several thousand of the singular stone graves of the mound builders of [[tennessee|Tennessee]]." His presentation to the Boston Society of Natural History included detailed illustrations of hundreds of artifacts recovered from graves and mounds, demonstrating what he described as "the great variety of articles of pottery and several large and many unique forms of implements of chipped flint" (004-008).

The most significant discovery of Putnam's [[tennessee|Tennessee]] campaign was the regular association of child burials with domestic structures. Within a walled town site on the Lindsley estate, situated in a bend of Spring [[creek|Creek]] and encircled by an earth embankment with accompanying ditch that enclosed approximately twelve acres, Putnam's team traced the remains of about seventy houses. Under the hard clay floors, which were in places much burnt, he found the graves of children — from one to four beneath nearly every house site explored. This pattern, combined with the fact that a single burial mound within the enclosure contained sixty adult skeletons in carefully constructed stone graves arranged in two rows forming the four sides of a square in three layers, persuaded Putnam that "it was a regular custom to bury the children in that way" — directly beneath the domestic hearth rather than in the communal mound (004-009).

Putnam further noted that the children had been "undoubtedly treated with affection," as their small graves contained "many of the best pieces of pottery he obtained, and also quantities of shell-beads, several large pearls, and many other objects which were probably the playthings of the little ones while living." This finding — that grave-good wealth was concentrated in the burials of the very young rather than of adults — offered a counterpoint to the expectation that the most elaborate [[inhumation|interment]]s would belong to persons of achieved status, suggesting instead a mortuary economy in which affection rather than rank determined the material investment in burial (004-009).

## Accounts Provided

Putnam's findings were published in volume XX of the *Proceedings of the Boston Society of Natural History* (October 15, 1878) and in the *Eleventh Annual Report of the Peabody Museum* (Cambridge, 1878). His work on the [[tennessee|Tennessee]] stone graves provided Yarrow with the most archaeologically rigorous evidence for the combination of cist burial and mound deposition in the southeastern United States.
