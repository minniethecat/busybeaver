## Overview

The Mississippi River and the state that bears its name appear in Yarrow's 1880 monograph as a significant cultural-geographic boundary. The river served as a dividing line between distinct mortuary traditions, while the state of Mississippi was home to mound-builder cultures whose burial practices were of considerable archaeological interest.

## Burial Sites & Practices

The Mississippi River served as a major cultural boundary for mortuary practices. Yarrow notes that "east of the Mississippi nearly every nation was acquainted" with certain burial customs that were less common or absent west of the river, suggesting the waterway functioned as both a geographical and cultural divide (015-013).

A remarkable account of secondary burial along the Mississippi describes an Indian who "resided on the Mississippi, hearing that his son had died" at a distant location, traveled to recover the remains. Finding the body already decomposing, "he then undertook, with a few friends, to clean off the bones. All the flesh was scraped off and thrown into the stream, the bones were carefully collected into his canoe, and subsequently carried down to his residence" (014-009). This account illustrates the practice of bone-cleaning and secondary [[inhumation|interment]] common among Mississippi Valley tribes.

Early Spanish explorers who "rambled up the Mississippi, and along its tributary streams" between 1500 and 1600 provided some of the earliest European observations of aboriginal burial practices in the region, including accounts of mound [[inhumation|interment]] and scaffold exposure (006-032).
