# Tsihalis

## Overview

The Tsihalis (also rendered Chehalis) are a Salishan-speaking people of the southern Puget Sound and lower Chehalis River region of present-day western Washington. They appear in Yarrow's 1880 monograph through the observations of [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], who documented the burial practices of the [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington Territory tribes for the *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1877). The Tsihalis are noted specifically for their use of the *tamahno-us* board — a carved spirit board placed at the grave of the deceased — a practice they shared with the neighboring [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] of the lower [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] (016-007).

## Mortuary Customs

The most distinctive feature of Tsihalis mortuary practice was the placement of the *tamahno-us* board near the deceased. This carved board, associated with the guardian spirit complex of the Northwest Coast peoples, was believed to embody the personal spirit power acquired by the individual during vision quests and other rites. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] recorded that among the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] and Tsihalis, the *tamahno-us* board of the owner was placed near him at the burial site — a custom not practiced by the Puget Sound Indians to the north, who did not make *tamahno-us* boards at all. The Puget Sound peoples instead sometimes constructed effigies of their chiefs, dressed in their usual costume and wearing the articles of which they were fond, as a comparable but distinct form of grave marker (016-007).

The differences in grave-marking practices between the Tsihalis and the Puget Sound tribes reflect broader cultural distinctions among the coastal and interior peoples of the Cascadia region. The Tsihalis, occupying a transitional zone between the lower Columbia and the Sound, shared with the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] a more elaborate system of spirit-board symbolism, while the Puget Sound peoples developed effigy construction as their primary means of commemorating chiefly dead (016-007).

The burial practices of the Tsihalis, like those of neighboring Northwest Coast tribes, exhibited marked distinctions based on rank and wealth. Persons of no consideration and slaves were buried with very little care or respect. For chiefs and men of note, by contrast, much pomp accompanied the burial rite. The canoes used in chiefly burials were the war or state canoes of the deceased — of great size and value — and frequently one canoe was inverted over the one holding the body, or multiple canoes were nested for additional protection and display (016-007).

## The Tamahno-us Board

The *tamahno-us* board represents one of the most important elements of Northwest Coast spiritual material culture. These carved boards were associated with the guardian spirit complex, in which individuals acquired personal supernatural protection through vision quests, inheritance, or purchase. The board served as a physical manifestation of this spirit relationship, and its placement at the grave ensured that the spirit power remained with the deceased in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. The practice of destroying or rendering unusable the property placed at graves — applying equally to the *tamahno-us* board and to kettles, baskets, and other grave goods — was nearly universal among the region's tribes and reflected the belief that objects, like the dead themselves, must transition to the spirit world (016-007).

## Historical Sources

The sole documentary source on Tsihalis mortuary customs is [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], whose ethnographic account was published in the *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (Vol. I, 1877, p. 200) and incorporated by Yarrow into his comprehensive survey. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]]'s attention to the presence or absence of *tamahno-us* boards among different tribal groups provides one of the earliest systematic comparisons of spirit-board practices in the southern Northwest Coast.
