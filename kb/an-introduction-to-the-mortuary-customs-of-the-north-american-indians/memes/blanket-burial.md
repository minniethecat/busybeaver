# Blanket Burial

The use of blankets and robes for wrapping the dead was a widespread mortuary practice among Native American tribes. Blankets served multiple functions in burial contexts: as shrouds for wrapping the corpse, as lining for graves, and as coverings for bodies placed on scaffolds or in trees.

## Burial Wrapping

Among the tribes of the Plains, blankets were the primary material for preparing a body for burial. The [[sioux|Sioux]] and related tribes wrapped the deceased in their best buffalo robes or blankets, closely sewed up. If the deceased was a male or chief, the body was then fastened in the branches of a tree, high enough to be beyond the reach of wolves, and left to slowly waste in the elements. The practice was documented as continuing until recent times. [014-004]

A similar practice was observed among the [[wichita|Wichitas]]: when a death occurred, the town-crier announced the fact throughout the village, and preparations were immediately made for burial. Blankets and buffalo robes were first laid in the bottom of the grave, then the body, followed by additional blankets on top before the earth was filled in. [001-009]

## Body Wrapping and Binding

The practice of tightly wrapping the body into a compact bundle using blankets was documented among multiple tribes. Among one group, the deceased was drawn up by the knees and wrapped into as small a bulk as possible in blankets, then tied firmly with cords before being placed in the grave. Beads, baskets, clothing, and everything owned by the deceased were also thrown into the grave. [002-008]

## Scaffold and Tree Burial

For tribes that practiced [[scaffold-burial|above-ground burial]], blankets and robes served as the outer protective covering. A [[dakota|Dakota]] burial observed in the field described how the remains lay upon a matting, wrapped in multiple layers: a gray woolen double shawl, followed by other cloth wrappings. Three bead-work hooded cloaks formed the outermost layers of the bundle. [014-017]

The [[dakota|Dakotas]] generally buried their dead in the tops of trees when limbs could be found sufficiently horizontal to support scaffolding. Where such natural supports were unavailable, scaffolds from 7 to 10 feet high were constructed. In all cases, the body was securely wrapped in blankets and robes before being placed on the elevated platform. [014-019]

## Grave Goods with Wrappings

Blankets were often accompanied by other burial goods. In one account, the preparations for burial included not only the wrapping blankets but also the deceased's saddle, spears, camp-kettles, and all personal accoutrements, which were piled around the wrapped body. [012-002]

## Cultural Significance

The use of the deceased's best blankets and robes reflected the belief that the dead would need these items in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. The practice of sewing the wrappings tightly closed may have served both practical purposes (deterring scavengers) and spiritual ones (containing the spirit of the deceased). The quality of the wrappings often corresponded to the rank and wealth of the deceased. [001-009] [014-004]
