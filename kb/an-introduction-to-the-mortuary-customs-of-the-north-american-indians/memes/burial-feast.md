# Burial Feast

## Overview

The burial feast was a central institution in the mortuary complex of North American tribes, functioning simultaneously as a religious ritual, a social gathering, and a formal closure to the [[mourning]] period. Food was offered to the deceased, shared among mourners, and believed to be jointly consumed by the living and the spirit of the departed (021-003).

Yarrow documented the practice from the Great Lakes to the Gulf Coast, with each tribe adapting the feast to its own cosmology and burial customs. The scale ranged from intimate family gatherings to massive inter-village ceremonies involving thousands of participants.

## Tribal Distribution

### Huron — Feast of the Dead

The most elaborate burial feast on record was the [[huron|Huron]] "Feast of the Dead," witnessed by the Jesuit missionary [[brebeuf-p-de|De Brebeuf]] at the village of Ossosane. The ceremony "took place in the presence of 2,000 Indians, who offered 1,200 presents at the common tomb, in testimony of their grief" (020-005). The bones of the dead were wrapped in "a gigantic shroud, composed of forty-eight robes, each robe being made of ten beaver skins" and deposited in a vast [[ossuary]] (020-005).

According to [[huron|Huron]] belief, "the souls of the dead remain near the bodies until the 'feast of the dead'; after which ceremony they become free, and can at once depart for the land of spirits, which they believe to be situated in the regions of the setting sun" (020-005).

### Choctaw

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] concluded their secondary burial ceremonies with a feast. After the bones were collected from the [[bone-house]] and interred in a communal mound, the day ended with "a festival, which is called the feast of the dead" (015-008). This feast marked the transition of the soul from earthly attachment to spiritual freedom (004-032).

Benson described the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] [[mourning]] feast — called the "last cry" — in which the widow "goes to the grave, and pours out, with unusual vehemence, her bitter [[mourning|wailing]]s" before the community gathered for a meal of corn and jerked beef, followed by dancing that continued until morning (019-010).

### Chippewa

Among the [[chippewa|Chippewas]] of the Great Lakes, the feast held a slightly different significance. The Indian Agent I. L. Mahan reported that pagan [[chippewa|Chippewas]] "still adhere to the ancient practice of feasting at the grave of departed friends; the object is to feast with the departed; that is, they believe that while they partake of the visible material the departed spirit partakes at the same time of the spirit that dwells in the food" (021-003).

### Sioux

An account of a [[sioux|Sioux]] funeral ceremony recorded the simultaneous performance of [[death-wailing|lamentation]] and festive singing: "the contrast exhibited by the terrific [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s and yells of one part of the company while the others were singing and dancing with all their might" (020-002). At a ceremony for a member of the Grand Medicine society, "an allowance was served up for the deceased out of every article of which [the feast] consisted" (020-003).

## Significance

The burial feast served multiple functions: it provisioned the dead for the journey to the spirit land, honored the deceased through communal generosity, established or renewed social bonds among the living, and ritually marked the end of formal [[mourning]]. As [[brinton-dg|Brinton]] noted, the practice was near-universal east of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] (015-013).
