# Bernard Roman

## Overview

Bernard Roman (fl. 1775) was a historian and cartographer whose *Natural History of East and West [[florida|Florida]]* (1775) stands among the earliest detailed European accounts of the indigenous peoples of the American Southeast. His description of [[choctaw|Choctaw]] mortuary customs — in which bodies were scaffolded, defleshed by ritual specialists, and the cleaned bones deposited in communal [[burial-vaults|bone-houses]] — provided Yarrow with one of the most complete ethnographic records of the scaffold-to-[[ossuary]] burial sequence. Roman's account is distinctive for its attention to the procedural and social dimensions of the practice, including the role of a specialized ritual order identifiable by their long fingernails (015-001). He also recorded the funeral customs of the [[chickasaw|Chickasaws]], noting their essential similarity to those of the [[creek|Muscogulges]]: immediate [[inhumation|interment]] beneath the very couch where death occurred (002-004).

## Contributions

Roman's principal contribution is his description of the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] two-stage burial process. According to his account, immediately after death a scaffold was erected and the corpse laid upon it, covered with a bear skin — the poles painted red with vermilion and bear's oil if the deceased was a person of distinction. For a child, the body was placed upon crossed stakes. At the scaffold, relatives assembled to weep and address questions to the corpse, asking why the deceased had left them — whether the wife had served poorly, the children dissatisfied, the corn insufficient, or enemies feared — in a ritual of [[death-wailing|lamentation]] accompanied by loud howling. The women maintained a constant presence, some fainting from the combined effects of putrefying air and sun, while the men came to mourn at night or at irregular hours to avoid being observed (015-001, 015-002).

The scaffold remained fenced with poles for a variable period, typically one to two months, though Roman noted it could extend to three or four. A distinct order of five venerable elders, distinguished by very long nails worn on the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of each hand, traveled continuously through the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] nation. One of these specialists would announce the appointed day for the second stage. When the day arrived, the assembly gathered, a fire was kindled, the body was taken down, and the ritual operator used his long fingernails to tear the remaining flesh from the bones, casting it with the entrails into the fire to be consumed. The bones were then scraped clean, the scrapings likewise burned, and the skull painted red with vermilion before being placed with the other bones into a neatly made chest — painted red for a chief — and deposited in the loft of a purpose-built [[bone-house]], each [[choctaw|Choctaw]] town maintaining one such structure (015-003).

After approximately one year, if the deceased was a person of note, the chest was brought down; the assembly wept once more, the head was repainted, the box refurbished, and the bones were then deposited into lasting oblivion. Roman specified that enemies and suicides were denied these ceremonial obsequies, being buried directly in the earth as persons to be immediately forgotten — a distinction Yarrow preserved as evidence of the social selectivity of [[choctaw|Choctaw]] mortuary ritual (015-003).

Roman's briefer note on the [[chickasaw|Chickasaws]] recorded that they interred their dead as soon as breath departed, directly beneath the bed where the deceased had expired — a simple, expedient inhumation practice that Yarrow contrasted with the elaborate [[choctaw|Choctaw]] two-stage rite to illustrate the diversity of mortuary custom even among culturally proximate Southeastern groups (002-004).

## Accounts Provided

Roman's *History of [[florida|Florida]]* furnishes the most procedurally complete account of [[choctaw|Choctaw]] scaffold burial and [[bone-house]] deposition available to Yarrow, together with a corroborating note on [[chickasaw|Chickasaw]] deathbed inhumation. [[bartram-william|Bartram]]'s later (1791) account of [[choctaw|Choctaw]] burial, also cited by Yarrow, was explicitly compared to Roman's, with Roman's version serving as the chronological and descriptive baseline (015-001, 015-007).
