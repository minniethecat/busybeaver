## Overview

The Feast of the Dead was the most elaborate mortuary ceremony recorded among the indigenous peoples of northeastern North America. Practiced by the [[huron|Huron]] (Wendat) Confederacy of the Great Lakes region, this periodic ceremony — held approximately every ten to twelve years — involved the mass exhumation of all who had died since the previous feast, the cleaning and wrapping of their bones, and their communal reinterment in a single great ossuary. Yarrow's 1880 monograph preserves the eyewitness account of the Jesuit missionary [[brebeuf-p-de|P. de Brébeuf]], who attended a Feast of the Dead at the village of Ossosane before the Huron were dispersed by Iroquois attacks in 1649 (020-005). The ceremony was noted by multiple later compilers; Hind, in his Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition report of 1860, drew attention to Brebeuf's account as a prime example of indigenous burial feasts (020-004).

## The Huron Ceremony

### Scale and Attendance

Brébeuf's account describes a ceremony of extraordinary scale: the Feast of the Dead at Ossosane "took place in the presence of 2,000 Indians, who offered 1,200 presents at the common tomb, in testimony of their grief." The participants came from five large villages, each bearing the bones of their deceased for collective reburial. The sheer number of attendees and the volume of offerings — 1,200 presents deposited at a single tomb — attest to the ceremony's central role in Huron social and spiritual life (020-005).

### The Great Shroud

The bones of the dead from all five villages were deposited in "a gigantic shroud, composed of forty-eight robes, each robe being made of ten beaver skins" — a total of 480 beaver skins woven into a single funerary wrapping. After being carefully wrapped in this shroud, the bones were "placed between moss and bark" within the common tomb. A wall of stones was built around the vast ossuary "to preserve it from profanation," marking the sacred boundary between the living and the collective dead (020-005).

### Ritual Elements

Before covering the bones with earth, "a few grains of Indian corn were thrown by the women upon the sacred relics" — an offering that linked the fertility of the agricultural cycle with the regeneration of the dead. According to Huron belief, "the souls of the dead remain near the bodies until the 'feast of the dead'; after which ceremony they become free, and can at once depart for the land of spirits, which they believe to be situated in the regions of the setting sun" (020-005). This eschatology — in which the soul's liberation was contingent upon ritual completion, not automatic upon biological death — placed enormous spiritual weight on the proper performance of the Feast ceremony. The ceremony occurred "before the dispersion of the Hurons," situating it within the pre-displacement cultural height of the Huron Confederacy in the early seventeenth century (020-005).

## Spiritual Framework

The theological basis of the Feast of the Dead was rooted in Huron beliefs about the afterlife. The soul's journey to the "land of spirits" — situated "in the regions of the setting sun" — was not automatic upon death; rather, souls remained earthbound, lingering near their physical remains, until the communal ceremony released them (020-005). This belief created a shared spiritual obligation across the community: until the Feast was properly conducted, the dead remained in a liminal state, neither fully departed nor fully present. The Huron burial feasts were thus not merely commemorative but functionally essential to the cosmic order, facilitating the transition of souls from the world of the living to the realm of the dead (024-208, 024-056).

## Cultural Significance

The Feast of the Dead represents the most elaborate expression of the widespread indigenous belief in a two-stage mortuary process: the initial disposal of the body (by scaffold, temporary burial, or platform exposure) followed by a secondary, collective reburial that released the soul. The ceremony's scale — involving multiple villages, thousands of participants, and hundreds of beaver-skin robes — underscores the centrality of the dead in Huron social organization, where the bones of ancestors, collectively housed and periodically renewed through ritual, served as a material anchor of inter-village alliance and identity. By binding five villages together in a shared obligation to their dead, the Feast simultaneously renewed both spiritual and political relationships, making it among the most significant documented ceremonies in the ethnographic record of indigenous North America.

## Comparative Practices

### Choctaw Bone-Picking

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] of the Southeast practiced a related but distinct form of secondary burial that Yarrow explicitly connects to the Feast of the Dead tradition. The Choctaw would erect a scaffold "18 or 20 feet high in a grove adjacent to the town" where the corpse was left to decompose, "visited and protected by the friends and relations, until the flesh becomes putrid, so as easily to part from the bones." Professional undertakers — "bone-pickers" — would then "carefully strip the flesh from the bones, wash and clean them," after which the bones were placed in a chest and deposited in a [[bone-house]] (015-008). The Choctaw ceremony, like the Huron Feast, culminated in a communal reburial and festival.

### Pyramid Burial

Yarrow also records a variant of collective burial in which "the respective coffins are borne by the nearest relatives of the deceased to the place of interment, where they are all piled one upon another in the form of a pyramid, and the conical hill of earth heaped above." The funeral ceremonies were "concluded with the solemnization of a festival called the feast of the dead" (004-032).

### Generic Burial Feast Beliefs

Beyond the specific Huron and Choctaw ceremonies, the concept of feasting with the dead was widespread among North American tribes. Dr. S. G. Wright, acting physician to the Leech Lake Agency in Minnesota, recorded that "Pagan Indians, or those who have not become Christians, still adhere to the ancient practice of feasting at the grave of departed friends; the object is to feast with the departed; that is, they believe that while they partake of the visible material the departed spirit partakes at the same time of the spirit that dwells in the food" (020-005). This pan-tribal belief — that food offerings and feasting provided spiritual sustenance to the dead — contextualizes the Huron Feast not as an anomaly but as the most elaborate expression of a widely shared indigenous theology of death and sustenance.
