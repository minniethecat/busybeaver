# Omaha

## Overview

The Omaha people inhabited the upper [[missouri|Missouri]] River region in present-day northeastern Nebraska, where they lived in permanent earth-lodge villages and pursued a mixed economy of bison hunting and maize agriculture. Among the many Plains tribes whose burial customs Yarrow (1880) documented, the Omaha are distinguished by the preservation of one of the most dramatically conceived individual [[inhumation|interment]]s in the entire ethnographic record: the obsequies of Blackbird, the great Omaha chief, whose burial atop a towering [[missouri|Missouri]] River bluff — astride his living war-horse — was witnessed and recorded by the celebrated painter and ethnographer [[catlin-george|George Catlin]].

## Mortuary Customs

### The Burial of Chief Blackbird

The funeral of Blackbird represents a confluence of personal authority, sacred geography, and the Plains tradition of horse [[sacrifice]] elevated to its most spectacular expression. [[catlin-george|George Catlin]]'s account, preserved by Yarrow, describes a ceremony that was "romantically conceived, and carried out to the fullest possible extent in accordance with the *ante mortem* wishes of the dead" (008-004).

Blackbird had requested that his body be taken down the [[missouri|Missouri]] River to his favorite haunt — a towering bluff from whose pinnacle he could see, as he said, "the Frenchmen passing up and down the river in their boats." Among his many horses, he owned a noble white steed. With great pomp and ceremony, in the presence of the entire Omaha nation, fur-traders, and the Indian agent, the deceased chief was placed astride this horse's back:

> He was placed astride of his horse's back, with his bow in his hand, and his shield and quiver slung, with his pipe and his medicine bag, with his supply of dried meat, and his tobacco-pouch replenished to last him through the journey to the beautiful hunting grounds of the shades of his fathers, with his flint and steel and his tinder to light his pipes by the way; the scalps he had taken from his enemies' heads could be trophies for nobody else, and were hung to the bridle of his horse. He was in full dress, and fully equipped, and on his head waved to the last moment his beautiful head-dress of the war-eagles' plumes (008-004).

### The Horse Sacrifice

The most remarkable element of Blackbird's burial was the [[sacrifice]] of his war-horse — not killed first, but buried alive beneath its rider. After the medicine-men performed the final funeral honors:

> Every warrior of his band painted the palm and fingers of his right hand with vermilion, which was stamped and perfectly impressed on the milk-white sides of his devoted horse. This all done, turfs were brought and placed around the feet and legs of the horse, and gradually laid up to its sides, and at last over the back and head of the unsuspecting animal, and last of all over the head and even the eagle plumes of its valiant rider (008-004).

The living horse, marked with the vermilion handprints of every warrior, was entombed by the gradual accumulation of turf until both it and its rider were completely covered. The burial mound, visible for miles from the [[missouri|Missouri]] River, remained "undisturbed to the present day" — a monument that fused the Plains traditions of horse [[sacrifice]], elevated burial, and the veneration of great leaders into a single, indelible image.

### Scalp Trophies

Blackbird's burial included a detail of particular significance: the scalps he had taken from his enemies' heads were hung from the bridle of his horse, for "they could be trophies for nobody else" (008-004). This practice — the [[inhumation|interment]] of war trophies exclusively with the warrior who had earned them — underscores the intensely personal conception of martial honor among the Omaha. Trophies were not heritable; they belonged to the individual alone and accompanied him into the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

## Historical Sources

The sole detailed source for Omaha mortuary custom in Yarrow is [[catlin-george|George Catlin]], whose *Manners, Customs, and Condition of the North American Indians* (1844) provided one of the most widely read and visually documented records of Plains Indian life in the nineteenth century. [[catlin-george|Catlin]]'s account of Blackbird's burial, while focused on a single exceptional individual rather than everyday Omaha practice, preserves a ceremony that would otherwise have been lost — the burial of a chief whose *ante mortem* instructions transformed his own funeral into a spectacle that embodied the highest values of Omaha warrior culture.
