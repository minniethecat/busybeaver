# Burial Vaults

**Burial vaults** — permanent structures for the deposit of the dead ranging from subterranean stone chambers to above-ground wooden sepulchers — represent some of the most elaborate mortuary architecture documented among North American Indian tribes. Yarrow described them under the heading of "sepulchral structures" and noted their distribution from the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]] to the Pacific Northwest.

## Chambered Mounds

The most substantial vaults were the chambered mounds of the [[ohio|Ohio]] and [[mississippi|Mississippi]] valleys. "These chambered mounds are situated in the eastern part of Clay County, [[missouri|Missouri]], and form a large group on both sides of the [[missouri|Missouri]] River. The chambers are, in the three opened by Mr. [[curtiss-edwin|Curtiss]], about 8 feet square, and from 4-1/2 to 5 feet high, each chamber having a passage-way several feet in length" [004-004]. The chambers contained multiple burials, suggesting family or lineage-group use over extended periods.

At a different site, a mound contained a central corpse "in a sitting posture, and over thirty skeletons buried around it in a circle, also in a sitting posture" [004-034]. This arrangement indicates that vaults could serve as communal repositories rather than individual tombs.

## Stone Vaults and Cists

Stone-lined burial chambers — cists — were widely distributed. Near the Licking River in [[ohio|Ohio]], chambered mounds containing stone-lined vaults were documented [004-023]. In [[tennessee|Tennessee]], "a pit was dug, into which the corpse was placed, surrounded by a wall of stone," covered with slab roofing [011-004]. This form, described as "stone sepulchers," provided permanent protection for the remains.

In [[kentucky|Kentucky]], a notable discovery involved "the exsiccated body of a female… found at the depth of about 10 feet from the surface of the cave bedded in clay strongly impregnated with nitre, placed in a sitting posture, incased in broad stones standing on their edges, with a flat stone covering the whole" [006-017]. This subterranean stone vault preserved the body and its wrappings for an extended period.

## Wooden Sepulchers of the Northwest

On the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]], the Watlala tribe constructed above-ground wooden sepulchers. An observer described "an ancient Indian burial-place; it consists of eight vaults, made of pine or cedar boards, closely connected, about eight feet square and six in height, the top securely covered with wide boards, sloping a little, so as to convey off the rain" [016-007]. One such burial island was named Sepulcher Island for the concentration of these structures [016-008].

These northwest coast vaults were often maintained over generations: "It was usual in the case of chiefs to renew or repair for a considerable length of time the materials and ornaments of the burial-place" [016-015]. For common persons, the bones were collected after flesh decay and wrapped in mats — a simpler form of secondary deposit.

## Bone-Houses

A specialized form of vault was the "[[bone-house]]" used by tribes practicing secondary burial. "East of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] nearly every nation was accustomed at stated periods — usually once in eight or ten years — to collect and clean the osseous remains of those of its number who had died in the intervening time, and inter them in one common sepulcher, lined with choice furs, and marked with a mound of earth" [015-013]. These periodic collective burials transformed individual deaths into communal ritual events.

The [[natchez|Natchez]] temple-tombs combined religious and funerary functions: "These tombs were located within or very near their temples. They rested upon four forked sticks fixed fast in the ground, and were raised some three feet above the earth" [015-006].

## Contents and Social Differentiation

"the distinctions of rank or wealth in all cases were very marked," an observer noted of northwest coast burial practices [016-010]. Chiefs and persons of status received elaborate vault [[inhumation|interment]] with accompanying deposits; slaves and persons of no rank received minimal treatment. This social stratification of burial architecture parallels the differentiation seen in grave deposits and scaffold construction.
