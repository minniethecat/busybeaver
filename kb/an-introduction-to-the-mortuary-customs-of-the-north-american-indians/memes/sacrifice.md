# Burial Sacrifice

## Overview

The practice of sacrifice at the time of burial — whether of horses, possessions, or in rarer cases, human lives — represented one of the most dramatic and widely misunderstood mortuary customs among North American Indian tribes. H. C. Yarrow's 1880 monograph collected numerous accounts from correspondents describing these practices, which ranged from the ritual slaughter of a deceased warrior's favorite horse to the voluntary self-sacrifice of widows. These customs, while often sensationalized by contemporary observers, served deeply embedded social and spiritual functions within their respective tribal contexts.

## Forms of Sacrifice

### Animal Sacrifice

The most commonly documented form of burial sacrifice involved horses. Yarrow's correspondents reported cases where a "number of horses which had been sacrificed no doubt during the funeral ceremonies" were found at burial sites across the Plains (009-002). This practice was particularly associated with warrior burials among equestrian tribes, where the deceased's mounts were killed to accompany him into the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

The sacrifice of horses served multiple purposes: it demonstrated the deceased's wealth and status, provided transportation in the spirit world, and, by destroying valuable property, performed the social function of preventing inheritance disputes.

### Human Sacrifice and Suttee

A practice analogous to the [[hind-hy|Hind]]oo suttee — the self-immolation of a widow on her husband's funeral pyre — was reported among several tribes. Yarrow notes one account which, if accurate, describes "a modification of the [[hind-hy|Hind]]oo suttee" practiced in North America (010-014).

[[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]], cited by Yarrow, provides this striking account:

> In Panama, when a cacique died, his widows were expected to accompany him, some being buried alive with the deceased, others voluntarily taking poison (016-017).

The extent to which such practices represented genuine voluntary sacrifice versus social compulsion remains a subject of scholarly debate, and Yarrow himself approached these accounts with cautious skepticism.

### Self-Mutilation as Living Sacrifice

In many tribes, the concept of sacrifice took the form of self-mutilation rather than death:

> The practice of absolute personal sacrifice... in later days, among our Indians, the sacrifice took the form of cutting off fingers, slashing limbs, and other forms of mutilation, offered as tokens of grief and loyalty to the deceased (014-022).

These acts of bodily sacrifice were intimately connected with [[mourning]] customs, representing a physical manifestation of spiritual devotion.

### Burial Offerings

Beyond the dramatic sacrifices of living beings, the more common practice was the placement of offerings with the deceased. These included "trinkets, and small bones, the offerings of friendship or affection" placed in or upon graves by mourners (016-007). Yarrow (1880) noted that "sacrifices were freely offered as oblations" at burial sites, suggesting that the practice was widespread and not confined to any single tribal group or region (006-003).

## The Tsinuk Burial Sacrifice

Among the [[chinook|Chinookan]] peoples of the Pacific Northwest (referred to by Yarrow as [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]]), burial sacrifice took a distinctive form. The [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] practice involved specific ritual elements documented in the monograph's index entries (024-085)(024-086)(024-395). Canoes, slaves, and personal possessions were interred or destroyed at the graves of prominent individuals, reflecting the stratified social structure of these coastal societies.

## Spiritual and Social Functions

Burial sacrifice served overlapping functions within tribal societies. Spiritually, it equipped the deceased for the journey to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] with the companions and possessions they would need. Socially, it performed critical roles: displaying the family's wealth and devotion, redistributing or destroying property to reset inheritance claims, and providing a sanctioned outlet for communal grief.

Yarrow's questionnaire to correspondents specifically inquired: "Are sacrifices, human or otherwise, voluntary or forced?" (022-006), demonstrating the systematic approach he brought to understanding this sensitive subject across tribal contexts.

## Historical and Comparative Context

The practice of burial sacrifice was not unique to North American tribes but represented a near-universal feature of early human societies, from the royal [[burial-vaults|tombs]] of ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia to the Viking ship burials of Scandinavia. Yarrow situated North American practices within this broader comparative framework, noting parallels and divergences that would inform later anthropological theories of sacrifice.

The decline of these practices accelerated in the late nineteenth century, driven by the combined pressures of the [[indian-removal|Indian Removal]] policy, the concentration of tribes on the [[reservation-system|reservation system]], and the influence of Christian missionaries who actively suppressed traditional mortuary rituals.
