## Overview

J. W. Foster was a nineteenth-century American archaeologist and author whose work *Pre-Historic Races* (1873) served as a key source for Yarrow's descriptions of mortuary customs among the [[mound-builders]] and [[cherokee|Cherokee]] peoples. His detailed accounts of urn-burial practices in the Southern states and a peculiar mode of partial [[cremation]] among the [[cherokee|Cherokees]] were incorporated into Yarrow's 1880 monograph on North American Indian burial customs.

## Contributions

Foster's *Pre-Historic Races* provided Yarrow with two significant descriptions of indigenous burial practices. First, Foster documented urn-burial among the [[mound-builders]] in the Southern states, noting that on the Wateree River near Camden, [[carolina|South Carolina]], "ranges of vases, one above the other, filled with human remains, were found" (007-001). Second, Foster described an unusual mode of partial [[cremation]] practiced by the [[cherokee|Cherokees]] or a neighboring tribe in [[carolina|North Carolina]], involving the excavation of a pit in which bodies were placed upon puncheons and covered with a layer of charcoal and ashes before being topped with earth (011-001).

## Accounts Provided

Foster's accounts from *Pre-Historic Races* contributed to two sections of Yarrow's monograph:

- **[[urn-burial|Urn-Burial]]** (Chapter VII): Foster documented the discovery of tiered burial urns in [[carolina|South Carolina]] mounds, providing evidence of urn-burial among the [[mound-builders]] of the Southern states (007-001).

- **[[partial-cremation|Partial Cremation]]** (Chapter XI): Foster described a distinctive burial mode practiced by the [[cherokee|Cherokees]] or a neighboring tribe in the [[tennessee|Little Tennessee River]] valley, involving charcoal and ash layering within burial pits (011-001).
