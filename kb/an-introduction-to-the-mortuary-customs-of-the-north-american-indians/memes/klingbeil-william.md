## Overview

William Klingbeil of Philadelphia brought to scientific attention a singular aboriginal burial discovered on the [[new-jersey|New Jersey]] bank of the Delaware River, a short distance below Gloucester City. His careful exhumation and examination of the site revealed evidence of a standing-position [[inhumation|interment]] with associated fire damage, suggesting the burial of an executed prisoner of war. Yarrow included Klingbeil's account in the 1880 monograph as an example of unusual sepulture practices (011-012).

## Contributions

Klingbeil's account, published in the *American Naturalist* (September 1878, p. 699), described the discovery of a human skeleton buried in a standing position within a high, red, sandy-clay bluff overlooking the Delaware River. The neck bones were found a few inches below the surface, with the remainder of the skeleton below — though the bones of the hands and feet were missing. Klingbeil's careful exhumation disclosed that large stones bearing traces of fire had been placed around the lower extremities, and the bones of the feet had been consumed by fire in conjunction with charred wood (011-012).

Klingbeil concluded that the individual had been executed, probably as a prisoner of war, and placed erect in a prepared pit before a fire was built around the lower body. The discovery represented one of the more unusual aboriginal burial modes documented in Yarrow's monograph, distinct from the more common extended, flexed, or scaffold burials.

### Significance of the Standing Burial

The Gloucester City burial documented by Klingbeil is unique within Yarrow's comprehensive survey of North American mortuary customs. No other instance of vertical, standing-position interment is recorded in the 1880 monograph, making Klingbeil's discovery the sole documented case of this practice. Several features of the burial warrant particular attention:

**The vertical posture** is unlike any of the conventional aboriginal burial positions — extended supine, flexed on the side, sitting, or scaffold-elevated — that dominate Yarrow's typology. The upright placement, with the head originally protruding above the surface, suggests a deliberate departure from normative burial practice, consistent with the treatment of individuals subjected to ritualized violence.

**The combination of fire and interment** represents a hybrid mode that falls between full cremation and simple inhumation. The fire was confined to the lower extremities — the stones bearing "traces of fire" were "around the lower extremities of the body" and "the bones of the feet had undoubtedly been consumed" — rather than consuming the entire corpse. This partial burning distinguishes the case from both the comprehensive [[cremation]] practiced by some tribes and the clay-coated cremation of the [[north-carolina|North Carolina]] [[cherokee|Cherokees]].

**The missing cranium** introduces an element of post-mortem mutilation or curation. Klingbeil notes that "no trace of the cranium could be found," speculating that it "had either been burned or severed from the body and removed, or else left a prey to ravenous birds." The removal or destruction of the head is consistent with warrior trophy-taking practices documented among Eastern Woodland tribes, where the heads of enemies killed or executed in war were sometimes taken as trophies or displayed as warnings.

**The stature of the skeleton** — "fully six feet in height" and "undoubtedly that of a man" — suggests a warrior or individual of physical significance, reinforcing the interpretation of the burial as relating to warfare or martial execution rather than the routine disposal of the dead.

### Comparative Context

Klingbeil's discovery invites comparison with other instances of atypical burial documented in Yarrow's survey. The [[cremation|partial cremation]] of the lower body at Gloucester City shares elements with the Delaware River site reported by Dr. Leibel, where both cremated and inhumed remains coexisted in stratified deposits on the [[new-jersey|New Jersey]] bank (011-012). The execution hypothesis aligns with the practice of differential mortuary treatment for captives attested across multiple North American culture areas, where the mode of death directly shaped the mode of burial. Klingbeil's careful documentation — noting the stratigraphic position of each bone, the disposition of the fire-affected stones, and the precise location of missing elements — exemplifies the observational standards that Yarrow valued in his contributors and that lent credibility to his comprehensive survey.
