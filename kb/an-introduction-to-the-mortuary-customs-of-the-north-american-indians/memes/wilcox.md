# Mr. Wilcox

## Overview

Mr. Wilcox (given name unrecorded in the Yarrow text) presented findings before the Academy of Natural Sciences of Philadelphia in November 1874, contributing to the *Proceedings* of that society (011-006). His report of an unusual burial method encountered in [[carolina|North Carolina]] — in which the deceased was covered with plastic clay, a fire built directly atop, and the body consumed while the clay baked into a permanent mould preserving the impression of the corpse — provided Yarrow with a rare and distinctive example of combined inhumation and [[cremation]].

Yarrow specifically noted that Wilcox's account corroborated the similar discoveries of clay body-moulds made by Mr. McDowell and Col. C. [[gardner-w|W.]] Jenkes in the same region, lending convergent evidentiary weight to what would otherwise have seemed an implausible mortuary technique (011-008).

## Contributions

Wilcox described a burial practice he personally investigated in [[carolina|North Carolina]], attributed to an ancient race of Indians in that vicinity. In numerous instances, burial places were discovered in which the bodies had been placed with the face upward and covered with a coating of plastic clay approximately one inch thick. A pile of wood was then placed on top of the clay covering and set alight. The fire consumed the body entirely while simultaneously baking the clay, which hardened to retain a detailed impression of the human form it had enveloped. This baked-clay mould was then lightly covered with earth, its negative impression preserving the contours of the deceased's body as a permanent funerary record (011-008).

Wilcox's findings were presented in the context of corroborating the independent observations of Mr. McDowell, who had discovered similar clay sepulchres on his farm in the [[tennessee|Little Tennessee River]] valley. McDowell had recorded three such moulds raised during his first year of occupancy (1821), followed by a fourth discovered in 1872. Col. C. [[gardner-w|W.]] Jenkes, superintendent of corundum mines in the same vicinity, had independently described identical constructions: a pit dug, the corpse placed face upward, a covering of mortar moulded to fit the form and features, and a hot fire built upon it to produce a pottery-like shield. The convergence of these three independent testimonies — McDowell, Jenkes, and Wilcox — constituted what Yarrow considered reliable evidence for a localized but well-established burial tradition in the [[carolina|Carolina]]-[[tennessee|Tennessee]] border region (011-006).

Yarrow situated these clay-mould burials within the broader comparative context of [[cremation]] and inhumation practices, noting that while the [[mound-builders]] of the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]] often placed a layer of clay over the dead before building fires, the [[carolina|North Carolina]] practice was distinct in its application of clay directly to the body surface and its production of a permanent body-mould. He recorded that no other instance of the practice had been encountered in his extensive preparatory reading, with the sole comparative analogue being [[bruhier-jacques-jean|Bruhier]]'s observation that the ancient Ethiopians covered corpses with plaster — though without applying fire to the casing (011-009).

## Accounts Provided

Wilcox's 1874 report to the Academy of Natural Sciences documents the [[carolina|North Carolina]] burned-clay burial technique: supine body placement, one-inch plastic-clay coating, superincumbent fire consuming the body while firing the clay, and retention of the body impression in the resulting baked-clay mould. His account serves as a corroborating witness to the independent findings of McDowell and Jenkes, together forming the primary evidentiary triad for this unusual mortuary practice in the Yarrow monograph.
