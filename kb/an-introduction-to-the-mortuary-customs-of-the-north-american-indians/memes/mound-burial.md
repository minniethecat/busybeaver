# Mound Burial

## Overview

Mound burial — the construction of artificial earthen tumuli as repositories for the dead — constituted one of the most extensive and archaeologically significant modes of mortuary practice in pre-Columbian North America. Yarrow (1880) acknowledged that "the subject of mound-burial is so extensive" that he could only present "a few interesting examples" to illustrate its principal features, noting that a comprehensive treatment would require a dedicated volume (004-001). The mounds varied enormously in scale, construction, and purpose: from simple grave-mounds containing a single individual to elaborate chambered structures housing multiple burials, and from primary [[inhumation|interment]]s to secondary ossuaries where cleaned bones were deposited after initial processing elsewhere.

## Types and Structures

### Chambered Mounds of Missouri

Among the most architecturally sophisticated mound burials were the chambered mounds of Clay County, [[missouri|Missouri]], investigated by [[putnam-fw|F. W.]] [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] of the Peabody Museum of Archaeology at Cambridge. These "rude dolmens" consisted of stone chambers approximately eight feet square and four to five feet high, each with a passageway several feet in length. The chambers were constructed of large stone slabs, roofed with similar material, and covered with earth to form mounds. [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] concluded that these chambers had been used for secondary burials — the bodies having first been cremated before the remaining skeletal elements were deposited within the stone chambers (004-002)(004-003)(004-004)(004-006).

### Ohio Burial Mounds

[[atwater-caleb|Caleb Atwater]]'s early nineteenth-century descriptions of the burial mounds of [[ohio|Ohio]] provided some of the first systematic documentation of these earthworks. A notable example near Circleville consisted of a tumulus about ten feet in height, with a semicircular pavement of river pebbles extending from its eastern side. Excavations revealed burned human remains — a skeleton "almost consumed" by intense fire — together with charcoal, wood ashes, and well-burnt bricks. This mound, like many in the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]], combined [[cremation]] with mound construction in a sequence that Yarrow's sources interpreted as ritual in character (004-012)(004-014)(004-018)(004-021).

### Grave-Mounds of Florida

The grave-mounds near Pensacola, [[florida|Florida]], investigated by [[sternberg-george-m|Dr. George M. Sternberg]] of the United States Army, exemplified a different pattern. At one mound, the dead had been cremated on funeral pyres erected directly upon the mound surface, with fragments of remaining bone gathered and placed in clay pots before burial. [[sternberg-george-m|Sternberg]] theorized that the mound had been "built by gradual accretion" — each new burial adding to the mound's height through the deposition of ash, bone, and earth. At the nearby Santa Rosa mound, by contrast, complete skeletons were found without evidence of [[cremation]], suggesting that even within a single geographic region, mound burial encompassed diverse ritual procedures (004-027)(004-028)(004-029)(004-030).

### Sterling County, Illinois

The mounds of Rock River, near Sterling, [[illinois|Illinois]], yielded dolmens — quadrilateral stone chambers — containing fragmentary human remains, flint implements, and pottery. One circular mound, situated on an elevated point commanding a panoramic view of the surrounding country, contained a central stone cist with charcoal, ashes, and "hard-burnt" clays — evidence of intense fires built directly over the burial deposit (004-024)(004-025)(004-026).

## Secondary Burial Function

A recurring theme in the archaeological literature reviewed by Yarrow was the use of mounds for secondary burial. Bodies were first exposed on scaffolds or in trees, or were cremated, and only later were the cleaned bones or ashes deposited in mounds. This sequence — primary exposure or [[cremation]] followed by secondary mound [[inhumation|interment]] — "added strength to the theory that mounds were for the most part used for secondary burial, although intrusions were doubtless common" (004-011). The chambered mounds of [[missouri|Missouri]] and the [[cremation]] mounds of [[ohio|Ohio]] and [[florida|Florida]] all exhibited variants of this two-stage mortuary process.

## Historical Accounts

The mound explorations of the late nineteenth century represented the beginning of systematic archaeological inquiry into North American prehistory. [[atwater-caleb|Atwater]]'s *Transactions of the American Antiquarian Society* (1820), [[putnam-fw|Putnam]]'s explorations in the [[tennessee|Cumberland Valley]] of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] (1878), and [[sternberg-george-m|Sternberg]]'s [[florida|Florida]] investigations (1875) together formed the evidentiary foundation of Yarrow's mound-burial chapter. The [[miami|Miami]] Valley, [[ohio|Ohio]], yielded a particularly striking burial: a central corpse in a sitting posture surrounded by over thirty additional skeletons, also seated, tipped inwards toward the central figure — an arrangement suggesting hierarchical social organization and collective [[inhumation|interment]] practices (004-033)(004-034). In [[carolina|North Carolina]], [[spainhour-j-mason|J. Mason Spainhour]]'s excavation of a mound on the farm of R. V. Michaux in Burke County provided additional data on the southern extension of mound-building traditions (004-036)(004-037).

## Cultural Significance

Mound burial represented more than a practical method of disposing of the dead. The scale of construction — with some mounds reaching ninety feet in height and containing thousands of skeletons — testified to the organized labor, social stratification, and enduring mortuary traditions of the mound-building cultures. The inclusion of elaborately worked grave goods (pottery, flint implements, gorgets of red stone, shell beads) and the evidence of ritual fires and feasting associated with mound construction suggested that mound burial was embedded within complex ceremonial cycles that affirmed communal identity and the relationship between the living and the ancestral dead.

## Related Practices

- [[inhumation]] — The commonest mode of burial, of which mound burial represents a monumental elaboration
- [[cremation]] — Fire burial, frequently combined with mound deposition as a secondary stage
- [[cave-burial]] — Natural repositories contrasting with constructed mounds
- [[ossuary]] — Communal bone depositories, functionally analogous to secondary mound burials
- [[scaffold-burial]] — Aerial sepulture often serving as the primary stage before mound deposition
