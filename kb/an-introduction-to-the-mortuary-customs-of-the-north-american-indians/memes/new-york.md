## Overview

New York, in the northeastern United States, appears in Yarrow's 1880 survey primarily through accounts of [[iroquois|Iroquois]] and Algonquian mortuary practices. The [[mohawk|Mohawks]] of the [[mohawk|Mohawk]] Valley and the [[huron|Hurons]] of western New York are documented with distinctive burial customs.

## Burial Sites & Practices

The [[mohawk|Mohawks]] of New York practiced a distinctive form of [[inhumation|interment]]. Yarrow records that "the [[mohawk|Mohawks]] of New York made a large round hole" in which the dead were placed in a seated posture, accompanied by provisions, weapons, and personal effects (001-002). This seated burial position was shared by many Iroquoian peoples of the region.

Franklin B. Hough provided additional observations on "the Indians of New York," documenting variations in burial practice across different [[iroquois|Iroquois]] nations (001-030). The tribes formerly inhabiting New York "used boxes much resembling those" employed by western tribes, placing the dead in wooden containers elevated above ground (013-002). Dr. Samuel L. Mitchill of New York City corresponded on aboriginal remains, including a well-preserved human body held in a New York collection (006-019, 006-023).

The [[huron|Hurons]] of New York practiced scaffold exposure followed by secondary burial. Yarrow notes a specific case of aerial burial "among the [[huron|Hurons]] of New York" where the body was placed on an elevated platform before the bones were collected for the Feast of the Dead (020-004).
