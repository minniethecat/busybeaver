# Miami

## Overview

The Miami people, an Algonquian-speaking group, originally occupied territories in present-day [[ohio|Ohio]], Indiana, and the Wabash River Valley. By the 1870s, when Yarrow's correspondents were reporting, a significant portion of the Miami had been removed westward, yet detailed accounts of their burial practices survived through local observers and the archaeological record of the Miami Valley. Yarrow's monograph preserves two distinctive Miami burial modes — mound [[inhumation|interment]] with circular skeletal arrangement and surface burial using both hollowed logs and protective log pens.

## Mortuary Customs

### Mound Burial in Circular Formation

One of the most archaeologically distinctive burial patterns recorded by Yarrow comes from the Miami Valley of [[ohio|Ohio]]. Florian Gianque of Cincinnati reported the excavation of a mound whose internal arrangement was unusually deliberate:

> A mound was opened in this locality, some years ago, containing a central corpse in a sitting posture, and over thirty skeletons buried around it in a circle, also in a sitting posture but leaning against one another, tipped over towards the right facing inwards (004-033).

The configuration — a single central figure surrounded by more than thirty individuals arranged in a radiating circle, all in sitting postures and oriented inward — suggests a socially stratified burial event. The central corpse, distinguished by its position and by the grave goods found nearby ("many ornaments, awls, &c., said to have been found near the central body"), was likely a person of elevated status around whom retainers, family members, or subordinates were interred (004-033). The shared sitting posture, with bodies leaning against one another and tipped toward the right, implies a single, coordinated burial episode rather than incremental accumulation over time.

Yarrow records this burial under the index entry "Miami Valley mound burial" (024-272) and "Miami Valley" (024-280), situating it within the broader phenomenon of [[ohio|Ohio Valley]] mound construction.

### Surface Burial in Hollow Logs

A second Miami burial practice — surface-level [[inhumation|interment]] using hollowed logs — was reported by R. S. Robertson of Fort Wayne, Indiana, in a communication received by Yarrow in 1877. Robertson documented two distinct variants:

> 1st. The surface burial in hollow logs. These have been found in heavy forests. Sometimes a tree has been split and the two halves hollowed out to receive the body, when it was either closed with withes or confined to the ground with crossed stakes; and sometimes a hollow tree is used by closing the ends (008-001).

The method was practical and resource-efficient: a living tree could be split lengthwise, each half hollowed to accommodate the body, then rejoined and secured with flexible withes (twisted branches or vines). Alternatively, a naturally hollow standing tree could be employed by simply sealing the open ends after the body was placed inside. In both cases, crossed stakes driven into the ground held the burial receptacle in place, preventing displacement by animals or weather.

### Surface Burial Under Log Pens

The second variant reported by Robertson was the log-pen burial:

> 2d. Surface burial where the body was covered by a small pen of logs laid up as we build a cabin, but drawing in every course until they meet in a single log at the top (008-001).

This construction — a miniature cabin built in diminishing courses that converged to a single ridge log — placed the deceased within a protective structure raised directly upon the ground surface. The architectural form echoed the construction techniques of frontier log cabins, suggesting a fusion of indigenous burial practice with introduced building technology. Unlike the hollow-log method, which concealed the body within an existing or modified tree, the log-pen burial created a visible above-ground monument marking the grave site.

Robertson's account is significant for confirming that the Miami practiced both methods concurrently: "the Miamis practiced surface burial in two different ways" (008-001). The coexistence of these two [[surface-burial]] techniques may reflect differences in local material availability (forest composition affecting the presence of suitably sized hollow trees), variation among Miami bands, or distinctions based on the age, sex, or status of the deceased.
