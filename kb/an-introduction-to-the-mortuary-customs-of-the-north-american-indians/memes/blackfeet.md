# Blackfeet

## Overview

The Blackfeet, or Blackfoot, were a major tribal confederation of the northern Great Plains, inhabiting the territory of present-day Montana and adjacent regions of Canada. Yarrow (1880) documented their mortuary customs primarily through the testimony of [[menard-john|John]] Young, the Indian agent at the Blackfeet Agency in Montana, whose first-hand accounts of [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]], [[mourning]] self-mutilation, and the ceremonial rites surrounding death provide one of the most detailed ethnographic records in the entire survey.

## Mortuary Customs

### Tree Burial

The primary mode of burial among the Blackfeet was [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]] — [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]] in which the deceased was enclosed in robes or blankets, tightly sewn, and elevated into the branches of a tree beyond the reach of wolves. According to Agent Young, if the deceased was a male or chief, the body was "fasten in the branches of a tree so high as to be beyond the reach of wolves, and then left to slowly waste in the dry winds." Weapons, pipes, and personal implements were enclosed with the body. If the deceased was a woman or child, the body "was thrown into the underbrush or jungle, where it soon became the prey of the wild animals" (014-003).

Young noted that he was "pained by often coming on skeletons in trees and the stench of half-consumed remains in the brush," and attempted to persuade the Blackfeet to adopt ground burial — with limited success. He recorded that "no [[cremation]] or burying in a grave was practiced by them at any time" (014-003).

### Lodge Burial

Alongside the [[sioux|Sioux]] and [[navajo|Navajos]], the Blackfeet also practiced lodge burial, in which the deceased was laid to rest within a tipi or lodge structure, surrounded by the implements of daily life. This practice is referenced in the chapter on burial above ground and reflects the broader Plains pattern of leaving the dead in their habitual dwellings rather than excavating graves (012-006).

## Mourning Observances

Blackfeet [[mourning]] rites were severe by any measure. Young described the relatives "cutting off, according to the depth of their grief, one or more joints of the fingers, divesting themselves of clothing even in the coldest weather, and filling the air with their [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s." He recounted an instance in which he intervened with a woman about to sever a finger in grief for her child: "she consented on entreaty to cut off only one joint, and on further entreaty was brought to merely making a cut and letting out some blood. This much she could not be prevailed upon to forego" (014-003).

Women relatives would gather on elevated ground near the burial site and "keep up a dismal wail, frequently even in extreme cold weather, the greater part of the night, and this is kept up often for a month." Young observed that men who wished to emerge from [[mourning]] often required the Indian agent to "supply the necessary clothing to cover his nakedness," as they had divested themselves of all garments during the [[mourning]] period (014-003).

The death-song was performed by women assembled in the lodge or tipi of the dying person, recounting the prowess and virtues of the departing individual. Meanwhile, the oldest man present would go into the open air and address the Great Spirit, "bespeaking a welcome for him into the happy hunting grounds" (014-003).

## Historical Sources

The principal source on Blackfeet mortuary customs in Yarrow is the firsthand account of [[menard-john|John]] Young, U.S. Indian agent at the Blackfeet Agency, Montana. His observations, transmitted directly to Yarrow, provide a uniquely detailed window into the burial practices and [[mourning]] rituals of the Blackfeet during the late nineteenth century. Young's commentary on the possible "Jewish origin" of certain customs — the avoidance of touching a dead body, the elaborate [[mourning|wailing]] — reflects the speculative ethnology of the period but does not diminish the ethnographic value of his descriptions (014-003).
