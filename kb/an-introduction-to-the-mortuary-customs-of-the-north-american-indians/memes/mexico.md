## Overview

Mexico and the adjacent territory of New Mexico figure in Yarrow's 1880 monograph primarily through archaeological evidence of distinctive burial practices. The region's indigenous peoples — including the Navajo of New Mexico and Arizona, Puebloan groups, and various Mexican tribes — shared certain mortuary customs with both their North American neighbors and the indigenous cultures of Central America.

## Burial Practices

### Urn Burial

Yarrow recorded the presence of burial urns in New Mexico, describing vessels recovered from ancient graves throughout the territory. He noted that he himself had "found bones in urns or ollas in the graves of New Mexico and California," though he believed these represented secondary interments — bones collected and deposited in ceramic vessels long after the initial decomposition of the body — rather than primary urn burial (007-004). Yarrow was careful to distinguish the North American practice from that of Central and Southern American tribes, observing that it was "not believed to have been customary, but *to a very limited extent*, in North America, except as a secondary interment" (007-004). He suggested that in long-used burying places, "as the bones were dug up time and again for new burials they were simply tossed into pots, which were convenient receptacles, or it may have been that bodies were allowed to repose in the earth long enough for the fleshy parts to decay, and the bones were then collected, placed in urns, and reinterred" (007-004).

The burial urns of New Mexico are described by E.A. Barber as baked clay vessels, shaped like the steatite ollas found in California coast graves but with bottoms "running down to a sharp apex," covered with ornamented lids (007-006, 007-007). This distinctive vessel morphology — the pointed base making the urn difficult to stand upright — suggests a specifically funerary design, distinct from domestic pottery forms.

### Cremation and Scaffold Burial

Among the Indians of the region, [[cremation]] was practiced on elevated scaffolds. Yarrow documents the account from [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s *Indian Tribes of the United States* (1853) stating that among the Indians of Clear Lake, California — a region with cultural ties to the broader Southwest — "the body is consumed upon a scaffold built over a hole, into which the ashes are thrown and covered" (010-016).

### The Navajo of New Mexico

The [[navajo|Navajo]] of New Mexico and Arizona followed burial customs similar to those of the [[chickasaw|Chickasaw]], as related by Dr. John Menard, formerly a physician to the Navajo agency (002-005). This cross-cultural similarity — between Athabaskan people of the Southwest and Muscogean people of the Southeast — is one of many parallels that Yarrow documents across widely separated tribal groups, suggesting either deep historical connections or convergent evolution in mortuary practice.

### Four-Night Grave Fire Tradition

A notable parallel between Algonquian and Mexican funerary ritual was the custom of maintaining a fire at the grave for four consecutive nights. Yarrow notes that "both Algonkins and Mexicans maintained it for four nights consecutively," a coincidence "to be explained by the universal sacredness of the number" (021-027). Various interpretations of the practice were recorded: some authors stated "that the soul thereby underwent a certain process of purification, others that demons were driven away by them, and again that they were to afford light to the wandering soul setting out for the spirit land" (021-027). One tradition related that "one of their ancestors returned from the spirit land and informed their nation that the journey thither consumed just four days, and that collecting fuel every night added much to the toil and fatigue the soul encountered, all of which could be spared it" had the living maintained the fire (021-027). The [[hidatsa|Hidatsa]] of the upper Missouri similarly believed that four days were required "before the spirit could finally leave the earth" (021-031), suggesting a broadly shared indigenous conception of the four-day liminal period between death and the spirit's final departure.

### Sacrificial Burial

Bancroft documented that in Panama, Nata, and other districts bordering the Mexican cultural sphere, "when a cacique died those of his concubines that loved him enough, those that he loved ardently and so appointed, as well as certain servants, killed themselves and were interred with him" in order to serve the deceased in the afterlife (016-017). Yarrow notes it is well known "to what an extreme this revolting practice has prevailed in Mexico, South America, and Africa" (016-017). This practice of retainer sacrifice represents one of the most dramatic expressions of the belief that social hierarchy persisted beyond death.
