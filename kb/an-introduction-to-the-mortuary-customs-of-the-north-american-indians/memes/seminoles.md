# Seminoles

## Overview

The Seminole people emerged as a distinct tribal identity in [[florida|Florida]] during the eighteenth century, formed from coalescing groups of [[creek|Creek]] migrants from [[georgia|Georgia]] and Alabama, together with remnants of [[florida|Florida]]'s indigenous peoples, and a significant component of self-emancipated Africans (Black Seminoles). By the time of Yarrow's 1880 survey, the Seminoles had endured three devastating wars with the United States (the Seminole Wars, 1817–1858) and the majority of the tribe had been forcibly removed to Indian Territory. A determined remnant, however, remained in the Everglades and Big Cypress Swamp of southern [[florida|Florida]], maintaining a fiercely independent existence into the late nineteenth century.

## Mortuary Customs

### Hollow-Tree Burial

The Seminoles practiced a distinctive mode of arboreal burial — [[inhumation|interment]] within the cavities of living or fallen trees — that set them apart from both their [[creek|Creek]] ancestors and the other tribes of the Southeastern culture area. According to Yarrow:

> The Seminoles of Florida are said to have buried in hollow trees, the bodies being placed in an upright position, occasionally the dead being crammed into a hollow log lying on the ground (008-001).

This practice involved two variants: the preferred mode placed the body upright within the hollow of a standing tree, while an alternative method deposited the corpse into a hollow log resting on the ground. The upright burial within a living tree is a remarkable image — the deceased literally integrated into the arboreal landscape of the [[florida|Florida]] hammocks and swamps, the tree serving simultaneously as coffin, grave marker, and living monument.

### Arboreal Burial in Comparative Context

Yarrow contextualized Seminole [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]] within a broader pattern of arboreal mortuary practice among the Eastern tribes. He noted that "with some of the Eastern tribes a log was split in half and hollowed out sufficiently large to contain the corpse; it was then lashed together with withes and permitted to remain where it was originally placed. In some cases a pen was built over and around it" (008-001). This split-log variant, documented among the [[miami|Miamis]] of present-day Indiana and corroborated by a communication received from Mr. R. S. Robertson of Fort Wayne in 1877, shares with the Seminole practice the fundamental principle of wood as both container and protector of the dead — a coffin fashioned from the forest itself.

### Temporary Expedient or Established Custom?

Yarrow introduced his discussion of arboreal burial with the caveat that [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]] "was practiced to only a limited extent, so far as can be discovered, and it is quite probable that in most cases it was employed as a temporary expedient when the survivors were pressed for time" (008-001). This qualification raises the question of whether Seminole [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]] represented a fully developed mortuary complex or an improvisational response to the exigencies of guerrilla existence during the Seminole Wars. The swamps and hammocks of southern [[florida|Florida]] — a landscape of standing water, dense undergrowth, and limited dry ground — may have made conventional earth burial impractical in certain circumstances, and the abundant cypress and hardwood trees with natural cavities would have offered a ready alternative.

## Historical Sources

The account of Seminole arboreal burial in Yarrow's survey appears to derive from early nineteenth-century military and traveler accounts of the Seminole Wars period. The brief treatment in Yarrow — a single paragraph — reflects the limited ethnographic documentation available for a people who were, by the 1870s, largely confined to inaccessible regions of southern [[florida|Florida]] or removed to the West.
