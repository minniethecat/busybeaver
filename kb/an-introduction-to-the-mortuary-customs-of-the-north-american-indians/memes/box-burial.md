# Box Burial

## Overview

Box burial — the deposition of the dead in wooden boxes, chests, or coffin-like receptacles — constituted a distinctive category within Yarrow's (1880) classification of "sepulture above the ground," occupying an intermediate position between the constructed platforms of scaffold burial and the natural repositories of cave and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]]. The practice was documented across a remarkably broad geographic range, from the elaborately carved wooden sarcophagi of the Northwest Coast to the woven-reed coffins of the Southeast, and from the stone-covered box graves of the Northeast to the suspended box burials of the Plains (012-001)(013-001)(013-002).

## Regional Distribution

### Northwest Coast: Carved Sarcophagi

The tribes of the Northwest Coast produced the most technically sophisticated examples of box burial. Yarrow described "wonderfully carved, large wooden chests" used as receptacles for the dead, supported upon low platforms or resting on the ground. In shape, these burial chests resembled small houses with angular roofs, their surfaces adorned with the distinctive iconography of Northwest Coast art — totemic figures, geometric patterns, and zoomorphic designs executed with remarkable precision. The Indians of Bellingham Bay, according to [[hammond-jf|Dr. J. F. Hammond]], placed their dead in "carved wooden sarcophagi," enclosing these within a rectangular tent of white material (013-001)(012-006).

### Tribes of the Southeast

Among the [[creek|Creeks]], [[choctaw|Choctaws]], and [[cherokee|Cherokees]], box burial took forms adapted to the forest environment of the Southeast. Some tribes "formerly living in [[new-york|New York]] used boxes much resembling those spoken of," suggesting a broader Eastern Woodlands tradition of box sepulture that predated European contact (013-002). [[creek|Creek]] burials observed in the field involved [[inhumation|interment]] "on the surface, in a box or a substitute made of branches of trees, covered with small branches, leaves, and earth" — a hybrid form combining elements of box burial with earth covering (013-005). The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] bone-pickers, after cleaning the bones of the deceased, placed them in a box made of canes before deposition in the communal [[ossuary]] or [[bone-house]] (015-006).

### Plains and Intermontane

Box burial on the Plains was less common than scaffold or [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]] but was by no means absent. Among the [[sioux|Sioux]], bodies were sometimes deposited in lodges upon the open prairie — a practice that Yarrow classified alongside box burial as a form of above-ground sepulture. The [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]] and [[navajo|Navajos]] also buried in lodges, and the [[sioux|Sioux]] of the upper [[missouri|Missouri]] placed their dead "laid out upon the ground, wrapped in their robes of buffalo-skin" within dismantled lodge structures (012-002)(012-003)(012-006).

## Variations and Materials

The materials and construction of burial boxes varied with available resources and cultural tradition. On the Northwest Coast, the predominant material was cedar, carved with stone and shell tools. Among the [[chinook|Chinook]] of the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]], a Hudson's Bay Company trade box was observed serving as a coffin for a [[clallam|Clallam]] woman — an example of the rapid incorporation of European trade goods into indigenous mortuary practice (016-002). The [[creek|Creek]] and [[choctaw|Choctaw]] used woven cane or reed boxes. At Santa Barbara, [[california|California]], redwood canoes and boxes were employed as burial receptacles, sometimes buried in the ground rather than elevated (001-033).

Small square boxes of particularly careful construction were observed among the [[chinook|Chinook]], containing infant remains — a specialized form of box burial reserved for children. These tiny coffins, placed on posts or in trees, represented a miniature version of the full-sized burial box (016-010)(016-013).

## Box Burial in Caves

Box burial was not restricted to above-ground contexts. In the cave burials of [[kentucky|Kentucky]] and the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands, bodies were sometimes placed in boxes or on board-like supports within the cave environment. The [[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree]] or [[congaree-santee-indians|Santee Indians]] of [[carolina|South Carolina]], practicing partial embalmment, deposited their prepared dead in boxes supported above the ground within caves — a hybrid form combining cave deposition with box sepulture (006-014). This convergence of box burial and cave burial illustrated the fluid boundaries between Yarrow's analytical categories in actual mortuary practice.

## Cultural Significance

The burial box, like the canoe in canoe burial, transformed a utilitarian object into a ritual vessel. The Northwest Coast chests, with their elaborate carving and house-like form, may have represented miniature dwelling places for the dead — symbolic houses that perpetuated the deceased's social identity beyond death. The practice of depositing grave goods within or alongside burial boxes — weapons, ornaments, food vessels — reinforced this domestic metaphor. Among the [[chinook|Chinook]], the burial boxes of chiefs were periodically repainted and their ornaments renewed, extending the period of active commemoration and maintaining the visible presence of the dead within the landscape of the living (016-015).
