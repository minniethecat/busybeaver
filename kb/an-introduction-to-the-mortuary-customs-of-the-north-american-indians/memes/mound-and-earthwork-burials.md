The great earthen mounds of North America — from the Cahokia complex in Illinois to the burial tumuli of the Cherokee and Choctaw — represent some of the most archaeologically significant mortuary structures documented in Yarrow's 1880 monograph. Mound burial was practiced across cultures from the Great Lakes to the Gulf Coast, encompassing both primary interment in purpose-built mounds and secondary burial in pre-existing earthworks. This page aggregates all pages documenting mound-building cultures and mound burial practices.

## Mound Burial in Yarrow's Survey

Yarrow devotes considerable attention to the "Mound-Builders" — a term his 19th-century sources used to describe the prehistoric cultures that constructed the great earthen monuments of the Mississippi and Ohio Valleys. His survey documents both the physical architecture of these structures (size, shape, orientation, internal chambers) and the grave goods found within them (pottery, copper ornaments, shell beads, stone implements). The pages indexed here cover both the cultures that built these monuments and the specific burial techniques associated with mound interment.
