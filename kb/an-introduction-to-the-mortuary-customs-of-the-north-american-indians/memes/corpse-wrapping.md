# Corpse Wrapping

Across North American Indian tribes, the practice of **corpse wrapping** — enclosing the dead body in skins, blankets, robes, or mats before burial or scaffold exposure — was nearly universal. The materials, methods, and elaborateness of wrapping varied with tribal custom, rank of the deceased, and available resources, but the underlying function was consistent: to protect the body, to honor the dead with their finest possessions, and in some cases to prepare the corpse for its journey to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

## Materials and Methods

The choice of wrapping material reflected both local ecology and social status. On the Plains, buffalo robes were the standard covering: among the [[sioux|Sioux]], bodies were "laid out upon the ground, wrapped in their robes of buffalo-skin, with their saddles, spears, camp-kettles, and all their accoutrements piled up around them" [012-002]. The [[wichita|Wichita]] wrapped their dead in blankets before transport: "the body is carried thither on the back of a pony, being first wrapped in blankets and then laid prone across the saddle" [001-009].

In the Eastern Woodlands, deerskins were preferred. An early account from [[virginia|Virginia]] describes how "the corpse being lapped in skins and mats with their jewels they lay them upon sticks in the ground" [006-010]. A more elaborate royal burial among the same tribes involved first drying the body, then wrapping it "very carefully in white skins" before rolling it in mats for preservation above ground [006-009].

Among the Pacific Coast tribes, Dr. [[sternberg-george-m|Sternberg]] documented a particularly elaborate wrapping from a scaffold burial: "The cradle was wrapped in two buffalo-robes of large size and well preserved; then came two blankets, and then a torn blanket, and last of all the dead body of an infant" [014-016]. The successive unwrapping revealed "a gray woolen double shawl, folded very carefully around the remains" [014-017].

## Wrapping for Scaffold and Tree Burial

For tribes that practiced scaffold or [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]], wrapping served the additional purpose of weatherproofing. The [[sioux|Sioux]] constructed scaffolds on which "the body is then carefully wrapped, so as to make it watertight, and laid to rest on the poles" [014-002]. Similarly, among the [[dakota|Dakotas]], when tree limbs were suitable, "the more general practice is to lay them upon scaffolds from 7 to 10 feet high," where proper wrapping protected the remains from scavengers [014-019].

The Flathead and Nez Percé practice was "to inclose the dead body in robes or blankets, the best owned by the departed, closely sewed up, and then, if a male or chief, fasten in the branches of a tree so high as to be beyond the reach of wolves" [014-004]. The body of a squaw or child, by contrast, "was thrown into the underbrush or jungle," a stark differential in mortuary treatment based on status.

## Wrapping in Flexed and Bundle Burials

Some tribes combined wrapping with a distinctive flexed position. An observer among early [[california|California]] tribes noted: "the Indians would dig a round hole in the ground, draw up the knees of the deceased Indian, and wrap the body into as small a bulk as possible in blankets, tie them firmly with cords, place them in the grave" [002-008]. This bundle-like treatment reduced the body to a compact parcel for [[inhumation|interment]].

The [[achomawi|Achomawi]] or [[achomawi|Pitt River Indians]] of [[california|California]] practiced a variation in which "their bodies being wrapped or bundled up in skins and laid in the grave in a standing position, the shoulders nearly even with the ground" [011-010]. The wrapping here was part of a broader mortuary complex that sometimes included decapitation of the corpse.

## Preservation and Mummification

In some cases, wrapping contributed to the natural preservation of remains. A remarkable find from a [[kentucky|Kentucky]] cave revealed an "exsiccated body of a female … enveloped in coarse clothes, … the whole wrapped in deer-skins, the hair of which was shaved off in the manner in which the Indians prepare them for robes" [006-017]. Another discovery yielded "several human bodies … found enwrapped carefully in skins and cloths" inhumed below the floor of a cave [006-023], the careful wrapping having contributed to their preservation.

## The Huron Shroud

Perhaps the most elaborate wrapping known was the communal shroud of the [[huron|Huron]] Feast of the Dead. The Jesuit missionary [[brebeuf-p-de|P. de Brebeuf]] described how the people of five large villages "deposited the bones of their dead in a gigantic shroud, composed of forty-eight robes, each robe being made of ten beaver skins" [020-005]. This communal wrapping, involving the accumulated wealth of entire communities, represented the apex of the wrapping tradition — transforming individual burial wrappings into a collective expression of grief and honor.
