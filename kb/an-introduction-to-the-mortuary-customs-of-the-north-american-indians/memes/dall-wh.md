# W. H. Dall

## Overview

William Healey Dall (1845–1927) was an American naturalist, paleontologist, and one of the earliest scientific explorers of [[alaska|Alaska]]. As a member of the Western Union Telegraph Expedition (1865–1868) and later with the United States Coast Survey, Dall spent decades documenting the natural history, geology, and Indigenous cultures of the [[alaska|Alaska]]n and Northwest Coast regions. His ethnological observations, published in the first volume of *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1877), included detailed descriptions of [[innuit|Innuit]] burial practices along the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian chain — accounts that Yarrow drew upon in his discussion of cave burial and mummification customs in the 1880 monograph (005-011)(006-036).

## Contributions

Dall provided Yarrow with one of the most important descriptions of [[alaska|Alaska]]n cave burial. In his Contributions to North American Ethnology, Dall recorded that the [[innuit|Innuit]] of the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands and the adjacent [[alaska|Alaska]]n coast interred their dead in dry caves and rock-shelters, often placing the body in a flexed or seated position and surrounding it with grave offerings that included hunting equipment, wooden masks, and personal ornaments. The dry, cold conditions of these caves resulted in natural mummification — a phenomenon that attracted considerable nineteenth-century scientific interest — and Dall was among the first trained naturalists to systematically document the practice. His observations contributed to Yarrow's broader treatment of cave burial as a continent-wide mortuary phenomenon, extending from the Atlantic seaboard through the Pacific Northwest (005-011)(005-012).

Dall also recorded accounts of Northwest Coast [[embalming]], whereby the bodies of high-status individuals were eviscerated, filled with preservative substances, and wrapped in multiple layers of cedar bark matting before being placed in mortuary houses or on elevated platforms. His dual training in natural science and ethnology lent unusual precision to his descriptions of the materials and techniques employed — a quality that distinguished his contributions from those of less methodically trained observers (006-036).

## Accounts Provided

Dall's published contributions to the 1877 *Contributions to North American Ethnology* volume cover [[innuit|Innuit]] cave inhumation, natural mummification in the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands, and the [[embalming]] customs of the Northwest Coast. His observations are especially valuable for their geological and paleontological context: Dall was attentive to the relationship between burial site characteristics (cave microclimate, stratigraphy) and the preservation outcomes Yarrow catalogued as "[[mummification|mummies]]."
