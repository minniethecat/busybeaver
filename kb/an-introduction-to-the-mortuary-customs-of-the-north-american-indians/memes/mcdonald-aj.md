# A. J. McDonald

## Overview

Dr. A. J. McDonald served as physician to the Los Pinos Indian Agency in Colorado during the late nineteenth century. In this capacity he gained first-hand knowledge of the mortuary practices of the [[ute|Ute]] Indians under the agency's jurisdiction. His communication describing [[ute|Ute]] crevice burial was incorporated by Yarrow into the 1880 monograph as a primary ethnographic source, offering one of the more detailed procedural accounts of rock-fissure [[inhumation|interment]] then available from the Rocky Mountain region (005-004).

## Contributions

McDonald's principal contribution to Yarrow's work was a comprehensive description of the [[ute|Ute]] crevice burial ritual, from the moment of death through the final disposition of the corpse. According to McDonald, when death occurred the event was immediately announced by the medicine-man, and the women of the band began preparing the body for burial without delay. The deceased's limbs were straightened, weapons were placed at his side, and his robes and blankets were wrapped securely around him — customarily the same garments in which he had died. Throughout the preparation, the women maintained a continuous dirge, a eulogistic death-chant whose doleful cadences could be heard for a considerable distance when the gathering of mourners was large (005-004).

The corpse, once prepared, was conveyed to a narrow cleft or fissure in the adjacent rock formations and deposited within. Stones were then piled over the body to deter wild animals. McDonald's account is notable for its meticulous attention to the sequence and social organization of the preparatory rites — details that in many other contemporary descriptions were either omitted or reduced to a single sentence (005-004).

## Accounts Provided

McDonald's written communication to Yarrow covers the full mortuary sequence of the [[ute|Ute]] bands attached to the Los Pinos Agency: the medicine-man's announcement of death, the rapid preparation of the corpse by women mourners, the wrapping of the body in its own robes and blankets, the accompaniment of the death-chant, and the final deposition in a rock crevice under a cairn of stones.
