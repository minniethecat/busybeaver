# John Wesley Powell

## Overview

John Wesley Powell (1834–1902) was an American geologist, ethnologist, and explorer who served as the founding Director of the Bureau of Ethnology at the Smithsonian Institution, the agency under whose auspices Yarrow's 1880 monograph *Introduction to the Study of Mortuary Customs Among the North American Indians* was published. His preface to the volume articulated the guiding philosophy of objective ethnological research — that the collector should record what Indians "do, and say, and believe" rather than speculate — a principle that shaped the entire work's documentary approach (P03-025)(P03-030). Powell's own field observations of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] cist-grave mounds, made during his geological surveys of the American West, were incorporated directly into Yarrow's chapter on burial in stone graves and mounds (003-004).

## Contributions

Powell's preface to Yarrow's monograph established the methodological framework for the entire study, emphasizing that "the mortuary customs of savage or barbaric people have a deep significance from the fact that in them are revealed much of the philosophy of the people by whom they are practiced." He identified five principal areas of inquiry — care of the lifeless body prior to burial, method of burial including site and attitude, gifts offered to the dead, customs of [[mourning]], and Indian explanations of human life and the spirit world — which together formed the organizational skeleton of Yarrow's subsequent chapters. Powell insisted on the primacy of direct observation and indigenous testimony over the accounts of "hasty travelers or subjective speculators," warning that "it is strange how much of accepted history must be rejected when the statements are carefully criticised and compared with known facts" (P03-030).

Powell's field observations of ancient cemeteries in [[tennessee|Tennessee]] contributed to the chapter on cist burial beneath mounds. He recorded that "these ancient cemeteries are exceedingly abundant throughout the State, often hundreds of graves may be found on a single hillside," noting the distinction between scattered graves and those collected in mounds, each mound being composed of a large number of individual cist graves accumulated over long periods. His observation that the earliest burials at the bottom and center of a mound exhibited a concentric arrangement with feet inward, progressively becoming more irregular as the mound expanded, demonstrated the dynamic, accretional character of these mortuary structures. He further documented the differential placement of grave goods — pipes, stone hammers, knives, and arrowheads with men; pottery, beads, and shells with women; and toy pottery and curious pebbles with children — revealing culturally patterned distinctions in burial treatment by age and sex (003-004).

Yarrow acknowledged in the volume's introduction that "since the inception of the work, most constant encouragement and advice has been received" from Powell, "to whom all American ethnologists owe a debt of gratitude which can never be repaid" (P03-030).
