# De Soto Expedition (1539–1542)

## Overview

The Hernando de Soto expedition (1539–1542) was the first major European incursion into the interior of the Southeastern United States. The expedition's chroniclers provided some of the earliest written descriptions of Native American mortuary practices east of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]], documenting [[ossuary|charnel-house]]s and burial temples that Yarrow (1880) cited as evidence for the sophistication of pre-contact Southeastern mortuary architecture (004-006)(005-003).

## Details

The chroniclers of De Soto's expedition described [[ossuary|charnel-house]]s — structures where the bones of the deceased were deposited after the flesh had decayed. These were typically located in temples or council-houses, with the remains placed in small chests of canes or splints. Yarrow noted that these were the "arks" that James Adair and other eighteenth-century authors likened to the Ark of the Covenant, part of a broader (and now discredited) theory tracing Native American descent from the ancient Israelites. The [[ossuary|charnel-house]]s represented a secondary burial practice — primary [[inhumation|interment]] or scaffold exposure was followed, after decomposition, by collection of the bones and deposition in communal ossuaries (004-006)(005-003).

## Significance

The De Soto accounts are historically significant as the earliest European documentation of Southeastern mortuary architecture. They established that the elaborate burial complexes — mounds, [[ossuary|charnel-house]]s, and temples — were functioning institutions at the time of contact, not merely prehistoric ruins. The expedition's descriptions of temple ossuaries in particular provided a terminus ante quem for the [[mississippi|Mississippi]]an mortuary complex before its disruption by European disease and colonization (004-006).
