## Overview

The Huron (also known as Wyandot) people of the Great Lakes region practiced one of the most elaborate and well-documented mortuary ceremonies in North America: the "Feast of the Dead." This communal secondary burial ceremony, witnessed in detail by the Jesuit missionary Père Jean de Brébeuf at the village of Ossosane in the 1630s, involved the exhumation of the dead from temporary graves, the ritual processing of their bones, and their collective reburial in a common ossuary. The ceremony was attended by as many as two thousand participants from multiple villages, who offered gifts "in testimony of their grief" (020-005). It represented both a religious event — freeing the souls of the dead for their journey to the spirit land — and a social occasion that reinforced inter-village alliances. Hind's Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition report of 1860 preserved and disseminated Brébeuf's account to the English-speaking scholarly community (020-004).

## The Ossosane Ceremony

Brébeuf's account describes a ceremony of remarkable scale. "The people belonging to five large villages deposited the bones of their dead in a gigantic shroud, composed of forty-eight robes, each robe being made of ten beaver skins" (020-005). The bones, carefully wrapped in this massive shroud — representing 480 beaver skins — were "placed between moss and bark" within a common tomb (020-005). A stone wall was constructed around the ossuary "to preserve it from profanation" (020-005). Before covering the bones with earth, Huron women scattered "a few grains of Indian corn" upon the sacred relics (020-005). The two thousand attendees offered some twelve hundred presents at the common tomb, an enormous material sacrifice that underscored both the ceremony's importance and the wealth of the Huron Confederacy at its cultural height.

## Spiritual Significance

The theological underpinnings of the Feast of the Dead were rooted in Huron beliefs about the soul's journey. According to Huron tradition, "the souls of the dead remain near the bodies until the 'feast of the dead'; after which ceremony they become free, and can at once depart for the land of spirits, which they believe to be situated in the regions of the setting sun" (020-005). This belief explains both the careful preservation of bones between death and the communal ceremony, and the importance placed on the proper performance of the feast — until its completion, the souls of the deceased were bound to the physical world. The ceremony thus carried enormous spiritual weight: failure to conduct it properly would condemn the dead to perpetual liminality, trapped between the world of the living and the spirit realm.

## Burial Feasts

The Hurons' burial feasts involved the serving of provisions for both the living and the dead, reflecting the pan-tribal belief "that while they partake of the visible material the departed spirit partakes at the same time of the spirit that dwells in the food" (020-005). These feasts were occasions for both mourning and celebration, with the distribution of gifts, the sharing of food, and the ceremonial deposition of the dead's personal possessions. The Huron burial feasts are recorded in the index as among the most notable examples of this widespread practice (024-208, 024-056). The ceremony occurred "before the dispersion of the Hurons" in the mid-seventeenth century (020-005), situating it within the pre-displacement cultural height of the Huron Confederacy.

## Historical Sources

The primary source for Huron burial customs is the account of Jean de Brébeuf, who "assisted at one of the 'feasts of the dead' at the village of Ossosane, before the dispersion of the Hurons" (020-004). Brébeuf's detailed observations, preserved in the Jesuit Relations, provide one of the most complete early European records of an indigenous North American mortuary ceremony. The account captures not only the material scale of the ceremony — forty-eight beaver-skin robes, two thousand attendees, twelve hundred presents — but also the theological framework that gave it meaning, making it an invaluable resource for understanding pre-contact Huron religion and social organization.
