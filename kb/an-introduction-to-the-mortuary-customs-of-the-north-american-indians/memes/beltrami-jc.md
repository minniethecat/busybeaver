# J. C. Beltrami

## Overview

Giacomo Costantino Beltrami (1779–1855) was an Italian jurist, author, and explorer who traveled through the Upper [[mississippi|Mississippi]] region in 1823, venturing into territories that few Europeans had previously visited. His two-volume travel narrative, *A Pilgrimage in Europe and America* (published in London, 1828), contained some of the earliest European eyewitness accounts of [[sioux|Sioux]] ([[dakota|Dakota]]) mortuary customs — recorded before the disruptions of widespread American settlement had substantially altered Indigenous funerary traditions. Beltrami's observations were cited by Yarrow for both his descriptions of funeral feasts and his account of grave-post practices along the Upper [[mississippi|Mississippi]] (020-001)(021-025).

## Contributions

Beltrami contributed a vivid firsthand description of a [[sioux|Sioux]] funeral ceremony performed in honor of his guide Cloudy Weather's son-in-law. He recorded what he termed the "singular and indeed ludicrous" contrast between the "terrific [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s and yells" of one part of the assembled company while others were "singing and dancing with all their might" — a simultaneity of grief and celebration that Beltrami, as an outsider steeped in European notions of solemn [[mourning]], found jarring enough to describe as a "funeral comedy." His account of a second ceremony, held for a member of the Grand Medicine society at which he was permitted to attend as "a man of another world," provided further detail: at the feast that followed, an allowance of every food article was served up for the deceased, while mourners beat, wounded, and tortured themselves, letting their blood flow "both over the dead man and his provisions." The deceased was then wrapped, together with his weapons, ornaments, and mystic medicine bag, in a buffalo skin, bound with tree-bark cord, and hung from a large oak rather than buried in the earth — a practice Beltrami was told reflected the belief that, since the man's favorite Manitou was the eagle, his spirit would more easily fly to [[spirit-land|paradise]] from such an elevated position (020-001).

Beltrami further described the grave-post practices he observed among the Upper [[mississippi|Mississippi]] tribes, recording posts surmounted by crosses alongside trunk-sections covered with hieroglyphics that recorded the number of enemies slain and the deceased's tutelary Manitous. His description of the juxtaposition of Christian crosses and Indigenous mortuary posts on neighboring graves provides one of the earliest documented instances of syncretic burial practice in the region — a phenomenon of considerable interest for the study of cultural contact and religious change among the upper [[mississippi|Mississippi]] tribes (021-025).

## Accounts Provided

Beltrami's *Pilgrimage in Europe and America* (1828, vol. ii, pp. 308 and 443) covers two [[sioux|Sioux]] funeral ceremonies with attendant feasts and self-mutilation practices, eagle-Manitou [[tree-burial]], and the coexistence of Christian and Indigenous mortuary markers. His narrative voice — by turns fascinated, ironic, and genuinely moved — is itself a valuable document of early-nineteenth-century European encounter with Native American deathways.
