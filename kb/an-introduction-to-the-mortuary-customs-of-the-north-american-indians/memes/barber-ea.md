# E. A. Barber

## Overview

Edwin Atlee Barber (1851–1916) was an American naturalist, archaeologist, and ceramic historian whose early career as a field researcher in the American West produced a series of valuable reports on Native American burial customs. His observations, published in *The American Naturalist* between 1873 and 1878, included detailed descriptions of communal [[urn-burial|urn burial]] among the [[mandan|Mandan]] of the Upper [[missouri|Missouri]] River and an unusual case of partial [[cremation]] — accounts that Yarrow incorporated without the editorial mediation applied to more prominent correspondents, granting Barber's testimony a directness that enhances its documentary value (007-007)(011-011).

## Contributions

Barber's most distinctive contribution to Yarrow's monograph is his description of [[mandan|Mandan]] [[urn-burial|urn burial]]: after the body had been exposed on a scaffold until the flesh was sufficiently decomposed, the bones were collected, cleansed, and deposited in communal earthenware urns, which were then buried in large circular pits. This practice — which combined the temporary scaffold exposure characteristic of the Plains tribes with the permanent urn [[inhumation|interment]] more commonly associated with Southeastern traditions — represented a unique synthesis of mortuary techniques that Barber documented with the eye of a trained naturalist. His record of the specific ceramic typologies of the burial urns and the spatial arrangement of the [[inhumation|interment]] pits is among the most archaeologically precise accounts in the Yarrow corpus (007-007).

Barber further contributed an account of a rare [[cremation]] case in which he described the partial [[cremation|burning]] of human remains within a burial context that showed signs of deliberate, rather than accidental, combustion. While acknowledging the unusual character of the evidence, Barber noted that the case appeared to represent a transitional practice between scaffold exposure and the more widespread Plains tradition of full body inhumation. His cautious handling of ambiguous evidence — refraining from over-confident cultural attribution while nonetheless recording the physical facts with care — exemplifies the empirical ethos that Yarrow valued in his network of correspondents (011-011).

## Accounts Provided

Barber's published contributions to the *American Naturalist* (vol. VII, 1873, and vol. XII, 1878) treat [[mandan|Mandan]] scaffold-to-urn secondary burial, the typology and arrangement of burial ceramics, and a disputed case of partial [[cremation]] from the Upper [[missouri|Missouri]] region. His work is noted for combining the observational precision of field archaeology with the comparative framework of natural history — a dual perspective that made his accounts especially useful to Yarrow's encyclopedic project.
