# Corpse Painting

The custom of **corpse painting** — applying pigments to the face and body of the deceased before [[inhumation|interment]] — was practiced by numerous North American Indian tribes. The colors, patterns, and occasions for painting varied by region and tribe, but the practice generally served to prepare the dead for their presentation to the spirit world, to mark social status, or to fulfill ritual obligations owed by the living to the deceased.

## Red Paint and the Symbolism of Life

Red was the most commonly used pigment in corpse painting, associated universally with blood, life, and vitality. Among the tribes of the Plains, red ochre or vermilion was applied to the face of the deceased to restore the appearance of life and to ensure that the spirit would be recognized and welcomed in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. The color red carried such strong symbolic weight that its absence from a burial was noted as an omission requiring explanation.

The practice was not limited to the face. Among the Southeastern tribes, the entire body was sometimes coated with red pigment before wrapping and [[inhumation|interment]]. This full-body application was associated particularly with chiefs and persons of high status, for whom the red coating served as both a mark of rank and a preservative. Archaeological excavations of mound burials have consistently yielded skeletal remains stained with red ochre, confirming the antiquity and wide distribution of the practice.

## Black Paint and Mourning

If red was the color of the dead, black was the color of the mourners. "The burial ended, the women being painted all their faces with black coal and oil do sit twenty-four hours in the houses [[mourning]] and lamenting" — this account from the Eastern Woodlands describes the painting of the living rather than the dead [006-010]. However, among some tribes, black was also applied to the corpse, particularly in cases of violent or untimely death, where the dark pigment was thought to contain or neutralize dangerous spiritual forces.

Among the [[california|California]] tribes, mourners blackened their faces with charcoal and grease for extended periods following a death — sometimes for months in the case of a chief. The contrast between the red-painted corpse and the black-painted mourners created a visual distinction between the dead, who were being prepared for a new existence, and the living, who were marked by loss.

## Facial Painting of the Corpse

The face received the most careful attention in corpse painting. Among the [[sioux|Sioux]], the face of the deceased was painted with designs that replicated those worn in life — in effect, the dead were sent to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] wearing their proper social face. This practice reflected the belief that recognition in the spirit world depended on the proper presentation of identity.

The [[dakota|Dakota]] and other Plains tribes painted the face of the deceased with vermilion, obtained through trade and highly valued for its brilliant red color. "Dr. [[sternberg-george-m|Sternberg]]," reporting on a scaffold burial, described "a bag of red paint" found among the wrappings — pigment intended for application to the body before or after [[inhumation|interment]] [014-017]. The inclusion of paint in the burial assemblage indicated that the painting of the corpse was not always a one-time event but could be renewed as part of ongoing mortuary ritual.

## Charcoal and Other Pigments

Beyond red and black, a range of other pigments were employed in specific contexts. White clay was used by some Southwestern tribes to coat the face of the dead, symbolizing purification. Yellow ochre appears in [[california|California]] burials, associated with particular clans or medicine societies. Charcoal — "pounded charcoal mixed with grease" — was the most accessible black pigment and was used across the continent for both corpse painting and mourner's body paint [006-010].

The pigments were applied with fingers, brushes made of chewed sticks, or stamps carved from wood or stone. The application was typically the responsibility of close female relatives or, in the case of chiefs and shamans, of designated ritual specialists who alone knew the proper designs and accompanying prayers.
