# Cave Burial

## Overview

Cave burial — the deposition of the dead in natural or partly artificial caves, caverns, and fissures in rock — represented one of the most ancient and geographically widespread modes of mortuary practice documented by Yarrow (1880). Unlike the constructed [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]] of mound or scaffold burial, cave burial appropriated features of the natural landscape, transforming geological formations into sacred repositories. Yarrow observed that such practices had been followed "since the earliest periods of time" and continued up to the present day not only among American Indians but among "peoples noted for their mental elevation and civilization" — a pointed acknowledgment of the practice's universality (005-001).

## Regional Distribution

### Western States and Territories

In 1872, Yarrow himself discovered a burial cave in the Territory of [[utah|Utah]], not far from the House Range of mountains. Its entrance resembled a mine shaft, and within, the [[gosh-ute|Gosi-Ute]] Indians had deposited their dead surrounded by various articles until the cave was "quite filled up" — at least twenty individuals, by Yarrow's estimate (005-003). In the majority of cases in the Western States and Territories, Yarrow concluded, the [[inhumation|interment]]s were primary deposits rather than secondary [[secondary-burial|reburial]]s (005-013).

### California Caves

Calaveras County, [[california|California]], yielded one of the most scientifically significant cave burials. Professor [[whitney-jd|J. D. Whitney]] described a cave near the Stanislaus River from which crania were collected for the Smithsonian Institution. The remains were found beneath a layer of stalagmitic breccia, suggesting considerable antiquity. Multiple caves in the vicinity had been used for burial, with human remains discovered in at least two or three of them (005-009)(005-010).

### Alaska and the Aleutian Islands

Among the earliest human remains found in [[alaska|Alaska]], according to [[dall-wh|W. H. Dall]], were crania discovered in the lowermost part of the Amaknak cave and at Adakh in the Bay of Islands. These remains, deposited in what [[dall-wh|Dall]] termed the Echinus layer, represented some of the oldest evidence of human occupation in the region (005-011)(005-012). In the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands, a volcanic cave on an island north of Ounalaska yielded mummified remains of Indians who had lived approximately one hundred and fifty years earlier. The cave "smelt strongly of hot sulphurous vapors," and the [[mummification|mummies]] were removed with great care along with their accompanying ornaments and trinkets (006-043)(006-044).

### Kentucky Saltpeter Caves

The saltpeter and other caves of [[kentucky|Kentucky]] produced a distinctive class of mummified remains whose preservation was the subject of archaeological debate. Unlike the deliberately embalmed bodies of the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands, Yarrow noted that it remained uncertain "whether any special pains were taken to preserve these bodies," as the impregnation of the cave soil with nitre and other minerals might have produced [[mummification|desiccation]] incidentally. One exsiccated female body was discovered at a depth of ten feet, "incased in broad stones standing on their edges, with a flat stone covering the whole," wrapped in coarse cloth and placed in a sitting posture (006-016)(006-017). Though some early observers speculated that these remains belonged to a race distinct from the historic tribes of [[kentucky|Kentucky]], Yarrow remained agnostic on the question (006-033).

## Connection with Mummification

Cave burial was closely linked to the practice of mummification and [[embalming]], as most preserved specimens were recovered from cave contexts. The dry, sheltered microclimate of caves — combined in some cases with mineral-rich soils — created conditions favorable to the preservation of soft tissue, hair, and textile wrappings. The [[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree]] or [[congaree-santee-indians|Santee Indians]] of [[carolina|South Carolina]], according to Lawson's accounts preserved in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]], practiced a form of partial embalmment but deposited their remains in boxes supported above the ground in caves rather than directly upon the cave floor (006-001)(006-014).

## Cultural Significance

Cave burial occupied a distinctive position in the spectrum of North American mortuary practices. Unlike earth burial, which required the excavation of graves, or scaffold burial, which demanded construction, cave burial appropriated natural spaces that already existed in the landscape. This appropriation often carried spiritual significance: caves, as liminal spaces between the surface world and the subterranean realm, were invested with symbolic meaning across numerous cultures. The practice of depositing grave goods alongside the dead within caves — tools, ornaments, and weapons — further underscored the conception of the cave as a dwelling place for the deceased in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

## Related Practices

- [[mummification]] — Natural and artificial preservation of bodies, commonly found in cave contexts
- [[embalming]] — Deliberate preservation techniques, distinct from incidental cave [[mummification|desiccation]]
- [[inhumation]] — Primary earth burial, the commonest alternative to cave deposition
- [[mound-burial]] — Constructed earthen [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]] contrasting with natural cave repositories
- [[ossuary]] — Communal bone depositories, sometimes located within caves
