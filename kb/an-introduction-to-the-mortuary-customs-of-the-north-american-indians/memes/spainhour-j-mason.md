# J. Mason Spainhour

## Overview

Dr. J. Mason Spainhour, a dentist practicing in Lenoir, Caldwell County, [[carolina|North Carolina]], conducted in March 1871 one of the more unusual amateur archaeological excavations recorded in Yarrow's 1880 monograph. Working on the farm of R. V. Michaux near [[menard-john|John]]'s River in Burke County, Spainhour opened a plowed-down mound and uncovered three human skeletons arranged in a configuration that he interpreted — controversially and with considerable ingenuity — as evidence that "the American Indians were in possession of at least some of the mysteries" of Freemasonry. Yarrow, while explicitly noting that he did not endorse Spainhour's Masonic interpretation, nevertheless reproduced the account in full, describing the burial as "unique, so far as known, and interesting as being *sui generis*" (004-035)(004-036).

## Contributions

Spainhour's excavation was conducted with a method that, while amateur by modern standards, was notably systematic for its time and context. He began by probing the mound with a sharpened stick four to five feet in length, striking a rock approximately eighteen inches below the surface. Excavation revealed a carefully constructed grave oriented due east and west, measuring approximately nine by six feet, with walls distinctly marked by the contrast between the surrounding black loam and the white or yellow sand — carried, Spainhour inferred, from the riverbank two hundred yards distant — that had been used to fill around the bodies. The grave contained three skeletons, each in a sitting posture and each associated with distinct grave goods that Spainhour documented in ascending order of elaboration (004-036).

The first skeleton, which Spainhour judged from cranial sutures to be twenty-five to twenty-eight years of age, faced east. Its right hand rested upon a flat stone, beside which lay a small stone implement resembling a tomahawk, approximately five inches in length. Coarse chalk-like beads encircled the neck, and a lump of red paint about the size of an egg was found near the right side. The top of the cranium rested approximately twelve inches below the plow mark. The second skeleton, judged to be about fifty years of age, also faced east, with a rock on its right supporting the hand bones and a tomahawk originally about seven inches in length — broken into two pieces — that was better finished than the first. The beads around its neck were smaller and finer, and a much larger quantity of red paint accompanied the burial. The third skeleton, estimated at forty years of age, faced west and was accompanied by the finest grave goods of the three: a tomahawk of approximately eight inches, broken into three pieces and composed of better material with superior finishing; the smallest and finest beads; and the largest quantity of paint (004-036)(004-048).

Spainhour's Masonic interpretation turned on several features of the grave's arrangement: the east-west orientation, the presence of what he interpreted as an altar in the center, the occupation of the south, west, and east positions while the north remained vacant, and the differentiated grave goods that he read as implements of lodge office. Yarrow reproduced this interpretation but appended a cautionary note: "If Dr. Spainhour's inferences are incorrect, still there is a remarkable coincidence of circumstances patent to every Mason." The account remains valuable not for its speculative conclusions but for its meticulous documentation of a three-person seated burial — a configuration for which few archaeological parallels existed then or exist now in the Southeastern archaeological record (004-048).
