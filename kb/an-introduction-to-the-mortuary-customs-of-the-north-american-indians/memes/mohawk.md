# Mohawk

## Overview

The Mohawks, the easternmost nation of the [[iroquois|Iroquois Confederacy]], inhabited the Mohawk River valley of present-day [[new-york|New York]]. Yarrow (1880) preserved an account of their mortuary customs drawn from [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1853), describing a distinctive mode of inhumation in which the body was interred upright in a large round hole, covered with timber and earth, and surmounted by a mound — a practice that produced earthworks resembling the tumuli of the mound-building cultures farther south.

## Mortuary Customs

### Upright Inhumation

[[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]] recorded that the Mohawks "made a large round hole in which the body was placed upright or upon its haunches, after which it was covered with timber, to support the earth which they lay over, and thereby kept the body from being pressed. They then raised the earth in a round hill over it" (001-002). This practice combined elements of inhumation (burial in a dug pit) with mound construction (the raised earth above the timber covering), resulting in a burial monument that would have been visible on the landscape.

The body was dressed "in all its finery," and wampum and other valuables were placed in the grave. The relatives maintained the grave site with evident devotion: they "suffered not grass nor any weed to grow upon the grave, and frequently visited it and made [[death-wailing|lamentation]]." This ongoing care of the burial mound — keeping it clear of vegetation and returning to mourn — indicates that the grave site served as an enduring locus of remembrance rather than a place abandoned after the funeral (001-002).

## Historical Sources

The account of Mohawk burial practices in Yarrow derives from [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]]'s monumental *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1853), which compiled early colonial observations of Iroquoian customs. The description likely reflects Mohawk practice as observed by seventeenth- and eighteenth-century European visitors to the Mohawk Valley. The timber-covered, mound-surmounted grave described by [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]] represents a mode of [[inhumation|interment]] that may have been influenced by or related to the mound-building traditions of the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]] cultures.
