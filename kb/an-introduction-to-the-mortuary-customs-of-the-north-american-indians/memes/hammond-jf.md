# J. F. Hammond

## Overview

Dr. J. F. Hammond served as a United States Army surgeon at posts in the Pacific Northwest during the 1870s. His medical duties at Bellingham Bay, Washington Territory, brought him into contact with the Indigenous communities of the Salish Sea region, and his description of their mortuary practices — specifically the use of carved wooden sarcophagi enclosed within rectangular tents of white material — provided Yarrow with one of the few documented accounts of box burial from the far Northwest Coast. Though Hammond's contribution to the monograph was brief, it filled a geographic gap in the coverage of surface-level burial structures between the more extensively documented practices of the Plains scaffold-burial tribes and the coastal [[canoe-burial]] traditions (012-006).

## Contributions

Hammond recorded that the Indians of Bellingham Bay placed their dead in carved wooden sarcophagi, which were then enclosed within a rectangular tent constructed of some white material — likely canvas or sailcloth obtained through trade. This practice combined Indigenous wood-carving traditions (the sarcophagi were carved, suggesting a degree of ceremonial elaboration beyond mere utilitarian construction) with the tent-enclosure form that reflected the wider Northwest Coast pattern of small purpose-built mortuary structures placed above ground. Yarrow cited Hammond's account in his chapter on burial above ground, alongside comparative examples from the [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]], [[sioux|Sioux]], and [[navajo|Navajo]], who buried in lodges, and from Costa Rica, where the dead were preserved in small palm-reed huts for three years. The Bellingham Bay practice, as described by Hammond, represented an intermediate form — neither the full-scale lodge burial of the Plains tribes nor the elevated canoe-and-[[scaffold-burial|platform burial]] of the outer Northwest Coast, but a distinctive regional variant that combined the sarcophagus form with a dedicated, small-scale surface enclosure (012-006).

The brevity of Hammond's account — a single sentence in Yarrow's text — is typical of many Army surgeon contributions to the monograph, reflecting the incidental nature of their observations: trained medical professionals who encountered Indigenous burial practices in the course of their official duties and recorded them with clinical precision, but without the extended ethnographic engagement that characterized the work of contributors like [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]] or [[powers-stephen|Powers]]. In this respect Hammond's contribution, though limited in scope, is representative of a valuable class of sources: observations made by scientifically trained witnesses who had no particular stake in the ethnological disputes of their era.

## Accounts Provided

Hammond's sole published contribution to Yarrow covers Bellingham Bay (Washington Territory) sarcophagus burial with white-material tent enclosure. The observation is brief but geographically significant, extending the known distribution of box/surface burial to the southern Salish Sea.
