# Missouri

## Overview

The Missouri River valley and the state of Missouri were among the richest archaeological regions for nineteenth-century investigations of prehistoric Native American mortuary practices. The region's numerous burial mounds and the urn-burial site at New Madrid provided key evidence for Yarrow's survey of mound-builder and [[mississippi|Mississippi]]an cultures (004-003)(007-002).

## Burial Sites & Practices

### Burial Mounds
The Missouri mounds were among the most extensively documented in Yarrow's work. These earthen structures, built over centuries by successive [[inhumation|interment]]s, ranged from simple conical tumuli to complex chambered mounds. The mounds contained multiple burials at different levels, with grave goods including pottery, stone tools, and ornaments. Yarrow cited accounts from various excavators who opened Missouri mounds, finding both primary and secondary burials (004-003)(004-006).

### Urn-Burial at New Madrid
Professor Swallow recovered a human skull enclosed in an earthen jar from a mound at New Madrid, Missouri — a find of particular interest because the jar's lip was too small to permit extraction of the skull. This indicated the clay must have been molded around the head after death, a practice with intriguing parallels to Chaldean urn-burial methods documented in Rawlinson's Herodotus. The New Madrid find was among the most technically puzzling urn-burial specimens in North America (007-002).
