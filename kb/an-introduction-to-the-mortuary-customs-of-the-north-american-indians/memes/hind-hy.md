# Henry Youle Hind

## Overview

Henry Youle Hind (1823–1908) was a Canadian geologist, explorer, and professor at the University of Toronto whose scientific expeditions across the Canadian West in the late 1850s produced the first systematic geographic and ethnographic surveys of the Assiniboine and Red River regions. His two-volume *Narrative of the Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition of 1857 and of the Assiniboine and Saskatchewan Exploring Expedition of 1858* (published 1860) included extensive observations of Indigenous peoples, among them an account of the [[huron|Huron]] Feast of the Dead that preserved the eyewitness testimony of the seventeenth-century Jesuit missionary Jean de [[brebeuf-p-de|Brebeuf]]. Yarrow cited this passage in the 1880 monograph as evidence of the enormous scale of communal [[ossuary]] [[secondary-burial|reburial]] among the [[huron|Huron]] and of the theological beliefs that accompanied this practice (020-004).

## Contributions

The passage from Hind's narrative cited by Yarrow preserves [[brebeuf-p-de|De Brebeuf]]'s description of a Feast of the Dead that took place at the [[huron|Huron]] village of Ossosane before the dispersal of the [[huron|Huron]] nation in the mid-seventeenth century. According to this account, the ceremony assembled two thousand Indians who offered twelve hundred presents at the common tomb in testimony of their grief. The people of five large villages deposited the bones of their dead in a single gigantic shroud composed of forty-eight robes, each robe itself made from ten beaver skins — a total of four hundred and eighty beaver pelts committed to the mortuary wrapping alone. After being carefully enveloped in this shroud, the bones were placed between layers of moss and bark, and a wall of stones was built around the vast [[ossuary]] to preserve it from profanation. Before the bones were covered with earth, the women threw grains of Indian corn upon the sacred relics, an offering that joined agricultural plenty to the ritual of collective [[secondary-burial|reburial]] (020-004).

The theological dimension of the Feast of the Dead, as transmitted through Hind's citation of [[brebeuf-p-de|De Brebeuf]], was central to its meaning. The [[huron|Huron]] believed that the souls of the dead remained near their bodies until the performance of the communal feast; only after this ceremony did the souls become free and able to depart at once for the land of spirits, situated in the regions of the setting sun. This conception of the soul's dependence upon ritual for its liberation — in which the physical [[secondary-burial|reburial]] of bones and the ceremonial gathering of the living were both necessary conditions for the dead to complete their journey to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] — represents one of the clearest articulations in the monograph of the theological logic underlying secondary burial (020-004).

Hind's broader ethnographic work on the Canadian expeditions, which documented the customs of the Cree, Assiniboine, and other Plains and Woodland nations, contributed to a growing body of mid-nineteenth-century Canadian scientific literature on Indigenous peoples that Yarrow selectively mined for comparative data on mortuary customs.
