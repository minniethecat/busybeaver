# Funeral Ceremonies

## Overview

Funeral ceremonies among the North American tribes ranged from elaborate multi-day spectacles involving thousands of participants to simple nocturnal burials with minimal ritual. Despite the diversity of form, common elements included [[death-wailing|lamentation]], offerings to the dead, ceremonial feasting, and prescribed periods of [[mourning]]. Yarrow documented these rites across dozens of tribes, revealing both the intensity of grief and the structured formality with which death was managed (019-001).

Among the [[klamath|Klamath and Trinity Indians]] of the Northwest coast, "the funeral ceremonies occupy three days during which the soul of the deceased is in danger from O-mah-u or the devil. To preserve it from this peril a fire is kept up at the grave and the friends of the deceased howl around it to scare away the demon" (001-020).

## Tribal Distribution

### Crow

One of the most dramatic funeral scenes recorded was the [[mourning]] for a Crow head chief, as witnessed by [[beckwourth-james|James Beckwourth]]. Upon news of the chief's death reaching the village, "every lodge [was] laid prostrate" and "blood was streaming from every conceivable part of the bodies of all who were old enough to comprehend their loss. Hundreds of fingers were dismembered; hair torn from the head lay in profusion about the paths" (019-002).

At a subsequent assembly of over ten thousand [[crows|Crows]], the self-mutilation intensified: "warriors would cut two gashes nearly the entire length of their arm, then separating the skin from the flesh at one end, would grasp it in their other hand and rip it asunder to the shoulder" (019-003).

### Chippewa

[[chippewa|Chippewa]] [[mourning]] for a deceased husband imposed a twelve-month regimen on the widow. She "mourns the loss of her husband; by day as by night she is heard silently sobbing; she is a constant visitor to the place of rest" (019-005). During this period she was "not permitted to wear any finery, neither is she permitted to slicken up and comb her head; this to avoid attracting attention" (019-005).

At the expiration of the term, "the female relatives of deceased assemble and, with greetings commensurate to the occasion, proceed to wash her face, comb her hair, and attire her person with new apparel" (019-006).

### Choctaw — The "Last Cry"

Benson described the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] funeral as "the last cry." The grave was prepared with the deceased's weapons, enclosed with pickets, and the widow became the chief mourner: "At night and morning she will go to the grave and pour forth the most piteous cries and [[mourning|wailing]]s" (019-010). After one lunar month, the community assembled for a feast of corn and jerked beef, followed by a dance that continued until morning, officially ending the [[mourning]] period (019-010).

### Iroquois — Dance for the Dead

The [[iroquois|Iroquois]] performed the O-he-wa, or "dance for the dead," danced exclusively by women to mournful vocal music. It "commenced at dusk or soon after and continued until towards morning, when the shades of the dead who were believed to be present and participate in the dance were supposed to disappear" (021-006). This dance was performed whenever a bereaved family called for it, typically a year after the death (021-006).

## Common Elements

Across tribal boundaries, funeral ceremonies shared certain features: the dressing of the corpse in its finest garments, the placement of weapons, tools, and food in the grave, the cutting of hair and gashing of flesh by mourners, and the killing of the deceased's horses at the graveside (001-009)(001-025). The [[wichita|Wichita]] grave was "dug from 3 to 4 feet deep," the body placed "with the head towards the west and the feet to the east," and a pen of poles built around the filled grave "forming a complete protection from the invasion of wild animals" (001-009).
