The Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition of 1857, together with the Assiniboine and Saskatchewan Exploring Expedition of 1858, was led by the Canadian geologist and professor [[hind-hy|Henry Youle Hind]] under the authority of the Province of Canada. The expedition surveyed the Red River of the North, the Assiniboine River, and the Saskatchewan River valleys, producing the first systematic geographic and ethnographic surveys of these regions. Hind's two-volume *Narrative of the Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition of 1857 and of the Assiniboine and Saskatchewan Exploring Expedition of 1858*, published in London in 1860, included extensive observations of the Indigenous peoples inhabiting the Canadian West.

## Ethnological Contributions

Hind's narrative made one contribution of particular significance to the study of North American mortuary customs: it preserved and disseminated the seventeenth-century account of the [[huron|Huron]] Feast of the Dead by the Jesuit missionary Jean de [[brebeuf-p-de|Brébeuf]].

**Preservation of Brébeuf's Account.** During his research, Hind excerpted Brébeuf's detailed eyewitness description of the Huron Feast of the Dead — a communal [[secondary-burial|ossuary reburial]] ceremony held approximately every ten to twelve years. Brébeuf had personally attended one such ceremony at the village of Ossosane in the 1630s, before the Huron were dispersed by Iroquois attacks in 1649. Hind's reproduction of this passage made the account accessible to the English-speaking scholarly community of the mid-nineteenth century.

**Chain of Citation.** Yarrow (1880) cited Brébeuf's account through Hind's intermediary publication, creating a chain of documentary transmission spanning two and a half centuries: from Brébeuf's original Jesuit *Relation* (1630s), through Hind's *Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition* (1860), to Yarrow's *Introduction to the Mortuary Customs of the North American Indians* (1880). This chain illustrates the deep temporal reach of the Jesuit missionary record in nineteenth-century American ethnology (020-004).

## The Feast of the Dead

The ceremony that Brébeuf described — and that Hind preserved for Yarrow's use — involved the mass exhumation of all community members who had died since the previous feast, the ritual cleaning and wrapping of their bones in beaver-skin bundles, and their communal reinterment in a single great ossuary pit. The ceremony was attended by as many as two thousand participants from multiple villages, who offered gifts "in testimony of their grief." It served simultaneously as a religious event — freeing the souls of the dead for their journey to the spirit land — and as a social institution reinforcing inter-village alliances (020-005).
