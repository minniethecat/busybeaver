# F. E. Grossman

## Overview

Captain F. E. Grossman was a United States Army officer stationed in the [[arizona|Arizona]] Territory during the early 1870s whose meticulous firsthand observations of [[pima|Pima]] ([[pima|Akimel O'odham]]) burial customs were published in the *Smithsonian Report* for 1871 and subsequently incorporated into Yarrow's 1880 monograph. His account is distinguished by its detailed description of the physical mechanics of [[pima|Pima]] inhumation — the binding of the corpse into a seated posture, the construction of the round grave with its side vault, and the practical measures taken to protect the burial from coyotes — as well as by his attention to the social dimensions of [[pima|Pima]] mortuary practice, including the anticipatory digging of graves, the nocturnal timing of [[inhumation|interment]]s, and the destruction of the deceased's property (001-022).

## Contributions

Grossman's description of [[pima|Pima]] burial, reproduced verbatim by Yarrow, documents a distinctive method of corpse preparation practiced by the [[pima|Pima]] of the Gila River region. The bodies of the dead were bound with ropes passed around the neck and under the knees, drawn tight until the body was forced into a doubled-up sitting position — a posture that minimized the size of the required grave and that Grossman recorded with the dispassionate precision of a military observer. The grave itself was dug four to five feet deep and perfectly round, approximately two feet in diameter, with a side vault hollowed out at the bottom large enough to contain the seated body. After deposition, the grave was filled level with the ground, and poles, trees, or pieces of timber were placed upon it to protect the remains from coyotes (001-022).

Grossman noted several features of [[pima|Pima]] mortuary practice that distinguished it from the customs of neighboring groups. Burials typically took place at night without elaborate ceremony; the mourners chanted during the burial, but Grossman observed that overt signs of grief were rare. Most strikingly, [[pima|Pima]] graves were frequently prepared before the patient actually died — a practice that sometimes resulted in open graves remaining visible in burial grounds when the anticipated deceased unexpectedly recovered. This anticipatory preparation of the grave, which Grossman described without judgment, offers a window into the practical, matter-of-fact orientation of [[pima|Pima]] attitudes toward death, in which the physical preparations for [[inhumation|interment]] proceeded independently of and sometimes in advance of the event they were designed to serve (001-022).

The social dimension of [[pima|Pima]] mortuary practice was equally notable. Immediately after burial, the house and personal effects of the deceased were burned, and his horses and cattle killed, the meat being cooked as a repast for the mourners — a practice that simultaneously honored the dead, provisioned the living, and effected a material severance between the deceased and the community of survivors. The nearest relatives remained in the village for weeks or sometimes months as a sign of sorrow, the men cutting off approximately six inches of their long hair and the women cutting their hair quite short. The selection of burial places some distance from the village, preferably in groves of mesquite bushes, further underscored the spatial separation between the realms of the living and the dead (001-022).
