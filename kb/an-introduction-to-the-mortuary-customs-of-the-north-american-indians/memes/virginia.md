## Overview

Virginia, one of the earliest regions of European contact in North America, appears in Yarrow's 1880 survey through references to aboriginal mummification practices and burial customs shared with neighboring [[carolina|Carolina]] and [[florida|Florida]] tribes.

## Burial Sites & Practices

Yarrow notes that certain mortuary practices were "confined to among certain tribes of Virginia, the [[carolina|Carolinas]], and [[florida|Florida]]," indicating a shared southeastern coastal tradition (006-004). The practice involved above-ground storage of the dead in charnel houses, often followed by secondary bundle burial.

The index of Yarrow's monograph includes specific entries for "Virginia [[mummification|mummies]]," indicating that natural or artificial mummification was practiced by the aboriginal inhabitants of the state (024-403). These [[mummification|mummies]], like those of [[kentucky|Kentucky]], were typically preserved through a combination of evisceration, smoke-drying, and wrapping in skins or matting. The Virginia mummy tradition shares affinities with the better-documented practices of the [[carolina|Carolina]] coastal region.

### The Beverly Account: Royal Mummification Process

The most detailed account of Virginia aboriginal mummification comes from Robert Beverly's *History of Virginia* (1722), which Yarrow quotes at length to illustrate the elaborate process reserved for persons of distinction — particularly the Werowanco, or tribal kings:

> "The *Indians* are religious in preserving the Corpses of their Kings and Rulers after Death, which they order in the following manner: First, they neatly flay off the Skin as entire as they can, slitting it only in the Back; then they pick all the Flesh off from the Bones as clean as possible, leaving the Sinews fastned to the Bones, that they may preserve the Joints together: then they dry the Bones in the Sun, and put them into the Skin again, which in the mean time has been kept from drying or shrinking; when the Bones are placed right in the Skin, they nicely fill up the Vacuities, with a very fine white Sand. After this they sew up the Skin again, and the Body looks as if the Flesh had not been removed. They take care to keep the Skin from shrinking, by the help of a little Oil or Grease, which saves it also from Corruption. The Skin being thus prepar'd, they lay it in an apartment for that purpose, upon a large Shelf rais'd above the Floor. This Shelf is spread with Mats, for the Corpse to rest easy on, and skreened with the same, to keep it from the Dust. The Flesh they lay upon Hurdles in the Sun to dry, and when it is thoroughly dried, it is sewed up in a Basket, and set at the Feet of the Corpse, to which it belongs. In this place also they set up a *Quioccos,* or Idol, which they believe will be a Guard to the Corpse. Here Night and Day one or other of the Priests must give his Attendance, to take care of the dead Bodies. So great an Honour and Veneration have these ignorant and unpolisht People for their Princes even after they are dead." (006-004, quoting Beverly 1722)

Yarrow adds the cautionary note that "this account and others like it are somewhat apocryphal, and it has been copied and recopied a score of times" (006-004), reflecting the nineteenth-century ethnologist's awareness that early colonial accounts often blended observation with embellishment. Nevertheless, the core elements — flaying, defleshing, drying, reassembly with sand packing, and curation in a dedicated mortuary structure — are consistent with other described practices of the southeastern coastal region.

### The Pinkerton Account

Yarrow also cites Pinkerton's *Collection of Voyages* (1812) for a corroborating description of Werowanco burial:

> "By him is commonly the sepulchre of their Kings. Their bodies are first bowelled, then dried upon hurdles till they be very dry, and so about the most of their joints and neck they hang bracelets, or chains of copper, pearl, and such like, as they used to wear. Their inwards they stuff with copper beads, hatchets, and such trash. Then lap they them very carefully in white skins, and so roll them in mats for their winding-sheets. And in the tomb, which is an arch made of mats, they lay them orderly. What remaineth of this kind of wealth their Kings have, they set at their feet in baskets. These temples and bodies are kept by their priests." (006-005)
