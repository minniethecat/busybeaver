# L. S. Turner

## Overview

Dr. L. S. Turner was a United States Army surgeon stationed at Fort Peck, Montana Territory, during the 1870s, whose six years of continuous interaction with several subdivisions of the [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[sioux|Sioux]]) people yielded the most comprehensive single account of Plains Indian scaffold burial and [[mourning]] in Yarrow's 1880 monograph. Where many of the monograph's correspondents contributed brief descriptions of isolated burial practices, Turner's narrative — reproduced in its entirety by Yarrow — spanned the entire mortuary sequence from the moment of death through the construction of the scaffold, the material culture of corpse preparation and grave goods, the self-mutilation of mourners, and the daily ritual of [[death-wailing|lamentation]] at the burial site (014-018).

## Contributions

Turner's account of [[dakota|Dakota]] burial practice begins with the preparation of the corpse, a task assigned exclusively to the women — usually the old women — who commenced work as soon as life was extinct. The face, neck, and hands of the deceased were thickly painted with vermilion or a locally obtained red earth. The personal clothing and trinkets of the deceased were used to ornament the body, which was then wrapped in a blanket, enveloped in a dressed buffalo skin with the flesh side outward, and securely bound with rawhide thongs. When available, a bright-red blanket formed the outermost layer, a covering that Turner noted rendered the scene "more picturesque until dimmed by time and the elements." The body was borne by the women to the scaffold — constructed upon four posts set into the ground at a height of seven to ten feet — and left "prone in its secure wrappings upon this airy bed of death," the ceremony accompanied by [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s that Turner described as "so wild and weird that one must see and hear in order to appreciate" (014-018).

The differential treatment of the dead by status was carefully recorded. If the deceased was a brave, dried buffalo heads were placed upon or beneath the scaffold. If he had distinguished himself in war, implements of battle were placed on the scaffold or securely tied to its timbers. If the deceased was a chief or a soldier related to his chief, it was not uncommon to slay his favorite pony and place the body beneath the scaffold, under the belief that the horse accompanied the man into the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. Turner's dry observation of this custom — he once loaned a delft urinal to an old man for his dying son, making him "promise faithfully that he would return it as soon as his son was done using it," only to see the urinal placed on the young man's scaffold, where it remained — captures both the ethnographic fact of grave-good provisioning and the cultural gulf between Euro-American and [[dakota|Dakota]] conceptions of property and death (014-018).

Turner's most harrowing descriptions concern the [[mourning]] customs of the [[dakota|Dakotas]]. The hair, never cut under ordinary circumstances, was cropped even with the neck. The head, forehead, and sometimes nearly the entire body were smeared with a white clay-like substance. The lodge, teepee, and all family possessions except the few shabby articles worn by the mourners were given away, leaving the family destitute. On the first, second, or third day after the funeral, the wives, mother, and sisters of the deceased frequently threw off their moccasins and leggings, gashed their legs with butcher knives, and marched through the camp and to the burial place with bare and bleeding extremities, chanting their dismal songs of [[mourning]]. Turner treated one chief who had gashed the outer side of both lower extremities at intervals of a few inches from ankle to hip after the death of his brother — wounds that had inflamed from exposure and were suppurating freely — and recorded that after dressing the wounds and administering an anodyne, the chief "slept long and refreshingly, and awoke to express his gratitude and shake my hand in a very cordial and sincere manner" (014-018).

Beyond these dramatic observances, Turner recorded the quieter daily rituals of grief: the old father going daily to his child's grave at sunset, pouring out his grief in wails "that would move a demon," until his figure melted with the gray twilight; the kindling of a small fire near the head of the scaffold, the [[mourning]] varied with smoking in silence. Turner's narrative, Yarrow noted, was "drawn from memory of personal observances during a period of more than six years' constant intercourse with several subdivisions of the [[dakota|Dakota]] Indians" — a depth of sustained engagement unmatched by any other contributor to the monograph (014-021).
