The Stansbury Expedition (1849–1850), officially the "Explorations of the Valley of the Great Salt Lake of Utah," was a U.S. Army topographical survey led by Captain Howard Stansbury. Among its incidental contributions to ethnology was the documentation of Indigenous burial practices in the Great Basin and Plains regions.

## Background

The expedition was commissioned as part of the U.S. Army Corps of Topographical Engineers' systematic survey of the American West. Its primary objectives were to map the Great Salt Lake and surrounding valleys, assess routes for military and emigrant travel, and document the natural resources of the newly acquired Utah Territory. Stansbury's resulting publication, *Exploration of the Valley of the Great Salt Lake of Utah* (1852), became an important primary source for nineteenth-century ethnological studies of the region's Indigenous peoples.

## Burial Documentation

### Sioux Lodge Burial

Stansbury provided one of the earliest detailed descriptions of lodge burial among the Sioux. This form of sepulture — classified by Yarrow under "sepulture above the ground" alongside box, scaffold, tree, and canoe burial — involved placing the deceased within a lodge structure rather than interring the body. Stansbury noted that this practice was by no means common, making his account particularly valuable for ethnological study (012-001).

The description which follows is by Stansbury and relates specifically to the Sioux (012-001):

> The body was placed in a lodge which had been erected for the purpose, and which was similar in form to the ordinary skin lodge. The body was placed on a scaffold within the lodge, and arms, ammunition, and other articles were deposited with it. The lodge was then closed, and a fire kindled near it, where the mourners assembled nightly to wail and chant.

This account combined with observations of the fire ritual provided one of the most comprehensive contemporary descriptions of upper-world Sioux burial practices then available.

### Great Salt Lake Context

The Great Salt Lake region was home to several Indigenous groups, including the Gosh-Ute (Goshute), who practiced a distinctive form of aquatic burial — sinking their dead in springs weighted with stones. Captain J. H. Simpson, during a subsequent exploration of the Great Salt Lake Valley in 1859, documented this practice, noting that Skull Valley derived its name from the number of skulls found there as a consequence of this custom (017-003). Simpson noted that his guide, George W. Bean, had actually seen the Indians bury their dead in springs near the town of Provo, where Bean resided (017-003).

As corroboration of the aquatic burial account, Captain Simpson mentioned that arriving at a spring one evening during the expedition, they were obliged to dig out the skeleton of an Indian from the mud at the bottom before using the water (017-004). Yarrow remarked that this peculiar mode of burial was entirely unique, so far as known, and but for the well-known probity of the relator might well be questioned — especially when considering that in the arid region water was scarce and Indians were generally careful not to pollute the streams or springs near which they lived (017-005).

## Legacy

Stansbury's published report became a frequently cited source in nineteenth-century ethnological compilations, including H. C. Yarrow's 1880 monograph on North American Indian mortuary customs. Stansbury's documentation bridged the initial phase of military exploration with the emerging scientific discipline of ethnology. The expedition's observations — together with Simpson's later account of Goshute burial practices — contributed key data points to the comparative study of Indigenous mortuary variation across the Great Basin and Plains culture areas. Yarrow drew directly upon both Stansbury's and Simpson's published reports in constructing the sections of his monograph dealing with lodge burial and aquatic interment (012-001)(017-003).
