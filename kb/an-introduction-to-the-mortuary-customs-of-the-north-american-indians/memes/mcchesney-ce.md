## Overview

Dr. Charles E. McChesney was a United States Army physician who served for some time among the Wahpeton and Sisseton [[sioux|Sioux]]. He furnished Yarrow with a detailed and important account of the "ghost gamble" — a distinctive funeral gambling custom peculiar to the [[sioux|Sioux]] — which was incorporated into the 1880 monograph on North American Indian mortuary customs (021-019).

## Contributions

Dr. McChesney's primary contribution to Yarrow's work was his detailed description of the [[sioux|Sioux]] "ghost gamble," a ritual game played with marked wild-plum stones during the funeral rites of wealthy Indians. According to McChesney's account, after the death of a wealthy individual, the near relatives would take charge of the deceased's effects and, at a stated time — usually during the first feast held after the death — the gambling ceremony would commence (021-019). McChesney noted that this practice appeared to be peculiar to the [[sioux|Sioux]], serving as a secondary or adjunct part of the funeral rites in which participants gambled for possession of the property of the deceased. For assistance in obtaining this account, McChesney acknowledged his indebtedness to Dr. C. C. Miller, physician to the Sisseton Indian Agency (021-024).

## Accounts Provided

McChesney's account of the [[sioux|Sioux]] "ghost gamble" contributed to Yarrow's coverage of the gaming and property-redistribution aspects of [[sioux|Sioux]] mortuary rites. The game utilized rare sets of figured wild-plum seeds, which McChesney noted were nearly a hundred years old at the time of his writing.
