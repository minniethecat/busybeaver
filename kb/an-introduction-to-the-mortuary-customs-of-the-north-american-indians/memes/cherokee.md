# Cherokee

## Overview

The Cherokee were one of the principal Native American tribes of the Southeastern United States, occupying lands in present-day [[carolina|North Carolina]], [[tennessee|Tennessee]], [[georgia|Georgia]], and [[carolina|South Carolina]]. At the time of Yarrow's compilation (1880), the Cherokee had already been subject to significant displacement, yet their mortuary customs were documented through archaeological evidence and early observer accounts (011-003)(013-008).

## Mortuary Customs

### Partial Cremation
The Cherokee, or a related tribe of [[carolina|North Carolina]], practiced a distinctive form of partial [[cremation]]. According to [[foster-jw|J. W. Foster]]'s *Pre-Historic Races* (1873), archaeological remains in the [[tennessee|Little Tennessee River]] valley suggested that bodies were partially burned before [[inhumation|interment]]. The Cherokee held this region until 1819, when treaty cessions opened the land to settlement, revealing burial sites that combined elements of [[cremation]] with traditional inhumation (011-001)(011-002).

### Aquatic Burial
Timberlake, an early observer of the Cherokee of [[tennessee|Tennessee]], reported that they "seldom bury the dead, but threw them into the river." Yarrow treated this claim with caution, noting that after careful search for well-authenticated instances of aquatic burial among North American tribes, only two convincing examples were found — the Cherokee practice may have been more limited or ritual-specific than Timberlake's broad statement suggested (017-003)(017-004).

### Burial in Boxes
Like the [[Creeks]] and [[Choctaws]], the Cherokee also practiced burial in boxes placed on the surface or in shallow graves. These boxes, constructed of wood or branches, served as coffins to contain the wrapped body along with grave goods. This method was part of a broader Southeastern pattern of above-ground or semi-subterranean box burial (013-007)(013-008).

## Historical Sources

Key sources on Cherokee mortuary customs include [[foster-jw|J. W. Foster]]'s *Pre-Historic Races* (1873), Timberlake's early accounts of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] Cherokee practices, and archaeological findings from the [[tennessee|Little Tennessee River]] valley after the 1819 treaty cessions. The variety of burial modes — partial [[cremation]], aquatic disposal, and box burial — may reflect differences between Cherokee bands or changes in practice over time (011-001)(017-003).
