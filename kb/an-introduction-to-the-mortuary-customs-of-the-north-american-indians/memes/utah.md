# Utah Territory

## Overview

The Territory of Utah, encompassing the Great Basin region between the [[rocky-mountains|Rocky Mountains]] and the Sierra Nevada, was home to a diverse array of Native American peoples at the time of Yarrow's survey. The [[ute|Ute]], Paiute, Shoshone, and [[gosh-ute|Goshute]] bands inhabiting this arid landscape developed mortuary customs adapted to their environment, including distinctive forms of cave burial, rock cemetery construction, and [[cremation]]. Yarrow's personal travels in the territory, combined with reports from military and civilian correspondents, provided first-hand documentation of these practices at a moment of rapid cultural change.

## Mortuary Customs

### Cave Burial

The most distinctive mortuary practice documented in Utah was cave burial — the placement of the dead within natural rock shelters and crevices throughout the region's canyon systems. Yarrow's index records "Utah cave burial" as a specific category of mortuary practice (024-400). These burials, often accompanied by grave goods including baskets, tools, and personal ornaments, took advantage of the arid climate, which naturally preserved remains through [[mummification|desiccation]].

The writer's personal observation in 1872 provides direct evidence:

> While in the Territory of Utah, in 1872, the writer discovered a cave burial site containing the remains of several individuals, the bodies desiccated by the dry climate and accompanied by woven baskets and stone implements (005-003).

### Rock Cemeteries

Distinct from cave burials were the "rock cemeteries" of central Utah — accumulations of burials placed within natural rock formations rather than excavated caves. Yarrow notes "these rock cemeteries in middle Utah, which had been used for a period of many years," suggesting continuous use across generations (009-002).

### Cremation in Southern Utah

The tribes of southern Utah practiced [[cremation]], a custom that connected them culturally to neighboring groups in present-day Nevada and [[california|California]]. Yarrow records that "the Indians of southern Utah burned their dead" in a practice witnessed by a correspondent identified as Dr. [[foreman-e|E.]], who provided details of the ceremony (010-028). The Utah [[cremation]] custom was indexed under "of Utah, [[cremation]]," indicating its recognition as a regionally specific variant (024-215).

### Cairn Burial

The construction of stone cairns over burial sites was documented across Utah. Yarrow's index entry "cairns, Utah" records this practice, which used locally abundant stone to mark and protect grave sites in the rocky terrain (024-071). These cairns served both practical and ritual functions, preventing disturbance by animals while providing visible memorials that could be revisited and maintained by the living.

### Great Salt Lake Valley Burials

The region surrounding the Great Salt Lake received special attention in Yarrow's sources. An account published in 1852 "relates to" burial customs observed in the "valley of the Great Salt Lake of Utah," providing some of the earliest Euro-American documentation of indigenous mortuary practices in that area (012-001). A later account from 1859 supplemented these observations with additional detail (017-002).

## Archaeological Significance

Utah's arid climate created exceptional conditions for the preservation of burial sites. The natural mummification produced by the dry environment allowed Yarrow's correspondents to observe not only skeletal remains but also soft tissue, hair, and perishable grave goods that would have decayed in more humid regions. This preservation made Utah burial sites particularly valuable for the comparative study of mortuary customs across North America.
