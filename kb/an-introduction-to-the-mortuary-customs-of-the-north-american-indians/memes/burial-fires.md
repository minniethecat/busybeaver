## Overview

The custom of building fires at or near graves and burial sites is documented among numerous Native American tribes. These fires served various ceremonial purposes: some were kept burning for a specified number of nights after death, others were kindled during mourning gatherings, and some were lit near scaffolds as a protective or ritual act.

## Spiritual Purpose

It is extremely difficult to determine why the custom of building fires on or near graves originated, some authors stating that the soul thereby underwent a certain process of purification, others that demons were driven away by them, and again that they were to afford light to the wandering soul setting out for the spirit land (021-027). The Algonkins believed that the fire lighted nightly on the grave was to light the spirit on its journey. Both Algonkins and Mexicans maintained the fire for four nights consecutively. The former related the tradition that one of their ancestors returned from the spirit land and informed their nation that the journey thither consumed just four days, and that collecting fuel every night added much to the toil of the spirit already fatigued by the journey (021-027).

The belief in a fiery purification of the soul or the warding off of spirits by fire appears across widely separated regions, suggesting either a cultural diffusion of the concept or an independent development from similar anxieties about the fate of the soul after death (021-027).

## Tribal Distribution

Among the Klamath and Trinity Indians, a fire was kept up at the grave to preserve the deceased from the devil, or O-mah-u, during the three days of funeral ceremonies when the soul was believed to be in peril (001-020). Should the fire and howling of friends prove unsuccessful in driving away the demon, the soul would be carried down the river, subject to redemption by Peh-ho-wan on payment of a big knife. After the expiration of three days, all was well with them (001-020). At the plateau of Independence Rock, traces of a fire kindled for the dead were found near the grave (001-031). The Dakota (Sioux) were observed kindling a small fire near the head of the scaffold, while mourners chanted and wailed nearby (014-020).

Among the Choctaw, when relatives assembled near the stage where bones were deposited, a fire was made and ceremonial proceedings began (015-003). At the Skokomish Agency, a fire was observed burning near the coffin box where a young woman was laid, with her mother singing the mourning song nearby (016-002). During mourning feasts, the kettle was taken from the fire and placed in the center of the gathering space (019-011).

## Duration and Ritual Timing

The number of nights for which a fire was maintained varied by tribe. The Algonkins and Mexicans maintained the fire for four nights consecutively, a period associated with the four-day journey of the soul to the spirit land (021-027). Yarrow notes that after death, certain tribes kept a fire burning for a designated number of nights at the burial place, and that the number of nights was sometimes regulated according to the character of the deceased — a righteous soul traversed the pole bridging the chasm of the debatable land quicker than a wicked one (021-028). The Hidatsa believed that a shade lingered four nights around the camp or village before departing to the village of the dead (021-030). The smell of burning leather was considered offensive to spirits, though why this should be the case Yarrow deemed fruitless to speculate upon (021-031).
