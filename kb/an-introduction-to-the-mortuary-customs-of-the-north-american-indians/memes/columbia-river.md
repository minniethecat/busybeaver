## Overview

The Columbia River, the largest waterway in the Pacific Northwest, formed a major cultural and mortuary boundary for the tribes documented in Yarrow's 1880 survey. The river's lower reaches were the site of distinctive burial houses and canoe-deposit practices.

## Burial Sites & Practices

Upon the Columbia River, the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] people maintained specialized burial structures. Yarrow records that "the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] had in particular" burial houses where the dead were placed in canoes elevated above the ground on posts (016-005). These burial houses, documented as "Columbia River burial houses," were constructed of wooden planks and served as family or clan charnel structures, containing multiple burials accumulated over generations (024-147).

Ross Cox, in his *Adventures on the Columbia River* (1831), provided early eyewitness accounts of mortuary practices along the river, describing the elaborate ceremonies and material culture associated with [[chinook|Chinookan]] burial rites (010-007). The confluence of canoe burial and scaffold burial traditions along the Columbia created a distinctive regional mortuary complex that combined elements of coastal and interior Plateau practices.

## Associated Tribes

- [[tsinuk]] — [[chinook|Chinook]] people of the lower Columbia, primary practitioners of burial houses
- [[chinook]] — broader [[chinook|Chinookan]] group at the river's mouth
