# Wichita

## Overview

The Wichita people, who called themselves *Kit-ki-la-tats* or "those of the tattooed eyelids," inhabited the Southern Plains in the region of present-day Oklahoma and [[kansas|Kansas]]. A semi-sedentary people who combined bison hunting with maize agriculture, the Wichita developed mortuary customs that blended Plains burial patterns with distinctive local practices. Yarrow (1880) preserved the remarkably detailed account of Dr. Fordyce Grinnell, physician to the Wichita Agency in Indian Territory, whose first-hand observations constitute one of the most complete descriptions of a single tribe's burial ceremony in the entire monograph.

## Mortuary Customs

### The Burial Procession

Upon a death in the Wichita community, the town-crier would immediately go through the village announcing the fact. Preparations for burial were undertaken without delay. If the grave site was located some distance from the village, the body — first wrapped in blankets — was carried on the back of a pony, laid prone across the saddle with one person walking on either side to support it (001-009).

### Grave Construction

The Wichita grave was dug three to four feet deep, of sufficient length to accommodate the extended body. The grave was prepared with care: blankets and buffalo robes were first laid in the bottom as a bedding. The body, removed from the horse and unwrapped from its travel coverings, was then dressed in its finest apparel and adorned with ornaments before being placed upon this couch of robes. The orientation was prescribed: the head lay to the west and the feet to the east (001-009).

### Grave Goods

Burial accompaniments were differentiated by gender. With a man were deposited his bows and arrows, or gun; with a woman, her cooking utensils and the other implements of her daily toil. All valuables belonging to the deceased were placed in the grave alongside the body (001-009). This gendered provisioning of the dead reflects the Wichita conception of the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] as a continuation of earthly roles and occupations, a belief shared widely across the Plains and documented throughout Yarrow's survey.

### Protective Covering and Grave Enclosure

After the body and grave goods were placed, sticks were laid six to eight inches deep across the grave opening, and grass was spread over these, forming a barrier so that the earth fill would not come into direct contact with the body or its trappings. Once the grave was filled, a pen of poles was constructed around it, or stakes were driven so that they crossed each other from either side midway over the grave — "thus forming a complete protection from the invasion of wild animals" (001-009).

A distinctive final touch completed the burial: the grass and other debris was carefully scraped away from around the grave for several feet, leaving the ground smooth and clean — a practice that suggests both aesthetic concern and the practical need to detect any disturbance by scavengers.

### Mourning and Horse Sacrifice

Wichita [[mourning]] customs paralleled those of neighboring Plains tribes. Mourners cut off their hair and fasted. Most strikingly, horses were killed at the grave — a practice of considerable economic cost that signaled the importance of the deceased and the depth of the loss (001-009). It was uncommon for relatives themselves to accompany the remains to the grave; they more often employed others, usually women, to perform the actual burial.

### The Caddo Parallel

Yarrow notes that the [[caddo|Caddoes]], or *[[caddo|Ascena]]* ([[caddo|Timber Indians]]), followed "nearly the same mode of burial as the Wichitas" (001-010). The [[caddo|Caddo]] shared the Wichita language family ([[caddo|Caddo]]an) and many cultural patterns, but with one noteworthy divergence: a [[caddo|Caddo]] killed in battle was never buried, but left to be devoured by beasts or birds of prey, and "the condition of such individuals in the other world is considered to be far better than that of persons dying a natural death" (001-010).

## Historical Sources

The principal source for Wichita burial customs is Dr. Fordyce Grinnell, physician to the Wichita Agency, Indian Territory, who provided Yarrow with a detailed first-hand account. The Wichita are further referenced in Yarrow's index under "Wichitas, inhumation of" (024-405).
