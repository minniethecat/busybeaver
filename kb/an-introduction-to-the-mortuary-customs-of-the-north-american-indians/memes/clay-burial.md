## Overview

Clay burial refers to a set of related mortuary practices in which clay played a central structural or ritual role in the [[inhumation|interment]] of the dead. Among the aboriginal peoples of North America, clay was employed in several distinct modes: as a molded encasement around the corpse, as a floor beneath which children were interred, as the material for burial urns, and as a sealing layer above [[cremation]] furnaces. These practices were documented across a wide geographic range, from the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]] [[mound-builders]] to the [[cherokee|Cherokee]] of [[tennessee|Tennessee]] and the ancient inhabitants of [[carolina|North Carolina]] (615-1).

## Tribal Distribution

The use of clay in burial contexts was most prominently associated with the mound-building cultures of the [[ohio|Ohio]] and [[tennessee|Tennessee]] River Valleys. At a walled town site in [[tennessee|Tennessee]], Professor [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] of the Peabody Museum discovered that nearly every house site contained from one to four graves of children buried beneath hard clay floors (292-1). The [[cherokee|Cherokee]] territory in the [[tennessee|Little Tennessee River]] Valley yielded baked-clay [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]] that preserved the impressions of human bodies (604-1). In [[carolina|North Carolina]], an unidentified ancient race practiced a distinctive method of covering bodies with a one-inch coating of plastic clay before [[cremation]] (615-1). Clay burial urns were also used by the [[creek|Muscogee]]s ([[creek|Creek]]) in [[georgia|Georgia]] and by tribes of the [[california|California]] coast (509-1).

## Variations

Three principal variant forms of clay burial can be distinguished in the ethnographic record:

**Clay Sepulcher Moulds ([[tennessee|Tennessee]]/[[cherokee|Cherokee]]).** In the [[tennessee|Little Tennessee River]] Valley, bodies were placed on the ground and covered with a thick layer of clay. A wood fire was then built on top, consuming the body and baking the clay into a hard shell that retained a detailed impression of the corpse. Mr. McDowell, who farmed the land after [[cherokee|Cherokee]] removal in 1819, uncovered several of these burnt-clay slabs, one of which preserved "the mould of a naked human figure" (604-2). A later excavation in 1872 found the body placed east to west, head toward the east, though the clay was only partially baked and the impression had largely crumbled away (610-1).

**Clay-Coated [[cremation|Cremation]] ([[carolina|North Carolina]]).** [[wilcox|Mr. Wilcox]] reported an unusual method in which bodies were placed face-up and covered with a coating of plastic clay approximately one inch thick. Wood was then piled on top and ignited, consuming the body while simultaneously baking the clay, which retained the body's impression. The baked-clay shell was subsequently covered with a light layer of earth (615-2).

**Clay-Floor Child Burial ([[tennessee|Tennessee]] Mound-Builders).** Within a walled town enclosure on the Lindsley estate, Professor [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] traced approximately seventy house sites. Beneath the hard, fire-baked clay floors of these dwellings he repeatedly found the graves of young children — a pattern so consistent that he concluded it represented a regular custom. These children were treated with evident affection: their small graves contained the finest pottery, shell beads, large pearls, and objects interpreted as playthings (292-2).

**Clay [[cremation|Cremation]] Furnace (Eagle Point, Iowa).** At a mound near Eagle Point on the [[mississippi|Mississippi River]], excavators found a layer of burnt, indurated clay approximately thirty inches thick, resting above a bed of charred human remains six to eighteen inches deep. The construction appeared to have been a deliberate furnace: bodies were placed at the bottom of an excavated pit, fuel was arranged among and above them, and a clay covering was laid over timber supports. The combustion beneath the clay seal was complete, reducing bones to "pulverized bone-black" (596-1).

## Historical Accounts

The most detailed accounts of clay burial come from the post-removal period in [[tennessee|Tennessee]] and [[carolina|North Carolina]]. Mr. McDowell's observations, recorded in 1872 and communicated through Colonel Jenkes, describe three burnt-clay [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]] discovered in his first farming season of 1821, followed by a fifty-one-year gap before further specimens were turned up by the plow (610-1). Professor Swallow documented a human skull encased in an earthen jar from a mound at New Madrid, [[missouri|Missouri]] — the jar's lip being too narrow for the skull to have been inserted after firing, indicating the clay must have been molded directly onto the head (501-1). Dr. Blanding reported ranges of burial vases, stacked one above the other and filled with human remains, from mounds on the Wateree River near Camden, [[carolina|South Carolina]] (501-2).

## Related Practices

Clay burial intersects with several other aboriginal mortuary customs documented in Yarrow's survey. When clay was used as a sealing layer over [[cremation]] fires, it functioned as part of the [[cremation]] process. The use of clay vessels as burial containers connects this practice to [[urn-burial]]. The [[cherokee|Cherokee]] baked-clay [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]], involving fire built atop the clay-covered body, represent a distinctive fusion of [[inhumation]] and [[cremation]] elements. The child burials beneath house floors relate to domestic [[inhumation|interment]] practices, while the clay impressions preserved in the moulds provide some of the only physical evidence of body position among the [[mound-builders]].
