# Aleut

## Overview

The Aleuts, or Unangan, inhabit the Aleutian archipelago stretching westward from the [[alaska|Alaska]] Peninsula. Their mortuary practices, documented in Yarrow (1880) through the accounts of the [[billings-expedition-1802|Billings Expedition]], Captain Henning of the [[alaska|Alaska]] Commercial Company, and archaeological investigations at Amaknak Cave, encompass a remarkable range of techniques: cave burial in whalebone enclosures, [[embalming]], and mummification — the latter producing well-preserved remains that survived for over a century in the volcanic caves of the islands.

## Mortuary Customs

### Cave Burial in Whalebone Sarcophagi

The earliest Aleut remains documented by Yarrow were discovered at Amaknak Cave on the Aleutian chain. Here, excavators found a burial enclosure constructed from "the very much decayed supra-maxillary bones of some large cetacean," arranged to form a rectangular sarcophagus approximately four feet long, two feet wide, and eighteen inches deep, with a stone-slab floor. Three such enclosures were found in close proximity, each containing a skeleton in advanced decay. Yarrow noted that the burial posture — the body tightly bound to fit the narrow container — was "precisely similar to that adopted by most of the continental [[innuit|Innuit]], but equally different from the modern Aleut fashion" (005-012).

### Embalming

Martin Sauer, secretary to the [[billings-expedition-1802|Billings Expedition]] of 1802, recorded the practice of [[embalming]] among the Aleutian Islanders: "They embalm the bodies of the men with dried moss and grass; bury them in their best attire, in a sitting posture, in a strong box, with their darts and instruments; and decorate the tomb with various coloured mats, embroidery, and paintings." Sauer observed a marked gender distinction in these rites — "With women, indeed, they use less ceremony." Mothers would keep the embalmed bodies of deceased children in their huts for months, wiping them dry, and only burying them "when it begins to smell, or when they get reconciled to parting with it" (006-040).

### The Kagamale Cave Mummies

The most extraordinary Aleut mortuary discovery was made by Captain Henning, an agent of the [[alaska|Alaska]] Commercial Company, who learned through Aleut oral tradition of Kagamale — an island north of Ounalaska said to be the resting place of the great chief Karkhayahouchak. After three days of attempting to land through difficult seas, Henning entered a cave permeated with "hot sulphurous vapors" and found eleven mummy bundles representing the chief, his family, and attendants (006-043).

The chief's body was enclosed in a large basket-like structure four feet in height, wrapped in "finely-wrought sea-grass matting, exquisitely close in texture, and skins," with a broad wooden hoop at the base and reed body armor adjoining the center. Over the entire package were stretched fish-nets made of sea lion sinew and bird-nets — the sea-otter skin covering serving as "always a mark of distinction in the [[inhumation|interment]]s of the Aleuts" (006-045).

The other bundles included the bodies of an infant and a very young child, each wrapped in sea-otter skins, and a rare double burial of a man and woman enclosed together in a single matting with sea-lion skins. The bodies had been prepared by severing muscles at the hip and knee joints, allowing the limbs to be folded down upon the trunk — a compact flexed position characteristic of Aleut mummification (006-045).

## Historical Sources

The Aleut burials are documented through three principal sources in Yarrow: the archaeological findings at Amaknak Cave, Sauer's 1802 account from the [[billings-expedition-1802|Billings Expedition]], and Captain Henning's 1870s discovery of the Kagamale [[mummification|mummies]]. The volcanic cave environment of the Aleutian Islands, with its hot vapors and dry conditions, contributed significantly to the preservation of the mummified remains.
