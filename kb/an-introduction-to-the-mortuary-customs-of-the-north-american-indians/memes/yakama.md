# Yakama

## Overview

The Yakama (also spelled Yakima) are a Sahaptin-speaking people of the Columbia Plateau whose traditional territory lay east of the Cascade Mountains in present-day south-central Washington State. Unlike the coastal and riverine peoples of the Northwest who practiced canoe burial, the Yakama were an equestrian people who used canoes only for ferriage and transportation. [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], who contributed extensive observations on the burial practices of [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington Territory tribes to Yarrow's 1880 monograph, documented the Yakama practice of placing graves in elevated and conspicuous locations — a custom reflecting both the practical demands of the arid plateau environment and the spiritual orientation of the dead toward the west (016-005).

## Mortuary Customs

The Yakama mode of [[inhumation|interment]] was adapted to the basaltic topography of the Columbia Plateau. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] observed that the graves were placed in conspicuous points of the basaltic walls lining the lower valleys, each designated by a clump of poles planted over them, from which fluttered various articles of dress belonging to the deceased. These grave-markers served both as monuments to the dead and as deterrents against disturbance by the prairie wolf — the ever-present scavenger of the arid interior (016-005).

The placement of the body followed a universal observance among the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] tribes: the head was always oriented toward the west, the direction of the *me-mel-us-illa-hee*, or country of the dead. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] recorded that the Indians believed that if the deceased were placed facing any other direction, they would become confused on their journey to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. This westward orientation bridges the Yakama practice with that of the fishing tribes along the Columbia, despite the marked differences in their respective modes of burial (016-005).

Among the most notable of Yakama funerary customs was the [[sacrifice]] of horses over the graves of the deceased. As an equestrian people of the prairie, the Yakama placed great value on their horses, and the killing of horses at the grave site constituted a significant offering. However, by the time of [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]]'s observations in the mid-nineteenth century, this practice was already falling into disuse — a direct consequence, [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] noted, of the teachings of the white settlers and missionaries who had increasingly entered the region (016-005).

## Regional Context

The Yakama burial practice of stone-covered inhumation contrasts sharply with the canoe burial that predominated among the coastal and lower [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] tribes. Among the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] and related peoples of the lower Columbia, the dead were deposited in canoes placed on prominent bluffs such as Mount Coffin and Coffin Rock, or in wooden vaults similar to sentry-boxes. The Yakama, living east of the Cascades where timber suitable for canoe construction was less abundant and where the prairie wolf posed a greater threat to exposed remains, developed an approach that used the natural basalt formations of the region as integral elements of the burial site (016-005).

## Historical Sources

The principal source for Yakama mortuary customs is [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], whose account appears in the *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1877, Vol. I, p. 200) and was reproduced in Yarrow's 1880 monograph. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]]'s observations were made during his extensive travels through [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington Territory, and his account is notable for distinguishing between the burial practices of equestrian interior tribes and those of the canoe-using coastal peoples.
