# Henry Gillman

## Overview

Henry Gillman (1833–1915) was an American archaeologist and field excavator who conducted mound explorations in [[florida|Florida]] and Michigan during the 1870s. His most notable contribution to Yarrow's monograph was the excavation of a burial mound at Cade's Pond, near [[florida|Santa Fe Lake]], [[florida|Florida]], in which he discovered two instances of a previously undocumented mortuary practice: crania filled with the incinerated remains of the deceased, used as depositories for their own ashes. Gillman presented his findings before the American Association for the Advancement of Science at its Saint Louis meeting in August 1878, and the account was published in the *American Naturalist* in November of that year (010-025)(010-027).

## Contributions

Gillman's excavation at Cade's Pond uncovered a burial mound containing a large number of human [[inhumation|interment]]s along with numerous pottery vessels, many painted in red, yellow, and brown and ornamented with indented patterns that Gillman judged to display "not a little skill in the ceramic art." The two skulls he found were the mound's most remarkable feature: each rested on its apex, base uppermost, at depths of two to two-and-a-half feet. The first skull was filled with fragments of half-incinerated human bones mingled with dark-colored dust and infiltrated sand; immediately beneath it lay the greater part of a human tibia exhibiting platycnemism to a degree of latitudinal index .512. The second skull, discovered on the opposite side of the mound, was similarly filled with a black mass — the residuum of burnt human bones — and was associated with a flattened tibia showing a longitudinal index of .527 (010-025).

Critically, Gillman noted that both crania were free from all action of fire, indicating that the [[cremation]] had occurred elsewhere and the skulls had been filled with the ashes only after the [[cremation|burning]] was complete. This finding — which Yarrow characterized as "certainly remarkable, if not unique" — represented a form of partial [[cremation]] distinct from the full-body incineration or scaffold-[[cremation|burning]] documented elsewhere. Gillman also recorded the cranial morphology of the mound's population, observing small orthocephalic crania with narrow retreating frontals, prominent parietal protuberances, and well-defined supraciliary ridges — features he recognized as consistent with crania he had previously excavated from Michigan mounds (010-025)(010-027).

## Accounts Provided

Gillman's published account (American Naturalist, November 1878, p. 753) and his presentation to the American Association for the Advancement of Science (August 1878) document the Cade's Pond mound excavation, the first published description of skull-as-ash-receptacle [[cremation]], and the ceramic typology of painted [[florida|Florida]] burial pottery. His dual attention to mortuary ritual and osteometric detail makes his account valuable to both the cultural and biological dimensions of Yarrow's study.
