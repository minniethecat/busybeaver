# Apache

## Overview

The Apache peoples of the American Southwest comprised numerous autonomous bands distributed across present-day [[arizona|Arizona]], [[new-mexico|New Mexico]], and adjacent territories. Yarrow (1880) documented the mortuary customs of one particular division — the [[coyotero-apache|Coyotero Apaches]] — through the observations of [[hoffman-wj|Dr. W. J. Hoffman]], a U.S. Army surgeon who recorded their notably utilitarian approach to corpse disposal. The Coyotero practice of wedging the deceased into natural rock cavities or stump hollows represents one of the most expedient forms of inhumation recorded in the entire Yarrow survey.

## Mortuary Customs

### Coyotero Inhumation

The [[coyotero-apache|Coyotero Apaches]], according to Dr. [[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]], appeared in their disposal of the dead to be actuated primarily by a desire to spare themselves needless trouble. When a member of the tribe died, the corpse was partially wrapped and deposited into the cavity left by the removal of a small rock or the stump of a tree. After the body had been crammed into the smallest possible space, the rock or stump was rolled back into its former position and a number of stones were placed around the base — a precaution directed not at ritual purposes but at the practical necessity of keeping out coyotes, the ever-present scavengers of the desert Southwest (001-026)(001-027).

### Mourning Observances

The nearest of kin were expected to mourn for the period of one month. During this time they gave utterance at intervals to what Dr. [[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]] described as the most dismal [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s, which he judged to be apparently sincere in character. A notable aspect of Coyotero [[mourning]] was its intermittent nature: during the daylight hours the obligation was frequently neglected or forgotten, but when the mourner was reminded of his duty, he renewed his howling with what [[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]] characterized as evident interest. This custom of [[mourning]] for a period of thirty days corresponded to the practice formerly observed by the [[natchez|Natchez]] of the lower [[mississippi|Mississippi]] Valley — a distant cultural parallel that Yarrow found noteworthy (001-027).

## Comparison with Other Southwestern Practices

The Coyotero mode of [[inhumation|interment]] contrasts instructively with other Southwestern burial customs documented by Yarrow. While the [[pima|Pima]] and Maricopa practiced [[cremation]], and the [[navajo|Navajo]] favored lodge burial and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]], the Apache bands of the eastern [[arizona|Arizona]] highlands adopted what was essentially an opportunistic use of the existing topography — rock cavities and stump hollows serving as ready-made graves requiring minimal excavation. This expediency may reflect the highly mobile subsistence pattern of the Apache, who moved frequently across rugged terrain in small bands, in contrast to the more sedentary Puebloan and riverine agricultural peoples of the region.

## Historical Sources

The principal source on Apache mortuary customs is [[hoffman-wj|Dr. W. J. Hoffman]], whose observations were published in the *United States Geological Survey of the Territories* for 1876 (p. 473). [[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]]'s account, while brief, is notable for its candid assessment of the Coyotero approach to burial — a rare instance in which an observer explicitly characterized a tribe's mortuary practice as motivated by convenience rather than by religious or cosmological considerations.
