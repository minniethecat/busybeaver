# Achomawi

## Overview

The Achomawi, or Pitt River Indians, inhabited the Pit River drainage of northeastern [[california|California]]. Their mortuary customs, documented in Yarrow (1880) through an eyewitness account furnished by General [[tompkins-charles-h|Charles H. Tompkins]], deputy quartermaster-general of the U.S. Army, represent one of the most unusual combined burial-and-[[cremation]] rites in the ethnographic record: the headless body was interred upright in the ground while the severed head was separately cremated on a funeral pyre.

## Mortuary Customs

### The Combined Rite

General [[tompkins-charles-h|Tompkins]] described the burial of an Achomawi man of distinction. The body was buried "in a standing position, the shoulders nearly even with the ground," in a grave of sufficient depth and circumference to accommodate the upright posture — the head having been cut off. Bows and arrows, bead-work, trappings, and quantities of dried fish, roots, and herbs were placed with the body in the grave (011-010).

After the grave was filled, covering the headless body, bundles of fagots were brought and placed upon the grave by members of the tribe. The severed head was then placed atop this pyre and the pile was set alight, the head being "consumed to ashes." The female relatives — their faces blackened with a preparation resembling tar or paint — dipped their fingers in the ashes of the cremated head and made three marks on their right cheeks. This constituted the [[mourning]] garb, worn until the black substance gradually wore off (011-010).

The ceremony was accompanied by an elaborate performance: "the old women of the tribe sat on the ground, forming a large circle, inside of which another circle of young girls were formed standing and swaying their bodies to and fro and singing a mournful ditty." Female blood relatives additionally cropped their hair short as a sign of grief (011-010).

### Gender Distinction in Burial

[[tompkins-charles-h|Tompkins]] noted a marked difference between male and female burial customs among the Achomawi. While men received the combined burial-[[cremation]] rite described above, the bodies of women "were wrapped or bundled up in skins and laid away in caves, with their valuables, and in some cases food being placed with them in their mouths. Occasionally money is left to pay for food in the spirit land" (011-010).

## Historical Sources

The sole source for Achomawi mortuary customs in Yarrow is the eyewitness account of General [[tompkins-charles-h|Charles H. Tompkins]], U.S. Army, who personally witnessed the burial described. [[tompkins-charles-h|Tompkins]]' account is notable for being, in Yarrow's assessment, "the only well-authenticated case on record" of this particular combined rite. The detail regarding the two concentric circles of women — elders seated, young girls standing and swaying — provides a rare glimpse of the performative and gendered dimensions of [[california|California]] Indian mortuary ritual (011-010).
