## Overview

Oregon, in the Pacific Northwest, appears in Yarrow's 1880 monograph primarily through accounts of canoe burial practiced by the coastal and riverine tribes of the region. Together with neighboring Washington Territory, Oregon formed a distinctive mortuary province characterized by watercraft-based [[inhumation|interment]] and [[cremation]].

## Burial Sites & Practices

Canoe burial was the most characteristic mortuary practice of the Oregon region. Yarrow documents that "the Indians of Oregon and Washington Territory" placed their dead in canoes, which were then elevated on scaffolds or placed in trees along riverbanks (016-004, 024-115). This practice was particularly common along the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] and its tributaries, where dugout canoes served both as transportation and as burial vessels.

[[cremation|Cremation]] was also practiced in parts of Oregon. Yarrow specifically notes "Oregon, [[cremation]] in" as a distinct entry in his burial-type index (024-313). The Tolkotins of Oregon performed elaborate mortuary ceremonies: "the ceremonies attending the dead" included ritual feasting, [[mourning|wailing]], and the distribution of the deceased's possessions among relatives (010-007).
