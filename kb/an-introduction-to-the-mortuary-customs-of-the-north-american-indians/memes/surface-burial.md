# Surface Burial

## Overview

Surface burial — the deposition of the dead upon the ground rather than beneath it — was a minor but distinctive mode of sepulture among tribes of the Eastern Woodlands. Unlike common inhumation, the body was left at ground level, enclosed within a log receptacle (008-001).

Yarrow observed that this method "was practiced to only a limited extent" and was probably "employed as a temporary expedient when the survivors were pressed for time" (008-001). It occupied an intermediate position between earth burial and the aerial burials of many tribes.

## Tribal Distribution

### Miamis of Indiana

The most detailed account came from Mr. R. S. Robertson of Fort Wayne, Indiana, who reported in 1877 that the [[miami|Miamis]] practiced surface burial in two distinct forms (008-001)(008-002).

The first method used hollow logs as receptacles in heavy forests. Robertson explained: "Sometimes a tree has been split and the two halves hollowed out to receive the body, when it was either closed with withes or confined to the ground with crossed stakes; and sometimes a hollow tree is used by closing the ends" (008-002).

The second method was more architectural: a small pen of logs was laid up like a cabin, "drawing in every course until they meet in a single log at the top" (008-002).

### Seminoles of Florida

The [[seminoles|Seminoles]] buried their dead in hollow trees, placing bodies in an upright position. In some cases, the dead were "crammed into a hollow log lying on the ground" when standing hollow trees were not available (008-001).

### Eastern Tribes

Certain Eastern tribes split a log lengthwise, hollowed each half, enclosed the corpse, and lashed the halves with withes. "In some cases a pen was built over and around it" for added protection from animals (008-001).

## Notable Example: Blackbird of the Omahas

The most celebrated surface burial was that of Blackbird, the great [[omaha|Omaha]] chief, as recorded by [[catlin-george|George Catlin]]. His body was placed astride his white war-horse on a bluff overlooking the [[missouri|Missouri]] River, the horse buried alive beneath him (008-004).

[[catlin-george|Catlin]] described the ceremony: "With great pomp and ceremony, in the presence of the whole nation and several of the fur-traders and the Indian agent, he was placed astride of his horse's back, with his bow in his hand, and his shield and quiver slung, with his pipe and his medicine bag" (008-004).

Turfs were laid around the horse's feet and built up over its body and the chief himself. [[catlin-george|Catlin]] wrote that "all together have smouldered and remained undisturbed to the present day" (008-004).

## Construction and Materials

Surface burial relied on forest materials. Hollow trees provided natural containers needing only end-closures. Split logs, hollowed by scraping or [[cremation|burning]], substituted where hollow trees were absent. Withes — green branches twisted into cordage — bound the halves together.

The log-pen method, used by the [[miami|Miamis]], required felling multiple logs and stacking them in a corbel pattern that narrowed toward the top, forming a durable above-ground tomb.

## Cultural Significance

Surface burial bridged earth inhumation and [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]]. It avoided excavation labor while protecting remains from scavengers. The temporary character of many examples suggests use when frozen ground or lack of tools precluded digging proper graves (008-001).
