# D. G. Brinton

## Overview

Daniel Garrison Brinton (1837–1899) was one of the most influential American anthropologists of the late nineteenth century. A physician by training, Brinton devoted his career to the study of Indigenous American languages, mythology, and cultural practices, publishing widely and serving as professor of American linguistics and archaeology at the University of Pennsylvania. His major work, *The Myths of the New World: A Treatise on the Symbolism and Mythology of the Red Race of America* (1868), contained an important synthetic treatment of mortuary customs — including the widespread practice of periodic secondary [[partial-scaffold-burial|bone-collection]] and communal [[ossuary]] [[inhumation|interment]] — that Yarrow cited as an authoritative framework for interpreting the archaeological evidence of mound burial across eastern North America (015-012).

## Contributions

Brinton's contribution to Yarrow's monograph was primarily synthetic rather than observational. Drawing on a wide range of historical sources, he described a practice that prevailed east of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]]: nearly every nation was accustomed, at stated periods — usually once in eight or ten years — to collect and clean the osseous remains of those who had died in the intervening time, and to inter them in a single common sepulcher lined with choice furs and marked with a mound of wood, stone, or earth. In a passage Yarrow quoted directly, Brinton observed that such was "the origin of those immense tumuli filled with the mortal remains of nations and generations, which the antiquary, with irreverent curiosity, so frequently chances upon in all portions of our territory" — a formulation that linked the archaeological phenomenon of the mound-builder cultures to historically documented Indigenous practices, implicitly arguing for cultural continuity where others had posited a vanished race (015-012).

Brinton further noted that in Central America the same usage obtained, with early writers and existing monuments testifying to the widespread distribution of the custom. He recorded the differential treatment of distinguished chieftains, whose bones — rather than being interred in the common mound — were deposited in temples or council-houses in small chests of canes or splints, and identified these structures as the "[[ossuary|charnel-house]]s" that the chroniclers of De Soto's expedition had so frequently mentioned. Brinton's comparative framework and his insistence on understanding mound burial as a living cultural practice rather than a mysterious archaeological anomaly provided Yarrow with an important theoretical underpinning for his chapter on partial scaffold burial and ossuaries (015-012).

## Accounts Provided

Brinton's *Myths of the New World* (1868, p. 256) provides synthetic coverage of secondary [[partial-scaffold-burial|bone-collection]], communal [[ossuary]] [[inhumation|interment]], and [[ossuary|charnel-house]] practices from the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] Valley to Central America. His work is valued not for firsthand observation but for its comparative reach and its effective refutation of the "lost race" theories that then dominated mound-builder archaeology.
