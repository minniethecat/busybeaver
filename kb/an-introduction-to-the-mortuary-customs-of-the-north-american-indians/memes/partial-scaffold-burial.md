# Partial Scaffold Burial

## Overview

Partial scaffold burial, also termed scaffold-and-ossuary burial, was a two-stage mortuary practice widespread among the tribes east of the [[mississippi|Mississippi River]]. In the first stage, the deceased was placed on an elevated scaffold where the body was left to decompose in the open air. In the second stage — typically months or years later — the cleaned bones were collected, often by specialist undertakers, and deposited in a [[bone-house]], communal [[ossuary]], or burial mound (015-001)(014-022).

Yarrow classified this practice under "burial by partial scaffold burial and ossuaries," distinguishing it from permanent scaffold burial in which the body remained elevated indefinitely. The two-stage process reflected a sophisticated mortuary culture in which the temporary exposure of the individual body gave way to permanent, collective deposition of skeletal remains — a transition from individual to communal identity in death.

## The Choctaw Model

The [[choctaw|Choctaws]] of the Southeast provided the most thoroughly documented example of this practice. [[bartram-william|Bartram]] observed that "as soon as a person is dead, they erect a scaffold 18 or 20 feet high in a grove adjacent to the town, where they lay the corps, lightly covered with a mantle" (014-020). The body remained on the scaffold, visited and protected by relatives, until decomposition had advanced sufficiently for the flesh to be easily separated from the bones.

At the appointed time, specialist undertakers — "a certain set of venerable old Gentlemen who wear very long nails as a distinguishing badge on the thumb, fore and middle finger of each hand" — performed the bone-cleaning ritual. [[roman|Roman]] recorded that the operator "with his nails tears the remaining flesh off the bones, and throws it with the entrails into the fire, where it is consumed; then he scrapes the bones and burns the scrapings likewise" (014-021). The head was painted red with vermilion and placed with the cleaned bones in a "neatly made chest," which for a chief was also painted red.

These chests were deposited in the [[bone-house]] — "a building erected for that purpose in every town" — where they remained until the structure was full. At that point a general funeral was convened: the coffins were carried in procession and arranged "forming a pyramid," then covered with earth to create a conical mound. The ceremony concluded with a communal feast (014-020).

## Geographic Distribution

East of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]], [[brinton-dg|Brinton]] reported that "nearly every nation was accustomed at stated periods — usually once in eight or ten years — to collect and clean the osseous remains of those of its number who had died in the intervening time, and inter them in one common sepulcher, lined with choice furs, and marked with a mound of wood, stone, or earth" (014-022). Yarrow identified this custom as the origin of the numerous tumuli encountered by antiquaries throughout the eastern United States.

The [[iroquois|Iroquois]] maintained ossuaries, as did the [[natchez|Natchez]] of the lower [[mississippi|Mississippi]] (024-231)(024-302). Among the [[huron|Hurons]], the Jesuit missionary [[brebeuf-p-de|P. de Brebeuf]] witnessed a "feast of the dead" at the village of Ossosane attended by two thousand Indians from five large villages, who deposited the bones of their dead in a "gigantic shroud, composed of forty-eight robes, each robe being made of ten beaver skins." A stone wall was built around the [[ossuary]] for protection, and before covering the bones with earth, women threw grains of Indian corn upon the relics. According to [[huron|Huron]] belief, the souls of the dead remained near the bodies until the feast; afterward they became free to depart for the land of spirits in the setting sun (015-003).

## Cultural Significance

The two-stage process served multiple functions. The scaffold stage allowed natural decomposition in the open air, an efficient method for reducing the body to clean bones without the labor of excavation. The communal deposition stage — the "feast of the dead" — served as a powerful social ritual that reinforced tribal and lineage solidarity across generations. The gathering of remains from dispersed individual scaffolds into a single [[ossuary]] or mound symbolically transformed individual deaths into a collective ancestral presence.

The professionalization of bone-cleaning — the long-nailed "undertakers" who traveled between [[choctaw|Choctaw]] towns — indicates that this was not an improvised folk custom but an institutionalized practice with recognized specialists, prescribed procedures, and ritualized timing. The scale of the final deposition ceremonies, involving thousands of participants and hundreds of beaver-skin robes, testifies to the central importance of these rites in the ceremonial life of the Eastern tribes.
