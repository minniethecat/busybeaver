# J. D. Whitney

## Overview

Josiah Dwight Whitney (1819–1896) was professor of geology at Harvard University and served as [[california|California]] State Geologist from 1860 to 1874. Although his primary scientific reputation rested on his geological surveys of [[california|California]] — including his controversial advocacy of the Calaveras Skull as evidence of Pliocene-age humans in North America — his incidental archaeological observations proved valuable to Yarrow's mortuary study. Whitney's description of a burial cave in Calaveras County, published in the Smithsonian Institution's Annual Report for 1867, provided one of the more detailed accounts of primary cave [[inhumation|interment]] from the [[california|California]] region, documenting the disposition of human crania, associated artifacts, and the stratigraphic context of the deposit (005-009).

## Contributions

Whitney described a cave near the Stanislaus River in Calaveras County, [[california|California]], located on an unnamed creek about two miles from Abbey's Ferry, that had been used as a burial place by the region's Indigenous inhabitants. The cave was approximately 27 feet deep at the mouth, extending 40 to 50 feet at the end, with a diameter of roughly 30 feet. Whitney recorded that crania obtained from this cave — subsequently deposited in the Smithsonian collection — had been taken some ten years before his own visit by a Dr. [[jones|Jones]], and that the condition of the cave had greatly changed in the interval: owing to an alteration in the road, mining operations, or some other cause he could not ascertain, some 20 feet of surface earth had accumulated on the formerly clean stalagmitic floor, completely concealing the bottom. This observation, though incidental, provides a rare contemporaneous record of site-formation processes affecting a [[california|California]] burial cave — information of considerable value for understanding the taphonomy of such deposits (005-009).

Whitney further recorded the testimony of local informants who stated that the skulls had originally been found lying on the surface of the cave floor rather than buried in the stalagmite, and that Dr. [[jones|Jones]] had found remains of bows, arrows, and charcoal with the crania — artifacts that were subsequently destroyed when the nearby village of Murphy's was burned. The prevailing opinion among those who knew the cave in its earlier state was that it had served as a primary burial place of the region's Indigenous population, a conclusion consistent with the broader pattern of cave [[inhumation|interment]] Whitney's account helped to document (005-009).

## Accounts Provided

Whitney's published account (Smithsonian Institution Annual Report, 1867, p. 406) covers the Calaveras County burial cave, associated artifacts, and site-formation observations. Though brief, it is distinguished by Whitney's geological training, which gave him an eye for stratigraphic and taphonomic details that less scientifically trained observers typically omitted.
