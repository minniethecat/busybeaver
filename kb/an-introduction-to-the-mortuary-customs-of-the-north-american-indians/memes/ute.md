# Ute

## Overview

The Utes inhabited the Great Basin region of present-day [[utah|Utah]] and Colorado. Yarrow (1880) documented their mortuary customs through both his own field observations and the communications of Dr. [[mcdonald-aj|A. J. McDonald]], physician to the Los Pinos Indian Agency in Colorado. The Ute practice of cave and crevice burial — depositing the dead in natural rock fissures and volcanic vents — reflected the arid, rocky terrain of their homeland and contrasted with the earth and scaffold burials of neighboring Plains tribes.

## Mortuary Customs

### Cave Burial

In 1872, while in the [[utah|Utah Territory]], Yarrow personally discovered a natural cave "not far from the House Range of mountains, the entrance to which resembled the shaft of a mine." The [[gosh-ute|Gosi-Ute]] Indians had deposited their dead within, "surrounded with different articles, until it was quite filled up" (005-003).

A [[gosh-ute|Gosi-Ute]] guide subsequently informed Yarrow of a much larger cave near the Nevada border, in which the guide had himself "assisted in placing dead members of his tribe." The guide described the cave as so vast that "no man knew its full extent," and reported that the narrow entrance had been deliberately sealed with large stones and sage brush roots years earlier to prevent game animals from entering. Yarrow's party spent six hours attempting to clear the blocked entrance before abandoning the effort. When asked about the quantity of remains within, the guide replied "Heaps, heaps," gesturing upward with extended arms (005-003).

### Rock-Crevice Burial

Dr. [[mcdonald-aj|McDonald]] described the standard Ute burial practice as deposition in natural rock clefts. The preparation of the body was exclusively women's work: the deceased was placed on a horse and conducted to the chosen crevice, where the body was "well covered with pieces of rock, to protect it against the ravages of wild animals." [[mcdonald-aj|McDonald]] noted that "it has always been customary among the Utes to select sepulchres of this character," and that "it is not usual to find the remains of more than one Indian deposited in one grave" (005-006).

### Property Destruction

A distinctive Ute custom was the comprehensive destruction of the deceased's property. While women prepared the body and conducted the burial, the men performed an equally important ritual duty: "In conformity with a long-established custom, all the personal property of the deceased is immediately destroyed. His horses and his cattle are shot, and his wigwam, furniture, &c., burned." [[mcdonald-aj|McDonald]] dryly observed that this assignment was "quite in accord with their taste and inclinations." He noted, however, that the Utes mitigated the economic impact of this custom by distributing property among their children at a very tender age, "retaining to themselves only what is necessary to meet every-day requirements" (005-006).

### Mourning

Ute [[mourning]] was comparatively restrained. The widow "goes into [[mourning]]" by smearing her face with a mixture of pitch and charcoal, applied once and allowed to remain until it wore off naturally. [[mcdonald-aj|McDonald]] recorded that this was "the only [[mourning]] observance of which I have any knowledge" (005-006).

## Historical Sources

The Ute mortuary customs in Yarrow benefit from two complementary sources: Yarrow's own field exploration of the [[gosh-ute|Gosi-Ute]] cave in 1872, and Dr. [[mcdonald-aj|A. J. McDonald]]'s systematic description of Ute burial practices from his service at the Los Pinos Agency. Together they provide both the archaeological evidence (the cave and crevice burials themselves) and the ethnographic context (the gendered division of mortuary labor and the [[property-destruction]] custom).
