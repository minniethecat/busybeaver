# William Bartram

## Overview

William Bartram (1739–1823) was an American naturalist, botanist, and explorer whose *Travels Through North and [[carolina|South Carolina]], [[georgia|Georgia]], East and West [[florida|Florida]]* (1791) provided some of the earliest European-American descriptions of Southeastern Indian mortuary customs. His eyewitness account of [[creek|Muscogulges]] burial practices was cited by Yarrow as a primary source for inhumation customs (001-010)(002-003).

## Contributions

Bartram's description of [[creek|Muscogulges]] ([[Creeks]]) burial practices was detailed and systematic: the deceased was interred in a four-foot square pit dug beneath the cabin or sleeping couch, with the body placed in a sitting position. The grave was then covered with earth and the domestic life of the household continued above it. Bartram's account captured a practice markedly different from the scaffold and mound burials of other Southeastern tribes, highlighting the diversity of mortuary customs even within a single region. His work was cited alongside Lawson and [[jones|Jones]] as foundational for understanding [[carolina|Carolina]] and [[georgia|Georgia]] tribal customs (001-010)(002-003).

## Accounts Provided

Bartram traveled extensively through the Southeast in the 1770s, documenting the customs of the [[cherokee|Cherokee]], [[creek|Creek]], [[choctaw|Choctaw]], and other tribes. His observations were valued for their firsthand quality at a time when many tribes still maintained pre-removal traditions. The [[creek|Muscogulges]] burial account is among the most detailed early descriptions of sub-floor inhumation in the Southeast (001-010).
