## Overview

The Aleut and Aleutian Islanders of the North Pacific practiced a distinctive form of burial centered on mummification and cave interment. Unlike the simple inhumation or scaffold burial common among many mainland tribes, Aleut mortuary customs involved an elaborate embalming process using dried moss and grass, followed by placement of the body in a sitting posture within a strong box or wrapped in finely-wrought sea-grass matting (006-040). The practice persisted into the early nineteenth century, with the cave of Kagamale serving as the final resting-place of a great chief and his family (006-043). Through the observations of Martin Sauer, Captain Henning, and the archaeological work of W.H. Dall, a remarkably detailed picture of Aleut mummification practices emerges — one that distinguishes sharply between the general ceremonial embalming tradition and the separate, more austere preservation customs of the whaling class (006-042).

## Mummification Method

Martin Sauer, secretary to Billings' Expedition in 1802, provided one of the earliest European accounts of Aleut embalming practices. According to Sauer, the Aleut "embalm the bodies of the men with dried moss and grass; bury them in their best attire, in a sitting posture, in a strong box, with their darts and instruments; and decorate the tomb with various coloured mats, embroidery, and paintings" (006-040). A mother would keep a dead child "thus embalmed in their hut for some months, constantly wiping it dry," burying it only "when it begins to smell, or when they get reconciled to parting with it" (006-040). This domestic preservation of infant remains suggests a particularly intimate relationship with the dead among the Aleut.

## The Kagamale Cave Discovery

A significant discovery of Aleut mummies was made on Kagamale Island, north of Ounalaska. Captain Henning, an agent of the Alaska Commercial Company, learned from Aleut tradition that Kagamale "was the last resting-place of a great chief, known as Karkhayahouchak" (006-043). The cave smelt strongly of hot sulphurous vapors — a natural environmental condition that may have contributed to the preservation of the remains over more than a century (006-043). With great care the mummies were removed from the cave, and all the little trinkets and ornaments scattered around were also taken away (006-043). The cave yielded eleven packages of bodies. The chief's body was enclosed in an elaborate "large basket-like structure, about four feet in height," wrapped in "finely-wrought sea-grass matting, exquisitely close in texture, and skins," with body armor of reeds and fine sea-otter skin covering (006-045). The bodies had been "doubled up at death by severing some of the muscles at the hip and knee joints and bending the limbs downward horizontally upon the trunk" (006-045). Among the grave goods recovered were "wooden vessels scooped out smoothly," ivory carvings of the sea-lion, a comb, a necklet of birds' claws, and "a cap plaited out of sea-grass and almost water-tight" (006-045).

## Kadiak Whaler Mummies: A Separate Tradition

W.H. Dall, in his *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1877), drew an important distinction between the general Aleut embalming tradition and a separate practice observed among the Kadiak Innuit whaling class. Dall noted that the practice of preserving whalers' bodies "has erroneously been confounded with the one now described" — that is, the ceremonial mummification of honored men and women (006-042). The general embalming tradition "included women as well as men, and all those whom the living desired particularly to honor," whereas the whalers "only preserved the bodies of males, and they were not associated with the paraphernalia of those I have described" (006-042). Dall's investigations revealed that the whalers' bodies "were preserved with stone weapons and actual utensils instead of effigies, and with the meanest apparel, and no carvings of consequence" (006-042). The sea-otter skin, consistently "a mark of distinction in the interments of the Aleuts" (006-045), was notably absent from whaler burials, reinforcing the status-based stratification of Aleut mortuary treatment.

## Archaeological Evidence

Excavations at the Amaknak cave revealed burial practices "totally different from the modern Aleut fashion" (005-012), indicating that Aleut mortuary customs evolved over time. The older burials in the lowermost part of the Amaknak cave showed a different treatment: bodies were deposited in "a rude rectangular inclosure" formed from the "very much decayed supra-maxillary bones of some large cetacean," measuring "somewhat less than 4 feet long, 2 feet wide, and 18 inches deep" (005-012). The bottom was formed of flat pieces of stone, and three such enclosures were found close together, "covered with and filled by an accumulation of fine vegetable and organic mold" (005-012). The skeleton within had "evidently been tied up in the Innuit fashion to get it into its narrow house," with all bones except the skull reduced "to a soft paste, or even entirely gone" (005-012). At Adakh, a similar sarcophagus was discovered under "a dense peaty mass" of sphagnous growth nearly 2 feet thick — attesting, through the "well-known slowness of this kind of growth in these northern regions," to the considerable antiquity of the remains (005-012).

## Related Practices

Aleut mummification shares features with other forms of preservation-based burial, including the [[mummification]] practiced by the ancient Peruvians and the cave burials of the [[aleutian-islands]] region. However, the Aleut method — embalming with moss and grass, wrapping in sea-otter skins, and placement in sitting posture within boxes — was distinctive in the North American context, differing both from the desiccation-based cave mummies of Kentucky and Indiana and from the deliberate evisceration-based mummification of the ancient Egyptians (005-013). The use of grave goods — darts, instruments, and decorative mats — parallels practices seen in [[grave-deposits]] across other North American tribes.
