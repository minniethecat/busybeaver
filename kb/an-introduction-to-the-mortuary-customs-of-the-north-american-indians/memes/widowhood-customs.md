# Widowhood Customs

The death of a husband imposed upon the widow a distinctive set of [[mourning]] obligations among North American Indian tribes — bodily modifications, social restrictions, grave-tending duties, and in extremis, self-[[sacrifice]]. These customs were among the most demanding and protracted of all mortuary observances.

## Bodily Modifications

Widows altered their appearance to signal their status. Among one tribe, "the widow 'goes into [[mourning]]' by smearing her face with a substance composed of pitch and charcoal. The application is made but once, and is allowed to remain on until the expiration of the period of [[mourning]]" [005-007]. The [[dakota|Dakota]] widow cropped her hair "even with the neck, and the top of the head and forehead, and sometimes nearly the whole body, are smeared with a species of white clay" [014-020].

These modifications served dual purposes: they marked the widow's ritual state to the community, and in some beliefs, they rendered her less visible or attractive to the potentially lingering spirit of the deceased.

## Grave Attendance and Tending

The widow's obligation to attend the grave was particularly onerous. Among one group, "the widow wholly neglects her toilet, while she daily goes to the grave during one entire _moon_ from the date when the death occurred" [019-011]. She subsisted on minimal food, with "the best and largest proportion" deposited upon the grave rather than consumed [019-005].

The [[natchez|Natchez]] widow faced an extended period of restriction. The deceased husband's body "is kept nine days laid out in his lodge" before burial, during which the widow remained in constant attendance [010-008]. Subsequently she might be required to maintain a vigil at or near the grave for months.

## The Widow's Burden

After a [[cremation]], the widow's duties continued: "the widow collects the larger bones, which she rolls up in an envelope of birch bark, and which she is obliged for some years afterwards to carry on her back. She is now considered and treated as a slave" [010-010]. The widow was "obliged for some years" to carry the physical remains — a literal and symbolic burden that marked her transitional status between wifehood and eventual remarriage or permanent widowhood.

## Destruction of Property

At the husband's death, property belonging to the couple was often destroyed. The [[ute|Ute]] account notes that for a male burial, weapons and personal effects were deposited; for a female death, "no destruction of property takes place" [005-008]. The differential treatment by gender reflects the economic and social asymmetry of marriage — the husband's death triggered material loss for the widow beyond the personal.

## Suttee and Self-Sacrifice

The most extreme widowhood practice was self-[[sacrifice]]. [[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]] recorded that in parts of Central America, "when a cacique died those of his concubines that loved him enough… as well as certain servants, killed themselves and were interred with him. This they did in order that they might wait upon him in the land of spirits" [016-017]. While less common in North America proper, variations on this practice — including the widow's exposure to fatal hardship — were reported in several regions.
