# Massasauga

## Overview

The Massasauga Indians inhabited the north shore of Lake Ontario during the period of early European contact. An Algonquian-speaking people whose name derives from the Mississauga band of the [[chippewa|Ojibwe]] (Anishinaabe) — and which also names a species of rattlesnake indigenous to the region — the Massasauga are among the more sparsely documented groups in Yarrow (1880). The limited references available nevertheless place them within the broader Great Lakes Algonquian mortuary complex, which was characterized by inhumation burial and shared ritual patterns extending across the Anishinaabe peoples from the Great Lakes to the subarctic.

## Mortuary Customs

### Inhumation

The Massasauga practiced earth burial, as indicated by Yarrow's index entry "Massasaugas, inhumation of" (024-264). [[inhumation|Inhumation]] — [[inhumation|interment]] of the body in the ground — was the predominant burial mode among the Algonquian peoples of the Great Lakes, contrasting with the [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]] (scaffold and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]]) of the Plains and the [[cremation]] practices of [[california|California]] and the Pacific Northwest.

### Geographic and Historical Context

Yarrow tentatively identifies a group practicing scaffold-like exposure as "probably the Massasauga Indians, then inhabiting the north shore of Lake Ontario, but who were rather intruders here, the country being claimed by the Oneidas" (001-032). This identification, while qualified, situates the Massasauga within the complex territorial dynamics of the Great Lakes during the colonial period, where Algonquian and Iroquoian peoples contested for control of the Ontario basin. The characterization of the Massasauga as "intruders" in Oneida-claimed territory reflects the political instability of the region and complicates any simple mapping of mortuary practice to tribal territory.

### Algonquian Mortuary Context

The Massasauga's inhumation customs placed them within a broader Algonquian pattern documented across the Great Lakes. Neighboring and related groups — the [[ojibwe|Ojibwe]] ([[chippewa|Chippewa]]), the Ottawa, and the Potawatomi — shared similar earth burial traditions, often accompanied by the [[inhumation|interment]] of grave goods, the construction of grave houses or spirit lodges, and [[mourning]] observances involving the cutting of hair and ritual fasting.

## Historical Sources

Documentation of Massasauga burial customs in Yarrow is limited. The primary references are the index entry for inhumation (024-264) and the tentative geographical identification in the body of the text (001-032). This sparse record reflects the broader challenge of documenting Great Lakes Algonquian mortuary practice: by the time systematic ethnographic observation began in the mid-nineteenth century, many Algonquian communities had been displaced, consolidated, or transformed by sustained contact with Euro-American society. The Massasauga case thus represents a type of ethnographic trace — enough to confirm the general pattern, but insufficient for the kind of detailed description Yarrow was able to assemble for better-documented groups.
