## Overview

Kentucky, bordering the [[ohio|Ohio]] River to the north, is documented in Yarrow's 1880 monograph for its distinctive cave burials and mummified remains. The state's limestone geology created extensive cave systems that were utilized by aboriginal peoples for [[inhumation|interment]].

## Burial Sites & Practices

Cave burial was the most distinctive mortuary practice in Kentucky. Yarrow notes that "many of the saltpeter and other caves of Kentucky" contained human remains, and "it is still a matter of speculation" whether these [[inhumation|interment]]s were primary burials or secondary deposits (006-016). The cave environment's cool, dry conditions sometimes produced natural mummification — Yarrow records "Kentucky [[mummification|mummies]]" as a distinct phenomenon, with well-preserved remains "discovered in Kentucky" now held in museum collections (024-240, 006-023).

Stone graves similar to those in [[tennessee|Tennessee]] were also present. Yarrow observes that "such graves are more common in Kentucky than [[ohio|Ohio]]," suggesting a northward diffusion of this burial practice from the Southeast (004-023). The cave systems of Kentucky, Indiana, and [[ohio|Ohio]] shared similar burial patterns, though Kentucky's more extensive karst topography yielded the greatest number of cave burial sites (005-013).
