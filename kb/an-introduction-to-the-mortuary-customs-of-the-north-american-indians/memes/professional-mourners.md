# Professional Mourners

Among several North American Indian tribes, the work of [[mourning]] at funerals was not left to spontaneous grief alone. Designated individuals — often older persons, ritual specialists, or those of particular social standing — were charged with leading the [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s and performing the prescribed [[mourning]] behaviors.

## The Choctaw Practice

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] employed a distinctive set of functionaries for the care of the dead. "A certain set of venerable old Gentlemen who wear very long nails as a distinguishing badge on the thumb, fore and middle finger of each hand" were responsible for cleaning the bones of the deceased after scaffold exposure — a specialized mortuary role that combined practical bone-handling with ritual status [015-003]. These "old gentlemen" were not mourners in the narrow sense but represented the class of ritual specialists who managed death ceremonies.

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] funeral procession itself displayed a structured division of roles. Observers recorded "a sett of people who seemed to be the mourners" leading the procession, their [[death-wailing|lamentation]] alternating between "allelujah and [[death-wailing|lamentation]]," as they slowly proceeded to the burial ground where the bones, now cleaned by the undertakers, were deposited in the tribal [[ossuary]] [015-008]. The mourners and the bone-handlers were distinct groups, each with specific ritual responsibilities.

## Designated Wailers

The near-universal practice of death [[mourning|wailing]] often involved persons beyond the immediate family. Among [[virginia|Virginia]] tribes, after burial "the women being painted with their faces coal black do sit twenty-four hours in the houses lamenting and crying out in a shrill voice" [006-010]. While family members participated, the continuous nature of the ritual — spanning full days and nights — suggests that the burden was shared among designated community members. The [[sioux|Sioux]] practice of women "keeping up a dismal wail, frequently even in extreme cold weather, the greater part of the night" similarly implies organized rather than purely spontaneous [[death-wailing|lamentation]] [014-006].

At [[cherokee|Cherokee]] funerals, the ritual questioning of the corpse — "asking many questions of the corpse, such as, why he left them? did not his wife serve him well? was he not contented with his children? had he not plenty of game, corn, &c.?" — was performed by the assembled relations who "come and weep" at the stage where the body lay [015-002]. This structured dialogic [[mourning|wailing]], with its formulaic questions and collective responses, points to a ritual performance that exceeded individual grief.

Yarrow's questionnaire specifically asked: "Is it spoken to after death as if alive? when and where? What is the character of the addresses?" [022-004]. These questions anticipate the existence of ritual speech at funerals that may have been performed by specialists rather than family members.

## Distinction from Family Grief

The sociological distinction between professional mourners and family mourners is significant. Professional mourners performed a ritual function — their [[mourning|wailing]] served protective, communicative, and social purposes beyond emotional expression. [[bruhier-jacques-jean|Bruhier]], cited by Yarrow, argued that "the particular part of the [[mourning]] ceremonies, which consisted in loud cries and [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s, may have had in earlier periods of time a greater significance than that of a mere expression of grief or woe" [014-024]. The ritualized, almost formulaic character of much death [[mourning|wailing]] supports the inference that designated performers — if not full-time professionals — at least fulfilled a specialized role.

## The Lamentation as Performance

The theatrical dimension of professional [[mourning]] is captured in a striking account of a [[sioux|Sioux]] funeral ceremony, where observers witnessed "the contrast exhibited by the terrific [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s and yells of one part of the company while the others were singing and dancing with all their might" [020-002]. This simultaneous performance of grief and celebration — what the observer called a "funeral comedy" — required a coordination that spontaneous family grief alone could not produce. The division of the company into lamenters and dancers suggests distinct ritual roles, each performed according to established custom.
