# Death Wailing

**Death [[mourning|wailing]]** — the loud, ritualized lamentation performed at the time of death and during funeral ceremonies — was a near-universal feature of North American Indian mortuary practice. Yarrow noted that the custom likely served purposes beyond the expression of grief, drawing on the observations of earlier authorities who saw in it a ritual function of protection, communication with the dead, and social cohesion.

## Ritual Form

The form of death [[mourning|wailing]] varied by tribe but consistently involved women as the primary performers. Among the [[sioux|Sioux]], "the women relatives getting on some elevated spot near where the body rests, and keeping up a dismal wail, frequently even in extreme cold weather, the greater part of the night" [014-006]. This practice continued for extended periods after the death.

The [[pima|Pima]] funeral involved women "giving vent to their sorrow by dashing themselves on the ground until covered with blood, and inflicting other tortures" [001-035]. At a [[florida|Florida]] [[cremation]] witnessed by one observer, the funeral pyre of a wealthy chieftain was accompanied by "frightful exhibitions of fanatic frenzy and infatuation" [010-018].

The intensity and duration of [[mourning|wailing]] varied by the status of the deceased. Among the [[dakota|Dakota]], the full [[mourning]] ceremony was described by one observer as "accompanied with lamentations so wild and weird that one might imagine, without any strain of the imagination, that the habitation was bewitched" [014-019]. For less prominent individuals, the [[mourning|wailing]] might last "two or three days" — during which the mourners were observed "smoking, and [[mourning|wailing]] out their lamentations" continuously [014-020].

## Protective Function

Yarrow, citing the French authority [[bruhier-jacques-jean|Bruhier]], suggested that "the particular part of the [[mourning]] ceremonies, which consisted in loud cries and lamentations, may have had in earlier periods of time a greater significance than that of a mere expression of grief or woe" [014-024]. One such function was apotropaic — to drive away malevolent spirits. Among one tribe, during the three days when "the soul of the deceased is in danger from _O-mah-u_ or the devil," the friends of the deceased "howl around it to scare away the demon" while a fire was kept [[cremation|burning]] at the grave [001-020].

## Addressing the Dead

Wailing frequently incorporated direct address to the deceased. The Carib practice involved "[[mourning|wailing]]s being interspersed with comical remarks and questions to the dead as to why he preferred to leave this world, having everything to make life comfortable" [014-025]. The [[cherokee|Cherokee]], at the scaffold where the body lay, practised a similar form of address, with the relations weeping and "asking many questions of the corpse, such as, why he left them? did not his wife serve him well? was he not contented with his children? had he not plenty of game, corn, &c.?" — the whole accompanied by "loud howlings" [015-002]. This suggests that death [[mourning|wailing]] served as a mode of communication with the departed spirit, not merely as an emotional outlet.

Yarrow's questionnaire specifically directed field observers to record: "Is it spoken to after death as if alive? when and where? What is the character of the addresses?" [022-004], indicating the ethnographic importance attached to these vocal practices.

## The Widow's Wailing

The most intense form of death [[mourning|wailing]] fell upon the widow. Among the [[creek|Creeks]], the widow's observance was prolonged and solitary:

> "The widow wholly neglects her toilet, while she daily goes to the grave during one entire moon from the date when the death occurred. On the evening of the last day of the moon the friends all assemble at the cabin of the disconsolate widow, bringing provisions for a sumptuous feast, which consists of corn and jerked beef boiled together in a kettle. While the supper is preparing, the bereaved wife goes to the grave, and pours out, with unusual vehemence, her bitter wailings and lamentations." [019-011]

This daily ritual — a full lunar month of grave visitation — made the widow the most visible and audible mourner in the community, her [[mourning|wailing]] a public marker of the passage of grief through its prescribed duration.

## Social Regulation of Grief

Death [[mourning|wailing]] also functioned as a socially prescribed channel for grief. Among [[virginia|Virginia]] tribes, after burial "the women being painted with their faces coal black do sit twenty-four hours in the houses lamenting and crying out in a shrill voice" [006-010]. The loud, public nature of the practice transformed private grief into a communal event, reinforcing kinship bonds and collective identity in the face of death.

The ritual could also take on a distinctively collective and even theatrical character. At one [[sioux|Sioux]] funeral, the observer noted "the contrast exhibited by the terrific lamentations and yells of one part of the company while the others were singing and dancing with all their might" [020-002]. Such simultaneous performances of grief and celebration reveal death [[mourning|wailing]] as part of a larger ritual complex in which different modes of expression were assigned to different participants — a division of ritual labor rather than a simple outpouring of individual sorrow.
