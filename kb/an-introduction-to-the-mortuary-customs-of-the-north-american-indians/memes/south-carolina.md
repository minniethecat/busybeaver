## Overview

South Carolina, situated on the southeastern coast of the United States, was home to several Native American tribes whose mortuary customs were documented by early European observers. The Congaree, Santee, and Catawba peoples of this region practiced distinctive forms of burial that included partial embalmment, secondary bone preservation, and the construction of above-ground burial houses described as "umbrella" structures (006-014). The region's burial practices, recorded in detail by John Lawson and later compiled by Henry Rowe Schoolcraft, provide valuable insight into the mortuary customs of the southeastern tribes.

## Burial Practices

The Congaree and Santee Indians employed a distinctive process of partial embalmment and secondary burial. According to Lawson's account, "As soon as the party is dead they lay the corpse upon a piece of bark in the sun, seasoning or embalming it with a small root beaten to powder, which looks as red as vermilion; the same is mixed with bear's oil to beautify the hair" (006-014). After one or two days of sun exposure, the body was placed upon crotched sticks elevated from the earth, anointed with the root powder and bear's oil mixture, and covered "over very exactly with the bark of the pine or cypress tree to prevent any rain to fall upon it" (006-014).

## Secondary Bone Preservation

A notable feature of South Carolina tribal burial was the practice of bone preservation after decomposition. Once "the flesh grows mellow and will cleave from the bone," the bones were cleaned, anointed with preservative ingredients, and carefully stored — the skull wrapped "very carefully" in cloth woven from opossum hair (006-014). The preserved bones were kept in a wooden box, with descendants "every year oiling and cleansing them," allowing preservation "for many ages that you may see an Indian in possession of the bones of his grandfather or some of his relations of a longer antiquity" (006-014).

## Burial Monuments

The above-ground burial structures of the South Carolina tribes were described in detail. "A mole or pyramid of earth is raised, the mould thereof being worked very smooth and even, sometimes higher or lower, according to the dignity of the person whose monument it is" (006-014). Atop this mound, an "umbrella" structure — "made ridgeways, like the roof of a house" — was supported by nine stakes or small posts, from which were hung "gourds, feathers, and other such like trophies" placed by the deceased's relations (006-014). For the more distinguished dead, a "subterranean vault" was constructed with "a roof of light wood or pitch pine," covered with bark and earth, until the bones were ready for removal to the Quiogozon — "the royal tomb or burial place of their kings and war captains" (006-014).

## The Quiogozon

The Quiogozon represented the highest form of burial repository among the South Carolina tribes — "a more magnificent cabin reared at the public expense" that served as a communal tomb for royalty and war captains (006-014). Bones retrieved from temporary graves were "cleaned, jointed, clad in white dressed deer skins" before being deposited in this venerated structure. The Quiogozon was "an object of veneration" where the king, old men, and conjurers would "spend several days with their idols and dead kings" (006-014). Europeans were reportedly never granted admittance to these sacred spaces.
