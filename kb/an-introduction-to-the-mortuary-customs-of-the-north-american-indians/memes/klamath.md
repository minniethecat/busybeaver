# Klamath

## Overview

The Klamath people occupy the eastern slopes of the Cascade Range in present-day southern [[oregon|Oregon]] and northern [[california|California]], a region of high desert, volcanic lakes, and pine forests. [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], in his contribution to [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1853, Part 3, p. 140), provided Yarrow with a detailed account of Klamath burial customs, which he characterized as exhibiting "very considerable taste and a laudable care." The Klamath, together with the neighboring Trinity Indians, developed burial practices that combined elements of the Northwest Coast [[canoe-burial]] tradition with distinctive interior Plateau features (005-003).

## Mortuary Customs

The standard Klamath burial involved enclosing the dead in rude coffins formed by placing four boards around the body and covering the whole with earth to some depth. A heavy plank, often supported by upright head and foot stones, was laid upon the top; alternatively, stones were built up into a wall rising approximately one foot above the ground, with the top flagged with additional stones. This construction — part coffin, part stone cist — represents a hybrid form of inhumation that is distinct from both the canoe burials of the coastal tribes and the scaffold burials of the Plains (005-003).

For chiefs and persons of elevated status, the graves were surrounded by neat wooden palings, with each pale ornamented with a feather from the tail of the bald eagle. The use of eagle feathers as grave decoration carries profound symbolic weight: the bald eagle, as a creature of the sky realm, was associated with spiritual power and vision across the Plateau and Northwest Coast culture areas. The paling enclosure itself, while practical in demarcating the chief's grave and protecting it from disturbance, also served as a visible marker of the deceased's rank, legible to all who passed through the burial ground (005-003).

Grave goods were an integral component of Klamath burial. Baskets were usually staked down beside the grave in numbers proportionate to the wealth or popularity of the individual, and other articles for ornament or use were suspended over the grave. These offerings, like those of the coastal tribes, were rendered unserviceable before deposition — a practice reflecting the widespread indigenous belief that objects, like their owners, must symbolically die in order to accompany the deceased to the spirit world (005-003).

The funeral ceremonies occupied three days, during which the soul of the deceased was believed to be in mortal danger from *O-mah-u*, or the Devil. To protect the departing spirit, a fire was kept [[cremation|burning]] continuously at the grave, and the friends and relatives of the deceased howled around it throughout the night to scare away the demon. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] noted that should the mourners fail in this protective effort, the soul would be carried down the river, though it remained subject to redemption by *Peh-ho-wan* upon payment of a big knife. After the expiration of the three-day vigil, it was believed that all was well with the deceased. This three-day liminal period — during which the soul was vulnerable and required the active intervention of the living — finds parallels in the [[mourning]] customs of numerous North American tribes, including the thirty-day [[mourning]] period of the [[coyotero-apache|Coyotero Apache]] and the year-long [[feeding-the-dead|spirit-feeding]] practice of the Yokai-a of [[california|California]] (005-003).

The ritual elements of the Klamath funeral — the continuous fire, the howling vigil, the three-day duration, and the personification of spiritual danger in the figure of O-mah-u — together constitute a coherent eschatology in which the journey of the soul to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] was neither automatic nor guaranteed. The active participation of the living community was essential to ensure the soul's safe passage, and failure in this duty carried the gravest spiritual consequences for the deceased (005-003).

## Historical Sources

The primary account of Klamath burial practices comes from [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], published in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]]'s monumental *Historical and Statistical Information Respecting the History, Condition and Prospects of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (Part 3, 1853, p. 140). [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]], who served as geologist and ethnologist on the Pacific Railroad Survey and later as an Indian agent in Washington Territory, was one of the most reliable nineteenth-century observers of Northwest indigenous cultures.
