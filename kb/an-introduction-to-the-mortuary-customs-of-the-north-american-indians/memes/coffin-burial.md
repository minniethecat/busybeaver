# Coffin Burial

The use of **coffins** — receptacles constructed to contain the body of the deceased — appeared in varied forms across North America, from rudely assembled boards to hollowed-out canoes serving as coffins. Yarrow distinguished coffin-burial from the related practice of [[box-burial]], noting differences in construction, cultural context, and the degree of European influence.

## Rude Board Coffins

The simplest form of coffin consisted of rough boards enclosing the body. Among the [[carolina|Carolina]] tribes, "the dead are inclosed in rude coffins formed by placing four boards around the body and covered with earth to some depth; a heavy plank often supported by a frame of posts" [001-020]. This form appears to represent an indigenous practice adapted to available materials, rather than direct European imitation.

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] used a similar construction: the deceased was "buried on the surface, in a box or a substitute made of branches of trees, covered with small branches, leaves, and earth" [013-005]. Over time exposure often revealed the remains, highlighting the impermanence of these structures.

## Canoe Coffins

"the use of canoes for coffins has occasionally been remarked," Yarrow noted, describing an 1875 excavation at Santa Barbara where "an entire skeleton which was discovered in a redwood canoe" was removed from the grave [001-033]. The [[canoe-burial|canoe-coffin]] practice was particularly associated with the northwest coast and [[california|California]], where the canoe served both as a vessel for the living and as a conveyance for the dead on their journey to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

On the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]], canoes were used as aerial [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]]: "corpses were deposited in a box made from part of a broken canoe" on scaffolds eight to ten feet high [014-008]. This combined the [[canoe-burial|canoe-coffin]] tradition with scaffold-burial, producing a distinctive regional variant.

## European Influence

Distinguishing indigenous coffin use from European-introduced practices is essential. Yarrow treated coffin-burial as a category that "has occasionally been remarked" rather than a universal practice, suggesting it was not an ancient, pan-Indian custom. The "rude coffins" described among eastern tribes may reflect an intermediate stage — indigenous adoption of the coffin concept using traditional materials and construction methods.

In some cases, the coffin was clearly adopted from settlers. The 1875 Santa Barbara excavation, conducted by a Euro-American observer, noted that the [[canoe-burial|canoe-coffin]] find was unusual rather than typical, and speculated that "the individual may have been a noted fisherman" [001-033]. This suggests [[canoe-burial|canoe-coffin]] burial was not a universal practice even where it occurred.

## Regional Distribution

Coffin use was most prevalent in regions with sustained European contact. Southeastern tribes — [[cherokee|Cherokee]], [[creek|Creek]], [[choctaw|Choctaw]] — adopted the practice more readily than Plains or Southwestern groups. The [[creek|Muscogulges]], while not using coffins in the European sense, lined graves with cypress bark [002-003], representing a functionally similar practice using indigenous materials.
