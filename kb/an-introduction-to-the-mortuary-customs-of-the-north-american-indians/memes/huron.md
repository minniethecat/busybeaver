# Huron

## Overview

The Huron, or Wendat, were an Iroquoian-speaking confederacy occupying the region between Lake Simcoe and [[georgia|Georgia]]n Bay in present-day Ontario. By the mid-seventeenth century, the Huron had been largely dispersed by warfare with the [[iroquois|Iroquois]], yet their mortuary customs were preserved in remarkable detail through the accounts of French Jesuit missionaries, particularly [[brebeuf-p-de|Jean de Brébeuf]], who lived among them and witnessed their ceremonies firsthand. Yarrow drew on these early missionary sources, transmitted through the Canadian explorer [[hind-hy|Henry Youle Hind]], to document what was among the most elaborately described communal burial ceremonies in the North American ethnographic record.

## Mortuary Customs

### The Feast of the Dead

The central mortuary institution of the Huron people was the "Feast of the Dead" — a periodic communal [[secondary-burial|reburial]] ceremony of enormous scale and social significance. De Brébeuf, who personally attended one such ceremony, provided an account that Yarrow preserves through [[hind-hy|Hind]]'s *Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition* (1860):

> The Jesuit missionary, P. de Brebeuf, who assisted at one of the "feasts of the dead" at the village of Ossosane, before the dispersion of the Hurons, relates that the ceremony took place in the presence of 2,000 Indians, who offered 1,200 presents at the common tomb, in testimony of their grief (020-004).

The scale of this gathering — two thousand participants drawn from multiple communities — underscores the Feast of the Dead's function not merely as a funerary rite but as a major social, economic, and political event that reinforced inter-village alliances and kinship networks.

### The Great Ossuary

The central act of the Feast of the Dead was the deposition of accumulated skeletal remains into a communal [[ossuary]]. De Brébeuf's account, as transmitted by Yarrow, describes the preparation of the burial receptacle:

> The people belonging to five large villages deposited the bones of their dead in a gigantic shroud, composed of forty-eight robes, each robe being made of ten beaver skins. After being carefully wrapped in this shroud, they were placed between moss and bark (020-005).

The shroud alone represented an immense accumulation of wealth — four hundred and eighty beaver skins — given over entirely to the honor of the dead. The construction of this communal tomb was not merely a practical measure but a statement of collective identity, binding five separate villages into a single mortuary community.

After the bones were arranged within their protective layers of moss and bark, "a wall of stones was built around this vast [[ossuary]] to preserve it from profanation" (020-005). The stone perimeter marked the [[ossuary]] as sacred space, physically separating the dead from the profane world and ensuring the integrity of the burial against both animal disturbance and human intrusion.

### Corn Offering

Before the final sealing of the [[ossuary]], the Huron women performed a ritual of agricultural offering. Yarrow records that "before covering the bones with earth a few grains of Indian corn were thrown by the women upon the sacred relics" (020-005). This gesture linked the cycle of human death and communal [[secondary-burial|reburial]] with the agricultural cycle of planting and harvest, suggesting a conceptual association between the regeneration of crops from seed and the hoped-for renewal or continuation of the dead in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

### Burial Feasts

Yarrow's index records the Huron under entries for "feast, Hurons" (024-056) and "Hurons, burial feasts of" (024-208), indicating that the ceremonial feast — the sharing of food among the living at the gravesite — was recognized as a distinct and significant element of Huron funerary practice. These feasts served to redistribute resources, reinforce social bonds, and mark the transition of the deceased from the community of the living to that of the ancestors.

## Historical Sources

| Source | Contribution | Date |
|--------|-------------|------|
| [[brebeuf-p-de|Jean de Brébeuf]] | Jesuit missionary — firsthand account of the Feast of the Dead at Ossosane | ca. 1636 |
| [[hind-hy|Henry Youle Hind]] | *Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition* — transmission of Brébeuf's account to nineteenth-century ethnography | 1860 |

De Brébeuf's account is exceptional among the sources used by Yarrow for its early date and its quality as direct participant observation. Few European observers in the early contact period provided comparably detailed descriptions of indigenous mortuary ritual at such scale.
