# Body Preparation

**Body preparation** encompassed the set of practices by which North American Indian tribes treated the corpse between death and final disposal — washing, painting, anointing, wrapping in skins or blankets, and dressing in the deceased's finest garments. These practices reflected beliefs about the body's transitional state and the needs of the deceased in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

## Washing and Painting

The body was commonly washed and painted after death. Yarrow's questionnaire directed observers to inquire: "How is the corpse prepared after death and disposed of?" [022-004]. Among the [[virginia|Virginia]] Algonquians, the dead were prepared with their "heads painted red with oil and pocones, finely trimmed with feathers" to appear before their deity [006-012]. The [[dakota|Dakota]] [[mourning]] practice involved "the top of the head and forehead, and sometimes nearly the whole body, smeared with a species of white clay" — applied to the living mourners but reflecting the same symbolic vocabulary used on the dead [014-020].

Red paint — vermilion or ochre — was particularly significant. One burial contained "a bag of red paint" folded among the wrappings of the deceased [014-017], and the use of red pigment on corpses was documented across the Eastern Woodlands as a marker of ritual preparation.

## Wrapping and Binding

Wrapping the body was nearly universal. Among the [[sioux|Sioux]], "their manner of burial has always been (until recently) to inclose the dead body in robes or blankets, the best owned by the departed, closely sewed up" [014-004]. The deceased was wrapped in his or her finest possessions — the "best" blankets and robes — indicating that the wrapping served both practical and symbolic functions. The [[apache|Coyoteros]] would "partially wrap up the corpse and deposit it into the cavity left by the removal of a small rock," cramming the body "into the smallest possible space" [001-027].

In the Southeast, the [[creek|Muscogulges]] wrapped the body in blankets and "tie them firmly with cords" before placement in the grave [002-008]. At the time of death, "the squaws are busily engaged in preparing the corpse for the grave. This does not take long; whatever articles of clothing may have been on the person at the time of death are removed and replaced by the finest that can be secured" [005-005].

## Flexed Posture

Many tribes bound the corpse into a flexed or seated position. The [[pima|Pimas]] "tie the bodies of their dead with ropes, passing the latter around the neck and under the knees and then drawing them tight until the body is doubled up and forced into a sitting position" [001-025]. This practice, widespread from [[california|California]] to the Southeast, has been interpreted as returning the deceased to a fetal position symbolic of rebirth, or simply as a practical measure to minimize grave size.

## Enshrouding in Mats and Bark

Beyond blankets and robes, tribes used locally available materials. The coastal Algonquians wrapped the corpse in mats before burial: "the corpse being lapped in skins and mats with their jewels they lay them upon sticks in the ground" [006-010]. In the [[ohio|Ohio Valley]], a cave burial yielded a body "enveloped in coarse clothes" and encased in stone slabs [006-017]. The Caribs of the circum-Caribbean region "place the corpse on a little seat in a ditch or grave" after wrapping [014-025].

## Regional Variation

Body preparation practices reflected local material culture and belief systems. Northwest coast tribes, with access to cedar bark and finely woven blankets, produced elaborate wrappings. Plains tribes used buffalo robes. Southeastern groups employed cane matting and bark. Despite the material diversity, the underlying logic was consistent: the dead must be properly dressed and wrapped for their journey to the spirit world, and the quality of preparation reflected the social status of the deceased.
