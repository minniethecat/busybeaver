# Dakota

## Overview

The Dakotas, or Santee Sioux, constituted the eastern division of the [[sioux|Sioux Nation]], inhabiting the upper [[mississippi|Mississippi]] and Minnesota River regions before their displacement westward. Yarrow (1880) devoted considerable attention to Dakota mortuary customs, drawing upon the detailed observations of [[turner-ls|Dr. L. S.]] [[turner-ls|Turner]], a U.S. Army surgeon stationed at Fort Peck, Montana, whose six years of continuous contact with the Dakotas yielded one of the richest ethnographic accounts in the monograph.

## Mortuary Customs

### Scaffold and Tree Burial

Dr. [[turner-ls|Turner]] recorded that the Dakotas preferred [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]] when suitable limbs were available: "The Dakotas bury their dead in the tops of trees when limbs can be found sufficiently horizontal to support scaffolding on which to lay the body." In the more open terrain of Dakota Territory, however, scaffold burial prevailed. These scaffolds were constructed upon four posts set into the ground, seven to ten feet high, with a flooring of small poles upon which the wrapped body was laid. The elevation served the practical purpose of protecting remains from scavenging wolves — a constant threat on the Plains (014-019).

### Burial Preparation

Upon death, preparation began immediately under the direction of the women, usually the elder women of the band. The face, neck, and hands were thickly painted with vermilion, or a red earth substitute when vermilion was unavailable. The body was wrapped first in blankets, then in a dressed buffalo skin with the flesh side outward, and finally bound with rawhide thongs. For a warrior or chief, weapons and implements of battle were placed on or secured to the scaffold. If the deceased had been a chief, it was not uncommon to slay his favorite pony and place the body beneath the scaffold (014-019).

## Mourning Observances

Dakota [[mourning]] was characterized by extreme physical self-punishment. [[turner-ls|Turner]] recorded that the hair — "never cut under other circumstances" — was cropped to the neck, and the head, forehead, and sometimes the entire body were smeared with white clay moistened with water. The lodge and all family possessions except the most essential clothing were given away, leaving the mourners destitute (014-020).

Women relatives would "throw off their moccasins and leggins and gash their legs with their butcher-knives, and march through the camp and to the place of burial with bare and bleeding extremities, while they chant or wail their dismal songs of [[mourning]]." Men gashed themselves similarly and sought solitude on distant prairie heights, remaining there "fasting, smoking, and [[mourning|wailing]] out their [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s for two or three days" (014-020).

[[turner-ls|Turner]] recounted the case of a chief who had lost a brother: "He had gashed the outer side of both lower extremities at intervals of a few inches all the way from the ankles to the top of the hips. His wounds had inflamed from exposure, and were suppurating freely." [[turner-ls|Turner]] treated the wounds with ointment and anodyne, after which the chief "slept long and refreshingly, and awoke to express his gratitude" (014-020).

The daily [[mourning]] ritual involved visiting the burial site at sunset and chanting grief "until apparently assuaged by its own expression." [[turner-ls|Turner]] observed: "I have seen few things in life so touching as the spectacle of an old father going daily to the grave of his child, while the shadows are lengthening, and pouring out his grief in wails that would move a demon, until his figure melts with the gray twilight" (014-020).

## Historical Sources

The primary source on Dakota mortuary customs is [[turner-ls|Dr. L. S.]] [[turner-ls|Turner]], U.S. Army, whose observations were made during "more than six years' constant intercourse with several subdivisions of the Dakota Indians" at Fort Peck. His medical training gave his accounts of wounding and self-mutilation a clinical precision rare in the ethnographic literature of the period. Additional observations from Belden's *Life of Belden, the White Chief* (1871) covered the [[yanktonais|Yanktonais]], a Dakota band, documenting their scaffold construction techniques (014-019)(014-020).
