# Pierre Muret

## Overview

Pierre Muret was a seventeenth-century French scholar whose treatise *Rites of Funeral, Ancient and Modern* (published in an English translation in 1683) compiled descriptions of mortuary customs from classical and Near Eastern sources for a European readership increasingly interested in comparative religious ethnology. His detailed account of the ancient Persian practice of exposing the dead to wild beasts — a custom that he described with a mixture of ethnographic curiosity and baroque moral horror — was transmitted to Yarrow's 1880 monograph through [[bruhier-jacques-jean|Jacques Jean Bruhier]]'s *L'incertitude des Signes de la Mort* (1740), which drew heavily on Muret's earlier compilation. Yarrow cited Muret's observations in discussing the comparative context of exposure burial and in his chapter on what Muret himself had termed "living [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]]" — the devouring of the dead by birds, beasts, or surviving relatives (001-014)(018-001).

## Contributions

Muret's most substantial contribution to the monograph, transmitted through [[bruhier-jacques-jean|Bruhier]]'s citation, was his account of Persian exposure burial. Muret expressed bewilderment that the Persians — whom he acknowledged as "one of the most civilized Nations in the world" — should nevertheless have practiced a custom so starkly at odds with European norms of [[inhumation|interment]]. Drawing on the late antique historians Procopius and Agathias, Muret recorded that the Persians exposed their dead naked in open fields — a treatment that, he noted, European law reserved for the most infamous criminals — and that they considered it a great misfortune if neither birds nor beasts devoured the corpse. The speed with which a body was consumed determined the family's estimation of the deceased's felicity: prompt consumption was greeted with joy and congratulations, for it signified that the soul had entered the Elysian Fields and would procure the same bliss for all family members; a body left untouched by animals was a cause of extreme sorrow and an infallible presage of misfortune, the soul believed to be dragged into Hell and destined to return with its demonic tormentors to trouble the living (001-014).

This inversion of European mortuary values — in which what Muret regarded as the most degraded form of bodily disposal was celebrated by the Persians as the most honorable — presented Yarrow with a striking comparative example of the cultural relativity of mortuary norms, a theme that runs throughout the 1880 monograph.

Muret was also the source of the term "living [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]]" (*vivants sepulchres*), which he used to describe the devouring of the dead by birds and animals or, in extreme cases, by surviving relatives. Yarrow adopted this term in his own chapter heading, noting that while the custom of cannibalistic consumption of the dead was documented among the ancient Massagetae, Derbices, and Issedones, as well as among the Irish and ancient Britons according to Horace and Tertullian, no credible evidence supported its practice among the North American Indians. Lafitau's assertion that certain South American Indians practiced ritual cannibalism of the dead was noted, but Yarrow explicitly declined to endorse the claim for North America, observing that "to the credit of our savages, this barbarous and revolting practice is not believed to have been practiced by them" (018-001).
