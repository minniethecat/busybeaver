# Mandan

## Overview

The Mandan people inhabited the upper [[missouri|Missouri]] River in present-day North [[dakota|Dakota]], where they lived in substantial, palisaded earth-lodge villages that served as major trading centers for the northern Plains. Unlike the nomadic bison-hunting tribes that surrounded them, the Mandan were sedentary agriculturalists whose permanent villages allowed for the development of fixed mortuary grounds of considerable size and elaboration. Yarrow (1880) noted the Mandan primarily through the observations of [[catlin-george|George Catlin]], whose evocative term for their burial scaffolds — the "Golgothas" of the Mandans — captured the visual impact of these elevated cemeteries on the nineteenth-century observer.

## Mortuary Customs

### The Golgothas — Scaffold Burial Grounds

The defining feature of Mandan mortuary practice was the use of elevated scaffolds for the exposure of the dead. [[catlin-george|Catlin]], whose paintings and writings introduced the Mandan to a wide Euro-American audience, described these burial grounds with the biblical term "Golgothas" — the place of skulls — thereby likening the Mandan scaffolds to the hill of crucifixion outside Jerusalem (015-015).

Yarrow's index preserves two related entries: "Golgothas, Mandans" (024-196) and "Mandan Golgothas" (024-262), confirming that the term had become attached specifically to the Mandan within the ethnographic literature. The Mandan scaffold was an elevated wooden platform, constructed of poles and supported by upright posts, upon which the deceased — wrapped in robes or blankets — was placed for aerial decomposition. The elevation served both the practical function of protecting the body from scavengers and the symbolic function of placing the deceased closer to the sky, the sun, and the elemental forces that dominated Plains cosmology.

### Village Burial Grounds

Unlike nomadic Plains tribes who might scatter their dead across a vast territory, the Mandan — tied to their permanent [[missouri|Missouri]] River villages — developed fixed burial grounds in close proximity to their settlements. These cemeteries, with their multiple scaffolds visible from a distance, presented to [[catlin-george|Catlin]] and other early visitors a spectacle that evoked European conceptions of sacred mortuary landscapes. The concentration of scaffolds in a single location distinguished Mandan practice from the more dispersed pattern of Plains scaffold burial and contributed to the "Golgotha" characterization.

### The Smallpox Catastrophe and Mortuary Disruption

It is important to note that the Mandan suffered catastrophic population loss in the smallpox epidemic of 1837–1838, which reduced the tribe from approximately 1,600 individuals to fewer than 150 survivors. This demographic collapse disrupted — and in many cases terminated — the transmission of traditional mortuary practice. The Mandan burials documented by [[catlin-george|Catlin]] and transmitted through Yarrow therefore represent the pre-epidemic mortuary complex, a tradition that survived only in fragmentary form among the remnant Mandan population and their [[hidatsa|Hidatsa]] and Arikara neighbors.

## Historical Sources

The principal source for Mandan mortuary customs is [[catlin-george|George Catlin]], who visited the Mandan villages in the 1830s — just before the smallpox epidemic that would devastate the tribe. [[catlin-george|Catlin]]'s visual and written record of Mandan life, including the "Golgothas" of their burial grounds, constitutes a documentary record of unique importance precisely because the world it describes was so soon destroyed. Yarrow incorporated [[catlin-george|Catlin]]'s Mandan observations into his comparative framework, indexing them under the distinctive terminology that [[catlin-george|Catlin]] had introduced.
