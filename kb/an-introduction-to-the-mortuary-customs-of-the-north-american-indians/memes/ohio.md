## Overview

Ohio, situated in the Midwest between the Ohio River and Lake Erie, is one of the most archaeologically significant regions in Yarrow's 1880 survey. The state's extensive earthworks, burial mounds, and limestone cavern burials provide some of the earliest scholarly documentation of North American aboriginal mortuary practices.

## Burial Sites & Practices

The burial mounds of Ohio were among the first scientifically investigated in North America. [[atwater-caleb|Caleb Atwater]], in his *Transactions of the American Antiquarian Society*, provided early descriptions of Ohio burial mounds, noting that "the [[mound-builders]] of the Ohio Valley" constructed earthworks of considerable sophistication (004-012, 011-005).

Cavern burials were a distinctive regional practice. [[putnam-fw|F. W.]] [[putnam-fw|Putnam]] reported that "many of the caverns of Ohio, Indiana, and [[kentucky|Kentucky]]" contained human remains placed in fissures and recesses, often accompanied by grave goods (005-013). Gianque of Cincinnati documented a particularly well-preserved cavern burial, noting the careful arrangement of the skeleton and associated artifacts (004-033).

### The Miami Valley Mound: Gianque's Account

A particularly striking example of Ohio mound burial was recorded by Florian Gianque of Cincinnati, who described the excavation of a mound in the Miami Valley that revealed an unusual circular arrangement of interments:

> "A mound was opened in this locality, some years ago, containing a central corpse in a sitting posture, and over thirty skeletons buried around it in a circle, also in a sitting posture but leaning against one another, tipped over towards the right facing inwards." (004-033)

The configuration — a single central figure surrounded by over thirty individuals arranged in a concentric circle, all seated and facing inward — suggests a burial of considerable ritual significance. The central corpse may represent a person of high status, with the surrounding skeletons possibly being attendants, family members, or captives interred to accompany the principal figure. Numerous ornaments, awls, and other artifacts were reported to have been found near the central body, further indicating differential treatment. Gianque vouched for the reliability of his informants, though he did not personally witness the opening of the mound.

### Stone Mounds of the Ohio Valley

Atwater's descriptions of Ohio's stone mounds provide additional detail about the region's mortuary architecture. He documented stone tumuli — conical piles of small stones showing no tool marks — that contained some of the most significant artifacts recovered from Ohio sites: "urns, ornaments of copper, heads of spears, &c., of the same metal, as well as medals of copper and pickaxes of horneblende" (004-012). Atwater distinguished between earth mounds used as cemeteries for distinguished persons (containing one or two skeletons) and mounds apparently built as commemorative monuments without human remains, the latter sometimes containing the most valuable artifacts.

One notable stone mound examined by Atwater, situated on Paint Creek at the western edge of the hill on which a "walled town" stood, was "piled on the surface of the earth on the spot where three skeletons had been buried in stone coffins, beneath the surface" (004-012). The graves were dug to a depth comparable to nineteenth-century burials, lined with thin flat stones, and covered with large flat stones before earth and a pile of stones were heaped above — a construction method that Atwater considered possibly the work of "the present race of Indians" rather than the earlier mound-builders.
