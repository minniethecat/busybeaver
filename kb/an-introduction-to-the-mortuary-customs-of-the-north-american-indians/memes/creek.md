# Creek

## Overview

The Creek people, also known as the Muscogee or Muscogulges, constituted a powerful confederacy of the American Southeast, originally occupying territories in present-day [[georgia|Georgia]] and Alabama. By the time of Yarrow's 1880 monograph, a significant portion of the Creek population had been relocated to Indian Territory following the [[indian-removal|Indian Removal]] of the 1830s. Yarrow's correspondents documented Creek mortuary customs both in their ancestral homeland and in their new western settlements, preserving accounts of a burial tradition that shared many features with neighboring Southeastern peoples.

## Mortuary Customs

### Surface Burial in Boxes

The Creeks practiced surface-level [[inhumation|interment]] using box-like structures or improvised frames made from tree branches. As documented in Yarrow's account of the Creeks in Indian Territory:

> The Creeks, Choctaws, and Cherokees used boxes much resembling those spoken of by the tribes formerly living in New York (013-002).

In Indian Territory, Creek burials were described as follows:

> They are buried on the surface, in a box or a substitute made of branches of trees, covered with small branches, leaves, and earth. I have seen several of their graves, which after a few weeks had become uncovered and the remains exposed to view (013-004).

The surface burial method — placing the deceased above ground within a protective covering — represented a distinctly Southeastern pattern, shared with the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] and [[cherokee|Cherokee]], and contrasted with the subterranean inhumation practiced by many other North American groups.

### Grave Orientation and Offerings

Creek burials followed a prescribed orientation: the feet of the deceased were always directed toward the east. Grave goods accompanied the dead according to age and status. Yarrow records:

> I saw in one Creek grave (a child's) a small sum of silver, in another (adult male) some implements of warfare, bow and arrows (013-004).

The inclusion of silver coinage in a child's grave suggests the incorporation of Euro-American trade goods into traditional Creek burial practice by the late nineteenth century, while the warrior's bow and arrows indicate continuity with pre-contact customs.

## Mourning Observances

Creek [[mourning]] rites involved the physical transformation of the mourner's appearance. During the [[mourning]] period, the nearer relatives of the deceased would smear their hair and faces with a composition made of grease and wood ashes, and would remain in that condition for several days, and in some cases up to a month (013-005).

This practice of covering oneself with a mixture of grease and ash — a visible and tactile sign of grief — parallels similar customs of self-abasement and ritual defilement recorded among other Southeastern tribes. The mourner's transformed appearance served as a public marker of bereavement, signaling the individual's altered social status during the [[mourning]] interval.

## Historical Sources

Yarrow's information on Creek mortuary customs derives primarily from observations made among the relocated Creeks in Indian Territory during the 1870s. These accounts, while detailed for that period, capture Creek burial practice after decades of displacement and cultural disruption. Pre-Removal sources such as [[bartram-william|William Bartram]] and [[roman|Bernard Roman]]s provide additional context for earlier Creek funerary traditions in the Southeast, though these are less extensively quoted in Yarrow's treatment of the tribe.
