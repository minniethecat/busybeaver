## Overview

The practice of depositing weapons with the dead is one of the most widely documented mortuary customs among Native American tribes. Weapons were placed in graves, on scaffolds, and in burial structures, reflecting the belief that the deceased would need them in the afterlife or as a mark of the individual's status as a warrior or hunter.

## Tribal Distribution

Among the Wichita, the deceased's bows and arrows or gun were deposited with the body before burial (001-009). The tribes of the Columbia River region lowered the deceased into the grave with bow, arrow, spear, and paddle (001-035). In the stone graves of Tennessee, pipes, stone hammers, knives, and arrowheads were commonly found with the dead (003-005).

## Historical Accounts

Yarrow notes that among many tribes, the weapons of war were laid by the deceased's side after the limbs were straightened out in preparation for burial (005-005). At a scaffold burial, the platform was decorated with weapons, scalps, and ornaments, marking the status of the deceased warrior (012-004). Among tribes that practiced scaffold burial, the weapons and pipes of men were included with the body before it was wrapped in robes or blankets (014-004). At the Skokomish Agency in Washington Territory, the deceased were sometimes interred with their arrows, spears, or other weapons placed beside them (016-012). In cairn burials of Utah, corpses were placed with weapons and ornaments before stones were piled over the grave (009-002).

## Variations

In some tribes where cremation was practiced, weapons were not deposited with the corpse but were instead distributed among relatives or destroyed separately (005-008). Among certain Pacific coast tribes, wooden facsimiles of weapons rather than actual weapons were placed in graves, reflecting a symbolic rather than literal provision for the afterlife (006-038).
