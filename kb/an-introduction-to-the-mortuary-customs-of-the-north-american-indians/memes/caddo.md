# Caddo

## Overview

The Caddo people, who called themselves *Ascena* or "Timber Indians," inhabited the Red River country of present-day Texas, Louisiana, Arkansas, and Oklahoma. A confederacy of sedentary agricultural peoples whose complex society — with hereditary chiefs, temple mounds, and elaborate ceremonial life — represented the southwesternmost extension of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]]an cultural tradition, the Caddo developed mortuary practices that closely paralleled those of their Caddoan relatives. Yarrow (1880) documented Caddo burial through the same Dr. Fordyce Grinnell who provided the principal account of [[wichita|Wichita]] customs, noting both the convergence of Caddo and [[wichita|Wichita]] burial modes and one remarkable point of divergence.

## Mortuary Customs

### Inhumation

The Caddo practiced earth burial in a manner nearly identical to the [[wichita|Wichita]]. Yarrow records that the Caddoes "follow nearly the same mode of burial as the [[wichita|Wichitas]]" (001-010). This shared pattern included the digging of a grave three to four feet deep, the placement of blankets and robes as a bedding, the dressing of the deceased in finest apparel, the inclusion of gender-differentiated grave goods, the construction of a stick-and-grass barrier to separate the earth fill from the body, and the erection of a protective pen of poles around the completed grave. Yarrow's index confirms the centrality of this practice with the entry "Caddoes, inhumation of" (024-107).

### The Battle-Death Exception

The single most distinctive feature of Caddo mortuary custom was the treatment of those killed in battle. Unlike the [[wichita|Wichita]] and most other tribes documented by Yarrow, the Caddo made a categorical distinction between natural death and death in combat:

> If a Caddo is killed in battle, the body is never buried, but is left to be devoured by beasts or birds of prey and the condition of such individuals in the other world is considered to be far better than that of persons dying a natural death (001-011).

This inversion of the expected hierarchy — the warrior denied burial yet granted a superior [[spirit-land|afterlife]] — challenges the assumption that mortuary elaboration correlates directly with spiritual privilege. For the Caddo, the warrior's body, abandoned on the battlefield to the consumption of raptors and scavengers, represented not dishonor but a direct translation to an elevated spiritual state. The "beasts or birds of prey" served not as violators of the corpse but as agents of a superior form of disposition, one that bypassed entirely the elaborate grave construction accorded to those who died ordinary deaths.

### Caddoan Context

The close correspondence between Caddo and [[wichita|Wichita]] burial customs reflects their shared Caddoan linguistic and cultural heritage. Both groups practiced inhumation with grave goods, protective grave enclosures, and horse [[sacrifice]]. The Caddo diverged most sharply from the [[wichita|Wichita]] not in the mechanics of burial but in the metaphysical framework that assigned radically different postmortem fates to the warrior and the civilian dead.

## Historical Sources

The primary source for Caddo burial customs is Dr. Fordyce Grinnell, physician to the [[wichita|Wichita]] Agency, who provided Yarrow with parallel accounts of [[wichita|Wichita]] and Caddo practice. Grinnell's account is notable for capturing both the material and the ideological dimensions of Caddo mortuary custom — the how of burial and the why of the battle-death exception. The Caddo are indexed in Yarrow under both "Caddoes" (024-217) and "Caddoes, inhumation of" (024-107).
