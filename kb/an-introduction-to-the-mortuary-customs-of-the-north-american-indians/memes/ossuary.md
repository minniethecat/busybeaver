# Ossuary

## Overview

The ossuary — a communal repository for cleaned human skeletal remains — represented one of the most distinctive mortuary structures documented by Yarrow (1880) among North American Indian tribes. Unlike individual graves or [[burial-vaults|tombs]], the ossuary served as a collective ancestral shrine, holding the bones of multiple generations after they had undergone secondary burial processing. This practice, most extensively documented among the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] and certain Eastern tribes, reflected a communal approach to ancestor veneration that contrasted sharply with the individualized burial customs of other tribal groups.

## Tribal Distribution

### The Quiogozon of the East

Among tribes of the eastern United States, the ossuary was known by various indigenous names. Yarrow documents one such structure, described by an early observer: the bones of the deceased were "cleaned, and deposited in an ossuary called the Quiogozon" (001-007). This practice of collective bone storage after ritual cleaning linked these eastern tribes to the broader secondary burial traditions of the Southeast.

### Choctaw Bone-Houses

The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] ossuary, or [[bone-house]], received extended attention from Yarrow's sources. [[bartram-william|Bartram]], the naturalist and traveler, provided one of the most detailed accounts: "in alluding to the ossuary or [[bone-house]]," he described a structure where the cleaned bones of the community's dead were accumulated over generations (004-031). The bone-pickers — ritual specialists of a distinct social caste — prepared the remains before depositing them in the ossuary, performing a ceremony that transformed the individual dead into collective ancestors.

### Preservation and Architecture

One account preserved by Yarrow describes a particularly elaborate ossuary: "a large structure was built around this vast ossuary to preserve it from profanation" (020-005). This protective architecture suggests that ossuaries were not merely functional repositories but sacred structures whose inviolability was of paramount cultural importance. The construction of protective enclosures around ossuaries parallels the building of burial mounds and other monumental mortuary architecture across North America.

## Cultural Significance

The ossuary represented more than a practical solution to the accumulation of human remains. It embodied a philosophy of death in which the individual, after passing through the liminal stage of decomposition and the ritual of bone-picking, was absorbed into the collective body of the ancestors. The periodic ceremonies at the ossuary — including the deposition of newly cleaned bones and communal feasting — reinforced tribal identity and the continuity of the living community with its dead.

This practice contrasts with the [[navajo|Navajo]] custom of dwelling abandonment, which emphasized radical separation from the dead, and with [[cremation]], which destroyed the physical remains entirely. The ossuary tradition thus occupied a distinctive position in the spectrum of North American mortuary customs, reflecting values of collective identity and ancestral continuity.
