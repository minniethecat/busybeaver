Vancouver Island, located off the Pacific coast of British Columbia, was the setting for several detailed early observations of Indigenous burial customs, recorded during George Vancouver's expedition (1791–1795) and by later residents.

## Burial Practices on Vancouver Island

### Esquimalt Harbor Graves

At Esquimalt Harbor, near present-day Victoria, an English resident reported graves marked by large stones at each corner, the interior space filled with rubbish. The origin of these graves was unknown to the Indigenous inhabitants at the time of the account, suggesting considerable antiquity (016-009).

### Vancouver's Observations

Vancouver's narrative records that a severe epidemic had recently swept through the region, evidenced by the quantity of human remains left exposed and uncared for at the time of his visit. The local inhabitants, fearing contagion, had reportedly burned a house in which the occupants had perished — a practice frequently observed among the Indigenous peoples of the Northwest Coast, who almost invariably abandoned places where sickness had prevailed, often destroying the dwelling as well (016-010).

### Penn Cove Sepulchers

At Penn Cove, Mr. Whidbey — one of Vancouver's officers — noted several sepulchers shaped like sentry-boxes. Some stood open, containing the skeletons of many young children tied up in baskets. Smaller bones of adults were also present, though no limb bones were found, leading to speculation that these had been repurposed by the living inhabitants for implements such as arrow points or spear tips. Yarrow, however, dismisses this interpretation as inconsistent with Indigenous character, suggesting the adult bones had been removed and reburied elsewhere (016-011).

### Puget Sound Burial Customs

The broader Puget Sound region exhibited a variety of burial practices. The Makah of Cape Flattery covered graves with rudely constructed board boxes, while elsewhere bodies were placed on elevated scaffolds. Those dwelling near water generally deposited the dead in canoes, and those farther inland buried them in the ground. Most graves were surrounded with strips of cloth, blankets, and other articles of property (016-009).
