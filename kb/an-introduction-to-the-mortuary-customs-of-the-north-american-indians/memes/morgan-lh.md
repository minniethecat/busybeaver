# Lewis Henry Morgan

## Overview

Lewis Henry Morgan (1818–1881) was an American anthropologist and social theorist whose pioneering ethnography *League of the Ho-de-no-sau-nee, or [[iroquois|Iroquois]]* (1851) established him as the foremost nineteenth-century authority on [[iroquois|Iroquois]] social organization and material culture. His fieldwork among the Seneca and other [[iroquois|Iroquois]] nations, conducted over many years of close association and culminating in his formal adoption by the Seneca Hawk clan, gave his descriptions a firsthand authority that few contemporary writers on Native American customs could match. Yarrow drew on Morgan's *League of the [[iroquois|Iroquois]]* at two critical junctures in the 1880 monograph: the discussion of scaffold burial among the [[iroquois|Iroquois]], and the account of funeral games as a component of mortuary ceremonialism (015-009)(021-005).

## Contributions

Morgan's description of [[iroquois|Iroquois]] scaffold burial, quoted directly by Yarrow, documented the distinctive practice of elevating the dead upon a bark scaffold within the deceased's own dwelling, where the body remained for an extended period before final [[inhumation|interment]]. Morgan explained that the [[iroquois|Iroquois]] constructed a scaffold of poles laid across the beams of the lodge, upon which the corps was placed wrapped in blankets and covered with bark. The body typically remained in this position for several days or even weeks while mourners visited and funeral preparations were completed, after which the remains were removed to a communal burial ground. This practice — combining elements of exposure, domestic proximity to the deceased, and delayed secondary burial — exemplified the complex temporality of [[iroquois|Iroquois]] mortuary ritual, in which the physical and social dissolution of the corpse were carefully staged and prolonged (015-009).

Morgan also provided Yarrow with the principal account of the funeral games that accompanied [[iroquois|Iroquois]] mortuary ceremonies. He described gymnastic exercises — dignified, he noted, with the name of funeral games — that were common to many [[iroquois|Iroquois]] nations and that were performed upon the occasion of a death or funeral. These athletic contests, which Morgan observed among the Seneca, served to honor the deceased through the display of physical prowess and to channel the emotional and social energies released by a death into structured communal activity. Morgan's account was notable for recognizing that the games were not merely diversions but integral components of the mortuary complex, functioning alongside the more obviously ritual elements of [[mourning|wailing]], oration, and grave preparation to constitute a complete ceremonial response to death (021-005).

Beyond these specific citations, Morgan's work influenced Yarrow's study through its demonstration that mortuary customs were embedded within and intelligible only in relation to the broader structures of kinship, clan organization, and political authority that Morgan had famously analyzed. The [[iroquois|Iroquois]] distinction between the burial treatment accorded to ordinary persons and that reserved for sachems and chiefs, which Morgan documented, reflected the hierarchical dimensions of mortuary practice that Yarrow sought to identify across all the tribes covered in the monograph.
