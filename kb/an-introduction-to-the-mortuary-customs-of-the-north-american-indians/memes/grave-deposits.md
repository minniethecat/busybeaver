# Grave Deposits

**Grave deposits** — objects, weapons, ornaments, and provisions placed with the deceased at burial — were a near-universal feature of North American Indian mortuary practice. The custom reflected the widespread belief that the dead continued to require the implements of daily life in the spirit world.

## Weapons and Tools

Warriors were interred with their arms. The [[creek|Muscogulges]] buried the dead in a sitting posture, "depositing with him his gun, tomahawk, pipe, and such other matters as he had the greatest value for in his lifetime" [002-003]. The Yarrow questionnaire specifically asked: "What articles are deposited with the corpse?  
What ceramics are placed in the grave?" [022-004]. When a husband died, "the gun, bow and arrows, hatchet and knife are deposited in the grave" [019-010].

Archaeological excavation confirmed the pattern. At one site, a skeleton in sitting posture had "a tomahawk which had been about 7 inches in length" resting beside the right hand [004-040]. At another, excavators recovered "tomahawks, maxillary bones, some of the teeth, beads, and other bones" [004-047]. The belief underlying weapon deposits was explicitly recorded: "the warrior had an idea that after he arose from the dead he would need, in the 'hunting-grounds beyond,' his bow and arrow, war-hatchet, and scalping-knife" [004-044].

## Ornaments and Personal Effects

Beads, clothing, and vessels accompanied the dead across many regions. One burial contained "beads, baskets, clothing, everything owned by the deceased" [002-008]. The [[gosh-ute|Gosi-Ute]] deposited their dead in a rock fissure "surrounded with different articles, until it was quite filled up" [005-003]. In [[ute|Ute]] practice, gender determined the deposit: "no weapons are deposited with the corpse" of a female [005-008]. A child's [[creek|Creek]] grave yielded "a small sum of silver" [013-005].

## Food Offerings

"Supplying food to the dead is a custom which is known to be of great antiquity," Yarrow observed, noting it was practiced "as among the ancient [[roman|Roman]]s" [014-022]. The [[dakota|Dakota]] widow placed "the best and largest proportion" of her limited food "upon the grave of her husband" [019-005]. At a Grand Medicine funeral feast, "an allowance was served up for the deceased out of every article of which it consisted" [020-003]. Relatives of the deceased returned annually to "bring provisions to the place where their fathers are buried" [001-031].

## Regional and Social Variation

The richness of deposits varied with rank. On the northwest coast, "persons of no consideration and slaves being buried with very little care or respect" [016-010]. In contrast, a wealthy [[florida|Florida]] chieftain had "two gold twenties" placed in his mouth upon the funeral pyre [010-018]. At a [[huron|Huron]] "feast of the dead," two thousand attendees "offered 1,200 presents at the common tomb, in testimony of their grief" [020-005] — demonstrating that deposits functioned both as provisions for the deceased and as communal expressions of [[mourning]].
