## Overview

Tennessee, situated in the southeastern United States, features prominently in Yarrow's 1880 survey of North American Indian mortuary customs. The Cumberland Valley and the Little Tennessee River basin contain extensive aboriginal burial sites, including distinctive stone graves and mound-builder cemeteries.

## Burial Sites & Practices

The stone graves of Tennessee are among the most thoroughly documented burial types in Yarrow's work. Joseph [[jones|Jones]] investigated "many burying grounds in West Tennessee with regular graves," describing how the inhabitants "dug through the soil" to inter their dead (003-002). [[fiske-moses|Moses Fiske]] provided additional accounts, noting that "these ancient cemeteries are exceedingly numerous" across the state (003-004).

Stone cists and mound burials were also prevalent. Joseph LeConte reported that "a number of cists have been found in Tennessee," describing stone-lined burial chambers of considerable size (003-001). [[atwater-caleb|Caleb Atwater]] documented mound-builder graves in the Cumberland Valley, where "skeletons were found in a sitting posture" accompanied by pottery and stone implements (004-008). [[cremation|Cremation]] is also noted: "[[cremation]] is by no means uncommon in Tennessee, as they are frequently mentioned by the pioneers" (004-010).

## Associated Tribes

- [[cherokee]] — The [[cherokee|Cherokees]] of Tennessee, described by Timberlake, "seldom bury the dead, but throw them into the river" (017-001). The Little Tennessee River valley was a major [[cherokee|Cherokee]] settlement area.
