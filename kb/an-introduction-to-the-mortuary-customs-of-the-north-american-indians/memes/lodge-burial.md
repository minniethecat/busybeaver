# Lodge Burial

Lodge burial is a form of above-ground sepulture in which the deceased is placed within a lodge, tipi, or similar dwelling structure. It was practiced by several Native American tribes, including the [[sioux|Sioux]], [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]], [[crows|Crows]], and [[navajo|Navajos]], though it was by no means common. [012-001]

## Sioux Lodge Burial

One of the most detailed accounts of lodge burial was provided by Captain Howard Stansbury during his exploration of the [[utah|Great Salt Lake Valley]] in 1852. Stansbury encountered five lodges pitched upon the open prairie, within which were found the bodies of nine [[sioux|Sioux]] laid out upon the ground. The deceased were wrapped in their buffalo-skin robes, with their saddles, spears, camp-kettles, and all their accoutrements piled up around them. Some of the lodges contained only one body each, while others contained several. [012-001] [012-002]

The practice may have been connected to epidemic disease — Stansbury's account was recorded during a cholera outbreak, and the use of lodges for burial may have been partly motivated by the dread of reusing lodges that had served as homes for those afflicted. However, the same practice was documented among the same tribe and others under non-epidemic circumstances. [012-003]

## Crow Lodge Burial

Among the [[crows|Crows]], the burial of Chief Long Horse was documented by Colonel Norris, superintendent of Yellowstone National Park, who was an eye-witness in 1876. The lodge poles of the burial structure enclosed an oblong circle approximately 18 by 22 feet at the base, converging to a point at least 30 feet high. The structure was covered with buffalo hides dressed without hair — except for a part of the tail switch, which floated outside, mingled with human scalps. The different skins were neatly fitted and sewed together with sinew, and all were painted in seven alternate horizontal stripes of brown and yellow. [012-004] [012-005]

## Other Tribes

The [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]] and [[navajo|Navajos]] also practiced lodge burial. Among the Indians of Bellingham Bay, according to Dr. [[hammond-jf|Hammond]], U.S.A., the dead were placed in carved wooden sarcophagi, with these enclosed within a rectangular tent of some white material. [012-006]

## Related Practices

A related custom was documented among certain Indians of Costa Rica by [[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]]: when a death occurred, the body was deposited in a small hut constructed of plaited palm reeds, where it was preserved for three years. Food was supplied to the deceased, and on each anniversary of the death the body was redressed and attended to amid ceremonies. A similar custom reportedly prevailed in Demerara. [012-007]

## Comparison

Lodge burial occupies an intermediate position among [[scaffold-burial|above-ground burial]] methods. Like scaffold and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]], it kept the body elevated and away from scavengers, but unlike those methods it employed an existing or purpose-built enclosed structure. The practice appears to have been largely restricted to the Plains and Rocky Mountain regions, where the lodge was the standard dwelling form. [012-003] [012-006]
