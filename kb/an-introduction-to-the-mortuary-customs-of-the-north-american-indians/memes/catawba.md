# Catawba

## Overview

The Catawba were the dominant indigenous nation of the [[carolina|Carolina]] Piedmont, residing along the Catawba River near the present-day border of North and [[carolina|South Carolina]]. Though not mentioned by name in Yarrow (1880), their mortuary customs may be inferred from the survey's account of the [[carolina|Carolina]] tribes generally, particularly the detailed description of burial ceremonialism among the peoples of the region that the Catawba dominated. As Yarrow's source material on the [[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree]] and [[santee|Santee]] — Siouan-speaking [[carolina|Carolina]] peoples closely related to the Catawba — demonstrates, the mortuary practices of the [[carolina|Carolina]] Siouans were among the most elaborate in eastern North America.

## Mortuary Customs

### Burial Ceremonialism Among the Carolina Tribes

Yarrow records that among the [[carolina|Carolina]] tribes, the burial of the dead was accompanied with special ceremonies, the expense and formality of which corresponded precisely to the rank of the deceased. The corpse was first placed in a cane bundle and deposited in an outhouse constructed for that purpose, where it remained for a day and a night, guarded and mourned over by the nearest relatives with disheveled hair. Those appointed to officiate at the funeral then went into the town and, from the backs of the first young men they encountered, stripped such blankets and matchcoats as they deemed suitable. In these the dead body was wrapped and covered with two or three mats made of rushes or cane. The coffin itself was constructed of woven reeds or hollow canes tied fast at both ends — an artifact of perishable material that accords with the archaeological record of the [[carolina|Carolina]] Piedmont, where organic burial containers have rarely survived (001-005).

When all was prepared for the [[inhumation|interment]], the corpse was carried from the outhouse into an orchard of peach-trees and deposited in another bundle. The family and tribe of the deceased, together with invited guests, seated themselves upon mats. The medicine man, or conjurer, having enjoined silence upon the assembly, then delivered a funeral oration in which he recounted the exploits of the deceased — valor, skill, love of country, property, and influence — alluded to the void created by the death, and counseled those who remained to supply the deceased's place by following in his footsteps. He concluded his address by picturing the happiness the departed would enjoy in the land of spirits and by an allusion to the prominent traditions of his tribe (001-005).

### Congaree and Santee Partial Embalmment

The [[congaree-santee-indians|Congaree]] and [[santee|Santee]] — Siouan-speaking peoples of the [[carolina|South Carolina]] lowcountry, linguistically and culturally related to the Catawba — practiced a distinctive process of partial embalmment. According to Lawson, as excerpted by [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]] and quoted by Yarrow, the method involved the careful preparation of the corpse prior to [[inhumation|interment]] in boxes supported above the ground by crotched sticks. This practice of aerial deposition combined with preservation treatment represents a notable variant within the broader Southeastern mortuary complex (006-014).

### Urn-Burial in the Wateree Valley

In the mounds on the Wateree River, near Camden, [[carolina|South Carolina]] — within the traditional territory of the Catawba and their predecessors — Dr. Blanding discovered ranges of earthenware vases, one above the other, filled with human remains. In some instances where the mouth of the vase was too small to admit the intact skull, the cranium was placed face downward in the opening, functioning as a cover for the vessel. The presence of urn-burial in the Wateree mounds suggests a material complexity to pre-contact Catawba mortuary practice that extended well beyond the historic-period customs described by eighteenth-century observers (007-002).

## Social Organization and Mortuary Hierarchy

The stark class distinction in [[carolina|Carolina]] burial ceremonialism — rank determining the scale and expense of funeral rites — is consistent with what is known of Catawba social organization. The Catawba maintained a stratified society with hereditary chiefs whose authority extended across multiple villages. The elaborate funeral orations, the stripping of garments from passersby to wrap the deceased, and the construction of specialized outhouses for the lying-in-state all point to a mortuary complex in which chiefly funerals were major communal events involving the redistribution of property and the public rehearsal of tribal history through the medicine man's oration.

## Later History

The Catawba population was devastated by smallpox epidemics in the eighteenth century, reducing them from an estimated five thousand to fewer than five hundred by the time of the American Revolution. Their subsequent displacement from much of their traditional territory means that the mortuary customs documented by Yarrow's sources — Lawson, [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]], and Blanding — represent practices that had already been severely disrupted by demographic collapse and colonization. The Wateree River mounds described by Dr. Blanding belong to an earlier archaeological horizon, attesting to the antiquity and depth of mortuary tradition in the Catawba homeland.

## Historical Sources

The principal sources on Catawba and [[carolina|Carolina]] tribe mortuary customs in Yarrow are [[lawson-john|John Lawson]]'s early eighteenth-century account of the [[carolina|Carolina]] Indians (transmitted via [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]]'s *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States*, 1854), and Dr. Blanding's archaeological observations on the Wateree River mounds. The funeral oration account derives from an unspecified eighteenth-century observer whose description Yarrow incorporated into his introductory survey of disposal methods.
