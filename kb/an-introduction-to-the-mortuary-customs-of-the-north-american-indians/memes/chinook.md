# Chinook

## Overview

The Chinook people inhabited the lower [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] region of the Pacific Northwest, in present-day [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington. Renowned as traders and fishermen, their mortuary customs included several distinctive practices: the aquatic disposal of deceased slaves, elaborate canoe burials for free persons, and a unique form of cradle-burial for infants who died in infancy. Yarrow (1880) drew on accounts from [[catlin-george|Catlin]] and other early observers to document these practices (016-005)(017-002).

## Mortuary Customs

### Aquatic Burial of Slaves
The Chinook, along with the Indians of [[nootka|Nootka]] Sound, were reported to dispose of their dead slaves by throwing them into the water. This practice, also attested among the [[Itzas]] of Guatemala and other circum-Pacific peoples, reflected the low social status of slaves, whose remains were not accorded the elaborate mortuary treatment given to free members of the tribe. Yarrow treated aquatic burial as rare among North American tribes overall, making the Chinook case notable (017-002)(017-003).

### Canoe Burial
For free Chinook, canoe burial was the primary mortuary practice. The deceased was placed in a canoe, often elevated on poles or placed in trees along the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]]. The canoe served both as a coffin and as transport to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] — a reflection of the Chinook's riverine and maritime culture. Grave goods, including fishing equipment, personal ornaments, and provisions, accompanied the dead (016-003)(016-005).

### Cradle-Burial of Infants
[[catlin-george|George Catlin]], in his *History of the North American Indians* (1844), described a distinctive Chinook practice for infants who died while still in the cradle-board: the child was left in its cradle, which was then fastened to a tree branch or placed on a scaffold. The cradle, with its strap that passed over the mother's forehead, became the infant's coffin. This practice was unique among the tribes [[catlin-george|Catlin]] documented (017-003)(014-001).

## Historical Sources

Primary documentation comes from [[catlin-george|George Catlin]]'s *History of the North American Indians* (1844, Vol. II), which provides the most detailed account of Chinook cradle-burial. [[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]]'s works on the native races of the Pacific States added context on the broader pattern of aquatic disposal among Pacific coast tribes. Yarrow cross-referenced these sources with reports from military officers and Indian agents stationed in the [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington territories (017-002)(017-003).
