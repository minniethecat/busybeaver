The charnel house — a dedicated structure for housing human skeletal remains — appears in multiple Indigenous North American burial traditions, from the ossuaries of the Eastern Woodlands to the bone-houses documented by early European explorers. These structures embody a distinctive approach to death and remembrance: rather than concealing the dead permanently, charnel houses kept ancestral remains accessible and visible, integrating them into the ongoing life of the community.

## Forms and Functions

Charnel houses served as repositories for the cleaned bones of the deceased, typically after a period of temporary burial or scaffold exposure during which the flesh separated from the skeleton. The structures varied enormously in scale: some were small chests of cane or splints used for individual distinguished persons, while others were large communal buildings housing the remains of entire communities accumulated over many generations.

The practice was closely tied to the widespread custom of periodic bone-cleaning and collective reburial. At intervals of eight to ten years, communities would gather all who had died during the intervening period, clean their bones, and deposit them in the charnel house — an event of profound ritual and social significance.

> Every eight or ten years, the tribes would collect and clean the osseous remains of those of its number who had died in the intervening time, and inter them in one common sepulcher, lined with choice furs, and marked with a mound of wood, stone, or earth. (015-013)

## Historical Descriptions

### De Soto's Expedition

The earliest European descriptions of Indigenous charnel houses in North America come from the chroniclers of Hernando de Soto's expedition (1539–1542). Traveling through the Southeast, the expedition repeatedly encountered structures that served as communal mortuary repositories — buildings filled with the bones of the dead, sometimes arranged in patterns or accompanied by grave goods.

> The historians of De Soto's expedition described charnel-houses among the Indigenous nations of the Southeast, structures that served as repositories for the osseous remains of the dead after the periodic bone-collection ceremonies. (015-013)

These accounts, though filtered through European cultural assumptions, provide invaluable evidence of mortuary practices that predated significant European contact and disruption.

### James Adair's "Arks"

The eighteenth-century trader and historian James Adair, who lived among the Cherokee and Chickasaw for forty years, interpreted Southeastern charnel houses through a biblical lens. Adair, who sought to trace Indigenous descent from the ancient Israelites, characterized these structures as "arks" — suggesting a parallel with the Ark of the Covenant. While Adair's diffusionist theory has been rejected by modern scholarship, his detailed descriptions of the structures themselves remain valuable ethnographic records.

### Eastern Woodlands

East of the Mississippi River, the custom of periodic bone collection and communal reburial was observed by nearly every nation. The resulting tumuli spread across the landscape — immense mounds containing the remains of generations.

> East of the Mississippi River, nearly every nation observed the custom of collecting the remains of those who had died during the intervening period and interring them in a common sepulcher. The practice was widely distributed and appears to have been of considerable antiquity. (015-013)

These communal repositories were often lined with choice furs and marked with mounds, creating enduring monuments that were both sacred sites and territorial markers.

### Central American Parallels

The practice of maintaining charnel houses extended well beyond the present-day United States. In Central America, similar customs were documented by early Spanish writers and confirmed by surviving monuments and archaeological evidence, suggesting a continuity of mortuary practice that spanned much of the Americas.

> In Central America, similar practices of collective bone curation were documented by early writers and confirmed by surviving monuments, indicating the wide geographic distribution of this custom. (015-013)

## Distinctions and Terminology

Precise terminology is essential when discussing Indigenous mortuary structures, as distinct forms served different functions:

- **Charnel house**: An above-ground structure specifically built for the long-term curation of cleaned skeletal remains
- **Ossuary**: A pit, vault, or underground chamber for bone storage, distinct from the above-ground charnel house
- **Bone-house**: A term used by the Choctaw and other Southeastern peoples for their specific mortuary structures, which may have differed in construction and ritual context from charnel houses of other regions
- **Burial mound**: An earthen structure covering burials or bone deposits, which might or might not contain a charnel house or ossuary beneath it

## Yarrow's Inquiry

In his 1880 circular of inquiry distributed to military officers, physicians, and Indian agents, U.S. Army surgeon H. C. Yarrow specifically requested information on the existence and use of charnel houses among Indigenous peoples, recognizing their significance as a distinctive mortuary form worthy of systematic documentation.

> The circular specifically requested information on whether charnel houses existed or had been used among any given people, indicating the importance Yarrow attached to this distinctive mortuary form. (022-005)
