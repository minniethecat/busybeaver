# Embalming

## Overview

Embalming — the artificial preservation of human remains — was practiced by several North American Indian tribes through techniques distinct from the natural [[mummification]] that occurred in arid environments. Yarrow (1880) devoted attention to "the subject of mummifying or embalming the dead" as a distinct category of mortuary practice, documenting methods that ranged from the application of vegetal preparations to elaborate multi-stage preservation processes (006-001).

## Methods

### Vegetal Embalming

One technique documented by Yarrow involved the use of plant materials for preservation. A correspondent described a process of "seasoning or embalming it with a small root beaten to a pulp" after drying the body in the sun (006-015). This combination of solar [[mummification|desiccation]] and botanical treatment represented a deliberate technological approach to body preservation, distinct from the accidental [[mummification|desiccation]] that occurred in dry cave burials.

### Aleutian Islanders' Embalming

Among the most detailed accounts preserved by Yarrow is the embalming practice of the [[aleut|Aleutian Islanders]]. He records that they practiced "embalming their dead" through a distinctive process that involved evisceration, drying, and the application of preservative substances (006-040). This practice, developed in the maritime environment of the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian archipelago, represented one of the most technically sophisticated preservation traditions documented in the monograph.

## Comparison with Mummification

Embalming differed from natural mummification in its intentionality. While the arid environments of [[utah|Utah]] and the American Southwest produced naturally desiccated remains, embalming represented a conscious technological intervention — the deliberate application of knowledge and materials to arrest decomposition. The distinction between embalming and [[mummification]] was not always clear in Yarrow's sources, as both practices aimed at the preservation of the body, but the use of applied substances (roots, barks, oils) characterized embalming as a more active process.

## Regional Distribution

Embalming practices were most commonly documented among coastal and Southern tribes. The combination of warm, humid climates (which accelerated decomposition) and cultural values favoring body preservation may have driven the development of embalming techniques in these regions. The practice was less commonly reported among Plains tribes, where scaffold exposure and subsequent earth burial followed a different mortuary logic.
