## Overview

Cairn burial, also referred to as rock burial, is a method of interment in which stones or rocks are piled over the deceased, forming a protective mound or cairn above the grave. This practice has prevailed among certain Native American tribes, particularly in regions where stone was abundant and where the terrain made other forms of burial impractical. Yarrow identifies cairn or rock burial as a distinct mode of sepulture, noting its prevalence among the tribes living in the Rocky Mountains and the Sierra Nevadas (009-001).

## Construction Method

Yarrow visited a rock cemetery in middle Utah in the summer of 1872, which had been used for a period not exceeding fifteen or twenty years. The site was situated at the bottom of a rock slide, upon the side of an almost inaccessible mountain, in a position so carefully chosen for concealment that it would have been almost impossible to find it without a guide. Several of the graves were opened and found to have been constructed in the following manner: A number of bowlders had been removed from the bed of the slide until a sufficient cavity had been obtained; this was lined with skins, the corpse placed therein, with weapons, ornaments, etc., and covered over with saplings of the mountain aspen; on top of these the removed bowlders were piled, forming a huge cairn, which appeared large enough to have marked the last resting place of an elephant (009-002).

## Grave Goods and Ritual

In the immediate vicinity of the graves were scattered the osseous remains of a number of horses which had been sacrificed no doubt during the funeral ceremonies (009-002). In one of the graves, said to contain the body of a chief, in addition to a number of articles useful and ornamental, were found parts of the skeleton of a boy, and tradition states that a captive boy was buried alive at this place (009-002).

## Tribal Distribution

The practice of cairn burial is documented among tribes of the Great Basin region, particularly in Utah. Yarrow notes the discovery of cairns in Utah Territory, where large stones were piled over burial sites (024-071). The cairns were of considerable size, in some cases large enough to contain multiple interments (009-002). The Navajo also practiced a related custom of covering bodies with stones or brush where they died, or removing the body to a cleft in the rocks and piling stones around it (002-006).

## Historical Accounts

Yarrow describes cairn or rock burial as a distinct mode of sepulture, noting that in some instances stones were piled over the corpse, forming what appeared to be a substantial cairn (009-001). In one account, weapons and ornaments were placed with the deceased before the stones were piled over the grave, forming a structure large enough to cover the entire burial site (009-002). The resulting cairns served both as a protective covering and as a visible marker of the burial place.

## Comparative Context

In connection with this mode of burial it may be said that the ancient Balearic Islanders covered their dead with a heap of stones, but this ceremony was preceded by an operation which consisted in cutting the body in small pieces and collecting in a pot (009-003).
