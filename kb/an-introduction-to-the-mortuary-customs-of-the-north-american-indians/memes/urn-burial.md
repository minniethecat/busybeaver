# Urn-Burial

## Overview

Urn-burial — the placement of human remains in ceramic vessels — was practiced by the [[mound-builders]] of North America, particularly in the Southern States. While Foster (1873) documented several notable urn-burial sites, Yarrow expressed reservation about the extent of this practice, suggesting it was more commonly a form of secondary [[inhumation|interment]] rather than primary burial among North American tribes (007-001)(007-002).

## Tribal Distribution

### Mound-Builders of the Southeast
On the Wateree River near Camden, [[carolina|South Carolina]], Dr. Blanding discovered ranges of vases, one above the other, filled with human remains. In some instances, when the vase mouth was small, the skull was placed face downward in the opening, forming a sort of cover. An entire cemetery of urn-burials was discovered on Saint Catherine's Island, on the coast of [[georgia|Georgia]] (007-001)(007-003).

### Mississippi Valley
Professor Swallow recovered a human skull enclosed in an earthen jar from a mound at New Madrid, [[missouri|Missouri]] — the jar's lip was too small to permit extraction, indicating the clay must have been molded around the head after death (007-002).

### Southwestern and California Tribes
In the graves of ancient [[california|California]] peoples and [[new-mexico|New Mexico]], bones were found deposited in ollas (earthen jars). Yarrow interpreted these as secondary [[inhumation|interment]]s: bones dug up during new burials were collected and placed in pots as convenient receptacles, or bodies were allowed to decay before the bones were gathered, placed in urns, and reinterred (007-005)(007-006).

## Variations

The practice varied from primary urn-burial (body placed in urn shortly after death) to secondary urn-burial (bones collected after decomposition). The Chaldeans of the ancient Near East practiced a similar mode, where funeral jars contained craniums too large to pass through the openings — a striking parallel noted by Rawlinson in his edition of Herodotus (007-002).

## Historical Accounts

[[foreman-e|Dr. E. Foreman]] of the Smithsonian Institution provided additional documentation of urn-burial sites. Foster's *Pre-Historic Races* (1873) remains the primary nineteenth-century source on the subject, though Yarrow's own fieldwork in [[california|California]] and [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] graves led him to question whether urn-burial was ever a widespread primary practice in North America, as opposed to a convenient method for secondary deposition of disturbed remains (007-003)(007-005).

## Related Practices

- [[inhumation]] — The most common alternative, simple earth burial
- [[cremation]] — Burning of the dead, sometimes followed by urn deposition of ashes
- [[mound-burial]] — Earthen mounds often contained urn-burials
- [[cave-burial]] — Contrasting practice using natural cavities
