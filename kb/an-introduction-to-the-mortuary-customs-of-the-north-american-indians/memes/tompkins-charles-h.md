# Charles H. Tompkins

## Overview

General Charles H. Tompkins (1830–1915) served as deputy quartermaster-general of the United States Army, a position that brought him into contact with Native American communities across the Western territories. His contribution to Yarrow's monograph consists of a single but uniquely valuable account: the description of an [[achomawi|Achomawi]] (Pitt River) burial he personally witnessed, in which the decapitated body was interred upright while the severed head was separately cremated atop a pyre of fagots — a practice combining inhumation and [[cremation]] in a single mortuary sequence that Yarrow considered the only well-authenticated case of its kind on record (011-011).

## Contributions

Tompkins's eyewitness account of [[achomawi|Achomawi]] burial is among the most detailed and procedurally complete in the Yarrow corpus. He described how the grave was dug to sufficient depth to admit the body in a standing position, with the shoulders nearly even with the ground; the head was cut off before [[inhumation|interment]]. Hunting equipment, bead-work, and quantities of dried food were placed with the body, after which the grave was filled, covering the headless torso. A bundle of fagots was then brought by members of the tribe and placed directly on the grave; the severed head was positioned atop the pile, the wood was fired, and the head was consumed to ashes. Tompkins recorded that the female relatives of the deceased then dipped their fingers in the ashes of the cremated head and drew three marks on their right cheeks, constituting the [[mourning]] garb whose duration lasted until the black substance wore off. During the [[cremation|burning]] of the head, older women of the tribe sat in a large circle while younger girls stood within it, swaying and singing a mournful ditty (011-011).

Tompkins further noted the pronounced gender differentiation in [[achomawi|Achomawi]] burial customs: while males of distinction received the upright [[inhumation|interment]] with cephalic [[cremation]] he had witnessed, females were wrapped in skins and deposited in caves with their valuables and, in some cases, food placed in their mouths — occasionally including money to pay for sustenance in the spirit land. This dual documentation of gendered mortuary practice within a single community, unusually precise in its procedural detail, makes Tompkins's account a benchmark of descriptive rigor in nineteenth-century American ethnology (011-011).

## Accounts Provided

Tompkins's sole published contribution to the Yarrow corpus covers [[achomawi|Achomawi]] (Pitt River) differential burial: upright male inhumation with cephalic [[cremation]] and community ritual performance, contrasted with female cave deposition. The account is notable for its attention to the spatial organization of ritual participants and the precise sequencing of mortuary acts — qualities that reflect the observational discipline of a senior military officer.
