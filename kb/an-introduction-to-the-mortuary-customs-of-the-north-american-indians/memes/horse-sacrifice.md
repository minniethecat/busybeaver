## Overview

The [[sacrifice]] of horses at funerals was a widely documented custom among the equestrian tribes of the North American Plains, Great Basin, and Plateau regions. Upon the death of a warrior or chief, his favorite horses — and in some cases all his horses and cattle — were killed at the graveside, driven by the belief that the animals would serve their master in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. Yarrow, who "frequently conversed with Indians upon this subject," recorded that "they have invariably informed him that when horses were slain great care was taken to select the poorest of the band" (694-1). The practice was noted to be "by no means peculiar to our Indians," with parallels among the ancient [[roman|Roman]]s and possibly extending back to the Reindeer period in Europe, where horse bones were exhumed from Paleolithic graves at Solutré, France (694-2).

## Tribal Distribution

The custom was most prevalent among the mounted tribes of the Plains and Intermountain West. The [[wichita|Wichita]] of the southern Plains killed horses at the grave immediately after burial (188-1). The [[pima|Pima]] of the Gila River region killed the deceased's horses and cattle, cooking the meat "as a repast for the mourners" (218-1). The [[ute|Ute]] of the Rocky Mountain region shot the deceased's horses and cattle as part of the destruction of all personal property, a duty assigned to the men of the tribe (387-1). The [[dakota|Dakota]] ([[sioux|Sioux]]) customarily slew the favorite pony and placed its body beneath the burial scaffold (688-1). Among the [[yakama|Yakama]] of the Columbia Plateau, the practice of killing horses over graves was described as "a custom now falling into disuse in consequence of the teachings of the whites" (770-1).

## Variations

**The [[sioux|Sioux]] Chief's Burial on Horseback.** The most dramatic account of horse [[sacrifice]] in Yarrow's survey is the burial of a [[sioux|Sioux]] chief on the [[missouri|Missouri]] River, who requested to be interred "on the back of his favorite war-horse, which was to be buried alive under him." The noble white steed was led to the top of a grass-covered bluff overlooking the river. The chief was placed astride in full war dress, with his bow, shield, quiver, pipe, and medicine bag. Each warrior of the band painted his right hand with vermilion and stamped it on the horse's white sides. Turfs were then gradually built up around the horse's legs and body until both horse and rider were completely covered, "where all together have smouldered and remained undisturbed to the present day" (528-1).

**Cairn-Burial Horse Remains ([[utah|Utah]]).** At a rock-slide cemetery in central [[utah|Utah]] used by the [[ute|Ute]], excavators found "the osseous remains of a number of horses which had been [[sacrifice]]d no doubt during the funeral ceremonies" scattered around the graves. One grave, said to contain a chief, also held parts of a boy's skeleton — indicating that human [[sacrifice]] could accompany horse [[sacrifice]] in cases of high-status burial (534-1).

**[[scaffold-burial|Scaffold Burial]] Pony Sacrifice ([[dakota|Dakota]]).** Among the [[dakota|Dakota]], if the deceased was a chief or a warrior related to a chief, "it is not uncommon to slay his favorite pony and place the body beneath the scaffold, under the superstition, I suppose, that the horse goes with the man" (688-2). This practice complemented the aerial scaffold burial method common on the treeless Plains.

**[[pima|Pima]] Post-[[burial-feast|Burial Feast]].** The [[pima|Pima]] combined horse killing with communal consumption: immediately after burial, "the house and personal effects of the deceased are burned, and his horses and cattle killed, the meat being cooked as a repast for the mourners" (218-2). This doubled as property destruction and a funeral feast.

## Historical Accounts

The account of the [[sioux|Sioux]] chief's mounted burial was recorded by the Indian agent and fur traders who witnessed it, and the grave mound remained visible for many years afterward as a landmark on the [[missouri|Missouri]] River bluffs (528-2). Dr. [[hoffman-wj|Hoffman]], describing the [[pima|Pima]] in 1878, noted that open graves were prepared in advance for the sick, and if the patient recovered the grave was simply left open until needed — with horse and cattle killing following immediately upon burial (218-3). Yarrow observed that by the 1870s the practice was already declining among the [[yakama|Yakama]] "in consequence of the teachings of the whites," suggesting active suppression of the custom by missionaries or Indian agents (770-2).

## Related Practices

Horse [[sacrifice]] belongs to the broader category of [[sacrifice]] at Native American funerals, which also included [[slave-sacrifice]] and the destruction of personal property ([[property-destruction]]). The [[sioux|Sioux]] variant, in which the horse was buried alive with its rider, represents the most extreme form of the custom. Among the [[pima|Pima]], the practice intersected with [[burial-feast]] customs, as the meat of [[sacrifice]]d animals was cooked and consumed by mourners. The belief that horses accompanied the deceased connects the practice to [[soul-beliefs]] and the concept of the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] journey.
