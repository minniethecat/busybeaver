## Overview

Copper ornaments and objects have been found in numerous Native American burial sites, indicating a widespread practice of depositing copper items with the deceased. These items include copper beads, bracelets, chains, crosses, and ornamental disks, often associated with individuals of higher social standing.

## Archaeological Evidence

In the burial mounds of Ohio, ornaments of copper were among the articles found, including heads of spears and decorative pieces (004-023). In a Tennessee mound excavation, a piece of copper in the form of a cross was discovered on the breast of a skeleton (004-022). Among the mound-builders of the Southeast, copper hatchets and beads were among the items deposited with the dead (006-012).

A skull-cap ornamented with disks of copper, found at a Cheyenne scaffold burial and subsequently examined at the Army Medical Museum, was one of the more remarkable specimens. The cap contained the bones of the deceased and exemplified the practice of decorating burial wrappings with metallic ornaments among the Plains tribes (014-017).

## Tribal Distribution

Among the Virginia Indians, copper was ceremonially cast into the water as an offering during burial rites (006-011). The tribes of the eastern woodlands placed bracelets or chains of copper and pearl with their dead, which were described by early observers as part of the funerary equipment (006-009). At the Werowanco burial ceremony, the bodies of kings were prepared by bowelling, drying upon hurdles, and then having bracelets and chains of copper and pearl hung about their joints and neck; their inwards were stuffed with copper beads, hatchets, and such trash before the body was lapped in white skins and rolled in mats for winding-sheets (006-009).

## Spiritual Significance

The Virginia Indians believed that their Werowances and priests, after death, would go beyond the mountains towards the setting of the sun, where they would remain in the form of their Okee with heads painted red, finely trimmed with feathers, and possessing beads, hatchets, copper, and tobacco, doing nothing but dance and sing with all their predecessors. The common people, however, were believed simply to rot in their graves (006-012). The ceremonial casting of copper objects into rivers was associated with the sacredness of burial places: the savages dared not pass certain holy sites by boat without solemnly casting some piece of copper, white beads, or pocones into the river for fear their Okee should be offended (006-011).
