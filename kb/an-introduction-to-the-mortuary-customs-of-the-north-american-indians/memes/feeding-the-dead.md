## Overview

The practice of feeding the dead — placing food at graves, in burial structures, or directly with the corpse — was one of the most geographically widespread mortuary customs among the aboriginal peoples of North America. Rooted in the belief that the soul continues to require physical sustenance after death, this custom took diverse forms: daily food deposits during the [[mourning]] period, provisions placed in the grave at [[inhumation|interment]], food passed through openings in burial chests and [[burial-vaults|tombs]], and annual commemorative feasts at which food was shared between the living and the dead (694-1). Yarrow noted that the custom was "known to be of great antiquity," with parallels among the ancient [[roman|Roman]]s, Jews, Chinese, and Caribs (694-2).

## Tribal Distribution

The custom was documented across the continent, from the Northwest Coast to the Southeast and from the Great Plains to [[california|California]]. On the Northwest Coast, carved wooden burial chests resembling small houses featured an opening "through which food may be passed to the corpse" (645-1). On the upper [[missouri|Missouri]], the [[sioux|Sioux]] planted stakes at gravesides and kindled fires "for the soul of the deceased to come and warm itself by and to partake of the food deposited near it" (230-1). The [[natchez|Natchez]] of the lower [[mississippi|Mississippi]] left an opening at the head of their basket-work [[burial-vaults|tombs]] "through which food was presented to the deceased" (726-1). In [[california|California]], the Pitt River or [[achomawi|Achomawi Indians]] placed "quantities of food, consisting of dried fish, roots, herbs, &c." in the grave with the body, and in some cases "food being placed with them in their mouths" (619-1).

## Variations

**Scaffold Deposits (Plains).** Among the [[sioux|Sioux]] and other Plains tribes, scaffolds used for aerial burial were accompanied by "many little buckets and baskets hanging on the scaffolds" that "had contained food and drink for the dead." When questioned about whether the food was actually consumed, a [[sioux|Sioux]] woman replied that "the food and water is always gone" — though Yarrow dryly noted "the hundreds of ravens perched on the scaffolds and could account for what became of most of the food and water" (658-1).

**Food Chests (Northwest Coast).** The tribes of the Northwest Coast constructed elaborately carved wooden chests as burial receptacles, each with a small opening through which food could be passed to the corpse. These chests were supported on low platforms or rested directly on the ground and were designed to permit ongoing provisioning of the deceased (645-2).

**Grave-Hut Provisions (Mosquito Coast / Costa Rica).** Among the Indians of Costa Rica, the body was deposited in a small hut of plaited palm reeds and preserved there for three years, "food being supplied" and the body redressed on each anniversary of the death. [[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]] recorded that a similar custom prevailed in Demerara (641-1).

**The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] Feast of the Dead.** The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] held an elaborate general funeral in which bones were collected from scaffolds, stripped of flesh, deposited in coffins, and carried in procession to a common burial mound. The day concluded with "a festival which is called the feast of the dead" (730-1). This was a communal rather than individual provisioning ritual, blending food offering with secondary burial.

**Widow's Food Deposit (Great Lakes).** Among certain Great Lakes tribes, the young widow was observed to take "the best and largest proportion" of her meager food and deposit it "upon the grave of her husband," continuing this practice throughout her [[mourning]] period (827-1).

**The Last Cry Feast ([[choctaw|Choctaw]]).** At the conclusion of the [[mourning]] moon, the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] held the "last cry" ceremony: friends assembled with provisions for a "sumptuous feast," and after the meal two elders quietly filled the grave and removed the grave flags. The widow then joined in a dance that "not unfrequently is continued till morning," after which "the days of [[mourning]] are ended" (839-1).

## Historical Accounts

Benson, writing in 1860 about the [[choctaw|Choctaw]], provided one of the most detailed descriptions of the transition from food-offering to communal feast. After a full lunar month of daily grave-visits and food deposits by the widow, the community gathered for a shared meal that marked the formal end of [[mourning]] (839-2). The Caribs, described by early observers, placed the corpse on a seat in a deep grave and "for ten days they bring food, requesting the corpse to eat. Finally, being convinced that the dead will neither eat nor return to life, they throw the food on the head of the corpse and fill up the grave" (700-1). At Independence Rock on the upper [[missouri|Missouri]], travelers observed that the children of the deceased "come every year to bring provisions to the place where their fathers are buried," indicating that the practice could extend well beyond the immediate [[mourning]] period (230-2).

## Related Practices

The feeding of the dead is closely related to [[food-offerings]] placed in graves as grave goods. It connects to [[burial-feast]] customs, where food is shared communally in honor of the deceased. The practice also intersects with [[soul-beliefs]], as the underlying rationale was that the soul required physical sustenance. On the Northwest Coast, the practice was integrated with [[scaffold-burial]] and wooden chest burial. The custom of placing food in the mouth of the deceased ([[food-offerings]]) was practiced by the [[achomawi|Achomawi]] of [[california|California]].
