## Overview

The [[sacrifice]] of slaves at the funerals of chiefs and persons of high rank was documented among several North American Indian tribes, reaching its most extreme development on the Northwest Coast and among the chiefdoms of the Southeast. Slaves were killed "in proportion to the rank and wealth of the deceased" (782-1), and the custom was rooted in the belief that servants were needed to attend the deceased in the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. Yarrow recorded that "in later days, among our Indians, human [[sacrifice]]s have taken place to only a limited extent, but formerly many victims were immolated" (694-1). By the late nineteenth century, the practice had been "almost entirely given up" under the influence of white settlers and missionaries, though cases were recorded as late as 1853 (782-2).

## Tribal Distribution

The custom was most thoroughly documented among the tribes of the Northwest Coast. The [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] ([[chinook|Chinook]]) practiced slave killing at funerals, with a well-documented case from 1853 in which Toke, a [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] chief at Shoalwater Bay, attempted to kill a slave girl belonging to his daughter who had requested the [[sacrifice]] on her deathbed. The woman escaped and was found by white citizens "half starved"; when Toke tried to reclaim her, he was "soundly thrashed and warned against another attempt" (782-3). In the Southeast, at the funerals of chiefs of the [[florida|Florida]] and [[carolina|Carolina]] Indians, "all the male relatives and wives were slain," a practice connected to the matrilineal descent of chiefly authority (694-2). Among the [[ute|Ute]] of [[utah|Utah]], a captive boy was buried alive with a chief, his skeletal remains found alongside the chief's grave goods (534-1).

## Variations

**Northwest Coast Burial Alive.** Among the Northwest tribes, it was "quite common" to "kill or bury with the dead a living slave, who, failing to die within three days was strangled by another slave" (788-1). In some instances, slaves were "starved to death, or even tied to the dead body and left to perish thus horribly" (782-4). The Northwest practice declined rapidly under white pressure; by the 1870s it had been largely abandoned.

**Voluntary Sacrifice (Panama/Nata).** [[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]] recorded that among the caciques of Panama and Nata, "those of his concubines that loved him enough, those that he loved ardently and so appointed, as well as certain servants, killed themselves and were interred with him. This they did in order that they might wait upon him in the land of spirits" (788-2). This represents a variant in which [[sacrifice]] was nominally voluntary, though the social pressure to conform must have been immense.

**Southeastern Chiefdom Immolation.** At the funerals of chiefs among the [[florida|Florida]] and [[carolina|Carolina]] Indians, the scale of [[sacrifice]] was especially large: "all the male relatives and wives were slain." According to Gallatin, this practice was linked to the matrilineal inheritance of chiefly dignity — the chief and all members of his clan could only marry persons of an inferior clan, and these inferior-clan spouses were [[sacrifice]]d at his death (694-3).

**Captive Boy Sacrifice ([[ute|Ute]]).** At a rock-slide cemetery in central [[utah|Utah]], excavators found a chief's grave containing not only weapons and ornaments but "parts of the skeleton of a boy," with tribal tradition stating that "a captive boy was buried alive at this place" (534-2). This indicates that the practice extended to captive [[sacrifice]], not limited to owned slaves.

## Historical Accounts

The [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] case of 1853, documented by [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], represents one of the last recorded attempts at slave [[sacrifice]] on the Northwest Coast before the practice was suppressed. The slave girl's escape and the physical punishment of her would-be killer by white settlers marked the effective end of the custom in the region (782-5). The [[choctaw|Choctaw]], by contrast, were noted to have buried slaves "without ceremonies" — an indication that while slaves might not be [[sacrifice]]d, they were also denied the elaborate funeral rites accorded to free persons (839-1).

## Related Practices

Slave [[sacrifice]] at funerals is the most extreme form of [[sacrifice]] documented in North American Indian mortuary customs, standing alongside [[horse-sacrifice]] and [[property-destruction]] as means of equipping the deceased for the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. The practice connects to [[soul-beliefs]], since the underlying rationale was that servants would attend the deceased in the spirit world. The Northwest Coast variant, with its elaborate hierarchy of slave killing proportional to rank, reflects the highly stratified social structure of those societies. The [[choctaw|Choctaw]] practice of burying slaves without ceremony also relates to social customs surrounding [[mourning]].
