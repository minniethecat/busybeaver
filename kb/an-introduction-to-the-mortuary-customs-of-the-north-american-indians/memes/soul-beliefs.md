North American Indian tribes held diverse and sophisticated beliefs about the human **soul** — its nature, number, post-mortem fate, and continuing relationship with the living. These beliefs directly shaped burial customs, [[mourning]] practices, and the treatment of the dead.

## The Soul in Life and Death

Yarrow's questionnaire directed field observers to record native beliefs about the soul, asking: "What is the nature of the address to the deceased? Is the corpse addressed as if alive?" [022-004]. Among the tribes of the Plains, the funeral ceremonies were understood to span "three days during which the soul of the deceased is in danger from _O-mah-u_ or the devil" [001-020]. This reflects a widespread conception that the soul faced perils during the transition from life to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]].

The [[huron|Huron]] conceived of multiple souls. At their great "feast of the dead" witnessed by the Jesuit missionary [[brebeuf-p-de|P. de Brebeuf]], the [[secondary-burial|reburial]] of ancestral remains was understood as the moment when souls were finally released to travel to the spirit world [020-005]. The elaborate ceremony, attended by two thousand people, demonstrates the centrality of soul-beliefs to communal ritual life.

## The Journey to the Afterlife

The destination of the soul varied by tribe. The [[virginia|Virginia]] Algonquians "think that their Werowances and priests… when they are dead do go beyond the mountains towards the setting of the sun, and ever remain there in form of their Okee, with their heads painted red with oil and pocones, finely trimmed with feathers" [006-012]. This western-direction journey appears in numerous accounts.

The journey itself was often conceived as a perilous crossing. The Se-nel of [[california|California]] believed that after death "bad Indians return into coyotes. Others fall off a bridge which all souls must traverse, or are hooked off by a raging bull at the further end, while the good escape across" [010-020]. The [[dakota|Dakotas]] described a similar ordeal: "the spirits of the departed are compelled to cross an extremely attenuated greasy pole, which bridges over the chasm of the debatable land, and they require the fire to light them on their darksome journey. A righteous soul traverses the pole quicker than a wicked one, hence they regulate the number of nights for [[cremation|burning]] a light according to the character for goodness or the opposite which the departed possessed" [021-028].

The custom of maintaining fires on or near graves — documented across numerous tribes — was directly linked to these journey beliefs. "The Algonkins believed that the fire lighted nightly on the grave was to light the spirit on its journey" [021-027]. Other interpretations included purification of the soul and the driving away of demons; all three functions — illumination, purification, and protection — were likely combined in practice.

The [[sioux|Sioux]] "ghost gamble" or spirit-lodge ceremony was performed to honor and communicate with the recently dead [021-019]. Accounts of this practice describe the soul as retaining personal identity and the ability to interact with the living through ritual.

## The Soul's Avian Nature

A belief common to many tribes held that the soul was of the form and nature of a bird. Yarrow, synthesizing the ethnographic evidence, proposed that this belief explained the widespread practice of tree and scaffold burial:

> "If we come to inquire why the American aborigines placed the dead bodies of their relatives and friends in trees, or upon scaffolds resembling trees, instead of burying them in the ground, or burning them and preserving their ashes in urns, I think we can answer the inquiry by recollecting that most if not all the tribes of American Indians, as well as other nations of a higher civilization, believed that the human soul, spirit or immortal part, was of the form and nature of a bird, and as these birds have the power of flight, the belief that in placing the body in the tops of trees or on scaffolds the soul, on being freed from the body, could at once take its flight to the happy hunting grounds." [014-030]

This avian soul-belief provides a coherent rationale for one of the most distinctive features of North American mortuary practice. The elevation of the body — whether on a scaffold, in tree branches, or on raised platforms — was not merely practical (protection from scavengers) but cosmological: it assisted the bird-soul in its ascent to the spirit world.

## The Soul's Continuing Needs

The near-universal deposition of grave goods rested on the conviction that the soul continued to require material objects. As one observer recorded of burial mounds: "the warrior had an idea that after he arose from the dead he would need, in the 'hunting-grounds beyond,' his bow and arrow, war-hatchet, and scalping-knife" [004-044]. Food offerings at the grave were understood as shared meals with the departed: "they believe that while they partake of the visible material the departed spirit partakes at the same time of the spirit of the food" [021-003].

The Se-nel held that "it necessary to nourish the spirits of the departed for the space of a year" — a duty typically performed by a woman who "takes pinole in her blanket, repairs to the scene of the in[[cremation]], or to places hallowed by the memory of the dead, where she scatters it" [010-020]. This annual nourishment of the spirit reflects the belief that the soul's journey was not instantaneous but extended over a significant period.

## Fear and Propitiation

Not all soul-beliefs were comforting. The [[ute|Ute]] funeral ceremony reflected anxiety about the soul's potential malevolence. The practice of destroying the deceased's property — so that nothing tied the spirit to the living — indicates a desire to sever the soul's connection to the world of the living [005-008].
