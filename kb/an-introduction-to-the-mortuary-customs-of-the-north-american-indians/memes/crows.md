# Crows

## Overview

The Crows, or Apsáalooke, inhabited the Yellowstone River valley of present-day Montana and Wyoming. Among the Plains tribes documented by Yarrow (1880), the Crows stand out for the extraordinary intensity of their [[mourning]] practices, described by the mountain man [[beckwourth-james|James Beckwourth]] — who lived among them for years and attained the rank of chief — in one of the most harrowing accounts of grief ritual in the ethnographic literature.

## Mortuary Customs

### Lodge Burial

The Crows practiced lodge burial, in which the deceased was interred within a tipi or lodge structure. [[beckwourth-james|Beckwourth]] recorded that the head chief received burial "according to his directions" before the [[mourning]] party proceeded to the village, suggesting that individual preferences were honored in burial arrangements. The Crows shared this lodge burial tradition with the [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]], [[sioux|Sioux]], and [[navajo|Navajos]] (012-006).

### Burial Above Ground

In common with other Plains tribes, Crows placed their dead above ground on scaffolds or in lodges. An encounter with a Crow burial party is referenced in the chapter on surface burial, where scouts came upon the aftermath of a Crow funeral observance and noted the characteristic Plains pattern of leaving grave goods — weapons, pipes, and personal possessions — with the deceased (012-002).

## Mourning Observances

The death of a Crow head chief, as recounted by [[beckwourth-james|Beckwourth]], triggered a ceremonial response of staggering intensity. Upon the party's return to the village: "We entered amid shrieks, cries, and yells. Blood was streaming from every conceivable part of the bodies of all who were old enough to comprehend their loss. Hundreds of fingers were dismembered; hair torn from the head lay in profusion about the paths, wails and moans in every direction assailed the ear, where unrestrained joy had a few hours before prevailed" (019-001).

A general [[mourning]] assembly was then summoned, drawing "over ten thousand Crows at the place indicated." [[beckwourth-james|Beckwourth]] described the scene as beyond the capacity of language: "Such a scene of disorderly vociferous [[mourning]] no imagination can conceive nor any pen portray." The self-mutilation intensified: "The cutting and hacking of human flesh exceeded all my previous experience; fingers were dismembered as readily as twigs, and blood was poured out like water" (019-003).

[[beckwourth-james|Beckwourth]] recorded specific forms of self-inflicted wounding that went beyond finger amputation: "Many of the warriors would cut two gashes nearly the entire length of their arm, then separating the skin from the flesh at one end, would grasp it in their other hand and rip it asunder to the shoulder. Others would carve various devices upon their breasts and shoulders and raise the skin in the same manner to make the scars show to advantage after the wound was healed." He noted with astonishment that the participants "would not appear to receive any pain from them" (019-003).

## Historical Sources

The principal and nearly sole source for Crow mortuary customs in Yarrow is the *Autobiography of [[beckwourth-james|James Beckwourth]]* (1856). [[beckwourth-james|Beckwourth]], a former enslaved person who became a mountain man, warrior, and eventually a Crow chief, spent years living among the tribe. His first-hand testimony, while subject to the embellishments common to nineteenth-century frontier memoirs, provides an irreplaceable record of Crow grief ritual at a scale — ten thousand mourners — that speaks to the tribe's substantial population and the central importance of mortuary ceremony in their social life.
