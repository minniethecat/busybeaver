Exposure burial — the practice of placing deceased bodies on the ground surface or on elevated platforms where they are exposed to the elements, scavenging animals, and natural decomposition — was documented among several Indigenous North American peoples. This form of mortuary treatment is distinct from both interment (burial in the ground) and cremation (destruction by fire), and occupies a specific place within the spectrum of Indigenous burial customs.

## Terminology and Distinctions

Exposure burial overlaps with, but is not identical to, several related practices:

- **Scaffold burial**: Bodies placed on elevated wooden structures; a form of exposure, but with the specific architectural element of a constructed platform.
- **Tree burial**: Bodies placed in trees or suspended from branches; another elevated exposure variant.
- **Surface burial**: Bodies placed directly on the ground, sometimes covered with stones, earth, or other materials — less deliberately exposed but still not interred.

Exposure burial in its purest form involves deliberately leaving the body accessible to natural processes, often with ritual or cosmological significance.

## Historical Accounts

Early European accounts occasionally describe practices resembling exposure burial. The French physician Jacques-Jean Bruhier, in his 1740 work *L'incertitude des Signes de la Mort*, noted a custom bearing "great similarity to the exposure of bodies to wild beasts" — a reference to the practice of placing the dead in locations where they would be consumed by scavengers as part of the mortuary process (001-012).

Among the Sioux (Dakota), the dead were sometimes placed on the ground or on low platforms, wrapped in buffalo robes or blankets, and left exposed. The body was oriented with the feet toward the west, and possessions were placed nearby for use in the afterlife (014-019, 014-020).

## Cultural Significance

Exposure burial was often associated with beliefs about the soul's journey after death. Among some groups, the consumption of the body by animals was seen as a positive outcome — the deceased's substance being returned to the natural world and the spirit being released more rapidly than through burial. The practice was particularly common among nomadic Plains peoples, for whom the digging of graves in frozen or hard ground presented practical difficulties.
