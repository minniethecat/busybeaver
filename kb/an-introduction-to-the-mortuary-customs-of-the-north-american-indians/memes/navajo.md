# Navajo

## Overview

The Navajo people (Diné), inhabiting the high desert country of present-day [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] and [[arizona|Arizona]], were described by Yarrow's correspondents as possessing mortuary customs markedly different from those of neighboring Pueblo and Plains tribes. Unlike the elaborate secondary burial rituals of the Southeastern peoples or the ceremonial [[cremation]] of [[california|California]] tribes, Navajo death customs were characterized by their simplicity, speed, and the overriding motivation of avoiding contact with the dangerous spirit of the deceased. "The Navajos of [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] and [[arizona|Arizona]]," Yarrow notes, "a wild nomadic tribe" whose customs reflected a deep-seated cultural preoccupation with the pollution of death (002-005).

## Mortuary Customs

### Dwelling Abandonment and In-Situ Burial

The most distinctive Navajo mortuary practice was the abandonment of the dwelling in which death occurred. Rather than preparing the body for transport to a separate burial ground, the Navajo would leave the deceased within the dwelling and seal or abandon the structure entirely:

> The Navajo custom is to leave the body within the dwelling where death occurred, the hogan being abandoned thereafter, as the structure is considered contaminated by the spirit of the dead (002-006).

This practice reflected a profound cultural belief in the dangerous and polluting nature of the dead — a belief that shaped every aspect of Navajo mortuary behavior. The spirit of the deceased, or *chindi*, was understood to linger at the site of death, making the location permanently uninhabitable.

Lodge burial among the Navajo involved placing the body within the dwelling and then sealing it, a practice also observed among other Plains and Southwestern tribes: "The [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]], [[sioux|Sioux]], and Navajos also bury in lodges" (012-006). A variation described by Yarrow involved a structure "resembling that used by the Navajo and other Indians of the southwest" for temporary or permanent [[inhumation|interment]] (014-017).

### Inhumation Practices

While dwelling burial was the most commonly noted practice, Yarrow's index also records "Navajos, inhumation of," indicating that earth burial was practiced under certain circumstances (024-304). The specific conditions determining whether a body was left in the dwelling or buried in the ground are not fully detailed in Yarrow's sources, but the general pattern suggests that prominent individuals might receive more elaborate earth burial while ordinary tribespeople were disposed of through dwelling abandonment.

### Fear of the Dead and Ghost Avoidance

Underlying all Navajo mortuary customs was a pervasive and intense fear of the spirits of the dead. This fear was not unique to the Navajo — many tribes shared taboos against naming the dead or lingering at gravesites — but among the Navajo it reached an exceptional degree, governing the entire response to death. The rapidity of disposal, the abandonment of valuable dwellings and possessions, and the avoidance of any unnecessary contact with the corpse all stemmed from this central cultural conviction.

This attitude contrasted sharply with the practices of tribes like the [[choctaw|Choctaw]], who maintained ossuaries and returned periodically to tend the bones of their ancestors, or the [[sioux|Sioux]], whose [[mourning]] customs involved prolonged and intimate interaction with the deceased's memory.

### The Afterlife Journey

Navajo beliefs about the [[spirit-land|afterlife]] emphasized the journey of the spirit to a land of the dead. Unlike the elaborate grave goods and [[sacrifice]]s that accompanied burials among other tribes, Navajo burial was minimal, reflecting a belief that excessive attachment to the physical remains was spiritually dangerous. The deceased's possessions were typically destroyed or abandoned rather than interred, breaking rather than maintaining the connection between the living and the dead.

## Comparison with Neighboring Tribes

The Navajo practice of dwelling abandonment stood in notable contrast to neighboring Pueblo peoples, whose compact settlements and permanent architecture necessitated different mortuary customs. A group described by Yarrow as practicing "[[cremation]] somewhat resembling that of the Navajos" suggests that even within the Southwest, significant variation existed (002-007).

## Historical Sources

Yarrow's information on Navajo mortuary customs came primarily from military officers and Indian agents stationed in [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] and [[arizona|Arizona]] Territory. The late 1870s, when Yarrow was collecting his data, coincided with a period of intense pressure on the Navajo following their return from the Bosque Redondo internment (1868), and traditional customs were undergoing adaptation under the combined pressures of reservation confinement and missionary activity.
