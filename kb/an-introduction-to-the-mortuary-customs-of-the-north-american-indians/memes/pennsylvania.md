## Overview

Pennsylvania, encompassing the Appalachian highlands and the Susquehanna River valley, was home to numerous Algonquian and Iroquoian peoples at the time of European contact. Yarrow's 1880 monograph preserves accounts of indigenous burial practices in the region, as well as the persistence of traditional mourning customs among the Moravian settlements of the state into the mid-nineteenth century.

## Burial Practices

### Surface Burial

Yarrow records an account from the life of Moses Van Campen describing the burial practices of the Indians formerly inhabiting Pennsylvania. The mode of sepulture involved a distinctive surface-level interment, where the body was deposited with grave goods and covered with earth and stones, forming a low mound or cairn visible upon the landscape (001-028). This practice, described as "somewhat similar" to the rude mode of sepulture found in other Eastern Woodland regions, reflects the Algonquian and Iroquoian customs of the Middle Atlantic region prior to the disruptions of the colonial period.

### Cave Interment

Like the limestone regions of [[ohio|Ohio]], [[indiana|Indiana]], and [[kentucky|Kentucky]], the cave systems of Pennsylvania were utilized for aboriginal [[inhumation|interment]]. The state's karst topography provided natural repositories for the dead, and the multiple States and Territories of the eastern United States share a common pattern of cave burial that Yarrow distinguishes from the cliff and rock-shelter burials of the West (005-013).

## Mourning Customs

### Moravian Syncretic Traditions

A notable feature of Pennsylvania's mortuary landscape in Yarrow's account is the persistence of indigenous-style mourning practices within the Moravian Christian settlements. Yarrow records, citing John Henry Boner, that "up to 1855 the Moravians of Pennsylvania, at the death of one of their number, performed mournful musical airs on brass instruments from the village church steeple and again at the grave" (014-026). This custom of public musical lamentation — described as "probably a remnant of the ancient funeral observances" — represents a remarkable instance of syncretism between European Christian burial and indigenous North American funeral traditions (014-026). The sounding of brass instruments from the church steeple recalls the loud vocal lamentation — the death wail — that was a universal feature of Native American [[mourning]] across the continent.

### Cross-Cultural Parallels

Yarrow draws a cross-cultural parallel between these Pennsylvania Moravian customs and practices in Picardy, France, where as late as 1743 "the relatives threw themselves on the corpse and with loud cries called it by name" (014-026). This comparative observation situates Pennsylvania funeral customs within a broader European-American continuum of mortuary lamentation, suggesting that the boundary between "Indian" and "European" mourning practices was more fluid than often assumed in the nineteenth century.
