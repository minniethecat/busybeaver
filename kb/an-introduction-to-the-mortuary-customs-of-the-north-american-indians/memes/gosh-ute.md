# Gosh-Ute

## Overview

The Gosh-Ute, also known as the Gosiute or Goshute, were a Shoshonean-speaking people inhabiting the arid valleys and mountain ranges of present-day western [[utah|Utah]] and eastern Nevada. Their territory encompassed the harsh environment of the Great Salt Lake Desert, and their mortuary customs were shaped by this distinctive landscape. Yarrow's monograph preserves two accounts of Gosh-Ute burial practice — aquatic [[inhumation|interment]] in desert springs and cave deposition in volcanic terrain — recorded by U.S. Army observers and by Yarrow himself during his 1872 fieldwork in [[utah|Utah Territory]].

## Mortuary Customs

### Aquatic Burial in Springs

The Gosh-Ute were one of the few North American groups documented as practicing true aquatic burial — the deliberate immersion of the dead in water. Yarrow notes the rarity of this custom, stating that "after a careful search for well-authenticated instances of burial, aquatic and semi-aquatic, but two have been found, which are here given. The first relates to the Gosh-Utes, and is by Capt [[simpson-jh|J. H. Simpson]]" (017-002).

Captain [[simpson-jh|Simpson]]'s account, drawn from his exploration of the [[utah|Great Salt Lake Valley]] in 1859, records the testimony of a local guide, [[catlin-george|George]] [[gardner-w|W.]] Bean:

> Skull Valley, which is a part of the Great Salt Lake Desert, and which we have crossed to-day, Mr. George W. Bean, my guide over this route last fall, says derives its name from the number of skulls which have been found in it, and which have arisen from the custom of the Goshute Indians burying their dead in springs, which they sink with stones or keep down with sticks. He says he has actually seen the Indians bury their dead in this way near the town of Provo, where he resides (017-002).

The method of aquatic burial was pragmatic: the body was submerged in a spring and held in position beneath the water by weighting it with stones or securing it with sticks driven into the spring bed. The place name "Skull Valley" itself — derived from the visible cranial remains that accumulated in and around these desert water sources — stands as a topographic testament to the persistence of this practice. The springs, rare and vital water sources in an arid landscape, were transformed by Gosh-Ute mortuary custom into repositories of the dead, merging the practical geography of desert survival with the sacred geography of burial.

Yarrow's index registers the Gosh-Ute under two additional entries: "Gosh-Utes" (024-008) and "Burial, aquatic, Gosh-Utes" (024-042), confirming the centrality of aquatic [[inhumation|interment]] to the ethnographic identity of this group in the monograph's organizational scheme.

### Cave Burial

A second, distinct Gosh-Ute mortuary practice — cave deposition — was observed by Yarrow personally during his 1872 fieldwork in [[utah|Utah Territory]]. He records:

> While in the Territory of Utah, in 1872, the writer discovered a natural cave not far from the House Range of mountains, the entrance to which resembled the shaft of a mine. In this the Gosi-Ute Indians had deposited their dead, surrounded with different articles, until it was quite filled up; at least it so appeared from the cursory examination made, limited time preventing a careful exploration (005-003).

The same season, Yarrow learned of a second cave burial site from a Gosi-Ute informant near the Nevada border. The Indian guide described "a large cave in which he had himself assisted in placing dead members of his tribe" (005-003), and drew "a rough diagram of its position and appearance within" (005-003). The cave entrance had been deliberately sealed by the Gosi-Ute "some years previous" with "large stones and roots of sage brush" to prevent game animals from entering (005-003). Though Yarrow's party labored for six hours attempting to clear the blocked entrance, the effort was abandoned. When asked how many bodies the cave contained, the guide replied "Heaps, heaps," moving his hands upwards as far as they could be stretched (005-003).

The cave burial practice of the Gosh-Ute represents a different mortuary logic from the aquatic burial: while springs received individual dead in a process of ongoing, incremental deposition, the caves functioned as collective ossuaries where the remains of many individuals accumulated over time, each accompanied by grave goods ("surrounded with different articles"). The deliberate sealing of cave entrances further suggests a concern with the permanent separation of the dead from the living, the stone and sagebrush barriers serving as ritual as much as practical closures.

The co-occurrence of two entirely distinct burial modes — aquatic [[inhumation|interment]] and cave deposition — within a single tribal group is notable. It may reflect variation among Gosh-Ute bands, differentiation by social status or cause of death, or temporal change in funerary practice during the mid-nineteenth century.
