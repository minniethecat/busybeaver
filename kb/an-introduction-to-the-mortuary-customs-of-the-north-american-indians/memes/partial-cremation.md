## Overview

Partial [[cremation]] occupied an intermediate position between full [[cremation]] and inhumation in the mortuary practices of several North American Indian groups. Unlike complete [[cremation]], in which the entire body was reduced to ash, partial [[cremation]] left unconsumed bone fragments — particularly the skull — which were then intentionally gathered and deposited in mounds, clay vessels, or even used as receptacles for the remaining ashes. Yarrow devoted a separate section of his 1880 monograph to "PARTIAL CREMATION," treating it as a distinct category allied to but separate from full [[cremation]] (600-1). The practice was documented archaeologically in [[florida|Florida]], [[tennessee|Tennessee]], and [[carolina|North Carolina]].

## Tribal Distribution

Evidence of partial [[cremation]] has been found primarily among the mound-building cultures of the Southeast and the [[cherokee|Cherokee]] of the southern Appalachian region. At Cade's Pond, near [[florida|Santa Fe Lake]] in [[florida|Florida]], the writer personally excavated two instances in which skulls had been "used as the depository of his ashes" — the crania were filled with "fragments of half incinerated human bones, mingled with dark-colored dust" (588-1). In the [[tennessee|Little Tennessee River]] Valley, the [[cherokee|Cherokee]] or their predecessors practiced a distinctive method in which bodies were covered with clay and partially cremated by fires built above the clay shell (604-1). In [[carolina|North Carolina]], an unidentified ancient race covered bodies with a one-inch clay coating before firing, which "consumed the body and baked the clay, which retained the impression of the body" (615-1).

## Variations

**Skull Ash Receptacles ([[florida|Florida]]).** The most archaeologically distinctive form of partial [[cremation]] was discovered at Cade's Pond, [[florida|Florida]]. In two separate instances within a burial mound, human skulls were found resting on their apices (base upward), each filled with "fragments of half incinerated human bones, mingled with dark-colored dust." The skulls themselves were "free from all action of fire," indicating that the head had been separated before [[cremation]] of the body and afterward used as a container for the ashes. Beneath one cranium lay a human tibia exhibiting platycnemism (latitudinal index .512), and surrounding bone fragments "probably constituting an entire individual" (588-2). The writer noted that no similar relics had been previously reported in [[florida|Florida]] or elsewhere (590-1).

**Clay-Mould [[cremation|Cremation]] ([[cherokee|Cherokee]]/[[tennessee|Tennessee]]).** In the [[tennessee|Little Tennessee River]] Valley, a method combining clay encasement with partial [[cremation]] was practiced. The corpse was placed face-up in a pit, and a covering of clay mortar was molded over the body. A hot fire was then built on top of the clay, which "formed an entire shield of pottery for the corpse." After the fire consumed the body within, the baked clay shell retained a cast of the body's form. Mr. McDowell, who farmed the land after [[cherokee|Cherokee]] removal in 1819, uncovered three such "burned clay [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]]" in his first season, including one that preserved "the mould of a naked human figure" (604-2). A later excavation in 1872 recovered only partial impressions of the forehead and lower limbs (610-1).

**[[carolina|North Carolina]] Clay-Coating Method.** [[wilcox|Mr. Wilcox]] reported a variant in [[carolina|North Carolina]] in which "the bodies had been placed with the face up and covered with a coating of plastic clay about an inch thick. A pile of wood was then placed on top and fired, which consumed the body and baked the clay, which retained the impression of the body" (615-2). This method produced a fired-clay shell with a detailed body impression but did not involve the same thick clay superstructure as the [[tennessee|Tennessee]] examples.

## Historical Accounts

[[foster-jw|J. W. Foster]], in his *Pre-Historic Races* (1873), provided the foundational description of the [[tennessee|Tennessee]] clay-mould burials, noting that the [[cherokee|Cherokee]] held possession of the region until 1819 (604-3). Colonel C. [[gardner-w|W.]] Jenkes, superintendent of the Corundum mines that opened in the area in the 1870s, reported Indian traditions "extending back for 500 years" and described a grave "just opened" with the clay-mould construction (610-2). The Cade's Pond discoveries were presented by the writer at the Saint Louis meeting of the American Association in August 1878 (590-2).

## Related Practices

Partial [[cremation]] bridges the gap between full [[cremation]] and [[inhumation]]. The [[florida|Florida]] skull-ash receptacle variant connects to [[skull-burial]] practices found elsewhere. The clay-mould technique used in [[tennessee|Tennessee]] and [[carolina|North Carolina]] represents a fusion of [[clay-burial]] and [[cremation]] elements. The practice also relates to [[urn-burial]], in which cremated remains were placed in ceramic vessels — the [[florida|Florida]] skulls serving the same function as urns.
