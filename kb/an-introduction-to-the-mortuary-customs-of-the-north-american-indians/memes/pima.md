# Pima

## Overview

The Pima people (Akimel O'odham, or "River People") inhabited the Gila and Salt River valleys of present-day southern [[arizona|Arizona]], where they practiced intensive irrigated agriculture along the desert waterways. As a Southwestern people living at the ecological margin of the arid Sonoran Desert, the Pima developed mortuary customs that — while sharing the broader Southwestern pattern of inhumation — were distinguished by several practices of striking ethnographic specificity. Yarrow (1880) preserved the detailed account of Capt. [[grossman-fe|F. E. Grossman]], U.S. Army, whose observations among the Pima were transmitted through the Smithsonian Institution in 1871.

## Mortuary Customs

### The Bound Corpse and Round Grave

Pima burial began with the preparation of the corpse itself. The bodies of the dead were tied with ropes, "passing the latter around the neck and under the knees and then drawing them tight until the body is doubled up and forced into a sitting position" (001-025). This flexed, seated posture — the body bound into a compact form — contrasts with the extended burial position practiced by many other tribes and represents a distinctive Pima mortuary technique.

The grave was dug to a depth of four to five feet and was "perfectly round," approximately two feet in diameter. At the bottom, a lateral vault was hollowed out to one side, large enough to contain the bound body in its seated position. The corpse was deposited in this side chamber, and the grave was filled level with the ground. Poles, trees, or pieces of timber were then placed upon the grave surface "to protect the remains from the coyotes" (001-025).

### Night Burials and Anticipatory Graves

Pima burials usually took place at night, "without much ceremony." Mourners chanted during the burial, but overt signs of grief were rare. The bodies were interred, if possible, immediately after death, and graves were generally prepared before the patient died — a practice of anticipatory grave-digging that had the curious consequence of leaving open graves for those who unexpectedly recovered:

> Sometimes sick persons (for whom the graves had already been dug) recovered; in such cases the graves are left open until the persons for whom they were intended die. Open graves of this kind can be seen in several of their burial-grounds (001-025).

This pragmatic approach — the grave prepared in advance, left open and waiting — represents a mortuary logic distinct from the more common pattern of digging the grave only after death had been confirmed. For the Pima, the grave was part of the illness trajectory, not solely a postmortem response.

### Destruction of Property

Following the burial, the house and all personal effects of the deceased were burned, and the deceased's horses and cattle were killed — the meat being cooked as a repast for the mourners (001-025). This comprehensive destruction of the deceased's material world parallels practices documented among other tribes, but the Pima version was notably thorough: the house itself was destroyed by fire, eliminating the physical dwelling-place of the deceased from the village landscape.

### Mourning and Hair Cutting

Pima [[mourning]] observances were extended and visible. The nearest relatives remained within the village for weeks, and sometimes months, after the death. As a sign of sorrow, the men cut off approximately six inches of their long hair, while the women cut their hair "quite short" (001-025). This gendered gradation of hair [[sacrifice]] — men's partial cutting versus women's complete cropping — encoded differential expectations of [[mourning]] intensity between the sexes.

### Burial-Ground Location

Pima burial-grounds were selected some distance from the village, and, if possible, in a grove of mesquite bushes — a preference that integrated the burial ground into the distinctive vegetation of the Sonoran Desert landscape and separated the realm of the dead from the spaces of daily life.

## Historical Sources

The principal source for Pima burial customs is Capt. [[grossman-fe|F. E. Grossman]], U.S. Army, whose report was published by the Smithsonian Institution in 1871. [[grossman-fe|Grossman]]'s account is notable for its attention to the material details of Pima burial — the dimensions of the grave, the technique of corpse-binding, the practice of anticipatory grave-digging — that together provide one of the most archaeologically informative descriptions in Yarrow's entire survey.
