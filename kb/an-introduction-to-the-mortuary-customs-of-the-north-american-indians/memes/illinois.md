# Illinois

## Overview

The Illinois Confederacy (Illiniwek) was an Algonquian-speaking alliance of tribes — including the Kaskaskia, Peoria, Tamaroa, Cahokia, and Michigamea — whose traditional territory encompassed much of present-day Illinois and adjacent regions of Iowa, [[missouri|Missouri]], and Arkansas. By the time of Yarrow's 1880 monograph, the Illinois population had been drastically reduced through warfare and disease, but the physical remains of their mortuary practices survived in the form of burial mounds along the Rock River and its tributaries. [[gardner-w|W.]] C. Holbrook's 1877 examination of Indian mounds near Sterling, Illinois, published in the *American Naturalist*, provided Yarrow with some of the most detailed archaeological evidence of upper [[mississippi|Mississippi]] Valley mound [[inhumation|interment]] (024-368).

## Mortuary Customs

The most striking feature of Illinois burial practice documented by Holbrook was the construction of stone *dolmens* — quadrilateral chambers built of limestone without mortar — within oval mounds. One such mound on the Rock River, measuring approximately twenty feet in length, twelve feet in width, and seven feet in height, contained a dolmen ten feet long, four feet high, and four and a half feet wide. The structure was built of locally quarried lime-rock, covered with large flat stones, and rested directly on the natural soil surface, the interior having been scooped out to enlarge the chamber. Within this stone chamber Holbrook discovered the partly decayed remains of eight human skeletons (024-368).

A second form of mound burial involved the excavation of a shallow depression in the natural soil surface, in which the bodies were placed and covered with approximately one foot of earth. Fires were then kindled upon the grave before the mound was completed. Notably, the bones were not charred by the fire, and charcoal was found in a distinct stratum approximately one foot above the skeletal remains — suggesting that the fire served a ritual or purificatory purpose rather than a crematory one. This layered construction, with its deliberate separation of fire from the body, indicates a more complex ritual program than simple inhumation (024-368).

A third type of mound, situated on an elevated point of land commanding an extensive view of the surrounding country, featured an oval altar of flat limestone pieces on its summit. The stones of this altar had been burned red, with some portions nearly converted to lime, and charcoal was abundant on and around it. Fragments of human bones, some charred, were found at the sides of the altar. This configuration suggests that partial [[cremation]] may have been practiced for certain individuals, with the altar serving as a raised platform for the mortuary fire — a practice linking the Illinois mounds to broader [[mississippi|Mississippi]]an ceremonial traditions (024-368).

## Archaeological Significance

The Sterling County mounds are notable for the presence of a circular cranial perforation — approximately the size of a silver dime — observed in one of the recovered skulls. Holbrook noted that the edges of this opening had begun to cicatrize, indicating that the procedure had been performed during life. This evidence of trepanation, combined with the finding of splintered long bones showing healed morbid growths (exostosis), suggests that the Illinois people possessed sophisticated medical knowledge and that individuals who had survived serious injuries received careful [[inhumation|interment]]. The dolmen construction itself, requiring the transport and placement of substantial limestone slabs without the use of mortar, demonstrates considerable engineering skill and organized communal labor (024-368).

## Historical Sources

The archaeological documentation of Illinois burial mounds comes from [[gardner-w|W.]] C. Holbrook, whose findings were published in the *American Naturalist* (1877, Vol. XI, No. 11, p. 688). Holbrook's methodical excavation and detailed recording of mound stratigraphy — distinguishing between fire strata, skeletal remains, and construction sequence — represents an early example of scientific archaeological practice in North America, predating the professionalization of the discipline by several decades.
