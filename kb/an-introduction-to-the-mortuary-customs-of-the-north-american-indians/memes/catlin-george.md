# George Catlin

## Overview

George Catlin (1796–1872) was an American painter, author, and traveler who dedicated much of his career to documenting the appearance and customs of Native American peoples before the transformative impact of westward expansion. His two-volume work *Letters and Notes on the Manners, Customs, and Condition of the North American Indians* (1844) provided Yarrow with some of the most vivid and detailed eyewitness accounts of Plains Indian mortuary practices, particularly the elaborate scaffold burials of the [[omaha|Omaha]] and the distinctive skull-circle memorials of the [[mandan|Mandan]] (024-303).

## Contributions

Catlin's most dramatic contribution to Yarrow's monograph is his account of the funeral of Blackbird, the great chief of the [[omaha|Omahas]]. According to Catlin, the dying chief requested that his body be taken to a towering bluff overlooking the [[missouri|Missouri]] River and there buried on the back of his favorite war-horse, which was to be buried alive beneath him. Catlin described how the noble white steed was led to the summit, and the deceased chief was placed astride the horse's back in full ceremonial dress — bow in hand, shield and quiver slung, war-eagle plume head-dress waving — with his medicine bag, dried meat, tobacco, and the scalps of his enemies hung from the bridle. The warriors of his band each painted their right hand with vermilion and stamped it upon the white sides of the horse before turfs were gradually built up around the animal's legs, sides, back, and finally over the head and eagle plumes of the entombed rider. Catlin's account preserves what may be the most elaborate description of equine entombment in the ethnographic literature of the Plains (024-303).

Catlin also documented what he termed the "Golgothas" of the [[mandan|Mandan]] — circles of twenty or thirty feet in diameter, each containing a central mound supporting two buffalo skulls (male and female) and a twenty-foot "medicine pole" adorned with objects of mystery and superstition. Within these circles, skulls of the deceased were placed upon bunches of wild sage. Catlin recorded the remarkable ongoing relationship between the living and these skulls: wives would visit daily with dishes of the best-cooked food their wigwams afforded, placing them before the skulls at night and retrieving the dishes in the morning. When the sage beneath a skull began to decay, it was carefully replaced with a fresh bunch. This practice, in Catlin's rendering, represented not grief — for several years had cured the anguish — but a sustained form of domestic devotion to the dead (024-303).

## Accounts Provided

Catlin's accounts encompassed the mortuary customs of the [[omaha|Omaha]], [[mandan|Mandan]], and other [[missouri|Missouri]] River tribes. His artist's eye for visual detail — the vermilion handprints on the white horse, the eagle plumes waving above the turf mound, the daily offerings of food before the skull circles — distinguishes his contributions from the more clinical reports of the Army surgeons who constituted many of Yarrow's other informants. His willingness to record practices at length, with attention to both material culture and emotional register, makes his accounts among the most humanly compelling in Yarrow's entire survey (024-303).
