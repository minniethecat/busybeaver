# Nootka

## Overview

The Nootka, or Nuu-chah-nulth, inhabited the deeply indented western coastline of Vancouver Island, subsisting primarily on the rich marine resources of the Pacific — whales, seals, salmon, and halibut. Yarrow (1880) mentions the Nootka only briefly, in the context of aquatic disposal of the dead, yet their placement within the broader Northwest Coast mortuary complex documented by [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]] and other observers allows a reconstruction of their funerary practices through regional comparison.

## Mortuary Customs

### Aquatic Disposal of Slaves

Yarrow records that the Indians of Nootka Sound, together with the [[chinook|Chinooks]] of the lower Columbia, were in the habit of disposing of their dead slaves by casting them into the water — a practice noted in the section on aquatic burial. This was not a formal rite of [[inhumation|interment]] but rather a means of discarding persons of the lowest social status, for whom the elaborate canoe burial, vault [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]], and grave goods accorded to free persons were deemed inappropriate. The practice reflects the deeply stratified nature of Northwest Coast society, in which the treatment of the body after death was precisely calibrated to the rank and wealth of the deceased (017-001).

### Regional Context: Northwest Coast Burial Hierarchy

The meager specific documentation of Nootka mortuary customs in Yarrow may be supplemented by the broader Northwest Coast pattern described by [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], who observed that among the tribes of Puget Sound and the adjacent coasts, the distinctions of rank and wealth in burial were in all cases very marked. Persons of no consideration and slaves were buried with very little care or respect, while chiefs and persons of note received elaborate funerary treatment. The canoes used for chiefly burial were of great size and value — the war or state canoes of the deceased — and frequently one canoe was inverted over that holding the body. In one instance near Shoalwater Bay, the corpse was deposited in a small canoe, which was then placed within a larger one and covered with a third. The most valuable articles of property were placed in or hung up around the grave, having been first carefully rendered unserviceable (016-013).

### Grave Violation and Sacred Secrecy

The Northwest Coast tribes observed what Captain Belcher described as great secrecy in all their burial ceremonies, and they regarded the violation of the grave as an offense of the first magnitude, provoking severe revenge. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] noted that the chiefs' canoes were watched and maintained — repainted and redecorated — and placed in sequestered spots to discourage disturbance from both Europeans and rival tribes (016-013).

### Wailing and Prolonged Mourning

[[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] described the [[mourning|wailing]] for the dead among the Northwest Coast peoples as continuing for a long time, noting that it seemed rather a ceremonial performance than an act of spontaneous grief. The duty belonged to the women, and the early morning was usually chosen for the purpose. A mourner would go out alone to a place some distance from the lodge or camp and, in a loud sobbing voice, repeat a stereotyped formula — as, for instance, a mother on the loss of her child: *Ah seahb shed-da bud-dah ah ta bud! ad-de-dah, Ah chief!* — meaning "My child dead, alas!" When in dreams they saw any of their deceased friends the [[death-wailing|lamentation]] was renewed (016-013).

## Historical Sources

The principal source on Nootka mortuary customs in Yarrow is a brief reference in the section on aquatic burial (017-001), embedded within a broader discussion of water disposal practices ranging from classical antiquity to the New World. The Northwest Coast ethnographic context is supplied primarily by [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], whose observations on the tribes of Puget Sound, the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]], and adjacent coasts were incorporated extensively into Yarrow's chapters on coastal burial. Captain James Cook's journals from his 1778 visit to Nootka Sound, though not cited by Yarrow, represent the foundational European contact with the Nootka and include observations of their material culture and social organization.
