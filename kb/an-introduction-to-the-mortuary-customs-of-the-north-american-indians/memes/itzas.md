# Itzas

## Overview

The Itzas were a Maya people of the Petén region of present-day northern Guatemala, whose principal settlement was located on the islands of Lake Petén Itzá — the lake that bears their name. As one of the last independent Maya polities to resist Spanish conquest (their island capital, Tayasal, did not fall until 1697), the Itzas preserved a distinctive cultural tradition into the early colonial period. Among the mortuary customs recorded by early observers and transmitted to Yarrow (1880) through the encyclopedic works of [[bancroft-hubert-h|Hubert Howe Bancroft]] was the practice of aquatic disposal — the consignment of the dead to the waters of the lake that was their home.

## Mortuary Customs

### Aquatic Burial in Lake Petén Itzá

The defining mortuary practice of the Itzas was the ritual deposition of the deceased into the waters of Lake Petén Itzá. As Yarrow records:

> The Itzas of Guatemala, living on the islands of Lake Peter, according to Bancroft, are said to have thrown their dead into the lake for want of room (017-001).

This laconic description — "for want of room" — suggests that the practice was shaped by the physical constraints of island habitation, where the finite surface area of the settlement precluded the accumulation of earth burials over generations. Unlike cultures that practiced aquatic burial as a primary ritual mode (such as the Norse ship-burial or the Lotophagians of antiquity, whom Yarrow discusses in the same passage), the Itza practice appears to have been an adaptive response to the spatial limitations of their lacustrine environment.

Yarrow's index entries "Itzas" (024-011) and "Itzas, aquatic burial of" (024-232) confirm that this practice was recognized as a distinct type within the survey's comparative framework — a form of water burial that stood apart from the occasional or incidental aquatic disposals documented among tribes who sank their dead in springs or watercourses on an irregular basis.

### Distinction from Occasional Aquatic Disposal

Yarrow was careful to distinguish the Itza practice from the irregular aquatic disposal practiced by other tribes. He observed that "as a confirmed rite or ceremony, this mode of disposing of the dead has never been followed by any of our North American Indians" (017-001), distinguishing the systematic Itza lake burial from the incidental practices of tribes such as the [[nootka|Nootka]] Sound people and the [[chinook|Chinooks]], who were known to dispose of deceased slaves in water, and the [[cherokee|Cherokees]] of [[tennessee|Tennessee]], who, according to Timberlake, "seldom bury the dead, but threw them into the river" (017-001). The Itza practice, by contrast, was a collective and systematic mode of disposal dictated by the geography of island life.

## Historical Sources

The account preserved by Yarrow derives from [[bancroft-hubert-h|Hubert Howe Bancroft]]'s monumental multi-volume *The Native Races of the Pacific States* (1874–1876), which synthesized centuries of Spanish colonial documentation and early ethnographic observation into a comprehensive survey of the indigenous peoples of the Americas. [[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]]'s treatment of the Itzas drew upon Spanish colonial accounts of the Petén region, including the records of the Franciscan missionaries who first contacted the Itza people in the decades following the conquest of Tayasal in 1697.
