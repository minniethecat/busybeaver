# Chickasaw

## Overview

The Chickasaw people inhabited the region of present-day northern [[mississippi|Mississippi]], western [[tennessee|Tennessee]], and adjacent portions of Alabama and [[kentucky|Kentucky]]. Linguistically and culturally the closest relatives of the [[choctaw|Choctaw]], the Chickasaw were renowned as formidable warriors and skilled diplomats during the colonial era. By the time of Yarrow's 1880 survey, the tribe had largely been relocated to Indian Territory, yet the documentary record preserved by eighteenth-century observers — particularly [[roman|Bernard Roman]]s — provides a valuable account of their traditional mortuary practices before the disruptions of removal.

## Mortuary Customs

### Immediate Sub-Couch Interment

The defining feature of Chickasaw burial practice was the immediate [[inhumation|interment]] of the deceased beneath the very couch or sleeping platform on which death had occurred. According to [[roman|Bernard Roman]]s, whose observations Yarrow preserves:

> The "funeral customs of the Chickasaws did not differ materially from those of the Muscogulges. They interred the dead as soon as the breath left the body, and beneath the couch in which the deceased expired" (002-004).

This practice of sub-domestic burial — placing the dead directly beneath the household furniture — represents a pattern distinct from both the scaffold exposure of the Plains tribes and the communal [[ossuary]] traditions of their [[choctaw|Choctaw]] neighbors. The immediacy of [[inhumation|interment]] ("as soon as the breath left the body") suggests a cosmology in which the transition from life to death demanded swift ritual action, with the domestic space itself becoming the grave.

### Relationship to Muscogulge Customs

The close correspondence between Chickasaw and Muscogulge ([[creek|Creek]]) burial customs noted by [[roman|Roman]]s places both groups within a shared Southeastern mortuary complex. Yarrow's index reinforces this connection with its entry for "Chickasaws, burial customs of" (024-133), which cross-references the broader Southeastern pattern.

### Comparative Distance: The Navajo Parallel

Yarrow (1880) makes the intriguing observation that the [[navajo|Navajos]] of [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] and [[arizona|Arizona]] — "a tribe living a considerable distance from the Chickasaws" — followed "somewhat similar customs" (002-005). This geographic outlier in the comparative record highlights a recurrent theme in Yarrow's survey: the independent emergence of parallel mortuary patterns across widely separated culture areas, a phenomenon that complicates simple diffusionist explanations.

## Historical Sources

Yarrow's principal source for Chickasaw burial customs was [[roman|Bernard Roman]]s, the eighteenth-century naturalist and surveyor whose observations of Southeastern tribes provided one of the earliest systematic ethnographic records of the region. [[roman|Roman]]s' account is necessarily limited to the pre-Removal period; no comparable documentation exists for Chickasaw mortuary practice in the post-Removal Indian Territory context, a significant gap in the ethnographic record.
