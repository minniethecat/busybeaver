# Funeral Dance

Among many North American Indian tribes, the **funeral dance** was an integral component of mortuary ritual. More than mere expression of grief, these dances served multiple functions: they honored the dead, propitiated or warded off spirits, reinforced community bonds, and in some cases facilitated the soul's passage to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. The forms ranged from solemn, measured movements around the corpse to ecstatic communal performances lasting through the night.

## The Ghost Dance and Spirit Communication

The most formally structured of the mortuary dances was the "ghost gamble" or spirit-lodge ceremony practiced by the [[sioux|Sioux]]. One detailed account describes a ceremony held "at the time of the first feast held over the remains of the deceased," where relatives gathered to gamble and dance as a means of communicating with the recently dead [021-019]. The ceremony combined ritual dancing with the use of specially prepared gaming sticks, and was understood as a vehicle through which the ghost could interact with the living community [021-020]. "Two seeds are simply blackened on one side" and used in the gamble, their fall believed to be guided by the spirit [021-022].

Among the tribes of the Plains, dancing around the scaffold or burial platform was a central element of the [[mourning]] period. The "scalp dance," while primarily a celebration of martial victory, often incorporated elements of funeral dance when performed in honor of warriors killed in battle. The relationship between war and mortuary dancing reflected the broader intertwining of death, honor, and community display in Plains culture.

## Regional Practices

In [[california|California]], [[mourning]] dances were described as particularly intense. An observer among the Pitt River or [[achomawi|Achomawi Indians]] recorded that after burial, the women "being painted all their faces with black coal and oil do sit twenty-four hours in the houses [[mourning]] and lamenting by turns with such yelling and howling as may be heard a great way from the place where they sit" [006-010]. While this describes stationary [[death-wailing|lamentation]] rather than dance per se, other [[california|California]] groups incorporated movement and rhythmic body-painting into [[mourning]] performances that observers classified as dances.

The Southeastern tribes, with their elaborate secondary burial complexes, integrated dance into the lengthy interval between initial and final burial. The bones of the dead, preserved in charnel houses during this period, were periodically honored with ceremonial dances timed to agricultural and seasonal cycles. At the final "Feast of the Dead" among the [[huron|Huron]], 2,000 participants gathered for a ceremony that combined feasting, gift-giving, and ritual movement around the common tomb [020-005].

## Dancing with the Remains

Some of the most striking mortuary dances involved physical interaction with the remains of the deceased. A widow among the Tahkali was required "to carry the bones of her deceased husband wherever she went for four years, preserving them in such a casket, handsomely decorated with feathers" [015-014]. While the account does not explicitly describe dancing, the extended carrying of remains through daily life and ceremonial occasions inevitably involved ritual movement.

The lock of hair taken from the deceased — suspended from scaffolds or preserved in bundles — also figured in ceremonial performances. "Some hair was suspended, which we at first mistook for a scalp, but our guide informed us that these were locks of hair torn from their heads by the relatives to testify their grief" [014-008]. The manipulation of these hair tokens in rhythmic, performative contexts constituted a form of mortuary dance, linking the living body of the mourner to the preserved remnant of the dead.

## Functions and Meanings

Across its varied forms, the funeral dance served consistent functions: it made grief public and communal, transforming private sorrow into collective performance; it reinforced the boundary between the living and the dead while simultaneously enabling controlled contact; and it demonstrated the wealth, cohesion, and piety of the grieving community to both human and spirit observers. The dance was, in essence, the kinetic dimension of the mortuary complex — the point at which belief, emotion, and social structure converged in patterned movement.
