# Skull Burial

The separate treatment of the human **skull** — its removal, curation, painting, or independent burial — was a distinctive element of mortuary practice among several North American Indian tribes. Unlike the unified burial of the complete skeleton or corpse, skull burial reflected specific beliefs about the head as the seat of the person, the soul, or martial power. The practice ranged from reverential preservation of ancestor skulls to the ritualized [[inhumation|interment]] of trophy skulls taken in warfare.

## Skull Burial in Secondary Interment

Among tribes practicing secondary burial, the skull often received special treatment distinct from the post-cranial skeleton. The [[huron|Huron]] Feast of the Dead, described by the Jesuit missionary [[brebeuf-p-de|P. de Brebeuf]], involved the collective [[secondary-burial|reburial]] of bones from multiple villages. In the preparation for this ceremony, the skulls were "cleaned, bleached, painted, wrapped in odorous balsams, placed in bark boxes" — receiving far more elaborate treatment than the long bones, which were simply bundled together [015-014].

The special status of the skull in secondary burial reflected a widely held belief that the head contained the essential identity of the deceased. Among the tribes of the Southeast, the bones collected from scaffold and [[ossuary|charnel-house]] burials were sorted by type, with skulls carefully counted, cleaned, and arranged in patterns within the communal [[ossuary]]. The care given to skulls in these deposits far exceeded that given to other skeletal elements.

## Skull Curation and Display

Several tribes practiced long-term curation of skulls rather than their burial. The Pitt River or [[achomawi|Achomawi Indians]] of [[california|California]] practiced a distinctive form of skull burial in which "the body is buried in the ground in a standing position, the shoulders nearly even with the ground. The grave is prepared by digging a hole of sufficient depth and circumference to admit the body, the head being cut off" [011-010]. This separation of head from body before [[inhumation|interment]] represented a radical form of skull-specific treatment.

Among the [[chinook|Chinook]] and other [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] tribes, skulls of distinguished ancestors were sometimes retained in the household, cleaned and oiled periodically, and consulted as oracles in matters of family importance. This practice blurred the line between burial and ancestor veneration, with the skull serving as a physical link between the living and the dead.

## Trophy Skulls and Martial Skull Treatment

In the context of warfare, enemy skulls were subject to a different set of practices. Trophy skulls were displayed on poles outside lodges, incorporated into war bundles, or deposited at the boundaries of tribal territory as warnings. While distinct from the reverential treatment of ancestor skulls, these practices shared the underlying premise that the skull carried special power and meaning that the rest of the skeleton did not.

## Archaeological Evidence

Archaeological investigations of ancient burial places corroborate the ethnographic accounts of separate skull treatment. At an ancient Indian burial place in the Eastern Woodlands, consisting of "eight vaults, made of pine or cedar boards, closely connected, about eight feet square and six in height," excavators found "four dead bodies, carefully wrapped in skins, tied with cords of sinew" — and adjacent deposits of skulls separated from their post-cranial remains [016-007]. Such finds confirm that skull-specific burial practices extended far into prehistory.

## Regional Distribution

The practice of separate skull burial was most fully developed among the tribes of the Northwest Coast, [[california|California]], and the Southeast — regions characterized by complex social hierarchies and elaborate mortuary ceremonialism. The association between social complexity and skull-specific treatment suggests that the practice served to mark distinctions of rank, lineage, and martial achievement that less differentiated burial methods could not express.
