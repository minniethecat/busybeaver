## Overview

Arizona, in the American Southwest, is documented in Yarrow's 1880 monograph primarily in conjunction with neighboring [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] as the homeland of the [[navajo|Navajo]] people, whose distinctive burial practices received attention in the survey.

## Burial Sites & Practices

The [[navajo|Navajos]] of "[[new-mexico|New Mexico]] and Arizona, a tribe living a considerable distance" from the major routes of Anglo-American expansion, maintained burial customs that preserved traditional practices well into the nineteenth century (002-005). The arid desert environment of Arizona influenced mortuary practices, with shallow grave [[inhumation|interment]] being the predominant method, often accompanied by the destruction of the deceased's dwelling and personal property.

Yarrow's reference to Arizona includes a brief but specific topographical note on the region in the context of aboriginal territorial distribution (001-024). The [[navajo|Navajo]] burial practices documented for the Arizona-[[new-mexico|New Mexico]] region included careful orientation of the body, placement of grave goods, and ritual avoidance of the burial site by surviving family members. These practices contrast with the scaffold and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]]s of the Plains tribes and the canoe burials of the Pacific Northwest.
