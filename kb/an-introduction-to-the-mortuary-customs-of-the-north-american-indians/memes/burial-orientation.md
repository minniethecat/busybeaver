## Overview

Burial orientation refers to the deliberate positioning of a corpse along a cardinal direction or toward a specific geographic feature at the time of [[inhumation|interment]]. Among the North American tribes documented by Yarrow (1880), orientation practices varied widely but were frequently tied to cosmological beliefs, particularly the perceived location of the spirit land (188-001).

## Tribal Distribution

**[[wichita|Wichita]]** — The body was placed in the grave "with the head towards the west and the feet to the east," wrapped in blankets and buffalo robes atop a prepared couch within the grave. When a [[wichita|Wichita]] died, the town-crier went "up and down through the village" to announce the fact, and preparations were made immediately for burial. If the grave was some distance from the village, the body was carried "on the back of a pony, being first wrapped in blankets and then laid prone across the saddle" (001-009).

**[[creek|Creek]]** — The dead were "all interred with the feet of the corpse to the east" (652-001). Mourning customs included smearing hair and face with a grease-and-ash mixture.

**Watlala (Upper [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]])** — Universal observance of "the head being always placed to the west." The reason given was that "the road to the *me-mel-us-illa-hee*, the country of the dead, is toward the west, and if they place them otherwise they would be confused" (770-001). This explicit theological rationale is one of the clearest statements of the belief-system underlying burial orientation.

**[[mohawk|Mohawks]] of [[new-york|New York]]** — The [[mohawk|Mohawks]] made "a large round hole in which the body was placed upright or upon its haunches," covered with timber to support the earth. They "always dressed the corpse in all its finery, and put wampum and other things into the grave with it" (001-002). This upright/flexed positioning represents a distinct departure from horizontal extended burial with orientation.

**[[sioux|Sioux]]** — Bodies were laid upon the ground within burial lodges, wrapped in buffalo-skin robes. While a specific cardinal direction was not uniformly prescribed, the positioning was done with care according to tribal custom (631-001).

**[[pima|Pima]]** — Bodies were bound with ropes "doubled up and forced into a sitting position" and deposited in a round vault at the bottom of a circular grave, representing a distinct departure from extended-position burial (218-001). The [[pima|Pima]] burial included the [[cremation|burning]] of the deceased's house and personal effects, with horses and cattle killed and the meat cooked as a repast for the mourners (001-025).

**[[coyotero-apache|Coyotero Apache]]** — The [[apache|Coyoteros]] "partially wrap up the corpse and deposit it into the cavity left by the removal of a small rock or the stump of a tree. After the body has been crammed into the smallest possible space the rock or stump is again rolled into its former position, when a number of stones are placed around the base to keep out the coyotes" (001-027). The nearest kin usually performed the burial, and the location was chosen for concealment rather than directional significance.

## Variations

Several tribes did not adhere to a fixed cardinal orientation. The [[ute|Ute]] placed bodies in rock clefts without regard to position, the space constraints of the natural sepulchre determining the arrangement (387-001). Among the Pitt River ([[achomawi|Achomawi]]) of [[california|California]], a standing-position burial was practiced, with the body interred vertically "the shoulders nearly even with the ground" (619-001).

The stone-grave builders of [[kentucky|Kentucky]] and [[ohio|Ohio]] placed corpses in "an eastern and western direction" within stone-lined cists covered by large flat stones (323-001).

## Theological Significance

The Watlala explanation for west-facing burial — that the road to the spirit land lies westward and a different orientation would confuse the deceased — represents the most explicitly documented theological rationale for burial orientation (770-001). Other tribes, while practicing similar orientation, did not always articulate the belief as clearly, though the consistency of west-facing head placement across geographically distant groups suggests a widespread cosmological conception.
