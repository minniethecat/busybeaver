# Canoe Burial

## Overview

Canoe burial constituted a prominent and distinctive form of sepulture among the fishing tribes of the Northwest Coast of North America, documented in considerable detail by Yarrow (1880). As one of the principal modes of "sepulture above the ground" — alongside lodge, house, box, scaffold, and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]] — canoe burial involved depositing the dead in canoes that were either elevated on posts, placed in trees, supported on the ground, or, less commonly, set adrift upon water (012-001)(016-001). The practice reflected the central role of the canoe in the economic and cultural life of these maritime peoples, transforming a vessel of sustenance into a vehicle for the final journey.

## Tribal Distribution

### Clallam and the Strait of Juan de Fuca

Among the [[clallam|Clallam]] people of Washington Territory, canoe burial was observed in vivid detail by the Reverend M. Eells, whose account Yarrow preserved at length. In one notable instance, a deceased woman was placed in a Hudson's Bay Company box serving as a coffin, which was then deposited in a canoe approximately twenty-five feet long. The canoe was elevated on posts and provisioned with "crackers, some crab-apples, and other food," along with small baskets and buckets — sustenance for the deceased in the afterworld (016-001)(016-002).

### Tsinuk and Columbia River Tribes

Upon the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]], the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] people and neighboring fishing tribes drew their dead-laden canoes into the woods at prominent points near their villages, placing them between the forks of trees or raising them from the ground on posts. After the smallpox epidemic of 1826, Captain Belcher of the British ship *Sulphur* reported that Corpse Island and Coffin Mount, as well as adjacent shores, were "studded not only with canoes, but at the period of our visit with numerous bodies" — a grim testament to the scale of the epidemic and the persistence of canoe burial even under catastrophic circumstances (016-005)(016-006).

### Makah and Puget Sound

On Puget Sound, canoe burial coexisted with other forms of sepulture. The [[makah|Makah]] of Cape Flattery covered their graves with rudely constructed boxes, while elsewhere on the Sound bodies were placed on elevated scaffolds. Those living near the water placed the dead in canoes; those at a distance from the shore adopted alternative methods (016-009).

## Variations

Canoe burial encompassed several distinct sub-forms. The most common involved drawing canoes into the woods and elevating them on posts or securing them between tree forks. A second form, practiced among the [[chinook|Chinook]] of the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]], involved burying the deceased directly in a bark canoe placed in the ground — a hybrid of canoe burial and inhumation (001-031). In Central America, among the Indians of the Mosquito Coast, [[bancroft-hubert-h|Bancroft]] recorded canoe burial in the ground as a common practice, with the corpse wrapped in cloth and placed in half of a pitpan (a type of dugout canoe) cut crosswise (001-034).

The practice of setting canoes adrift upon water — aquatic canoe burial — was occasionally observed but never constituted "a confirmed rite or ceremony" among North American Indians, though Yarrow noted that among the nations of antiquity it had been more systematically practiced (017-001).

## Historical Accounts

[[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], in his *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1877), provided what Yarrow considered the most comprehensive account of burial ceremonies among the Indians of [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington Territory. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] described how the common mode of disposing of the dead among fishing tribes was in canoes, noting that the canoes were of great size and value and were typically drawn into the woods at prominent points. For chiefs, the burial canoe was repainted and its ornaments renewed for a considerable length of time — a practice that reflected the enduring bond between the living and their honored dead (016-004)(016-005)(016-015).

Additional accounts from Santa Barbara, [[california|California]], documented the use of redwood canoes as coffins, with one skeleton discovered in 1875 interred in a canoe — though it was thought this individual may have been a noted fisherman rather than a representative of a widespread regional practice (001-033).

## Cultural Significance

The canoe, as a burial receptacle, embodied the maritime identity of the Northwest Coast peoples. For tribes whose livelihood depended upon the waters of the Pacific, the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]], and Puget Sound, the canoe was at once a tool of subsistence, a vehicle of trade and warfare, and ultimately a vessel for the passage from the world of the living to the realm of the dead. The provision of food, paddles, and other grave goods within the burial canoe underscored the conception of death as a journey — one for which the deceased required both transportation and sustenance.

The physical elevation of the canoe upon posts or in trees served a practical purpose — protection from wild animals — while simultaneously marking the burial as a form of [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]], lifting the dead toward the sky rather than consigning them to the earth (016-001)(014-001).
