## Overview

Kansas, in the central Great Plains, appears in Yarrow's 1880 monograph through a specific account of scaffold burial observed on the banks of Walnut [[creek|Creek]], illustrating the Plains tradition of elevated [[inhumation|interment]].

## Burial Sites & Practices

A detailed account of scaffold burial was recorded "on the banks of Walnut [[creek|Creek]], Kansas," where a burial platform was observed "elevated about eight feet from" the ground (014-015). The scaffold was constructed of poles lashed together with rawhide, supporting a wrapped body that had been placed there according to Plains tribal custom. The elevation served both practical and ceremonial purposes: protecting the remains from wolves and coyotes while facilitating the spirit's ascent.

Yarrow's entry for Kansas in his geographical index confirms the state as a documented location for aboriginal burial practices within the broader Plains mortuary tradition (014-014). The Kansas region, traversed by the Santa Fe and [[oregon|Oregon]] trails, was home to several Plains tribes whose burial customs reflected the mobile, equestrian lifestyle of the nineteenth-century Plains cultures.

### The Walnut Creek Scaffold: Sternberg's Account

The Walnut Creek burial was discovered by Brevet Major Sternberg, U.S. Army, and its contents were later inventoried at the Army Medical Museum in Washington, D.C. The case pertained to the [[cheyenne|Cheyennes]] of Kansas. Sternberg, noting "the unusual care manifested in the preparation of the case," inferred that "some important chief was inclosed in it" and determined to send the entire structure unopened to the Museum, believing that "articles of interest were inclosed with the body" whose value "would be enhanced if they were received at the Museum as left by the Indians" (014-015).

Upon opening the case at the Museum, the inventory revealed an elaborate multi-layered burial:

> "The case consisted of a cradle of interlaced branches of white willow, about 6 feet long, 3 feet broad, and 3 feet high, with a flooring of buffalo thongs arranged as a net-work. This cradle was securely fastened by strips of buffalo-hide to four poles of ironwood and cottonwood, about 12 feet in length." (014-015)

The wrappings were extraordinarily elaborate, comprising seven buffalo robes, five new blankets (two scarlet, two blue, and one white), a striped sack, and a United States Infantry overcoat. Beneath these outer layers were three hooded cloaks of buffalo-calf skin, each about four feet in length, elaborately decorated with bead-work in stripes — the outer in blue and white, the second in green and yellow, and the third in blue and red — all adorned with spherical brass bells attached by strings of beads (014-015).

The remains proved to be those of a child, approximately one year old. The body wore a beaver-cap ornamented with copper disks, long wampum necklaces incorporating *dentalium*, *unionida*, and *auricula* shells interspersed with beads, and strings of *Haliotis* shell pieces from the Gulf of California — highly valued trade items. The burial costume included a red-flannel cloak, a red tunic, beaded frock-leggins, red and black worsted yarn stockings, and deerskin beaded moccasins. Among the accompanying trinkets were a porcelain image, a China vase, numerous strings of beads, toys, mittens, a fur collar, and a pouch of mink skin (*putorius vison*) (014-015).

The extraordinary richness of the burial — seven robes, five blankets, three beaded cloaks, wampum, and trade goods from as far as the Gulf of California — suggests that the deceased child was of very high status, possibly the child of a Cheyenne chief or distinguished warrior. The inclusion of a U.S. Infantry overcoat among the wrappings indicates the burial took place during the period of active trade and military contact between Plains tribes and the United States.
