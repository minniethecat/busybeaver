# The Carolinas

## Overview

The Carolinas — encompassing the present-day states of North Carolina and South Carolina — were home to a rich diversity of Native American tribes at the time of European contact, including the [[catawba|Catawba]], [[santee|Santee]], Waxhaw, and numerous smaller groups. By the time of Yarrow's 1880 survey, most of these tribes had been decimated by disease, warfare, and displacement, but the documentary record — particularly the early eighteenth-century observations of [[lawson-john|John Lawson]] and later archaeological discoveries — preserved evidence of their sophisticated mortuary customs.

## Mortuary Customs of Carolina Tribes

### Earth Burial and Mound Construction

Yarrow (1880) records that "the Carolina tribes" practiced inhumation as their primary burial mode (024-120). Archaeological evidence documented in the monograph includes references to "North Carolina burial mounds," indicating that at least some groups constructed earthen tumuli over their dead (024-310). The mound-building tradition of the Carolina region connected these tribes to the broader [[mississippi|Mississippi]]an cultural complex that extended across the Southeast.

An early account preserved by Yarrow describes the general practice:

> Among the Carolina tribes, the burial of the dead was conducted with solemn ceremony, the body being interred in a sitting posture, facing the east, with the deceased's most valued possessions placed alongside (001-005).

### Santee Burial Customs

The [[congaree-santee-indians|Santee Indians]] of South Carolina provide one of the most detailed accounts preserved by Yarrow. Citing the eighteenth-century explorer and naturalist [[lawson-john|John Lawson]], Yarrow records a distinctive burial method:

> The Santee Indians of South Carolina, according to Lawson, used a specific form of scaffold and platform burial, the body being elevated above the ground for a prescribed period before final interment (006-014).

### Mummification in the Carolinas

One of the most remarkable findings documented by Yarrow is the reference to "South Carolina [[mummification|mummies]]" (024-368). The preservation of human remains through intentional or environmental processes was noted in several locations across the Southeast, including the Carolina region. The practice connected Carolina tribes to the broader [[mummification]] traditions documented among other groups in the monograph.

### Muscogulge Mortuary Practices

Yarrow preserves accounts "regarding the [[creek|Muscogulges]] of the Carolinas," a group whose burial customs reflected the broader Muskhogean pattern of scaffold exposure followed by secondary bone deposition — practices shared with their linguistic relatives the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] and [[creek|Creek]] (002-002).

### Virginia-Carolina-Florida Continuum

Yarrow identifies a cultural continuum in mortuary practices spanning "certain tribes of [[virginia|Virginia]], the Carolinas, and [[florida|Florida]]" (006-004). This coastal Southeast corridor exhibited shared features including scaffold burial, secondary bone processing, and the use of charnel structures, suggesting either common cultural origins or extensive inter-tribal contact.

## Archaeological Discoveries

The late nineteenth century saw increasing archaeological interest in Carolina burial sites. Yarrow notes a discovery in "Caldwell County, North Carolina, March 11, 1871, on the farm of a local resident," where a burial site yielded important evidence of prehistoric mortuary customs (004-036). Another account describes how "when recently in North Carolina his attention was called to a burial site" near the Moravian settlement of Salem, revealing continuing discoveries that enriched the ethnographic record (011-008)(014-026).

## Regional Significance

As a geographic region, the Carolinas exemplified the pattern that Yarrow's survey sought to document: a landscape in which diverse tribal groups, related by language and culture but distinct in their specific customs, had developed varied responses to the universal human challenge of death. The Carolina evidence also highlighted the importance of early documentary sources — particularly Lawson (1709) — in preserving customs that had largely vanished by Yarrow's time.
