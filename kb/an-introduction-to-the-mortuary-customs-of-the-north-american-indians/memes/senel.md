# Senel

## Overview

The Senel Indians inhabited the region of present-day northern [[california|California]], where they lived in proximity to the [[yuki|Yo-kai-a]] and other Pomoan-speaking groups. Yarrow (1880) documented Senel mortuary customs through the account of [[menard-john|John]] Tenney, an eye-witness to a condolence ceremony held by a Senel delegation for the deceased chief of the [[yuki|Yo-kai-a]]. This account provides a rare window into the inter-tribal diplomatic dimension of Native [[california|California]] [[mourning]] — the protocols by which one tribe honored the loss of another's leader through extended ceremonial observance.

## Mortuary Customs

### Cremation

Yarrow's index records the entry "Senel Indians, [[cremation]] of," indicating that [[cremation]] was the primary mode of mortuary disposal among the Senel (024-356). [[cremation]] was the dominant burial mode across much of aboriginal [[california|California]], practiced by tribes from the Pacific coast to the Sierra Nevada foothills. The Senel shared this custom with their Pomoan and [[yuki|Yuki]]an neighbors, making [[cremation]] a central element of their mortuary complex.

### The Condolence Ceremony

The most detailed account of Senel mortuary practice comes from a condolence visit paid by a Senel delegation to the [[yuki|Yo-kai-a]] following the death of the latter's chief. The ceremony, witnessed by [[menard-john|John]] Tenney, lasted three days and involved approximately two hundred participants. The ceremony took place in a specially constructed assembly house, its massive roof supported by peeled poles painted white and ringed with black and ornamented with crude devices. Wheat had been scattered across the floor to feed the spirit of the deceased captain.

The formal proceedings were supervised by "four officials connected with the building," assistants to the chief, who wore black vests trimmed with red flannel and shell ornaments. These served as officers of the assembly chamber. Additionally, an old man and a young woman functioned as priest and priestess, the latter dressed distinctively in white fabric covered with red flannel spots cut in neat figures and ornamented with shells — "it looked gorgeous and denoted some office" (Tenney, in Yarrow).

### Ritualized Wailing

The ceremony opened with a brief dedicatory service by the priest and priestess, after which the visitors were admitted and seated. The chief of the visiting Senel tribe made a brief speech, undoubtedly referring to the death of the [[yuki|Yo-kai-a]] chief and offering the sympathy of his tribe. Tenney observed that as the chief spoke, "some of the women scarcely refrained from crying out, and with difficulty they suppressed their sobs."

When the speech concluded, the entire assemblage — some two hundred people — burst forth into "a bitter [[mourning|wailing]], some screaming as if in agony." The sound was so overwhelming that Tenney "was compelled to stop my ears. The air was rent and pierced with their cries." This collective catharsis lasted between three and five minutes, at which point "at a given signal they ceased, wiped their eyes, and quieted down" (Tenney, in Yarrow).

### Economic Dimensions

The condolence ceremony carried significant economic implications for the host community. During the three days of the event, the Senel delegation were guests of the [[yuki|Yo-kai-a]], and the latter were "subjected to a considerable expense" in hosting them. This reciprocal obligation — the expectation that grieving communities would be supported by their allies — represents a social mechanism for distributing the material burden of mortuary ritual across the broader inter-tribal network.

## Historical Sources

The sole detailed account of Senel mortuary custom is that of [[menard-john|John]] Tenney, whose eye-witness description of the condolence ceremony was transmitted to Yarrow. Tenney's account captures the performative, diplomatic, and economic dimensions of [[california|California]] mortuary ritual — aspects easily overlooked in accounts focused exclusively on the physical disposal of the corpse. The Senel are referenced in Yarrow's index both under "Senel Indians" and specifically "Senel Indians, [[cremation]] of."
