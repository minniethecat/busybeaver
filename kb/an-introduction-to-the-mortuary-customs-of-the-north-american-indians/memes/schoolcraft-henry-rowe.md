# Henry Rowe Schoolcraft

## Overview

Henry Rowe Schoolcraft (1793–1864) was among the most influential early American ethnologists, whose comprehensive studies of Native American cultures provided foundational source material for subsequent generations of researchers. His magnum opus, the six-volume "Historical and Statistical Information Respecting the History, Condition and Prospects of the Indian Tribes of the United States" (1851–1857), appears repeatedly in Yarrow's citations, serving as a critical repository of mortuary customs observations collected from correspondents across the continent.

## Contributions

### The Schoolcraft Compendium

Schoolcraft's "History of the Indian Tribes" was more than a single-author monograph; it was a compendium that synthesized reports from military officers, Indian agents, physicians, and missionaries stationed throughout the frontier. Yarrow (1880) drew heavily upon this rich archive, citing Schoolcraft's work at multiple points throughout his own study.

Yarrow noted that [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]] contributed observations that were published "in Schoolcraft" rather than as independent publications, indicating the work's role as the central clearinghouse for ethnographic data in mid-nineteenth century America (010-016)(001-019). [[johnston-adam|Adam Johnston]] provided an account of mortuary customs "in Schoolcraft" as well (010-005).

### Specific Accounts Preserved

The Schoolcraft volumes preserved accounts that would otherwise have been lost to scholarship. Among these was a notable description extracted by Yarrow:

> From the subjoined extract from Schoolcraft, the following account of burial customs was obtained, demonstrating the systematic collection methodology that characterized his ethnographic enterprise (006-014).

### Theoretical Framework

Schoolcraft's theoretical approach, while rooted in the assumptions of his era, provided a comparative framework that Yarrow explicitly engaged with. Yarrow acknowledges his debt to "the investigations of those propounded by Schoolcraft in his well-known work on the Indian tribes," positioning his own monograph as building upon and extending Schoolcraft's foundational research program (022-001).

Schoolcraft's importance to Yarrow's work extended beyond individual citations. His questionnaire methodology — distributing standardized inquiries to correspondents in the field — provided the direct model for Yarrow's own circular of questions, establishing a tradition of systematic ethnographic data collection that shaped American anthropology for decades.

## Legacy

Schoolcraft died in 1864, sixteen years before Yarrow's monograph was published, but his influence permeates the work. The index to Yarrow's volume lists "Schoolcraft, [[gillman-henry|Henry]] R." as a cited authority (024-354). His compilation methodology, his network of field correspondents, and his commitment to preserving accounts of cultures undergoing rapid transformation all prefigured the approach Yarrow would adopt for his mortuary customs survey.
