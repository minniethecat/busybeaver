# Sioux

## Overview

The Sioux, or [[dakota|Dakota]], were among the most extensively documented tribes in Yarrow's survey of North American mortuary customs. Occupying the vast territory of the northern Great Plains — present-day Minnesota, the [[dakota|Dakotas]], Montana, and Nebraska — the Sioux practiced a diverse range of burial modes including lodge burial, scaffold burial, and [[scaffold-burial|tree burial]], with elaborate [[mourning]] rituals that drew the attention of multiple military and civilian observers in the mid-nineteenth century (012-001)(014-007)(019-010).

## Mortuary Customs

### Lodge Burial
Stansbury, in his *Explorations of the Valley of the Great Salt Lake of [[utah|Utah]]* (1852), described an encounter with Sioux lodge burials on the open prairie: five lodges containing the bodies of nine Sioux, laid out on the ground, wrapped in buffalo-skin robes, with saddles, spears, camp-kettles, and all accoutrements piled around them. Some lodges contained three bodies, others only one. The deceased were left in their tipis as they had lived, surrounded by the implements of daily life. The [[Blackfeet]] and [[Navajos]] also practiced similar lodge burial (002-003)(002-004).

### Scaffold and Tree Burial
Among the Sioux bands, scaffold burial was widely practiced. [[turner-ls|Dr. L. S.]] [[turner-ls|Turner]], stationed at Fort Peck, Montana, provided a detailed account: "The [[dakota|Dakotas]] bury their dead in the tops of trees when possible," placing the wrapped body on an elevated scaffold constructed of poles. The practice was driven by practical necessity — in a region with abundant wolves and bears, ground burial risked exhumation by scavengers. Small buckets and baskets hung on the scaffolds contained food and drink for the deceased's journey (014-001)(014-007).

### Burial Above Ground
Some Sioux bands placed their dead above ground without scaffolding — the body simply laid on the surface, wrapped in robes, with possessions arranged around it. This approach, documented in the chapter on burial above ground, was among the simplest forms of Sioux mortuary practice and was typically employed for common tribespeople rather than chiefs or distinguished warriors (012-001)(012-002).

## Mourning Observances

Sioux [[mourning]] customs were notably elaborate. Col. P. [[gardner-w|W.]] Norris, superintendent of Yellowstone National Park, witnessed in 1876 the "Ghost Gamble" — a ceremonial practice in which mourners wagered possessions on games of chance, believing the spirits of the dead influenced the outcomes. Dr. [[turner-ls|Turner]] documented extensive [[mourning]] observances among the [[dakota|Dakotas]], including the cutting of hair, self-laceration, and the [[sacrifice]] of horses at the burial site — practices with parallels across the globe (019-008)(019-010).

## Historical Sources

The Sioux benefit from unusually rich documentation in Yarrow: Stansbury (1852) on lodge burial, [[turner-ls|Dr. L. S.]] [[turner-ls|Turner]] on [[dakota|Dakota]] scaffold burial and [[mourning]], Col. Norris (1876) on the Ghost Gamble, and Belden's *Life of Belden, the White Chief* (1871) on [[yanktonais|Yanktonais]] scaffold burial. This wealth of sources reflects the intensity of U.S. military and scientific contact with the Sioux during the Plains Indian Wars period (002-003)(014-007)(019-008).
