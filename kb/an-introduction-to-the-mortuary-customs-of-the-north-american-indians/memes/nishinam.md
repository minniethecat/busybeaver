# Nishinam

## Overview

The Nishinams, or Nisenan, inhabited the American River and Bear River drainages of present-day [[california|California]]. Their mortuary customs, documented in Yarrow (1880) through the ethnographic work of [[powers-stephen|Stephen Powers]], centered on [[cremation]] — a practice widespread among [[california|California]] tribes. The Nishinams are distinguished in the Yarrow survey by their possession of a detailed origin myth accounting for the introduction of [[cremation]], one of the few explicitly mythological treatments of mortuary practice in the monograph.

## Mortuary Customs

### Cremation Practice

The Nishinams practiced [[cremation]] as their primary mode of disposing of the dead, a custom shared with many other [[california|California]] tribes. [[powers-stephen|Powers]] recorded that the Nishinams "to this day set great store" by the traditions surrounding the origin of their [[cremation]] practice, suggesting that the mythological narrative continued to inform the ritual behavior of the tribe at the time of his fieldwork (010-004).

### Cremation Origin Myth

[[powers-stephen|Powers]] preserved a myth accounting for how the Nishinams first acquired fire for the purpose of cremating their dead. In the myth, a member of the tribe died, and the people, lacking knowledge of fire, were uncertain how to dispose of the body. A supernatural being — a coyote or culture hero, following common [[california|California]] mythological patterns — intervened to provide fire and instruct the people in the proper method of [[cremation]] (010-002).

The myth established [[cremation]] as a divinely sanctioned practice, distinguishing it from mere practical disposal. The Nishinams' retention of this narrative into the historic period, and the "great store" they set by it, indicates that [[cremation]] among them was not merely a technical method but a ritually significant act embedded in a cosmological framework that linked the living, the dead, and the supernatural (010-002)(010-004).

## Historical Sources

The documentation of Nishinam mortuary customs in Yarrow derives entirely from [[powers-stephen|Stephen Powers]], whose *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1877) provided one of the earliest systematic surveys of [[california|California]] Indian cultures. [[powers-stephen|Powers]]' fieldwork with the Nishinams captured both the material practice of [[cremation]] and its accompanying mythology — a rare instance in which the Yarrow monograph preserves not only the "how" of a burial custom but also the indigenous explanation of its origin.
