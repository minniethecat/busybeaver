# Tol-kotin Funeral Ceremonies

The Tolkotins (or Tol-kotin) were a Native American tribe of [[oregon|Oregon]] whose funeral ceremonies were documented by Ross Cox in his 1831 account _Adventures on the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]]_.

## Ceremonial Process

The funeral ceremonies of the Tolkotins were notably elaborate and distinctive. Upon a death, the body of the deceased was kept for nine days laid out in the deceased's lodge. On the tenth day, burial preparations commenced. A rising ground was selected as the site, upon which a number of cypress sticks, about seven feet long and neatly split, were laid. In the interstices between the sticks, gummy wood was placed to facilitate [[cremation|burning]]. During these preparations, invitations were dispatched to neighboring villages requesting attendance at the ceremony. [010-007] [010-008]

When the preparations were complete, the corpse was placed on the pile and immediately ignited. During the [[cremation|burning]] process, the bystanders appeared to be in a high state of merriment. If a stranger happened to be present, they would invariably plunder him; if denied that pleasure, they never separated without quarreling among themselves. [010-008]

## Property and Offerings

Whatever property the deceased possessed was placed about the corpse on the pyre. If the deceased happened to be a person of consequence, the friends would generally purchase a capote, a shirt, a pair of trousers, and similar articles, which were also laid around the pile. [010-008]

## Role of the Doctor

If the doctor who had attended the deceased had escaped uninjured, he was obliged to be present at the ceremony and, for the last time, try his skill in restoring the defunct to animation. Failing in this, he threw on the body a piece of leather or some other article as a present, which in some measure appeased the resentment of the relatives and preserved the unfortunate practitioner from being maltreated. [010-008]

## Widow's Ordeal

The treatment of the widow during the Tolkotin funeral rites was particularly severe. During the nine days the corpse was laid out, the widow was obliged to sleep alongside it from sunset to sunrise, with no relaxation from this custom even during the hottest days of summer. While the doctor performed his last operations, she was required to lie on the pile. After the fire was applied, she could not stir until the doctor ordered her removal — which was never done until her body was completely covered with blisters. [010-008] [010-009]

After being placed on her legs, the widow was obliged to pass her hands gently through the flame and collect some of the liquid fat issuing from the corpse, with which she was permitted to wet her face and body. When the friends of the deceased observed the sinews of the legs and arms beginning to contract, they compelled the unfortunate widow to go again onto the pile and, by dint of hard pressing, straighten those members. [010-008] [010-009]

## Post-Cremation Practices

After the [[cremation|burning]] of the corpse had terminated, the widow collected the larger bones, which she rolled up in an envelope of birch bark. She was obliged for some years afterwards to carry these bones with her wherever she went. [010-010]

## Treatment of Men

The men of the Tolkotins were condemned to a similar ordeal following a death, but according to Cox they did not bear it with equal fortitude. Many fled to distant quarters to avoid the brutal treatment which custom had established as a kind of religious rite. [010-011]

## Comparative Notes

The Tolkotin [[cremation]] practice shares features with other North American tribes: the nine-day retention of the corpse (unusually long by general Indian practice, and likely intended to allow relatives to assemble), the destruction of property on the pyre, and the harsh treatment of the widow — which appears to be a modification of the [[hind-hy|Hind]]u [[sacrifice|suttee]]. The hilarity displayed during the [[cremation|burning]] is similar to Japanese funeral customs, where attendees rejoice that the troubles of the world are over for the fortunate dead. The plundering of strangers was also practiced among the Indians of the [[carolina|Carolinas]]. [010-012] [010-014]
