## Overview

Tree burial — the deposition of the dead within or upon trees — constituted one of the principal forms of "[[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]] proper," alongside scaffold burial, and was "quite extensively practiced even at the present time" according to Yarrow (1880). The choice between trees and constructed scaffolds depended largely on environmental circumstances: "where timber abounds, trees are used; if absent, scaffolds are employed" (014-001). The practice encompassed several distinct variants, from fastening wrapped bodies high in tree branches, to depositing remains within hollow trunks, to the elaborate construction of split-log coffins suspended between trees — each variant reflecting local arboreal resources and cultural preferences.

## Tribal Distribution

### Blackfeet

Among the [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]] of Montana, tree-burial was the customary mode of disposing of the dead until the reservation period. [[menard-john|John]] Young, Indian agent at the [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]] Agency, provided Yarrow with a detailed account: the deceased was enclosed in the finest robes or blankets owned by the departed, closely sewed up, and then — "if a male or chief" — fastened in the branches of a tree "so high as to be beyond the reach of wolves, and then left to slowly waste in the dry winds." Weapons, pipes, and other personal possessions were enclosed with the body. The women relatives would gather on elevated spots near the burial tree and maintain a "dismal wail" through the greater part of the night, a [[mourning]] observance that continued for up to a month (014-003)(014-004)(014-006).

### Sioux (Dakotas)

The [[dakota|Dakotas]], according to [[turner-ls|Dr. L. S.]] [[turner-ls|Turner]] of Fort Peck, Montana, "bury their dead in the tops of trees when limbs can be found sufficiently horizontal to support scaffolding on which to lay the body." However, as such arboreal growth was uncommon on the [[dakota|Dakota]] plains, the more general practice was to construct scaffolds seven to ten feet high. This adaptive flexibility — trees where available, scaffolds where not — illustrated the pragmatic basis of [[scaffold-burial|aerial sepulture]] among Plains tribes (014-019).

### Loucheux (British America)

[[gardner-w|W.]] L. Hardisty provided Yarrow with "a curious example of [[surface-burial|log-burial]] in trees" practiced by the Loucheux of British America. The body was enclosed in a neatly hollowed piece of wood — a log about eight feet long, split in two, each half carefully hollowed out to the required size — and the two pieces were then lashed together and secured to two or more trees, about six feet from the ground. A small aperture was cut in the log through which food and drink could be offered to the deceased. This elaborate form of tree-burial combined elements of both coffin-making and arboreal deposition (014-027)(014-028).

### Seminoles and Miamis

In the Eastern Woodlands, certain tribes practiced surface burial in hollow trees and logs. The [[seminoles|Seminoles]] of [[florida|Florida]] were reported to have buried in hollow trees, the bodies "being placed in an upright position" — a practice that Yarrow suggested was often "employed as a temporary expedient when the survivors were pressed for time" (008-001). The [[miami|Miamis]] were documented as practicing surface burial in two forms: first, in hollow logs lying on the ground, the dead being "crammed into" the log cavity; and second, in log pens constructed as miniature cabins, "drawing in every course until they meet in a single log at the top" (008-002)(008-003).

## Historical and Comparative Context

Tree-burial, Yarrow noted, was by no means unique to North America. The practice possessed considerable antiquity in the Old World: "the Colchiens enveloped their dead in sacks of skin and hung them to trees; the ancient Tartars and Scythians did the same" (014-023). This cross-cultural recurrence of arboreal sepulture suggested to Yarrow that the practice answered a fundamental human impulse — the desire to elevate the dead above the reach of terrestrial scavengers and, perhaps, closer to the celestial realm. Among the [[roman|Roman]]s, the custom of *conclamatio* — loudly calling the name of the deceased — paralleled the [[mourning]] wails of [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]] women at tree-burial sites, further suggesting universals in mortuary behavior (014-024)(014-026).

## Cultural Significance

Yarrow offered a provocative hypothesis for the origin of tree and scaffold burial among American aborigines: "most if not all the tribes of North America were at one time habituated to the practice of forming artificial and natural caves in the sides of hills and rocks." When tribes migrated into alluvial plains — "the vast delta of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]]" — where neither rocks nor natural elevations existed, they turned to trees and scaffolds as elevated alternatives that simulated "the firmamental and symbolic use of caverns and fissures" (014-030). This diffusionist theory, while speculative, situated tree-burial within a broader evolutionary framework of mortuary adaptation to environment.
