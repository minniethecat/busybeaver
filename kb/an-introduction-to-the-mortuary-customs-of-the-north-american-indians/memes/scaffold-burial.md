# Scaffold Burial

## Overview

Scaffold and tree burial — collectively termed "aerial sepulture" — was one of the most extensively practiced modes of disposing of the dead among North American Indians, particularly on the Great Plains and in the timbered regions of the Northwest. The choice between trees and scaffolds depended largely on available materials: where timber abounded, tree branches were used; where it was scarce, purpose-built scaffolds were constructed (014-001)(014-002).

## Tribal Distribution

### Yanktonais (Sioux)
Among the [[Yanktonais]], scaffolds were constructed 7 to 8 feet high, 10 feet long, and 4 to 5 feet wide. Four stout forked posts were set firmly in the ground, with cross and side poles laid in the forks to form a flooring of small poles. The body was carefully wrapped to be watertight before being laid to rest upon the poles. Small buckets and baskets containing food and drink for the dead were hung on the scaffolds — provisions for the soul's journey (014-001)(014-003).

### Blackfeet
[[menard-john|John]] Young, Indian agent at the [[Blackfeet]] Agency, Montana, described [[tree-burial]] among the tribe: the dead body, enclosed in the finest robes or blankets owned by the deceased, was sewed up tightly and fastened high in the branches of a tree, beyond the reach of wolves. The body was left to slowly waste in the dry winds. Weapons and pipes of men, and small toys of children, were enclosed with them (014-005)(014-006).

### Sioux
The [[Sioux]] practiced burial above ground, with scaffolds serving as the primary mode in many bands. The practice was partly practical — in regions where wolves and bears were numerous, bodies buried underground would be dug up and devoured. Ravens and other scavengers, which consumed offerings left on scaffolds, were sometimes interpreted by the bereaved as evidence that the dead consumed the provisions (014-003)(014-004).

## Variations

The practice encompassed several sub-forms. **Tree burial** proper involved fastening the wrapped body directly in tree branches. **Scaffold burial** used constructed platforms. **Canoe burial** placed the body in a canoe elevated on poles or in trees. **Box burial** used constructed boxes placed above ground. Some tribes combined scaffold burial with later [[ossuary]] collection — after the flesh had decayed, the bones were gathered and placed in communal [[burial-vaults|bone-houses]] or ossuaries (014-010)(015-001).

## Historical Accounts

Belden's *Life of Belden, the White Chief* (1871) provided detailed eyewitness accounts of [[yanktonais|Yanktonais]] scaffold burial practices. [[menard-john|John]] Young's reports from the [[blackfeet|Blackfeet]] Agency offered equally valuable documentation from the Montana territory. Accounts consistently noted the practical rationale — protection from scavenging animals — as well as the ceremonial aspects, including offerings of food and the ritual [[mourning]] that accompanied scaffold deposition (014-001)(014-005).
