## Overview

Moses Fiske was an early nineteenth-century American antiquarian whose observations of indigenous burial sites in [[tennessee|Tennessee]] provided some of the earliest systematic descriptions of stone cist graves in the region. His account, published in the *Transactions of the American Antiquarian Society* (1820, vol. 1, p. 302), was cited by Yarrow in the 1880 monograph as evidence of deliberate care taken by Native Americans in constructing burial receptacles.

## Contributions

Fiske described numerous burying grounds in West [[tennessee|Tennessee]] containing regular graves dug twelve to eighteen inches deep, with stone slabs placed at the bottom, ends, and sides to form a kind of stone coffin (003-001). After laying in the body, the grave was covered with earth. Yarrow noted that cist graves of almost identical construction were found among men of the reindeer period near Solutré, France, suggesting a widespread and ancient mortuary tradition. The [[tennessee|Tennessee]] cist graves, however, were shallower than their European counterparts — a difference Yarrow attributed to centuries of soil deposition at the older French sites (003-003).

## Accounts Provided

Fiske's description of West [[tennessee|Tennessee]] stone cist graves contributed to Yarrow's chapter on cist burial (Chapter III). The account demonstrated the care taken by Native Americans to provide the dead with what they considered a suitable resting place, utilizing locally available flat stones to construct coffin-like receptacles within excavated graves.
