# Iroquois

## Overview

The Iroquois, or Haudenosaunee, constituted the most politically sophisticated confederacy in northeastern North America, comprising originally five and later six nations — the [[mohawk|Mohawk]], Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and Tuscarora. Their territory extended across present-day [[new-york|New York]] State and adjacent regions. [[morgan-lh|Lewis Henry Morgan]]'s foundational ethnographic study, *League of the Iroquois* (1851), provided Yarrow with the principal source for Iroquois mortuary customs, preserving detailed accounts of scaffold burial, [[ossuary]] [[inhumation|interment]], and the ceremonial burial dance that marked the community's final farewell to the dead.

## Mortuary Customs

### Scaffold Exposure and Secondary Bone Collection

The Iroquois practiced a distinctive two-stage burial process that began with scaffold exposure. [[morgan-lh|Morgan]]'s account, quoted by Yarrow, describes the initial disposition:

> The body of the deceased was exposed upon a bark scaffolding erected upon poles or secured upon the limbs of trees, where it was left to waste to a skeleton. After this had been effected by the process of decomposition in the open air, the bones were removed either to the former house of the deceased, or to a small bark-house by its side, prepared for their reception (015-009).

The preservation of skeletal remains within or adjacent to the family dwelling meant that "the skeletons of the whole family were preserved from generation to generation by the filial or parental affection of the living" (015-009). This practice of keeping ancestral bones close to the domestic sphere reflects a conception of death in which the deceased remained integrated within the household rather than being permanently separated from the living community.

### Communal Ossuary Interment

The second stage of Iroquois mortuary practice occurred at longer intervals — typically in times of community relocation or public insecurity — when the accumulated family skeletons were gathered for collective [[inhumation|interment]]:

> After the lapse of a number of years, or in a season of public insecurity, or on the eve of abandoning a settlement, it was customary to collect these skeletons from the whole community around and consign them to a common resting place (015-011).

Yarrow explicitly connects this custom to the broader archaeological record of eastern North America, noting that "to this custom, which is not confined to the Iroquois, is doubtless to be ascribed the barrows and bone-mounds which have been found in such numbers in various parts of the country" (015-011). The [[ossuary]] mounds, when excavated, typically revealed skeletons "arranged in horizontal layers, a conical pyramid, those in each layer radiating from a common center," while in other cases the remains were "found placed promiscuously" (015-011).

### The Burial Dance

A distinctive ceremonial element of Iroquois mortuary practice was the burial dance, recorded in Yarrow's index under "Iroquois, burial dance of" (024-230) and "dance, Iroquois" (024-053). While the specific choreography of this dance is not elaborated in Yarrow's sources, the notation of a dance specific to burial rites underscores the performative and communal nature of Iroquois funerary practice. The dance likely served to mark the transition of the deceased from the world of the living and to reinforce social bonds among the participants, functioning as an integral component of the larger [[ossuary]] ceremony.

## Historical Sources

Yarrow's treatment of Iroquois mortuary customs draws primarily from:

| Source | Contribution | Date |
|--------|-------------|------|
| [[morgan-lh|Lewis Henry Morgan]] | *League of the Iroquois* — scaffold burial, bone collection, [[ossuary]] [[inhumation|interment]] | 1851 |
| [[brinton-dg|D. G. Brinton]] | *Myths of the New World* — collected bone [[inhumation|interment]], periodicity of [[ossuary]] ceremonies | 1868 |

[[morgan-lh|Morgan]]'s account is the most authoritative, based on direct observation and sustained engagement with Iroquois communities. [[brinton-dg|Brinton]]'s comparative observations situate Iroquois practice within the broader Eastern Woodlands pattern of periodic communal [[secondary-burial|reburial]], which he notes occurred "usually once in eight or ten years" across nations east of the [[mississippi|Mississippi]] (015-011).
