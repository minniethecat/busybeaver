# W. Gardner

## Overview

Dr. W. Gardner was a surgeon in the United States Army during the latter half of the nineteenth century. His observations on American Indian mortuary customs were recorded by Yarrow in the 1880 monograph, where Gardner's theoretical reflections on scaffold burial — linking the practice to a bird-shaped conception of the soul — were presented as a notable interpretative contribution from a military-medical observer serving in the field (014-029).

## Contributions

Gardner contributed to Yarrow's chapter on scaffold burial by advancing a theory that explained the practice of elevated corpse deposition in symbolic rather than merely practical terms. According to Gardner, the widespread Indigenous custom of placing the dead upon scaffolds or in the branches of trees was connected to a belief that the human soul possessed the form and nature of a bird. Because birds are essentially arboreal in their habits, Gardner reasoned that a soul-bird would have readier access to its former dwelling-place if the body were elevated on a tree or scaffold rather than buried in the earth. He further suggested that this elevated position would keep the body secure from wolves and other profane beasts, enabling the souls of the dead to act as sentinels guarding the homes and hunting-grounds of their loved ones (014-029).

The Gardner hypothesis, while speculative in character, represents an early attempt within American ethnology to interpret mortuary practices through the lens of Indigenous cosmology rather than dismissing them as merely curious or primitive. Yarrow presented Gardner's remarks without endorsement but clearly considered them worthy of inclusion alongside documentary descriptions — a testament to the seriousness with which frontier medical officers were sometimes regarded as informal ethnographers (014-029).

## Accounts Provided

Gardner's communication to Yarrow consists of the theoretical interpretation of scaffold burial reproduced above. No additional case reports or field descriptions from Gardner appear in the 1880 monograph.
