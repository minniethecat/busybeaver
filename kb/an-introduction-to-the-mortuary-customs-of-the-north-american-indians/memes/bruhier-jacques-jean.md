# Jacques Jean Bruhier

## Overview

Jacques Jean Bruhier was an eighteenth-century French physician and medical writer whose principal work, *L'incertitude des Signes de la Mort* (The Uncertainty of the Signs of Death), published in 1740 and subsequently expanded, compiled an extraordinary range of mortuary customs from antiquity and the early modern world. Yarrow, who read Bruhier in the original French, drew upon his work repeatedly throughout the 1880 monograph, citing Bruhier as a comparative authority on customs ranging from Persian exposure of the dead to beasts, to Ethiopian corpse preservation, to Corsican widow-beating, to the interpretation of funeral [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s as safeguards against premature burial (001-012)(014-024).

## Contributions

Bruhier's most significant contribution to Yarrow's study was his documentation of the practice of exposing the dead to wild beasts among the ancient Persians, Parthians, Medes, Iberians, Caspians, and other peoples. Bruhier recorded that these groups "had such a horror and aversion of the corruption and decomposition of the dead and of their being eaten by worms that they threw out the bodies into the open fields to be devoured by wild beasts," believing that persons so consumed would "enjoy at least a partial sort of life in their living sepulchres." He further noted that the Bactrians and Hircanians trained dogs specifically for this purpose, known as *Canes sepulchrales*, which received the greatest care and attention because it was "deemed proper that the souls of the deceased should have strong and lusty frames to dwell in" (001-012)(001-018).

Yarrow drew on Bruhier for a striking comparative observation concerning funeral [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s. Bruhier argued that the loud cries and [[death-wailing|lamentation]]s characteristic of [[mourning]] ceremonies across cultures may have had in early periods a significance beyond the mere expression of grief — namely, to prevent premature burial. He interpreted the vigorous vocal demonstrations as a practical safeguard against interring a person who might yet show signs of life, a concern hardly academic in an era before reliable clinical determination of death (014-024).

Bruhier documented a less sympathetic custom observed in Corsica as late as 1743: upon a husband's death, women of the community "threw themselves upon the widow and beat her severely." Bruhier's dry assessment — that this custom "obliged women to take good care of their husbands" — captured an instance of mortuary violence that Yarrow cited in discussing the general relationship between [[mourning]] ritual and social coercion. Bruhier also recorded that the ancient Ethiopians covered the corpses of their dead with plaster, a practice Yarrow compared to the mud-and-clay coverings he had observed or read of among certain Native American groups (011-009)(010-015).

Bruhier's compendious and unsystematic methods — he drew freely from earlier authorities including the seventeenth-century writer [[muret-pierre|Pierre Muret]] — made his work a repository of both reliable comparative data and unchecked travelers' tales. Yarrow was consequently selective in his use of Bruhier, typically citing him alongside other corroborating sources such as Lafitau and [[muret-pierre|Muret]] (018-001).
