# P. de Brebeuf

## Overview

Jean de Brébeuf (1593–1649), known in the Yarrow text as P. de Brebeuf, was a French Jesuit missionary who spent nearly two decades among the [[huron|Huron]] ([[huron|Wendat]]) people of present-day Ontario before his capture and martyrdom during the [[iroquois|Iroquois]] wars. His detailed ethnographic writings, published in the *Jesuit Relations*, constitute some of the earliest and most valuable European records of [[huron|Huron]] society. For Yarrow's monograph, Brebeuf's eyewitness account of the [[huron|Huron]] Feast of the Dead — a collective secondary burial ceremony involving the dis[[inhumation|interment]] and communal [[secondary-burial|reburial]] of the dead from multiple villages — provided a canonical example of the [[ossuary]] tradition among the indigenous peoples of eastern North America (020-004, 020-005).

## Contributions

Brebeuf personally assisted at one of the great Feasts of the Dead held at the village of Ossosane, an event that took place before the dispersal of the [[huron|Huron]] nation. His account records that the ceremony assembled approximately two thousand Indians, who offered twelve hundred presents at the common tomb as testimony of their grief — a scale of material tribute that conveys both the ceremonial importance of the event and the social resources mobilized across the five participating villages (020-005).

The bones of the dead from all five villages were deposited in a single gigantic shroud composed of forty-eight robes, each robe itself made from ten beaver skins. After being carefully wrapped in this communal burial wrapping, the remains were placed between layers of moss and bark. A wall of stones was then constructed around the vast [[ossuary]] to protect it from profanation. Before earth was cast over the bones, the women present threw a few grains of Indian corn upon the sacred relics — a ritual gesture that Brebeuf recorded without interpretive overlay, preserving its ethnographic detail for later analysis (020-005).

Brebeuf also documented the [[huron|Huron]] theological framework surrounding the ceremony. According to [[huron|Huron]] belief as he recorded it, the souls of the dead remained near their bodies until the Feast of the Dead was performed. Only after the completion of this ceremony did the souls become free to depart at once for the land of spirits, which the [[huron|Huron]] believed to be situated in the regions of the setting sun. This linkage between physical [[secondary-burial|reburial]] and spiritual liberation — in which eschatological release was ritually contingent on the collective treatment of skeletal remains — represents a precise correlation between mortuary practice and cosmological belief that Yarrow preserved in his discussion of burial feasts and their significance (020-005).

Brebeuf's account was transmitted to Yarrow through the intermediary of [[hind-hy|Henry Youle Hind]]'s *Canadian Red River Exploring Expedition* (1860), which had excerpted the Brebeuf passage as a comparative reference for the burial customs [[hind-hy|Hind]] observed among the Indigenous peoples of the Canadian West. The chain of citation — Brébeuf (1630s) → [[hind-hy|Hind]] (1860) → Yarrow (1880) — illustrates the deep temporal reach of the Jesuit missionary record in nineteenth-century American ethnology (020-004).

## Accounts Provided

Brebeuf's Ossosane account documents the [[huron|Huron]] Feast of the Dead in its procedural, material, and cosmological dimensions: the multi-village assembly, the beaver-robe shroud, the stone wall, the corn offering, and the theological doctrine of soul liberation through collective [[secondary-burial|reburial]]. As the earliest first-hand European description of an Iroquoian [[ossuary]] ceremony in the Yarrow corpus, it anchors the historical depth of the scaffold-to-[[bone-house]] burial tradition.
