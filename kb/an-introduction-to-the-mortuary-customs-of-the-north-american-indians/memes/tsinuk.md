# Tsinuk

## Overview

The Tsinuk, or [[chinook|Chinook]], inhabited the lower [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] near its confluence with the Pacific Ocean, and were among the most prominent of the fishing tribes of the Northwest Coast. Yarrow (1880) devoted considerable attention to their mortuary customs, drawing upon the observations of early explorers — Lewis and [[clarke-william|Clark]], Vancouver's lieutenant Broughton, Captain Wilkes, and Captain Belcher — as well as the detailed ethnographic notes of [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]]. The Tsinuk maintained two of the most celebrated indigenous cemeteries on the Columbia: Mount Coffin and Coffin Rock.

## Historical Sources

The principal sources on Tsinuk mortuary customs are the journals of Lewis and [[clarke-william|Clark]] (1805–1806), the observations of Captain Belcher (1839), and the accounts assembled by Captain Charles Wilkes's United States Exploring Expedition. The most systematic ethnographic treatment, however, comes from [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]], whose extended residence in the Pacific Northwest in the 1850s allowed him to observe the customs described and to interview living Tsinuk informants. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]]'s account was transmitted to Yarrow and forms the core of the Tsinuk material in the 1880 monograph.

## Mortuary Customs

### Canoe Burial and Mount Coffin

The common mode of disposing of the dead among the fishing tribes of the Columbia was canoe burial. The vessels were drawn into the woods at prominent points a short distance from the village, sometimes placed between the forks of trees or raised from the ground on posts. The Tsinuk maintained two especially noted cemeteries: Mount Coffin, a high isolated bluff about three miles below the mouth of the Cowlitz River, and Coffin Rock, some distance above. The growth of Mount Coffin as a burial site appears to have accelerated dramatically over the early nineteenth century. Lieutenant Broughton, who explored the Columbia with Vancouver, noted only several canoes at the site; Lewis and [[clarke-william|Clark]], traversing the river shortly thereafter, recorded the mount but did not remark upon the canoes at all. Yet by the time of Captain Wilkes's United States Exploring Expedition, it was conjectured that no fewer than three thousand canoes containing human remains were deposited there. The entire assemblage was subsequently destroyed by a fire caused by the carelessness of a member of Wilkes's party, to the great indignation of the Tsinuk people (016-005).

Captain Belcher of the British ship *Sulphur*, visiting the Columbia in 1839, noted that the smallpox epidemic of 1826 and subsequent outbreaks of ague had greatly accelerated the accumulation of remains: Corpse Island and Coffin Mount, as well as the adjacent shores, were studded not only with canoes but with skulls and skeletons strewed about in all directions (016-005).

### Vault Sepulchers at the Cascades

Farther up the Columbia, at the Cascades, the Tsinuk and their affiliates adopted a different form of [[inhumation|interment]], described in detail by Captain [[bartram-william|William]] [[clarke-william|Clark]]. The burial-place consisted of eight vaults constructed of pine or cedar boards closely connected, each approximately eight feet square and six feet in height, with the tops securely covered by wide boards sloping slightly to convey off the rain. All were oriented east and west, with the door on the eastern side partially stopped with wide boards decorated with rude pictures of men and other animals (016-008).

Inside these vaults [[clarke-william|Clark]] found bodies carefully wrapped in skins, tied with cords of grass and bark, lying on mats in an east-west orientation. Some vaults contained bones piled to a height of four feet. Upon the tops of the vaults and on attached poles hung brass kettles and frying-pans with holes in their bottoms, baskets, bowls, sea-shells, skins, pieces of cloth, hair bags of trinkets, and small bones — all offerings of friendship or affection preserved by pious veneration. The walls and doors were decorated with strange figures cut and painted, and wooden images of men — some so old and decayed as to have almost lost their shape — were placed against the sides of the vaults, most probably intended as resemblances of the deceased (016-008).

The Watlala, a tribe of the Upper Tsinuk whose burial place [[clarke-william|Clark]] described, were by [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]]'s time nearly extinct, though a number of the [[burial-vaults|sepulchers]] remained in varying states of preservation. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] observed that the position of the body — the head always placed to the west — was of universal observance among the tribes of the region. The reason given to him was that the road to the *me-mel-us-illa-hee*, the country of the dead, lay toward the west, and placing the head otherwise would confuse the spirit on its journey (016-008).

### Tamahno-us Boards and Effigies

Among the Tsinuk and the neighboring [[tsihalis|Tsihalis]], the *tamahno-us* board — a carved panel associated with the guardian-spirit complex of the region — was placed near the deceased. The Puget Sound tribes, who did not manufacture these boards, sometimes constructed effigies of their chiefs resembling the person as nearly as possible, dressed in the deceased's usual costume and wearing the articles of which he was fond. One such effigy, representing the Skagit chief Sneestum, stood conspicuously upon a high bank on the eastern side of Whidbey Island (016-013).

The most valuable articles of property were placed in or hung up around the grave, having been first carefully rendered unserviceable — a practice reflecting the broader Northwest Coast belief that objects, like persons, had spirit counterparts that must be dispatched to the [[spirit-land|afterlife]]. The living family were literally stripped of possessions to do honor to the dead, an act of self-denial that impressed [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] with its sincerity. The graves of women were distinguished by a cup, a kamas stick, or other implements of their domestic occupation (016-013).

### Slave Sacrifice

Slaves were killed in proportion to the rank and wealth of the deceased. In some instances they were starved to death, or tied to the dead body and left to perish. [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]] recorded that this practice, though almost entirely abandoned by the time of his observations, had persisted into the 1850s: in 1853, Toke, a Tsinuk chief living at Shoalwater Bay, undertook to kill a slave girl belonging to his daughter, who in dying had requested the [[sacrifice]]. The woman fled and was discovered by citizens in the woods half-starved. When her master attempted to reclaim her, he was soundly thrashed by the white settlers and warned against a second attempt (016-014).

### Aquatic Disposal of Slaves

According to a separate passage in Yarrow, the [[chinook|Chinooks]] were also among the tribes that disposed of dead slaves by casting them into the water — a practice noted alongside that of the [[nootka|Nootka]] Sound tribes. This was not, however, a formal rite of burial but rather a means of disposing of persons of the lowest social standing (017-001).

## Grave Violation and Secrecy

The violation of a grave was regarded as an offense of the first magnitude and provoked severe revenge. Captain Belcher observed that the Tsinuk observed great secrecy in all their burial ceremonies, partly from fear of Europeans, and would instantly punish by death any violation of the tomb — or wage war if the violation was perpetrated by another tribe. He noted that part of the crew of a vessel returning to the Columbia had suffered retribution because a person formerly belonging to the ship had taken a skull as an object of curiosity. Belcher qualified this, however, by observing that the intense protectiveness appeared to extend chiefly to the recently deceased and to persons of rank, as at the time of his visit skulls and skeletons were scattered about in all directions on the burial grounds he visited (016-013).
