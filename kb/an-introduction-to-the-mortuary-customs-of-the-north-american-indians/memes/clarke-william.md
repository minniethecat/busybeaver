# William Clarke

## Overview

William Clarke (1770–1838) was an American explorer, soldier, and Indian agent who, together with Meriwether Lewis, led the Corps of Discovery Expedition (1804–1806) across the newly acquired Louisiana Territory to the Pacific Ocean. His detailed journal descriptions of the indigenous peoples encountered along the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]], including the first systematic account of [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] burial vaults at the Cascades, were incorporated by [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]] into his comprehensive survey of Northwest Coast mortuary practices and subsequently reproduced by Yarrow in the 1880 monograph (016-005).

## Contributions

Clarke's most significant contribution to Yarrow's monograph is his description of an ancient Indian burial-place at the Cascades of the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] — an account recorded during the expedition's descent of the river and later transmitted through [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]]. Clarke documented eight vaults constructed of pine or cedar boards, closely connected, each approximately eight feet square and six feet in height, with tops securely covered by wide boards sloping to convey off rain. All were oriented east to west, with doors on the eastern side partially stopped with wide boards decorated with rude pictures of men and other animals (016-005).

Inside the vaults, Clarke found bodies carefully wrapped in skins, tied with cords of grass and bark, and laid on mats in an east-west direction. Some vaults contained four bodies; others held only bones, piled in certain cases to a height of four feet. On the tops of the vaults and on attached poles hung brass kettles and frying-pans with holes in their bottoms, baskets, bowls, sea-shells, skins, pieces of cloth, hair bags of trinkets, and small bones — the offerings of friendship or affection preserved by pious veneration from the ferocity of war or the temptation of individual gain. The walls and doors were decorated with strange carved and painted figures, and wooden images of men — some so old and decayed as to have almost lost their shape — were placed against the sides of the vaults (016-005).

Clarke astutely observed that these images did not appear to be objects of adoration but were most probably intended as resemblances of those whose decease they indicated. He also noted the universal observance of head-to-west orientation in body placement — a detail later confirmed by [[gibbs-george|Gibbs]], who learned from his Indian informants that the road to the *me-mel-us-illa-hee*, the country of the dead, lay toward the west, and that improper orientation would confuse the deceased on their journey. Near the standing vaults Clarke observed the remains of others completely rotted and moss-covered, leading him to conclude that this retired spot had served as a burial depository for a very long series of years (016-005).

## Accounts Provided

Clarke's account of the Cascades burial vaults is one of the earliest and most detailed European-American descriptions of a Northwest Coast cemetery. The specificity of his architectural observations — dimensions, materials, orientation, and decorative elements — provided later researchers with a baseline against which the subsequent destruction of these sites, including the catastrophic fire during Captain Wilkes's expedition that consumed an estimated three thousand canoes at Mount Coffin, could be measured. His recognition that the wooden images were memorial rather than votive in character reflects an unusual degree of interpretive caution for early nineteenth-century exploration literature (016-005).

## Reception and Scholarly Assessment

Clarke's description of the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] burial vaults at the Cascades of the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] entered the Yarrow monograph through an indirect transmission chain: Clarke's original journal entry (1805–1806) was incorporated into [[gibbs-george|George Gibbs]]'s comprehensive survey of Northwest Coast mortuary practices (1877), which was in turn reproduced by Yarrow. This double mediation — from expedition journal to synthetic ethnological report to government monograph — illustrates the layered character of much of the source material available to nineteenth-century compilers of ethnographic data (016-005).

The interpretive caution that Clarke displayed in his field observations has been noted by subsequent scholars of the Lewis and Clark Expedition. His recognition that the carved wooden images placed against the vault walls were memorial rather than votive — "most probably intended as resemblances of those whose decease they indicated" rather than as objects of adoration — was unusual for early American exploration literature, which frequently misidentified indigenous iconography as idol worship. This interpretive restraint, combined with his precise architectural documentation of vault dimensions, materials, and orientation, provides a methodological bridge between the descriptive travelogue tradition and the systematic archaeological survey methods that would emerge later in the nineteenth century.

The subsequent fate of the Cascades burial ground — its destruction by fire during Captain [[catlin-george|Wilkes]]'s United States Exploring Expedition, which consumed an estimated three thousand canoes at the nearby site of Mount Coffin — further enhances the documentary value of Clarke's account. His description, together with the accounts of [[townsend-john-kirk|Townsend]] and later observers, now constitutes one of the few surviving records of a mortuary landscape that no longer exists in material form. The detail that near the standing vaults Clarke observed the remains of others "completely rotted and moss-covered" suggests a depth of historical time — a "very long series of years," in his phrase — that complicates any simple attribution of the vaults to a single generation of [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] practice.
