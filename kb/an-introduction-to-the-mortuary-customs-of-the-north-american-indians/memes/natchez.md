# Natchez

## Overview

The Natchez people inhabited the Lower [[mississippi|Mississippi]] Valley in the region of present-day southwestern [[mississippi|Mississippi]] and eastern Louisiana. Unlike the confederacies and tribal nations of the interior Southeast, the Natchez were organized as a highly stratified chiefdom under the absolute authority of a paramount chief known as the Great Sun — a social structure that distinguished them from their neighbors and profoundly shaped their mortuary customs. Yarrow (1880) preserved the account of Natchez burial through the antiquarian [[jones|Charles C.]] [[jones|Jones]], whose *Antiquities of the Southern Indians* (1873) transmitted the observations of early French colonial sources to the nineteenth-century ethnographic record.

## Mortuary Customs

### Raised Tombs

The Natchez practiced two modes of disposition: inhumation and raised-tomb burial. For those accorded the latter, [[burial-vaults|tombs]] were constructed on four forked sticks fixed in the ground, elevated approximately three feet above the earth. Each tomb — about eight feet long and a foot and a half wide — was prepared for the reception of a single corpse. The Natchez dead were "either inhumed or placed in [[burial-vaults|tombs]]" (015-006), with the distinction between these two modes likely reflecting differences in social rank and proximity to the chiefly lineage.

After the body was placed upon the elevated platform, a basket-work of twigs was woven around it and covered with mud, forming an enclosed burial chamber. An opening was left at the head end, through which food could be presented to the deceased — a practice that reflects the belief, widespread across the Southeast, that the dead continued to require nourishment during the transitional period between death and final disposition.

### Temple Ossuaries

The [[burial-vaults|tombs]] were located within or very near the Natchez temples — sacred structures that served simultaneously as places of worship and repositories for the curated remains of the dead. When the flesh had rotted away from the bones, the skeletal remains were removed from the tomb, placed in a box made of canes, and then deposited within the temple itself. This secondary burial practice — the transition from individual tomb to collective temple [[ossuary]] — parallels the bone-picking and [[ossuary]] traditions of the [[choctaw|Choctaw]] and other Southeastern peoples, but with the distinctive Natchez integration of the temple as the final repository.

The index entry "Natchez ossuaries" in Yarrow (024-289) confirms that the communal storage of ancestral bones was a defining trait of Natchez mortuary practice, linking them to the broader [[ossuary]] tradition of eastern North America.

### Mourning Periods

Natchez [[mourning]] observances were differentiated by the circumstances of death. The "common dead" — those dying natural deaths within the community — were mourned and lamented for a period of three days. Those who fell in battle, however, "were honored with a more protracted and grievous [[death-wailing|lamentation]]" (015-006). This distinction between ordinary and martial death — the warrior's passing demanding a longer and more intense period of public grief — parallels the [[caddo|Caddo]] practice of according special postmortem status to the battle-slain, though the Natchez expressed this distinction through [[mourning]] duration rather than through differential treatment of the corpse.

Yarrow notes additionally that the thirty-day [[mourning]] period observed by the [[coyotero-apache|Coyotero Apaches]] "corresponds to that formerly observed by the Natchez" (001-027), suggesting that the Natchez may have observed a longer [[mourning]] cycle for certain categories of the dead, perhaps the chiefly class.

## Historical Sources

The primary sources for Natchez mortuary custom are the early French colonial observers of the eighteenth century — the period before the Natchez were devastated in the Natchez War of 1729–1731 and subsequently dispersed among neighboring tribes. These accounts were transmitted to Yarrow through [[jones|Charles C.]] [[jones|Jones]]'s *Antiquities of the Southern Indians* (1873), a work that synthesized the scattered documentary record of Southeastern Indian life. The temporal gap between the French observations and Yarrow's compilation — roughly 150 years — means that the Natchez customs recorded represent the pre-dispersal, temple-centered mortuary complex rather than any post-survival adaptation.
