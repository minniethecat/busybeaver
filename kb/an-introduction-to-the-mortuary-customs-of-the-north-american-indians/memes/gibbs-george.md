# George Gibbs

## Overview

George Gibbs (1815–1873) was an American geologist, ethnologist, and Indian agent whose systematic observations of the indigenous peoples of [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington Territory provided some of the most detailed nineteenth-century accounts of Northwest Coast mortuary practices. His contributions to [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s *History of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1853) and to the *Contributions to North American Ethnology* (1877, Vol. I) were extensively cited by Yarrow in the 1880 monograph, making Gibbs arguably the single most important source for the chapters on canoe burial, scaffold burial, and the regional variations in disposal practices between coastal and interior tribes (016-005).

## Contributions

Gibbs's account of the burial ceremonies of the Indians of [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington Territory, reproduced in its entirety by Yarrow, is the most comprehensive single-source description of Northwest Coast mortuary diversity in the monograph. He documented the canoe burial practices of the fishing tribes, who drew canoes into the woods at prominent points and placed them between the forks of trees or raised them on posts. He recorded that the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] of the [[columbia-river|Columbia River]] maintained two major cemeteries — Mount Coffin and Coffin Rock — the latter of which, by the time of Captain Wilkes's expedition, was conjectured to contain at least three thousand canoes before being destroyed by fire (016-005).

Beyond canoe burial, Gibbs documented the full range of Northwest Coast disposal customs. Among the [[yakama|Yakama]] of the interior plateau, he observed graves placed in conspicuous points of basaltic walls with pole markers from which articles of dress fluttered. He recorded that the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] and [[tsihalis|Tsihalis]] placed the *tamahno-us* spirit board of the deceased at the grave — a custom absent among the Puget Sound tribes, who instead constructed effigies of their chiefs. He further documented the practice of slave [[sacrifice]] at chiefly funerals, noting that as late as 1853 a [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]] chief named Toke attempted to kill a slave girl at his daughter's behest (016-005)(016-007).

Gibbs also provided one of the earliest ethnographic accounts of [[klamath|Klamath]] burial customs, published in [[schoolcraft-henry-rowe|Schoolcraft]]'s 1853 volume. He described their use of rude board coffins covered with earth, the eagle-feather-ornamented palings surrounding chiefs' graves, and the three-day funeral vigil during which a fire was maintained at the grave to protect the soul from the demon *O-mah-u*. His observation that the soul, if not protected, would be carried down the river — but remained subject to redemption by *Peh-ho-wan* upon payment of a big knife — remains one of the most intriguing recorded instances of Northwest Coast eschatological belief (005-003).

## Accounts Provided

Gibbs's accounts covered the burial practices of the [[tsinuk|Tsinuk]], [[yakama|Yakama]], [[tsihalis|Tsihalis]], [[makah|Makah]], [[klamath|Klamath]], Watlala, and Puget Sound peoples. He carefully distinguished between the canoe burial of the coastal and riverine tribes, the stone-covered inhumation of the equestrian interior tribes, and the scaffold burial adopted in certain localities on the Sound. His work is notable for its attention to the relationship between burial form and ecological context — noting, for instance, that the interior tribes who used canoes only for ferriage did not practice canoe burial — and for documenting the rapid transformation of traditional practices under Euro-American influence, including the abandonment of horse [[sacrifice]] among the [[yakama|Yakama]] and the suppression of slave killing (016-005).
