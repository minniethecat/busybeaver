# Billings' Expedition (1802)

## Overview

The Billings Expedition of 1802, a Russian exploratory mission to the [[aleut|Aleut]]ian Islands and North Pacific, produced some of the earliest European documentation of [[aleut|Aleut]]ian mortuary customs. Martin Sauer, secretary to the expedition, recorded observations of [[aleut|Aleut]]ian [[embalming]] practices that were cited by Yarrow (1880) as primary evidence for American mummification (006-006).

## Details

Martin Sauer's account, published in *Billings' Expedition* (1802, p. 167), described the [[aleut|Aleutian Islanders]] paying respect to the memory of the dead through [[embalming]]. The [[aleut|Aleuts]] carefully preserved the bodies of prominent individuals, wrapping them in furs and placing them in elevated positions within dry volcanic caves. Sauer noted that these preserved bodies remained intact for extended periods, protected by the constant cool temperatures and low humidity of the cave environments. His observations, made at a time when European contact with the [[aleut|Aleut]]ians was still limited, provided Yarrow with crucial early evidence for the chapter on mummification (006-006)(006-005).

## Significance

The Billings Expedition account is significant as one of the earliest Russian-language sources (translated into English) documenting indigenous mortuary customs of the North Pacific. It established that [[aleut|Aleut]]ian mummification was not a product of post-contact cultural disruption but an established pre-contact practice. The expedition's observations complemented later American military and scientific reports from the [[alaska|Alaska]] territory (006-006).
