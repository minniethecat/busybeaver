## Overview

Betrayal is the narrative engine of *[[romola|Romola]]*. [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]], the novel's charming and increasingly corrupt anti-hero, moves through a sequence of betrayals — of his adoptive father, his wife, his political patrons, and ultimately the city of [[florence|Florence]] itself. Each act of treachery is motivated not by malice but by [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s fatal preference for ease: he betrays because loyalty is difficult, dangerous, or inconvenient.

George Eliot traces the psychology of betrayal with clinical precision, showing how small moral compromises accumulate into catastrophic treachery. [[tito-melema|Tito]] does not wake up a villain; he becomes one through a series of choices, each of which seems defensible in isolation but which collectively destroy every bond of trust.

## Tito's Escalating Betrayals

[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s betrayals follow a downward arc, each more serious than the last:

| Act of Betrayal | Victim | Motivation |
|-----------------|--------|------------|
| Selling [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s ring | [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] | Desire for wealth and freedom |
| Concealing his identity from Bardo | Bardo, [[romola|Romola]] | Social ambition |
| Denying [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] in the street | [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] | Fear of exposure |
| Marrying [[romola|Romola]] under false pretences | [[romola|Romola]] | Desire for her social position |
| Selling Bardo's library | [[romola|Romola]], Bardo's memory | Financial self-interest |
| Betraying [[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]] | Bernardo, [[romola|Romola]] | Political expediency |

Each betrayal is enabled by the last: having concealed his past, [[tito-melema|Tito]] must lie to protect the lie; having lied, he must commit graver acts to avoid the consequences of earlier ones.

## The Psychology of Betrayal

Eliot's narrator is explicit about the psychological mechanism at work. Betrayal is not a single act but a condition of character — a habitual preference for the easy path:

> There would always be a risk of betrayal; besides even little Tessa might be troublesome (018-002).

Here, [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s mind has already begun to frame relationships as problems to be managed rather than bonds to be honoured. The word "risk" is revealing: for [[tito-melema|Tito]], betrayal is a practical calculation, not a moral category.

[[romola|Romola]], by contrast, senses the danger without being able to name it. At a climactic moment, the narrator observes that she "might be walking towards betrayal" (047-006) — the passive construction capturing her [[power-in-romola|power]]lessness in a [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] built on [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s lies.

## Betrayal and Justice

The novel's treatment of betrayal is inseparable from its vision of [[justice-in-romola|justice]]. [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]], the adoptive father whom [[tito-melema|Tito]] abandoned, becomes an agent of [[justice-in-romola|retribution]]. The narrator frames [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s pursuit of [[tito-melema|Tito]] in almost cosmic terms:

> Time had brought home justice to the unjust (038-019).

Betrayal, in Eliot's moral universe, is self-correcting — but the correction is destructive, not redemptive. [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[death-in-romola|death]] in [[arno-river|the Arno]] is the logical terminus of a life built on the refusal of all obligations.
