## Overview

The Bargello is the fortress-like palace that served as [[florence|Florence]]'s prison and the seat of its chief magistrate — the Podestà. Its "older square tower" (008-008) stands in stark contrast to the civic elegance of the [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo Vecchio]]. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, the Bargello is the site of imprisonment, judicial violence, and the novel's darkest scenes of public [[death-in-romola|execution]].

## Location

The Bargello stands on the Via del Proconsolo in central [[florence|Florence]], roughly midway between the Duomo and the [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo Vecchio]]. Its courtyard, where [[death-in-romola|execution]]s were staged, is enclosed within the palace walls, creating a claustrophobic theatre of [[justice-in-romola|justice]].

## Key Events

The Bargello features at several of the novel's most harrowing moments:

- **Emptying the prison (022-026)**: During a period of civic unrest, the Bargello is "emptied" — prisoners released in a gesture of populist appeasement. This act foreshadows the volatility of Florentine [[justice-in-romola|justice]].
- **[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s confinement**: After his failed attempt to enter the [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo Vecchio]] and denounce [[tito-melema|Tito]] (038-028), [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] is imprisoned in the Bargello. His confinement there marks the triumph of [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s lies over truth.
- **The [[death-in-romola|execution]] preparations (060-003, 060-004, 060-013, 060-014)**: The judicial Eight go to the Bargello to prepare for the [[death-in-romola|execution]] of Savonarola's supporters. [[romola|Romola]], her "heart bent on clinging" to her godfather [[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]], follows the path to the Bargello. [[otto-di-guardia|The Eight]] order the "instant erection" of the scaffold within the courtyard.
- **[[romola|Romola]]'s final plea (060-016)**: Arriving within the court of the Bargello, [[romola|Romola]] is allowed to approach the condemned. This scene — the novel's emotional nadir — takes place in the Bargello's shadowed courtyard.
- **The [[death-in-romola|execution]] (068-001, 068-003)**: The [[death-in-romola|execution]] of [[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]] and the other condemned men takes place "in the court of the Bargello, where the flickering flames" illuminate a scene of ritualised [[death-in-romola|death]]. [[romola|Romola]]'s memory of the "dark scene in the Bargello" haunts her thereafter.

## Associated Characters

- **[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]** — Imprisoned in the Bargello after his failed denunciation of [[tito-melema|Tito]]
- **[[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]]** — [[romola|Romola]]'s godfather, executed in the Bargello courtyard
- **[[romola|Romola de' Bardi]]** — Witnesses the [[death-in-romola|execution]] of her godfather in the Bargello; the trauma defines her subsequent journey
- **[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]** — Instrumental in the judicial process that leads to the Bargello [[death-in-romola|execution]]s

## Symbolism

The Bargello represents the dark underside of Florentine civic order — the coercive violence that sustains the republic's legal façade. Its square tower, a blunt architectural statement, contrasts with the soaring Campanile and Duomo: this is a city that imprisons and executes as efficiently as it prays. For [[romola|Romola]], the Bargello is the site where her [[religious-faith|faith]] in Florentine [[justice-in-romola|justice]] dies alongside her godfather.

## Historical Context

The real Palazzo del Bargello, built in 1255, is one of [[florence|Florence]]'s oldest public buildings. Originally the seat of the Podestà, it later became the headquarters of the Bargello (chief of police) and a notorious prison. Executions were indeed carried out in its courtyard. Today it houses the Museo Nazionale del Bargello, with an important collection of Renaissance sculpture. Eliot's depiction of the Bargello as a site of political [[death-in-romola|execution]]s reflects the historical reality of factional violence in 1490s [[florence|Florence]].
