## Overview

Bardo de' Bardi is the blind, ageing scholar and father of [[romola|Romola]], whose household on the Via de' Bardi provides the novel's first domestic setting. A member of the once-great Bardi [[family-in-romola|family]], now reduced in fortune, Bardo has devoted his life to classical scholarship — collecting manuscripts, busts, and antiquities — at the cost of human connection. His blindness, both literal and metaphorical, is one of the novel's central symbols: he cannot see his daughter's [[sacrifice-in-romola|sacrifice]], cannot perceive the world beyond his study, and cannot recognise the moral emptiness of the young scholar ([[tito-melema|Tito]]) who flatters his intellectual vanity.

## Biography

### The Bardi Legacy

Bardo belongs to the ancient Florentine [[family-in-romola|family]] of [[bardi-family|the Bardi]], once among the city's greatest banking houses but by the late fifteenth century diminished in wealth and influence. The narrator describes the house where Bardo lives:

> The house in which Bardo lived was situated on the side of the Via de' Bardi, looking towards the Arno. (005-004)

His home is filled with the fragments of classical civilisation — marble busts, manuscripts, intaglios — the material traces of a scholarly life devoted to recovering the lost world of antiquity.

### Blindness and Dependence

Bardo's blindness is not congenital but progressive, the result of years of intense study in poor light. His dependence on [[romola|Romola]] is total: she reads to him, organises his library, and manages the household. Yet Bardo is largely unconscious of the cost this imposes:

> "It is true, Romola," said Bardo, when she had finished reading, "it is a loss to thee not to have been born a son." (005-021)

This casual dismissal of his daughter's intellectual worth — she cannot, as a woman, be a true scholar — is delivered with paternal affection but reveals the fundamental blindness of his worldview.

### The Arrival of Tito

When [[nello|Nello the barber]] brings [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] to Bardo's household, the old scholar is immediately captivated. [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s fluency in Greek and Latin, his evident learning, and his deferential manner present everything Bardo has longed for: a scholarly heir. [[tito-melema|Tito]], for his part, recognises the opportunity and cultivates Bardo's favour:

> Bardo, though usually susceptible to flattery, was not easily imposed upon; but the young Greek's knowledge was genuine. (005-029)

Bardo's blessing of the match between [[tito-melema|Tito]] and [[romola|Romola]] — offered without perceiving either [[romola|Romola]]'s doubts or [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s true character — seals the novel's central tragedy.

### Death

Bardo dies in the early chapters, his passing coinciding with the definitive turn in [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s moral trajectory. His [[death-in-romola|death]] frees [[tito-melema|Tito]] from any remaining constraint of filial obligation while leaving [[romola|Romola]] bound to a [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] partly contracted to please her father.

## Character Analysis

Bardo embodies the limitations of Renaissance [[renaissance-humanism|humanism]] when divorced from human sympathy. His classical erudition is genuine, but his absorption in the ancient world has rendered him incapable of perceiving the living reality around him — most tragically, his daughter's intelligence and devotion. Eliot uses his blindness as a controlling metaphor: Bardo can see the bust of Ariadne on his shelf in his mind's eye but cannot see that his daughter "has the same cast of feature" (005-039).

His scholarly obsession represents a form of idolatry: the substitution of dead texts for living relationships. The narrator's treatment is not unsympathetic — Bardo's learning is real and his affection for [[romola|Romola]] genuine — but the critique is unmistakable. The Renaissance cult of antiquity, when pursued to the exclusion of moral and emotional awareness, becomes a species of blindness more profound than the physical.

## Historical Context

The Bardi [[family-in-romola|family]] was one of [[florence|Florence]]'s great medieval banking dynasties, alongside the Peruzzi and Acciaiuoli. Their decline in the fourteenth century — precipitated by the default of Edward III of England on massive loans — is one of the great cautionary tales of Florentine financial history. By the late fifteenth century, when the novel is set, the [[family-in-romola|family]] retained its aristocratic prestige but little of its former wealth.

Bardo's scholarly obsession also reflects historical realities. The recovery of classical texts was a central project of the Italian Renaissance, and scholars devoted their lives (and fortunes) to assembling libraries of Greek and Latin manuscripts. Bardo's library — with its busts, its codices, its intaglios — represents the material culture of this intellectual enterprise, now dispersed and mostly lost.
