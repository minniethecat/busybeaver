## Overview

The [[compagnacci|Compagnacci]] (Evil Companions) were a gang of young, armed Florentine aristocrats who violently opposed [[savonarola|Savonarola's]] theocratic regime in the 1490s. Led by [[dolfo-spini|Dolfo Spini]], a coarse and belligerent patrician, the Compagnacci disrupted sermons at the [[florence|Duomo]], ambushed Savonarola's supporters, and assembled in force — five hundred strong — at the [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]]. In [[romola|*Romola*]], the Compagnacci represent the raw, violent edge of the [[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]] opposition, acting where political maneuvering failed. Their intimidation campaign, combined with the [[otto-di-guardia|judicial machinery of the Eight]], sealed Savonarola's fate.

## History

The Compagnacci emerged as the street-level enforcers of the [[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]] (anti-Savonarola) faction. Their name — meaning "evil companions" or "bad company" — reflected both their rowdy behavior and their reputation among Savonarola's followers as instruments of vice. Under [[dolfo-spini|Spini's]] leadership, they recruited from the disaffected young men of [[florence|Florence]]'s patrician families, who resented Savonarola's moral reforms and the Dominican influence over Florentine politics.

> That was Dolfo Spini, leader of the Compagnacci, or Evil Companions — that is to say, of a band of young men who made it their business to hunt down and molest the Piagnoni. (045-009)

## Role in the Novel

The Compagnacci serve as the violent instrument of Savonarola's opponents:

1. **Ambush and intimidation**: In chapter 46, [[dolfo-spini|Spini]] and his Compagnacci prepare to ambush [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] en route to [[romola|Romola's]] house. [[romola|Romola's]] black-shrouded figure passes through them, and even these hardened men are unsettled.

2. **The [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]] (ch65)**: Five hundred Compagnacci under Dolfo Spini assembled before the [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo Vecchio]], guarding the perimeter as the [[franciscan-order|Franciscans]] and [[san-marco-convent|Dominicans]] prepared for [[trial-by-fire|the ordeal]]. When the trial collapsed, the Compagnacci exploited the chaos to discredit Savonarola.

3. **The April riots (ch66–67)**: Compagnacci disrupted Savonarola's evening sermon at the Duomo, attempting to prevent him from speaking. Their armed presence in the cathedral escalated tensions, contributing to the mob violence that led to Savonarola's arrest.

> Five hundred Compagnacci under Dolfo Spini, far-off ones laughing and joking, the nearer ones looking savage. (065-005)

## Historical Context

The Compagnacci (*Compagnacci* or "Bad Company") were a historically attested group in late 1490s [[florence|Florence]]. They were indeed a band of young aristocrats opposed to Savonarola's puritanical reforms, and their leader Dolfo Spini was a real historical figure. The group functioned as the paramilitary wing of the [[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]] faction, using street violence and intimidation to undermine Savonarola's popular support. Their role in the events surrounding the [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]] and Savonarola's subsequent arrest is broadly accurate to the historical record, though Eliot dramatizes their actions for narrative effect.
