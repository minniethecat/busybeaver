## Overview

Bratti Ferravecchi is one of the novel's most memorable minor characters — a Florentine ironsmith and dealer in old metal whose earthy pragmatism and sharp business sense introduce the reader to the world of the [[mercato-vecchio|Mercato Vecchio]]. He is the first character to encounter [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] after the shipwreck and serves, in the opening chapters, as a kind of street-level Virgil guiding the stranger (and the reader) through [[florence|Florence]]'s commercial life.

## Biography

### The Market Encounter

Bratti appears in the novel's very first chapter, encountering the shipwrecked [[tito-melema|Tito]] in the [[mercato-vecchio|Mercato Vecchio]]. His immediate interest is commercial — he spots the antique ring on [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s finger and assesses its value:

> "Bratti Ferravecchi is not the man to pass over a chance of making a fair bargain." (001-005)

His patter is relentless and knowing:

> "Anybody might say the ring is more worth than your bargain." (001-007)

### The Connector

Despite his mercenary instincts, Bratti performs an essential narrative function: he brings [[tito-melema|Tito]] to [[nello|Nello]]'s barber shop, the social hub from which [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s Florentine career will be launched:

> "I've made a good bargain for you," said Bratti, triumphantly. (001-021)

His introduction of [[tito-melema|Tito]] to the [[mercato-vecchio|Mercato]]'s social world — pointing out [[tessa|Tessa]], bantering with [[nello|Nello]], navigating the market's dense social geography — anchors the novel's opening in a convincingly material world of trade, gossip, and street-level survival.

## Character Analysis

Bratti represents the Florentine _popolo minuto_ — the working-class citizen whose world is defined by the market, not the study or the council chamber. His speech is quick, his eye for value sharper than any scholar's, and his morality is practical rather than philosophical.

Eliot uses him as a foil to the [[classical-scholarship|classical learning]] that dominates Bardo's household: where Bardo's world is one of dead languages and marble busts, Bratti's is one of living commerce, cold cash, and the thousand small transactions that make a city function. His presence in the opening chapters insists that [[florence|Florence]] is not only a city of Renaissance [[renaissance-humanism|humanism]] but also one of ironsmiths, rag-dealers, and milk-sellers.

## Historical Context

The figure of the _ferravecchio_ (old-iron dealer) was a familiar presence in Renaissance [[florence|Florence]]'s market life. These dealers bought and sold scrap metal, secondhand goods, and occasionally items of genuine value — the boundary between junk dealer and informal pawnbroker could be thin. Bratti's eye for the antique ring on [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s finger is precisely the skill that such a dealer would cultivate: the ability to recognise value beneath the patina of age.
