## Overview

Pisa is the great maritime rival of [[florence|Florence]], whose long-sought subjugation and subsequent revolt form a persistent political thread throughout *[[romola|Romola]]*. More than a mere geographic reference, Pisa represents the external pressures that shape Florentine politics — the costly wars, the fragile alliances, and the domestic discontent that Savonarola must navigate. [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]'s diplomatic missions to Pisa mark key turns in his political ascent.

## Location

Pisa lies approximately 80 kilometres west of [[florence|Florence]], near the mouth of [[arno-river|the Arno]] River on the Tyrrhenian coast. In the Renaissance, it was [[florence|Florence]]'s most prized possession among its subject territories — a port city granting access to Mediterranean trade routes. The road from [[florence|Florence]] to Pisa passed through the Porta San Frediano (021-004), the western gate that looked "towards Pisa."

## Key Events

Pisa features throughout the novel's political narrative:

- **Historical conquest (008-004)**: [[florence|Florence]]'s victory over "hated Pisa, whose marble buildings were too high and beautiful, whose masts were too much honoured on Greek and Italian coasts" is recalled as a source of Florentine [[pride-in-romola|pride]] — the conquest of a once-mightier rival.
- **The Pisan revolt (021-015)**: After Piero de' Medici surrendered key fortresses to Charles VIII, Pisa seized the opportunity to rebel. The revolt "had been encouraged by" French presence, becoming a festering wound in Florentine politics and a source of constant military expenditure.
- **[[prophecy-in-romola|Savonarola's prophecy]] (024-012)**: [[savonarola|Fra Girolamo]] promises that "rebellious Pisa shall be given to you again," linking the city's recovery to Florentine moral reform — a [[prophecy-in-romola|prophecy]] that bolsters his political [[power-in-romola|authority]].
- **[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s diplomatic missions (043-018, 045-001)**: [[tito-melema|Tito]] is sent to Pisa "on a private embassy," and later delivers to [[florentine-signoria|the Ten]] the "results of his private mission to Pisa, intended as a preliminary to an avowed embassy." These missions cement his role as a political operator — [[romola|Romola]] sees him returning from Pisa as a horseman, but the recognition "brought no new flash of joy into her eyes" (043-018).
- **War as political backdrop (027-019, 029-008, 032-015, 035-005, 039-007, 042-004)**: Throughout the novel, the ongoing war "to recover Pisa" hangs over Florentine politics, draining resources and creating factional tension. The Savonarolan party's failure to retake Pisa becomes a point of vulnerability for its opponents to exploit.

## Associated Characters

- **[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]** — Undertakes private diplomatic missions to Pisa; his political career advances through these embassies (043-018, 045-001)
- **[[domenico-cennini|Domenico Cennini]]** — Discusses Pisan affairs and the political climate in Rome with [[tito-melema|Tito]] (045-016)
- **Piero de' Medici** — His surrender of frontier fortresses to Charles VIII triggered Pisa's successful revolt (021-015)
- **Savonarola** — Prophesies the recovery of Pisa as a divine reward for Florentine righteousness (024-012)

## Symbolism

Pisa functions as a symbol of [[florence|Florence]]'s perpetual external entanglements — the wars, the expense, and the political vulnerability that accompany territorial ambition. The city's successful revolt, and [[florence|Florence]]'s inability to recover it, exposes the limits of Savonarola's temporal [[power-in-romola|power]]: he can promise divine favour, but the Pisan war drags on regardless. For [[tito-melema|Tito]], Pisa is a stepping-stone — his missions there mark his transformation from Greek scholar to [[medici-family|Medicean]] diplomat. That [[romola|Romola]] sees him returning from Pisa with no joy in her eyes signals the widening gulf between them.

## Historical Context

The real Pisa was [[florence|Florence]]'s great medieval rival. After a long struggle, [[florence|Florence]] purchased Pisa in 1405 and conquered it definitively in 1406. The Pisan revolt of 1494, triggered by the French invasion of Italy under Charles VIII, was a major crisis for the [[florence|Florentine Republic]]. Pisa would not be retaken until 1509 — well after the events of *[[romola|Romola]]* conclude. Eliot's depiction of the Pisan war as an ongoing drain on Florentine morale and finances accurately reflects the historical situation of the 1490s.
