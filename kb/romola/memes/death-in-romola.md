## Overview

Death is the constant horizon of *[[romola|Romola]]*. The novel opens in the shadow of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]]'s death, moves through plague and execution, and ends with multiple deaths — [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s drowning, [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s vengeance fulfilled, Savonarola's execution. George Eliot uses mortality not as a source of melodrama but as a philosophical condition: the fact of death gives moral life its urgency and its meaning.

The novel treats death in several registers: as historical reality (the plague that periodically devastated [[florence|Florence]]), as political spectacle (public executions), as narrative culmination ([[tito-melema|Tito]]'s death in [[arno-river|the Arno]]), and as existential limit (the deaths that strip [[romola|Romola]] of every defining attachment). In each register, death is not an interruption of life but the condition that makes life morally significant.

## Death as Historical Reality

Fifteenth-century [[florence|Florence]] lived with death as a daily reality. Plague, war, political violence, and high infant mortality made death familiar in ways that Eliot's Victorian readers could still recognise. The novel registers this historical truth without sensationalism:

> The plague had carried off many of the inhabitants (cf. ch56–58).

[[romola|Romola]]'s work among the plague-stricken is the novel's fullest engagement with death as a social fact. The plague village is a place where death is not exceptional but ordinary — where the task is not to prevent death (impossible) but to ensure that the dying are not alone. This is the novel's most radical moral vision: the good life is one lived in proximity to death, not in flight from it.

## Public Death: Execution as Spectacle

The novel includes several public deaths that function as political theatre. Savonarola's execution — the hanging and burning of his body in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]] — is the most consequential. It represents the collapse of his theocratic project and the reassertion of civic [[power-in-romola|power]] over [[prophecy-in-romola|prophetic authority]].

Eliot treats public execution with her characteristic ambivalence. It is both a legal proceeding and a form of collective violence. The crowd that cheers Savonarola's death is the same crowd that had worshipped him months before. Public death, in the novel's vision, reveals the instability of public opinion as much as it enacts [[justice-in-romola|justice]].

## Tito's Death: Narrative Justice

[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]'s death in [[arno-river|the Arno]] is the novel's most narratively significant death. Pursued by [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]], [[tito-melema|Tito]] leaps from a bridge into [[arno-river|the river]] and is drowned — a death that is both accident and [[justice-in-romola|retribution]]. [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] does not push him; [[tito-melema|Tito]] jumps to escape, and [[arno-river|the river]] does the rest.

This death is the culmination of the novel's logic of [[justice-in-romola|justice]]: [[tito-melema|Tito]] is killed not by a judicial process or a divine intervention but by the convergence of his own choices. The Arno that kills him is the same river he crossed when he first entered [[florence|Florence]]. His death is the end point of a trajectory he set in motion from his first act of [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]].

## The Death of Attachments

For [[romola|Romola]], death is not a single event but a cumulative process. Her mother dies before the novel begins. Her brother [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]] dies estranged. Her father Bardo dies, leaving her alone. Her [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] dies, killed by [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]s. Savonarola dies, taking with him the last framework of [[power-in-romola|authority]] she trusted.

Each death strips away an identity — daughter, sister, wife, believer — until [[romola|Romola]] is left with nothing except her capacity to act. This progressive stripping-away is the novel's most rigorous moral discipline. [[romola|Romola]] does not transcend death; she learns to live in its aftermath, to make meaning in the space that [[grief-in-romola|loss]] has cleared.

## Mortality and Moral Urgency

The novel's philosophical claim about death is that it gives life its moral shape. If death were not real, nothing would be at stake. The fact that actions have irreversible consequences — that a [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]] cannot be undone, that a death cannot be reversed — is what gives moral choice its weight.

This is Eliot's secular version of the *memento mori* tradition: not "remember you must die" as a call to repentance, but "remember that life is finite" as a call to seriousness. [[romola|Romola]]'s moral maturity consists in her acceptance of mortality — not as a tragedy to be lamented but as the condition that makes love, loyalty, and [[sacrifice-in-romola|sacrifice]] meaningful.
