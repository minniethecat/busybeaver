## Overview

[[dinos-crucifix|Dino's Crucifix]] is a small ivory crucifix that belonged to [[dino-de-bardi|Dino de' Bardi]], the estranged brother of [[romola|Romola de' Bardi]]. On his [[death-in-romola|death]]bed at the [[san-marco-convent|Dominican convent of San Marco]], [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]] gave the crucifix to [[romola|Romola]] as a token of his blessing and a call to spiritual awakening. The crucifix subsequently becomes one of the most potent symbolic objects in [[romola|*Romola*]], passing through the hands of [[romola|Romola]], [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]], and [[tessa|Tessa]], and serving as a silent witness to [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s escalating [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]s. Its journey — from [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]]'s dying grasp to the [[tabernacle-of-the-crucifix|painted tabernacle]] to the [[death-in-romola|plague]] procession at [[santa-croce|Santa Croce]] — traces the novel's central themes of [[religious-faith|faith]], [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]], and the persistence of the sacred in a fallen world.

## Description

The crucifix is a small ivory sculpture of Christ on the cross, small enough to be held in one hand. It is not described in elaborate artistic detail in the novel; rather, its significance derives from its associations and its movement between characters. [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]] held it during his final illness at [[san-marco|San Marco]], his eyes fixed upon it. [[romola|Romola]] kept it in her bedroom. [[tito-melema|Tito]] carried it concealed, then sold it in the marketplace — a transaction that marks one of his most callous [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]s. The crucifix's material (ivory, a precious substance associated with purity and [[sacrifice-in-romola|sacrifice]]) and its subject (the crucified Christ) give it a religious weight that contrasts sharply with the secular cynicism of [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s actions.

> He had been gazing at the crucifix, and as they looked up at her from the crucifix, she was absorbed in that old life. (015-003)

## In the Novel

The crucifix traces a narrative arc through four phases:

1. **[[dino-de-bardi|Dino]]'s [[death-in-romola|death]]bed (ch15)**: The dying [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]] presses the crucifix into [[romola|Romola]]'s hand, asking her to take it in remembrance. For [[romola|Romola]], who resists her brother's Dominican piety, the object is charged with ambivalence — love for her brother, resistance to his [[religious-faith|faith]].

2. **[[romola|Romola]]'s keepsake (ch17)**: [[romola|Romola]] keeps the crucifix in her bedroom. Its presence troubles her, a reminder of [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]]'s vision of her spiritual peril. She cannot discard it, but she cannot fully embrace what it represents.

3. **[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]] (ch20)**: [[tito-melema|Tito]], needing money for his secret life with [[tessa|Tessa]], steals the crucifix from [[romola|Romola]]'s room and sells it. When [[romola|Romola]] discovers its absence, the [[grief-in-romola|loss]] is a wound — the crucifix was her last tangible connection to [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]].

4. **Recovery and concealment (ch20–43)**: [[tito-melema|Tito]] recovers the crucifix and places it inside the [[tabernacle-of-the-crucifix|painted tabernacle by Piero di Cosimo]], which he then gives to [[tessa|Tessa]]. The crucifix remains hidden within the locked tabernacle, its sacred nature paradoxically both preserved and profaned by its concealment in [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s secret household. In the [[death-in-romola|plague]] procession of chapter 43, the tabernacle — with the crucifix still inside — is carried through the streets as an object of supplication.

> "Take the crucifix, my daughter," said Fra Girolamo, after a few minutes. "It is his desire." (015-040)

## Creator

The creator of the ivory crucifix is unknown — it is described only as a small ivory devotional object of the kind commonly available in Renaissance Italy. Ivory crucifixes were produced by specialized workshops in France, Germany, and Italy throughout the medieval and Renaissance periods, often for private devotion among the wealthy. In the novel, the crucifix's anonymity as an artwork contrasts with the named artist of the tabernacle ([[piero-di-cosimo|Piero di Cosimo]]) that later houses it — the crucifix derives its significance not from its maker but from its associations with [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]], [[romola|Romola]], and [[tito-melema|Tito]].

## Historical Context

Ivory crucifixes were luxury devotional objects in Renaissance Italy, typically owned by patrician families or religious houses. The use of ivory — an imported, costly material associated with purity — made such crucifixes markers of both wealth and piety. [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]]'s possession of an ivory crucifix would have been consistent with his patrician Bardi background and his Dominican vocation. The practice of giving a crucifix as a [[death-in-romola|death]]bed gift had deep roots in Catholic tradition, symbolizing the transmission of [[religious-faith|faith]] from the dying to the living. Eliot's use of the crucifix as a narrative device — an object that circulates, accumulates meaning, and exposes character — draws on the Victorian novel's broader fascination with "thing theory," where material objects carry moral and emotional weight across the plot.
