## Overview

The Florentine Signoria was the supreme executive body of the [[florence|Florentine]] Republic during the period of George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]* (1492–1498). Composed of the *Gonfaloniere di Giustizia* (Standard-Bearer of Justice) and eight *Priori* (Priors), the Signoria governed [[florence|Florence]] for two-month terms and was the ultimate [[power-in-romola|authority]] in matters of state — including war, diplomacy, and the administration of [[justice-in-romola|justice]]. The novel's political plot unfolds against a backdrop of Signoria machinations, as [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] rises to become Latin Secretary to the Ten (the war council that operated alongside the Signoria) and navigates the shifting allegiances of Florentine politics.

## Structure and Function

The Signoria's composition reflected [[florence|Florence]]'s guild-based constitution:

| Position | Number | Selection |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Gonfaloniere di Giustizia | 1 | Elected by lot from eligible guild members |
| Priori | 8 | 2 from each quarter of the city; 6 from major guilds, 2 from minor guilds |

The Signoria met in the [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo della Signoria]] (now [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo Vecchio]]), the fortress-like palace whose tower dominated the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]] — the same square where the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]] would be lit and where Savonarola would be executed. The Signoria's two-month terms and lottery-based selection were designed to prevent any single faction from consolidating [[power-in-romola|power]], but in practice the system was susceptible to manipulation by wealthy families and political blocs.

## In the Novel

The Signoria appears in *[[romola|Romola]]* primarily through its committees and subsidiary bodies. The **Ten of Liberty and Peace** (*Dieci di Libertà e Pace*), the republic's war council, is particularly significant: it is to this body that [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] becomes Latin Secretary, a position that gives him "considerable influence in the conduct of affairs" (022-011). [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s manipulation of the Ten's diplomatic correspondence — his selective translations and strategic omissions — exemplifies the novel's concern with how [[power-in-romola|political power]] can be wielded without accountability.

The Signoria's relationship with Savonarola is central to the novel's political narrative. Initially sympathetic to the friar's calls for moral reform, the Signoria gradually turned against him as his influence threatened both the city's commercial interests (through the papal interdict) and the prerogatives of its ruling families. The Signoria ultimately authorised Savonarola's arrest in April 1498 and oversaw the [[trial-of-savonarola|trial]] that led to his [[death-in-romola|execution]].

## Historical Context

The Florentine Signoria of the 1490s faced an extraordinary series of crises: the [[death-in-romola|death]] of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]] (1492), the French invasion under Charles VIII (1494), the expulsion of [[medici-family|the Medici]], the establishment of the Savonarolan republic, the papal excommunication and interdict (1497), and the friar's trial and [[death-in-romola|execution]] (1498). Eliot's novel captures these events through the lens of individual character — showing how the abstract machinery of republican government was operated by men whose moral choices, like [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s, shaped the course of history.
