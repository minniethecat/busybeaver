## Overview

Florentine political life in *[[romola|Romola]]* is structured by faction. The expulsion of [[medici-family|the Medici]] in 1494 created a [[power-in-romola|power]] vacuum that was filled not by a stable republican government but by competing parties — the Piagnoni (Savonarola's supporters), the Arrabbiati (his opponents), and various intermediate groups whose loyalties shifted with events. George Eliot presents factional politics as both the lifeblood of Florentine liberty and the source of its instability.

The novel traces how Savonarola's religious movement became a political faction, how that faction's dominance provoked counter-factions, and how the resulting conflict destroyed both Savonarola and the republican experiment he had championed. Faction, in Eliot's treatment, is the tragic flaw of republican politics: the very freedom that allows citizens to organise also enables them to destroy one another.

## The Piagnoni

The Piagnoni were Savonarola's followers — the "weepers" whose religious fervour gave his movement its popular base. They were drawn largely from the middling classes: artisans, small merchants, and women who responded to Savonarola's call for moral reform. The Piagnoni organised the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]], enforced sumptuary laws against luxury, and provided the political muscle for Savonarola's theocratic programme.

Their weakness was their dependence on Savonarola himself. When his [[prophecy-in-romola|prophetic authority]] collapsed — when the trial by fire failed, when the expected French deliverance did not come — the Piagnoni had no independent organisational structure to sustain them.

## The Arrabbiati

The Arrabbiati ("the enraged ones") were the principal opponents of Savonarola. Their name captures their defining emotion: fury at [[savonarola|the Dominican friar]] who had displaced the traditional ruling class from [[power-in-romola|power]]. The Arrabbiati drew support from the old oligarchic families who had been sidelined by [[medici-family|the Medici]] and then by Savonarola, and from those who simply preferred the old order to the uncertainties of theocratic rule.

The novel registers the Arrabbiati's presence without making them central characters. They appear as a collective force — the rumble of aristocratic discontent, the whispered plots against Savonarola, the willingness to collaborate with Rome to bring him down.

## Factions in the Novel's Plot

Factional politics drive several of the novel's key events:

- The political crisis that follows the [[death-in-romola|death]] of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]] exposes [[florence|Florence]] to French invasion and internal upheaval
- Savonarola's rise is enabled by the [[power-in-romola|power]] vacuum that factional conflict creates
- [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] exploits factional divisions for personal advantage, playing all sides against each other
- The conspiracy to restore [[medici-family|the Medici]] — in which [[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]] is implicated — is a factional plot that ends in [[death-in-romola|execution]]
- Savonarola's fall is orchestrated by his factional enemies, using the papal excommunication as their legal cover

## Historical Context

Florentine factionalism was proverbial in Renaissance Italy. The Guelph-Ghibelline conflicts of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries had given way to more fluid party alignments, but the underlying pattern — families and neighbourhoods mobilising against each other — persisted. The Medici had suppressed factionalism by suppressing politics itself; their expulsion released the energies they had contained.

Eliot's picture of Florentine factional politics draws on the historical record, particularly the chronicles of contemporaneous observers like Luca Landucci (a Piagnone apothecary whose diary records the daily fluctuations of public opinion). The novel captures the volatility of factional loyalty — the way a crowd that cheers Savonarola one month can cheer his [[death-in-romola|execution]] the next.
