## Overview

Pride in *[[romola|Romola]]* is both a virtue and a vice, a source of strength and a cause of isolation. George Eliot treats pride with characteristic complexity: Bardo's intellectual pride sustains his devotion to classical scholarship in the face of poverty and blindness, yet it also blinds him to the realities of the world around him. [[romola|Romola]] inherits her father's pride — it is her armour against [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s manipulations — but must learn to temper it with [[charity-in-romola|compassion]].

The novel distinguishes between forms of pride: the pride of intellect, the pride of [[family-in-romola|family]], the pride of social position, and the pride that masks as independence. Each form shapes character in different ways, and the novel's moral education consists in part of learning which forms of pride to preserve and which to surrender.

## Bardo's Intellectual Pride

[[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]], [[romola|Romola]]'s blind scholar-father, embodies intellectual pride in its most elevated and most destructive form. His devotion to [[classical-scholarship|classical learning]] is genuine and in some ways heroic:

> He had inherited the old family pride and energy, the old love of placing a Bardi among the scholars of Italy (005-003).

But this pride comes at a cost. Bardo's absorption in the classical past makes him indifferent to the needs of those around him, including his own daughter. His pride in [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] name and in his scholarly mission isolates him from the moral claims of the present.

## Florentine Civic Pride

[[florence|Florence]]'s collective pride — its sense of itself as the preeminent city of Italian culture and commerce — is a recurring motif. The Florentines are proudly conscious of their republican traditions, their artistic achievements, and their commercial wealth. This civic pride is the psychological foundation of Savonarola's appeal: his message that [[florence|Florence]] is specially chosen by God flatters the city's self-image even as it demands moral reform:

> An agreeable stirring of pride at showing a stranger what was worthy of being seen (008-035).

## Tito's Personal Vanity

[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]'s pride is of a different order: it is personal vanity, the delight in being admired. His charm, his physical beauty, his facility with languages and social situations — all feed a pride that requires constant external validation. Unlike Bardo's pride, which is rooted in genuine achievement (however narrow), [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s pride is narcissistic: it depends on the gaze of others.

This form of pride is the most dangerous in Eliot's moral taxonomy. It has no content beyond self-regard, and it corrodes every relationship it touches.

## Pride and Moral Isolation

The novel's deepest insight about pride is that it isolates. Bardo's intellectual pride cuts him off from his daughter's emotional life. [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s vanity cuts him off from genuine human connection. Even [[romola|Romola]]'s legitimate pride in her integrity becomes, at moments of crisis, a barrier to [[charity-in-romola|compassion]]:

> In Romola's face pride and passion seemed to be quivering together (005-015).

[[romola|Romola]]'s moral growth requires her to preserve what is noble in pride — self-respect, integrity — while surrendering what is isolating. Her final acts of [[charity-in-romola|charity]] represent pride transformed into [[charity-in-romola|compassion]].
