## Overview

The [[franciscan-order|Franciscan Order]] (*Frati Minori*, or Grey Friars) was one of the two dominant [[catholic-church-in-romola|mendicant orders]] in Renaissance [[florence|Florence]], alongside their rivals the [[san-marco-convent|Dominicans]]. In [[romola|George Eliot's *Romola*]], the Franciscans appear as a counterweight to Savonarola's Dominican faction, most dramatically in the [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]] episode (chapters 63–65), where the two orders were pitted against each other in a failed ordeal. The Franciscans were based at the [[santa-croce|Church of Santa Croce]], and their grey habits marked them visibly distinct from the black-and-white [[san-marco-convent|Dominican tunics]].

## History

The Franciscan presence in [[florence|Florence]] dates to the thirteenth century, with the [[santa-croce|Basilica of Santa Croce]] serving as their principal convent. By the 1490s, the Franciscans' influence in the city was rivaled by the rising [[power-in-romola|power]] of the [[san-marco-convent|Dominicans of San Marco]] under [[savonarola|Savonarola's]] leadership. The [[florentine-factions|Franciscan–Dominican rivalry]] was not merely theological — it mapped onto the broader political struggle between the *[[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]]* (opponents of Savonarola) and the *[[florentine-factions|Piagnoni]]* (Savonarola's followers).

> The Frati Minori are trying to make Florence a very uncomfortable place to live in. (001-019)

## Role in the Novel

The Franciscans serve three key narrative functions in *[[romola|Romola]]*:

1. **Spiritual alternative**: [[romola|Romola]] seeks counsel from her Franciscan confessor Fra Cristoforo at [[santa-croce|Santa Croce]], offering a spiritual path distinct from [[savonarola|Savonarola's]] charismatic Dominican [[power-in-romola|authority]].

2. **Political opposition**: The Franciscans aligned with the *[[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]]* faction that opposed Savonarola's theocratic rule, reflecting the historical reality of inter-order rivalry in Renaissance [[florence|Florence]].

3. **The [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]]**: In chapters 63–65, the Franciscans were appointed to face Savonarola's Dominican champion in a trial by ordeal. The event ended in anticlimax when neither side would enter the flames, discrediting both orders and precipitating Savonarola's downfall.

> On the Franciscan side of the Loggia there was a large array of monks; but no champion had yet been seen moving yet. Pale Franciscan faces were looking uneasily out from their grey cowls. [065-011, 065-013]

## Historical Context

The Franciscan Order was founded by St. Francis of Assisi in 1209 and established a major presence in [[florence|Florence]] at [[santa-croce|Santa Croce]]. Historically, the rivalry between Franciscans and Dominicans was a defining feature of medieval and Renaissance religious life, with each order competing for patronage, pulpits, and popular devotion. Eliot's portrayal of the Franciscans in *[[romola|Romola]]* draws on this historical tension — the order's grey habits, their intellectual tradition, and their institutional rivalry with the Dominicans are all grounded in Renaissance Florentine reality.
