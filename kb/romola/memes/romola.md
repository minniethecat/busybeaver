## Overview

Romola de' Bardi is the titular heroine of George Eliot's historical novel, a young Florentine noblewoman whose story traces a moral and spiritual awakening against the backdrop of late fifteenth-century [[florence|Florence]]. The daughter of [[bardo-de-bardi|the blind scholar]] Bardo, Romola begins the novel as a devoted and intellectually gifted daughter, assisting her father with [[renaissance-humanism|classical scholarship]] in their library on the Via de' Bardi. Her [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] to [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] and his subsequent moral disintegration force her to confront the inadequacy of pagan learning as a guide to life — propelling her toward a new understanding of [[religious-faith|religious faith]], [[charity-in-romola|compassion]], and active [[duty-in-romola|duty]].

## Biography

### Daughter of a Scholar

Romola is introduced as a maiden of "seventeen or eighteen" standing before a carved reading-desk in her father's library, her reddish-gold hair "enriched by an unbroken small ripple, such as may be seen in the sunset clouds on grandest autumnal evenings" (005-007). She reads aloud from Politian's *[[politians-miscellanea|Miscellanea]]* — a passage about the nymph Chariclo and the blinding of Teiresias — while her blind father listens:

> "Is it in the right place, Romola?" asked Bardo, who was perpetually seeking the assurance that the outward fact continued to correspond with the image which lived to the minutest detail in his mind. (005-013)

Romola has been her father's eyes and hands since childhood, learning Greek and Latin to assist his scholarly work. Her face bears the same "refinement of brow and nostril" as Bardo's, "counterbalanced by a full though firm mouth and [[power-in-romola|power]]ful chin, which gave an expression of proud tenacity and latent impetuousness" (005-008). This latent impetuousness — the capacity for decisive, passionate action — will prove the engine of her moral development.

### Marriage and Disillusionment

When [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]], the shipwrecked Greek scholar, is introduced into Bardo's household, Romola is captivated by his beauty, charm, and intellectual gifts. Their [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]], which occurs early in the novel, initially seems the union of two kindred scholarly spirits. But [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s concealments — his abandonment of [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]], his secret [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] to [[tessa|Tessa]], his political duplicity — gradually reveal the chasm between his intellectual brilliance and his moral character.

Romola's disillusionment is not merely personal but philosophical. Raised on the classical virtues of her father's beloved paganism, she finds that the Stoic ideal of endurance and the intellectual satisfactions of scholarship cannot answer the question of how to live when the foundations of her world have crumbled. Her flight from [[florence|Florence]] — and her dramatic return, prompted by Savonarola's intervention — marks the novel's turning point.

### The Awakening

> "Yes, father," said Romola, gently; "but your letters at the back are dark and plain still — fine Roman letters; and the Greek character is more beautiful than that of any of your bought manuscripts." (005-018)

The filial devotion shown in this early scene prefigures Romola's eventual transformation. Her love of learning, while genuine, is revealed as insufficient: it is only through suffering, and through her encounter with the suffering of others — the [[death-in-romola|plague]]-stricken village, the executed [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]], the widowed [[tessa|Tessa]] — that Romola discovers the active [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] that becomes her life's meaning. The novel's final image of Romola caring for [[tessa|Tessa]] and her children completes her arc from learned noblewoman to woman of practical goodness.

## Character Analysis

Romola is Eliot's study of the conscience in formation. Unlike [[tito-melema|Tito]], whose moral deterioration follows the logic of egoism, Romola's development traces the arduous path of moral growth — a path that requires her to abandon inherited certainties (her father's paganism, her wifely [[duty-in-romola|duty]], her class privileges) and discover her own ethical ground.

Her physical beauty is consistently described in terms that suggest latent [[power-in-romola|power]]: the "proud tenacity and latent impetuousness" of her face, the "queenly step" of her walk (005-012), the way "[[pride-in-romola|pride]] and passion seemed to be quivering in the balance with native refinement and intelligence" (005-015). Hers is not a passive beauty but a beauty charged with the potential for decisive action — a potential that the novel's plot brings to fulfilment.

## Historical Context

Romola is a fictional character, but her situation reflects the limited options available to educated Florentine women in the 1490s. The historical Florentine woman Alessandra Macinghi Strozzi, whose letters survive, offers a parallel: a woman of intelligence and energy whose scope of action was constrained by [[family-in-romola|family]], [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]], and political circumstance. Romola's scholarly education, unusual for a woman of her time, is made plausible by her father's blindness and his need for a literate assistant.

The Bardi [[family-in-romola|family]], to which Romola belongs, was historically one of [[florence|Florence]]'s great banking dynasties. Their fall from commercial prominence — described at length in Chapter V — provides the backdrop of faded grandeur against which Romola's story unfolds. The tension between [[bardi-family|the Bardi]]'s medieval martial glory and the scholarly isolation of Bardo's library mirrors the novel's larger theme: the struggle between the active and contemplative lives.
