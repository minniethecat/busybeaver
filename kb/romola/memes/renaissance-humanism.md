## Overview

[[renaissance-humanism|Renaissance Humanism]] is the intellectual framework within which much of George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]* unfolds. The movement — a revival of classical Greek and Latin learning that placed human reason, eloquence, and moral philosophy at the centre of education — reached its fullest expression in fifteenth-century [[florence|Florence]] under the patronage of [[medici-family|the Medici]] and the scholarship of figures like Marsilio Ficino, Angelo Poliziano, and Pico della Mirandola. In the novel, humanism is represented by [[bardo-de-bardi|the blind scholar]] [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]], by the intellectual promise of [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]], and by the classical education of [[romola|Romola]] herself.

## Definition and Core Tenets

Renaissance Humanism centred on the *studia humanitatis* — the study of grammar, rhetoric, history, poetry, and moral philosophy through classical texts. Its key tenets included:

- **Ad fontes** (to the sources): direct engagement with Greek and Latin texts rather than medieval commentaries
- **Civic virtue**: the belief that [[classical-scholarship|classical learning]] should produce not merely scholarly accomplishment but active participation in civic life
- **Human dignity**: the affirmation of human potential and the value of earthly achievement alongside spiritual concerns

In Bardo's library, this humanist ideal finds its purest expression. The blind scholar's devotion to the "divine" ancients is both noble and, as the novel subtly suggests, limited: his [[classical-scholarship|classical learning]] has given him a rich inner life but has not equipped him — or his daughter — for the moral crises the world will bring.

## Humanism in the Novel

### Bardo's Pagan Devotion

[[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]] represents an extreme of humanist commitment: his scholarly life is devoted almost exclusively to the recovery and preservation of classical texts. His blindness is symbolic — physically cut off from the visible world, he lives entirely among the "marble fragments of the past" (005-003). His daughter [[romola|Romola]] has been raised on Politian and [[nonnus-dionysiaca|Nonnus]] rather than on Christian scripture, and her early worldview is consequently shaped by Stoic endurance and classical virtue rather than by the [[religious-faith|Christian compassion]] that will eventually transform her.

### Tito: Humanism Without Ethics

[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] represents the dark potential of humanist education when divorced from moral principle. His facility with Greek and Latin, his eloquence, and his intellectual adaptability are all products of the humanist training that makes him so valuable to Florentine patrons. But [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s humanism is instrumental — a tool for advancement rather than a guide to living. The novel asks whether [[classical-scholarship|classical learning]], with its emphasis on prudence, eloquence, and self-cultivation, provides any barrier to moral corruption when unmoored from religious [[religious-faith|faith]].

### Romola's Transformation

[[romola|Romola]]'s arc from classical scholar to active Christian is the novel's answer to the question of humanism's adequacy. Her early learning — reading Politian to her father, reciting Greek — provides intellectual depth but no moral direction. It is only through suffering, and through her encounter with [[religious-faith|Savonarola's faith]], that she discovers the active [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] her classical education could not supply.

## Historical Context

The historical humanists who appear or are referenced in the novel include:

| Figure | Role in History | Role in Novel |
|--------|----------------|---------------|
| **Angelo Poliziano** | Classical scholar, tutor to [[medici-family|the Medici]] children | His *[[politians-miscellanea|Miscellanea]]* is the text [[romola|Romola]] reads aloud in ch05 |
| **Pico della Mirandola** | Philosopher, author of *Oration on the Dignity of Man* | Referenced by [[nello|Nello]] as "our incomparable Pico" (004-014) |
| **Marsilio Ficino** | Translator of Plato, head of the Platonic Academy | Background presence in Florentine intellectual life |
| **Demetrios Chalcondyles** | Greek émigré scholar | Referenced as a model for Greek learning in [[florence|Florence]] |
