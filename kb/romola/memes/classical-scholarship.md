## Overview

Classical scholarship — the study of ancient Greek and Latin texts — forms the intellectual backdrop of George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]*. Set in late 15th-century [[florence|Florence]] at the height of the Renaissance revival of classical learning, the novel portrays scholarship as both a noble pursuit and a retreat from the pressing moral and political crises of the day.

## Context

The revival of classical learning, centered in [[florence|Florence]] under the patronage of [[medici-family|the Medici]] and other wealthy families, reached its peak in the decades before the novel's opening in 1492. Scholars like Marsilio Ficino and Angelo Poliziano translated and commented on Plato, Aristotle, and other ancient authors. Knowledge of Greek, in particular, was a mark of the highest intellectual achievement.

## In the Novel

In *[[romola|Romola]]*, classical scholarship is embodied most fully in the character of [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]], [[romola|Romola]]'s blind father. Bardo is a scholar devoted to the recovery and preservation of ancient texts, and his library is his life's work. Eliot uses Bardo's scholarship to explore tensions between intellectual pursuits and moral engagement with the world.

Bardo's classical learning isolates him from the political and religious turmoil of [[florence|Florence]]. His daughter [[romola|Romola]], trained as his amanuensis, inherits both his learning and his detachment — a detachment she must later overcome through direct engagement with suffering and in[[justice-in-romola|justice]].

## Key Passages

The novel establishes the scholarly context early. In the Proem, the narrator describes "a liberal scholar" who "must entertain all speculation" (P01-008), positioning scholarship as an ideal of open-minded inquiry inherited from the classical tradition. Bardo himself reflects that scholarship is "a system of licenced robbery" (005-039) — a bitter admission that the preservation of ancient wisdom often depends on the wealth and [[power-in-romola|power]] of patrons.

## Related Characters

Characters associated with classical scholarship include [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]], [[romola|Romola]]'s father and a dedicated classical scholar, and [[romola|Romola de' Bardi]], who serves as her father's assistant and inherits his learning.
