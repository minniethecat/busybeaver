## Overview

[[florence|Florence]] at the close of the fifteenth century is not merely the backdrop but the animating force of George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]*. The novel opens on the morning after the [[death-in-romola|death]] of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]] (8 April 1492) — a moment that Florence itself recognises as epochal — and the city's political convulsions, religious fervour, and intellectual ferment drive the narrative from first page to last. From the narrow streets around the [[duomo|Duomo]] to the loggias of the [[mercato-vecchio|Mercato Vecchio]], Florence in *[[romola|Romola]]* is rendered with an archaeological precision that reflects Eliot's extensive historical research.

## Geography and Landmarks

Florence in the 1490s was a walled city on [[arno-river|the Arno]], compact enough that one could walk from the Porta Romana in the south to the Porta San Gallo in the north in under half an hour. The novel's geography centres on several key locations:

|----------|--------------------------|
| **Via de' Bardi** | Home of Bardo and [[romola|Romola]]; [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] [[family-in-romola|family]]'s historic street in [[oltrarno|Oltrarno]] |
| **[[duomo|The Duomo]] ([[duomo|Santa Maria del Fiore]])** | Religious and civic heart of Florence; site of Savonarola's sermons |
| **[[san-marco|San Marco]]** | [[fiesole|Dominican monastery]] where Savonarola lived and preached |
| **[[mercato-vecchio|Mercato Vecchio]]** | The old market, where [[tessa|Tessa]] lives and where much street-level action occurs |

| **San Miniato** | Church on the hill overlooking Florence, seen in the Proem |

> The Angel of the Dawn, as he travelled with broad slow wing from the Levant to the Pillars of Hercules and from the summits of the Caucasus across all the snowy Alpine ridges to the dark nakedness of the Western isles, saw nearly the same outline of firm land and unstable sea — saw the same great mountains lift their shields above the clouds. (Proem)

The Proem's panoramic description of Florence from the heights of San Miniato establishes the city as a character in its own right — ancient, beautiful, and morally precarious.

## Political History (1492–1498)

The Florence into which [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] arrives is a city in crisis. [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]] — "the very pivot of Italy" (004-001) — has just died, and with him the delicate balance of Italian [[power-in-romola|power]] that his diplomacy had maintained. The novel traces Florence's trajectory through:

1. **The Medici succession** (1492): [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s son Piero proves incapable of maintaining his father's [[power-in-romola|authority]]
2. **The French invasion** (1494): Charles VIII of France enters Italy, and Piero de' Medici's craven capitulation leads to [[medici-family|the Medici]] expulsion
3. **The Florentine Republic under Savonarola** (1494–1498): The Dominican friar [[savonarola]] establishes a theocratic republic
4. **The [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]]** (1497): Savonarola's followers burn books, art, and luxury goods in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]]
5. **Savonarola's downfall** (1498): The friar is excommunicated, tried, and executed

## Cultural and Intellectual Life

Florence in the 1490s was the epicentre of [[renaissance-humanism|Renaissance Humanism]]. The novel's characters move through a world dense with the figures and artefacts of [[renaissance-humanism|classical revival]]: Bardo's library contains manuscripts of [[nonnus-dionysiaca|Nonnus]], Politian, Apollonius Rhodius, and Callimachus; [[piero-di-cosimo|the painter]] [[piero-di-cosimo|Piero di Cosimo]] works in his studio; the printer [[domenico-cennini|Cennini]] produces editions of classical texts. [[nello|Nello]]'s barber shop, where the novel's dialogue of ideas often unfolds, functions as a democratic space where scholars, artists, and citizens debate the affairs of the day.

Yet this intellectual brilliance coexists with profound religious ferment. The sermons of Savonarola — apocalyptic, uncompromising, and politically charged — draw enormous crowds to the Duomo, and the conflict between classical [[renaissance-humanism|humanist values]] and [[religious-faith|Christian morality]] is the novel's central intellectual tension.

## Historical Context

Eliot's Florence is the product of meticulous scholarship. She consulted dozens of historical sources — Villani's *Cronica*, Machiavelli's *Istorie Fiorentine*, Nardi's *Istorie*, Varchi's *Storia*, and numerous secondary works — to reconstruct the physical and social fabric of the city. The novel's Florence is historically accurate in its topography, its political factions (the *[[florentine-factions|Piagnoni]]*, *[[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]]*, *[[compagnacci|Compagnacci]]*, and *Bigi*), its religious practices, and its material culture. This historical fidelity serves the novel's realist aesthetic: Florence is not a romanticised Renaissance backdrop but a specific, documented place whose textures and tensions are rendered with nineteenth-century novelistic precision.
