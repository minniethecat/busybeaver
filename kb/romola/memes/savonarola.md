## Overview

Girolamo Savonarola is the most significant historical figure in [[romola|Romola]] — a Dominican friar whose fiery preaching against corruption and worldly vanities transforms [[florence|Florence]] into a theocratic republic. George Eliot portrays him as a figure of immense spiritual [[power-in-romola|power]] and genuine moral conviction, yet also as one whose prophetic certainty becomes entangled with political ambition. His rise and fall frame the novel's exploration of [[religious-faith|religious faith]], [[power-in-romola|political power]], and the tension between sincere belief and fanaticism.

## Biography

### Early Life and Vocation

Savonarola was born in Ferrara in 1452. Even before his arrival in [[florence|Florence]], he had acquired a reputation for ascetic devotion and prophetic fervour. The narrator introduces him as a figure whose influence on Florentine affairs would prove more consequential than any Medici prince:

> That man was Fra Girolamo Savonarola, Prior of the Dominican convent of San Marco, and the most powerful voice in Florence in the last decade of the fifteenth century. (P01-009)

### The Preacher of San Marco

By the time [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] rises in Florentine society, Savonarola's sermons at the Duomo have become the city's central political and spiritual events. The narrator describes his [[power-in-romola|power]]:

> Savonarola's voice had been rising in impassioned exhortation, till it quivered with the tears of the prophet who saw the sword coming. (024-009)

His preaching combined apocalyptic warning with democratic vision — he called for a "[[great-council|popular government]]" and denounced the corruption of both [[medici-family|the Medici]] and the Roman Church. The narrative notes that no preacher ever had more massive influence than Savonarola (025-019).

### The Bonfire of the Vanities and Political Rule

Savonarola's moral crusade culminated in the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]], where Florentines burned books, artworks, and luxuries in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]]. During this period, he effectively governed [[florence|Florence]], using the pulpit as his legislative chamber.

### Romola's Spiritual Guide

Savonarola becomes a pivotal figure in [[romola|Romola]]'s spiritual journey. After her flight from [[tito-melema|Tito]], she encounters Savonarola on the road and is turned back by his commanding presence:

> But when she heard Savonarola invoke martyrdom, she sobbed with the rest, and felt herself carried along by a great tide of emotion. (027-021)

His influence over her represents both genuine spiritual guidance and the dangerous [[power-in-romola|power]] of charismatic [[power-in-romola|authority]] — a tension Eliot explores throughout the novel's final Books.

### Trial and Execution

Savonarola's political enemies, allied with Pope Alexander VI, eventually moved against him. His [[trial-of-savonarola|trial and execution]] in 1498 marks the novel's climactic event, exposing the limits of [[prophecy-in-romola|prophetic authority]] against institutional [[power-in-romola|power]].

## Character Analysis

Eliot's Savonarola embodies the paradox of religious charisma: genuine spiritual insight fused with an unshakeable conviction of personal divine mission. The narrator reflects:

> We can give him a reverent admiration for the spiritual force that impelled him, without pretending that he was either a saint or a philosopher. (025-020)

His tragedy lies in the gradual conflation of divine purpose with personal vindication. The narrator warns against dismissing him as a mere "ambitious monk" (025-018), insisting instead on the authenticity of his religious experience while acknowledging the human flaws that distorted it.

## Historical Context

Girolamo Savonarola (1452–1498) was a Dominican friar from Ferrara who became Prior of [[san-marco|San Marco]] in [[florence|Florence]]. His apocalyptic preaching against Medici corruption and Papal decadence made him the de facto ruler of [[florence|Florence]] from 1494 to 1498. He instituted a "[[great-council|popular government]]" with Christ declared King of [[florence|Florence]], organised the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]] (1497), and was eventually excommunicated by Pope Alexander VI. After a trial by ordeal (which he declined), he was hanged and burned in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]] on 23 May 1498.

Eliot's portrayal draws heavily on contemporary accounts and later histories, particularly those of Villari and Perrens. She presents a nuanced figure: neither saint nor charlatan, but a man of genuine vision whose certainty of divine election became indistinguishable from political ambition.
