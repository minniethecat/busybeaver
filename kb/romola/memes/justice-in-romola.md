## Overview

Justice in *[[romola|Romola]]* operates on multiple levels: divine, civic, and personal. George Eliot, writing from a secular perspective, does not invoke a providential order that guarantees just outcomes. Instead, she traces how justice emerges — imperfectly, painfully — from human actions and their consequences. The novel's moral architecture insists that evil actions generate their own retribution, not through supernatural intervention but through the logic of cause and effect in a densely interconnected social world.

The novel's central plot — [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s rise and fall — is a study in poetic justice. Every [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]] [[tito-melema|Tito]] commits generates a consequence that eventually converges on him. [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]], the father he abandoned, becomes the agent of his [[death-in-romola|death]].

## Forms of Justice

Eliot distinguishes three forms of justice, each with its own logic and limitations:

| Form | Agent | Mechanism | Limitation |
|------|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Divine justice** | God (via Savonarola's preaching) | Prophetic warning, moral exhortation | Dependent on belief; fails when [[prophecy-in-romola|prophecy]] is discredited |
| **Civic justice** | Florentine institutions | Law, trial, [[death-in-romola|execution]] | Corruptible; political rather than moral |
| **Personal justice** | [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]], [[romola|Romola]] | Retribution, moral witness | Partial, destructive, no restoration |

None of these forms is wholly adequate. Divine justice requires [[religious-faith|faith]] that the novel's rationalist narrator cannot fully endorse; civic justice is contaminated by faction and interest; personal justice, however satisfying, destroys the agent who carries it out.

## The Sword of God's Justice

Savonarola's preaching repeatedly invokes divine justice as an imminent reality:

> The sword of God's justice — which was speedily to descend (021-010).

This apocalyptic rhetoric gave Savonarola immense political leverage: if God's justice was coming, then the existing order must be reformed or it would be destroyed. But the novel treats such claims with ambivalence. Divine justice is rhetorically [[power-in-romola|power]]ful but empirically elusive; what actually unfolds is human justice, driven by human passions.

The narrator offers a more secular formulation:

> Time had brought home justice to the unjust (038-019).

Justice here is not a divine intervention but a temporal process — the working-out of consequences over time.

## Baldassarre as Agent of Justice

[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]], the scholar whom [[tito-melema|Tito]] abandoned in slavery, becomes the novel's most visceral agent of justice. His pursuit of [[tito-melema|Tito]] is relentless and ultimately lethal. But Eliot complicates any simple satisfaction: [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s quest for justice has made him a figure of pure vengeance, stripped of every human quality except the will to punish.

> Thanks to struggling justice, the heart that never quivered (038-022).

The word "struggling" is key: justice does not arrive effortlessly or cleanly. It is wrested from circumstances by damaged people at great cost.

## Justice and the City

The novel also examines justice as a civic ideal. In Savonarola's [[florence|Florence]], justice is proclaimed as the foundation of the reformed republic:

> Do justice: love mercy: put away all uncleanliness (024-010).

But the gap between prophetic rhetoric and political reality is vast. The same city that hears Savonarola's call to justice also executes him. The novel's pessimism about institutional justice — any institution — is one of its most distinctive features.
