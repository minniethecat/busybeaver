## Overview

The pestilence and famine that swept through [[florence|Florence]] in 1497 form the novel's great crucible of suffering. Against the backdrop of hunger and disease, [[romola|Romola]]'s personal crisis — her discovery of [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]] — is subsumed into a larger crisis of collective need. The pestilence is both a historical reality and a symbolic device: the physical sickness of the city mirrors its moral sickness, and the response to suffering becomes the measure of character.

## Background

[[florence|Florence]] in the 1490s faced multiple pressures: the war to recover [[pisa|Pisa]] drained resources; the French invasion disrupted trade; and Savonarola's prophecies of divine chastisement gained plausibility as material conditions worsened. References to pestilence early in the novel (008-004) establish it as one of the recurring calamities of Florentine life — "some floods, famine, and pestilence; but still much wealth and glory." By 1497, the threat had become acute.

## Description

The pestilence unfolds across the novel's middle chapters:

- **Early warnings**: As early as ch12, the pestilence is a topic of anxious conversation — "if he means the pestilence, the Signory ought to put a stop to it" (012-026). The disease is discussed with the fatalism of a population accustomed to periodic outbreaks, yet with [[fear-in-romola|dread]]. The [[fear-in-romola|fear]] of contagion shapes daily life: "with the chances of the pestilence that wise men talk of" (014-005).
- **Social breakdown**: The pestilence breeds suspicion and scapegoating. A charlatan is accused of "trying to raise the pestilence by selling secret medicines" (016-053), and [[tessa|Tessa]] [[fear-in-romola|fear]]s that leaving her home puts her "in worse danger of the pestilence than before" (014-013).
- **Savonarola's interpretation**: The Frate incorporates the pestilence into his prophetic framework, declaring it divine punishment for Florentine wickedness. "In the path of wantons shall come the pestilence" (024-006). But he also promises protection for the righteous: "the pestilence shall not enter" the homes of those who reform (024-010). He wields the [[death-in-romola|plague]] as both threat and promise — "He drives them by the breath of His mouth" (024-008).
- **[[romola|Romola]]'s response**: In the face of widespread suffering, [[romola|Romola]] transforms from a woman paralysed by personal [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]] into an active agent of [[charity-in-romola|charity]]. The pestilence becomes the occasion for her selfless service, following the example of Savonarola's call to practical mercy.
- **The famine**: Parallel to the disease runs a famine, compounding the city's misery. Hunger and sickness together create a landscape of suffering through which [[romola|Romola]] moves as a ministering figure.

## Participants

- **[[romola|Romola de' Bardi]]** — Transformed by the crisis from private [[grief-in-romola|grief]] to public [[charity-in-romola|charity]]; the pestilence is the arena of her moral awakening
- **Savonarola** — Interprets the pestilence as divine chastisement and calls for moral reform as the remedy (024-006–010)
- **[[tessa|Tessa]]** — Fears the pestilence as a threat to her domestic security (014-013)

## Aftermath

The pestilence recedes, but its effects linger. [[romola|Romola]]'s experience of serving the sick reshapes her identity — she is no longer merely Bardo's daughter or [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s wife, but a woman who has found purpose in action. The crisis also tests Savonarola's [[prophecy-in-romola|prophetic authority]]: his promises of divine protection create expectations that, when unmet, contribute to his eventual downfall. For [[florence|Florence]], the pestilence of 1497 is one more scar in a decade of accumulating wounds.

## Historical Context

[[florence|Florence]] experienced recurrent [[death-in-romola|plague]] outbreaks throughout the Renaissance, with major epidemics in 1348 (the Black Death), 1400, 1430, and 1478. The 1497 outbreak coincided with famine conditions exacerbated by the [[pisa|Pisa]]n war and political instability. Savonarola's sermons from this period show him interpreting natural disasters as divine judgment — a common rhetorical strategy among Renaissance preachers. Eliot's depiction of the social effects of epidemic disease — scapegoating, fatalism, and [[charity-in-romola|charity]] — draws both on historical accounts and on nineteenth-century understandings of public health crises.
