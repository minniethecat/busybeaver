## Overview

The Arno River is more than geography in the novel — it is a boundary between worlds, a site of commerce and violence, and the place where [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] meets his end at the hands of [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]. The narrator's first description of [[florence|Florence]] centres on the river:

> "The long valley of the Arno that seems to stretch its grey-green length through the heart of Tuscany." (P01-004)

Its waters bookend [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s Florentine career: he arrives by sea (a shipwreck survivor) and departs by river (drowned by the father he betrayed). The Arno is the novel's principal geographic symbol — the flowing current that carries the narrative from beginning to end.

## Location

The Arno rises in the Apennine Mountains of eastern Tuscany and flows westward through [[florence|Florence]], [[pisa|Pisa]], and into the Ligurian Sea. Within [[florence|Florence]], it divides the city into two halves: the northern bank (including the Duomo, [[san-marco|San Marco]], and the [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo Vecchio]]) and the southern [[oltrarno|Oltrarno]] district (including the Via de' Bardi and the [[pitti-palace|Pitti Palace]] area). The [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]] and [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte]] alla Carraja are among the bridges that span it.

## Symbolism

The Arno functions as the novel's Heraclitean river — the flowing water that is never the same twice, the current of time and fortune that carries all things away. [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s arrival by water and departure by water form a narrative chiasmus: the sea brought him to [[florence|Florence]] as a stranger of promise; the river takes him from [[florence|Florence]] as a man whose promises have all been broken.

The river is also a democratic space — unlike the palaces and churches that mark social hierarchy, the Arno belongs to everyone and no one. Its waters wash the foundations of the [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo Vecchio]] and the tenements of the poor alike, and its floods are indifferent to rank.

## Historical Context

The Arno has been [[florence|Florence]]'s defining geographic feature since the city's Roman foundation. Its waters [[power-in-romola|power]]ed the wool mills that generated Florentine wealth, and its bridges were strategic points in the city's military history. The river was also notorious for its floods — the great flood of 1333 destroyed the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]]'s predecessor, and floods in 1966 caused catastrophic damage to the city's artistic heritage. Eliot, who visited [[florence|Florence]] in 1860, would have seen the Arno much as it appears in the novel — the silver thread running through the city's fabric of stone, art, and history.
