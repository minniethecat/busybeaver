## Overview

The [[otto-di-guardia|Otto di Guardia]] (Eight of Guard), also known as [[lorenzo-de-medici|the Magnificent]] Eight, was the principal criminal magistracy of [[florentine-signoria|Renaissance Florence]]. In [[romola|*Romola*]], the Eight appear as the judicial [[power-in-romola|authority]] presiding over criminal cases, including the prosecution of [[savonarola|Savonarola's]] followers and ultimately the [[trial-of-savonarola|trial of Savonarola himself]]. Their [[power-in-romola|power]] to investigate, arrest, and sentence made them one of the most [[fear-in-romola|fear]]ed institutions in the Florentine republic, and their members — drawn from the city's elite families — wielded immense political influence.

## History

The Otto di Guardia was established in the late fourteenth century as a temporary commission and gradually evolved into a permanent magistracy responsible for internal security and criminal [[justice-in-romola|justice]]. By the 1490s setting of *[[romola|Romola]]*, the Eight had become a central pillar of the Florentine state, with [[power-in-romola|authority]] over everything from common crimes to political conspiracy. Their jurisdiction often overlapped with — and sometimes conflicted with — the [[power-in-romola|authority]] of the [[florentine-signoria|Signoria]].

> Judgment properly lay with the Eight, who presided over the administration of criminal justice. (058-002)

## Role in the Novel

The Eight feature in *[[romola|Romola]]* primarily in connection with Savonarola's downfall:

1. **Instruments of political [[justice-in-romola|justice]]**: The Eight were responsible for investigating and prosecuting Savonarola after the failed [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]], when the [[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati faction]] gained the upper hand.

2. **Fear and [[power-in-romola|authority]]**: The novel depicts the Eight as an object of popular [[fear-in-romola|dread]] — their summons portended imprisonment or worse. This atmosphere of judicial [[fear-in-romola|terror]] reflects the factional violence of 1490s [[florence|Florence]].

3. **Oligarchic [[power-in-romola|power]]**: By staffing the Eight with members of elite families like the Rucellai and Spini, Eliot shows how the machinery of Florentine [[justice-in-romola|justice]] served the interests of the anti-Savonarola patriciate.

> The Eight have been frightened at last into passing a sentence of death upon the Frate. (058-011)

> That's why the Magnificent Eight have turned out some of the poorer people, that a magnanimous new set may come in. (042-027)

## Historical Context

Historically, the Otto di Guardia e Balia was one of the key magistracies of the [[florence|Florentine Republic]], established in 1378 and reformed over subsequent centuries. The Eight had broad [[power-in-romola|power]]s over criminal [[justice-in-romola|justice]], including the [[power-in-romola|authority]] to torture suspects and impose capital punishment. Their role in Savonarola's historical trial (1498) is well documented: the Eight oversaw the judicial proceedings that led to Savonarola's [[death-in-romola|execution]], alongside two fellow Dominicans, in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]]. Eliot's portrayal of the Eight captures their dual nature as both a legal institution and a political weapon in the factional struggles of Renaissance [[florence|Florence]].
