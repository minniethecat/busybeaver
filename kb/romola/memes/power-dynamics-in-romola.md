## Thesis

Power in George Eliot's *Romola* operates through three distinct modes embodied by three interconnected entities: **Florence** represents institutional and structural power, **Romola de' Bardi** embodies moral authority rooted in integrity and self-sacrifice, and **Tito Melema** wields amoral power through charm, manipulation, and instrumental relationships. These three nodes form a dynamic triangle that drives the novel's central conflicts. The trajectory of the narrative traces institutional power's collapse (the fall of Medici and Savonarola), moral power's quiet triumph (Romola's survival and service), and amoral power's inevitable self-destruction (Tito's death in the Arno).

## Evidence

### Institutional Power: Florence as Political Arena

The novel opens with Florence in crisis. Lorenzo de' Medici — "the very pivot of Italy" (004-001) — has just died, destabilizing the delicate balance that maintained Florentine independence. The Medici and other powerful families of the *popolani* (001-022) had constructed a system where wealth, patronage, and political connections determined influence.

Into this vacuum steps two competing claimants to institutional authority. The [[florentine-signoria|Florentine Signoria]] represents secular republican governance, while [[savonarola|Girolamo Savonarola]] claims divine mandate through his Dominican preaching at [[san-marco|San Marco]]. The [[medici-family|Medici family]]'s exile creates space for Savonarola's theocratic experiment — the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities]] and the establishment of the [[great-council|Great Council]] (1494) both represent attempts to redefine where legitimate power resides.

Eliot shows institutional power as inherently unstable. Savonarola's [[savonarola-excommunication|excommunication]] and eventual [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]] demonstrate that neither religious nor political authority can maintain itself without underlying moral legitimacy. The [[compagnacci|Compagnacci]] — a gang of aristocratic youth led by [[dolfo-spini|Dolfo Spini]] — illustrates how institutional power fragments into factional violence when its moral foundation erodes.

### Moral Power: Romola's Journey

[[romola|Romola de' Bardi]] begins the novel as a dependent figure — her father [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo]]'s scholarly assistant, then Tito's wife. Her power is initially defined by what she lacks: political voice, economic independence, social autonomy. Yet Eliot traces her transformation into the novel's most durable moral force.

Romola's power emerges from her capacity to discern that power was to be won by other means than by setting up a new Fiesole (005-002) — that is, not through the masculine modes of political ambition and territorial control that Tito and Savonarola pursue. Her escape from Florence across the [[arno-river|Arno]] and her eventual return during the [[florentine-pestilence|Florentine Pestilence]] mark her transition from passive virtue to active moral agency. She serves the suffering, feeds the starving, and shelters the abandoned — acts that carry no political title but accumulate genuine authority through witnessed sacrifice.

### Amoral Power: Tito's Ascent and Destruction

[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] arrives in Florence with nothing — a shipwrecked Greek scholar with a secret (his betrayal of his foster-father [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre Calvo]]). His rise is powered entirely by charm and instrumental intelligence. With a face as warm and bright as a summer morning (004-007), he gains access through [[nello|Nello]]'s barbershop, the social hub of Florentine gossip, and through strategic marriages and political alignments.

Tito's power is the novel's most dynamic but also its most fragile. He gains a considerable influence in the conduct of affairs (022-011) by serving whoever holds power — first the Medici, then the Signoria, then playing both sides during Savonarola's ascendancy. His linguistic facility, classical education, and absence of moral constraint make him the perfect operator in a destabilized political environment.

Yet Eliot's structure makes clear that amoral power contains the seeds of its own destruction. Tito's deception of [[tessa|Tessa]], his sale of [[dinos-crucifix|Dino's crucifix]], his denial of Baldassarre — each betrayal adds weight to the eventual reckoning. His death in the Arno, strangled by the father he betrayed, is the novel's most explicit moral arithmetic: power without conscience flows inevitably toward self-annihilation.

## Analysis

The power triangle of *Romola* reveals Eliot's deeply Augustinian understanding of social order. Institutional power (Florence, the Church, the Signoria) is necessary but insufficient — it requires moral legitimacy to endure. Amoral power (Tito) is effective in the short term but self-consuming. Only moral power rooted in self-sacrifice (Romola) proves durable, yet it operates outside formal institutions and cannot directly govern.

This creates the novel's central tragic tension: the best people cannot rule, and those who rule cannot be trusted. Savonarola's tragedy is that he partially bridges this gap — he possesses genuine religious conviction — but his moral vision becomes corrupted by political ambition. Romola's triumph is quieter: she survives the plague, the political chaos, and Tito's betrayals to become a figure of sustained service, but her power never translates into institutional change.

Eliot's implicit argument is that moral power must precede and constrain institutional power, but the two cannot be wholly fused in a fallen world. The novel offers no political program, only a moral anthropology: institutions rise and fall with the virtue of those who inhabit them.

## Counterpoints

This triangular reading necessarily simplifies. The novel contains other significant power modalities not fully captured: the intellectual power of [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo]]'s classical scholarship, the social power of the [[bardi-family|Bardi family]]'s patronage networks, and the affective power of characters like [[tessa|Tessa]] whose simplicity grants them a kind of immunity from the political drama. Additionally, Eliot's narration itself exercises a kind of power — the power of historical interpretation — that deserves analysis beyond the scope of this article.

## Conclusion

The Florence-Romola-Tito triangle provides a framework for understanding power in *Romola* as a dynamic, multi-polar phenomenon. Institutional authority collapses without moral foundation; amoral cunning wins battles but loses the war; and moral integrity, though politically marginal, proves the only sustainable form of influence. Eliot's 1863 novel, written during her own period of religious and political questioning, uses Renaissance Florence to explore timeless questions about the relationship between character and governance, virtue and effectiveness, and the price of power.
