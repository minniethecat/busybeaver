## Overview

The entry of Charles VIII of France into [[florence|Florence]] in November 1494 is the political earthquake that reshapes the novel's world. The French king, whom Savonarola hailed as the "new Cyrus" and divine instrument of Florentine renewal, arrived at the head of an army that had swept through Italy unopposed. His presence precipitated the expulsion of Piero de' Medici, the establishment of the Great Council, and Savonarola's brief period of political dominance. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, the French entry is both historical backdrop and narrative hinge — the event that transforms [[florence|Florence]] from a [[medici-family|Medicean]] client state into a Savonarolan republic.

## Background

Charles VIII of France had inherited a claim to the Kingdom of Naples, and in 1494 he led an army across the Alps to enforce it. Piero de' Medici, [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s weak successor, attempted to appease the French by surrendering key Florentine fortresses — a capitulation that outraged the city and led to his expulsion. Savonarola had prophesied the coming of a foreign deliverer, and the French king's arrival seemed to fulfil his predictions.

## Description

The French entry occupies several chapters of the novel:

- **The [[prophecy-in-romola|prophecy]] fulfilled (021-006)**: The "conviction that the advent of the French king and his army into Italy was one of those events" foretold by [[prophecy-in-romola|prophecy]] spreads through [[florence|Florence]]. Savonarola identifies Charles as the "new Cyrus" — the Persian king who freed the Israelites from Babylon (021-013, 021-014).
- **Medici appeasement and collapse**: Piero de' Medici's surrender of frontier fortresses (021-015) is portrayed as the final proof of his unfitness to rule. The Medici palace in the Via Larga is prepared for the French king's reception (021-004), but Piero's [[power-in-romola|authority]] evaporates. The Medici are expelled, and [[florence|Florence]] is left to negotiate directly with the conqueror.
- **The entry itself (ch22–27)**: Charles enters [[florence|Florence]] in splendour — "one of the few really fine things" in the spectacle (027-003). But the Florentines quickly discover that their saviour-king comes with demands: "to pay large subsidies to the French king" (027-019). The "issue of the French king's visit" becomes a source of [[fear-in-romola|anxiety]], not liberation (027-020).
- **Savonarola's intervention**: At the crucial moment, Savonarola visits Charles in person, warning him of divine judgment if he harms [[florence|Florence]]. The Frate's moral [[power-in-romola|authority]], not military [[power-in-romola|power]], persuades the king to moderate his demands.
- **Piero's fate**: The French king's indifference to Piero's appeals — "whatever else the French king wants to make us swallow" (022-006) — seals [[medici-family|the Medici]]'s fate. Piero becomes an exile, and [[medici-family|the Medici]] palace stands empty.

## Participants

- **Charles VIII** — The French king whose invasion reshapes Italian politics; portrayed as a figure of ambiguous agency, driven by advisers and circumstances
- **Savonarola** — Hails Charles as the "new Cyrus"; his political influence peaks when he negotiates directly with the king
- **Piero de' Medici** — [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s son, whose capitulation to Charles triggers his expulsion from [[florence|Florence]]
- **[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]** — Observes and navigates the political upheaval, positioning himself for advantage regardless of outcome

## Aftermath

Charles departed [[florence|Florence]] in late November 1494, having secured subsidies but not the conquest of Naples he had sought. In his wake, [[florence|Florence]] established the Great Council, a [[great-council|popular government]] on the Venetian model, with Savonarola as its spiritual guide. The French invasion also triggered the revolt of [[pisa|Pisa]] — one of the novel's persistent political threads. For Savonarola, the French alliance would prove double-edged: it gave him a brief ascendancy but also made him dependent on a foreign king whose fortunes he could not control.

## Historical Context

The real Charles VIII invaded Italy in September 1494, crossed the Alps with an army of some 25,000 men, and reached [[florence|Florence]] on 17 November. Piero de' Medici had surrendered the fortresses of Sarzana, Pietrasanta, and [[pisa|Pisa]] without consultation — an act of capitulation that led the [[florentine-signoria|Florentine Signoria]] to expel him on 9 November. Savonarola's meeting with Charles, in which he supposedly warned the king of divine punishment, became a cornerstone of his legend. Charles continued to Naples, which he took in February 1495, but the Italian [[power-in-romola|power]]s formed a League against him and he was forced to retreat. His Italian adventure, though militarily inconclusive, permanently destabilised the peninsula's political order.
