## Overview

The *Dionysiaca* of Nonnus of Panopolis is a late-antique Greek epic poem in 48 books — the longest surviving Greek epic, and the most comprehensive mythological account of the god Dionysus. In George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]*, the *Dionysiaca* appears in a single but pivotal scene: [[romola|Romola]]'s reading from [[politians-miscellanea|Poliziano's *Miscellanea*]] in Chapter V, where a Greek quotation from Nonnus prompts her father Bardo to request his own manuscript copy.

## The Scene (Chapter V)

When [[romola|Romola]], reading from Poliziano's *[[politians-miscellanea|Miscellanea]]*, reaches [[tito-melema|the Greek]] quotation from the fifth book of the *Dionysiaca*, Bardo interrupts:

> "Stay, Romola; reach me my own copy of Nonnus. It is a more correct copy than any in Poliziano's hands, for I made emendations in it which have not yet been communicated to any man. I finished it in 1477, when my sight was fast failing me." (005-011)

This moment reveals Bardo's scholarly [[pride-in-romola|pride]] and his tragic isolation: he has spent his life improving texts that no other scholar will ever benefit from. His emendations to Nonnus, completed as his blindness descended, represent a life's work that will die with him — unless, as the novel's plot suggests, [[tito-melema|Tito]] might publish them.

## The Work

Nonnus of Panopolis (fl. c. 450–470 CE) was a Greek poet from Panopolis in Upper Egypt. The *Dionysiaca* recounts the life and deeds of Dionysus, from his birth to his apotheosis, in a style that combines Homeric epic conventions with the baroque ornamentation of late-antique rhetoric. Its 48 books — deliberately matching the combined books of the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* — make it a summa of Greek mythological learning.

The work was largely unknown in the medieval West. Greek manuscripts began arriving in Italy after the Fall of Constantinople (1453), and the *Dionysiaca* was among the texts that attracted the attention of Renaissance humanists. Bardo's possession of a manuscript — and his claim to have made emendations superior to Poliziano's — places him within the front rank of contemporary classical scholarship.

## Thematic Significance

Eliot's choice of Nonnus is thematically resonant. The *Dionysiaca* is a work about a god of intoxication, ecstasy, and transformation — the very forces that drive the novel's plot. Dionysus, like [[tito-melema|Tito]], is a figure of charm and danger; his cult, like Savonarola's [[religious-faith|Christianity]], offers ecstatic transcendence. The contrast between the pagan god and the Christian prophet structures the novel's central conflict between [[renaissance-humanism|classical culture]] and [[religious-faith|religious faith]].
