## Overview

Marriage in *[[romola|Romola]]* is both a narrative device and a moral laboratory. [[romola|Romola]]'s marriage to [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] is the central plot mechanism of the novel: it brings the two protagonists together, sets in motion the chain of [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]s that drives the story, and becomes the site of [[romola|Romola]]'s deepest moral education. George Eliot uses marriage to explore the tension between social convention and personal integrity, between the legal bond and the moral reality.

The novel's Proem frames the stakes explicitly, describing the story as concerning "the eternal marriage between love and [[duty-in-romola|duty]]" (P01-012). This metaphor elevates marriage from a social arrangement to a philosophical problem: how can love and obligation coexist in a single bond?

## The Marriage Plot

[[romola|Romola]]'s marriage to [[tito-melema|Tito]] unfolds as a study in asymmetrical commitment. [[romola|Romola]] enters the marriage in good [[religious-faith|faith]], believing [[tito-melema|Tito]] to be the scholar and partner her father Bardo had hoped for. [[tito-melema|Tito]] enters it as a strategic move — a way to secure his position in Florentine society and access Bardo's library.

The novel traces the deterioration of the marriage with clinical precision:

| Phase | [[romola|Romola]]'s State | [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s State |
|-------|---------------|--------------|
| Courtship | Idealistic trust | Strategic charm |
| Early marriage | Devotion, growing unease | Concealment, secondary life ([[tessa|Tessa]]) |
| Crisis | Discovery of [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]], attempted flight | Escalating [[betrayal-in-romola|deception]] |
| Dissolution | Moral independence, [[charity-in-romola|charity]] | Death in [[arno-river|the Arno]] |

## Marriage as Social Contract

In fifteenth-century [[florence|Florence]], marriage was not primarily a romantic union but a social and economic contract between families. The novel registers this reality through [[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]]'s involvement in arranging [[romola|Romola]]'s marriage and the political implications of marital alliances:

> He had interposed his advice that the marriage should be deferred till Easter (019-001).

Eliot is attentive to the gap between the social form of marriage and its lived reality. [[romola|Romola]]'s marriage is legally valid but morally void — [[tito-melema|Tito]] has violated every term of the unspoken contract before the wedding takes place.

## The Failure of the Marital Bond

[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]s destroy the marriage from within. His secret marriage to [[tessa|Tessa]], his sale of Bardo's library, his [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]] of [[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]] — each act widens the gap between the legal fact of the marriage and its moral content. [[romola|Romola]]'s discovery of these [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]s is the novel's central crisis:

> Romola might be walking towards betrayal (047-006).

The passive construction — "might be walking" — captures [[romola|Romola]]'s position: she is acted upon, unable to control the trajectory of her own marriage, moving toward a truth she cannot yet see.

## Marriage and Moral Independence

[[romola|Romola]]'s ultimate response to the failure of her marriage is neither revenge nor despair but moral independence. She does not seek to punish [[tito-melema|Tito]]; she separates her life from his. Her work among the [[death-in-romola|plague]]-stricken and her care for [[tessa|Tessa]] and her children represent a repudiation of the marriage as a defining structure of her identity.

This is Eliot's most radical claim about marriage in the novel: that the moral person survives the failure of the institution. The marriage bond is real and its violation is grievous, but it does not define the self.
