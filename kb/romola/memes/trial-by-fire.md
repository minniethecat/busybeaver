## Overview

The Trial by Fire — the *ordalia* or ordeal — was the dramatic public test proposed by Savonarola's Franciscan opponents and reluctantly accepted by Fra Domenico da Pescia on the Frate's behalf. The ordeal, which was to see a Dominican and a Franciscan walk through fire to prove the truth of Savonarola's prophetic claims, became instead the instrument of his undoing. When it failed to take place after days of theological wrangling, the disillusioned Florentine crowd turned against Savonarola, leading directly to his arrest.

## Background

By early 1498, Savonarola's position in [[florence|Florence]] had weakened. The papal excommunication had silenced his preaching; the failure to retake [[pisa|Pisa]] had undermined his political promises; and the [[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]] faction agitated openly against him. In this tense atmosphere, a Franciscan friar, Fra Francesco di Puglia, challenged Savonarola's claim to divine inspiration by proposing a trial by fire — a medieval ordeal in which God would protect the truthful and consume the false. Fra Domenico da Pescia, Savonarola's ardent disciple, accepted the challenge in his master's place (064-004).

## Description

The event unfolded over several days in April 1498:

- **The build-up (ch63)**: [[florence|Florence]] buzzed with anticipation. "When we have had the Trial by Fire," a shopkeeper declares, framing it as the city's next spectacle (063-036). The [[compagnacci|Compagnacci]] mocked it as "this little comedy of the Trial by Fire" (063-050), while Savonarola's enemies were "much inclined to urge on the Trial by Fire" (064-004).
- **The theological dispute (ch64)**: The two orders argued over conditions — whether Fra Domenico could carry the Host into the flames, whether Savonarola himself must walk. The [[franciscan-order|Franciscans]], Eliot notes, displayed "characteristics which have usually been considered virtues," while the Dominicans hesitated (064-006).
- **The day of the ordeal (ch65)**: On the appointed day, "the doubleness which is the peculiar curse of all human testimony" manifested (065-008). The crowd gathered in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]]; the pyre was prepared; the disputants entered. But the arguments continued, and the ordeal was postponed — hour by hour, until a sudden rainstorm provided a pretext for cancellation.
- **The collapse**: The Signoria ordered the proceedings halted. "Everybody knew now that the Trial by Fire was not to happen" (065-033). The crowd, cheated of its spectacle, turned its fury on Savonarola.

## Participants

- **Fra Domenico da Pescia** — Savonarola's devoted follower, who volunteered to walk through the fire on his master's behalf
- **Fra Francesco di Puglia** — The Franciscan challenger who proposed the ordeal
- **Savonarola** — The figure at the centre of the controversy, who did not personally participate but whose [[power-in-romola|authority]] hung on the outcome
- **The [[compagnacci|Compagnacci]]** — The anti-Savonarolan young aristocrats who mocked and exploited the spectacle
- **The Signoria** — The civic government that ultimately halted the proceedings

## Aftermath

The failed ordeal destroyed Savonarola's popular [[power-in-romola|authority]]. The crowd stormed [[san-marco|San Marco]] that night; Savonarola and his closest followers were arrested. Within weeks, he was tried, tortured, and executed. [[romola|Romola]], who had already left [[florence|Florence]], heard of these events only through distant reports: "reports reached her about the Trial by Fire, she had gone on to [[prato|Prato]]" (070-001). The ordeal's failure confirmed what Savonarola's enemies had long claimed — that his [[prophecy-in-romola|prophetic authority]] was a political fiction, unsustainable under the pressure of a concrete test.

## Historical Context

The real Trial by Fire was proposed in late March 1498 and scheduled for 7 April in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]]. The Dominican Fra Domenico da Pescia and the Franciscan Fra Giuliano Rondinelli were to be the participants. After days of dispute over conditions — particularly whether Fra Domenico could carry a consecrated Host — [[florentine-signoria|the Signoria]] cancelled the event. The crowd's fury led to the storming of [[san-marco|San Marco]] and Savonarola's arrest. Eliot's account in *[[romola|Romola]]* follows the historical record closely, capturing both the theological absurdity and the political tragedy of the event.
