## Overview

The [[tabernacle-of-the-crucifix|Tabernacle of the Crucifix]] is a small painted tabernacle, created by the Florentine artist [[florence|Piero di Cosimo]], that [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] gave to [[tessa|the contadina]] [[tessa|Tessa]] as a gift. The tabernacle was designed to house [[dinos-crucifix|Dino de' Bardi's ivory crucifix]], which [[tito-melema|Tito]] had stolen from his wife [[romola|Romola]]. Throughout [[romola|*Romola*]], the tabernacle serves as a tangible symbol of [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s double life — an object of genuine devotion for [[tessa|Tessa]], but a token of [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]] for [[romola|Romola]]. In the novel's later chapters, during the [[florentine-pestilence|famine and pestilence of 1497]], [[tessa|Tessa]] brings forth the tabernacle in a desperate act of [[religious-faith|faith]], believing it possesses miraculous [[power-in-romola|power]].

## Description

The tabernacle is described as a small, portable devotional object — a painted panel, likely tempera on wood, of the kind commonly produced in Renaissance [[florence|Florence]] for domestic worship. It was decorated by [[florence|Piero di Cosimo]], the eccentric Florentine painter known for his fantastical mythological scenes and inventive religious imagery. The tabernacle was fitted with a lock and key, and designed to enclose [[dinos-crucifix|the ivory crucifix]] within. Its small size allowed it to be concealed under a garment or placed discreetly in a room.

> Tito drew back his garment, he showed the little tabernacle which had been painted by Piero di Cosimo. (020-027)

## In the Novel

The tabernacle appears at pivotal moments in [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s secret relationship with [[tessa|Tessa]]:

1. **The gift (ch20)**: [[tito-melema|Tito]] presents the tabernacle — containing [[dino-de-bardi|Dino]]'s crucifix — to [[tessa|Tessa]], concealing it under his garment. He gives her the key, making her the keeper of this secret object.

2. **[[tessa|Tessa]]'s devotion (ch35–37)**: [[tessa|Tessa]] cherishes the locked tabernacle, visiting it in her room and addressing it with simple [[religious-faith|faith]]. When she discovers it empty (the crucifix removed), her distress reveals how deeply she invested the object with meaning.

3. **The [[death-in-romola|plague]] procession (ch42–43)**: During the [[florentine-pestilence|pestilence of 1497]], [[tessa|Tessa]] brings the tabernacle to [[santa-croce|Santa Croce]], believing the hidden crucifix within can work miracles. The tabernacle is carried in the great procession alongside other sacred objects, its painted exterior masking the personal — and morally compromised — history of what it contains.

> The empty tabernacle remained locked, and she placed it where she could see it from her bed. (041-013)

## Creator

The tabernacle was painted by **[[piero-di-cosimo|Piero di Cosimo]]** (1462–1522), a Florentine Renaissance painter renowned for his imaginative mythological scenes, portraits, and religious works. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, Piero appears briefly as a character — a friend of [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s with a taste for the grotesque and fanciful. His "strange freaking" sketch is mentioned early in the novel (003-022), and his choice to paint a tabernacle for [[tito-melema|Tito]] reflects his versatility as both a devotional and secular artist. Historically, [[piero-di-cosimo|Piero di Cosimo]] was a pupil of Cosimo Rosselli and worked alongside Botticelli and Ghirlandaio on the Sistine Chapel frescoes.

## Historical Context

Domestic tabernacles — small, portable shrines for private devotion — were common in Renaissance [[florence|Florence]]. They ranged from simple painted panels to elaborate gilded structures, and were used by families who could not afford a private chapel. [[piero-di-cosimo|Piero di Cosimo]]'s tabernacle for [[tessa|Tessa]] fits within this tradition, though its function in the novel is as much narrative as devotional: the locked box, the hidden crucifix, and the key entrusted to [[tessa|Tessa]] create a chain of secrecy that mirrors [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s compartmentalized life. The tabernacle's appearance in the [[death-in-romola|plague]] procession of chapter 43 also reflects the historical practice of carrying sacred objects through the streets during times of crisis, a form of collective supplication documented in Florentine chronicles.
