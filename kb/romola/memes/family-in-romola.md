## Overview

Family in *[[romola|Romola]]* is both a source of identity and a site of conflict. The novel traces the fate of [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] family — once one of the great Florentine houses, now reduced to a blind scholar and his daughter in a single room — and uses family bonds to explore questions of loyalty, inheritance, and [[duty-in-romola|moral obligation]]. George Eliot contrasts biological kinship with adoptive bonds, legitimate family with secret ones, and inherited [[duty-in-romola|duty]] with chosen commitment.

The novel's family structures are notably fractured. Bardo's family has been reduced by [[death-in-romola|death]] and disgrace; [[tito-melema|Tito]] has no known family, his origins mysterious; [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]], who raised [[tito-melema|Tito]] as a son, is not his biological father; [[tessa|Tessa]] and her children form a secret second family that [[tito-melema|Tito]] keeps hidden from his wife. This pattern of broken and hidden families is not incidental — it is central to the novel's vision of a social world in which traditional bonds have lost their binding force.

## The Bardi Legacy

The Bardi family represents the weight of the past. Once wealthy bankers and patrons, [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] have declined to a single room in the [[oltrarno|Oltrarno]]. Bardo's obsession with his family's scholarly reputation — his determination that a Bardi should be counted among the great scholars of Italy — drives much of the novel's early action:

> He had inherited the old family pride and energy (005-003).

For [[romola|Romola]], [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] legacy is both a gift and a burden. It gives her an education and a sense of purpose unavailable to most Florentine women, but it also chains her to a dying past. Her moral development requires her to honour what is valuable in that legacy while freeing herself from its constraints.

## Adoptive vs. Biological Kinship

The novel's most complex family relationship is not biological but adoptive: [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s relationship to [[tito-melema|Tito]]. [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] rescued the orphaned [[tito-melema|Tito]], raised him, educated him, and loved him as a son. [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s abandonment of [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] — selling the old man's ring, denying his identity, leaving him to slavery — is the novel's primal [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]], more fundamental even than [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]] of [[romola|Romola]].

Eliot uses this adoptive bond to ask what makes a family real. [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] has no legal claim on [[tito-melema|Tito]] and no biological connection to him, but the moral force of his claim is absolute. The novel insists that the obligations created by care and dependence are as binding as any blood tie — and that violating them is as serious as any crime.

## Secret Families

[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s secret [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] to [[tessa|Tessa]] and their children creates a hidden family structure that shadows the legitimate one. [[tessa|Tessa]], innocent and trusting, knows nothing of [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s other life. Her children are [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s only biological heirs, yet they exist entirely outside the social recognition that family normally confers.

[[romola|Romola]]'s final act — taking responsibility for [[tessa|Tessa]] and her children after [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[death-in-romola|death]] — represents the novel's resolution of the family theme. She creates, through an act of moral choice, a family that is not based on blood or law but on [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] and need.

## Family as Moral Obligation

The novel consistently treats family not as a sentimental bond but as a structure of [[duty-in-romola|moral obligation]]. To be part of a family is to owe something — care, loyalty, truthfulness. [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s systematic violation of these obligations is the measure of his corruption. [[romola|Romola]]'s gradual assumption of responsibility for others — first her father, then [[tessa|Tessa]]'s children — is the measure of her moral growth.

## Related Characters

- **[[romola|Romola de' Bardi]]**: The last of [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] line, who transforms family obligation into chosen [[charity-in-romola|compassion]]
- **[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]**: The orphan who betrays every family bond — adoptive, marital, and paternal
- **[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]**: The adoptive father whose claim on [[tito-melema|Tito]] is the novel's most [[power-in-romola|power]]ful assertion of non-biological kinship
- **[[tessa|Tessa]]**: The mother of [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s secret children, whose innocence makes her the novel's most vulnerable family figure
