## Overview

The Catholic Church in *[[romola|Romola]]* is not the Church of [[religious-faith|faith]] and devotion but the Church of institutional [[power-in-romola|power]] — the papacy, the cardinalate, the monastic orders, and the ecclesiastical courts that together constituted one of the most formidable political forces in fifteenth-century Italy. George Eliot presents the Church as a deeply ambiguous institution: it is the source of Savonarola's moral [[power-in-romola|authority]] and the agent of his destruction, the framework of [[charity-in-romola|charity]] and the object of [[tito-melema|Tito Melema's]] manipulation.

The novel's treatment of the institutional Church reflects Eliot's characteristic ambivalence toward organised [[religious-faith|religion]]. The Church's doctrines may be questionable, and its leadership may be corrupt, but its institutional presence shapes the moral landscape in which characters must act.

## History

The novel unfolds during the papacy of Alexander VI (Rodrigo Borgia, pope 1492–1503), one of the most controversial pontiffs in Church history. Alexander VI's papacy was marked by political intrigue, nepotism, and the consolidation of papal temporal [[power-in-romola|power]] in the Romagna. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, the Borgia papacy appears primarily as a hostile force — the [[power-in-romola|power]] that ultimately excommunicates Savonarola and demands his [[death-in-romola|execution]].

The Church in [[florence|Florence]] was closely intertwined with civic politics. The archbishop of [[florence|Florence]] was a political figure as much as a religious one, and the city's monastic houses — particularly [[san-marco|San Marco]] — were centres of both spiritual and political activity.

## Role in the Novel

The institutional Church serves three functions in the novel's plot:

**As antagonist**: The papacy of Alexander VI is the external force that ultimately brings Savonarola down. Savonarola's refusal to obey the Pope's summons to Rome and his continued preaching under excommunication create the legal-theological crisis that leads to his [[death-in-romola|execution]].

**As political actor**: The Church is not a neutral spiritual [[power-in-romola|authority]] but an active participant in Italian [[power-in-romola|power]] politics. [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]], ever the opportunist, navigates the ecclesiastical hierarchy as he navigates every other institution — seeking advantage, avoiding commitment.

**As moral framework**: Despite the corruption of its leadership, the Church provides the vocabulary and the institutional structure for the novel's moral concerns. Concepts of sin, repentance, [[charity-in-romola|charity]], and [[justice-in-romola|justice]] are articulated through the Church's language even when they are practised outside its formal boundaries.

## Historical Context

Eliot's portrait of the Renaissance Church draws on the nineteenth-century Protestant and liberal historiography that viewed the Borgia papacy as the nadir of ecclesiastical corruption. Her research for *[[romola|Romola]]* — conducted in [[florence|Florence]] in 1860–61 and through extensive reading in Italian Renaissance history — gave her a nuanced understanding of the period's religious politics.

The novel does not present the Church as uniformly corrupt. The Dominican order, and [[san-marco|San Marco]] in particular, represented a tradition of reform and moral seriousness within the Church. Savonarola's tragedy is partly that he belongs to an institution whose highest ideals are betrayed by its actual leadership.
