## Overview

[[religious-faith|Religious faith]] in George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]* is not the conventional piety of fifteenth-century Catholic [[florence|Florence]] but the transformative, politically charged Christianity of [[savonarola|Girolamo Savonarola]]. The novel stages a profound conflict between two worldviews: the [[renaissance-humanism|pagan-classical]] tradition represented by Bardo's scholarship and [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s instrumental reason, and the Christian call to active [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] and moral accountability represented by Savonarola's preaching and, ultimately, by [[romola|Romola]]'s own spiritual development.

## Paganism vs. Christianity

The novel's religious landscape is defined by three competing forces:

| Tradition | Representative | Core Value |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| **Pagan Classicism** | [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]] | Intellectual devotion to antiquity; Stoic endurance |
| **Conventional Catholicism** | The Florentine populace, the papacy | Ritual observance; institutional [[power-in-romola|power]] |
| **Savonarolan Reform** | Savonarola, [[romola|Romola]] (ultimately) | Moral purification; active [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] |

Bardo's paganism is treated with ambivalence. His devotion to classical antiquity is genuine and in some ways noble, but it has left him "a moneyless, blind old scholar" (005-003) whose intellectual world cannot address the needs of those around him. Conventional Catholicism appears largely as a corrupt institution — the papacy of Alexander VI, the worldly clergy — against which Savonarola's prophetic reform defines itself.

## Savonarola's Influence

[[savonarola|Girolamo Savonarola]] (1452–1498), [[savonarola|the Dominican friar]] who dominated Florentine politics from 1494 to 1498, is the novel's central religious figure. His sermons in the [[duomo|Duomo]] drew thousands with their apocalyptic warnings and calls to moral renewal:

- He preached that [[florence|Florence]] was chosen by God for renewal but must first undergo purification
- He denounced [[medici-family|the Medici]], the papacy, and the [[renaissance-humanism|pagan learning]] of the humanists
- He established a theocratic republic with Christ declared "King of [[florence|Florence]]"
- His influence culminated in the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]] (1497)

For [[romola|Romola]], Savonarola's intervention is decisive. When she attempts to flee [[florence|Florence]] — and her failed [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] — in despair, it is Savonarola who stops her at the city gate and commands her to return to her [[duty-in-romola|duty]]. His moral [[power-in-romola|authority]], whatever its limitations, redirects her life toward active [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] rather than passive endurance.

## Romola's Spiritual Evolution

[[romola|Romola]]'s religious journey is the novel's central spiritual drama. Raised by Bardo on classical texts, she begins the novel without any formed religious sensibility. Her experiences — [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]], her encounter with [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s suffering, Savonarola's command, the [[death-in-romola|plague]] village — gradually transform her Stoic endurance into something recognisably Christian: an ethic of active love for the vulnerable and suffering.

Eliot, herself a religious sceptic who had translated Strauss's *Life of Jesus* and Feuerbach's *Essence of Christianity*, does not endorse Savonarola's theology. But she presents the *effects* of his faith — the moral seriousness, the demand for [[justice-in-romola|justice]], the orientation toward the suffering of others — as genuinely redemptive. [[romola|Romola]]'s final role as protector of [[tessa|Tessa]] and her children embodies a "religion of humanity" that transcends any specific doctrinal commitment.
