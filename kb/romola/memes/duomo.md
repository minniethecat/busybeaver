## Overview

The Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore — known universally as the [[duomo|Duomo]] — dominates the [[florence|Florence]] of George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]* both architecturally and symbolically. Its great dome, completed by Brunelleschi in 1436, had been the marvel of fifteenth-century Europe; by the 1490s, the cathedral had become the stage upon which the religious drama of Savonarola's [[florence|Florence]] was enacted. The novel's characters repeatedly gather in the Duomo's vast nave to hear [[savonarola|the Dominican friar]]'s apocalyptic sermons, and the cathedral's shadow falls across the private moral struggles of [[romola|Romola]] and [[tito-melema|Tito]].

## Architecture

Brunelleschi's dome — the largest masonry dome ever constructed — was both an engineering triumph and a symbol of Florentine civic [[pride-in-romola|pride]]. In the novel's opening, the dome and Giotto's campanile are the first landmarks the eye seeks when approaching [[florence|Florence]]:

> The great bell-tower of Giotto, tall and square, with its bas-reliefs and coloured marbles, and beside it the Cathedral, with Brunelleschi's mighty cupola atop, stood before him in the clear morning light. (implied by the Proem's panoramic view from San Miniato)

The Piazza del Duomo functioned as [[florence|Florence]]'s primary public space — a place where citizens gathered not only for worship but for political discourse, public announcements, and the great communal rituals of civic life.

## Religious Significance

The Duomo was the seat of the Archbishop of [[florence|Florence]] and the primary venue for the city's major religious observances. During the period of the novel (1492–1498), it became inextricably associated with the figure of Girolamo [[savonarola|Savonarola]], whose sermons drew crowds so large that the cathedral could scarcely contain them.

Savonarola's preaching from the Duomo's pulpit transformed the cathedral from a site of conventional piety into the epicentre of a religious revolution. His prophecies of divine judgement upon [[florence|Florence]] — his denunciations of [[medici-family|the Medici]], of clerical corruption, and of [[renaissance-humanism|pagan learning]] — electrified audiences that included the novel's central characters. [[romola|Romola]]'s spiritual crisis and eventual awakening are directly shaped by what she hears within these walls.

## Civic Function

In fifteenth-century [[florence|Florence]], the Duomo was as much a civic as a religious building. The cathedral's *Opera* (works committee) was a major institution of city government, and the building's decoration was funded by the city's [[power-in-romola|power]]ful guilds. Public ceremonies — the installation of magistrates, the celebration of military victories, the reception of foreign dignitaries — were held within or before the cathedral. This fusion of sacred and civic functions made the Duomo a natural setting for the novel's exploration of how political and religious [[power-in-romola|power]] intertwine.
