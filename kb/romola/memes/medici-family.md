## Overview

The [[medici-family|House of Medici]] was the dominant political and banking dynasty of [[florence|Florence]] for most of the fifteenth century. From Cosimo de' Medici's rise in 1434 to the expulsion of his great-grandson Piero in 1494, the Medici ruled [[florence|Florence]] not through formal office but through patronage, wealth, and strategic alliances — a system of "crypto-rule" that preserved republican forms while concentrating [[power-in-romola|power]] in a single [[family-in-romola|family]]. The novel opens on the morning after the [[death-in-romola|death]] of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]], "the very pivot of Italy" (004-001), and the political vacuum his [[death-in-romola|death]] creates is the engine of the novel's plot.

## The Medici in the Novel

### Lorenzo the Magnificent

Although [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]] (1449–1492) dies before the novel's action begins, his presence haunts every page of its political narrative. Referenced in [[nello|Nello]]'s barber shop as the irreplaceable centre of Italian diplomacy — "[[florence|Florence]] orphaned — the very pivot of Italy snatched away" (004-001) — [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]] represents the order that his [[death-in-romola|death]] destroys. His patronage of [[renaissance-humanism|humanist scholarship]] created the intellectual world in which Bardo, Poliziano, and Pico della Mirandola flourished.

### Piero the Unfortunate

Piero de' Medici (1472–1503), [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s eldest son, inherited his father's position but none of his political skill. His craven capitulation to Charles VIII of France in 1494 — surrendering Florentine fortresses without resistance — led directly to the Medici expulsion and the establishment of the Savonarolan republic. Piero appears in the novel as a background figure of weakness and failure, the negative space against which Savonarola's charismatic [[power-in-romola|authority]] defines itself.

## Medici Patronage and Humanism

The Medici were the principal patrons of Florentine [[renaissance-humanism|humanism]]. [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s circle included:

| Scholar | Contribution | Reference in Novel |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Marsilio Ficino | Translated Plato into Latin | Background intellectual presence |
| Angelo Poliziano | Classical philologist, poet | His *[[politians-miscellanea|Miscellanea]]* read by [[romola|Romola]] in ch05 |
| Pico della Mirandola | Syncretic philosopher | Referenced by [[nello|Nello]] as "our incomparable Pico" (004-014) |

The Medici bank, with branches across Europe, funded not only political operations but also the acquisition of Greek and Latin manuscripts — the very texts that fill Bardo's library and shape [[romola|Romola]]'s education. The Medici thus represent both the cultural achievement and the political entanglement of Renaissance [[renaissance-humanism|humanism]].
