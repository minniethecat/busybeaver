## Overview

Sacrifice in *[[romola|Romola]]* is the novel's highest moral value. George Eliot traces a movement from sacrificial obligation — the [[duty-in-romola|duty]] [[romola|Romola]] owes her father — to sacrificial freedom — the gift [[romola|Romola]] gives the [[death-in-romola|plague]]-stricken without expectation of return. The novel distinguishes between sacrifice that is coerced by social expectation ([[romola|Romola]]'s service to Bardo) and sacrifice that is freely chosen ([[romola|Romola]]'s ministry to the suffering). The second kind of sacrifice is the novel's moral destination.

This arc mirrors Eliot's own Feuerbachian [[renaissance-humanism|humanism]]: the true meaning of [[religious-faith|Christianity]] is not doctrinal belief but the willingness to give oneself for others. [[romola|Romola]], who begins the novel without religious [[religious-faith|faith]], ends it as the fullest embodiment of the Christian ideal of self-sacrifice.

## Bardo's Claim: Sacrifice as Obligation

[[romola|Romola]]'s life with her father is structured by sacrifice. She gives up the ordinary pleasures of youth — companionship, [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]], independence — to serve Bardo's scholarship. This sacrifice is real and costly, but the novel treats it with ambivalence. Bardo's claim on [[romola|Romola]] is legitimate — he is her father, he is blind, he depends on her — but it is also limiting. His demand that [[romola|Romola]] serve his intellectual project leaves no room for her own life:

> The sense of love and duty to her father's memory. All Romola's ardour was thrown into that channel (027-020).

The metaphor of the "channel" is revealing: [[romola|Romola]]'s capacity for devotion is real, but under Bardo it flows in only one direction, toward the dead past rather than the living present.

## Romola's Transformation: Sacrifice as Freedom

[[romola|Romola]]'s later sacrifices — caring for the [[death-in-romola|plague]]-stricken, taking responsibility for [[tessa|Tessa]] and her children — are of a different order. They are not coerced by [[family-in-romola|family]] obligation or social expectation. They are freely chosen acts of self-giving that cost [[romola|Romola]] everything and promise nothing in return.

This transformation reframes sacrifice from burden to freedom. [[romola|Romola]] is never more free than when she is giving herself to those who cannot repay her. The novel's most radical claim is that self-sacrifice, far from diminishing the self, is the only path to genuine moral autonomy.

## Baldassarre: The Dark Side of Sacrifice

[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] offers a cautionary counter-example. His sacrifice for [[tito-melema|Tito]] — raising an orphan as his son, giving him education, love, and [[family-in-romola|inheritance]] — is genuine and generous. But when [[tito-melema|Tito]] betrays that sacrifice, [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s love curdles into vengeance. His pursuit and killing of [[tito-melema|Tito]] is the dark twin of [[romola|Romola]]'s self-giving: sacrifice that, once betrayed, becomes destructive rather than generative.

The contrast between [[romola|Romola]] and [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] clarifies the novel's moral logic. Sacrifice is not inherently good; it is good only when it is freely offered and does not demand repayment. [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s sacrifice came with an implicit claim on [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s loyalty; when that claim was violated, the sacrifice became a weapon.

## Sacrifice and the Novel's Moral Vision

The novel's treatment of sacrifice is inseparable from its treatment of [[religious-faith|religion]]. Savonarola preaches the sacrifice of worldly goods (the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]]) and the sacrifice of self-will (submission to God). But [[romola|Romola]]'s sacrifice exceeds Savonarola's teaching. Where Savonarola's sacrifice is bound up with [[power-in-romola|power]] — the [[power-in-romola|power]] to command others to renounce — [[romola|Romola]]'s sacrifice is purely personal. She asks nothing of anyone and gives everything she has.

This is Eliot's secular saint: a person who sacrifices not for God but for other human beings, whose moral life needs no supernatural sanction. [[romola|Romola]]'s sacrifice is the novel's answer to the question posed by its religious plot: what does genuine goodness look like in a world without guaranteed [[justice-in-romola|divine justice]]?
