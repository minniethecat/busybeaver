## Overview

The papal excommunication of [[savonarola|Fra Girolamo]] Savonarola by Pope Alexander VI in the summer of 1497 was the decisive ecclesiastical blow against the Dominican reformer. It forbade him from preaching, separated him from the sacraments, and placed all Florentines who supported him under the same spiritual penalty. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, the excommunication is a watershed: it transforms Savonarola from a triumphant prophet into a besieged dissident, and it forces every character — [[romola|Romola]] most of all — to choose between institutional [[power-in-romola|authority]] and personal conviction.

## Background

Savonarola's conflict with Rome had been building since his refusal to join Alexander VI's Holy League against the French. The Pope, a Borgia whose worldly interests were threatened by Savonarola's calls for reform, issued a brief in 1495 forbidding the Frate from preaching. Savonarola obeyed temporarily, but resumed in early 1496, arguing that divine mandate overrode papal command. The Pope's patience exhausted, formal excommunication followed.

## Description

The excommunication unfolds in stages throughout the novel's second half:

- **The imminence**: Savonarola, preaching in the Duomo, "knew that excommunication was imminent, and he had reached the climax of his daring" (052-003). Rather than retreat, he escalates his rhetoric — the approaching ban only intensifies his prophetic defiance.
- **The silencing**: [[romola|Romola]], attending the Duomo, cannot "witness the silencing and excommunication of a man whose distinction from the common crowd had been so sharply marked" (055-002). The excommunication is both a legal act and a public spectacle — the silencing of [[florence|Florence]]'s most [[power-in-romola|power]]ful voice.
- **[[romola|Romola]]'s crisis**: For [[romola|Romola]], the excommunication has a double significance. She sees in it "only the menace of hostile slander" (055-006) — the same forces that destroyed her trust in [[tito-melema|Tito]] now arrayed against Savonarola. Yet she has already "bowed" to Savonarola's spiritual [[power-in-romola|authority]]; the excommunication forces her to reconcile institutional Church [[power-in-romola|authority]] with the prophet who spoke to her soul.
- **The public rift**: The [[florentine-signoria|Florentine government]] and citizens face a brutal choice. Those who hear Savonarola preach are "forbidden to hear on pain of excommunication" (062-003). The Signoria must navigate between loyalty to the Frate and obedience to Rome. A proclamation declaring the excommunication void — "_Excommunicatio contra Fratrem Hieronymum nulla est_" — circulates, but the legal reality remains (063-035).
- **The aftermath**: After Savonarola's [[death-in-romola|execution]], the excommunication's legacy persists. [[romola|Romola]] reflects on the difficulty of justifying "the disregard of the sentence of excommunication" (071-006). The Church's verdict on Savonarola's soul remains, even as his political influence is extinguished.

## Participants

- **Savonarola** — The object of the excommunication; his response — defiance followed by silence — defines the novel's final movement
- **Pope Alexander VI** — The Borgia pope who issued the excommunication; his motives were political rather than theological
- **[[romola|Romola de' Bardi]]** — Struggles to reconcile her loyalty to Savonarola with the [[power-in-romola|authority]] of [[catholic-church-in-romola|the Church]]; the excommunication deepens her spiritual crisis (055-002–006)
- **The [[florentine-signoria|Florentine Signoria]]** — Caught between the Frate's popular support and the diplomatic necessity of papal obedience

## Aftermath

The excommunication silenced Savonarola's preaching for months, depriving him of his primary instrument of influence. It emboldened his enemies — the [[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]] and [[compagnacci|Compagnacci]] — and isolated his supporters. It provided the legal-theological framework for his eventual arrest and trial. Without the excommunication, the [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]] might never have been proposed, and Savonarola might have retained enough [[power-in-romola|authority]] to survive. In the novel's moral economy, the excommunication represents the tragic collision between institutional [[power-in-romola|power]] and individual conscience.

## Historical Context

Alexander VI (Rodrigo Borgia) excommunicated Savonarola by a papal brief dated 12 May 1497, after the Frate's refusal to cease preaching and submit to papal [[power-in-romola|authority]]. The excommunication threatened any city or individual harbouring Savonarola with interdict. Savonarola's response — that an unjust excommunication was invalid before God — drew on medieval conciliarist traditions that distinguished between the office of the papacy and its fallible occupant. He was executed in May 1498 without the excommunication having been lifted. In 1558, Pope Paul IV declared Savonarola's writings free of heresy, and his cause for canonisation has been intermittently pursued.
