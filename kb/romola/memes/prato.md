## Overview

Prato is a Tuscan town situated within [[florence|Florence]]'s regional orbit. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, it appears as part of the geographic panorama that frames the novel's Florentine world — one of the satellite towns whose fates are bound to the dominant city. Its presence, though limited in the narrative, contributes to the novel's richly textured sense of Tuscan geography.

## Location

Prato lies approximately 20 kilometres north-west of [[florence|Florence]], on the plain of the Bisenzio River. In the Renaissance, it lay within [[florence|Florence]]'s *contado* (subject territory), its political and economic life firmly tied to the dominant city. From [[fiesole|Fiesole]]'s heights, the view extends towards Prato, placing it within the novel's panoramic geography.

## Key Events

Prato's references are contextual rather than scenic:

- **The panoramic view (P01-004)**: From [[fiesole|Fiesole]], the gaze extends across [[arno-river|the Arno]] valley towards "the walls of Prato" — the town forming part of the wider Tuscan landscape that Eliot meticulously sketches in the Proem.
- **Regional politics**: As a Florentine subject town, Prato's subordination reflects the political structure of Renaissance Tuscany — a city-state exerting control over its surrounding territories, a dynamic that shapes the novel's implicit political geography.

## Associated Characters

None directly; Prato serves as geographic context rather than a character-associated setting.

## Symbolism

Prato represents the broader Tuscan world beyond [[florence|Florence]]'s walls — a reminder that the city's dramas unfold within a regional framework. The town's visibility from [[fiesole|Fiesole]], and its mention alongside other Tuscan landmarks, establishes the geographic scope of the novel's world: [[florence|Florence]] is not an island but the centre of a political and cultural network.

## Historical Context

The real Prato has been a significant Tuscan centre since the Etruscan period. In the Renaissance, it was known for its textile industry (especially wool) and came under Florentine control in 1351. Its Duomo contains important frescoes by Fra Filippo Lippi. The relationship between [[florence|Florence]] and its subject towns is a recurring theme in Renaissance historiography, and Eliot's geographic references reflect her careful research into Tuscan topography.
