## Overview

The trial and [[death-in-romola|execution]] of [[savonarola|Girolamo Savonarola]] in May 1498 bring George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]* to its political and spiritual climax. Just fifteen months after the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]] had seemingly confirmed his dominance over [[florence|Florence]], [[savonarola|the Dominican friar]] was arrested, tortured, tried by a joint papal-Florentine commission, and executed on the very [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]] where his bonfire had blazed. The event crystallises the novel's themes of [[power-in-romola|political power]], [[religious-faith|religious authority]], and the cost of moral conviction in a world of competing interests.

## The Fall of Savonarola

Savonarola's fall from [[power-in-romola|power]] was the result of converging forces:

1. **Papal opposition**: Pope Alexander VI had excommunicated Savonarola in May 1497, cutting him off from the sacraments and ordering [[florence|Florence]] to silence him
2. **Political opposition**: The *[[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]]* (the "Enraged") — Florentine aristocrats hostile to Savonarola's populist theocracy — gained political strength
3. **Economic pressure**: The papal interdict threatened [[florence|Florence]]'s commercial interests
4. **The failed Ordeal by Fire**: In April 1498, Savonarola's supporter Fra Domenico da Pescia agreed to walk through fire to prove the friar's divine mandate; when [[trial-by-fire|the ordeal]] was cancelled at the last moment due to procedural disputes, public support for Savonarola collapsed

In the novel, [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] plays a significant role in Savonarola's downfall. Having initially manoeuvred between factions, [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s political calculations lead him to align with the forces seeking Savonarola's destruction. His instrumental intelligence, devoid of loyalty or conviction, is the antithesis of Savonarola's prophetic certainty — and in the short term, it is [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s worldliness that prevails.

## The Trial

Savonarola was arrested on 8 April 1498 and imprisoned in the [[palazzo-vecchio|Palazzo Vecchio]]. Under torture (the *strappado*), he made confessions that he later recanted — a pattern that his supporters cited as proof of his sanctity (he recanted under no duress) and his enemies as proof of his fraud (he confessed under the only conditions that produced truth).

The trial was a hybrid proceeding, combining Florentine civil [[power-in-romola|authority]] with papal commissioners sent from Rome. The charges included heresy, schism, and false [[prophecy-in-romola|prophecy]]. The outcome was never in serious doubt: Savonarola's enemies controlled the process, and the papal interest in his elimination was absolute.

## The Execution

On 23 May 1498, Savonarola and two fellow Dominicans — Fra Domenico da Pescia and Fra Silvestro Maruffi — were led to a scaffold erected in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]]:

> The three friars were first degraded from their holy orders, then hanged in chains from a tall gibbet. A great fire was kindled beneath them, and their bodies were reduced to ashes, which were thrown into the Arno to prevent any relic-taking by the devout.

Eliot presents the [[death-in-romola|execution]] with restraint but with clear moral weight. For [[romola|Romola]], who had been redirected toward [[duty-in-romola|duty]] by Savonarola's intervention at the city gate, the friar's [[death-in-romola|death]] raises profound questions: if the prophet who spoke with such moral [[power-in-romola|authority]] could be destroyed by the same political forces that sustain [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s career, what remains of the moral order Savonarola claimed to represent?

## Romola's Response

[[romola|Romola]]'s response to Savonarola's [[death-in-romola|death]] is the novel's deepest spiritual reckoning. She does not abandon the moral awakening he catalysed — indeed, her subsequent life of active [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] in the [[death-in-romola|plague]] village and her care for [[tessa|Tessa]] and her children represent the fruits of that awakening — but she must now ground her morality in human sympathy rather than in [[prophecy-in-romola|prophetic authority]]. This is Eliot's Feuerbachian resolution: the divine is found not in a supernatural realm but in the bonds of human love and [[duty-in-romola|duty]].
