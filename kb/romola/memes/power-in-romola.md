## Overview

Power in *[[romola|Romola]]* is never stable. It is contested, transferred, lost, and seized — by [[medici-family|the Medici]], by Savonarola, by the French king, and by individuals like [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] who exploit instability for personal gain. George Eliot depicts fifteenth-century [[florence|Florence]] as a city where institutional authority has collapsed and power flows unpredictably between factions, families, and charismatic individuals.

The novel examines power in three registers: political (who governs [[florence|Florence]]), ecclesiastical (who speaks for God), and personal (who controls whom in intimate relationships). These registers are not separate — Savonarola's religious authority becomes political power; [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s personal charm becomes a tool for political manipulation.

## The Medici and the Power Vacuum

The novel opens in 1492, the year of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]]'s [[death-in-romola|death]]. [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]] had kept [[florence|Florence]] stable through a combination of wealth, patronage, and political skill. His [[death-in-romola|death]] creates a vacuum:

> The Medici and other powerful families of the _popolani_ (001-022).

The departure of [[medici-family|the Medici]] — and the succession of the incompetent Piero — leaves [[florence|Florence]] vulnerable to external threats (Charles VIII of France) and internal upheaval (Savonarola's theocratic revolution). The novel traces how power, once concentrated in a single [[family-in-romola|family]], disperses and re-forms around new centres of authority.

## Savonarola's Theocratic Power

Savonarola represents a new kind of power: authority derived not from wealth or birth but from prophetic charisma. His control over [[florence|Florence]] from 1494 to 1498 is theocratic — he claims to speak for God, and his claim is believed by enough Florentines to make him the city's effective ruler.

Eliot presents Savonarola's power as genuinely transformative but also deeply unstable. His authority depends on the continued credibility of his prophetic claims. When those claims fail — when the French king does not fulfil his predicted role, when the trial by fire collapses — his power evaporates:

> Capable of discerning that power was to be won by other means than by setting up a new Fiesole (005-002).

## Tito's Personal Power

[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] embodies a different form of power: the power of charm, intelligence, and moral ruthlessness. Unlike Savonarola, [[tito-melema|Tito]] does not believe in anything beyond his own advancement. His power is purely instrumental — he cultivates relationships, accumulates secrets, and manoeuvres between factions.

[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s tragedy is that personal power, pursued without moral constraint, ultimately destroys the person who wields it. The network of [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]s and [[betrayal-in-romola|deception]]s that sustains [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s rise becomes the trap that kills him.
