## Thesis

The Savonarola era occupies a disproportionate share of *Romola*'s narrative space — roughly the final third of the novel — and forms its densest thematic cluster, spanning 10 wiki pages across four content types. George Eliot uses the historical arc of Girolamo Savonarola's rise and fall (1494–1498) not merely as period setting but as a controlled experiment in the collision between prophetic religious authority and political power. The experiment's failure — Savonarola's excommunication, the failed Trial by Fire, and his execution — articulates Eliot's central thesis: that religious conviction, when it seeks to govern rather than to witness, inevitably corrupts itself into the very worldly power it claims to transcend.

## Evidence

### Rise: From San Marco to the Signoria

[[savonarola|Girolamo Savonarola]] arrives in Florence as a Dominican friar at [[san-marco|San Marco]], preaching a message of repentance and renewal. His initial authority derives entirely from moral and spiritual sources — the power of his oratory, the perceived authenticity of his prophetic claims, and the widespread discontent following [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]]'s death.

The [[entry-of-charles-viii|Entry of Charles VIII]] (1494) serves as the pivot. The French invasion and Medici expulsion create a power vacuum that Savonarola fills, positioning himself as Florence's spiritual guide through crisis. The establishment of the [[great-council|Great Council]] (1494) — a popular republican reform — bears Savonarola's imprint, fusing religious revival with political restructuring. This is the high-water mark of prophetic influence: spiritual authority successfully reshaping civic institutions.

### Peak: The Bonfire and Theocratic Florence

At the height of his influence, Savonarola orchestrates the [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]] in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]] — a ritual destruction of luxury goods, artworks, books, and mirrors symbolizing worldly vanity. The bonfire represents the most dramatic fusion of religious and political power in the novel: a friar commanding the public square where civic authority normally resides.

The Savonarola cluster extends far beyond the man himself. The [[catholic-church-in-romola|Catholic Church]] hierarchy, the [[florentine-signoria|Signoria]]'s secular officials, the [[compagnacci|Compagnacci]] youth gang, and the rival [[franciscan-order|Franciscan Order]] all orbit around Savonarola as either allies or antagonists. [[religious-faith|Religious faith]] as a concept takes concrete political form, while [[fear-in-romola|fear]] — of damnation, of French invasion, of civil chaos — becomes the emotional currency of theocratic governance.

### Fall: Excommunication, Trial, Execution

The [[savonarola-excommunication|excommunication]] by Pope Alexander VI marks the turning point — institutional Church authority withdraws its sanction, forcing a choice between obedience to Rome and continued prophetic mission. Savonarola chooses the latter, and his support begins to erode.

The [[trial-by-fire|Trial by Fire]] — an ordeal proposed by the Franciscans to test Savonarola's divine mandate — becomes the dramatic climax of his arc. When the trial is cancelled (ostensibly due to rain, actually due to procedural disputes), Savonarola's prophetic authority collapses. The crowd that once revered him turns hostile. He is arrested, tried by the [[otto-di-guardia|Otto di Guardia]], and executed in the Piazza della Signoria — the same square where his bonfire had blazed.

## Analysis

Eliot's treatment of Savonarola reveals a sophisticated political theology. She does not dismiss him as a fanatic — indeed, the novel grants him genuine spiritual insight and moral seriousness. His critique of Medici corruption, his care for the poor, and his theological convictions are all treated with respect. Yet the narrative arc is unambiguous: prophetic authority, when institutionalized, becomes another form of tyranny.

This pattern operates at three levels:
1. **Personal**: Savonarola's character arc — from ascetic preacher to political operator to condemned heretic — traces the corruption of spiritual authority by political necessity.
2. **Institutional**: The San Marco convent, the Dominican Order, and the Florentine republic all demonstrate how religious movements become entangled in the power structures they originally opposed.
3. **Theological**: The novel quietly endorses a separation of spiritual and political spheres — not as a constitutional principle, but as a psychological and moral necessity.

The key insight is that Savonarola's failure is not a failure of conviction but of scope. Religious faith can guide individual conscience (as it does for [[romola|Romola]]), but when it seeks to govern a city, it must adopt the tools of coercion, surveillance, and factional politics — tools that are incompatible with its original moral purpose.

## Conclusion

The Savonarola era in *Romola* functions as Eliot's most sustained meditation on the relationship between religion and politics. The density of the Savonarola cluster in the wiki — 10 pages spanning persons, events, organizations, and concepts — reflects the narrative weight Eliot gives this period. Savonarola's Florence is a laboratory in which prophetic authority is tested against the realities of governance, and the experiment fails. The novel's conclusion, in which Romola survives to serve rather than to rule, quietly endorses a different model: moral witness operating at the scale of individual relationship, not institutional power.
