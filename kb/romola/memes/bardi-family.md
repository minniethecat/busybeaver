## Overview

The [[bardi-family|Bardi family]] was one of [[florence|Florence]]'s ancient noble houses, prominent in banking and patronage of the arts. In [[romola|*Romola*]], the [[family-in-romola|family]] is represented through [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]], the blind classical scholar, and his son [[dino-de-bardi|Dino de' Bardi]], who abandoned scholarly life to become a Dominican friar at [[san-marco-convent|San Marco]]. The [[family-in-romola|family]]'s decline from its medieval banking zenith mirrors the broader theme of decay and renewal that runs through the novel. The [[bardi-family|Bardi]] gave their name to the Via de' Bardi in [[oltrarno|the Oltrarno district]], where the [[family-in-romola|family]] palazzo and library were located.

## History

The Bardi were among the earliest Florentine banking dynasties, rising to prominence in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries as financiers to European monarchs. By the time of *[[romola|Romola]]* (the 1490s), however, the [[family-in-romola|family]]'s commercial fortunes had waned — a decline symbolized by [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo's]] blindness and his retreat into classical scholarship. Bardo's singular ambition was to preserve the [[bardi-family|Bardi Library]], his collection of Greek and Latin manuscripts, as a lasting monument to the [[family-in-romola|family]] name.

> The names of the Bardi, father and son, might have been written in human blood and fire. (005-030)

## Role in the Novel

The Bardi [[family-in-romola|family]] serves as a lens through which Eliot explores several of the novel's central themes:

1. **The [[death-in-romola|death]] of pagan learning**: Bardo's blindness — both physical and metaphorical — represents the limits of classical [[renaissance-humanism|humanism]] in the face of Savonarola's Christian revival. His beloved [[bardi-family|Bardi Library]] is ultimately sold and dispersed.

2. **Filial [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]**: [[dino-de-bardi|Dino's]] abandonment of his father for the Dominican order mirrors [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s later [[betrayal-in-romola|betrayal]]s, establishing a pattern of broken [[family-in-romola|family]] bonds.

3. **The old [[florence|Florence]]**: The Bardi palazzo on the Via de' Bardi represents the ancient Florentine aristocracy, whose [[power-in-romola|power]] was fading in [[medici-family|the Medici]] era. The [[family-in-romola|family]]'s noble [[family-in-romola|lineage]] is invoked as a contrast to the rising mercantile class.

> However, a descendant of the Bardi, and of that very branch which had made a particularly noble figure in the days of the Republic, might still be found in Florence. (005-003)

## Historical Context

Historically, the Bardi [[family-in-romola|family]] were one of the great Florentine banking houses of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, alongside the Peruzzi and Acciaiuoli. Their bank collapsed in 1345 when Edward III of England defaulted on his loans — an event that contributed to the broader Florentine economic crisis of the mid-fourteenth century. By the 1490s setting of *[[romola|Romola]]*, the [[family-in-romola|family]]'s political and financial [[power-in-romola|power]] had diminished, though they retained their patrician status. Eliot uses the Bardis as an emblem of Florentine nobility in decline, contrasting their scholarly retreat with the turbulent religious politics of Savonarola's [[florence|Florence]].
