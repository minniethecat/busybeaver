## Overview

Messer Bernardo del Nero is [[romola|Romola]]'s godfather and one of [[florence|Florence]]'s most respected citizens — a man whose political loyalties to [[medici-family|the Medici]] party place him in fatal opposition to the Savonarolan republic. His [[death-in-romola|execution]] alongside other [[medici-family|Medicean]] conspirators is one of the novel's most politically charged events, testing [[romola|Romola]]'s loyalties and exposing the human cost of factional politics.

## Biography

### The Godfather

Bernardo is introduced early in the novel as a [[family-in-romola|family]] friend of [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] household. Bardo describes him warmly:

> "Messer Bernardo del Nero, my oldest, I had almost said my only friend among the citizens of Florence." (006-077)

His relationship to [[romola|Romola]] is one of genuine affection, and he serves as a source of worldly wisdom that complements Bardo's scholarly isolation.

### Political Observer

Bernardo's political instincts are acute. He perceives [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s character with more clarity than most:

> "Bernardo del Nero's eyes had retained their keenness for character." (012-051)

His "contempt for [[tito-melema|Tito]] as a fawning Greek" (013-037) marks him as one of the few characters who shares [[piero-di-cosimo|Piero di Cosimo]]'s ability to see through [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s charm.

### The Conspiracy and Execution

As a prominent [[medici-family|Medicean]], Bernardo becomes implicated in the conspiracy to restore Medici rule after Savonarola's fall. His arrest and condemnation place [[romola|Romola]] in an agonising position — torn between her godfather's life and Savonarola's refusal to intervene. Bernardo's [[death-in-romola|execution]] is one of the events that shatters [[romola|Romola]]'s [[religious-faith|faith]] in Savonarola's spiritual [[power-in-romola|authority]].

## Character Analysis

Bernardo del Nero embodies the old Florentine political class — men of substance and experience whose loyalties were shaped by [[medici-family|the Medici]] patronage system that had governed [[florence|Florence]] for generations. His tragedy is that of a man caught between epochs: too [[medici-family|Medicean]] for the Savonarolan republic, too honourable to abandon his friends, and too visible to escape the consequences.

Eliot presents him with the respect due to a man of integrity operating within a flawed political system. His fate is not deserved in any simple moral sense, but is rather the consequence of political forces larger than any individual — a theme that runs throughout the novel.

## Historical Context

The historical Bernardo del Nero was indeed a prominent Florentine citizen executed in 1497 for his involvement in a [[medici-family|Medicean]] conspiracy against the Savonarolan republic. His [[death-in-romola|execution]], along with those of four other leading citizens (including [[lorenzo-tornabuoni|Lorenzo Tornabuoni]]), was a pivotal moment in Savonarola's downfall — the refusal of [[florentine-signoria|the Signoria]] to grant an appeal to the accused was seen by many Florentines as a violation of traditional legal rights, and Savonarola's failure to speak against the [[death-in-romola|execution]]s cost him support even among his followers.
