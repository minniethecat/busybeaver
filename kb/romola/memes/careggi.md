## Overview

Careggi is [[medici-family|the Medici]] [[family-in-romola|family]]'s countryside villa in the hills north of [[florence|Florence]], immortalised in *[[romola|Romola]]* as the place where [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]] — [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]] [[lorenzo-de-medici|the Magnificent]] — spent his final hours. The novel opens in the shadow of this [[death-in-romola|death]]: news travels from Careggi to [[florence|Florence]] on the morning of 9 April 1492, setting the political machinery of the republic in motion.

## Location

The Villa Medici di Careggi lies approximately five kilometres north of [[florence|Florence]]'s centre, in the hill country that rises towards [[fiesole|Fiesole]]. In the fifteenth century, it was one of [[medici-family|the Medici]]'s principal rural retreats — a place for intellectual gatherings (the Platonic Academy met here) and, in [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s case, a place to die. A footman's journey from Careggi to [[florence|Florence]] (001-060) marks the distance between the dying ruler and the city he dominated.

## Key Events

Careggi appears at three pivotal moments:

- **The Proem's rumination (P01-010)**: The Florentine Spirit, surveying the city from San Miniato, wonders whether the quarrel between Savonarola and [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]] was "ever made up" and asks: "did he get over that illness at Careggi? It was but a sad, uneasy-looking man that rode away." This reflection frames [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s [[death-in-romola|death]] as an unresolved moral question.
- **News of the [[death-in-romola|death]] (001-060)**: In the novel's opening scene, a man reports that "Guccio there met a footman going back to Careggi, and he told him the Frate had been sent for yesternight, after the Magnifico had confessed and had the holy sacraments." The rumour of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s [[death-in-romola|death]] — and Savonarola's refusal to grant absolution unless [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]] renounced his [[power-in-romola|power]] — crackles through [[florence|Florence]].
- **Niccolò Ridolfi's memory (039-010)**: At a [[medici-family|Medicean]] dinner gathering, Ridolfi recalls "one day at Careggi, when Luigi was in his rattling vein," describing Luigi Pulci's witty scepticism at [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s table. The anecdote evokes Careggi as the site of [[medici-family|the Medici]] circle's intellectual brilliance — a world now fading.

## Associated Characters

- **[[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]]** — Dies at Careggi in April 1492; his illness and [[death-in-romola|death]] at the villa frame the novel's opening (P01-010, 001-060)
- **Savonarola** — Summoned to Careggi to administer last rites to [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]; their fateful encounter defines the novel's political-theological tensions
- **Luigi Pulci** — The poet's witty performances at Careggi are recalled as emblematic of [[medici-family|the Medici]] intellectual circle (039-010)

## Symbolism

Careggi symbolises the end of an era. [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s [[death-in-romola|death]] there, and Savonarola's visit, mark the transition from [[medici-family|Medicean]] [[florence|Florence]] — worldly, cultured, sceptical — to the Savonarolan republic of virtue. The villa, perched in the hills above the city, is literally and figuratively above the fray: a place where the great confront [[death-in-romola|mortality]] away from the political stage. Ridolfi's nostalgic memory of Pulci's wit at Careggi (039-010) reinforces the elegiac tone — a golden age already passing as the novel begins.

## Historical Context

The real Villa Medici di Careggi was acquired by [[medici-family|the Medici]] [[family-in-romola|family]] in 1417 and remodelled by Michelozzo for Cosimo il Vecchio. It became the meeting-place of the Platonic Academy under Marsilio Ficino, making it one of the most important intellectual centres of the Renaissance. [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]] died there on 8 April 1492, aged 43. The historical record confirms that Savonarola visited [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]] on his [[death-in-romola|death]]bed, though accounts of what passed between them vary. The villa still stands, now part of the Careggi Hospital complex.
