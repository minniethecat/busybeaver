## Overview

Prophecy in *[[romola|Romola]]* is both a spiritual claim and a political weapon. [[savonarola|Girolamo Savonarola]]'s prophetic authority — his assertion that he speaks for God and can foresee [[florence|Florence]]'s fate — is the source of his immense [[power-in-romola|power]] over the city and over [[romola|Romola]] herself. Yet Eliot, ever the rationalist, treats prophecy with characteristic ambivalence: she presents Savonarola's prophetic gifts as psychologically real in their effects while leaving their divine origin an open question.

The novel explores prophecy as a mode of moral persuasion that bypasses reason. Savonarola does not argue [[romola|Romola]] into compliance; he prophesies her future suffering if she abandons her [[duty-in-romola|duty]], and the prophecy itself becomes the instrument of her transformation.

## Savonarola's Prophetic Authority

Savonarola's [[power-in-romola|power]] in [[florence|Florence]] rested on his claim to divine revelation. He preached that God had chosen [[florence|Florence]] for renewal and that he, Savonarola, was the vessel of that revelation. The novel captures both the magnetism and the danger of such claims:

- His sermons in the [[duomo|Duomo]] warned of apocalyptic judgement
- He claimed to foresee political events — the coming of Charles VIII, the scourging of Italy
- His prophecies created a feedback loop: believers saw their fulfilment in events, which strengthened belief
- The political establishment [[fear-in-romola|fear]]ed his prophecy as a rival source of [[power-in-romola|authority]]

Eliot's narrator notes the psychological mechanism with irony:

> In others the gift of prophecy was very much like a farthing candle (021-007).

The simile cuts both ways — prophecy illuminates but also distorts, casting long shadows from small flames.

## Prophecy as Moral Leverage

Savonarola's most consequential prophetic act in the novel is his intervention with [[romola|Romola]]. When she attempts to flee [[florence|Florence]], he stops her at the city gate and, in a scene of extraordinary intensity, commands her to return:

> "That is a terrible prophecy," said the Greek (003-009).

The words are spoken by [[tito-melema|Tito]] in an early scene, but they anticipate the novel's central dynamic: prophecy as a force that compels action irrespective of its truth. [[romola|Romola]] does not return to [[florence|Florence]] because she believes Savonarola's theology; she returns because his moral [[power-in-romola|authority]], channelled through the rhetoric of prophecy, makes flight feel impossible.

At the novel's end, the failure of Savonarola's prophecy is complete. His last words record his sense of abandonment:

> He hath withdrawn from me the spirit of prophecy (072-001).

## Prophecy and Politics

The Florentines understood prophecy in political terms. As one character warns:

> Never mount your prophecy; politics is the better horse (001-038).

This cynical advice — that political calculation is more reliable than prophetic fervour — is proven right by the novel's conclusion. Savonarola's prophetic authority collapses when his political position becomes untenable, and his [[death-in-romola|execution]] demonstrates that prophecy without institutional [[power-in-romola|power]] is fatal.
