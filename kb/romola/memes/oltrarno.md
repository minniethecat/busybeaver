## Overview

Oltrarno — literally "beyond [[arno-river|the Arno]]" — is the district that clothes the southern bank of [[florence|Florence]]'s river. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, it encompasses both the aristocratic palaces of the old families and the "thinly inhabited" outskirts where the dispossessed find shelter. The district embodies the city's social contrasts: the Via de' Bardi with its noble houses, and the outlying areas where [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] hides after his escape from slavery.

## Location

Oltrarno extends from the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]] southward and eastward, encompassing the Via de' Bardi — "a street noted in the history of [[florence|Florence]]" (005-001) — which runs from the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]] to the Piazza de' Mozzi at the head of the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte]] alle Grazie. Its northern buildings flank [[arno-river|the river]]; its southern edge rises towards the hill of Bogoli, the ancient stone-quarry that supplied [[florence|Florence]]'s paving. The district is reached from central [[florence|Florence]] by crossing [[arno-river|the Arno]] bridges — the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]], the [[santa-trinita|Ponte Santa Trinita]], and the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte]] alle Grazie.

## Key Events

Oltrarno provides the setting for several significant moments:

- **The Proem's view (P01-006)**: The Florentine Spirit notes the "cloth-dressers in Oltrarno" among the city's working population — the district of artisans and dyers whose labour sustains [[florence|Florence]]'s textile wealth.
- **Via de' Bardi (005-001)**: The novel introduces [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] [[family-in-romola|family]]'s street as lying in Oltrarno, "or that portion of the city which clothes the southern bank of [[arno-river|the river]]." This thorough description establishes the district's geography and its association with old Florentine nobility.
- **[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s hurried business (010-012)**: Fleeing with [[tessa|Tessa]] after the mock-[[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] at [[badia-fiorentina|the Badia]], [[tito-melema|Tito]] explains he is "in a hurry to get to Oltrarno" — the district as destination, its separation from the city centre providing a pretext for his escape.
- **[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s refuge (030-017)**: After selling his sapphire, [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] takes lodging "in a thinly inhabited part of Oltrarno" — an outhouse with straw, in the district's neglected fringe. Here, on the margins of the city, he plots his vengeance.
- **[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s flight (067-007)**: During his final desperate flight, [[tito-melema|Tito]] hears sounds suggesting the mob might be "coming into Oltrarno," forcing him to change course towards the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]]. The district becomes a trap — its private roads denied him, he must take the more exposed route.

## Associated Characters

- **[[romola|Romola de' Bardi]]** — Her [[family-in-romola|family]] home stands on the Via de' Bardi in Oltrarno; the district is her domestic geography (005-001)
- **[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]** — Finds refuge in a poor outhouse in a thinly settled part of Oltrarno, a space of concealment and plotting (030-017)

## Symbolism

Oltrarno represents [[florence|Florence]]'s dual identity — splendid and squalid — contained within a single district. The Via de' Bardi, home to an ancient noble [[family-in-romola|family]], stands at the district's riverfront; a short walk brings one to [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s outhouse with its heap of straw. The Arno, which separates Oltrarno from the civic centre, is more than a geographic boundary: it marks the division between public and private, between the stage of political action and the hidden spaces where personal dramas — [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s vengeance, [[romola|Romola]]'s domestic anguish — unfold.

## Historical Context

The real Oltrarno district remains one of [[florence|Florence]]'s most distinctive quarters. In the Renaissance, it was home to both aristocratic families ([[bardi-family|the Bardi]], the Pitti, the Capponi) and the city's cloth-workers and dyers, whose workshops lined [[arno-river|the river]]. The district's character — more residential, less monumental than the city centre — made it a natural setting for the novel's private scenes. The Via de' Bardi, the [[ponte-vecchio|Ponte Vecchio]], and the [[pitti-palace|Pitti Palace]] all survive today, making Oltrarno one of the best-preserved Renaissance neighbourhoods in [[florence|Florence]].
