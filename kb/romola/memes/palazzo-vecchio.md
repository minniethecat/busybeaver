## Overview

The Palazzo Vecchio — also called the Palazzo della Signoria — is the fortified seat of [[florence|Florence]]'s republican government. Its "huge, square, stone-asserted" mass dominates the city skyline (P01-006), and its great bell, the *vacca*, tolls to summon citizens for emergency assemblies. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, the palace is the stage on which political machinations unfold, from [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s diplomatic manoeuvring to [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s desperate plea and Savonarola's final crisis.

## Location

The Palazzo Vecchio fronts onto the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]], the principal civic square of [[florence|Florence]]. Adjacent to the Loggia dei Lanzi, it commands the political heart of the city. Its bell tower can be seen from nearly every quarter, making it an omnipresent symbol of Florentine [[power-in-romola|authority]].

## Key Events

The Palazzo Vecchio is the setting for many of the novel's most dramatic political scenes:

- **[[tito-melema|Tito]]'s diplomatic ascent (023-010, 029-032)**: [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] makes his way to the Palazzo Vecchio to find Bartolommeo Scala and later hurries there for council business. The palace becomes the locus of his political ambition, where his facility with language and intrigue finds its natural arena.
- **[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s arrest (038-028)**: [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]] attempts to enter the Palazzo Vecchio to make his way into a council chamber and denounce [[tito-melema|Tito]] publicly. His arrest at the threshold marks the failure of direct [[justice-in-romola|justice]] and his consignment to the [[bargello|Bargello]].
- **The great bell tolls (043-027, 045-001)**: As political crisis engulfs [[florence|Florence]], the great bell of the Palazzo Vecchio begins to swing, summoning citizens to a *parlamento*. [[tito-melema|Tito]] pauses at the palace, awaiting events — his characteristic posture of calculated observation.
- **[[romola|Romola]]'s search (047-008)**: Arriving in front of the Palazzo Vecchio, [[romola|Romola]] looks eagerly into the crowd, searching for a sign of her godfather [[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]] amid the political tumult.

## Associated Characters

- **[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]** — Uses the Palazzo Vecchio as his stage for political advancement; his repeated presence there tracks his rise from outsider to [[power-in-romola|power]]-broker
- **[[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]** — Attempts to breach the palace to expose [[tito-melema|Tito]]; his failure at its gates symbolises the triumph of [[betrayal-in-romola|deception]] over truth
- **[[romola|Romola de' Bardi]]** — Navigates the turbulent piazza in front of the palace during the crisis
- **[[bernardo-del-nero|Bernardo del Nero]]** — A Gonfaloniere whose presence at the palace embodies the old Florentine political order

## Symbolism

The Palazzo Vecchio embodies the paradox of Florentine republicanism — a fortress-palace that houses a government theoretically elected by the people yet perennially vulnerable to faction and manipulation. [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s ease within its chambers signals his assimilation into the corrupting structures of [[power-in-romola|power]], while [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s exclusion from them marks his [[power-in-romola|power]]lessness. The bell, ringing across the city, represents both civic participation and the violence of mob politics.

## Historical Context

The real Palazzo Vecchio, begun in 1299 and completed in the early 14th century, served as the seat of the [[florentine-signoria|Florentine Signoria]] throughout the Renaissance. Its imposing Arnolfo di Cambio design and its tower make it one of [[florence|Florence]]'s most recognisable landmarks. Eliot's depiction draws on detailed knowledge of Florentine civic architecture and political procedure. During Savonarola's ascendancy (1494–1498), the palace was the scene of fierce factional struggles between the *[[florentine-factions|Piagnoni]]* (Savonarola's followers) and the *[[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]]* (his opponents).
