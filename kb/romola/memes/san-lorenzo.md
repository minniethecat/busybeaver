## Overview

The Basilica of San Lorenzo is one of [[florence|Florence]]'s oldest and most significant churches — the parish church of [[medici-family|the Medici]] [[family-in-romola|family]] and, in *[[romola|Romola]]*, the principal pulpit from which [[savonarola|Fra Girolamo]] Savonarola delivered his electrifying Quaresima (Lent) sermons. "Lights shooting over San Lorenzo for three nights together" (001-033) marks the church as a site charged with supernatural portent and popular religious fervour.

## Location

San Lorenzo stands north of the Duomo, in the quarter that bears its name. The Borgo San Lorenzo, a major thoroughfare, runs past the church towards the city centre. Its proximity to [[medici-family|the Medici]] Palace reinforces its status as the [[family-in-romola|family]]'s spiritual base.

## Key Events

San Lorenzo appears at critical moments in the novel's religious and political landscape:

- **Savonarola's Lenten sermons (001-044, 012-029)**: The Frate preaches at San Lorenzo during Quaresima, drawing vast crowds. [[bratti-ferravecchi|Bratti]] mentions having "been to hear the Frate in San Lorenzo," and Fra Cristoforo recounts Savonarola's [[power-in-romola|power]]ful Lenten addresses. These sermons form the bedrock of Savonarola's moral [[power-in-romola|authority]] in [[florence|Florence]].
- **The old man's memory (P01-009)**: In the Proem's frame narrative, an aged Florentine recalls having "listened in San Lorenzo, not without a mixture of satisfaction," to Savonarola's preaching — situating the church in the longer arc of Florentine memory.
- **The diversion (022-025)**: During a public disturbance, prisoners are "turned up the Borgo di San Lorenzo," making a partial diversion — the street serving as a channel for civic unrest.

## Symbolism

San Lorenzo symbolises the intersection of [[power-in-romola|political power]] and religious [[power-in-romola|authority]] in Renaissance [[florence|Florence]]. As [[medici-family|the Medici]] church, it represents the [[family-in-romola|family]]'s patronage of sacred space; as Savonarola's pulpit, it becomes a platform for challenging that very [[family-in-romola|family]]'s worldliness. The "lights shooting over San Lorenzo" — a portent recorded by chroniclers — epitomises the atmosphere of apocalyptic expectation that Savonarola cultivated and that Eliot meticulously recreates.

## Historical Context

The real Basilica of San Lorenzo was consecrated in 393 CE, making it the oldest church in [[florence|Florence]]. Rebuilt by Brunelleschi under Medici patronage in the 15th century, it served as [[medici-family|the Medici]] [[family-in-romola|family]]'s burial place and the site of major civic-religious ceremonies. Savonarola's Lenten sermons at San Lorenzo were historical events — his preaching drew thousands and laid the groundwork for his political ascendancy after the expulsion of [[medici-family|the Medici]] in 1494.
