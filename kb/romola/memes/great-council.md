## Overview

The Great Council — the *Consiglio Maggiore* — was the centrepiece of [[florence|Florence]]'s post-Medici constitutional reform. Modelled on the Venetian Great Council, it was a broadly-based legislative body that gave voting rights to several thousand citizens who had held major office or whose ancestors had done so. In *[[romola|Romola]]*, the Council represents the political promise of Savonarola's revolution: government by the many rather than the few, under the moral guidance of the Frate. But it also becomes the arena for the factional conflicts that would ultimately destroy him.

## Background

After the expulsion of Piero de' Medici in November 1494, [[florence|Florence]] faced a constitutional vacuum. The Medici had spent sixty years hollowing out republican institutions while maintaining their forms. A new structure was needed — one that would prevent the return of one-man rule. Savonarola, preaching from the Duomo pulpit, threw his moral [[power-in-romola|authority]] behind the Great Council, arguing that only a broad-based government could embody the city's spiritual renewal.

## Description

The Council's establishment unfolds over the novel's middle chapters:

- **The plan emerges (032-015)**: As [[florence|Florence]] grapples with the French presence and the [[pisa|Pisa]]n revolt, political reformers advance "the plan of a Great Council simmering already." The proposal gains momentum precisely because the alternative — oligarchic rule — had been discredited by [[medici-family|the Medici]] experience.
- **The debate (035-007–009)**: The question divides [[florence|Florence]]: was the city "to have a Great Council after the Venetian mode, where the citizens could vote?" (035-007). The debate is binary — the "best for all" to "Choose the Great Council" (035-008) — but the rhetoric conceals deep divisions. The Great Council is "the only practicable plan for a people" (035-009).

## Participants

- **Savonarola** — The Council's most [[power-in-romola|power]]ful advocate; he frames it as the political instrument of divine will, connecting constitutional reform to moral regeneration
- **The [[florentine-factions|Piagnoni]]** — Savonarola's followers, who provide the popular pressure that makes the Council politically inevitable
- **The [[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]]** — The "Enraged" — aristocratic opponents who see the Council as Savonarolan mob rule
- **[[tito-melema|Tito Melema]]** — Navigates the new political landscape with characteristic adaptability, building influence within the Council's framework (039-017, 039-025)

## Aftermath

The Great Council was voted into existence and became [[florence|Florence]]'s sovereign legislative body. But its promise exceeded its performance. The factional divisions it was meant to transcend — between [[florentine-factions|Piagnoni]] and [[florentine-factions|Arrabbiati]], between rich and poor, between pro-French and pro-papal — were reproduced within its walls. "They'll get their Great Council finally voted tomorrow" (039-025) captures the fatalism with which even its supporters regarded it: a mechanism, not a miracle. When Savonarola fell, the Council survived him — an institutional legacy that outlasted the prophet who had championed it. But the return of [[medici-family|the Medici]] in 1512 would demonstrate its ultimate fragility.

## Historical Context

The real Great Council was established in December 1494, shortly after [[medici-family|the Medici]] expulsion. It gave voting rights to all male citizens over 29 whose fathers, grandfathers, or great-grandfathers had held one of the three major offices — approximately 3,200 men out of a population of some 70,000. It elected [[florentine-signoria|the Signoria]] and other magistracies and voted on legislation. The Council represented the most broadly-based government in Florentine history, though it fell far short of democracy by modern standards. It survived Savonarola's [[death-in-romola|execution]] and functioned until [[medici-family|the Medici]] restoration of 1512, when it was abolished. Its brief existence made it a touchstone for subsequent republican movements in [[florence|Florence]], including the last [[florence|Florentine Republic]] of 1527–1530.
