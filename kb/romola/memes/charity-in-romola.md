## Overview

Charity in *[[romola|Romola]]* evolves from a conventional social practice into the novel's highest moral ideal. Early in the narrative, charity appears as routine Christian almsgiving — the distribution of food and coin that was an expected part of Florentine religious life. By the novel's end, charity has become something far more demanding: an active, self-giving compassion that transforms [[romola|Romola]] from a scholar into a ministering figure and that defines the novel's version of moral heroism.

George Eliot, who had translated Feuerbach's *Essence of [[religious-faith|Christianity]]*, understood charity not as doctrinal obligation but as the practical expression of human solidarity. [[romola|Romola]]'s journey from classical scholarship to active compassion embodies this Feuerbachian ideal: the true meaning of [[religious-faith|religion]] is love for one's fellow human beings.

## From Almsgiving to Compassion

The novel distinguishes between mere charity — the conventional distribution of alms — and what Savonarola calls "Divine charity," a transformative love that changes both giver and recipient:

> When the fire of Divine charity burns within you, and you behold the needs of your fellow-creatures (040-030).

This distinction maps onto [[romola|Romola]]'s moral development. Early in the novel, she is capable of kindness but not of the radical self-giving that marks her later actions. The turning point comes when she leaves [[florence|Florence]] — not in escape, as she had attempted before, but in a deliberate act of service, caring for the victims of [[death-in-romola|plague]] in a coastal village.

## Romola's Ministry

[[romola|Romola]]'s work among the [[death-in-romola|plague]]-stricken represents the novel's fullest realisation of charity. She does not merely give money or prayers; she gives herself — her presence, her labour, her willingness to share the risk of contagion:

> It was heroism or exalted charity in her to seek these beings (070-011).

Eliot's narrator hedges between "heroism" and "charity," suggesting that the distinction may not matter. What [[romola|Romola]] does transcends both categories: it is simply the right action, performed without calculation of cost or hope of reward. Another character confirms its human value in plainer terms:

> If you can comfort her again, you'd be doing a charity, for she cried at parting with you (070-016).

## Savonarola's Teaching on Charity

Savonarola's preaching provides the theological vocabulary for [[romola|Romola]]'s transformation. He taught that:

> It was not the duty of the rich to be luxurious for themselves (039-014).

And called his followers to "a law of [[justice-in-romola|justice]], peace, and charity" (040-022). Yet [[romola|Romola]] ultimately exceeds Savonarola's teaching. Where his charity remained bound up with [[power-in-romola|political power]] and [[prophecy-in-romola|prophetic authority]], hers becomes entirely selfless — a gift without expectation of return or recognition.

## Charity and Justice

The novel's treatment of charity is inseparable from its treatment of [[justice-in-romola|justice]]. Where [[justice-in-romola|justice]] demands [[justice-in-romola|retribution]] — [[baldassarre-calvo|Baldassarre]]'s vengeance, [[tito-melema|Tito]]'s [[death-in-romola|death]] — charity offers an alternative: the repair of suffering rather than the punishment of wrong. [[romola|Romola]]'s care for [[tessa|Tessa]] and her children at the novel's end is an act of charity that answers the in[[justice-in-romola|justice]] [[tito-melema|Tito]] inflicted on all of them, not by punishing him but by caring for those he harmed.
