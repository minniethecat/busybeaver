## Overview

The [[bonfire-of-the-vanities|Bonfire of the Vanities]] (*Falò delle vanità*) of 7 February 1497 was the most spectacular expression of [[savonarola|Girolamo Savonarola]]'s campaign to purify [[florence|Florence]] of moral corruption. On that day, Savonarola's followers — the *fanciulli* (children) organized into brigades for moral policing — gathered books, manuscripts, paintings, mirrors, cosmetics, cards, dice, musical instruments, and other "vanities" from Florentine households and heaped them into a great pyramid in the [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]], before the [[duomo|Duomo]], and set them alight. In George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]*, this event marks the zenith of Savonarola's [[power-in-romola|power]] and the point at which the novel's competing moral visions — [[renaissance-humanism|pagan learning]] and [[religious-faith|Christian reform]] — collide most violently.

## Historical Background

The Bonfire of the Vanities occurred during Carnival season 1497, in the third year of Savonarola's dominance over Florentine public life. Its immediate context included:

| Event | Date | Significance |
|-------|------|-------------|
| French invasion of Italy | 1494 | [[entry-of-charles-viii|Charles VIII's entry]] destabilised Italian politics |
| Expulsion of [[medici-family|the Medici]] | November 1494 | Piero de' Medici fled [[florence|Florence]] |
| [[florence|Florentine Republic]] under Savonarola | 1494–1498 | Theocratic democracy with Christ as "King of [[florence|Florence]]" |
| Papal excommunication | May 1497 | Alexander VI excommunicated Savonarola shortly after the bonfire |

The bonfire was preceded by months of moral campaigning: Savonarola's *fanciulli* went door to door collecting "vanities," and his sermons intensified in their apocalyptic rhetoric. The burning was conceived as a ritual of collective purification — [[florence|Florence]] offering up its worldly attachments in exchange for divine favour.

## The Event in the Novel

In *[[romola|Romola]]*, the Bonfire of the Vanities represents the confrontation between two incompatible value systems. The books and artworks consigned to the flames include manuscripts of the kind that [[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]] spent his life studying — the very [[renaissance-humanism|classical learning]] that has formed [[romola|Romola]]'s intellect. The bonfire thus crystallises the novel's central question: can pagan learning and Christian morality coexist, or must one be [[sacrifice-in-romola|sacrifice]]d to the other?

[[romola|Romola]]'s response to the bonfire is complex. She does not participate in the destruction — her reverence for learning prevents that — but neither does she dismiss Savonarola's moral seriousness. This ambivalence is Eliot's own: the author, who had translated radical biblical criticism, understood both the value of learning and the dangers of a purely intellectual life disconnected from moral commitment.

[[tito-melema|Tito]], characteristically, navigates the bonfire's political implications without moral engagement. His concern is not with whether the burning is right or wrong but with how the shifting political winds will affect his position. The bonfire, for [[tito-melema|Tito]], is simply another event to be managed.

## Historical Assessment

The historical Bonfire of the Vanities has been variously interpreted:

- **Contemporary supporters** saw it as a genuine act of repentance and renewal
- **Critics then and now** have compared it to book-burning episodes throughout history, seeing it as an act of cultural vandalism
- **Historians of art** lament the irreplaceable works — possibly including paintings by Botticelli — that may have been destroyed
- **Political historians** view it as the high-water mark of Savonarola's theocratic experiment, after which his [[power-in-romola|authority]] began to wane

The bonfire contributed to the growing opposition to Savonarola. Less than fifteen months later, he would be tried and executed on the very same [[piazza-della-signoria|Piazza della Signoria]].
