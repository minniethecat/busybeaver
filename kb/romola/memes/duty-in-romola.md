## Overview

The concept of **duty** in George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]* is the moral fulcrum on which the novel's central conflicts turn. [[romola|Romola de' Bardi]], raised in the world of classical scholarship by her blind father Bardo, inherits a conception of duty rooted in Stoic obligation and filial piety. The novel traces how this inherited notion of duty is tested, broken, and ultimately transformed by experience — particularly through [[romola|Romola]]'s [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] to [[tito-melema|Tito Melema]] and her encounters with Savonarola's [[religious-faith|Christian morality]].

## Classical vs. Christian Duty

[[romola|Romola]] begins the novel with a pagan-classical understanding of duty, absorbed from her father's devotion to antique texts. For Bardo, duty is a matter of intellectual devotion and familial obligation — his daughter must serve his scholarship, preserve his legacy, and uphold the honour of [[bardi-family|the Bardi]] name. This classical framework, however, proves inadequate when confronted with the moral complexities of adult life.

Savonarola introduces an alternative conception: duty as active Christian [[charity-in-romola|compassion]]. When [[romola|Romola]] attempts to flee [[florence|Florence]] and her failed [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]], Savonarola intercepts her and commands her to return. His intervention reframes duty not as passive endurance but as active moral engagement with the needs of others.

> It was the Frate's voice that had come to her — the voice of a man who believed in the duty of Christian men not to live for themselves alone (P01-009).

## Romola's Conflicting Duties

The novel stages [[romola|Romola]]'s moral development through a series of conflicting duties:

| Obligation | Object | Nature |
|------------|--------|--------|
| **Filial duty** | Bardo | Preserving her father's library and scholarly legacy |
| **Marital duty** | [[tito-melema|Tito]] | Loyalty to a husband who has betrayed every trust |
| **Civic duty** | [[florence|Florence]] | Serving the suffering during [[death-in-romola|plague]] and political crisis |
| **Religious duty** | God (via Savonarola) | Active [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] rather than Stoic endurance |

Each conflict forces [[romola|Romola]] to re-examine what duty truly requires, moving from a rule-based morality toward one grounded in love and [[justice-in-romola|justice]].

## Key Passages

The tension between classical obligation and moral awakening is captured when [[romola|Romola]] reflects on her father's expectations:

> She had been driven by the sense of love and duty to her father's memory. All Romola's ardour was thrown into that channel (027-020).

Savonarola's challenge to [[romola|Romola]] directly questions whether duty without [[charity-in-romola|compassion]] is sufficient:

> Sorrow without duty — bitter herbs, and no bread with them (040-024).

Eliot's narrator foregrounds the problem of duty as the novel's organising moral question from the Proem, describing the story as concerning "the eternal [[marriage-in-romola|marriage]] between love and duty" (P01-012).

- **[[romola|Romola de' Bardi]]**: The protagonist whose moral education is defined by evolving conceptions of duty
- **[[bardo-de-bardi|Bardo de' Bardi]]**: [[romola|Romola]]'s father, who embodies classical, Stoic duty
