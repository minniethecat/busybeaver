## Overview

Angelo Poliziano's *Miscellanea* (1489) is the work of Renaissance classical scholarship that [[romola|Romola de' Bardi]] is shown reading aloud to her blind father Bardo in Chapter V of George Eliot's *[[romola|Romola]]*. The *Miscellanea* — a collection of one hundred philological notes on classical texts — represents the apex of fifteenth-century [[renaissance-humanism|humanist scholarship]]: learned, meticulous, and devoted to the recovery of ancient learning through the collation of manuscripts and the emendation of corrupt passages.

## The Reading Scene (Chapter V)

The specific passage [[romola|Romola]] reads from the *Miscellanea* is a learned digression on the myth of Teiresias, the Theban seer who was blinded after seeing the goddess Minerva bathing:

> "There was a certain nymph of Thebes named Chariclo, especially dear to Pallas; and this nymph was the mother of Teiresias..." (005-009)

[[romola|Romola]] reads in Latin from the eightieth chapter of the *Miscellanea*, which includes a Greek quotation from [[nonnus-dionysiaca|Nonnus]]'s *[[nonnus-dionysiaca|Dionysiaca]]* — a passage that prompts Bardo to request his own, more correct manuscript of [[nonnus-dionysiaca|Nonnus]]:

> "Stay, Romola; reach me my own copy of Nonnus. It is a more correct copy than any in Poliziano's hands, for I made emendations in it which have not yet been communicated to any man." (005-011)

This moment is rich with thematic significance: Bardo's [[pride-in-romola|pride]] in his unpublished emendations, his blindness that makes him dependent on [[romola|Romola]]'s eyes, and the world of classical scholarship that constitutes his entire existence.

## The Author: Angelo Poliziano

Angelo Ambrogini (1454–1494), known as Poliziano (Politian in English) from his birthplace Montepulciano, was the foremost classical scholar of [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo de' Medici]]'s [[florence|Florence]]. As tutor to [[lorenzo-de-medici|Lorenzo]]'s children and professor of Greek and Latin at the Florentine *Studio*, Poliziano embodied the fusion of scholarship and patronage that characterised [[medici-family|Medicean]] [[renaissance-humanism|humanism]]. The *Miscellanea*, published in 1489, was his most influential scholarly work: a model of the philological method that would dominate classical studies for centuries.

## Significance in the Novel

The *Miscellanea* serves multiple functions in *[[romola|Romola]]*:

1. **Characterisation**: [[romola|Romola]]'s fluent Latin reading establishes her as an educated woman, shaped by her father's scholarly world
2. **Theme**: The contrast between Poliziano's printed text and Bardo's manuscript emendations dramatises the transition from medieval manuscript culture to Renaissance print culture
3. **Foreshadowing**: The Teiresias myth — blindness as the price of divine vision — resonates with Bardo's own blindness and the novel's concern with different kinds of sight and insight
