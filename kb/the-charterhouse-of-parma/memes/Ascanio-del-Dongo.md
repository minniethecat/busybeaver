# Ascanio del Dongo

## Role in the Novel

Ascanio del Dongo is Fabrizio's elder brother, a Jesuit whose controversial writings and unsympathetic personality make him a foil to Fabrizio. Where Fabrizio is passionate, impulsive, and rebellious, Ascanio embodies the institutional Church — disciplined, calculating, and aligned with the reactionary order their father represents. His brief appearances in the novel serve to highlight Fabrizio's difference from his family of origin.

## Biography

Ascanio is the elder son of the [[Marchese del Dongo]] and the [[Marchesa del Dongo]], raised at the family castle of Grianta on Lake Como. Unlike his younger brother [[Fabrizio del Dongo]], Ascanio embraced the Jesuit path favored by their father. He wrote a book on controversial subjects that brought unwanted attention to the family.

> Three members of your family: Ascanio del Dongo who wrote a book in...
> (006-071)

The book appears to have been on genealogy or noble lineages — a topic that, in the charged political atmosphere of Restoration Lombardy, carried diplomatic risks. Ascanio's literary and clerical ambitions reflected his father's values: institutional power, reactionary politics, and suspicion of liberal thought.

At one point, Fabrizio (or perhaps a servant) impersonates Ascanio, declaring:

> I am the Marchesino Ascanio del Dongo, my castle is quite...
> (010-004)

## Historical Context

The figure of the intellectually ambitious Jesuit was a recognizable type in Stendhal's Italy — younger sons of the nobility who entered the Church not from vocation but from family strategy. Ascanio represents this institutional path, contrasted with Fabrizio's more chaotic (but ultimately more authentic) spiritual journey from Waterloo soldier to Chartreuse monk.

## Relationships

### Family

Ascanio is his father's son in temper and politics — a fact that made him odious to [[Gina Sanseverina]], who referred to him as "that unspeakable Jesuit." His relationship with Fabrizio is one of mutual estrangement; Fabrizio's world of passion, battle, and romantic intrigue has no overlap with Ascanio's clerical career.

### The Great-Uncle

Ascanio is named after his great-uncle, the famous **Archbishop Ascanio del Dongo**, a powerful churchman whose influence in Parma opened doors for Fabrizio. The Archbishop represents the ecclesiastical heights to which the family aspired — and which the younger Ascanio's Jesuit career was meant to emulate.

> [Fabrizio presented himself as] nephew of the famous Archbishop Ascanio del Dongo.
> (010-028)

## Character Analysis

Ascanio functions in the novel primarily as a symbolic figure: he is what Fabrizio would have become had he submitted to his father's authority. As a Jesuit who writes controversial books, Ascanio paradoxically combines institutional loyalty with intellectual risk-taking — but unlike Fabrizio's romantic risk-taking, Ascanio's is cold, calculated, and ultimately self-serving.

Gina's contempt for Ascanio ("that unspeakable Jesuit") mirrors Stendhal's broader anticlerical satire. The Jesuit brother and the reactionary father together form the family system that Fabrizio and Gina must escape.
