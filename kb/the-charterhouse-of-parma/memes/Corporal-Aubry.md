# Corporal Aubry

Corporal Aubry is a French soldier whom Fabrizio del Dongo encounters during the Battle of Waterloo. He appears throughout Chapter 4 as Fabrizio's guide and comrade in the chaos of battle, providing one of the novel's most vivid depictions of ordinary soldiers.

## Encounter at Waterloo

Fabrizio meets Aubry through a cantinière (sutler woman) who calls out: "Aubry, my lad" (PN 004-008). Fabrizio immediately asks him: "Are we really going to fight?" (PN 004-009). Aubry becomes Fabrizio's informal mentor in the realities of combat.

## Soldier's Demeanor

Aubry is described as having "a stout heart" (PN 004-012) and attends to business with a professional gravity. After combat, he asks Fabrizio: "Did you kill your man?" (PN 004-022), introducing the young protagonist to the grim accounting of war. Fabrizio notices that "Corporal Aubry avoided speaking to any of the soldiers" (PN 004-029) during a tense moment, suggesting his tactical awareness.

When an incident occurs, "Corporal Aubry and I are going to give you some advice" (PN 004-059) is how he and the cantinière approach Fabrizio, and he replies "gravely" (PN 004-067) when questioned. His steady presence provides an anchor for Fabrizio's disorienting first experience of war.

## Significance

Aubry represents the common soldier — competent, unromantic, and practical. He is one of the few named rank-and-file soldiers in the Waterloo chapters, giving a human face to the military machine. His matter-of-fact approach to killing and survival contrasts sharply with Fabrizio's romantic ideals of battle, serving as one of the novel's key vehicles for its anti-heroic portrayal of war.
