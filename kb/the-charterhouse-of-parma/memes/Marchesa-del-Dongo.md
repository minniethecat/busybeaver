# Marchesa del Dongo

## Role in the Novel

Fabrizio's long-suffering mother — a quiet, devout woman trapped in a loveless marriage to the reactionary Marchese, whose tender love for her son contrasts with the passionate devotion of her sister Gina.

## Biography

The Marchesa del Dongo is the wife of the reactionary Marchese and the mother of Fabrizio. She endures her husband's avarice and cruelty with Christian patience, finding solace in her love for her son. Unlike her sister Gina, who acts boldly in the world, the Marchesa acts through quiet endurance and secret generosity. She sends Fabrizio what little money she can extract from her miserly household and prays for his safety. Her love is maternal where Gina's is fierce; she represents the domestic, private face of love in a novel otherwise dominated by its public and political manifestations.

## Character Analysis

The Marchesa del Dongo occupies a small but significant place in the novel's emotional economy. She is the moral counterweight to the Marchese's cruelty and the Duchessa's worldliness. Her love for Fabrizio is the only love in the novel that is purely maternal, asking nothing in return. Stendhal treats her with unusual tenderness — she is one of the few characters in the novel who is never subjected to irony. Her quiet suffering and faithful love represent a moral ideal that the novel respects without making central to its dramatic action.
