# Madonna

The **Madonna** (the Blessed Virgin Mary) plays a crucial symbolic and plot-driving role in [[The-Charterhouse-of-Parma]]. Religious devotion to the Madonna, deeply embedded in Italian Catholic culture, becomes the mechanism through which the novel's central love story reaches its tragic denouement.

## Clelia's Vow

The most significant appearance of the Madonna in the novel is [[Clelia-Conti]]'s vow. When her father [[Fabio-Conti]] falls ill, Clelia promises the Madonna that she will never see [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]] again if her father is spared. This vow, made in a moment of filial desperation, becomes the insurmountable barrier between the two lovers.

> [020-070] …life, I had vowed that to the Madonna. Now, as soon as I can leave this place I shall go to have it recorded at the convent.

> [025-001] …but she had made a vow to the Madonna, at the time when her father fell so dangerously ill.

## The Vow's Consequences

Clelia's adherence to her vow creates the central tragedy of the novel's later chapters. She refuses to see Fabrizio, and when they do meet, it is always in darkness so that she may technically keep her promise. The narrator portrays this as both an expression of genuine religious conviction and a source of profound suffering.

> [026-001] …faithful to the vow she made to the Madonna, and never to see me again.

> [026-003] …confidence in the help of the Madonna were from then onwards her sole support.

## Religious Culture

Beyond Clelia's personal vow, references to the Madonna throughout the novel reflect the pervasive Catholic culture of early nineteenth-century Italy. Characters pray to the Madonna in moments of crisis, and miraculous images of the Virgin serve as objects of popular devotion. The Madonna at the [[Certosa]] (Charterhouse) where Fabrizio ultimately retreats represents the broader religious framework within which the characters live and die.

> [022-057] …which she had made to the Madonna at the time when the General fell ill.
