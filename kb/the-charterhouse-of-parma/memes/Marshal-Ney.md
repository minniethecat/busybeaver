# Marshal Ney

Marshal Michel Ney (1769–1815), one of Napoleon's most celebrated commanders, appears in the Waterloo chapters of the novel. Fabrizio del Dongo encounters him—or rather, his escort—during the chaotic battle.

## At Waterloo

Fabrizio, a young volunteer eager to fight for Napoleon, finds himself near Marshal Ney's escort during the Battle of Waterloo. A soldier tells him, "Marshal Ney, you fool! I say, where have you come from?"—capturing the confusion of the battlefield. Fabrizio follows the Marshal's group for a time, and later recalls "the ground he had covered with Marshal Ney's escort."

The Marshal represents the glamour of Napoleonic warfare that so captivates the young Fabrizio, even as the reality of the battle proves disillusioning.

## Later Reference

Later in the novel, as political tensions mount in Parma, someone mentions "a French army commanded by Marshal Ney, and marching upon the town"—a rumor that stirs the court. Ney thus serves as a symbol of the Napoleonic past that continues to haunt the restored courts of Italy.

## Historical Note

The historical Marshal Ney was executed by firing squad in 1815 after the Bourbon Restoration, an event not directly referenced in the novel but adding resonance to his symbolic role.
