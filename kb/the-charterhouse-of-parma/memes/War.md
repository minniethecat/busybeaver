# War

**War** is the founding experience of [[The-Charterhouse-of-Parma]] and of its protagonist, [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]]. The novel opens in 1796 with the French army's entry into [[Milan]], and its most famous episode — the [[Battle-of-Waterloo|Waterloo]] chapters (Chapters 2–5) — is one of the first and greatest depictions of modern warfare in fiction. War in Stendhal's novel is not a heroic tableau but a chaos of noise, smoke, confusion, and misunderstanding.

## The Novel's Military Opening

The first chapter situates the novel's world within the military history of the Revolutionary and Napoleonic wars. The French army enters Milan on 15 May 1796, and "this frenzied joy, this gaiety, this thirst for pleasure" that follows marks a generation [001-018]. Lieutenant [[Robert]], the young French officer billeted in the [[Marchese-del-Dongo]]'s palace, embodies the energy of the conquerors — his "officer's epaulettes were of wool, and the cloth of his tunic was stitched to the lining of the sleeves so that its scraps might hold together" [001-008], yet he brings with him an idea of freedom that transforms the young [[Gina-Sanseverina|Gina del Dongo]].

The military fortunes of the Napoleonic era are traced in miniature through the del Dongo family: the father's Austrian loyalism, the sister's affair with a French officer, the nephew's hero-worship of [[Napoleon-Bonaparte|Napoleon]]. When the Austrians "again drew near to Milan" after the battle of Cassano, Lieutenant Robert is wounded; "a few days later, Napoleon won the battle of Marengo" [001-023, 001-028]. History moves at the speed of armies, and individual lives are swept along with it.

## Waterloo: The Chaos of Battle

The Waterloo episode occupies Chapters 2 through 5 and represents Stendhal's most original contribution to the literature of war. Fabrizio, a seventeen-year-old idealist who has crossed [[Lake-Como]] and passed through [[Switzerland]] to join Napoleon's army, experiences the battle not as a coherent military action but as a series of disconnected, bewildering events. He buys a horse from a cantinière's wife, follows a regiment without knowing which army it belongs to, fires his only shot at a Prussian cavalryman, and never once sees Napoleon — the object of his devotion.

The narrator's technique is deliberately disorienting. Fabrizio "was our hero, quite carried away" [003-001], but his heroism consists of survival rather than action. He is wounded in the thigh by a spent ball without having seen the enemy who fired it. A corporal tells him he has been in a battle; Fabrizio replies: "Is that all? Is that really all?" [005-001]. The gap between expectation and experience — between the painted battle scenes in the del Dongo galleries, "any number of battles, and everywhere one saw some hero of the name dealing mighty blows with his sword" [001-029], and the mud, smoke, and confusion of the real thing — is the novel's definitive comment on war.

## Military Character and Military Values

The novel's characters are shaped by war even when they are not fighting. [[Conte-Mosca]], the Prime Minister, had served in Napoleon's armies and carries the Napoleonic style into the court of the Restoration. [[Fabio-Conti]], the governor of the Citadel, is a soldier whose military competence is an open question — the novel's portrait of a military man who has never been tested in battle. The [[Cantinière]], who helps Fabrizio at Waterloo, represents the civilians — especially women — who followed the armies and whose labor made military campaigns possible.

The novel's judgment on military values is characteristically double. War produces courage, loyalty, and the "frenzied joy" of the Milanese spring of 1796. It also produces death, disillusionment, and the "incompetent Generals" imposed by political favor who "lost a succession of battles in those same plains of Verona" [001-023]. Stendhal, who had served in the Napoleonic administration and been present at the retreat from Moscow, knew both faces of war intimately. The novel refuses either to glorify or to condemn; it simply shows what war looks like to someone who has no idea what he is doing — which is to say, to someone at war for the first time, as most soldiers are.

## The Aftermath

The Napoleonic wars haunt the novel's Restoration present. The older characters — Mosca, Gina, the [[Marchese-del-Dongo]] — have been shaped by the years of conflict, and their choices in the novel's court intrigues are informed by what they learned under Napoleon. Fabrizio, whose childhood was spent "reading engravings of Napoleon's battles" at [[Grianta]], enters adulthood as a man formed by a war that is already over. His life at the Parma court — the ecclesiastical career, the love affairs, the imprisonment — is lived in the shadow of an experience that he and his generation arrived too late to share.
