# The Napoleonic Propaganda

## Overview

The Napoleonic propaganda that floods Milan after the French occupation in 1796 is the first body of work to shape Fabrizio's consciousness. The bulletins announcing Napoleon's victories, the sonnets celebrating the Army of Italy, the pamphlets proclaiming the Rights of Man — these are the texts that form the imaginative world of the novel's opening chapters. Stendhal, who had served in Napoleon's administration and whose own youthful Bonapartism survived disillusionment, treats this propaganda with a complex mixture of nostalgia and irony: he knows it is propaganda, but he also knows that the ideals it proclaimed were real [001-001].

## In the Novel

The Napoleonic propaganda appears primarily through its effects: it is what makes Fabrizio cross into France at seventeen to join an army he does not understand, what makes the Marchese del Dongo tremble behind his powdered wig, what makes Milan in the 1790s the most exciting city in Europe. The propaganda is the novel's absent cause — the force that sets everything in motion before the narrative has properly begun [001-028].

Stendhal's treatment of this propaganda exemplifies his narrative method. He does not quote the bulletins or reproduce the sonnets; he shows us the world they created — a world where a boy from Grianta can imagine himself following Napoleon to glory. The propaganda is not judged; it is embodied in the effects it produces, which include both genuine heroism and catastrophic delusion [002-034].

## Significance

The Napoleonic propaganda is the work that most directly raises the question of art and politics in the novel. Is propaganda art? Is what moves people to action — even foolish action — to be valued or condemned? Stendhal's answer is characteristically dialectical: the propaganda is both true and false, both liberating and enslaving, and the novel's task is to hold these opposites in tension [001-028].

## Key Passages

> Napoleon entered Milan at the head of that young army which had shortly before crossed the bridge of Lodi. A few days later, he won the battle of Marengo, and all Italy was French [001-028].
