## Role in the Novel

Clara-Paolina is the Princess Consort of Parma, wife of the reigning Prince, and mother of the Crown Prince (PN 006-053). She occupies a position of supreme dignity at the Parma court but leads a life of profound unhappiness and isolation, "surrounded with honours but a prisoner to the most antiquated etiquette" (PN 006-107).

## Character and Appearance

When the Duchessa Sanseverina is first presented to her, Clara-Paolina appears as "a very tall and very thin woman, who was not thirty-six and appeared fifty" with a "symmetrical and noble face" somewhat spoiled by "large round eyes which could barely see" (PN 006-053). She receives visitors with extreme shyness, to the point that "certain courtiers, enemies of Conte Mosca, ventured to say that the Princess looked like the woman who was being presented and the Duchessa like the sovereign" (PN 006-053).

Despite her timidity, the Princess is not unintelligent. She proves to be "really learned" in botany and maintains "some very fine hothouses with quantities of tropical plants" (PN 006-053). The Duchessa wins her over through a shared interest in botany, making a "lifelong conquest" of the Princess during an audience that, "in defiance of all the rules of etiquette," lasts for "no less than an hour and a quarter" (PN 006-053).

## Marriage and Isolation

Clara-Paolina's marriage is a source of deep unhappiness. Her husband keeps a mistress, the Marchesa Balbi, which leads Clara-Paolina to imagine herself "the most unhappy person in the universe, a belief which had made her perhaps the most trying" (PN 006-053). The Prince "saw his wife only at dinner: this meal lasted for thirty minutes, and the Prince would spend whole weeks without saying a word to Clara-Paolina" (PN 006-107).

She "looked upon herself as the unhappiest of women" (PN 006-107), and the Duchessa Sanseverina "paid her various attentions and tried to prove to her that she was by no means so unhappy as she supposed" (PN 006-107).

## Relationship with the Duchessa

The Princess and Gina Sanseverina develop a genuine friendship. The Duchessa becomes the Princess's confidante and ally at court. Clara-Paolina also holds authority in her own part of the Palace—she wears "a little gold key, which is worn in the belt, and is the badge of supreme authority in the part of the Palace which belongs to the Princess" (PN 023-074).

In a later scene, the Princess confides her fear that her son, the Crown Prince, "will treat me worse than his father did" (PN 023-074), revealing the depth of her vulnerability even within her own family. The Duchessa reassures her: "That is what I shall prevent" (PN 023-075).

## Court Role

As Princess Consort, Clara-Paolina is one of the key figures at the Parma court. Fabrizio del Dongo, following the Duchessa's advice, "paid a sagacious court to the two Princes, father and son, to the Princess Clara-Paolina and Monsignore the Archbishop" (PN 007-071). She is thus grouped among the four principal figures whose favour is essential to court advancement.

## Significance

Clara-Paolina represents the human cost of the rigid court system. Her melancholic isolation—surrounded by ceremony yet starved of genuine human connection—makes her one of the novel's most sympathetic figures. Her friendship with the Duchessa Sanseverina is one of the few relationships in the novel based on genuine affection rather than political calculation.
