# The Jesuit Order

## Overview

The Society of Jesus (Jesuits) was the most powerful and controversial religious order in Catholic Europe. Founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540, the Jesuits were known for their intellectual rigor, their role in education, and their political influence at courts across the continent. Suppressed by the Pope in 1773 under pressure from the Bourbon monarchies, they were restored in 1814 — a timeline that coincides precisely with the novel's action. In *The Charterhouse of Parma*, the Jesuits appear primarily through the college at Milan where the young Fabrizio receives his education [001-029].

## Role in the Novel

The Jesuit college at Milan is Fabrizio's intellectual cradle — and the source of the religious sensibility that runs beneath his worldly adventures. Stendhal, who was educated by Jesuits himself in Grenoble, treats the order with characteristic ambivalence: the Jesuits have given Fabrizio a moral vocabulary he cannot entirely discard, even when he is killing Giletti, escaping from prison, or preaching to enraptured crowds. The Jesuit education is a formation of the soul that neither Napoleon's battles nor Parma's court intrigues can entirely erase [001-030].

The Marchese del Dongo, Fabrizio's reactionary father, sends his son to the Jesuit college precisely because he hopes the order's conservative influence will counteract the dangerous Bonapartist enthusiasm that pervades the family [001-035]. The irony — deeply Stendhalian — is that the Jesuits' intellectual training makes Fabrizio a sharper observer of the world, including the injustices of the Restoration regime his father supports.
