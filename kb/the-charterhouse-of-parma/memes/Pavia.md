Pavia is a city in [[Lombardy]], situated on the Ticino River south of [[Milan]]. In Stendhal's novel, it is referenced primarily as a destination for medical consultation — [[Conte-Mosca|Conte Mosca]] considers travelling there to consult the city's renowned doctors [022-014]. The city's surrounding countryside, particularly the village of San Nazzaro to the west of Pavia, also figures in the novel's narrative.

## Location and Description

Pavia lies in the [[Po]] Valley, south-west of [[Milan]], on the banks of the Ticino River. During the period of the novel (early nineteenth century), it was part of the Austrian Kingdom of [[Lombardy]]-Venetia. The city was famous throughout Italy — and indeed Europe — for its university, one of the oldest in the world, and particularly for its medical faculty. This reputation for medical expertise is what draws the novel's characters to consider Pavia as a destination.

The village of San Nazzaro, "to the west of Pavia" [023-006], lies in the countryside outside the city. It is described as being near [[Casalmaggiore|the Po River]], in the region of the Pavese — the rural territory surrounding Pavia. The area's geography, with its proximity to both the Ticino and [[Po]] rivers, places it at a key junction in the landscape of [[Lombardy]].

## Role in the Novel

Pavia's role in the novel is closely tied to [[Conte-Mosca|Conte Mosca]]'s health concerns. After his departure from [[Parma]], Mosca considers travelling "to consult the doctors of Pavia" [022-014], reflecting both the city's medical reputation and Mosca's deteriorating condition. The consultation is never described in detail, but the reference establishes Pavia as a node in the network of northern Italian cities that the novel's characters traverse.

The village of San Nazzaro, near Pavia, plays a more specific narrative role. Lodovico, Fabrizio's loyal servant, travels there on a mission — and a "Latin epistle which the worthy parish priest of San Nazzaro had composed" [023-006] becomes a document of some importance. Lodovico returns from San Nazzaro "in hot haste" [023-007], suggesting urgency in whatever business was conducted there.

## Historical Background

Pavia's university, founded in 1361, was one of the leading medical schools in Europe during Stendhal's time. The practice of travelling to consult distant physicians was common among the Italian aristocracy — medical expertise was concentrated in a few university cities, and patients of means would travel considerable distances for consultation. The novel's brief reference to "the doctors of Pavia" [022-014] accurately reflects this historical reality.
