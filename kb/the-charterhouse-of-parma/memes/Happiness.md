Happiness (''bonheur'' in Stendhal's French) is the central emotional value in ''The Charterhouse of Parma'', functioning as both a personal pursuit and a philosophical touchstone. For Fabrizio del Dongo, happiness is the unspoken goal of every action — from his naive enthusiasm at [[Battle-of-Waterloo|Waterloo]] to his transcendent contentment in the [[Farnese-Tower|Farnese Tower]] prison. Stendhal presents happiness not as a static state but as a quality of attention and engagement, often found in the most unlikely circumstances.

## The Pursuit of Happiness

Stendhal's treatment of happiness is inseparable from his conception of the "pursuit of happiness" (''la chasse au bonheur''), a phrase he inscribed on his own tombstone. In the novel, this pursuit defines the novel's moral geography: characters who actively seek their own happiness — [[Gina-Sanseverina|Gina Sanseverina]], [[Conte-Mosca]], and Fabrizio himself — are portrayed sympathetically, while those who obstruct others' happiness — [[Prince-of-Parma|Prince Ranuccio-Ernesto IV]], [[Marchesa-Raversi|General Fabio Conti]] — are the villains of the piece.

The narrator describes the early Napoleonic period as one of collective happiness: "those ten years of progress and happiness, from 1800 to 1810" [001-029]. This retrospective framing establishes happiness as historically contingent — possible under Napoleon's meritocratic order, threatened under the restored absolutism of the [[Parma|Parmese]] court.

## Happiness in Captivity

The novel's most striking treatment of happiness occurs during Fabrizio's imprisonment in the [[Farnese-Tower|Farnese Tower]]. Contrary to expectation, his confinement becomes the site of his greatest happiness:

> Fabrizio was not sufficiently mature to realise that it was the rare moments of excitement of this kind that made the happiness of his life [008-018].

The paradox of prison happiness is central to Stendhal's argument: authentic happiness requires the removal of distractions. Stripped of social ambition, court intrigue, and the pursuit of reputation, Fabrizio discovers that "a feeling of purity and virtue which formed his happiness at that moment" [008-018] is accessible in the barest circumstances. The proximity to [[Clelia-Conti|Clelia Conti]] transforms imprisonment from punishment to privilege — "he was literally mad with happiness" [006-062].

## Happiness and Love

In Stendhal's moral universe, happiness and [[Love|love]] are inseparable. Count Mosca articulates this connection explicitly: "happiness enhances love" [006-118], he tells Gina, making the case that emotional fulfilment amplifies rather than exhausts romantic feeling.

The narrator repeatedly foregrounds the question of whether characters are happy, making happiness the implicit metric by which all actions are judged. The Duchessa's political machinations are motivated by her desire for Fabrizio's happiness; Mosca's entire career is sustained by the "intimacy that constituted his happiness in life" [007-035] with Gina. For Stendhal, the pursuit of happiness legitimises actions that conventional morality would condemn — a position that scandalised contemporary readers.

## Happiness as Moral Criterion

The novel's radical proposition is that happiness — personal, emotional, immediate — is a more reliable guide to conduct than duty, honour, or social convention. The narrator's tone when describing unhappy characters (the Prince, General Conti, the courtiers) is consistently ironic and dismissive:

> The sight of happiness made him furious [006-107]

This line, describing Prince Ranuccio-Ernesto IV's reaction to Fabrizio's contentment, encapsulates the novel's moral taxonomy: those who resent others' happiness are spiritually dead; those who pursue it are alive, whatever their moral failings.
