# Colonel Scotti

## Role in the Novel

Colonel Scotti is a military officer attached to the Court of Parma and a figure from Gina Sanseverina's romantic past. He is described as having been one of Gina's lovers, "admirably presented" [005-105] at court. His presence adds texture to the intricate social world of Parma, where personal histories and political ambitions are inextricably intertwined. Through his relationship with Gina, he exemplifies the pattern of strategic romantic alliances that characterize court life under the Prince of Parma.

## Biography

Colonel (later General) Scotti was a lover of [[Gina-Sanseverina]] in the period before the novel's main action [005-105]. By the time of the story, their romantic relationship has ended, but he remains a figure at the Parma court. Gina is noted to have sought out his counsel or company on occasion — she "tried to meet" with men including "her old friend General Scotti" [006-004], indicating an ongoing social connection. The transition from lover to trusted friend reflects the maturity and pragmatism that define Gina's character.

The queue entry notes that Scotti is "admirably presented" by Stendhal, suggesting a character who, unlike many of the scheming courtiers, retains a certain dignity and honor. His trajectory from colonel to general also reflects the professional military path available to competent officers in the Parma establishment.
