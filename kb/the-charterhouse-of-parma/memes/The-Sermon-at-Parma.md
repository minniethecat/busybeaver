# The Sermon at Parma

## Overview

Years after his escape and return, Fabrizio has become a monsignor — and, unexpectedly, a celebrated preacher. His sermons draw crowds from across the city, and his fame as an orator rivals his earlier notoriety as a prisoner. The sermon at Parma is the culmination of Fabrizio's transformation: the boy who wandered lost at Waterloo, the man who killed Giletti in a roadside fight, the prisoner who loved Clelia through the bars of the Farnese Tower — all of these selves are subsumed into the figure who now stands before an enraptured congregation, his voice carrying conviction born of suffering (027-041).

## Role in the Novel

The sermon marks Fabrizio's apotheosis as a public figure. Where his earlier life was defined by impulsive action, his later life is defined by language — by the power of words to move, to persuade, to transform. Stendhal, ever alert to the gap between inner experience and public performance, does not resolve the ambiguity: is Fabrizio's faith genuine, or has he simply found a new stage for his native charisma? The novel refuses to answer, and in that refusal lies its modernity (027-045).

The sermon also reunites Fabrizio with Clelia, who attends despite her vow. Their meeting in the church — wordless, across a crowd — is one of the novel's most charged scenes, a reunion that is also a renewed parting. Stendhal understood that the deepest dramatic events are often the quietest (027-044).
