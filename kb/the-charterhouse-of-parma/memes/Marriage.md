# Marriage

In Stendhal's _The Charterhouse of Parma_, **marriage** operates primarily as a strategic and economic institution rather than a romantic union. Across the novel's social spectrum — from the nobility of Milan to the court of Parma — matrimonial arrangements consistently subordinate individual desire to family ambition, political calculation, and class preservation.

## Marriage as Social Contract

The novel opens with a sweeping portrait of Lombard aristocratic society, where marriage is explicitly described as a transactional arrangement: "whenever the marriage occurred of a young lady belonging to some rich or noble family" [001-002], the event was commemorated with printed sonnets on rose-coloured taffeta handkerchiefs, and "two or three years after that great event in her life, the young lady in question used to engage a devoted admirer" — the _cicisbeo_ system embedded in marriage contracts themselves. This sets the tone for marriage throughout the novel: a formal structure that accommodates, and even expects, extra-marital passion.

## Gina's Strategic Marriages

Gina Sanseverina, the novel's most politically astute character, navigates the marriage system with remarkable agency. Her brother the Marchese del Dongo "had arranged the marriage of his young sister Gina with a personage of great wealth and the very highest birth" [001-022], but Gina rebels, instead marrying Conte Pietranera — "a very fine gentleman... but ruined for generations past in estate" and "a fervent supporter of the new ideas." This marriage of love brings her happiness but no security; after Pietranera's death, she is left impoverished, her brother having "refused to pay her marriage portion" [001-023].

Her second marriage — to the Duca Sanseverina-Taxis — is arranged by Conte Mosca as a purely political transaction. Mosca himself proposes it: "when I made him a proposal of marriage; my first condition was, you can understand, that he must never set foot again in Parma" [006-041]. The Duca, sixty-eight years old, agrees solely to obtain the Grand Cordon. Gina never sees him after the altar. Mosca assures her: "you may be quite sure that this marriage will not be thought odd among us until the day on which I am disgraced" [006-043]. This arrangement epitomizes the novel's cynical view of aristocratic marriage: a legal fiction serving dynastic ends.

## Clelia's Forced Marriage

The most tragic marriage in the novel is Clelia Conti's union with the Marchese Crescenzi, the richest courtier in Parma. Clelia loves Fabrizio but is bound by her vow to the Madonna — made during Fabrizio's imprisonment — to marry Crescenzi and never see Fabrizio again. This marriage, while not directly coerced by family, is coerced by religious obligation, demonstrating how even apparently "free" marital choices are constrained by the social and spiritual codes of Parma.

## Marriage and Class

Throughout the novel, marriage serves as a mechanism of class preservation. Nobles marry nobles; the del Dongo family's obsession with lineage is expressed through marital alliances. The Conte Mosca, though separated from his wife, remains "a married man" — a fact that complicates his relationship with Gina and exposes the double standard of Parma's court, where men's marriages are invisible while women's are scrutinized. The marriage contract becomes a document not of love but of property, status, and political convenience.
