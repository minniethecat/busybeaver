# Miseno

**Miseno** (ancient Misenum) is a coastal site on the Bay of [[Naples]], known in Stendhal's novel for archaeological excavations and as a place of solitary contemplation. The site yielded ancient artifacts, including a bust that features in the narrative [007-001].

## Archaeology

Excavations at Miseno unearthed significant finds. Archaeological digging at the site turned up a bust which became a point of interest — reportedly a bust of Tiberius, described as the finest surviving depiction of that emperor [007-001]. The discoveries at Miseno reflect the broader nineteenth-century fascination with classical antiquity.

## Role in the Novel

The shores of Miseno serve as a setting for introspection. Characters pace the shoreline of Miseno, removed from the complicated interests and intrigues of the [[Parma|court]] [009-004]. The site offers a geographical and psychological distance from the political machinations that dominate much of the narrative.
