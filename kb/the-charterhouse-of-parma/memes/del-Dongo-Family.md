# del Dongo Family

## Overview

The del Dongo family is the aristocratic Lombard house at the heart of *La Chartreuse de Parme*.
Their ancestral castle at [[Grianta]], perched on the shores of [[Lake-Como]], serves as the
novel's symbolic point of origin — a place of youthful freedom that [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]]
spends the rest of his life trying to recover. The family is divided between the reactionary
loyalties of the [[Marchese-del-Dongo]] and the quiet resistance of the [[Marchesa-del-Dongo]],
a tension that shapes Fabrizio's political formation.

## Historical Background

The del Dongo name evokes the historical Dongo family of Lombardy, though Stendhal freely
invents the family's details. The fictional marchionate places the family among the minor
aristocracy of Austrian-ruled Lombardy — wealthy enough to maintain a castle and retainers,
but politically subordinate to the Habsburg administration in Milan. This position mirrors
the predicament of many Lombard noble families after the Congress of Vienna restored Austrian
rule in 1815.

## Role in the Novel

The del Dongo family's divided loyalties set the novel's political stakes in motion. The
Marchese, an Austrian loyalist who "trembled at the word **liberty**," rejoices at the
return of Austrian troops to Milan in 1815 (002-008). The Marchesa, by contrast, nurses a
secret Bonapartist sympathy and raises Fabrizio on tales of Napoleonic glory. This domestic
civil war — played out in the draughty halls of Grianta — prefigures the larger political
conflicts Fabrizio will navigate at the [[Court-of-Parma]].

[[Gina-Sanseverina]] (née Pietranera, later del Dongo by her second marriage) is the
Marchese's sister and Fabrizio's beloved aunt — arguably the novel's most powerful character.
Through her, the del Dongo bloodline exerts influence over the Parma court via her
relationship with [[Conte-Mosca]], the Prime Minister.

[[Ascanio-del-Dongo]], Fabrizio's younger brother, takes after their father and serves as
a foil — conventional, obedient, and deeply uninteresting.

## Key Passages

> The Marchese del Dongo, a staunch supporter of the old order, experienced unspeakable joy
> at seeing the Austrian troops reenter Milan. In obedience to his principles, he had always
> regarded the French as Jacobin usurpers. (002-008)

The family dynamic is thus established: a father who celebrates foreign occupation, a mother
who grieves for liberty, and a son caught between them.
