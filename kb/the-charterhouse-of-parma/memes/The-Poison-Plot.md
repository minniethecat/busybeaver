# The Poison Plot

## Overview

When Fabrizio is sentenced to death, the Duchessa Sanseverina, having exhausted all legal and political means, turns to Ferrante Palla and contemplates poisoning the Prince. The plot involves a specially prepared poison, a network of republican conspirators, and a plan to contaminate the citadel's water supply. Stendhal presents the Duchessa's decision without moral commentary — it is simply what her love demands. The poison plot is the dark twin of her social brilliance: the same determination and intelligence that make her the centre of court life are here deployed in service of assassination.

## Role in the Novel

The poison plot represents the novel's moral limit case. The Duchessa, the most sympathetic and brilliant character in the novel, is willing to commit mass murder for love. Stendhal refuses to judge her — and in that refusal lies his modernity. The question is not whether her action is right or wrong, but whether the reader, having understood her love for Fabrizio, can condemn it. The poison plot is the test of Stendhal's moral psychology: it asks whether love, taken to its extreme, transcends morality or destroys it.
