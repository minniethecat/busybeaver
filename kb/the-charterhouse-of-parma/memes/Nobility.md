# Nobility

The **nobility** in Stendhal's _The Charterhouse of Parma_ constitutes the social framework within which all significant action unfolds. From the del Dongo family of Milan to the courtiers of Parma, noble birth is the primary determinant of identity, opportunity, and fate — even as the novel relentlessly exposes the mediocrity, fear, and pettiness that aristocratic privilege breeds.

## The Old Nobility and the New Order

The novel's opening chapters present a nobility in crisis. The French Revolution and Napoleon's campaigns have upended the old aristocratic order: the noble families had been given "a distressing prominence in the allocation of the forced loans exacted for the French army." The Marchese del Dongo embodies the reactionary noble, living in "a sullen revolt against the prevailing high spirits," retreating to his country estate while the world changes around him. His hatred of Jacobins and Bonapartists is less political conviction than class panic.

The younger generation — Fabrizio, Gina, Conte Pietranera — represents a break with this rigid aristocratic mindset. Pietranera, though "of the most personable appearance," is "ruined for generations past in estate" and a "fervent supporter of the new ideas." His marriage to Gina scandalizes the del Dongo family precisely because it crosses ideological lines that noble society treats as absolute. The Marchesa del Dongo was "obliged to visit those of the nobility who had accepted public office, and even some highly influential persons who were not of noble birth" — a humiliation that illustrates the aristocracy's diminishing practical power even as its social prestige endures.

## Nobility at the Court of Parma

In Parma, nobility operates as both a prerequisite and a performance. The Prince of Parma rules through a nobility that is simultaneously servile and scheming. Conte Mosca, the Prime Minister, navigates this world with cynical mastery — he tells Gina that absolute power "sanctifies everything in the eyes of the public," and that "everyone who is not either a noble or a bigot is in prison, or is packing up to go there."

The obsession with titles and honors — the Grand Cordon, the Order, the various ranks — drives characters to absurd lengths. The Duca Sanseverina-Taxis, Gina's nominal husband, is sixty-eight with "an insane passion for the Grand Cordon"; his "grandfather the tax-gatherer" is a source of shame that his noble title must erase. The novel's satire of aristocratic vanity is unsparing.

## Birth vs. Merit

Fabrizio del Dongo, despite being born into one of Lombardy's great families, repeatedly finds that his noble birth is both a protection and a prison. It shields him from consequences (after the Giletti duel, after Waterloo) but also limits his freedom — he is expected to follow a career path appropriate to his station. His eventual rejection of court life for the Charterhouse represents a repudiation of the noble world that formed him.

## The Nobility's Decline

Throughout the novel, Stendhal documents the slow erosion of aristocratic power. The Napoleonic interlude demonstrated that talent and ambition could triumph over birth; the Restoration may have returned the old families to power, but their authority is now visibly hollow. Conte Mosca's cynical management of the Parma court reveals a system sustained by habit and fear rather than genuine legitimacy. The nobility's pretensions to honor and grandeur are undercut at every turn by the realities of greed, cowardice, and petty intrigue.
