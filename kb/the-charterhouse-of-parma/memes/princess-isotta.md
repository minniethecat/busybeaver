# Princess Isotta

Princess Isotta is an elderly cousin of Ranuccio-Ernesto IV, the Prince of Parma. She appears in the narrative as a potential marriage prospect proposed to Conte Mosca as part of court intrigue.

## Marriage Proposal

The Duchessa Sanseverina, seeking to secure Mosca's position at court, proposes that he marry the Princess. The suggestion shocks Mosca: "The idea of marrying this elderly Princess; nothing would less have suited him" (PN 017-014). Nonetheless, the Duchessa persists, telling him: "You aspire to the hand of the Princess Isotta, and it is a noble ambition" (PN 017-022). She later repeats the strategy: "Obtaining for me the hand of the Princess Isotta, and so forth" (PN 017-051).

## Court Visit

Mosca dutifully visits the Princess, an encounter rendered with Stendhal's characteristic irony. He goes "to pay a call on the elderly Princess Isotta. Never in his life had he felt so ill at ease" (PN 017-056). The visit produces an unexpected reaction — Mosca observes "the Princess Isotta, who had blushed deeply" (PN 017-059), suggesting the elderly royal cousin is not indifferent to the powerful minister's attention.

## Character and Significance

The Princess speaks with the dignity of her rank. When distinctions of status arise, "said the Princess: 'the daughter of a King of France'" (PN 017-057), invoking royal precedent. At fifty years old, she is described as "only fifty" (PN 017-012) — a detail that underscores the transactional nature of court marriages, where personal feelings are secondary to political alliance.

Princess Isotta's role, though brief, illuminates the court's machinery of marriage as politics. That Mosca never actually marries her is beside the point; the very proposal serves as a maneuver in the ongoing struggle between the Sanseverina-Mosca faction and their rivals.
