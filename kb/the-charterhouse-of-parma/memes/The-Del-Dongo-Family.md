# The del Dongo Family

## Overview

The del Dongo family of Grianta, on the shores of Lake Como, is the novel's foundational social unit. The Marchese del Dongo, Fabrizio's father, is an Austrian loyalist who wears a powdered wig and believes in the divine right of aristocracy; his sister Gina (later the Duchessa Sanseverina) is a Bonapartist whose wit and beauty will conquer the court of Parma. Fabrizio, the younger son, grows up between these poles — his father's reactionary certainties and his aunt's romantic energies — and his entire life can be read as an attempt to reconcile them [001-022].

## Role in the Novel

The del Dongo family is the novel's first and most intimate institution — the organization that shapes Fabrizio before the court, the Church, or the army ever touch him. Stendhal treats the family with his usual double vision: it is at once a source of love (Gina's devotion to Fabrizio is the novel's emotional constant) and a system of constraint (the Marchese's patriarchal authority attempts to dictate his children's lives down to their marriages and careers) [001-023].

The family is also the novel's political microcosm. The Marchese's Austrian loyalism, Gina's Bonapartism, and Fabrizio's romantic indifference to all political labels replay, in domestic form, the divisions of Restoration Italy. When the Marchese arranges Gina's marriage to an impoverished nobleman, he is acting as a father — but also as the representative of a class system that treats women as currency. Gina's subsequent career — the mistress of a Prime Minister, the power behind the throne of Parma — is a lifelong revenge against the family that sold her [001-022].
