# The Canon Borda's Journal

## Overview

The Canon Borda's journal is the second layer in the novel's Chinese-box frame narrative. The narrator of the Preface claims to have received the story from his uncle, Canon Borda, who compiled it from the manuscript of 1830. The journal thus functions as a filter — an editorial layer that justifies omissions, explains gaps, and vouches for the manuscript's authenticity. Stendhal's genius is to make this device do double duty: the Canon's editorial interventions are at once a claim of documentary seriousness and a parody of documentary seriousness [P01-005].

## In the Novel

The Canon's journal appears only in the Preface, but its presence colours the entire narrative. When the narrator writes that he has "reproduced this narrative without any alteration from the manuscript of 1830," he is invoking the Canon's authority as a guarantor of textual fidelity. But the Canon himself is a fiction — a character invented by Stendhal to create the illusion of a documentary chain that does not exist. The journal is thus a work that functions as a kind of literary trompe-l'oeil: it looks like evidence but is actually artifice [P01-006].

## Significance

The Canon's journal exemplifies Stendhal's play with the conventions of historical fiction. Where Walter Scott's prefaces claim to have discovered real documents, Stendhal's preface claims to have discovered a document that the reader knows to be invented — and this knowingness is the point. The Charterhouse of Parma is a novel that wears its fictionality on its sleeve, and the Canon's journal is the garment in which that fictionality is dressed [P01-005].

## Key Passages

> Let me give you my uncle's journal, which, under the heading "Parma," contains nothing that could compromise anyone. My uncle's manuscript was deposited with a notary in Padua [P01-005].
