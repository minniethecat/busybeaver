Amiens is a city in northern France, in the region of Picardy. In *The Charterhouse of Parma*, it serves as a place of recovery for [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo|Fabrizio del Dongo]] after he is wounded at the [[Battle-of-Waterloo|Battle of Waterloo]]. The city represents a transitional space in Fabrizio's journey — between the chaos of the battlefield and his eventual return to [[Italy]].

## Location and Description

Amiens lies on the River Somme, roughly halfway between [[Paris]] and the Channel coast. In the early nineteenth century it was a significant staging post on the routes connecting the French capital with the northern frontier. The city's position made it a natural destination for soldiers and travellers moving southward from the Low Countries theatre of the Napoleonic Wars.

Stendhal's novel does not describe Amiens in detail; it appears primarily as a backdrop to Fabrizio's convalescence. The protagonist "reached Amiens in great pain from the cut he had received" [005-020], suggesting a difficult journey from the battlefield to the relative safety of the city.

## Role in the Novel

Amiens is the location where Fabrizio recovers from his Waterloo wound. The novel references his "convalescence at Amiens" [029-004], where he encounters [[Colonel-Le-Baron|Colonel Le Baron]], a wounded French officer who had also been at [[Waterloo]]. This encounter provides Fabrizio with a connection to the French military world he had briefly joined and a perspective on the battle in which he participated.

Fabrizio's stay at Amiens marks the end of his direct involvement in the Napoleonic adventure. From here, his journey will take him back towards [[Italy]] and into the political intrigues of the [[Parma|Parmese]] court. The city thus functions as a threshold — the last French location of note before Fabrizio's return to Italian soil and the beginning of his life under the shadow of Restoration absolutism.

## Historical Background

Amiens was an important cathedral city and administrative centre in early nineteenth-century France. It lent its name to the Treaty of Amiens (1802), which briefly paused hostilities between Revolutionary France and Britain. By 1815, when the novel's Waterloo episode takes place, the city lay on the route used by wounded soldiers and stragglers making their way south from the battlefield. Its hospitals and lodgings would have received many casualties from the campaign.
