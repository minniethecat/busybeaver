# Canon Borda

## Role in the Novel

Canon Borda is a minor figure at the court of Parma who appears in connection with the Contessa Pietranera's social circle. A charming young cleric, he exemplifies the worldly churchmen who populate Stendhal's Parma — more concerned with social intrigue than spiritual matters. His jealousy over the Contessa's attentions provides a small but telling illustration of court society's pettiness.

## Biography

Borda is introduced as a young canon known for his charm and good looks. He is part of the social circle that surrounds the Contessa [[Pietranera]] during her time at court.

> "She was reminded of a certain Canon Borda, a most charming young man." (005-091)

When the Contessa enters a room, Borda is described as falling to his knees before her — an exaggerated gesture that reveals both his devotion and the theatrical nature of court manners.

> "The Contessa entered the room; Borda fell on his knees." (005-093)

Borda plays a role in explaining a complex legal and social case to the Contessa, demonstrating the clergy's function as intermediaries in court affairs. His jealousy, however, is noted with ironic detachment by the narrator.

> "Let us admit frankly that Canon Borda's jealousy was not altogether without cause." (006-001)

## Character Analysis

Borda represents the worldly clergy — a recurring type in Stendhal's fiction. He is charming, socially adept, and entirely comfortable operating within the court's system of favors and intrigues. His religious vocation is purely nominal; his real occupation is social advancement. The narrator's ironic treatment of Borda's jealousy typifies Stendhal's satirical view of court society.

## Relationships

- **[[Pietranera]]**: The Contessa in whose social circle Borda moves; he is devoted to her
- **Court society**: Borda is emblematic of the worldly clerics who populate the Parma court
