# Rassi

## Role in the Novel

The bourgeois Chief Justice of Parma — a legal functionary of bottomless servility whose skill at manipulating the law makes him indispensable to the Prince and dangerous to everyone else.

## Biography

Fiscal Rassi rises from the middle classes to become Chief Justice of Parma through a combination of legal talent and absolute moral flexibility. He is despised by the aristocracy for his low birth and feared for his power. Rassi understands that his position depends entirely on pleasing the Prince, and he applies his considerable intelligence to anticipating the Prince's wishes before they are expressed. He serves as the legal architect of Fabrizio's imprisonment and later, when the political winds shift, of his release. His social ambition is a source of comedy in the novel — the Duchessa mocks him mercilessly even as she uses him. Rassi represents a new type in Restoration society: the professional functionary whose power derives not from birth but from competence and servility.

## Character Analysis

Rassi is Stendhal's study in the psychology of the court functionary. Unlike Mosca, who serves with intelligence and a degree of independence, Rassi serves with pure instrumentality. He has no convictions, no loyalties, no passions — only the desire to survive and rise. This makes him both useful and contemptible. Stendhal's portrayal is not without sympathy: Rassi is a product of a system that rewards servility, and his venality is the mirror image of the aristocracy's privilege. In a novel of grand passions, Rassi represents the triumph of small ones — ambition, fear, and the pleasure of power over those who despise him.
