# Marchese del Dongo

## Role in the Novel

The Marchese del Dongo is the patriarch of the del Dongo family and Fabrizio's father. An Austrian loyalist who despises the French and Napoleon, he embodies the reactionary aristocracy of Restoration-era Lombardy. His role is primarily antagonistic — his coldness toward his wife and children, his miserliness, and his political espionage create the oppressive domestic atmosphere from which Fabrizio and Gina must escape. He appears most prominently in the early chapters, setting the family dynamics that drive the novel's opening.

## Biography

The Marchese del Dongo is a nobleman of Grianta on Lake Como, holding an ancestral castle and significant estates under Austrian rule. During the Napoleonic period, he fled his castle, returning only after the Austrian restoration. Stendhal notes that after "not guillotining anyone, the Marchese del Dongo returned from his castle of Grianta" to Milan, where he became an active agent of the Austrian regime.

> [The Marchese] returned from his castle of Grianta, on the Lake of Como, whither he had taken flight at the coming of the French.
> (001-014)

Irritated by liberal developments and his wife's independent spirit, the Marchese retreated from Milan back to Grianta. His political allegiance to Austria deepened after his disgrace, doubling his aristocratic pretensions. Stendhal describes him as having "an exalted position in the Austrian secret police," his agents surveilling even his own family.

> The Marchese del Dongo, irritated by the success of the ideas of the French Revolution, and more especially by the sight of his wife at a ball at the Casa Tanzi, had retired to his castle of Grianta.
> (001-020)

The Marchese arranged for his son Fabrizio to be educated at a Jesuit College in Milan, insisting on a reactionary upbringing. He left his young wife at Milan, effectively abandoning the marriage emotionally. His miserly habits — "miserly as a rule" — and his police connections made him feared and despised even by his own children.

> The Marchese del Dongo, miserly as a rule, was none the less determined to uphold the reputation of his house, and nothing was too good for a daughter who was to carry its name into so brilliant a family.
> (002-017)

## Relationships

### Family

The Marchese's most defining relationship is with his wife, the [[Marchesa del Dongo]], whom he married for fortune and status. He left her isolated at Milan while he retreated to Grianta, and their marriage became a cold arrangement. Toward his children, particularly [[Fabrizio del Dongo]], he was distant and authoritarian, insisting on the Jesuit education Fabrizio detested.

His sister [[Gina Sanseverina]] (formerly Pietranera) detested him. She actively undermined his authority and eventually became Fabrizio's protector, spiriting the boy away from the Marchese's influence. One agent's report captured the sentiment: Gina referred to "that abominable *seccatore* like that Marchese del Dongo."

> What a prospect — to be delivered from the attentions of that horrible face of the Marchese del Dongo, as well as that unspeakable Jesuit, his son Ascanio!
> (005-106)

### Political

The Marchese del Dongo was a dedicated agent of the Austrian secret police in Milan. His political connections and espionage work gave him power but also made him a despised figure. He saw French and liberal ideas as existential threats, doubling down on his reactionary positions after Napoleon's fall.

## Character Analysis

Stendhal paints the Marchese as a caricature of reactionary aristocracy: ugly, miserly, paranoid, and politically repulsive. He serves as the novel's symbol of the old order — the Austria-dominated Lombardy that stifles youth, love, and liberal ideals. His physical ugliness mirrors his moral pettiness, a technique Stendhal uses throughout the novel.

The Marchese functions as the first of many father-figures against whom Fabrizio must define himself. Unlike Conte Mosca (who becomes a benevolent paternal figure) or General Conti (a rival father), the Marchese represents the biological father whose world must be rejected for the protagonist to find his own path.
