# Clelia Conti

## Role in the Novel

The governor's daughter who becomes the object of Fabrizio's most transcendent love — a figure of luminous purity whose devotion is constrained by duty to her father and her vow to the Madonna.

## Biography

Clelia Conti is the daughter of General Fabio Conti, the governor of the Farnese Tower. She first meets Fabrizio when he is imprisoned in her father's citadel, and they fall in love through the barred windows and signal systems that connect their separate worlds. After his escape, she is forced by her father's political disgrace to marry the wealthy Marchese Crescenzi. Her secret relationship with Fabrizio continues after her marriage, and they have a son together. She has sworn a vow to the Madonna never to look at Fabrizio — a vow she keeps literally by meeting him only in darkness. When their son dies, she believes it is divine punishment for breaking her vow and dies shortly afterward.

## Character Analysis

Clelia is the novel's most enigmatic major character. Unlike Gina, whose passions are expressed in action, Clelia's love is expressed in restraint. Her vow to the Madonna — to never look at Fabrizio — is both absurd and sublime, a literalised metaphor for the impossibility of their love. She is the one character Fabrizio cannot charm or manoeuvre, and this resistance is precisely what elevates his feeling for her above all his other loves. In a novel of political intrigue and social performance, Clelia represents an ideal of purity that can exist only in separation — and her death confirms the novel's implicit thesis that perfect love cannot survive in the world.
