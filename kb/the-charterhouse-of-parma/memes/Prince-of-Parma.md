# Prince of Parma

## Role in the Novel

The absolute ruler of Parma — a petty tyrant governed by vanity, fear, and frustrated desire, whose arbitrary power makes him both dangerous and pitiable in Stendhal's ironic portrait.

## Biography

Ranuccio-Ernesto IV rules the miniature absolutist state of Parma with a combination of boredom, suspicion, and vanity. He is obsessed with the Duchessa Sanseverina, who consistently refuses his advances. His rule depends on Conte Mosca's administrative skill and Rassi's legal servility. The Prince sees conspiracy everywhere and imprisons anyone who threatens his fragile self-esteem. His persecution of Fabrizio is motivated less by political concern than by jealousy and the desire to punish Gina for rejecting him. After the death of his father (the Prince in the novel's early chapters), Ranuccio-Ernesto V ascends — younger but equally susceptible to the Duchessa's influence. Stendhal's portrait of the Prince is a masterpiece of political satire: he is terrifying because he has absolute power, and ridiculous because he is governed by petty emotions.

## Character Analysis

The Prince of Parma is one of Stendhal's great comic creations — a figure who would be merely absurd if he did not hold the power of life and death. Stendhal's achievement is to make him simultaneously ridiculous and dangerous. The Prince's tragedy (if so grand a word can be used) is that he knows he is not respected: he suspects, correctly, that everyone at his court is performing for him. This insecurity makes him cruel. He is the novel's most thorough study in the psychology of petty power — and a warning about what happens when vanity, fear, and absolute authority converge in one person.
