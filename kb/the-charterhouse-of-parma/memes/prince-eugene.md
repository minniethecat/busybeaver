# Prince Eugène de Beauharnais

Prince Eugène de Beauharnais (1781–1824) was the Viceroy of Italy under Napoleon Bonaparte and the Emperor's stepson. In Stendhal's novel, his court at Milan forms the glittering social setting of the del Dongo family's pre-narrative history.

## The Court at Milan

The novel opens by establishing the brilliance of Eugène's viceregal court. Fabrizio's mother and aunt were "brilliant ladies at the court of Prince Eugène, the Viceroy of Italy" (PN 001-029). Stendhal emphasizes the court's exceptional social quality: the brief reign was that of "the sociable Prince Eugène" (PN 001-031), a period marked by culture, elegance, and relative liberty.

The del Dongo brothers — Fabrizio's father and uncle — were attached to this court, where "ten times daily he used to see Prince Eugène" (PN 002-041), a proximity that gave them considerable standing before the Napoleonic collapse.

## The Fall of the Viceroyalty

With Napoleon's defeat, the viceregal court dissolved. The del Dongo family, "though tenderly cherished by the Prince Viceroy, were expelled from Italy" (PN 001-030) — or rather, the new political order made their position untenable. Gina Sanseverina, years later, still recalls the court as a lost golden age: "Who knew me six years ago, at Prince Eugène's court, may still find me charming" (PN 006-001).

## Historical Significance in the Novel

Prince Eugène functions as a symbol of the Napoleonic era's cultural and political vitality, contrasted with the oppressive atmosphere of the restored court of Parma under Ernesto IV. His court represents freedom, meritocracy, and French enlightenment values — everything the Parma court is not. Fabrizio's admiration for Napoleon, his flight to Waterloo, and his ultimate disillusionment all trace back to the world Prince Eugène embodied.
