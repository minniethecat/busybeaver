# Love

## Definition

Love in *The Charterhouse of Parma* is a multi-faceted passion that takes many forms: the patriotic love of country that swept through Milan upon Napoleon's arrival; the obsessive romantic love that consumes Fabrizio, the Duchessa Sanseverina, and Conte Mosca; the tender familial love between Gina and her nephew; and the idealised, almost religious devotion that Fabrizio feels for Clelia Conti. For Stendhal, love is never simple — it is entangled with ambition, vanity, jealousy, and the pursuit of happiness in a world governed by political intrigue.

## Historical Context

In the Italy of the Napoleonic era and the subsequent Restoration, love operated under strict social codes. Aristocratic marriages were political arrangements; passionate love was often pursued outside marriage, in the tradition of the *cicisbeo* or gallant. Stendhal himself was a student of love, having written the treatise *De l'Amour* (1822), in which he classified love into four types: passionate love (*amour-passion*), mannered love (*amour-goût*), physical love (*amour-physique*), and vanity-love (*amour de vanité*). This taxonomy visibly informs the novel's treatment of its characters' emotional lives.

## Role in the Novel

Love is the engine of the novel's plot. Fabrizio's romantic entanglements — with Marietta the actress, with the actress Fausta, and ultimately with Clelia Conti — drive his movements across Europe and his imprisonments. The Duchessa Sanseverina's fierce, quasi-maternal love for Fabrizio motivates her political machinations at the court of Parma. Conte Mosca's love for Gina leads him, a seasoned diplomat, to risk his career repeatedly. Indeed, the novel opens with a different kind of love: the Milanese populace "madly in love" with Napoleon and the ideals of the French Revolution (see (001-028)). This political love sets the stage for the personal dramas that follow.

Stendhal repeatedly shows that love cannot be separated from power. When Fabrizio is imprisoned in the Farnese Tower, it is precisely there — stripped of freedom — that he experiences his most transcendent love for Clelia (020-007). The prison becomes a paradoxical haven where love flourishes precisely because it is forbidden and endangered.

## Key Passages

> It was necessary to love one's country with a real love and to seek out heroic actions. They had been plunged in the darkest night by the continuation of the jealous despotism of Charles V and Philip II. (001-002)

> "To swear that I feel an insane passion for you," the Contessa said to him one day at Milan, "would be a lie; I should be only too glad to love today at thirty odd as I used to love at two-and-twenty! But I have seen so many things decay that I had imagined to be eternal! I have the most tender affection for you." (006-034)

> Passing one of her hands through the bars, by flashes of the lamp: "I love you — courage — health — hope. Exercise your arms." (020-007)
