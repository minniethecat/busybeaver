# Barbone

## Role in the Novel

Barbone is a minor antagonist in *The Charterhouse of Parma*, serving as a clerk and functionary in the Farnese Tower where Fabrizio is imprisoned. He embodies the petty tyranny of institutional power — the cruelty that flourishes not from grand political schemes but from the unaccountable authority of minor officials. His confrontation with Fabrizio is a pivotal moment in the prison narrative, demonstrating both Fabrizio's residual aristocratic pride and the strange freedoms that imprisonment paradoxically affords.

## Biography

Barbone appears in the prison section of the novel as "Barbone, the clerk, a terrifying personage" (015-004). He is not the governor of the prison — General Fabio Conti holds that position — but as the clerk who manages the day-to-day administration, he exercises considerable power over the prisoners' daily lives.

His defining moment comes when, "in making an insolent and extremely offensive remark" to Fabrizio (015-008), he provokes an explosive response. Fabrizio, despite being a prisoner, "dealt him such a blow that Barbone fell from his chair against the wall" (015-013). This act of defiance is immediately celebrated among the guards and other prisoners — Fabrizio has "given that insolent Barbone a proper smack" (015-015) — but it carries serious consequences.

The incident leads to Fabrizio being questioned "sharply why he had struck Barbone" (015-022), and it escalates the tension between Fabrizio and General Conti. Yet paradoxically, the act of violence against Barbone earns Fabrizio a measure of respect within the prison hierarchy. The guards, who themselves resent Barbone's petty tyranny, treat Fabrizio with increased deference after the incident.

## Character Analysis

Barbone is a study in petty power. Unlike the Prince or Rassi, who wield power on a grand political scale, Barbone's authority is narrow but intensely felt by those subject to it. He represents the bureaucratic face of oppression — the functionary whose cruelty is not ideological but habitual, the natural expression of a small mind given authority over others.

Fabrizio's punch is one of the novel's most satisfying moments of direct action. After chapters of courtly indirection and verbal sparring, the physical confrontation with Barbone releases a tension that has been building since Fabrizio's arrest. The blow is both an assertion of aristocratic privilege — Fabrizio refuses to be treated as a common criminal — and a genuinely democratic gesture: anyone who has suffered under petty officials can appreciate it.

Stendhal's treatment of Barbone is characteristic of his method for minor characters: a few sharp strokes create a vivid, memorable figure who serves his narrative purpose and then recedes. Barbone never appears again after the prison section, but his brief presence enriches the novel's portrait of institutional power at every level of society.
