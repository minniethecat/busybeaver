# Napoleon Bonaparte

## Role in the Novel

Napoleon Bonaparte is the historical figure whose dramatic return from exile on Elba sets the entire plot of *The Charterhouse of Parma* in motion. His landing in the Gulf of Juan in March 1815 electrifies Europe and inspires the seventeen-year-old [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]] to abandon his sheltered life at Grianta and seek glory on the battlefield. Though Napoleon himself appears only indirectly in the narrative, his presence looms over the entire first half of the novel as the object of Fabrizio's hero-worship and the symbol of a new, more dynamic order that threatens the reactionary world of Parma.

## Biography

The novel opens with Napoleon's Italian campaign of 1796, when the French army's arrival in Milan awakened the dormant energies of Lombardy. The narrator describes how "Napoleon won the battle of Marengo" [001-028], a victory that cemented French control over northern Italy. In March 1815, while the del Dongo family is at their castle on Lake Como, "Napoleon had just landed from the Gulf of Juan" [002-027], returning from his first exile on Elba.

Fabrizio's aunt [[Gina-Sanseverina]] supports his desire to "join Napoleon" [002-036], seeing in the Emperor's cause an opportunity for her nephew to escape the stifling atmosphere of the del Dongo household. The Abbot Blanès, however, attempts to warn Fabrizio of "the fate in store for Napoleon" [002-033], foreseeing the Emperor's eventual defeat. His final words before Fabrizio's departure are charged with prophetic intensity:

> "You are going to join Napoleon," he cried; and he fell on my neck and kissed me. (002-036)

The supper scene at the del Dongo castle captures the household's division over the Emperor's return. The Marchesa declares her despair:

> "You are taken from us? As for Napoleon's succeeding, that, my poor boy, you may be quite certain he will not." (002-032)

Fabrizio, undeterred, departs for France armed with romantic notions of battle drawn from "Napoleon's proclamations" [003-106], only to encounter the brutal reality of war at Waterloo. The gap between Fabrizio's imagined Napoleon and the distant, invisible commander he finds on the battlefield forms the novel's central disillusionment.

The Emperor's defeat at Waterloo and subsequent exile to Saint Helena mark the triumph of the reactionary order that governs Parma for the remainder of the novel. Napoleon thus functions as both a historical catalyst and a symbol of lost possibilities — a world of energy and merit that briefly opened and then closed, leaving characters like Fabrizio and Gina to navigate the diminished world of the Restoration.

## Fabrizio's Napoleon Worship

Fabrizio's devotion to Napoleon is a central thread of the novel's opening chapters. Raised on heroic accounts of the Italian campaign, he learns to read by deciphering "prints of the battles won by Napoleon: it was by spelling out the legends beneath these pictures that he had learned to read" (005-101). This visual, almost devotional education shapes his understanding of war as a gallery of triumphs rather than a field of suffering — an illusion Waterloo will shatter.

Stendhal himself shared this early enthusiasm, having served in Napoleon's army during the Italian campaign of 1800 and the Russian campaign of 1812. The autobiographical dimension gives Fabrizio's naive hero-worship a poignant authenticity.

## Historical Context

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) was Emperor of the French from 1804 to 1814, and briefly again during the Hundred Days of 1815. His Italian campaigns of 1796–1797 and 1800 (culminating at Marengo) brought much of northern Italy under French control, ending Austrian domination and introducing liberal reforms. The reaction against Napoleon after 1815 created the political environment that Stendhal satirizes throughout the novel — a world of petty princes, police spies, and the suppression of the liberal energies that Napoleon had briefly unleashed.
