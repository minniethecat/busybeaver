# The Landing of Napoleon

## Overview

On 1 March 1815, Napoleon Bonaparte landed at the Gulf of Juan after escaping from exile on Elba, beginning the Hundred Days that would end at Waterloo. In *The Charterhouse of Parma*, the landing is not directly narrated — it happens off-stage, in the world beyond Stendhal's fiction — but its shockwaves are felt in every corner of the novel's opening. The landing transforms Fabrizio from a restless adolescent into a would-be soldier, and transforms the political landscape of Italy from Austrian domination into a moment of revolutionary possibility. Stendhal's handling of this great historical event is typical of his method: he shows us not the event itself but its refraction through the lives of his characters [002-027].

## Role in the Novel

Napoleon's landing is the novel's first and most important historical event — it is the spark that ignites the entire plot. Without it, Fabrizio remains at Grianta, the Marchese del Dongo's younger son, reading French newspapers in secret. With it, he becomes a Bonapartist volunteer, crosses into France, and experiences the chaos of Waterloo — the formative event of his life [002-034].

The landing also serves as a structural model for how Stendhal integrates history into fiction. Unlike the historical novelists of his time (Scott, Hugo), Stendhal does not chronicle great events from the centre. He shows them from the periphery, through the eyes of characters who do not fully understand what they are witnessing. Napoleon's landing at the Gulf of Juan is a world-historical event, but in this novel it arrives as a rumour, an eagle seen in flight, a name whispered in a café — fragments of meaning that the characters must assemble for themselves [002-033].
