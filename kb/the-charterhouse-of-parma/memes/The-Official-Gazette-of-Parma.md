# The Official Gazette of Parma

## Overview

The Official Gazette of Parma is the state newspaper through which the Prince's regime controls public information. Every report, every announcement, every omission is a political act — the Gazette does not report the news; it manufactures it. Stendhal, who had observed the workings of Napoleonic and Restoration press controls at close quarters, treats the Gazette with the special venom he reserved for official lies. It is the novel's most sustained satire of institutional mendacity, and its reports on Fabrizio's trial are a case study in how language can be weaponised by power [005-111].

## In the Novel

The Gazette appears most prominently during Fabrizio's trial, when its accounts of the proceedings bear no relation to what actually happens in the courtroom. The Gazette reports guilt where the evidence suggests innocence; it prints confessions that were never made; it presents the Prince's political decisions as legal inevitabilities. The Duchessa, reading these reports, experiences the peculiar vertigo of seeing reality replaced by official narrative — an experience that Stendhal's readers in the age of Metternich's press censorship would have recognised immediately [023-055].

The Gazette also functions as a running joke about the reliability of public information. Characters throughout the novel refer to the Gazette with knowing irony: everyone knows it lies, but everyone reads it, because in an absolute monarchy, the official lie is itself a form of political intelligence. What the Gazette chooses to announce — and what it chooses to suppress — tells you what the regime is afraid of [006-102].

## Significance

The Gazette embodies the novel's critique of institutional language. Just as the Parma court perverts justice through procedure, and the fiscal office perverts law through interpretation, the Gazette perverts truth through official discourse. It is one of three works in the novel (alongside the manuscript of 1830 and Palla's sonnets) that thematise the relationship between writing and power [006-102].

## Key Passages

> The Prince insisted on having a newspaper that was ultra-monarchist, and the Gazette of Parma was founded in consequence [006-102].

> If you read our Gazette, you will see that a clerk at the post office has been arrested for intercepting letters [023-055].
