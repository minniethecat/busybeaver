# Assassination of the Prince

## Overview

After Fabrizio's escape, the political situation at court becomes untenable. The Prince's persecution of Fabrizio has made him the Duchessa's mortal enemy. Ferrante Palla, the republican revolutionary devoted to Gina, carries out the assassination of the Prince. The event is treated with typical Stendhalian indirection — reported rather than described — but its consequences are immense. The old Prince is succeeded by his son Ranuccio-Ernesto V, creating a new political landscape at court. The Duchessa's triumph is ambiguous: the Prince who tormented Fabrizio is dead, but the means of his death mark a moral boundary that cannot be uncrossed.

## Role in the Novel

The assassination is the Duchessa's victory and her moral limit. Throughout the novel, she has operated through charm, intelligence, and political skill. The assassination represents her crossing a line — from politics to violence. Stendhal presents this without melodrama: the Prince's death is neither celebrated nor mourned. It is simply the logical endpoint of the forces the novel has set in motion. In the economy of Stendhal's world, absolute power provokes absolute resistance, and absolute love justifies absolute action.
