# Hypocrisy

**Hypocrisy** pervades the world of Stendhal's _The Charterhouse of Parma_. From the court of Prince [[Ernesto-IV]] to the chambers of the Catholic hierarchy and the drawing rooms of the nobility, characters must constantly dissimulate — hiding their true feelings, ambitions, and loyalties behind a mask of propriety. The novel portrays hypocrisy not merely as individual moral failing but as the structural condition of life under absolutist rule.

## At the Court of Parma

The court is a theater of hypocrisy. The Prince, his ministers, and the ambitious courtiers who surround them all practice calculated deception. [[Ascanio-del-Dongo]] is accused of "hypocrisy at the very moment when our hero" acts [012-009], and the charge of hypocrisy becomes a political weapon — Fabrizio himself is later accused: critics "go as far as to accuse Fabrizio of hypocrisy" [026-021]. Power at court is maintained through a careful performance of loyalty and deference, where words rarely mean what they appear to mean.

[[Conte-Mosca]], despite being one of the novel's most honest characters, must navigate this world as a master dissimulator. His entire career as Prime Minister depends on his ability to read the hypocrisies of others while concealing his own intentions. The court's elaborate etiquette and ceremonial grandeur are the stage machinery of a society built on pretense.

## Religious Hypocrisy

The Catholic Church in Parma provides an institutional framework for hypocrisy. Fabrizio's ecclesiastical career is itself a form of dissimulation — he wears the vestments of a prelate while living a life of romantic passion. His celebrated sermons draw crowds, yet his personal life contradicts everything he preaches. The novel does not single Fabrizio out for condemnation; rather, it suggests that the Church as an institution requires this kind of performance from all who serve it.

## In Personal Relationships

Hypocrisy shapes the novel's central love stories. [[Gina-Sanseverina]] conceals the depth of her feelings for [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]] behind the role of devoted aunt, performing a social role that masks emotional reality. [[Clelia-Conti]] makes a vow to the Madonna that forces her into a life of concealment — married to one man while loving another, her entire existence becomes an exercise in dissimulation. Fabrizio himself, in his later career as a preacher, performs a piety that masks his worldly disillusionment.

## Stendhal's Critique

The novel's treatment of hypocrisy extends beyond individual character to social critique. Stendhal, writing under the Restoration, depicts a society in which Napoleonic energy and sincerity have been replaced by the cautious dissimulation required by absolutist monarchy. The Charterhouse itself — a monastery where Fabrizio ends his days — can be read as the ultimate retreat from a world built on pretense. In withdrawing from the court, Fabrizio finally escapes the necessity of hypocrisy.
