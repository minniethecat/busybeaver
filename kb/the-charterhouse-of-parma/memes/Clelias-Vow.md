# Clelia's Vow to the Madonna

## Overview

During Fabrizio's imprisonment in the Farnese Tower, Clelia Conti — the governor's daughter who has fallen in love with the prisoner — makes a solemn vow to the Madonna: if her gravely ill father recovers, she will never set eyes on Fabrizio again. The vow is made in a moment of filial anguish, but its consequences are absolute and irreversible. Stendhal presents the vow without irony: it is a genuine act of religious devotion, and it binds Clelia with a force that no human persuasion can undo. The vow transforms what might have been a conventional romance into something closer to tragedy.

## Role in the Novel

The vow is the pivot on which the novel's second half turns. Before it, the love between Fabrizio and Clelia is a prison idyll — forbidden but possible, a secret garden within the citadel's walls. After it, their love becomes an impossibility haunted by guilt. Every subsequent meeting between them is shadowed by the vow: Clelia will meet Fabrizio in darkness, communicate through alphabets, arrange the escape — but she will not look at him directly, because to look would be to break her oath (019-036).

The vow also introduces the novel's deepest theological strain. Unlike the political machinations of the court or the worldly calculations of Gina and Mosca, Clelia's vow operates in a different register — one where love, sin, and salvation are real categories, not rhetorical flourishes (017-023). This religious seriousness distinguishes *The Charterhouse of Parma* from Stendhal's earlier, more purely sceptical works.
