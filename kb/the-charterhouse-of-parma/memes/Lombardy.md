# Lombardy

## Location and Description

Lombardy is the fertile plain of northern Italy, stretching from the [[Alps]] in the north to [[Casalmaggiore|the Po River]] in the south, from [[Piedmont]] in the west to Venetia in the east. In Stendhal's time, it was the heartland of the Austrian Kingdom of Lombardy-Venetia, ruled from [[Vienna]] through a viceroy in [[Milan]]. The region's principal city, [[Milan]], was a centre of Italian cultural and political life, while its countryside — the lakes, the Alpine foothills, the rich agricultural plains — formed the landscape of the novel's opening and its recurrent pastoral interludes.

Stendhal describes the region with the precision of a traveller who knew it intimately: "the plains of Lombardy" [009-011] are fertile but also subject to the damp that afflicts Fabrizio [006-101]; the high road "which runs from Lombardy into [[Switzerland]]" [010-001] marks the boundary between Austrian rule and the freedom of the mountains.

## Role in the Novel

Lombardy is the **point of origin** for the novel's central characters and conflicts. The del Dongo family seat is at [[Grianta]] on [[Lake-Como|Lake Como]], within Austrian Lombardy; Fabrizio's formative years are spent between the family castle and [[Milan]]. The region's political subjugation to Austria shapes the loyalties of its inhabitants — the elder [[Marchese-del-Dongo|Marchese del Dongo]] is an Austrian loyalist, while his wife and sister secretly admire the French.

The arrival of the French revolutionary armies in Lombardy in 1796 is the novel's founding event. The "happiness and pleasure which burst into Lombardy in the wake of these French regiments" [001-006] transforms the political landscape, awakening liberal hopes that the subsequent Austrian restoration cannot fully extinguish. The Republicans of Lombardy, who "had given proof of a valour equal to that of the French" in the Middle Ages [001-002], are presented as heirs to a tradition of civic freedom that predates and survives Napoleonic conquest.

As the seat of Austrian power, Lombardy — and specifically [[Milan]] — houses the "terrible [[Po]]lice" [006-052] under Barone Binder, whose surveillance reaches across the border into [[Parma]]. But it is also the site of the Scala opera house, the salon of the [[Gina-Sanseverina|Contessa Pietranera]], and the intellectual circles of the Liberal party — a place where culture and politics intertwine. The prospect of a "Constitutional King of Lombardy" [016-047, 017-024] haunts the novel's political imagination, a fantasy of liberal reform that Prince Ernesto IV of [[Parma]] both fears and manipulates.

## Historical Background

The Kingdom of Lombardy-Venetia was created by the Congress of [[Vienna]] in 1815, placing [[Milan]] and [[Venice]] under direct Austrian rule. The restoration of Austrian power after two decades of French domination was deeply resented by the Italian liberal aristocracy — the class from which Stendhal drew his characters. The secret societies, police surveillance, and political conspiracies that fill the novel reflect the real climate of the 1810s and 1820s, when the Carbonari and other revolutionary groups plotted Italian unification from [[Milan]]ese salons and Lombard country houses.
