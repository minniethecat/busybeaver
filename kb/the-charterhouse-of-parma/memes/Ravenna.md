Ravenna is a city in the Papal States, situated near the Adriatic coast south of the [[Po]] Delta. In Stendhal's novel it appears in two distinct contexts: as a suggested escape route destination for Fabrizio, and as the home of Giacomo Marini, a historical figure renowned for his mnemonic techniques whom Fabrizio studies in prison.

## Location and Description

Ravenna lies on the Adriatic coastal plain of the [[Rome|Roma]]gna region, in the territory of the Papal States during the novel's period. The city was connected to [[Casalmaggiore|the Po River]] system by canals and waterways, making it reachable by boat from inland cities like [[Ferrara]]. This waterborne accessibility is what makes it viable as an escape destination — [[Gina-Sanseverina|Gina Sanseverina]] advises Fabrizio to "hire a boat and get away to Ravenna or [[Ferrara]]" [011-060], suggesting that the river routes offer a means of flight beyond the reach of road-based pursuit.

The city's distance from [[Parma]] — across the Papal States frontier — gave it value as a refuge beyond the jurisdiction of the Parmese authorities. Escape to the Papal States was a recurring motif in the political geography of the Italian states, where different legal jurisdictions offered different degrees of safety.

## Role in the Novel

Ravenna's most specific narrative role comes through its association with Giacomo Marini, described as "that marvellous master, Giacomo Marini of Ravenna" [008-043]. Marini was a real historical figure — a celebrated practitioner of the ''ars memorativa'' (art of memory), a system of mnemonic techniques that dated back to classical antiquity and was still studied in the early nineteenth century.

During his imprisonment in the [[Farnese-Tower|Farnese Tower]], Fabrizio attempts to distract and improve himself by studying Marini's system: "More than once he announced to [[Clelia-Conti|Clelia]] that he had learned an entire book by heart using Marini's methods" [008-043]. The mnemonic exercises serve a double purpose in the narrative — they are both a genuine intellectual pursuit and a way for Fabrizio to maintain contact with [[Clelia-Conti|Clelia]] through reports of his progress.

Ravenna's other reference is pragmatic rather than intellectual: as an escape route. When Gina advises Fabrizio about fleeing [[Parma]], she lists "Ravenna or [[Ferrara]]" [011-060] as destinations reachable by boat, emphasising [[Casalmaggiore|the Po River]] as an artery of clandestine movement. The pairing of Ravenna with [[Ferrara]] — both Papal States cities accessible by water — defines a geography of refuge that structures the novel's many episodes of flight and pursuit.

## Historical Background

Ravenna had been part of the Papal States since the sixteenth century and remained under papal rule until Italian unification (1860–61). The city was famous for its Byzantine mosaics and for being the burial place of Dante. Giacomo Marini (or Marino) of Ravenna was one of the last notable practitioners of the classical art of memory in Italy, and his techniques were studied throughout the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. The art of memory, which used spatial visualisation (the "memory palace" technique), had been systematised in the Renaissance and survived as a learned discipline into Stendhal's time.
