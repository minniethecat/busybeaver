## Connection to the Novel

Alessandro Farnese appears in the novel not as a character but as a historical reference. Above the entrance to the Farnese tower—the prison where Fabrizio is held—there is "a magnificent bas-relief representing Alessandro Farnese, the famous general, forcing Henri IV to withdraw from Paris" (PN 018-002). This image of martial triumph stands in ironic contrast to the tower's function as a place of confinement.

## Historical Background

Alessandro Farnese (1545–1592) was Duke of Parma, Piacenza, and Castro, and one of the most celebrated military commanders of the sixteenth century. A grandson of Emperor Charles V and great-grandson of Pope Paul III, he served as Governor of the Spanish Netherlands and is best known for his campaigns during the French Wars of Religion, including forcing Henri IV of France to abandon the siege of Paris in 1590.

## The Farnese Dynasty in the Novel

The Farnese family looms large in the architecture and history of Parma as depicted in the novel. The Torre Farnese, "constructed on the model of Hadrian's Tomb, at Rome, by the Farnese, grandsons of Paul III, in the first half of the sixteenth century" (PN 006-063), is the novel's most significant architectural symbol. The tower is "so large in diameter that on the platform in which it ends it has been possible to build a palazzo for the governor of the citadel and a new prison called the Farnese tower" (PN 006-063).

The Farnese name carries immense weight at court. The family is "surely as old as that of the Farnese" (PN 013-032), used as a benchmark of ancient lineage. The dynasty's founding is traced to "Pier-Luigi, the first sovereign of the Farnese family, so renowned for his virtues" who was "a natural son of His Holiness Pope Paul III" (PN 030-006).

A member of the family remarks: "we Farnese must always keep up a certain..." (PN 017-057), reflecting the pride and self-consciousness of the dynasty.

## Significance

The historical Alessandro Farnese represents the martial glory of the Farnese dynasty, which in the novel's present has become a force of repression embodied in the prison tower that bears the family name. The contrast between the conquering general on the bas-relief and the imprisoned Fabrizio beneath it underscores one of the novel's central themes: the transformation of power from Renaissance vitality to reactionary stagnation.
