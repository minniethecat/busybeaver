# The Citadel Garrison of Parma

## Overview

The garrison of the Parma citadel is a small military unit with an outsized role in the novel's plot. Stationed at the Farnese Tower under the command of General Fabio Conti, the garrison is responsible for guarding the state's most important political prisoners — including, for nine months, Fabrizio del Dongo. Stendhal's depiction of the garrison is characteristically deflationary: these are not hardened gaolers but bored soldiers whose discipline can be undermined by a well-timed distribution of wine. The garrison is the institution through which the novel explores the corruptibility of power at its most granular level [020-040].

## Role in the Novel

The garrison's corruption is the condition of possibility for Fabrizio's escape. The Duchessa, through intermediaries, arranges for the soldiers to receive extra wine rations on the night of the escape; the garrison, softened by drink and bribes, fails to prevent the prisoner from climbing down a rope from the Farnese Tower. The escape succeeds not because Fabrizio is a master of disguise but because institutions are permeable — because the men who enforce power are as human as the men who suffer it [022-049].

The garrison also embodies the novel's democratic irony: the most dramatic event in the plot — the escape from an inescapable prison — turns on the mundane fact that soldiers like to drink. Stendhal understood that history is made not by great men but by ordinary appetites. The garrison's drunkenness on the night of the escape is a small masterpiece of anti-heroic narration [020-043].
