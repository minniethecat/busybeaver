# General Fontana

## Role in the Novel

General Fontana serves as the Aide-de-Camp to the [[Prince-of-Parma]], acting as the trembling intermediary between the volatile monarch and the court. His role is minor in terms of plot but significant in terms of what it reveals about the nature of power at Parma: even a high-ranking military officer — a general, no less — lives in constant, abject terror of the Prince's unpredictable rages. Fontana is less a character with agency than a mirror held up to the Prince's tyranny.

Fontana appears in two distinct phases of the narrative. In the early court scenes (Chapters 14–15), he is the Prince's obedient attendant, repeatedly presenting himself at the study door only to be met with dismissive fury. Later, during Fabrizio's imprisonment and escape (Chapters 24–25), Fontana becomes an unwitting participant in the ruses that surround Fabrizio's flight from the [[Citadel-of-Parma]].

## Biography

Fontana is introduced in his official capacity at court. When [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]], newly returned from [[Naples]] as *Monsignore*, requests an audience with the Prince, it is Fontana who must navigate the impossible task of presenting this request. The Prince's treatment of his Aide-de-Camp is casually brutal:

> "The faithful Fontana presented himself again at the door of the Prince's study."
(014-008)

The word "again" tells the whole story: Fontana must repeatedly expose himself to the Prince's wrath, retreating and returning like a dog that cannot learn its lesson. The Prince's fury at being disturbed is directed not at the substance of any request but at the mere fact of being approached. Fontana's face, when he emerges, tells the cost:

> "Poor General Fontana showed a pallid face of complete terror."
(014-026)

This is the face of life under absolutism — not the face of a conspirator or a dissident, but of a loyal servant who has done nothing wrong except be present when his master was in a bad mood.

## Involvement in Fabrizio's Escape

Fontana's role in the escape plot (Chapters 24–25) is entirely unwitting. He is manipulated by the [[Duchessa-Sanseverina]] and her allies, who exploit his access to the Citadel and his predictable routines. His waistcoat is used in a ruse, and Fabrizio, during the escape, shouts "Fontana has come to save me" as a coded signal — a phrase that, if overheard, could have incriminated the innocent general:

> "Fontana has come to save me."
(025-034)

The irony is characteristic of Stendhal. Fontana, who has spent his career being terrorised by the Prince, is now exposed to danger from the opposite direction — not because he has done anything, but because he is useful to the Duchessa's plot. The powerless are always available for use.

## Character Analysis

Fontana embodies the psychological cost of the absolutist court. He is competent and dutiful, but these qualities offer no protection. In a rational system, a general who faithfully executes his duties would be valued. In Parma, he is a target. His terror before the Prince is at once comic — the "pallid face" is almost a cartoon of fear — and deeply pitiable. Stendhal does not allow us to laugh at Fontana without also recognising that his situation is, in its essence, obscene.

Fontana also serves as a structural counterpoint to [[Conte-Mosca]]. Both men serve the Prince; both are trapped in the machinery of absolutist power. But Mosca has mastered the art of navigating it — he is the political operator who can bend the system to his will, at least some of the time. Fontana, by contrast, can only endure. He is what Mosca might have become, had Mosca lacked the intelligence, the cynicism, and the love of the [[Duchessa-Sanseverina]] that give his life meaning and purpose.

## Relationships

- **[[Prince-of-Parma]]**: His master and tormentor. Fontana lives in perpetual fear of the Prince's unpredictable temper. Their relationship is not one of mutual respect between monarch and soldier but of abuser and victim, dressed in the costumes of court protocol.
- **[[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]]**: Their paths cross at court and again during the escape. Fabrizio, who has his own reasons to fear the Prince, recognises in Fontana a fellow sufferer — but Fabrizio's aristocratic connections protect him in ways Fontana's military rank cannot.
- **[[Conte-Mosca]]**: The contrast between the two men — the political master and the helpless functionary — illuminates the range of possible responses to absolutist power.
