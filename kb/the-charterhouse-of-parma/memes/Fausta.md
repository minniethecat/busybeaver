# Fausta

## Role in the Novel

Fausta is a Bolognese actress and singer who briefly becomes the object of Fabrizio's romantic obsession. Her role, though limited to a few chapters, is significant: she embodies a particular kind of worldly, capricious femininity that Stendhal contrasts with the idealised spirituality of Clelia Conti. Through Fabrizio's pursuit of Fausta, Stendhal explores the dynamics of vanity, jealousy, and the folly of romantic competition at court.

## Biography

Fausta F——, as she is coyly referred to in the novel, is introduced as "the famous Fausta" — an opera singer of established reputation in Bologna (013-020). She is described as possessing remarkable talent as a performer but also significant personal defects: "Fausta has these defects and many more" that the world overlooks because of her talent (013-021).

Fabrizio first encounters Fausta when he hears her sing and is immediately captivated by the power of her voice — "He was astonished by the divine quality of her singing" (013-022). His initial attempts to attract her attention are met with ridicule; seeking to catch Fausta's eye at the theatre, he is "greeted by peals of laughter" (013-023).

Undeterred, Fabrizio pursues her with letters and persistent attention. He attends the church where she hears mass every morning at eleven o'clock (013-028), and his campaign eventually yields results. Fausta, "madly in love that day with our hero" (013-054), becomes his mistress — but her affections are fickle, and the relationship soon becomes entangled with court politics, particularly when the Crown Prince also seeks her favour.

The affair with Fausta represents a period of worldly dissipation for Fabrizio, one that Gina Sanseverina regards with characteristic irony. Fausta's ultimate significance lies less in her own character — "Fausta was too great a fool to be able to feel true passion" (013-031) — than in what her pursuit reveals about Fabrizio's restless heart and the competitive vanity of the court.

## Relationships

- **[[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]]** — brief lover; Fausta is one of several romantic attachments that precede Fabrizio's transcendent love for Clelia Conti
- **Conte M——** — a rival for Fausta's affections whose "passion for Fausta revived with furious intensity" (013-039) upon learning of Fabrizio's involvement
- **The Crown Prince** — another rival, whose interest in Fausta elevates the affair to a matter of court politics

## Character Analysis

Fausta functions primarily as a foil to Clelia Conti. Where Clelia is virginal, pious, and capable of a pure, self-sacrificing love, Fausta is worldly, mercenary, and emotionally shallow. The contrast is deliberate: Stendhal uses Fausta to illustrate the *amour de vanité* (vanity-love) that he analysed in his treatise *De l'Amour*, preparing the reader for the *amour-passion* that Fabrizio will later experience with Clelia.

Her status as a performer — an actress and singer — is thematically significant. Fausta is always performing, her emotions a role she plays rather than feels. Fabrizio's attraction to her reflects his own youthful susceptibility to surfaces and spectacle, a weakness he must outgrow before he is capable of genuine love.

## Key Appearances

- Fabrizio first hears Fausta sing and is captivated (013-020 through 013-023)
- Daily mass attendance as ritual courtship (013-028 through 013-029)
- Fausta's growing affection and the rivalry with Conte M—— (013-039 through 013-042)
- The Crown Prince enters the competition for Fausta's favour (013-054 through 013-055)
