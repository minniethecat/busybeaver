# The Cantinière

## Role in the Novel

The Cantinière is a vivandière serving with the French army at the Battle of Waterloo. She encounters the young and inexperienced [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]] on the battlefield in Chapter 3 and becomes his first guide through the confusion of war. Her practical wisdom and motherly concern provide Fabrizio with his initial orientation amid the chaos, and her cart serves as a mobile reference point for him throughout the day.

## Biography

The Cantinière first appears early in the Waterloo sequence when Fabrizio, pale and bewildered, stumbles near her cart. She is immediately "touched by Fabrizio's pallor" [003-002] and begins to explain the basics of battle to him — advising him to eat first, to take a musket from a fallen soldier, and to stay near her cart. Throughout the day, Fabrizio repeatedly looks for her little cart [003-080], and she becomes a reassuring presence amid the horror. When he is separated from his regiment and wanders lost, she directs him with increasing authority [003-017]. After the battle turns against the French, she tells him frankly that the regiment has been scattered [003-036] and eventually she wheels her cart away to safety.

Unlike the military officers who ignore or dismiss Fabrizio, the Cantinière treats him with practical kindness. She is one of the few characters at Waterloo who speaks to Fabrizio directly and honestly about the reality of the battle, without pretense or military formality. Her role as a canteen woman — neither soldier nor civilian — gives her a unique perspective on the battlefield, and she navigates between French and enemy lines with a vivandière's pragmatic neutrality.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 3 (Waterloo)**: The Cantinière appears throughout the battle sequence, from Fabrizio's first encounter with her [003-002] through his repeated returns to her cart [003-080, 003-082]. She instructs him on basic survival, points him toward his regiment, and ultimately departs as the French position collapses.
