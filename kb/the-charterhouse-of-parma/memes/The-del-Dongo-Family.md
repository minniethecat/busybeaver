# The del Dongo Family

## Overview

The del Dongo family is the aristocratic Milanese household at the novel's centre — a family riven by the same political divisions that fracture post-Napoleonic Italy. The [[Marchesa-del-Dongo|Marchese del Dongo]] is a reactionary of the old school, loyal to Austria and hostile to everything the French Revolution represents; his wife, the Marchesa, is a liberal who maintains a salon sympathetic to Napoleonic ideals. Their younger son [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]] inherits his mother's romantic temperament and his father's stubbornness, and the tension between these inheritances drives his character. Stendhal uses the del Dongo household as a microcosm of Italian society: a family in which love and ideology are inseparable, and in which the political is always personal.

> The Marchese del Dongo, irritated by the spectacle of so many Jacobins discovering sentiments of gratitude and good sense, returned from his castle of Grianta on the Lake of Como, to which he had gathered his family for safety.
> [001-014]

## Structure and Dynamics

The del Dongo family is structured around a fundamental conflict of values. The Marchese represents the old order — autocratic, suspicious, obsessed with lineage and propriety. The Marchesa represents the new — liberal, emotional, loyal to people rather than principles. Their older son, Ascanio, follows his father into reactionary politics; their younger son, [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo|Fabrizio]], follows his mother into the world of feeling. The household is completed by the two sisters of the Marchesa: [[Gina-Sanseverina|Gina]], the brilliant Contessa Pietranera, and her quieter sister. It is Gina who becomes Fabrizio's true spiritual parent — the aunt whose passionate loyalty will shape the entire course of the novel.

## Role in the Novel

The del Dongo family provides the novel's emotional foundation. Fabrizio's childhood at [[Grianta]] on [[Lake-Como|Lake Como]], the annual visits to [[Milan]], the political arguments around the dinner table — these early scenes establish the emotional geography that will structure the entire narrative. The family is the novel's first "court" — a miniature version of the [[The-Parma-Court|Court of Parma]] that Fabrizio will later navigate, complete with its own factions, alliances, and unspoken rules. And Gina's transformation from Milanese Contessa to Duchessa of [[Parma]] — a journey that begins in the del Dongo household — is the novel's central character arc.
