# Marietta

## Role in the Novel

Marietta is a minor but plot-catalytic character in *The Charterhouse of Parma*. As a young actress in a provincial theatre company, she becomes Fabrizio's first romantic interest after his arrival in Parma. Her entanglement with the violent actor Giletti sets in motion the chain of events — the roadside brawl, Giletti's death, Fabrizio's flight — that propels the novel's middle act. Though Marietta herself appears only briefly, her presence triggers consequences that reverberate through the remainder of the narrative.

## Biography

Marietta Valserra is introduced when Fabrizio, newly arrived in Parma and bored by the formality of court life, encounters her at the theatre. He asks "what her name was, and was told: 'Marietta Valserra'" (008-002). Within three days of learning her address — a "wretched apartment" on the fourth floor of a modest building (008-004) — Fabrizio has inserted himself into her life.

Marietta is described as young and attractive but of limited means. She "liked him well enough, but was frightened of her protector" (008-005), the jealous and physically imposing actor Giletti. Her position is precarious: she is dependent on Giletti for her place in the theatre company, yet she feels genuine affection for the charming young monsignor.

The crisis erupts when Giletti, enraged by Marietta's liaison with Fabrizio, confronts the young nobleman on the road. In the ensuing fight, Fabrizio kills Giletti — an act that forces him to flee Parma and eventually leads to his imprisonment. Marietta, frightened and bereft of protection, fades from the narrative as the plot's gravity shifts to the graver consequences of the duel.

## Relationships

- **[[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]]** — brief lover; Marietta is Fabrizio's first Parma romance, a relationship of convenience and youthful indiscretion
- **[[Giletti]]** — Marietta's protector and lover before Fabrizio; his jealousy over Marietta leads directly to the fatal duel

## Character Analysis

Marietta represents the world of the theatre that Stendhal uses as a counterpoint to the rarefied atmosphere of the Parma court. Her poverty and vulnerability contrast with the wealth and power of the aristocrats who move through the novel's main plot. Unlike Fausta, who is a star performer courted by princes, Marietta is at the bottom of the theatrical hierarchy — her dependence on Giletti is economic as much as romantic.

Stendhal portrays Marietta with a mixture of sympathy and irony. She is not a deep character; her function is largely instrumental. Yet her very ordinariness — her genuine liking for Fabrizio, her fear of Giletti, her disappearance from the story when her narrative purpose is served — makes her one of the novel's more realistic minor figures. She embodies a truth the novel elsewhere romanticises: that most human entanglements are fleeting, and that the grand passions of the protagonists are the exception, not the rule.

- The affair and its discovery by Giletti lead to the confrontation on the road (Chapter VIII–IX)
