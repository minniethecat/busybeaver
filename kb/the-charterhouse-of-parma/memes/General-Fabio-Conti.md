# General Fabio Conti

## Role in the Novel

The governor of the Farnese Tower and Clelia's father — a proud, ambitious military officer whose devotion to his fortress is matched only by his blindness to the love affair unfolding within its walls.

## Biography

General Fabio Conti is the military governor of the citadel of Parma, a position he has achieved through ambition and a ferocious commitment to the appearance of competence. He is obsessed with his fortress — its walls, its lightning conductors, its reputation for impregnability — to the point of comedy. His daughter Clelia is the one person he genuinely loves, but his love takes the form of control: he plans her life without consulting her desires. When Fabrizio is imprisoned in his citadel, Conti sees only a prisoner to be guarded; he is utterly blind to the romance developing between his daughter and his captive. His disgrace after Fabrizio's escape is the catastrophe from which he never recovers.

## Character Analysis

General Conti is one of Stendhal's most successful comic portraits. His love for his fortress is a form of vanity — the Farnese Tower is his monument, his proof of importance. His blindness to Clelia's love for Fabrizio is both absurd and poignant: he loves his daughter but does not see her. Conti represents a particular type of Restoration official — competent within a narrow sphere, ambitious without imagination, and ultimately vulnerable because he cannot comprehend human motives that lie outside his own experience. Stendhal's treatment of him is characteristic: satirical but not cruel, exposing his limitations without denying his humanity.
