# Emperor

The **Emperor** — [[Napoleon-Bonaparte]] — is a defining presence in [[The-Charterhouse-of-Parma]], even though he appears only briefly in the Waterloo chapters. His shadow falls across the entire novel, shaping the political landscape of post-Napoleonic Italy and the personal destiny of the protagonist.

## The Emperor as Ideal

For the young [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]], the Emperor represents the embodiment of heroic possibility. Raised on stories of Napoleonic glory, Fabrizio sets out from [[Grianta]] to join the Emperor's forces with the passion of a true believer. His journey to [[Waterloo]] is motivated not by political calculation but by romantic devotion to the imperial cause.

> [002-029] …"I am going to join the Emperor who is also King of Italy; he was so good a friend to your uncle!"

The narrator captures Fabrizio's enthusiasm with characteristic irony, noting how he "spoke of the Emperor and of Liberty in terms of the most ardent devotion" (002-044). This youthful idealism is one of the few moments of uncomplicated emotion in the novel.

> [002-044] …our hero who spoke of the Emperor and of Liberty in terms of the most ardent devotion.

## Encounter at Waterloo

Fabrizio's actual encounter with the Emperor at [[Battle-of-Waterloo|Waterloo]] is deliberately anti-climactic. He glimpses Napoleon passing by on horseback but fails to recognize him in the chaos of battle. When told afterwards, "So that was the Emperor who went past then?", his question captures the gap between heroic imagination and mundane reality that defines his initiation into the world.

> [003-092] …"Don't you see the Emperor, curse you!"

> [003-094] "So that was the Emperor who went past then?" he asked.

## Imperial Legacy

After Napoleon's fall, the Emperor's legacy haunts the restored absolutist regimes. [[Parma]]'s Prince defines himself against the Napoleonic threat, and characters associated with the imperial cause face suspicion and persecution. Fabrizio's Bonapartist past becomes both a romantic credential and a political liability in the reactionary world of the novel's later chapters.
