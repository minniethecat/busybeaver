# Tuscany

## Location and Description

Tuscany is a region of central Italy, centred on [[Florence]], that in Stendhal's time was governed as the Grand Duchy of Tuscany under the House of Habsburg-Lorraine. In the novel, it appears only in passing references, yet each reference carries political weight: the Grand Duke of Tuscany is invoked as a point of comparison for understanding the constitutional arrangements of other Italian states.

Stendhal does not describe Tuscany's landscape or cities — [[Florence]] appears separately in the novel — but rather uses the Grand Duchy as a political benchmark. The "Grand Dukes of Tuscany" represent a specific model of enlightened absolutism distinct from the capricious despotism of the [[Prince-of-Parma]] [028-079].

## Role in the Novel

Tuscany's role is exclusively political and comparative. In a late chapter, the narrator compares the governance of one Italian state to "that of the Grand Dukes of Tuscany," using the Grand Duchy as a known quantity against which other regimes can be measured [028-079]. This passing reference assumes the reader's familiarity with Tuscan governance as a model of relatively benign, bureaucratic absolutism — the opposite of the personal, passion-driven rule of [[Parma]].

The novel's postscript notes that the Grand Duke of Tuscany was the "legal heir" to certain territories, a detail that grounds the fictional political arrangements of the novel in the actual dynastic politics of Restoration Italy [029-007]. This is typical of Stendhal's method: the real political geography of Italy provides a framework within which his fictional [[Parma]] can operate as a plausible anomaly.

> The government of this little state was comparable to that of the Grand Dukes of Tuscany.
> [028-079]

## Associated Characters

- [[Prince-of-Parma]] — the novel's absolutist ruler, implicitly contrasted with the more bureaucratic Grand Dukes of Tuscany
- [[Conte-Mosca]] — the [[Conte-Mosca|Prime Minister of Parma]], whose diplomatic knowledge encompasses the political models of all Italian states

## Historical Background

The Grand Duchy of Tuscany was, after 1815, ruled by Ferdinand III of Habsburg-Lorraine, who had been restored to his throne by the Congress of [[Vienna]]. The Tuscan model of governance was generally considered more liberal and efficient than that of the smaller Italian duchies — a reputation Stendhal draws on when he uses Tuscany as a comparative benchmark. The Grand Duchy survived until 1859, when it was annexed to the Kingdom of Sardinia during the Risorgimento.

Stendhal's novel, set in the 1820s, takes place during the period when Tuscany was, along with [[Lombardy]]-Venetia, one of the better-administered states of Restoration Italy. The contrast with [[Parma]] — "only a small town" governed by a Prince's personal whims — is deliberate and pointed.
