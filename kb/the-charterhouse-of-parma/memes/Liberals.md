# Liberals

The **Liberals** in [[The-Charterhouse-of-Parma]] represent the political opposition to the absolutist regime of Prince [[Ernesto-IV]]. They are the objects of constant suspicion, surveillance, and persecution, embodying the ideological conflicts that simmer beneath the novel's court intrigues.

## The Liberal Threat

The Prince's court defines itself in opposition to Liberalism. Conte [[Mosca]] explains to [[Gina-Sanseverina]] that the Prince's severities target the "Liberals of Milan," and the regime's legitimacy rests in part on its suppression of liberal ideas. The narrator presents this with characteristic irony, noting the "senseless fashion" in which the Prince rails "against Liberals and liberty."

> [006-014] …most senseless fashion against Liberals and liberty.

> [006-004] …for his severities, which the Liberals of Milan called cruelties.

## Persecution and Imprisonment

The [[Citadel-of-Parma]], governed by General [[Fabio-Conti]], serves as the prison for Liberals condemned by [[Rassi]]'s judicial machinery. The novel makes clear that being labeled a Liberal is sufficient grounds for imprisonment, regardless of actual political activity. The threat of being denounced as a Liberal hangs over characters who fall from favor at court.

> [006-101] …in which were imprisoned the Liberals condemned by Rassi.

> [006-052] …one fine day Ernesto IV had two Liberals hanged.

## Ideological Complexity

Stendhal complicates any simple binary between absolutism and liberalism. The narrator notes that even "the Liberals themselves paid a tribute" to certain court figures (006-030), and questions what Liberals mean by "virtue" (016-023). [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]], though associated with Napoleonic ideals through his youthful enthusiasm for the Emperor, navigates a world where political labels are both dangerous and ambiguous.

> [006-030] …that people said of him; the Liberals themselves paid a tribute to his merit.

> [016-023] …may indeed add that what the Liberals understand by "virtue" (seeking the happiness of the greatest number) is not precisely the virtue of a court.
