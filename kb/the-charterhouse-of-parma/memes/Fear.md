# Fear

## Definition

Fear in *The Charterhouse of Parma* is the shadow side of passion — a force as powerful as love but operating in the opposite direction. Stendhal portrays a world governed by fear: the Prince fears humiliation; his ministers fear disgrace; prisoners fear poison; lovers fear rejection. Fear is the principal tool of political control in the absolutist state, and the novel shows how it shapes behaviour at every level of society, from the contadini who fear the magician-priest Blanès to the Duchessa who must calculate every move against the Prince's unpredictable terrors.

## Historical Context

The Restoration courts of Italy were police states in miniature. Arbitrary arrest, political imprisonment, and espionage were routine. The novel's Prince of Parma rules through fear — the Farnese Tower, "visible from all parts of the country," is a monument to intimidation. Stendhal himself lived through the post-Napoleonic reaction and understood how fear operated as a political instrument. The "fever of hope or fear" (006-010) that grips Milan after Waterloo reflects the existential uncertainty of the period.

## Role in the Novel

Fear is omnipresent. The Prince is governed by fear — of appearing weak, of Fabrizio's escape, of the Duchessa's departure. Rassi fears losing his position. Clelia fears both her father's wrath and her own feelings for Fabrizio: "it is precisely the fear of being sent away from the citadel and shut up in a convent that is governing all my conduct!" (019-007). Fabrizio, remarkably, is the character least governed by fear — his courage and naivety are what set him apart. Yet even he is not immune: when Clelia fails to appear at her window, he is plunged into an agony of fear (018-038).

Stendhal makes an important psychological observation: "the fear was a hundred times worse than the evil" (018-017). This insight — that imagined suffering exceeds actual suffering — runs through the novel. Fabrizio, once imprisoned, discovers that prison life is not what he feared; it is, in fact, the setting of his greatest happiness. Fear, Stendhal suggests, is a tyrant greater than any prince.

## Key Passages

> The question for Italy at that time was to be or not to be; everyone at Milan was in a fever, a fever of hope or fear. (006-010)

> It is precisely the fear of being sent away from the citadel and shut up in a convent that is governing all my conduct! (019-007)

> I who was so much afraid of prison, I am in prison, and I do not even remember to be sad! It is certainly a case where the fear was a hundred times worse than the evil. (018-017)
