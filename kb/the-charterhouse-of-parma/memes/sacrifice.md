# Sacrifice

## Definition

Sacrifice in *The Charterhouse of Parma* is the act by which characters reveal their deepest loyalties. Stendhal, the ironist, treats sacrifice with unusual seriousness — it is one of the few human behaviours that his narrative voice rarely undermines. The novel's most powerful moments are acts of sacrifice: Gina risking everything for Fabrizio's safety, Clelia breaking her vow for love, Conte Mosca surrendering his position for Gina's sake. Sacrifice is the moral counterweight to the novel's pervasive themes of ambition, vanity, and self-interest.

## Historical Context

In the post-Revolutionary world, the idea of sacrifice carried complex political meanings. The Revolution had celebrated sacrifice for the nation; the Restoration had attempted to reclaim sacrifice for throne and altar. Stendhal, disillusioned with both, locates authentic sacrifice in the personal realm — in love, loyalty, and friendship — rather than in political or religious causes. This privatisation of sacrifice reflects a broader post-Napoleonic turn away from public heroism toward private emotion.

The novel's opening chapters establish this pattern immediately: the Marchesa del Dongo's family connections obliged her to ally with families "who were kind enough to sacrifice themselves to it" [002-008] — even minor sacrifices of social convenience are registered in Stendhal's ironic voice. The language of sacrifice pervades the novel, from the grandest gestures to the smallest renunciations.

## Role in the Novel

Sacrifice structures several of the novel's central relationships. Gina's entire life after Waterloo becomes a series of sacrifices for Fabrizio — she marries the Duke of Sanseverina to gain political influence to protect him, she risks her position at court, and ultimately she threatens to destroy herself for his sake. Conte Mosca, responding to one of Gina's demands, sighs deeply: "it is a cruel sacrifice! I admit, I had not taken into account this particular misfortune" [006-089] — the weariness in his voice makes the sacrifice more, not less, convincing.

Clelia's sacrifice is of a different order: she breaks her vow to the Madonna to save Fabrizio's life, and this violation becomes the source of her ongoing inner torment. "The sacrifice has been made. Do not ask me to speak of it again" [027-023] — her words capture the finality and privacy of true sacrifice. Conte Mosca's sacrifices are quieter — he repeatedly subordinates his own political interests to Gina's wishes. Even the Prince, in his way, sacrifices domestic peace for the demands of power. The novel suggests that the capacity for sacrifice is the truest measure of a character's moral worth.

At the political level, sacrifice takes on a different valence. "To resign is the greatest sacrifice a Prime Minister can make" [016-018] — Mosca's reflection frames renunciation of power as a form of sacrifice that reveals more about a person than the acquisition of power ever could.

## Key Passages

> Gina's final threat to the Prince — to sacrifice her own life for Fabrizio's freedom — represents the novel's supreme moment of personal sacrifice. [027-010]

> The Marchesa dissolved in tears on learning her son's strange plan; she could not feel its heroism. [002-040]

> "It is a cruel sacrifice! I admit, I had not taken into account this particular misfortune." [006-089]

> "The sacrifice has been made. Do not ask me to speak of it again." [027-023]

> To resign is the greatest sacrifice a Prime Minister can make; and what follows from this truth is that the cleverest ministers are those who never resign. [016-018]

> Families who were kind enough to sacrifice themselves to it found the Marchesa's drawing-room always open. [002-008]
