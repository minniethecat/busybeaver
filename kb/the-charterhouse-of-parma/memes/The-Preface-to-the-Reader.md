# The Preface "To the Reader"

## Overview

The Preface to The Charterhouse of Parma is one of literature's most elegant exercises in authorial misdirection. Published with the novel in 1839, it announces itself as the work of an editor who has received a manuscript from his uncle, Canon Borda of Padua. The Preface explains the provenance of the story, warns the reader that it contains "nothing political," and disclaims any responsibility for its contents — all while winking broadly at the reader who knows that every word of it is a fabrication. Stendhal's Preface is a masterclass in the art of the ironic paratext [P01-001].

## In the Novel

The Preface functions as the novel's tonal tuning fork. Its tone — amused, detached, faintly superior — establishes the narrative persona that will guide the reader through the story. The Preface tells us, in effect, not to take the story too seriously, not to look for political allegory, not to confuse fiction with fact. And then the novel proceeds to be deeply political, emotionally devastating, and (in its final chapters) genuinely serious. The Preface is the mask that allows Stendhal to speak the truth [P01-005].

The Preface also establishes the novel's relationship with the reader. Stendhal's narrator is not a friend or a guide; he is a curator, presenting a document for our inspection and inviting us to draw our own conclusions. This stance of editorial neutrality is, of course, a pose — but it is a productive pose, one that frees the narrator to describe his characters' most intimate thoughts without sentimentality [P01-006].

## Significance

The Preface is a work of literary criticism embedded in the work it criticises. Stendhal uses it to anticipate objections, to mock potential critics, to define the terms on which the novel should be read — all while pretending not to be the author. It is a virtuoso performance in the management of reader expectations, and it rewards careful rereading as much as any chapter of the novel proper [P01-006].

## Key Passages

> I have reproduced this narrative without any alteration from the manuscript of 1830, a course which may have its drawbacks, but which has at least the merit of fidelity. The author of these pages is no friend of mine [P01-006].
