# Courage

## Definition

Courage in *The Charterhouse of Parma* is not a single virtue but a spectrum of behaviours that Stendhal examines with his characteristic psychological precision. There is the physical courage of the soldier at Waterloo, the moral courage of those who resist the Prince's tyranny, and — most distinctively — the "courage of the fool" that Stendhal identifies with resignation and passive endurance. The novel's treatment of courage is typically ironic: actions that appear courageous may be motivated by vanity or ignorance, while genuine courage often goes unrecognised.

## Historical Context

The Napoleonic era had transformed European conceptions of courage. Napoleon's meritocratic army rewarded personal bravery regardless of birth, creating a new model of heroic individualism that the Restoration sought to suppress. Fabrizio, born into the uneasy peace after Napoleon's fall, inherits this Napoleonic ideal of courage but must practise it in a world that no longer values it. His flight to Waterloo represents an attempt to find a theatre for courage in an age that has closed the stage.

In the Lombard Republics of the Middle Ages, the Italian city-states had demonstrated a martial valour equal to that of the French — a historical memory that Stendhal invokes to establish the novel's Italian setting as a landscape of latent heroism awaiting reawakening. The Restoration's suppression of this tradition is part of the novel's political critique: courage is not dead, merely exiled from public life into the private sphere.

## Role in the Novel

Courage shapes the novel's key moments. Fabrizio's decision to join Napoleon at Waterloo is an act of youthful courage — impulsive, romantic, and politically dangerous for his family. Gina displays a different kind of courage in her defiance of the Prince: a willingness to risk everything for those she loves. Clelia's courage is quieter still — the courage to love Fabrizio despite her father's authority and her vow to the Madonna.

Stendhal is equally interested in false or compromised courage. The "absurd form of courage which is called resignation, the courage of a fool who allows himself to be hanged without saying a word" [002-012] is his most pointed critique — a courage that serves tyranny rather than resisting it. Throughout the novel, characters must distinguish between genuine courage and its imitations.

The Conte Mosca embodies a fourth kind of courage: the courage required to act decisively in politics. "Finally he had the courage to listen to the voice of reason" [008-006] — Stendhal's phrasing is characteristic, treating even rational calculation as an act requiring courage in a world where passion and vanity constantly threaten to overwhelm judgment.

## Key Passages

> That absurd form of courage which is called resignation, the courage of a fool who allows himself to be hanged without saying a word. [002-012]

> In the Middle Ages the Republicans of Lombardy had given proof of a valour equal to that of the French. [001-002]

> The Marchesa dissolved in tears on learning her son's strange plan; she could not feel its heroism. [002-040]

> "Ernesto IV is courageous only in war. On the field of battle he is a different man." [006-014]

> Finally he had the courage to listen to the voice of reason, and to confess to her the question that haunted him. [008-006]

> The Conte had a sense of his own valour, he was full of generous impulses, but he lacked the courage to remain steady in his resolves. [002-011]
