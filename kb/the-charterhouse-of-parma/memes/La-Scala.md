La Scala (Teatro alla Scala) is the famous opera house in [[Milan]], one of the most prestigious theatres in Europe. In *The Charterhouse of Parma*, it appears as a marker of social status and cultural sophistication within the Lombard aristocratic world. [[Conte-Mosca|Conte Mosca]], before his permanent move to [[Parma]], maintained "his box in the theatre alla Scala and his villa in the country" [002-011] — the two indispensable possessions of a Milanese gentleman.

## Location and Description

La Scala is situated in the centre of [[Milan]], in what was then the capital of Austrian-ruled [[Lombardy]]. Built in 1778, the theatre was, by the time of the novel's setting, already established as one of Europe's leading opera houses. Its neoclassical façade and celebrated interior — with tiers of boxes rising above the stalls — made it the social epicentre of [[Milan|Milanese]] life.

Stendhal himself was a devoted opera-goer and knew La Scala well from his years in [[Milan]]. His evocation of the theatre in the novel, though brief, carries the weight of personal familiarity.

## Role in the Novel

La Scala appears as a symbol of the world [[Conte-Mosca|Conte Mosca]] leaves behind when he commits himself to the [[Parma|Parmese]] court. His box at La Scala represents the cultivated, cosmopolitan life of [[Milan]] — a city with a vibrant public sphere, artistic freedom, and social pleasures that [[Parma]], with its petty absolutism and suffocating court intrigue, cannot match.

The theatre also features indirectly in the novel's cultural texture. A "ballet by Viganò, given at the Scala" is mentioned [002-026], situating the Milanese theatre within the broader European artistic culture that the novel's characters inhabit. [[Gina-Sanseverina|Gina Sanseverina]], who spent her early years in [[Milan]] society, would have been familiar with La Scala's world.

## Historical Background

Teatro alla Scala was inaugurated in 1778 and quickly became the spiritual home of Italian opera. Under [[Austria|Austrian]] rule (which lasted until 1859), [[Milan]] was the capital of the Kingdom of Lombardy–Venetia, and La Scala flourished as a centre of musical and social life. The early nineteenth century — the period of the novel — saw the theatre host premieres by Rossini, Donizetti, and Bellini. Stendhal's reference to Salvatore Viganò (1769–1821), the celebrated choreographer who created ballets for La Scala, anchors the novel's cultural references in historical reality. The theatre survived bombing in World War II and was meticulously restored, remaining today one of the world's most iconic opera houses.
