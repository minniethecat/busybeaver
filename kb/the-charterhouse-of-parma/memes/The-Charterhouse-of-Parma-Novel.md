# The Charterhouse of Parma

## Overview

*The Charterhouse of Parma* (*La Chartreuse de Parme*) was published in 1839, composed by Stendhal (Marie-Henri Beyle) in an astonishing burst of creative energy — dictated over fifty-two days between November and December 1838. The novel traces the life of Fabrizio del Dongo from his youthful enthusiasm for Napoleon to his final retreat into the Carthusian monastery that gives the book its title. Along the way, it encompasses the Battle of Waterloo (in one of literature's most famous anti-heroic set-pieces), a political thriller set in the miniature absolutist court of Parma, a prison love story of almost unbearable intensity, and a spiritual biography that ends in silence. It is, by common consent, one of the greatest novels ever written. Honoré de Balzac, reviewing it on publication, declared it "the masterpiece of modern French literature" [028-077].

## Significance

The novel's significance as a literary work is immeasurable. It influenced Tolstoy (who acknowledged its impact on *War and Peace*), anticipated the psychological depth of Proust, and exemplified a style — rapid, ironic, emotionally precise — that remains startlingly modern. Its political analysis of Restoration Europe is as sharp as any historian's; its psychology of love and power is as subtle as any philosopher's. But its greatest achievement is tonal: a narrative voice that is at once amused and passionate, detached and involved — the voice of a man who has loved the world enough to see through it [028-077].
