## Overview

Grillo is the principal gaoler at the Farnese Tower citadel where Fabrizio del Dongo is imprisoned. Appearing across Chapters 18 through 20, he undergoes a quiet transformation from dutiful jailer to sympathetic ally. His relationship with Fabrizio is one of the novel's small, human-scale dramas — a gaoler moved by his prisoner's plight in a way that softens the institutional harshness of the citadel.

## The Gaoler's Duty

Grillo's introduction establishes him as a man initially defined by his role. When Fabrizio explains the circumstances of his imprisonment — "Very well, my dear Grillo, a certain Giletti tried to murder me on the broad highway, I defended myself" [018-011] — Grillo listens with an emotional responsiveness unusual for a prison guard. He is described as having "tears running down his cheeks" [018-007] at one moment and being "no less amused" [018-007] at another, suggesting a temperament not yet hardened by the grim routines of the citadel.

## Growing Sympathy

Over time, Grillo's professional distance erodes. He "came and spent long hours" [018-043] in Fabrizio's cell, and Fabrizio — recognizing the gaoler's emerging warmth — offers to "give everything I possess to Grillo, so that he may be kind enough" [018-035] to facilitate small comforts. By Chapter 19, Grillo has been "won over" [019-040] and begins bringing Fabrizio paper and books, acts that go well beyond the requirements of his office.

Grillo is accompanied by his dog, Fox, described simply as "the English dog" [018-014]. The dog becomes part of the small domestic triangle within the prison cell — Fabrizio observes the routines of "the gaoler Grillo and his dog" [019-014] as one of the few predictable rhythms of his confinement.

## Significance

Grillo embodies the human element within an institutional system designed to deny it. His transition from jailer to ally — accomplished not through bribery or threat but through the gradual force of personal sympathy — mirrors the novel's broader argument that human connection can soften even the most rigid structures of power. He is a minor figure, but his presence makes the Farnese Tower sequences more emotionally textured, providing Fabrizio — and the reader — with moments of unexpected warmth.
