# The Satirical Sonnets of Parma

## Overview

The satirical sonnets printed on handkerchiefs are the novel's most vivid image of pre-modern media — the Restoration equivalent of a viral tweet. Composed anonymously, printed on rose-coloured fabric, passed from hand to hand at balls and in theatre boxes at [[The-Theatre-alla-Scala|La Scala]], these sonnets are weapons of social warfare disguised as entertainment. Stendhal, who had witnessed the power of satirical verse in Restoration Italy (where a well-turned couplet could destroy a reputation more effectively than a legal indictment), treats the sonnets with the respect due to a genuine political force. In a society without a free press, a poem could function as an opposition newspaper — and the authorities, however absolute, could not arrest a couplet.

> In Milan, the great occupation was the printing of sonnets upon handkerchiefs of rose-coloured silk. A well-turned sonnet could circulate through every salon in the city before the police had finished their breakfast.
> [001-002]

## In the Novel

The satirical sonnets appear early in the novel — in the opening pages, set in the revolutionary atmosphere of 1796 [[Milan]] — as markers of a society in transformation. Their circulation is a sign that the old order is crumbling: the aristocrats who once governed by decree now tremble at the prospect of being mocked in verse. The sonnets represent a form of popular justice that the authorities cannot control, a precursor to the modern public opinion that will eventually sweep away the restored monarchies.

Throughout the novel, the sonnet motif recurs as a touchstone of the relationship between art and [[Power]]. The handkerchief sonnets of Milan, [[Ferrante-Palla|Ferrante Palla]]'s prison poetry, and Fabrizio's sermons form a triptych: each represents a different mode in which aesthetic force confronts political authority — and, with striking consistency, shows art winning.

## Key Themes

### Poetry as Journalism

In Restoration Italy, censorship made political journalism impossible — but it could not silence poetry. The sonnets function as the court's unofficial opposition press: anonymous, untraceable, and devastatingly effective. Stendhal's insight is that political speech is most powerful when it is also beautiful — wit disarms where argument provokes resistance.

### The Power of the Anonymous

The sonnets' anonymity is crucial to their power. Without an author to prosecute, the authorities are helpless; the poem belongs to everyone and no one. This anonymity also democratises the sonnets: anyone might have written them, and everyone feels entitled to share them. In a court society built on hierarchy and attribution, the anonymous sonnet is a radical solvent.
