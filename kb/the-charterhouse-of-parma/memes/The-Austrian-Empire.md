# The Austrian Empire

## Overview

The Austrian Empire under the Habsburgs was the dominant power in Restoration-era northern Italy. After the Congress of Vienna (1815), Austria controlled Lombardy and Venetia directly and exercised hegemonic influence over the smaller Italian duchies, including Parma. For Stendhal — a Bonapartist veteran who preferred the energy of Napoleon's meritocracy to the sclerotic ritual of the old monarchies — Austria represented everything stifling about the Restoration: police surveillance, aristocratic privilege, and the suppression of national feeling. In *The Charterhouse of Parma*, the Austrian presence is felt everywhere, even when it is not seen [001-025].

## Role in the Novel

Austria is the novel's great off-stage antagonist. Its troops reenter Milan after Napoleon's fall, its police network extends into Parma, its political assumptions — that birth trumps merit, that order trumps liberty — define the world against which Fabrizio and Gina define themselves. The Marchese del Dongo, Fabrizio's father, is an Austrian sympathiser who wears a powdered wig and believes in the divine right of princes; his son, by contrast, worships Napoleon and crosses into France to fight at Waterloo — a family drama that is also a political allegory [002-008].

The Austrian police apparatus is a particular source of narrative tension. Fabrizio's flight from Parma, his exile to Romagnano, and his eventual arrest all unfold in the shadow of Austrian surveillance. The empire is a network of informants, passport controls, and political prisons — a system of control that Fabrizio, with his romantic spontaneity, instinctively violates [005-022].
