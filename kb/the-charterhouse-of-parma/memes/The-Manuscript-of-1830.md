# The Manuscript of 1830

## Overview

The Charterhouse of Parma opens with one of literature's most elegant frame narratives: the narrator claims to have received the story from a Canon Borda in Padua, who in turn transcribed it from a manuscript written by an unnamed Italian chronicler in 1830. This manuscript — which the narrator presents as an authentic historical document — is the novel's founding fiction. Stendhal never seriously expected readers to believe in its authenticity; the frame is an ironic device, a way of claiming veracity while winking at the reader who knows it is a novel. The manuscript of 1830 is a work that exists only as a literary conceit — and yet it is the work that contains all the other works in the novel [P01-005].

## In the Novel

The manuscript appears in the Preface ("To the Reader"), where the narrator explains its provenance: "Let me give you my uncle's journal, which, under the heading 'Parma,' contains nothing that could compromise anyone." This gesture — the found document, the disclaiming editor, the claim of documentary fidelity — places The Charterhouse of Parma in a tradition stretching from Cervantes to Manzoni. But Stendhal's use of the device is characteristically subversive: where a conventional historical novelist would use the frame to assert truth, Stendhal uses it to undermine the very distinction between truth and fiction [P01-005].

## Significance

The manuscript device is essential to the novel's peculiar tone. It allows Stendhal to narrate with the detachment of an editor presenting someone else's work, even as he indulges in the most intimate psychological analysis of his characters. The frame creates a distance between narrator and story that enables both irony and tenderness — the narrator can mock his characters' romantic postures while also loving them. This tonal duality — at once detached and passionate — is the signature of Stendhal's mature style, and it depends on the manuscript fiction to function [P01-006].

## Key Passages

> Let me give you my uncle's journal, which, under the heading "Parma," contains the most exact account of the events of that little court [P01-005].

> I have reproduced this narrative without any alteration from the manuscript of 1830, a course which may have its drawbacks, but which has at least the merit of fidelity [P01-006].
