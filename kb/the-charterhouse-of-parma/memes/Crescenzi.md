# Marchese Crescenzi

## Role in the Novel

The Marchese Crescenzi is the wealthiest nobleman at the court of Parma and serves as a foil to [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]] in the novel's central romantic plot. His engagement to [[Clelia-Conti]] creates the principal obstacle to Fabrizio's love for her, and his immense fortune places him at the apex of Parma's social hierarchy.

## Biography

Crescenzi is introduced as one of the most eligible bachelors at the Prince of Parma's court, possessing a fortune far exceeding that of any other courtier. As the novel progresses, he becomes engaged to Clelia Conti, the daughter of [[General-Fabio-Conti]]. The engagement is arranged with the approval of Clelia's father and the court establishment.

> "Clelia Conti to the Marchese Crescenzi, the richest man at court." (018-051)

Fabrizio views the Marchese with intense jealousy, and the very mention of Crescenzi's name is enough to provoke his fury. Clelia's marriage to Crescenzi is driven by a promise she made to the Madonna during Fabrizio's imprisonment.

> "But the name of the Marchese Crescenzi revived all his fury." (020-033)

The marriage of Crescenzi's sister is an important social event at court, attended by all of Parma's nobility.

## Wealth and Social Standing

Crescenzi's defining characteristic is his immense wealth. His fortune is described as far surpassing that of other courtiers, making him the most desirable match in Parma. This wealth, combined with his noble title, gives him significant influence at court. His financial position is contrasted with Fabrizio's relative poverty and dependence on ecclesiastical appointments.

## Relationships

- **[[Clelia-Conti]]**: His betrothed, whom he marries after she fulfills her vow to the Madonna
- **[[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]]**: His rival for Clelia's affections
- **[[General-Fabio-Conti]]**: His future father-in-law, who approves of the match
- **[[Conte-Mosca]]**: A political adversary through court factional dynamics
