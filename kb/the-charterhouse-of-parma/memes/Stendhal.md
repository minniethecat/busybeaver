# Stendhal

## Role in the Novel

Stendhal (Marie-Henri Beyle) is the author of *La Chartreuse de Parme*, the 1839 novel from which
this wiki's entire knowledge base is drawn. While he does not appear as a character in the narrative,
his voice, historical perspective, and personal experiences — as a Napoleonic veteran, a visitor to
Italy, and a French consul — suffuse every chapter. The novel was dictated in an extraordinary burst
of creativity over 52 days (November 4 – December 26, 1838) in Paris.

## Biography

Born in Grenoble in 1783, Henri Beyle served in Napoleon's army, participating in the Italian
campaign of 1800 and the Russian campaign of 1812. After Napoleon's fall, he settled in Milan
(1814–1821), where he began writing under the name "Stendhal." His love for Italy — its art,
music, politics, and passionate social life — shaped the world of the Charterhouse. He served
as French consul in Civitavecchia (1830–1842) and died in Paris in 1842.

The novel draws deeply on Stendhal's own experiences: the Battle of Waterloo (Chapters 2–4)
reflects his Napoleonic past; the court intrigues of Parma echo the petty tyrannies he observed
in Italian princely states; and Fabrizio's romantic idealism mirrors the author's own youthful
enthusiasms.

## The Writing of the Novel

The manuscript of the *Chartreuse* had a dramatic passage through the press. Romain Colomb,
Beyle's executor, recounted an episode that reveals the author's improvisational method:

> Unable to find it among the mass of papers that littered his room, Beyle rewrote the sixty
> pages, and the new version was already in type when he told Colomb of his loss. Colomb at
> once searched for and found the missing cahier, whereupon Beyle, "stupefied by the ease of
> my discovery, dreading, in a sense, the sight of this manuscript, would not even glance over
> it, much less compare it with the pages that had taken its place." (029-003)

After the first edition appeared in April 1839, Beyle undertook a thorough revision:

> In the same year, Beyle began to correct, reduce and amplify the whole work, before he was
> moved by Balzac's criticism to condense the first fifty-four pages into four or five. Three
> copies thus annotated are in existence, one of which has been reproduced in facsimile.
> (029-004)

## Historical Context

*La Chartreuse de Parme* was published in the twilight of the July Monarchy, when memories of
Napoleon still shaped French politics. Stendhal's manuscript notes and correspondence reveal
that the novel was partly inspired by the *Farnese* family chronicles and by Alessandro Farnese
(1520–1589), the historical Cardinal who became Pope Paul III's grandson. The character of
[[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]] is loosely based on this Renaissance figure, transposed into the
post-Napoleonic world that Stendhal knew intimately.

The choice of Parma as the novel's setting was a calculated artistic decision rather than a
documentary one:

> It is probable that Beyle was led to place the scene of his story at Parma, which, in *Rome,
> Naples et Florence*, he had dismissed, not unjustly, as *ville d'ailleurs assez plate*,
> precisely because there was not, in 1838, any reigning dynasty in that State. (029-007)

The irony is that history soon caught up with fiction:

> The Bourbon dynasty, which had been transplanted to the brief Kingdom of Etruria, and in 1814
> had been placated with the Republic of Lucca as a temporary Duchy, returned, and rapidly
> converted Stendhal's fiction into historical fact. (029-007)
