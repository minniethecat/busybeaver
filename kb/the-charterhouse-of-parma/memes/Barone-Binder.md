## Overview

Barone Binder is the Chief of Police of Parma who becomes involved in the case of Fabrizio del Dongo when the Marchesa del Dongo appeals to him on behalf of her son. He is portrayed as a competent and fundamentally decent official navigating the treacherous political currents of the Parma court.

## Character

Binder is introduced as a man of measured judgment. He "takes everything seriously" and replies "gently" to the Marchesa's entreaties [005-079]. He is described as "a wise and solid man" [005-079] who, despite his position, is "well-disposed as they were" [005-107]. His ambition is noted — the narrator alludes to "whatever Barone Binder's ambition may be" [005-088] — but this ambition does not override his professional judgment. He is one of the few court officials in the novel who is not depicted as corrupt or cruel.

## Role in the Novel

Binder's most significant involvement is in the early chapters, where the Marchesa del Dongo repeatedly appeals to him regarding Fabrizio's passport and the threat of arrest [005-078, 005-090]. He receives these appeals with patience, though his replies cannot always satisfy the anxious mother [005-082]. He later features as a figure whom Conte Mosca must consider in his political calculations [006-034].
