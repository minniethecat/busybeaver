# Clelia's Marriage to the Marchese Crescenzi

## Overview

After her father's recovery and her vow to the Madonna, Clelia Conti marries the Marchese Crescenzi, a wealthy and respectable nobleman of Parma. The marriage is arranged by her father, the governor of the citadel, and is socially impeccable — the kind of match that the Parma aristocracy considers a triumph of good sense over romantic folly. For Clelia, it is a living death: every day of her marriage is a day she must not look at the man she loves. Stendhal reports the marriage with the same dry precision he brings to the trial or the arrest, and the effect is devastating — the machinery of social obligation grinding down a human heart [026-017].

## Role in the Novel

The marriage is the novel's most intimate event — a private ceremony with public consequences. Unlike the Battle of Waterloo or the escape from the Farnese Tower, it has no dramatic set-piece, no suspenseful sequence. It happens in a single sentence, almost an aside, and the reader must supply the emotional weight. This is Stendhal's genius: the most consequential events in his characters' lives are narrated with the fewest words, as if to say — this is how the world works. Love is defeated not by villains but by weddings [026-019].

The marriage also completes the pattern of Clelia's tragedy. The vow she made in the tower — never to see Fabrizio — was a private act of conscience; the marriage makes that private act a public, legal, and social reality. There is no escape now, no alphabets in the dark, no rope from the tower. The marriage is the event that transforms Clelia's vow from a struggle into a sentence [019-036].
