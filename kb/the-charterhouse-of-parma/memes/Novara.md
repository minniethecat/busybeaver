Novara is a city in the [[Piedmont]] region of north-western Italy, situated west of [[Milan]]. In the novel it serves as [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo|Fabrizio del Dongo]]'s place of exile and re-education after his flight from [[Parma]]. The surrounding countryside, particularly the village of [[Romagnano]] near Novara, is where Fabrizio is confined under the supervision of the Austrian authorities and his family's influence.

## Location and Description

Novara lies in the [[Piedmont]]ese plain between [[Milan]] and [[Turin]]. In the early nineteenth century (the period of the novel), it was under the Kingdom of Sardinia ([[Piedmont]]-Sardinia). The city is described as sufficiently distant from [[Parma]] and [[Milan]] to serve as a place of exile — far enough to remove Fabrizio from the centres of political intrigue, but close enough for news and influence to travel.

The countryside around Novara, particularly the village of [[Romagnano]] (described as "near Novara, to be precise, at [[Romagnano]]" [005-089]), is where the del Dongo family owns property. This rural estate becomes Fabrizio's gilded prison. The "ramshackle country house outside Novara" [013-017] is where Fabrizio passes periods of his confinement, going to confession, hunting, and living under the watchful eye of his family's agents.

## Role in the Novel

Novara first appears when Fabrizio's aunt, [[Gina-Sanseverina|Gina Sanseverina]], arranges for him to be sent there after his first scandal in [[Parma]]. The del Dongo family, particularly his father [[Marchese-del-Dongo|the Marchese]], wishes to "pack him off to Novara" [008-027] to remove him from circulation and re-establish control over his life. The ostensible purpose is to have Fabrizio "begin his education over again at Novara" — studying theology under the supervision of a priest selected by his family [006-098].

Fabrizio himself resents this imposed exile, preferring to be at [[Romagnano]] "going to confession, hunting" rather than "eating his head off at Novara with my mistress authorised by the police" [007-002]. The reference to a "mistress authorised by the police" underscores the controlled, surveilled nature of Fabrizio's existence in Novara — even his personal relationships are subject to oversight.

The Signora C——, mentioned in connection with Novara [006-095], appears to be a figure of some importance in the city. When Fabrizio's departure is arranged, there is concern about him taking leave of her, suggesting she represents a connection that the authorities wish to sever.

Later in the novel, Novara recurs as a point of reference — a place of exile that Fabrizio has escaped from, and a comparison point for other cities where chance encounters occur: "which chance has brought me at Novara or at [[Naples]]" [013-016]. The pairing of Novara with [[Naples]] — one a provincial [[Piedmont]]ese city, the other the great southern capital — highlights the range of Fabrizio's geographical and existential displacements.

## Historical Background

Novara was historically significant in the Napoleonic and post-Napoleonic period. The city and its province were part of the Kingdom of Sardinia ([[Piedmont]]) after the Congress of [[Vienna]] (1815). Its position between [[Lombardy|Austrian Lombardy]] and French-influenced [[Piedmont]] gave it strategic importance. In the novel's timeframe, Novara represented the [[Piedmont]]ese — and thus relatively more liberal — alternative to Austrian-ruled [[Lombardy]], though Fabrizio experiences it primarily as a place of restriction.
