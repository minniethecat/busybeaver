# Friendship

## Definition

Friendship in *The Charterhouse of Parma* is a rare and precious commodity. In a world where every relationship is political, genuine friendship — disinterested, loyal, warm — offers a counterpoint to the relentless calculations of the court. Yet Stendhal is too honest a writer to sentimentalise friendship: it can be betrayed, neglected, or simply outgrown. The novel's most moving friendships are those that survive the pressure of passion and politics.

## Historical Context

In the aristocratic society of Restoration Italy, friendship was often indistinguishable from alliance. The *amicizia* of the court was a political relationship dressed in the language of affection. Stendhal, who had known the camaraderie of Napoleon's army and the solitude of exile, valued authentic friendship as one of life's few reliable goods.

## Role in the Novel

The friendship between Gina and Conte Mosca is the novel's emotional anchor — a relationship that begins as political alliance and deepens into genuine devotion. Fabrizio's friendship with Ludovic offers a model of loyalty outside the court's corruption. The absence of friendship in certain characters — the Prince, Rassi — is itself a commentary on their natures. Stendhal suggests that the capacity for friendship is a measure of character: those who cannot be friends cannot truly love either.
