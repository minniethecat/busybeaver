# Annetta Marini

Annetta Marini is "the only daughter and heiress of the rich cloth merchant" (PN 027-056) in Parma. Her sudden fame — and the jealousy it provokes — becomes a minor subplot in the novel's later chapters.

## Rise to Fame

Annetta Marini's notoriety begins when "the name of this Annetta Marini, the cloth merchant's daughter, was on every tongue" (PN 027-057) in Parma. Her reply to some social encounter was "thought charming" (PN 027-058), and she becomes "the talk of the whole town" (PN 027-058). A second mass at "Signorina Marini's parish church" (PN 027-059) draws attention from the entire city.

## The Jealousy Plot

The Marchesa Balbi becomes obsessed with the attention Annetta receives. She "emerged" (PN 028-009) from her seclusion because of "what was said about little Annetta Marini" (PN 028-009). The Marchesa is described as jealous "of the little Marini" (PN 028-027), and courtiers note she "also had a fortune of a hundred thousand crowns" (PN 028-011) — jealousy over both beauty and wealth.

Counselors advise that the Prince "may soon sit down to the little Marini" (PN 028-019), suggesting she might become a new royal favorite, further inflaming Balbi's jealousy.

## Significance

Annetta Marini represents the citizen class of Parma — the wealthy merchants whose daughters could, by a twist of fortune or royal favor, ascend into court society. Her brief prominence shows how beauty, wealth, and gossip intersect in the novel's social world, and how even minor figures outside the aristocracy can become pawns in court intrigues. The episode also reveals the fragility of a court mistress's position — Balbi's jealousy of a cloth merchant's daughter underscores how precarious and competitive court favor truly is.
