# Liberty

## Definition

Liberty in *The Charterhouse of Parma* operates on multiple levels. Politically, it is the revolutionary and Napoleonic ideal that the novel's opening chapters celebrate and that the Restoration has crushed. Personally, it is the condition of freedom from the arbitrary power of the Prince, from the surveillance of the police state, and from the social constraints of the court. Stendhal, a product of the French Revolution and an admirer of Napoleon, portrays liberty as both a lost cause and an enduring personal necessity — something that can be denied by political regimes but never entirely extinguished in the human heart.

## Historical Context

The novel opens with the French army's arrival in Milan in 1796 — a moment experienced by many Italians as liberation from Austrian rule. Stendhal describes how "the whole people discovered" that the old regime was built on "absurd" foundations, and the enthusiasm for French ideas of liberty swept through Lombardy. The Restoration of 1815 reversed this current. The novel's political world is defined by the tension between these two poles: the memory of liberty and the reality of absolutism.

The Prince of Parma, though a petty tyrant in a minor state, embodies the Restoration's hostility to liberty in concentrated form. "Presently he began to imagine himself speaking after the fashion of the day against Liberals and liberty" [006-014] — the irony is that his despotism is as theatrical as it is real, a performance of absolutism for an audience of courtiers who have learned to applaud on cue.

## Role in the Novel

Liberty functions as the novel's political and moral compass. The opening chapters are an extended hymn to the liberating effects of the French Revolution in Italy. Fabrizio's flight to join Napoleon is motivated by a love of liberty inherited from this moment. Throughout the novel, the Prince of Parma's regime stands as the antithesis of liberty — a world of spies, arbitrary arrests, and the constant threat of the Citadel.

Gina's defiance of the Prince represents a personal assertion of liberty against political power. Conte Mosca, the novel's supreme political realist, navigates the unfree world of the court while preserving an inner liberty of judgment. The Citadel, where Fabrizio is imprisoned, becomes paradoxically a space of personal liberation through love — Stendhal's most subversive comment on the nature of freedom.

"The desire for liberty, the fashion and cult of the supreme happiness of being oneself" [007-021] — this passage captures the novel's central insight: liberty is not only a political condition but a psychological need, a hunger for authenticity that survives even when political structures deny it.

## Key Passages

> The whole people discovered, with a thrill of pleasure never before experienced, that everything in the past had been absurd or hateful. [001-003]

> This was the state of things in Lombardy when the French army entered Milan in 1796. [001-001] — the novel's opening situates liberty as its founding theme.

> The desire for liberty, the fashion and cult of the supreme happiness of being oneself, was the only thing that interested the young Milanese of that day. [007-021]

> Presently he began to imagine himself speaking after the fashion of the day against Liberals and liberty. [006-014]

> "I am quite at liberty to do it," he said to himself, as though seeking reasons to justify a decision already taken. [003-095]

> In their immediate circle there reigned a freedom of conversation which seemed to the Duchessa the most precious of all liberties. [007-020]
