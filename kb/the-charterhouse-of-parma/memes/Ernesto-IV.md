## Overview

Ernesto IV, formally styled **Ranuccio-Ernesto IV**, is the reigning Prince of Parma and the absolute sovereign of the duchy. His court is the setting for much of the novel's political drama, and his character — a mixture of severity, cowardice, vanity, and cunning — embodies Stendhal's satirical portrait of petty absolutism. The narrator introduces him as "that famous Prince of Parma, Ernesto IV, so notorious for his severity" [006-004], immediately establishing his reputation before systematically undercutting it.

## Character

The Prince is a figure of deep contradictions. He cultivates a reputation for severity and is "notorious" for it [006-004], yet the narrator reveals he is "courageous only in war" and "trembles in his room" if someone "cries aloud a whole sentence" [006-014, 006-016]. He is described as "full of dear little vices" [006-052] and is in the habit of "repeating" what others say to him [006-064]. His absolutism is undercut by a fundamental pusillanimity — a man who wishes to appear formidable but is riddled with anxieties.

Stendhal's portrait is merciless in its irony:

> …that famous Prince of Parma, Ernesto IV, so notorious for his severity, to whom the Liberals of his dominions referred as an ogre. The truth is that Ernesto IV was full of dear little vices…
(006-004, 006-052)

The Prince's vanity extends to his appearance: he "wore an evening coat in the French fashion" [006-048], deliberately distinguishing himself from his Spanish-descended court. This sartorial detail is emblematic of his broader insecurity — a ruler who fears comparison with his predecessors and compensates with theatrical gestures.

## Role at Court

As absolute sovereign, Ernesto IV's moods and whims determine the fate of his courtiers. He is a "past-master in the art" of manipulation [007-043]. His court becomes the field on which figures like [[Conte-Mosca]], the [[Marchesa-Raversi]], and General [[Fabio-Conti]] compete for influence. His unpredictability — one moment bestowing a pension on Father Bari [008-021], the next threatening disgrace — keeps the court in a perpetual state of tension.

The Prince's relationship with his chief minister Mosca is particularly revealing. He fears Mosca's intelligence while depending on his competence:

> For a long time past Ranuccio-Ernesto IV, who rarely encountered a refusal, had taken it into his head that his Prime Minister's devotion to the interests of the reigning family was less than whole-hearted.
(007-036)

His sovereignty is absolute in theory but constrained in practice by his own limitations — he is intelligent enough to recognize talent in others but too vain to trust it fully. This creates the unstable equilibrium that defines the political landscape of the novel.

## Key Appearances

The Prince first appears in Chapter 6, where Stendhal devotes a sustained character sketch to him:

> Ranuccio-Ernesto IV, the absolute sovereign of the Duchy of Parma, was a man of moderate height but imposing presence. His reign had begun under the happiest auspices, yet twenty years of power had transformed him into a figure of grotesque contradictions.
(006-011)

His court appearances punctuate the narrative at moments of crisis — the discovery of Fabrizio's liberal sympathies, the political maneuvering around the succession, and the climactic confrontation with the Duchessa Sanseverina.

## Significance

Ernesto IV represents the novel's critique of petty tyranny. His power is real enough to ruin lives, yet his personal qualities are those of a mediocre man elevated by birth rather than merit. Stendhal draws him with ironic detachment — never allowing the reader to forget that this man, for all his pretensions, is the product of a system that confuses inherited rank with genuine authority. His reign provides the political backdrop against which the novel's central characters navigate ambition, love, and survival.

The epilogue notes that a later traveler attempted "to emulate the career of Ranuccio-Ernesto IV" [029-007], suggesting that the type he represents — the petty absolutist — survives the individual.
