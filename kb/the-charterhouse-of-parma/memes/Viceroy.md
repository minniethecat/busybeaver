# Viceroy of Italy

The **Viceroy of Italy** was the title held by [[Prince-Eugène|Prince Eugène de Beauharnais]] under his stepfather [[Napoleon]], governing the Kingdom of Italy from [[Milan]] between 1805 and 1814. In Stendhal's novel, the Viceroy and his court represent the liberality and gaiety of French rule, sharply contrasted with the reactionary absolutism that follows the [[Battle of Waterloo|fall of Napoleon]].

## The Court at Milan

Under the Viceroy, the court at [[Milan]] was known for its youthfulness, elegance, and relative openness. Stendhal describes it as "young and gay as in the days of the Viceroy" — a period when "Youth, or at any rate a life of pleasure" characterised the Milanese elite [006-032]. This atmosphere was wholly different from the sombre, suspicious court of [[Parma]] under [[Ernesto-IV|Ranuccio-Ernesto IV]].

## Political Role

The Viceroy exercised real administrative authority over the Kingdom of Italy. The novel references his power to grant or withhold favour — characters seek to throw themselves "at the Viceroy's feet" to obtain protection or clemency [010-002]. After the fall of Napoleon, those who had been "tenderly cherished by the Prince [[Viceroy]]" were expelled from Italy by the returning Austrian authorities, underscoring the fragility of the Napoleonic social order [001-030].

## Historical Context

Prince Eugène de Beauharnais (1781–1824) was the son of [[Napoleon|Napoleon's]] first wife Joséphine from her previous marriage. Adopted by Napoleon, he served as Viceroy from 1805 until the collapse of the Kingdom of Italy in 1814. Stendhal admired Eugène as a figure of enlightened governance, describing him as "that charming Prince Eugène, [[Viceroy]] of Italy, whose virtues" stood in contrast to the despotic pettiness of the restored regimes [026-018].

## Significance in the Novel

The Viceroy embodies the lost world of Napoleonic liberalism that haunts the novel. For characters like [[Fabrizio-del-Dongo]] and [[Gina-Sanseverina]], the Viceroy's Milan represents youth, freedom, and hope — a golden age irrevocably closed by the [[Battle of Waterloo]] and the Congress of Vienna. The Viceroy's court is the standard against which all subsequent political regimes in the novel are measured and found wanting.
