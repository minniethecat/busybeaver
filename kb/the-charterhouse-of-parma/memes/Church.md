# Church

The **Church** in Stendhal's _The Charterhouse of Parma_ functions on multiple levels: as a career institution offering advancement to ambitious young men like Fabrizio del Dongo, as a source of moral authority that often conflicts with political power, and as a backdrop against which the novel's central tensions between sacred devotion and worldly passion play out.

## The Church as Career

Fabrizio's ecclesiastical path is chosen for him by his family, particularly his aunt Gina Sanseverina and Conte Mosca, who see the Church as the safest route to power and prestige in the post-Napoleonic restoration. Fabrizio himself initially has no vocation: he "rejected the idea of the Church" and "spoke of going to New York" [006-086]. His aunt and Mosca gradually persuade him, and "came back to the idea of the Church" [006-087] — but his eventual acceptance of holy orders is more strategic than spiritual.

The Prince of Parma, Ernesto IV, takes a personal interest in Church appointments, viewing them as instruments of political control. "It was important for the Church in Parma that Monsignor Fabrizio" be advanced [011-014], and Fabrizio's rapid rise through the ecclesiastical ranks reflects the entanglement of Church and State. Conte Mosca frankly acknowledges the political nature of Church careers: advancement in the hierarchy depends as much on court favor as on piety.

## The Church and Political Power

The Church in Parma serves as an arm of the princely state. The Archbishop of Parma is effectively a political appointee, and sermons delivered from prominent pulpits are monitored for seditious content. When Fabrizio preaches at the Church of the Visitation — "about three o'clock, the Church of the Visitation was packed" [027-047] — his popularity threatens the court. People came "forcing their way into the Church of the Visitation" [027-052] to hear him. The Prince seeks to neutralize Fabrizio through ecclesiastical channels, demonstrating that the Church is not an independent spiritual authority but deeply enmeshed in the machinery of absolutist rule.

The Bible, the "Orders of the Church, Tradition and everything else" [007-015] are invoked by characters throughout the novel not as sources of spiritual truth but as instruments of social control and political maneuvering.

## Sacred vs. Secular

A central irony of the novel is that Fabrizio, an ordained prelate who declares himself "a prelate of the Holy Roman Church" [015-005], leads a life far removed from priestly ideals. His love affairs — with Marietta, Fausta, and ultimately Clelia Conti — violate his vows of celibacy. Yet the novel does not condemn him; instead, it suggests that the Church's institutional demands are incompatible with genuine human passion.

Fabrizio himself acknowledges this tension: "I shall remove from the Church an unworthy priest" [026-004]. His eventual retirement to the Charterhouse of Parma represents a reconciliation of sorts: he leaves the institutional Church for a life of contemplation, finding in monastic withdrawal the peace that the ecclesiastical career never offered.

## Key Passages

> Fabrizio "rejected the idea of the Church. He spoke of going to New York" — only later persuaded by his aunt and Mosca [006-086]

> "It was important for the Church in Parma that Monsignor Fabrizio" be advanced through the hierarchy [011-014]

> "I am a prelate of the Holy Roman Church," Fabrizio said to him firmly [015-005]

> "I shall remove from the Church an unworthy priest, and, under [vows], retire to the Charterhouse" [026-004]

> The Church of the Visitation, where Fabrizio's celebrated sermons draw crowds: "at the Church of the Visitation, opposite your windows" [028-028]
