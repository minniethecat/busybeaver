# Passion

## Definition

Passion is the lifeblood of Stendhal's characters. It is what separates the vital from the merely conventional, the heroic from the prudent. In *The Charterhouse of Parma*, passion takes many forms: the patriotic passion that sweeps Milan when Napoleon arrives; the erotic passion that consumes Fabrizio, Gina, and Conte Mosca; the passion for art and music that offers a refuge from politics; and the passion for power that drives the Prince and his ministers. Stendhal's own treatise *De l'Amour* distinguished passionate love from other kinds of love, and the novel consistently values authentic passion over calculated behaviour — even when passion leads to catastrophe.

## Historical Context

The Romantic movement, of which Stendhal was both a product and a critic, elevated passion as a supreme value. The post-Napoleonic generation, raised on tales of heroic glory, found themselves in a Restoration world that rewarded prudence and conformity. The novel's characters embody this contradiction: they are passionate souls trapped in a society that punishes spontaneity. Fabrizio's grandmother's generation "loved their country with a real love" — the Restoration court, by contrast, is a machine for channelling and suppressing passion.

## Role in the Novel

Passion is the novel's engine. The Duchessa's "insane passion" for Fabrizio — which she herself struggles to name and define — drives the political plot. Conte Mosca's passion for Gina makes him, a supremely rational statesman, willing to risk everything. Fabrizio's serial passions — for Napoleon, for Marietta, for Fausta, for Clelia — trace his arc from youthful enthusiasm to mature devotion. Even secondary characters are defined by their passions or lack thereof: the Prince burns with frustrated desire; Rassi burns with ambition; Clelia burns with a love she cannot acknowledge.

Stendhal's narration itself is passionate — ironic, urgent, digressive, alive. The famous opening of Chapter One, describing the Milanese "madly in love" with Napoleon, sets the tone for a novel in which passion, not reason, is the true motive force of history and of human life.

## Key Passages

> To swear that I feel an insane passion for you would be a lie; I should be only too glad to love today at thirty odd as I used to love at two-and-twenty! But I have seen so many things decay that I had imagined to be eternal! I have the most tender affection for you. (006-034)

> Her heart was in need of consolation and novelty. On arriving in Milan she conceived a passion for the fashionable opera. (006-004)

> Clelia looked at Fabrizio, and in spite of herself that look portrayed in full the passion that had thrown her into despair. (025-002)
