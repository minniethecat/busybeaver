# The Correggio Frescoes

## Overview

Antonio da Correggio (1489–1534) was the greatest painter of the Parma school, and his frescoes — particularly the Assumption of the Virgin in the dome of Parma Cathedral and the mythological scenes in the Camera di San Paolo — are among the supreme achievements of the Italian Renaissance. Stendhal, who loved Italian painting with an almost religious intensity, invokes Correggio's name at crucial moments in the novel as a touchstone of a beauty that belongs to a different order of time than the political intrigues of the Restoration court. Correggio represents art as permanence [023-008].

## In the Novel

Correggio's frescoes appear in the novel as an ideal of aesthetic perfection against which human imperfection is measured. When Stendhal describes Clelia's beauty as "a Correggio face," he is not making a casual comparison: he is invoking an entire aesthetics of grace, of the spiritual made visible in the flesh, that Correggio's paintings embody. The frescoes are real works of art that exist in the real Parma — and their presence in the novel reminds the reader that the world Stendhal depicts is not entirely invented [005-105].

## Significance

Correggio's frescoes function as a mute chorus in the novel — a reminder that beauty, unlike power, does not decay. The Prince of Parma will die and be forgotten; the intrigues of the Raversi faction will dissipate; Fabrizio's sermons will fade from memory. But Correggio's dome, with its ascending figures of saints and angels, will remain — a permanent reproach to the transience of political ambition. This is the novel's deepest aesthetic conviction: that art is the one human activity that genuinely transcends time [023-008].

## Key Passages

> The Canon added bitterly: "He has a Correggio face — that dangerous expression in his eye." [005-105]

> The local painters, after the divine Correggio, were all of the first and second rank. They were decorating the chapels with frescoes when Fabrizio passed through [023-008].
