# Conte Mosca's Resignation

## Overview

When the Prince of Parma's demands threaten Gina's interests, Conte Mosca offers his resignation — not as a genuine withdrawal from power but as a political manoeuvre. "If my presence at court is no longer acceptable," he tells the Prince, "I am ready to retire." The resignation is a calculated risk: Mosca gambles that his indispensable competence outweighs the Prince's irritation. Stendhal, who served in the Napoleonic administration and understood political bureaucracy from the inside, renders this moment with the precision of a diplomat's memorandum — and with the novelist's eye for the emotional stakes beneath the procedural surface [006-032].

## Role in the Novel

Mosca's resignation is a study in the power of the indirect. He does not confront the Prince, does not threaten, does not even argue. He simply offers to remove himself — and in that offer is contained an implicit ultimatum: accept my terms or lose my service. The Prince, who has built his regime on Mosca's administrative genius, cannot afford the loss. The resignation is refused, and Mosca emerges stronger than before. It is a lesson in political jiu-jitsu that Gina will later adapt for her own purposes [006-032].

The event also illuminates Mosca's character in a way no amount of narration could. He is not a passionate man — his resignation is cool, calculated, almost mathematical in its calibration of risk and reward. Yet beneath the calculation is something genuine: his devotion to Gina, which is the only loyalty that takes precedence over his loyalty to power itself.
