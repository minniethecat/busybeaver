## Overview

Henrik Rissler's evening confession to Martin Birck is the novel's most sustained exploration of male intimacy and the psychology of love. Returning from Uppsala with his preliminary degree, on the eve of a European tour to write his thesis on Romantic Irony, Rissler arrives at Martin's room with a burden he can share with no one else. Over wine and cigarettes, as the copper afternoon light fades through pale twilight into darkness, he unburdens himself of a fear that governs his entire emotional life: that in love, as in everything else, he will be the one who grows tired first.

## The Setting

Rissler arrives at Martin's door blinking "at the sunlight, whose copper-red rays struck horizontally across the room" [024-011]. Martin has been expecting him for days and has "a bottle of wine and a pack of cigarettes ready" [024-012]. The scene is carefully staged as a ritual of male friendship — the wine, the cigarettes, the fading light, the two young men in Martin's room with its writing desk and rocking chair. Rissler's opening line sets the emotional key: "Here everything is the same. Here time has stood still" [024-014]. It is both an observation about Martin's static life and an admission of his own transformation — he has been changed by experience, and Martin's room is the still point against which he measures his own motion.

## The Conversation

The conversation begins in abstraction — the melancholy of outgrowing old books, old music, old associations. Martin and Rissler trade philosophical observations about the self that is "dead and buried" and the paradox of feeling loyalty to a past identity one no longer inhabits. But Rissler steers the talk toward something more urgent:

> It was really something else I've been thinking of all the time. I've been thinking of love and women. If one comes into that province, it isn't only just melancholy any longer; no, one can't get off so cheaply. [024-043]

## The Confession

Rissler's confession is remarkable for its candor:

> A man is fond of a woman. He wants the whole of eternity to be in that feeling. And yet he can't escape reflecting that this emotion must be subordinate to the same law of growth as everything else in the world, that some day he will weary of what he loves just as one wearies of the moonlight music in Faust. I have not had many love affairs, but, believe me, I have never even in my imagination begun the game otherwise than with the thought: may she be the first to tire, and not I! [024-043]

He is immediately ashamed: "I'm ashamed of the prayer, for I know it comes straight from my heart's great cowardice." But the shame does not negate the feeling. He would rather be betrayed than be the betrayer — not from nobility but from a terror of guilt so profound that even his own death would be no consolation: "It came over me that not even the thought that I myself was dead and lying under the earth could be any consolation to me in such a case; the only conception that could bring any relief was that I was living as wretched and exhausted as she" [024-051].

## The Old Woman

The confession is triggered by an uncanny encounter on the street outside Martin's door. An old, pale, shabbily dressed woman — "one of the poor who are too proud to beg" — fleetingly resembled the young woman Rissler loves:

> The girl I care for stood before me, she passed me, withered, discarded, a little comic. It came over me that not even the thought that I myself was dead and lying under the earth could be any consolation to me in such a case. [024-051]

The vision is a secular prophecy: the beloved aged into desolation, and Rissler seeing himself as the agent of that ruin. The horror is compounded by the ordinariness of the moment — "it seemed to me only all the worse that everything else was as usual: the sun was shining and people were on the street."

## The Revelation

Martin finally asks who the girl is. Rissler answers "in a subdued voice": "It is Sigrid Tesch" [024-057]. Martin sees before him "a young and supple figure, with dark abundant hair and delicate regular features" — a woman he has met cursorily and who has sometimes passed through "his own twilight thoughts with a pallid dream smile." The naming makes the confession irrevocable: Rissler is going to marry Sigrid Tesch despite knowing — or believing he knows — that love decays.

> "Yes," said Henrik, "isn't it inexplicable that one can dare go into such a thing as love? And yet..."
> "Yes," said Martin, "and yet..."
> They both smiled. [024-065]

The shared smile across the ellipsis is the novel's most compressed statement about love: it is irrational, it is doomed, and one does it anyway.

## The Aftermath

By the novel's later chapters, Rissler has married — presumably Sigrid — and leads "a migratory life" from one damp flat to another on Stockholm's margins. He and Martin "seldom met now, and that was a pity, for Martin knew of no one with a better appreciation of friendship, ideas, and cigars than he" [031-015]. The evening confession remains suspended between prophecy and paradox: Rissler's fear has not been disproven, but neither has it prevented him from loving.

## Significance

Rissler's evening confession is a portrait of male intimacy at its most vulnerable. In an era when men's friendships could easily be restricted to convivial surfaces, Söderberg gives us two young men sharing their deepest terrors across a table as the light dies. Rissler's thesis topic — Romantic Irony, the stance of passionate engagement combined with ironic self-distance — is the key to his character: he loves, but watches himself loving; he marries, but foresees the marriage's decay. The confession is an act of trust that momentarily bridges the ironic distance, and Martin's silent, sympathetic reception — asking only "who is she?" and echoing "and yet..." — is the friendship's finest hour.
