## Overview

The evening at the theater with his mistress is one of the most intimate and melancholy scenes in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. It is the appointed anniversary of their secret union — her "bridal day" — and they go to see *Hamlet*, but the real drama is in what must remain unseen: their love, hidden in a dark corner of the theater, as hidden as they are from the world.

## The Dressing Room

"She stood at her dressing table, where two narrow candles burned before the mirror" [030-001]. Martin watches from the sofa as she arranges her brown hair and applies powder. "Their glances met in the mirror and were fixed on each other in a long smile. The trembling of the candle flames and the distance, which the mirror lengthened, made this smile dark and mysterious" [030-001].

She turns and strokes his cheek with the powder puff, making him "as white as a Pierrot" [030-002]. Then she presses his head to her breast and whispers: "I am so happy, because it is my bridal day today, and because I am going to the theater with you to sit in a little nook where no one can see us" [030-002].

## Martin's Secret Shame

Her words wound him. "He felt a secret stab in the heart when he heard her speak so, for he knew almost to a certainty that if there had been any chance of it she would much rather have sat with him in a place where all could see them" [030-002]. He knows she never alludes to marriage and understands its impossibility, but "he on his part could never cease to feel it as a secret disgrace that it was not in his power to give her the happiness which belonged to a secure and respected social position" [030-002].

He dismisses these thoughts — he is "no strong man who could take her in his arms and break a way for them both" [030-002]. She, for her part, had known strong men and held back from them, for "it was precisely the strong men who rarely acted as he could have wished to do" [030-002]. Their love is what it is: two chilled people warming themselves at a fire that must remain hidden.

## The Walk Through Snow

They leave separately, as always. She waits in the dark while he goes ahead, so that no one will see them on the stairs. But this evening the snow provides cover: "On this particular evening the snow was falling so thick and white that nobody could have recognized them. People passed them in the white night like phantoms without name or distinction. Close together, nameless themselves and somewhat like the silhouettes which children cut out in pairs from folded paper, they made their way through the snow" [030-004].

## The Theater

It is *Hamlet* they have come to see. They slip into their dark corner after the first act has begun, to avoid acquaintances. From his seat Martin cannot see the stage — only hear the lines and watch "their effects on the curving rows of pale human masks — a sloping flower bed full of large curious flowers, colorless as are plants that grow without sunlight" [031-001].

During the graveyard scene, he watches his mistress instead of the stage, and through her absorbed face he sees the whole scene mirrored: "He saw Hamlet stand there in his mantle of night and mystery with Yorick's skull in his hand, he saw the funeral procession, the lowering of the coffin, and the queen as she strewed flowers on the grave" [031-002]. He hears Hamlet's voice: "I loved Ophelia" [031-002].
