## Overview

Hasselbacken, the celebrated restaurant on the island of Djurgården, is the setting for one of the novel's most vivid and thematically dense scenes: Martin Birck's graduation supper. In the evening following Martin's confirmation, he and his companions gather at Hasselbacken, where the mingling of youthful exuberance with the worldly sophistication of older intellectuals — notably the elder Billfelt and Doctor Markel — produces the novel's most concentrated sequence of philosophical conversation and social observation [002-008].

## Description in the Text

The Hasselbacken episode is preceded by Martin's mother's anxious warnings — she takes him aside to caution against drinking too much and against "a class of dreadful women who do nothing else but try to lead young men to their destruction" [002-008]. Her fear of the worldly dangers awaiting her son at the restaurant introduces a note of domestic anxiety that contrasts sharply with the scene that follows.

Martin's father, more sanguine, gives him five crowns "to offer to the joy of youth" [002-008]. The juxtaposition of the mother's dread and the father's complicity captures the dual nature of the occasion — it is at once a rite of passage into adult society and a potential fall from innocence.

Martin walks to Hasselbacken through the park, absorbed in thought — "All this and a lot more Martin Birck thought as he went out the Avenue across the park on the way to Hasselbacken" [002-008] — reflecting on his mother's warnings, his uncertain future, and the strange threshold moment he inhabits.

The scene at Hasselbacken brings together Uppsala academics and Stockholm students. Doctor Markel and the elder Billfelt, having "eaten dinner at Hasselbacken," invite themselves to join the student supper [002-039]. The elder Billfelt's toast, delivered "in well-rounded periods," formally inducts the young men — future alumni — into the company of the educated.

## Narrative Function

Hasselbacken functions as a threshold space — a place of transition between Martin's sheltered childhood and the adult world of intellectual ambition, social performance, and moral ambiguity. It is here that Martin first encounters the figure of Doctor Markel, whose pantheistic worldview and rhetorical brilliance will come to exert a powerful influence on his thinking.

The restaurant also serves as a stage for Söderberg's characteristic irony. The students' celebration is disrupted by rain, and the party decamps to the Hamburg Bourse — a café Martin had always assumed was a merchants' exchange. The movement from the elegant, open-air Hasselbacken to the stuffy, bourgeois café mirrors the novel's broader thematic arc from youthful idealism to adult compromise.

## Associated Characters

- **Martin Birck** — The protagonist, celebrating his graduation and confirmation.
- **Doctor Markel** — Uppsala intellectual whose pantheistic philosophy captivates Martin.
- **The elder Billfelt** — Uppsala graduate who presides over the toasts.
- **Martin's mother** — Whose anxious warnings frame the episode.
- **Martin's father** — Who provides the five crowns for the celebration.

## Notable Passages

> In the evening Martin and his companions were to have a supper at Hasselbacken. Martin's father gave him five crowns to offer to the joy of youth, and his mother took him aside and said: "Martin, Martin, you must promise me to be careful tonight and not be led into any foolishness. Don't make a point of emptying your glass every time anyone drinks a toast with you, or you'll lose your head. The best thing would be just to pretend you drank. And I must tell you, Martin, that there is a class of dreadful women who do nothing else but try to lead young men to their destruction. You must beware of them especially."
