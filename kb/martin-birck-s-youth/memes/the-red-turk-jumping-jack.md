## Overview

The Red Turk Jumping-Jack is a gift-giving scene embedded in the snowy days following Christmas (Old Street VII). Uncle Abraham presents Martin with a pasteboard jumping-jack and delivers a half-playful, half-bitter philosophical monologue about free will and self-deception. Though young Martin "didn't understand much of this," he treasures the Red Turk above all his other toys—an early encounter with irony that resonates through his later life.

## Narrative Account

During the deep winter after Christmas, Martin is kept indoors with his alphabet book, multiplication tables, color-box, and jumping-jacks [419-419]. Among these toys, one called the Red Turk becomes his favorite because of what Uncle Abraham said when giving it to him.

Uncle Abraham explains that the Red Turk is "the jolliest jumping-jack in all the world" [419-419]. It is not merely a pasteboard figure that kicks when the strings are pulled—"the Red Turk can think and choose the same as we." Abraham lays out a two-part philosophy: when the strings are jerked and the Turk prances, he tells himself, "I am a being with free will, I kick just as I want to and exclusively for my own entertainment." But when the strings stop, the Turk decides, "To the deuce with the kicking! The finest thing there is is to hang on a hook on the wall and stay entirely still" [419-419].

Martin "didn't understand much of this, but he understood that the Red Turk was amusing and set greater store by him than ever" [421-421].

## Significance

Abraham's parable about the Red Turk is one of the novel's most compressed philosophical moments. The jumping-jack who rationalizes both motion and stillness as freely chosen is a miniature of human self-deception—we tell ourselves stories to make our circumstances seem like choices. Coming from Abraham, a man who will later take his own life [592-592], the parable acquires a tragic undertow: Abraham, too, has been the Turk, pulled by invisible strings (his Jewishness, his solitude, his sorrow) while telling himself he was free.

Martin's childhood incomprehension of the message—he values the toy for its amusement value—is part of the irony. The lesson sinks in without his knowing it, as later events in his life attest.
