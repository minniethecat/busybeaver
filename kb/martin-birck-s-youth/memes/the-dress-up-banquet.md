## Overview

On a gray autumn day at the Dupont household, Ida Dupont — the daughter of a former ballet dancer and a Catholic Frenchman — initiates Martin into an elaborate game of dress-up and pretend feasting. Drawing from her mother's trunk of theatrical costumes, she transforms Martin into a cross-dressed banquet guest, complete with burnt-cork mustaches and dark-red wine in green glasses. The scene is a vivid study in childhood's mimicry of the adult world, gender-play, and the intoxicating thrill of ritual imitation. It is also Martin's first sustained encounter with a household unlike his own: smaller, less prosperous, Catholic, and rich with the artifacts of a vanished theatrical career.

## The Dupont Household

The Duponts occupied a distinct social position in Martin's world. Earlier, during the pear-tree episode in the courtyard, the neighborhood children gathered to fight for falling pears while "a little way off stood little Ida Dupont, with great eyes, her hands behind her back, not venturing into the turmoil." [003-110] Mrs. Lundgren, the neighborhood enforcer of fairness, "did not get any pears for her because she was ill-disposed toward Mr. Dupont, who was a violinist in the royal orchestra." [003-110] This small detail establishes the Duponts as outsiders — Ida's father was a musician, a French Catholic in Lutheran Stockholm, living in two small rooms one flight up.

"Mr. Dupont had two small rooms, one flight up. At this time of day he was away at rehearsal, so Martin and Ida were alone." [003-197] The absence of adult supervision is crucial — the dress-up game and the wine banquet could only happen because Mr. Dupont was at rehearsal, leaving the children the run of the apartment.

The apartment itself was rich with exotic objects. Ida showed Martin a peepshow box: "There were white castles and gardens with colored lanterns in long gleaming rows, yellow and red and blue. There were strange cities with churches and bridges, and steamboats and big ships on a wide river." [003-201] "I got that from mamma," Ida declared — a refrain that runs through the chapter, tying every artifact to the mother's past in the ballet. [003-203]

## The Costumes

Ida asked Martin: "Do you know what we'll do now? We'll dress up." [003-219] She pulled out a bureau drawer and began taking out the costumes — "red bodices of satin, silk, and rep with a multitude of ribbons and rosettes; silk gloves, silk stockings, and long veils of lace — pink, blue, and white." [003-221] These, Ida explained, had come from her mother's time in the ballet. [003-223]

She took a thin, light blue veil with silver spangles and draped it around Martin's head, then added a red bodice, a shawl of silver gauze, and a white skirt. [003-225]

> "My, but you look funny! Just like a girl." [003-227]

Martin looked at himself in the glass and they both roared with laughter. [003-229]

## The Mustaches

Ida then complicated the costume with a further transformation: "Come here, and I'll put mustaches on you." [003-231] Martin objected that mustaches didn't fit a girl's costume, but Ida was undeterred. She blackened a cork over a candle and traced big black mustaches on Martin, then put black eyebrows on herself. [003-233]

They looked into the mirror again and laughed. "It's so handsome to have black eyebrows," Ida declared, asking Martin if he thought she was handsome. [003-235]

The cross-dressing and re-cross-dressing — girl's costume plus mustache — creates a layered, unstable gender performance that neither child takes seriously. It is play as pure experimentation, unburdened by the meanings adults attach to dress and gender. Ida, the director of this private theater, treats identity as costume — something to be put on and taken off at will.

## The Banquet

Ida's resources seemed inexhaustible. She proposed: "If you'll be terribly nice, we'll have a banquet." [003-241] From a cupboard she produced a half-filled bottle of wine and two green glasses, then laid a cloth on the toilet table and filled the glasses. [003-243]

Martin's eyes grew big. "Does your papa let you?" he asked. [003-247] But the question was rhetorical — the banquet proceeded regardless. They clinked glasses and drank. "It was a sweet and pleasant wine, and its dark red shone splendidly in the green glasses." [003-253]

The wine — real wine, not pretend — marks the scene's transgressive quality. This is not mere imitation of adult ritual but actual participation in it. The green glasses and the dark red wine, the toilet table turned banquet table, the costumes and the cork mustaches: everything is simultaneously real and make-believe, a hybrid state that belongs uniquely to childhood.

## Themes

The dress-up banquet is one of the novel's richest scenes of childhood play as social apprenticeship. Ida, with her access to theatrical costumes, wine, and a household less governed by conventional propriety, introduces Martin to a world of adult rituals — costume, alcohol, ceremony — remade in the key of childhood. The cross-dressing and mustache-painting suggest a fluid, experimental attitude toward identity that is purely playful, yet reveals the constructedness of the adult roles the children are learning to inhabit.

The scene also establishes the Dupont household as a counter-world to the Birck home. Where Martin's household is structured by mother's moral instruction and father's quiet play, Ida's is a realm of theatrical excess, Catholic ritual objects, and adult pleasures repurposed for children. The two worlds — the Protestant, bourgeois Bircks and the Catholic, bohemian Duponts — will remain in productive tension throughout Martin's childhood.
