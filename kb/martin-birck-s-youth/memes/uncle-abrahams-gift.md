## Overview

Uncle Abraham's gift of the Red Turk is one of the most philosophically charged moments in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. What appears to be a simple Christmas gift — a pasteboard jumping-jack — becomes, through Abraham's commentary, a miniature parable of free will, self-deception, and the limits of human autonomy.

## The Visit

The episode takes place during the New Year's visits, when Martin and Maria are sent to pay their respects to relatives. Uncle Abraham's home is atmospherically distinct from the other holiday stops: a large, half-dark room full of books, snow falling outside in great flakes, and Abraham sitting alone at a table with a bottle of wine, playing chess against himself [006-001]. There is no Christmas tree; instead there is a parrot that swears in French and a clever black cat named Kolmodin.

## The Red Turk

In a subsequent visit, Abraham gives Martin the Red Turk — a pasteboard jumping-jack. But the gift is inseparable from the commentary that accompanies it. Abraham explains that the Red Turk is "no common pasteboard man; he can think and choose the same as we," and that when his strings are pulled he tells himself: "I am a being with free will, I kick just as I want to and exclusively for my own entertainment" [006-003].

## The Parable

The parable is devastating in its simplicity. The Turk believes he kicks freely, but his every movement is determined by the strings Martin pulls. The analogy to human free will is left implicit — Söderberg does not spell it out — but the implication is clear: if we are all pasteboard men, pulling our own strings while telling ourselves we choose freely, then the very concept of autonomous action is an illusion sustained by self-deception.

## The Aftermath

When the holiday ends, Martin is left with his "already faded" gifts, including the Red Turk — whose philosophical significance the boy only dimly grasps. The jumping-jack survives the holiday as an emblem of lost illusion, a toy whose lesson about free will haunts the boy long after the gift itself is forgotten. Uncle Abraham's subsequent suicide by hanging — "one rainy day, when he didn't find it worth the trouble to live any more" [012-001] — retroactively darkens the parable: if we are all puppets, Abraham chose to cut his own strings.
