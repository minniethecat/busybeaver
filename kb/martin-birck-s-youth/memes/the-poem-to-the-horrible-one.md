## Overview

"The Poem to the Horrible One" is the creative culmination of Martin's crisis of faith in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. In a single sleepless night, Martin composes a poem that gives artistic form to his loss of belief — a direct address to a remote, indifferent deity he calls "the Horrible One." It is the most personal poem he ever writes and marks a turning point in his intellectual and artistic development.

## The Night of Composition

"It was late when Martin came home, and he was dead tired but could not sleep" [022-001]. Black butterflies fluttered before his eyes, and "thoughts and rhythms came to him as he lay and stared into the dark" [022-001]. He relit his candle, where paper and pen were always at hand. His state was not feverish excitement but "a deep weariness, which pained him but did not delude" [022-001]. He worked deliberately: "He saw clearly where his thought wavered and needed the support of a rhythm, a bit of melody; he changed and erased, and finally a poem evolved" [022-001].

## The Poem: "You Up Yonder"

The poem is a direct address to a god Martin no longer believes in. He names the deity "You up yonder / Who are deaf and dumb!" — a god who squeezes "the fresh and sweetly-smelling fruit of Good" with one hand while constricting "the poison-dripping maggot nest of Ill" with the other, looking upon both "with equal satisfaction" [022-001].

The central confession is that Martin himself had once tried to make a god in his own image: "In youthful days / I myself made a god / In mine own image, / A warm and living and aggressive god" [022-001]. He went out to seek this god through "all the world and heavens" — but found instead only the terrible remote deity: "Not life's divinity / But death's I found under the mask of life" [022-001].

The poem ends with a bitter prayer: "Take the memory of the sight of you / Away, O horrible One!" [022-001] The vision of this indifferent god has become a worm gnawing at the root of his life-tree, preying on "All that in me which is of human worth, / All that which dares, all that which wills and works" [022-001].

## Significance

The poem represents Martin's definitive break with the Christianity of his childhood. He had made "a warm and living and aggressive god" in his own image, but found only Death under the mask of Life [022-001]. It is his most personal poetic work — an act of intellectual honesty given artistic form. Unlike his other poems, which he later judges childish and worthless, this one captures a genuine moment of existential clarity.
