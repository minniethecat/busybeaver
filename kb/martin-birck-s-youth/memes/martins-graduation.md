## Overview

Martin Birck's graduation from secondary school is the threshold event of the novel's second part, "The White Cap." The white student cap (*studentmössa*) — a symbol of completed schooling and admission to the university — functions in the novel as both a literal object (bought, worn, reflected in shop windows) and a symbolic marker of Martin's passage into adult society. The event unfolds in three movements: the solitary moment of receiving the certificate, the public celebration at Hasselbacken, and the long night walk through Stockholm that ends at dawn before the statue of Charles XII.

Söderberg renders the graduation with his characteristic blend of irony and tenderness: Martin "could not understand how he had become a student" [013-002], having believed until the last moment that he would fail. The surprise of his success — crowned by the words "Graduated with honor" on his certificate — produces in him a complex emotional response that mingles joy, gratitude, and a quiet shame at having "distinguished himself."

## Context

Martin's school years had been marked by deepening apathy. "During the last years there had come over him a dull apathy for everything connected with school, against which he could do nothing" [013-002]. He had seldom known his lessons and had not even troubled to use the common student tactic of hiding textbook leaves inside a Bible to study during morning prayers. The inner distance between Martin's private intellectual life — his poetry, his dreams, his nascent skepticism — and the institutional demands of the school had grown unbridgeable.

It is against this background of disengagement that the surprise of his success registers: his gratitude toward his teachers for the honor on his certificate is genuine, but it is also slightly absurd — the gratitude of a student who had mentally checked out long ago toward an institution that had somehow failed to notice.

## Narrative Account

**The White Cap.** Immediately after receiving his certificate, Martin performs a small, impulsive act that encapsulates the emotional confusion of the moment: he goes into a cigar booth to buy a cane of cinnamon wood and a package of cigarettes, and when the young shop girl congratulates him on his white cap, he seizes "her dirty hand, which lay outstretched across the counter display of Cameo and Duke of Durham, and tenderly kissed it" [013-001]. He repents almost at once, embarrassed by his own forwardness, and escapes into the street.

Walking home, he stops "in front of every store window to see the reflection of his white cap" [013-002]. But the street of his childhood is no longer the same: "From a single plot the cherry tree still stretched its branches out over a mossy board fence; everything else was great red brick buildings and small commonplace meetinghouses" [013-003]. The transformation of the neighborhood mirrors Martin's own: the boy who once climbed the pear tree is now a student in a white cap, and the world he returns to is both familiar and irrevocably altered.

**The Celebration at Hasselbacken.** The graduation supper takes place at Hasselbacken, the open-air restaurant and concert pavilion on Djurgården. The orchestra plays student songs; dusk falls over the rooftops and the heights of the southern mountains. Here Martin meets a larger world: fellow students, older graduates down from Uppsala, journalists and poets emerging from Lagerlunden, and — in the gathering darkness — the "moths" who slip their arms under those of the young men. A pale girl with black eyes catches Martin's attention and haunts his imagination for the rest of the night.

**The Long Night.** The celebration spills into the streets. The company walks past the opera house, and Martin looks reverently at the group of poets and journalists emerging from Lagerlunden, "wondering whether it would ever be vouchsafed him to become one of them" [017-003]. At Charles XII's Square, Josef Marin insists on prophesying before the statue, and couples waltz around the square in the mist. Martin, immobilized by hesitation, watches the pale girl dance away with another student. As dawn breaks, the morning breeze clears the mist, and "the cross on St. Katarina's cupola burned like the morning star in the first rays of the dawn" [017-004].

**Aftermath.** Alone in his room, unable to sleep, Martin's thoughts return to the pale girl. He imagines a life with her — a little house by a lake deep in the woods, a boat among the rushes, a new beginning far from the city. The fantasy, both tender and self-deceiving, reveals the shape of Martin's idealism: the desire to rescue, to redeem, to escape. It is the first of several such fantasies in the novel, each one a variation on the theme of flight from the ordinary.

## Significance

The graduation event occupies a pivotal position in the novel's architecture. It is the last scene in which Martin is unambiguously part of a collective — the student body, with its songs and rituals and shared white caps. Everything that follows in the novel concerns Martin's progressive estrangement from collective identities: from the church (his confirmation and subsequent loss of faith), from the civil service (his half-hearted career), from marriage (his ambivalence toward domesticity), and from the literary world (his inability to treat poetry as a profession).

The white cap itself — displayed in every shop window reflection, worn through the long night — becomes a visual motif for this moment of belonging from which Martin will spend the rest of the novel departing. The dawn that ends the graduation night is also, in the novel's symbolic economy, the last dawn of Martin's uncomplicated youth.

## Notable Passages

> When one asks a young man who has just passed his school examinations, "What do you intend to be?" he cannot answer, "A poet." People would turn away their heads and put their hands over their mouths. He may answer, a lawyer or a painter or a musician, for a man can train himself for all these fields at some public institution, and even in one's apprenticeship one has a modest place in the community, a profession to follow, one already is something; a student at the university, or a pupil in the art school or the conservatory. It is not much, but still it is always a sop to throw to indiscreet questioners.

> (018-001)
