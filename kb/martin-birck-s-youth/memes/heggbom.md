## Overview

Heggbom is the porter of the tenement where the Birck family lives. He is a background figure rendered through sound and smell: his "rummy voice" singing in the yard [002-001], his unsteady walk through the gateway as he "avoided them by a long tack and removed his cap very politely" [002-009], the brandy that clings to him. His death, announced by Lotta with the solemn words "I'm sorry to have to tell you that Heggbom is dead" [005-001], precipitates one of the novel's gentlest theological moments: Martin lying awake in the rain, "thinking about Heggbom and wondering if he could get to heaven. He always smelled of brandy" [005-002].

## Role in the Narrative

Heggbom appears only in the early chapters, embedded in the sensory world of Martin's childhood tenement. His first appearance is auditory—Martin hears him singing in the yard, then hears Lotta laughing at something he has said [002-001]. A little later, Martin encounters him in the gateway: "he was walking a bit unsteadily. He avoided them by a long tack and removed his cap very politely" [002-009]—a single sentence that sketches a whole character: the drinker's careful dignity, the exaggerated courtesy of the intoxicated.

He is absent from the pear-tree scene itself—his boys are the tree-raiders—but his wife invokes him theatrically: "Lord in heaven what a clatter, and Heggbom lying at his death!" [002-006]. The narrator's dry aside—"Heggbom had been sick in bed awhile ago, and his wife's imagination often turned back to that comparatively happy time" [002-007]—suggests that Mrs. Heggbom's dramatics are habitual.

His death is announced without ceremony. Lotta tells the family with "a very solemn expression" [005-001]. That night Martin lies awake, the rain against the window, contemplating the theological status of a porter who smelled of brandy. Lotta later weaponizes him: "Heggbom will come and bite you if you don't" sleep [005-003]—the dead porter transformed into a nursery bogeyman.

## Key Relationships

- **Mrs. Heggbom** — His wife, who scolds him, mythologizes his illness, and after his death becomes "a lady" [010-001].
- **Lotta** — The Bircks' maid, who chats with him at the kitchen window, laughs at his jokes, and later uses his name to frighten Martin into sleeping.
- **Martin Birck** — The child through whose senses Heggbom is perceived; Martin's theological worry about Heggbom's salvation is one of the novel's most tender moments.

## Significance

Heggbom is the novel's smallest and most perfectly realized minor character. He exists in the text for perhaps a dozen sentences, none of them his own words. Yet in those sentences—the rummy voice, the "long tack" through the gateway, the polite removal of the cap—he is as vivid as any character in the novel. His function is to be the first dead person Martin knows, and the particularity of his death—a porter, a drinker, a man who smelled of brandy—makes Martin's question about heaven urgent and concrete in a way that the Lord's Prayer, with its "hallowed be Thy name" and "Thy kingdom come," can never be.
