## Overview

Pella Oldhusen never appears directly in the novel. She enters it through a death notice in the newspaper and exits through a story told by Martin's grandmother: she was a "plaguy smart and amusing woman" of independent means who suffered from kleptomania [004-002]. The grandmother's anecdote about Pella stealing her housekeeper's linen underskirts—hanging in the same wardrobe as her own—to have them made into a communion dress for a girl she had taken under her wing is one of the novel's most vivid inset stories, a miniature comic portrait delivered entirely in reported speech.

## Role in the Narrative

Pella Oldhusen is a figure of pure anecdote. Martin's grandmother, reading the newspaper, exclaims "Lord deliver us, did you see there's a Miss Oldhusen has died?" [004-001] and then, correcting herself, explains that the deceased was not the Secretary's wife but his aunt. The story that follows is a set piece of oral narration: the grandmother recalls meeting Pella at Vaxholm, relays the warning her acquaintances used to give ("Be careful, my dear, and don't leave anything around loose this evening; Pella Oldhusen's coming!"), and recounts the episode of the stolen underskirts in forensic detail.

The punchline is Pella's response when confronted: "'Could anyone imagine such rascals?' said Pella. 'That's frightfully annoying, but what can I do about it?'" The grandmother adds, with the fairness of a good storyteller, that Pella gave the housekeeper money for new linen afterward—"for she was well off and not stingy neither"—but the girl "went to the blessed Lord's Supper in the stolen underskirts" [004-002]. The detail is perfect: Pella's kleptomania, her wealth, her generosity, and her imperviousness to shame are all held in a single, absurd equilibrium.

## Key Relationships

- **Secretary Oldhusen** — Her nephew; the "finest tenant" of the Birck family's building.
- **Martin's grandmother** — The teller of Pella's story; she "remembered her very well" and delivers the anecdote with evident relish.

## Significance

Pella Oldhusen belongs to the novel's gallery of offstage characters—people who exist only in anecdote but are rendered with the same precision as the principals. Her story is a miniature of the grandmother's narrative art: earthy, morally untroubled, and deeply satisfying as a piece of gossip. The kleptomaniac who pays for what she steals, the girl who takes communion in contraband linen, the housekeeper who knows exactly who the thief is but cannot prove it—these are the materials of comedy, and the grandmother handles them with the skill of a born raconteur.

## Notable Passages

> "Her name was Pella, Pella Oldhusen. I remember her very well, I met her at Vaxholm. A plaguy smart and amusing woman she was, but she was a kleptomaniac. Her acquaintances used to say, 'Be careful, my dear, and don't leave anything around loose this evening; Pella Oldhusen's coming!'" [004-002]

> "Just the same she gave the housekeeper money for new linen a while afterward, for she was well off and not stingy neither; but the girl went to the blessed Lord's Supper in the stolen underskirts." [004-002]
