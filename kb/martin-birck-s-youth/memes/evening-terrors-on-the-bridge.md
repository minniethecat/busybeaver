## Overview

Johan, the bridge-tender's teenage assistant and Martin's best friend, fills the evening hours with stories no adult would tell: devils in toddy glasses, ministers in league with the devil, and a murdered man whose groans can still be heard on the highroad. Walking home across the dark bridge afterward, Martin is gripped by terror — a fear that Johan himself stokes, then dispels with a burst of song. The scene is one of the novel's most vivid renderings of childhood fear and the rituals that contain it, set within the larger summer world of the Nockeby bridge and its cast of steamboats, sloops, and storytellers.

## The World of the Bridge

The scene takes place during the Birck family's summer sojourn at the bridge-tender's house at Nockeby. The house sat at the abutment of the bridge, and it was a world unto itself:

> "Martin's parents had rented several low-ceiled rooms with rickety white furniture and the bluest window-blinds in the world for the small square windows. Close to these windows passed the state highroad. Here wagoners and wayfarers from the islands of the Malar went by continually to and from the city, all stopping to pay the bridge toll." [009-455]

The bridge-tender himself was a figure of romance: "a little old white-haired man, who had for many years had the tenancy of the bridge and had become a well-to-do citizen." [009-455] He had been a sea captain who "voyaged to many strange lands," and spent his evenings on the porch drinking toddy, "chatting and telling yarns." [009-455]

For Martin, the bridge was the center of a summer universe. He learned to know each steamboat by its whistle: the *Fyris*, the *Garibaldi*, the *Bragë*, the lovely blue *Tynnelsö*, and the brown *Enköping* — called the *Coffeepot* because "it sputtered like boiling coffee." [009-469] "Each boat had for him its particular expression, so that he could distinguish them one from another a long way off." [009-469] But on stormy days, "no steamboats could vie with the coasting sloops for first place in Martin's heart. In every skipper he saw a hero who defied wind and wave to reach some strange, unknown port." [009-469]

It was in this world of water, boats, and summer twilight that Johan told his stories.

## Johan's Stories

Johan was fourteen or fifteen, the bridge-tender's assistant, and Martin's best friend. He made bows and arrows and bark boats, but Martin admired him for something else entirely: "Johan always had so many wonderful things to tell about, things that papa and mamma and grandmother never told about." [009-507]

In the dusk, sitting on a beam by the drawbridge and waiting for the approaching steamboat, Johan would tell his tales. Old Moberg, the bridge-tender, "used to see tiny little devils jumping up and down, up and down, in his toddy glass." [009-507] The minister at Lovö was worse — "a friend of Old Spotty himself" — for how else could he preach for a full hour? Johan had seen the minister's room: "chock-full of books from floor to ceiling. Oh, yes, he was in with the Old Boy sure enough!" [009-507]

But Johan's most terrifying story concerned a murder:

> "It was just there where the wood is so thick on one side, and on the other is a willow alongside of a telegraph pole. It was an evening in November that it happened, and now if anybody goes by at the right time, he can hear the most terrible groaning in the ditch — But they never got the fellow that did it." [009-507]

The specificity of the location — the wood, the willow, the telegraph pole — makes the story terrifyingly local. This is not a distant legend; it happened *right there*, on the road Martin walks every day.

## The Walk Across the Bridge

When Martin heard such things, he squeezed close to Johan's arm, relieved only when the steamboat's lanterns appeared and the familiar thump of the engine called them to work the drawbridge. [009-509] But the walk home was another matter.

When they went home across the bridge, "they were both excited with thoughts of ghosts and murders," and Johan whispered: "Listen, he's after us!" [009-509]

Martin did not know whether "he" was the murderer or the murdered, but "he fancied he heard steps on the bridge and didn't dare to look around." [009-511] This ambiguity — hunter or victim, living or dead — is the essence of the fear. The threat is undefined, and therefore limitless.

## The Song

Johan, who had a cheerful disposition, drove off his fear by striking up a jolly song to the tune of a street ballad:

> "I go to my death wherever I go, *killivillivippombom*!" [009-513]

Martin joined in and sang along with him. [009-515] When they reached the bridge-tender's house, Johan fell silent while Martin continued singing at the top of his voice. [009-517] But old Moberg, sitting on his porch drinking toddy with two farmers, rose in outrage at hearing "a funeral hymn to the tune of an insolent street song" sung by a child. [009-521]

"These are strange times, I may say," Moberg declared, and went inside without finishing his toddy. [009-523]

Moberg's outrage introduces an unexpected coda. What the children experience as a charm against fear — the cheerful nihilism of "I go to my death wherever I go" — the old man hears as blasphemy. The generation gap is also a gap in understanding: the same song that protects Martin offends Moberg, and neither side can see the other's perspective. The scene ends not with the dispelling of fear but with a collision of sensibilities.

## Themes

The bridge scene captures the economy of childhood fear: terror is contagious and communal, passed from Johan to Martin in the darkness, then dispelled — not by rational reassurance but by the older ritual of song. Johan's cheerful nihilism ("I go to my death wherever I go") is the perfect antidote to his own ghost stories, a verbal charm against the supernatural he has himself conjured.

The richly observed world of the bridge — the steamboats, the coasting sloops, the old sea-captain bridge-tender, the wagoners and wayfarers — provides a counterpoint to the supernatural terror. This is a real, solid, working world of tolls and toddy and steamboat whistles. The ghosts and devils Johan describes are intruders from another register, and the song that drives them off restores the bridge to its daytime self.
