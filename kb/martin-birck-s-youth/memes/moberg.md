## Overview

Old Moberg is the bridge-tender at Nockeby, a minor but vividly drawn figure in the middle section of "The Old Street." He is an elderly man who drinks toddy every evening—"people said he couldn't last much longer"—and whose defining trait is an almost pathological unwillingness to confront death. If he heard anyone speak of illness or death, "it was to him as if he had heard something indecent, or indeed it was much worse, for indecent talk rather raised his spirits than offended his ears" [009-001].

He is the employer of Johan, Martin's friend, and serves as a foil to the novel's other attitudes toward mortality: where Uncle Abraham finds life not worth the trouble and ends it, Moberg clings to it with desperate, superstitious refusal. His toddy glass is populated by "tiny little devils jumping up and down, up and down"—hallucinations that blur the line between alcoholic delirium and authentic visionary experience [009-002].

## Role in the Narrative

Moberg appears in a single extended scene that unfolds across the Nockeby bridge on a summer evening. Martin, having learned a funeral hymn set to a "jolly" street tune, sings it while walking home. Moberg hears the song from his porch, where he sits "embowered in hop vines, drinking toddy with two farmers in the light of a round Japanese lantern" [009-001].

The confrontation is at once comic and menacing. Moberg rises with "tottering steps" and confronts Martin, his face gone blue, trembling as he delivers his rebuke: "And this child is supposed to come of respectable people! These are strange times, I may say" [009-001]. Martin looks around for Johan, but Johan has vanished—a desertion that compounds the scene's absurdity.

The episode is framed by Johan's earlier account of Moberg's supernatural visions: the devils in the toddy glass, the minister at Lovö who is "a friend of Old Spotty himself," and a murdered man whose groans can still be heard in the ditch on November evenings [009-002]. These tales invest the bridge-tender's world with a folklore density that Martin's respectable domestic life lacks.

Moberg reappears briefly as a name among Martin's schoolfellows in "The White Cap," mentioned alongside Jansson at the graduation party [016-001], suggesting that the bridge-tender has a son or relative among Martin's classmates—a small link between the world of the Nockeby bridge and the world of the Latin school.

## Key Relationships

- **Johan** — Moberg's young assistant at the drawbridge; Johan both works for him and tells stories about his eccentricities and visions.
- **Martin Birck** — Their single encounter—the funeral-hymn confrontation—is a clash between childhood irreverence and aged terror of death.

## Significance

Moberg represents one pole of the novel's treatment of death: terrified, superstitious refusal. His opposition to death is not philosophical but visceral—death is "indecent," a violation of propriety rather than a metaphysical problem. This stands in sharp contrast to Uncle Abraham's intellectual despair (suicide as rational conclusion) and Martin's father's unreflective acceptance.

The devils in the toddy glass are characteristic of the novel's ambiguous supernaturalism. Are they real—a glimpse of a folk-Catholic underworld coexisting with Stockholm's Protestant modernity? Or are they the projections of an old man's alcoholic solitude? The novel, characteristically, offers both readings and endorses neither.
