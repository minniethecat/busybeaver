## Overview

Harriet Skott (rendered in the text as "Harriet Skottë") is a young woman spending the winter in Stockholm "to study something or other, French or art-tapestry or something of the sort—in order to get engaged, to put it briefly" [023-001]. Martin Birck becomes infatuated with her from a distance, composing an unsent love letter and murmuring her name to himself "Harriet, Harriet" in an effort to conjure her presence [023-002]. The infatuation evaporates the moment it is touched by reality—Henrik Rissler's confession of his own love for Sigrid Tesch—and Martin recognizes his feeling for Harriet as "just a mood burst, which would soon have burst anyhow" [025-001]. She reappears years later at the theater, pale and slender, married to a fat, varnished-looking husband, and Martin observes with a mixture of pity and distance that "she hadn't been too lucky in her marriage choice" [031-001].

## Role in the Narrative

Harriet Skott exists in the novel almost entirely as a projection. Martin never speaks to her directly; he learns her name through discreet inquiry and writes her a letter that remains forever in his drawer. Her most vivid presence in the narrative occurs during Martin's almost Proustian analysis of her name: the forename "Harriet" evokes "something English and pale and blonde, a sensation of tea fumes and benevolence and chilly bedrooms," while the full name "Harriet Skottë" suddenly transforms into "something quite different and new"—he feels "as if she herself passed through the room with her brown hair glinting in a sunbeam" [023-002].

After Henrik's confession, Martin's infatuation collapses. He asks himself what Harriet's engagement, marriage, or even death would mean to him—"Nothing, no reality lost, no expectation gone to shipwreck" [025-001]. The novel grants him a coda: at the theater, years later, he sees her married. Her husband is fat and varnished; she is pale and unhappy. The narrator's judgment is swift and tender: "Poor little child" [031-001].

## Key Relationships

- **Martin Birck** — The would-be lover; his relationship with Harriet is entirely imaginary, a self-conscious exercise in romantic feeling rather than a genuine attachment.
- **Henrik Rissler** — Henrik's real love for Sigrid Tesch serves as the contrast that exposes Martin's infatuation as insubstantial.

## Significance

Harriet Skott is the novel's sharpest study of romantic self-deception. Martin's elaborate name-analysis, his unsent letter, his whispering of "Harriet, Harriet"—all of it is feeling rehearsed rather than felt, a posture learned from books. The novel grants her two appearances, one as a sunbeam-haired dream and one as an unhappy wife, and the gap between them measures the distance between romantic fantasy and adult life. She is the flame that was never lit, the mood that "would soon have burst anyhow."

## Notable Passages

> But if he whispered to himself the whole name, "Harriet Skottë," there came in a new element which quite excluded the others, then it became something quite different and new, then he felt as if she herself passed through the room with her brown hair glinting in a sunbeam. [023-002]

> Martin thought about his love and what he had said about it, and he was at once struck with the fact that at the mere touch of this bit of reality his own love affair evaporated and was gone like mist and dream. Harriet Skottë... He asked himself: "If I should read in the paper tomorrow that she was engaged or married, or that she was dead—what would that signify to me? Nothing, no reality lost, no expectation gone to shipwreck—just a mood burst, which would soon have burst anyhow." [025-001]

> And there, far up in the center, that pale slender woman—was it not she who had been his flame on certain spring evenings many years ago, Harriet Skottë? He had written her a letter, too, which had never been sent... Life had gone a bit poorly with her since then; she did not look happy. She was married now, and her husband was beside her. He was fat, very well dressed and looked as if he had been varnished. [031-001]
