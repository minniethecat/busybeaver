## Overview

Henrik Rissler is Martin Birck's closest friend and intellectual companion in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth* (1907). He is the novel's representative of the life of the mind — a figure who moves naturally between poetry, philosophy, and friendship, and whose critical intelligence provides a counterpoint to Martin's more emotional and aesthetic temperament. Unlike Martin's other schoolfellows, Rissler actively seeks Martin's company outside the classroom, and their conversations — about "girls and God and the immortality of the soul" [016-003] — form one of the novel's central relationships.

Rissler embodies a particular ideal of modern Scandinavian intellectual life at the turn of the century: widely read, philosophically engaged (he is reading Nietzsche), aesthetically attuned (he admires Baudelaire), and emotionally reserved. His observation upon returning from Uppsala — "Here everything is the same, here time has stood still" [024-004] — is one of the novel's most resonant lines, marking the divergence between the friend who has moved outward into the world and the friend who has remained.

## Role in the Narrative

Rissler appears at three key junctures in the novel, each marking a stage in Martin's development.

**First meeting — The concert.** Rissler is introduced during an outdoor concert, sitting with Martin by the balcony railing, "listening to the music, looking out across the terraces, and saying little" [016-002]. His physical description — "a smooth white forehead and calm limpid eyes," a glance that "seemed to slip over the objects nearest it in order more quickly to reach those farther off" [016-002] — signals his intellectual restlessness, his preference for the distant over the immediate. The two friends admire together a lithograph of *Don Juan in Hades* with a motto from Baudelaire, establishing the French Symbolist as a shared reference point for their aesthetic sensibilities.

**Return from Uppsala.** Years later, Rissler reappears at Martin's door, "blinking at the sunlight" [024-001]. He has just taken his preliminary degree and is preparing to travel through Europe while writing his thesis, *On Romantic Irony* — a title that encapsulates the Söderbergian sensibility of ironic detachment. Funded by a wealthy uncle (a "bank lawyer, politician, and millionaire"), Rissler represents the path Martin might have taken: university, travel, critical writing, intellectual independence. Their conversation ranges across "Uppsala, about books and women, and about a new philosopher by the name of Nietzsche" [024-005].

Martin surveys his friend and finds him changed: "his face was leaner, stronger, and more masculine in contour." He speculates about whether Rissler is in love — with his cousin Anna Rissler? With Maria Randel? With Sigrid Tesch? The uncertainty itself is significant: Rissler's inner life, like his glance, seems to slide past the immediate object toward something more distant.

**New Year's gathering.** In the novel's final section, Rissler appears among the guests at a New Year's Eve party. Married now, leading "a migratory life" from one damp apartment to another on the outskirts of Stockholm — from the Vasa Quarter to South Stockholm, and from there to Kungsholm [031-003] — he has not yet "finished the odyssey of the newly married couple." Martin, observing him from across the room, feels that "they would find each other again, if life would only grant them both a little more repose."

## Intellectual Profile

Rissler's intellectual coordinates situate him within the late 19th-century European avant-garde:

| Influence | Evidence | Significance |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Baudelaire** | Admiration for lithograph *Don Juan in Hades* with Baudelaire motto [016-002] | French Symbolism; the aesthetic of modern melancholy |
| **Nietzsche** | Discussed during Uppsala reunion [024-005] | The new philosophy of will, critique of morality, and the death of God |
| **German Romanticism** | Thesis on "Romantic Irony" | Friedrich Schlegel's concept of ironic self-consciousness in art |
| **French Naturalism** | Critical studies for magazines, interest in Flaubert and Maupassant | The modern novel's turn toward unsparing observation |

The constellation of Baudelaire, Nietzsche, and Romantic Irony defines Rissler as a distinctly *modern* intellectual — one for whom traditional beliefs (God, the soul, romantic love) have become objects of critical scrutiny rather than articles of faith.

## Significance

Henrik Rissler serves as Martin's intellectual conscience and alter ego. Where Martin feels deeply and articulates slowly, Rissler thinks rapidly and speaks precisely. His question — "Here everything is the same, here time has stood still" — is both a reproach and an invitation: a reproach to Martin's stasis and an invitation to join him in the world of ideas and movement.

Yet Söderberg complicates the contrast. Rissler's own life, for all its intellectual sophistication, follows the same middle-class pattern he might critique: marriage, migratory apartments, literary journalism. His thesis on Romantic Irony remains, as far as the novel tells us, unfinished. The gap between Rissler's critical intelligence and his actual life mirrors the novel's larger theme — the distance between what we understand and how we live.

## Notable Passages

> They used to go to each other's homes in the afternoon to talk and smoke cigarettes, and once in a while they had gone on long walks together, often in rain, snow, or wind, out to the park or through the suburbs, talking the while of everything that concerns young men, of girls and God and the immortality of the soul.
