## Overview

The kiss in the moonlit stairway is the climactic love scene of *Martin Birck's Youth*. Coming immediately after the Hamlet performance, it transposes the play's themes — death, memory, the impossibility of holding onto love — into a moment of transcendent physical union. The kiss becomes a voyage through space and time, dissolving the boundaries between her and him, summer and winter, joy and anguish, the living and the dead.

## The Walk Home

The winter night has stopped snowing. Martin and his mistress walk home "in a white moonlit mist through the snowdrifts" [030-074]. They enter her building and climb the stairs. "It got brighter and brighter the higher they climbed" [030-074] — the ascent is literal but also symbolic, a rising out of the mist of the everyday into clarity.

## The Window

They stop at a stairway window. Below them, "the greater part of the mist was now below them, it lay wrapped around the yards and open spaces beneath, but in the upper regions of the air everything was almost clear; it was bluish and bright as a night in August" [030-074]. A wide ring of light surrounds the moon. The world below appears "icebound and petrified." From the mist rises "a lonely gable wall without a window, which absorbed the cold glance of the moon and stared blindly and emptily back" [030-074].

This gable wall — blank, blind, absorbing light without returning it — is an image of utter loneliness, the world outside their love. A long shiver goes through them both, and "they pressed hard against each other, closing their eyes, and everything was lost to them in a kiss" [030-074].

## The Vision

The kiss releases a synesthetic vision. Martin hears "the sound of distant bells from a little country church far away between hedges and wheat fields" and sees a summer garden: "red peonies were glowing from the flower beds, white and yellow butterflies were fluttering about the bushes and the lawn" [030-076]. But autumn enters the vision — "the yellow butterflies were yellow leaves, and some were already dark with frost" — and with it the echo of the actor's voice from the theater: "I loved Ophelia" [030-076].

The kiss thus contains both love and its inevitable loss. Hamlet's words remind us that Ophelia is dead — that all love ends — even as the kiss itself seems to transcend time.

## The Red Star

Martin does not relinquish her mouth. He seems to be "voyaging through space: in the white moon-mist burned a red star, first faint and expiring, then more powerful and ever nearer, growing and broadening into a flaming spring of fire, to which he fastened his lips tightly" [030-078].

The red star is a transformation of Venus — the "solitary and mighty star" that Henrik pointed out at Hasselbacken, and the star Martin watched through Harriet Skottë's window. But here it is no longer distant and cold: it becomes a "flaming spring of fire," a source that he drinks from directly.

The passage ends with one of the novel's most extraordinary sentences: "He seemed to burn without suffering, the flames cooled his tongue like a slightly bitter wine, until he felt that he was drinking in everything: satiety and hunger, thirst and coolness, the sun's health and the midnight's anguish, the lucid thought of day and the morbid brooding of moonlit dusk, all the joy and all the misery of the earth — from this one spring" [030-078].
