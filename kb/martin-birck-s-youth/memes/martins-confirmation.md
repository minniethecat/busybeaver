## Overview

Martin Birck's confirmation, which takes place when he is sixteen, is the novel's central rite-of-passage event. In the Lutheran tradition of late-19th-century Sweden, confirmation marked the transition from childhood religious instruction to full adult participation in the church — and, by extension, in the moral community. For Martin, however, the experience does the opposite: it crystallizes his doubts, alienates him from the faith of his fathers, and forces him to confront the gap between public ritual and private conviction.

The event is narrated across two chapters of "The White Cap" section. Söderberg presents it not as a single dramatic scene but as a process — the weeks of religious instruction, the mounting pressure to believe, the ceremony itself, and the lasting aftermath of intellectual estrangement. The confirmation becomes, in the novel's architecture, the point at which Martin's childhood world of unexamined belief definitively ends.

## Context

Martin had "been a Christian up to his sixteenth year" [020-001], accepting what his elders taught as true without active doubt. Religion had been "a detail of school work set side by side with other details" [020-002]. But the approach of confirmation transforms it into "the one essential, that which daily demanded his time and consideration" [020-002].

The social context is one of near-universal Lutheran conformity. Martin's father accepts confirmation as a duty — he "reverenced the proverb: Speech is silver, silence is golden" [020-004] — and neither questions the institution nor encourages his son to do so. Martin's classmates, for their part, treat the whole affair with "mere thoughtlessness and conventionality" [020-002], an evasion that Martin finds intellectually unavailable to himself.

## Narrative Account

**The Religious Instruction.** The weeks leading up to confirmation are described as an intensification of religious pressure. The requirement to weep when one "went forward" is presented as a social expectation rather than a spiritual experience. Martin, unable to accept the easy conventionality of his peers, frames the question in stark binary terms: "if religion was the truth, then it was right in this claim, the claim to be above everything else, and he must devote all his powers and his whole soul to it; he must become religious. But if it was not the truth, then he must seek the truth wherever he could find it; he must become a freethinker" [020-002].

**The Ceremony.** The confirmation and first communion take place on "a spring day with sun and tender green in the old trees of the churchyard" [020-004]. The sensory richness of the occasion — "the bells roar and sing and the organ begin the processional hymn" — moves Martin to tears, but the tears are those of grief rather than faith: "he grieved in his heart that he was not as the others and could not believe and feel as they did" [020-004].

The moment turns from pathos to disgust when Martin observes the mechanics of the ceremony: "when he came to the actual ceremonies and saw the ministers in their surplices going back and forth before the altar, while they dispensed the bread and wine and carried napkins over their arms like waiters, he felt faint and disgusted and could not understand that he had let himself be fooled into such mummery" [020-004]. The comparison of ministers to waiters is characteristically Söderbergian — a deflationary simile that collapses the sacred into the mundane.

**The Inner Crisis.** Throughout the ceremony, Martin is tormented by the question of collective belief versus individual doubt: "How is it possible that all these can believe, and not I? It's mad to think that I alone can be right against all these and against all the dead who sleep in their graves out there, who lived and died in the faith I reject" [020-004]. The argument from consensus — the sheer weight of the believing dead — exerts a pressure that is emotional rather than intellectual. It is the clearest expression in the novel of the loneliness that accompanies intellectual independence.

| **Mr. Birck** | Martin's father, who regards confirmation as a civic duty and offers no counsel on its spiritual dimension |
| **The ministers** | Unnamed clergy who "shuffled about there in the gloom"; they appear to Martin as "shameless hypocrites" during the ceremony |
| **The other neophytes** | Martin's peers, who treat confirmation with "thoughtlessness and conventionality," providing a foil to Martin's anguished sincerity |

## Significance

The confirmation event is the novel's most concentrated treatment of religious doubt, and it establishes a pattern that will recur throughout Martin's adult life: the inability to accept what "everyone" believes, coupled with a painful awareness of the social cost of dissent. Martin does not reject Christianity from a position of confident atheism; he rejects it because he cannot make himself believe, and he cannot pretend to believe when the pretense would be a form of dishonesty.

This episode also marks the moment when Martin's relationship to language itself changes. The words of the liturgy, which once carried emotional weight, now strike him as empty gestures. The "deep and wonderful old words" that later, at a performance of *Hamlet*, will move him to tears [029-005], are here reduced to "mummery." The confirmation thus initiates Martin's lifelong search for a language — poetic, philosophical, or simply honest — that can bear the weight of genuine conviction.

In the broader arc of the novel, the confirmation stands between the unreflective faith of childhood and the melancholy freethinking of adulthood. It is the door through which Martin passes — alone, and without the comfort of shared belief — into the intellectual solitude that will define his later years.

## Notable Passages

> But when he was sixteen he was confirmed. Up to then religion had been a detail of school work set side by side with other details; now it became all of a sudden the one essential, that which daily demanded his time and consideration. The question could not be appeased by the thought: "This is just a matter of the emotions," since it was customary to weep when one "went forward." It freely developed the claim to be the highest of all, the dominant force in life, the one thing that mattered.
