## Overview

Uncle Abraham is a Jewish doctor who occupies a distinctive place in the Birck family's social circle. Unlike the other relatives Martin visits on New Year's Day, Uncle Abraham lives alone—a widower with no children, attended only by an old housekeeper [006-001]. He possesses a sophisticated, slightly exotic sensibility: a black beard, a long crooked nose, a parrot "that could swear in French," and a black tomcat named Kolmodin, "the cleverest cat in the world" who could pull a bell-cord to be let into rooms [006-001].

He appears to Martin's parents as a figure of pathos: they say he "was not a happy man" and "never was really cheerful" [006-002]. Yet to young Martin, he is also a source of wondrous gifts and strange, philosophical humor—most notably the "Red Turk" jumping-jack and the parable about free will that accompanies it. His eventual suicide by hanging is recorded in a single devastating sentence, yet his image persists in Martin's dreams as "a dark and disturbing riddle" [012-001].

## Role in the Narrative

Uncle Abraham first appears in the novel as one of Martin's father's old student comrades, with whom he sings "Gluntar" and improvises on the piano [004-001]. His most extended portrait occurs during the New Year's visits, when Martin and Maria are sent to pay their respects [006-001]. The scene is atmospherically dense: a large, half-dark room full of books, snow falling outside in great flakes, and Uncle Abraham sitting alone at a table with a bottle of wine, playing chess against himself.

In a subsequent visit, he gives Martin the Red Turk—a pasteboard jumping-jack that becomes, through his commentary, a philosophical toy. Uncle Abraham explains that the Red Turk is "no common pasteboard man; he can think and choose the same as we," and that when his strings are pulled he tells himself: "I am a being with free will, I kick just as I want to and exclusively for my own entertainment" [006-003]. This parable of free will and self-deception is Uncle Abraham's most sustained speech in the novel—and his last significant appearance before his death.

The novel records his suicide with characteristic compression: he "had hanged himself with a stove-cord one rainy day, when he didn't find it worth the trouble to live any more" [012-001]. The sentence is placed in a passage about forgetting—Martin has already forgotten the Red Turk—and the swiftness of the narration mirrors the erasure of memory in childhood.

## Key Relationships

- **Martin Birck** — Uncle Abraham appears exclusively through Martin's childhood perspective. He gives Martin the Red Turk, whose philosophical lesson about free will haunts the boy long after the gift is forgotten.
- **Martin's father** — An old student comrade; they share music, song, and conviviality.
- **Maria Birck** — Accompanies Martin on the New Year's visit; shares the oppressive silence of the half-dark room.
- **Kolmodin the cat** — Uncle Abraham's clever tomcat, who pulls the bell-cord to enter rooms and stares "gloomily at his master" before the porcelain stove [006-001].

## Significance

Uncle Abraham embodies one of the novel's central preoccupations: the question of whether life is worth living, and on what terms. His suicide—committed on a "rainy day" for the simplest of reasons: he "didn't find it worth the trouble"—is the starkest negation the novel offers. It stands in counterpoint to Martin's father's quiet contentment, old Moberg's terrified clinging to life, and Martin's own evolving melancholy.

His Jewishness, mentioned without elaboration, adds a dimension of social otherness that the novel neither exploits nor develops—it is simply part of his portrait, like the black beard and the parrot. The chess game played alone, the half-dark room full of books, the wine bottle: these details construct an image of intellectual solitude that is never explained, only shown. The Red Turk episode is a miniature of the novel's epistemological concerns—if we are all pasteboard men, pulling our own strings while telling ourselves we choose freely, then Uncle Abraham's suicide is not a failure of understanding but its terminal achievement.

## Notable Passages

> Uncle Abraham was a doctor and had often cured Martin and Maria of measles and scarlatina and pains in the chest. He had a black beard and a long crooked nose, for he was a Jew. He had also a parrot that could swear in French, and a black tomcat. The cat was named Kolmodin and he was the cleverest cat in the world, for when he was outside the office door and wanted to get in, he didn't mew as other cats do, but got up on his hind feet, caught his claws in the bell-cord, and pulled it hard. [006-001]

> "You see," he had said one evening, "in itself it is neither amusing nor remarkable that an old pasteboard man kicks about when one pulls the strings. But the Red Turk is no common pasteboard man; he can think and choose the same as we. And when you jerk the strings and he begins to prance, he says to himself: 'I am a being with free will, I kick just as I want to and exclusively for my own entertainment. Hoho! there's nothing so delightful as to kick.'" [006-003]

> And Uncle Abraham, who had given him to Martin and who had hanged himself with a stove-cord one rainy day, when he didn't find it worth the trouble to live any more, was soon forgotten as well, though he now and again came up in Martin's dreams as a dark and disturbing riddle. [012-001]
