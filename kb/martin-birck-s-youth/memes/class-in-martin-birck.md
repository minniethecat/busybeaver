## Overview

Social class in *Martin Birck's Youth* operates less as an explicit theme than as an atmospheric condition — the unstated framework within which every character moves. Martin's father, a government clerk and "son of a mechanic," measures his contentment not against his superiors but against "the poor home from which he had come," concluding that "he was lucky and only wished that the luck he had should never be dimmed" [004-001]. This quiet self-positioning — neither resentful nor ambitious — establishes the Birck family's place in Stockholm's social order: respectable but precarious, comfortable but without margin.

The novel maps class across multiple sites: the schoolroom, where boys sort themselves by intellectual and social rank; the office, where von Heringslake's income of forty-six hundred crowns buys "the pleasant ease which comes with an independence"; the streets, where streetwalkers and lamplighters and ragmen occupy the city's lower depths; and the ballroom, where young women of the better classes are preserved for marriage while their male counterparts visit prostitutes without stigma. Throughout, Martin occupies an uneasy middle position — not poor enough to be pitied, not rich enough to be free, not conventional enough to accept the system without question.

Martin's own confrontation with class difference comes through his friendship with Henrik Rissler, to whom he confesses his repulsion at the thought of paid sex with women of a lower class:

> "Tell me, do you think it's possible for a man to live chaste till real happiness in love comes to him? That's surely what one would wish. To be with women whom one has no feeling for, who belong to another class, who have dirty linen and use ugly words and only think about being paid — that must be loathsome."
>
> (016-012)
