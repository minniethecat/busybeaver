## Overview

Josef Marin is one of Martin Birck's schoolmates, a slight and pale figure who was intended by his "obstinate old mother" to become a clergyman but who "was still not quite settled in his faith" [016-002]. At the graduation party he emerges as a voice of earnest theological doubt, declaring his belief in God while refusing to conceive of Him "as a personal being" [016-003]. As the punch flows he becomes a convulsive prophet, reading out "long rigmaroles" of marriage announcements mixed with Tegnér and Norbeck [016-005]. When Martin encounters him again years later at the theater, he has not become a clergyman: instead he covers "the music halls and funerals for a large newspaper," his faith still unconsummated—"it is often with faith as with appetite—it comes with eating; but he had never got to where the eating began" [031-002].

## Role in the Narrative

Josef Marin appears principally in two scenes separated by many years. In Chapter IV of Part Two, he is a central figure at the student graduation supper. He opens the evening with a toast "to the ontological proof"—a joke met with "rather halfhearted acclaim" [016-001]—and later presses forward, pale and slightly drunk, to declare his qualified belief in God during Doctor Markel's philosophical lecture [016-003]. As the night wears on and he drinks more, he mutters that Markel "ought to have a smack on the jaw" [016-004] and later can only "with difficulty" be "restrained from prophesying"—his prophecies being a collage of newspaper announcements, Tegnér, and theological fragments recited "in the solemn monotone with which he imagined Elisha had chastised Ahab" [016-005]. After the party breaks up, he insists on prophesying before the statue of Charles XII in the square [016-006].

Years later, Martin spots him at the theater: "that little wrinkled face that reminded one both of a child's and of an old man's." The narrator traces his failed vocation—he had "at bottom perhaps a thirst for sincerity which made his course a bit too difficult"—and describes his journalistic accommodation: he writes what he thinks, takes pains to think as the editor does, while the editor thinks as "the educated and well-to-do folk of the community thought" [031-002].

## Key Relationships

- **Martin Birck** — Schoolmate; Marin's trajectory from theological skeptic to compromised journalist mirrors Martin's own struggles with faith and vocation.
- **Doctor Markel** — Marin is hostile to Markel's philosophical lecture, muttering that he "ought to have a smack on the jaw" [016-004].
- **Gabel, Planius, Tullman, Moberg** — Fellow schoolmates at the graduation party; Marin stands out among them for his earnest, slightly absurd intensity.

## Significance

Josef Marin embodies the novel's treatment of religious doubt as a slow attrition rather than a dramatic crisis. His faith is not lost—it is never "settled." The narrator's diagnosis—that he "had never got to where the eating began"—is one of the novel's most precise metaphors for the erosion of conviction. His later career as a journalist, writing what he thinks while thinking what the editor thinks, is a miniature of the novel's satire of intellectual accommodation. He is the clergyman who never was, the prophet who prophesies nonsense, the believer who believes without a personal God—a character whose contradictions are never resolved, only abandoned.

## Notable Passages

> Josef Marin was short and slight; he looked pale and overworked. The two or three glasses of punch he had drunk had already made him a bit convivial; but now when he heard a serious question proposed and could not see that there was any joke behind it, he broke in with all the earnestness he could summon up at the moment: "I believe in God. But I don't conceive Him as a personal being." [016-003]

> It was only with difficulty that Josef Marin could be restrained from prophesying. When Josef Marin prophesied he would read out long rigmaroles of stuff, marriage announcements from the Daily News mixed with bits from Tegnér's "Svea" and Norbeck's Theology, all recited in the solemn monotone with which he imagined Elisha had chastised Ahab, and Ezekiel foretold the destruction of Israel and Judah. [016-005]

> He had never become a clergyman as he should have according to the ideas of his obstinate old mother. But he never got firm in his faith. It is often with faith as with appetite—it comes with eating; but he had never got to where the eating began, and he had also at bottom perhaps a thirst for sincerity which made his course a bit too difficult. [031-002]
