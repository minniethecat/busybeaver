## Overview

Martin Birck wants to be a poet. This is not a passing fancy or an adolescent affectation — it is the organizing desire of his inner life, the identity around which all his other selves are constellated. But Söderberg's novel is not a portrait of the artist as a young man in the triumphant mode of Joyce; it is a study in the impossibility of authentic artistic vocation within the constraints of bourgeois society. Martin's poetry never finds its audience, and more troublingly, it never satisfies Martin himself. Each New Year he discards the previous year's work as "childish and worthless." The poet he meets on the street corner — pale, syphilitic, shaking his fist at gas lamps — is a walking cautionary tale. This article traces the arc of Martin's poetic ambition from dream to disillusionment, arguing that the novel's real subject is not the making of a poet but the systematic foreclosure of poetry as a viable mode of truthful living.

## The Mother's Dream

Mrs. Birck loves poetry. She weeps when Martin reads her his verses, and she dreams that he might one day become a great poet — decorated with the Order of Vasa, like the auditor in the Court of Exchequer she knew in her youth who "wrote verses about everything that was grand and beautiful, about the sea and the sun and the king" (015-003). Her vision of the poetic life is entirely institutional: Martin would sit at an office desk on weekdays and compose verses about sunsets on Sundays, send them to the Swedish Academy, win a prize, and become "at the same time a great poet and a respected business man with an assured income." Tegnér was a bishop; Bellman worked in the lottery bureau — why should Martin be any different?

Mrs. Birck's dream, offered in all maternal sincerity, is the novel's most devastating satire of bourgeois aesthetics. Poetry, in her conception, is a decoration — something you fit into the margins of a respectable life, like the silk dress she no longer owns. The idea that poetry might challenge the order that awards Vasa medals, that it might be dangerous or disruptive or true, simply does not occur to her. And yet she is not stupid; she genuinely loves the verses Martin reads her. Her error is not aesthetic but categorical: she cannot conceive of art as anything other than an ornament to respectability.

## Martin's Counter-Dream

Martin dreams something very different. His fantasy of the poetic life, elaborated in a long passage of free indirect discourse (015-004), is a Romantic bildungsroman in miniature: he will search for truth, give what he finds to mankind, become famous, earn money, buy a house for his father and a silk dress for his mother. He will be envied by men and sought after by women, but he will love only one woman — one who loves another man — so that "this unhappy love should give his thoughts depth and bitterness and his poems wings."

But this fantasy contains the seed of its own destruction. Even as Martin imagines his triumph, he foresees its hollowness: "he had a dark feeling that while he sought for truth he should only find truths." He will despise himself for reaping honor for "the morsels he had found by accident," and this self-contempt will "eat into his soul and make of him an empty husk." The fantasy ends not in glory but in suicide — "a glass of dark red wine spiced with a strange herb" — and Martin goes to sleep to "awake no more."

What is remarkable about this passage is that Martin, still an adolescent dreaming in his childhood bedroom, already knows the entire arc of his disillusionment. The dream contains the nightmare; the Romantic script is legible to him as a script, and he can already feel the falseness that will attend its fulfillment. This is not youthful naivety but something more unsettling: a clarity that arrives too early, before it can be put to any practical use.

## The Social Impossibility of Poetry

When Martin's father asks what he intends to be, Martin cannot answer "a poet." The novel explains with surgical precision why: "People would turn away their heads and put their hands over their mouths" (018-003). A lawyer, a painter, a musician — these are professions with institutions behind them, paths one can point to. "But he who is to become a poet is nothing but a mockery before God and man until he is recognized and famous." Poetry, uniquely among the arts, has no institutional middle ground between amateur and celebrity. There is no "apprentice poet" position in the civil service.

So Martin tells his father he will work in a government office, and his father is delighted. The lie is the first practical consequence of Martin's vocation: to become a poet, he must pretend to be something else. The "false sign" he hangs over his door is the price of admission to a society that has no category for the life he wants to lead. The irony is that the office, once entered, will consume the time and energy that poetry requires — the false sign becomes the real life, and poetry retreats to the margins his mother had always imagined for it.

## The Office and the Empty Gesture

The novel's later chapters trace the consequences. Martin writes "now and then some poems and stories to earn a little money and to prove how far his words could follow his thoughts," but "with every new year all he had written in the old seemed to him childish and worthless, and he felt that nothing would amount to anything which could not fill him completely with the joy of creation" (020-007). The office work — arriving late, leaving early, performing "almost automatically the sum of actions, or more properly gestures" — is the institutional complement to the poetry that never arrives. Both are forms of going through the motions.

Martin's self-diagnosis is exact: nothing he writes can fill him "completely with the joy of creation." This is an impossibly high standard, and he knows it. But the standard is not arbitrary — it is the logical consequence of his original dream. If poetry is to be the vehicle of truth, the thing that justifies a life, then anything less than complete creative joy is a sign that the vehicle is empty.

## Verse as Costume

Martin's encounter with the established poet (023-009) crystallizes the novel's critique of poetry as a social form. The poet, with his "thin yellow-green ulster" and "thin red beard," declares himself a decadent who worships "everything that was disintegrating, rotten at the core, and doomed to destruction." He hates the sun, loves green alcohol, has "most of the well-known venereal diseases," and cannot cross Gustavus Adolphus Place diagonally — a symptom he treasures as "the forerunner of general paralysis."

And yet his published poems are indistinguishable from the "loveliest verses" that win Academy prizes — verses "mostly about girls and flowers and June nights on the lowlands of Scania." When Martin gently points out the discrepancy, the poet draws a knife. The poems he publishes, he explains, are "mere skits. Good enough for the mob." His real work — presumably the poems that would express his decadent inner life — remains unpublished, perhaps unwritten.

This is the novel's darkest joke about poetry. The gap between the poet's lived experience (venereal disease, impending madness, knife in pocket) and his published verse (girls, flowers, June nights) is not hypocrisy but something closer to structural necessity. The institutions that publish and reward poetry — the Academy, the newspapers, the "well-known critic" who mildly deplores "a certain tinge of sensualism" — demand a product that bears no relation to the life that produces it. The young Martin who dreamed of speaking truth through verse has become, or will become, the older poet who publishes "skits" for the mob while his real self festers in private.

Martin has already seen this fate coming. His adolescent fantasy of poetic glory ended in painted cheeks, a mask-like countenance, and a suicide by poison — a prophetic image of the poet who can maintain the performance but not the self that performs it. The decadent poet on the street corner is the living confirmation: poetry, as actually practiced in Stockholm, is a costume worn over decay.

## What Remains of the Dream

By the novel's end, Martin's poetic ambition has not been renounced so much as hollowed out. He still writes, occasionally; he still sends poems to the *Home Friend* and receives a few crowns. But the dream of poetry as a vehicle for truth — the dream that distinguished Martin from his mother's fantasy of Vasa medals and Sunday versifying — has collapsed under the weight of its own contradictions. Truth cannot be spoken in verse without becoming "just verse" (022-003), and verse cannot earn a living without becoming a commodity.

Martin's tragedy, in his poetic life as in his education, is that he sees too clearly to succeed. He understands that the institutions of poetry — the Academy, the critics, the reading public — are structured to reward exactly what he despises: the ornamental, the inoffensive, the pretty. He understands that "ideas, when woven into verse and beautiful words, were no longer contraband; they were not even noticed" (022-004). And he understands, perhaps most painfully, that this understanding does not make him a better poet — it makes him a worse one, because it steals from him the naivety that poetry requires. The poet who knows too much about how poetry is consumed cannot write it with a whole heart.

## Conclusion

Martin Birck wants to be a poet. The novel grants him everything he needs for that vocation — sensitivity, intelligence, the capacity for deep feeling, even a modest talent — and withholds only the one thing that would make it possible: a world in which poetry can be true. The world he actually inhabits — the world of government offices and Vasa medals, of Academy prizes and "friendly" reviews, of verses about June nights that bear no relation to the life of the man who writes them — makes poetry impossible without self-deception. And self-deception is the one thing Martin, for all his flaws, cannot manage.

He is left, at the novel's close, in a condition the book has been preparing from the start: a poet without poetry, a vocation without a practice, a self formed around an absence. It is not the worst fate the novel imagines — the decadent poet's general paralysis is worse — but it is, in its quiet way, a kind of death. The death of the boy who dreamed of writing a book that would change the world, replaced by the man who understands that the world does not want to be changed, only decorated.

## Notable Passages

> But Martin dreamed other dreams. He wanted to be a poet. He would write a book; a novel or a lyric sequence, or best of all a drama of ideas in the same verse form as Brand or Peer Gynt. He would devote his life to searching for the truth and giving to mankind what he found or thought he had found of it. He would also become famous, a great man; he would earn a lot of money, he would buy a little house for his father and a new silk dress for his mother—her old one was worn and faded.
