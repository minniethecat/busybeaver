## Overview

On a winter afternoon at the General Post Office, the chief clerk von Heringslake introduces a small domestic ritual into the bureaucratic space: roasting apples over the embers of the government stove. The scene, rendered with Söderberg's characteristic attention to sensory detail and mild irony, captures the intersection of official routine and private comfort, and Martin's outsider perspective on both.

## The Scene

"It was warm in the room, the remains of a great birchwood fire glowed in the porcelain stove, for there was no inducement to spare the government's wood in these times when one had to skimp one's fuel at home" [029-151]. Von Heringslake "sat squatted in front of the stove and roasted apples over the embers. On his bald pate — which his mortal enemy, Auditor Camin, asserted was the result of early dissipations but which in reality shone with the innocence of early childhood — glinted the triangular reflection of a green lamp" [029-151].

The fragrance of roasting apples fills the office and reaches Martin at his desk. He is "bitterly annoyed that he had not in all ways the same views concerning this and the future life as Heringslake, for then he would surely have been offered an apple" [029-151]. The thought is characteristic of Martin's self-irony — he recognizes that his alienation from conventional belief costs him not only metaphysical comfort but also roasted apples.

## The Office Atmosphere

Around Heringslake, the office continues its familiar rhythms. From Auditor Camin's desk sounds "for the hundredth time the old pronouncement, 'The country will never be right till we make the farmers pay for shooting licenses'" [029-151]. At the bottom table near the door, where it is drafty and smells of wet umbrellas, the youngest clerks work and whisper about "the orgies of last night and the number of punch bottles emptied" [029-151].

After finishing his apples, Heringslake resumes his place beside Martin and "surveyed him mournfully" [029-165]. The gesture is small but suggests a recognition — perhaps of Martin's youth, perhaps of the life of bureaucratic routine that awaits him.

## Significance

The apple-roasting scene is a minor moment that encapsulates the novel's treatment of office life. The government stove, the cheap fuel, the petty rivalries, the smell of apples cutting through the odor of damp coats — these details build a world in which Martin is present but not at home. Heringslake's small domestic ritual represents a form of adaptation to bureaucratic life that Martin cannot achieve.
