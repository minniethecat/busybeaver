## Overview

On a gray October afternoon at Ida Dupont's house, the first snowfall of the year begins. Martin and Ida kneel together on a chair, arms around each other's necks, noses pressed against the cold pane, watching the heavy flakes transform the world outside. The moment is brief, luminous, and wordless — one of the novel's purest renderings of childhood's capacity for absorption in sensory experience. It is also a threshold: the snow that falls outside the window marks the end of autumn and the beginning of the long Stockholm winter, while inside, Ida's Catholic ritual introduces Martin to a religious world utterly foreign to his Lutheran upbringing.

## The Snow Begins

Martin and Ida had been absorbed in their banquet — dressing up in ballet costumes, blackening mustaches with burnt cork, and drinking sweet dark-red wine from green glasses. [003-253] It was then that the world outside intruded:

> "Outside it had begun to snow. There were great heavy flakes; the window sill was already white. It was the first snowfall, and the church bells rang in the black cupola: *Bing bong, bing bong!*" [003-255]

Martin and Ida knelt together on a chair, "their arms around each other's necks and their noses pressed against the pane." [003-255] The image — two children silhouetted against the falling snow, the church bells tolling — captures a particular quality of childhood intimacy: physical closeness, shared attention, the suspension of time. Söderberg renders the moment with a precise, cinematic composition: the dark room, the white window, the two heads pressed against the glass, the black cupola and its tolling bells beyond.

## After the Snow

The snowfall marked a transition. Ida poured out more wine and clinked glasses with Martin, then took down an old violin from the wall. She played and danced, swaying in a white veil: "It sounded very queer the way Ida played the violin. Martin held his ears, laughed, sung, and screamed." [003-257] But then Martin felt "a creepy feeling down his back, and he recalled that his mother had said Ida Dupont had fleas." [003-257]

The intrusive thought — fleas — is a characteristic Söderberg touch. Just as the scene threatens to become too lyrical, the narrator punctures it with a detail of physical discomfort, a child's half-understood echo of adult prejudice. The violin, the dancing, the white veil: all are suddenly shadowed by the possibility of dirt.

## The Catholic Ritual

The spell of the snowfall gave way to a different kind of discovery. In the sleeping alcove, in the semidarkness, Martin noticed "an image of the madonna behind two half-burned wax candles, and below hung a crucifix." [003-259] He stared in astonishment: "What's that?" [003-263]

Ida became solemn: "That is our religion." [003-265] Her father was a Catholic — a fact that, in Lutheran Stockholm, marked the Dupont household as different. Ida offered: "Wait, sit over there and be quiet, and I'll teach you our religion." [003-269]

What follows is a remarkable scene of childhood ritual improvisation:

> "Ida swathed herself in pink tulle with gold spangles. Then she advanced and lighted the candles under the madonna, two calm bright flames. On a little stand below the crucifix she lighted a pastille of incense. In long blue clouds the incense curled from under the curtain of the alcove, and the air grew heavy with a strong spicy fragrance." [003-271]

The madonna glowed "like a theatre queen with red, blue, and gold, and the stars on her mantle blinked and sparkled in the light of the wax candles." [003-273] Martin shivered with delight. [003-275]

Ida fell on her knees before the madonna. "Her thick, dark-red plaits glowed like bright copper in the candlelight. She muttered something which Martin did not understand, and made strange gestures with her hands." [003-277]

"What's that?" inquired Martin; "why do you act so?" [003-279]

For Martin, the Catholic ritual is pure theater — beautiful, incomprehensible, and slightly frightening. The madonna is "like a theatre queen," the incense is spicy and strange, the Latin-sounding mutterings are opaque. Ida, who earlier played at being a banquet hostess, now plays at being a priestess, and the line between game and genuine devotion is deliberately blurred.

## The Winter Context

The first snowfall participates in the novel's larger seasonal imagery. Winter in Martin's world is associated with specific sensory markers: "sleigh bells and snow and ice-flowers on the windowpane." [003-407] Martin's mother called the ice-flowers "the dead summer flowers come back again." [003-407] Christmas brought "a dark and timid fir which smelt of the woods," and the star of New Year's Eve "was burning across the white roofs, and people said to each other, 'Good night, and thanks for the year!'" [003-407]

The first snowfall in October, then, is the opening note of a long seasonal movement — the first of many snowfalls that will carry Martin through Christmas, New Year's, and the gray winter days that seem "interminably long till summer." [003-407] But on this particular afternoon, none of that matters. There is only the snow, the window, and the two children watching.

## Themes

The first snowfall scene is structured around a series of thresholds: the seasonal threshold of the year's first snow, the sensory threshold between the warm indoor world of play and the cold world outside, and the cultural threshold between Martin's Lutheran upbringing and the Duponts' Catholicism. The snow itself — silent, transformative, indifferent — is the perfect backdrop for a moment of childhood wonder that flickers between the sacred and the profane, between the intimacy of two children at a window and the tolling bells of the larger world.

Ida's Catholic ritual, performed with theatrical costumes and incense, is both a genuine expression of her family's faith and another form of dress-up play. The madonna is at once a religious icon and a "theatre queen" — and for Ida, there is no contradiction. This fusion of the sacred and the theatrical is the gift the Dupont household gives to Martin: a glimpse of a world where religion wears pink tulle and smells of incense, utterly unlike the restrained Lutheranism of his own home.
