## Overview

The arrival at Nockeby Bridge marks the beginning of the pastoral interlude at the heart of Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. The Birck family rents rooms from old Moberg, the bridge-tender, and the first evening — with packing boxes still "higgledy-piggledy" — establishes a landscape that will become the theater of Martin's most idyllic summer experiences.

## The Journey

Martin's parents had rented "several low-ceiled rooms with rickety white furniture and the bluest window-blinds in the world for the small square windows." The white house stood at the abutment of Nockeby Bridge, and close to its windows passed the state highroad, where "wagoners and wayfarers from the islands of the Malar went by continually to and from the city, all stopping to pay the bridge toll" [009-001].

## The First Evening

On the evening of the first day, the rooms are still in disarray: "packing boxes, trunks, and clothes-baskets were still standing higgledy-piggledy in the room — which still looked a little strange, though every wardrobe and chair, every flower in the wallpaper seemed to say, 'We shall soon get acquainted.'" The evening meal — "butter and cheese and some small broiled fish" — is spread by the window [009-001].

Martin sits silent "on the corner of a chest surveying the strange and new picture": the gray highroad with telegraph poles in which the wind sings, the dark shadowy figures of horses and peasants outlined against the greenish-blue western sky. Obliquely across the way is a slope with a clump of oaks, their verdure standing out "strong and heavy in the summer twilight." Among these oaks, one — "naked and black" — cannot put out leaves like the others, and in its branches "the crows had built a nest" [009-001].

The bridge-tender's cottage itself is a world unto itself:

> Close to these windows passed the state highroad. Here wagoners and wayfarers from the islands of the Malar went by continually to and from the city, all stopping to pay the bridge toll, for the white house belonged to the bridge-tender and stood just at the abutment of Nockeby bridge.
>
> (009-002)

## The Dream Tree

Martin cannot take his eyes from the black tree with the crow's nest. He feels he knows this tree — that he has "seen it before, or heard a story about it." The image lodges in his consciousness and will later enter his dreams, becoming one of the novel's recurring symbols of the uncanny within the pastoral — a dark presence at the edge of the summer idyll [009-001].

## Significance

The arrival scene establishes the Nockeby landscape as a space suspended between the known and the unknown. The "naked and black" oak — refusing to leaf like its neighbors — is an early intimation of Söderberg's method: beauty is never unmixed, and the pastoral always contains within it the seed of darker knowledge. The telegraph poles singing in the wind, the crows' nest, the bridge-tender "twined about with hop vines, drinking toddy" — these details together compose a world at once strange and familiar, the perfect setting for the discoveries that await Martin across the summer.
