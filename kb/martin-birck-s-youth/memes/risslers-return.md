## Overview

Henrik Rissler's return from Uppsala is a pivotal scene in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. Years after their school days, Rissler reappears at Martin's door, having completed his preliminary degree. The visit crystallizes the divergent paths of the two friends — Rissler poised for European travel and a literary career, Martin anchored in Stockholm — and provides the novel with one of its most resonant lines about time and change.

## The Arrival

Rissler reappears at Martin's door "blinking at the sunlight" [024-001]. He has just taken his preliminary degree and is preparing to travel through Europe while writing his thesis, *On Romantic Irony* — a title that encapsulates the Söderbergian sensibility of ironic detachment. Funded by a wealthy uncle described as a "bank lawyer, politician, and millionaire," Rissler represents the path Martin might have taken: university, travel, critical writing, intellectual independence.

## The Conversation

Their conversation ranges across "Uppsala, about books and women, and about a new philosopher by the name of Nietzsche" [024-005]. Rissler embodies the ideal of modern Scandinavian intellectual life at the turn of the century: widely read, philosophically engaged, aesthetically attuned, and emotionally reserved.

## "Here Everything Is the Same"

Rissler's observation upon returning — "Here everything is the same, here time has stood still" [024-004] — is one of the novel's most resonant lines. It marks the divergence between the friend who has moved outward into the world and the friend who has remained. For Martin, the remark carries an implicit judgment on his own stationary life.

## The Engagement Revelation

During the conversation, Martin learns that Rissler is engaged to Sigrid Tesch. The revelation lands with quiet finality, closing off one of Martin's own half-formed romantic possibilities and deepening his sense of exclusion from the forward movement of life.

## Significance

Rissler's return functions as a mirror for Martin's own unrealized ambitions. Where Rissler has pursued university, travel, and intellectual independence, Martin has remained in Stockholm, his literary aspirations unfulfilled. The scene encapsulates the novel's central tension between the life of the mind and the constraints of practical existence.
