## Overview

Planius is a minor figure among Martin Birck's schoolmates, defined almost entirely by his intellectual mediocrity and his pairing with his friend Tullman. He appears briefly in two scenes: the graduation dinner, where he and Tullman listen with "docile countenances" to philosophical conversation, and the nighttime aftermath, where his impulsive action deprives Martin of a romantic encounter. In Soderberg's economy of characterization, Planius serves as a foil — the unreflective, conventional student against whom Martin's interior turmoil is measured.

## Role in the Narrative

At the graduation celebration, the former classmates gather around punch-bowls and naturally sort themselves into old groupings: "Gabel and Billfelt, Jansson and Moberg, Planius and Tullman" [The White Cap, II]. Planius and Tullman present "the same docile countenances with which they had listened in class to the exposition of the instructor" — a description that captures their passive, unquestioning absorption of whatever is placed before them.

Later, when the company spills into the streets and encounters a group of streetwalkers, Martin stands paralyzed with desire, staring at "a pale little piece of mischief with eyes as black as charcoal" and wondering if he dares approach her. "But while he was wondering, Planius put an arm around her waist and scampered off" [The White Cap, II]. Martin is left alone, watching them "whirl about in the mist, pair after pair," as the morning breeze clears the fog and the cross on St. Katarina's cupola burns in the first rays of dawn.

## Significance

Planius embodies the uncomplicated, action-oriented counterpart to Martin's paralyzing self-consciousness. Where Martin deliberates, Planius acts — but the action is devoid of meaning. He takes the girl not because he desires her specifically but because she is available, and he has been described as "as stupid as a codfish" who "had only had a plain 'graduated' on his certificate." His success with the streetwalker is, in Soderberg's ironic treatment, a comment on the arbitrary distribution of life's small victories.


> Some of the others had come up and were now listening to the conversation. Planius and Tullman presented the same docile countenances with which they had listened in class to the exposition of the instructor. Gabel simpered sarcastically with his fine aristocratic face, and behind him Josef Marin pressed up. Josef Marin was short and slight; he looked pale and overworked. The two or three glasses of punch he had drunk had already made him a bit convivial; but now when he heard a serious question proposed and could not see that there was any joke behind it, he broke in with all the earnestness he could summon up at the moment: "I believe in God. But I don't conceive Him as a personal being."
