## Overview

The old lamplighter appears in two widely separated passages of the novel, each time at twilight, each time performing the same unhurried ritual: moving from lamp to lamp, bringing light to the darkening streets. He is never named, never given dialogue, never developed as a character in any conventional sense. Instead, he functions as a recurring visual motif — a fixed point of labor and order in a world of flux, whose presence marks the passage from day to night and, across the span of the novel, the passage of years.

## Role in the Narrative

The lamplighter first appears when Martin is a child on Long Row: "Down on the street the lamplighter went about his task; he was old and bent, and had a leather cap which came far down over his forehead. Now he came to the lamp just in front of the window on the opposite side of the street; when he had lighted it, the whole room brightened" [The Old Street, II]. The white lace curtains cast their broken pattern on the ceiling, and the calla lilies and fuchsias paint fantastic shadows — the lamplighter's act transforms the domestic interior into a theater of light.

He reappears later in the novel, when Martin is a young man: "Martin was roaming about in the twilight. The streets and squares lay white, snow was falling softly and silently. A man went in front of him on a zigzag course lighting a lamp here and a lamp there" [The White Cap]. The lamplighter is unchanged — still old, still bent, still tracing his zigzag path — but Martin is adrift in a different way, now burdened by adult longings rather than childhood fears.

## Significance

The lamplighter's reappearance creates one of the novel's quiet structural rhymes. He is part of Soderberg's technique of returning to a small set of recurring images — lamps, twilight, snow, solitary walkers — and investing them with cumulative emotional weight. The lamplighter does not speak or think or change; he simply persists, lighting the darkness one lamp at a time, while Martin passes from childhood to adulthood around him.
