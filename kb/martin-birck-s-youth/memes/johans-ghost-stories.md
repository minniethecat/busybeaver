## Overview

Johan's ghost stories represent a crucial episode in the Nockeby summer in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. Johan, the bridge-tender's fourteen-year-old assistant, becomes Martin's first hero, and his dusk recitations on the bridge introduce the boy to a world of supernatural folklore that runs parallel to — and undermines — the respectable, rational world of the Birck household.

## The Storyteller

Johan is Moberg's teenage assistant, described in practical terms: he makes bows and arrows and bark boats for Martin, and Martin helps him wind up the drawbridge. But it is neither the toys nor the games that earn Martin's devotion. It is that Johan "always had so many wonderful things to tell about, things that papa and mamma and grandmother never told about" [009-001].

Johan's tales form a kind of oral anthology, delivered in the dusk as he and Martin sit "on a beam by the opening in the bridge" waiting for the approaching steamboat, "whose lanterns would sooner or later pop out from behind the cape, first the green and then the red" [009-001].

## The Tales

Johan's repertoire encompasses three distinct registers of the uncanny:

**Old Moberg's Devils.** "One time it would be about old Moberg, who used to see tiny little devils jumping up and down, up and down, in his toddy glass; it was about them he talked when he sat muttering to himself and stirring his glass" [009-001]. The devils in the toddy glass are characteristic of the novel's ambiguous supernaturalism — real glimpses of a folk-Catholic underworld, or the projections of an old man's alcoholic solitude. The novel characteristically offers both readings and endorses neither.

**The Minister at Lovö.** "But the minister at Lovö was still worse. Why, he was a friend of Old Spotty himself, the whole parish knew that." Johan's evidence is folk-rational: "how otherwise could he get up in the pulpit and preach the way he did for a whole hour; where did he get all his words from?" Johan claims to have seen the minister's room "chock-full of books from floor to ceiling" — a detail that collapses the distinction between erudition and diabolism [009-001].

**The Murdered Man.** The third tale is the darkest: a man murdered on the highroad three years before, at a spot Johan can describe precisely — "just there where the wood is so thick on one side, and on the other is a willow alongside of a telegraph pole. It was an evening in November that it happened, and now if anybody goes by at the right time, he can hear the most terrible groaning in the ditch — But they never got the fellow that did it" [009-001].

## Martin's Response

When Martin hears such things, he "squeezed close to Johan's arm," and he feels lighter at heart only when the steamboat's lanterns shine out of the dark. Walking home across the bridge afterward, both boys are "excited with thoughts of ghosts and murders," and Johan whispers, "Listen, he's after us!" Martin does not know whether *he* is the murderer or the murdered, but "he fancied he heard steps on the bridge and didn't dare to look around" [009-001].

Johan, "who had a cheerful disposition," disperses the terror by singing a jolly song, and the episode closes on the threshold between genuine folk dread and the consolations of the everyday.

## Significance

Johan's ghost stories introduce Martin to a folkloric reality that the Birck household, with its government-clerk rationality, cannot provide. The tales invest the Nockeby landscape with a density of supernatural association: the ditch by the telegraph pole, the minister's book-lined study, the toddy glass itself becomes charged with infernal possibility. Johan is, in effect, Martin's introduction to an alternative epistemology — a world in which knowledge comes not from schoolbooks but from oral tradition, and in which the boundary between the living and the dead, the natural and the demonic, remains porous.
