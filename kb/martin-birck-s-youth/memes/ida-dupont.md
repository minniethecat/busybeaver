## Overview

Ida Dupont is Martin Birck's childhood friend and a pivotal figure in his early development. The daughter of Mr. Dupont, a violinist in the royal orchestra, Ida inhabits a bohemian, artistically rich world that stands in stark contrast to the conventional respectability of Martin's household. Her mother, a former ballet dancer, has left the family for another man — a circumstance that lends Ida's world an air of both glamour and melancholy.

Through Ida, Martin is introduced to experiences that expand his imaginative horizons: costume and performance, wine and ritual, and the aesthetic allure of Catholic devotion. She is at once a playmate, a teacher, and an unwitting guide to the complexities of adult life.

## Role in the Narrative

Ida first appears in the pear-tree scene, where she stands apart from the other children — "with great eyes, her hands behind her back, not venturing into the turmoil" [002-007]. Her marginal status (as the daughter of a Catholic musician in Protestant Stockholm) and her quiet dignity immediately distinguish her.

The most significant Ida episode occurs when Martin visits her father's apartment on a gray October morning [003-001]. There, Ida reveals a world of theatrical richness: tinted peepshow pictures, her mother's ballet costumes of satin and silk, and — most dramatically — a Catholic shrine with a madonna statue behind wax candles. Ida dresses Martin in veils and bodices, paints mustaches on him with a burnt cork, and serves wine in green glasses — a scene that is by turns comic, unsettling, and strangely beautiful.

In this scene, Ida assumes the role of priestess and instructor, teaching Martin "our religion" [003-014]. The ritual — lighting candles, burning incense, kneeling before the madonna — is both a game and a genuine transmission of cultural difference. Martin "shivered with delight" [003-013], his Protestant sensibilities confronted with the sensual appeal of Catholic imagery.

## Key Relationships

- **Martin Birck** — Her closest friend and audience for her imaginative performances.
- **Mr. Dupont** — Her father, a violinist who "lets me do whatever I like" [003-009]. His permissive parenting contrasts with Martin's more conventional upbringing.
- **Ida's mother** — Absent but present through the costumes and gifts she sends. Her departure with "a strange gentleman" [003-004] marks Ida's world with loss.

## Significance

Ida Dupont functions as a catalyst for Martin's aesthetic and moral awakening. She represents an alternative model of living — more sensuous, more artistic, less bound by bourgeois convention — that both attracts and unsettles Martin. The Catholic ritual scene is particularly significant: it is Martin's first encounter with religious difference presented as beauty rather than doctrine, an experience that foreshadows Söderberg's broader themes of relativism and the limits of inherited belief.

## Notable Passages

> Ida showed Martin a peepshow box with tinted pictures. There were white castles and gardens with colored lanterns in long gleaming rows, yellow and red and blue. [003-002]

> Ida fell on her knees before the madonna. Her thick, dark-red plaits glowed like bright copper in the candlelight. She muttered something which Martin did not understand, and made strange gestures with her hands. [003-014]
