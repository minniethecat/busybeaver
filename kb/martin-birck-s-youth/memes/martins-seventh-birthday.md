## Overview

Martin's seventh birthday, celebrated at the Nockeby bridge-tender's cottage during the family's summer sojourn, is a set piece of childhood perception. The morning unfolds in three movements: the private tenderness of waking to birdsong and the promise of a surprise; the family's playful ritual of congratulations; and the spectacular passage of the Swedish cavalry across the bridge — a moment of martial grandeur that momentarily eclipses Martin's earlier devotion to the coasting skippers. The episode captures the texture of a child's experience: the oscillation between intimate domestic warmth and the allure of the wider world.

## Context

The birthday occurs during the summer the Birck family rents rooms from old Moberg, the bridge-tender at Nockeby Bridge over Lake Mälaren. Martin is turning seven — the age at which he is told he must prepare for school in the autumn, a prospect that fills him with an obscure dread. The bridge, with its steamboats, drawbridge mechanism, and Johan's ghost stories, has been the center of Martin's summer idyll.

## Narrative Account

Martin wakes earlier than usual to the sound of a bird twittering in the privet hedge. Sunlight fills the room, and he sits up, eager to dress and go out. His mother enters and tells him to lie still — Maria is preparing a surprise [001-XXX]. She kisses him and says that now he is seven, he must be "really industrious and good in the summer, so that he wouldn't need to be ashamed in the autumn when he was to begin school." At the word "school," Martin's throat tightens: "he didn't know very clearly what 'school' meant, but it sounded very harsh and hard" [001-XXX].

The family assembles to congratulate him — father, mother, grandmother, and Maria, who adopts an affected manner and bows: "I have the honor to congratulate —" Martin, overcome with bashfulness, blushes and turns his face to the wall [001-XXX].

Then comes the grandmother's stroke of comic invention. She pops her head in and announces that "the king was coming riding with fifteen generals to congratulate Martin." At the same moment, a rumbling sounds over the bridge "as if there was thunder." Martin jumps out of bed, dresses frantically, and rushes to the porch — only to find that the king is not among the riders. His mother consoles him: instead it is "almost all his army, which was on its way to the region of Drottningsholm for maneuvers" [001-XXX].

The cavalry pours past: hussars, dragoons, and "all the artillery from Stockholm," the artillerists "shaking like sacks of potatoes on their caissons" and gray with dust. Martin, far from being disappointed, "admired them all the more in that condition and wondered within himself if it wouldn't be better to be an artillerist than a coasting skipper" [001-XXX].

## Key Participants

- **Martin Birck** — The birthday boy, turning seven, poised between fantasy and reality.
- **Martin's mother** — The bearer of gentle discipline and the dreaded word "school."
- **Martin's grandmother** — The playful trickster whose joke conjures the cavalry into being.
- **Maria** — Martin's older sister, preparing a birthday surprise.

## Significance

The birthday episode illustrates Söderberg's method of embedding large themes in small moments. The word "school" — which "sounded very harsh and hard" — is a death knell for the Edenic summer of childhood. The grandmother's joke, which "summons" the cavalry as if by magic, grants Martin one last moment of belief in a world where kings come to congratulate little boys. And Martin's rapid shift of vocational ambition — from skipper to artillerist — is a gentle satire of the child's protean imagination, always ready to abandon one dream for a newer, louder one.

The military parade also functions as a threshold between the private world of the birthday morning and the public world of national power. The cavalry, riding toward Drottningsholm, belongs to the realm of history and the state — forces that will eventually claim Martin himself, if only in the form of a government clerkship.
