## Overview

Martin's mother's warning before the Hasselbacken supper is a small domestic scene that encapsulates the emotional distance growing between mother and son. Her anxious litany of worldly dangers — drink, disease, and "dreadful women" — and Martin's dismissive response reveal the failure of a religious, protective love to reach a son who has already begun to drift away. The scene culminates in her weeping alone in a dark corner, knowing she is losing him.

## The Emptying Home

The scene opens with domestic absence: Maria has been married for a year and lives far away in the country, and grandmother is no longer there. "Home was quieter and more empty than before" [018-001]. Already the family is dispersing, and Martin is next.

## The Warning

On the evening of the white cap supper, Mr. Birck gives Martin five crowns, and his mother takes him aside. Her warning comes in a rush: "Don't make a point of emptying your glass every time anyone drinks a toast with you... pretend you drank. And I must tell you, Martin, that there is a class of dreadful women who do nothing else but try to lead young men to their destruction." She warns him that "their very breath is poisonous" and that merely standing on the street talking to such a woman could bring "the most frightful diseases that no doctor in the world can cure" [018-002].

Underneath the medical alarmism is a deeper fear — that Martin has not given himself to the Lord, and without religion she has no way to protect him. "Dear Martin, if I only knew you had given yourself to the Lord and had your thoughts fixed on Him, I shouldn't be anxious about you; but I know you don't do that" [018-002].

## Martin's Deflection

Martin's response is weary and dismissive: "Mother dear, you're always getting off on that" [018-003]. He takes his white cap, says goodbye, and leaves. He is already gone before he has left the room.

## The Weeping

Alone in the parlor, Mrs. Birck sits in a dark corner and weeps. "For she knew she was going to lose him as mothers always lose their sons" [018-004]. The sentence belongs neither to Martin's perspective nor to neutral narration but to an older, more knowing voice — it frames her grief as a universal maternal condition, the inevitable loss that every mother must endure.
