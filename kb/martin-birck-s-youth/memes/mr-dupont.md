## Overview

Mr. Dupont is a violinist in the royal orchestra of Stockholm and the father of Ida Dupont. He represents the artistic, bohemian dimension of late 19th-century Stockholm society — a world of continental sophistication, Catholic faith, and relaxed domestic morality that contrasts sharply with the Protestant respectability of Martin Birck's family.

Though he appears only briefly in person, Mr. Dupont's presence suffuses the novel through his apartment — with its violin, its madonna shrine, and its traces of his absent wife — and through the freedom he grants his daughter Ida.

## Role in the Narrative

Mr. Dupont first appears in the pear-tree scene, leaning out of his window "in his shirt sleeves, smoking a pipe, with a red skullcap on his head" [002-008]. His amused, detached observation of Mrs. Lundgren's officiousness immediately establishes him as a figure outside the petty social dramas of the yard.

His apartment — two small rooms, one flight up — is the setting for one of the novel's most memorable scenes [003-001]. Mr. Dupont is away at rehearsal when Martin visits, but his absence is as revealing as his presence would have been. The apartment contains: a violin on the wall, a half-filled bottle of wine, a Catholic shrine with a madonna and crucifix, and a drawer full of ballet costumes sent by Ida's absent mother. These objects paint a portrait of a man who values art, pleasure, and religious tradition — and who has suffered loss.

Martin's mother later notes that Ida "had fleas" [003-011], a small detail that suggests the respectable world's discomfort with the Duponts' bohemian lifestyle. Yet Mr. Dupont's permissive parenting — "He lets me do whatever I like" [003-009] — is portrayed not as neglect but as a kind of freedom that Martin's more conventional upbringing lacks.

## Key Relationships

- **Ida Dupont** — His daughter, whom he raises alone after his wife's departure. His relationship with Ida is marked by affection and unusual liberty.
- **Ida's mother** — His wife, a former ballet dancer, who left the family for another man. Her absence is a defining fact of the Dupont household.
- **Mrs. Lundgren** — A neighbor who "was ill-disposed toward Mr. Dupont, who was a violinist in the royal orchestra" [002-007], suggesting class or cultural prejudice.

## Significance

Mr. Dupont embodies the artistic vocation in Söderberg's world — solitary, aesthetically rich, socially marginal. His Catholicism, in predominantly Lutheran Stockholm, marks him as doubly an outsider. Through Mr. Dupont and his apartment, Söderberg introduces themes of art, religion, and domestic unconventionality that serve as counterpoints to the novel's prevailing atmosphere of bourgeois convention.
