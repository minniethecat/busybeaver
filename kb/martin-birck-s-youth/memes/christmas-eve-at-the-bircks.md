## Overview

Christmas in the Birck household is rendered with sensory immediacy in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. The holiday arrives in a Stockholm blanketed with snow, bringing the rituals of a bourgeois family Christmas — the tree, the candles, the family gatherings.

The season leaves behind the "already faded" [007-001] remnants of childhood wonder, stretching from Christmas Eve through New Year's Day in a passage so swift "it was like turning the page of a picture-book" [006-011].

## The Christmas Tree

"White winter came with sleigh bells and snow and ice-flowers on the windowpane" [006-011]. Martin's mother tells him the frost-flowers are "the dead summer flowers come back again" [006-011]. As Christmas approaches, evergreen firs are brought "from the darkness and solitude into the city streets and squares" [006-011].

Söderberg's prose renders the transformation of the humble fir into something luminous:

> White winter came with sleigh bells and snow and ice-flowers on the windowpane. Evergreen forests out in the country came from the darkness and solitude into the city streets and squares, and when the Christmas bells rang in the holy day, there stood in Martin's home a dark and timid fir which smelt of the woods, till evening came and it stood aglitter with candles, white candles and colored candles, and was covered with winter apples and sugarplums with mottoes which were so stupid that even Martin and Maria could see how stupid they were.
>
> (006-001)

## New Year's Morning Service

On New Year's morning, Martin is woken while it is still dark to attend early church service. Half asleep, he scrambles through the snow beside his parents. As they round the corner, "there stood the church like a giant lantern shining out across the white square" [006-013], with people crawling in from all directions through the snow.

Inside, the organ roars and candles shine, and "Martin felt happy and good and thought this was just the right way to begin the new year" [006-013]. When the minister begins to preach, Martin falls straight to sleep, waking only when "the pale hue of dawn was shining in through the windows in the cupola" [006-013] and his mother rouses him for coffee.

## Visits to Relatives

Later that morning, Martin and Maria are sent on New Year's visits to their uncles and aunts, where they receive "cakes and wine and sugarplums from the Christmas trees" [006-015].

At Uncle Abraham's, however, there is no Christmas tree. A widowed doctor with a black beard and a long crooked nose — "for he was a Jew" [006-015] — Uncle Abraham lives alone with an old housekeeper, a parrot that can swear in French, and a black tomcat named Kolmodin. The cat is clever enough to pull the bell-cord with his claws when he wishes to enter a room.

The children find Uncle Abraham playing chess alone with a bottle of wine, in a large, half-dark room full of books. Martin and Maria feel an unspoken oppression, having heard their parents say "that Uncle Abraham was not a happy man and that he never was really cheerful" [006-017].

## The Passing of Christmas

The holiday passes quickly — "it was like turning the page of a picture-book" [006-011]. The star of New Year's Eve "was burning across the white roofs, and people said to each other, 'Good night, and thanks for the year!'" [006-011].

Martin is left with a "shivering sensation" [006-011] as he contemplates "the line of gray winter days that were waiting, to which he could see no ending, for it was interminably long till summer, and still longer till next Christmas" [006-011].

## Aftermath

When the holiday ends, Martin is left with his "already faded" [007-001] gifts: "his alphabet book and multiplication tables, with his color-box and jumping-jacks" [007-001]. Among them is the Red Turk, a pasteboard jumping-jack given by Uncle Abraham, who explains its philosophical significance as "the jolliest jumping-jack in all the world" [007-001].
