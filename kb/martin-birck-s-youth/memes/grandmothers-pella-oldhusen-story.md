## Overview

Grandmother's story of Pella Oldhusen is a small, sharp gem of domestic comedy embedded in the twilight melancholy of Old Street IV. It reveals the grandmother as a storyteller with a taste for the scandalous and the absurd, and it offers a glimpse of the wider social world beyond the Birck household — a world of secretaries, kleptomaniacs, and communion dresses that Söderberg treats with affectionate irony.

## The Twilight Scene

The scene begins in characteristic Old Street domesticity: Martin's mother has been singing at the piano — old songs "such as no one sings any more," about a soldier's canteen and a shepherdess fleeing an avalanche [002-024]. "There was a sense of loneliness when she had stopped singing" [002-025]. The room seems to grow larger and more empty as twilight falls.

Martin, restless, turns to his grandmother, who is sitting by the window reading the *Stockholm Journal* with "her glasses far down on her nose" [002-026]. "Tell us a story, please, grandmother," Martin begs. But grandmother "didn't know any new stories, and the old ones Martin had heard many times before" [002-027].

## The Obituary

Then grandmother looks up from her paper with a start: "Lord deliver us, did you see there's a Miss Oldhusen has died?" [002-028].

"Is she a sister of the Secretary?" Martin's father asks, thinking of Secretary Oldhusen, the most important tenant in their building. "Goodness, no; she was his aunt," grandmother replies [002-030].

## The Story

The story grandmother tells is a masterwork of anecdotal gossip:

Pella Oldhusen was "a plaguy smart and amusing woman," but she was a kleptomaniac. Her acquaintances would warn each other: "Be careful, my dear, and don't leave anything around loose this evening; Pella Oldhusen's coming!" [002-030].

The crowning episode concerns a girl Pella had taken in as a ward. When the girl was to be prepared for her first communion, Pella stole "her old housekeeper's linen underskirts that hung in the same wardrobe with her own clothes and had them made up for the girl" [002-030].

The housekeeper, who knew Pella's "peculiarities" after many years of service, confronted her with theatrical innocence: "Look here, Miss Oldhusen, there's been thieves in the wardrobe! And the mischief's in it, they've stolen all my underskirts, but not yours, though they were hanging side by side" [002-030].

Pella's response is a masterpiece of feigned indignation: "Could anyone imagine such rascals? That's frightfully annoying, but what can I do about it?" [002-030]. And yet — grandmother adds with relish — Pella later gave the housekeeper money for new linen, "for she was well off and not stingy neither." The girl, meanwhile, "went to the blessed Lord's Supper in the stolen underskirts" [002-030].

## The Story's Effect

Martin and Maria listen "with wide-open mouths." The narrator observes dryly: "Grandmother had told a story, after all. Of such stories she knew plenty" [002-031]. The tale is a perfect miniature — morally ambiguous (Pella is generous but a thief; the housekeeper is victimized but complicit), socially precise (class, clothing, communion), and darkly funny. It is exactly the kind of story Söderberg himself loved to tell.
