## Overview

Martin Birck's neighborhood is the primary setting of Hjalmar Söderberg's novel. It is a microcosm of late 19th-century Stockholm society, encompassing the full social spectrum from the respectable Secretary Oldhusen to the poor residents of the Long Row. The neighborhood is rendered with the precision of a naturalist — Söderberg's eye for detail brings each building, each character, and each social boundary into sharp relief.

## Description in the Text

The street itself is "lined with wooden houses and tile roofs and green trees" [002-009], an image of modest, almost pastoral urban life. The house where Martin lives is the exception: "the only large stone house on the street" [002-009], marking his family's relative prosperity. Across from it lies Long Row — "a low, dirt-gray range of houses" where "only really poor people lived, Martin's mother said. Only scum, said Mrs. Lundgren" [002-009].

The neighborhood contains a dye-house, a corner tavern, and a nearby church whose clock marks the passing hours. The yard shared by the residents features a beloved pear tree — old and large, "many of its boughs already dry and dead, but the others still furnished blossoms and greenery every spring and fruit every autumn" [002-006].

## Narrative Function

The neighborhood serves as both setting and social diagram. Söderberg arranges its spaces along axes of class, religion, and respectability:

- **The stone house vs. Long Row** — The physical contrast between Martin's building and the poor housing across the street encodes the class divisions that Martin is just beginning to perceive.
- **The yard** — A shared space where social boundaries are temporarily suspended during events like the pear-tree stripping, though hierarchies quickly reassert themselves.
- **The street** — A stage for urban life: the goat woman with her goats, the ragman with his greasy stick, the drunken men from the tavern, the policeman feeding chickens [002-010].

## Associated Characters

- **Martin Birck** — The neighborhood is his entire world in childhood.
- **Mrs. Lundgren** — The self-appointed arbiter of yard events.
- **Heggborn family** — The porter and his family, residents of the stone house.
- **Secretary Oldhusen** — The neighborhood's most distinguished resident, occupying "the largest floor."
- **Mr. Dupont and Ida** — Neighbors whose bohemian Catholic household is subtly set apart.

## Notable Passages
