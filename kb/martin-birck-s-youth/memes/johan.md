## Overview

Johan is the fourteen- or fifteen-year-old assistant to old Moberg, the bridge-tender at Nockeby. He "soon became Martin's best friend" during the period when the Birck family lived near the bridge [009-001]. His friendship with Martin is built on a shared world of practical activity—making bows and arrows and bark boats, winding up the drawbridge, playing hide-and-seek—but it is Johan's storytelling gift that secures Martin's "extraordinary" admiration.

Johan knows things that "papa and mamma and grandmother never told about"—a world of devils in toddy glasses, ministers who consort with the Devil, and murdered men whose groans can still be heard in the ditches at night [009-001]. He is, in effect, Martin's introduction to a folkloric reality that runs parallel to the respectable, rational world of the Birck household. His cheerful disposition—striking up a jolly song to drive off fear after telling ghost stories—makes him one of the novel's most affectionate portraits of working-class boyhood.

## Role in the Narrative

Johan appears in a sustained sequence set around the Nockeby bridge. He is first introduced as Moberg's assistant, described in practical terms: he makes toys for Martin, and Martin helps him wind up the drawbridge [009-001]. But the relationship is quickly elevated by Johan's storytelling, which takes place primarily at dusk, when the two boys sit on a beam by the bridge opening, waiting for the approaching steamboat.

Johan's tales form a kind of oral anthology: "One time it would be about old Moberg, who used to see tiny little devils jumping up and down, up and down, in his toddy glass. ... But the minister at Lovö was still worse. Why, he was a friend of Old Spotty himself, the whole parish knew that" [009-001]. He tells of a murdered man whose ghost groans in a ditch on November evenings, describing the exact location: "It was just there where the wood is so thick on one side, and on the other is a willow alongside of a telegraph pole" [009-001]. These stories, with their precise geography and matter-of-fact supernaturalism, are Johan's gift to Martin—a gift of a world richer and darker than the one his parents inhabit.

The bridge sequence culminates in the funeral-hymn episode. Martin, having learned the hymn from Johan, sings it while walking home—to the tune of "an insolent street song." When Moberg confronts Martin, "blue in the face" with outrage, Johan vanishes [009-001]. This desertion is characteristic of Johan's role: he provides the material for transgression but disappears when consequences arrive. His cheerful disposition—"he had a cheerful disposition, drove off his fear by striking up a jolly song" [009-002]—allows him to traffic in darkness without being consumed by it.

## Key Relationships

- **Martin Birck** — His best friend during the Nockeby period; Johan opens Martin's imagination to a world beyond domestic respectability.
- **Old Moberg** — Johan's employer; he both works for Moberg and tells stories about him, occupying a position of intimate subordination.
- **Maria Birck** — One of the children who joins in the evening games of hide-and-seek.

## Significance

Johan is the novel's principal conduit for folk belief and oral storytelling—a register of experience that the Birck household, with its government-clerk rationality and domestic contentment, cannot provide. His tales of devils, ministers in league with Satan, and murdered men whose groans persist in the landscape constitute an alternative epistemology: a world in which the supernatural is not a matter of belief but of local knowledge, as geographically specific as a telegraph pole or a stand of trees.

The contrast between Johan's world and the Birck household is central to Martin's development. Martin's parents and grandmother offer a world that is orderly, legible, and morally clear; Johan offers a world that is mysterious, dangerous, and thrillingly unresolved. That Martin is "so fond of Johan and admired him so extraordinarily" is not merely childhood friendship but an early expression of the hunger for experiences beyond the domestic sphere that will define his adolescence and young adulthood.

He made bows and arrows and bark boats for Martin, and Martin helped him to wind up the drawbridge. ... It was because Johan always had so many wonderful things to tell about, things that papa and mamma and grandmother never told about. [009-001]
