## Overview

Uncle Abraham's Chess Game is a New Year's Day scene in Old Street VI that introduces a figure of quiet tragedy. Martin and Maria visit their uncle, a Jewish doctor and widower who lives alone with an old housekeeper, a French-swearing parrot, and a black cat named Kolmodin. The children find him playing chess alone before a porcelain stove, and though he stuffs their pockets with treats, the room's half-darkness and the cat's gloomy stare leave them with a feeling of oppression.

## Narrative Account

On New Year's Day, after visiting other relatives where they received cakes and wine and "the most beautiful intentions" from the minister's sermon, Martin and Maria go to Uncle Abraham's home [411-411]. There is no Christmas tree—Abraham is a widower with no children, living alone with his housekeeper, a parrot that can swear in French, and a clever black cat named Kolmodin who rings the doorbell by pulling the cord with his claws [411-411].

When the children arrive, they find Uncle Abraham sitting alone at a table with a bottle of wine, playing chess with himself [411-411]. The room is large, half-dark, and filled with books, while snow falls in great flakes outside. Abraham welcomes them cordially, fills their pockets with treats, and makes the parrot swear, but he says little [413-413]. Before the fire in the porcelain stove sits Kolmodin, staring gloomily at his master. Martin and Maria stand silent, oppressed—they have heard their parents say that Uncle Abraham "was not a happy man" and that he "never was really cheerful" [413-413].

## Key Participants

- **Uncle Abraham**: A Jewish doctor and widower, isolated from the family's festive life. His solitary chess game and silence suggest a deeper unhappiness tied to his status as an outsider.
- **Martin and Maria**: The child observers through whose eyes the scene's quiet sadness is filtered.
- **Kolmodin**: The clever black cat whose gloomy stare mirrors his master's solitude.

## Significance

The scene is one of the novel's most economical portraits of alienation. Abraham's solitary chess—playing both sides—metaphorically enacts his position as a Jew in Christian Sweden: always playing alone, never fully part of the game. The later revelation (in Old Street X) that Abraham hanged himself "with a stove-cord one rainy day, when he didn't find it worth the trouble to live any more" [592-592] retroactively darkens this New Year's visit, transforming it from a scene of mild childhood unease into a prefiguration of suicide.
