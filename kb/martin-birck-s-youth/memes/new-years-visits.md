## Overview

New Year's Day in the Birck household follows a fixed ritual: an early-morning church service attended by the entire family, followed by coffee at home and a round of visits to relatives — Uncle Jan, Aunt Louisa, and notably Uncle Abraham, the widowed Jewish doctor who lives alone with a housekeeper, a swearing parrot, and a remarkably intelligent black cat named Kolmodin. These visits represent the rhythm of family obligation and seasonal renewal, anchoring Martin's childhood in a predictable cycle of domestic traditions.

## The Early Service

New Year's morning begins in darkness, with Martin woken while still half asleep to walk through the snow alongside his parents to the early church service [001-166]. The church emerges around a corner "like a giant lantern shining out across the white square," with worshippers converging from all directions through the snow [001-166]. Inside, the roar of the organ, the singing, and the many candles create an atmosphere in which Martin feels "happy and good," convinced this is "just the right way to begin the new year" — before promptly falling asleep during the minister's sermon [001-166]. He wakes to the pale dawn light through the cupola windows and his mother's words: "Now we'll go home and drink our coffee" [001-166].

## The Round of Visits

After the church service and coffee, Martin and his sister Maria are sent out on the traditional New Year's visits to relatives. At Uncle Jan's and Aunt Louisa's and other aunts and uncles, they receive cakes, wine, and sugarplums from the Christmas trees [001-167]. These visits follow a predictable pattern of hospitality and gift-giving that defines the family's holiday economy.

## Uncle Abraham

The most distinctive stop on the New Year's circuit is Uncle Abraham's apartment. Unlike the other relatives, Uncle Abraham — a widowed doctor who has "often cured Martin and Maria of measles and scarlatina and pains in the chest" — keeps no Christmas tree. He lives alone with an old housekeeper, a parrot that can swear in French, and a black tomcat named Kolmodin [001-167]. Uncle Abraham is described as having "a black beard and a long crooked nose, for he was a Jew" [001-167]. His cat Kolmodin is "the cleverest cat in the world": when outside the office door, the cat catches his claws in the bell-cord and pulls it hard rather than mewing [001-167]. On this particular New Year's visit, Martin and Maria find Uncle Abraham sitting alone with a bottle of wine, playing chess with himself [001-167].

## Aftermath and Reflection

Following the visits, Martin confronts the sameness of the new year. The almanac he had given his father as a Christmas gift has a red cover instead of the old blue one, and he is surprised and disappointed to discover that "this was the only difference he could see between the new year and the old" [001-169]. The days continue as before with bells, snow, and a somber sky, and Martin is left with "the longing to be big" — a desire he both yearns for and fears, haunted by his mother's warning that a disobedient boy may end up like the ragman who pokes through ash barrels with a black stick [001-169].

## Significance

The New Year's visits encapsulate the tension between ritual and stasis that runs through the novel. The church service, the round of family visits, and the predictable hospitality all provide a comforting structure to Martin's childhood year. Yet Martin's disappointment at the sameness of the new year gestures toward a deeper dissatisfaction with cyclical repetition — a yearning for real change that the rituals cannot provide. Uncle Abraham, sitting alone with wine and chess in a childless, Christmas-tree-less apartment, represents an alternative model of adult life: solitary, intellectual, and outside the Christian holiday framework that defines the rest of the family's year.
