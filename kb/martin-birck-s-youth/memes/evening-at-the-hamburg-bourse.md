## Overview

The evening at the Hamburg Bourse is a telling episode in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. Following the graduation supper at Hasselbacken — itself upended by Doctor Markel's provocative lecture on pantheism and an abrupt thunderstorm — the company seeks shelter at a café Martin had always assumed was a merchants' exchange. The scene provides a gentle comic deflation of youthful literal-mindedness, a survey of the various futures the graduates imagine for themselves, and a first glimpse of the adult world Martin is about to enter. The episode then spills into the streets of early-morning Stockholm, where student caps gleam white in the mist.

## Before the Bourse: Markel's Lecture

The graduation supper at Hasselbacken is dominated by Doctor Markel, a guest from Uppsala, who delivers an extended disquisition on the ideal god for a modern poet: a pantheistic, impersonal deity that "sets the imagination going and comes out finely in poetry without in return entailing any obligation" [016-010]. The lecture is poorly received — "Josef Marin, who had been drinking more punch meanwhile and had become even paler than before, muttered something to the effect that he ought to have a smack on the jaw" [016-011] — and the elder Billfelt hastily orders champagne to restore good feeling. Markel's lecture is cut short when "some big raindrops now began to fall on the railing" [016-010], and the darkening sky heralds the storm that will drive the party indoors.

## The Rainstorm

The scene pivots on weather: "It rained as if the heavens were opened. They crowded into a street car to go into the city and have coffee. Most of them voted to go to the Hamburg Bourse" [016-013]. The storm is a narrative convenience but also a tonal shift — from the open-air, moonlit expansiveness of Hasselbacken to the enclosed, gaslit interior of the city café.

## The Revelation

Martin had always believed the Hamburg Bourse was a place "where the German merchants of Stockholm assembled to do business" [016-013]. He is surprised to find himself entering a café that "seemed to irradiate a fabulous magnificence" [016-013]. The discrepancy between the name and the reality is a small-scale version of the novel's recurring theme: the gap between childish literalism and adult complexity. Inside, he recognizes some of his former teachers and "a lot of oldsters who lifted their glasses and nodded genially" [016-013] — the adult world already installed in its accustomed places.

## Inside the Café

Coffee and liqueurs are brought, and "there was talk of future plans. Most of them were to study law and expected to spend the summer in reading up" [016-013]. The talk of futures is earnest but already touched with the irony of the narrator's retrospect. "Enthusiasm rose, and rash promises were made to keep in touch and not forget each other" [016-013]. At one end of the table, Gabel and Billfelt "swore eternal friendship"; at the other, Jansson "expatiated on his feeling for Moberg" [016-013].

The evening's wild card, Josef Marin, threatens to prophesy — a performance involving "marriage announcements from the *Daily News* mixed with bits from Tegnér's 'Svea' and Norbeck's *Theology*, all recited in the solemn monotone with which he imagined Elisha had chastised Ahab" [016-013]. He is restrained only with difficulty.

## The Dissolution

Time passes, and the party thins: "It was one o'clock, getting on towards two, and various members of the party had already said good night and gone off, especially those who seriously meant to read up for law" [016-014]. The café itself begins to close around them: "The crowd was thinning, the electric light had long ago been turned off, only a couple of gas jets were still burning, and the waiters stood with the air of martyrs as they yearned for sleep and pourboire" [016-014]. The final line is a quiet epitaph for the evening: "There was nothing to do but break up" [016-014].

## The Walk Through Dawn

Outside, "the glimmer of dawn had already begun to spread over the streets and squares. It was no longer raining, but the air felt moist and cold and misty, and through the mist the clock-face of Jacobs church shone like a moon in a comic paper" [016-015]. The company walks along the car tracks past the opera house. Passing Lagerlunden, Martin sees "a group of poets and journalists" and looks at them "reverently, wondering whether it would ever be vouchsafed him to become one of them" [016-016].

The student caps, gleaming white in the night, attract attention: "moths came fluttering from right and left, slipping their arms under those of the young men and tempting them with promises of the greatest happiness in life" [016-016]. The scene turns carnivalesque — Josef Marin insists on prophesying before the statue of Charles XII, while Gabel waltzes with a girl around the statue and Martin stands "with a pounding heart staring at a pale little piece of mischief with eyes as black as charcoal" [016-016], wondering if he dares approach. He does not; Planius gets there first.

The episode ends with a policeman's appearance dispersing the crowd, and Martin's encounter with Hulda — a streetwalker who offers to embroider him a nightshirt — which he evades with the polite fiction of having no money, though "it was not quite true that Martin had no money; he still had a few crowns left from the honorarium for a poem published in the *Home Friend*" [016-018].

## Significance

The Hamburg Bourse episode functions as the final movement of Martin's graduation night. The arc from Hasselbacken's open-air rhetoric (Markel's lecture) to the café's enclosed conviviality to the misty streets of dawn traces a descent from intellectual aspiration through social performance to the ambiguous realities of the adult city — prostitutes, policemen, the fading gleam of student caps. The Bourse itself, with its grand name and café reality, is a gentle emblem of the novel's method: things are rarely what Martin has imagined them to be.
