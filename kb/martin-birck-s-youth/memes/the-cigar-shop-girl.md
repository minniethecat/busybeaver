## Overview

The encounter with the cigar-shop girl is a small but revealing episode in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. It takes place moments after Martin receives his white student cap — the symbol of his graduation — and captures his awkward, impulsive navigation of the adult world he is entering.

## The Impulsive Kiss

"When Martin Birck had got the white cap, his first errand was to go into a cigar booth to buy a cane of cinnamon wood and a package of cigarettes" [013-001]. The young shop-girl had "black eyes and a thick bang" — not the blonde Gretchen of his romantic dreams [013-001]. But when she congratulated him pleasantly on his white cap and regarded him with kindliness, "he suddenly felt all warm about the heart, caught her dirty hand, which lay outstretched across the counter display of Cameo and Duke of Durham, and tenderly kissed it" [013-001].

## Immediate Regret

The regret was instantaneous. "He had no doubt behaved badly" [013-001]. Martin's self-reproach is characteristically overthought: he did not imagine she was innocent — "she had no doubt a lover, possibly several" — but "that was no reason why anyone at all had the right to come in from the street and kiss her hand just like that" [013-001]. Embarrassed and at a loss, "he finally plucked up courage to select a cane, light a cigarette, and go out" [013-001].

## The White Cap in the Window

The embarrassment quickly yields to joy. Walking Queen Street, Martin "stopped in front of every store window to see the reflection of his white cap" [013-002]. He could hardly believe he had become a student: "Up to the last he had believed he would be flunked" [013-002]. The certificate's closing words — "Graduated with honor" — caused his heart to "swell with deep gratitude toward his corps of teachers" [013-002].

The city itself seemed transformed. "Queen Street was still wet after the last shower, little ladies with jogging bustles lifted their skirts to jump over puddles, which mirrored the blue above" [013-002]. Over the gray roofs "the restless white spring clouds hurried in fluttering haste" [013-002]. Martin hastened home "to the blossoming street of his childhood" — but the street was no longer the same: "From a single plot the cherry tree still stretched its branches out over a mossy board fence; everything else was great red brick buildings and small commonplace meetinghouses" [013-003].
