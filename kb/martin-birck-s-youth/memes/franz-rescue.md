## Overview

The Franz rescue episode in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth* dramatizes one of the novel's recurring preoccupations: the collision between the moral codes Martin absorbs from books and the brutal pragmatism of the schoolyard. Martin's intervention on behalf of his tormentor, Franz, is at once an act of genuine courage and a miscalculation born of reading too many Indian stories — and its bitter aftermath teaches him a lesson no book could provide.

## The Ambush

On the way to school, Martin finds "two of his comrades in a fight with Franz at a street corner; in fact they had already overcome him, thrown him down, and were pummeling him with their fists" [010-001]. Franz is the "horrible Franz, who used to live in the Long Row" — the same boy who spat on Martin's jacket, called him "stuck-up," and had long "directed all his energies and intelligence toward embittering Martin's life by day and even pursued him into his nocturnal dreams" [010-001].

## The Intervention

Martin had recently "begun to read Indian books, so that he at once saw in Franz a parallel to the noble redskin" [010-001]. Seizing the opportunity to make Franz "his ally against other rowdies," Martin advances and argues to his comrades "how cowardly it was to fight two against one, said that Franz lived in his street and was a very decent rowdy, and proposed that they let him go in peace" [010-001]. While Martin distracts them, "Franz managed to get up and run away" [010-001].

## The Aftermath

The consequences are swift and doubly bitter. Martin receives "all the licking intended for Franz" and must endure "the scorn of his comrades for being the friend of a rowdy" [010-001]. Worse still, the next time he meets Franz on the street, "the latter tripped him so that he fell into the gutter, then gave him a bloody nose, tore his books apart, swore at him frightfully, and ran off" [010-001]. The narrator's laconic conclusion — "He had not understood that he was supposed to be a noble redskin" — is one of the novel's sharpest ironies [010-001].

## Significance

The Franz rescue is a miniature morality tale about the limits of literary idealism. Martin's Indian-book ethics — the code of the noble savage, the fair fight, the gesture that turns an enemy into an ally — fail utterly when applied to the real world of the Long Row. The episode is also one of the novel's most economical characterizations of Franz, whose ferocity is so complete that it cannot be domesticated by any narrative, least of all Martin's romantic one.
