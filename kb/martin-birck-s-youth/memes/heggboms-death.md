## Overview

Heggbom's death marks one of the earliest encounters with mortality in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. On a rainy evening in the Birck household, the maid Lotta delivers the news with solemn formality, and the family processes it through their characteristic lenses — grandmother's earthy pragmatism, mother's charitable impulse, and Martin's uncomprehending theological anxiety.

## The Announcement

"While Martin, still sobbing, made the rounds to say good night, Lotta came in with the tea-tray. She had a very solemn expression as she said, 'I'm sorry to have to tell you that Heggbom is dead'" [005-001]. The room falls silent; Martin stops crying.

Grandmother responds with a characteristic mix of piety and realism: "Well, and has he really passed away? Has it come that suddenly? ... Glory be! and has he passed away? Ah, 'twas the brandy!" [005-001]. She concludes that "it was for the best that he should die, though 'twill be hard for the missus; he was the porter, anyway, and maintained his wife and children" [005-001].

Lotta adds the precise detail: "He died just at seven" [005-001], then withdraws to the kitchen when no one says more. Martin's mother, ever practical, suggests "it might be a good idea to send out a list to the neighbors and start a little subscription" [005-001].

## Martin's Wakeful Night

Martin is sent to bed, and his mother abbreviates his prayers to "God Who hast us in Thy care" because he is so tired. But Martin had only pretended sleepiness "to get out of the long prayers that he didn't understand. It is impossible for a little child to associate any idea with such expressions as 'hallowed be Thy name' or 'Thy kingdom come'" [005-002].

He lies awake "a long time listening to the rain as it plashed against the window" [005-002], preoccupied with a child's theological puzzlement: "He lay thinking about Heggbom and wondering if he could get to heaven. He always smelled of brandy" [005-002].

## Aftermath

Martin, afraid of the dark, asks Lotta to leave a candle. She refuses: "You must sleep, Martin. Heggbom will come and bite you if you don't" [005-003]. With that she takes the candle and departs.

The death of the porter, already a ghost, becomes a nursery threat. The chapter ends with Martin crying afresh as the wind whistles, gates bang, and a dog howls — and before the curtains are drawn, he thinks he sees "a red glow in the sky" [005-004], perhaps a fire in South Stockholm.
