## Overview

Martin's mistress is introduced in *The Winter Night, II* of Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. She is not named, but her inner life is rendered with unusual sympathy. A woman approaching thirty who works in a life insurance office, she chose Martin after years of watching her prospects dwindle — and found in him something the other men could not offer: understanding.

## The Walk Home

On a snowy winter evening, Martin turns into Long Western Street, choosing it for shelter from the wind: "in the narrow winding rift between the tall old houses one was as if half indoors, in the lee of the worst wind gusts" [028-001]. He meditates on cold, fire, and human goals, reflecting that he has "been frozen too long" to thaw out even now that "a little sunlight has come into my life" [028-003].

At the street corner near his home, he stops and looks up at a fourth-story window: "Yes, there was a light there; she was home then" [028-005]. He knows she expects him after dinner — they are to go to the theater together that evening, sitting "in a stage box behind a screen where nobody could see them" [028-005].

## Her History

The chapter then departs from Martin's perspective to reveal the woman's story. "She worked in a life insurance office in the morning counting money. She worked for her living" [028-007]. Like other young girls, she had "dreamed of a happiness which should be correct, and had guarded her jewel in the hope of being married" [028-007]. She had been in love with men who never noticed; "these small flames had gone out when they had no fuel" [028-007].

The years slipped away. "She had danced in the winter and bicycled in the summer, and many men had let her divine by their looks and veiled words that they would gladly possess her; but no one had wanted to marry her, for she had no dowry and did not belong to a family with influence" [028-007]. Those who might have proposed were scared off by her elegance — she made her own dresses from cheap remnants, and her taste suggested a costliness that made skeptical men doubt her virtue [028-007].

## The Decision

She had "felt the minutes running through her fingers like sand, and had decided that the years to come would be still more wretched and worthless than those before and that the jewel she guarded was losing its value every day" [028-009]. The desire that had once been "a sweet and indefinite longing" now "burned in her veins like poison" [028-009]. She searched for a lover among her circle, but every time an understanding approached, "she read in the man's eyes the ineradicable crudity of his heart" — that what was for her a life-changing experience was for him merely "an amorous adventure" [028-010].

## Meeting Martin

With Martin, everything was different. "When she gave herself to him she felt no more fear, because she saw that he had understood her, that his thoughts were not like those of the others, and she felt that he loved her" [028-011]. Though "vanished illusions had made her bitter at heart and crude of speech," Söderberg adds: "the bitter heart beat warm and fast when it rested on his, and the ugly words did not make her mouth less sweet to kiss" [028-011].
