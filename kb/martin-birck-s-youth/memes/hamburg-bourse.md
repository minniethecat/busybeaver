## Overview

The Hamburg Bourse is a café in central Stockholm that Martin Birck visits after his graduation supper at Hasselbacken is cut short by rain. The café embodies a particular stratum of Stockholm's intellectual life — teachers, "oldsters," and minor literati — and provides the setting for one of the novel's most charming comic moments, when Martin realizes that the "Hamburg Bourse" is not a merchants' exchange but a coffeehouse. The revelation is miniature Söderberg: a deflation of boyish literal-mindedness by adult reality, rendered with gentle irony rather than mockery [002-054].

## Description in the Text

The scene is set in the aftermath of the Hasselbacken supper. A sudden downpour sends the party scrambling: "It rained as if the heavens were opened. They crowded into a street car to go into the city and have coffee. Most of them voted to go to the Hamburg Bourse" [002-054].

Martin's confusion about the nature of the establishment is rendered with wry specificity:

> Martin, who had always believed the Hamburg Bourse was a place where the German merchants of Stockholm assembled to do business, found himself to his surprise entering a café that seemed to irradiate a fabulous magnificence. Here and there on the couches sat some of his former teachers and a lot of oldsters who lifted their glasses and nodded genially. [002-054]

The gap between Martin's mental image — sober German merchants in suits, conducting serious commerce — and the reality of a slightly shabby café populated by teachers and elderly drinkers is the engine of the comedy. But the humor is not at Martin's expense alone; Söderberg uses the misapprehension to draw a wider portrait of the café as a social institution, a halfway house between public and private life where the boundaries of class and profession blur.

## Narrative Function

The Hamburg Bourse episode functions as a coda to the Hasselbacken scene and a pivot from celebration to aftermath. The move from the open-air restaurant to the enclosed café mirrors the novel's broader trajectory from the expansiveness of youth to the confines of adult accommodation. The café, with its "former teachers" and "oldsters," is a glimpse of the world Martin is about to enter — a world of modest pleasures and diminished horizons.

The episode also exemplifies Söderberg's technique of defamiliarization through naming. Martin takes the name "Hamburg Bourse" literally and is surprised by the reality; the reader, who may have made the same assumption, shares the surprise. It is a small lesson in the gap between words and things — a gap that Martin, as a poet *manqué*, will spend his life navigating.
