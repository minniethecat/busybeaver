## Overview

Martin's first visit to the Dupont apartment is one of the novel's most densely symbolic scenes. Guided by his playmate Ida Dupont, Martin enters a world utterly unlike his own Protestant, bourgeois home: a twilight room filled with the scent of incense, a peepshow box of colored wonders, a glowing madonna shrine, and a father who is a violinist in the Royal Orchestra. The encounter introduces art, religion, and performance as intertwined modes of being — and plants in Martin the seeds of an aesthetic sensibility that will define his character.

## Context

The visit occurs early in the first chapter, shortly after Martin and Ida meet on the street outside their homes. Martin lives in the only stone house on the street; Ida lives nearby. Her father, Mr. Dupont, is a Catholic and a violinist — an exotic combination in late 19th-century Stockholm, where the established church is Lutheran and the arts are viewed with a mixture of admiration and suspicion.

## Narrative Account

Ida leads Martin into the Dupont apartment, where the inner room lies in "semi-twilight, with a high Venetian blind in front of the window." Pushing aside a corner of the blind, Martin sees "between two gray house gables a part of the great black church cupola" as the bells toll [001-XXX].

Ida shows Martin a peepshow box — a miniature theatre of tinted pictures: "white castles and gardens with colored lanterns in long gleaming rows, yellow and red and blue... strange cities with churches and bridges, and steamboats and big ships on a wide river." Söderberg adds a characteristic note of demystification: "what looked like lights were just little holes made with pins" [001-XXX]. The peepshow is the novel's first image of art as illusion — beautiful, compelling, and mechanically produced.

The scene deepens as Martin explores the sleeping alcove. In the semidarkness he discovers "an image of the madonna behind two half-burned wax candles, and below hung a crucifix" [001-XXX]. When Martin asks about it, Ida becomes solemn and whispers, "That is our religion" [001-XXX]. Her whispered disclosure transforms the apartment into sacred space.

Ida then enacts an elaborate ritual: she swathes herself in pink tulle with gold spangles, lights the candles under the madonna — "two calm bright flames" — and burns a pastille of incense from which "long blue clouds... curled from under the curtain of the alcove" [001-XXX]. She falls on her knees before the madonna, her "thick, dark-red plaits glow like bright copper in the candlelight," and mutters words Martin does not understand [001-XXX].

The scene is at once a child's game of dress-up and a genuine religious performance. Ida is actress, priestess, and guide — a figure who will haunt Martin's imagination throughout the novel.

## Key Participants

- **Martin Birck** — The visitor, wide-eyed and receptive, encountering a world of beauty and mystery.
- **Ida Dupont** — Martin's guide and performer, who enacts the madonna ritual with solemn theatricality.
- **Mr. Dupont** — The violinist father, absent from the scene but present through his art and religion.

## Significance

The Dupont apartment scene functions as Martin's aesthetic awakening. The room is a heterotopia — a space that contains and juxtaposes multiple worlds: the Catholic South and the Lutheran North, childhood play and adult performance, sacred ritual and theatrical spectacle. The peepshow box, with its artificial lights made of pinpricks, is Söderberg's sly emblem for the novel's own method: art as crafted illusion, beautiful precisely because it is made.

The madonna ritual introduces the theme of performance as a mode of identity. Ida, costumed in pink tulle, is not pretending to be a priestess — she is one, within the logic of the game. This fluidity between roles — child/priestess, player/believer — anticipates Martin's own lifelong negotiation between his inner poetic self and his outward bourgeois persona.
