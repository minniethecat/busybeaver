## Overview

The afternoon Martin writes to Harriet Skottë is one of the most psychologically revealing scenes in Söderberg's novel. It is a meditation on desire, anonymity, and the creative act itself: Martin's letter is both a genuine expression of longing and a literary performance — "half in play, with an intention here and there yet without exactly knowing whither he was tending" [025-040]. The letter is never sent, but the writing of it transforms an infatuation into something permanent.

## The Setting

"The afternoon sun fell across the writing table and gilded everything: the inkstand, the books, and the words he wrote on the paper" [025-002]. Across the street, "a young Jewess was playing with her child" in a window — a quiet, domestic counterpoint to Martin's romantic inwardness. The smoke from chimneys rises "straight and tranquilly toward heaven" [025-002]. Everything in this scene is still, luminous, suspended.

## The Letter to Maria

Martin first writes a letter to his sister Maria, which is by turns affectionate, ironic, and sad. He mocks the tedious relatives ("Uncle Janne, Aunt Louise, etc., are still, unfortunately, alive and in health, though it doesn't make much difference anyhow, since we are not likely to be their heirs"), shares a dream about receiving the Order of Vasa with his father, and declines her summer invitation [025-003].

After finishing, he reflects on Maria's marriage: she was happy six years ago, "but she hasn't had any sudden fall from the peak of happiness since then. She has just very quietly slipped down, as usually happens with the years." He suspects her husband has been unfaithful. "A curious bird, happiness..." [025-019].

## The Love Letter

Martin then begins to write — "slowly and half in play, with an intention here and there yet without exactly knowing whither he was tending" [025-041]. The letter is addressed to Harriet Skottë, a young woman he has seen walking in the sunlight. Its opening is one of the novel's most lyrical passages:

"You do not know me. I met you one day in the sunlight. It is weeks, yes, months since then. You went on the side of the street where the sun shone; you went alone with head lowered and smiled to yourself" [025-042].

The letter is an exercise in asymmetrical intimacy. He describes watching her from a hidden gate, knowing the star (Venus) that stands before her window at night, and the yellow lamp whose glow he has memorized. "You go in the sunlight, I go for the most part in the shadow" [025-048]. He ends with a playful threat: if she shows the letter to her friends, "a little devil from hell will sit on the edge of your bed and look at you from evening till morning" [025-054].

## The Weighing of Fate

Martin holds the letter in his hand "as if it had been a human destiny" and imagines its possible effects. He concludes it would do no harm — it would only tell her "that she is desired by a man, and as she is twenty and from head to foot an uncommonly beautiful and glorious creation of nature, she must already have noticed that men desire her. And that doesn't at all make her angry, but on the contrary happy and joyous, and that is why she walks in the sunlight and smiles" [025-058].

He addresses the envelope in "a thin and noncommittal girlish style," having learned her name through discreet inquiry. "Harriet Skottë" — he whispers the name to himself, finding that the combination of the "English and pale and blonde" forename with the solid family surname creates "something quite different and new, then he felt as if she herself passed through the room with her brown hair glinting in a sunbeam" [025-064].

## Rissler's Interruption

The hall bell rings. Henrik Rissler stands in the doorway "blinking at the sunlight, whose copper-red rays struck horizontally across the room" [025-006]. Martin stuffs the letter into his pocket. The letter is never sent.

## Notable Passages

> Amid such thoughts he sat a long while weighing the letter in his hand as if it had been a human destiny, till in the end he found his hesitation ridiculous, put the letter in an envelope of thick untransparent paper, and wrote the address in a thin and noncommittal girlish style so as not to rouse any curiosity in the young lady's family. Without revealing any special interest on his part he had succeeded in learning her name. She was a Miss Harriet Skottë. Her father had an estate in the country, in the Malar district, and she was now spending the winter in Stockholm with some relatives to study something or other, French or art-tapestry or something of the sort ... in order to get engaged, to put it briefly.
>
