## Overview

On a winter evening in the Birck household, after grandmother has told stories to the children, father puts aside his cigar and invents an impromptu game: building a miniature castle from empty matchboxes inside the porcelain stove, then setting it alight and narrating its spectacular destruction. For young Martin, the burning castle is a moment of pure enchantment — and the disappointment when father refuses to repeat it is equally real. The scene forms part of a triptych of domestic warmth in the novel's fourth chapter: grandmother's tales, mother's songs at the piano, and father's playful improvisation.

## The Domestic Setting

The scene unfolds in a specific sensory atmosphere. Earlier that evening, Martin's mother had been at the piano: "Martin Birck's mother, when twilight fell, often sat at the piano and sang to her own accompaniment. She had the sweetest of voices." [004-301] She sang songs of soldiers and dying princes, of shepherdesses in Alpine defiles — "the songs she sang were such as no one sings any more." [004-301] Martin and Maria would stand behind her stool "entranced," joining in when they could. [004-301] The piano itself had a distinctive character: "The strings had a brittle, glassy sound, and the pedals sighed and groaned. A string was broken in the bass, and it would buzz now and again." [004-301]

Grandmother, too, had contributed to the evening's imaginative atmosphere. She had told the children stories — "Martin and Maria listened with wide-open mouths. Grandmother had told a story, after all. Of such stories she knew plenty." [004-319]

It was against this backdrop of music and story that father now stepped forward with his own contribution: a game built from empty matchboxes and fire.

## The Game

Father had lighted a cigar and pushed his chair nearer the fire, then motioned to Martin and Maria: "Come, children, now we'll play." [004-321] The blaze had almost burned out. Father broke apart two or three empty match boxes and built out of the fragments a house deep in the porcelain stove. He put in matches as pillars and beams and twisted up a piece of stiff paper for a tower, cutting a hole at the top for a chimney. [004-323]

The creation was "a stately castle like the old Stockholm castles in Dahlberg's *Swedish Monuments*." [004-323] When it was complete, father set fire to all the corners. It hissed and sputtered and burned. [004-325]

Father narrated the destruction with dramatic flair:

> "Look — just look how it's burning! — now the farthest corner is catching — now the eastern gate's on fire, now it's falling! — and the tower's burning, the tower's tumbling —" [004-327]

When it was over, Martin begged: "Again, papa. Oh, again! Just once more!" [004-331] But father refused — "it's no fun the second time" [004-333] — and went to the piano to stroke his wife's hair. [004-335]

## Martin's Enchantment

Martin remained sitting in front of the fire, his cheeks burning, unable to tear himself away: "It flamed and glowed so finely away in there. It glimmered and glowed and burned." [004-337] Eventually grandmother came, shut the damper, and put down the slats. [004-339]

Then Martin went to the window. The sun was long gone, and "murky cloud masses were driving along in broken lines over the thin, glassy blue of the sky." [004-341] Long Row lay in deep twilight. The lindens and cherry trees of the garden were stripped of leaves, and "here and there a light was already gleaming in a window from out the dark net of boughs." [004-341] Down on the street, the old lamplighter with his leather cap went about his task. When he lit the lamp directly opposite the window, "the whole room brightened. The white lace curtains outlined their broken pattern on the ceiling and walls, while the calla lilies and fuchsias painted fantastic shadows." [004-341]

The scene captures a particular quality of childhood experience: the intensity of a fleeting moment of beauty, the father's gift of imaginative play, and the child's inability to understand why something so magical cannot be repeated on demand.

## The Piano as Family Center

The chapter returns to the piano motif — and with it, a moment of childhood anxiety. Later that same evening, Martin overheard his father say to his mother: "Perhaps you're right that we ought to try to sell the piano and buy a pianino on instalment. It goes out of tune in a couple of weeks, and a pianino would be prettier." [004-363]

Martin's reaction was visceral: "Martin gave a start at the words 'sell the piano.' He had no clear idea of what a pianino was, but he didn't believe it could be a real piano; he pictured it rather as something that was worked with a handle." [004-365] He loved every dent and crack in the mahogany frame — "for he himself had made most of them, and he remembered almost every key from its special color. Sell the piano! To his ears it sounded like something impossible. It was almost as if he had heard his parents calmly sitting and talking about selling grandmother and buying an aunt instead." [004-365]

This moment — placed immediately after the burning castle game — reveals how Martin's emotional world is anchored in the physical objects of the household. The piano, like the matchbox castle, is charged with meaning that adults cannot perceive.

## Themes

The burning city game belongs to a larger pattern in the novel of father's quiet, improvisational devotion to his children. Unlike mother's moral instruction or grandmother's folk tales, father's contribution to the children's imaginative world is wordless craft — building something beautiful and transient, setting it alight, and letting the fire do the storytelling. His refusal to repeat the game ("it's no fun the second time") is both a practical observation and an unwitting lesson in the nature of beauty: it is fleeting, and its power lies partly in its irrecoverability.

The chapter as a whole — grandmother's stories, mother's songs, father's game, and Martin's terror at the thought of losing the piano — maps the emotional geography of the Birck household. Each adult contributes a different register of imagination to the children's lives: folklore, music, play, and the precarious material world of objects that hold memory.
