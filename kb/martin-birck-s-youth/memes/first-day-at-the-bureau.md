## Overview

Martin Birck's first day at the government bureau is one of the most darkly comic episodes in Hjalmar Söderberg's novel. The scene condenses the gap between the dignity of official life and its mundane reality into a single indelible image: a young man in formal dress nearly tripping over a chamberpot while being lectured on honesty by a half-shaven superior in a satin dressing-gown.

## The Arrival

Martin must "attend upon the chief of the bureau to which he most desired to submit his services at eight o'clock in the morning in a frock coat and white necktie" [019-011].

He arrives "cold and hungry, for he had not had time to eat," and ascends "the steps of a quiet house in a fashionable street" [019-011]. An attendant in gold braid announces him and opens the door to "a dark private room with curtains only half up" [019-011].

## The General Director

What Martin finds inside defies all expectations of bureaucratic dignity: "various articles of dress lay scattered about here and there on the chairs, a great green laticlave hung on the mirror, and at the threshold stood a chamberpot, which he nearly tripped over" [019-011]. He checks himself in time and makes "an awkward bow."

In the middle of the room stands "a venerable old man in a purple-red satin dressing-gown, gesticulating with a razor, his chin covered with lather" [019-011].

## The Interview

Out of "the red satin and the white lather" comes a voice: "You have a fine student certificate, young gentleman, but don't forget that honesty and diligence are and will continue to be the highest requisites in government service. You are accepted and may report tomorrow to begin your duties, if there is anything to do. Above everything, be honest! Goodbye" [019-011].

## The Daily Routine

Martin is given a place at a table and a thick ledger to inspect, adding column after column. If the figures come out right, his duty is "to put ticks in the margin; if they did not, he was to make notes of the fact" [019-013]. But they always come out right.

He gradually arrives at "the conviction that there were never any mistakes in these accounts, and when this conviction became rooted in him, he gave up adding entirely and merely put in ticks" [019-013].

Sometimes he looks up from his work and listens to "the buzzing of the flies or the rain plashing on the windowpanes, or to the conversation and grumbling of the older men, or to a blind man playing a flute in the yard" [019-013].

He says to himself: "So this is life" [019-015].

## Significance

The bureau episode is Söderberg's satire of the gap between official rhetoric and institutional reality. The General Director's exhortation to "honesty" — delivered while half-dressed and indifferent to the absurdity of the scene — becomes an ironic leitmotif for Martin's subsequent career.

The chamberpot at the threshold, which Martin nearly stumbles over, is the novel's emblem of the indignities that await the ambitious young man in the world of respectable employment. Martin's silent acceptance of meaningless work captures the quiet despair of bureaucratic life.
