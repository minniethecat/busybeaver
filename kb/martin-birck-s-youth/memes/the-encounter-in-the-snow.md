## Overview

The encounter in the snow occurs when Martin, now in his early twenties, is seized by the memory of a young woman he saw walking in the sunlight at noon. Unable to concentrate on his poetry, he goes out into a snowy March twilight to find her. His search leads not to the woman but to a prostitute's room, a bench in a snowy park, and a bitter reflection on the sexual hypocrisy of Stockholm society.

## The Noon Sighting

At midday, Martin had seen a young woman — "slender and straight, her brown hair shining in the sunlight" — and had followed her at a distance to a gateway in Östermalm [021-002]. The encounter left him intoxicated. Hours later, as he sits in the dusk "twisting and turning over an old poem," her image returns and "left him no rest" [021-003].

## The Snowy Search

Martin goes out into the falling snow and walks to the house where she vanished. He paces the pavement, watching windows light up, then reads the names on the doors — before deciding he is "childish and stupid" [021-004]. He pulls up his collar and "took by the arm the first girl that gave him a meaningful glance and went home with her" [021-004].

## The Prostitute's Room

In her room, he stands "stiffly and silently surveying her as she took off her clothes." He is neither tempted nor repelled — he "only knew that she might have been prettier without tempting him more and uglier without tempting him less" [021-006]. Suddenly confused about why he came, he mutters an excuse, pays her, and leaves.

## The Meditation

Sitting on a snow-covered bench, Martin asks: "What are we doing with life, we mortals?" [021-009]. He reflects on the two kinds of love that respectable society recognizes — the pure (reserved for marriage, which is unaffordable) and the sensual (a weekly hygienic diversion with prostitutes). The young men "drank punch, visited girls of the streets, became fat and florid" while the young women of better families were left to "preserve their virtue" until it became "too heavy to carry" [021-014].
