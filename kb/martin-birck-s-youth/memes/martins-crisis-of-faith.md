## Overview

Martin's crisis of faith is the intellectual turning point of Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. At the age of sixteen, on the eve of his confirmation, he confronts the impossibility of believing in Christianity and emerges with a new resolve: "To seek for truth and be honest with oneself in the search" [020-004].

## The Parting of the Ways

Martin had been a Christian up to his sixteenth year. "It is natural for a child to believe what his elders say is true. He had believed everything and had not doubted" [020-003]. But confirmation forced the question: "if religion was the truth, then it was right in this claim, the claim to be above everything else, and he must devote all his powers and his whole soul to it; he must become religious. But if it was not the truth, then he must seek the truth wherever he could find it; he must become a freethinker" [020-004]. The middle ground — "the Christianity of use and custom such as is professed and believed in by the multitude" — struck him as "mere thoughtlessness and conventionality" [020-004].

## The Night at the Window

One night, unable to sleep, Martin lay awake with the moon shining into his room. "Suddenly it stood clear to him that he did not believe" [020-005]. But what disturbed him more was the question: "How was it possible that the others could believe in this when he could not?" [020-005] He meant not only his classmates but "his parents, his teachers, all the grownups, who must know more of life and the world than he did" [020-005]. The paradox seemed "almost as wildly absurd as the faith he had just rejected" [020-005].

He got out of bed and went to the window. "Snow was glittering white on the roofs, it was dark in the houses, and the street lay empty. The moon stood high in the heavens, but it was a gray-white winter moon, small and frostbitten and infinitely far away" [020-005]. Standing at the window he traced on the pane with his finger: "'Give me a sign, God!' he whispered" [020-005]. He stood long, watching the moon glide behind a black factory chimney and creep out again. "But he received no sign" [020-005].

## The Confirmation

Martin went through confirmation feeling hollow. When he saw the church full of serious people and heard the preacher's voice enjoining the young to hold fast to their fathers' faith, "he felt unrest and confusion through his inmost soul, and again the question came to trouble him: 'How is it possible that all these can believe, and not I?'" [020-007] But when the actual ceremony began and he saw the ministers in their surplices "dispensed the bread and wine and carried napkins over their arms like waiters, he felt faint and disgusted and could not understand that he had let himself be fooled into such mummery" [020-007].

## A New Creed

Martin emerged from the experience without faith in God or immortality. He "set out in life without any other belief than that he would grow up, get old and die like a tree in the ground, as his forefathers had done" [020-009]. Yet he was not nihilistic — he "wished to create happiness and beauty around him and to clear away illusions; he meant to speak and write so that all would have to perceive at once that he was right" [020-009]. He believed that religion's days were numbered, "that he lived at the dawn of a new time, and he wanted to play his part in thought and poetry toward breaking the road for what was to come" [020-009].

The guiding principle he took from this crisis was simple: "To seek for truth and be honest with oneself in the search, that was the one clue he could find" [020-005].
