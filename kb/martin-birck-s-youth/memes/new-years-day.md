## Overview

New Year's Day in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth* is a day of ritual and reflection, marking the transition from the Christmas season to the long gray stretch of winter. For the young Martin, it is both a continuation of holiday warmth and a reminder of time's passage. The day begins in darkness with early church service, continues through rounds of family visits laden with cakes and wine, and ends with the sobering recognition that the new year looks remarkably like the old one.

## The Night Before

On New Year's Eve, "the star of New Year's Eve was burning across the white roofs, and people said to each other, 'Good night, and thanks for the year!'" [006-006]. Already Martin feels a shiver of apprehension: "With a shivering sensation Martin thought of the line of gray winter days that were waiting, to which he could see no ending, for it was interminably long till summer, and still longer till next Christmas" [006-006].

## The Morning Service

New Year's morning begins in darkness. Martin is woken "while it was still dark to go to early service" [006-007]. Half asleep, he scrambles through the snow at his parents' side. As they round a corner, the church appears "like a giant lantern shining out across the white square" [006-007], with worshippers "crawling in across the snow from all directions."

Inside the church, the sensory richness of organ music, singing, and "many shining candles" [006-007] fills the space. Martin feels "happy and good and thought this was just the right way to begin the new year" [006-007]. The minister's sermon proves less captivating — Martin falls asleep. He wakes to the "pale hue of dawn" [006-007] shining through the cupola windows and his mother's practical summons: "Now we'll go home and drink our coffee" [006-007].

The family leaves the church with "their hearts full of the most beautiful intentions, for Martin understood without telling that it was this sort of thing the minister had preached about" [006-007]. The moral uplift, though vague in content, is sincere in feeling.

## The New Year's Visits

Later in the morning, Martin and his sister Maria are dispatched on the traditional New Year's rounds: "Martin and Maria were sent around on the New Year's visits to Uncle Jan and Aunt Louisa and other uncles and aunts, where they were given cakes and wine and sugarplums from the Christmas trees" [006-007]. The visits follow established ritual — each household offering the children sweets and holiday cheer, tokens of a family network bound by seasonal obligation and genuine affection.

## Uncle Abraham

The visit to Uncle Abraham stands apart from the others. "At Uncle Abraham's there was no Christmas tree, for he was a widower and had no children but lived alone with an old housekeeper" [006-007]. Abraham is a doctor who has treated Martin and Maria for "measles and scarlatina and pains in the chest" [006-007]. He has "a black beard and a long crooked nose, for he was a Jew" [006-007] — a detail delivered with the stark matter-of-factness of a child's observation.

His room tells its own story: "The room was large and half dark and full of books. Outside the snow was falling in great flakes" [006-008]. Two companions share his solitude: a parrot "that could swear in French" [006-007] and a black tomcat named Kolmodin, "the cleverest cat in the world" [006-007], who pulls the bell-cord with his claws when he wants to enter. When Martin and Maria arrive, "he was sitting alone with his bottle of wine on the table playing chess with himself" [006-007].

Abraham receives the children warmly — "stuffed their pockets full of goodies, made the parrot swear in French, and was very cordial; but he didn't say much" [006-008]. The cat Kolmodin "sat staring gloomily at his master" [006-008] before the porcelain stove. Martin and Maria feel the weight of something unspoken: "Martin and Maria stood silent and looked at each other with a feeling of oppression. For they had more than once heard their parents say that Uncle Abraham was not a happy man and that he never was really cheerful" [006-008].

The visit to Uncle Abraham is the emotional core of New Year's Day — a moment where holiday ritual brushes against adult loneliness, where the children's festive rounds encounter a man whose solitude the season only deepens.

## The New Almanac

The new year's only visible change is the almanac's cover: "The almanac which Martin had given his father for Christmas had a red cover, whereas the old one had been blue" [007-001]. Martin is struck by how the new year looks the same as the old — "the days passed as they had passed before with ringing of bells and snow and a somber sky" [007-001]. The continuity disappoints him; the year's turning promised more.

With the holidays over, "Martin had to keep indoors with his alphabet book and multiplication tables, with his color-box and jumping-jacks and all splendid things — already faded — which Christmas had left behind" [007-002]. Among his toys, the Red Turk jumping-jack — a gift from Uncle Abraham — holds special significance (see [[the-red-turk-jumping-jack]]).

## The Long Winter Ahead

The post-holiday return to ordinary life carries a melancholy particular to childhood: "the days passed as they had passed before with ringing of bells and snow and a somber sky, with weariness of the old games and the old stories, and with the longing to be big" [007-001]. Martin both yearns for adulthood and fears it, haunted by his mother's warnings about the ragman — a cautionary figure of social decline that makes him "see himself slinking in through the gate at dusk with a pack on his back and poking in the ash barrel with a black stick, while father and mother and sister and grandmother were sitting together around the lamp as before" [007-001].

## Themes

New Year's Day in the novel serves as a pivot between the warmth of Christmas and the gray expanse of winter. It is a day of ritual observance — church, family visits, holiday foods — that cannot quite mask the existential undertow: time passes, nothing visibly changes, and the line between childhood and adulthood, between happiness and loneliness, runs through the quietest moments. The visit to Uncle Abraham, with his chess game played against himself and his gloomily staring cat, embodies this tension with the economy of a short story embedded within the chapter.

## Notable Passages

> New Year's morning he was waked while it was still dark to go to early service. Half asleep he scrambled through the snow by the side of his parents, and as they came around the corner, there stood the church like a giant lantern shining out across the white square where people were crawling in across the snow from all directions. Within the church was the organ's roar and singing and many shining candles, and Martin felt happy and good and thought this was just the right way to begin the new year; and when the minister began to preach, he went straight to sleep.

> (006-002)
