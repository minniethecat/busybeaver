## Overview

Von Heringslake is Martin Birck's senior colleague at the government office, a chief clerk with "an income of forty-six hundred crowns" who performs his duties "with the pleasant ease which comes with an independence" [027-001]. He is introduced in a single vivid image: squatted in front of the porcelain stove, roasting apples over the embers, the triangular reflection of a green lamp glinting on his bald pate—which "his mortal enemy, Auditor Camin, asserted was the result of early dissipations but which in reality shone with the innocence of early childhood" [027-001]. He is a figure of mild comedy and genuine comfort, the man who has made his peace with the modest satisfactions of a government salary and a warm stove.

## Role in the Narrative

Heringslake appears principally in two brief scenes set at the government office. In the first, he roasts apples while Auditor Camin repeats his eternal pronouncement about farmers and shooting licenses; Martin, hungry and irritable, is "bitterly annoyed that he had not in all ways the same views concerning this and the future life as Heringslake, for then he would surely have been offered an apple" [027-001]. After eating his apples, Heringslake resumes his seat beside Martin and "surveyed him mournfully" [027-002]—a glance that may express pity, curiosity, or simply the digestive melancholy of a man who has finished his snack.

He reappears, indirectly, in Martin's long interior monologue about cold and heat: "Heringslake, who has a heart in his breast and loves his native land, regards cold as preferable to heat. But when it's cold, he always puts on furs" [028-001]. The observation is characteristic of the novel's treatment of minor characters: a single ironic detail that opens onto a wider reflection. Martin's speculation that cold preserves human beings as it preserves provisions—"Why not human beings as well?"—is prompted by Heringslake but leaves him far behind.

## Key Relationships

- **Martin Birck** — Junior colleague; Heringslake sits beside him and surveys him mournfully, a wordless relationship of office proximity.
- **Auditor Camin** — "Mortal enemy"; Camin claims Heringslake's baldness results from "early dissipations," while the narrator insists it "shone with the innocence of early childhood."

## Significance

Heringslake is one of the novel's minor functionaries—a figure of settled, slightly comic contentment who serves as a foil for Martin's restlessness. His roasted apples, his fur coat, his forty-six hundred crowns: these are the modest coordinates of a life that has asked for little and received it. Martin envies him not for his income or his position but for his views "concerning this and the future life"—the ease of a man who has stopped asking questions.
