## Overview

Nockeby Bridge, spanning a narrows of Lake Mälaren west of Stockholm, is the setting for Martin Birck's most idyllic summer experiences. Martin's parents rent rooms in the white house of the bridge-tender, old Moberg, and the bridge becomes the center of Martin's world: a place for steamboat watching, ghost-story evenings, and the first stirrings of childhood friendship and fear. The extended Nockeby episode — spanning the final portion of the first chapter — is the novel's longest sustained passage of pastoral writing and represents the paradise from which Martin's subsequent city life is a gradual exile [001-XXX].

## Description in the Text

Söderberg introduces the bridge through Martin's arrival at the summer lodging:

> Martin's parents had rented several low-ceiled rooms with rickety white furniture and the bluest window-blinds in the world for the small square windows. Close to these windows passed the state highroad. Here wagoners and wayfarers from the islands of the Malar went by continually to and from the city, all stopping to pay the bridge toll, for the white house belonged to the bridge-tender and stood just at the abutment of Nockeby bridge. [001-XXX]

The bridge-tender himself — old Moberg, a former sea captain — is sketched with affectionate detail: "a little old white-haired man, who had for many years had the tenancy of the bridge and had become a well-to-do citizen," who sits every evening on his hop-vine porch "drinking toddy, holding out his money-box to the passersby, chatting and telling yarns" [001-XXX].

The bridge's central feature — its drawbridge mechanism — provides the narrative with both structure and spectacle. Martin learns to identify every steamboat by its whistle: the *Fyris*, the *Garibaldi*, the *Bragë*, the "lovely blue" *Tynnelsö*, and the brown *Enköping* — "called the *Coffeepot*, because it sputtered like boiling coffee" [001-XXX]. When a steamboat approaches, "its lanterns would sooner or later pop out from behind the cape, first the green and then the red," and Martin and Johan rush to wind up the drawbridge [001-XXX].

The bridge is also the stage for Martin's exposure to the adult world's darker currents. Johan, the bridge-tender's assistant, fills the dusk hours with tales of the supernatural and the criminal: the minister at Lovö who is "a friend of Old Spotty himself," a murder on the highroad three years past where "if anybody goes by at the right time, he can hear the most terrible groaning in the ditch" [001-XXX]. Martin, caught between terror and fascination, "squeezed close to Johan's arm" as they walk home across the bridge, hearing phantom footsteps behind them [001-XXX].

In a memorable tableau, the Swedish cavalry thunders across the bridge on maneuvers: "a rumbling over the bridge as if there was thunder... a cloud of dust over the road, horses' hoofs rang on the ground and the bridge, and there were lightnings of drawn swords" [001-XXX]. Martin watches in awe, momentarily torn between the romance of the artillery and his earlier devotion to the coasting skippers.

## Narrative Function

Nockeby Bridge functions as a pastoral interlude — an Eden of childhood perception bracketed by the urban chapters that precede and follow it. The bridge is a threshold in multiple senses: between city and country, between the mundane and the marvelous, between Martin's safe domestic world and the larger world of danger and death that Johan's stories disclose.

The bridge as architectural form is itself symbolic: it connects two shores but belongs to neither, suspended over the water. Martin's childhood, seen in retrospect, occupies a similar position — a span between the innocence of infancy and the knowledge of adulthood. The drawbridge, which must open to let boats pass, is a figure for the permeability of Martin's world: the larger forces (steam technology, military power, commerce) move through it, and Martin watches them pass, learning their names and rhythms.

The character of old Moberg, drinking todddy under his Japanese lantern "most unwilling to die," introduces the theme of mortality that will deepen throughout the novel. His porch, embowered in hop vines, is a liminal space between the domestic interior and the public highway — a position that echoes Martin's own situation as a child on the edge of adult consciousness.

## Associated Characters

- **Martin Birck** — Spends his seventh summer at the bridge, learning the world of boats and ghost stories.
- **Johan** — The bridge-tender's teenage assistant, Martin's first hero, who tells tales of murder and the devil.
- **Old Moberg** — The bridge-tender, a former sea captain, unwilling to die, drinking toddy every evening.
- **Martin's mother** — Teaches him letters in the garden and comforts him after his birthday disappointment.
- **Martin's father** — Commutes to the city on the *Bragë*.
- **Maria** — Martin's older sister, who shares the summer games.
