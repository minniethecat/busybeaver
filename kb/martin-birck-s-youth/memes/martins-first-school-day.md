## Overview

Martin Birck's first day at high school is a pivotal event in Hjalmar Söderberg's novel — the moment when the protected world of home gives way to an institution governed by its own harsh rules. The experience marks the beginning of Martin's education in what might be called the "harder school" of life, where the moral framework of family proves useless and survival demands a rapid recalibration of behavior. The episode is rendered with characteristic Söderberg economy: a single paragraph carries Martin from innocence to strategic cunning.

## The New World

Martin enters the high school to find "everything was strange and cold" [011-001]: "Gray walls, long corridors. The school yard was like the desert of Sahara" [011-001]. The comparison of the school yard to the Sahara is telling — it is not merely empty but alien, a landscape stripped of the domestic warmth and green gardens Martin has known. At the first recess, he tries to slip away, a gesture of avoidance that proves futile. During the second recess, the other boys surround him in a ring, studying him in silence — a ritual of assessment, a tribunal of children. Finally, "a little red-haired boy with a broad pate" [011-001] demands: "What sort of devil are you?" [011-001].

The question is existential as much as social. "What sort of devil are you?" means: What kind of creature have we here? What are your allegiances, your weaknesses, your place in the order we maintain? Martin has no prepared answer because, until this moment, he has never been asked to define himself against others.

## The Betrayal of Home Morality

Martin immediately understands that "a new stage of his life was beginning" [011-001]. The narrator elaborates with a reflection that is one of the novel's most penetrating passages on the collision between domestic morality and institutional reality:

> He had been as happy as a plant in the earth, as is every little child with kind parents and a good home. Now the doors were opened upon an entirely new world, a world where one could not get on by the same simple means that his father and mother had shown him: i.e., by being polite and friendly towards all he met and never taking advantage of others.

The "simple means" of home — politeness, friendliness, fairness — are exposed as strategies suited to a protected environment, not to the competitive arena of peers. The implication is not that Martin's parents were wrong to teach him these values, but that those values were incomplete, designed for a world that does not exist outside the household. The school becomes the first of many institutions that will force Martin to question the adequacy of his upbringing.

## The New Code

At school, the rules are different: "Here the thing was to decide quickly and firmly in what case one should use one's fists, in what one should take to one's heels, and under what circumstances one could benefit by cunning and deceit" [011-001]. The three options — violence, flight, deception — form a taxonomy of strategic response that Martin has never needed before. The syntax itself is instructive: "decide quickly and firmly," as if hesitation is the first lethal error.

## Martin's Adaptation

Martin adapts with surprising speed. He remembers "various curses and ugly words that he had heard from the bridge-tender's assistant in the country" [011-001] and deploys them strategically, "wherever he thought they would go" [011-001]. The bridge-tender's assistant — a figure from the margins of Martin's earlier, rural world — becomes an unexpected resource. What seemed merely coarse or frightening before now proves instrumental: the curses are tools, social currency in the schoolyard economy.

The irony is that Martin succeeds precisely by abandoning the moral training his parents gave him. He does not resist the new code; he masters it. The novel does not condemn this adaptation — it simply records it, with the cool clarity that is Söderberg's signature.

## The Teachers' Taxonomy

In exchange for Martin's linguistic offerings, the other boys "enlightened him in much that a newcomer might find useful" [011-001]. They share the school's oral tradition — a body of practical knowledge about survival:

- Which teachers **flog** and which only give bad marks
- The worst of all: **Director Sundell**, who "had mirrors in his spectacles so that he saw what was done behind his back and always wore galoshes so that he couldn't be heard in the corridors" [011-001]
- **"Sausages"** was decent, though he marked hard
- **"The Flea"** was "a damned sneak" [011-001]

The nicknames — Sausages, The Flea — are a parallel taxonomy to the one the boys apply to each other. Just as Martin must be classified ("What sort of devil are you?"), so must the teachers. The school is a world of endless classification, a miniature society with its own language, its own myths (Director Sundell's mirror-spectacles), and its own hierarchies of threat.

Director Sundell is the apex predator of this ecosystem — a figure rendered almost supernatural by the boys' folklore. The mirrors in his spectacles make him omniscient; the galoshes make him silent and therefore everywhere. He is the institutional panopticon made flesh, a bogeyman who is also real.

## Themes

The first school day encapsulates one of the novel's dominant concerns: the gap between the morality taught in the home and the morality demanded by the world. Martin's parents have raised him to be "polite and friendly toward all," but the schoolyard requires a calculus of violence, evasion, and deceit. The episode is not a morality tale — Martin is not punished for his adaptation, nor does he suffer guilt. He simply learns, and in learning, loses something he cannot name.

The chapter also inaugurates the theme of institutional education as a form of brutality that runs through the novel. The school is not evil, but it is indifferent — its gray walls and long corridors are architecture as argument, a physical assertion that the individual child does not matter. Martin's survival depends on his willingness to absorb and reproduce the school's unofficial curriculum, a body of knowledge far more immediately useful than anything in the official syllabus.
