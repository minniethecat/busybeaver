## Overview

The garden dream is the novel's opening scene — a surreal, anxiety-laden episode that sets the tone for Martin Birck's inner life. It combines a child's desire to please his mother with the sudden terror of loss and deception.

## The Marvelous Garden

"It was twilight of a summer evening, a green and tranquil twilight, and Martin went holding his mother's hand through a big and marvelous garden where the shadows lay dark in the recesses of the walks" [001-013]. On both sides grew strange blue and red flowers swaying in the wind. His mother warned: "You must pick only the blue ones; the red ones are poisonous" [001-013].

## The Flower That Changed

Martin let go of her hand and stopped to pick a big blue flower that "nodded heavily, poised on its stem" [001-013]. He looked at it and smelled it — then looked again with astonishment: "it wasn't blue, after all, but red. It was quite red! And such an ugly, poisonous red!" [001-013]. He threw the flower down and trampled it like a dangerous animal.

## The Disappearance

When Martin turned around, his mother was gone. He ran calling for her, near tears in the darkening garden, until a voice said: "Here I am, Martin. Don't you see me?" Running toward the lilac bush where the voice seemed to come from, he found not his mother but Franz from the Long Row, making ugly faces and sticking out his tongue — a tongue that "got longer and longer; there was no end to it" [001-013]. The dream then dissolved into a dark corner of the yard.
