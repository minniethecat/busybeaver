## Overview

Martin's Dream of the Garden is a dream sequence in the novel's opening chapter (Old Street I) that establishes the thematic tension between beauty and ugliness, trust and deception. As a small child, Martin dreams of walking through a green twilight garden holding his mother's hand. The dream shifts from wonder to horror when a flower he picks turns from blue to red, his mother vanishes, and the figure behind the lilac bush is not his mother but Franz from Long Row, sticking out an impossibly long, blistered tongue.

## Narrative Account

In the dream, Martin walks with his mother through a large garden at twilight. She tells him to pick only the blue flowers—the red ones are poisonous [071-075]. He reaches for a blue flower, but when he looks at it closely, it has turned to an ugly, poisonous red. Horrified, he tramples it [075-075]. When he turns around, his mother has vanished.

He runs through the darkening garden, near tears, until he hears her voice: "Here I am, Martin. Don't you see me? Here I am all the time. Why don't you come?" [075-075]. The voice leads him behind a lilac bush. Expecting his mother, he finds instead Franz from Long Row, making an ugly face and sticking out his tongue—"it got longer and longer; there was no end to it; and it was covered with little yellowish-green blisters" [075-075].

## Significance

The dream is the novel's first symbolic episode, prefiguring Martin's later experiences of disillusionment. The blue flower—a traditional Romantic symbol of poetic yearning—turns poisonous red, suggesting the danger concealed in beauty. The mother's disappearance and replacement by the grotesque Franz from Long Row (the embodiment of poverty and coarseness in Martin's world) dramatizes the loss of childhood security and the intrusion of a harsher reality.
