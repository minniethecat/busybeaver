## Overview

The outdoor concert is the scene of Martin Birck's first meeting with Henrik Rissler, the friendship that becomes the novel's model of genuine intellectual companionship. Set on the concert terraces of Stockholm, the scene establishes Rissler's character and the shared aesthetic sensibilities that will bind the two friends.

## First Meeting

Rissler is introduced during an outdoor concert, sitting with Martin by the balcony railing, "listening to the music, looking out across the terraces, and saying little" [016-002]. Söderberg tells us nothing about what is being played — the concert is less about the music itself than about the social geometry it enables: Martin and Rissler side by side, the terraces spread out below, the audience as a field for observation.

## Rissler's Description

Rissler's physical description signals his intellectual restlessness. He has "a smooth white forehead and calm limpid eyes," and his glance "seemed to slip over the objects nearest it in order more quickly to reach those farther off" [016-002]. This preference for the distant over the immediate defines Rissler's character throughout the novel.

## Shared Aesthetic

The two friends admire together a lithograph of *Don Juan in Hades* with a motto from Baudelaire, establishing the French Symbolist as a shared reference point for their aesthetic sensibilities. This moment of shared appreciation crystallizes the foundation of their friendship: a common commitment to beauty, irony, and the life of the mind.

## Significance

The outdoor concert scene introduces the friendship that will become the novel's most important intellectual relationship. Through Rissler, Martin gains access to a world of philosophical conversation, literary reference, and critical irony that his conventional upbringing has not provided. The concert terraces, with their blend of public spectacle and private communion, become the symbolic birthplace of Martin's intellectual coming of age.
