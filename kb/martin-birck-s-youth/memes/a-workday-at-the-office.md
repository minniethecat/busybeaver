## Overview

The office scene in the first chapter of "The Winter Night" section of Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth* renders the particular atmosphere of a late-nineteenth-century Swedish government bureau — its rituals, characters, and the quiet desperation of intelligent men caught in mechanical labor. The chapter opens on a winter afternoon with snow falling outside, and ends with Martin departing alone, wearing crape on his hat.

## The Green Lamps

The scene is introduced through its most distinctive visual motif: "Over Martin's table in the office an electric light with a green shade swung, like a pendulum, gently to and fro on its silken cord" [027-001]. Martin reaches to stop it but instead waits for the swinging to die. Six such lamps illuminate the semidarkness of the room.

Martin loves these lamps — "they gave out no heat or bad odor, and whose glow had the pure and cold sheen of jewels" [027-001] — and he sees in their modern installation within an old building "a symbol of continuous development, an unbroken chain of hands and wills, from those which had wearied long since to those which were now in embryo, the new inwoven with the old" [027-001].

The opening lines establish the office as a liminal space suspended between past and future:

> Over Martin's table in the office an electric light with a green shade swung, like a pendulum, gently to and fro on its silken cord. Martin loved these green lamps, which gave out no heat or bad odor, and whose glow had the pure and cold sheen of jewels; and he longed for the day when electric light should be cheap enough to make its way down even into the homes of the poor.
>
> (027-001)

## The Colleagues

The office is a small society. Von Heringslake, the chief clerk with an income of forty-six hundred crowns, "sat squatted in front of the stove and roasted apples over the embers" [027-002]. His bald pate — "which his mortal enemy, Auditor Camin, asserted was the result of early dissipations but which in reality shone with the innocence of early childhood" [027-002] — catches the triangular reflection of a green lamp.

Auditor Camin, for his part, sounds his eternal note: "The country will never be right till we make the farmers pay for shooting licenses" [027-002]. Martin later explains to a younger colleague that Camin doesn't really believe this — it is merely "an old saw that he heard in his youth when he was an assistant," expressing "a collective antipathy, a class hatred" [027-003] that belongs to "commonplace men" who "always need to hate and love collectively" [027-003].

At the bottom table by the door, "where it was draughty and there was a wet odor of umbrellas and overcoats, the youngest generation was eagerly at work putting in ticks and trying at the same time to recount in whispers the orgies of last night and the number of punch bottles emptied" [027-002].

## Martin's Work

Martin no longer merely puts down ticks — "he carefully scanned every item and added up every column" [027-001]. When he discovers mistakes, he is glad for the opportunity to show zeal and collect his percentage, but feels "a faint sensation of sorrow" remembering "that he had desired a quite different sort of joy from life" [027-001].

## The Banquet and the Crape

A December banquet subscription circulates; Martin does not sign. "He had other uses for his money" [027-003]. When his younger colleague asks why not, Martin invents another engagement and offers to "assume that we have drunk brotherhood just the same" [027-003]. Von Heringslake, now returned from his apple-roasting, turns to survey him and pronounces: "You have no heart" [027-003].

The chapter closes with a single, devastating detail: "Martin got up, took his coat and hat, put out his green lamp, and departed. He had crape on his hat, for his mother was dead" [027-003].
