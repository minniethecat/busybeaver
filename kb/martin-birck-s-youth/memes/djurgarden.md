## Overview

Djurgården appears in the novel primarily through the ritual of the Royal Family's spring drive. The arrival of spring in Stockholm is not a meteorological event but a ceremonial one: "the Royal Family drove out to the big park with their plumed and golden equipage." The entire city pours into the streets in bright costumes, the light blue guardsmen stand at duty, and after the procession passes, "everybody went out to the park to drink fruit juices and mineral water at Bellmansruh" [009-001]. For the young Martin, this day represents a moment of uncomplicated happiness — violins and street-organs whine, the crowd cheers, and he feels "completely happy."

The park is also evoked in the preface, where the translator's sample passage describes moonlit clouds driving "till over the woods of Djurgården they congested into a low black wall" — the park as dark horizon, the green boundary of the city.
