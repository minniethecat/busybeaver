## Overview

Mrs. Lundgren is a neighbor in the street where Martin Birck spends his childhood, defined by physical amplitude and vocal command: she is "broad of build and loud of voice." She appears most prominently during the pear-tree episode, where she presides over the distribution of stolen pears from the Heggboms' tree [002-001]. She stands at the center of the neighborhood's social theater—arbitrating, scolding, and enforcing the class distinctions that structure life on the street.

She is "ill-disposed toward Mr. Dupont, who was a violinist in the royal orchestra" [002-001]—a resentment that hints at the social tensions between the street's working-class majority and its few artistic or professional residents. Her voice carries across the yard, across the street, and into the novel's network of neighborhood relationships, making her one of the principal vectors through which the street's social order is articulated.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Lundgren's major scene unfolds during the Heggbom pear-tree tumult. As the Heggbom boys sit in the tree throwing down pears, and the other children fight for every fallen fruit, Mrs. Lundgren positions herself at the center: she "tried to enforce a fair distribution, but no one paid any attention to her" [002-001]. She takes particular care to exclude little Ida Dupont from the spoils—an act of petty vindictiveness that immediately signals the neighborhood's social fault lines.

When Martin arrives in the yard, Mrs. Lundgren "at once took him under her protection" [002-002] and offers to peel his pear for him. This selective kindness—protecting the middle-class boy while excluding the violinist's daughter—reveals the neighborhood hierarchy in operation. Martin is worthy of protection; Ida is not, because of her father's occupation.

The pear-tree scene escalates into a full-scale confrontation between Mrs. Lundgren and Mrs. Heggbom, with Mrs. Lundgren feeling "somewhat huffed" at her displacement as the scene's authority figure. She "enjoyed scolding" and does not miss the opportunity to criticize Mrs. Heggbom for allowing such a disturbance [002-003]. The set-to draws listeners from across the street and opens all the kitchen windows—a moment of collective neighborhood spectacle in which Mrs. Lundgren is both participant and impresario.

Her other contribution to the novel's social mapping is her verdict on the Long Row: "Only scum, said Mrs. Lundgren" [002-004]. In three words, she delivers the street's class judgment with a finality that no other character would presume.

## Key Relationships

- **Ida Dupont** — Mrs. Lundgren is "ill-disposed" toward Ida's father, the violinist, and withholds pears from the child as a consequence.
- **Martin Birck** — Taken under her protection during the pear-tree tumult; she peels his pear for him.
- **Mrs. Heggbom** — Her antagonist in the pear-tree showdown; their confrontation provides the scene's climactic set-piece.
- **The Heggbom boys (John and Willie)** — She attempts but fails to control their pear-throwing.

## Significance

Mrs. Lundgren is one of the novel's most effective minor characters—a figure who, in a handful of paragraphs, crystallizes the social dynamics of the street. She is at once comic (her "broad of build and loud of voice" attempts at authority that no one heeds) and revealing (her selective kindness maps the class structure of the neighborhood with sociological precision). Her enjoyment of scolding is a trait the narrator records without censure, as if it were simply one of the facts of neighborhood life, like the wooden houses and the green trees.

Her class judgments—the violinist is suspect, the Long Row is scum, the middle-class boy deserves a peeled pear—are delivered without self-consciousness or qualification. She is, in effect, the voice of the street's unwritten social code, and her presence in the novel ensures that class is not merely a backdrop but an active, vocal force in Martin's childhood world.

## Notable Passages

> In the midst of the troop stood Mrs. Lundgren, broad of build and loud of voice, trying to enforce a fair distribution, but no one paid any attention to her. A little way off stood little Ida Dupont, with great eyes, her hands behind her back, not venturing into the turmoil. Mrs. Lundgren did not get any pears for her because she was ill-disposed toward Mr. Dupont, who was a violinist in the royal orchestra. [002-001]

> But Mrs. Lundgren felt somewhat huffed; she had to a certain extent presided over the tumult. Furthermore, she enjoyed scolding and therefore did not miss the opportunity of showing Mrs. Heggbom with some sharpness the unsuitability of making such a disturbance. [002-003]

> Only really poor people lived there, Martin's mother said. Only scum, said Mrs. Lundgren. [002-004]
