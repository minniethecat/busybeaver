## Overview

The death of Heggbom the porter is one of the novel's earliest encounters with mortality, and it reaches young Martin not through theological abstraction but through a servant's casual cruelty: Lotta tells him that Heggbom will come and bite him if he does not sleep. This small, domestic threat lodges itself in Martin's imagination, blending the ordinary figure of the neighborhood porter with the supernatural terror of a returning ghost. The episode exemplifies Soderberg's method of rendering childhood consciousness — where death, fear, and guilt are experienced through concrete details rather than abstract understanding.

## Heggbom the Porter

Heggbom is introduced as a fixture of the yard where Martin lives. He is a drinker with a "rummy voice" who sings bawdy songs about buying "a hundred lasses" from King Solomon's treasure chest [The Old Street, II]. His wife scolds him, the neighbors gossip about him, and his boys climb the pear tree with the other children. He is, in other words, thoroughly embedded in the noisy, communal life of the building — a figure more comic than menacing.

## The Death

Heggbom's death arrives with startling suddenness. Lotta enters with the tea-tray and announces, "I'm sorry to have to tell you that Heggbom is dead" [The Old Street, III]. The room falls silent. Grandmother attributes it to brandy and immediately begins calculating the practical consequences for his widow and children. Martin, who had been sobbing moments before, stops crying — the shock of death displacing whatever childish grief had occupied him.

## The Fear

That night, alone in the dark, Martin lies awake thinking about Heggbom and wondering "if he could get to heaven. He always smelled of brandy" [The Old Street, III]. When he begs Lotta to leave the candle, she refuses with the threat: "You must sleep, Martin. Heggbom will come and bite you if you don't" [The Old Street, III]. The threat transforms Heggbom from a remembered neighbor into a supernatural menace. Martin begins to cry afresh as the wind whistles through the window chinks and a dog howls outside — the boundary between the living and the dead, the natural and the spectral, dissolves in the darkness of a child's bedroom.

## Significance

This episode introduces several motifs that resonate throughout the novel: the proximity of death to everyday life, the failure of adults to provide comfort, and the child's lonely struggle to make sense of experiences for which no one has given him adequate language. Heggbom's ghost is never literal — it is the product of a servant's thoughtless words and a child's overheated imagination — but it is no less real to Martin for that.
