## Overview

The trip to Drottningholm is a rare family excursion in Martin Birck's childhood, occurring on his seventh birthday after his father departs for work on the *Bragë* steamboat.

It combines the grandeur of the royal summer palace — preceded by a passing cavalry parade on maneuvers — with the intimate comedy of grandmother feeding crumbs to the palace swans. The episode offers a brief idyll of family togetherness before the novel's darker currents reassert themselves with school anxieties and autumn's approach.

## The Birthday Morning

The excursion takes place on Martin's seventh birthday, a summer morning when he awakens early to a bird twittering in the privet hedge. His mother tells him to stay in bed while Maria prepares a surprise.

She tells him that "now he was seven he ought to be really industrious and good in the summer, so that he wouldn't need to be ashamed in the autumn when he was to begin school" [009-033]. The word "school" chokes Martin — he does not clearly know what it means, "but it sounded very harsh and hard" [009-033].

The family comes in to congratulate him: father, grandmother, and Maria, who says with a bow, "I have the honor to congratulate—" [009-035]. Martin becomes bashful, blushes, and turns his face to the wall.

## The Cavalry Parade

Before the family departs, a dramatic martial spectacle passes by. Grandmother announces that "the king was coming riding with fifteen generals to congratulate Martin" [009-037], and a rumbling like thunder rolls over the bridge. A cloud of dust rises from the road, horses' hoofs ring on the ground, and "there were lightnings of drawn swords" [009-037].

The army is on its way to Drottningholm for maneuvers — hussars, dragoons, and all the artillery from Stockholm. The artillerists are "shaking like sacks of potatoes on their caissons and were gray and black with dust and dirt" [009-037], yet Martin admires them all the more and wonders if it might be better to be an artillerist than a coasting skipper.

## The Palace and the Swans

After the father departs on the *Bragë*, Martin is allowed to go with his mother, grandmother, and sister Maria to Drottningholm.

"There stood the king's white summer palace, mirrored in the bright inlet. The trees in the park were bigger than any other trees, and the shade under them was deep and cool" [009-043].

On the dark waters of the ponds and canals, white swans glide with stiffly outstretched necks. Martin imagines "that they never troubled themselves about anything else in the world than their own white dreams" [009-043].

## Grandmother's Offering

The solemn beauty of the scene is undercut by grandmother's practical gesture: she had brought a French roll with her, "which she broke into crumbs and fed to them as one feeds chickens" [009-045]. The contrast between Martin's romantic vision of the swans and his grandmother's matter-of-fact feeding captures the gentle domestic humor of the novel.

The trip belongs to a brief, golden stretch of summer before the darker episodes that follow: the forbidden song scandal with the bridge-tender, the arrival of autumn, and Martin's anxieties about starting school.
