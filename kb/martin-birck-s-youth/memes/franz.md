## Overview

Franz is a "little rowdy" who lives in the Long Row, the impoverished housing block diagonally across from Martin Birck's middle-class stone house. He is Martin's most persistent childhood tormentor—a figure who embodies the class friction and casual cruelty of street life in the novel's Stockholm. Unlike the abstract fears of Martin's inner life, Franz is a concrete, physical antagonist: he spits on Martin's jacket, trips him into the gutter, gives him bloody noses, and "directed all his energies and intelligence toward embittering Martin's life by day and even pursued him into his nocturnal dreams" [010-001].

He is distinguished from the novel's other rowdies by the narrator's pointed assessment: "this Franz was not a rowdy of the usual sort; he was a thoroughly awful rowdy" [010-003]. His trajectory—from boyhood bully to adult inmate of Langholm prison—traces one of the novel's bleakest arcs, suggesting that the class divisions of childhood harden into the criminal categories of adulthood.

## Role in the Narrative

Franz makes his first appearance in Martin's dream-life, materializing behind a lilac bush where Martin expects to find his mother: "behind the bush stood Franz from the Long Row, making an ugly face with his thick, raw-looking lips, till he finished by sticking out his tongue as far as he could. And such a tongue as he had; it got longer and longer; there was no end to it" [001-001]. This dream sequence establishes Franz as a figure who displaces comfort—the maternal presence replaced by grotesque malice.

His waking depredations follow a consistent pattern: spitting on Martin's jacket and calling him "stuck-up" [001-002], tripping him into the gutter, tearing his books apart, and swearing at him "frightfully" [010-002]. Martin's attempt at reconciliation—inspired by "Indian books" that lead him to see "in Franz a parallel to the noble redskin"—ends in disaster. After Martin intervenes to stop two comrades from beating Franz, the bully repays him by giving him "a bloody nose" and tearing his books apart at the next encounter [010-002].

The novel's final mention of Franz comes near the close of "The Old Street" section, in a passage of return and disillusionment. Martin, now wearing the white cap of a graduate, walks home through the street of his childhood and finds it transformed: "The rowdy Franz could no longer disturb what idyllic atmosphere was still left, for he had grown up and become big, and had now been for some time behind the bars of Langholm jail" [014-001]. The news is offered without commentary—a fact noted in passing, like the new red brick buildings that have replaced the wooden houses.

## Key Relationships

- **Martin Birck** — Franz's primary victim; their antagonism structures Martin's experience of street life throughout his childhood.
- **The Long Row** — Franz is inseparable from his setting, the dirt-gray range of houses where "only really poor people lived" [002-001]; he is, in effect, the Long Row's ambassador to Martin's consciousness.
- **Martin's school comrades** — Briefly allied with Martin against Franz, then turn on Martin for defending him.

## Significance

Franz functions in the novel as an agent of class friction—the boy from the poor block who makes the middle-class boy pay for his relative privilege in the coin of physical intimidation. His transformation from bully to prisoner is one of the novel's quietest tragedies, recorded without moralizing: the boy who "directed all his energies and intelligence" toward tormenting Martin ends up directing nothing at all, behind bars.

The failed "noble redskin" episode is characteristic of the novel's ironic method. Martin's bookish attempt to impose a literary frame on real social conflict—to see Franz as a noble savage who can be won over through magnanimity—collapses instantly upon contact with the actual Franz, who has no interest in playing the role Martin has written for him. The gap between Martin's romantic imagination and the world's indifference is one of the novel's persistent preoccupations.
