## Overview

The cavalry crossing Nockeby bridge is one of the most visually striking set pieces in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. Occurring on Martin's seventh birthday during the family's summer sojourn, the episode captures a child's oscillation between domestic warmth and the allure of the wider world.

The realm of history, power, and national destiny is embodied in the thundering horses and drawn swords of the passing army.

## The Morning

Martin's seventh birthday unfolds at the bridge-tender's cottage. The morning begins with the private tenderness of waking to birdsong and the promise of a surprise from Maria. But "while his heart was troubled over having to start school in the autumn, they all came in and congratulated him on his birthday: papa and grandmother and Maria" [009-035].

Then comes his grandmother's stroke of comic invention: she pops her head in and announces that "the king was coming riding with fifteen generals to congratulate Martin" [009-037]. At the same moment, a rumbling sounds over the bridge "as if there was thunder" [009-037].

## The Cavalry

Martin jumps out of bed, throws on his clothes, and the noise comes nearer. "There was a cloud of dust over the road, horses' hoofs rang on the ground and the bridge, and there were lightnings of drawn swords" [009-037].

When he comes out on the porch, the foremost riders have already passed. His mother consoles him with the fact that the king had not been with them. Instead it had been "almost all his army, which was on its way to the region of Drottningsholm for maneuvers" [009-037]. The spectacle includes hussars, dragoons, and "all the artillery from Stockholm" [009-037].

## The Spectacle

The artillerists come "shaking like sacks of potatoes on their caissons and were gray and black with dust and dirt" [009-037]. Martin admires them all the more in this rough state. A "fresh wind came from the lake and took with it the odor of dirt and sweat which remained" [009-039] after the army passes.

## The Shift of Ambition

Far from being disappointed by the king's absence, Martin admires the soldiers "all the more in that condition and wondered within himself if it wouldn't be better to be an artillerist than a coasting skipper" [009-037].

This rapid shift of vocational ambition — from skipper to artillerist — is Söderberg's gentle satire of the child's protean imagination, always ready to abandon one dream for a newer, louder one. The ambition itself lasts only as long as the dust hangs in the air.

## The Birthday Table

When Martin turns around after the army has passed, he finds "beside the breakfast table a little table set especially for him; Maria had decorated it with flowers and green leaves" [009-039].

On the table stands a cake his mother baked, wild strawberries Maria picked under the oaks, a twenty-five-öre piece from his father, and stockings his mother knitted. Martin cares most for the coin: he has learned that "a pair of stockings was just a pair of stockings, and a cake was a cake, but a twenty-five-öre piece was an indefinite number of fulfilled wishes" [009-039].

## Significance

The cavalry crossing functions as a threshold between the private world of the birthday morning and the public world of national power. The soldiers, riding toward Drottningholm, belong to the realm of history and the state — forces that will eventually claim Martin himself, if only in the humble form of a government clerkship.

The swift passage of the army also mirrors the novel's treatment of childhood itself: moments of grandeur that arrive unannounced, thunder past, and leave only the odor of dust on the air before the domestic world reasserts itself in the form of cake, strawberries, and knitted stockings.
