## Overview

Ida Dupont's peepshow is one of the most vivid childhood scenes in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. On a gray October morning, Martin visits Ida in her father's apartment, where she introduces him to a miniature world of tinted pictures, her mother's ballet costumes, and the mysterious rituals of Catholicism — an encounter that expands Martin's imaginative horizons.

## The Visit

"On a gray October morning Martin received permission from his mother to go down and play with Ida Dupont" [003-001]. Mr. Dupont is away at rehearsal, and the two children are alone in the apartment's two small rooms. The day is "dark and somber" [003-001], the inner room in semi-twilight with a high Venetian blind, through which one can glimpse "a part of the great black church cupola" [003-001] as the bells toll.

## The Peepshow Box

Ida shows Martin "a peepshow box with tinted pictures" [003-002]. Through its lens, he sees "white castles and gardens with colored lanterns in long gleaming rows, yellow and red and blue" [003-002] — "strange cities with churches and bridges, and steamboats and big ships on a wide river" [003-002], and "halls illuminated with radiant candelabra" [003-002] that are really just pinholes. The world in the box seems "so big and alive" [003-002]; Martin senses something magical about it.

## Dressing Up

Ida pulls open a bureau drawer of her mother's ballet costumes: "red bodices of satin, silk, and rep with a multitude of ribbons and rosettes; silk gloves, silk stockings, and long veils of lace — pink, blue, and white" [003-002]. She drapes Martin in a blue veil with silver spangles, a red bodice, and a white skirt, then draws mustaches on him with a blackened cork. They look in the mirror and "roared with laughter" [003-002].

## The Banquet and the Madonna

Ida serves wine in green glasses from a cupboard. Outside, the first snow begins to fall. Then she leads Martin to a sleeping alcove where "an image of the madonna" [003-002] stands behind half-burned candles beneath a crucifix. She lights the candles, burns incense, and falls to her knees, muttering incomprehensible words. "That is our religion" [003-002], she whispers. Martin is simultaneously awed and disoriented — his head grows heavy, and when Mr. Dupont returns, both children are asleep on the bed, the candles burned out.
