## Overview

Lotta is the maid in the Birck household, a minor figure whose presence registers through brief, vivid scenes that illuminate the domestic world from the servants' perspective. Her exchanges with Heggbom, the porter, establish a parallel social world — rougher, earthier, more openly comic — that runs alongside the Birck family's more restrained emotional register. Lotta's most significant moment comes when she announces Heggbom's sudden death, delivering the news with a solemnity that marks the boundary between the everyday and the irrevocable.

## Role in the Narrative

Lotta first appears in the morning bustle of the Birck household, laughing at the kitchen window while she chats with Heggbom in the yard below [002-003]. Their banter is teasing and familiar: when Heggbom boasts of something, she retorts, "What would you do with them all, you that can't manage even your own wife?" When the porter's wife appears and begins scolding both Heggbom and Lotta, "Lotta only laughed and slammed the window" [002-004]. In these brief exchanges, Söderberg sketches a complete social world: the hierarchies of the tenement yard, the comedy of domestic life, the casual cruelties of everyday speech.

Her second major appearance comes in the evening, when she enters with the tea-tray wearing "a very solemn expression" and announces: "I'm sorry to have to tell you that Heggbom is dead" [005-002]. The suddenness of the news — Heggbom was laughing at the kitchen window that morning — is characteristic of the novel's treatment of mortality. Lotta's solemnity dignifies the moment; her earlier laughter at Heggbom does not diminish her gravity now, but rather humanizes it.

Later that night, when Martin is afraid of the dark, Lotta brings a candle into his room. Her reassurance is characteristically unsentimental: "You must sleep, Martin. Heggbom will come and bite you if you don't" [005-005]. It is a joke that is also, in its way, a kindness — the servant's mode of comfort, practical and slightly macabre.

## Key Relationships

- **Heggborn** — The porter; their kitchen-window banter suggests an easy, teasing familiarity. His death is announced by Lotta.
- **Martin Birck** — The child of the house; Lotta's interactions with him are brief but revealing of the servant's role in a middle-class household.
- **The Birck family** — Her employers; she moves through their domestic space as an intimate but subordinate presence.

## Significance

Lotta's function in the novel is primarily atmospheric — she is part of the dense texture of domestic life that Söderberg establishes in the Old Street section. But her scenes also serve to mark the boundaries of the Birck family's world. The servants have their own dramas (the Heggbom-Lotta banter, the porter's wife's jealousy), their own modes of speech and humor, and their own relationship to death — Lotta's solemn announcement of Heggbom's passing is one of the novel's most effective moments of tonal shift. She is a reminder that the Birck household contains multitudes.
