## Overview

Billfelt is a school friend of Martin Birck who appears exclusively at the graduation party. He is paired with Gabel from the opening description of classroom coteries—"Gabel and Billfelt, Jansson and Moberg, Planius and Tullman" [016-001]—and the two are treated as a unit throughout the evening. Billfelt's distinction among the schoolmates is his older brother, an Uppsala student who arrives with Doctor Markel, gives a talk about "alma mater," orders champagne when the conversation turns sour, and departs with a polished speech [016-002]. Gabel's remark—"Your brother is a gentleman"—is the evening's verdict on the Billfelt family [016-003].

## Role in the Narrative

Billfelt exists almost entirely in relation to others: to Gabel, to his brother, to the party. He has no individual voice in the text—no reported speech, no described features beyond his membership in a social pair. The elder Billfelt functions as a proxy for the adult world of Uppsala and alma mater, momentarily visiting the school-leavers' celebration before disappearing "off with Markel" [016-002].

At the evening's end, Gabel and Billfelt "swore eternal friendship" [016-004]—a promise that the novel's retrospective structure, in which Martin years later surveys his scattered schoolmates from a theater box, quietly dismantles. Billfelt is never seen again after the graduation party; like Gabel, he is a figure of youth that the novel abandons to adulthood.

## Key Relationships

- **Gabel** — Inseparable school friend; their pairing is so complete that the narrator never mentions one without the other.
- **The elder Billfelt** — Older brother at Uppsala; his presence at the party gives Billfelt a momentary social elevation.
- **Doctor Markel** — Arrives at the party with the elder Billfelt, linking the school world to the university.

## Significance

If Gabel represents aristocratic confidence, Billfelt represents the borrowed status of family connection. His older brother's champagne and speech reflect glory onto him—"Your brother is a gentleman"—but the younger Billfelt himself remains undefined, a blank space in a social pair. He is what remains when the older brother pays the bill and leaves: a boy promising eternal friendship in a room where everyone is already saying good night.
