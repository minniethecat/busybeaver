## Overview

Auditor Camin is a minor figure in the government office where Martin Birck works, memorable for exactly one thing: his endlessly repeated pronouncement that "the country will never be right till we make the farmers pay for shooting licenses" [027-001]. He is Von Heringslake's "mortal enemy"—the narrator notes that Camin attributed Heringslake's baldness to "early dissipations" though it "in reality shone with the innocence of early childhood" [027-001]. He exists at the border between comedy and pathos, a man whose entire intellectual life appears to have been poured into a single, inexplicable grievance.

## Role in the Narrative

Auditor Camin appears in a single office scene, but his presence saturates it. His pronouncement about farmers and shooting licenses sounds "for the hundredth time" from his corner of the room, apparently so familiar to his colleagues that no one bothers to respond. When a younger man asks Martin, "why does Auditor Camin want to charge the farmers for shooting licenses?" [027-003], the question functions as both joke and diagnosis: Camin's obsession is so hermetic that even his colleagues cannot explain it.

The office hierarchy maps onto these obsessions. At the top, Heringslake roasts apples with the contentment of a man on forty-six hundred crowns. At the bottom, by the door "where it was draughty and there was a wet odor of umbrellas and overcoats," the junior clerks whisper about the previous night's drinking [027-001]. Camin occupies an intermediate zone: not senior enough for apples, not junior enough for the damp doorway, defined instead by the ritual repetition of a single sentence.

## Key Relationships

- **Von Heringslake** — "Mortal enemy"; their animosity is presented as an office institution, its origins obscure.
- **Martin Birck** — Colleague; Martin is the audience for Camin's pronouncement and the target of the younger man's question about it.

## Significance

Auditor Camin is a character reduced to a catchphrase—the novel's most economical illustration of how a human being can shrink to fit a government post. The "hundredth time" is the point: Camin has become what he repeats. That no one can explain why he wants farmers to pay for shooting licenses is the deeper joke. His obsession has outlasted its own rationale, if it ever had one.

## Notable Passages
