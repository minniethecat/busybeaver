## Overview

Sigrid Tesch is a young woman of Martin Birck's acquaintance who becomes engaged to his closest friend, Henrik Rissler. She never speaks directly in the novel and appears only through Martin's and Henrik's perceptions, yet her presence catalyzes one of the novel's most quietly devastating scenes: the conversation in Henrik's room where Martin learns of the engagement and, afterward, confronts the insubstantiality of his own romantic fantasies. She is described as having "dark abundant hair and delicate regular features" and a "young and supple figure" [024-003].

## Role in the Narrative

Sigrid Tesch enters the novel through speculation. During a conversation in Henrik's room, Martin notices his friend has changed—his face is "leaner, stronger, and more masculine in contour"—and guesses he is in love [024-001]. Martin runs through the possibilities: Anna Rissler, Maria Randel, Sigrid Tesch. When Henrik confesses, subdued, "It is Sigrid Tesch," Martin sees her before him: a figure who "had sometimes passed by with a pallid dream smile" in his own "twilight thoughts" [024-003]. The revelation that she is to be Henrik's bride lands with a quiet finality [024-004].

The scene's significance lies less in Sigrid herself than in what her engagement to Henrik reveals about Martin—his emotional life is secondhand, his romantic objects are borrowed. Sigrid Tesch is the woman his best friend will marry, and Martin's own "flame" (Harriet Skott) evaporates by comparison.

## Key Relationships

- **Henrik Rissler** — Fiancé; their engagement is presented as a settled fact, the emotional center of Henrik's transformed appearance and subdued confidence.
- **Martin Birck** — She has passed through Martin's "twilight thoughts" with "a pallid dream smile," a peripheral romantic possibility that Henrik's confession both crystallizes and forecloses.

## Significance

Sigrid Tesch is one of the novel's structural absences—a character significant not for what she does but for what her existence reveals about the men around her. Henrik's love for her gives him substance and direction; Martin's faint, unacted attraction to her underscores his passivity. She is the bride who never speaks, the face that passes through "twilight thoughts" without ever fully entering the daylight of the narrative. In a novel preoccupied with unconsummated possibilities, Sigrid Tesch is the consummation that belongs to someone else.
