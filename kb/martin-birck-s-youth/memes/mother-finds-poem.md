## Overview

The scene in which Martin's mother finds his poem is among the most painful in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. It crystallizes the central tragedy of the novel: the inability to communicate across the boundaries of language, generation, and worldview, even — or especially — between those who love each other most.

## The Discovery

"Suddenly his mother stood in the doorway. She held a lamp in her hand and was leaning forward, looking at him" [025-001]. She tells him his father is out and asks to sit with him. She settles with her sewing, then raises "eyes, large with tears and sleeplessness" [025-001].

Her confession comes hesitantly, almost apologetically: "one day when you were out I couldn't help pulling out a drawer of your table and glancing at your papers. Otherwise I should never know what you're thinking about" [025-001]. What she found horrified her: "I didn't understand it, I don't know if it was supposed to be verse or what it was, but I thought it was only full of terrible blasphemies" [025-001]. She feared for a moment that he was "out of your head" [025-001].

She adds the maternal plea that is also a judgment: "you'll never get anywhere with writing that way. You can write very finely, too, if you want to" [025-001].

## The Silence

Martin does not answer. He understands that beneath her practical worry about his future, she is really reaching out: "she wanted him to explain himself, to talk to her about his thoughts. She was pounding at the door, 'Let me in! don't make me stand outside; I'm cold and it's so lonely!'" [025-002]. But he cannot open the door: "he hadn't fastened it, it had locked itself" [025-002].

His discouragement is total. He had aspired "to write so that each and all who really cared to could understand him" [025-002], but now he sees the hopelessness of this ideal. "How should he be able to speak with her about his thoughts, when her vocabulary, as the monotony of the years had developed it, did not even suffice to express what she herself thought and felt at the bottom of her heart?" [025-002].

The god of his poem is Spinoza's god, the World Soul — "merely an intellectual experiment, whereas hers — his mother's — was at least a product of the imagination and as such had a bit more life and more blood" [025-002]. How could he explain that the blasphemies she perceived were not aimed at her god?

## Aftermath

He imagines embracing her but rejects the gesture as false: "he didn't care for that sort of deception and she didn't either" [025-003]. She has asked him a question and is waiting for an answer, and he has none.

"The lamp flame fluttered in the night wind. She rose and said good night, took the lamp, and went out" [025-003]. She takes the light with her, leaving Martin alone in the dark, listening to "the Saturday tread of tired feet on the pavement, and the rain which began to fall against the windowpanes" [025-002].
