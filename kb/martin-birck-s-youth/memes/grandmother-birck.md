## Overview

Grandmother Birck is a peripheral but vividly drawn figure in the Birck household — a reader of newspapers, a teller of stories, and a representative of an older world of anecdote and oral tradition. She appears in the domestic interludes of Martin's childhood: by the window with her glasses "far down on her nose" [004-003], shutting the damper at bedtime, feeding French rolls to the swans at Drottningholm. Her most memorable contribution to the novel is the story of Pella Oldhusen, a kleptomaniac acquaintance whose theft of her housekeeper's linen underskirts is recounted with the relish of a practiced raconteur.

## Role in the Narrative

Grandmother first appears when she calls Martin in from the yard [002-008]. Her role throughout the Old Street section is to provide the counterpoint of age and experience to the children's world. When Martin begs for a story, she protests that she doesn't know any new ones — then proceeds to tell one of the most distinctive tales in the novel: the story of Pella Oldhusen, a "plaguy smart and amusing woman" who was also a kleptomaniac [004-007].

The Pella Oldhusen narrative is a miniature work of oral art. Pella steals her housekeeper's linen underskirts to have them made up for a girl she has taken in; when the housekeeper discovers the theft, Pella affects bewilderment ("Could anyone imagine such rascals?"), then quietly compensates the housekeeper with money for new linen. Grandmother's delivery is matter-of-fact, punctuated by oaths of authenticity: "It's God's own truth; I heard it from a lady that knew all about her." The story is simultaneously a moral lesson, a character sketch, and a piece of entertainment — grandmother's mode of teaching without appearing to teach.

Her other appearances are briefer but consistent: she clasps her hands at news of Heggbom's death, commenting that " 'twas the brandy" [005-002]; she pretends the king is coming to congratulate Martin on his birthday [008-010]; she feeds crumbs to the swans at Drottningholm [009-005]. By the novel's middle section, she is gone: "grandmother was no longer there" [014-001], her absence noted in the same sentence as Maria's marriage, linking the two departures as joint markers of the household's dissolution.

## Key Relationships

- **Martin Birck** — Her grandson; she indulges his requests for stories and participates in the gentle deceptions of his childhood.
- **Maria Birck** — Her granddaughter, who listens with "wide-open mouth" to the Pella Oldhusen story.
- **Mrs. Birck** — Her daughter-in-law (or possibly daughter); they share the domestic work of the household.

## Significance

Grandmother represents the oral, anecdotal mode of transmitting knowledge that the novel associates with an older generation. Her stories are not moralizing in the manner of the minister's sermons; they are concrete, comic, and human — Pella Oldhusen is neither condemned nor excused, merely presented in her full contradiction: thief and benefactor, amusing and appalling. This is the novel's preferred mode of moral understanding — descriptive rather than prescriptive — and grandmother, in her small way, is one of its first practitioners.

## Notable Passages

> Grandmother continued to read the paper with her glasses far down on her nose. [004-003]

> "Goodness, no; she was his aunt," said grandmother. "Her name was Pella, Pella Oldhusen. I remember her very well, I met her at Vaxholm. A plaguy smart and amusing woman she was, but she was a kleptomaniac." [004-007]
