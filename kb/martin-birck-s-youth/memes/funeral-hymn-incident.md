## Overview

The funeral hymn incident is a small but revealing episode in the Nockeby bridge section of Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. Martin, having absorbed Johan's tales of ghosts, devils, and murdered men, sings a funeral hymn to a street tune — provoking a confrontation with old Moberg that exposes the gap between the child's world of transgressive play and the adult world of propriety and death.

## The Background

Martin's friendship with Johan, the bridge-tender's fourteen-year-old assistant, has introduced him to a world of supernatural folklore. Johan tells tales of old Moberg's visions of "tiny little devils jumping up and down" in his toddy glass, a minister at Lovö who is "a friend of Old Spotty himself," and a murdered man whose ghost groans in a ditch on November evenings [009-002]. These stories invest the bridge-tender's world with a density that Martin's respectable domestic life lacks.

## The Incident

The bridge sequence culminates when Martin, having learned the funeral hymn from Johan, sings it while walking home — to the tune of "an insolent street song" [009-001]. The juxtaposition of sacred text and profane melody is a child's act of unconscious transgression.

## The Confrontation

Moberg hears the song from his porch, where he sits "embowered in hop vines, drinking toddy with two farmers in the light of a round Japanese lantern" [009-001]. He rises with tottering steps, his face gone blue, and confronts Martin: "And this child is supposed to come of respectable people! These are strange times, I may say" [009-001]. Moberg's outrage is rooted in his pathological unwillingness to confront death — if he heard anyone speak of illness or death, "it was to him as if he had heard something indecent."

## Johan's Desertion

Martin looks around for Johan, but Johan has vanished. This desertion is characteristic of Johan's role: he provides the material for transgression but disappears when consequences arrive. His cheerful disposition — "he drove off his fear by striking up a jolly song" [009-002] — allows him to traffic in darkness without being consumed by it.

## Significance

The funeral hymn incident is miniature Söderberg: a moment of childhood mischief that opens onto larger themes of mortality, respectability, and the gap between the comic and the sacred. Moberg's disproportionate reaction — blue in the face over a child's song — reveals an adult world whose rigid proprieties are a defense against the terror of death.
