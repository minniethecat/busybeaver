## Overview

The threat of the family piano being sold is a small but emotionally charged episode from Martin Birck's childhood. It dramatizes the gap between adult practical concerns and a child's attachment to the material anchors of daily life.

## The Overheard Conversation

One evening, as Martin sat half-listening to the adults, he was jolted awake by words he could scarcely believe: "Perhaps you're right that we ought to try to sell the piano and buy a pianino on instalment. It goes out of tune in a couple of weeks, and a pianino would be prettier" [005-015].

## Martin's Distress

Martin "gave a start at the words 'sell the piano'" [005-017]. He had no clear idea what a pianino was but did not believe it could be a real piano — he pictured "something that was worked with a handle" [005-017]. He loved every dent and crack in the red mahogany frame, most of which he had made himself, and remembered almost every key by its color. The proposition struck him as unthinkable: "It was almost as if he had heard his parents calmly sitting and talking about selling grandmother and buying an aunt instead" [005-017]. He began to cry before he knew it, though the adults attributed his tears to tiredness and sent him to bed — where the news of Heggbom's death soon displaced the piano crisis from the evening's conversation.
