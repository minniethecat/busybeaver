## Overview

Mrs. Heggbom is the wife of Heggbom the porter and a vivid minor presence in the early chapters of the novel. She first appears in the pear-tree scene, a morning tableau of tenement life in which her boys are "sitting up in the tree, throwing down pears after having first stuffed their pockets full" [002-005]. She intervenes with theatrical indignation—"Lord in heaven what a clatter, and Heggbom lying at his death!"—though Heggbom had merely been sick in bed "awhile ago" and her "imagination often turned back to that comparatively happy time" [002-007]. She then engages in a full-throated shouting match with Mrs. Lundgren, setting her "arms akimbo" as "listeners streamed up, and all the kitchen windows were opened wide" [002-008]. Her later transformation—from porter's wife to "lady" after the neighborhood's redevelopment—is one of the novel's quietest jokes about social aspiration.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Heggbom is a character of the Ladgårdsland tenement, defined by noise and combat. Her first appearance is auditory: Martin, half-asleep, hears "the porter's wife" throwing out a tub of dishwater and scolding Heggbom and Lotta [002-003]. A moment later, staring at the ceiling, he notices "a crack that was just like Mrs. Heggbom if one looked at it right" [002-004]—a detail that fixes her as part of the furniture of Martin's childhood world.

The pear-tree episode is her centerpiece. While Heggbom's boys strip the tree, Mrs. Lundgren attempts to impose order, and Mrs. Heggbom erupts from the house with the cry about her dying husband. The ensuing set-to between the two women is pure comic spectacle: Mrs. Lundgren "enjoyed scolding" and Mrs. Heggbom is more than equal to the occasion, releasing her sons to plant her hands on her hips as the yard fills with spectators [002-008].

Her final appearance, years later, is a punchline. After the redevelopment of Ladgårdsland into Östermalm, "Mrs. Heggbom had become a lady. If anybody called her by her former title, she would answer politely but decidedly, 'Not any more!'" [010-001].

## Key Relationships

- **Heggbom** — Her husband, the porter; she scolds him and mythologizes his illness, yet his death leaves her to ascend socially on her own.
- **Mrs. Lundgren** — Adversary in the pear-tree shouting match; both women enjoy combat.
- **Lotta** — The Bircks' maid; Mrs. Heggbom includes Lotta in her scolding of Heggbom.
- **John and Willie Heggbom** — Her sons, the pear-tree raiders.

## Significance

Mrs. Heggbom is one of the novel's minor comic figures, drawn with a few sharp strokes: the crack in the ceiling, the arms akimbo, the nostalgia for her husband's illness. Her later demand to be called a "lady" is a small, perfect piece of social satire—the redevelopment of Ladgårdsland has not only demolished Long Row but has also produced Mrs. Heggbom's gentility, which consists entirely of a change of address. She is the city's transformation written in miniature.
