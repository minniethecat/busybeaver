## Overview

The demolition of Long Row is the novel's most concentrated image of urban modernization as violence. The low wooden houses with their gardens, cherry trees, and chickens — the entire pre-modern ecology of Martin's childhood neighborhood — are erased and replaced by a tall brick building that literally blocks the view. The district is renamed, the social order is scrambled, and the physical world that shaped Martin's earliest sensory memories is gone. The event marks the end of childhood not through any internal psychological development but through the brute fact of the world being torn down around him.

## What Was Lost

Before its demolition, Long Row was vividly present in Martin's childhood landscape. It lay "slantwise across the street" from the Bircks' stone house — a "low, dirt-gray range of houses" where "only really poor people lived," according to Martin's mother, and "only scum," according to Mrs. Lundgren [010-002]. Yet the poverty was part of a rich neighborhood ecology: the dyer stood at his gate in slippers chatting with his wife, a brewery wagon's horse ate oats from a nosebag, chickens trailed from the yard with the cock at their head, and the old lamplighter made his evening rounds [010-002].

From Martin's window at twilight, Long Row was beautiful: "Long Row lay in deep twilight. The lindens and cherry trees of the garden were stripped of leaves, and here and there a light was already gleaming in a window from out the dark net of boughs" [004-059]. The view extended beyond — Humlegård Park with its rotunda roof between old lindens, and farthest off, the Observatory on its hill. Long Row was not merely a slum; it was the foreground of Martin's entire visual and emotional world.

## The Demolition

The event is announced with devastating brevity:

> In the city there was much that was new. Long Row was gone with its gardens and sheds; in its place a great brick building rose aloft, growing higher every day, obscuring both the lindens of Humlegård Park and the Observatory on its hill. Everywhere people were pulling down and building up, and dynamite blasts resounded every day in the district, which was now no longer to be called Ladgårdslandet but Östermalm. [010-013]

The language is matter-of-fact but the details are devastating. "Long Row was gone" — the definitive erasure. The new brick building "growing higher every day" is a vertical intrusion that literally blocks the view: the lindens and Observatory that framed Martin's childhood twilights are now invisible. The "dynamite blasts" that "resounded every day" transform the neighborhood into a war zone, normalizing the violence of demolition.

## The Renaming

The district's renaming from Ladgårdslandet ("Farmstead Land" — a name evoking the area's rural origins) to Östermalm ("Eastern District" — a modern bureaucratic designation) is itself a form of erasure. The old name carried history; the new name carries only a cardinal direction. Alongside the physical demolition, language is deployed to sever the present from the past.

## Social Scrambling

The demolition rearranges not only buildings but the social order: "And Mrs. Heggbom had become a lady. If anybody called her by her former title, she would answer politely but decidedly, 'Not any more!'" [010-013]. The porter's wife, a coarse and brawling figure in earlier chapters, seizes the moment of urban transformation to demand upward reclassification. The detail is darkly comic but acute: when the physical markers of class are bulldozed, everyone must scramble to reposition themselves.

## The Human Residue

Not everything disappears. Franz, the neighborhood bully who "used to live in the Long Row," still resides "in the same street" — the human tensions of the old neighborhood survive the physical demolition. Franz "directed all his energies and intelligence toward embittering Martin's life by day and even pursued him into his nocturnal dreams" [010-023]. The class antagonisms embodied in the old Long Row are unmoored from their origin but not dissolved.

## Significance

The demolition of Long Row is Söderberg's quiet indictment of urban modernization in late nineteenth-century Stockholm. The novel refuses the rhetoric of progress: what is gained (a brick building) is unnamed and characterless, while what is lost (gardens, cherry trees, views, a neighborhood's organic life) is rendered in loving sensory detail. The event is also a developmental marker for Martin: his childhood ends not because he grows up but because the world he grew up in is physically destroyed. The external demolition mirrors the internal losses that accumulate across the novel — of faith, of love, of creative ambition — but here the violence is literal, and the dust is real.
