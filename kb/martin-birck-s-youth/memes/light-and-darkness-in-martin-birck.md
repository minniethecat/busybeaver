## Overview

Light and darkness form one of the most persistent sensory patterns in *Martin Birck's Youth*. The novel opens in twilight — "a green and tranquil twilight" in a dream-garden — and closes in the long darkness of a winter night. Between these poles, Söderberg repeatedly returns to the image of lamps being lit against the encroaching dark: the old lamplighter on Long Row, the green electric lamps of the General Post Office, the yellow lamp in a woman's window, the gas jets turned half-down in an empty café.

The motif functions at multiple levels. At the sensory level, it roots the novel in the material world of pre-electric Stockholm, where lamplighters made their rounds and rooms were transformed by the lighting of a single lamp. At the psychological level, light and darkness map onto Martin's emotional states — the "circle of yellow light" that contains the family but excludes him, the darkness into which he retreats. At the philosophical level, the novel's famous line — "Light is good, and darkness is good too. But sometimes darkness is bad, and light too" — refuses the easy symbolism that would make light simply good and darkness simply evil.
