## Overview

Doctor Markel's lecture on pantheism is a pivotal scene in the graduation supper sequence of Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. Delivered under a darkening sky at Hasselbacken, the lecture presents an alternative to Protestant orthodoxy — but Söderberg treats it with characteristic ambivalence, as a performance whose brilliance is inseparable from its emptiness.

## The Arrival

Markel arrives at the Hasselbacken supper in the company of an older brother of Billfelt's, having come from Uppsala that day [016-053].

He is "a man carelessly dressed, with a student cap, a pale narrow face and black mustaches which hung down over his mouth so that it wasn't easy to see whether he smiled or was serious" [016-049]. His face looks "oldish for the white cap, and it was not entirely clean" [016-049].

He sits down beside Henrik Rissler and Martin without ceremony and immediately asks: "Two young poets, eh? I venture to assume so, since the gentlemen sit here by themselves apart from the vulgar throng and talk about the stars. May I ask what your attitude toward life is? Do you believe in God?" [016-057]

## The Opening Gambit

Henrik Rissler looks at the stranger in surprise, and Martin shakes his head. Doctor Markel looks "entirely serious, except that there was a slight mist over his eyes, which were large and mournful" [016-061].

Josef Marin, pale and overworked, breaks in with earnestness: "I believe in God. But I don't conceive Him as a personal being" [016-063]. Markel seems pleasantly surprised: "Oh, you are a pantheist, charming!" [016-067].

## The Faust Argument

Markel pivots to his central thesis — that pantheism is less a conviction than an instrument. He evokes Faust's answer to Gretchen's simple question about belief: if Faust had answered honestly, "No, my child, I don't believe in God," the girl would have "crossed herself, run home to her quiet chamber, and turned the key twice in the lock" [016-067].

Instead Faust claims he both believes and disbelieves — which "gives the impression of deep spiritual conflict" — and says that God is "a name for the feeling that two lovers have when they lie in the same bed" [016-067].

Markel notes that Faust says this "with much feeling and in beautiful language, so that it does not shock her modesty; on the contrary, she thinks he talks like a priest, and the rest we know" [016-067].

## The Poet's God

Markel elaborates his cynical taxonomy of belief. A churchman poet "will be given the Order of Charles XIII and a good income, but will only be read by missies and be ridiculed by the liberal papers" [016-071]. An atheist poet "will be considered a shallow and superficial fellow, a poor sort, and he will have a hard time to borrow money" [016-071].

The solution: "a poet should believe in God, but in a god who is out of the ordinary run, something not yet existent, never before shown in any circus, that one can never really get hold of, for then the game would be up" [016-071].

The pantheistic god is the "ideal for a god. Each and every one can carve him to his own taste, he is never without humor, he never punishes and of course never rewards either" [016-071]. This god "lacks a small characteristic that even the simplest of the town rowdies possesses to some extent: namely, personality" [016-071].

Markel concludes: "to stick to a personal god entails a lot of unnecessary trouble, to be without a god entirely is ticklish. Therefore one must have an impersonal god" [016-071].

## The Climax

Markel gestures toward a star to illustrate his point about the namelessness of the impersonal god. But "the gesture with which Doctor Markel sought and, as it were, beckoned to the star met only a dark and sullen firmament, for great clouds had gathered, the star was gone" [016-075]. Big raindrops begin to fall on the railing — the universe itself seems to mock his performance.

## The Reception

The lecture is not well received. Josef Marin, who has been drinking more punch, mutters that Markel "ought to have a smack on the jaw" [016-077]. The others get up in groups and discuss whether to go home.

The elder Billfelt takes in the situation, rings for the waiter and orders champagne. He raises his glass and "returned thanks in well-rounded periods for the cordiality with which he and his friend, Doctor Markel, representatives of Uppsala and alma mater, had been received by the future alumni" [016-079]. He pays for the champagne and departs with Markel.

## Significance

Markel functions as a cautionary figure in the novel's treatment of intellectual life. His pantheism is a genuine alternative to orthodoxy, but it is presented as a gesture rather than a conviction — a performance delivered to a captive audience and withdrawn at the first sign of opposition.

The cosmic punchline — the star vanishing behind clouds at the very moment Markel points to it — is Söderberg's understated judgment on the lecture. The universe does not cooperate with Markel's rhetoric. In this, he prefigures Martin's own anxieties about the relation between thought and action: can one hold convictions that cost nothing, and if so, what are they worth?
