## Overview

"The Decadent Poet" is one of the most darkly comic episodes in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*. On a winter evening in Stockholm, Martin Birck encounters a minor poet on a street corner — a figure who embodies, with self-conscious theatricality, the full repertoire of fin-de-siècle literary postures. The encounter, conducted over green chartreuse at the Café Anglais, is at once a satire of decadent affectation and a mirror of Martin's own unresolved relationship to the literary life.

## The Meeting

Martin meets the poet on a street corner in winter. The poet is "freezing in a thin yellow-green ulster" — the color of the coat already announcing his aesthetic loyalties. He is "a few years older than Martin and already a bit famous, for he wrote with fabulous ease the loveliest verses on any theme, mostly about girls and flowers and June nights on the lowlands of Scania, whence he had come" [021-002].

His appearance is carefully unprepossessing: "a pale face and a thin red beard," and "when he met a fellow-artist, his great childlike eyes took on a wild and staring expression, as if he were considering within himself: 'Shall I murder him, or shall we go in somewhere and consume alcohol?'" [021-002].

## The Confession

They go to the Café Anglais and drink green chartreuse. The poet, warmed by the liquor, delivers a manifesto of calculated degeneracy:

"He confided to Martin that he was a decadent. He worshiped everything that was disintegrating, rotten at the core, and doomed to destruction. He hated the sun and light — here he shook a clenched fist at the gas candelabra on the ceiling — he loved the night and sin and all alcoholic drinks of a green shade. He had most of the well-known venereal diseases and an insane fear of crowded squares. Nothing in the world could make him go diagonally across Gustavus Adolphus Place" [021-002].

The fear of crowded squares is, for the poet, not a disability but a distinction: "This disease gave him a very special pleasure, for he took it as the forerunner of general paralysis. And general paralysis was the great sleep; it was nirvana" [021-002].

## The Poet's Existence

The poet declares that he "didn't live anywhere" and "hadn't had any dwelling-place for three weeks, and he didn't need any." He writes his poems "on the table of the café and slept with girls." His worldly possessions are reduced to a single traveling bag: "In the house of one of them he had his green-edged traveling bag with some extra collars and the poems of Verlaine, and there too were his own manuscripts" [021-002].

The detail of the green-edged bag — matching the green chartreuse, the green ulster — is Söderberg's quiet joke about the systematization of bohemian dishevelment, the careful curation of the appearance of carelessness.

## The Aftermath

Martin drinks too: "The strong green liquor burned in his palate and his brain." Under its influence, the image of the woman from the March afternoon — Harriet, walking in sunlight — returns to him unbidden: "Thereupon the woman of the morning was there, the one who walked in the sunlight and smiled. Was she asleep now, did she dream, did she smile in her dreams? Or did she twist about sleepless on her bed in longing for a man?" [021-002]. The juxtaposition — the decadent poet's theatrical nihilism against Martin's genuine romantic longing — defines the distance between adopted posture and authentic feeling.

The poet's own end is recorded by the novel with characteristic understatement. Later, Martin sees his face in a bookstore window among "the celebrities of the day," displayed between an Excellency and a forger: "He was not there because he was a great man but because he was dead" [028-001].

## Significance

The decadent poet is a satirical portrait of the literary type that Söderberg knew well from Stockholm's bohemian circles. His manifesto — the worship of disintegration, the hatred of sunlight, the insistence on green alcohol — is a compendium of fin-de-siècle commonplaces delivered with the earnestness of a man who has read too much Baudelaire and understood too little. Yet the episode is not merely satire. Martin's own drinking of the green chartreuse, and the unbidden return of Harriet's image, suggests that the decadent's despair is not entirely foreign to Martin's own sensibility — only that Martin's version is genuine where the poet's is performed.
