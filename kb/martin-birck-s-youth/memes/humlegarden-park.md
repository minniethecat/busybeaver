## Overview

Humlegården Park is a green space in the Östermalm district of Stockholm, visible from Martin Birck's home and serving as a fixed point in the novel's shifting urban geography. With its rotunda, old linden trees, and the Observatory on its hill, Humlegården represents the pastoral past of Ladgårdsland — a landscape increasingly obscured by the brick buildings of the modernizing city [001-240]. It appears at key moments in the novel as both a literal vista and a symbolic register of loss.

## Description in the Text

Söderberg first introduces Humlegården through Martin's elevated childhood perspective:

> One could see so far up above — far off over the low buildings of the old suburb with its wooden houses and gardens. One could see Humlegård Park with the roof of the rotunda between the old naked lindens. And farthest off in the west rose a gray outline, the Observatory on its hill. [001-137]

The view is expansive, almost nostalgic — a panorama of the old city seen from a height, before the encroachment of brick and dynamite. The rotunda, the lindens, and the Observatory form a constellation of landmarks that orient Martin's world and give it scale.

The park reappears in a more elegiac register later, when the new brick building on the site of the demolished Long Row rises "higher every day, obscuring both the lindens of Humlegård Park and the Observatory on its hill" [001-240]. The verb "obscuring" does double work: it describes a physical fact — a building blocking a view — and a cultural condition — the erasure of a way of seeing. What Martin could once see, the new city has taken from him.

The park also figures, tangentially, in Martin's childhood fears. The "marsh rowdies" — legendary toughs from "the tract between the Humlegård and Roslagstorg" — inhabit Martin's imagination as threats he has "never seen and of whose existence he was not entirely sure" [001-240]. The park thus marks a boundary between the safe, domestic world and the shadowy realm of urban danger.

## Narrative Function

Within the novel's symbolic geography, Humlegården operates as a vanishing point — both literally, as a point on the horizon toward which sight converges, and figuratively, as a marker of disappearance. The park belongs to the old Stockholm of gardens, wooden houses, and open views. Its gradual occlusion by new construction is one of the novel's most economical images of modernization as loss.

The Observatory on the hill adds a further dimension: it is a place of looking, of scientific seeing, and its obstruction by a speculative brick building suggests the triumph of commerce over knowledge, of the new over the contemplative.
