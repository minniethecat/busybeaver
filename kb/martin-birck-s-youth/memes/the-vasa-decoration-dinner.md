## Overview

Mr. Birck's embarrassment at a men's dinner over his Vasa decoration is a minor domestic anecdote that opens a window onto the character of Martin's father: a man who earned his medal through forty years of ill-paid government work, yet feels awkward about wearing it among men who could afford the more discreet miniature version. The episode reveals the subtle class anxieties of the lower civil service and the generational distance between father and son.

## The Incident

Over coffee and cigar after dinner, Mr. Birck tells Martin about a men's dinner he attended the day before. He had worn his "large official medal, which was the only one he had, whereas the other men had had the small miniatures" [029-002]. His conclusion is blunt: "I looked like the biggest fool of the company" [029-002].

## Martin's Response

Martin tries to console him with irony: "in reality the miniature medals of the others gave the clearest proof that their foolishness was greater, since because of their decoration they went to more expense than was strictly necessary" [029-003]. His father acknowledges the logic — "I thought of that too" — but adds, "I felt awkward, anyhow" [029-003].

## Martin's Unspoken Thoughts

Martin privately recalls stories about how decorations are obtained: a man given the Vasa medal "because he had sent flowers to the royal hospital on the days when the queen was to visit it," another who "got the North Star because he had bought a house" [029-004]. But he does not tell these stories — he recognizes that his father "had earned his decoration by forty years of ill-paid work in the government service" and "could hardly fail to think of it with some respect, although in conversation he might make fun of it" [029-004].

## Significance

The Vasa dinner anecdote captures the quiet dignity of Mr. Birck, a man whose life has been defined by duty rather than ambition. The father's embarrassment over a decoration earned through genuine service — in a society where such honors are often bought or politicked — is a small tragedy of misplaced shame that Martin perceives but cannot remedy.
