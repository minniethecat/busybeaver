## Overview

The first afternoon sun of the year is a small but luminous domestic ritual in the Birck household. When the afternoon sunlight finally reaches the west-facing hall after the long Stockholm winter, it signals the turning of the seasons. The event is marked by Mrs. Birck's announcement and by the children's ecstatic chase after the rainbow-colored reflections cast by the crystal candelabra.

## The Event

One afternoon, "a beam of sunlight fell into the hall which faced the west" [008-002]. Mrs. Birck announces it to the children: "Look at the sun, children. That's our first afternoon sun this year" [008-002].

The sunbeam strikes the polished glass of the candelabra, breaking into "rainbow-colored patches all over the room on the furniture and wallpaper" [008-002]. Mr. Birck passes through and sets the glass pieces in motion, triggering "a tumultuous dance of the colored patches around the walls, a dance as of fluttering butterflies" [008-002].

## The Children's Chase

Martin and his sister Maria chase the patches, "flushed and hot, striking their hands against the walls," screaming with delight when they catch one: "Now I've got it!" [008-002]. But the joy is fleeting — "in the next second it glided away, the sunbeam paled, and the butterflies, weary of fluttering and shining, departed — Martin saw the last of them expire on his hand" [008-002].

## Significance

The first afternoon sun is one of the novel's rare moments of pure domestic happiness. It precedes the darker events of Good Friday and contrasts with the later dissolution of the Birck home. The fleeting quality of the colored patches — beautiful and then gone — mirrors the novel's elegiac view of childhood joy.
