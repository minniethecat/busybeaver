## Overview

Franz is a "little rowdy" who lives in the Long Row, a poor housing block across from Martin's childhood home. He appears first in Martin's dreams and later in reality as a figure of menace and humiliation — spitting on Martin's jacket, tripping him into the gutter, and giving him a bloody nose. The encounters crystallize the childhood terror of arbitrary violence and the social gulf between Martin's respectable family and the rougher elements of the neighborhood. But the most illuminating episode is Martin's attempt, inspired by adventure books, to recast Franz as a "noble redskin" — a literary intervention in real life that fails spectacularly.

## First Appearance: The Dream

Franz first appears to Martin in a dream that opens the novel. Martin dreams he is in a marvelous garden with his mother, picking blue flowers. When his mother vanishes and he runs to find her behind a lilac bush, "behind the bush stood Franz from the Long Row, making an ugly face with his thick, raw-looking lips, till he finished by sticking out his tongue as far as he could" [001-001]. The tongue takes on nightmare proportions: "it got longer and longer; there was no end to it; and it was covered with little yellowish-green blisters" [001-001]. The dream ends with Martin paralyzed, unable to scream.

## The Real Franz

In the waking world, "Franz was a little rowdy who lived in the 'Long Row' slantwise across the street. The Sunday before he had spat on Martin's new brown jacket and called him 'stuck-up'" [001-001]. The epithet "stuck-up" encapsulates the social tension: Franz perceives Martin's respectable family — his new brown jacket — as a provocation, while Martin can only suffer the assault as inexplicable persecution.

## The Context of Fear: Rowdies and Marsh Rowdies

The walk to and from school is "filled with all sorts of perils and adventures. Those ill-disposed beings who were called rowdies and who called Martin and his comrades stuck-ups might be in ambush around any corner" [010-005]. The neighborhood hierarchy of menace includes the legendary "marsh rowdies," who inhabit the tract between Humlegård Park and Roslagstorg. Their weapons are "said to be lead balls on the end of short ropes" [010-005]. Yet Martin fears Franz more than these mythical figures: "more than these marsh rowdies, whom Martin had never seen and of whose existence he was not entirely sure, he feared the horrible Franz, who used to live in the Long Row and still resided in the same street" [010-005]. The known terror outweighs the unknown — Franz is real, and "directed all his energies and intelligence toward embittering Martin's life by day and even pursued him into his nocturnal dreams" [010-005].

## The School Confrontation

The critical encounter occurs during morning recess. Martin finds "two of his comrades in a fight with Franz at a street corner; in fact they had already overcome him, thrown him down, and were pummeling him with their fists" [010-006]. At this moment, Martin is under the influence of a new literary passion: "At this time Martin had begun to read Indian books, so that he at once saw in Franz a parallel to the noble redskin and did not want to miss so favorable a chance of making him his ally against other rowdies" [010-006].

Martin intervenes with a moral argument — "how cowardly it was to fight two against one" — and vouches for Franz's character: "Franz lived in his street and was a very decent rowdy" [010-006]. While Martin distracts his comrades with this rhetoric, "Franz managed to get up and run away" [010-006].

## The Betrayal

Martin's reward is immediate and comprehensive: "In return Martin got all the licking intended for Franz. Furthermore he had to endure the scorn of his comrades for being the friend of a rowdy" [010-007]. The physical punishment from his peers is compounded by social ostracism — he is now tainted by association.

The final blow comes directly from Franz: "the next time he met Franz on the street in front of the dyer's gate, the latter tripped him so that he fell into the gutter, then gave him a bloody nose, tore his books apart, swore at him frightfully, and ran off" [010-007]. The narrator's comment is dry and devastating: "He had not understood that he was supposed to be a noble redskin" [010-007]. Franz has not read the same books Martin has; he is not playing by the rules of adventure fiction. The conclusion is blunt: "this Franz was not a rowdy of the usual sort; he was a thoroughly awful rowdy" [010-007].

## Themes

The Franz episodes are among the novel's sharpest illustrations of the gap between literary imagination and social reality. Martin, nourished on Indian books, attempts to impose a narrative template on the neighborhood — the noble savage, the honorable enemy — but Franz refuses the role. He is not a character in Martin's story; he is an agent of his own, operating by a logic Martin cannot comprehend. The episode is a small, brutal education in the limits of empathy-by-projection: good intentions and literary analogies cannot bridge the gap between social worlds. Martin's bloody nose and torn books are the physical tokens of a lesson he will learn again in different forms throughout the novel — that other people are not extensions of one's own imagination.
