## Overview

The encounter with the streetwalker is a key episode in Hjalmar Söderberg's *Martin Birck's Youth*, marking Martin's first confrontation with raw sexual desire after his graduation ball. The scene unfolds entirely within Martin's sleepless imagination, revealing the gap between his romantic fantasies and the reality of his situation.

## The Sleepless Night

After the student ball, Martin lay awake a long time, unable to sleep. "It was the little pale girl with the black eyes that left him no rest. She had stood there so pale and still and lonely; she had not taken anyone's arm or laughed or chattered like the others" [017-001]. His imagination immediately constructs a tragic backstory for her: "She had surely been seduced and deserted; she perhaps had a little child that would freeze or starve to death if she didn't get it food and clothes by selling her body" [017-001].

## The Rescue Fantasy

Martin's thoughts spiral into a romantic rescue fantasy. "How he would kiss her if he had her in his arms now, how he would caress her and give her the tenderest names, so as to make her forget who she was, a common streetwalker, and who he was, a chance customer like all the rest!" [017-001]. He imagines giving up everything — "all his dreams of poetic fame and his future" — to marry her and live far from society "in a little house by a lake deep in the woods" [017-001]. They would row among the rushes and spend nights together under the stars, and he would "kiss away all sorrow, all dark memories from her brow, and would be as fond of her little child as if it was his own" [017-001].

## Planius and Jealousy

The fantasy is poisoned by jealousy. Martin knows she went off with Planius — a classmate he considers beneath him: "He was no better looking than Martin and he was as stupid as a codfish. He had been one of the worst grinds and had only had a plain 'graduated' on his certificate" [017-001]. Yet Martin rationalizes her choice: "she had made no choice; she had just taken the first that came along" [017-001]. In his mind, she had already "given away her heart and soul and had no longer anything to give but her body" [017-001].

## Self-Knowledge at Dawn

As dawn approaches, Martin's self-deception collapses. "While Martin let his fancy wander thus, he knew quite clearly at the same time that under all these reveries lay nothing but desire — a young man's hunger for a woman's white body" [017-001]. The chapter closes on a note of bitter regret: "the more bitterly he regretted that he had said no to the other girl, the fat one" [017-001]. The episode crystallizes Martin's predicament — caught between romantic idealism and physical longing, between moral scruple and the simple wish he had been less timid.
