## Overview

The white cap supper at Hasselbacken is a pivotal scene in Martin Birck's coming of age. It marks his graduation from school, the formal beginning of his friendship with Henrik Rissler, and the moment his private poetic ambitions crystallize into a conscious life direction. The evening's music — the overture to *Mefistofele* — and the spring twilight give the scene a dreamlike quality that lifts it above mere student revelry.

## The Walk Through the Park

Walking along the Avenue to the Park, Martin thinks of his mother and the growing distance between them — her inability to understand his religious doubts, the conversations that have shrunk to "shirts and socks and buttons to be sewed on" [018-005]. But his thoughts soon turn to his own future. He dreams of becoming a poet: writing a drama of ideas in the verse form of *Brand* or *Peer Gynt*, devoting his life "to searching for the truth and giving to mankind what he found" [018-016].

His fantasy takes on a characteristically ironic shape — he imagines becoming famous, winning the Swedish Academy, then despising himself for the gap between his ambition and his achievement. In a darker version, he would burn his papers and drink poisoned wine. Then he mocks his own melodrama: "When he thought it over more carefully, this seemed to him even a trifle banal" [018-018]. He imagines instead retiring to a small town "to live alone with a parrot and a black cat," an object of local curiosity and pity.

## Mefistofele

At Hasselbacken, "the orchestra struck up the opening bars of *Mefistofele*" [018-022]. Martin sits by the balcony railing with Henrik Rissler, who has "a smooth white forehead and calm limpid eyes" with a glance "long and questing; it seemed to slip over the objects nearest it in order more quickly to reach those farther off" [018-022].

The choice of *Mefistofele* — Boito's opera about Faust — is thematically apt: it is music about a man who bargains his soul for knowledge and experience, played on the evening when Martin is first consciously choosing what kind of life he wants.

## The Friendship with Rissler

Rissler is "the only one of Martin's comrades who had sought his company outside of school." Their friendship was sealed by a shared love of Jacobsen's *Niels Lyhne*, a novel about a man who loses his faith and drifts through life — an apt touchstone for two young men on the edge of adulthood. They "talked the while of everything that concerns young men, of girls and God and the immortality of the soul" [018-023].

## Venus and Chastity

As dusk falls, the crowd below melts into "an indefinite mass" and the fountain's plash becomes audible. Venus appears in the west — "a solitary and mighty star" [018-029]. Henrik points it out, and Martin, drawing a woman's form on the table, asks a question that reveals his romantic idealism: "Do you think it's possible for a man to live chaste till real happiness in love comes to him?" [018-031].

Henrik blushes slightly. "It's possible — for some it's always possible. People are so different. But I know this much of myself, that it will hardly be possible for me. Then at least the great love mustn't keep me waiting much longer" [018-033]. The exchange marks the beginning of a lifelong friendship and the first articulation of Martin's idealistic — and sexually fraught — attitude toward love.
