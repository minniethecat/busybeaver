## Overview

Gabel is a school friend of Martin Birck who appears exclusively in the graduation party scene of the novel. He is paired from the start with Billfelt: the narrator lists them together as one of the classroom coteries—"Gabel and Billfelt, Jansson and Moberg, Planius and Tullman"—social groupings that have persisted "even on the school benches" [016-001]. He is described only once in physical terms: he "simpered sarcastically with his fine aristocratic face" as the conversation turns serious [016-002]. At the party's end, he and Billfelt "swore eternal friendship"—a promise the novel, with its long perspective on the dispersal of youth, quietly undercuts [016-003].

## Role in the Narrative

Gabel is a background figure in the graduation party's ensemble cast. His function is social marking: his "fine aristocratic face" and sarcastic simper place him among the privileged students, those for whom life after school is an extension rather than a rupture. After Doctor Markel's lecture, he offers Billfelt an oblique compliment—"Your brother is a gentleman" [016-004]—which serves as social currency among the Uppsala set.

His final appearance is in the post-party exodus to Charles XII's Square, where, "while Josef Marin was prophesying, Gabel caught the prettiest girl around the waist and began to waltz with her around the statue" [016-005]. The contrast with Martin—who stands with "a pounding heart" unable to approach a girl—is characteristic: Gabel acts, Martin watches.

## Key Relationships

- **Billfelt** — Inseparable school friend; they sit together, swear eternal friendship, and define each other by contrast with the other classroom pairs.
- **The elder Billfelt** — Gabel admires Billfelt's brother, calling him "a gentleman" for ordering champagne and delivering a polished speech.

## Significance

Gabel and Billfelt are the novel's representatives of a certain kind of school friendship: intense, exclusive, and doomed to dissolve. Their "eternal friendship" is sworn in the same paragraph that records the party's breakup—"It was one o'clock, getting on towards two, and various members of the party had already said good night." The novel never shows them again. Like the graduation caps gleaming white in the night, their friendship is a thing of the moment, beautiful and brief.
