## Overview

Shrove Tuesday Indians is a brief, luminous childhood scene in Old Street VIII. Maria wakes Martin on Shrove Tuesday morning with a branch of tinted feathers and news of almond-iced buns. The two children strip the feathers from the branch, dress up as "Indians," and play through the morning—but with a small, telling detail: "they played Indians and white men, but they were both Indians," refusing to make enemies of each other even in imagination.

## Narrative Account

One morning, Martin wakes to a sky of "brighter blue than they had for a long time," with dripping from the eaves and bare branches of the pear tree [427-427]. Maria comes in carrying a branch "that seemed to blossom in a hundred colors"—not flowers but tinted feathers. She flicks him with the branch, dances, and sings that it is Shrove Tuesday, she has a holiday from school, and there will be "buns with almond icing for dinner" [427-427].

The children strip the feathers from the branch and dress up in them. They "played Indians and white men, but they were both Indians" [429-429]. The detail is small but revealing: even in their imagined conflict, Martin and Maria instinctively reject the roles that would set them against each other.

## Key Participants

- **Maria**: Martin's lively sister, the bearer of the feather branch and bringer of Shrove Tuesday joy.
- **Martin**: Still young enough for make-believe, a willing co-conspirator in his sister's game.

## Significance

The scene's charm lies in its quiet subversion of the games children typically play. "They played Indians and white men, but they were both Indians"—a line that transforms a simple play episode into a small moral statement. In a novel increasingly concerned with social hierarchies, exclusion, and the pain of difference, this childhood moment offers a counter-image: two children who, given the choice to divide into antagonists, choose instead to be on the same side. It prefigures Martin's later struggles with belonging in a society that does not let people choose their allegiances so freely.
