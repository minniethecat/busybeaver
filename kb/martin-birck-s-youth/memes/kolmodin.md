## Overview

Kolmodin, Uncle Abraham's black tomcat, appears in a single passage of the novel — the New Year's visit that Martin and his sister Maria pay to their uncle. He is a minor detail in a minor scene, yet Soderberg invests him with a specificity that makes him one of the novel's most memorable minor presences: he does not mew to be let in but stands on his hind legs and pulls the bell-cord with his claws. In a novel preoccupied with loneliness in its many forms, Kolmodin's quiet companionship with the widowed Uncle Abraham is a small but telling image.

## Role in the Narrative

Martin and Maria are sent on obligatory New Year's visits to various relatives. At Uncle Abraham's, there is no Christmas tree — he is a widower, childless, living alone with an old housekeeper. The room is "large and half dark and full of books," snow falls outside, and Uncle Abraham sits "alone with his bottle of wine on the table playing chess with himself" [The Old Street, III].

In the midst of this subdued scene, Kolmodin is introduced: "He had also a parrot that could swear in French, and a black tomcat. The cat was named Kolmodin and he was the cleverest cat in the world, for when he was outside the office door and wanted to get in, he didn't mew as other cats do, but got up on his hind feet, caught his claws in the bell-cord, and pulled it hard" [The Old Street, III]. Later, "in front of the fire which glowed in the porcelain stove sat the cat Kolmodin staring gloomily at his master."

## Significance

Kolmodin's intelligence — his ability to ring the bell rather than mew — is a charming anomaly, but his "gloomy" gaze at his master suggests something deeper. Uncle Abraham, a Jew with a "long crooked nose" who is "not a happy man" and "never really cheerful," shares his solitude with a parrot that swears in French and a cat that rings doorbells. Both animals are clever, both are companions, and neither can fill the human absence at the center of the room.
