# The Devil and Witchcraft in Simplicissimus

The supernatural — and specifically the demonic — is woven into the fabric of *Der Abenteuerliche Simplicissimus* with an insistence that can disconcert modern readers. Witches, sorcerers, magic bullets, invulnerability charms, prophetic dreams, and the Devil himself appear not as allegorical flourishes but as agents with real causal power in the narrative world. This is not mere literary convention: Grimmelshausen's depiction of the supernatural reflects the authentic cosmology of the seventeenth-century soldier and peasant, for whom the Thirty Years' War was as much a spiritual conflict as a military one.

## Definition

In the world of Simplicissimus, the supernatural operates along a spectrum from folkloric magic to full demonology:

- **Folk magic**: Charms, amulets, "Passau Tickets" (bullets alleged to confer invulnerability), and minor sorceries — the everyday supernatural economy of the soldier's camp.
- **Witchcraft**: The organized practice of maleficium, including the Witches' Sabbath, pacts with the Devil, and the ability to "freeze" men against bullets (*Gefrorenheit*).
- **Demonic presence**: The Devil as an active agent, intervening in human affairs through possession, temptation, and the manipulation of events.

Grimmelshausen treats all three as, at minimum, **possible** — and in many cases, he narrates them as straightforward fact. His notorious defense of the reality of witchcraft in Books II, Chapters 17–18 is not a rhetorical exercise; it is a serious argument, buttressed by "a multitude of preposterous stories," that the Witches' Sabbath is a real event and that denying its reality is a step toward atheism.

## In Simplicissimus

**The Provost Marshal**: The novel's most concentrated figure of supernatural evil is the Provost — "a fully qualified sorcerer, necromancer and wizard" who is immune to bullets but vulnerable to the axe [B2C24-P004]. His death at Wittstock at the hands of his own musketeer, compelled by young Herzbruder, is both judicial (the executioner executed) and supernatural (the loophole in his magic exploited).

> He was a fully qualified sorcerer, necromancer and wizard, and in his own person not only as wound-proof as steel, but could make others wound-proof also, yea, and conjure whole squadrons of cavalry into the field. [B2C24-P004]

**The Witches' Sabbath (Book II, Chapters 17–18)**: Simplicissimus's account of being transported to a Witches' Sabbath — complete with diabolical feasting, dancing, and copulation — is one of the novel's most notorious passages. Modern readers have debated whether it is meant literally or as a hallucination (Simplicissimus has been drugged), but Grimmelshausen's narratorial voice refuses to undercut it. The author himself, in a digression, defends the reality of witchcraft with an earnestness that suggests genuine belief:

> …and I am firmly persuaded that there be witches and sorcerers, and that the devil doth often lead them a dance… [B2C17-P005]

**Invulnerability magic (*Gefrorenheit*)**: The belief that certain individuals could be rendered "frozen" or proof against bullets was one of the most widespread military superstitions of the Thirty Years' War [Introduction-P067]. The novel documents this belief extensively — from the Provost's magic to the peasant in Book I, Chapter 14 who cannot be killed by a bullet "perhaps by reason of the thickness of his skull." Grimmelshausen's treatment is characteristically ambivalent: he reports the belief seriously while also noting the mundane explanations (thick skulls, weak ammunition, lucky angles) that might account for it.

**The Fortune-teller of Soest (Book III, Chapter 17)**: The fortune-teller who predicts Simplicissimus's future represents the ambiguous zone between folk magic and genuine prophecy. Herzbruder "thinks meanly of the art of palmistry," but the fortune-teller's predictions prove accurate — leaving the reader, like the protagonist, uncertain whether to attribute the result to supernatural knowledge or coincidence.

## Historical Context

The Thirty Years' War coincided with the peak of the European witch-hunts. The Bishop of Würzburg's "fortnightly autos-da-fé" — mass burnings of accused witches — were only stopped when some victims denounced the prelate himself as their accomplice, "apparently believing it" [Introduction-P066]. In this environment, belief in witchcraft was not superstition but orthodoxy; skepticism was the dangerous position.

Among soldiers, the most practically significant superstition was **invulnerability magic**. Whole regiments were believed to be "frozen" against lead bullets. Christian of Brunswick manufactured glass balls to use against Tilly's supposedly invulnerable troops [Introduction-P067]. Grimmelshausen documents this belief not as an anthropologist but as a participant — he was a soldier who had lived among men who believed these things, and in some measure he believed them himself.

## Literary Significance

The supernatural in Simplicissimus serves a function beyond local color. It establishes the **moral physics** of the novel's world: evil is not an abstraction but an active force, and the war is not merely a human catastrophe but a theater of spiritual warfare. When Simplicissimus finally withdraws to his hermit's cave, his rejection of the world is not merely social but metaphysical — he is fleeing not just soldiers and princes but the Devil himself, whose presence he has felt in every camp and court.
