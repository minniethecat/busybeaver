# Springinsfeld (Jump-in-the-Field)

**Springinsfeld** — whose name translates literally as "Jump-in-the-Field" — is a wandering soldier, trickster, and adventurer who appears in Grimmelshausen's *Der Abenteuerliche Simplicissimus* and later as the title character of his own novel, *Der seltzame Springinsfeld* (1670). Together with Simplicissimus and Courage, he completes the trio of first-person narrators in the Simplician cycle — each telling the story of the Thirty Years' War from a different social and moral vantage point.

## Identity

Springinsfeld is introduced as a rootless soldier — one of the countless men who spent their entire adult lives following the armies of the Thirty Years' War. Unlike Simplicissimus (who begins as an innocent) or Courage (who begins as a victim), Springinsfeld appears to have been born into rootlessness. His name — Jump-in-the-Field — is itself a kind of anti-identity: a nickname that describes not who he is but what he does, and what he must do to survive.

> …and there sat Springinsfeld, who had once been a conjurer, and could do all manner of tricks… [Introduction-P045]

His companions describe him as a man who has tried every trade and mastered none — a jack-of-all-trades whose only consistent skill is the improvisation required by the moment. He is at various times a soldier, a beggar, a conjurer, a thief, and a storyteller.

## Narrative Role

In the architecture of the Simplician cycle, Springinsfeld occupies the **middle register** of the social scale:

| Character | Social Origin | Survival Strategy | Moral Arc |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Simplicissimus | Peasant (orphaned) | Innocence → Cunning → Piety | Redemption |
| **Springinsfeld** | Rootless soldier | Eternal adaptation | None (static) |
| Courage | Bohemian ward (orphaned) | Sexuality → Cunning → Defiance | Defiance |

His story lacks both Simplicissimus's arc of redemption and Courage's arc of defiant self-assertion. He simply endures — a figure who is always in motion not because he is going anywhere but because stopping would mean death. His narrative pessimism is arguably the most realistic of the three.

## Key Appearances

### In *Simplicissimus*

Springinsfeld appears as a companion figure in the later books, encountered during Simplicissimus's wanderings. The two recognize each other as fellow survivors of the war — men who have seen everything and belong nowhere.

### In *Der seltzame Springinsfeld*

His standalone novel (full title: *The Strange Springinsfeld*) is the third of the Simplician cycle's major first-person narratives. Told in Springinsfeld's voice — rough, colloquial, and darkly humorous — it recounts a life spent bouncing between armies, occupations, and identities. Where *Simplicissimus* is structured as a spiritual autobiography and *Courasche* as a defiant self-vindication, *Springinsfeld* is a picaresque in its purest form: a series of episodes linked only by the protagonist's unquenchable will to survive.

## The Trickster Archetype

Springinsfeld belongs to the archetype of the military trickster — a figure common in the literature of the Thirty Years' War. His closest analogues include:

- **The Spanish *pícaro*** (Lazarillo, Guzmán): The masterless man who survives by his wits
- **The German *Landsknecht*** figure: The mercenary soldier whose only loyalty is to pay
- **The Shakespearean rogue** (Autolycus, Falstaff's companions): Comic, untrustworthy, and strangely sympathetic

What distinguishes Springinsfeld from these analogues is his **lack of self-narration as tragedy**. Unlike the Spanish pícaro, who typically tells his story as a confession, Springinsfeld tells his without remorse — not out of defiance (like Courage) but out of a kind of existential exhaustion. He has been too many things to too many masters to believe in any single identity.

## Historical Context

The figure of the rootless soldier was a demographic reality of the Thirty Years' War. By the war's end, entire generations had grown up knowing nothing but army life. The *Tross* — the vast train of camp followers, wives, children, sutlers, and hangers-on — often outnumbered the fighting soldiers by three to one [Introduction-P007]. Springinsfeld is a literary incarnation of this demographic: a man for whom the war is not an interruption of normal life but the only life he has ever known.
