# The Hermit (Captain von Ramsay)

A central figure in the early books of *Der Abenteuerliche Simplicissimus*, the Hermit is the first person to show the young, terrified Simplicissimus kindness after the destruction of his family's farm. His teachings form the moral and spiritual foundation of the novel, and his death sets the protagonist on his picaresque journey through the world.

## Identity

The Hermit lives in a hut deep in the Spessart forest, subsisting on herbs and water in conditions of extreme poverty [007-002]. His true identity is revealed in B1C22: he is a Scottish nobleman, Captain von Ramsay, who served as a brave soldier and was the brother-in-law to the Governor of Hanau [022-002].

After losing the Battle of Höchst and believing his pregnant wife to have perished, von Ramsay renounced his noble status, gave away his possessions — including his horse, money, and precious rings — and retreated to the wilderness to live as a hermit. The pastor of the nearby village provided him with books, religious pictures, and the furnishings found in his hut [022-002].

## Narrative Role

The Hermit embodies the novel's central tension between worldly engagement and spiritual withdrawal. As a former soldier turned ascetic, he represents the possibility of authentic conversion from a life of violence to one of contemplation. His death in B1C12 — after teaching Simplicissimus to read, write, and pray — marks the end of the protagonist's childhood innocence and the beginning of his exposure to the wider world.

The Hermit's farewell letter, discovered by the young Simplicissimus, functions as a spiritual testament, advising his "son" to remain constant in faith, avoid worldly temptations, and remember the vanity of all earthly things. This letter reappears at critical moments throughout the narrative as a touchstone of moral guidance.

## Key Appearances

- **B1C06–B1C07**: First encounter: the terrified Simplicissimus, fleeing from soldiers, stumbles upon the Hermit's hut. Despite initially screaming that the old man is a wolf who will eat him, Simplicissimus is gradually calmed by the Hermit's gentle kindness [007-002].
- **B1C08–B1C10**: The Hermit teaches Simplicissimus Christian doctrine, reading, and writing. He renames the boy "Simplicissimus" and begins his education in earnest.
- **B1C12**: The Hermit's death and burial, which Simplicissimus performs himself, digging the grave and singing hymns. The Hermit's final instruction is a model of the *ars moriendi* (art of dying well) tradition.
- **B1C22**: The full revelation of the Hermit's past, told by the pastor to Simplicissimus at the Governor's court [022-002].

## Significance

The Hermit represents the novel's ascetic ideal — the possibility that a person can reject worldly status, wealth, and ambition in favor of spiritual devotion. His Scottish origin and noble blood also complicate the novel's treatment of social hierarchy: true nobility, the Hermit's example suggests, lies not in birth but in virtue and piety. His influence on Simplicissimus persists throughout the five books, and the protagonist's final decision to renounce the world in Book V represents a return to the Hermit's original teaching.
