# Courage (Courasche)

**Courage** — also known by her German name *Courasche* — is the arch-adventuress of Grimmelshausen's Simplician cycle. She appears first as a character in the later books of *Der Abenteuerliche Simplicissimus* and subsequently as the first-person narrator of her own novel, *Trutz Simplex: oder Lebensbeschreibung der Ertzbetrügerin und Landstörtzerin Courasche* (c. 1670), one of the earliest examples of a female picaresque voice in German literature. Her name — a deliberate conflation of "courage" and the French *courasche* — announces her defining quality: an indomitable will to survive by any means necessary.

## Identity

Courage is a camp-follower (*Tross*) who has lived through the entire Thirty Years' War. Her story, as told in *Courasche*, traces her transformation from a beautiful Bohemian noblewoman's ward into a hardened soldier's woman, thief, and survivor. In *Simplicissimus*, she appears as an older woman — a gypsy-like figure — who encounters the protagonist during his wanderings in Book V and recounts her life story:

> "…no man hath ever overcome me, yet have I, by the blessing of the Most High — although I say it myself — preserved myself in honour, so that no man can reproach me." [B5C06-P012]

The irony is, of course, that her "honour" is preserved only in her own definition — she has been wife, mistress, camp-follower, soldier, and beggar, navigating each role with the same pragmatic ruthlessness that the war itself demands.

## Narrative Role

Courage serves as Simplicissimus's **foil**: where he moves (eventually) toward repentance and withdrawal from the world, she remains embedded in it, unrepentant and defiant. Her narrative voice is the polar opposite of Simplicissimus's pious retrospection:

| Dimension | Simplicissimus | Courage |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Narrative arc | Fool → Sinner → Pilgrim → Hermit | Victim → Survivor → Trickster → Defiant |
| Gender | Male; soldiers through the war | Female; survives through men, then against them |
| Worldview | Eventually Christian-stoic | Thoroughly materialist |
| Ending | Withdrawal from the world | Defiant embrace of the world |
| Tone | Reflective, moralizing | Cynical, darkly comic |

Her presence in the Simplician cycle transforms what would otherwise be an all-male picaresque into a dialogue across gender — and across fundamentally different experiences of the Thirty Years' War.

## Key Appearances

### In *Simplicissimus* (Book V)

Courage appears in Book V as a travelling companion whom Simplicissimus encounters on the road. She recounts her life story — a picaresque autobiography in miniature — which includes:

- Her Bohemian origins and the loss of her protectors
- Her career as a camp-follower, moving between armies and lovers
- Her survival strategies: disguise, deception, and an unshakeable will

> …for I had made a vow to Saint James of Compostella, and my courage buoyed me up, though my old limbs were weary enough… [B5C17-P003]

### In *Trutz Simplex* (Courasche)

Her standalone novel, published shortly after *Simplicissimus*, is a landmark of early modern German prose. Written in the first person, it presents her life from youth to old age — a picaresque narrative that is at once earthy, violent, and darkly satirical. The novel's full title — roughly *Defiance, Simplex: or the Life-Story of the Arch-Deceiver and Vagabond Courasche* — signals its combative relationship to the parent novel. Courage is telling HER story, in HER voice, and Simplicissimus's pious framing is nowhere to be found.

## Historical and Literary Significance

Courage is more than a character — she is a literary archetype. Her name and story would echo through German literature:

1. **Brecht's Mother Courage** (*Mutter Courage und ihre Kinder*, 1939): Brecht borrowed the name and the camp-follower setting from Grimmelshausen, though his Courage is a very different figure — a tragic victim of war's economic logic rather than a triumphant survivor.

2. **The Female Picaresque**: *Courasche* is one of the earliest sustained female picaresque narratives in European literature. Where the male picaro wanders through a corrupt world and becomes corrupted, the female picara faces a world that is doubly hostile — and her survival strategies are correspondingly more radical.

3. **Documentary Value**: Courage's account of camp life, army logistics, and the sexual economy of the Thirty Years' War is an invaluable historical document — one of the few contemporary voices that records the experience of the women who followed the armies and outnumbered the soldiers three to one.
