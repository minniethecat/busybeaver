# The Provost Marshal (Profoss)

The Provost Marshal (*Profoss*) of the Swedish regiment is one of the most memorable and sinister minor figures in *Der Abenteuerliche Simplicissimus*. As the regimental executioner and disciplinarian — a role historically responsible for camp police, punishment, and the control of camp followers — the Provost wields a terrifying combination of institutional authority and supernatural power. He is both the arm of military law and its darkest perversion.

## Identity

The Provost is introduced as an old, seasoned soldier of the type "commonly the custom in war to make provosts." Grimmelshausen's description is unsparing:

> He was a fully qualified sorcerer, necromancer and wizard, and in his own person not only as wound-proof as steel, but could make others wound-proof also, yea, and conjure whole squadrons of cavalry into the field: his countenance was exactly like what our painters and poets would have Saturn to be, save that he had neither stilts nor scythe. [B2C24-P004]

The Saturn comparison — the god who devours his children — encapsulates the Provost's narrative function: he is a figure of institutionalized death, the grim enforcer whose presence makes prisoners "hold themselves the more unlucky."

## The Conspiracy with Olivier

The Provost's most consequential action in the plot is his role in framing young Herzbruder for the theft of the colonel's silver-gilt cup. Olivier, envious of Herzbruder's favor, conspires with the Provost to use sorcery to plant the cup in Herzbruder's quarters:

> At which young Herzbruder was brought to wait at table. Which, when he of his courtesy willingly did, he gave the longed-for opportunity to Oliver to bring into the world the piece of roguery of which he had long been in labour. [B2C24-P005]

The Provost's supernatural abilities make the frame-up undetectable — the cup is found where it should not be, and Herzbruder's protestations of innocence are useless against what appears to be material proof.

## Narrative Role

The Provost embodies three interrelated themes:

1. **The fusion of authority and evil**: He is no rogue operating outside the system — he IS the system, the official enforcer of military discipline who uses his position for personal malice.

2. **The superstition of invulnerability (*Gefrorenheit*)**: The Provost's immunity to bullets reflects the widespread seventeenth-century belief that certain individuals could be rendered "frozen" or proof against lead — a superstition that Grimmelshausen documents extensively [Introduction-P067]. The solution — an axe — reflects the folkloric logic that magic protection has specific loopholes.

3. **Poetic justice**: The Provost's death is carefully staged as a reversal of his own methods. Herzbruder, the victim of his sorcery, compels the Provost's own musketeer to execute him with an axe. The executioner is executed; the invulnerable man is killed by the simplest of weapons.

## The Death at Wittstock

The Provost's death scene at the Battle of Wittstock [B2C27-P003] is one of the novel's most dramatically satisfying passages. As the Swedish forces under Banér break the Imperial line, the Provost attempts to flee with his prisoners — only to be intercepted by young Herzbruder:

> "Lookye, old dog," says he, "is it the time now to breed young puppies? Now will I pay thee for thy pains."

Herzbruder shoots him — "But the shot harmed the provost as little as if it had struck an anvil." Recognizing the sorcerer's invulnerability, Herzbruder compels a musketeer of the Provost's own guard to "cut him down with an axe. And so that provost got his reward."

## Historical and Folkloric Context

The figure of the provost marshal in seventeenth-century armies was a real and feared office. Provosts were responsible for discipline, punishment, and the regulation of the *Tross* (camp followers) — a role that made them both necessary and despised. Grimmelshausen's addition of sorcery to this institutional role reflects the genuine beliefs of the soldiery, for whom the boundary between military authority and supernatural menace was often blurred in practice.
