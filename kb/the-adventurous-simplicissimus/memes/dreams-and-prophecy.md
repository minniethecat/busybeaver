# Dreams and Prophecy in Simplicissimus

Dreams, omens, and prophecies punctuate *Der Abenteuerliche Simplicissimus* with a regularity that amounts to a structural principle. Almost every major turn in Simplicissimus's fortunes is prefigured by a dream or a prophecy — most notably those of old Herzbruder, whose predictions prove uncannily accurate. This patterning is not merely a Gothic ornament but a theological claim: the apparent chaos of the Thirty Years' War is, in fact, a divinely ordered narrative in which every event has been foreseen and every outcome predetermined.

## Definition

In the literary and theological context of the Baroque, prophetic dreams operated at the intersection of three cultural frameworks:

- **Biblical precedent**: The dreams of Pharaoh, Nebuchadnezzar, and Joseph established prophecy through dreams as a legitimate mode of divine communication.
- **Neoplatonic cosmology**: The belief that the soul, freed from the body during sleep, could access higher realms of knowledge — including knowledge of future events.
- **Folk belief**: A vast popular tradition of dream interpretation, omen-reading, and prognosticatory practices that permeated all levels of seventeenth-century society.

Grimmelshausen draws on all three, but his treatment is characteristically ambivalent: the prophecies in Simplicissimus are almost always accurate, yet the novel also includes skeptical voices (like Herzbruder's dismissal of palmistry) that complicate any simple reading.

## In Simplicissimus

**Old Herzbruder's prophecies (Book II, Chapters 23–24)**: The most significant prophetic figure in the novel is old Herzbruder — the father of Simplicissimus's friend Ulrich Herzbruder — who possesses the gift of prophecy. His predictions, delivered in a quasi-biblical style, prefigure the major events of the subsequent narrative:

- He foresees Simplicissimus's rise to fame as the Huntsman of Soest
- He warns of the Provost's treachery
- He predicts his own son's suffering and eventual vindication

> …and the old man looked upon me and said things that made my hair stand on end, for he told me what would befall me in the years to come as plainly as if he had read it in a book… [B2C23-P008]

**The fortune-teller of Soest (Book III, Chapter 17)**: A well-known local character — a fortune-teller whose predictions are taken seriously by the soldiers of the Soest garrison. She reads Simplicissimus's palm and delivers a series of predictions that are subsequently fulfilled. The episode is notable for the tension it creates between skepticism (Herzbruder "thinks meanly of the art of palmistry") and the undeniable accuracy of the predictions.

**Simplicissimus's own dreams**: The protagonist himself experiences prophetic dreams at key moments — most notably before the Battle of Wittstock, where a dream prefigures both the battle's outcome and his own narrow escape. These dreams function as warnings, though Simplicissimus (characteristically) fails to heed them until after the fact.

**The providential frame**: The cumulative effect of these prophecies is to establish a providential frame around the narrative. Events that appear random — a chance encounter, a sudden reversal of fortune — are retrospectively revealed as having been foretold. The reader, like Simplicissimus, comes to understand that nothing in this world is truly accidental.

## Narrative Function

Prophecy in Simplicissimus serves a specific narrative function that distinguishes it from mere fortune-telling:

1. **Structural prefiguration**: By announcing future events in advance, prophecy transforms the episodic picaresque structure into a unified narrative — a story with a divine plot.

2. **Theological argument**: The accuracy of prophecy is evidence for Providence. If the future can be foretold, it must already exist in the mind of God. The novel's prophetic apparatus is thus a form of implicit natural theology.

3. **Dramatic irony**: The reader who remembers Herzbruder's prophecies reads subsequent events with a double vision — seeing both what happens and what was foretold. This is the Baroque aesthetic of *vanitas* made narrative: everything passes, and everything was always going to pass.

## Historical Context

The belief in prophecy was not marginal in seventeenth-century Germany but mainstream. The turmoil of the Thirty Years' War produced a flood of prognosticatory literature — almanacs, pamphlets, and broadsheets claiming to predict the war's outcome through astrology, biblical exegesis, and direct revelation. Grimmelshausen, who wrote almanacs himself (under the pseudonym "Simplicissimus"), was intimately familiar with this culture. His treatment of prophecy in the novel is thus both a reflection of popular belief and, at times, a subtle critique of its exploitation by charlatans.
