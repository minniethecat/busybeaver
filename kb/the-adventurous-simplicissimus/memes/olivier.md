# Olivier (Oliver)

Olivier (rendered as "Oliver" in Goodrick's English translation) is the most significant antagonist in *Der Abenteuerliche Simplicissimus* — a figure whose career of treachery, framed from the colonel's secretary through brigand chief, embodies the Thirty Years' War's most destructive consequence: the corruption of character itself. More than any other single figure in the novel, Olivier represents the moral abyss into which the war plunges those who surrender to its logic of power, greed, and violence.

## Identity

Olivier first appears as the secretary (*Schreiber*) of Simplicissimus's colonel — a position of trust that he systematically abuses. His intimacy with the wicked Provost Marshal, a known sorcerer, signals his moral allegiances early: "the more his envy of young Herzbruder increased... the thicker grew the intimacy between him and the provost" [B2C24-P004].

Olivier's defining trait is his capacity for patient, premeditated evil. Unlike the impulsive violence of the marauding soldiers, Olivier's villainy is calculated — he frames Herzbruder for the theft of the colonel's silver-gilt cup in a plot of Machiavellian subtlety [B2C24-P005], destroying a young man's reputation to advance his own position.

## Narrative Role

Olivier serves three key narrative functions:

1. **The false friend**: He is appointed Simplicissimus's "governor" (tutor/supervisor) after old Herzbruder's death, placing the innocent protagonist under the tutelage of the novel's most corrupt figure [B3C02-P001]. This ironic arrangement — the fool guided by the knave — is one of Grimmelshausen's darkest satirical strokes.

2. **The agent of destruction**: His plot against young Herzbruder — exploiting the provost's sorcery to frame an innocent man — sets in motion a chain of events that culminates in the death of old Herzbruder at Wittstock. The provost's eventual death by axe [B2C27-P003] is Herzbruder's revenge, but the damage Olivier set in motion is irreparable.

3. **The brigand philosopher**: In Book IV, Olivier reappears as the chief of a bandit gang, delivering a notorious self-justification for his crimes — a speech that, in the tradition of the picaresque *picaro*, blurs the line between villainy and the survival strategies that war demands of all men [B4C13].

## Old Herzbruder's Prophecy

The novel's supernatural dimension intersects with Olivier's story through old Herzbruder's deathbed prophecy: "he that should slay Oliver would meet his end at the hands of me, Simplicissimus" [B2C24-P008]. The prophecy also assures that Olivier and Simplicissimus "will never slay each other; for neither of you shall perish in fight." This providential framing of Olivier's eventual fate — that he will die violently, and his killer will in turn be slain by Simplicissimus — casts the antagonist's entire career as part of a divine design that neither he nor the protagonist can escape.

## The Brigand's Defense

Olivier's extended self-justification in Book IV is one of the novel's most rhetorically sophisticated passages. He argues that his brigandage is no different in kind from the "legitimate" violence of soldiers and princes — merely less hypocritical. This speech places Olivier in the tradition of the picaresque rogue who exposes the moral bankruptcy of "respectable" society by taking its logic to its honest conclusion.
