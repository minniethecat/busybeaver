# Disguise and Identity in Simplicissimus

No character in seventeenth-century German literature changes clothes — and with them, identities — as often or as radically as Simplicissimus. Over the course of the novel, he is, in sequence: a peasant child in rags, a fool in calfskins, a page in livery, a soldier in uniform, a Huntsman in green, a cavalier in fine clothes, a pilgrim in homespun, a beggar, Jupiter in fantastic costume, and finally a hermit in rough wool. Each change of costume is also a change of self — and the cumulative effect is to suggest that there is no self beneath the costumes, only the act of changing them.

## Definition

In the literary criticism of Grimmelshausen, **disguise** (*Verkleidung*) operates at three levels:

1. **Plot device**: Simplicissimus repeatedly escapes danger by changing his appearance — the most basic function of disguise in picaresque narrative.

2. **Social critique**: By passing through every social station (peasant, fool, soldier, courtier, pilgrim, hermit), Simplicissimus exposes the arbitrariness of social identity. The count and the peasant are distinguished only by their clothes; beneath them, both are mortal, fallible, and afraid.

3. **Metaphysical inquiry**: The instability of Simplicissimus's identity raises the Baroque question — *quis ego?* (who am I?) — with a radicalism unusual for the period. If identity can be changed as easily as a coat, is there any essential self at all?

## In Simplicissimus

The novel's treatment of disguise unfolds through a series of increasingly self-conscious transformations:

**The calfskin fool (Book I)**: Simplicissimus's first disguise is imposed on him — the pastor dresses him in calfskins to present him to the Governor of Hanau as a "fool" whose simplicity will provide amusement [B1C21-P003]. The boy does not choose this identity; he is costumed by others. Yet the experience teaches him a lesson he will never forget: that what others see is not who you are but what you wear.

**The page (Book II)**: After the fool episode, Simplicissimus is dressed as a page — a halfway identity between the fool and the soldier he will become. The page's livery is a costume of service, marking him as belonging to someone else.

**The Huntsman of Soest (Book III)**: This is Simplicissimus's first **self-chosen** identity, and it is a complete fabrication. He takes the name, costume, and skills of a huntsman without any legitimate claim to any of them. The green huntsman's coat becomes his signature — and the source of his reputation. Men fear the coat as much as the man inside it.

> …the Huntsman of Soest, as they called me, was known far and wide, and the enemy would sooner have faced the devil himself than me… [B3C08-P022]

**Jupiter (Book IV–V)**: After his capture, Simplicissimus reinvents himself yet again — this time as "Jupiter," a figure of mock-majesty whose very name is a parody of authority. The Jupiter disguise is the most self-conscious of his transformations: he knows he is playing a role, and the narrative voice (the older, converted Simplicissimus) regards this period with particular irony.

**The pilgrim**: The turn toward pilgrimage in Book V is accompanied by yet another change of costume — the rough wool of the penitent replacing the finery of the cavalier. Unlike the earlier disguises, this one aspires to authenticity: the pilgrim's costume is supposed to reflect an inner state, not mask it. Whether it succeeds is one of the novel's open questions.

**The hermit**: In the *Continuatio*, Simplicissimus's final costume is the simplest — the rough garment of the ascetic. It is the anti-costume, the rejection of all disguise. And yet the reader cannot help but wonder: is the hermit's identity any less constructed than the Huntsman's? Or has Simplicissimus simply found the most convincing costume of all?

## Theatricality and the Baroque Self

The motif of disguise in Simplicissimus belongs to the larger Baroque fascination with *theatrum mundi* — the world as stage. If all the world's a stage, then all identities are performances, and the distinction between the "real" self and the "assumed" self collapses. This is the radical implication of Simplicissimus's career of self-reinvention: there is no authentic Simplicissimus to be discovered beneath the costumes, only the continuous act of wearing them.

The novel's first-person narration intensifies this instability. The Simplicissimus who tells the story is yet another identity — the old hermit looking back on his younger selves with a mixture of amusement, shame, and detachment. He is the final costume: the retrospective narrator who claims to have found the truth beneath the masks, even as his own narration demonstrates that the search for an essential self is itself a form of performance.
