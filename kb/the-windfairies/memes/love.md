# Love

The binding emotional force that animates every major narrative arc in the collection — love appears as romantic devotion, as the bond between a creature and its habitat, as a wife's fidelity tested by enchantment, and as the joy of dance and music that characters would sacrifice safety to experience.

## Definition

Love in these tales is never mere sentiment. It is the active principle that sets plots in motion: [[lucilla|Lucilla]]'s love of dancing draws her to [[the-king|the King]]'s court (001-021); [[the-pool|the pool]]'s love for the tree drives it to uproot itself and follow (003-037); [[the-potter|the potter]]'s love for his wife makes him vulnerable to the gipsy's cup's enchantment (005-021). Love is both the highest value characters possess and the point at which they are most susceptible to testing, loss, and supernatural intervention. It is never self-generated — it is discovered, bestowed, and sometimes stolen — but always requires active fidelity to endure.

## Manifestations

### In "The Gipsy's Cup"

The tale is built entirely around love as the object of magical manipulation. [[the-gipsy|The gipsy girl]] crafts the little brown cup with a charm — "one of his cups would make you wise if you drank out of it, and another would give you your true love's heart if she drank from it" (005-006). The potter gives it to his wife, who drinks and "will be a true wife to you, and love you better than anything on earth" (005-025). When [[the-gipsy-man|the gipsy man]] later enchants her to drink again, her love is inverted to hatred: "I do not love you, nay, I hate you" (005-054). The cup's magic reveals that love, once magically bestowed, can be magically withdrawn — only freely chosen fidelity endures. The wife's eventual return — "she loved him as at first" (005-080) — restores the natural order.

### In "The Pool and the Tree"

The love between the pool and the tree is the collection's most lyrical depiction of mutual devotion between unlike beings. "The two loved each other better than anything else on earth" (003-001). The tree watches "its beloved friend" (003-004) and the pool cannot bear separation when the tree is felled: "it knew that the pool it had loved so had followed it" (003-037). This love transcends species, form, and even the destruction of one partner — it is the tale's central miracle.

### In "The Windfairies"

Lucilla's love is expressed through her defining passion: "dancing is the thing I love best in the world" (001-021). But the tale deepens this: [[windfairies|the windfairies]] warn that "no one will overtake whatever you love best in this world" (001-016) unless she keeps faith. Her love of dance and her love of home are placed in tension, and her faithfulness to her promise proves the deeper love. The King, who "loved nothing so much as beautiful dancing" (001-027), provides the counterpoint — love as appreciation rather than sacrifice.

## Associated Characters

- **Lucilla** — Her love of dancing draws her into danger; her love of home and fidelity draws her back (001-021)
- **The Pool** — Loves the tree with a devotion so complete it physically follows when the tree is destroyed (003-001) (003-037)
- **The Potter** — His love for his wife is the emotional stakes upon which the gipsy's magic operates (005-021) (005-025)
- **The Potter's Wife** — Her enchanted love, its loss, and recovery trace the full arc of love tested by magic (005-054) (005-080)

## Related Concepts

- [[keeping-faith]] — Fidelity in love is the ultimate test of character across these tales
- [[beauty]] — Love is often first kindled by beauty, but must survive beyond it
- [[music]] — Music and dance are the languages through which love is expressed and celebrated
- [[home]] — Love of home pulls characters back from enchantment and danger

## Moral Dimension

The tales teach that love freely given is the highest good, but love magically compelled is fragile and reversible. The gipsy's cup creates a laboratory for this lesson: the potter's wife loves him only while the charm holds; when the gipsy man recasts the spell, her love vanishes (005-054). Only when the charm is broken does authentic love return (005-080). The pool's love for the tree, by contrast, is never magically mediated — it is depicted as natural, inevitable, and therefore indestructible even by death (003-037). The implicit moral: love rooted in nature and choice endures; love dependent on enchantment is illusion. Lucilla's story adds the corollary — love of one's art or passion must be held in right relation to love of duty and home (001-016).
