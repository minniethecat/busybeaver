# Pity

Fellow-feeling for the suffering of others — a quiet but persistent moral emotion that marks the boundary between good characters and bad, between those who can be saved and those who are beyond help.

## Definition

Pity in these tales is the capacity to feel for another's misfortune. It is the emotional foundation of mercy — characters who can be moved by pity are redeemable; those who cannot are morally lost. Pity operates at both the human and supernatural level: the windfairies might be said to pity Lucilla, and the cat's clever revenge is in part a response to the absence of pity in its master (006-001). Unlike the grand virtues of faith-keeping and truth-telling, pity is quiet — it manifests in small mercies and moments of recognition rather than heroic stands (005-074).

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

The King and Queen are defined by their absence of pity. They imprison Lucilla, attempt to drown her, try to burn her, and plan to behead her — all without a flicker of compassion (001-043) (001-049). The Queen's refusal to pity Lucilla even when she weeps and begs (001-055) marks her as irredeemable. The windfairies, by contrast, consistently respond to Lucilla's distress with protection — supernatural pity in action (001-058).

### In "Vain Kesta"

Kesta's scheme depends on evoking pity falsely — she tells each suitor a sob story of being pursued against her will (002-007) (002-012) (002-019). Her manipulation of pity is part of her moral failure: she uses the appearance of vulnerability to extract advantage, corrupting an emotion meant to connect.

### In "The Gipsy's Cup"

Genuine pity appears in the wife who tells "all the truth about the charms in the cup" (005-074) — her revelation is an act of compassionate truth-telling that enables the resolution.

### In "The Story of a Cat"

The master's defining trait is his absence of pity: "greedy and hard, and cared for nobody or nothing" (006-001). The cat's triumph represents poetic justice against a man incapable of compassion.

## Associated Characters

- **Lucilla** — Evokes pity in readers and in the windfairies through her undeserved suffering (001-055) (001-058)
- **Kesta** — Weaponizes false pity to manipulate suitors (002-007)
- **The Cat's Master** — Defined by his incapacity for pity: "cared for nobody or nothing" (006-001)
- **The Gipsy's Wife** — Acts from genuine pity when she reveals the truth (005-074)

## Moral Dimension

Pity is the tales' quietest virtue — never the central theme of a story, yet persistently present as the moral baseline. Characters who lack pity (the King, the Queen, the cat's master) are the tales' true villains. Characters who feel pity, or evoke it, align with the moral order. The tales suggest that pity is not weakness — it is the emotional prerequisite for justice. Without pity, law becomes persecution (Lucilla's trial) and power becomes cruelty (the King's tyranny) (001-043).
