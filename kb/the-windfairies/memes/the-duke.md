# The Duke

The Duke is the highest-ranking authority in "Vain Kesta," the second tale in *The Windfairies* collection. He rules from [[the-dukes-palace|a palace]] in the town and serves as the ultimate symbol of [[kesta|Kesta]]'s overreaching ambition. Though initially willing to marry Kesta based on her claim of being pursued by many suitors, he violently rejects her upon discovering her true identity as a common dairymaid.

The Duke embodies the story's critique of [[social-class|social hierarchy]] and [[vanity]]. He is the apex of the system Kesta tries to climb — and his rejection demonstrates that social status is treated as an immutable quality rather than something that can be disguised with a fine story. His casual cruelty, his vanity, and his idle fishing all serve the tale's satirical portrait of aristocratic authority.

## Role in the Story

### Kesta's Approach

The Duke appears as the final target of Kesta's escalating ambition. After securing promises of marriage from [[adam|Adam]] the farmhand, [[the-miller|the miller]], [[the-bailiff|the bailiff]], [[the-banker|the banker]], and [[the-general|the General]] of the army, Kesta decides to "try higher, and see what the Duke himself would say" (002-029). The Duke is introduced as a passive figure, "sitting by the stream in the garden, where he sits fishing all day" (002-029), dressed in green and "half asleep" (002-029).

Kesta's approach is audacious: she simply walks through the courtyard into the garden and addresses him directly. A servant tells her "he is sitting by the stream in the garden, where he sits fishing all day, and you can go and speak to him if you choose" (002-029) — a detail that reveals the Duke's accessibility and his detachment from serious governance. He is a ruler who fishes while the world passes by.

### The Duke's Decision

When Kesta presents herself, the Duke is initially skeptical of her beauty but is persuaded by her claim that multiple men of standing wish to marry her. His vanity mirrors Kesta's — he is willing to marry her not out of affection, but because her desirability to others validates her worth:

> "I should not have thought you so very pretty, but if what you say is true you must be." (002-036)

The Duke's reasoning is circular and comically shallow. First he asks whether the General really wanted to marry her: "The General! Is this true? does he really want to marry you?" (002-034). When Kesta confirms, the Duke is impressed not by Kesta herself but by the implied judgment of his subordinates. His vanity is social rather than personal: if others want her, she must be valuable. This logic mirrors Kesta's own chain of escalating suitors — both characters judge worth by the desire of others.

### The Climactic Reversal

The Duke's role is to deliver the story's climactic reversal. When Kesta's cheeses fall from her basket, his reaction is immediate and violent:

> "Good gracious, you made them! Then you must be a common dairymaid, and your hands are quite rough. How terrible! And I was just going to marry you." (002-047)

He orders his pages to hunt her from the palace by blowing [[the-dukes-horn|his horn]]: "he sprang to his feet in a towering passion, and seizing his horn blew it so loudly that the four pages ran up in great alarm" (002-047). His command — "Hunt her away, she is an impostor — a common farm wench and makes cheeses. She thought herself good enough to be the Duchess!" (002-047) — triggers the cascade of rejections from every suitor she had previously charmed. The Duke's outrage is not moral but social: he is not angry at being deceived so much as horrified at having nearly married beneath his station.

## Character Traits

### Vanity and Social Pride

- **Vain**: The Duke judges worth entirely by social standing, willing to marry Kesta when he believes her desirable to others but instantly revolted by her working-class origins. His famous line — "I should not have thought you so very pretty, but if what you say is true you must be" (002-036) — reveals a man who cannot form his own judgments and relies entirely on the opinions of others.
- **Impulsive**: He decides to marry Kesta immediately upon accepting her story, ordering a feast and the chaplain without any investigation: "bid them to prepare a feast and fitting clothes for the bride, and tell the chaplain to be ready, for I mean to marry her at once" (002-038). His impulsiveness is the mark of a ruler accustomed to instant gratification.
- **Quick to anger**: Upon discovering Kesta's deception, he flies into "a towering passion" (002-047) and orders her humiliating expulsion. His rage is disproportionate to the offense — a dairymaid's social climbing merits a public hunt and expulsion from town.
- **Passive and idle**: He spends his days fishing by the stream, "half asleep" (002-029), suggesting a ruler disconnected from active governance. When Kesta asks what he has caught, he answers "Nothing yet" (002-041), and when she asks why he continues, he replies "Because I'm sure to catch something soon, and it's amusing" (002-043) — a portrait of aristocratic leisure at its most vacuous.

### Relationship to Power

The Duke wields absolute authority within his domain but exercises it capriciously. He summons four pages "dressed in blue and gold" (002-038) with a blast of his horn and commands them to prepare a wedding at a moment's notice. With equal suddenness, he orders them to hunt the would-be bride from the palace. His power is theatrical rather than judicious — expressed through horns, pages, and public spectacle rather than through deliberation or justice.

## Key Actions

### The Courtship and Betrothal

The Duke's actions follow a dramatic arc from acceptance to rejection:

1. **Listens to Kesta's plea**: When Kesta approaches him in the garden with her "sad story," he hears her out with bemused skepticism. His first words — "Good gracious! who are you? — don't you know I am the Duke?" (002-030) — establish his self-importance and his surprise at being approached so directly.

2. **Challenges her claim**: He questions whether the General truly wished to marry her: "Is this true? does he really want to marry you?" (002-034). The question reveals his competitive relationship with his own subordinates.

3. **Agrees to marry her**: Convinced by her story and the implied validation from his subordinates, he decides she is "very pretty" and suitable: "I'm not sure if it would not suit me to marry you myself" (002-036). He frames the question as one of his own convenience: "Of course you would be delighted to marry me and be the Duchess?" (002-036).

4. **Orders wedding preparations**: He summons four pages "dressed in blue and gold" (002-038) to prepare a feast, bridal clothes, and the chaplain. The theatrical summons — blowing his horn to call the pages — emphasizes the performative nature of his authority.

### The Rejection

5. **Rejects her with fury**: When the cheeses reveal her true station, he "sprang to his feet in a towering passion, and seizing his horn blew it so loudly" (002-047), ordering the pages to "Hunt her away" (002-047). The horn, which moments before summoned pages to prepare a wedding, now summons them to drive out an impostor — a symmetrical reversal that underlines the Duke's capriciousness.

6. **Triggers the cascade**: The Duke's rejection sets off the chain reaction that drives Kesta from every door in town. The pages shout "Away with the impertinent dairymaid, who thought herself good enough to marry the Duke" (002-051), and each subsequent suitor — the General, the banker, the bailiff, the miller — echoes the Duke's judgment. His authority is such that his rejection becomes everyone's rejection.

## Relationships

- [[the-general]] — The General is the Duke's military commander. The Duke's interest in Kesta is partly piqued by learning that his own General wanted to marry her, suggesting rivalry among the ruling class (002-034)
- [[kesta]] — The vain dairymaid who tries to marry the Duke. Their encounter forms the climax of "Vain Kesta," where both characters' vanity is exposed
- [[the-duchess]] — The position Kesta briefly aspires to fill. The Duke offers it as the ultimate prize in the town's social hierarchy: "you would be delighted to marry me and be the Duchess?" (002-036)
- [[the-bailiff]] — The Duke's bailiff, who harbors a personal grudge against the miller and takes pleasure in thwarting him. The bailiff's allegiance to the Duke is part of the social chain that Kesta tries to climb (002-013)

## Significance

The Duke embodies the story's critique of social hierarchy and vanity. He is the apex of the system Kesta tries to climb — and his rejection demonstrates that social status is treated as an immutable quality rather than something that can be disguised with a fine story. The Duke's willingness to marry Kesta based solely on her perceived desirability to other men mirrors Kesta's own vanity: both judge worth by external validation rather than intrinsic merit.

His casual cruelty in ordering Kesta's public humiliation — a dairymaid "hooted out of the town" (002-063) — exposes the brutality underlying the rigid class system of the fairy tale world. The Duke, like every other suitor in the story, is revealed to value status above all else.

The Duke also serves as a foil to other authority figures in the collection. Unlike [[the-king|the King]] in "The Windfairies," who is manipulated by [[the-queen|the Queen]] into persecuting [[lucilla|Lucilla]], the Duke acts on his own initiative — his vanity and cruelty are entirely his own. And unlike the supernatural authorities — the [[windfairies]], [[the-gnome|the gnome]], [[the-fairy-shepherd|the fairy shepherd]] — who reward [[keeping-faith|faithfulness]] and punish [[greed]], the Duke's authority is purely social, rooted in nothing deeper than the accident of birth. The destruction of the King's palace in "The Windfairies" (001-066) suggests what the collection thinks of such authority: it is built on sand, and the wind will level it.
