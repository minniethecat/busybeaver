# Hulda

Hulda is the co-protagonist and emotional anchor of "Dumb Othmar" (Chapter 7), an orphan girl of humble station whose steadfast love proves the decisive force in the story. Described as "neither pretty nor clever," she dwells alone with an old woman near the woods, working as a village helper (007-004). When the dwarf musician steals Othmar's voice, it is Hulda — alone among the villagers — who intuits the supernatural truth and undertakes a perilous quest to restore what was lost. Her journey transforms her from a quiet, overlooked presence into the story's true heroic figure, demonstrating that devotion and courage matter more than beauty or brilliance.

## Role in the Story

### Witness and Warning

Hulda first appears during the dwarf musician's performance, sitting silently behind Othmar. Unlike the other villagers who are transported by the music, Hulda alone feels unease: "She shuddered as she heard it" (007-004). Her instinctive wariness marks her as uniquely perceptive — she sees danger where others see only wonder.

When the dwarf invites Othmar to guide him over the hill, Hulda emerges from the shadows to deliver her warning: "Oh, Othmar, don't let him take you far — come back soon" (007-009). Othmar dismisses her with irritation — "How silly you are, Hulda!" (007-010) — but her misgiving proves tragically justified when Othmar returns mute.

### Vigil and Discovery

Hulda is the first to see Othmar return from the hillside. She stands in the road, "shading her eyes from the sun, and watching the way he came" (007-027), having already searched for him far and near (007-028). When Othmar cannot speak, Hulda's intuition pierces what the villagers mistake for illness or fright: "only Hulda said to herself, 'it was the wicked little man'" (007-034).

Her response is immediate tenderness — "Never fear, Othmar, your voice will soon come back ... let me nurse you, and you shall soon be well" (007-033) — but this is not naive optimism. It is the first expression of a resolve that will carry her far beyond the village.

### The Quest and Reunion

After a year of Othmar's silence, Hulda makes her decision on midsummer night. She comes to Othmar at midnight and declares: "I am going into the big world to find him, for if he has done you this ill he will know how to cure you, and I have saved all my money for a year" (007-037). Othmar, weeping, shakes his head — but Hulda does not heed him (007-038).

Her quest — fully chronicled in [[huldas-quest]] — takes her through encounters with a shepherd, a schoolmaster, a cake-maker, and finally Tommy the fool, whose cryptic advice to "follow the raven" (007-058) leads her to the dwarf's encampment. There, following the fool's further counsel to "do what the raven does" (007-064), she captures [[the-green-snake]] that destroys the singing girl who stole Othmar's voice (007-067).

Hulda's return is marked by exhaustion and doubt. "I have done you no good," she tells Othmar, believing she has failed (007-105). Yet it is precisely her persistence — carrying the snake through mockery, imprisonment in the schoolhouse, and escape through a window (007-100, 007-102) — that enables the miraculous restoration. When Othmar breathes her name upon the snake, it transforms into a golden trumpet, and his voice returns in a burst of music (007-106).

## Character Traits

### Watchful and Perceptive

Hulda's defining quality is her attention. She sits behind Othmar during the concert, unseen but attentive (007-004). When the dwarf arrives, she instinctively follows. Where others celebrate, she shudders. This watchfulness is not passivity but a form of care — she sees what others overlook because she cares more deeply.

### Steadfast in Devotion

Hulda's love for Othmar is the story's unwavering constant. She has played with him "ever since they were babies, and loved him dearly" (007-004). When he dismisses her warning, she does not withdraw — she waits. When he cannot speak, she stays. When the villagers believe he is merely ill, she alone suspects the truth. When he weeps silently at midnight, she comes to him (007-036). And when all hope seems lost, she undertakes a journey no one else would attempt.

### Humble and Self-Effacing

The text is explicit about Hulda's plainness: "She was neither pretty nor clever" (007-004). She is an orphan who "dwelt alone with an old woman, who gave her food and lodging for sweeping out her room, and cleaning and cooking" (007-004). Her work is menial, her station low. Yet this humility is precisely what arms her for the quest — she is willing to ask a fool for advice when the schoolmaster cannot help (007-047, 007-057), and she follows a raven across strange country on nothing more than faith (007-061).

### Courageous and Resolute

Hulda's courage is not the dramatic bravery of a warrior but the quiet, stubborn kind. She walks for days until "footsore and weary, but she would not give up" (007-061). She grasps a snake "though she was very much afraid of it" (007-065). She endures mockery from the cake-maker and the baker, imprisonment in the schoolhouse (007-098), and climbs through a window clutching the snake (007-102). At every turn, she could have abandoned her quest — and at every turn, she chose to continue.

## Key Actions

### 1. The Warning (007-008 — 007-010)

When the dwarf invites Othmar to guide him, Hulda emerges from behind to caution him. Her simple plea — "come back soon" — is the story's first expression of protective love. Othmar's dismissal of her as "silly" establishes the dramatic irony that will sustain the entire narrative.

### 2. The Vigil and Discovery (007-027 — 007-034)

After Othmar disappears with the dwarf, Hulda searches for him and waits. She is the first to see him return, pale and silent. When he sobs without sound, "Hulda knew that Othmar was dumb" (007-031). Her tears and tender words mark the moment the story shifts from supernatural adventure to emotional ordeal.

### 3. The Midnight Vow (007-036 — 007-039)

On midsummer night, Hulda comes to Othmar at midnight and declares her intention to find the dwarf. She asks for three things: a curl of his hair, a kiss, and a promise that her name will be his first word when speech returns (007-039). This ritual transforms their parting from a desperate venture into a covenant.

### 4. Seeking the Fool (007-042 — 007-058)

Hulda's quest is distinguished by her willingness to consult the lowly. When the shepherd, the animal-healer, and the schoolmaster all fail her, she asks the cake-maker who is "the most ignorant and foolish person in these parts" (007-050, 007-051). This leads her to Tommy the fool, who gives her the cryptic instruction to follow the raven (007-058).

### 5. The Snake and the Dwarf's Camp (007-062 — 007-069)

Following the raven to the plain, Hulda hides behind a bush and witnesses the instruments' transformation. Heeding the fool's advice — "do what the raven does" — she digs in the earth and seizes the bright green snake (007-064, 007-065). The snake darts into the dancers and coils around the singing girl's throat, destroying her (007-067).

### 6. The Return and Restoration (007-105 — 007-107)

Hulda returns to the village, exhausted and convinced of failure. She presents the snake to Othmar, weeping that "I have done you no good" (007-105). But when Othmar breathes her name and touches the snake, it transforms into a golden trumpet. He cries "Hulda!" and his voice returns in a burst of music (007-106). The village rejoices, and Othmar marries Hulda (007-107).

## Relationships

- [[othmar]] — The musician Hulda loves. Their bond, formed in childhood, survives his dismissal of her warning, his muteness, and her long absence. Othmar's recognition of her worth — and his lifelong insistence that "it is not really my voice, it is my wife's, Hulda's" (007-107) — is the tale's final affirmation of her devotion.

- [[the-dwarf-musician]] — The supernatural antagonist Hulda instinctively distrusts. While others marvel at his instruments, Hulda shudders. Her suspicion is vindicated when the dwarf steals Othmar's voice.

- [[the-raven]] — The supernatural guide whose croaking counsel — "Poor Hulda, come with me, and I will show you where the dwarf is" (007-060) — leads Hulda to the dwarf's camp. Though she cannot understand its speech, she follows it faithfully.

- [[the-green-snake]] — The creature Hulda seizes from the earth and carries through mockery and imprisonment. It destroys the singing girl who stole Othmar's voice and ultimately transforms into the golden trumpet through which Othmar regains his speech.

- the-old-woman — Hulda's guardian, who gives her food and lodging in exchange for sweeping, cleaning, and cooking (007-004). Hulda's humble circumstances with this woman underscore her low station and make her heroic journey all the more remarkable.

## Significance

Hulda embodies the tale's central moral: that true worth resides not in beauty, cleverness, or supernatural gifts but in steadfast love and selfless action. The text's deliberate emphasis on her plainness — "neither pretty nor clever" — sets her in deliberate contrast to the bewitching beauty of the dwarf's musical daughter. Where beauty steals, plainness restores; where cleverness schemes, simple devotion acts.

Hulda also inverts the fairy-tale convention of the passive beloved awaiting rescue. Though Othmar is the story's named protagonist, it is Hulda who drives the narrative forward — warning, watching, journeying, enduring, and ultimately redeeming. Her success through obedience to a fool's advice and a raven's guidance suggests a deeper wisdom: that humility and openness to unlikely counsel may unlock paths hidden to the conventionally wise.

Hulda's quest unites key thematic strands of the collection: the testing of fidelity explored in [[keeping-faith]], the transformative power of [[love]], and the motif of [[journey]] as spiritual trial. Her story demonstrates that love, when anchored in action rather than sentiment, proves stronger than enchantment and more enduring than beauty.
