# Lucilla

Lucilla is the central protagonist of "The Windfairies," the title story of Mary De Morgan's 1900 fairy tale collection. The daughter of a miller at a [[windmill-on-the-downs]] by the sea (001-001), Lucilla's life is shaped by a childhood encounter with the [[windfairies]] that forever alters her destiny. When they teach her to [[dance]] as no mortal can and bind her by a solemn [[promise]] of secrecy (001-015), she enters into a lifelong covenant of [[keeping-faith]] with the supernatural world — a bond that will sustain her through poverty, imprisonment, and mortal danger, culminating in a miraculous rescue that reaffirms the power of loyalty.

## Role in the Story

Lucilla is the central figure of "The Windfairies" (Chapter 1). Her narrative arc spans childhood to adulthood, tracing a journey from innocence through suffering to final redemption. She serves as the reader's point of entry into the magical world — her innocence and curiosity make the fairy realm accessible (001-003, 001-005).

### Childhood at the Windmill

As a very young child living at a windmill on the downs by the sea, Lucilla is motherless — her mother died when she was a baby — and spends her days alone while her father works (001-001). One night she lies awake watching the great sails turn, and the Windfairies come to her in the form of little grey figures dancing as lightly as if made of feather-down (001-001). She boldly asks them who they are and what they do (001-003, 001-005), and they explain that they blow the winds, sweep the earth, make hurricanes, and turn the mill wheel (001-006). Her courage impresses them — she declares she will not cry (001-007) — and they lift her onto a windmill sail, sweeping through the air with her as she clings to the great wooden arm (001-008). Finding her brave and good, the little grey elves offer to teach her their magical dance, on condition that she swear never to reveal who taught her (001-009, 001-010, 001-011). Lucilla promises faithfully, entreating them with all her heart (001-013, 001-015). The Windfairies kiss her hands and feet, and she feels as if she were made of feather-down, swaying up and down as lightly as they, never so happy in all her life (001-018). At sunrise her father finds her and is angry — but she dares not tell him what kept her, or say that she had been playing with windfairies (001-019).

### Marriage and Departure

Years pass, and Lucilla never sees the Windfairies again, though she watches for them every night (001-020). She grows into a beautiful young woman, tall and lithe as a willow wand, whose dancing on the downs and by [[the-sea]] draws wonder from all who see her (001-020). Her father marvels at her skill, saying he has never seen a dancer who can come near her (001-020). She rejects many suitors because none can dance as she can, declaring she will wait for a husband who can match her step (001-021). Then a great storm wrecks a ship near the mill, and among the sailors is a young man with black curly hair and bright eyes who resolves to marry her (001-022). Though he cannot match her dancing — she is too fast, too light — he catches her by the waist and declares his arms are strong enough to hold her still (001-022). She marries [[the-sailor]] and moves to [[the-cottage-by-the-sea]], where her aging father comes too (001-023). They have two little children and live happily until one winter's hardship drives her husband to take a long sea voyage to earn money for a new boat (001-024). Left alone with her father and children, Lucilla feels lonely and sad, and she goes down to the water's edge to pray the Windfairies to protect her husband's ship and bring it safe home again (001-024, 001-025).

### Trial at the King's Court

One day, as Lucilla dances on the shore, courtiers from a foreign land see her and marvel at her extraordinary grace (001-026). They persuade her to come to the court of [[the-king]], who adores dancing above all else, promising her a sack of gold that would lift her family from poverty (001-027). Though reluctant to leave her children, she is persuaded by her father, who says the gold would make them all rich (001-028). She says goodbye to her little ones with eyes red from crying, and before departing she goes out alone onto the cliffs, stretches out her arms, and calls on the Windfairies to go with her and help her, begging them to be true to her as she has been true to them (001-028). They sail many days to a land she has never heard of, to a crowded town with a royal palace on the hill (001-029). At the court she pleads to wear a light grey gown like the Windfairies, remembering how they looked when they danced on the downs (001-032). Before her performance, she throws wide her window, holds out her arms, and cries to the Windfairies for help — though she can scarce keep from crying with thoughts of her husband at sea and her babies at home (001-033). She dances before the King and court at the royal wedding festivities, feeling as light as sea foam, and the applause that follows is like nothing ever heard in that hall (001-034).

But [[the-queen]] grows jealous of the admiration Lucilla receives (001-036, 001-041). She secretly visits Lucilla and demands to be taught the same dancing, but despite Lucilla's patient lessons, the Queen remains awkward and heavy beside her, dancing just as other mortals might (001-040). Furious at being deceived — though Lucilla taught her honestly — the Queen schemes against her (001-041). When the King demands to know who taught Lucilla her marvellous art, she answers only that she must have learned it from watching the windmill sails go round (001-043). The King, enraged by what he takes for insolence, threatens punishment, and the Queen insinuates that Lucilla's dancing must be witchcraft taught by the Evil One (001-045). For refusing to break her promise, Lucilla is imprisoned in a dark cell where the walls are so thick she can hear no noise from without, and she bitterly grieves that she left her home and children (001-050, 001-052). The Queen, visiting her in prison, coaxes and threatens her in turn, then tells her she will be killed that very week (001-053, 001-055).

### Rescue and Return

The King orders Lucilla drowned with heavy weights tied to her feet and her arms bound to her sides (001-056). As the guards row her out to sea and the crowd lining the beach jeers "She is a witch! she is a witch!" (001-057), Lucilla cries out to the Windfairies that now is the time of her direst need, begging them to keep faith with her as she has kept faith with them (001-058). Though thrown into the water, she never sinks — the Windfairies hold her up, and the salt waves rock her gently and draw her back to shore, laying her on the sand (001-058). Enraged, the King and Queen order her burned on a pyre in [[the-marketplace]], but the flames divide on each side and are blown away from her on all sides, so that she sits in the midst quite unhurt (001-060). The crowd cries out that she must indeed be a witch, since she will not drown and the fire will not burn her (001-061). At last she is led to the scaffold to be beheaded, with [[the-bag-of-gold]] placed beside her as a final temptation to confess her secret (001-062, 001-065). As the executioner lifts the axe in the air, the Windfairies unleash a sudden roar of wind: the axe is swept from his hand, the houses in the marketplace totter and fall, and the palace high on the hill is reduced to a mass of ruins (001-066). Only Lucilla remains unharmed, kneeling on the scaffold.

She lifts her head and looks out to sea. A big ship with shining white sails rides over the crested waves, untroubled by the gale (001-064, 001-067). When she looks at the sailors who come ashore to help, the first whom she sees is her husband (001-067). She gives a great cry and holds out her arms, knowing now that the Windfairies have kept faith with her and saved her in her direst hour of need (001-067). Her husband tells her that whatever way they steered, the wind had blown their ship without guidance straight to the town where she was to be killed (001-068). Lucilla takes the bag of gold and returns with her husband to the cottage by the sea, to her father and her babies, and she lives happily with him for the rest of her life (001-069).

## Character Traits

- **Innocent and trusting** — As a child, Lucilla readily accepts the Windfairies' existence without fear, and eagerly wants to learn about them (001-003, 001-005). Her openness to the magical world sets her apart from ordinary mortals, who are said never to keep a secret (001-012).
- **Loyal and faithful** — She vows never to reveal the Windfairies' secret, and she keeps this promise through years of solitude, poverty, and mortal peril (001-013, 001-015, 001-019, 001-046, 001-054). Her entire life becomes a testament to the power of [[keeping-faith]].
- **Curious and eager to learn** — She repeatedly asks the Windfairies to teach her their magic and dances, displaying a hunger for wonder that transcends fear (001-011, 001-017).
- **Brave** — As a child, she declares she will not cry when the Windfairies first approach, and she springs up eagerly to meet them (001-007). As an adult, she faces drowning, fire, and the executioner's axe without breaking her word (001-058, 001-065).
- **Loving and devoted** — Her longing for her husband and children never leaves her, and even as she dances before the King, her thoughts are with her husband at sea and her babies in the cottage (001-033, 001-034, 001-046).
- **Resilient** — Despite being torn from her home, imprisoned, and repeatedly threatened with death, Lucilla never wavers. She weeps and laments, but she does not yield (001-051, 001-054).

## Key Actions

1. **First encounter** — Lucilla meets the Windfairies as a very young child while lying awake at the windmill. She asks them who they are, and they identify themselves as the beings who blow the winds and turn the mill wheel (001-001, 001-003, 001-005, 001-006).

2. **The ride on the sail** — She is lifted onto a windmill sail and sweeps through the air with the little grey elves clinging beside her, proving her courage beyond any doubt (001-008, 001-009).

3. **Learning to dance** — The Windfairies touch her hands and feet, and she is transformed: she feels as if she were made of feather-down, swaying and dancing with a lightness no mortal has ever known (001-018).

4. **The promise** — Before they depart, the Windfairies bind her by a solemn vow: if she keeps faith, they will come to her help in her direst hour of need; if she breaks it, her feet will become heavy as lead and great misfortune will strike whatever she loves best (001-016).

5. **Keeping the secret** — Years pass and Lucilla never sees the Windfairies again, but she tells no one about them — not her father, not her husband, not anyone (001-019, 001-020). Her secret becomes the invisible thread running through her whole life.

6. **Marriage** — She marries the sailor whose strength holds her still when his dancing cannot match hers, and they live happily at the cottage by the sea with their two children (001-022, 001-023, 001-024).

7. **The journey to court** — Persuaded by courtiers and her own father, she leaves her children and sails to a foreign kingdom, calling on the Windfairies to be true to her as she has been true to them (001-028, 001-029).

8. **Dancing before the King** — Dressed in a light grey gown like the Windfairies, she dances at the royal wedding festivities and stuns the entire court, who declare it the greatest of luck that the travellers met her (001-031, 001-032, 001-034).

9. **Imprisonment and three trials** — When she refuses to reveal her secret, she is imprisoned in a dark cell (001-050, 001-052). She survives drowning when the Windfairies buoy her up and the waves carry her to shore (001-057, 001-058). She survives burning when the flames are blown away from her on every side (001-060). On the scaffold, as the axe is raised, the Windfairies unleash their full power and annihilate the palace and marketplace, leaving Lucilla unharmed (001-065, 001-066).

10. **Reunion and return** — Her husband's ship, piloted by the wind without human guidance, arrives at the very moment of her deliverance. Lucilla takes the bag of gold and returns home with him to her children, living happily for the rest of her life (001-067, 001-068, 001-069).

## Relationships

- [[windfairies]] — The supernatural beings who befriend her in childhood, teach her their magical dance, and rescue her from execution in adulthood. Their reciprocal bond — her faithfulness for their protection — forms the moral and narrative core of the story (001-003, 001-016, 001-018, 001-058, 001-066).
- [[the-sailor]] — Her husband, a young sailor with black curly hair, bright eyes, and white teeth, who loves her deeply. He wins her not by matching her dancing but by holding her still with the strength of his arms (001-022). Years later, his ship is brought to her by the same Windfairies who save her life (001-067, 001-068).
- [[windmill-on-the-downs]] — Her childhood home, perched on the downs by the sea, where she first encounters the Windfairies as a child and learns to dance as no mortal can (001-001, 001-018).
- [[the-king]] — The foreign monarch who admires Lucilla's dancing but, when she refuses to reveal her teacher, orders her imprisoned and executed. He is ultimately punished when the Windfairies destroy his palace (001-043, 001-044, 001-050, 001-066).
- [[the-queen]] — The jealous young Queen who tries and fails to learn Lucilla's dancing, then accuses her of witchcraft and schemes to have her killed (001-036, 001-040, 001-041, 001-055). Her envy is the proximate cause of Lucilla's persecution.
- [[dance]] — The supernatural art that defines Lucilla's identity from childhood onward. Taught to her by the Windfairies, it is both the source of her wonder and the reason for her persecution at court (001-011, 001-018, 001-034).
- [[keeping-faith]] — The central moral principle of Lucilla's life: her unbroken promise to the Windfairies, which they in turn honour by saving her in her direst hour of need (001-016, 001-025, 001-067).
- [[the-sea]] — Lucilla's lifelong companion and stage. She dances with the waves as a girl on the downs (001-020), prays by the water's edge for her husband's safety (001-025), and is carried back to shore by the sea when the Windfairies save her from drowning (001-058).

## Significance

Lucilla embodies the fairy-tale virtue of faithfulness — not as passive endurance, but as active, chosen loyalty renewed at every test. Her story is a meditation on the binding power of a promise kept across years of silence, poverty, exile, and mortal danger. Unlike many fairy-tale heroines who are rescued by princes, Lucilla is rescued by the very supernatural beings to whom she has remained true. The Windfairies' warning in her childhood (001-016) functions as a prophecy the entire narrative fulfills with structural precision: because she keeps faith, they come to her help in her direst hour of need. Every act of persecution — drowning, burning, beheading — is answered by a reciprocal act of supernatural deliverance.

Lucilla's dancing is the outward sign of her inward condition. It is a gift from the supernatural world, marking her as different from all other mortals. Her dancing makes her the object of wonder and desire (the courtiers, the King), but also of envy and hatred (the Queen). It is both her vulnerability — it draws the attention that leads to her persecution — and her salvation — it is the bond that ties the Windfairies to her. When the Windfairies warn that if she breaks faith her feet will become heavy as lead (001-016), they identify dancing not merely as a skill but as a spiritual state contingent on fidelity.

Her arc from windmill child to persecuted prisoner to triumphant wife and mother, reunited with her family and carrying a sack of gold, makes her one of the most fully developed and psychologically rounded characters in Mary De Morgan's collection. Where other De Morgan heroines are defined by a single trait — vanity (Kesta), disobedience (Nanina), muteness (Othmar) — Lucilla is defined by the integrated whole of her choices: courage, loyalty, love, resilience, and faith.
