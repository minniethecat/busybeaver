# The Shepherd's Daughter's Wedding

The shepherd's daughter's wedding to the Prince is the climactic event of "The Rain Maiden" — a grand celebration that turns to catastrophe when the rain maiden herself claims the bride back to the elemental world, fulfilling the prophecy spoken years before.

## Context

The shepherd's daughter, who had been beloved by the rain maiden since she sheltered her as an infant, grew into a beauty who caught the Prince's eye. The King ordered great preparations for a wedding to take place immediately. Fine clothes were ordered, and all was made ready — but in the days before the ceremony, the rain fell as it had never been known to fall (008-022).

## The Event

On the evening before the wedding day, the shepherd's daughter knelt beside her mother and pleaded: "Dear mother, let me stop with you and nurse you when you are old. Do not send me away to the palace to live with the King's son" (008-023). But the marriage went forward.

All night the rain fell in torrents. The bride was dressed in her finery, stepping through pools on the ground to enter the grand carriage the King had sent. During the marriage service, the priest's voice could scarcely be heard for the pattering of drops upon the roof.

At the wedding banquet, the King's son sat with his young wife beside him, dressed in white and gold. All the courtiers declared that surely the world had never contained such a beautiful young woman. But just when the goblets were filled with wine to drink to the health of the bride and bridegroom, a cry went up: "The floods! the floods!" The servants ran into the hall, crying that the waters were pouring in. In one moment the rooms were filled with water, and no one thought of anything but to save themselves (008-024).

When the hurricane had subsided and the waters gone down, the Prince's wife was nowhere to be found. Everyone said she had been swept away by the torrents, drowned in all her youth and beauty. Only the shepherd's wife wept alone and remembered the words of the woman who came on the night of the storm: "When you love aught on earth better than your daughter and her happiness, she will go from you" (008-024).

## Outcome

The shepherd's daughter disappeared in the flood. The Prince mourned his wife and for long would not be comforted. Years later, when he married a beautiful Princess, he lived happily — but whenever the rain fell in torrents it would seem to him as if he heard the sound of dancing feet and a voice calling: "Come and dance with me, come and dance with me and my brothers and sisters, oh, King's son, and feel our drops upon your face" (008-025).

## Significance

This wedding is one of the collection's most haunting events — a supernatural claim exercised at the moment of highest human joy. The rain maiden's love, initially a blessing to the household, becomes possessive. The catastrophe fulfills the prophecy spoken at the daughter's birth: the rain maiden would take back what she loved. The wedding thus enacts the tale's central tension between mortal love and elemental attachment.
