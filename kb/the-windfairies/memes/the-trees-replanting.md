# The Replanting of the Rare Tree

The replanting of the rare tree in the city is the tragic turning point of "The Pool and the Tree" — the moment the two companion beings are physically separated, setting in motion the pool's quest to reunite with its lost friend.

## Context

Two botanical travellers had discovered a rare tree species growing beside a pool in the wilderness. Recognizing its uniqueness, they decided to collect the specimen. Men were summoned with spades to dig up the tree, severing its roots from the earth it had always known. The tree was loaded onto a cart and transported away from the pool, over hills and through valleys, to a distant city.

## The Event

The rare tree was replanted in the city — a foreign environment of stone and human bustle rather than quiet wilderness. The soil was different, the air was different, and most importantly, the pool was gone. The tree, accustomed to the pool's companionship and the water it provided, began to wither.

The tree's leaves yellowed and drooped. Its branches grew brittle. The city's gardeners watered it, but no water was the same as the pool's water. No soil was the same as the wilderness soil. The tree was dying of loneliness as much as transplant shock — a supernatural bond severed by human hands that saw only a botanical specimen, not a being with attachments.

## Participants

- **[[the-talking-tree]]** — the rare tree, forcibly separated from its companion pool, now dying in an alien environment
- **[[the-pool]]** — the tree's companion, left behind in the wilderness, soon to transform and begin its search
- **the-travellers** — the botanists whose discovery set the tragedy in motion, seeing a specimen where there was a being

## Outcome

The tree's withering in the city was the catalyst for the pool's transformation. Unable to bear the separation, the pool allowed the sun to draw it up into a cloud, giving up its fixed form to travel across the sky in search of its friend. The replanting thus initiated the tale's central quest — one being crossing the world in a new form to find the other.

## Significance

This event dramatizes the collection's recurring theme: the natural world has bonds and loyalties that humans fail to recognize. The travellers see a rare tree to be collected; they do not see the tree's relationship with the pool. The replanting is an act of ecological violence disguised as botanical science, and its consequence — the tree's withering and the pool's transformation — is nature's response to human objectification.
