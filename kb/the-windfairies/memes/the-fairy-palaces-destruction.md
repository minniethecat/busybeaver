After seven nights of burying her bleeding feet in the beech tree's roots to resist the fairy shepherd's enchanted pipe, Nanina recovers her last lost lamb — and the fairy palace, its power broken by her steadfastness, collapses into rubble with a thunderous crash. It is the story's climactic resolution: endurance against enchantment, and the quiet triumph of the natural world over the seductions of the supernatural.

## Context

For seven nights, Nanina had returned to the hollow in the hills where the fairy shepherd played his magical pipe. Each night, the music pulled at her with irresistible beauty, and each night, she buried her feet in the beech tree's roots to hold herself still. The pain was excruciating — the roots bit into her flesh like iron bands — but it was the only anchor strong enough to resist the enchantment [004-023–025].

The beech tree, ancient and wise, had counselled her from the first night. Its voice — deep as earth, slow as seasons — taught her that fairy magic could not be fought with force but only with endurance. "Hold fast," it would command when the music grew wilder and Nanina's will faltered. Each night of successful resistance restored one of her lost lambs. By the seventh night, six lambs had returned to her flock. Only one remained [004-023–026].

## The Seventh Night

On the seventh and final night, Nanina limped down to the beech tree. Her feet, wounded and bandaged, throbbed with every step. She was nearly crying with pain before the ordeal had even begun. But she knelt in the cold earth, worked her torn feet into the roots, and prepared for one last battle of will [004-025].

The fairy shepherd played as he always did. The black goats danced their ecstatic circles. The music poured over Nanina like honeyed water — and she held fast. The beech tree's voice was steady at her ear: "Hold fast!" [004-028].

> Presently the music grew quicker, and Nanina's longing to dance grew stronger; but the voice from the beech tree cried, "Hold fast!" — and she held fast. [004-028]

When the last lamb was restored to her flock — the seventh and final creature blinking back into existence as if it had never been gone — something in the air changed. The enchantment, fed for seven nights by Nanina's capitulation, had been starved for seven nights by her resistance. It could not hold.

## The Collapse

The fairy shepherd and his black goats began to dance ever more frantically. Their movements, once graceful and hypnotic, turned wild — now solemn and slow, now frenzied and desperate, as if the music itself had lost its coherence [004-028].

Then the fairy palace began to tremble. Cracks raced up its glowing walls. The coloured lights in its windows flickered and dimmed. With a sound like the breaking of the world — a thunderous crash that shook the hills — the entire palace collapsed into rubble. The hollow where its beauty had blazed for seven nights was filled with nothing but broken stone and settling dust [004-028].

The fairy shepherd, the black goats, the pipe music — all vanished in that moment, as if they had never been anything more than a dream from which Nanina had finally woken.

## The Farewell

As the dust settled and the silence stretched out across the hills, the voice from the beech tree spoke one last time:

> "Goodbye, Nanina," said the voice from the beech tree. [004-028]

It was not a triumphant declaration but a quiet benediction — the voice of the natural world acknowledging that its work was done. Beneath the tree, where Nanina's bleeding feet had been buried night after night, the earth bore the marks of her sacrifice: gouged soil, dark stains, the imprint of a girl who had chosen pain over enchantment and won [004-028–030].

- **[[the-beech-tree]]** — The ancient tree whose counsel and physical anchor enabled Nanina's victory. Its farewell is the story's emotional climax: a single "Goodbye" that carries the weight of ancient wisdom and the quiet pride of a teacher whose student has graduated [004-028]

## Outcome

The fairy palace was utterly destroyed and the enchantment broken. Nanina had all seven of her lambs back — restored not by magic but by her own steadfastness. The fairy shepherd and his black goats vanished from the world, never to return. The beech tree's farewell marked the end of Nanina's ordeal, and the magic that had tormented her for seven nights was gone forever [004-028–030].

## Significance

The destruction of the fairy palace is the climactic resolution of Nanina's story, and it embodies the tale's deepest moral logic. The palace is not destroyed by violence, a counterspell, or the intervention of a more powerful magical being. It collapses because Nanina refused to dance. Her steadfastness — night after night, in agony, against music that promised ecstasy — is what breaks the enchantment.

This is a remarkably unsentimental resolution for a fairy tale. The fairy shepherd is not defeated in battle or outwitted by cleverness; he is simply outlasted. The beech tree's wisdom — that fairy magic cannot be fought, only endured — proves to be the story's governing principle. Nanina's victory is not a flash of heroism but the slow accumulation of seven nights of choosing pain over pleasure, duty over desire, the real over the enchanted.

The beech tree's farewell — "Goodbye, Nanina" — is one of the most quietly moving moments in the collection. It is not a victory cry but a release: the ancient tree, having served its purpose, steps back and lets the mortal girl walk forward into her ordinary life, scarred but whole, with all seven of her lambs at her side.
