# The Pool's Journey

After the rare tree was uprooted and carried away by men, the little pool that had loved it embarked on an extraordinary journey — transformed by the sun into a cloud, carried by winds across the world, and ultimately sacrificing its sky-borne freedom to rejoin its beloved in the dark earth in "The Pool and the Tree" (Story 3).

## Context

The pool and the tree had lived together in the wilderness, loving each other above all else (003-001–004). When men dug up the rare tree and transported it to a distant garden, the pool was left exposed to the sky, mourning inconsolably. The swallow advised it to ask the moon for help, and the moon confirmed the tree was alive but fading in its new home (003-013–017). When the pool despaired that it could never move, a passing cloud offered the solution.

## The Transformation

A little cloud sailing nearby revealed that it had once been "in the earth like you" and promised that the sun could draw the pool up into the sky:

> "Tomorrow, if the sun shines brightly, he will draw you up into the sky, and you can sail along till you find your tree." (003-019)

The next day, the pool called out to the sun:

> "Draw me up into the sky, dear Sun, that I may be a little cloud and sail all the world over, till I can find my beloved tree." (003-020)

The sun responded by throwing down "hundreds of tiny golden threads which dropped over the pool," and gradually the pool grew "thinner and lighter" and rose through the air until it had become "a tiny cloud sailing above in the blue sky in the sunshine" (003-021).

## The Journey Across the World

As a cloud, the pool could see vast distances but could not find the tree. The sun told it that the tree was "away on the other side of the world" but that the wind would carry it there (003-022). The wind blew the little cloud for many hundred miles, past other clouds that danced and called to it:

> "Why do you not come and dance with us? Why do you sail on so rapidly?" (003-024)

> "I cannot stay, I am seeking a lost friend," answered the cloud. (003-025)

When the first wind grew tired, the west wind took over, telling the cloud it knew exactly where the tree was planted — in a great garden with railings around it, tended by a dedicated gardener, admired by strangers from far and near (003-030).

## The Reunion

The west wind warned the cloud that falling upon the tree would mean giving up its freedom — it would go "straight into the dark earth" and perhaps never rise to the sky again (003-032). The cloud replied:

> "Then that will be what I long for. For then I can lie in the dark where no one may see me, but I shall be close to my tree, and I can touch its roots and feed them." (003-033)

The wind blew the cloud low over the garden where the tree stood dying — its leaves falling, branches drooping. The cloud broke and fell in a shower, seeping down through the earth to the tree's roots:

> "Have you come at last? Then we need never be parted again," cried the tree. (003-038)

In the morning, the gardeners found the tree "looking quite fresh and well, and its leaves quite green and crisp," unaware that beneath the earth the pool now lay, hidden in darkness, saving the tree from within (003-039–040).

## Significance

The pool's journey is the emotional and symbolic heart of "The Pool and the Tree." It is a tale of sacrificial love — the pool gives up the freedom of the sky, the company of other clouds, and the visible beauty of the world above, choosing instead to dwell in darkness at the tree's roots. The journey also represents a cycle of transformation: from water to cloud to rain to underground water, the pool's identity changes but its devotion remains constant. The event parallels Nanina's painful resistance in Story 4 — both protagonists accept suffering as the price of restoration.
