# Adam's Proposal to Kesta

Adam, a farm worker at the large farm where Kesta worked as a dairymaid, proposed marriage to her, setting in motion the central chain of events in "Vain Kesta." This proposal, made in the humble setting of the cowshed, is the inciting incident of the tale — the honest offer of love that Kesta's vanity will ultimately betray.

## Context

At a large farm, a young girl named Kesta worked as the dairymaid — "milking the cows and making cheese and butter to go to market," carrying her cheeses into town every week to sell at the highest prices (002-001). It was "a big farm, so big that it was more like a village," with barns, stables, cowsheds, sheep-pens, and "plenty of fine horses" (002-001).

Adam was one of the farm workers. Each day he drove the cows in for Kesta to milk and "watched her as she worked, thinking her comely and diligent" (002-001). He became convinced she would make him a good wife.

## The Proposal

One day Adam spoke directly to Kesta. His proposal was practical and sincere:

> "How would you like to marry me? and then we can save our money and some day buy a farm for ourselves, and I should be a farmer and you should be the farmer's wife, and have servants to wait on you." (002-002)

The proposal was rooted in farm life — shared work, shared savings, a shared future. Adam offered Kesta not wealth or status but honest partnership and a plan: save enough to buy their own farm, rise from farmhand to farmer, and secure a comfortable life together.

Kesta responded that she "liked the idea very much" but could not answer at once — she was going to town the next day with her cheeses and would give him an answer upon her return (002-003).

## Kesta's Private Calculation

That night, alone with her mirror, Kesta revealed the vanity that would drive the entire story. Looking at her reflection, she reasoned:

> "I wonder why Adam wishes to marry me? but as he does, most likely some better man would like to do so; it would be folly to marry him till I see if I can't do better." (002-004)

This private calculation set the pattern for everything that followed: each man she met became a stepping-stone to the next, driven by her belief in her own exceptional beauty. She saw Adam not as a loving partner but as a baseline — proof that she was desirable, and therefore worthy of someone higher.

## Outcome

Kesta's deferral of Adam's proposal sent her to town, where she would successively approach the miller, the bailiff, the banker, the General, and ultimately the Duke himself — each time believing herself too good for the previous suitor. The honest farm boy who loved her became the first rung on a ladder of ambition that led nowhere but down.

## Significance

Adam's proposal is the inciting event of the tale. It establishes Kesta's ordinary station as a dairymaid, Adam's genuine affection for her, and most importantly, the fatal character flaw — vanity — that will lead her through an escalating series of rejections back to where she started. The moral architecture of the story is already visible in this first scene: Adam offers love and a future; Kesta offers only the calculation "perhaps a better man would like to do so." By the story's end, she will have lost even this first, honest chance.
