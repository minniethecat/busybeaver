## Context

After demonstrating her superior pottery skills, the gipsy girl had already humbled the proud potter (005-009–011). She had made jugs, pots, and jars with uncanny speed and skill, catching colour from the moonlight to decorate them — colours that, when baked the next day, produced "the most wonderful colours that he had ever seen: silver, blue, grey and yellow, in all sorts of patterns" (005-017). The potter stared in frustrated silence as she turned to her final creation (005-011).

## The Event

Declaring she would make "one last thing," the gipsy announced she would create "a cup that has a spell in it" as a parting gift (005-012). Unlike her previous colourful creations, this cup would be deliberately plain — "very plain, and there will be no gay colours in it" (005-012). The plainness itself was significant: the cup's power lay not in its appearance but in the magic sung into it.

She explained the cup's dual magic with precise warning. If the potter drank from it first and then gave it to his true love, the first draught would be drunk to love, winning her heart completely. But the second draught would be drunk to hate, transforming all love into hatred (005-012). She delivered the crucial caveat: "And as you are so ignorant how to make bowls and cups, you will not know how to fashion one so as to win back her love again" (005-012). The potter's mundane craft, however skilled, could never undo what magic had done.

The actual creation was an act of ritual magic. The gipsy took clay, placed it on the wheel, then:

> "Bent her head, which glittered with beads and coins, low over it, and placing her rosy lips close to the mouth of the cup, sang some words into it, while she moulded it with her hands, and turned the wheel with her foot." (005-013)

The words were in "some strange language that the potter had never heard before" (005-013) — a language of magic inaccessible to ordinary craft. The combination of physical moulding and sung spell made the cup unlike anything the potter could produce: it was a vessel of both clay and incantation.

## The Gipsy's Warning

She departed with a final, pointed warning: "only one draught, for if there are two maybe you will need the gipsy's help again" (005-014). Then she laughed, nodded her head over her shoulder, and tripped lightly away in the moonlight while the potter stared after her (005-014). Her laughter and easy departure contrast with the deadly seriousness of the gift she left behind — she has set a trap that the potter, in his ignorance of magical craft, is destined to spring.

## Participants

- [[the-potter]] — The stunned recipient of the magical cup, unable to comprehend what has been given to him (005-012–013)

## Outcome

The gipsy laughed, nodded her head, and vanished into the night, leaving the potter alone with the plain brown cup (005-014). At first the potter thought he had been asleep, but the rows of beautifully crafted jugs and pots surrounding him proved otherwise (005-015). The next day, when the pots were baked, the gipsy's creations emerged with wondrous colours — "all save the little brown cup" (005-017). Looking at the plain cup, the potter "felt a little uncomfortable, and began to wonder if it really did contain the charm as she had declared" (005-017). The enchanted cup would become the story's central magical object, determining the fate of the potter's marriage through its love-to-hate mechanism.

## Significance

This event is the pivotal moment of "The Gipsy's Cup" — the point where the supernatural enters the potter's mundane world. The gipsy's act of singing spells into clay transforms a simple vessel into a moral instrument. The cup's dual nature (love then hate) mirrors the story's warning about the fragility of magically-obtained affection. The gipsy's creation is both gift and trap: it can grant love but the potter lacks the knowledge to undo its secondary effect, making him dependent on forces beyond his understanding. The plainness of the cup — in contrast to the gipsy's other beautifully coloured wares — is a sophisticated narrative choice: the dangerous magic wears the humblest face.
