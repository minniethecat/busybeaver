## Overview

Gardens appear as significant settings in multiple tales of the collection, serving as places of encounter, transformation, and revelation. They appear in both humble cottage gardens and grand estate grounds.

## Appearances

### The Duke's Garden (Vain Kesta)

In "Vain Kesta," the Duke sits "by the stream in the garden, where he sits fishing all day" [002-029]. When Kesta flees the Duke's advances, she runs "into the garden where the miller was" [002-070], highlighting the garden as a space of both leisure and confrontation.

### The Beech Tree's Garden (The Pool and the Tree)

In "The Pool and the Tree," the magical beech tree is transplanted to a "big garden" where "it has not taken root in the earth" [003-017]. Later, it is planted "in a great garden, and there are railings round" [003-030], representing domestication and constraint of the wild. The story describes the tree finding itself "over a big garden, in which there were all sorts of flowers and fruits" [003-035], and how "in the morning when the gardeners came they found" [003-039] the transformed tree.

### The Gipsy's Garden (The Gipsy's Cup)

In "The Gipsy's Cup," the Gipsy's wife serves in the house while the Gipsy's mother "wandered round the garden, and in and out of the house" [005-080], suggesting a domestic garden setting.

### The Shepherd's Cottage Garden (The Rain Maiden)

In "The Rain Maiden," the shepherd's little girl "had gone into the little garden in front of the cottage" [008-010] to pick flowers, a scene of pastoral innocence before the magical encounter.

## Significance

- Gardens serve as **transitional spaces** between the domestic and the wild
- The beech tree's garden represents **domestication of magic** — a wild spirit confined within cultivated grounds
- Cottage gardens represent **pastoral simplicity** and innocence
