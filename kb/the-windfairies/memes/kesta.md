# Kesta

Kesta is the protagonist of "[[vain-kesta|Vain Kesta]]" (Chapter 2), a young dairymaid at a large farm near the town of Ploetzin. Remarkable for her beauty and vanity, she is one of the collection's most memorable characters — not for her virtue, but for her flaws. Her story is a cautionary tale about the dangers of excessive pride in one's appearance and the social climbing that [[vanity]] enables (002-004).

## Role in the Story

Kesta is the central character of "Vain Kesta" (002-001 to 002-083). She works as a dairymaid at a large farm, where she milks the cows, makes cheese and butter, and sometimes takes the produce to town to sell for her master (002-001). A young farm labourer named [[adam|Adam]] courts her and proposes marriage, offering a future of shared work and eventual farm ownership (002-002). But Kesta's nightly ritual of admiring herself in the mirror — asking "Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?" — feeds a conviction that she deserves better than a simple farm life (002-004).

Kesta defers her answer to Adam and sets out for town the next morning dressed in her best clothes, carrying cheeses in a basket under her arm (002-005). What follows is an escalating campaign of social ambition: at each stop on her journey, she encounters a progressively more powerful man, flatters him with a fabricated story about being pursued by lesser suitors because she is "so pretty," extracts a marriage proposal, and then defers her answer while setting her sights higher still (002-007, 002-010). Her pursuit carries her all the way to [[the-duke]] himself — and when her true identity as a cheesemaking dairymaid is exposed, her house of deceptions collapses in a cascade of public humiliation (002-047, 002-048).

### The Social Climbing Campaign

Kesta's journey from the farm to the Duke's palace unfolds as a deliberate campaign of social ascent. She first stops at a mill, where [[the-miller]] — an older widower standing in his yard covered in flour — offers her rest (002-005). Kesta tells him she has had to run from the farmer's man who wanted to marry her, adding "I am too good for him, a pretty girl like me" (002-007). The miller, intrigued, proposes marriage; Kesta says she will answer on her way back, then walks on "feeling very merry" (002-009).

Near the town, she encounters [[the-bailiff]] on a white horse. She sits by the roadside lamenting "what a thing it is to be a poor girl who has to run away from all the men she meets" (002-010). The bailiff, who hates the miller, is amused at the prospect of taking the woman the miller wanted, and offers marriage too (002-013). Kesta defers and proceeds to the high street, where [[the-banker]] — a fat, ugly man whose hands are covered with gold rings — stands in his doorway. She sighs loudly: "Alack a day, how hard is my lot!" (002-015). She tells him that "all the men want to marry me: first it is the farmer, then the miller, and lastly the duke's bailiff" (002-019). The banker, impressed by her supposed desirability, offers marriage and the prospect of fine carriages and silk gowns (002-020).

Kesta next reaches the barracks square, where soldiers drill with helmets and swords glittering in the sun, and [[the-general]] rides at their head swearing dreadfully in a hoarse voice (002-021). She walks so close under his horse that he shouts a warning, and she cries out "Alas! what a life is mine, hunted here and there" (002-021). When the General protests that he does not think her so pretty, she retorts angrily: "But I am... it's only stupid people who don't see it. Go and ask the men in the town" (002-027). He too offers marriage after hearing her list of previous suitors (002-028).

The campaign culminates at [[the-dukes-palace]], where [[the-duke]] sits half-asleep beside a stream in his garden, dressed in green and holding a fishing rod (002-029). Kesta approaches and appeals for protection from pursuing suitors, reciting her full list: "first the man at the farm, then the miller I met on the road, then your bailiff, then the banker, then the General of your army" (002-033). The Duke, though skeptical that she is truly that pretty, agrees to marry her on condition she has told the [[truth-telling|truth]], and summons his four pages in blue and gold to prepare a wedding feast (002-036, 002-038). But as Kesta sits beside him watching him fish, her basket falls open and the cheeses roll out. The Duke screams at the discovery that she is "a common dairymaid" with rough hands, and orders his pages to hunt her away (002-045, 002-046, 002-047). She flees back through the town pursued by pages, soldiers, clerks, and farmhands, each of her former suitors disowning her in turn as the crowd swells (002-048 to 002-074).

## Character Traits

- **Vain** — Every night Kesta gazes at herself in her mirror and admires her own [[beauty]], asking "Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?" This vanity is her defining flaw and the engine of the entire plot (002-004)
- **Calculating** — Rather than accepting Adam's sincere proposal, she reasons: "most likely some better man would like to do so; it would be folly to marry him till I see if I can't do better" (002-004). She treats marriage not as an emotional bond but as a market transaction to be optimized
- **Hesitant but strategic** — When Adam proposes, she says she must think it over first (002-003). This pattern repeats with every subsequent suitor: she always says she will "give an answer on the way back," using deferral as a strategy to keep options open
- **Easily frightened** — When the crowd pursues her through the town after her exposure, she flees in genuine terror, sobbing: "It's too hard; what have I done to be treated like this?" (002-067)
- **Capable of self-deception** — Kesta genuinely believes in her own exceptional beauty, telling the General: "But I am... it's only stupid people who don't see it" (002-027). Her vanity is not purely performative — she has internalized her own myth
- **Capable of regret** — By the story's end, her experiences have stripped away her self-absorption. But unlike a conventional redemption arc, her transformation comes too late to salvage any prospect: she is left sitting alone, crying, having lost everything (002-083)

## Key Actions

1. **Courtship by Adam** — Adam, a farm labourer who drives in the cows for her to milk, proposes marriage to Kesta during her work. She defers her answer, saying she needs to think it over overnight (002-001, 002-002, 002-003).

2. **The mirror ritual** — Each night Kesta looks into her mirror and admires her own beauty with self-satisfaction. She reasons that if Adam wants her, "most likely some better man would like to do so" (002-004). This conviction drives her to seek a grander match beyond the farm.

3. **The journey to town** — Dressed in her best clothes with a basket of cheeses under her arm, Kesta heads to town, passing the mill on the way. She deliberately seeks out encounters with progressively more powerful men (002-005).

4. **The chain of proposals** — Kesta extracts marriage proposals from [[the-miller]] (002-008), [[the-bailiff]] (002-013), [[the-banker]] (002-020), [[the-general]] (002-028), and [[the-duke]] (002-036), each time deferring her answer while eyeing a higher target. She justifies each new attempt with the thought that she might "do better" (002-010).

5. **The audience with the Duke** — At [[the-dukes-palace]], Kesta tells the Duke her fabricated story of being pursued by all the men of the town (002-033). The Duke, swayed by her claims, instructs his pages in blue and gold to prepare a wedding feast and summons the chaplain (002-038).

6. **The discovery and humiliation** — While sitting beside the Duke at the fishing stream, Kesta's basket falls open and the cheeses roll out. The Duke realizes she is a common dairymaid with rough hands, flies into a towering passion, blows his horn, and orders her hunted away (002-045, 002-046, 002-047). She is then pursued through the town by pages, soldiers, clerks, and farmhands, each former suitor disowning her in turn (002-048 to 002-074).

7. **Return to Adam and final rejection** — Fleeing back to the farm, Kesta consoles herself: "anyhow I can go back to Adam; he's sure to be glad to have me" (002-075). Adam initially welcomes her with a kiss (002-076), but after the pursuing crowd reveals what she has done, he gravely refuses to marry her: "Indeed, Kesta, I cannot marry you now, since you've been to the town and tried to get a finer husband than me" (002-083).

## Relationships

- [[adam|Adam]] — The farm labourer who loves Kesta faithfully and drives in the cows for her to milk. His patient, steady devotion is the moral counterweight to Kesta's vanity. He welcomes her back with a kiss when she returns, but ultimately rejects her after learning of her social climbing (002-002, 002-076, 002-083)
- [[the-miller]] — The first suitor Kesta encounters on her journey. An older widower covered in flour, he is intrigued by her claim of being too good for Adam. When he discovers her deception, he orders his men to throw flour at her (002-005, 002-008, 002-074)
- [[the-bailiff]] — The Duke's bailiff, whom Kesta meets on the road near town. He hates the miller and is amused at the thought of marrying the woman the miller wanted. He later calls her behavior "impertinence" and joins the pursuit (002-010, 002-013, 002-070)
- [[the-banker]] — A wealthy man whose hands are covered with gold rings. Kesta calculates that marrying him would let her "hold up my head with anyone" (002-015). He later rages that she is "not good enough for me" and sends his clerks to chase her (002-066)
- [[the-general]] — Commander of the army, encountered during a drill at [[the-barracks]]. He is the first to openly doubt Kesta's beauty, telling her "I don't think you are so pretty" (002-026), but is won over by her list of previous suitors. He later declares her pursuit "disgraceful" and dispatches soldiers to drive her from town (002-054)
- [[the-duke]] — The highest target of Kesta's ambition. He sits fishing in green at his palace garden and nearly marries Kesta before the cheeses expose her. His rejection triggers her public humiliation, as he orders his pages to "hunt her away" (002-029, 002-036, 002-047)
- [[the-farm-at-ploetzin]] — The large farm where Kesta works and lives as dairymaid, and to which she finally returns in defeat

## Significance

Kesta represents the moral danger of [[vanity]] — not as passive self-love, but as active social climbing that distorts judgment and destroys relationships (002-004). Unlike fairy-tale protagonists whose [[beauty]] is a gift humbly borne, Kesta weaponizes her appearance as a tool for advancement, telling each suitor a carefully crafted story about being pursued by previous men (002-007, 002-019). Her tale is a pointed study in [[social-class]] transgression: a dairymaid who dares to aim above her station is ruthlessly punished by the collective mockery of an entire town (002-063).

The moral of Kesta's story is not simply that vanity is wrong, but that the vain person is self-defeating. Her schemes collapse under the weight of her own deceptions — each new suitor is won by reference to the previous ones, so when the chain unravels at the Duke's palace, every link breaks simultaneously. Her [[cleverness]] in playing suitors against one another proves no match for the truth of her station. She ends up with nothing, sitting alone and crying at the farm, having lost the one man — Adam — who truly loved her, "because she was so false and vain" (002-083).

Kesta's story also explores the theme of [[truth-telling]]: her entire social ascent is built on half-truths about her desirability, and her downfall comes the moment her true identity is exposed (002-047). In this sense, the tale functions as both a moral fable about honesty and a dark comedy of social ambition. The punishment fits the crime: Kesta, who tried to climb above everyone, ends up beneath everyone — chased through the streets by the very society she sought to conquer.
