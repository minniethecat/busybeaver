On the second day of grazing her seven lambs, drawn by curiosity, Nanina peers into a hollow in the hills and discovers a glowing fairy palace. A beautiful fairy shepherd boy emerges playing a magical pipe whose music compels all who hear it to dance — and Nanina, unable to resist, dances the night away, losing her first lamb to the enchantment. This event establishes the central pattern of the story: each night's dance costs her a lamb, and each loss drives her closer to confronting the fairy's power.

## Context

Nanina was a shepherdess entrusted with seven lambs to graze on the hills. The first day passed without incident — she led them out at dawn, watched over them through the long hours, and brought them safely home at dusk [004-001].

On the second day, however, curiosity stirred. Nanina had glimpsed strange lights flickering in a hollow among the hills — lights that seemed to pulse with an unearthly rhythm, appearing only after dark. Though a voice within her warned against it, she resolved to look more closely [004-002–004].

## The Discovery

As evening fell, Nanina approached the hollow and peered down into it. What she saw stopped her breath: a fairy palace blazed with light, its windows casting coloured beams across the darkness. Music drifted up from within — a melody so beautiful it seemed to pull at her very bones [004-006–008].

From the palace emerged a handsome fairy shepherd boy, a pipe raised to his lips. Behind him came a flock of black goats that moved not like animals but like dancers — lifting their hooves in time to the music, turning and leaping in perfect synchronisation with the melody [004-008–010].

> Nanina gaped to see the goats dance and to hear the wonderful music of the shepherd's pipe. [004-012]

## The Enchanted Dance

The fairy shepherd turned his gaze upon Nanina and began to play directly to her. The music wrapped around her like a net of silver thread, and though some part of her knew she should flee, her body no longer answered to her will [004-012].

The shepherd took her hand, and Nanina found herself dancing. She danced with a lightness and grace she had never known — as if the music had unlocked something in her limbs that had always been waiting to be freed. Around them, the black goats circled in their own ecstatic dance, and the fairy palace blazed brighter than ever [004-012–014].

What made the enchantment so dangerous was not its violence but its beauty. Nanina did not dance in terror or pain — she danced in joy. The music promised a world more vivid and alive than anything her ordinary life as a shepherdess could offer. Every note said: stay, dance, forget your sheep, forget the dawn, forget everything but this moment [004-013–014].

Hour after hour passed uncounted. Nanina's own sheep, forgotten, wandered untended on the dark hillside.

## The Vanishing

Just before sunrise, the music stopped. The fairy shepherd dropped Nanina's hand as abruptly as he had taken it. He turned without a word, and he, the black goats, and the fairy palace vanished from sight — leaving Nanina alone in the grey pre-dawn light, standing in the hollow where moments before there had been a palace of light [004-016].

> Just ere sunrise, the shepherd stopped, and dropped her hand, and he and his goats vanished from sight. [004-016]

The silence that followed was deafening. Nanina's ears rang with the absence of the music, and her limbs ached with the memory of dancing. She staggered back to where she had left her sheep — and discovered that one of her seven lambs was gone [004-016].

## Participants

- **[[the-fairy-shepherd]]** — A beautiful fairy boy with a magical pipe whose music compels anyone who hears it to dance. He is not portrayed as malevolent — he simply plays, and the music does the rest. His abrupt vanishing at dawn underscores the fairy world's indifference to mortal consequences [004-008–012]

## Outcome

Nanina lost her first lamb to the fairy's enchantment. The full horror of her situation dawned on her gradually: she would need to return to the hills each day with her remaining lambs, and the fairy music would call to her each night. If she could not find a way to resist, she would lose all seven lambs, one by one — and with them, her livelihood, her trust, and perhaps her very self [004-016–017].

## Significance

This event establishes the pattern that drives the entire story. The fairy's enchantment is not a single crisis to be overcome but a recurring ordeal that must be endured night after night. Each dance costs Nanina a lamb; each loss deepens her desperation; each failure forces her to seek new strategies of resistance.

The episode also establishes the story's central paradox: the fairy world is not evil but indifferent. The shepherd boy does not steal the lambs — they simply vanish as a consequence of Nanina's distraction. The music is not a weapon but a gift — a gift so beautiful that accepting it destroys everything else in her life. This is a more sophisticated moral structure than simple good-versus-evil: Nanina's enemy is not malice but enchantment itself, and her struggle is not to defeat the fairy but to resist the part of herself that wants to dance.

The physical details of the scene — the pulsing lights, the dancing goats, the pipe's irresistible melody — create a sensory richness that makes the fairy world feel tangibly real. This is crucial to the story's effect: the reader, like Nanina, is seduced by the beauty of the fairy realm even while understanding its danger.
