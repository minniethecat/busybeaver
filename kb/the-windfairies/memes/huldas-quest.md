Hulda's Quest is the central redemptive journey in "Dumb Othmar" (Story 7). After the dwarf steals Othmar's singing voice at the Midnight Concert, Hulda — the young woman who loves him — leaves the village alone to find the dwarf and compel him to restore what was taken. Though she was "neither pretty nor clever," she was known throughout the village as a good girl who helped anyone in trouble [007-004].

## Context

Othmar had been the village's finest singer, but his voice was stolen when the dwarf's musical instruments transformed into singing beings at midnight [007-013][007-023]. After the dwarf departed with a mocking farewell, Othmar was left mute and desolate — he "flung himself on the ground, and began to sob, but his sobs gave no sound, though the ground was wet with his tears" [007-031][007-035]. While some villagers thought Othmar had simply caught cold or been frightened, Hulda alone recognized the truth and "said to herself, 'it was the wicked little man'" [007-034]. She resolved not to let him suffer alone, though Othmar, in his despair, told her she would soon forget him [007-036].

## The Event

### Departure

Determined to help Othmar, Hulda made the difficult decision to leave the village and search for the dwarf. "I must seek out some wise man who knows about fairies and dwarfs and such things," she declared, "and ask him what I am to do to get back Othmar's voice" [007-042][007-058].

### Seeking Counsel

Her journey took her through a series of encounters, each offering a small piece of guidance:

**The Shepherd**: Hulda first approached a shepherd tending his flock, asking if he knew of a very wise man who could answer her questions. The shepherd directed her to a man living in "a little cottage with a red gate" who had cured his sheep two years back when the flock was sick [007-042][007-043].

**The Schoolmaster**: Following another lead, Hulda climbed to the schoolhouse on the hill, believing a learned man might help. But the school proved empty — school was out — and no one there could assist her [007-046][007-048].

**The Shopkeeper**: Continuing up the hill, Hulda found a small group of cottages with a shop. She bought a cake and, as she ate, timidly asked the shopkeeper if he knew many people in the neighborhood [007-051]. When she asked who was the silliest person around — reasoning that a fool might be more open to fairy matters — the shopkeeper directed her to a well-known village idiot [007-053][007-055].

**The Wise Man (the Fool)**: Surprisingly, it was the village fool who provided the crucial direction. Hulda came up close and "watched him, as he ran round smiling and giggling," and asked her question [007-057]. The fool pointed to a raven that had been following her and cried out, "The raven, the raven, follow the raven!" [007-058]. Though Hulda felt "inclined to burst into tears with disappointment," she ran after the bird, murmuring to herself, "It was what the fool said, 'Follow the raven'" [007-059][007-061].

### The Raven's Guidance

The raven proved to be a supernatural helper. When the fool turned back, the raven slackened its pace, cawed, and lighted on a tree, waiting for Hulda [007-059]. Though Hulda could not understand the raven's speech — it croaked, "Poor Hulda, come with me, and I will show you where the dwarf is" — she followed it faithfully [007-060].

They traveled until evening, through unfamiliar places and strange paths "where Hulda had never been" [007-061]. She was "footsore and weary, but she would not give up," trusting the fool's instruction despite her doubts [007-061].

### The Discovery

Just before dawn, Hulda and the raven emerged onto a broad plain "where there were neither houses nor trees," with distant mountains visible on the horizon [007-062]. The raven flew ahead and lit upon a scrubby bush near a sleeping figure — the dwarf in his yellow cap, lying beside his donkey and instruments [007-062][007-063].

Hulda crouched behind the bush and witnessed the same supernatural event that Othmar had seen: the instruments transformed one by one into singing beings [007-063][007-064]. She watched as they danced and sang, and "she too felt as if she had never heard such music or seen such beauty" [007-064].

Through a hole in the ground, Hulda also observed a long, lithe, green serpent — an earth-spirit connected to the dwarf's magic [007-065]. The dwarf then summoned his daughters, and the youngest and fairest came forward to present a song assembled from "all the great sounds that are of the land" [007-066]. This song carried Othmar's stolen voice within it.

## Outcome

Following the raven's guidance, Hulda finds the sleeping dwarf and witnesses the instruments' nightly transformation into singing beings. Through her courage and patient watching, she observes the dwarf's court and learns the nature of the magic that holds Othmar's voice. When the dwarf wakes and speaks — his voice "sounded like nothing earthly" — Hulda trembles but holds her ground [007-068]. Ultimately, she is able to reclaim Othmar's voice. When she returns to the village, Othmar's voice is restored, and the village rejoices [007-106][007-107]. Her journey transforms her from a humble village girl into a heroic figure whose love conquers supernatural malevolence.

## Significance

Hulda's Quest is the structural heart of "Dumb Othmar." While Othmar represents passive suffering — the victim of supernatural theft — Hulda embodies active love. Her willingness to leave the safety of the village and venture into the unknown establishes her as the story's true protagonist. Significantly, Hulda is not presented as especially gifted — the text explicitly notes she was "neither pretty nor clever" — yet she succeeds through persistence, faith in others' counsel (even a fool's), and an unwavering refusal to abandon the man she loves [007-004].

The quest follows the classic fairy-tale pattern of a journey to restore what was lost, with a notable subversion: the "wise man" Hulda seeks turns out to be a village fool, and the fool's advice — to follow a bird — initially seems absurd. This paradox contains the story's deeper wisdom: that humility and trust in the seemingly foolish may unlock paths hidden to the conventionally wise.
