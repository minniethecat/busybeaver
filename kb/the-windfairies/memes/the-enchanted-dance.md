# The Enchanted Dance

The enchanted dance is the collection's most recurrent supernatural event — appearing across "The Windfairies," "Nanina's Sheep," and echoed in "Dumb Othmar." It is the primary mode of contact between mortals and fairies: the windfairies teach Lucilla to dance (001-010), the fairy shepherd compels Nanina's flock to dance (004-012), and the dwarf's music forces supernatural dancing upon Othmar's village. Dance in these tales is never merely performance — it is enchantment made visible.

## Context

The fairy tales inhabit a world where dance is the native language of supernatural beings. The windfairies themselves are perpetually dancing — Lucilla first sees them "dancing and springing from the ground as lightly as thistledown" (001-001). When they offer to teach her, dance becomes the medium of initiation into their world: "Teach me to dance, I beg. I will never tell anyone where I learnt it" (001-011). The condition imposed — never reveal the source of the gift — establishes dance as a secret covenant between mortal and fairy. In "Nanina's Sheep," the context is different: the fairy shepherd's pipe compels dance as a form of trial, testing Nanina's obedience against her desire for the music's beauty.

## The Event

### The windfairies' gift

Lucilla's enchanted dance begins with the windfairies' offer: "Should you like us to teach you how to dance as we dance?" (001-010). She accepts, and they teach her through the night — "she would gladly have danced for hours" (001-018) — but with the strict condition: if she reveals who taught her, "you will never dance again" (001-016). The gift transforms her: "when she ran or danced it seemed as if she were as light as the wind" (001-020). This dance later becomes both her salvation (it brings her to the King's court) and her trial (she must dance while the palace burns to prove her gift is genuine, 001-060).

### Nanina's compulsion

In "Nanina's Sheep," the enchanted dance is not a gift but a coercion. The fairy shepherd's pipe makes "the black goats and sheep all skipping merrily" (004-012). Nanina herself cannot resist: "when I hear the music I shall begin to dance" (004-023). Her struggle against this compulsion — refusing to dance with the fairy even as the music grows ever more irresistible — is the tale's central moral action. The pipe's tone shifts with her resistance: "it was not merry dance-music it made, but long sad notes" (004-025), and when the music finally accelerates, "the music grew quicker, and her longing to dance grew stronger" (004-028).

### The courtly transformation

Lucilla's dance at the King's court transforms the enchanted gift into public art. "The musicians began to play, and then Lucilla danced" (001-034). She dances "as light as the sea foam" (001-034), and the court erupts in "a noise of applauding and cheering... as had never been heard before" (001-035). This is the enchanted dance at its most triumphant — the fairy gift operating in the mortal world as transcendent beauty.

## Outcome

The enchanted dance produces different outcomes depending on the moral stance of the dancer. For Lucilla, who keeps faith with the windfairies' condition, the dance leads to reward: marriage, wealth, and eventual return home (001-069). For those who are merely compelled — Nanina's flock, Othmar's fellow villagers — the dance is a temporary loss of self that passes when the music stops. The enchanted dance is thus morally neutral in itself: it amplifies the existing character of the dancer, rewarding fidelity and exposing weakness.

## Significance

The enchanted dance represents the collection's central metaphor for the human encounter with the supernatural. Dance is involuntary when the fairy music plays — it bypasses rational choice and works directly on the body. What matters is not whether one dances but how one responds to the compulsion. Lucilla embraces the gift and keeps the secret; Nanina resists the compulsion and saves her flock. The dance is thus a form of moral testing through aesthetic experience — the beauty of the fairy realm is real, but surrender to it without discernment is dangerous.
