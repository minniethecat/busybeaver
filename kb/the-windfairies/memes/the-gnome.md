# Nanina

Nanina is the protagonist of "[[naninas-sheep|Nanina's Sheep]]" (Chapter 4), a young shepherdess who keeps a flock of six sheep and eight lambs for an old farmer. When her curiosity leads her to the forbidden fairy palace and her flock is stolen by the enchantment of a [[the-fairy-shepherd|beautiful shepherd boy]] with a magical pipe, she must endure seven nights of bodily torment — burying her feet in the earth among the roots of a [[the-talking-tree|talking beech tree]] — to resist the compulsion to dance and win back her sheep one by one. Her story is a tale of disobedience, costly redemption, and the extraordinary power of perseverance and self-sacrifice.

## Role in the Story

Nanina is the central figure of "Nanina's Sheep" (004-001). She embodies the tale's core tension between mortal responsibility and fairy enchantment. Unlike many fairy-tale protagonists who are passive victims of magic, Nanina's plight is largely self-inflicted: her master explicitly warns her not to go near the old palace on the other side of [[the-hills|the hill]], for it is filled with wicked fairies (004-001). She disobeys out of curiosity, reasoning that it will not hurt her just to look (004-003). This act of disobedience sets the entire plot in motion and transforms what might have been a simple cautionary tale into a complex narrative of redemption earned through suffering.

### The Arc of Disobedience and Redemption

Nanina's story unfolds in three distinct movements. First, there is the fall: drawn by curiosity and the seductive beauty of the fairy shepherd's enchanted pipe, she dances night after night, losing one lamb each morning until her entire flock is gone (004-016). Second, there is the counsel: a [[the-beech-tree|beech tree]] whose branches she tends speaks to her, offering a way to recover her sheep — but only if she can resist the pipe's compulsion (004-018). Third, there is the ordeal: for seven nights, she buries her feet in the earth among the tree's roots, allowing them to hold her immobile while the fairy dances before her in ever more splendid attire, the music pulling at her very bones (004-023).

What distinguishes Nanina's arc from a simple morality tale is the physical cost of her redemption. She does not merely apologize or promise to do better; she pays for each lamb with her own flesh. By the ordeal's end, her feet are so crushed and bleeding that she must crawl home on her hands and knees (004-028). The scarlet flowers that later spring up where her blood fell (004-030) serve as a permanent memorial to the price of her restoration.

## Character Traits

Nanina's character is revealed not through narration but through her choices under escalating pressure. Each trait emerges more sharply as the ordeal intensifies.

### Curiosity and Willfulness

Nanina is, from the outset, driven by a lively curiosity that overrides prudence. Her master's warning about the fairy palace only sharpens her interest: "If it is filled with fairies, it won't hurt me just to look at it; I should like to see a fairy" (004-003). This willfulness is not portrayed as wickedness but as the natural restlessness of youth — she finds it "dull sitting on the hillside" and wonders why she must always keep to one side of the hill (004-002). Her disobedience is born of boredom and wonder, not malice.

### Perseverance and Determination

Once the crisis is upon her — all her sheep lost, her master's return imminent — Nanina does not collapse into despair. She wanders all day searching, and when she finds the talking tree, she listens and acts (004-018). Her response to the tree's warning that the cure will hurt is immediate and absolute: "Hurt me as you please, and I shan't mind. If only I can get back my sheep I will bear any pain" (004-023). This is the turning point of her character — the moment willfulness transforms into determination, and passive curiosity becomes active sacrifice.

### Compassion and Gratitude

Even in her desperation, Nanina notices the broken branches of the beech tree and takes the time to tie them up so they might grow together again (004-018). This small act of kindness — performed when she is at her lowest, weeping and exhausted — is what earns her the tree's help. Later, after the ordeal is over, she kneels and smooths over the torn earth among the tree's roots, puts her arms around its trunk, and kisses and thanks it (004-028). Her compassion is as much a part of her character as her perseverance.

### Honesty Within Limits

When the old farmer returns and asks how she came by such sore feet, Nanina tells him it was "in saving the lambs when they got into dangerous places" (004-029). This is not a lie — she did save the lambs, and they were in a dangerously enchanted situation — but it is a careful framing of the truth that spares her master the knowledge of her disobedience. It suggests a practical wisdom: having paid the price and learned her lesson, she sees no need to burden him with the full supernatural truth.

## Key Actions

### The Disobedience

Nanina's master departs for a month-long journey to buy pigs, leaving her with explicit instructions: keep the six sheep and eight lambs safe, and above all, do not go near the old palace on the other side of the hill (004-001). On the second day, bored and curious, she deliberately drives her flock across the forbidden boundary to look at the half-ruined palace (004-003). She waits until sunset and sees bright lights in the palace windows (004-004). This single act of disobedience is the story's inciting incident.

### The Enchanted Dances

When the fairy shepherd emerges from the palace — a beautiful boy in glittering green with a soft brown hat trimmed with leaves, carrying a crook and a pipe — Nanina is entranced (004-004). He begins to play, and the music itself seems to speak words, calling all who hear it to dance (004-005). The black goats behind him skip and spring in perfect time, and Nanina feels her own feet beginning to move of their own accord. When the shepherd offers his hand, she takes it, reasoning that if her flock follows, there can be no harm (004-012).

The dance lasts the entire night, though to Nanina it feels like ten minutes. At sunrise, the shepherd plays one long note, and he and the goats vanish into the palace. Nanina counts her sheep: one lamb is missing (004-016). Despite her resolve each morning, the pattern repeats night after night. She cannot resist the pipe's music. Each dawn, another lamb is gone. After a fortnight, every one of the fourteen animals has vanished (004-017).

### The Counsel of the Beech Tree

Desperate and exhausted, Nanina sits beneath a beech tree near the palace, sobbing. She notices that someone has torn down the lowest branches, and despite her own misery, she raises them and ties them up (004-018). A shadowy voice whispers from the tree: "Thank you, Nanina; Nanina, don't dance" (004-018). The tree — a [[the-talking-tree|talking beech]] with a small twisted face among its leaves — tells her that if she refuses to dance with the fairy, her sheep will be returned one by one (004-020).

When Nanina protests that she cannot resist — "as I hear the pipe beginning, my feet begin to move of themselves" (004-021) — the tree offers a terrible solution: bury her feet deep in the earth among its roots, and the roots will hold her immobile. But it will hurt, and she may walk lame afterwards (004-022).

### The Seven-Night Ordeal

Nanina accepts without hesitation. She kneels, digs a deep hole among the roots, and places her feet in the loose earth. Something moves beneath, tightening around her ankles and drawing her feet deep into the ground, holding them like cords (004-023). When the fairy shepherd emerges and begins to play, she feels her feet straining to move beneath the earth, but the roots hold fast. She nearly screams with the pain. At dawn, the shepherd vanishes — and beside her is one of her own lambs.

She returns night after night. Her feet grow more swollen, more bruised, more crushed with each session. By the final nights she can scarcely hobble to the tree, and her feet are bleeding where the roots grip them (004-024). On the last night, the tree's voice tells her: "Never mind, Nanina; tonight is the last, and though it will hurt you the most, it will soon be past" (004-025).

The final night is the worst. The shepherd appears more beautiful than ever, crowned with jewels and dressed in scarlet and [[gold-wealth|gold]]. But the pipe no longer plays merry dance music — it plays long, sad notes like a funeral march, and the music seems to sing: "Join us, Nanina, dance again, one last dance will ease your pain" (004-026). Nanina sways and screams, her longing to dance almost overwhelming. Just when she feels she can bear no more, a single long low note sounds, and with a crash like thunder, the shepherd, the goats, and the entire palace vanish — the walls collapse into a heap of stones (004-028). The last sheep lies beside her on the grass. The enchantment is broken.

### The Aftermath

Nanina's feet are so wounded that she must crawl home on her hands and knees (004-028). When the farmer returns the next day, he is pleased to find his flock safe, though he wonders at her lameness. Nanina tells him she injured her feet saving the lambs from dangerous places (004-029). Beneath the beech tree, where her blood had fallen among the earth, scarlet flowers spring up — a living memorial to her sacrifice (004-030).

## Relationships

- [[the-fairy-shepherd]] — The beautiful supernatural piper whose enchanted music compels Nanina to dance and costs her the flock. He is not portrayed as evil but as dangerously indifferent, a force of enchantment that Nanina must learn to resist (004-004).
- [[the-old-farmer]] — Nanina's elderly master who entrusts her with the flock and warns her about the fairy palace. His trust and her failure to honour it form the moral stakes of the story (004-001).
- [[the-beech-tree]] — The wise, sentient tree that counsels Nanina and physically restrains her during the ordeal. Its roots hold her feet immobile, enabling her to resist the pipe's compulsion (004-018).
- [[naninas-flock]] — The six sheep and eight lambs entrusted to Nanina's care. They are both her responsibility and the story's central stakes: each lamb recovered represents a night of pain endured (004-016).
- [[the-black-goats]] — The fairy shepherd's supernatural flock, jet black with silky curls, that dance alongside the pipe. They serve as a dark mirror to Nanina's mortal sheep (004-004).
- [[the-magic-pipe]] — The enchanted instrument whose music compels all who hear it to dance. Its power is the central supernatural force Nanina must overcome (004-005).

## Significance

Nanina represents a distinctive kind of fairy-tale heroine: one whose heroism lies not in cleverness or magical aid but in sheer, painful endurance. She is not rescued; she rescues herself through an act of sustained physical sacrifice. Every lamb she recovers costs her a piece of her own body, and she pays willingly.

Thematically, Nanina embodies the virtue of [[keeping-faith]] — not in a grand romantic sense, but in the humble, unglamorous duty of a shepherdess to her flock. The story insists that responsibility to the ordinary is as sacred as any fairy oath. Her disobedience is real and consequential, but the narrative does not condemn her for it; instead, it shows that mistakes can be redeemed, provided one is willing to pay the full price.

The scarlet flowers that bloom where she bled (004-030) elevate her suffering to the symbolic. In the logic of the tale, pain borne bravely does not merely pass — it transforms the world, leaving behind something beautiful and enduring. Nanina walks lame for a long time afterward, but she has learned her lesson: "she remembered how she had been punished for disobeying her master, and made up her mind never to do so again" (004-030).
