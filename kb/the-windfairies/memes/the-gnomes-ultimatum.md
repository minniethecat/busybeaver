# The Gnome's Ultimatum

The gnome's ultimatum to the ploughman is the first open confrontation in "The Ploughman and the Gnome" — the moment the supernatural compact's hidden costs become terrifyingly explicit as the gnome begins to claim the wife herself, piece by piece.

## Context

The ploughman had married without consulting the gnome, and his new wife, unused to sharing the household's best with a hole in the wall, had brought home fine clothes without offering any to the gnome. One night, the gnome emerged at full size — about eight inches high — and confronted the ploughman.

## The Event

The gnome grew before the ploughman's eyes until she stood eight inches tall, speaking with quiet authority. She declared that he was breaking their compact: he had married without her leave, his wife had taken fine clothes without sharing, and the balance of their bargain was in jeopardy (009-019-022).

Then came the ultimatum. The gnome demanded the wife's beautiful hair — all of it, cut off close to the head. "Mad or not mad, I mean to have it, and, moreover, it is my due. You married without consulting me, and if I kept you to your bargain, I should have a great deal that I have not got. Certainly your wife has the best head of hair in the house, so you must cut it off near her head and bring it all to me" (009-023-024).

The ploughman was horrified but trapped. If he refused, the gnome would leave — and as she reminded him, she would take all his wealth with her, for it was he who had broken the compact. He was given a deadline: "This day week at twelve o'clock."

## Outcome

Faced with the ultimatum, the ploughman found himself trapped in an impossible position. The demand for the wife's hair was only the beginning — the gnome would later escalate to demanding the wife's skin (her complexion) and eventually the wife's very life. Each compliance only fed the gnome's appetite, teaching the ploughman that supernatural bargains have no upper limit.

## Significance

This event crystallizes the tale's central horror: a bargain with a supernatural being is not a contract between equals but a predator-prey relationship disguised as trade. The gnome's logic — claiming the wife as "my due" because the compact was broken — reveals that the original terms were never about fair exchange but about establishing a claim that could be escalated indefinitely. Each concession the ploughman makes only validates the gnome's ownership.
