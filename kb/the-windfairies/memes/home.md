# Home

The place from which characters depart and to which they long to return — not merely a location but the site of identity, [[family]], and rightful belonging. In these fairy tales, home is the moral center of the universe: to leave it is to risk everything; to return is to be restored.

Home takes many physical forms across the collection: a [[the-windmill|windmill]] on the downs, [[the-cottage-by-the-sea|a cottage by the sea]], [[the-farm-at-ploetzin|a farm at Plotzin]], a humble cottage that becomes prosperous. But in every case, home is defined by relationships — father, husband, children, flock — rather than by walls. The tales encode a pre-modern understanding of home as moral center, a conviction that the journey away and the journey back together constitute the full arc of a meaningful life.

## Definition

Home in these tales is never just a house. It is the windmill by the sea where [[lucilla|Lucilla]] danced with the waves (001-001), the cottage where her children wait (001-024), the farm where [[nanina|Nanina]] tends her sheep (004-001). Home is defined by relationships — father, husband, children, flock — rather than by walls. Departure from home is always dangerous; return is the tales' preferred happy ending. The tales encode a pre-modern understanding of home as moral center: to leave home is to risk losing oneself; to return is to be restored (001-069).

The physical geography of home reinforces its moral meaning. [[the-downs|The downs]] by the sea are windswept and solitary — a liminal space between land and water, between the human and fairy realms. The cottage by the sea is humble but sufficient. [[the-farm-at-ploetzin|The farm]] is a place of honest labor. In every case, home is modest, grounded, and authentic — the opposite of [[the-kings-palace|the palace]] with its false grandeur, its corrupt court, and its eventual destruction by the very windfairies who protect the simple dwellings of the faithful.

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

Home is the story's emotional and structural center. [[lucilla|Lucilla]] leaves her windmill home for the King's court — a departure motivated by the promise of gold to bring back to her family (001-028). Her eyes "were red with crying at leaving her home" (001-028), and before she departs she goes out "alone on to the cliffs, and stretched out her arms, and called to the windfairies to go with her and help her" (001-028) — instinctively seeking the protection of the powers that guard her true place.

Her entire ordeal is defined by longing for home: "Would that I had never left my babes and my home" (001-046). In her darkest moment in prison, she cries out that "I shall never see my husband or my babies again" (001-051) — the loss of home figured as the ultimate tragedy. The story's resolution is not wealth or fame but return: Lucilla "went back to the little cottage by the seashore" with her husband and gold (001-069). Home is the destination that gives the [[journey]] meaning.

The destruction of [[the-kings-palace|the palace]] at the story's climax — "the houses in the marketplace tottered and fell, and high up on the hill the palace was a mass of ruins" (001-066) — is the tale's definitive statement about true and false dwelling places. The palace, a monument to power and vanity, is leveled by the same wind that preserves Lucilla. The [[the-cottage-by-the-sea|little cottage by the seashore]], humble and beloved, stands untouched.

### In "Vain Kesta"

[[kesta|Kesta]]'s story inverts the home pattern. She leaves [[the-farm-at-ploetzin|the farm]] to find a richer husband — not to return but to ascend. Her homeward journey at the story's end is a humiliating retreat, not a triumphant return. She runs back through town, pursued by pages, soldiers, clerks, and farmhands, each door closing behind her (002-048 through 002-075). When she finally reaches the farm, [[adam|Adam]] rejects her: "I cannot marry you now, since you've been to the town and tried to get a finer husband than me" (002-083).

The tale contrasts proper homecoming (Lucilla) with failed escape from home (Kesta). Kesta's error is not merely vanity but a fundamental misunderstanding of what home is. She sees home as a starting point to be escaped, a low rung on a ladder of social ascent. The tales insist that home is not a rung but the whole ladder — the place from which all journeys begin and to which all rightful journeys return.

### In "Nanina's Sheep"

[[nanina|Nanina]]'s home is the farm where she tends her master's flock. Her departure — crossing the hill to [[the-old-palace|the fairy palace]] (004-002) — is a transgression precisely because she leaves her assigned place. "If it is filled with fairies," she reasons, "it won't hurt me just to look at it" (004-003) — a violation of the boundary her master set.

The story's resolution requires her return to the proper side of the hill, but the return is hard-won. She must bind her feet in the earth among the roots of [[the-beech-tree|the beech tree]], enduring nights of agony, to win back what she lost by leaving her proper place. The tale teaches that home is a responsibility as much as a refuge — a place you must earn the right to return to after transgression.

### In "Dumb Othmar"

The theme of exile from home reaches its most poignant expression in the story of [[othmar|Othmar]]. When he follows [[the-dwarf-musician|the dwarf musician]] underground, he leaves his mountain village — his home — and returns unable to speak. His muteness is a kind of continued exile: physically present but unable to communicate, cut off from the community that constitutes home. [[hulda|Hulda]]'s quest to recover his voice is simultaneously a quest to restore him to true home — not just the place but the full participation in its life that voice enables.

## Associated Characters

- **[[lucilla|Lucilla]]** — Home is her constant reference point: she measures every ordeal by her distance from her cottage and children (001-046) (001-069)
- **[[kesta|Kesta]]** — Home is what she seeks to escape; her failure to value home is part of her moral failure
- **[[nanina|Nanina]]** — Home is the site of duty; departure from home is disobedience (004-001)
- **[[the-sailor|The Sailor]]** — His return from sea brings Lucilla home; his ship, guided by the windfairies, is the vehicle of homecoming (001-067)
- **[[adam|Adam]]** — The farmhand who wants to build a home with Kesta, and whose rejection of her marks the final closing of home's door (002-083)

## Moral Dimension

Home in these tales is a moral category, not merely a spatial one. The good character longs for home; the vain character seeks to escape it. Return home is the standard happy ending — wealth, marriage, and adventure are secondary. The tales teach that home is where one belongs by right and duty, and that the journey away from home is a test from which one must return changed but faithful (001-069). The destruction of the palace (001-066) — an unnatural, rootless place of power — and the preservation of the cottage by the sea encode this value in spatial terms.

The tales also acknowledge that home can be lost through one's own fault. Nanina loses her flock by leaving her assigned place; Kesta loses her chance at a home with Adam through vanity and deception. But the possibility of return — through suffering, through penance, through faithfulness — remains open. Home is not an entitlement but a gift that must be honored, and the tales are relentless in showing what happens to those who take it for granted.
