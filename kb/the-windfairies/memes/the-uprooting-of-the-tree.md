## Context

Two botanical travellers had discovered the rare tree during the summer and declared it the finest find of their travels (003-005–006). The summer passed, autumn winds blew (003-007), and the tree and pool prepared to say their seasonal goodbyes — the pool would freeze over and the tree would drop its leaves, but both expected the spring sun to wake them together as always (003-008–009). This expectation was to be shattered.

## The Event

Long before winter set in, before the last leaf had fallen, a number of men rode across the prairie straight to the tree (003-010). Foremost among them were the two travellers who had discovered it months earlier (003-010). They brought with them horses, mules, and a long wagon (003-010).

The pool sensed danger first, crying uneasily, "Why do they come? What do they want?" — but the tree "feared nothing" (003-011). The men brought spades and pickaxes. They dug a deep ditch all around the tree's roots, then dug beneath them — until both the pool and the tree understood they intended to dig it up (003-011).

The tree cried out in desperation:

> "What are you doing? Why are you trying to wrench up my roots and to move me? Don't you know that I shall die if you drag me from my pool which has fed and loved me all my life?" (003-012)

The pool, equally anguished, lamented:

> "Oh, what can they want? Why do they take you? The sun will come and dry me up without your shade, and I never, never shall see you again." (003-012)

But the men heard nothing. They loosened all the earth around the roots, lifted the tree, wrapped it in cloths, placed it on their wagon, and drove away (003-012). The tree's and pool's voices, however desperate, fell on deaf human ears — the men perceived only a rare specimen, not a living being with attachments.

## Participants

- [[the-talking-tree]] — The rare tree, forcibly uprooted from its lifelong home and companion; its unheard protests underscore the story's theme of nature's voicelessness before human ambition (003-011–012)
- [[the-pool]] — Left behind, bereft, facing the sun without its tree's protective shade; its lament "I never, never shall see you again" (003-012) marks the story's emotional nadir

## Outcome

The tree was transported away to an unknown destination. For the first time, the pool looked straight up at the sky "without seeing the delicate tracery made by the leaves and twigs against the blue" (003-013). It mourned so loudly that the birds flying past stopped to listen, crying: "My tree, my tree, where have they taken my tree? When the hot sun comes it will dry me up, if it shines down on me without the shade of my tree" (003-013). A swallow, pausing on the wing, advised the pool to ask the moon for help — "The moon sees everywhere, and she will tell you" (003-014). This set the pool on its journey toward transformation, ultimately leading to its metamorphosis into a cloud (003-019–025).

## Significance

The uprooting of the tree is the central tragic turning point of "The Pool and the Tree." It represents the violent intrusion of human ambition into a perfect natural harmony. The tree and pool's unheard protests underscore the story's theme: that nature possesses its own relationships and consciousness, which human enterprise — however scientifically motivated — callously disregards. The event transforms the story from idyll to quest. The men's deafness to the tree and pool's cries is particularly significant: they are not malicious, merely oblivious — their scientific enthusiasm has rendered them incapable of perceiving the living relationship they are destroying.
