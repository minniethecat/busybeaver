## Overview

The Village Women are the female residents of the mountain village in "The Story of the Singing Mouse." They form part of the community backdrop against which Othmar's story unfolds. Among them is Hilda, a young girl who sits near Othmar and later figures in his story.

## Appearances

### The Story of the Singing Mouse

The village women appear throughout the tale as part of the daily life of the mountain community. One of them, Hilda, is introduced sitting near Othmar:

> Just behind where Othmar lay sat a young girl, named Hilda. She was going out to work for the other women in the village. [007-004]

Hilda is described as neither pretty nor particularly noticeable — an ordinary village girl who works alongside the other women.

When the dwarf with the yellow cap arrives in the village, all the villagers stare with surprise [007-003]. The community dynamic is further illustrated when the villagers look around for someone to meet the dwarf's challenge of singing:

> On this the villagers began to look round for all the young folk who could sing [007-005]

The women participate in the midsummer-night merrymaking:

> It was midsummer-night, and the villagers had been having a merrymaking [007-036]

And later, one of the village women is seen going quietly to the window at night:

> When it was quite dark, and all the village was quiet, she went to the window [007-101]

## Role in the Tales

The Village Women represent:
- **Community backdrop** — the ordinary life that surrounds magical events
- **Collective witness** — the village as an audience for Othmar's gift and loss
- **Feminine domesticity** — the work and rhythms of village women's daily life
