## Context

The shepherd's daughter was no ordinary girl — she was the child of the mysterious rain woman who had visited the shepherd's cottage on a stormy night ten years earlier (008-001–007). The girl loved rain and water, shunned fire and sun, and would dance in the rain calling it her "brothers and sisters" (008-008). When the King's son saw her by the river, he fell in love with her beauty and determined to marry her, despite her reluctance to leave her mother and the cottage (008-011–017).

## The Event

The old King ordered great preparations for the wedding (008-022). In the days leading up to the ceremony, the rain fell with unprecedented fury — it beat through cottage roofs, the river swelled and overflowed its banks, and everyone was frightened (008-022). Only the shepherd's daughter rejoiced, going out to dance in the torrents as was her custom (008-022).

On the eve of the wedding, the daughter knelt beside her mother and begged to stay: "Dear mother, let me stop with you and nurse you when you are old. Do not send me away to the palace to live with the King's son" (008-023). But the mother was angry, calling her ungrateful for refusing such fortune (008-024).

On the wedding day, the rain continued in torrents. The bride stepped through pools to reach the grand carriage (008-025). During the marriage service, the priest's voice could scarcely be heard over the rain hammering the roof (008-025). At the wedding banquet, as the goblets were raised to toast the bride and groom, the waters burst through the doors:

> "The floods! The floods!" and the servants ran into the hall, crying out that the waters were pouring in, and in one moment the rooms were filled with water, and no one thought of anything but to save themselves. (008-025)

## Outcome

When the hurricane subsided and the waters receded, the Prince's wife was nowhere to be found. Everyone believed she had been swept away by the torrents and drowned in her youth and beauty (008-025). Only the shepherd's wife wept alone, remembering the rain woman's prophecy: "When you love aught on earth better than your daughter and her happiness, she will go from you" (008-006, 025). The King's son mourned for many years before eventually marrying another princess (008-026).

## Significance

The wedding catastrophe is the climax of "The Rain Maiden" tale. It fulfills the rain woman's warning — the mother's desire for wealth and status, placed above her daughter's happiness, causes the daughter's loss. The flood is not merely a natural disaster but an elemental reclaiming: the rain maiden returns to her true nature, dancing forever in the rain. The Prince, even years later, hears "the sound of dancing feet, and a voice that called out, 'Come and dance with me...'" whenever the rain fell in torrents (008-026).
