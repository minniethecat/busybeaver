# Gold and Wealth

A pervasive material motif functioning as narrative currency — gold appears as a promised reward for service, as a temptation that reveals character, and as the happy ending's tangible proof of moral vindication. Across five tales in the collection, the handling of wealth separates the deserving from the undeserving, the faithful from the [[greed|greedy]], and the wise from the foolish.

Gold in these stories is never neutral. When [[lucilla]] accepts it as a byproduct of faithful service, it blesses her. When [[kesta]] pursues it as her primary goal, it destroys her. When [[the-ploughman|the ploughman]] earns it through honest partnership with [[the-gnome|the gnome]], it prospers him. And when the [[the-fairy-shepherd|fairy shepherd]] appears "dressed in scarlet and gold" (004-025), the precious metal marks the boundary between mortal poverty and supernatural splendor — a boundary that characters cross at their peril.

## Definition

Gold in these tales is never merely money. It is the visible token of supernatural favor or moral deserving. Characters who earn gold through faithful service or honest labor are blessed; those who pursue it through greed or deception are frustrated. The motif bridges the mortal and fairy realms — gold changes hands between humans and supernatural beings, marking moments of covenant, test, and resolution (001-027).

The tales employ gold as a narrative signal. When gold appears onstage — the bag on the scaffold, the rings on [[the-banker|the banker]]'s fingers, the gnome's promise of prosperity, the shepherd's scarlet-and-gold raiment — the audience knows a moral test is underway. The question is never whether gold is real but whether the character's relationship to it is righteous.

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

[[the-bag-of-gold|The bag of gold]] is the story's central material object — the King's courtiers promise [[lucilla|Lucilla]] "a sack of gold to take home with her" if she dances at court (001-027). The gold sits on the scaffold throughout her ordeal as both temptation and promised reward (001-065). The courtiers use it as a bargaining chip: "This is your last chance, speak now and confess the truth to the King, and here is your gold, and you shall go" (001-065). Lucilla refuses to trade her faith for gold, and in the end she receives both — the gold and her freedom — because she valued the right thing more. "Lucilla and her husband took the bag of gold, and went back to the little cottage by the seashore" (001-069) — gold earned through suffering and faith-keeping, not mere performance.

The gold also serves as the temptation that lures Lucilla from [[home]] in the first place. Her father persuades her to go because "it would make them all rich if she brought home a sack of gold" (001-028). This detail is crucial: even the well-intentioned can be led astray by the promise of wealth, and Lucilla's ordeal is partly the price of that initial decision.

### In "Vain Kesta"

[[kesta|Kesta]]'s ambition is defined by wealth. She rejects [[adam|Adam]] the farmhand for [[the-miller|the miller]], the miller for [[the-bailiff|the bailiff]], the bailiff for [[the-banker|the banker]] — each escalation driven by the prospect of greater riches. The banker's "hands were covered with rings, and Kesta knew his pockets were full of gold" (002-015). Her greed-driven social climbing is the engine of her downfall. She measures every suitor by his visible wealth, and in doing so reveals her own poverty of character.

The tale's judgment is precise: Kesta does not merely want wealth; she wants wealth without earning it, status without deserving it. The cheeses that expose her — the products of her own honest labor — become the instruments of her humiliation precisely because she was ashamed of them. She tried to pass as something she was not, and gold was the measure of the distance between her pretense and her reality.

### In "The Ploughman and the Gnome"

The gnome's bargain — "Agreed" (009-016) — establishes a proper economic relationship with the supernatural. The ploughman agrees to share the best of everything with [[the-gnome|the gnome]], and in return "all things will go well with you" (009-014). The deal transforms him from poverty to prosperity: "things began to improve very much with him... he was able to get more land and buy his own animals, and in a little while had a farm of his own, and had grown to be quite a rich man" (009-020).

This tale presents the model of righteous wealth acquisition: honest exchange, faithful fulfillment of terms, and prosperity as a byproduct of right relationship rather than greed. The gnome herself says "You are a poor man now, but you will soon be a rich one" (009-014) — wealth as promise, not as goal. When the ploughman's wife threatens the arrangement with her demand for new clothes, the gnome's response — demanding equal share for herself (009-029) — reinforces the principle: wealth must be shared with the supernatural partner who enables it.

### In "Nanina's Sheep"

Though gold is less central here, it marks the boundary between realms. [[the-fairy-shepherd|The fairy shepherd]] on the final night appears "dressed in scarlet and gold" with "a crown set with jewels" (004-025) — the supernatural realm displaying its wealth as a lure. The contrast with [[nanina|Nanina]]'s humble station — a shepherdess with nothing but her master's flock — frames the entire tale as a test of whether she will abandon her duty for fairy splendor. The gold-adorned shepherd is beautiful but dangerous; Nanina's resistance, binding her feet in the earth, is a refusal of wealth that is not rightfully hers.

### In "The Pool and the Tree"

The discovery of the rare tree — a botanical treasure — triggers a narrative about possession and displacement. The tree is valued for its rarity, a form of natural wealth, and the attempt to transplant it to the city represents the human impulse to possess and control what is precious. The tree's subsequent withering suggests that some forms of wealth cannot be taken from their proper place without destroying them — a lesson that resonates with the collection's broader teaching about the limits of acquisition.

## Characters and Objects Related to Wealth

- **[[lucilla|Lucilla]]** — Receives gold as deserved reward after moral testing (001-027) (001-069)
- **[[kesta|Kesta]]** — Pursues gold through vanity and deception; the banker's rings symbolize her misplaced values (002-015)
- **[[the-ploughman|The Ploughman]]** — Engages in honest material exchange with the gnome, demonstrating proper wealth-seeking (009-016)
- **[[the-banker|The Banker]]** — Embodies wealth without virtue: "fat and ugly and old, but his hands were covered with rings" (002-015)
- **[[the-bag-of-gold|The Bag of Gold]]** — The physical token of reward, placed on the scaffold as both temptation and promise (001-065)
- **[[the-gnome|The Gnome]]** — The supernatural source of prosperity, whose terms must be honored exactly (009-014)

## Moral Dimension

The tales distinguish sharply between gold earned and gold pursued. Lucilla's gold comes to her as vindication — she never sought it, only a way home. Kesta's pursuit of ever-richer husbands brings her to ruin. The ploughman's prosperity flows from faithful observance of his compact with the gnome. The moral is consistent: wealth is a legitimate reward for virtue and suffering but a corrupting object of desire. When gold is the goal rather than the byproduct, it leads characters astray. The fairy tales thus encode a pre-modern economic morality in which wealth flows from right relationship with the supernatural order (001-028).

Crucially, the tales do not condemn wealth itself — they condemn the wrong relationship to it. Lucilla ends rich, the ploughman ends rich. But neither sought riches as their primary aim. The difference between Kesta's rings and Lucilla's bag of gold is the difference between grasping and receiving, between vanity and humility, between faithlessness and [[keeping-faith|faith]].
