# The King

The King is a central authority figure in "The Windfairies" (Chapter 1), the ruler of a distant land to which Lucilla and the Sailor are brought shortly before his wedding (001-029). He presides over the court into which Lucilla's Windfairy-gifted dancing is introduced, and his role illustrates the tension between personal admiration and political pressure. Though he genuinely appreciates beauty and initially possesses a sense of fairness, his failure to resist his Queen's jealous scheming transforms him from a benevolent patron into an instrument of persecution.

## Role in the Story

The King functions as both patron and antagonist — a ruler whose appreciation for Lucilla's art is corrupted by his inability to withstand his Queen's manipulation. His arc traces a descent from admiration to complicity, and finally to the destruction of his own palace as a consequence of his moral failure (001-066).

### The Wedding and Lucilla's Arrival

The King is introduced through the courtiers who bring Lucilla to his kingdom. They tell her "they came from a country where the King loved nothing so much as beautiful dancing, and that he would give great sums of money to anyone who danced well" (001-027). He had sent emissaries searching "all over the world for the best dancers" (001-027), and the Windfairies have chosen his wedding as the occasion for Lucilla's public debut (001-030). "The Princess who was to marry the King arrived, and the wedding was celebrated with much magnificence" (001-032). Lucilla is taken into "a great hall close to the palace, where musicians were playing on every kind of instrument" to prepare for her performance (001-031). On the evening of her dance, she prays to the Windfairies: "Now help me, dear windfairies, as you have done before; keep faith with me, as I have kept faith with you" (001-033). The King, seated on his throne beside the new Queen, watches Lucilla dance — and is immediately captivated (001-034, 001-035).

### The King's Admiration Turns to Suspicion

After Lucilla's performance, the King summons her. "The King sent for her, and asked her who she was, and where she came from" (001-035). He is impressed by her humility but puzzled by her refusal to reveal her teachers. On the following evening, "he said to her, 'Well, now will you tell us what we asked you last night, so that we may send for your teachers, and have others taught to dance as you do?'" (001-047). Lucilla's silence — compelled by her sacred promise to the Windfairies (001-015) — becomes the crux of the conflict. The Queen seizes on this silence to sow suspicion: "it was not possible that she could have learnt dancing from looking at windmill sails" (001-044).

### Capitulation and Persecution

The King's moral failure is his inability to resist the Queen's relentless pressure. Though he initially believes "Lucilla had done no wrong" (001-050), the Queen "insisted that she was deceiving them" (001-050). At a decisive moment, "the King was silent" (001-049) — and his silence licenses the Queen's escalating cruelty. Eventually he becomes an active participant: "The King sent down to the prison and told her that if she would not speak, she must die" (001-052). After Lucilla miraculously survives drowning, "both the King and Queen were very angry, and said that now it was quite clear that she was a witch, and that she must be burnt" (001-059). The King's descent from admirer to executioner is complete.

### The Windfairies' Retribution

The story's climax brings divine judgment on the King's authority. The Windfairies summon a catastrophic storm that destroys the palace: "High up on the hill the palace was a mass of ruined walls" (001-066). The King's worldly power is shattered by the supernatural forces he had dismissed, and Lucilla is vindicated. The destroyed palace stands as a permanent monument to the cost of failed justice — a ruler who could not defend the innocent against his own court's corruption (001-062, 001-066).

## Character Traits

- **Appreciative of beauty** — The King "loved nothing so much as beautiful dancing" and had searched the world for great performers (001-027). His genuine aesthetic appreciation is what draws Lucilla into his orbit in the first place.
- **Initially fair-minded** — Unlike the Queen, he recognizes that "Lucilla had done no wrong" and is reluctant to punish her without cause (001-050).
- **Weak-willed and easily swayed** — Despite his better judgment, the King capitulates to the Queen's demands. His silence in the face of injustice is his defining moral flaw (001-049).
- **Possessive of wonder** — The King's appreciation of Lucilla's dancing is possessive rather than reverent: he wants to own the wonder by extracting her secret, rather than being transformed by it (001-027, 001-047).
- **Corrupted by power** — Once he commits to the Queen's narrative, the King escalates ruthlessly. After the failed drowning, he is described as "very angry" alongside the Queen, declaring Lucilla a witch who must be burnt (001-059).

## Key Actions

1. **The search for dancers** — Even before the story begins, the King has sent emissaries throughout the world seeking the finest dancers for his court, a testament to his genuine love of the art (001-027).

2. **The wedding feast** — The King presides over his own wedding celebrations, seated on a throne with the Queen. He watches Lucilla dance with evident pleasure and wonder (001-030, 001-034).

3. **Summoning Lucilla** — After her first performance, the King sends for Lucilla and questions her: who she is, where she comes from, and who taught her to dance (001-035). When she cannot answer, he gives her another chance the following evening (001-047).

4. **The concession to the Queen** — Faced with the Queen's insistence that Lucilla is a deceiver, the King initially resists but eventually yields. "The King was silent" (001-049), and his silence authorizes all that follows.

5. **The condemnation** — The King sends word to the prison: confess or die (001-052). When drowning fails to kill Lucilla, he joins the Queen in rage, declaring her a witch and ordering her burnt (001-059).

6. **The destruction of his power** — The Windfairies' storm destroys the King's palace, reducing it to ruins. The ruler who could not uphold justice loses the very seat of his authority (001-062, 001-066).

- [[lucilla]] — The dancer whose supernatural gift captivates him. He is caught between genuine admiration and the political pressure to condemn her (001-035, 001-047, 001-050).

- [[windfairies]] — The supernatural beings whose gift to Lucilla sets the entire conflict in motion, and whose storm ultimately destroys the King's palace (001-027, 001-066).

## Significance

The King represents the theme of failed authority — the tragedy of a ruler who possesses good instincts but lacks the moral courage to act on them. His character illustrates that appreciation of beauty is not the same as commitment to justice. The King "loved nothing so much as beautiful dancing" (001-027), yet he was willing to destroy the dancer when doing so became politically convenient.

His arc mirrors a broader theme in the collection: mortal power, however magnificent, is fragile before supernatural justice. The King's palace — the physical embodiment of his authority — is reduced to rubble by the Windfairies' storm (001-066). The destruction is not arbitrary; it is the direct consequence of his failure to protect the innocent under his own roof. Where Lucilla's keeping of faith with the Windfairies is rewarded with rescue, the King's betrayal of justice is punished with ruin.

The King also serves as a foil to the Queen. Where she is actively malicious, he is passively complicit — a distinction that the story treats as morally significant but not exculpatory. His silence (001-049) is portrayed as a form of action: it is what allows the Queen's schemes to proceed unchecked. In this sense, the King embodies the danger of weak authority — the ruler whose failure to say "no" makes him as culpable as the one who says "yes" to evil.
