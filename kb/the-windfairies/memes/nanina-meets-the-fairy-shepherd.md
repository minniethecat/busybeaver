Nanina, bored with tending sheep on the hillside, ignored her master's explicit warning and crossed to the forbidden side of the hill, where she encountered a beautiful fairy shepherd whose enchanted pipe drew her into a night-long dance.

## Context

Nanina's master had departed for a month-long trip to buy pigs, leaving her with strict instructions to keep the six sheep and eight lambs safe, and most importantly, never to go near the old palace on the other side of the hill, which was filled with wicked fairies (004-001). The first day passed uneventfully, but by the second day Nanina grew restless and curious (004-002).

## The Encounter

Driven by curiosity, Nanina drove her flock to the other side of the hill and sat watching the half-ruined palace. As the sun set, she saw bright lights appearing in the palace windows (004-003–004). The front door opened, and out came a shepherd boy of extraordinary beauty, dressed in glittering green with a soft brown hat trimmed with leaves, carrying a crook in one hand and a pipe in the other (004-004). Behind him followed a flock of jet-black goats with thick, silky curls.

## The Enchanted Music

The shepherd began to play his pipe and dance, and the black goats skipped and danced behind him in perfect time. The music itself seemed to speak words:

> "When the young birds sing,
> And the young plants spring,
> Then dance we so merrily together, oh." (004-005–007)

The shepherd danced up to Nanina, bowed low, and offered his hand. Overwhelmed by the joyous music, she took it, and the dance swept her away. Her own flock began to move with the music too, and she reasoned that if the sheep followed, there could be no harm (004-012).

## The First Loss

Nanina danced the entire night, thinking only minutes had passed. Just before sunrise, the shepherd played one long slow note, and the goats gathered around him and vanished into the palace. When the sun rose, Nanina counted her flock and discovered one lamb was missing (004-016).

## Outcome

This encounter initiated a fortnight-long pattern: each night Nanina returned to the palace, each night she danced until dawn, and each morning another lamb was lost. The first dance set in motion the central conflict of the tale — Nanina's struggle between the irresistible enchantment of the fairy music and her duty to protect her flock.

## Significance

This event establishes the story's central tension: the seductive pull of magical beauty versus the mundane obligations of duty and obedience. Nanina's curiosity and desire for wonder — "I should like to see a fairy" (004-003) — prove stronger than her master's warning, and the cost compounds night after night. The shepherd, beautiful and joyful, embodies a temptation that is not evil in itself but dangerous in its consequences.
