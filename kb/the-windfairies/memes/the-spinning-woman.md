## Overview

The Spinning Woman is a minor character in "The Gipsy's Cup" (Chapter 5). She was a poor widow who made her living by travelling about the country spinning and weaving, accompanied by her two daughters. She set up a stall beside [[the-potter|the Potter]] at the annual fair, where her golden-haired daughter caught the Potter's eye.

## Role in the Story

The Spinning Woman and her daughters attended the fair each year, displaying their fine cloths, spinning wheels, and looms. She was willing to let her daughter marry the Potter — the narrative notes that "she would have been quite willing that the potter should marry her daughter." However, her daughter was hesitant, laughing off the proposal and deferring her answer until the following year's fair.

## Family

The Spinning Woman had two daughters. The elder (or more prominently featured) daughter had golden hair and became [[the-potter|the Potter's]] wife. Both daughters travelled with their mother, learning the crafts of spinning and weaving that provided their livelihood.

## Significance

As a widow supporting her family through skilled craft, the Spinning Woman represents the self-sufficient working poor of the story's world. Her willingness to accept the Potter as a son-in-law stands in contrast to her daughter's initial reluctance — a generational difference in approaching marriage and security.
