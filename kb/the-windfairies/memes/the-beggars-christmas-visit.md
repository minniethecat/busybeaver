## Context

A rich old gentleman lived alone in a handsome house, loving money "better than anything else in the world" (006-001). He never gave to others and was angry when asked. His servants "knew that he was greedy and hard, and cared for nobody" (006-001). On a snowy Christmas Day, he sat by his fire congratulating himself on not needing to go outside (006-002–003).

## The Event

As the old gentleman looked out at the snowy street, an old beggar appeared at his window — a man "about the same age as the old gentleman inside the window, and the same height, and had grey, curly hair, like his" (006-004). They looked so alike that "if they had been dressed alike anyone would have taken them for two brothers" (006-004).

The beggar spoke, and though he did not speak loudly, the old gentleman could hear "every word he said quite plainly through the window" (006-006). The beggar made three descending requests:

1. **A silver shilling** for a Christmas dinner at an eating-shop (006-006)
2. **A single copper penny** — "just one penny, that today I may not starve" (006-008)
3. **Broken victuals from the table** — scraps to eat in a doorway (006-010)

The old gentleman refused all three. He called the beggar wicked ("you must have done something very wicked to become so poor," 006-007), declared he never gave to beggars, and finally stamped his foot threatening to call the police (006-011).

The beggar's response was unsettling: instead of seeming distressed, "he was laughing merrily" (006-012). He looked back at the window and called out words "in a foreign tongue" that the old gentleman could not understand (006-012).

## Outcome

The old gentleman returned to his fire, still murmuring angrily (006-012). The next morning, despite the snow, a mysterious cat appeared on his hearthrug — a cat that "looked very well fed and cared for" and "seemed to the old gentleman to suit the room" perfectly (006-013). This cat, clearly of supernatural origin, would become the instrument of the old gentleman's transformation.

## Significance

The beggar's Christmas visit is the moral test that opens "The Story of a Cat." The beggar's physical resemblance to the rich man emphasizes the story's theme: wealth and poverty are accidents of fortune, not measures of worth. The three escalating refusals — from a silver shilling to mere table scraps — expose the old gentleman's cruelty as absolute, not circumstantial. The beggar's merry laughter and foreign words suggest he is no ordinary pauper but a supernatural agent, making the visit a test the old gentleman fails catastrophically.

## Quotes

> "It is Christmas Day, and you will have your dinner here in your warm room. Of your charity give me a silver shilling that I may go into an eating-shop, and have a dinner too." — the beggar (006-006)

> "I will give you nothing. Go away at once, or I shall send for the policeman to take you away." — the old gentleman (006-011)
