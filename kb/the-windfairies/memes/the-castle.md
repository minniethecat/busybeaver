# The Castle

The Castle is a ruined royal residence on the mountainside that appears in "The Rain Maiden," the eighth tale in *The Windfairies* collection. Once the seat of the region's kings, it serves as the setting for the wedding banquet where Hulda, the rain maiden bride, vanishes back into her elemental form.

## Description

The Castle is introduced as an ancient structure now in decline:

> on the mountainside stood an old castle, where formerly the Kings of that country lived [008-011]

The description emphasizes its former grandeur and present abandonment — a place of past royal power now repurposed for the current King's court. Despite its ruined state, it retains enough functional grandeur to host a royal wedding banquet. The great hall, where the feast takes place, is large enough to accommodate the entire court.

The Castle's most dramatic moment comes during Hulda's disappearance:

> when they went into the castle to the banquet, the water burst through the doors [008-025]

This suggests the Castle stands near a water source — likely the river where Hulda, as a rain maiden, would sit and where the Prince first encountered her.

## Location

The Castle is situated "on the mountainside" [008-011], overlooking the landscape below. It is near a river where the rain maiden Hulda habitually sits [008-009]. The Prince rides "down to the cottage" [008-016] to fetch Hulda, suggesting the Castle is at a higher elevation than the surrounding countryside — a position befitting its former role as a royal seat surveying its domain.

## Events

Two key events unfold at the Castle in "The Rain Maiden":

1. **The royal wedding**: After the Prince brings Hulda to court, she is dressed in fine clothes and presented at the Castle as a suitable bride. The wedding ceremony proceeds, and the court gathers for the celebratory banquet in the great hall

2. **Hulda's disappearance**: During the banquet, Hulda's true nature as a rain maiden — a water elemental — is revealed. The servants cry "'the floods! the floods!' and the servants ran into the hall" [008-025]. Water bursts through the Castle doors, and when the court looks for the Prince's bride, she is "nowhere to be found" [008-025]. The Castle, a place of human authority and ceremony, cannot contain a being of elemental nature

## Symbolism

The Castle represents the tension between human institutions and elemental nature. As the former seat of kings, it embodies worldly power, tradition, and social order — all the forces that demand Hulda conform to the role of a royal bride. Its ruined state foreshadows the impermanence of human constructions against the eternal forces of nature.

When water "burst through the doors" [008-025] during the banquet, the Castle's walls — symbols of human containment and control — are breached by Hulda's true elemental self. The Castle cannot hold a rain maiden any more than royal protocol can constrain the rain. Its ruined architecture becomes a metaphor for the limits of social convention when confronted with the supernatural.
