# Punishment

Punishment is a central moral mechanism in *The Windfairies* collection, functioning as the narrative's primary means of enforcing justice and restoring moral order. Characters who transgress — through vanity, disobedience, cruelty, or presumption — face consequences that range from social humiliation to supernatural retribution and death.

## Definition

In these fairy tales, punishment is the price of wrongdoing. It is not merely a plot device but the engine of moral instruction: each tale demonstrates that certain behaviors (vanity, cruelty, deception, disobedience) will inevitably bring suffering upon the transgressor. Punishment in *The Windfairies* takes three primary forms: legal/social punishment (imposed by human authority), supernatural punishment (imposed by magical or divine forces), and self-inflicted punishment (the natural consequence of one's own actions).

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

Punishment takes a dark, institutional form in the opening tale. Lucilla is accused of witchcraft and threatened with execution:

> she should have three days to prove she was no witch, and if she could not, she must go to prison and wait her punishment [001-050]

The punishment here is unjust — Lucilla has done nothing wrong — but it is wielded by the Queen with chilling authority. The windfairies' response is itself a form of counter-punishment: their hurricane destroys the marketplace and the palace, punishing the King and Queen for their cruelty. The threat is made explicit earlier in the tale:

> if you had made it, we should punish you severely [001-014]

This establishes punishment as a force that can be wielded by the powerful against the weak, or by nature against human authority.

### In "Vain Kesta"

Punishment in "Vain Kesta" takes the form of social humiliation. Kesta's presumption — a dairymaid aspiring to marry a Duke — is punished not by imprisonment but by mockery and exile. The suitors and townsfolk agree:

> she merits punishment for such behaviour [002-070]

> she deserves to be punished for her presumption [002-082]

The punishment is collective and public: Kesta is chased through the town by pages, soldiers, clerks, and farmhands, hooted and jeered until she returns to the farm alone. Her punishment is poetic — the social-climbing dairymaid is publicly stripped of all social standing.

### In "Nanina's Sheep"

Nanina faces punishment for disobedience. When she refuses to dance with the fairy shepherd, her flock is taken from her one by one:

> she remembered how she had been punished for disobeying her master [004-030]

The punishment is transactional and supernatural — each refusal costs a sheep. The fairy's punishment is not cruel but pedagogical: it teaches Nanina that her stubbornness has real consequences, and that obedience to benevolent authority is a virtue.

### In "Dumb Othmar"

The punishment in this tale falls upon the wicked singing girl who took Othmar's voice. The supernatural agent — a snake sent by the little man — delivers retribution:

> this snake did punish the singing girl [007-074]

> I have punished the wicked girl who took your voice [007-105]

The punishment is precise and targeted — it falls only on the guilty party, and its purpose is restorative (returning Othmar's voice) as well as retributive.

## Moral Dimension

Punishment in *The Windfairies* is consistently portrayed as just and inevitable. The tales do not question whether punishment is deserved — they assume it. The moral framework is straightforward: good behavior is rewarded, bad behavior is punished, and the punishment always fits the crime.

Notably, the collection distinguishes between just and unjust punishment. Lucilla's threatened execution is unjust because she is innocent, and the tale's resolution — the destruction of those who condemned her — restores moral balance. Kesta's humiliation is portrayed as just because her vanity and deception were genuine transgressions. The fairy tales thus teach not only that wrongdoing brings consequences, but that the truly innocent will ultimately be vindicated.
