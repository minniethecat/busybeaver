# The Cat's Transformation and Departure

After a year of mysterious companionship, the grey cat vanishes from the old gentleman's home as inexplicably as it arrived. The cook's jest — that it was a fairy cat, able to pass through keyholes — carries the tale's deeper truth: the miser has been taught to love by a creature he could never own.

## Context

For a year, a large, handsome grey cat had lived with a rich but miserly old gentleman. It arrived mysteriously one evening, walking in "as if the house belonged to it" (006-011), and had become the old gentleman's sole companion. Despite his obsessive love of money — he had refused a beggar a single silver shilling on Christmas Day, declaring "I've not got sixpence in the world" while sitting beside a heap of gold (006-008) — the old gentleman had grown genuinely fond of the creature.

He had even planned to exhibit the cat at a Cat Show to win a prize, encouraged by an acquaintance who told him the new Princess was "quite crazed about cats" and would pay handsomely (006-025–026). But in the days before the show, the cat's behaviour changed: it refused to eat, would not lap its milk, and lay panting on the rug. When the acquaintance suggested the cat might need to be killed if ill, the old gentleman protested:

> "Why, I declare, though it does seem absurd, I have lived with this creature for a year, and I feel as if it were a friend." (006-043)

This was a profound transformation for a man who had earlier valued money above all else. He now cared more about the cat's wellbeing than about the prize money.

## The Event

Seven nights after a stray brown cat had crawled in from the cold — an "ugly brute" the gentleman had reluctantly allowed to stay on the hearthrug beside the grey cat (006-032) — the old gentleman awoke to find something extraordinary:

> He felt something rub itself against his face, and opening his eyes, he found that the grey cat was no longer there — it had vanished entirely. (006-047)

The transformation was complete and silent. The brown stray cat remained, but the grey cat, his companion of a year, was gone without a trace, without a sound, without explanation.

When the old gentleman lamented this disappearance aloud, his cook offered a remarkable explanation:

> "Perhaps," she said, bursting out laughing, "'twas a fairy cat, as it could get away through a keyhole, and had no need to be let out at doors." (006-049)

## Immediate Aftermath

The old gentleman was left only with the brown stray cat — the "disgraceful-looking beast" (006-032) he had initially reviled. Yet he had fed it milk and allowed it to stay. The grey cat's departure marked the end of the old gentleman's first experience of genuine, uncalculating affection — the year in which he had come to care for a creature not for its monetary value, but simply for its companionship.

## Outcome

The grey cat departed as mysteriously as it had arrived, leaving no explanation. The cook's suggestion that it was a fairy cat — able to pass through keyholes — was the only answer offered. The old gentleman, who had denied charity to a freezing beggar, had been taught something about love and loss by a creature he could not own, sell, or control.

## Significance

The cat's transformation and departure completes the moral arc of "The Story of a Cat." The old gentleman, defined by his love of money and refusal to give, had unknowingly harboured a fairy creature and learned to love it. The cat's disappearance — as inexplicable as its arrival — leaves him with the brown stray he had initially despised, a quiet inversion of his earlier values: the ugly creature remains, the beautiful one is gone. The cook's remark that the cat must have been a fairy underscores the tale's genre as a moral fable: supernatural intervention disguised as ordinary domestic life, teaching a hard heart to soften. The transformation is not just the cat's but the old gentleman's: from miser who valued gold above all to a man who could say of a cat, "I feel as if it were a friend."
