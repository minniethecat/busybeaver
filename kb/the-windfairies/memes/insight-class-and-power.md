# Class and Power in The Windfairies

## Thesis

The Windfairies collection presents a remarkably coherent social vision across its tales. Power in this world is not distributed neutrally -- it flows from a rigid class hierarchy that characters may navigate but never dismantle. The King and the Duke exercise absolute but brittle authority; the Bailiff administers a class system he does not create; the Gipsy operates outside the hierarchy entirely, drawing power from supernatural knowledge rather than social position; and the Sailor, a commoner, achieves mobility only through marriage and the intercession of the Windfairies themselves. Taken together, these five figures map a social order in which mortal hierarchies are simultaneously inescapable and fragile -- vulnerable to supernatural justice, to the folly of their own rulers, and to the quiet resilience of ordinary people who keep faith.

## Evidence

### The King and Queen: Absolute Power and Its Limits (001-xxx)

The King in "The Windfairies" represents the apex of mortal political authority. He wields life-and-death power over his subjects, commanding a palace, a prison, and a scaffold. His authority is absolute in scope: he can summon dancers from across the world (001-027), imprison the innocent without trial (001-052), and order execution on the mere suspicion of witchcraft (001-059).

Yet the story systematically exposes the fragility of this power. The King's authority, for all its political reach, has no purchase on the supernatural. When Lucilla refuses to reveal the Windfairies' secret, she obeys a higher law than the King's command (001-015, 001-047). The King's ultimatum -- "if she would not speak, she must die" (001-052) -- is the ultimate expression of temporal power, and it fails entirely. Lucilla survives drowning because the Windfairies protect her (001-059). The King's subsequent escalation to burning at the stake is not a display of authority but of its exhaustion: he has no power left except greater violence.

The story's climax delivers a structural critique of absolute rule. The Windfairies' storm reduces "the palace" to "a mass of ruined walls" (001-066). The physical destruction of the seat of power is the story's verdict on the King's moral failure. Power wielded without justice is not merely illegitimate -- it is unsustainable. The supernatural world intervenes to correct what the mortal hierarchy cannot.

Crucially, the King is not purely villainous. He initially "refused, for he said that Lucilla had done no wrong" (001-050). His failing is weakness, not malice: he capitulates to the Queen's relentless pressure. "The King was silent" (001-049) -- and his silence licenses persecution. The collection thus distinguishes between the active cruelty of the Queen (001-036, 001-041, 001-055) and the passive complicity of the King. Both are condemned, but the King's weakness is treated as a structural problem of absolute monarchy rather than individual wickedness. When power is concentrated in a single fallible person, the story suggests, injustice is inevitable.

### The Duke: Aristocratic Authority (002-xxx)

If the King represents the tragic dimension of power, the Duke in "Vain Kesta" represents its absurdity. He is introduced in a posture of almost parodic idleness: "sitting by the stream in the garden, where he sits fishing all day" and "half asleep" (002-029). When Kesta asks what he has caught, he answers "Nothing yet" (002-041), and when she asks why he persists: "Because I'm sure to catch something soon, and it's amusing" (002-043). This is aristocratic authority at its most vacuous -- a ruler whose engagement with the world is recreational rather than responsible.

The Duke's exercise of power is theatrical rather than substantive. He summons "four pages in blue and gold" (002-038) with a blast of his horn, first to prepare a wedding feast, then moments later to "Hunt her away" (002-047). The horn -- an object so closely tied to the Duke's authority that it has its own entity page, [[the-dukes-horn]] -- is the perfect symbol of his rule: loud, performative, and utterly arbitrary. The same instrument that summons celebration also summons expulsion; the Duke's will changes nothing except the direction of the blast.

The Duke's most revealing line comes when he decides to marry Kesta: "I should not have thought you so very pretty, but if what you say is true you must be" (002-036). He cannot form his own judgment of beauty; he relies entirely on the reflected desire of others -- specifically, of his own subordinates. This epistemological dependency is the story's quiet critique of aristocratic authority. The Duke's power is social, not intellectual; he does not know what he thinks until others tell him.

His rejection of Kesta exposes the class system's foundational irrationalism. "Good gracious, you made them! Then you must be a common dairymaid, and your hands are quite rough. How terrible! And I was just going to marry you" (002-047). The discovery that Kesta makes cheeses -- that is, that she works -- is treated as a revelation of fundamental unfitness. The Duke was willing to marry her when he believed her an object of universal male desire; the moment he learns she produces rather than merely charms, she becomes "an impostor" (002-047). Class in this world is not about wealth or accomplishment but about distance from labour. The Duke's horror is not that Kesta deceived him but that he almost contaminated his aristocratic body by marrying a woman who works with her hands.

### The Bailiff: Middle-Management Power (002-xxx)

The Bailiff occupies a distinct structural position in the hierarchy: he is the Duke's officer, an agent of aristocratic authority rather than its source. His authority is delegated and therefore doubly anxious -- he must enforce a class system to which he himself is subject.

His defining trait is not ambition but resentment. The Bailiff "hated the miller" (002-013), and his interest in Kesta is inseparable from his rivalry with his social peer. When Kesta tells him of the Miller's forwardness, "the bailiff, who hated the miller," presses her eagerly: "did the miller really want you for his wife?" (002-013). His "chuckling at the thought of how he would torment the miller" (002-014) reveals a man for whom power is a zero-sum game within his own stratum. He does not aspire to the Duke's position; he simply wants to deny satisfaction to his rival.

The Bailiff represents the class system as experienced from within -- not from its apex (the Duke) or its bottom (Adam, the farmhand), but from its anxious middle. He rides "a white horse" (002-010), a marker of status that distinguishes him from foot-traveling farmers, yet his authority is entirely borrowed from the Duke. His pettiness is not a character flaw so much as a structural feature of delegated authority: when power is loaned rather than owned, it tends to be exercised with special vindictiveness.

### The Gipsy: Outsider's Power (005-xxx)

The Gipsy represents the collection's most radical alternative to class-based power: power derived not from social position but from supernatural knowledge inherited outside any hierarchy. She appears in "The Gipsy's Cup" as a figure entirely unconstrained by the social structures that govern the other characters.

Her introduction establishes her outsider status immediately. She appears at the potter's roadside workshop on the night before the fair -- a liminal moment in a transitional space, neither inside the town nor fully outside it. She claims no title, no patron, no social standing. Her authority comes from lineage alone: "My grandfather used to make pots on a wheel, and he had charms to say to them when he threw them" (005-008). This is a vertical transmission of power -- from ancestor to descendant -- that bypasses the horizontal structures of class entirely. The Gipsy does not need the King's permission or the Duke's approval; her grandfather's charms are her entire credential.

Her relationship to class is not oppositional but orthogonal. When she works the potter's clay, she produces vessels of "the most wonderful" colours "that he had ever seen: silver, blue, grey and yellow" (005-014). This is craft elevated to magic -- the transformation of common clay into objects of supernatural beauty. The potter, a tradesman embedded in the class system, "stared in silence" (005-009) while she worked. His silence is the silence of a class-bound person confronted with power that does not recognize the categories he inhabits.

The Gipsy's power is also distinctly feminine and oral. She speaks charms "in some strange language that the potter had never heard before" (005-010), channeling a tradition that is both foreign and ancient. The collection's other power figures -- the King, the Duke, the Bailiff -- exercise authority through commands, decrees, and horns. The Gipsy exercises hers through song. Her power is creative rather than coercive, inherited rather than seized, and indifferent to recognition rather than hungry for it.

Yet her power is not benign. The enchanted cup she leaves behind becomes the instrument of the story's central conflict, binding love only to have it stolen. Her warning -- "be sure you do not sell the little brown cup, but keep it and give it to your true love to drink out of; but only one draught, for if there are two maybe you will need the gipsy's help again" (005-014) -- is a moral condition attached to a magical gift. The Gipsy's power, unlike the Duke's, comes with terms. It must be used correctly or not at all. In this, she embodies a form of authority that the collection treats with far more respect than it accords to mortal rulers: authority grounded in knowledge and constrained by moral law.

### The Sailor: Commoner's Rise (001-xxx)

The Sailor represents the collection's limited model of class mobility. A "young fellow with black hair and white teeth" (001-022), he begins the story as an ordinary seafarer with no title, no land, and no power beyond his own decisiveness. His trajectory -- from sailor to husband to the man who bargains with supernatural forces for his wife's return -- is the collection's closest approach to a story of upward mobility.

The Sailor's mobility is achieved through two channels, neither of which involves climbing the class ladder. The first is marriage: he marries Lucilla and establishes a household "by the sea" (001-023), a space at the margin of the social world rather than within it. The sea is neither the court nor the farm; it is a realm of its own, and the Sailor's power derives from his mastery of this liminal environment. The second channel is wealth: he gathers "a sack of gold" (001-028) and uses it to bargain with the Windfairies' agents. Unlike Kesta, who pursues wealth as an end that will elevate her status, the Sailor uses wealth instrumentally -- as a tool to recover what he loves.

The Sailor's story is significant for what it does not include. He never enters the court hierarchy. He never seeks titles, lands, or recognition from the King. When Lucilla is imprisoned by royal authority, the Sailor does not appeal to the King -- he appeals to the Windfairies. His channel of power is not the class system but the supernatural economy of gift and bargain. The Windfairies transport him and Lucilla to "a country of which Lucilla had never even heard" (001-029), bypassing the geography of class entirely.

The Sailor thus represents a specifically non-hierarchical form of agency. He acts, he loves, he bargains, he recovers -- but he does not ascend. His power is horizontal rather than vertical: he moves through the world's margins rather than climbing its center. The collection, in presenting him as its most purely sympathetic male character, seems to endorse this model: the commoner who acts with fidelity and courage is more worthy than the king who rules with weakness and cruelty.

## Comparative Analysis

Taken together, these five figures reveal the collection's social vision, which can be summarized in four principles.

**First, mortal power is contingent.** The King's palace crumbles (001-066), the Duke's authority is shown to be comically arbitrary (002-029, 002-036, 002-047), and the Bailiff's borrowed power is petty and self-defeating (002-013, 002-014). The collection systematically undermines every claim to legitimacy based on birth or position. No mortal ruler in these tales is competent, wise, or just. The King is weak, the Duke is vain, the Bailiff is spiteful. The collection's political pessimism is thorough: concentrated power corrupts, and the class system that sustains it is a machine for producing humiliation rather than justice.

**Second, supernatural authority supersedes mortal hierarchy.** The Windfairies' storm that destroys the King's palace (001-066) is the collection's most dramatic assertion of this principle, but it operates throughout. The Gipsy's inherited charms (005-008, 005-010) are more powerful than the Duke's horn. The Sailor recovers his wife not through law or petition but through direct transaction with the Windfairies (001-028). The tales consistently subordinate political power to supernatural justice, suggesting that the true hierarchy is not social but cosmic.

**Third, wealth functions differently depending on one's relationship to power.** Lucilla and the Sailor receive gold as a reward for faithfulness (001-069). Kesta pursues gold as a tool of social climbing and is humiliated (002-015). The ploughman in "The Ploughman and the Gnome" earns prosperity through honest partnership with supernatural forces (009-014, 009-016). The collection's moral economy is consistent: wealth acquired through faithful service or honest exchange is blessed; wealth pursued as an end in itself, or as a means to transcend one's station, is cursed. [[gold-wealth]] operates as a moral test that reveals character rather than conferring status.

**Fourth, the collection's most admirable figures operate outside the class system.** The Gipsy belongs to no polity; she appears from the road and vanishes into moonlight (005-014). The Sailor lives by the sea, mastering a realm that no king can govern. Even Lucilla, the collection's heroine, begins at a windmill on the downs -- a liminal space between land and sky, far from court and town. The tales seem to suggest that virtue flourishes at the margins of social hierarchy, and that power -- real, effective power -- comes from faithfulness, knowledge, and love, not from titles or thrones.

These four principles cohere into a vision that is neither revolutionary nor conservative. The collection does not advocate for the overthrow of kings; it simply shows their palaces in ruins. It does not propose an alternative social order; it shows characters living meaningful lives outside the existing one. Its critique of class is implicit in its narrative architecture: the characters we are meant to admire are those who navigate around hierarchy rather than through it.

## Conclusion

The Windfairies collection is not a systematic treatise on social class, but its treatment of power is more coherent than a casual reading might suggest. Across its tales, the collection constructs a consistent moral argument: mortal hierarchies are real but fragile; supernatural justice is the true arbiter of outcomes; wealth is morally neutral but socially dangerous; and the figures who earn the narrative's approval are those who operate outside or at the margins of the class system.

The King, the Duke, and the Bailiff represent a descending scale of institutional authority -- absolute, aristocratic, and delegated -- and each is shown to be inadequate to the demands of justice. The Gipsy and the Sailor represent alternative sources of power -- knowledge and fidelity, respectively -- that the tales treat with far more respect. The collection does not promise that the meek shall inherit the earth, but it does insist that the proud shall lose their palaces.

In this sense, the tales offer a subtle but radical social vision. They do not deny the reality of class; they simply deny its legitimacy as a source of moral authority. Power, in the Windfairies' world, is something you earn by keeping faith -- not something you inherit by being born in the right bed.
