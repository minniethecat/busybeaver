## Context

Two botanical travellers had discovered a rare tree growing beside a pool in a vast wilderness. Despite the tree's anguished cry — "don't you know that I shall die if you drag me from my pool which has fed and loved me all my life?" — the men dug it up, wrapped its roots in cloth, loaded it onto a wagon, and drove away (003-010–012). The pool, now "looking straight up at the sky without seeing the delicate tracery made by the leaves and twigs against the blue," mourned inconsolably (003-013).

The pool called out to all things near it, and at last a swallow directed it to ask the moon where the tree had been taken (003-013–014). The moon revealed the tree's location but also its deteriorating condition.

## The Event

The moon told the pool that it had seen the tree a few hours ago: "They have taken it many miles away and it is planted in a big garden, but it has not taken root in the earth, and its foliage is fading" (003-017). Though strangers came from far and near to admire it because it was so rare, the tree was failing. The moon confirmed the tree was prized and protected — there were "only one or two like it in the world" (003-017).

Later, the west wind provided a fuller account of the tree's decline. The tree "was drooping" in its new setting: "They have planted it in a great garden, and there are railings round it and no one may touch it; and there is one gardener who has nothing to do but to attend to it" (003-030). Despite this privileged care — a dedicated gardener, railings to protect it, visitors who came from far and near — the tree "longs to be back in the desert, stooping over you and seeing its face in your water" (003-030).

When the cloud (the transformed pool) finally arrived over the garden, it beheld the tree at its lowest point: "Its leaves were falling off, its branches were drooping, and its buds dropped before they opened, and the poor tree looked as if it were dying" (003-035). The tree, prized as a botanical rarity by the humans who had taken it, was perishing from the separation.

## Immediate Aftermath

The cloud, recognizing its beloved, called out and begged the wind to blow it down upon the tree. The wind blew the cloud lower until "it broke and fell in a shower, and crept down through the earth to its roots" (003-037). When the tree felt the water reach its roots, "it lifted up its leaves and rejoiced, for it knew that the pool it had loved so had followed it" (003-037). The next morning, the gardeners found the tree "looking quite fresh and well, and its leaves quite green and crisp" (003-039). They attributed the revival to the cool night wind and rain, never knowing the pool lay hidden beneath.

## Outcome

The tree's withering was reversed only when the pool, transformed into a cloud, fell as rain upon it and crept down to its roots. The two were reunited beneath the earth, and the tree "blooms in splendour, and people come from far and near to admire it" (003-039–040). The pool remains hidden "in the darkness where no one can see it, but the tree feels it with its roots" (003-040).

## Significance

The tree's withering represents the cost of severing natural bonds for human purposes. The botanists prized the tree for its rarity — a purely scientific valuation — but their transplantation nearly killed it. The tree could not thrive on admiration alone; it needed the pool's sustaining presence. The contrast between the human caretakers (dedicated gardener, protective railings, admiring visitors) and what the tree actually needed (the pool's water at its roots) underscores the story's theme: that love and connection sustain life in ways that prestige and protection cannot.
