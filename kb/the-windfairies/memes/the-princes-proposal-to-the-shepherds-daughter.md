# The Prince's Proposal to the Shepherd's Daughter

The King's son, fishing at a river near an old castle, encounters the water-loving shepherd's daughter and falls irrevocably in love. His proposal sets in motion the central conflict of "The Rain Maiden," forcing the girl's mother to choose between social ambition and the love that the prophecy demands.

## Context

A little way from the shepherd's cottage stood an old castle "where formerly the Kings of the land used to come and stay, but which now had not been used for very many years" (008-011). One day, the shepherd's wife saw preparations being made at the castle, and soon the King's son arrived.

The shepherd's daughter, now a young woman, had always been drawn to water. She "would never go near a fire, however cold she was, neither did she love the sunshine, but always ran from it and crept into the shade" (008-008). She would sit for hours by the river, "dabbling her feet in the water, and singing sweet little songs to herself" (008-009). This affinity for water — a trait marking her as the rain maiden's child — set the scene for the Prince's first glimpse of her.

## The Event

One day, the King's son came down to the river to fish and encountered the shepherd's daughter. He asked who she was and what she was doing there. She answered fearlessly — "Who are you, and what are you doing here?" — for "she was afraid of no one" (008-012). When the Prince declared himself, she responded not with deference but with a gentle critique:

> "It is cruel to take the fishes out of the water. Leave them alone, and come and dance on the bank with me." (008-014)

She led him under the shade of a large tree and danced, and the Prince was captivated. Day after day he returned to the river, and "each time found her more enchanting" (008-015). At last, he told her he could live without her no longer — she must be his wife, the future Queen (008-015). He then went to the shepherd's cottage and formally asked the shepherd's wife for her daughter's hand.

The shepherd's wife "went nearly wild with joy" at the prospect of her daughter becoming Queen (008-017). But when she told her daughter the news, the response was unexpected:

> "But I don't want to marry the King's son, mother, or anyone. I will never be the wife of any man; I will stay with you and nurse you when you are old." (008-020)

Her mother became "very angry" and told her she was mad (008-021). At first the old King was "very wroth" at his son's choice, but he loved his son and knew "nothing would shake him from his word," so he consented — on condition that the Prince bring his bride home (008-016).

## Immediate Aftermath

The old King ordered "great preparations to be made for the wedding, which was to take place immediately" (008-022). Fine clothes were ordered for the shepherd's daughter, and the entire kingdom prepared for the royal marriage. But the evening before the wedding, the daughter knelt beside her mother and begged once more: "Dear mother, let me stop with you and nurse you when you are old. Do not send me away to the palace to marry a man I have never seen before" (008-023). The mother, angry and ambitious, scolded her for ingratitude.

## Outcome

The Prince's proposal was accepted by the mother against the daughter's will. The wedding preparations proceeded, and the daughter's reluctance was overruled by her mother's ambition. The stage was set for the tragedy foretold by the rain maiden: "as long as you love it better than anything in the world it will remain with you, but when you love anything else better than your little daughter and her happiness, it will go from you" (008-006).

## Significance

The Prince's proposal is the pivotal moment that tests the rain maiden's prophecy. The shepherd's wife had been warned that her child would remain only "as long as you love it better than anything in the world" (008-006). Her joy at the royal marriage — and her anger at her daughter's refusal — reveals that she now loves social advancement more than her daughter's happiness. The daughter's refusal — "I will never be the wife of any man" — is consistent with her nature as a water spirit; she belongs to the rain and the river, not to a human court. The mother's inability to see this, blinded by ambition, sets the tragedy in motion. The prophecy is about to be fulfilled.
