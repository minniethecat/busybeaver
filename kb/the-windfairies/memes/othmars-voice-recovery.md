## Context

Othmar, the sweetest singer in his mountain village, had been mute ever since a mysterious dwarf musician visited the village with his magical self-playing instruments. During a midnight concert on the hillside, one of the dwarf's enchanted singers — a girl made from a violin — had kissed Othmar on the lips and stolen his voice, leaving him unable to speak or sing (007-022–024).

His childhood friend Hulda, who loved him dearly, undertook a quest to find the dwarf and recover Othmar's voice. She followed the dwarf's trail across mountains and valleys, driven by love and determination (007-036–041).

## The Recovery

Hulda tracked the dwarf to his mountain dwelling, where she found him surrounded by his enchanted instruments — the fiddles, viols, horns, trumpets, pipes, and drums that by night transformed into singing beings. She pleaded with the dwarf to restore Othmar's voice.

The dwarf, though mischievous and strange, was not entirely without mercy. He acknowledged that Hulda's devotion had earned her a hearing. After testing her resolve, he agreed to release the stolen voice — but not without a price or condition.

The musical girl who had stolen Othmar's voice was summoned, and in a ritual of reversal, she gave back what she had taken. The dwarf declared that "the one who stole Othmar's voice will never use it again" (007-073).

## Aftermath

Hulda returned to the village with the recovered voice. When she reached Othmar, she called to him: "Othmar, I have done you a service" (007-105). As the voice was restored, Othmar found he could speak and sing once more. His first words were of gratitude to Hulda, whose steadfast love had accomplished what no one else could.

The recovery of his voice marked not only Othmar's return to wholeness but also his recognition of Hulda's worth. Where before he had dismissed her as "silly" for warning him against the dwarf (007-026), he now understood the depth of her devotion. The village celebrated the return of its sweetest singer, and Othmar's voice — once used to charm strangers — was now dedicated to those who had proven their love through action rather than words.

## Participants

- **Hulda** — An orphan girl of plain appearance but extraordinary devotion; her love for Othmar drives her to cross mountains and confront a magical being to recover his stolen voice (007-036–041, 105)
- [[othmar]] — The voiceless singer, mute since the dwarf's visit; his recovery restores not only his speech but his understanding of true worth (007-022–026, 105)
- **The dwarf musician** — A strange magical being who travels with self-playing instruments that transform into singers; though mischievous in stealing voices, he honours Hulda's sacrifice and releases Othmar's voice (007-058, 069, 073)

## Outcome

Othmar's voice was fully restored. The dwarf departed with his instruments, having acknowledged Hulda's triumph. Othmar gained more than his voice back — he gained the wisdom to see beyond beauty and charm, recognizing the true love that had been beside him all along. The event marks the conclusion of "Dumb Othmar," transforming what began as a tale of magical theft into a story about the redemptive power of steadfast love.

## Significance

Othmar's voice recovery is the climax and resolution of "Dumb Othmar." It inverts the story's central theft (voice stolen by a beautiful magical being) through its central restoration (voice recovered by a plain but loving mortal). The event articulates the tale's moral: that surface beauty and enchanting art may steal one's gifts, but only genuine devotion can restore them. Hulda's quest — undertaken without magic, armed only with love — proves more powerful than the dwarf's enchantments.
