# The Gipsy Boy

The Gipsy Boy is a supporting character in "The Gipsy's Cup" (Chapter 5), the young son of [[the-gipsy-woman]] who is left in the care of [[the-potter]] and his wife. Despite his difficult circumstances, he proves to be clever, resourceful, and loyal to his foster mother.

## Arrival at the Potter's Home

The boy first appears when his mother, the gipsy woman, arrives at the potter's door in desperate circumstances:

> "… up outside the window a poor gipsy woman leading a little boy by the hand." [005-058]

The gipsy woman, knowing she must flee, entrusts her son to the potter's wife:

> "… who will take care of my little boy, and see that he does not starve?" [005-070]

The potter's wife agrees to raise him, promising the gipsy woman that she will keep the boy always (005-071, 005-077).

## Character and Abilities

The boy grows into a clever and industrious child. He has dark eyes and is described as a "pretty boy" (005-080). His most notable contribution is the invention of straw-mat weaving.

When the potter's wife laments that she has nothing to weave, the boy suggests using straw:

> "… something to weave," said the boy, and he ran out into the road to gather straw. [005-083]

He successfully demonstrates that straw can be woven into mats, creating a new livelihood:

> "… the straw into a mat, as the gipsy boy had said." [005-089]

## The Gipsy Boy as Adviser

The boy shows wisdom beyond his years, advising the potter's wife on how to handle the mystery of the little brown cup:

> "… don't try to tell him," said the gipsy boy. "You should tell it to everyone." [005-096]

His advice is for her to share her secret openly rather than trying to hide it from her husband alone — a strategy that ultimately helps her (005-102, 005-124).

## Reunion with His Father

When the [[the-gipsy-man|gipsy man]] returns years later, the boy hides in a haystack, fearful of the stranger (005-141). When confronted, he answers boldly:

> "It is true I am a gipsy's child," answered the boy. [005-145]

He speaks eloquently of his foster mother's kindness, telling how she "taught her to love her home" (005-147). The potter ultimately accepts the boy, moved by the child's loyalty.

## Relationship with the Potter's Wife

Throughout the story, the gipsy boy is a constant companion to the potter's wife. He helps her weave mats and watches for the gipsy man's return:

> "… the gipsy boy would watch too, and look out for the coming of the gipsy." [005-126]

The potter's wife refuses to give him up, even when her own husband is at stake, keeping the promise she made to his mother (005-127).
