# Vanity

The overestimation of one's own worth and beauty — a vice that blinds characters to their limitations, leads them into folly, and is invariably punished with humiliation.

## Definition

Vanity in these tales is not mere self-regard. It is self-regard that distorts perception — the vain character cannot see herself clearly. Kesta looks in her glass and sees a prize to be auctioned to the highest bidder (002-004). Her vanity is not passive self-love but active self-marketing: she weaponizes her beauty as a tool of social climbing. The tales present vanity as self-defeating — those who think too highly of themselves are brought low, often through their own machinations (002-007). Vanity is the obverse of humility, and the tales' moral arithmetic is ruthless: the vain are humiliated; the humble are exalted.

## Manifestations

### In "Vain Kesta"

Kesta is vanity personified — the tale's title announces this: "Vain Kesta." Her entire plot is driven by the conviction that she deserves better than what she has. She rejects Adam, the miller, the bailiff, for ever-richer prospects, each time invoking her beauty as justification (002-007) (002-012) (002-019). Her vanity is not delusional — she is indeed pretty — but it is corrosive: it prevents her from recognizing a good match when she has one. Her downfall is engineered by her own vanity-driven scheming.

### In "Dumb Othmar"

Vanity appears in a minor key. The wife's concern with appearances and social standing — "they didn't think much of her beauty if she was willing to marry him" (005-107) — shows how vanity operates as social pressure, not merely individual failing.

## Associated Characters

- **Kesta** — Vanity's exemplar: her self-regard drives the entire plot (002-004) (002-007)
- **The Wife in The Gipsy's Cup** — Social vanity: concern with others' opinion of her marriage choice (005-107)
- **Lucilla** (contrast) — The anti-Kesta: beautiful but never vain, she never uses her beauty strategically (001-020)

## Related Concepts

- [[beauty]] — Beauty is morally neutral; vanity is beauty corrupted by self-regard
- [[greed]] — Vanity and greed are sibling vices in Kesta: vanity makes her think she deserves more; greed makes her pursue it
- [[cleverness]] — Kesta's cleverness serves her vanity; the combination is particularly destructive

## Moral Dimension

Vanity is treated with more humor than cruelty in the tales — Kesta is ridiculous, not evil. Her punishment is humiliation, not destruction. This is proportionate: vanity is a vice of perception, and its punishment is corrected perception — seeing oneself as others see you. The tales suggest that vanity carries its own punishment: the vain character is always found out, and the discovery is always embarrassing (002-004). Vanity is the most forgivable of vices in this moral universe, but also the most reliably punished.
