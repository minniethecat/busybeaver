## Context

Years had passed since the gipsy girl created the enchanted cup and vanished. The potter had prospered from the sale of her magical wares but had seen nothing more of her. He kept the plain brown cup on his shelf, and though he sometimes took it down and looked at it, he "still laughed when he remembered what the gipsy had said about leaving a charm in it" (005-019). He had drunk from it many times himself without any apparent effect, and his scepticism remained intact.

## The Event

One year at the annual fair, a poor widow arrived with her two daughters. They made their living travelling the country, spinning flax and weaving fine linen cloths "woven in pretty patterns" (005-019). One daughter sat at the spinning-wheel; the other worked a hand-loom. The girl at the loom had "the sweetest face the potter had ever seen" — eyes of deep blue, hair like "golden corn," and a smile that seemed to make "the sun shine" (005-019).

The potter was instantly captivated. He watched her every day of the week-long fair, unable to attend to his own stall or customers. "The more he looked at her the more he wanted to look, till at last he said to himself that somehow or other he must get her for his wife" (005-020).

When the fair ended, the potter begged the girl to marry him. The widow was willing enough — she was poor and the potter offered security — but the girl "only laughed, and said that she scarcely knew the potter" (005-020). She promised to give her answer when they returned the following year. The widow and her daughters departed, leaving the potter with only "a lock of golden hair, which he had begged from the daughter" (005-020).

## The Enchanted Draught

The year that followed "seemed the longest year he had ever lived" (005-021). As the next fair approached, the potter took down the brown cup and examined it again. Though he still professed not to believe in charms, he reasoned pragmatically: "what harm could it do?" (005-021).

When the fair opened and the widow returned with her daughters, the potter found the golden-haired girl more beautiful than ever — her eyes bluer, her smile brighter. He placed the brown cup on his stall among his other wares and waited.

As the fair ended and the family prepared to leave, the potter pressed the maid for her answer. When she still hesitated, he poured choice wine into the little brown cup and offered it to her, saying: "If then you wish to go away, and never see me again, I pray you drink one draught, in remembrance of the happy days we have had together" (005-024).

The girl took the cup and tasted the wine. Instantly, her demeanour transformed. She put down the cup and turned her eyes on the potter, saying in a low voice: "I will stay with you always, if you want me, and will be a true wife to you, and love you better than anything on earth" (005-025–026).

## The Marriage

The potter married the golden-haired girl, and she went to live in his little cottage (005-026). In time, they had a baby girl "with blue eyes like its mother's" (005-027). The potter considered himself "the happiest man on earth" (005-027).

Yet even surrounded by happiness, his scepticism remained. Looking at the brown cup on the shelf, he told himself: "My wife loved me for my own sake, and not for any silly charm or nonsense" (005-027). He could not accept that magic — not his own merit — had won his bride, a denial that would later prove catastrophic.

## Participants

- [[the-potter]] — The craftsman whose longing for the golden-haired girl drives him to use the enchanted cup despite his professed disbelief in its power (005-020–026)
- **The golden-haired weaver** — A poor widow's daughter who travels the country fairs spinning and weaving; her beauty captivates the potter, and the enchanted draught binds her heart to his (005-019, 025–026)
- **The widow** — The weaver's mother, willing to accept the potter's proposal for her daughter's security (005-020)

## Outcome

The potter achieved his heart's desire — a beautiful wife, a child, and a happy home. But the marriage was built on a magical foundation he refused to acknowledge. His wilful blindness to the cup's power — "my wife loved me for my own sake" — set the stage for the tragedy to come. When a dark gipsy man later tricked his wife into taking the second draught, the love charm would reverse, and the potter's happiness would unravel completely.

## Significance

The potter's marriage is the central turning point of "The Gipsy's Cup." It demonstrates the gipsy's charm working exactly as promised — one draught wins love — while simultaneously planting the seeds of destruction through the potter's fatal flaw: his insistence that the magic had nothing to do with his success. The scene is also notable for its ambiguity: did the golden-haired girl truly love the potter, or was her love entirely the cup's doing? The story never resolves this question, leaving the reader to ponder the boundary between enchantment and genuine feeling.
