# Lucilla's Wedding to the Sailor

Lucilla's wedding to the sailor marks her passage from the mill on the downs — and from the windfairies — into adult life as a fisherman's wife. It is the quiet pivot between the enchanted childhood of the opening and the trials that await her.

## Context

The sailor had passed Lucilla's dance test in a way no other suitor had: not by matching her supernatural lightness but by seizing her waist and holding her still, declaring his arms strong enough to keep her from dancing with any man but him (001-027). Lucilla accepted him, and they prepared to begin their life together.

## The Event

Lucilla married the sailor and went with him to live in his little cottage by the sea, many miles away from the mill. Her father, now growing old and no longer caring to work, went with her too (001-028).

The wedding was not a grand affair described in detail — the tale simply records the fact of the marriage and the move. What matters is the transition: Lucilla leaves the mill where the windfairies first touched her, trading the high downs for the sea's edge, beginning a life as wife and soon mother of two children.

## Outcome

For some time the sailor and Lucilla lived together very happily. They had two little children, and Lucilla would go down to the waves and dance with them as she had done in her old home. She tried to teach her children to dance as she did, but they could not learn, because the windfairies had never touched them (001-028). The gift was hers alone — it could not be inherited.

## Significance

The wedding is a structural hinge in the tale. It closes the arc of Lucilla's supernatural childhood and opens the arc of adult trials. The windfairies' gift, given freely to a child, cannot be passed to the next generation — establishing that the covenant is personal, not hereditary. The happy early years of the marriage also create the emotional stakes for the poverty and separation that follow.
