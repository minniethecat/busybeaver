# The Great Storm

The Great Storm is the central supernatural event of "The Rain Maiden" (Story 8), a catastrophic rainstorm that bookends the entire tale. It first appears on the night a mysterious grey-clad woman seeks shelter at a lonely shepherd's cottage high in the mountains (008-001). It returns years later on the eve of the shepherd's daughter's royal wedding, swelling rivers and bursting through castle doors until the bride is swept away and drowned in all her youth and beauty (008-025). The storm is no ordinary weather — it is the elemental manifestation of the [[the-rain-maiden|rain maiden]] herself, the force that brings life through prophecy and takes it back through flood when the conditions of that prophecy are broken (008-006, 008-025).

## Context

The shepherd and his wife lived in a wild, lonely cottage near the mountains, far from any town or village. The wind blew fiercely across those mountains, and the rain often fell so heavily it seemed the cottage would be washed away (008-001). Into this isolated world came the rain maiden one evening when the shepherd was out — a tall woman wrapped in a grey cloak, knocking at the door through the roar of a great storm (008-001). She was impervious to the rain: her clothes were dry, her grey cloak revealed itself to be covered with diamond-like jewels, and she asked only for a glass of water, refusing all other food (008-003-004).

The rain maiden delivered a prophecy to [[the-shepherds-wife]]: a daughter would be born to her, but the child would remain only as long as the mother loved her above everything else in the world. The moment she loved anything more than her daughter and her daughter's happiness, the child would go from her (008-006). This prophecy, spoken amid the first great storm, set the terms for everything that followed — the birth of [[the-shepherds-daughter]], her strange affinity for rain and water, her reluctance to marry, her mother's ambition, and the catastrophic flood that fulfilled the prophecy at last (008-007, 008-025).

The shepherd's daughter grew into a beauty unlike any other, with clear light-blue eyes and hair of an indeterminate pale colour hanging in heavy masses (008-010). She shunned fire and sunlight, running always to the shade, but when rain pattered on the windowpanes she would cry out with joy and dance (008-008). A river ran near the cottage, and by it she sat for hours, dipping her feet in the water and singing (008-009). When the [[the-prince|King's son]] saw her there, fishing from his boat, he fell irreversibly in love (008-011-013). Against her own protests — she declared she would never marry, would never live in a palace, and wished only to stay with her mother (008-020-021) — the wedding was arranged. It was in the days leading up to this wedding that the great storm returned.

## The Event

The Great Storm unfolds in two climactic waves, each corresponding to the rain maiden's direct presence in the mortal world: first as the harbinger of prophecy, then as the agent of its fulfilment.

### The First Storm: The Rain Maiden's Arrival

One evening when the shepherd was away, a great storm of rain descended upon the cottage with extraordinary violence. The rain beat against the doors and windows, and as the shepherd's wife sat listening, the raindrops sounded like the knock of a hand seeking admittance (008-001). When she could bear it no longer, she opened the door, and a gust of rain blew in her face. In the doorway stood the rain maiden herself — tall, grey-cloaked, with long hair falling down her back, speaking in a voice so low yet so clear that the shepherd's wife could hear every word plainly through all the storm (008-001).

The woman asked only for water and rest. She was neither wet nor dishevelled, and her dull cloak proved to be covered in flashing jewels, clear as diamonds, of every colour (008-003-004). She spoke the prophecy — the child would come, but would remain only as long as the mother's love held undivided — and then walked quietly out into the rain and disappeared, the drops pelting around her without wetting her clothes or stirring her hair (008-006).

A year later, on the night the shepherd's daughter was born, the wind howled and the rain fell as fiercely as on the night the grey woman had visited — as if the storm itself acknowledged the prophecy's first fulfilment (008-007).

### The Second Storm: The Wedding Catastrophe

Years passed, and [[the-shepherds-daughter|the shepherd's daughter]] grew to womanhood. The Prince, having seen her by the river, determined to marry her despite her refusals. The old King ordered great preparations for the wedding, and fine clothes were commissioned so she might appear properly as the Prince's wife (008-022). But in the days just before the wedding, the rain returned — not as ordinary weather but as an elemental onslaught.

The rain fell as it had never been known to fall. It beat through the roofs of cottages. The river swelled and overflowed its banks. Everyone was frightened — everyone except the shepherd's daughter, who went out into the wet and danced as was her custom, letting the torrents fall upon her head and shoulders (008-022). Her mother, blinded by ambition, saw only the royal elevation to come. On the eve of the wedding, the daughter knelt and begged: "Dear mother, let me stop with you and nurse you when you are old. Do not send me away to the palace to live with the King's son" (008-023). But the mother grew angry, calling her ungrateful for refusing such fortune (008-024).

All night long the rain fell in torrents. On the wedding morning, the bride stepped through pools on the ground to reach the grand carriage the King had sent (008-025). During the marriage service, the priest's voice could scarcely be heard for the pattering of the drops upon the roof. At the banquet, with goblets raised to toast the bride and bridegroom, the waters burst through the doors (008-025). Servants ran into the hall crying out that the floods were pouring in, and in one moment every room was filled with water. The wedding guests scattered, thinking only of saving themselves.

When the hurricane subsided and the waters receded, [[the-prince|the Prince]]'s wife was nowhere to be found. Everyone said she had been swept away by the torrents and drowned in all her youth and beauty (008-025). Only [[the-shepherds-wife|the shepherd's wife]] wept alone, and remembered the words of the woman who had come to her on the night of the first storm: "When you love aught on earth better than your daughter and her happiness, she will go from you" (008-025).

## Participants

- [[the-rain-maiden]] — The supernatural grey-clad woman who arrives in the first storm, delivers the prophecy, and whose elemental power drives the second storm. She controls rain as her domain and cannot be touched by it (008-001, 008-006, 008-022).
- [[the-shepherds-wife]] — The mother whose longing for a child is answered by the prophecy, but whose ambition for royal status — loving wealth and position above her daughter's happiness — triggers the prophecy's fulfilment (008-005, 008-017, 008-024-025).
- [[the-shepherds-daughter]] — The rain maiden's child by prophecy, born with an affinity for rain, water, and dancing in storms. She is the bridge between mortal and elemental worlds, reclaimed by the flood (008-007-009, 008-025).
- [[the-prince]] — The King's son who falls in love with the shepherd's daughter by the river and insists on marrying her against her will, setting the stage for the catastrophic wedding (008-011-013, 008-015-016).
- [[the-king]] — The old King who orders the wedding preparations and whose palace cannot withstand the elemental fury of the flood (008-022, 008-025).

## Outcome

The immediate outcome was the disappearance of the shepherd's daughter — swept away by the flood at the very moment of her elevation to queen. The wedding banquet became a scene of chaos and flight, and the bride who had been dressed in white and gold vanished into the waters that were her true element (008-025). The Prince mourned his wife and for many years would not be comforted. When he eventually married a beautiful Princess and lived happily, still whenever the rain fell in torrents he would hear the sound of dancing feet and a voice calling: "Come and dance with me, come and dance with me and my brothers and sisters, oh, King's son, and feel our drops upon your face" (008-026).

The deeper outcome was the fulfilment of the rain maiden's prophecy. The shepherd's wife, in her moment of grief, finally understood what she had lost and why. Her ambition — her [[love]] for the status and wealth that a royal marriage would bring — had displaced her love for her daughter's happiness. The prophecy's condition, spoken amid the first great storm and remembered only after the second, proved absolute (008-006, 008-025).

## Significance

The Great Storm is the story's moral engine and dramatic climax. It transforms "The Rain Maiden" from a simple tale of a poor girl marrying a prince into a tragedy about the cost of misplaced love. The storm is both promise and judgement: it brings the gift of a child through the rain maiden's first visit, and it takes that child back when the mother's heart turns from daughter to ambition (008-006, 008-025).

The storm also serves as the boundary between the mortal and elemental realms. The shepherd's daughter belongs to the rain — she dances in it, calls the raindrops her brothers and sisters, and cannot be made to love fire or sun (008-008). Her disappearance into the flood is not merely a death but a return: she goes back to the element that gave her life, joining the rain maiden whose prophecy shaped her destiny. The Prince, even years later, hears her voice in every torrential rain, calling him to dance — a haunting reminder that some beings cannot be contained by palaces or royal titles (008-026).

Thematically, the storm embodies the fairy-tale principle that supernatural gifts come with conditions, and that [[keeping-faith|faithfulness]] — here, the mother's fidelity to loving her daughter above all else — is the price of keeping what the supernatural realm bestows. Unlike [[the-windfairies]] who reward Lucilla's faithfulness with miraculous rescue, the rain maiden enforces the prophecy's terms with implacable elemental force. The two storms together — first as gentle prophecy, then as annihilating flood — form a complete narrative arc in which the supernatural visits the mortal world, offers a conditional gift, and returns to reclaim it when the condition is broken.
