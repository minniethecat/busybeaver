# The Hills

The hills form the elevated terrain that shapes the physical and symbolic geography of the tales. From the "rocky hill" where Hulda pauses to see the distant sea (007-036) to the hillside where Nanina watches her sheep and glimpses the old palace (004-003), the hills are a landscape of transition -- between village and wilderness, safety and danger, the known and the enchanted.

## Description

The hills rise above the villages and roads that connect the story-world. They are places of vantage: "high up on the hill the palace was a mass of ruined walls" (001-066). From a hilltop, "afar the sea could be seen when the weather was fine" (007-036). The hillside is where shepherds graze their flocks -- Nanina "sat looking at the old palace on the other side of the hill" (004-003) and later encounters the fairy shepherd there. The hills mark boundaries: the old palace stands "on the other side of the hill, for it is filled with wicked fairies" (004-001), making the hill itself the threshold between the ordinary and the perilous.

## Location

The hills rise between the coastal downs and the inland villages. The windmill stands "on the downs by the sea" (001-001), where hill-meets-coast. The King's ruined palace sits "high up on the hill" above the town (001-029) (001-066). A schoolhouse stands "on the hill" (007-046) along Hulda's journey. The hills connect the scattered settlements -- villages, windmills, palaces -- forming the elevated paths that characters travel between story locations.

## Events

### Tales of Discovery

1. **The ruined palace** -- Lucilla is shown "above the town on the hill... a roofless palace" (001-029), the ruined seat of a former king. Later, during her trial by dance, "high up on the hill the palace was a mass of ruined walls" (001-066) as the storm breaks.

2. **Nanina's vigil** -- Nanina drives her sheep "to the other side of the hill" (004-003) where she can see the old palace. It is on this hillside that she first hears the fairy shepherd's pipe (004-004).

### Tales of Journey

3. **Hulda's quest** -- Hulda goes "straight down the hill and journeyed for a long way" (007-041) in search of Othmar. The hills frame her quest: the schoolhouse "on the hill" (007-046), the baker "a mile up the hill" (007-051).

4. **The traveller's return** -- In "The Gipsy's Cup," the potter watches "a man coming over the hill, who looked spent and tired" (005-141) -- the hills are the approach to homecoming and revelation.

## Symbolism

### Threshold and Boundary

The hills represent transition and threshold. To go over the hill is to leave the familiar behind -- Othmar follows the dwarf "over the hill" into danger (007-007); Nanina crosses to "the other side of the hill" into fairy territory (004-003). The ruined palace on the hill (001-066) is a memento mori of earthly power -- what was once a seat of kings is now broken stone.

### Perspective and Vision

Yet the hills are also places of perspective: they offer the widest views of the story-world, linking the sea to the villages, the palaces to the roads. From the hilltops, characters see what lies ahead and what they have left behind. In the moral geography of the tales, the hills embody the necessary ascent before every significant journey -- a physical elevation that mirrors the spiritual and emotional climbing each protagonist must undertake.
