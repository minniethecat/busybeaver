# Lucilla's First Dance with the Windfairies

Lucilla's first dance with the windfairies is the foundational supernatural event of "The Windfairies" — the moment the child Lucilla receives the gift of enchanted dancing and enters into a covenant of secrecy that will shape the rest of her life.

## Context

Lucilla was the miller's young daughter, living with her father in a windmill on the downs. With "no other children to play with," she was "alone nearly all day, and had to amuse herself as best she could" [001-001]. One night, the wind began to blow, and the mill sails turned — and with them came the windfairies, riding the sails like a carousel. Lucilla watched them from her bed, entranced.

The windfairies noticed the child. One of them, holding Lucilla in her "tiny white arms," whispered to the others: "She is quite good. I really think we might teach her to dance, for she has not cried at all" [001-009]. This assessment — that Lucilla was worthy because she showed no fear — determined her fate.

## The Event

### Descent to the Downs

The windfairies slid off the mill sail with Lucilla in their arms and carried her gently down to the grass of the downs [001-011]. Now standing on the earth among the supernatural beings, Lucilla did not shrink back. Instead, she cried out: "Teach me to dance, I beg. I will never tell anyone where I learnt it" [001-011].

### The Warning and the Promise

The windfairies hesitated. One who had not yet spoken voiced the ancient fairy concern: no mortal could keep a secret. "Never yet was one known who could be silent," she warned [001-016]. They told Lucilla plainly: if she broke her word, she would never dance again, for her feet would become heavy like other mortals'.

Lucilla pleaded earnestly: "Teach me, teach me. Indeed I will never, never tell, and I long to dance as you do" [001-017]. The windfairies relented and gave their formal warning: "Well then, you may try. Only remember, if you break your word to us, and tell any mortal who it was that taught you how to dance, you will never dance again" [001-016].

### The Gift

Then the fairies began their work. Some came behind her, some in front — some took her arms and some her feet. All at once Lucilla felt a strange lightness spread through her body. As the text describes it: "She felt as if she were made of feather-down. She swayed up and down as lightly as they, and it seemed to her quite easy. Never had she been so happy" [001-018]. She would gladly have danced for hours.

### The Ending

The dance ended only when the sun began to show a red light in the eastern sky and the sound of her father's horse galloping across the downs broke the spell. In an instant the windfairies vanished without a trace.

When the miller reached her, he was angry to find her "out on the grass instead of warm in bed," but Lucilla "dared not tell him what had kept her" [001-019]. The promise of secrecy had already begun to bind her.

## Outcome

Lucilla returned to the mill unable to explain her night adventure. For "years passed, and Lucilla never saw the windfairies again, though she watched for them every night" [001-020]. But the gift remained. As she grew into a beautiful young woman, she retained the ability to dance with a lightness no mortal could match — "as if she were made of feather-down" [001-022]. This gift would draw suitors, then the attention of a king, and ultimately place her in mortal danger when she was ordered to reveal its source.

## Significance

This event establishes the central covenant of "The Windfairies": the supernatural gift of dance given in exchange for a promise of eternal secrecy. This bargain — skill for silence — becomes the engine of the entire story. Lucilla's dancing gift elevates her from miller's daughter to court performer to condemned prisoner, all because of the promise she made as a child in the dark on the downs.

Notably, the windfairies do not seek Lucilla out for her beauty or cleverness — they notice her because she shows no fear and has no companions. Her isolation makes her both available to them and capable of keeping their secret: a child with no one to tell.

The scene also establishes the windfairies' character as beings who are neither wholly benevolent nor malevolent. They give a magnificent gift freely, but with a condition that will eventually bring Lucilla to the brink of execution. Their magic is transactional, and the terms are absolute.
