# The Banker

The banker is a minor character in "Vain Kesta," appearing only in Kesta's escalating boasts about the men who supposedly want to marry her. He represents the professional class in the social hierarchy Kesta attempts to climb.

## Role in the Story

As Kesta makes her way from the farm to the town, she accumulates an ever-growing list of men she claims have proposed to her. By the time she reaches the Duke's garden, her catalogue of suitors has grown to include: "first the man at the farm, then the miller, then the duke's bailiff, then the banker, then the General of your army" (002-033).

The banker occupies the middle tier of Kesta's imagined suitors — above the farmhand and miller, but below the duke and general. His presence in her list illustrates the step-by-step nature of Kesta's vanity: each man she mentions has slightly higher social standing than the last.

## Significance

Though the banker never appears directly in the story, his inclusion in Kesta's litany serves a narrative purpose. The progression from farmhand → miller → bailiff → banker → general → duke maps the entire class structure of the story's world, with the banker representing the commercial middle class — those who hold economic power through money rather than land or title.

Kesta's claim that even the banker wanted to marry her is part of her self-deception. By the story's end, not even the farmhand Adam will have her.
