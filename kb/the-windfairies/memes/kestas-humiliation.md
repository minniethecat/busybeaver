After the Duke exposed Kesta as a common dairymaid and ordered her chased from the palace, she fled back through the town. Each of her previous suitors, upon learning the truth, joined the pursuit — creating a swelling cascade of public humiliation that formed the tale's most dramatic sequence.

## Context

The Duke, enraged to discover Kesta was a dairymaid who made cheeses, had his four pages shout after her: "Down with the impertinent hussy who wanted to marry the Duke, a common dairymaid who makes cheeses!" [002-048]. Kesta fled the palace grounds and began her desperate run back through town.

## The Chase Through Town

### The General's Rejection

Kesta ran first to the General's house, where he waited at his window in his fine uniform. She approached him breathlessly, pretending nothing was wrong: "See, I have come back to marry you as I promised" [002-050]. But the pursuing pages arrived shouting, "Away with the impertinent dairymaid, who thought herself good enough to marry the Duke!" [002-051]. When the General asked if even the Duke had refused her, the pages confirmed: "Of course not; she is nothing but a farm wench, and she is to be chased from the town for her impertinence" [002-053]. The General was outraged — not that Kesta had been rejected by the Duke, but that she had considered herself fit for him too. "She thought she was fit for me too—it is disgraceful!" he cried, and summoned soldiers to join the chase [002-054]. Before he had finished speaking, Kesta was already "running down the street with all her might to the banker's" [002-055].

### The Banker's Fury

At the banker's big square house beside the bank, Kesta arrived breathless. The banker himself stood on the steps in his shiny black clothes with gold watch-chains [002-055]. "Here I am," cried Kesta, "and let me in quickly, for I am out of breath with running" [002-056]. But as he spoke, the pages and soldiers came round the corner.

Kesta rushed inside the house, but the pursuers arrived shouting, "Away with her!" [002-059]. When the banker asked what she had done, they replied: "She thought herself good enough to marry the Duke and the General, and she is to be hooted out of the town for her impudence!" [002-063]. The banker's concern was purely hierarchical: when he confirmed that the General hadn't wanted her — "she's only a dairymaid, and not fit for him" — he reasoned, "Then I'm sure she can't be good enough for me, for I'm quite as good as he" [002-065][002-066]. In a rage, he summoned his clerks from the bank next door: "Here, my good fellows, help to chase this impudent jade from the town" [002-066].

"It's too hard," sobbed Kesta; "what have I done to be treated like this?" [002-067]. But she could not outrun them.

### The Bailiff's Indignation

At the bailiff's house, the growing crowd — now pages, soldiers, and clerks — arrived close behind Kesta, "shouting and gesticulating" [002-067]. When the bailiff asked why they were chasing "this poor maid," they explained: "She wanted to marry first the Duke, and the General, and the banker, and of course they would not have her, because she is only a common dairymaid" [002-069]. The bailiff now remembered she had approached him too. "What impertinence!" he cried, "and, now I come to think of it, she asked to marry me too; indeed she merits punishment for such behaviour." He summoned his farm workers to drive her out of town [002-070].

### The Miller's Vengeance

Kesta reached the mill first, running into the garden where the miller stood. "I was in such a hurry to get back. Now let's go into the house," she said, trying to deflect him [002-071]. When the miller asked about the shouting crowd, Kesta tried to lie: "'Tis only the farmers bringing home pigs from the market" [002-073]. But the miller was not deceived. "Pigs don't make a noise like that," he said, "I will go and see what it is about" [002-074].

When he learned the truth — that Kesta had been rejected by the Duke, the General, the banker, and the bailiff — the miller flew into a rage. He ordered his men to "throw some flour at her, for she is an impudent hussy, and asked me to marry her" [002-074]. Kesta was covered in white flour, the mark of her true station made visible in humiliation.

- The Duke's Pages — the original pursuers, shouting her exposure at each stop [002-051]

## Outcome

Kesta fled from the town covered in flour and shame, pursued by a long line of pages, soldiers, clerks, and farm workers — a spectacle of public humiliation witnessed by the entire town. Even in her desperation, she clung to one last hope: "How unfortunate I am," she sobbed; "but anyhow I can go back to Adam; he's sure to want me" [002-075].

## Significance

The chase is the physical manifestation of Kesta's moral descent and the tale's structural set piece. Each suitor's rejection follows an identical logic that reveals the town's rigid class hierarchy: no man objects to Kesta for her deception itself, but each is outraged that she considered him beneath others who had already rejected her. The banker is not angry that Kesta lied — he is angry that "she can't be good enough for me, for I'm quite as good as" the General. The cumulative scorn creates a snowball effect that transforms Kesta from a scheming social climber into a figure of public ridicule, physically marked by the miller's flour.
