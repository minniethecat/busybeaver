## Overview

The farmer appears in several tales of the collection. In "Nanina's Sheep," he is an old farmer who employs Nanina as a shepherdess. In "Vain Kesta," the farm is the setting from which the hand Adam and later Kesta seek better fortunes.

## Appearances

### Nanina's Sheep

In "Nanina's Sheep," the farmer is Nanina's employer. She keeps sheep for this "old farmer" [004-001]. After Nanina encounters the magical beech tree and obtains the golden apple, the farmer returns home and is pleased with the growing flock:

> Next day when the farmer came home, he was well pleased [004-029]

### Vain Kesta

In "Vain Kesta," the farm setting is introduced through Adam, who works on a farm. Adam tells Kesta: "I should be a farmer and you should be the farmer's wife" [002-002]. This represents the farm life as a modest but honest alternative to the wealth and status Kesta pursues.

### Other Appearances

In "The Ploughman and the Gnome," the gnome promises the ploughman he will become "the richest farmer in the countryside" [009-014]. Farmers collectively appear as villagers bringing home pigs [002-073] and sowing fields before the snow falls [007-001].

## Role in the Tales

The farmer represents:
- **Honest rural labor** — contrasted with the vain pursuits of characters like Kesta
- **Economic stability** — farming is portrayed as a reliable, if humble, livelihood
- **The pastoral backdrop** — against which magical encounters unfold
