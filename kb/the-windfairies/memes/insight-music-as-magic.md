# Music as Magic in The Windfairies

## Thesis

In the world of The Windfairies, music is not mere art or entertainment — it is a supernatural force in its own right, operating on the same plane as fairy enchantment, elemental power, and ritual magic. Across three of the collection's tales, music functions as a conduit between the mortal and fairy realms, capable of transforming human bodies, stealing human voices, and compelling human wills. The dwarf musician's self-playing instruments (007-013), the fairy shepherd's singing pipe (004-004), and the windfairies' rhythm of the elements (001-010) are not metaphors for music's emotional power — they are literal manifestations of music as magic. And like all magic in these tales, music is morally ambivalent: it can be the highest expression of beauty and joy or an instrument of coercion and theft. The moral lies not in music itself but in the musician's intent and the hearer's response.

## Evidence

### The Dwarf Musician: Music That Steals and Restores

The most elaborate exploration of music's supernatural power in the collection occurs in "Dumb Othmar" (Chapter 7), where the [[the-dwarf-musician|dwarf musician]] arrives in the mountain village with a donkey "covered with numberless musical instruments. Fiddles of all sorts, and viols, horns, trumpets, and pipes, and a big drum, and a small one with triangles and cymbals" (007-002). These are no ordinary instruments. The dwarf declares that "in each is a machine which makes it go on playing by itself, if once I start it" (007-002), and when he plays, the effect is immediate and overwhelming: the music makes the assembled villagers "weep and laugh" (007-003), and to [[othmar|Othmar]] himself, "it seemed to him as if the sound were made of silver" (007-003). Yet the enchantment carries danger — [[hulda|Hulda]] alone "shuddered as she heard it" (007-004), sensing what the others cannot.

The instruments are not merely magical objects; they are living beings. When the dwarf "clapped his hands, giving a long, shrill whistle" (007-013), the instruments transform: "no longer did they look like musical instruments, but like human beings" (007-013). The flutes and pipes become "beautiful girls with slender throats," the trumpets become "men and boys of different sizes," and the drum becomes "a fat man with very short legs" (007-013). Music here is literally alive — the boundary between instrument and musician dissolves entirely. There are thirteen of these instrument-children (007-013), and the dwarf calls them "my children" (007-013), confirming that music in this tale is a form of embodied supernatural life.

The dwarf's music serves a predatory purpose. After a night of enchanted performance, the youngest violin-girl kisses Othmar on the lips while he sings and declares "that will be your last note for many a long year" (007-021), stealing his voice into herself (007-020). The dwarf's parting words — "When you hear my fiddles again, they will be sweeter than ever, for I have added your voice to them" (007-024) — reveal music as a zero-sum economy: beauty for one being is purchased with silence for another. The raven, who lost its own voice to the dwarf "thousands of years ago" (007-026), confirms that this is the dwarf's established method — a ritual of music-theft repeated across ages.

The horror of this theft is registered through Othmar's transformed relationship with music itself. After losing his voice, "the sound of any music was quite horrible to him" (007-035). The boy who once found music "made of silver" can no longer bear to hear it. Music has become synonymous with loss. Yet the tale's resolution restores the balance: Hulda's quest succeeds, and Othmar's voice is recovered. When it returns, it comes with "a burst of music from all around" (007-106) — music as restoration, music as wholeness regained. The dwarf departs quietly, leading his mule "as if nothing had happened" (007-069), and the tale closes the circle: music that stole is answered by music that heals.

### Othmar: Voice as Magical Gift

If the dwarf represents music as external enchantment, [[othmar|Othmar]] represents music as innate magical gift. Before the dwarf's arrival, Othmar is defined by his voice — he is the sweetest singer in the mountain village, a boy whose relationship with music is so natural that it approaches the supernatural. The narrative identifies him as one who "sang like the birds" (007-022), blurring the line between human art and the natural world's own music. His voice is not a skill he acquired but a gift he possesses — something closer to fairy endowment than to mortal accomplishment.

Othmar is the only listener who does not find the dwarf's music beautiful (007-004). This detail is crucial: Othmar's innate musical sensitivity makes him immune to the dwarf's enchantment even as it marks him as the dwarf's target. He perceives something wrong in the music that others, less gifted, cannot detect. His instinctive rejection of the dwarf's sound proves to be wisdom rather than deficiency — but it does not save him. The tale suggests that possessing a supernatural gift makes one vulnerable to supernatural predation: the dwarf does not steal from just anyone but from the village's finest singer, as if the quality of the voice determines its value as a prize.

After his muting, Othmar's relationship with music inverts entirely. The boy who sang like the birds now finds music horrible (007-035). His silence is not merely physical but existential — he is severed from the medium that defined his identity. When Hulda eventually recovers his voice and declares "Othmar, I have done you a service" (007-105), what is restored is not merely the ability to speak but the capacity to participate in music's supernatural economy on the side of creation rather than theft.

### The Fairy Shepherd's Pipe: Compulsive Dance

In "Nanina's Sheep" (Chapter 4), music takes the form of the [[the-fairy-shepherd|fairy shepherd]]'s [[the-magic-pipe|enchanted pipe]], an instrument whose power is compulsion rather than theft. The pipe does not merely sound — it speaks. Its notes form words that [[nanina|Nanina]] cannot ignore:

> "When the young birds sing, / And the young plants spring, / Then dance we so merrily together, oh." — (004-005–007)

The pipe's music speaks directly "to her heart" (004-004). This is not metaphor — the text insists on the literalness of the communication. The pipe addresses Nanina in language she understands, and its message is irresistible. "When once she heard the pipe, Nanina could not keep still" (004-017). The compulsion operates directly on the body, bypassing rational choice: "as I hear the pipe beginning, my feet begin to move of themselves, it is no use my trying" (004-021).

The pipe's moral ambivalence is demonstrated through its shifting tone. When Nanina resists the fairy, "it was not merry dance-music it made, but long sad notes, like a funeral march" (004-025). When the temptation intensifies, "the music grew quicker, and her longing to dance grew stronger" (004-028). The pipe's music tracks the narrative's emotional arc with precision — merry, sad, urgent — adapting its character to Nanina's spiritual state. It is less an instrument than an intelligence, a supernatural agent that uses sound as its medium of persuasion.

The beech tree's counsel reveals the terms of the enchantment: "Every time you refuse to dance with the fairy, one of your flock will be returned" (004-020). But refusal is precisely what the pipe makes impossible. The tree's solution is brutally physical — Nanina must bury her feet in the earth among the tree's roots, letting the roots hold her immobile while the music tears at her will (004-022, 004-023). The image is stark: a human being literally rooted to the ground to resist the pull of supernatural music. When the ordeal ends, it ends with music — "one long low note on the pipe" (004-028) — and "with a mighty crash like thunder the shepherd and the goats disappeared, and not only had they gone, but the walls of the old palace had fallen, and nothing was left of it but a heap of stones" (004-028). The final note destroys the entire fairy realm.

### The Windfairies' Dance: Supernatural Rhythm

In the collection's title story (Chapter 1), music appears in its most integrated form — inseparable from dance, from the wind itself, and from the elemental power of the [[windfairies]]. The windfairies are beings of perpetual motion, first seen "dancing and springing from the ground as lightly as thistledown" (001-001). Their offer to [[lucilla|Lucilla]] — "Should you like us to teach you how to dance as we dance?" (001-010) — frames dance not as a skill to be learned but as an initiation into the fairy mode of being.

Lucilla accepts, promising "I will never tell anyone where I learnt it" (001-011), and the windfairies transform her: "She felt as if she were made of feather-down" (001-018), swaying "up and down as lightly as they" (001-018). The gift of dance is a physical transformation, a re-making of the mortal body to participate in supernatural rhythm. The condition attached — "If ever you tell anyone in this world who it was that taught you how to dance, you will never dance again" (001-016) — establishes dance as a secret covenant between mortal and fairy, a bond maintained through silence.

When Lucilla dances at [[the-king|the King]]'s court years later, the scene collapses the distinction between dance and music: "The musicians began to play, and then Lucilla danced" (001-034). She dances "as light as the sea foam" (001-034), and the court erupts in applause "as had never been heard before" (001-035). Music here is the frame for transcendent human art — but the art itself originates in fairy enchantment. The court musicians are mortal; the dance they accompany is not.

The windfairies' climactic intervention is itself a kind of anti-music — a "sudden roar of wind" (001-066) that sweeps an axe from an executioner's hand, topples houses, and reduces the royal palace to ruins while leaving Lucilla unharmed. This is music at its most elemental: the wind itself as instrument, the storm as symphony. The windfairies' power is not expressed through pipes or fiddles but through the raw sound of the elements they command.

## Comparative Analysis

These four manifestations of music-as-magic reveal a coherent supernatural economy operating across the collection. Music is never neutral. It always does something — compels movement, steals voice, transforms the body, summons elemental forces. And it always extracts a cost, whether that cost is borne by the hearer (Nanina's seven nights of agonized resistance), the performer (Othmar's stolen voice), or the environment (the destroyed palace in both "Nanina's Sheep" and "The Windfairies").

The collection distinguishes between music that is freely given and music that is coercively imposed. The windfairies teach Lucilla to dance as a gift, conditional only on secrecy — and when she keeps faith, they honour their covenant with the elemental rescue that saves her life (001-066). The fairy shepherd's pipe, by contrast, is an instrument of trial — Nanina does not seek it out but is subjected to it, and her ordeal is a test she must pass through suffering. The dwarf musician's instruments fall somewhere between: he offers his music freely, even generously, refusing payment with the words "I am very rich, and don't need it" (007-005), yet his generosity conceals a predatory purpose. The same music that makes the villagers weep and laugh is the mechanism by which Othmar's voice is stolen.

This taxonomy of musical magic maps onto a moral spectrum. At one end is Lucilla's dance — music as covenant, freely entered, faithfully kept, ultimately redemptive. In the middle is Nanina's ordeal — music as compulsion, resisted through suffering, ending in liberation. At the other end is Othmar's muting — music as theft, inflicted without consent, reversed only through heroic devotion. The collection does not condemn music itself but traces the moral quality of each musical encounter to the intentions of its supernatural source and the response of its human recipient.

A deeper pattern emerges when we consider what music replaces or displaces. In "Dumb Othmar," the dwarf's music replaces speech — Othmar's voice is literally transferred from his own body into the violin-girl's (007-020). In "Nanina's Sheep," the pipe's music replaces volition — Nanina's will is overridden by the sound. In "The Windfairies," the windfairies' dance replaces ordinary mortal movement with something supernaturally light and swift. Music in these tales is not an addition to ordinary life but a transformation of it — and transformation, in the fairy-tale logic of the collection, is always dangerous even when it is ultimately redemptive.

The tales also share a structural pattern: music that begins as enchantment must be answered by a counter-magic. Nanina's resistance at the beech tree, Hulda's quest with the raven's guidance, and Lucilla's faithful silence across decades of persecution — these are the human answers to supernatural music, and they determine whether the music's power becomes curse or cure. The collection's implicit argument is that the proper response to enchanted music is discernment, not surrender — to hear the beauty while recognizing the danger, and to oppose compulsion with steadfastness rather than with force.

## Conclusion

Music in The Windfairies is a supernatural force operating on the same plane as fairy enchantment. It transforms instruments into living beings (007-013), compels human bodies to dance against their will (004-021), steals voices across millennia (007-026), and summons storms that level cities (001-066). But the collection does not treat music as an unqualified good or an absolute evil. It is a medium — like language, like wind, like love — that amplifies the intentions of its wielder and reveals the character of its hearer. The dwarf musician's predatory music, the fairy shepherd's testing pipe, and the windfairies' covenantal dance are all expressions of the same supernatural principle: music bridges the mortal and fairy realms, and crossing that bridge always carries risk. What saves the collection's protagonists is not immunity to music's power but the right response to it — Lucilla's fidelity, Nanina's endurance, Hulda's love. The tales suggest that in a world where music is magic, the human virtues of faithfulness, courage, and devotion are the only reliable counter-spells.
