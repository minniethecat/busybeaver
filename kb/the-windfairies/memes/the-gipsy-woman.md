## Overview

The Gipsy woman is the late mother of the Gipsy protagonist in "The Gipsy's Cup." A poor widow who raised two children — a son and a daughter — she is remembered for her wisdom, resilience, and the moral lessons she imparted to her children.

## In the Story

Although the Gipsy woman has died before the main events of the tale, her influence pervades the story. Her son recalls her teachings throughout his adventures:

> "That is what my mother told me to do, if I wanted to prosper in the world" [005-096]

She taught her children to show kindness to animals and to help those in need. This wisdom becomes central to the Gipsy's success.

## Character and Legacy

The Gipsy woman raised her family in poverty as a widow [005-020]. Despite hardship, she passed on a code of conduct that emphasized compassion over greed. When the Gipsy encounters magical and dangerous situations, his mother's guidance is what sees him through:

> "I am all in rags, but if my mother had a handful of straw she could spin gold from it" [005-087]

Her legacy of helping the vulnerable — including taking in a stranger who was "brought...into your house to die" [005-094] — becomes a debt that others repay to her son.

## Significance

- Represents the **guiding voice of maternal wisdom** even beyond death
- Embodies the theme that **kindness is a form of wealth** more valuable than gold
- Her teachings form the **moral backbone** of "The Gipsy's Cup"
