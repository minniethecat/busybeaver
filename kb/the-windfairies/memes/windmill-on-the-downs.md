# The Sailor

The Sailor is a supporting character in "The Windfairies" (Chapter 1), a young seafarer whose bold courtship of [[lucilla|Lucilla]] and determined pursuit of her after the [[windfairies|Windfairies]] reclaim her mark him as the tale's embodiment of mortal love and perseverance. A "young fellow with black hair and white teeth" (001-022), he appears among a shipwrecked crew, wins Lucilla through strength rather than skill, and later risks everything to bring her back from the fairy realm.

## Role in the Story

The Sailor enters the story when a great storm drives his ship ashore near the [[windmill-on-the-downs]], where Lucilla has grown up under the Windfairies' protection. He functions as the mortal counterweight to the supernatural forces that claim Lucilla — where the Windfairies offer her flight and enchantment, the Sailor offers her a human life of [[home]], family, and [[love]].

### Courtship and Marriage

The Sailor's courtship of Lucilla is unconventional. Unlike the fishermen and country folk who tried and failed to match Lucilla's supernatural dancing, he passes her test through boldness: when he cannot out-dance her, he seizes her by the waist and holds her still, declaring that his arms are strong enough to keep her from dancing with any man but him (001-027). This moment — captured in the event page [[the-sailors-courtship]] — reveals the Sailor's defining quality: he does not try to compete with magic but finds another way entirely.

Lucilla accepts him, and they marry in a quiet ceremony recorded in [[lucillas-wedding]]. He takes her to live in [[the-cottage-by-the-sea]], a small dwelling on the beach many miles from the mill, where he supports the family by fishing (001-023). For some time they live happily, and Lucilla bears two children (001-024).

### The Bargain with the Windfairies

The Sailor's most dramatic role begins when the Windfairies return to claim Lucilla. While she dances on the shore, the grey figures sweep down and draw her back into their realm. The Sailor does not yield to despair. He gathers [[the-bag-of-gold]] — all the wealth he possesses — and goes to negotiate with the Windfairies for Lucilla's release (001-028). His gold cannot buy her freedom from beings who have no use for mortal currency, but his persistence moves the Windfairies to agree to a compromise: they will carry both the Sailor and Lucilla to a distant land where neither has ever been (001-029).

Together, husband and wife are transported to the Windfairies' great hall in a strange country — a [[journey]] that marks the transition from the tale's domestic middle section to its climactic court intrigue (001-031). The Sailor's importance endures through Lucilla's subsequent trials: it is for his sake, bound by the Windfairies' gift, that she refuses to dance for any other man even at the cost of her life — a fidelity that sustains her through the imprisonment and near-execution culminating in [[the-great-storm]] and [[the-windfairies-rescue]].

## Character Traits

The Sailor is defined by a set of traits that together make him the tale's model of steadfast mortal devotion.

### Decisive

He makes up his mind instantly and acts on his decisions without hesitation. Upon seeing Lucilla among the fisher folk, "he said to himself, 'I will marry that girl'" (001-022). This decisiveness extends to his actions throughout the story: when the Windfairies take Lucilla, he does not deliberate — he gathers his gold and pursues her.

### Determined

The Sailor's determination is his most prominent trait. He does not give up when faced with supernatural opposition. Confronted with Lucilla's impossible dance test, he finds a creative solution rather than admitting defeat (001-027). When the Windfairies — beings of elemental power — reclaim his wife, he follows with everything he has, offering his [[gold-wealth]] as ransom even knowing it may be worthless to them (001-028). His determination is rewarded: the Windfairies ultimately accede to his plea and agree to carry both husband and wife together.

### Loving

His love for Lucilla is the emotional engine of his every action. He promises to give up the sea — his livelihood and identity — to make a home with her (001-022). He is described as living "very happily" with her during their early married years (001-024). When she is taken from him, his love drives him to pursue her across the boundary between the mortal and fairy worlds. It is for his sake that Lucilla, imprisoned by the King, refuses to dance for any other man — her fidelity to her husband mirrors his fidelity to her, a mutual [[keeping-faith]] that the tale celebrates.

### Adventurous

As a sailor by trade, he is comfortable with risk and the unknown. He travels to "a country of which Lucilla had never even heard" without fear (001-029). His profession has prepared him for the tale's central test: venturing into the Windfairies' realm is, in a sense, the ultimate sea voyage — a journey into an element as vast and uncontrollable as the ocean itself. This connection between his identity as a sailor and his willingness to follow Lucilla into the fairy world ties his character to the broader motif of [[the-sea]].

## Key Actions

1. **Meeting Lucilla** — Among a group of sailors who come ashore near the windmill, he sees Lucilla and is immediately struck by her beauty. He decides then and there to marry her, setting the entire human plot in motion (001-022).

2. **The dance test** — When Lucilla demands that any suitor must first prove he can dance with her, the Sailor accepts the challenge. Finding he cannot match her supernatural lightness, he catches her by the waist and holds her still — the only suitor clever enough to bypass the test rather than fail it (001-027). This action defines his character and earns him Lucilla's hand.

3. **Marriage and domestic life** — He marries Lucilla and brings her to live in [[the-cottage-by-the-sea]], where they enjoy a happy life together and raise two children. He supports the family by fishing, fulfilling the promise he made during his courtship (001-023, 001-024).

4. **The ransom attempt** — When the Windfairies draw Lucilla back to their realm, the Sailor gathers [[the-bag-of-gold]] and begs them to release her. Though his mortal wealth cannot compel fairy beings, his determination moves them to offer an alternative (001-028).

5. **The journey to the Windfairies' realm** — Together with Lucilla, he is transported to the Windfairies' great hall in a strange country, crossing the boundary between the mortal and fairy worlds at her side (001-029, 001-031).

## Relationships

- [[lucilla]] — His wife, the woman he loves and for whom he risks everything. Their marriage is the tale's central human relationship, a bond of mutual [[keeping-faith]] that endures through supernatural separation and mortal peril.
- [[windfairies]] — The supernatural beings who compete with him for Lucilla's allegiance. They are not his enemies in any simple sense — he bargains with them rather than fights them — but they represent a claim on Lucilla that his mortal love must reckon with.
- [[windmill-on-the-downs]] — The place where he first sees Lucilla and resolves to marry her, the origin point of their story.
- [[the-cottage-by-the-sea]] — Their marital home, the domestic space he provides and the symbol of the ordinary human happiness that the Windfairies disrupt and Lucilla ultimately returns to.
- [[the-bag-of-gold]] — His earthly wealth, which he offers freely in his attempt to ransom his wife, demonstrating both the depth of his love and the limits of material value against fairy power.

## Significance

The Sailor represents the worth and dignity of ordinary human love in a world populated by supernatural powers. He has no magic, no special gift, no fairy patronage — only his decisiveness, his determination, and his willingness to risk everything for the woman he loves. In a tale where the Windfairies can fly, summon storms, and reduce palaces to rubble, the Sailor's power is of a different order: the power of steadfast fidelity.

His role parallels and contrasts with the Windfairies themselves. Both love Lucilla; both claim her allegiance; both offer her a kind of home. But where the Windfairies' love is conditional on secrecy and exacts a toll of isolation, the Sailor's love builds a shared life — a cottage, children, a community of fisher folk. The tale does not ask Lucilla to choose between them permanently — she belongs to both realms — but the Sailor's presence ensures that the human realm has a champion, and that Lucilla's return to ordinary life at the story's end is not a defeat but a restoration.

His most distinctive act — seizing Lucilla by the waist when he cannot out-dance her — encapsulates his thematic function. He does not try to match magic. He finds another way. He honors Lucilla's gift without competing with it, making him the one mortal who truly understands what she is.
