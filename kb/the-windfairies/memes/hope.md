# Hope

The belief that suffering is not final, that help will come, that faithfulness will be rewarded — the emotional engine that drives characters through their darkest hours and is never disappointed in these tales.

## Definition

Hope in these tales is not wishful thinking. It is the conviction, grounded in the promise of supernatural aid, that endurance will be vindicated. Lucilla does not merely hope vaguely that things will improve — she hopes specifically in the windfairies' promised protection: "while I keep faith with you, you will keep faith with me" (001-061). Hope is anchored in covenant: the supernatural beings have made promises, and hope is trust that those promises will be kept (001-016). This distinguishes hope from optimism — hope has a ground, and that ground is the moral structure of the tale-world itself (001-067).

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

Hope sustains Lucilla through imprisonment, near-drowning, near-burning, and the scaffold. Each time she prays to the windfairies, she is renewing her hope — not creating it from nothing but reasserting her trust in the covenant (001-046) (001-058) (001-063). Her hope is never disappointed. The windfairies save her from water, from fire, and finally from the executioner's axe (001-067). The tale is hope's fullest vindication.

### In "Vain Kesta"

Hope operates ironically. Kesta "hopes" to marry each successive richer man (002-004) (002-009), but her hope is really ambition — untethered from any moral ground. The tale distinguishes true hope (grounded in promise and fidelity) from mere wishing (grounded in vanity).

### In "The Story of a Cat"

The cat's actions might be read as hope in action — the hope that cleverness and persistence can overcome greed and cruelty. The cat's patience, "lapping not greedily or in a hurry" (006-015), models hope as steady perseverance rather than anxious grasping.

## Associated Characters

- **Lucilla** — Hope's exemplar: her hope in the windfairies' protection is never shaken, even at the scaffold (001-061) (001-065)
- **Kesta** — False hope: her "hopes" are ambitions, confused with genuine expectation of good (002-004)
- **The Ploughman** — Quiet hope: his honest dealing with the gnome embodies hope as trust in fair exchange (009-016)

## Moral Dimension

Hope is the most rewarded disposition in the tales. It is never shown to be foolish, never refuted by events. Lucilla's hope is vindicated at every turn. The tales' optimism is not naive — they acknowledge real suffering and real danger — but it is absolute: the moral universe is structured so that hope grounded in fidelity will be rewarded. "Now, dear windfairies, do I indeed know that you have kept faith with me" (001-067) is hope's consummation — the moment when sustained trust meets its vindication. The tales teach that hope is rational in a morally ordered universe (001-067).
