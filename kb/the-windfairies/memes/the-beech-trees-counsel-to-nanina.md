# The Beech Tree's Counsel to Nanina

After fourteen nights of enchanted dancing had cost her all but one of her sheep, Nanina sat weeping beneath a beech tree near the fairy palace. Her act of kindness toward the tree — binding its broken branches — earned her supernatural counsel that would prove essential to breaking the fairy shepherd's spell in "Nanina's Sheep" (Story 4).

## Context

For a fortnight, Nanina had been unable to resist the fairy shepherd's magical pipe. Each night she danced with him till dawn, and each morning another lamb was missing from her flock. With her master due to return and only one sheep remaining, Nanina was in despair (004-017).

## The Wounded Tree

Weary from weeping, Nanina leaned against the trunk of a beech tree growing near the ruined palace. She noticed that someone had torn down the lowest branches, leaving them "hanging down broken" (004-018). Moved by instinctive compassion, she raised the broken boughs and tied them up so they might grow together again.

As she did so, she heard a shadowy voice:

> "Thank you, Nanina; Nanina, don't dance." (004-018)

Looking up, she saw among the leaves "a small twisted face" looking at her. The voice came from the beech tree itself:

> "Thank you, Nanina, for saving my bough. And if you mind me, you shall get all your sheep back again." (004-018)

## The Counsel

Nanina cried out that she would do anything to recover her flock. The tree's advice was simple but painful:

> "Then you must not dance. Every time you refuse to dance with the fairy, one of your flock will be returned." (004-020)

When Nanina protested that her feet moved of themselves when she heard the pipe, the tree offered a physical solution:

> "Bury your feet in the earth like my roots. Dig a hole deep down, and I will hold your feet so that you shall not move them, only you must bear the pain, and not mind if you walk lame afterwards, for I shall hold them very tight, and it will hurt you." (004-022)

## Nanina's Acceptance

Nanina accepted without hesitation:

> "Hurt me as you please, and I shan't mind. If only I can get back my sheep I will bear any pain." (004-023)

She knelt beneath the tree, dug a deep hole among its roots, and placed her feet into the loose earth. She felt something moving near them, tightening around and drawing them far down into the ground, holding them "as if they were bound with cords" (004-023).

## Significance

This event marks the turning point in Nanina's story. Unlike Kesta in "Vain Kesta," whose encounters with strangers feed her vanity, Nanina's encounter with the beech tree rewards her selfless act of kindness. The tree's counsel — to resist enchantment through physical rootedness, accepting pain as the price of freedom — embodies the tale's moral: obedience and sacrifice bring restoration, while disobedience brings loss. The scarlet flowers that later spring from where Nanina's feet bled among the tree's roots become a permanent memorial to her painful redemption (004-030).
