## Context

On Christmas Day, a rich but miserly old gentleman — who "never loved anything but himself" (006-001) and thought of nothing except saving and making more money — had refused a freezing beggar's three requests for charity. The beggar asked first for a silver shilling for a Christmas dinner (006-006), then for a single copper penny (006-008), and finally for mere broken victuals from the table (006-010). Each time the old gentleman refused, stamping his foot and threatening to call the police (006-011). The beggar departed laughing merrily, looking back to call out words "in a foreign tongue" that the old gentleman could not understand (006-012).

## The Event

The next morning, the snow fell more thickly than ever and the streets were almost impassable (006-013). The old gentleman, comfortable in the knowledge that he need not go out, came down to breakfast and found something utterly unexpected:

> "There was a cat on the hearthrug in front of the fire, looking into it, and blinking lazily." (006-013)

The old gentleman had never kept an animal in his house before. He immediately went to it and said "Shoo-shoo!" and tried to turn it out — but "the cat did not move" (006-013). When he looked at it more closely, he found himself unable to dislike it. The cat was "very large, grey and black" with "extremely long soft hair, and a thick soft ruff round its neck" (006-013). It looked well-fed and well-cared-for, as if it had always lived in comfortable places (006-013).

Most curiously, "it seemed to the old gentleman to suit the room and the rug and the fire, and to make the whole place look more prosperous and cosy even than it had done before" (006-013). The cat's presence enhanced the very domestic comfort that the old gentleman had hoarded so jealously for himself alone.

## The Mystery

Several details marked this arrival as supernatural. Though the ground outside was deep in snow and slush, "the cat was quite dry, and its fur looked as if it had just been combed and brushed" (006-015). The cook declared she had not seen it before and believed "it must have come down the chimney as all the doors and windows had been shut and bolted" (006-015).

The cat's behaviour was equally unlike an ordinary stray. When given a large saucer of milk, it lapped it up "not greedily or in a hurry, but as if it were quite used to good food and had had plenty of it always" (006-015). The old gentleman, ever mindful of profit, decided to keep it while looking for a reward notice — but found none (006-016).

The old gentleman began "to take a sort of interest in the strange cat" (006-017), wondering if it was Persian or Siamese or some curious new breed. He liked "the sound of its lazy contented purring after its food, which seemed to speak of nothing but comfort and affluence" (006-017). The cat stayed on, and nearly a year passed (006-017).

## Participants

- [[the-old-gentleman]] — The miser whose life is about to be transformed by the cat's presence; his uncharacteristic tolerance — he who never loved anything — signals that the spell is already beginning to work (006-013, 017)

## Outcome

The cat made itself at home, following the old gentleman from room to room, sleeping on his bed, and refusing to leave. The old gentleman, who "never loved anything but himself" (006-017), found himself increasingly attached to the creature. This attachment would prove transformative: the cat's presence — and later its illness — would teach the old gentleman to care for something beyond his money. Later, a knowledgeable acquaintance would identify the cat as a valuable rare breed worth a great deal at the Cat Show, particularly since the new Princess was "quite crazy about cats" and "doesn't mind what she gives for a cat if she sees one she likes" (006-025–026).

## Significance

The cat's arrival is the supernatural response to the old gentleman's Christmas cruelty. The timing (the morning after Christmas, during heavier snow) and the cat's inexplicable presence in a locked, bolted house mark it as explicitly magical — likely connected to the laughing beggar and his foreign words. The cat's immediate aesthetic harmony with the room ("suit the room and the rug and the fire") suggests it is not a stray but a deliberately placed agent of moral transformation. Its dry fur, well-groomed appearance, and refined manners all point to a creature not of the ordinary world but of a magical order — a fairy-tale test disguised as a domestic pet.
