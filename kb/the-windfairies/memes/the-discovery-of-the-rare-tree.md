## Context

A tree and a pool lived in mutual devotion in the middle of a vast wilderness, loving each other "better than anything else on earth" (003-001). They thought of nothing but each other and cared not who came and went in the world around them (003-001).

Their bond was one of deep mutual dependence. The pool declared that without the tree's shade it would have been "dried up by the sun long ago" (003-002), while the tree answered that without the pool's "shining face" it would never have seen itself or known what its boughs and blossoms were like (003-003). Each year, when winter froze the pool and stripped the tree bare, they endured the cold silence together, counting each returning leaf and blossom as spring returned (003-004).

## The Event

One day, two travellers rode across the moorland in search of rare plants and flowers (003-005). Hot and tired, they dismounted and sat in the shade of the tree, speaking gloomily of their fruitless expedition. One of them declared it "scarcely worth while to come so far for so little" (003-005).

The elder traveller, dismissing his comrade's pessimism with the wisdom that "One may hunt for many years before one finds anything very rare" (003-006), absently picked up a fallen leaf — and "at once he sprang to his feet, and pulled down one of the branches to examine it" (003-006). He called his companion over, and together they closely examined the leaves and blossoms, declaring with growing excitement that this tree was "the best thing they had found in all their travels" (003-006).

Crucially, neither the pool nor the tree paid any attention to the travellers. The pool "lay looking lovingly up to the tree, and the tree gazed down at the clear water of the pool, and they wanted nothing more" (003-006). Their absorption in each other rendered them wholly oblivious to the moment that would soon destroy their world.

## Immediate Aftermath

The travellers mounted their horses and rode away — but not for good. The summer passed and the cold winds of autumn blew (003-007). The tree and pool, still unaware of what the discovery portended, prepared for their seasonal parting. "Soon your leaves will drop and you will fall asleep for the winter, and we must bid each other goodbye," said the pool (003-008). The tree answered with quiet confidence: "And you too when the frost comes will be numbed to ice; but never mind, the spring will follow, and the sun will wake us both" (003-009).

But that spring would not come as they expected. Before winter had fully arrived, before the last leaf had fallen, the travellers returned with men, spades, and a wagon — and uprooted the tree (003-010–012).

## Outcome

The two travellers mounted their horses and rode away — but not for good. The tree and pool remained unaware of what the discovery portended (003-006). Before the winter set in, the travellers would return with a crew of men, spades, and a wagon to uproot the tree and carry it away (003-010–012).

## Significance

The discovery of the rare tree is the inciting event of "The Pool and the Tree." It transforms the timeless bond between the tree and the pool from a private idyll into a threatened relationship. The botanists' purely scientific interest in the tree's rarity blinds them to the living connection they are about to sever — a quiet commentary on how the pursuit of knowledge, when divorced from empathy, can destroy what it seeks to preserve. The tree and pool's complete indifference to being "discovered" — they "wanted nothing more" than each other (003-006) — underscores the story's central contrast between natural contentment and human restlessness.
