# Wonder

The delighted astonishment that beauty and magic evoke — the emotional signature of the fairy-tale mode, marking moments when the mundane world opens onto the supernatural.

## Definition

Wonder in these tales is not passive amazement. It is the active capacity to be moved by beauty, by dancing, by the sight of fairy shepherd boys and enchanted pools. Characters who experience wonder are open to the supernatural; those who are merely calculating or ambitious (the King, the Queen, Kesta in her scheming mode) are closed to it. Wonder is the aesthetic counterpart to faith — both require openness to something beyond the self (001-020).

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

Wonder suffuses Lucilla's dancing. The courtiers and crowd are struck with wonder at her grace: "It is wonderful, it is marvellous" (001-026). The King loves "nothing so much as beautiful dancing" (001-027) — but his response to wonder is possessive rather than reverent. He wants to own the wonder rather than be transformed by it. The crowd's wonder at Lucilla's survival of drowning and fire (001-058) (001-060) is the proper response to supernatural intervention.

### In "Vain Kesta"

Wonder is notably absent. Kesta is never wonderstruck — she is always calculating. Her response to the miller, the bailiff, the banker is never "how wonderful" but always "how useful." Her incapacity for wonder is her spiritual poverty, the obverse of her vanity.

### In "Nanina's Sheep"

Nanina's encounter with the fairy shepherd boy is the collection's purest wonder scene. She sees him emerge from the ruined palace, dressed in "glittering green" with a "brown hat trimmed with leaves," playing a pipe as his black goats dance (004-004). The pipe's music speaks words — a moment of pure enchantment. Nanina is "open-mouthed" with wonder (004-004), and this openness, though it leads her into trouble, marks her as a character capable of encountering the fairy realm.

### In "The Pool and the Tree"

The pool's transformation into a cloud (003-021) is an object of wonder — natural beauty that shades into the supernatural, inviting contemplation rather than exploitation.

## Associated Characters

- **Lucilla** — The object of wonder: her dancing evokes wonder in all who see it (001-026)
- **Nanina** — The subject of wonder: her openness to enchantment defines her encounter with the fairy shepherd (004-004)
- **Kesta** — The absence of wonder: her calculating nature makes her spiritually impoverished

## Moral Dimension

Wonder is the tales' most positive emotional register. It is never punished, never satirized, never treated as dangerous naivety. Even Nanina's wonder-led disobedience is treated gently — her openness to enchantment, though it causes trouble, is fundamentally good. The tales implicitly argue that the capacity for wonder is the prerequisite for encountering grace. Those who cannot wonder — Kesta, the King, the Queen — are spiritually blind, and their blindness is their punishment (001-026).
