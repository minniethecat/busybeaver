# The Dwarf Musician

The Dwarf Musician is a supernatural creature in "Dumb Othmar" (Chapter 7), a diminutive figure in a distinctive yellow cap who leads a donkey laden with musical instruments into a remote mountain village. He is at once a seductive tempter and an agent of tragic transformation — his music comes at the price of [[othmar|Othmar]]'s voice — and his powers extend across thousands of years (007-026). Neither wholly evil nor capable of mercy in any conventional sense, the Dwarf operates according to his own inscrutable rules, proving himself to be a force of supernatural consequence that mortal characters can only dimly perceive.

## Nature

### A Spirit of Music

The Dwarf is a spirit of music and the underground. He appears unannounced among a gathering of village musicians in [[the-mountain-village|the mountain village]], instantly recognized as something not wholly human — "a dwarf in a yellow cap leading a donkey covered with fiddles" (007-002). His instruments are no ordinary devices: each contains "a machine which makes it go on playing by itself, if once I start it" (007-002), and when he sets them playing, the sound is so beautiful it makes the listeners "weep and laugh" (007-003). He embodies the folkloric motif of the supernatural musician who bestows extraordinary gifts while demanding an equally extraordinary price.

### An Ancient Being

The Dwarf is not merely old — he predates recorded time. When the [[the-raven|raven]] speaks to Othmar after the voice theft, it reveals that the Dwarf came to its nest "thousands of years ago" (007-026), stealing the raven's voice as well and leaving it with nothing but a harsh croak. This testimony places the Dwarf in a mythological timescale, a being who has been traveling from place to place across ages, collecting voices by the same ritual method — the kiss of one of his instrument-daughters on an open, singing mouth (007-020, 007-021).

## Appearance

### Physical Form

The Dwarf is small of stature, consistently described as "the little dwarf" (007-011) and "the little man" (007-002). His most distinctive feature is his yellow cap (007-002), which makes him immediately recognizable to the villagers. His size suggests a child, but his bearing and authority indicate great age. When he speaks, his voice carries the weight of command. When he gives "a terrible cry" underground, it "sounded like nothing earthly" (007-068), revealing the inhuman power beneath his diminutive form. He is referred to as "the little old man" (007-069), confirming his ancient appearance despite his small stature.

### The Donkey and Instruments

The Dwarf travels with a mule "on whose back were fastened numberless musical instruments. Fiddles of all sorts, and viols, horns, trumpets, and pipes, and a big drum, and a small one with triangles and cymbals" (007-002). He jealously guards these instruments, forbidding anyone to touch them and declaring "mine is no common music" (007-002). The instruments are not merely tools but extensions of his being — by night they transform into human-like creatures, each retaining something of its original shape: "The flutes and pipes were tall and thin, and they and the violins had changed into beautiful girls with slender throats, and the trumpets were all men and boys of different sizes, but the drum was the strangest of all, for it was a fat man with very short legs" (007-013). They are thirteen in number (007-013), and the Dwarf calls them "my children" (007-013).

## Powers & Abilities

### Music Enchantment

The Dwarf's primary power is music that transcends ordinary human experience. When he plays, his instruments continue performing by themselves after he sets them down (007-002). The sound makes the villagers "weep and laugh" (007-003), and for Othmar, "it seemed to him as if the sound were made of silver" (007-003). The music has a double effect: while most listeners are transported with joy, [[hulda|Hulda]] alone "shuddered as she heard it" (007-004), sensing the danger beneath the beauty.

### Voice Theft

The Dwarf's most devastating power is the capacity to steal a voice. He does not take it himself; rather, he directs the theft through his instrument-children. His youngest daughter, a violin-girl, kisses Othmar on the lips while he sings, declaring "that will be your last note for many a long year" (007-021), and transfers his voice into herself (007-020). The Dwarf then announces, "When you hear my fiddles again, they will be sweeter than ever, for I have added your voice to them" (007-024). The raven confirms this is the Dwarf's established method — it lost its voice identically millennia before (007-026).

### Dominion Over the Underground

The Dwarf exercises absolute authority over a subterranean realm. When he "waved his arms" (007-013), his instruments assemble into a "weird crowd" (007-013) and begin to perform. He sits "on a hillock near, and did not seem to heed them much" (007-014) — a silent overseer. Nocturnal creatures obey his presence: bats, owls, moths, wood-snakes, toads, and lizards gather in stillness to watch the performance (007-014), and even after the Dwarf departs, they remain "ranged around" (007-025), suggesting his lingering dominion over the animal kingdom.

### Agelessness

The Dwarf appears to be immortal or extraordinarily long-lived. The raven's testimony places his activities "thousands of years ago" (007-026), and his powers show no sign of diminishing across eras. He travels from place to place, repeating the same pattern — arrive in a village, play music, identify a gifted singer, and claim their voice through his ritual. His agelessness makes him less a character with a biography and more an eternal collector, a fixture of the natural-supernatural order.

## Role in the Story

### The Tempter

The Dwarf arrives in [[the-mountain-village|the mountain village]] as a seemingly benign entertainer. He plays his instruments for free, refusing payment with the words "I am very rich, and don't need it" (007-005), and asks only that the village's young singers perform for him in return. This apparent generosity conceals his purpose: identifying the most gifted voice to steal. He singles out Othmar immediately, watching him intently while others sing (007-006), and then requests that Othmar guide him over [[the-hills|the hill]] (007-007), initiating the journey that leads to the voice theft.

### The Silent Overseer

Throughout the underground concert, the Dwarf remains almost entirely passive. He sits "on a hillock near, and did not seem to heed them much" (007-014) while his instrument-children dance and sing. He does not participate in the actual theft — it is his youngest daughter who kisses Othmar (007-020, 007-021). The Dwarf's role is that of a director or ringmaster: he summons the performers with a clap and a whistle (007-013), sets the scene, and allows events to unfold according to patterns established long ago.

### The Instrument of Transformation

The Dwarf is never explicitly condemned by the narrative's wisest characters. When Hulda seeks help from the animal-healer, he replies, "I know nothing of dwarfs and witches" (007-044), and the old schoolmaster dismisses talk of fairies as "foolish talk" (007-049). This ambiguity is central to the Dwarf's nature. He does not lie — he warns Othmar to "be sure that you always use [your voice] well" (007-007) — and when his ritual is disrupted, he accepts the outcome without resistance, leading his mule "quietly away, as if nothing had happened" (007-069). He is a force of supernatural consequence, neither good nor evil, operating by rules that mortal characters can only dimly perceive.

## Dwelling / Realm

### The Underground Kingdom

The Dwarf's realm is accessed from the hilltops near the village. When Othmar follows him, they travel to "the top of a high hill where they could see the country all round for miles, and the moon was beginning to rise" (007-011). The underground kingdom itself is a vast space filled with strange forests and the Dwarf's thirteen instrument-children (007-013). It is visited by nocturnal animals — bats, owls, moths, wood-snakes, toads, and lizards — who do not belong to the ordinary day world and who assemble in silent witness whenever the Dwarf commands a performance (007-014).

### A Realm Outside Time

The underground kingdom operates on a different timescale from the surface world. Othmar believes he has only watched the dancers "for a few minutes" when the Dwarf suddenly cries "The dawn, the dawn, my children; see, there is red in the sky" (007-014), and an entire night has passed. The realm's inhabitants are bound to darkness: when the sun rises, they revert to their inanimate forms in a "great burst of sound like a chord from all the music folk, followed by a flash of light like lightning" (007-023). This temporal distortion mirrors the Dwarf's own agelessness — his realm exists outside the ordinary flow of time.

## Bargains & Conditions

### The Price of Music

The Dwarf's bargains are implicit rather than stated. He offers his music freely and asks only for songs in return (007-005). But the true price is never disclosed upfront: those who follow him may lose what they value most. Othmar follows expecting only to hear more music, and instead becomes the victim of a voice theft he never saw coming (007-024). The Dwarf's parting words — "be sure that you always use it well" (007-007) — may be read as either authentic advice or a veiled condition, a warning whose breach, however unwitting, justifies the theft that follows.

### The Unwritten Rules

The Dwarf operates by rules that govern his world but remain invisible to human perception. His instrument-children must return to their inanimate forms at dawn — the sun's rays destroy their human shapes (007-023). The voice theft itself is performed through a ritual kiss on an open, singing mouth (007-020, 007-021), suggesting a ceremonial logic rather than mere malice. When the voice is eventually recovered through Hulda's quest — by following the raven's instruction and using the [[the-green-snake|green snake]] to destroy the violin-girl (007-066, 007-067) — the Dwarf does not resist or retaliate. He gives "a terrible cry" that "sounded like nothing earthly" (007-068), but then quietly packs his instruments and leaves (007-069), as if acknowledging that the rules, however obscure, have been satisfied.
