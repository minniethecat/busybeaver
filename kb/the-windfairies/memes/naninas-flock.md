# Nanina's Flock

The flock of sheep entrusted to Nanina by the old farmer is both her responsibility and the story's narrative engine. The loss of one lamb sets the plot in motion; the flock's enchanted dancing to the fairy shepherd's pipe creates the tale's most vivid image; and Nanina's determination to recover every last sheep defines her character. The flock is at once ordinary livestock and the medium through which the supernatural tests a mortal girl.

## Description

Nanina's flock is a modest herd of sheep kept for an old farmer. She "drove her flock to the other side of the hill" (004-003) to graze -- a routine pastoral task that becomes the gateway to enchantment. The flock includes at least one lamb, whose disappearance triggers the crisis: "she counted her sheep, and, alas! there was one lamb missing" (004-016). The sheep are not merely passive livestock; they are susceptible to the fairy pipe's enchantment, dancing alongside the fairy shepherd's black goats: "the black goats and sheep all skipping merrily" (004-012). The flock thus occupies the threshold between the natural and supernatural worlds -- ordinary animals that become extraordinary under fairy influence.

## Properties

### Enchantment-Sensitive

- **Enchantment-sensitive** -- The flock responds viscerally to the fairy shepherd's pipe, dancing involuntarily when the music plays: "the goats dance and spring in time to the music" (004-012)
- **Plural and vulnerable** -- The flock's safety depends entirely on Nanina's vigilance; the loss of even one lamb is a catastrophe she cannot accept
- **Symbol of duty** -- The flock represents the mortal responsibility that Nanina must honour even when the fairy realm exerts its strongest pull: her sheep matter more than the music's beauty
- **Restored through sacrifice** -- The missing lamb is only recovered when Nanina endures seven nights of dancing at the fairy's court, making the flock's wholeness the tangible reward of her ordeal

## Role in the Story

### Loss and Crisis

The flock is the story's organising principle. Every major event is defined in relation to it:

1. **The grazing** -- Nanina drives the flock to pasture, where she first hears the fairy pipe (004-003) (004-004).

2. **The loss** -- After dancing to the music, she discovers "one lamb missing" (004-016). The loss transforms a pastoral idyll into a crisis.

3. **The search** -- Nanina "wandered about and cried, but no sheep were to be found" (004-018). Her search leads her to the [[the-fairy-shepherd|fairy shepherd]], who has taken the lamb.

### Bargain and Restoration

4. **The bargain** -- The fairy shepherd offers to return the lamb if Nanina dances with him for seven nights. Her acceptance -- putting the flock's wholeness above her own safety -- is the tale's moral centre.

5. **The restoration** -- At the ordeal's end, the flock is made whole. The missing lamb is returned, and Nanina's duty is fulfilled. The [[the-old-farmer|old farmer]] finds his flock safe on his return (004-029).

## Owner / Keeper

- [[Nanina]] -- The young shepherdess whose devotion to her flock defines her character. She counts them obsessively (004-016), grieves the lost lamb (004-018), and endures supernatural ordeal to recover it.

## Significance

Nanina's flock is the tangible embodiment of mortal responsibility in a world of fairy enchantment. The music of the pipe is beautiful, the dance is intoxicating -- but the flock's safety is the real, unglamorous duty that Nanina cannot abandon. The one missing lamb carries the weight of the entire moral: no enchantment, however seductive, excuses the neglect of what has been entrusted to one's care. The flock's eventual restoration -- every sheep accounted for -- is the story's proof that [[keeping-faith|fidelity]] to the ordinary is rewarded as surely as any fairy gift. The [[the-black-goats|black goats]] that dance alongside the sheep serve as a dark mirror: supernatural creatures that belong to the fairy realm, contrasting with the mortal flock that must be returned to the human world.
