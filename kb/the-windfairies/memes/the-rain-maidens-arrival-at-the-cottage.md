# The Rain Maiden's Arrival at the Cottage

During a fierce mountain rainstorm, a tall woman in grey appears at a shepherd's cottage door. She drinks only water, wears diamonds disguised as dull cloth, and delivers a prophecy that will govern the entire story: a child will be born, but she will remain only as long as her mother loves her above all else.

## Context

A shepherd and his wife lived in a lonely cottage near the mountains, far from any town or village. It was a wild, remote place where "the wind blew across the mountains fiercely, and the rain often fell so heavily that it seemed as if the cottage would be washed away" (008-001). The shepherd's wife had suffered a deep sorrow: ten years earlier, her baby girl had died, and she still longed for another child (008-004).

One evening when the shepherd was out, a great storm of rain began to beat against the doors and windows with unusual, almost deliberate force.

## The Event

As the shepherd's wife sat listening to the storm by the fire, she noticed something strange: the raindrops sounded "like the knock of a hand that was knocking to gain admittance" (008-001). The knocking continued, insistent, until she could bear it no longer. She rose and opened the door, though she knew it would let the wind and rain inside.

In the doorway stood a tall woman wrapped in a grey cloak, with long hair falling down her back (008-002). Her voice was low, yet the shepherd's wife could hear it "plainly through all the storm" (008-002). The woman thanked her for opening the door:

> "Many would have let me stand outside." (008-002)

She asked only for rest and a glass of water, declining all other food — "water was all she needed" (008-003).

The shepherd's wife then noticed two remarkable things. First, the woman's clothes were not wet at all despite the raging storm. Second, what she had taken for a dull grey cloak was in fact covered with jewellery — "clear stones, like diamonds with many flashing colours" — and all her clothes were of the finest quality (008-003).

The woman asked if the shepherd's wife was happy. The wife confessed her sorrow and her longing. In response, the woman rose and delivered the prophecy that would govern the child's entire life:

> "Before long a little child will be born to you, and as long as you love it better than anything in the world it will remain with you, but when you love anything else better than your little daughter and her happiness, it will go from you; so remember my words." (008-006)

She then walked quietly out into the rain and disappeared, her clothes still untouched by the storm.

## Immediate Aftermath

A year later, the shepherd's wife gave birth to a lovely daughter with "the bluest eyes and the softest skin," and the evening of her birth was marked by the same fierce wind and rain as the night the grey woman had visited (008-007). The prophecy hung over the child's life — a covenant of conditional love, binding the daughter's presence to her mother's undivided devotion.

## Participants

- [[the-rain-maiden]] — A tall grey-clad woman, impervious to rain, adorned with diamond-like jewels that appeared as dull grey cloth. She drinks only water, speaks in prophecy, and vanishes into the storm as mysteriously as she arrived (008-002–006)
- [[the-shepherds-wife]] — The lonely shepherd's wife who lost a child ten years before and longs for another. Her reception of the rain maiden — opening the door despite the storm — proves her kindness, but the prophecy will test the limits of her love (008-001, 008-004)

## Outcome

The rain maiden's prophecy was fulfilled exactly: a daughter was born to the shepherd's wife one year later, on a night of wind and rain identical to the visitation. The condition — that the child would remain only so long as her mother loved her above all else — would prove to be the central tension of the child's life and the story's eventual tragedy.

## Significance

The rain maiden's arrival establishes the supernatural frame of the tale. Every detail marks her as a nature spirit — the rain itself personified: her imperviousness to water, her diamond jewellery mistaken for grey cloth (as rain looks dull from a distance but sparkles close), her refusal of all sustenance but water, and her prophetic knowledge. The condition she sets is a classic fairy-tale covenant: a gift bound by a moral test, whose breaking will exact a price. Her warning to "remember my words" (008-006) is not merely advice but a structural promise to the reader — this condition will be invoked, and when it is broken, the child will go. The tragedy is announced at the very moment the blessing is given.
