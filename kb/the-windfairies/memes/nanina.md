# Nanina

Nanina is the protagonist of "Nanina's Sheep" (Story 4), a young shepherdess who tends a flock of six sheep and eight lambs for an old farmer. When her master departs for a month-long journey to buy pigs, he warns her never to go near the old palace on the other side of the hill, for it is filled with wicked fairies (004-001). Nanina's curiosity overcomes her obedience, setting in motion a chain of supernatural encounters that test her resolve, cost her everything she was charged to protect, and ultimately forge her character through an ordeal of excruciating physical sacrifice. Her story is a moral tale about the cost of disobedience, the possibility of redemption through suffering, and the enduring marks that transgression leaves upon the body and memory alike.

## Role in the Story

### The Faithful Shepherdess

Nanina is introduced as a young girl entrusted with the care of [[naninas-flock|the flock]] by her master, [[the-old-farmer|the old farmer]]. Her role is that of a responsible servant: she is given clear instructions, a specific charge of six sheep and eight lambs, and a solemn warning about the forbidden [[the-old-palace|old palace]] on the other side of [[the-hills|the hill]] (004-001). The farmer's parting words frame the entire narrative: "whatever you do, you don't go near the old palace on the other side of the hill, for it is filled with wicked fairies who might do you an ill turn" (004-001). Nanina promises obedience, and for the first day all goes well. She drives the flock in safely at night, and the story seems set to be one of quiet pastoral duty.

### The Disobedient Child

Nanina's role shifts sharply from dutiful servant to wayward child. Finding it "dull sitting on the hillside watching the lambs at play" (004-002), she begins to question her master's prohibition. Her reasoning reveals both her youth and her naivety: "If it is filled with fairies, it won't hurt me just to look at it; I should like to see a fairy" (004-003). This moment of curiosity-driven transgression is the catalyst for the entire story. By driving her flock to the forbidden side of the hill, Nanina places herself and her charges directly in the path of the supernatural. She waits until sunset, and when she sees the palace windows light up, her fascination deepens into compulsion — she must see more.

### The Penitent Sufferer

After losing her entire flock to [[the-fairy-shepherd]]'s enchanted pipe, Nanina's role transforms again — this time into that of the penitent who must suffer to undo the consequences of her actions. She becomes an active agent of her own redemption, accepting the [[the-beech-tree|beech tree]]'s counsel and enduring seven nights of immobilising pain to win back each lost animal. Her role in this phase is defined not by action but by inaction — by her willingness to be held still while every instinct screams at her to dance. The tree tells her plainly that refusal will hurt her, but Nanina's response is immediate and absolute: "Hurt me as you please, and I shan't mind. If only I can get back my sheep I will bear any pain" (004-023).

## Character Traits

### Curiosity and Wonder

Nanina's defining initial trait is her intense curiosity about the forbidden. She wonders "why her master had told her always to keep on that side, and away from the old palace on the other" (004-002). This is not malicious disobedience but the natural inquisitiveness of a young mind. Her desire to see a fairy, and her fascination with the lit windows of the old palace after dark, drive her toward the encounter that will change everything. Her curiosity is childlike and genuine — she marvels at the shepherd boy's beauty, noting that "she had never seen anyone so beautiful before" (004-004), and gapes at the dancing [[the-black-goats|black goats]] with innocent delight. She is not defiant but simply unable to resist the pull of the unknown.

### Remorse and Resolve

Once the consequences of her disobedience become clear — the loss of first one lamb, then another, then all of them — Nanina's character deepens into genuine remorse. She "wept and lamented" (004-017) as her flock dwindled, and when the last lamb is gone she spends "all the day wandering about and crying" (004-018). Yet her remorse quickly transforms into fierce determination. When the beech tree offers a path to recovery, Nanina does not hesitate: "Only tell me, and I will do anything" (004-019). This transition from despair to resolve marks her moral maturation. She cannot undo her disobedience, but she can pay its price.

### Extraordinary Endurance

The most remarkable trait Nanina displays is her capacity for physical endurance. Though the beech tree warns her plainly that "I shall hold them very tight, and it will hurt you" (004-022), she submits to the ordeal without reservation. Night after night, she buries her already-bruised feet in the earth to be gripped by the tree's roots. By the final nights, "her feet were so bad that she could scarcely hobble, for they were crushed and bleeding" (004-024), and on the last night she limps to the tree "almost crying with pain" (004-025). This endurance is not stoic but agonised — she cries, screams, and sobs — yet she never quits. Her suffering is the price of her redemption, and she pays it in full, night after night, until every lamb is restored.

## Key Actions

### The First Transgression

Driven by boredom and curiosity, Nanina drives her flock to the forbidden side of the hill. She waits until sunset and sees the palace windows light up — confirming the truth of her master's warning even as she ignores it (004-003). This decision, made in a moment of idle curiosity, is the single action from which all subsequent events flow. She compounds it by remaining until dark, ensuring that she will witness whatever emerges from the palace after sunset.

### Dancing with the Fairy Shepherd

When [[the-fairy-shepherd]] emerges with his flock of [[the-black-goats|black goats]] and begins to play his magic pipe, Nanina is drawn irresistibly into [[the-enchanted-dance]]. The pipe's [[music]] speaks words that embody the springtime joy of the fairy realm: "When the young birds sing, / And the young plants spring, / Then dance we so merrily together, oh" (004-005). Nanina's feet begin to move of their own accord (004-012), and she dances through the entire night, losing track of time entirely. In the morning she finds one lamb missing (004-016). Though she resolves each day not to dance again, the compulsion proves irresistible: "when once she heard the pipe, Nanina could not keep still" (004-017). Night after night, the pattern repeats — a fortnight of enchanted dancing that costs her the entire flock.

### An Act of Kindness

In her despair, Nanina shows unexpected compassion. Sitting beneath a beech tree near the palace, "she saw that someone had torn down the lowest branches of the tree and they were hanging down broken. She raised them and tied them up, so that they would grow together" (004-018). This small, unprompted act of care — performed without any expectation of reward — earns her the tree's supernatural aid. The beech tree speaks to her with a "shadowy voice," offering the only counsel that can break the enchantment: "Nanina, don't dance" (004-018). The tree's instruction is simple but its execution will prove agonising.

### The Seven Nights of Resistance

Following the beech tree's instruction, Nanina digs a hole among its roots each night and buries her feet in the earth. The tree's roots tighten around her ankles "as if they were bound with cords" (004-023), holding her immobile while the fairy shepherd dances before her. The ordeal is both psychological and physical: she grows "quite wild to dance" and tries "her hardest to break her feet free," nearly screaming "with the pain they cost her" (004-023). Yet each morning, one lamb is restored. Over seven nights — the last being the most agonising, when the fairy appears in a crown and scarlet robes playing "long sad notes, like a funeral march" (004-025) — she recovers her entire flock. Her feet are left "wounded and bleeding, where she had dashed them about in trying to dance" (004-028), and she must crawl home on her hands and knees.

### The Truth Told Slant

When the farmer returns and finds his flock intact, he questions Nanina about her injured feet. Her response is a carefully crafted half-truth: "Of a truth, master, it was in saving the lambs when they got into dangerous places" (004-029). She does not confess her disobedience, yet her words are not entirely false — she did indeed save the lambs, and they were in a dangerous place. This morally ambiguous moment underscores Nanina's continuing humanity: she has been redeemed through suffering, but she is not perfected. She has learned [[obedience]] through pain, but she has not yet learned full [[truth-telling]].

## Relationships

- **[[the-old-farmer]]** — Nanina's master and employer. He entrusts her with his flock, gives her clear instructions about the six sheep and eight lambs, and issues the specific warning about the fairy palace that she disregards (004-001). His return at the story's end provides the moment of accountability that Nanina partially evades through her half-truth (004-029).

- **[[the-fairy-shepherd]]** — The beautiful supernatural being who emerges from [[the-old-palace|the old palace]] each night with his [[the-black-goats|black goats]]. He is both tempter and tester: his enchanted pipe compels Nanina to dance, costing her the flock, yet his nightly appearances also provide the mechanism of her redemption as each resisted dance restores a lamb. On the final night he appears more magnificent than ever, "dressed in scarlet and gold" with a jewelled crown (004-025).

- **[[the-beech-tree]]** — The sentient tree near the old palace that becomes Nanina's saviour. She binds its broken branches in an act of unsought kindness, and it repays her with the counsel that saves her flock and her soul: the instruction to bury her feet in the earth and let its roots hold her still (004-022). After the palace falls, the tree speaks its farewell — "Goodbye, Nanina; now you have all your flock again" (004-028) — and falls silent forever.

- **[[naninas-flock]]** — The six sheep and eight lambs placed in Nanina's charge. They are both her responsibility and the tangible measure of her failure and redemption. Each lost and recovered animal marks a step in her moral journey through the seven nights of resistance.

- **[[the-black-goats]]** — The fairy shepherd's enchanted flock, jet black with "locks curling and thick and soft as silk" (004-004). They dance alongside the shepherd and serve as a visual reminder of the supernatural realm into which Nanina has strayed. Their disappearance into the palace each dawn marks the boundary between the mortal and fairy worlds.

## Significance

### The Cost of Disobedience

Nanina's story is fundamentally about the consequences of disregarding rightful authority. Her transgression is not malicious — it springs from curiosity and boredom — yet the cost is devastating. She loses everything entrusted to her, and recovering it requires suffering that leaves her physically marked. The tale does not condemn curiosity itself but insists that [[obedience|disobedience]], however innocent in motive, carries a price that must be paid. The master's warning was not arbitrary; it was protective. By ignoring it, Nanina places herself and those dependent on her in mortal danger.

### Redemption Through Suffering

Unlike many fairy-tale heroines who are rescued by external agents, Nanina must actively participate in her own redemption. The beech tree provides the method, but Nanina must supply the will. Each night she chooses to bury her feet, knowing the pain will be worse than the night before. Her redemption is earned through bodily sacrifice — the crushing of her feet, the bleeding, the limping that persists long after the story ends (004-030). The mode of her deliverance is also thematically precise: she is saved not by doing but by refusing to do, by learning to be still when every impulse drives her to move. This inverts the pattern of her fall, which began with too much action — going where she should not go, dancing when she should not dance.

### The Scar as Memorial

The story's final image is deeply significant: "Underneath the beech tree, where Nanina's feet had bled among the earth, there sprang up pretty little scarlet flowers, and whenever she passed and saw them she remembered how she had been punished for disobeying her master, and made up her mind never to do so again" (004-030). Her suffering is not erased but transformed into beauty. The scarlet flowers are a permanent memorial — to the beech tree's kindness, to Nanina's courage, and to the lesson she learned through pain. They also signify that some wounds do not fully heal: "for long she must walk lame" (004-029). Nanina carries the physical evidence of her ordeal into her future, a living reminder of what disobedience cost and what redemption required. The flowers, blooming from her blood, make the site of her suffering a place of [[keeping-faith|remembrance]] — a private monument to a hard-won moral education.
