# Greed

Excessive desire for wealth, power, or possession — presented across multiple tales as a corrosive force that blinds characters to moral duties and invites supernatural retribution.

## Definition

Greed in these tales is not merely wanting things. It is the disordered prioritization of material gain over human relationships, promises, and moral obligations. Greedy characters are consistently described as "hard" and uncaring — their desire for wealth has calcified their capacity for empathy and loyalty (006-001). The tales present greed as self-defeating: those who grasp for more than their share invariably lose what they already have.

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

Greed operates as the motivating force behind Lucilla's displacement. Her father and the courtiers persuade her to leave home "if she brought home a sack of gold" (001-028). The King's greed for her dancing — he wants to keep her at court forever rather than let her return home — drives the story's central conflict. His unwillingness to "lose the dancer" (001-045) leads him to imprison and attempt to execute an innocent woman.

### In "The Story of a Cat"

The master is introduced as fundamentally "greedy and hard, and cared for nobody or nothing in the world except his own advantage" (006-001). This characterization is the story's moral premise — the cat's subsequent triumph over such a master represents supernatural justice against greed. The cat itself drinks milk "not greedily or in a hurry, but as if it were a pleasure" (006-015), modeling the proper, measured relationship to material goods that the master lacks.

### In "The Ploughman and the Gnome"

The gnome's bargain — "Agreed, but remember" (009-016) — carries the implicit warning that greed in dealing with supernatural beings is perilous. The ploughman who deals fairly prospers; the subtext warns against pressing for more than one's due.

## Characters Who Embody This

- **The Cat's Master** — The embodiment of greed: "greedy and hard, and cared for nobody or nothing except his own advantage" (006-001)
- **The King** — His greed for Lucilla's dancing drives him to imprison and attempt to kill an innocent woman (001-045)
- **Kesta** — Though vanity is her primary vice, greed fuels her escalating husband-shopping (002-015)

## Moral Dimension

Greed is presented as the most straightforward vice in the collection — it requires no subtle analysis to recognize. The greedy master is introduced with flat moral clarity: he is greedy, therefore he is bad (006-001). More interesting is the tale's treatment of systemic greed — the King's desire to possess Lucilla's dancing permanently, backed by the state apparatus of guards and executioner, shows how individual greed scales into institutional injustice. The tales answer greed with supernatural justice: the wind destroys the King's palace, and the cat outwits its greedy master. The moral universe is structured so that greed contains the seeds of its own punishment (001-066).
