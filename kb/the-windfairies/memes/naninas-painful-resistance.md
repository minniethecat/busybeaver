After losing her entire flock of sheep to the fairy shepherd's enchanted dances, a desperate Nanina received counsel from a talking beech tree and endured night after night of agonizing resistance to win back her sheep, one lamb at a time.

## Context

Nanina had lost all fourteen of her sheep — six ewes and eight lambs — over a fortnight of dancing with the fairy shepherd. Desperate and weeping, she sat beneath a beech tree near the old palace, where she noticed its lowest branches had been torn down (004-018).

## The Tree's Counsel

As Nanina carefully tied up the broken branches, she heard a shadowy voice whisper her name. A small twisted face appeared among the leaves of the beech tree. Grateful for her kindness in tending its branches, the tree offered her a way to recover her sheep: she must refuse to dance with the fairy. Each time she resisted, one of her lost flock would return (004-018–020).

When Nanina protested that she could not resist — her feet moved of themselves when the pipe began — the tree offered its help:

> "Bury your feet in the earth like my roots. Dig a hole deep down, and I will hold your feet so that you shall not move them, only you must bear the pain, and not mind if you walk lame afterwards, for I shall hold them very tight, and it will hurt you." (004-022)

Nanina accepted without hesitation: "Hurt me as you please, and I shan't mind. If only I can get back my sheep I will bear any pain." (004-023)

## The Ordeal

### First Night

Nanina knelt and dug a deep hole among the tree's roots, placing her feet into the loose earth. She felt something moving beneath, tightening around her feet and drawing them deep into the ground, holding them like cords (004-023). When the fairy shepherd emerged with his pipe and danced before her, she felt her feet straining to move beneath the earth, but the roots held firm. She nearly screamed with the pain. At dawn, the shepherd vanished — and beside her was one of her own lambs. She drew her feet from the earth to find them blue and bruised (004-023).

### Subsequent Nights

Night after night she returned, slipping her sore, swollen feet back into the roots, feeling them twist around the damaged flesh (004-023–024). Each time the fairy danced before her in ever more splendid attire — eventually appearing in scarlet and gold with a jeweled crown — and each time the roots held fast while tears of pain streamed down her cheeks. One lamb returned each morning, until all the flock was restored save one (004-024).

### The Final Night

On the last night, the fairy shepherd's pipe no longer played merry dance music but long, sad notes like a funeral march. The music seemed to sing:

> "Join us, Nanina, dance again,
> One last dance will ease your pain." (004-026–027)

Nanina swayed and screamed as the fairy and the goats danced around her — now solemn and slow, now joyous and wild. Just when she thought she could bear no more, a single long low note sounded, and with a thunderous crash, the fairy shepherd and his goats vanished, and the walls of the old palace collapsed into a heap of stones (004-028). Beside Nanina on the grass lay the last of her lost sheep.

## Outcome

Nanina recovered her entire flock, but at a lasting cost. Her feet were so crushed, bleeding, and wounded that she had to crawl home on her hands and knees (004-028). She would walk lame for a long time afterward. The beech tree spoke its farewell, and beneath the spot where Nanina's blood had fallen among the earth, small scarlet flowers later sprang up as a living memorial.

## Significance

This ordeal is the moral and emotional heart of "Nanina's Sheep." It transforms Nanina from a curious but disobedient girl into a figure of genuine sacrifice and redemption. The physical pain she endures — deliberately chosen, night after night — is the price of her earlier weakness. This is not a tale of punishment but of costly restoration: every lamb recovered requires Nanina to give something of herself. The scarlet flowers that bloom where she bled symbolize the beauty that can grow from suffering bravely borne.
