# The Princess

The Princess is a royal bride who appears in the frame narrative of "The Windfairies," the opening tale of the collection. Her arrival for marriage to the King initiates the festivities that bring musicians, entertainers, and storytellers to the court — setting the stage for the tales that follow. A different princess also appears in "The Story of a Cat" as a cat-loving bride.

## Role in the Story

In "The Windfairies," the Princess serves as a catalyst rather than an active character. Her impending arrival is announced early in the tale:

> in a few days the Princess will arrive, and there will be great rejoicings, for the King is to be married. [001-030]

The narrator describes her arrival in brief but vivid terms:

> By and by the Princess who was to marry the King arrived, and she was very beautiful, and the King was delighted with her. [001-032]

Her presence initiates the celebrations during which the little girl Lucilla, the miller's daughter, becomes lost. The storytellers, musicians, and entertainers gathered for the royal wedding create the chaotic, festive atmosphere that allows Lucilla to slip away unnoticed and encounter the windfairies.

A separate princess — "the new Princess" — appears briefly in "The Story of a Cat," where she is described as "quite crazy about cats" [006-025], providing a contrast to the regal figure in the opening tale.

## Character Traits

- **Beautiful**: She is described as "very beautiful" [001-032], fitting the fairy tale archetype of the lovely bride
- **Catalytic**: Though barely characterized, her arrival sets the entire narrative in motion — without the royal wedding, the storytellers' gathering would not occur
- **Regal**: As a princess destined to marry a king, she represents the highest social station in the fairy tale world

## Key Actions

1. **Arrives for the wedding**: The Princess travels to the King's court for the arranged marriage, triggering the celebrations [001-032]

2. **Delights the King**: Her beauty meets with the King's approval: "the King was delighted with her" [001-032]

3. **Unwittingly enables Lucilla's adventure**: The festivities surrounding her wedding create the circumstances in which Lucilla becomes separated from her father

## Relationships

- [[the-king]] — The King is the Princess's betrothed. Their wedding is the occasion for the court celebrations that frame "The Windfairies"
- [[lucilla]] — The miller's daughter whose encounter with the windfairies is made possible by the wedding celebrations
- [[the-prince]] — In "The Rain Maiden," a prince eventually marries "a beautiful Princess" [008-026], creating a thematic link between royal brides across the collection

## Significance

The Princess is a narrative device as much as a character — her wedding creates the crowded, festive court that frames Lucilla's adventure with the windfairies. In this sense, she represents how seemingly peripheral figures in frame narratives can be essential to the story's machinery. Without the Princess's arrival, there would be no gathering of storytellers, and the tales that form the heart of the collection might never have been told.

Her minimal characterization — defined almost entirely by her beauty and her function as a bride — reflects the fairy tale genre's use of princesses as symbolic figures rather than fully realized characters. She is what the King desires, what the court celebrates, and what sets events in motion, but she has no voice or agency of her own within the narrative.
