## Overview

The Widow's Daughters are minor characters in "The Gipsy's Cup." They are the two daughters of a poor widow who earns her living by spinning. The family attends the fair together, and the daughters witness the events surrounding the Gipsy's Cup, including the miraculous healing and the redemption of the gipsy woman.

## Appearances

### The Gipsy's Cup

The widow and her daughters appear at the fair. The mother is a spinner who makes a modest living:

> The mother was a poor widow, and she and her daughters made a little money by spinning [005-020]

When the fair is announced, the widow and her daughters prepare for it:

> A fair should be held, and the widow and her daughters should return with fine clothes [005-021]

After the fair, the spinning woman and her daughters return:

> The spinning woman and her two daughters came back with their fine clothes [005-022]

The daughters serve as witnesses to the story's central events — the encounter between the gipsy family and the potter, and the transformative power of the Gipsy's Cup. Though they are not named individually, their presence anchors the story in the rhythms of ordinary village life.

## Role in the Tales

The Widow's Daughters represent:
- **The ordinary village folk** — background characters who witness magical events
- **Family loyalty** — daughters who help their widowed mother and accompany her to the fair
- **The domestic world** — spinning, fairs, and the simple economy that the magical cup disrupts and redeems
