# Kesta's Encounter with the Banker

Having secured three marriage proposals — from Adam, the miller, and the bailiff — Kesta arrived in the town's high street and set her sights on an even wealthier target: the banker. Her encounter with him marks the fourth and most materially motivated proposal in the escalating chain of "Vain Kesta" (Story 2).

## Context

Kesta entered the town in "high good-humour," congratulating herself on her cleverness in not accepting Adam's proposal before coming to town (002-015). Each successive proposal had inflated her self-regard and sharpened her ambition.

## The Encounter

Passing the bank in the high street, Kesta saw the banker standing in the doorway. He was "fat and ugly and old," but his hands were "covered with rings" and Kesta knew "his pockets were full of gold" (002-015). She calculated that marrying him "would be a fine thing" and that she "could hold up my head with anyone" (002-015).

She initiated the encounter with a theatrical sigh:

> "Alack a day, how hard is my lot!" (002-015)

When the banker asked what troubled her, Kesta inflated her list of supposed suitors — now claiming she was pursued by "the farmer, then the miller, and lastly the duke's bailiff, who would scarcely let me pass on the road till I had promised him" (002-019). She framed her predicament as the curse of being "much too pretty for any of them" (002-019).

## The Banker's Proposal

The banker was impressed not by Kesta's appearance but by the social proof of her other suitors:

> "If so, there must be something very superior about you. Perhaps you would be good enough for me. How would you like to be my wife, and ride in a fine carriage, and wear silk gowns all day?" (002-020)

Kesta accepted, telling the banker that from the first she had thought him "much more suitable to be my husband than any of the others" — echoing the same conditional acceptance she had given every previous suitor, promising to return after completing her "business" in town (002-021).

## Significance

The banker's proposal represents Kesta's most nakedly materialistic calculation. Unlike Adam (who offered partnership), the miller (who offered comfort), or the bailiff (who acted from spite), the banker was chosen purely for his visible wealth — rings, gold, carriages, and silk gowns. The encounter demonstrates how each step in Kesta's chain of proposals moves her further from genuine affection and closer to pure avarice.
