# The Wife's Arrival at the Farm

The ploughman's marriage and the arrival of his new wife at the farm is the pivotal disruption in "The Ploughman and the Gnome" — the moment when the carefully balanced supernatural compact begins to unravel, setting in motion the gnome's escalating demands.

## Context

Under the gnome's bargain, the ploughman had grown prosperous. His farm flourished, his wealth increased, and all went well — as long as he faithfully shared the best of everything with the small black woman who lived in the hole in his wall. When the time came that he wished to marry, he chose a very pretty girl from the next village, said to be the prettiest in all the neighbourhood. Many young men would have liked to marry her, but she preferred the handsome, cheery ploughman (009-017).

## The Event

The ploughman and the girl were married, and she came home to live at the farm. That evening after the wedding, they had a fine fat fowl for supper. Before he helped his wife, the ploughman — as was his custom — cut off the choicest slice from the breast and took it to the gnome's hole (009-017).

His new wife, seeing this, was astonished. When he explained that the food was for the "rats and mice," she declared: "If you are not mad, I think your grandfather was! It is only the best poison that is good for rats and mice, and they shall have it soon, now that I am in the house" (009-018-019).

The ploughman caressed his wife and begged her to let him keep his promise to his "grandfather." Though she held her peace on her wedding day not to seem bad-tempered, the wife's arrival had already breached the gnome's unspoken rule: she did not like women in the house.

## Outcome

The wife got used to her husband putting down bits of food "for the rats and mice," and though she always declared she was going to poison them, she did not try, as her husband seemed grieved when she talked about it. But the gnome had noticed the intrusion. Soon she would confront the ploughman about the wife's fine clothes and the share she was owed — and begin her campaign to claim everything the wife possessed.

## Significance

The wife's arrival marks the collision of two incompatible worlds: the ploughman's supernatural contract with the earth, and the ordinary domestic life he craves. The gnome's dislike of women is not personal but structural — a wife brings rival claims on the household's resources, and the gnome's share must come first. This event sets up the tale's central conflict between marital love and supernatural obligation.
