After successively approaching the miller, the bailiff, the banker, and the General on her way through town, Kesta set her sights on the highest target of all -- the Duke himself -- in the climactic episode of "Vain Kesta."

## Context

Having extracted promises of marriage from the miller, the bailiff, the banker, and the General, Kesta grew bolder with each success (002-029). Reasoning that if all these men found her desirable, she should aim higher, she decided to approach the Duke at his palace (002-029). When Kesta arrived at the palace and asked to see the Duke, a servant directed her through the courtyard and into the garden, where the Duke sat beside a stream "with a long rod in his hand fishing" (002-029). He was dressed in green and appeared half-asleep.

## The Audience

### Kesta's Approach

Kesta approached the Duke not as a suitor seeking favor, but with a cleverly crafted story. Rather than proposing marriage directly, she presented herself as a victim in need of protection (002-030). "That is why I have come to you to ask you to protect me from all the men who pursue me," she told him (002-031).

When the Duke asked why so many men pursued her, Kesta replied simply, "Because I am so pretty. They all want to marry me: first the man at the farm, then the miller I met on the road, then your bailiff, then your banker, and now your General" (002-033). She recited her growing list of suitors -- farm man, miller, bailiff, banker, General -- each time framing them as unwanted pursuers (002-032) (002-034).

### The Duke's Skepticism

The Duke was initially incredulous that the General -- a man of high military rank -- would want to marry a dairymaid. Kesta responded boldly: "If you doubt what I say you had better send to the town and ask" (002-035). This self-assurance, rather than revealing her as a fraud, impressed the Duke.

### The Duke's Proposal

Though the Duke admitted he would not have thought Kesta especially pretty, her confidence and the impressive list of suitors convinced him (002-036). Declaring that if what she said was true she must be exceedingly beautiful, he decided: "I'm not sure if it would not suit me to marry you myself" (002-036).

When Kesta heard this, she "grinned with delight" and cried, "Aye, that I should!" (002-037). The Duke immediately summoned four pages in blue and gold and ordered them to prepare a wedding feast, bridal clothes, and the palace chaplain. He would marry her at once (002-038). The pages ran off in haste, while the Duke invited Kesta to sit beside him: "Come and sit by me and watch me fish till all is ready" (002-039).

## The Unmasking

### Waiting by the Stream

Kesta sat beside the Duke as he fished with his long rod, but the waiting proved her undoing. Though she was afraid of offending him, she grew restless and impatient (002-040) (002-044). She asked what he had caught, and he told her "nothing." When he offered her the fishing rod so she might try (002-041), the shift in position caused her basket to slip, and her cheeses rolled out onto the ground (002-042) (002-044).

### The Discovery

The Duke saw the round objects and asked what they were, noting their strange smell (002-045). Kesta, who could not think of a lie quickly enough, confessed: "They are my cheeses. I made them yesterday, and was taking them to sell, when-" but she did not finish the sentence (002-046).

The Duke's expression changed to horror. "Good gracious, you made them!" he cried with a scream. "Then you must be a common dairymaid, and your hands are quite rough. How terrible! And I was just going to marry you" (002-047). His rage swelled further: "How dare you think yourself good enough to marry me!" (002-047).

### The Expulsion

The Duke flew into a towering rage, blew his horn to summon his pages, and ordered Kesta chased from the palace grounds as an impostor -- "a common farm wench who makes cheeses" (002-048). The pages pursued her, hooting and shouting: "Down with the impertinent hussy who wanted to marry the Duke, a common dairymaid who makes cheeses!" (002-048).

## Outcome

Kesta fled from the palace grounds, with the Duke's pages chasing and mocking her. This exposure triggered the cascade of public humiliation that followed -- she rushed on from one household to the next, each former suitor now rejecting her in turn (002-049). The Duke's rejection was the most devastating because it was the most public and because she had come closer to her goal than ever before (002-049).

## Significance

This episode is the climax of Kesta's ambition and the turning point of the tale. Her ascent through progressively higher-status suitors reaches its peak at the Duke, but her downfall comes not from malice or a rival's scheming, but from the simple, undeniable evidence of her true station -- the cheeses she carried to sell. The scene distills the tale's moral: pretension cannot survive contact with reality. The Duke's scream of horror at learning his bride-to-be made her own cheese captures the rigid [[social-class|class consciousness]] that the story both depicts and satirizes. Kesta's [[vanity]] and ambition bring her within reach of becoming [[the-duchess]], but her true identity -- as a dairymaid -- cannot be hidden from those who would judge her by it.
