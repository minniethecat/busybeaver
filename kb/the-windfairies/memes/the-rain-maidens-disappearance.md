## Context

The rain maiden had prophesied to the shepherd's wife that a daughter would be born to her, but warned: "as long as you love it better than anything in the world it will remain with you, but when you love anything else better than your little daughter and her happiness, it will go from you; so remember my words" (008-006).

The shepherd's wife had since received a proposal from the King's son for her daughter's hand in marriage. Overcome with ambition and joy, she had ignored her daughter's repeated pleas not to be forced into the marriage (008-020, 008-023). The evening before the wedding, the daughter knelt and begged: "Dear mother, let me stop with you and nurse you when you are old. Do not send me away to the palace to marry a man I have never seen before" (008-023). The mother, furious, called her ungrateful and insisted on the marriage.

## The Event

All night long before the wedding day, "the rain fell in torrents" (008-025). On the morning of the ceremony, the shepherd's daughter was dressed in all her bridal finery. She stepped outside — "through pools on the ground" — and climbed into the royal carriage that had been sent to convey her to the palace and the waiting Prince (008-025).

But she never arrived at her wedding. The passage records only that the rain fell without ceasing, and the shepherd's daughter disappeared. The rain maiden's prophecy was fulfilled: the mother had loved the prospect of royal status and social advancement more than she loved her daughter and her daughter's happiness. The child, who had always been drawn to water and shade rather than sun and fire — who would sit for hours by the river "dabbling her feet in the water" (008-009) — returned to the element from which she had come.

## Immediate Aftermath

The King's son "mourned his wife, and for long would not be comforted" (008-026). Only after many years did he marry a beautiful Princess, and with her "lived very happily" (008-026). But the text notes that "only when the rain fell heavily and he could not go out to hunt would a look of sorrow cross his face, and he would sit silent and thoughtful" (008-026), remembering the rain-maiden's daughter who had vanished into the storm.

The shepherd's wife, who had "gone nearly wild with joy" at the prospect of royal status (008-017) and had angrily rejected her daughter's plea, was left with nothing — no Queen for a daughter, no child to nurse her in old age, only the bitter fulfillment of a prophecy she had been warned to remember.

## Outcome

The prophecy was irrevocably fulfilled. The daughter was gone, returned to the rain from which her true mother — the rain maiden — had come. The shepherd's wife lost the child she had prayed for, punished for loving worldly advancement above her daughter's happiness. The Prince, the only innocent party, carried the memory of his lost bride into his future happiness, the sound of rain forever stirring his sorrow.

## Significance

The rain maiden's disappearance is the story's fulfillment of the fairy-tale covenant established at the beginning. The moral structure is precise: a gift is given with a condition; the condition is broken; the gift is withdrawn. The mother's fault is not in wanting a good marriage for her daughter — it is in overriding her daughter's clearly expressed wishes. The daughter says "I will never be the wife of any man" (008-020), and her nature as a water-being makes this not stubbornness but truth. The mother, deaf to this, loves the idea of being mother to a Queen more than she loves the actual daughter before her. The rain maiden, who had said "remember my words," keeps her word with the same finality with which rain falls.
