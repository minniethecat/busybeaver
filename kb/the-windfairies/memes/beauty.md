# Beauty

Physical loveliness that draws admiration and desire — a recurring attribute of protagonists that functions as both gift and peril, rewarding the humble and corrupting the vain.

## Definition

Beauty in these tales is morally neutral but spiritually consequential. It attracts suitors, kings, and supernatural attention — what matters is how the beautiful person responds to this attention. Beauty humbly borne, as with Lucilla, becomes part of a character's virtue. Beauty wielded as a weapon of advancement, as with Kesta, becomes the instrument of downfall. The tales thus encode a sophisticated moral psychology: beauty amplifies character rather than determining it (001-020).

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

Lucilla's beauty is described in terms of natural grace: "She grew up to be a beautiful young woman... as tall and as lithe as a willow wand" (001-020). Her beauty is inseparable from her dancing — both are gifts from the windfairies, expressed through her body. Significantly, she does not use her beauty strategically; it is simply part of who she is. The sailors and courtiers are "amazed" by her, but she remains focused on returning home.

### In "Vain Kesta"

Kesta's beauty is the engine of her ambition. She looks in her glass and calculates: "I wonder why Adam wishes to marry me? but as he does, most likely some better man would like to do so" (002-004). She weaponizes her appearance, telling each new suitor that previous men pursued her because "I am so pretty" (002-012). Unlike Lucilla, Kesta's beauty is entirely instrumental — a tool for social climbing that ultimately fails her.

### In "The Rain Maiden"

Beauty intersects with tragedy. The rain maiden is described as beautiful, and "all who saw her admired her beauty" (008-025). Yet her beauty cannot save her: "she drowned in all her youth and beauty; only the shepherd's wife wept" (008-025). Beauty here is poignant and fleeting — something to be mourned, not leveraged.

### In "The Gipsy's Cup"

The wife's concern with beauty drives the story: "they didn't think much of her beauty if she was willing to marry him" (005-107). Beauty is social currency whose value depends on the company one keeps.

## Characters Who Embody This

- **Lucilla** — Beauty borne with humility, inseparable from her natural grace (001-020)
- **Kesta** — Beauty as weapon and currency, wielded with calculating vanity (002-004)
- **The Rain Maiden** — Tragic beauty, lovely and lost (008-025)

## Moral Dimension

The tales refuse a simple moralization of beauty. It is neither virtue nor vice — it is a test. Lucilla's beauty leads to suffering (she is abducted to the King's court) but also to vindication (the windfairies protect her). Kesta's beauty enables her schemes but brings her to ruin. The Rain Maiden's beauty is powerless against death. The consistent message is that beauty is dangerous to the vain and a burden to the humble, but it carries no inherent moral weight. What matters is the character of the beautiful person, not the fact of beauty itself (001-020) (002-004) (008-025).
