# The Great Storm

The Great Storm is the climactic event of "The Windfairies," a supernatural tempest summoned by the Windfairies to save their protegee [[lucilla]] from beheading. It destroys the King's palace, scatters the court, and delivers Lucilla's husband [[the-sailor]] to the shore at the precise moment of her deliverance. The storm is the culminating act of the Windfairies' pact with Lucilla: because she has [[keeping-faith|kept faith]], they now honour theirs.

## Context

### The Pact

When Lucilla was a child, the [[windfairies]] taught her to dance and extracted a solemn promise: "if you break your word to us, and tell any mortal who it was that taught you how to dance, you will never dance again... but if you keep faith with us, then the windfairies will never forget you, but will come to your help in your direst hour of need" (001-009). This reciprocal bond — secrecy in exchange for supernatural protection — is the moral architecture underlying everything that follows.

### Lucilla's Trial

Lucilla is brought to the King's court to perform her Windfairy-gifted dancing. When the King and [[the-queen]] demand she reveal who taught her, she refuses, honouring her childhood promise (001-048). The Queen, consumed by jealousy, persuades the King that Lucilla's dancing is witchcraft (001-050). Lucilla is imprisoned in a dark cell and subjected to three execution attempts: drowning, from which the Windfairies bear her up so that "in spite of the heavy weights on her feet she never sank" (001-058); and fire, where "the flames divided on each side, and were blown away from her all round" (001-060).

### The Final Test

Having survived water and fire, Lucilla faces the third and most terrible test. The King commands that a scaffold be erected in [[the-marketplace]] with the execution block, and a [[the-bag-of-gold|bag of gold]] is placed beside it as both temptation and taunt: if Lucilla will confess even upon the scaffold, she may take the gold and go free (001-062). She holds her silence. The Windfairies' promise — and her own — now hang in the balance. The moral logic of the tale sets the stage: faithfulness will be rewarded, and [[cruelty]] will be answered with destruction.

## The Event

### The Gathering Storm

The evening before the scheduled execution, the wind begins to rise. Lucilla, alone in her cell, feels no fear — she has learned through two prior deliverances that the Windfairies will not abandon her. The storm builds through the night, and by morning its force is unmistakable.

> The wind was beginning to blow in all directions, and all round she heard it roaring, and the trees were bending and breaking in the gale. — (001-063)

The gale is so violent that the guards struggle to walk, and along the coast boats are being driven ashore. Yet the King and Queen press forward with the execution, insisting on proceeding despite the elemental chaos (001-064). Their obstinacy in the face of supernatural warning signals their doom.

### The Ship

Even as the storm rages, a remarkable vessel appears on the horizon. While other ships are dismasted and wrecked, this one seems untouched by the gale (001-064). The people marvel at the captain's skill, unaware that the ship is guided not by human hands but by the same Windfairies who drive the storm. The ship carries Lucilla's husband, the sailor, whose vessel "would only take one course," steered irresistibly toward the shore by the wind itself (001-068).

> A big ship with shining white sails, riding over the crested waves... this boat seemed no way hurt by it. — (001-064)

### The Cataclysm

Lucilla is led onto the scaffold. For a moment, her faith wavers — "Lucilla began to fear that at last the windfairies had forsaken her" (001-065). She weeps and cries out:

> Oh, dear windfairies, indeed I have kept my faith with you, surely, surely you will keep yours with me. — (001-065)

The guards give her one final chance to confess. She refuses. The executioner raises the axe.

> There came a sudden roar of wind, and the axe was swept from his hand, and the houses in the marketplace tottered and fell, and high up on the hill the palace was a mass of ruins. — (001-066)

The destruction is total and instantaneous. The seat of royal power — the palace that stood on the hill as a symbol of mortal authority — is reduced to rubble. The marketplace collapses. The King, the Queen, the executioner, and the jeering crowd are all swept aside. Only one figure remains untouched:

> Only Lucilla knelt upon the scaffold unhurt. — (001-066)

## Participants

### The Windfairies

The [[windfairies]] are the agents of both destruction and protection. They summon the storm, guide the sailor's ship, shield Lucilla from harm, and demolish the palace. Their power is elemental and absolute: "When there are many of us together we make a great hurricane, and human beings are frightened" (001-003). The storm is the fulfilment of their long-ago promise and the demonstration that their bond with Lucilla transcends any mortal authority.

### Lucilla

[[lucilla]] kneels on the scaffold, "unhurt" amid the devastation (001-066). She is the still point at the centre of the storm — the one person the Windfairies protect while destroying everything around her. Her role in the event is not active but faithful: she has endured three execution attempts without breaking her word, and the storm is the answer to that fidelity.

### The King and Queen

[[the-king]] and [[the-queen]] are the targets of the Windfairies' wrath. The King, who allowed his wife's jealousy to override his better judgment, sees his palace — the symbol of his reign — obliterated. The Queen, whose scheming drove the persecution, is swept away with the rubble. Their fates illustrate the tale's central moral: those who persecute the faithful will be answered by forces they cannot control.

### The Sailor

[[the-sailor]], Lucilla's husband, arrives in the storm-guided ship. His presence is no coincidence: the Windfairies have steered his vessel to the shore so that he may witness his wife's deliverance and take her home. He is the living proof that the Windfairies' protection extends beyond Lucilla herself to those she loves (001-068).

## Outcome

### The Destruction of Royal Power

The most immediate consequence is physical: "the palace was a mass of ruins" (001-066). The King's authority, which had seemed absolute when he ordered Lucilla's execution, is shown to be nothing before elemental forces. The palace on the hill — visible from everywhere in the town, the architectural embodiment of royal power — is gone in an instant. The court that persecuted Lucilla is scattered, and no further attempt is made to harm her.

### Reunion and Return

Lucilla lifts her head from the scaffold block to see her husband's ship coming in to shore. The sailors, untouched by the storm, come ashore to help the stricken townspeople. Lucilla tells her husband everything that has happened, and she knows with certainty "that it was the windfairies that had brought her husband to her" (001-068). Together they take the [[the-bag-of-gold|bag of gold]] and return to [[the-cottage-by-the-sea]], where her father and children wait. The storm has not only saved her life but restored everything that had been taken from her.

## Significance

### A Turning Point

The Great Storm is the story's decisive turning point. Before it, Lucilla is a prisoner facing death; after it, she is free. Before it, the King's power appears unassailable; after it, his palace is rubble. The event inverts the power structure of the entire tale: the humble miller's daughter who kept her word is exalted, while the mighty who abused their power are humbled.

### Moral Architecture

The storm is not random violence — it is precisely targeted moral judgment. The Windfairies destroy the palace and the marketplace, the two sites of Lucilla's persecution, while leaving her untouched. The sailor's ship is guided safely through the same gale that wrecks every other vessel. The event demonstrates a universe governed by moral reciprocity: faithfulness earns protection; [[cruelty]] earns destruction. As Lucilla herself declares in the aftermath, the Windfairies have "saved me in my direst hour of need" (001-067), honouring the exact terms of the childhood pact.

### The Pattern of Three

The storm completes the fairy-tale pattern of three trials. Lucilla survives drowning (water), burning (fire), and beheading (wind/air), with each deliverance escalating in scale. The first two are defensive — the Windfairies simply prevent harm. The third is offensive: they do not merely deflect the axe but annihilate the entire structure of power that threatened her. The Great Storm is not just a rescue; it is a reckoning.
