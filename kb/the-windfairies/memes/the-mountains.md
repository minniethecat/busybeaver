# The Mountains

The Mountains are the remote highland setting of "Dumb Othmar," the seventh tale in *The Windfairies* collection. A village perched on the mountaintop, cut off from the world during the long winter months, provides the isolated backdrop for Othmar's journey from a voiceless shepherd to a young man who must confront the supernatural forces that threaten his community.

## Description

The mountain is described as formidable and isolating — a place of steep ascents and dangerous paths:

> the mountain was so steep and the path so narrow that, when it was blocked with ice, it was dangerous to ascend [007-001]

During winter, the villagers are completely cut off: "during the winter months the villagers saw no one but each other" [007-001]. The mountain's harshness shapes their lives — they survive the "long dreary evenings" with communal "games and dancing and singing and playing on pipes" [007-001], transforming enforced isolation into a culture of music and cheer.

The view from the mountain is expansive and awe-inspiring. When Othmar's sweetheart looks out from the village, she sees:

> a long line of mountains; a little further in the center was a great plain, and beyond that the blue sea, stretching far away [007-062]

This panoramic vista emphasizes both the village's remoteness and its connection to a wider world visible but inaccessible during winter.

## Location

The village sits "on the top of a mountain" [007-001], high above the surrounding landscape. From this vantage point, one can see the plain below and "the distant mountains where she knew lay her home" [007-103]. The mountains also appear in "The Rain Maiden" (ch.008), where "on the mountainside stood an old castle, where formerly the Kings of that country lived" [008-011] — a ruined royal residence that connects the mountain landscape to a lost era of grandeur.

## Events

The Mountains are the setting for the key events of "Dumb Othmar":

1. **Winter isolation**: The village endures the annual winter cutoff, when ice and snow make the mountain path impassable. This isolation creates the close-knit community that nurtures Othmar's musical gifts [007-001]

2. **Othmar's enchantment**: The mountainside is where Othmar encounters the supernatural — the mysterious fiddles that place a spell on him and the village, transforming their joyful music-making into something darker

3. **The journey back**: When Othmar's sweetheart must flee the village, she looks back from the mountainside and sees her home receding into the distance: "she looked back as she went and saw the village growing smaller and smaller" [007-040]

4. **The return home**: The story's resolution brings the characters back through the mountain passes toward the village, a journey that represents both physical return and spiritual restoration

## Symbolism

The Mountains in "Dumb Othmar" embody the dual nature of isolation: it can foster community and art (the villagers' winter gatherings of song and dance) or breed vulnerability to outside threats (the supernatural fiddlers who exploit the village's remoteness). The mountain's inaccessibility is both protective and perilous — a natural fortress that also traps its inhabitants.

The panoramic view from the mountaintop — encompassing the plain, the sea, and distant peaks — symbolizes perspective and longing. When Othmar's sweetheart gazes toward "the distant mountains where she knew lay her home" [007-103], the mountains become not just a physical location but a spiritual anchor, a place worth fighting to return to.
