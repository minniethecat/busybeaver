# The Rain Maiden's Arrival at the Cottage

The rain maiden's arrival at the shepherd's cottage is the inciting event of "The Rain Maiden" — a supernatural visitation disguised as a vagrant's plea for shelter on a stormy night, rewarded with the kindness of a woman who opens her door.

## Context

The shepherd and his wife lived in a lonely little cottage near the mountains, far from any town or village. It was a wild neighbourhood where the wind blew fiercely across the mountains and the rain often fell so heavily it seemed the cottage would be washed away. One evening when the shepherd was out, a great storm of rain came on, beating against the doors and windows violently (008-002).

## The Event

As the shepherd's wife sat listening to the storm by the fire, the raindrops sounded like the knock of a hand seeking admittance. It went on until she could bear it no longer, and she rose and opened the door, though she knew it would let the wind and rain into the room (008-003).

As the door opened, a gust of rain blew in her face, and in the doorway stood a woman who had been knocking. She was tall, wrapped in a grey cloak, with long hair falling down her back. She spoke in a voice very low, yet the shepherd's wife could hear it plainly through all the storm: "Thank you. Thank you for opening the door to me. Many would have let me stand outside. Now may I come into your cottage and rest?" (008-004–005).

The shepherd's wife welcomed her in, an act of simple kindness that would bind the rain maiden to her household — and to her daughter — in ways neither could foresee.

## Participants

- **[[the-rain-maiden]]** — a tall, grey-cloaked woman with long hair, one of the elemental rain spirits, seeking shelter among mortals
- **[[the-shepherds-wife]]** — the kind woman who opens her door to a stranger in the storm

## Outcome

The rain maiden entered the cottage and stayed. Out of gratitude for the shelter given so freely, she became a presence in the household. Her bond with the shepherd's daughter would grow so strong that when the daughter was to marry a prince and leave the cottage, the rain maiden would claim her back to the elemental world.

## Significance

This arrival scene establishes the central pattern of "The Rain Maiden": supernatural beings depend on mortal kindness for entry into human lives, and once admitted, they form attachments that transcend ordinary bonds. The rain maiden's gratitude creates obligations on both sides — the shepherd's wife gave shelter freely, but the rain maiden's claim on the daughter will prove the cost of that gift.
