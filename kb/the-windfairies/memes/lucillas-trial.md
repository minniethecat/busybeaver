Accused of witchcraft by a jealous rejected suitor, Lucilla endures the medieval ordeals of water and fire before the townsfolk prepare to execute her on the scaffold. At the final moment, the Windfairies — silent since her childhood — return in a storm of supernatural fury, rescuing their old friend and destroying the instruments of her death. This climactic sequence is the turning point of "The Windfairies," bringing the supernatural back into Lucilla's adult life with terrifying force.

## Context

Lucilla had grown from a child who danced with the [[windfairies|Windfairies]] into a woman married to a sailor. Her husband was often away at sea, and Lucilla lived alone in a cottage by the shore. Her strangeness — she danced barefoot by the water's edge, seemed to speak to the waves, and was seen talking to the wind — had long unsettled the superstitious townsfolk [001-024].

The spark that ignited the witch-hunt was personal, not theological. A man she had rejected — the [[the-gipsy-man|Gipsy Man]] — spread whispers that she was a witch who commanded the wind and waves, that her husband's ship always returned safely not through seamanship but through her sorcery [001-024]. The accusation found ready ears among people who had always found Lucilla uncanny, and who needed little encouragement to turn their unease into violence.

## The Trial by Water

The townsfolk seized Lucilla and subjected her to the first ordeal: the trial by water, the ancient test for witchcraft. They bound her hands and feet and threw her into the sea. The logic was merciless: if she floated, she was a witch and would be executed; if she sank and drowned, she was innocent — and dead.

> They seized her, and threw her into the water, and the salt waves splashed over her head. [001-058]

But Lucilla did not drown. The waves, as if recognising a friend, bore her up and carried her gently back to shore. To the terrified crowd, this was not mercy but proof — only a witch could command the sea to spare her. They dragged her from the surf and prepared the second ordeal [001-058–060].

## The Trial by Fire

A pyre was built in the marketplace, and Lucilla was bound to the stake. The crowd gathered to watch, their fear now hardened into righteous certainty.

> Already men were busy setting fire to it. But as Lucilla and the guards stood there, the sky grew black with clouds. [001-060]

Even as flames were kindled at the base of the pyre, a sudden gale rose from nowhere. The wind shrieked through the marketplace, flattening stalls and tearing at the flames until they guttered and died. The sea churned white, and the sky darkened to a bruised purple. The fire, battered by winds that seemed to answer to some unseen will, refused to catch [001-060–062].

The crowd, now convinced beyond doubt of Lucilla's witchcraft — for who but a witch could summon such a storm? — resolved on a final, irreversible execution: the scaffold in the marketplace square [001-062].

## The Scaffold and the Rescue

The morning of the execution dawned under a sky of unnatural fury. The wind howled with such force that the guards struggled to stand.

> It would be hard to get her on to the scaffold with a gale like this blowing. [001-063]

But the townsfolk were determined. Lucilla was led up the scaffold steps, and as she stood before the waiting noose, even she — who had trusted the wind all her life — began to fear that this time it could not save her [001-063–064].

Then the sky filled with a sound that was not the wind: the beating of innumerable tiny wings. The Windfairies, whom Lucilla had not seen since she was a child dancing on the shore, swept down from the storm clouds in a whirlwind of light. They were no longer the playful companions of her girlhood but something far more terrible — elemental forces that scattered the executioners like leaves, shattered the scaffold into splinters, and lifted Lucilla from the platform as easily as a dried leaf caught in a gust [001-065].

The fairies carried Lucilla away over the sea, and she was never seen again by the townsfolk. The town was left in ruins from the storm — a silent rebuke carved in splintered wood and shattered stone [001-065–067].

## Participants

- **[[lucilla|Lucilla]]** — The accused, a mortal woman whose childhood bond with the Windfairies proves stronger than the townsfolk's hatred. Her calm throughout the ordeals suggests a quiet faith that the wind has not forgotten her [001-058–065]
- **[[the-gipsy-man|The Gipsy Man]]** — The rejected suitor whose jealous whispers ignite the witch-hunt. His motivations are intensely personal — not religious zeal but wounded pride — and this pettiness behind the persecution makes the townsfolk's complicity all the more damning [001-024]
- **[[windfairies|The Windfairies]]** — Supernatural beings of wind and storm, silent throughout Lucilla's adult life until the moment of her greatest need. Their rescue is not gentle but cataclysmic — they destroy the scaffold and scatter the crowd with the indifference of a natural disaster [001-065]
- **The townsfolk** — The accusers and would-be executioners, whose superstitious fear transforms them from neighbours into a mob. Their tragedy is not malice but credulity — they believe the Gipsy Man's lies because they confirm what they already suspected about the strange woman by the sea

## Outcome

Lucilla was saved from execution and carried away over the sea by the Windfairies. The town was left in ruins from the supernatural storm, and the story of her trial became a warning passed down through generations — a caution against the violence that superstition and jealousy can unleash. The Windfairies' loyalty, dormant through all the years of Lucilla's ordinary adult life, proved itself absolute at the final test. Their bond, forged in childhood dances on the shore, had never been broken — only waiting [001-065–067].

## Significance

The trial sequence is the moral and dramatic climax of "The Windfairies." It operates on multiple levels simultaneously. As spectacle, it is a masterfully paced escalation: water, fire, scaffold — each ordeal more certain than the last, each rescue more dramatic. As moral fable, it is an indictment of mob justice and the persecution of difference: Lucilla's real crime is not witchcraft but being strange, being a woman alone, being someone who danced barefoot and talked to waves.

The episode also enacts the story's deepest theme: the persistence of childhood bonds into adult life. The Windfairies have been absent for years — Lucilla has married, grown up, entered the ordinary world — but when she needs them, they come. Their rescue is not a return to childhood innocence but an eruption of that childhood power into the adult world, with adult consequences. They destroy the scaffold, scatter the executioners, and bear Lucilla away not to a happy ending but to an unknown fate beyond the sea. The childhood friends who once danced with her in the shallows have become forces that can level a town.
