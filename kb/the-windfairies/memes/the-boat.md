# The Boat

The Boat appears as a significant object in two tales of the Windfairies collection. In "The Windfairies" (Chapter 1), it is the means of Lucilla's rescue from prison; in "The Rain Maiden" (Chapter 8), it carries the King's son as he courts the mysterious maiden.

## Lucilla's Rescue

When [[lucilla]] is imprisoned and condemned to be burned for witchcraft, the [[windfairies]] come to her aid. They lift her from her cell and place her in a boat:

> "… they lifted her down and placed her in a boat on the seashore, and they rowed her away." [001-057]

The guards attempt to follow, but the windfairies' power protects her:

> "Then, when they had rowed the boat out a little way, the guards saw it and tried to follow." [001-058]

While other boats are battered by the storm the windfairies conjure, Lucilla's boat sails safely:

> "… and although all the other boats seemed troubled by the wind, hers sailed smoothly on." [001-064]

The boat thus symbolizes the windfairies' protection and Lucilla's deliverance from unjust punishment.

## The King's Son's Courtship

In "The Rain Maiden" (Chapter 8), a boat is the vessel from which the King's son first encounters the shepherd's daughter:

> "Presently there came up a boat, and it was a grand young man who sat in it." [008-011]

The King's son continues to use his boat as a vantage point for observation and courtship:

> "… day after day he came down to the river to fish…" [008-015]

It is from this boat that he ultimately declares his love:

> "… when he was again sitting in the boat on the river, and the maiden on the bank…" [008-021]

## Symbolic Significance

Across both tales, the boat represents a vehicle of transition — carrying Lucilla from imprisonment to freedom, and the King's son from observation to love. It appears at critical turning points, linking two stories through this shared motif.
