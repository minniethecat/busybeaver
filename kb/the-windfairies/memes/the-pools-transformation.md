## Context

After the tree was uprooted and taken away, the pool was left alone for the first time, staring at a bare sky where delicate leaves once traced patterns (003-013). A swallow advised it to ask the moon for help (003-014). The moon revealed that the tree had been planted in a garden many miles away but was failing to take root (003-017). The pool swelled with pride at the tree's fame, then cried in anguish that it could never move to find it (003-018).

## The Event

A little cloud sailing nearby heard the pool's lament and offered unexpected hope:

> "That is nonsense. I was once in the earth like you. Tomorrow, if the sun shines brightly, he will draw you up into the sky, and you can sail along till you find your tree." (003-019)

All night the pool rested in peace, waiting (003-020). The next morning, with no clouds in the sky, the pool called out to the sun:

> "Draw me up into the sky, dear Sun, that I may be a little cloud and sail all the world over, till I can find my beloved tree." (003-020)

The sun answered. It threw down "hundreds of tiny golden threads which dropped over the pool" (003-021). Slowly and gradually, the pool began to change — growing thinner and lighter, rising through the air, until it had completely left the earth. Where it had lain was nothing but a dry hole, but the pool itself was transformed into a tiny cloud, sailing in the blue sky (003-021).

## Outcome

The newly-formed cloud kept apart from the other clouds, searching far and near for the tree (003-021). Unable to find it, the cloud appealed again to the sun, which revealed the tree was "away on the other side of the world" (003-022). The wind then arose and carried the cloud on a journey of hundreds of miles, passing other clouds that invited it to dance and play — but the little cloud pressed on: "I cannot stay, I am seeking a lost friend" (003-024–025).

## Significance

The pool's transformation is the story's most magical moment — a literal metamorphosis driven by love and loss. It embodies the fairy tale's central thesis: that love transcends physical form and limitation. The pool cannot walk, so it learns to fly. The sun's "golden threads" evoke both natural evaporation and divine intervention, blurring the line between science and miracle. The cloud's refusal to play with others and its single-minded quest establish the tone of the story's second half: a pilgrimage across the sky for reunion.
