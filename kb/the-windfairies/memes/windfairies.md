# Windfairies

The Windfairies are the title creatures of the collection, grey-clad wind spirits who come to children at night. They are beings of the air — swift, silent, and bound by ancient rules of secrecy. Their name echoes through the book: every story in the collection is named after them, and their presence infuses the collection's thematic focus on the boundary between the human and elemental worlds. They are first encountered on the downs by the sea at midnight, sweeping down from the great windmill sails where they ride the wind (001-008).

## Nature

The Windfairies are elemental spirits who dwell in the wind itself. They appear as "grey figures" that sweep across the downs at night and can lift a human child into the sky. They are neither malevolent nor benevolent — they are forces of nature given form and voice (001-004). Their primary interaction with humans is through adoption: they choose children of promise, teach them their arts, and bind them with oaths of secrecy. Those who keep faith with them are protected; those who break faith lose all memory of them. They speak with one voice through their leader, addressing humans directly and offering bargains that carry both blessing and curse.

### Origins & Identity

The Windfairies present themselves as a sisterhood of elemental spirits whose origin is never fully explained in the tale. They identify themselves simply — "We are windfairies" (001-004) — and when the child [[lucilla|Lucilla]] asks what they are, they offer no further explanation beyond their name (001-005). This reticence is itself a narrative assertion: the Windfairies are elemental, primordial, and beyond human categories of origin or purpose. They exist as the wind exists — without genealogy, without history, without explanation. They are plural by nature, always appearing as a group of "little grey elves" moving in unison, and their collective identity is central to their power: no single windfairy ever acts alone. They are connected to the great windmill on the downs, whose sails they ride as a vehicle for flight, and their identity is inseparable from the air and motion that define them (001-008).

## Appearance

The Windfairies are described as grey figures whose forms seem to flicker between solid and vapourous. Their hands and arms are "tiny" and "white" (001-009). When Lucilla remembers them years later, she wears "a light grey gown like the windfairies" in remembrance (001-032). They are associated with the [[windmill-on-the-downs|windmill on the downs]] and the open sky — their forms are always described in motion, never at rest. Their number is vague but always plural: "little grey elves" who come in a flock, sweeping and gliding through the air. When they first encounter Lucilla, one of them holds her in "tiny white arms," whispering to the others an assessment of her character (001-009). Their physical presence is paradoxically both intimate — they touch, carry, and kiss the child — and ungraspable, vanishing "in an instant" the moment their conditions are challenged.

## Powers & Abilities

- **Flight** — They can fly on the wind and carry humans with them: "they were sweeping down from the downs with a rush like a flock of birds" (001-011)
- **Teaching flight** — They can teach human children to fly by transforming their bodies, carrying them up onto the windmill sails and sweeping them through the air (001-008)
- **Transformation of the body** — When they kiss a child's hands and feet, the child "felt as if she were made of feather-down" and could sway "up and down as lightly as they" (001-018)
- **Invisibility and secrecy** — They vanish "in an instant" (001-018). Only those who have been touched by them can see them
- **Conditional blessing** — If a person keeps faith, "the windfairies will never forget you, but will come to your help in your direst hour of need" (001-016). This blessing is conditional on secrecy
- **Storm-summoning** — In the climactic scene, they summon a catastrophic wind that sweeps an axe from an executioner's hand, topples houses, and reduces a royal palace to ruins, while leaving their protected human untouched (001-066)

## Role in the Story

The Windfairies open the collection as the first supernatural beings the reader encounters. They choose the child [[lucilla|Lucilla]] because she is unafraid and promises faithfully to keep their secret (001-013, 001-015). They teach her to dance and fly — gifts that shape her entire life. They are not seen again for many years, during which time Lucilla grows to womanhood, marries [[the-sailor|a sailor]], and becomes a mother (001-020).

Years later, when Lucilla has grown and married, they return to claim her through a chain of events they orchestrate. Sailors arrive at the mill, telling Lucilla of a distant [[the-king|King]] who loves beautiful dancing and would reward any dancer with a sack of gold (001-027). Lucilla travels to the royal court and dances before the King, who demands to know who taught her — the question that tests her fidelity to the Windfairies' command (001-035, 001-043). When Lucilla refuses to reveal her teachers, [[the-queen|the Queen]], consumed by jealousy, accuses her of witchcraft. Lucilla endures trial by water — she cannot be drowned — and is sentenced to burning, then to beheading (001-058, 001-063).

At the final moment, the Windfairies stage their greatest intervention. As the executioner raises his axe, "there came a sudden roar of wind, and the axe was swept from his hand, and the houses in the marketplace tottered and fell, and high up on the hill the palace was a mass of ruins" (001-066). Only Lucilla remains unharmed on the scaffold. They reunite her with her husband, whose ship they have guided unerringly to the town, and provide a bag of gold for her journey home (001-064, 001-065). Through Lucilla, the Windfairies' gifts ripple outward to affect kings and queens, brides and rivals. Their power is the invisible engine that drives the entire plot.

## Dwelling / Realm

The Windfairies dwell in the open air — on the downs, in the wind, over the sea. They are not bound to any house or underground realm but move freely wherever the wind blows. Their closest earthly anchor is the [[windmill-on-the-downs|windmill on the downs]] where Lucilla's father [[the-miller|the miller]] lived, a place where the wind is always present. Unlike many fairy creatures who inhabit underground palaces or enchanted forests, the Windfairies belong to no fixed geography. They are creatures of passage, encountered at thresholds — at midnight, on the downs, between land and sky.

## Bargains & Conditions

The Windfairies impose a binding magical contract on those they adopt. The terms are stated with precision: Lucilla must never reveal who taught her to dance. If she breaks this promise, three consequences follow — "your feet will become heavy as lead," "some great misfortune will overtake whatever you love best in this world," and she will never dance again (001-016). But if she keeps faith, the Windfairies pledge their reciprocal loyalty: they "will never forget you, but will come to your help in your direst hour of need" (001-016).

This bargain is tested throughout the story. Lucilla keeps faith across decades, never telling her father, her husband, or her children about the Windfairies. When she is brought before the King and pressed to reveal her teachers, she invents explanations — she learned from watching windmill sails — but never betrays the secret (001-035, 001-043). When the Queen privately interrogates her, Lucilla offers only the partial truth that she did learn her dancing from someone, but refuses to say whom (001-054). The cost of fidelity is high: she faces drowning, fire, and the axe. But the Windfairies fulfill their side of the covenant completely, rescuing her at every trial and ultimately devastating the entire city to protect her (001-058, 001-066).

The bargain creates the story's central moral tension: [[obedience]] to a sacred promise against [[obedience]] to royal command, [[secrecy]] against self-preservation. Lucilla's [[fear]] of breaking faith with the Windfairies outweighs her fear of death — "woe is me, for I dare not break faith with the windfairies, and yet if I do not, I shall never see my husband or my babies again, for I fear lest they may put me to death here" (001-051).

## Quotes

> "We are windfairies," said the first grey figure. (001-004)

> "If you keep faith with us, then the windfairies will never forget you, but will come to your help in your direst hour of need." (001-016)

> "Try me," cried Lucilla again, "I will never tell. Indeed, I would never tell anyone, never." And in an instant the windfairies had vanished. (001-018)

> "Oh, kind windfairies," she cried, "see, I have kept faith with you, so do you now keep faith with me, and do me no hurt." (001-025)

> "Alas! alas! what have I done to deserve this? surely I have done no wrong to be so cruelly treated. Dear windfairies, come to my help, for in truth now is the time of my direst need, and if you desert me I am lost; but I pray you keep faith with me, as I have kept faith with you." (001-058)

> Then the executioner lifted the axe in the air, but before it fell, there came a sudden roar of wind, and the axe was swept from his hand, and the houses in the marketplace tottered and fell, and high up on the hill the palace was a mass of ruins. Only Lucilla knelt upon the scaffold unhurt. (001-066)
