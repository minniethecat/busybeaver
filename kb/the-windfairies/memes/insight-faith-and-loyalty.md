# Faith and Loyalty in The Windfairies

## Thesis

The Windfairies collection does not treat faith and loyalty as passive sentiments or abstract ideals. Across its tales, faithfulness is an active, costly moral labor — requiring physical endurance, the risk of failure, and the surrender of easier paths — and the collection insists with striking consistency that such labor is invariably rewarded. Nanina must endure seven nights of excruciating pain to redeem her broken promise. Hulda must walk across strange country on swollen feet, following a bird she cannot understand, to restore a voice she may never hear speak her name. Lucilla must face imprisonment, drowning, burning, and beheading rather than reveal a secret she swore to keep. In each case, the faithful act is not a single heroic moment but an extended ordeal — and the reward, when it comes, is proportionate to the suffering endured. The collection's moral architecture is built on this conviction: that keeping faith is the supreme test of character, the bond that links mortals to the supernatural, and the one virtue whose practice guarantees protection in a world where invisible powers watch, judge, and intervene.

## Evidence

### Nanina: Seven Nights of Faithful Endurance

Nanina's story in "Nanina's Sheep" (Story 4) begins with a broken promise — the simplest and most consequential form of failed faith. Her master warns her to stay away from the old palace, and "Nanina promised, and her master started" (004-001). The promise is made freely and broken casually, driven not by malice but by the idle curiosity of a bored child: "If it is filled with fairies, it won't hurt me just to look at it; I should like to see a fairy" (004-003). This is the collection's first lesson about faith: broken promises carry consequences that cascade far beyond the breaker's intentions. Nanina loses her entire flock — six sheep and eight lambs — to the [[the-fairy-shepherd|Fairy Shepherd]]'s enchanted pipe, not because she meant harm but because she did not mean to keep her word.

The story's deeper argument about faith, however, lies not in the fall but in the recovery. Redemption is offered — but only on terms of extraordinary suffering. The [[the-beech-tree|beech tree]], whose broken branches Nanina bound in an unsought act of kindness (004-018), reveals the terms: "Every time you refuse to dance with the fairy, one of your flock will be returned" (004-020). The condition is cruelly precise. Refusal is exactly what the [[the-magic-pipe|fairy's pipe]] makes impossible — its music compels movement, and "when once she heard the pipe, Nanina could not keep still" (004-017). The solution is not willpower but physical immobilization: Nanina must bury her feet in the earth each night, letting the tree's roots grip her ankles "as if they were bound with cords" (004-023), while the [[the-fairy-shepherd|Fairy Shepherd]] dances before her playing music that tears at her resolve.

> "Bury your feet in the earth like my roots. Dig a hole deep down, and I will hold your feet so that you shall not move them, only you must bear the pain, and not mind if you walk lame afterwards, for I shall hold them very tight, and it will hurt you." — (004-022)

Nanina's response is immediate and absolute: "Hurt me as you please, and I shan't mind. If only I can get back my sheep I will bear any pain" (004-023). This is the moment her faith transforms from passive contrition into active endurance. Each night the ordeal worsens. By the final nights, "her feet were so bad that she could scarcely hobble, for they were crushed and bleeding" (004-024), and on the last night she limps to the tree "almost crying with pain" (004-025). The Fairy Shepherd appears in his most magnificent form — "dressed in scarlet and gold" with a jewelled crown — and his pipe plays "long sad notes, like a funeral march" (004-025), no longer an invitation to joy but a seduction of despair. Nanina resists, and the enchantment shatters:

> With a mighty crash like thunder the shepherd and the goats disappeared, and not only had they gone, but the walls of the old palace had fallen, and nothing was left of it but a heap of stones. — (004-028)

The reward is total: all fourteen sheep are restored. But the story's final note is more ambiguous and more truthful. Nanina limps home, her feet "wounded and bleeding, where she had dashed them about in trying to dance" (004-028), and when her master asks what happened, she offers a half-truth: "Of a truth, master, it was in saving the lambs when they got into dangerous places" (004-029). Faith has been kept — the flock is safe — but full honesty has not. The collection refuses to present Nanina as perfected; she has learned the cost of faithfulness through her body, but her moral education is incomplete. The scarlet flowers that bloom where her blood fell — "whenever she passed and saw them she remembered how she had been punished for disobeying her master, and made up her mind never to do so again" (004-030) — are a permanent memorial to what faith cost and what its breach required.

Nanina's mode of faith is penitential: she must undo her own transgression through suffering, and the reward is restoration of what she lost. The collection presents this as the minimum form of faith — keeping promises already made, repairing breaches already committed — and treats even this minimum as exacting a heavy price.

### Hulda: Loyalty Across Distance

If Nanina's faith is penitential, [[hulda|Hulda]]'s is redemptive. She bears no guilt for Othmar's loss; she warned him against the dwarf and was dismissed with irritation: "How silly you are, Hulda!" (007-010). Her loyalty is entirely voluntary — a love that persists through rejection, silence, and the apparent hopelessness of the task she sets herself. And unlike Nanina, who must be still to be faithful, Hulda must move — across strange country, through mockery and imprisonment, following guidance she cannot verify from sources no one else would trust.

Hulda is introduced with deliberate plainness: "She was neither pretty nor clever" (007-004). She is an orphan who works for an old woman in exchange for food and lodging, of no particular station or talent. What defines her is not gifts but a quality of attention: she sits behind Othmar during the dwarf's concert and "shuddered as she heard it" (007-004) when everyone else is transported. She is the only one who intuits danger in the music, and after Othmar returns mute — "Hulda knew that Othmar was dumb" (007-031) — she is the only one who suspects the truth: "only Hulda said to herself, 'it was the wicked little man'" (007-034).

Her declaration of intent, made at midnight on midsummer after a year of Othmar's silence, defines the active form of faith that the collection most admires:

> "I am going into the big world to find him, for if he has done you this ill he will know how to cure you, and I have saved all my money for a year." — (007-037)

Othmar weeps and shakes his head — he believes the quest futile — but Hulda does not heed him (007-038). She asks for three things before leaving: a curl of his hair, a kiss, and the promise that her name will be his first word when speech returns (007-039). The ritual transforms their parting from a desperate venture into a covenant. Faith, here, is not merely felt but enacted — a binding agreement that Hulda will return with what was lost, and Othmar will be ready to receive it.

Her [[huldas-quest|quest]] reverses the hierarchy of wisdom that the collection's other tales uphold. She seeks counsel from a shepherd, an animal-healer, and a schoolmaster — all fail her. The schoolmaster, the story's representative of institutional learning, dismisses her outright: "This is a foolish talk about fairies and sorcerers, I know nothing of such things" (007-048). It is when Hulda, in desperation, asks for the *most ignorant and foolish* person in the district that she finds her guide: Tommy the fool, who tells her to "follow the raven" (007-058). Hulda's willingness to trust a fool's advice — to follow a bird she cannot understand, across country "where Hulda had never been" (007-061) — is itself an act of faith. She must trust without verification, act without knowing whether the action will succeed.

> She was footsore and weary, but she would not give up. — (007-061)

The ordeal compounds. She must grasp a live snake "though she was very much afraid of it" (007-065). The villagers of a town she passes through believe she is mad and lock her in a schoolhouse, attempting to kill the snake she has carried across a continent (007-098). She escapes through a window at night, clutching the snake to her chest, her faith in the fool's cryptic instructions the only thing sustaining her. When she finally returns to Othmar, her triumph collapses into despair — she believes she has failed:

> "Othmar," she cried, "I have done you no good, save that I have punished the wicked girl who took your voice." — (007-105)

But her loyalty, expressed in action rather than sentiment, has already accomplished what she cannot yet see. Othmar breathes her name upon the snake, and it transforms — not into the weapon she feared but into a golden trumpet. His voice returns in a burst of music, and his first spoken word is her name: "Hulda!" (007-106). The reward is more than restoration of what was lost; it is the transformation of suffering into something beautiful. The snake becomes a trumpet. Silence becomes a name. And Othmar's lifelong tribute confirms that her faith has permanently reshaped their bond:

> "It is not really my voice, it is my wife's, Hulda's, for I should have been dumb forever if she had not sought it and brought it back to me." — (007-107)

Hulda's mode of faith is quest-loyalty: she risks everything for another, with no guarantee of success, and the reward is not merely the restoration of voice but the transformation of her own place in the world — from overlooked orphan to the acknowledged center of Othmar's life and art.

### Keeping Faith as Divine Principle

The collection embeds these two narratives of faithful endurance within a larger theological framework, most explicitly articulated in "The Windfairies" (Story 1) and crystallized in the concept of [[keeping-faith]]. The windfairies' covenant with [[lucilla|Lucilla]] establishes the cosmic terms under which all the tales operate:

> "But if you keep faith with us, then the windfairies will never forget you, but will come to your help in your direst hour of need." — (001-016)

This is not a vague promise but a binding contract. Lucilla is taught to dance by the windfairies on condition of absolute [[secrecy]]: she must never reveal who taught her or risk losing the gift and bringing misfortune on all she loves. The test is exacting. She is brought before [[the-king|the King]] and [[the-queen|Queen]], imprisoned when she refuses to explain her dancing, and sentenced to death by drowning, burning, and beheading — each attempted execution failing through the windfairies' intervention. Her refrain at every crisis is a prayer that doubles as a legal claim:

> "Oh, dear windfairies, I have not broken faith with you, so don't break faith with me, and come to my help and save me in my trouble." — (001-046)

The language is striking in its reciprocity. Lucilla does not beg; she reminds. She has kept her side of the covenant, and she expects — demands — that the supernatural powers keep theirs. This is faith conceived as a two-way bond, not a one-sided submission. The windfairies respond not out of pity but out of obligation, honoring a contract that Lucilla's fidelity has kept in force.

At the scaffold, facing execution, her faith becomes explicit theological assertion:

> "Dear windfairies, come to my help, for in truth now is the time of my direst need, and if you desert me I am lost; but I pray you keep faith with me, as I have kept faith with you." — (001-058)

The windfairies answer with a storm that destroys the palace and scatters the court. Lucilla's reward is complete vindication — reunion with her husband, restoration of her children, and the promised bag of gold (001-067). Her final words are not a plea but a confirmation: "Now, dear windfairies, do I indeed know that you have kept faith with me, and saved me in my direst hour of need" (001-067). Faith has moved from hope to knowledge — she now *knows* what she previously only believed.

Between Lucilla's cosmic contract, Nanina's penitential endurance, and Hulda's redemptive quest, the collection constructs a comprehensive moral architecture of faithfulness. Lucilla demonstrates that the supernatural realm honors its covenants as rigorously as it demands them. Nanina demonstrates that even broken faith can be restored — but only through suffering proportional to the breach. And Hulda demonstrates that faith need not be obligatory to be rewarded — her loyalty to Othmar was freely chosen, and the collection treats it as no less sacred for being voluntary.

The counter-example is [[kesta|Kesta]], whose story in "Vain Kesta" (Story 2) illustrates what happens when faith is never offered at all. Kesta makes promises to Adam, the miller, [[the-bailiff|the Bailiff]], and [[the-banker|the Banker]] with no intention of keeping any of them. Her refrain — "I will come back and give you an answer" — is a formula of evasion, not commitment. The tale's verdict is delivered through Adam's blunt rejection: "Indeed, Kesta, I cannot marry you now, since you've been to the town and tried to get a finer husband than me" (002-083). Faithlessness, in the collection's moral calculus, destroys even the humblest possibility of return. Kesta ends as she began — alone — not through supernatural punishment but through the inevitable logic of a life lived without fidelity.

## Comparative Analysis

The collection's three portraits of faith — Lucilla, Nanina, and Hulda — form a graduated taxonomy of the virtue, each mode distinct in its motivation, its cost, and its scope.

**Motivation**. Lucilla's faith is covenantal: she keeps her promise to the windfairies because the promise itself is sacred, a bond whose breaking would be a cosmic wrong. Nanina's faith is penitential: she endures the seven nights not out of abstract principle but because she has already broken faith once and must repair the breach. Hulda's faith is redemptive: she has broken no promise and bears no guilt, yet she undertakes a quest whose only motive is love for another person. The collection thus moves outward in concentric circles — from faith to the divine, to faith as self-repair, to faith as gift to another.

**Cost**. All three modes of faith exact a physical price, and the collection insists on recording it. Lucilla faces imprisonment and three attempted executions. Nanina's feet are crushed and bleeding; she limps for the rest of her life. Hulda walks until her feet swell and her legs stiffen; she is mocked, imprisoned, and nearly loses the very thing she carried to save Othmar. The collection never abstracts faithfulness into a purely spiritual virtue. It is inscribed on the body, measured in wounds and exhaustion, and the narrative does not flinch from describing the pain.

**Trial structure**. Each ordeal follows a pattern of incremental escalation. Lucilla faces drowning, then burning, then beheading — each attempt more final than the last. Nanina endures seven consecutive nights, each more agonizing than the previous, with the fairy's music shifting from joyful dance tunes to a funeral march. Hulda's quest moves from the village to the wider world, through a series of guides who fail her, until she is reduced to following a fool and a raven. In each case, faith is tested progressively, each stage demanding more than the last, and the narrative rhythm of escalation creates a structural argument: that faithfulness is not a single decision but a continuous act of renewal under worsening conditions.

**Reward**. The pattern of reward is as consistent as the pattern of trial — and as graduated. Lucilla's fidelity brings apocalyptic vindication: the destruction of the palace, the scattering of her enemies, and the restoration of her family. Nanina's endurance brings back exactly what she lost — fourteen sheep — and leaves her with a permanent memorial of scarlet flowers and a permanent limp. Hulda's quest transforms what was lost into something greater: Othmar does not merely regain his voice; his voice becomes "my wife's, Hulda's," permanently marked by her gift. The collection suggests that the scale of reward corresponds to the purity of motive. Lucilla kept faith with the divine and was vindicated absolutely. Nanina kept faith to undo her own error and was restored to where she began. Hulda kept faith for another's sake and was rewarded with transformation — the snake became a trumpet, silence became a name, and her place in the world changed forever.

**What breaks the ordeal**. In each case, faith is not the only agent of resolution. Lucilla is saved by the windfairies, Nanina by the beech tree, Hulda by the fool and the raven. Faithfulness, in the collection's moral universe, does not operate in isolation. It opens a channel through which supernatural aid can flow — but only if the mortal continues to act. Lucilla must keep calling on the windfairies at each crisis. Nanina must bury her feet each night, knowing the pain will worsen. Hulda must follow the raven across strange country, grasp the snake she fears, and carry it home though everyone she meets tries to take it from her. The collection's theology is synergistic: the supernatural realm stands ready to help, but the mortal must prove worthy of that help through sustained, active faithfulness.

## Conclusion

The Windfairies presents faith and loyalty not as soft virtues — sentiments one feels or intentions one holds — but as the hardest demands the moral life can make. Across three stories told in different keys — the cosmic drama of Lucilla, the pastoral ordeal of Nanina, and the quest romance of Hulda — the collection builds a consistent argument: that faithfulness is costly, bodily, incremental, and invariably rewarded in proportion to the purity of its motive and the depth of its suffering.

This insistence on reward is the collection's most distinctive theological claim. In a universe governed by invisible powers who watch and judge, faith-keeping is not merely good — it is *safe*. The windfairies' promise — "if you keep faith with us, then the windfairies will never forget you" (001-016) — is both a blessing and a warning. Those who keep faith earn supernatural protection. Those who break it, even through innocent curiosity, pay a price measured in lost flocks and crushed feet. Those who never offer it at all, like Kesta, simply disappear into the silence of their own inconsequence.

The tales do not pretend that faithfulness is easy. Nanina screams, bleeds, and limps. Hulda is imprisoned and mocked. Lucilla faces execution. But they insist, with a unanimity rare in any story collection, that it is *possible* — and that the world, both natural and supernatural, is arranged to honor it. The scarlet flowers blooming from Nanina's blood, the golden trumpet born from Hulda's snake, the ruined palace scattered by windfairies who kept their word: these are not merely happy endings. They are the collection's articles of faith, set down in narrative form.
