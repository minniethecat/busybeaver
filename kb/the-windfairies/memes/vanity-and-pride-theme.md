# Vanity and Pride in The Windfairies

## Thesis

The Windfairies collection does not treat pride as a simple, unitary vice. Instead, it distinguishes among several distinct species of pride -- appearance-based vanity, status-rooted arrogance, and possessive entitlement -- and assigns each a different moral weight and consequence. Kesta's vanity, rooted in her conviction that her beauty entitles her to a station above her birth, is punished with public humiliation but stops short of destruction: she is laughed out of town, not destroyed. The Duke's aristocratic pride is satirized, its absurdity exposed when he nearly marries a dairymaid. And the King's possessive pride -- his determination to keep Lucilla's dancing for himself -- shades into cruelty when thwarted, yet the narrative treats him as weak rather than evil. The collection reserves its harshest judgments not for the proud themselves but for those who, like [[the-queen|the Queen]], weaponize pride into active persecution. Through this graduated moral taxonomy, the tales offer something subtler than simple fairy-tale moralizing: pride is a spectrum, and its punishments are calibrated to its degree of malice.

## Vanity in "Vain Kesta"

Kesta is the collection's most fully realized study in vanity, and the tale's title announces her defining trait. Her vanity is not a passive self-regard but an active, strategic self-marketing. From the story's opening, Kesta's relationship with her own beauty is instrumental: she looks in her mirror not simply to admire herself but to calculate what her beauty can buy.

> "I wonder why Adam wishes to marry me? but as he does, most likely some better man would like to do so; it would be folly to marry him till I see if I can't do better." — (002-004)

This is the engine of the entire story. Kesta does not merely think herself pretty; she thinks herself pretty enough to transcend her station. Each man she encounters -- [[adam|Adam]], the miller, [[the-bailiff|the Bailiff]], [[the-banker|the Banker]], [[the-general|the General]], and finally [[the-duke|the Duke]] -- is evaluated not as a potential partner but as a rung on a social ladder. Her refrain is always the same: she is "too good" for the one who wants her, and therefore entitled to the next rung up.

> "And well I may be," said Kesta, "such a run as I have had. I've come from the farm yonder, and it was as much as I could do to get away, for the farmer's man was very angry because I would not marry him, and of course I am too good for him, a pretty girl like me." — (002-007)

The irony that drives the story is that Kesta's vanity is not delusional in the narrow sense. She is, by the tale's own account, genuinely pretty. Her error is not in overestimating her beauty but in misunderstanding its currency -- beauty is not a ladder out of one's class, and social mobility through marriage is a fantasy the tale dismantles with comic precision. Each suitor she deceives into proposing is equally deceived about her, wanting her only because of the reflected desire of others. The chain of proposals is a bubble inflated entirely by vanity and gossip, and it bursts the moment her true identity as a "common dairymaid" is exposed (002-047).

[[lucilla|Lucilla]] serves throughout the collection as the implicit foil to Kesta. Where Kesta weaponizes beauty, Lucilla barely seems aware of hers. She "grew up to be a beautiful young woman, and her father was very proud of her," yet she "never saw the windfairies again, though she watched for them every night" (001-020). Her beauty is simply a fact about her, not a strategy. She never uses it to advance herself; when she goes to the King's court, it is under duress, and what captivates the court is not her appearance but her [[dance|dancing]], a gift she received through faithfulness, not self-promotion. The collection's moral arithmetic is clear: the humble are exalted; the vain are humiliated.

The closing line of "Vain Kesta" delivers its verdict with a bluntness that is almost jarring after the comic energy of the preceding chase:

> "Indeed, Kesta, I cannot marry you now, since you've been to the town and tried to get a finer husband than me," and he went back to his work, and left Kesta sitting all alone; and there she sat and cried by herself, and did not get any husband after all, because she was so false and vain. — (002-083)

The punishment fits the crime with narrative economy: Kesta's vanity drove her to reject [[adam|Adam]] in pursuit of ever-grander prospects; she ends up with no one. But the punishment is civil -- she is not imprisoned, tortured, or killed. She is simply left alone with the consequences of her choices. The tale treats vanity as a moral error that corrects itself, almost automatically, once the vain person's true station is revealed.

## Pride and Authority: The King

If Kesta's vanity is rooted in appearances, [[the-king|the King]]'s pride is rooted in power. He represents a different species altogether -- the possessive pride of those accustomed to getting what they want. The King is introduced as a connoisseur of beauty: he "loved nothing so much as beautiful dancing, and he would give great sums of money to anyone who danced well" (001-027). But what begins as aesthetic appreciation slides into possessiveness. When Lucilla dances, the King wants to keep her -- and her gift -- for himself:

> "Wife, if this dancer persists in her silence, and will not tell us how she has learnt, there is another thing which we must do. We must keep her here to dance for us as much as we choose, and not let her return at all to the home from which she came." — (001-044)

This is pride of a specifically political kind: the assumption that anything beautiful within the King's domain belongs to him by right. The King never questions whether he has the right to detain Lucilla -- he simply asserts it. His pride is less personal than Kesta's but more dangerous because it is backed by the machinery of state. When Lucilla refuses to reveal her secret, "the King became angry" (001-043). His anger is not the comic rage of the Duke discovering a dairymaid, but the cold anger of authority defied.

Yet the narrative is notably gentle with the King. After all, "the King refused, for he said that Lucilla had done no wrong" (001-050) -- he resists the Queen's schemes initially, and only capitulates because "he was very angry with Lucilla for wanting to go home, and much disappointed to think he should see her dancing no more." He is weak, not wicked. The Queen is the story's true villain, and the King's sin is less pride itself than the abdication of moral responsibility in the face of his wife's manipulations. The collection seems to suggest that pride can be venial when it remains passive, but becomes mortal when it crosses the line into active cruelty.

Contrast this with [[the-duke|the Duke]], whose pride is not possessive but aristocratic -- the assumption that birth alone determines worth. When he discovers Kesta makes cheeses, he screams "How dare you think yourself good enough to marry me!" (002-047). The Duke's pride is treated as absurd -- he very nearly did marry her, after all -- and he is the most broadly comic of the prideful figures. The collection reserves its sharpest satire for the pride of inherited status.

## The Moral Economy of Pride

The Windfairies collection operates on a graduated scale of moral judgment for pride. At one end sits Kesta: her vanity is punished, but proportionately. She loses what she undervalued -- a good match with Adam -- through her own machinations. The punishment is delivered not by supernatural agents but by the social world itself: the mob that chases her through town is composed of the very suitors she tried to manipulate. Vanity is its own punishment because the social ladder she tried to climb collapses under her.

At the other end sits [[the-queen|the Queen]], whose pride-fueled jealousy crosses the line into active persecution. She lies, schemes, and attempts to have an innocent woman executed. Unlike Kesta's social humiliation or the King's uneasy complicity, the Queen's malice is met with unambiguous destruction: the windfairies level the entire town, "the palace was a mass of ruins," and the King and Queen are blown "right and left, amidst the ruins of the houses" (001-066). The collection's harshest judgment falls not on pride per se but on the cruelty that pride enables.

Between these extremes lies a more nuanced space. The Duke's aristocratic pride is mocked but not punished -- he simply looks foolish. The King's possessive pride is lamented but not condemned -- he is portrayed as fundamentally decent but weak. Even the miller, the bailiff, the banker, and the general in "Vain Kesta" all share in Kesta's vanity insofar as each believes he deserves a prize wife because someone else wanted her first. Their pride is collective, almost institutional -- the pride of men in a patriarchal social order who assume beautiful women are commodities to be acquired. The tale exposes this too, but gently: they all simply go back to their lives, having learned nothing, and the narrative does not seem to mind.

This graduated moral landscape distinguishes the collection from simpler fairy-tale didacticism. [[vanity]] is not an absolute evil but a context-dependent failing. The collection's real moral target is not pride in oneself, but the cruelty one inflicts on others because of it. Both [[keeping-faith]] and [[love]] -- the collection's highest virtues -- are defined by their orientation toward others, not toward the self. Pride, in its various forms, is essentially an inward turn, a failure to see other people clearly; the punishments the tales mete out are calibrated to how much damage that inward gaze causes.

## Conclusion

The Windfairies does not offer simple moralizing about pride and vanity. Kesta's story is comic, the King's is tragicomic, and the Queen's is genuinely dark -- yet all three are studies in the same fundamental failing, differing only in degree and in malice. The collection's moral insight is that pride is not one thing: it is a self-regarding orientation that can express itself as vanity about one's looks, arrogance about one's station, or possessiveness about one's pleasures. What matters is not the pride itself but what the proud person does with it. Those who merely overestimate themselves, like Kesta, are allowed to learn from their humiliation. Those who actively harm others, like the Queen, are destroyed. And those in between, like the King, simply endure the consequences of their weakness.

By distinguishing among these varieties of pride and assigning each its own moral weight, the collection achieves something more subtle than the conventional fairy-tale warning against vanity. It suggests that pride is universal -- even the virtuous miller is "very proud" of Lucilla (001-001) -- and that the moral question is not whether one is proud but whether one's pride leads one to diminish or destroy others. That is a surprisingly humane and psychologically acute position for a collection of fairy tales.
