On Christmas morning, immediately after a miserly old gentleman cruelly refused charity to a beggar at his door, a mysterious grey cat materialised on his hearthrug — impossibly dry and immaculate despite the blizzard outside. This uncanny arrival is the inciting event of "The Story of a Cat," setting in motion a slow-working enchantment that would ultimately humble the old man's greed.

## Context

A rich old gentleman lived alone in a handsome house, served by a good cook. He had earned his wealth through trade but had grown "so fond of money that he loved it better than anything else in the world" [006-001]. Though he enjoyed fine food and wine, he rarely shared them, and his servants "knew that he was greedy and hard, and cared for nobody" [006-001].

On Christmas Day, with snow thick on the ground and a fierce wind howling outside, the old gentleman sat comfortably by his fire, a screen before his face, warming his feet on the marble hearth. Through the window, he saw a beggar — an old man in rags, about his own age, with grey curly hair like his own, looking "half frozen" as he pressed his face to the glass [006-005–006].

## The Christmas Refusal

The beggar asked for a silver shilling to buy a Christmas dinner. The old gentleman refused indignantly, declaring he "never" gave to beggars [006-011]. When the beggar asked instead for broken victuals from the table, the old gentleman stamped his foot and threatened to call the police [006-013].

What struck the old gentleman as odd was that instead of seeming distressed, the beggar "was laughing merrily" as he turned away [006-014]. Looking back at the window, he called out words "in a foreign tongue" that the old gentleman could not understand [006-014]. The old gentleman dismissed the encounter and returned to his comfortable fireside, confident he had done nothing wrong.

## The Mysterious Arrival

The next morning, with the snow falling more thickly than ever, the old gentleman came down to breakfast and found, to his "great surprise," a cat on the hearthrug in front of the fire, "looking into it, and blinking lazily" [006-013].

The old gentleman had never kept any animal before. He immediately tried to shoo it away, but the cat did not move. Looking at it more closely, he could not help but admire it:

> It was a very large cat, grey and black, and had extremely long soft hair, and a thick soft ruff round its neck. Moreover, it looked very well fed and cared for, and as if it had always lived in comfortable places. [006-013]

Most peculiarly, though the ground outside was deep in snow and slush, the cat's fur was "quite dry, and its fur looked as if it had just been combed and brushed" [006-016]. The cook declared she had not seen it before and believed "it must have come down the chimney as all the doors and windows had been shut and bolted" [006-016].

The cat's behaviour was equally strange. It did not mew for food or attention like an ordinary stray. Instead, it sat by the fire with the quiet confidence of a creature who belonged exactly where it was. As the narrator later observed:

> It lapped its milk not greedily or in a hurry, but as if it were quite used to good food and comfortable living. [006-016]

## The Decision to Keep It

The old gentleman, ever mindful of profit, thought the cat was so fine that "a reward would be offered for such an animal" [006-014]. His interest in the creature was purely mercenary — he saw it not as a companion but as a potential source of money. He decided to keep it temporarily while scanning the newspapers for a reward notice.

He fed it a large saucer of milk, which the cat lapped with dignified restraint. Though he searched the newspapers and street notices diligently, the old gentleman found no advertisement for a lost grey-and-black cat. So the cat "stayed on with him from day to day" [006-016], and nearly a year passed.

During this year, the cat's "lazy contented purring after its food" seemed to speak "of nothing but comfort and affluence" [006-017]. It became a fixture of the old gentleman's house — not as a pet he loved, but as a beautiful possession he did not wish to part with.

## Participants

- **[[the-old-gentleman]]** — A wealthy, miserly man whose refusal of charity on Christmas Day precedes the cat's uncanny arrival. His only interest in the animal is its potential monetary value; he keeps it for nearly a year not from affection but from the hope of reward money [006-001, 013–017]
- **The beggar** — An old man in rags who appears at the window on Christmas Day, looking remarkably like the old gentleman himself. His mysterious laughter and foreign words suggest he is no ordinary beggar but a supernatural agent testing the old man's character [006-005–014]
- **The grey cat** — A large, well-groomed, grey-and-black cat of extraordinary beauty that appears as if by magic through a locked and bolted house in a snowstorm. It is clearly no stray but a creature of comfort and mystery, and its purring contentment masks a deeper purpose [006-013, 016–017]

## Outcome

The cat remained with the old gentleman for nearly a year, its luxurious presence becoming part of the household routine. When a knowledgeable acquaintance later identified it as an extremely rare and valuable breed, the old gentleman's greed was reignited — setting in motion events that would lead to the cat's mysterious transformation and the old gentleman's reckoning with his own avarice.

## Significance

The cat's arrival is the inciting event of "The Story of a Cat," and its timing — Christmas morning, immediately after the old gentleman's cruel refusal — is no coincidence. The beggar's resemblance to the old gentleman, his mysterious laughter, and his foreign words all mark him as a magical agent. The cat, appearing impossibly through a locked house in a blizzard, with dry fur and a well-groomed coat, is almost certainly the beggar's doing.

The scene establishes the tale's central moral pattern: wealth without charity is a form of poverty, and those who refuse to give will find that something is taken from them. The old gentleman sees the cat as a potential windfall — a valuable animal he can profit from — and this very greed is what the enchantment will ultimately punish. The cat, meanwhile, embodies a paradox: it is both a blessing (a beautiful creature that asks nothing) and a test (will the old gentleman learn to love something beyond its monetary worth?).

The supernatural mechanics are deliberately understated. There is no flash of lightning or spoken curse — only a beggar's laughter, a foreign phrase, and a cat that could not possibly be there. This restraint is characteristic of the story's narrative economy: the supernatural is present but almost invisible, working through ordinary domestic details and the slow accumulation of consequences.
