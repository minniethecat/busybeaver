# The Prince

The Prince is a young royal who appears in "The Rain Maiden," the eighth tale in *The Windfairies* collection. He defies convention by falling in love with a peasant girl rather than an arranged bride, embodying the fairy tale ideal of love transcending social boundaries. A brief reference to a prince also appears in "The Windfairies," where a princess arrives to marry the King.

## Role in the Story

In "The Rain Maiden," the Prince is heir to his father the King. When the time comes for him to marry, his father brings a suitable princess — but the Prince's heart belongs elsewhere. He confides to his father:

> At last a day came when the Prince said to his father, "My father, I cannot marry this princess, for my heart is given to another who is only a beggar-maid." [008-015]

Rather than submit to the arranged marriage, the Prince rides down to the cottage where the rain maiden Hulda lives and brings her to the court. His actions set up the central conflict of the tale — whether the court will accept a peasant as the Prince's bride.

When Hulda undergoes her trial at the court, where she is dressed in fine clothes and presented as a noblewoman, the marriage proceeds. However, when Hulda's true nature as a rain maiden is revealed and she vanishes, the Prince is left bereft: "they looked around for the Prince's wife, who was nowhere to be found" [008-025].

The story concludes with the Prince finding happiness after all: "he married a beautiful Princess, and with her lived very happily ever after" [008-026].

## Character Traits

- **Romantic and independent-minded**: The Prince chooses love over political convenience, openly defying his father's matrimonial plans
- **Brave**: He "rode down to the cottage" [008-016] personally to fetch his beloved, rather than sending servants — a direct, hands-on approach uncharacteristic of his station
- **Sincere**: Unlike the suitors in "Vain Kesta," the Prince's love is genuine and unwavering — he tells his father the truth directly rather than scheming
- **Resilient**: After losing Hulda, he eventually recovers and finds happiness with a princess, suggesting emotional resilience

## Key Actions

1. **Confesses his love**: The Prince openly tells his father that he cannot marry the arranged princess because his heart belongs to "a beggar-maid" [008-015]

2. **Fetches Hulda**: He "rode down to the cottage" [008-016] himself to bring the rain maiden Hulda to court

3. **Marries Hulda**: The wedding proceeds after Hulda is dressed appropriately, though the narrator notes she was only "proper as the wife of the Prince" for "the few days just before the wedding" [008-022] — hinting at the impermanence of her disguise

4. **Loses his bride**: When Hulda vanishes after the wedding, the Prince and the court search for her in vain

5. **Finds eventual happiness**: The final note that he "married a beautiful Princess, and with her lived very happily ever after" [008-026] provides a conventional fairy tale resolution

## Significance

The Prince represents a gentler model of romance than that found in "Vain Kesta." Where Kesta pursues husbands for social advancement, the Prince chooses love over status — sacrificing a politically advantageous marriage for a "beggar-maid." His willingness to defy his father and court convention aligns him with the fairy tale tradition of love conquering all.

However, the story's resolution is bittersweet. The Prince's love for Hulda cannot last, as her nature as a rain maiden (a supernatural elemental being) makes her incompatible with mortal life at court. His eventual marriage to a human princess suggests that while transcendent love is celebrated in fairy tales, lasting happiness comes from unions within one's own kind — a more pragmatic moral beneath the romantic surface.
