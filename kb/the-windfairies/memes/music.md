# Music

Music in these tales is never mere entertainment. It is a conduit for supernatural power — the [[windfairies]]' dancing, the [[the-fairy-shepherd|fairy shepherd]]'s pipe, and the [[the-dwarf-musician|dwarf musician]]'s fiddles all channel enchantment through sound. Music compels movement, alters consciousness, and reveals hidden truths that words cannot express.

## Definition

Music functions as the audible form of magic in the collection. When characters hear enchanted music, they lose volition — they must dance, must follow, must listen. The fairy shepherd's pipe "spoke to her heart" (004-004) with words Nanina understands; the dwarf musician's instruments transform into a "weird crowd" of human forms (007-013); the windfairies' dance is their very mode of being. Music bridges the mortal and fairy realms more directly than any other medium — it is the sensory experience of enchantment itself. Yet music also carries danger: [[othmar|Othmar]], robbed of his voice, finds "the sound of any music was quite horrible to him" (007-035), a punishment that severs him from the beauty he once commanded.

## Manifestations

### In "Dumb Othmar"

This tale is the collection's most elaborate exploration of music's power. The dwarf musician arrives leading a donkey "covered with numberless musical instruments" (007-002) — fiddles, flutes, pipes, and horns of silver and gold. When he plays, "Othmar was the only one who listened to the music who did not think it beautiful" (007-004), a first hint of his difference. The instruments later transform: "no longer did they look like musical instruments, but like human beings, men and women" (007-013). Music here is literally alive — the instruments are enchanted people. When Othmar's voice is stolen, his relationship with music becomes inverted: "the sound of any music was quite horrible to him after that night" (007-035). His eventual restoration comes through a final "burst of music from all around" (007-106) that echoes his freed voice.

### In "Nanina's Sheep"

The fairy shepherd's pipe carries the enchantment. When [[nanina|Nanina]] first hears it, "she listened open-mouthed to the music of the pipe, she heard it speak to her heart" (004-004). The music compels dance: "when I hear the music I shall begin to dance" (004-023). But when Nanina refuses to dance with the fairy, the pipe's tone changes — "it was not merry dance-music it made, but long sad notes" (004-025). Then when the music returns, "the music grew quicker, and her longing to dance grew stronger" (004-028). The pipe's music tracks the narrative's emotional arc — merry, sad, urgent — and controls the human characters' bodies.

### In "The Windfairies"

Music and dance are inseparable from the fairy realm. [[the-king|The King]]'s court features "musicians playing on every kind of instrument" (001-031) and [[lucilla|Lucilla]]'s dance is performed as "the musicians began to play" (001-034). Music here is the frame for human excellence — Lucilla's dancing is the response to music that the entire court witnesses as proof of her transcendent gift.

## Associated Characters

- **The Dwarf Musician** — His instruments are living beings; his music enchants entire villages and steals voices (007-002) (007-013)
- **Othmar** — A boy whose relationship with music defines his identity — first through his beautiful voice, then through its loss and the horror music becomes, finally through restoration (007-004) (007-035) (007-106)
- **Nanina** — Her vulnerability to the pipe's music drives the central action of her tale (004-004) (004-023)
- **The Fairy Shepherd** — Wields the magic pipe whose music speaks directly to the heart and compels dance (004-004) (004-025)

## Related Concepts

- [[love]] — Music and dance are the languages through which joy and love are expressed; Nanina's story links music to the love of her flock
- [[beauty]] — The beauty of music is a test — those who cannot perceive it (like Othmar after his loss) are marked as different
- [[wonder]] — Enchanted music produces wonder in its hearers, the entry point to the supernatural
- [[obedience]] — The compulsion of magic music tests obedience, as Nanina's struggle with the pipe's call demonstrates

## Moral Dimension

Music in these tales is morally ambivalent. It can be the highest expression of beauty and joy — Lucilla's dancing at court, the windfairies' revels — or an instrument of coercion and theft. The dwarf musician uses music to steal Othmar's voice; the fairy shepherd uses it to compel Nanina's dance. The moral lies not in music itself but in the musician's intent and the hearer's response. Nanina's struggle against the pipe's compulsion (004-023) represents resistance to enchantment that would override free will; Othmar's instinctive rejection of the dwarf's music (007-004) proves to be wisdom rather than deficiency. The tales suggest that the proper response to enchanted music is discernment, not surrender — to hear the beauty while recognizing the danger.
