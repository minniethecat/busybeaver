# The Sailor's Courtship of Lucilla

The sailor's courtship of Lucilla on the downs is the turning point from her solitary gift to shared life — the one suitor among many who finds a way past Lucilla's impossible dance test, not by dancing better but by the strength of his embrace.

## Context

Lucilla, now grown into a beautiful young woman, had many suitors from among the fishermen and country folk who came to the mill. But she set them all the same condition: "First let me see how you can dance" (001-024). None could match her supernatural lightness. Then a great storm blew a ship ashore, and among its crew was a young sailor who decided at once to wed her.

## The Event

The sailor — a young fellow with black curly hair, bright eyes, and white teeth — saw Lucilla and resolved: "I will wed that girl and take her home for my wife" (001-025). One day on the downs, he begged her to marry him, promising to give up the sea and live with her in a little cottage, making their bread by fishing (001-025).

Lucilla gave her standard condition: "First let me see how you can dance, for I will never marry any man who cannot dance with me." The sailor swore he could dance as well as any man in the world, for "all sailors can dance" (001-025–026).

They danced together on the downs. The sailor danced well and merrily, but Lucilla danced faster, seeming as if she were made of feather-down. The sailor, seeing his dancing was as nothing to hers, did something no other suitor had tried: he caught her by the waist and held her still, crying, "My sweetheart, I cannot dance as you can, but my arms are strong enough to hold you still and keep you from dancing with any man but me" (001-027).

## Outcome

Lucilla accepted the sailor, and they married. She went with him to live in his little cottage by the sea, many miles from the mill, taking her aging father with her. For some time they lived very happily and had two little children, though Lucilla could not pass her gift to them because the windfairies had never touched them (001-028).

## Significance

The sailor's courtship matters because it shows the limitations and the unexpected fulfillment of the windfairies' gift. Lucilla's supernatural dancing, meant to be a gift, becomes a barrier to ordinary human love — every suitor fails until one man simply bypasses the test entirely by holding her still rather than trying to match her. His solution honors her gift without trying to compete with it, making him the one mortal who truly understands her.
