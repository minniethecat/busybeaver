# Keeping Faith

The act of honoring a promise or oath, even at great personal cost — a cardinal virtue in the fairy-tale moral universe where faithfulness is invariably rewarded and betrayal brings ruin.

[[lucilla]] embodies this virtue most completely, but the theme runs through every tale in the collection: from the [[windfairies]]' covenant with a child, to [[nanina]]'s promise to her master, to [[kesta]]'s chain of broken promises. The tales present faith-keeping not as a passive quality but as an active moral struggle — one waged under threat of death, imprisonment, or social ruin — and they insist that those who persevere earn supernatural protection and ultimate vindication.

## Definition

In the world of these tales, keeping faith means more than merely honoring a promise. It is a sacred bond — once made, even to supernatural beings, it must be kept regardless of the consequences. The moral logic is reciprocal: those who keep faith with the fairies and spirits earn their protection in turn; those who break it suffer severe punishment. Faith-keeping functions as the central moral mechanism by which mortal characters prove their worthiness and earn supernatural aid (001-016).

The tales establish a clear hierarchy of promises. A promise to a supernatural being — the [[windfairies]], the [[the-fairy-shepherd|fairy shepherd]], the [[the-dwarf-musician|dwarf musician]] — carries absolute obligation. A promise to a mortal authority figure, like [[nanina]]'s master, carries strong but lesser weight. And a promise made for personal gain, like [[kesta]]'s successive engagements, carries no moral force at all because it is hollow from the start. This hierarchy reflects the tales' underlying theology: the supernatural realm is the source of moral order, and faithfulness to it is the highest virtue.

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

The tale of [[lucilla|Lucilla]] is the purest expression of faith-keeping in the collection. When the [[windfairies]] teach her to dance, they extract a solemn promise of [[secrecy]]: "if you break your word to us, and tell any mortal who it was that taught you how to dance, you will never dance again, for your feet will become heavy as lead" (001-016). Lucilla's entire subsequent ordeal — imprisonment, attempted drowning, burning, and beheading — tests this promise. She refuses to reveal her secret even to [[the-king|the King]] and [[the-queen|Queen]] at the cost of her life, crying out: "I dare not break faith with the windfairies" (001-051). Her fidelity is ultimately rewarded: the windfairies save her from execution, reunite her with her husband [[the-sailor]], and grant her the promised bag of gold (001-067).

Lucilla's faith-keeping is not passive. At each crisis point she actively calls upon the windfairies, reminding them of the mutual covenant. In prison she prays: "Oh, dear windfairies, I have not broken faith with you, so don't break faith with me, and come to my help and save me in my trouble" (001-046). On the scaffold she repeats her plea, and the windfairies respond with the terrible storm that destroys the palace and saves her life. The tale thus portrays faith-keeping as an active, reciprocal relationship — the mortal must continually reaffirm the bond, and the supernatural responds in kind.

### In "Vain Kesta"

[[kesta|Kesta]]'s story provides the counter-example. She makes promises to multiple suitors — [[adam|Adam]], [[the-miller|the miller]], [[the-bailiff|the bailiff]], and [[the-banker|the banker]] — with no intention of keeping them. Each promise is a tool of ambition, not a bond of honor. "I can't say yes at once," she tells Adam (002-003), but in truth she never intends to say yes at all. Her refrain to each suitor — "I will come back and give you an answer" — is a formula of evasion, not commitment.

The tale implicitly condemns this faithlessness: Kesta's vanity and broken promises lead to her humiliation. The structure of the story — a chain of progressively broken commitments — demonstrates that faith-keeping is the foundation of trustworthy character. When [[adam]] finally rejects her, his reason is explicit: "I cannot marry you now, since you've been to the town and tried to get a finer husband than me" (002-083). Her faithlessness has destroyed even the humblest possibility of return.

### In "Nanina's Sheep"

[[nanina|Nanina]]'s promise to her master — "Nanina promised, and her master started" (004-001) — sets up the tale's central tension. Her master warns her to stay away from [[the-old-palace|the fairy palace]]. Her subsequent disobedience, though driven by innocent curiosity rather than malice, shows that even well-intentioned promise-breaking can lead to supernatural entanglement and danger. "If it is filled with fairies," she reasons, "it won't hurt me just to look at it" (004-003) — a rationalization that leads to the loss of her entire flock.

Nanina's story adds an important nuance: faith-keeping is not merely about intentions but about actions. She did not mean to break her promise; she only meant to look. But the consequences cascade regardless. Her redemption comes through suffering — binding her feet in the roots of [[the-beech-tree|the beech tree]], enduring "excruciating pain" (004-023) night after night — a physical penance that mirrors the moral cost of broken faith.

### In "Dumb Othmar"

The theme of faith-keeping extends into [[dumb-othmar|"Dumb Othmar"]], where [[othmar|Othmar]] follows [[the-dwarf-musician|the dwarf musician]] underground. The dwarf's music is itself a kind of promise — a lure that offers beauty and enchantment in exchange for Othmar's voice. When Othmar returns mute, the broken trust between mortal and supernatural realms becomes literal: he can no longer speak. [[hulda|Hulda]]'s subsequent quest to recover his voice is an act of faith-keeping on his behalf — she honors a bond he could not keep, and her fidelity restores what his curiosity lost.

## Characters Who Embody This

- **[[lucilla|Lucilla]]** — The exemplar of faith-keeping, who endures imprisonment and near-execution rather than break her word to the windfairies (001-051) (001-058)
- **[[kesta|Kesta]]** — The antithesis: a vain promise-breaker whose faithlessness to Adam, the miller, the bailiff, and the banker reveals her shallow character (002-005) (002-019)
- **[[nanina|Nanina]]** — An intermediate case: a good-hearted girl who breaks a promise through curiosity rather than malice, and suffers proportionate consequences (004-001)
- **[[hulda|Hulda]]** — The faithful partner who keeps faith even when Othmar cannot, undertaking a perilous quest to restore what was lost (007-025)

## Moral Dimension

The tales teach an unambiguous lesson: keeping faith is the supreme moral test, and those who pass it earn supernatural protection. The windfairies' code — "if you keep faith with us, then the windfairies will never forget you" (001-016) — establishes a covenant that governs the entire narrative. Faith-keeping is not merely transactional; it is transformative. Lucilla's fidelity earns her not just rescue but vindication — the destruction of the corrupt court and her restoration to [[home]] and family. The tales suggest that faith-keeping binds the mortal and supernatural realms, creating obligations on both sides (001-061). Those who fail this test, like Kesta, lose all they schemed to gain.

This moral framework has a distinctly pre-modern character. Promises are not private agreements but cosmic bonds, witnessed by supernatural powers who enforce them. The windfairies' warning — "some great misfortune will overtake whatever you love best in this world" (001-016) — makes explicit what the tales imply throughout: faith-keeping is not optional. It is the price of safety in a world where invisible powers watch, judge, and intervene.
