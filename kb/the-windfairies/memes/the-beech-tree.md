# The Beech Tree

The beech tree is a sentient tree that stands near the old fairy palace in "Nanina's Sheep." Unlike the malevolent fairies of the palace, the beech tree is a benevolent spirit who helps Nanina resist the fairy shepherd's enchantment and recover her lost flock.

## Description

The beech tree grows near the old palace on the other side of the hill. Its lowest branches had been torn down by unknown hands, leaving them hanging broken. When Nanina finds the tree, she raises the broken branches and ties them up so they will grow together again — an act of kindness that awakens the tree's sentience and gratitude (004-018).

Within its leaves, Nanina sees "a small twisted face" looking at her (004-018). The tree speaks with a "shadowy voice" and a "soft sighing voice" (004-025).

## Counsel and Aid

When Nanina weeps beneath the tree, having lost all her sheep to the fairy shepherd's enchanted dancing, the beech tree offers her a plan. Its counsel is simple: "Nanina, don't dance" (004-018). It promises that if she minds its advice, she "shall get all your sheep back again" (004-018).

The tree's method is physically painful but effective:

1. Nanina digs a deep hole among the tree's roots and places her feet within (004-023)
2. The roots tighten around her feet "as if they were bound with cords," holding her in place (004-023)
3. When the fairy shepherd plays his pipe, Nanina's feet cannot move despite her desperate longing to dance (004-023)
4. Each night she resists, one of her lost sheep returns (004-023)

## The Final Night

On the last evening, Nanina's feet are "crushed and bleeding" from the roots' grip (004-025). The tree warns her: "Tonight is the last, and though it will hurt you the most, it will soon be past" (004-025). That night, the fairy shepherd appears in his most magnificent form — "a crown set with jewels... dressed in scarlet and gold" — playing not merry dance music but "long sad notes, like a funeral march" (004-025).

As the pipe plays its final long low note, "with a mighty crash like thunder the shepherd and the goats disappeared," the walls of the old palace collapsed into rubble, and the last sheep was restored (004-028). The tree's voice spoke one final time: "Goodbye, Nanina; now you have all your flock again" (004-028). Nanina "put her arms round the trunk and kissed and thanked it," but "the voice did not speak again" (004-028).

## Legacy

After Nanina's ordeal, the earth beneath the beech tree was left smooth where her feet had been held. The tree's roots had bruised her legs — marks of her painful resistance that proved her determination. Years later, the villagers believed the beech tree was a sacred place, and newlywed couples would visit it to receive its blessing (004-030).
