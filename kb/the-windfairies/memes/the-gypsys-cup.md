# The Gipsy's Cup

The Gipsy's Cup is the enchanted title object of "The Gipsy's Cup" (Chapter 5), a small brown clay vessel hand-made by [[the-gipsy|a gipsy girl]] on the potter's wheel one moonlit night. Unlike the brilliantly coloured pots and jugs she fashions alongside it, the cup is deliberately plain — "no gay colours in it" (005-012) — yet it carries the story's most potent magic: a love-charm sung into the wet clay in a strange language (005-010). This unassuming cup functions as the narrative engine of the entire tale, setting in motion a chain of love, betrayal, loss, and eventual redemption that spans years and crosses the paths of potters, gipsies, and wanderers. It is simultaneously a gift, a test, and a weapon — its ambiguous power making it one of the collection's most structurally significant magical objects.

## Description

### Physical Appearance

The cup is described as "a little brown cup" (005-014), small and modest in appearance, deliberately made "very plain" with "no gay colours in it" (005-012). Unlike the other vessels the gipsy creates — which emerge from the kiln with "the most wonderful colours that he had ever seen: silver, blue, grey and yellow, in all sorts of patterns" (005-014) — the cup remains unadorned. Its power lies not in its appearance but in what has been sung into it. The potter keeps it on a shelf for years, where it sits inconspicuously among his other wares. Its rough, unglazed surface and small size give no outward hint of the enchantment it contains.

### Craftsmanship

Despite its plainness, the cup is made with extraordinary skill. The gipsy "placed it on the wheel and threw a little jug, and he wondered to see how deft she was" (005-009). She moulds the clay with her "little brown hands" (005-009), bending her head low over the cup's mouth and singing words into it in "some strange language that the potter had never heard before" (005-010). This fusion of physical craft and vocal charm defines the cup's creation — it is simultaneously a work of pottery and a vessel for spoken magic. The potter himself, though initially sceptical, later admits "when he saw how well and cleverly it was made" (005-015), he could not help but wonder at its hidden power.

## Properties

### The Love Charm

The cup's primary magical property is its ability to bind [[love]] between two people through a shared draught. The gipsy explains that her grandfather "had charms to say to them when he threw them" (005-008), and that "one of his cups would give you your true love's heart if she drank from it" (005-006). The charm is specific: the owner must drink from the cup first, and then the beloved drinks. When the potter's future wife takes her first draught, the effect is immediate — she "turned her eyes on the potter, and said in a low voice, 'I will stay with you always, if you want me, and will be a true wife to you, and love you better than anything on earth'" (005-025). The charm creates a love that is absolute and unconditional — as long as only one draught is taken.

### The Two-Draught Rule

The cup's magic operates under a precise and unforgiving rule: one draught binds love, two draughts reverse it. The gipsy's warning is explicit: "beware that she doesn't take a second draught. For though the first draught that she drinks will be drunk to love, the second draught will be drunk to hate" (005-012). This binary mechanism — love/hate, blessing/curse — makes the cup a moral test as much as a magical tool. The rule is absolute; there is no partial reversal or gradual decline. When the gipsy man tricks the wife into drinking a second time, her love transforms instantly and completely into hatred: "I do not love you, nay, I hate you, and shall always hate you" (005-045).

### The Reversal Charm

The cup's enchantment can be undone, but only through the creation of a second cup — a counter-charm that requires the original maker's knowledge and the same ritual of singing into wet clay. The dying gipsy woman, returning years later to make amends, creates this second cup on the potter's wheel at midnight: "she took the clay in her thin little brown hands, and moulded it as she had done years before; and then she set it on the wheel, and set the wheel spinning, and formed it into a little brown bowl, and bent her head over it, and whispered into it" (005-077). When the wife drinks from this second cup, her hatred dissolves and her original love returns, proving that while the cup's magic is powerful, it is not irreversible.

## Owner / Creator

- **[[the-gipsy]]** — The gipsy girl who creates the cup on the potter's wheel, singing her grandfather's inherited charm into the wet clay (005-009) (005-010). She gives it as a parting gift with the explicit warning about the two-draught rule (005-014), then vanishes into the night.
- **[[the-potter]]** — The cup's first owner, who keeps it on his shelf for years, drinks from it himself many times (005-015), and eventually uses it to win his true love's heart at the fair (005-021). He loses the cup when the gipsy man steals it, then reclaims it in the tavern confrontation (005-094).
- **[[the-gipsy-man]]** — The antagonist who recognizes the cup's enchantment, recasts its charm for his own purposes, and steals it from the potter's wife. He later brandishes it publicly, declaring "here is the cup to prove it" (005-052), before the potter seizes it back.

## Role in the Story

### The Gift and the Binding

The cup first appears as the gipsy girl's parting gift: "be sure you do not sell the little brown cup, but keep it and give it to your true love to drink out of" (005-014). The potter, half-sceptical and half-hopeful, uses it at the next year's fair to win the heart of the golden-haired weaver. The cup's charm works perfectly: "no sooner had she tasted it than she put it down and turned her eyes on the potter" (005-025) and pledged her love. For a time, the cup brings happiness — the potter and his wife live together "very happily, and every day he thought her fairer and sweeter" (005-028), and the cup sits quietly on the shelf, its darker potential dormant.

### The Theft and the Breaking

The story's crisis begins when [[the-gipsy-man]] arrives while the potter is away. He "cast his eyes about the room, and they lit upon the little brown jug standing upon the shelf" (005-032), immediately recognizing its magical nature. He "seized it eagerly, and poured wine in it, and looked inside it, and then he laughed, and stooping his head over it, said a few words, and then laughed again" (005-034). By manipulating the wife's [[fear]] and [[vanity]], he persuades her to drink from the cup a second time, unknowingly triggering the reversal charm. The result is catastrophic: her love turns to hatred, the potter is driven from his home, and the cup is stolen away.

### The Revelation and the Restoration

The cup's final narrative function is redemptive. The dying gipsy woman — the original gipsy girl, now aged and broken — returns to the potter's cottage and "told her all the truth about the charms in the cup" (005-074). She creates a second cup, a counter-charm, and the wife drinks from it. The cup's original enchantment is broken. The potter, wandering abroad, discovers a straw mat woven by his wife bearing a rhyme about the cup — "From the gipsy's cup I drank for love, / From the gipsy's cup I drank for hate" (005-087) — and he recognises it, reclaims his cup from the gipsy man in a tavern (005-094), and returns home. The cup, having served as the instrument of both his greatest joy and deepest sorrow, is finally neutralized.

## Rules & Conditions

The cup's magic is governed by a precise set of conditions, reflecting the rule-bound nature of gipsy folk magic:

1. **The owner must drink first** — The potter drinks from the cup many times before presenting it to his beloved (005-015). The charm is activated for the beloved only after the owner has established the bond through his own draught.

2. **One draught binds, two draughts reverse** — "The first draught she will drink to your love, and the second draught she will drink to your hate" (005-012). There is no middle ground: the binary nature of the charm makes it both powerful and perilous.

3. **The charm survives baking** — The cup is fired in the kiln alongside the other vessels (005-014), yet its magic persists. The firing does not diminish the enchantment; if anything, it seals it into the clay permanently.

4. **The charm can be recast by those with the knowledge** — The gipsy man, sharing the gipsy girl's magical tradition, is able to "say a few words" over the cup (005-034) and reactivate or redirect its power. The charm is not locked to its original caster.

5. **Only the original maker can create a counter-charm** — The reversal requires the dying gipsy woman to "throw my last cup" (005-077) on the same wheel, using the same ritual. No other character, not even the gipsy man, possesses the knowledge to undo the original spell.

## Fate

The cup's fate is one of neutralization rather than destruction. After the potter reclaims it from the gipsy man in the tavern, he takes it home. The cup's power has been spent — the love it bound and the hatred it caused have both been undone by the second cup the gipsy woman made. The story ends with the potter and his wife reunited, the gipsy boy adopted into their family, and the brown cup reduced to a mere object — no longer an instrument of enchantment but a relic of a trial survived. The cup remains, but its magic has been answered and dissolved. It becomes, like the tale itself, a [[keeping-faith|testament to fidelity]] and the endurance of genuine [[love]] over magical manipulation.
