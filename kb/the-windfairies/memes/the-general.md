# The General

The General is the commander of the Duke's army and one of the suitors Kesta targets during her ambitious trip to town in "Vain Kesta," the second tale in *The Windfairies* collection. A proud and temperamental military man, he is initially charmed by Kesta's claims of desirability but turns viciously against her when her true station is revealed.

## Role in the Story

The General is the fifth suitor Kesta encounters during her escalating pursuit of a wealthy husband. She finds him drilling soldiers in the town square:

> at their head rode the General of the army. His voice was hoarse with shouting at his men, and he swore dreadfully, but he was covered with gold, and looked very grand. [002-021]

Kesta's approach is characteristically bold — she walks so close under his horse's feet that he shouts at her. She then deploys her standard tale of being pursued by would-be husbands. The General is initially skeptical of her beauty: "I don't think you are so pretty" [002-026], but Kesta's indignant insistence and the list of prior suitors convinces him: "If this is all true, of course you must be exceedingly pretty" [002-028].

When the Duke later rejects Kesta, the General is the first suitor she flees to. His response is swift and cruel — he calls his soldiers to "help to chase this good-for-nothing hussy out of the town" [002-054], exposing the mercenary nature of his earlier interest.

## Character Traits

- **Proud and temperamental**: His voice is "hoarse with shouting" and "he swore dreadfully" [002-021], suggesting an irritable, authoritarian personality
- **Status-conscious**: Like the Duke, he values Kesta only when he believes other powerful men desire her, and instantly rejects her when her low birth is exposed
- **Cruel in rejection**: He orders his soldiers to humiliate Kesta, calling her a "good-for-nothing hussy" [002-054] despite having previously proposed marriage to her
- **Gullible**: He accepts Kesta's self-serving story without any verification, swayed purely by the social proof of other suitors

## Key Actions

1. **Commands the army drill**: The General is introduced in his element, "covered with gold" [002-021] and leading soldiers in the town square

2. **Questions Kesta's beauty**: Unlike other suitors who accept her claim immediately, he openly doubts: "I don't think you are so pretty" [002-026], forcing her to argue her case more aggressively

3. **Proposes marriage**: Convinced by her story, he tells her: "as you say you are much too good for them, perhaps you might suit me" [002-028]

4. **Waits for her return**: After Kesta departs for the palace, the General sits at his window in his "fine uniform" [002-049] waiting for her return

5. **Orders her expulsion**: Upon learning the Duke rejected Kesta for being a dairymaid, he calls soldiers to chase her from town: "she thought she was fit for me too — it is disgraceful!" [002-054]

## Relationships

- [[the-duke]] — The Duke is the General's sovereign. The General's interest in Kesta partly validates her in the Duke's eyes — and the Duke's rejection gives the General permission to similarly scorn her
- [[kesta]] — The vain dairymaid who tries to marry the General. He is the highest-ranked suitor she charms before reaching the Duke himself
- [[the-banker]] — The General is ranked above the banker in Kesta's hierarchy of suitors, reflecting the military's high social standing

## Significance

The General illustrates the story's moral that social approval is fickle and transactional. His rapid shift from proposing marriage to ordering Kesta's public humiliation — within a single scene — exposes the cruelty underpinning the town's rigid hierarchy. His stated reason for rejecting her ("she thought she was fit for me too — it is disgraceful!" [002-054]) reveals that his pride was wounded not by Kesta's deception, but by the implication that he might be associated with someone of lower status.

The General's character also demonstrates the chain of social validation that drives the story: each suitor's interest in Kesta depends on the approval of the one above him, creating a pyramid of vanity that collapses instantly when the Duke at the apex withdraws his favor.
