A young gipsy girl, dismissed by a proud village potter as a mere vagabond, seizes his wheel and clay to perform a feat that transcends craft entirely — moulding enchanted vessels that catch colour from the moon, and a plain brown cup whose spell can turn love to hate with a single draught. This confrontation is the inciting event of "The Gipsy's Cup," establishing the story's central tension between mundane skill and magical artistry.

## Context

A young potter in a small village was working late at his wheel by the roadside, preparing bowls for the annual fair [005-001]. A gipsy caravan had arrived for the fair, and a young gipsy girl with "big black eyes, and rosy round cheeks" approached him, her black hair "twisted up in little red beads and chains" and adorned with rings and bracelets [005-001].

The encounter began with mutual hostility. When the potter snapped that he lived by his work and "neither by stealing nor begging" [005-003], the gipsy retorted that his cups were "stupid cups — only fit to drink out of" [005-004]. She boasted of her grandfather, who had made cups with charms: one that made you wise, another that won you your true love's heart, and a third that made you forget everything — "yes, even your true love, and all your mirth and all your sorrow, and I think that was the best cup of all" [005-006]. The potter dismissed this as "real child's talk" [005-007], disbelieving that a gipsy girl could know anything of the potter's craft.

## The Challenge

Angered by the potter's disbelief, the gipsy issued a direct challenge: "Give me a piece of your clay, and let me come to your wheel and you shall see" [005-008].

To the potter's astonishment, she took the clay in her "little brown hands, and moulded and modelled it with the greatest skill" [005-009]. She threw a little jug on the wheel, then made "jugs and pots and jars, far more quickly and skilfully than the potter could have done" [005-010]. The potter, who had spent years mastering his craft, found himself outdone by a stranger who worked as if the clay obeyed her thoughts rather than her hands.

> "I should think I had taught you that I know more about your trade than you do yourself." — the gipsy [005-010]

## The Moonlight Colouring

Then came a feat that transcended craft entirely. She declared she would colour the pots by catching "the colour from the moon" [005-010]. She put her little brown hands out into the moonlight — her fingers covered with rings "which glittered and shone" — and it seemed to the potter as if her palms were "full of some strange glittering liquid" [005-010]. She rubbed her palms over the pots and traced patterns with her fingers [005-010]. The potter felt "almost angry" at being outdone, but the gipsy only laughed in his face [005-011].

> "Now I will colour them too. See, I shall catch the colour from the moon." — the gipsy [005-010]

This moment is the story's most visually arresting passage — the fusion of the mundane (pottery glazing) with the supernatural (catching colour from moonbeams) rendered with such concrete sensory detail that it hovers between metaphor and magic.

## The Enchanted Cup

Then came her final and most significant act: she created a plain brown cup with a spell embedded in it. Bending her bead-adorned head low over the clay, she placed "her rosy lips close to the mouth of the cup" and sang words in a strange language the potter had never heard, while moulding it with her hands and turning the wheel with her foot [005-013].

She explained the cup's terrible power with chilling precision:

> "The first draught that she drinks will be drunk to love, the second draught will be drunk to hate." — the gipsy [005-012]

She added a warning that revealed both the cup's danger and her own ambiguous motives: the potter, ignorant of magical craft, "will not know how to fashion one so as to win back her love again" [005-012]. The spell was not merely punitive — it was a trap, carefully baited to exploit the potter's inevitable scepticism.

## Aftermath and Verification

When the gipsy vanished into the night, the potter thought he had been dreaming — but the rows of beautifully crafted pots proved otherwise [005-015]. The next day, when he baked them in his kiln, the truth was revealed: the pots emerged with "the most wonderful colours that he had ever seen: silver, blue, grey and yellow, in all sorts of patterns" [005-017]. Only the little brown cup remained plain. At the fair, rich folk bought all the gipsy's wares at high prices, and the potter "found that he had made more money than he had earned in many a long month past" [005-018]. Of the gipsy herself, he saw nothing more, though he searched for her throughout the three days of the fair [005-018].

## Participants

- **[[the-potter]]** — A skilled but proud craftsman, humbled by the gipsy's superior art. His initial scepticism and later unease frame the story's central tension between mundane craft and magical artistry. His fatal flaw is not lack of skill but lack of belief — he keeps the cup for years, drinks from it himself, and "still laughed when he remembered what the gipsy had said about leaving a charm in it" [005-003, 009–011, 015–019]
- **The gipsy girl** — A young traveller with "big black eyes, and rosy round cheeks" whose effortless mastery of the potter's craft exposes the limits of his understanding. She is neither wholly benevolent nor wholly malicious — she gives the potter both beautiful wares that earn him a fortune and a cursed cup whose rules she explains clearly, leaving the moral choice in his hands [005-001, 008–014]

## Outcome

The gipsy departed, warning the potter to keep the cup and give only one draught to his true love: "if there are two maybe you will need the gipsy's help again" [005-014]. The potter kept the cup on his shelf for years. He "still laughed when he remembered what the gipsy had said about leaving a charm in it, for though he himself had drunk out of it many times, he never thought it had brought any spell on him" [005-019]. His scepticism would prove his undoing — when he eventually gave the cup to his golden-haired bride, her first draught won her love, but her second turned it to hate, exactly as the gipsy had foretold. The potter, who had dismissed magic as "child's talk," discovered too late that the gipsy's words were not a boast but a precise technical description of the spell's mechanism.

## Significance

This encounter is the inciting event of "The Gipsy's Cup." It establishes the story's core tension between mundane craft and magical artistry. The gipsy's effortless mastery exposes the potter's limitations — not in technical skill but in understanding. His pots are hollow vessels; hers carry spells.

The challenge scene is a masterful piece of folk-tale economy: in a single encounter it introduces the magical object (the cup), establishes its rules (love then hate), provides the protagonist's fatal flaw (scepticism), and sets the trap (the potter will use the cup despite his disbelief). The gipsy tells him exactly what the cup does and exactly what will happen if he gives two draughts — and still he disbelieves. The tragedy, when it comes, is entirely of his own making.

The moonlight-colouring scene is particularly striking for its fusion of the mundane with the supernatural, rendered with such concrete detail that the reader, like the potter, is left uncertain whether this is magic or simply a craft he does not understand. This ambiguity is the story's deepest insight: the gipsy's art is not different in kind from the potter's, only in degree. She does what he does, but better — and the difference between "better" and "magical" is, in the end, only a matter of perspective.
