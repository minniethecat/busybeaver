# The Gipsy Man

A travelling gipsy who appears at the potter's home years after the gipsy girl's visitation. He recognises the little brown cup's enchantment for what it is and exploits it -- first to estrange the potter's wife from her husband, then to use the cup's magic for his own designs. He is the story's antagonist, the counter-force to the gipsy girl's original gift.

## Role in the Story

The gipsy man arrives at the potter's house while the potter is away. He immediately recognises the brown cup on the shelf as an enchanted object: "The gipsy man seized it eagerly, and poured wine in it, and looked inside it, and then he laughed" (005-034). He exploits the cup's charm -- which the gipsy girl made to bind true love -- and recasts it to destroy that love. He persuades the wife to drink from it, and "when she had done so he laughed again, long and low, till her heart sank with fear" (005-043). He then takes the cup and departs, leaving the wife's love turned to hatred. He later reappears at a fair, using the cup's fame to draw the wife back -- intending to wed her himself after the charm has done its work.

## Character Traits

### Cunning and Predatory

- **Cunning and predatory** -- He immediately identifies the enchanted cup for what it is and devises a plan to exploit it: "he laughed, and stooping his head over it, said a few words, and then laughed again" (005-034)
- **Manipulative** -- He plays on the wife's vanity, telling her "your dress is plain, and your rooms are bare; now, were you the wife of some wealthy man, he would give you pearls and diamonds" (005-030)
- **Patient** -- He waits years between taking the cup and reappearing to claim the wife, demonstrating the long-game patience of a seasoned trickster
- **Possessor of inherited magical knowledge** -- Like the gipsy girl, he knows the charms and spells associated with the cup, suggesting a shared gipsy magical tradition

### The Dark Mirror

Where the [[the-gipsy|gipsy girl]] used her craft to create [[love]], the gipsy man uses it to foster [[greed]] and [[vanity]]. He is not merely a thief of objects but a thief of affections -- his true target is the wife's heart, which he steals not through genuine [[love]] but through magical manipulation and social envy. His laughter -- "long and low" (005-043) -- becomes a signature of malice, contrasting with the gipsy girl's "low" laugh that was playful rather than predatory (005-008).

## Key Actions

### The Recognition and Seduction

1. **The recognition** -- Entering the potter's house, he immediately spots the brown cup: "his eyes lit upon the little brown jug standing upon the shelf" (005-032). He pours wine into it and recites words over it, recognising and reactivating its charm.

2. **The seduction** -- He works on the wife's dissatisfaction with her modest life, contrasting her present circumstances with the wealth and adornment she might have (005-030). He persuades her to drink from the cup a second time -- the act that reverses the love-charm.

### The Theft and Return

3. **The theft** -- After the wife drinks and her love turns to hatred, "he picked up the cup and put it into his bundle, and went his way" (005-043), taking the enchanted object with him.

4. **The return** -- He reappears at a distant fair where the wife, now seeking him, finds him. He intends to wed her, having used the cup's magic to clear the path. But his plan is ultimately foiled when the potter reclaims both cup and wife.

## Significance

### The Corruption of Magic

The gipsy man embodies the corrupting potential of magical knowledge when separated from benevolent intent. Where the gipsy girl used her grandfather's charms to create love, the gipsy man uses the same charms to destroy it. He is the tale's agent of entropy -- proving that the gipsy girl's warning ("if there are two draughts maybe you will need the gipsy's help again," 005-014) was not idle.

### The Limits of Enchantment

His arc also demonstrates that enchantment can be contested: the charm he recasts is eventually broken, and the natural love between husband and wife proves stronger than any spell. The gipsy man's ultimate failure is evidence that [[keeping-faith|fidelity]] and [[truth-telling|honest love]] can overcome even the most cunning magical manipulation. His defeat is not through counter-magic but through the potter's patient devotion and the wife's eventual return to her true feelings.
