## Context

A young potter lived in a village and made his living by making pots. When the annual fair came to the village, he was working late at his wheel by the roadside, preparing bowls and jugs to sell (005-001). A gipsy caravan had arrived among the fair's travellers, bringing with it a mysterious young woman whose arrival would change the potter's life forever.

## The Event

As the potter worked alone in the moonlight, a young gipsy girl approached him. She had "big black eyes, and rosy round cheeks," and her black hair was "twisted up in little red beads and chains" (005-001). Her fingers were covered with rings that glittered in the moonlight.

Their first exchange was marked by mutual suspicion. When the potter rebuffed her, the gipsy laughed and declared her superior knowledge of the potter's craft, boasting that her grandfather had made magical cups — one for wisdom, one for love, and one for forgetting everything (005-006). The potter dismissed her claims as "real child's talk" (005-007).

Stung by his disbelief, the gipsy issued her challenge: "Give me a piece of your clay, and let me come to your wheel and you shall see" (005-008). She then demonstrated extraordinary skill, taking the clay in her "little brown hands" and moulding it with expertise far beyond the potter's own (005-009–010).

Her most astonishing feat was colouring the pots by moonlight. She held her palms up to the moon's rays, and it seemed to the potter as if they were "full of some strange glittering liquid" (005-010). She rubbed her palms over the pots and traced patterns with her fingers, creating decorations of unearthly beauty.

## The Enchanted Cup

After making the coloured wares, the gipsy took one more piece of clay and created a plain brown cup. Bending her "rosy lips close to the mouth of the cup," she sang words into it "in some strange language that the potter had never heard before," while moulding it with her hands (005-013).

She gave the cup to the potter with a warning: keep it and give it to his true love to drink from, but only one draught — "for if there are two maybe you will need the gipsy's help again" (005-014). The first draught would be drunk to love, winning the drinker's heart; the second to hate, turning all love away.

## Departure

Having completed her work, the gipsy "laughed, and nodding her head over her shoulder, tripped lightly away in the moonlight while the potter stared after her" (005-014). She vanished as mysteriously as she had arrived, leaving behind only the rows of pots and the plain brown cup as evidence of her visit.

At first the potter thought he had been asleep, but the beautifully crafted wares proved otherwise (005-015). The next day, when baked in the kiln, they emerged with "the most wonderful colours that he had ever seen: silver, blue, grey and yellow, in all sorts of patterns" (005-017). Only the little brown cup remained unadorned — its magic hidden within.

## Participants

- **The gipsy girl** — A mysterious young woman of the gipsy caravan, possessed of preternatural pottery skills and knowledge of magical charms; her "little brown hands" create wares of impossible beauty, and her songs in a strange tongue embed spells in clay (005-001, 006, 009–014)
- [[the-potter]] — A skilled but proud craftsman whose scepticism blinds him to the gipsy's true power; he keeps the cup despite his disbelief (005-003, 007, 015)

## Outcome

The gipsy departed the fair, never to be seen by the potter again. Her enchanted wares sold at the fair for more money than the potter "had earned in many a long month past" (005-018). The plain brown cup remained on his shelf for years — a seemingly ordinary vessel that held the power to win or destroy love. The potter kept it without believing in its charm, a scepticism that would later prove his undoing.

## Significance

The gipsy's arrival is the inciting event of "The Gipsy's Cup." In a single moonlit encounter, it introduces the story's magical object (the enchanted cup), establishes its rules (love, then hate), reveals the protagonist's fatal flaw (disbelief in magic despite witnessing it), and sets the trap that will spring years later. The scene is a masterful piece of folk-tale economy: the gipsy appears, demonstrates her power, leaves her gift, and vanishes — all within the span of one night.
