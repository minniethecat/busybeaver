# Kesta's Encounter with the Bailiff

On the road to town, after securing promises from both Adam and the miller, Kesta encounters the Duke's bailiff riding a white horse. Through a fabricated tale of persecution by lovestruck men, she manipulates him into becoming her third suitor in "Vain Kesta" (Story 2).

## Context

After leaving the miller with a promise to return and marry him, Kesta continued toward town, her vanity swelling with each success. She had begun the day as a dairymaid with one proposal from Adam; she now held promises from two men and had resolved not to settle until she had tested every possibility (002-009).

## The Encounter

Near the town gates, Kesta spotted the bailiff of the great Duke approaching on a white horse. She immediately calculated that this was an opportunity to climb higher still:

> "Who knows but that he may want a wife? I can but try." (002-010)

She sat down by the roadside and called out a lament designed to catch his attention:

> "Ah me, what a thing it is to be a poor girl who has to run away from all the men she meets!" (002-010)

The bailiff stopped his horse and asked why she was pursued. Kesta answered with an artful distortion — claiming that men wanted to marry her because she was "so pretty," that the farmhand Adam had pursued her, and that "now it is the miller, who would not let me pass his door unless I promised to come back and marry him, but I am far too good for such as he" (002-011–012).

## The Bailiff's Proposal

The bailiff, who harboured a personal hatred for the miller, was provoked less by Kesta's beauty than by the chance to thwart his rival:

> "Did the miller really want to marry you? If you're too good to marry him, it may be you would suit me." (002-013)

Like the miller before him, the bailiff offered himself as a husband. Kesta accepted conditionally — she had "business in town" and would arrange everything on her return — leaving the bailiff "chuckling at the thought of how angry the miller would be if he married Kesta" (002-014).

## Significance

This encounter marks the third proposal in Kesta's escalating chain. Each successive suitor holds higher social rank — farmhand, miller, bailiff — and each is won not through Kesta's genuine charms but through her increasingly elaborate fabrications. The bailiff's motivation is particularly revealing: he acts not from admiration of Kesta but from spite toward the miller, underscoring the shallow and self-interested nature of every proposal in the chain.
