# The Gipsy

A young gipsy girl of supernatural origin who appears to the potter one moonlit night and demonstrates knowledge of enchanted pottery-making passed down from her grandfather. Her gift of the little brown cup -- charmed to bind true love -- drives the entire arc of "The Gipsy's Cup."

## Role in the Story

The gipsy appears unannounced at the potter's roadside workshop on the eve of the fair. She watches him work, then reveals her own lineage: "My grandfather used to make pots on a wheel... he had charms to say to them when he threw them" (005-008). Taking the potter's clay and wheel, she fashions a series of vessels of extraordinary beauty -- and one small brown cup into which she sings a love-charm in a strange language (005-010). Her gift and her warning -- "be sure you do not sell the little brown cup, but keep it and give it to your true love to drink out of" (005-014) -- set the story's entire chain of enchantment, love, loss, and eventual restoration into motion. After that single night, she vanishes and is never seen again.

## Character Traits

### Supernatural Craft

The gipsy's defining trait is her inherited magical craft, passed down from her grandfather. Her hands work the clay with a speed and artistry far beyond mortal capability: the potter "stared in silence, while the gipsy took another bit of clay" and produced vessels of impossible beauty (005-009). The colours that emerged from the kiln were "the most wonderful... that he had ever seen: silver, blue, grey and yellow" (005-014). Her power is not innate but learned -- a [[secrecy|secret]] tradition passed through her family line, blending artistry with [[music|song]] and spoken charm.

### Enigmatic Presence

The gipsy embodies playful mystery. She "laughed low, and showed her white teeth in the moonlight" (005-008), treating her powers with casual amusement rather than gravity. She does not explain herself, demand payment, or seek recognition. Her motives remain deliberately opaque -- is she a benevolent gift-giver, a trickster testing mortal hearts, or an agent of fate indifferent to consequences? This ambiguity is central to her power as a narrative figure.

### Bearer of Ancient Knowledge

She speaks charms "in some strange language that the potter had never heard before" (005-010), channeling her grandfather's magical craft. Her knowledge is oral and inherited, suggesting a tradition of gipsy magic that passes through generations. The charm she sings into the cup is specific and rule-bound: one draught binds love, two draughts undo it. This precision marks her magic as craft rather than wild sorcery -- a discipline with its own laws and consequences.

## Key Actions

### The Visitation and Creation

1. **The visitation** -- On the night before the fair, the gipsy girl appears at the potter's wheel. She watches him work, then reveals her knowledge of enchanted pottery-making inherited from her grandfather (005-006) (005-008).

2. **The creation** -- Taking clay and wheel, she fashions a set of exquisite pots and jugs, singing charms into them as she works. The vessels emerge from the kiln with colours "the most wonderful... that he had ever seen: silver, blue, grey and yellow" (005-009) (005-010) (005-014).

### The Gift and the Warning

3. **The fateful gift** -- She leaves the potter the little brown cup with explicit instructions: give it to his true love, but only one draught -- "for if there are two maybe you will need the gipsy's help again" (005-014). Then she vanishes into the moonlight.

4. **The lingering enchantment** -- Though she never reappears physically, her enchantment governs the entire story. The cup binds the potter's wife in love, then -- when the gipsy man later recasts the charm -- in hatred. The story's resolution requires undoing what she began.

## Significance

### Catalyst Figure

The gipsy embodies the fairy-tale archetype of the supernatural visitor who bestows a mixed gift -- one that brings both blessing and curse. She is neither wholly benevolent nor malevolent; her charm creates [[love]], but love that can be stolen. Her disappearance after a single night of action makes her a catalyst figure -- she sets events in motion and withdraws, leaving mortals to navigate the consequences of enchantment.

### Between Worlds

In the collection's taxonomy of supernatural beings, she occupies a unique position: not a fairy, not a witch, but a human bearer of inherited magical craft whose powers blur the line between artistry and sorcery. Unlike the [[windfairies]] who demand [[keeping-faith|faithful]] silence, the gipsy imposes no [[promise|oath]] of secrecy -- only a warning about the cup's proper use. Her magic is transactional and rule-bound rather than moralistic. She does not judge; she simply gives, warns, and vanishes.
