# Promise

The concept of the Promise — a solemn pledge that must be kept at all costs — is one of the most pervasive moral themes in the Windfairies collection. Across multiple tales, characters face trials that test their faithfulness to promises made, with their fates turning on whether they honor their word.

## Promise to Supernatural Beings

### Lucilla and the Windfairies

The most consequential promise in the collection is [[lucilla]]'s vow to the [[windfairies]]. When they teach her to dance, they extract a solemn pledge:

> "… if you broke your promise to us when once you had made it, we could do you an ill turn." [001-014]

Lucilla replies: "I promise faithfully" (001-015). This promise becomes the central conflict of her story: imprisoned and threatened with execution, she refuses to break her word to the windfairies, even when doing so could save her life. Her faithfulness is ultimately rewarded when the windfairies rescue her.

### Hulda and the Dwarf

In "The Story of a Cat" (Chapter 7), [[hulda]] is bound by a promise to the dwarf musician. She must promise that his name will be the first word she speaks when she returns home:

> "… you must promise that my name shall be the first word you speak." [007-039]

## Promise in Marriage

The marriage vow appears as a binding promise in several tales. In "The Gipsy's Cup" (Chapter 5), the gipsy woman recalls:

> "… that was on the day when I promised my husband I would be his wife." [005-036]

The potter's wife, in turn, makes and keeps a promise to the gipsy woman to raise her child:

> "… she would keep him, as she had promised his mother." [005-080]

The potter's wife's promise is tested when the gipsy man returns to claim his son. She refuses to break her word:

> "… the potter's wife promised that she would never give up the little boy." [005-127]

## Promises Made Under Duress

Not all promises in the tales are freely given. In "Kesta's Pride" (Chapter 2), characters are forced into promises as the price of passage:

> "… he would only let me pass his door unless I promised to come back and marry him." [002-012]

These coerced promises raise the moral question of whether such pledges are truly binding — a tension the stories explore through Hulda's journey.

## Promise as Moral Foundation

Across the Windfairies tales, the keeping of promises functions as the primary measure of a character's moral worth. Those who keep faith — [[lucilla]], the potter's wife, [[nanina]] — are ultimately rewarded. The concept thus serves as the collection's central ethical pillar, closely related to [[keeping-faith]].
