# Social Class

Social class is a pervasive theme across the Windfairies collection, shaping characters' aspirations, conflicts, and fates. The stories present a world of rigid hierarchy — from dukes and kings down to dairymaids and beggars — where characters who attempt to cross class boundaries often face harsh consequences.

## The Class Ladder

The stories depict a clearly stratified society:

- **Nobility**: Dukes, kings, and generals who wield political power
- **Professionals**: Bankers, millers, and bailiffs who hold economic power
- **Farm workers**: Dairymaids, ploughmen, and farmhands who labour for others
- **The poor**: Beggars, fishermen's widows, and those at the margins

## Kesta's Transgression

The most explicit treatment of social class appears in "Vain Kesta," where the dairymaid Kesta attempts to rise above her station. Despite being a "common dairymaid" (002-001), she claims to be "too good" for every man she encounters — the farmer's man, the miller, the bailiff, the banker, and even the General of the army (002-033).

When the Duke discovers that Kesta is "nothing but a farm wench" who "makes cheeses" (002-047), he is outraged: "How dare you think yourself good enough to marry me!" (002-047). The pages echo the class judgment: "She is nothing but a farm wench... she is to be chased from the town for her impertinence" (002-066).

Kesta's punishment for class transgression is complete: she is hunted through the town by pages, soldiers, clerks, and farm workers (002-070), and ultimately rejected even by Adam, the farmhand she had scorned. The story's moral reinforces class boundaries: those who reach above their station are humiliated.

## Wealth as Moral Test

Class intersects with morality in several stories:

- In "The Story of a Cat," the old gentleman's response to a beggar is harsh: "Go away, I never give to beggars, and you must have done something very wicked to be so poor" (006-007). This equation of poverty with moral failing is challenged by the story's resolution, where the magical cat rewards generosity over wealth.

- In "The Gnome's Revenge," the ploughman is told "You are a poor man now, but you will soon be rich" (009-014) — the gnome's bargain promises class mobility through supernatural means, but at a hidden cost.

- In "The Gipsy's Cup," the potter's enrichment through the gipsy's magic comes at the cost of his compassion: the cup brings wealth to sell, but also a curse that drives the potter from his wife.

## Class and Happiness

The collection ultimately questions whether class ascent brings happiness. When Kesta returns to the farm humiliated, Adam's rejection leaves her "sitting all alone" (002-075). The ploughman, despite his bargain-earned wealth, "grieved over it in his heart" when the gnome's revenge costs his wife her hair (009-069). And the ploughman's wife is told that "poor folk could be quite as happy as the rich" (009-084) — a sentiment that serves as the collection's quiet moral on class.
