# Cruelty

The willful causing of suffering to the innocent or helpless — a vice concentrated in figures of authority whose power, unchecked by pity or justice, becomes persecution.

## Definition

Cruelty in these tales is not random violence. It is systematic: the King and Queen use the apparatus of law — guards, prisons, executioners — to torment an innocent woman (001-043). Cruelty is the corruption of power, the weaponization of authority against the vulnerable. It differs from greed in being actively destructive rather than merely acquisitive: the cruel character does not merely take — they harm for the satisfaction of dominance (001-055). The tales present cruelty as self-defeating — it invariably provokes supernatural retaliation (001-066).

## Manifestations

### In "The Windfairies"

The King and Queen's cruelty escalates systematically: first imprisonment, then attempted drowning, then burning, then beheading (001-052) (001-056) (001-060) (001-062). Each escalation is motivated not by justice but by wounded pride — the King cannot tolerate that Lucilla might leave, the Queen cannot tolerate being outperformed. The cruelty is institutional: guards, executioners, and crowds all participate, making cruelty a social phenomenon, not merely an individual failing. The wind's destruction of the palace (001-066) is proportional justice — the institution that enabled cruelty is destroyed.

### In "The Rain Maiden"

The rain maiden's drowning is an act of elemental cruelty: "she drowned in all her youth and beauty; only the shepherd's wife wept" (008-025). The detail that only one person wept underscores the indifference that enables cruelty — most people simply do not care enough to intervene.

### In "The Ploughman and the Gnome"

The gnome's potential for cruelty is held in check by the bargain: "Agreed, but remember" (009-016). The tale explores the thin line between fair dealing and cruel exploitation in supernatural bargains.

## Associated Characters

- **The King** — Institutional cruelty: uses state power to persecute Lucilla for refusing to reveal her secret (001-043) (001-052)
- **The Queen** — Personal cruelty driven by jealousy: "she was very jealous, and it made her angry" (001-036); she manipulates the King toward ever-harsher punishments (001-055)
- **The Crowd** — Collective cruelty: they shout "She is a witch! she is a witch!" (001-057), participating in persecution from safe anonymity

## Related Concepts

## Moral Dimension

Cruelty is the most severely punished vice in the collection. The King and Queen's palace is destroyed (001-066). The rain maiden's death is mourned as tragedy. The tales' moral arithmetic is clear: cruelty unleashes supernatural forces that no human power can withstand. The destruction of the palace — "high up on the hill the palace was a mass of ruins" (001-066) — is the tales' most overt act of divine judgment. Cruelty is not merely wrong; it is cosmically unsustainable (001-066).
