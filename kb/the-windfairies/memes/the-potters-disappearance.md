## Context

The gipsy had warned the potter: the enchanted cup's first draught would be drunk to love, but the second to hate (005-012). The potter had given the cup to his wife, who drank once and loved him deeply. But in his absence, a gipsy man came to the cottage, asked for the cup, and the wife — unaware of its magic — drank from it a second time while toasting his health (005-044, 050–051).

## The Event

The potter returned home to find his once-loving wife transformed: she refused to kiss him, turned away from his touch, and would not speak to him (005-046). Day after day, things "grew from bad to worse" — she looked at him with anger and "would not even suffer him to touch the hem of her dress" (005-046).

When the potter noticed the brown cup missing from the shelf, he felt "very much alarmed" (005-046). His wife scornfully admitted giving it to a gipsy man who had asked for it, after drinking his health from it as he wished (005-048, 051).

> "Then the potter was stricken with deadly fear, and remembered the words of the gipsy. 'The first draught she will drink to your love, and the second draught she will drink to your hate,' and he knew in his heart that the words were true." (005-052)

After waiting many days in vain hope that his wife would change, the potter made his decision. He packed a wallet of clothes, took money, and told his wife:

> "Wife, there is a man somewhere who has done me a great wrong, and perhaps he did it unwittingly. I am going out to find him, and to make him right it." (005-053)

He warned her he might be gone for "months or in years" (005-053). His wife wept, confessing she now hated him but did not understand why — she "used to think that I had the best husband on all this earth" (005-054). The potter departed, leaving his wife and baby behind.

## Participants

- [[the-potter]] — The husband, driven from home by his wife's magically-induced hatred (005-053–054)

## Outcome

The potter's wife was left alone with their baby. She would survive by weaving cloth to sell, gradually spending all their money while she waited for her husband's return (005-055). The potter embarked on a long quest to find the gipsy who could reverse the spell, setting up the second half of the story.

## Significance

The potter's disappearance marks the story's tragic low point — the inevitable consequence of the gipsy's magical gift. The cup's spell does exactly what was foretold, transforming love into hate with mechanical precision. The event illustrates the story's central warning: magical shortcuts to love contain the seeds of their own destruction. The potter's decision to leave and seek the gipsy rather than remain in a loveless home shows both hope and desperation — he believes the spell can be undone, but has no certainty of when or if he will return.
