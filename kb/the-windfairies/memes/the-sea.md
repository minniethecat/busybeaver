# The Sea

The sea is the defining natural boundary of the story-world, appearing most prominently in "The Windfairies" as both the setting of Lucilla's humble home and the stage for her supernatural dancing. It is a place of return, identity, and elemental beauty that represents the life Lucilla fights to reclaim.

## Description

The sea appears as a wild, elemental presence -- "all the little waves on the sea" are made by the windfairies themselves (001-006). It is both beautiful and dangerous: "the boat was dashed to pieces, and the sea froze so that all the fish died" (001-024). Lucilla dances "in time to the sea as it moved" (001-026), her art in harmony with the natural rhythm of the waves. The sea foam becomes a simile for her own lightness when she dances at court: "she felt as light as the sea foam" (001-034). At the story's end, she returns "to the little cottage by the seashore" (001-069), the sea marking the boundary between the enchanted world of the court and her true home.

## Location

The sea lies at the edge of the downs, where Lucilla's family windmill stands "far from any town or village" (001-001). The seashore is her favourite place: "sometimes she would go down to the seashore, and this she loved to do best of all" (001-020). From a rocky hill in "Dumb Othmar," "afar the sea could be seen when the weather was fine" (007-036), placing the sea as a distant but visible presence even in stories where it is not the primary setting.

## Events

### The Sea as Stage

1. **The windfairies' domain** -- Lucilla first learns of the supernatural world when the windfairies tell her they "make all the little waves on the sea" (001-006), establishing the sea as a site of fairy activity.

2. **Dancing by the shore** -- Before her departure to court, Lucilla dances on the beach: "she danced in time to the sea as it moved, and the stranger who watched her thought she was a fairy" (001-026). This is the dance that leads to her discovery.

### The Sea as Danger and Deliverance

3. **The frozen sea** -- A dark memory in Lucilla's family history: "the boat was dashed to pieces, and the sea froze so that all the fish died" (001-024), explaining why her father distrusts the sea.

4. **The return** -- Lucilla's journey ends at the seashore: she "went back to the little cottage by the seashore, and her father and her mother and her little ones were waiting for her on the shore" (001-069). The sea is the site of reunion and resolution.

## Symbolism

### Authenticity and Return

The sea represents the natural, authentic life that stands in contrast to the artificial splendour of the court. Lucilla's ease at court -- she dances "as light as the sea foam" (001-034) -- comes precisely from her connection to this elemental place. The sea is also the site of danger and loss (her father's shipwreck), but it is ultimately the place of return and belonging.

### Endurance and Boundary

The tales use the sea as the border between the human world and the unknown -- it is where the windfairies do their work and where Lucilla's identity is rooted. Unlike the palace (which crumbles), the sea endures. It outlasts kings and kingdoms, storms and calms, serving as the one constant in a world of enchantment and change. It is both the source of livelihood (fishing) and the source of peril (shipwrecks), embodying the dual nature of the elemental world.
