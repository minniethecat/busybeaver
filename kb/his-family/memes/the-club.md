# The Club

## Overview

The club is the all-male retreat where Roger Gale spends his evenings away from home — a space of cigars, conversation, and the comfortable rituals of masculine privilege. It is the counterpart to the domestic sphere: where the home is family, the club is escape; where the home is obligation, the club is leisure; where the home is female, the club is exclusively male. Roger's club attendance is both a social habit and a small betrayal — time he is not spending with his daughters, with his memories of Judith, with the family he is trying to hold together.

## Description in the Novel

The club is evoked through its atmosphere — leather chairs, cigar smoke, the murmur of male voices. It is a place of unwinding, where the pressures of business and family are temporarily suspended. The novel does not judge the club harshly, but it registers its cost: every evening at the club is an evening not at home, and Roger's daughters notice his absences even when they do not name them.

## Thematic Significance

The club represents the gendered separation of public and private life in early twentieth-century America. Men have their spaces and women theirs, and the novel quietly questions what is lost when those spaces never overlap. Roger's club is not villainous — it is simply one more force pulling him away from the home he claims to value above all else.
