## Overview

**Community** in *His Family* represents the bonds that extend beyond the immediate [[family]] — the networks of neighbors, students, and fellow citizens whose lives intersect with the Gales. The novel explores community at multiple scales, from the intimate world of the tenement to the broader civic sphere of [[new-york-city|New York]]. As Roger observes, "in the big school in the tenements" (002-025) a new kind of community takes shape — one built not on family lineage but on shared need and daily proximity. The "millions of foreign tenement people, always shoving" (002-085) that Bruce complains about represent, from another angle, the raw human material of community — strangers becoming neighbors through the pressure of shared space.

## Community in the Tenements

The most vivid image of community in the novel emerges from [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s work in the [[tenement-district|tenement]] neighborhoods. [[roger-gale|Roger]] walks through streets that were once his own, noting how "they had already spoiled his neighborhood" (002-086), the old familiar blocks giving way to tenement crowds and immigrant life. He observes what he calls "this queer community motherhood" (017-081) — the way women in the crowded tenements collectively care for children, sharing burdens across household lines. This organic form of community, born of proximity and necessity, stands in contrast to the more formal bonds of the Gale family's social circle. For Roger, it is both alien and admirable, a glimpse of a world organized around mutual need rather than individual ambition.

## Deborah's School as Community

[[deborahs-school|Deborah's school]] becomes the novel's primary model of intentional community. Through the school, [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] builds a web of relationships that includes not only children but their families, creating what Roger comes to see as "a world in itself" — a community with its own rhythms, conflicts, and loyalties. This school community represents the novel's vision of community as an active, constructed project rather than an inheritance. The school grows out of "a block of tenements far downtown" (002-041), grounding its mission in the physical reality of its neighborhood. For Deborah, community is not merely a setting for education — it is education's ultimate aim, the creation of a shared life that transcends the divisions of class and origin that mark the novel's [[new-york-city|New York]].
