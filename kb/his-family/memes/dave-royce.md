## Identity

Dave Royce is a minor but vividly drawn character in *His Family*, described as "Roger's farmer" who has worked the Gale family's New Hampshire farm for over twenty years (006-033), (038-063)--(038-065). He embodies the traditional, cautious New England farmer -- experienced, deliberate, and sometimes at odds with the ambitious modern ideas of the younger generation. Unlike the urban characters who populate most of the novel, Dave represents the rural, agrarian America that [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] loves and to which he repeatedly retreats.

## Character and Speech

Dave is characterized by his deep drawl and measured deliberation. When [[george-sidney|George Sidney]] and Roger come to the farm with ambitious plans, Dave serves as a voice of practical caution. His speech patterns, rendered in dialect ("I've druv all around these parts," "I dunno but what you're right"), mark him as a rooted, rural New Englander (038-099), (038-105).

His deliberation is not indecision but the product of long experience. Poole renders Dave's speech rhythms carefully, capturing the hesitation and slow unfolding of a farmer's thought: "Of course it's none o' my business, but if I was startin' in with cows, I'd start slow if I was you--" Here Dave deliberated (038-091). This moment illustrates the careful, almost ritualistic quality of his counsel.

> "But cattle are only part of it, Dave," the boy declared, in earnest tones ... Then Dave's deep drawl: (038-075), (038-088)

## As Foil to George

Dave Royce serves as a foil to young [[george-sidney|George Sidney]]'s enthusiasm. When George dreams of expanding the farm with thirty or forty cattle and modern methods, Dave pragmatically points out the limitations of the local area -- he could only find three suitable cows "in all these parts" (038-094)--(038-102). This tension between youthful ambition and rural practicality runs throughout the farm chapters.

Their dialogue is a collision of two worlds. George, the modern city-bred boy with a Ford and books on beekeeping, fires off rapid plans: "with bees, too, Dave -- I've got a book on 'em I'd like you to read" (038-082). Dave responds with the accumulated wisdom of decades spent on the same land, the same roads: "But you only had a buggy, Dave! Gee! I'm going to have a Ford!" George exclaims, to which Dave replies with characteristic understatement, "I dunno but what you're right" (038-102), (038-105).

> "Three? Why, Dave, I wrote we want thirty or forty!" "Yes -- you wrut," Dave answered. "But I've druv all around these parts -- " (038-098)--(038-099)

## Role in the Novel

Dave represents continuity and tradition on the farm. Roger values him precisely because of his long service and reliability: "Take old Dave Royce, for instance, who when all is said and done has worked our farm for twenty odd years and never once run me into debt" (038-063)--(038-065). This is high praise from Roger, who measures people by their trustworthiness and freedom from financial entanglement.

Dave is part of the farm community that includes his wife and children, all of whom [[bruce|Bruce]] generously takes for automobile rides (014-222). He also corresponds regularly with [[george-sidney|George Sidney]] about the farm's livestock, serving as the boy's eyes and ears on the ground (006-033). When a horse falls sick, it is Dave who works alongside George to poultice the animal back to health: "but we poulticed him, me and Dave did -- and now" (014-040). This shared labor cements the bond between the old farmer and the young city boy.

In Chapter XXXVIII, Roger reflects that regardless of George's ambitious plans, "old Dave Royce will still be there" (036-091) -- a phrase that captures Dave's essential quality: permanence. He is the fixed point around which the farm's future, whatever form it takes, will revolve.

## Relationships

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]** -- Employer of over twenty years; Roger trusts Dave implicitly and values his judgment
- **[[george-sidney|George Sidney]]** -- Correspondent and collaborator on farm plans; George's youthful ambition contrasts with Dave's seasoned caution, yet the two share genuine mutual respect
- **[[bruce|Bruce]]** -- Employs Dave's wife and children indirectly through his generous farm visits and automobile rides (014-222)
- **[[allan-baird|Allan Baird]]** -- Maintains a correspondence with Dave about the farm's livestock (006-033)

## Themes

Dave Royce embodies the theme of generational continuity and the wisdom of practical experience. He represents the old New England -- steady, cautious, and deeply knowledgeable about the land -- in contrast to the modernizing impulses of the younger Gale generation. In a novel largely concerned with urban change, immigration, and social upheaval, Dave anchors one corner of the story in the enduring rhythms of rural life.

His dialect speech, rendered with care by Poole, is not merely local color but a marker of a way of life under pressure from modernity. When George arrives with his Ford, his books, and his ambitious plans, Dave's slow drawl is a form of resistance -- not hostile, but deeply skeptical of change that outpaces understanding.
