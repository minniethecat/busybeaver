# Urban Transformation

## Overview

The [[new-york-city|New York City]] of *His [[family|Family]]* is not a backdrop but a [[nature|character]] — a living entity undergoing metamorphosis as [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] watches. The novel opens with a description of [[new-york-city|the city]] as "a city of [[home|house]]s, separate homes, of quiet streets with rustling trees" (001-014). By the novel's middle, those quiet streets are overshadowed by "tier upon tier of modern homes, all overhanging [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s [[home|house]] as though presently to crush it down" (002-122). The transformation is physical, social, and moral — and the novel documents it with the precision of a journalist and the feeling of a poet.

## Manifestations

Urban transformation operates on three levels. As **physical change**: the skyline rises, the streets fill with automobiles, electric lights replace gas, and [[new-york-city|the city]]'s population swells with immigration. [[rogers-evening-walks|Roger's walks]] through [[new-york-city|the city]] become tours of a disappearing world — landmarks replaced, neighborhoods transformed, the "sparkling pinnacles of this tumultuous modern town" (001-189) rising on every side. As **social reorganization**: the influx of immigrants creates new communities, new tensions, and new possibilities. The tenements where [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] teaches are "swarming with strange, dirty little [[childhood|children]]" — a world [[roger-gale|Roger]] had never known existed, now pressing against the edges of his comfortable life. As **psychological pressure**: [[new-york-city|the city]]'s transformation is felt as stress, as noise, as "the oppressive sensation of a savage modern town unrelentingly pressing" (006-197). [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s [[fear|anxiety]] about his business, his daughters, and his place in the world is indistinguishable from his [[fear|anxiety]] about [[new-york-city|the city]] itself.

The novel is remarkable for capturing [[new-york-city|New York]] at a transitional moment — no longer the small-scale city of the 19th century, not yet the fully modern metropolis of the mid-20th. It is a city in the throes of becoming.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: The flâneur-observer whose walks through the changing city are the novel's chief vehicle for urban description and reflection. He sees what is being lost and cannot quite embrace what is rising.
- **[[bruce|Bruce]]**: The architect who builds the new city — and is broken by [[the-great-war|the war]] that the modern world produces. His [[work-and-vocation|career]] embodies [[new-york-city|the city]]'s transformation as both creation and destruction.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: The [[reform|social reform]]er who [[work-and-vocation|work]]s in [[new-york-city|the city]]'s poorest quarters, seeing [[modernity|urban transformation]] from below rather than from [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s elegiac distance.
