# Human Nature

## Overview

"Human nature" in *His [[family|Family]]* serves as a catch-all explanation for the baffling behavior of [[love-and-courtship|love]]d ones. When characters cannot fully understand each other, they fall back on the phrase — part diagnosis, part surrender. It is the novel's shorthand for the irreducible mystery of why people act as they do, particularly within families. This reliance on "human nature" as an explanatory category reflects the novel's psychological realism: Poole is less interested in systematic theories of behavior than in how ordinary people actually account for one another's actions in daily life.

## Manifestations

The concept operates at three levels. As **explanation**: John Harding, [[laura-sloane|Laura]]'s husband, answers a difficult question about human behavior with the laconic explanation: "Fellers. Human nature" (022-055). The terseness is the point — "human nature" is what you say when you have exhausted more specific explanations and are left with the brute fact of how people are. As **revelation**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s fierce [[freedom|independence]] "revealed a part of her nature" (007-024) that her family struggled to comprehend — nature here is not a fixed essence but something disclosed through action, surprising even to those who know her best. As **[[peace|acceptance]]**: throughout the novel, characters are described as "good-naturedly" accepting what they cannot change: [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s grumbling response to his wife (001-256), [[laura-sloane|Laura]]'s smile despite pro[[work-and-vocation|vocation]] (040-047).

The recurrence of "good-natured" as a modifier suggests the novel's central moral stance: human nature is flawed, but it can be met with patience and humor rather than judgment. This is the wisdom [[roger-gale|Roger]] arrives at by the novel's end — not that people are good, but that they can be faced good-naturedly.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: The character whose arc represents the movement from bafflement at human nature to [[peace|acceptance]] of it.
- **John Harding**: His laconic "Fellers. Human nature" distills the novel's pragmatic anthropology.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Her nature is revealed through action rather than description — the novel's most dynamic treatment of character.
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]**: Her "good-natured" responses to pro[[work-and-vocation|vocation]] represent the novel's ideal of social grace.
