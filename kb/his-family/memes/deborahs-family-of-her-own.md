# Deborah's 'Family of Her Own'

## Overview

"Down in my school we've a family of our own" (009-020) — with these words,
Deborah Gale redefines the novel's central concept. Family is not only blood;
it is the community of care and commitment that she has built in her tenement
school. This declaration is less a single dramatic event than a philosophical
turning point, but it has the weight of an event: after Deborah says this,
Roger can no longer pretend that his definition of family is the only one.

## Context and Build-up

Deborah has been building her school — and the community around it — for years.
"In the big school in the tenements" (002-025) she works with immigrant children,
their families, visiting nurses, and colleagues like Allan Baird and Johnny Geer.
These are her people. The school had "long curious arms these" (002-042) days,
reaching into the mayor's office and into the lives of hundreds of families.
Roger has resisted this vision, seeing it as competition for Deborah's loyalty
and dismissing it as an "infernal zoo school" (003-143). But Deborah's work
endures despite his skepticism — "Deborah and that school of hers in the heart
of a vast" (002-128) city represents something Roger cannot easily dismiss.

## The Declaration

The declaration lands like a challenge. Deborah is not asking permission; she is
stating a fact. Her family exists — it includes students, their parents, the
nurses, her colleagues — and many of them are people Roger has never met. The
moment forces Roger to confront what he has been avoiding: that his daughter's
life has a center of gravity that is not him, and that the word "family" itself
might be larger than he has allowed.

## Consequences

Roger's gradual acceptance of Deborah's expanded definition of family is the
novel's most significant arc. The declaration is the catalyst — after it, he can
no longer pretend the old definitions are adequate. The school, which Deborah
sometimes seems to be "dropping school out of her life" (002-030) when exhausted,
persists as her true calling. And the family she has built there proves more
enduring than even Roger's traditional vision.
