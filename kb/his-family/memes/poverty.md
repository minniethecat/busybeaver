## Overview

Poverty in *His [[family|Family]]* appears not as a direct experience of the Gale family but as the condition of the world beyond their comfortable home — and increasingly, as a moral claim on their attention. Through [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[education|schools]] in the immigrant districts, poverty becomes visible, urgent, and impossible to ignore. [[roger-gale|Roger]], whose life's [[work-and-vocation|work]] has been building a family sheltered from want, is confronted in his later years with the limits of that shelter.

## Roger's Encounter with Urban Poverty

The novel confronts poverty most directly when Roger walks through the immigrant districts. "Here in garish daylight were poverty and ugliness" (010-006) — a stark juxtaposition with the Gales' domestic comfort on Washington Square. A broader litany follows: "death, of poverty and vice and crime" (010-135), linking economic deprivation to a cascade of social ills. [[roger-gale|Roger]], walking these streets, is forced to reconcile his image of a benevolent city with its harsher realities.

His response is characteristically personal rather than political. He sees individual faces, individual stories — not abstract "conditions." The experience unsettles him because it reveals a world his own prosperity has kept invisible. [[roger-gale|Roger]] had spent his life building financial security for his daughters; the discovery that security is a privilege, not a norm, is one of the novel's quiet revolutions.

## Deborah's Schools as Response

[[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[education|schools]] become the novel's primary response to poverty — not charity but education, not pity but empowerment. The narrator notes "the strain of her poverty until she should be well and" (017-069), referring not to [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s own poverty but to the condition of the families her schools serve. When "in her five schools the poverty was rapidly becoming worse" (027-128), the progressive worsening suggests that poverty is dynamic, not static — a force that deepens when unaddressed.

The young intellectuals who gather around [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] discuss "socialism, poverty, marriage and religion" (015-042), placing poverty at the center of the era's reform debates. Through these discussions, Poole dramatizes the Progressive Era's conviction that poverty was a solvable problem — that social institutions, not just individual charity, must address it.

## Poverty and Family

The Gales' relationship to poverty is mediated by class and geography. Their [[the-gale-home|home]] on Washington Square is a refuge from the city's hardship, but the novel repeatedly questions whether such a refuge is morally sustainable. [[roger-gale|Roger]] reading "day and night among the poor" (009-114) experiences a discomfort that is simultaneously moral and generational — his daughters, especially [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], refuse the shelter he built, choosing instead to engage with the poverty he would prefer to forget.
