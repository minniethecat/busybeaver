# Generational Change

## Overview

"His [[family|Family]]" is fundamentally a novel about generations — about what endures and what transforms between one era and the next. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s [[new-york-city|New York]], rooted in the 19th century of "many generations of stolid folk in [[new-england|New England]]" (001-071), is being remade by his daughters into something he struggles to recognize. The novel traces this transformation not as rupture but as a gradual, layered process: "an im[[death|mortality]], made up of generations, an endless succession of others" (001-181). From the old [[new-hampshire-farm|New Hampshire]] homestead to the modern apartment buildings crowding the family [[home|house]], generational change is the novel's deepest temporal rhythm.

## Manifestations

Generational change operates through three mechanisms in the novel. As **physical succession**: the old [[home|house]], the family name, the accumulated wisdom of "countless generations reaching back" (044-047) — all of which [[roger-gale|Roger]] feels slipping away as his daughters marry and disperse. As **value transformation**: the shift from [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s Victorian ethos of duty and thrift to [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[modernity|progress]]ive idealism about education and social [[law|justice]], [[laura-sloane|Laura]]'s embrace of modern luxury, and [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s pragmatic domesticity. As **institutional layering**: [[new-york-city|the city]] itself is "built on through generations by many men and [[womens-roles|women]]" (014-057), and a school, a business, a family are all "made of many generations" — with "certain figures standing out" in the long chain of influence (036-140).

The novel is subtle about where it locates value. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s nostalgia is real but not romanticized; the new generation's freedoms are genuine but not costless. The novel's conclusion — in which [[roger-gale|Roger]], near death, glimpses "innumerable lives, the many generations, of [[family|his family]]" (038-164) — suggests that generational change is not [[death|loss]] but transformation, the family story "clothed anew by countless generations" (039-143).

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]** embodies the older generation. His memories of [[judith-gale|Judith]], his [[new-hampshire-farm|New Hampshire]] childhood, and his discomfort with modern ways make him the novel's primary vehicle for exploring [[loss|what is lost]] between generations.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]** represents the bridge generation — she honors her father's world while building a new one through her school and her [[freedom|independence]].
- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]** carries generation forward biologically: her [[childhood|children]] are the literal next generation, and her domestic focus ensures the family line continues.
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]** represents generational departure — she leaves the family orbit most completely, moving into a world of wealth and travel that [[roger-gale|Roger]] cannot follow.
