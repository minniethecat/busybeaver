# The Birth of Edith's Children

## Overview

Edith Sidney's five children — including Betsy, George, and Tad — represent the
continuity of the Gale family into a new generation. "New lives in his family"
(004-202) is how Roger sees them, and the births are the events that most directly
fulfill his deepest wish: that the family will continue. "Edith had four children"
(001-040) when the novel opens, and she is "so" thoroughly immersed in motherhood
that it defines her identity. Unlike Deborah's alternative family of students and
colleagues, Edith's children are family in the traditional sense — blood
descendants who carry the Gale name forward.

## Context and Build-up

Edith's marriage to George Sidney established a domestic household modeled on the
one she grew up in. Roger remembers the house where "his wife's first baby, Edith,
had begun her life" (001-308), and now Edith is repeating that cycle with her own
children. "Edith's winsome babies had attracted" (001-153) Roger's interest during
the difficult years after his wife's death, pulling him back toward life when he
was withdrawing from it. Her pregnancies and the births of her children are the
natural outcome of the conventional domestic path she chose — a path the novel
treats with respect, honoring it as one genuine expression of family.

## The Births

The births are not dramatized individually — the novel registers their cumulative
effect. The Sidney household fills with children; family gatherings include a new
generation; Roger's role gradually shifts from father to grandfather. Edith's
children appear in the narrative through small, telling moments: "Edith had four
children, and was so" (001-040) absorbed in their care; "Edith's voice" (001-272)
breaks into conversations with news of them; "Edith said they couldn't afford it"
(002-051) when a family outing is proposed — a reminder that children reshape a
household's priorities. Each birth is quiet in narrative terms, but collectively
they are the novel's most concrete evidence that family endures across generations.

## Consequences

The children anchor Edith's role in the novel and provide Roger with tangible
proof of continuity — the thing he values above all else. They also highlight by
contrast what Deborah and Laura have chosen: Deborah, a family of students rather
than children; Laura, travel and society rather than domesticity. When "Edith's
winsome babies" (001-153) gather at family occasions, they embody the future that
Roger has invested his life in building. They are his answer to mortality.
