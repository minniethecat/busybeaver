## Overview

Nostalgia is the emotional engine of *His Family*. Roger Gale's consciousness is oriented backward — toward his dead wife Judith, toward the rural New England of his youth, toward a New York that no longer exists. The novel understands nostalgia not as mere sentimentality but as a condition of aging itself: the accumulation of a past so dense that it begins to obscure the present.

## Roger Gale's Backward Glance

The novel opens with Roger in a posture of retrospection, thinking of "the town he had known" as a "young New York, the mighty throbbing city to which he had come long ago as a lad" (001-004). His nostalgia is spatial and temporal simultaneously — a longing for both a younger city and a younger self. The "old, old men" from whom he once heard tales of an even earlier New York now include himself, and his memories have become the very thing he once dismissed as "garrulity" (001-001).

When Roger listens to his daughter Deborah speak, "the memory came of her voice as a girl" (002-008). He "remembered her on his lap like a kitten" (003-024), the image evoking a time when his children were small and his role as father was unambiguous. The novel repeatedly returns to this dynamic: the present moment triggering a memory that measures the distance between then and now.

## Memory as Consolation and Burden

For characters who have suffered loss, memory carries a dual charge. Roger's memories of his wife Judith are both a solace and a wound. The "curiously bound up memories of small happenings" (027-085) that couples accumulate become, after death, the only remaining texture of the relationship. Roger "remembered her a few hours ago" (028-094) — the immediacy of the memory suggesting that the dead are never fully absent from the living.

Memory is also a form of inheritance. One passage articulates this directly: "this is my memory living on in you" (014-284), framing memory not as private possession but as transmission across generations. The line invites the reader to consider the entire novel as an act of memory — Poole's own backward glance at a New York already vanishing at the time of writing.

## Childhood and the Distant Past

The novel reaches backward past Roger's adult life to the memories of his own childhood. "Far back in his childhood a memory flashed into his mind" (025-088), a moment that suggests how the deepest layers of self are formed in experiences long antecedent to the novel's present action. Roger is sometimes "in a kind of a stupor," remembering (021-001) — a state in which the boundary between past and present dissolves.

On a park bench, Roger "groped for the memory she had stirred" (001-306), the verb "groped" conveying the effortful, tactile quality of recollection in old age. Memory is no longer instantaneous for Roger; it must be hunted down in the recesses of a mind crowded with decades.

## The Generational Arc

Nostalgia cuts across generations in the novel. Edith Sidney, like her father, lives in the shadow of a defining loss — her child — and her memories shape her present choices. The novel's closing image captures nostalgia at its most elemental: the survivor performing rituals of remembrance for a family that now exists only in the past. Roger's nostalgia is the nostalgia of a man approaching the end of his own story, looking back on a life that feels at once his own and already distant. The "memory now was the story of his family" (001-149) — nostalgia transformed from mere sentiment into narrative art.
