# Allan Baird

## Overview

Allan Baird is the doctor-reformer who shares Deborah Gale's commitment to social justice and serves as one of the novel's clearest voices for progressive ideals. "Allan Baird was not only the doctor but the friend" (006-172), and his presence in the novel provides both a professional and a personal counterpoint to Deborah's educational work. Where Deborah builds schools, Allan practices medicine — two forms of service that, together, represent the novel's vision of social conscience in action.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Allan enters the novel through his friendship with Deborah — they dine together, debate, and share the work of serving New York's poorest communities. He is described dining with Deborah (004-019), discussing her school and her students, and confronting her about her choices with an affectionate directness. His medical practice in the tenements gives him a perspective on poverty and suffering that parallels Deborah's educational work.

His confrontations with Deborah are among the novel's most vivid scenes. "You'll do no such thing," Allan says indignantly at one point (006-280), and "It isn't, eh," he sternly rejoined at another (006-283). These exchanges reveal a man who cares deeply about Deborah and about the work they share.

## Relationships

- **Deborah Gale**: Colleague, friend, and moral ally. Their relationship is one of mutual respect and shared purpose.
- **Roger Gale**: A respectful acquaintance. Allan represents a world of progressive reform that Roger observes with cautious interest.
- **Bruce**: Fellow reformer, though Allan's domain is medicine rather than architecture.

## Themes

Allan embodies the novel's interest in the "religion of brotherhood" (040-087) — a secular faith expressed through service to the poor and sick. His medical practice is not merely a job but a vocation, parallel to Deborah's educational calling.
