# New Hampshire Farm

## Overview

The New Hampshire farm is the place of Roger Gale's origins — the mountain home where his "deep zest" was "bred in his boyhood" among "the pines and forests" (001-069). It is the counterpoint to New York City throughout the novel: rural against urban, natural against constructed, past against present. Roger keeps it as a summer retreat, returning there with his family, and it represents the values — independence, simplicity, connection to land — that the modern city threatens to erase.

## Description in the Novel

The farm is in the New Hampshire mountains, a place of pines, forests, and the kind of boyhood freedom that Roger associates with his deepest self. "Young Roger had discovered, even in those early years" (001-074) the pleasures of physical work and natural beauty. The mountain setting shapes his character: his stubbornness, his love of hard work, his discomfort with the social performances of city life.

The farm is also the site of the "old New Hampshire home which he had kept as a summer place" (001-201) — a deliberate preservation of the past. Roger brings his daughters there, and the summer visits become part of the family's rhythm. The farm is where the Gales are most themselves, away from the pressures and pretensions of the city.

## Scenes and Events

- Roger's boyhood memories — discovering his "deep zest" in the forests
- Summer family visits that offer respite from city life
- Roger's comparison of the mountains' permanence with the city's constant change
- The farm as retirement possibility — Roger's half-formed dream of return

## Thematic Significance

The New Hampshire farm represents the America that is being lost — rural, rooted, and slow to change. It is Roger's spiritual home even as his material life is in New York. The novel's tension between new and old is spatial as well as temporal: the farm (old) and the city (new) pull Roger in opposite directions, and his inability to return permanently to the farm is one of the novel's quiet tragedies.
