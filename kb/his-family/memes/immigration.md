# Immigration

**Immigration** forms a persistent background in *His Family*, part of the larger pattern of urban transformation that [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] observes with unease. The novel's New York is a city being remade by successive waves of new arrivals, and the established order — represented by Roger's generation — reacts with a mixture of condescension, anxiety, and occasional sympathy.

## In the Novel

The immigrant presence is felt in the crowded tenement districts where [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] runs her school. A sharp authorial aside describes prejudice "against these immigrant women for breeding so inconsiderately" (010-121), capturing the nativist sentiment that the novel both records and implicitly critiques. In Deborah's work, "all the other questions that rose among these immigrants jammed into this" (015-043) city become tangible — issues of health, education, and opportunity that the Gale family's own prosperity has insulated them from. The immigrant experience in Poole's novel is rendered not through individual immigrant characters developed at length, but through the institutional and social pressures their presence exerts on the city and its institutions.

## Deborah's School and the Immigrant Community

[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]'s school for immigrant children is the novel's primary vehicle for engaging with immigration. Through her work, the novel explores the tension between established New York society — represented by Roger's generation — and the rapidly changing demographic reality of the early twentieth-century city. Deborah's commitment to serving immigrant families brings her into conflict with her father's more traditional understanding of [[duty]], which he initially conceives as obligation to one's own family rather than to strangers. The school thus becomes a site where the novel's family themes intersect with broader social questions about [[class]], [[duty]], and the boundaries of community.

## Connection to Family Themes

Immigration in *His Family* is more than sociological background. It is a measure of the distance between Roger's world and the world his daughters — particularly [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] — are choosing to engage with. The immigrant presence challenges the insularity of the Gale family and raises questions about [[duty]] that extend beyond blood relations.
