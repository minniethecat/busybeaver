## Overview

Peace is both a desired state and a thematic destination in *His [[family|Family]]*. For [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], peace is what he seeks in his home, in his relationships with his daughters, and ultimately in his reconciliation with [[death|mortality]]. The novel charts his journey from the anxious striving of midlife to a hard-won peace at the end, an acceptance that is not resignation but a deeper understanding of what family means. The word recurs throughout the novel as a refrain — [[roger-gale|Roger]] repeatedly finds the "peace of his home" disrupted (007-001), only to seek it again, and the novel's emotional arc is defined by the tension between his longing for peace and the forces that deny it.

## Role in the Novel

The absence of peace defines much of the novel's emotional landscape. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s home is frequently disrupted — by arguments, by [[disease|illness]], by the competing demands of his daughters' lives. His wife [[judith-gale|Judith Gale]]'s death has left a void that no amount of [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al success can fill. The city itself, with its relentless energy, is the antithesis of peace.

Yet the novel also records moments of respite — "one of those peaceful times, when his home" life settled into a calm rhythm (018-082). These interludes are fragile and temporary; they give way to new disturbances, new conflicts among the daughters, new crises. The pattern — disruption, struggle, brief peace, renewed disruption — structures the novel's domestic plot and mirrors [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s inner life.

Peace arrives only gradually and incompletely. [[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]'s reconciliation with her father represents a restoration of peace within the family, a healing of the rift that opened after her decision to found her school. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s final acceptance is an arrival at peace, not because his problems are solved but because he has learned to embrace the life he has rather than mourn the life he expected. In one of the novel's late reflective passages, [[roger-gale|Roger]] "sank deep in his chair and took peace to his soul by thinking of" the family that remains (037-232).

## Key Manifestations

### Domestic Peace

The novel's most persistent use of "peace" concerns the [[home|household]] — "the peace of his home" (007-001), the need for "a little peace in his [[home|house]]" (029-150), the wish "if only there could be peace in the [[home|house]]" (030-018). [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s desire for domestic tranquility is repeatedly thwarted by the very [[family|family life]] he cherishes. Poole presents this not as a failure but as a paradox: the family that disturbs [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s peace is also the only source of the deeper peace he ultimately finds.

### Peace as Bargain

The novel is realistic about the cost of peace. [[roger-gale|Roger]] discovers that domestic peace often comes "at the price of her peace with her God" (032-114) — that is, through compromises and sacrifices made by the [[womens-roles|women]] in [[family|his family]]. Late in the novel, he reflects that "at least he had bought some peace in his home" (034-302), the commercial metaphor suggesting that tranquility in [[family|family life]] is not given but purchased, through patience, concession, and the willingness to let go of one's own demands.

### Peace and Death

The relationship between peace and [[death|mortality]] runs through the novel. The "peace for [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s soul" (014-067) that follows moments of crisis suggests that peace is ultimately a spiritual condition, not a circumstantial one. It is tied to [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s gradual acceptance of his own [[death|mortality]] and of the [[death|loss]]es — his wife, his younger daughter, his former life — that have shaped him.

## Thematic Significance

Peace in the novel is not presented as passivity or escape. It is an active [[success|achievement]] — the result of struggle, acceptance, and the choice to [[love-and-courtship|love]] what remains rather than grieve [[loss|what is lost]]. Poole ties peace to the novel's central question: what does it mean to be a family across time? The answer the novel offers is that family is the vessel within which peace becomes possible — not because [[family|family life]] is peaceful, but because it gives [[death|loss]] its meaning and change its context.
