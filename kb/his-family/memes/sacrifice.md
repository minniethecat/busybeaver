# Sacrifice

## Overview

Sacrifice in *His [[family|Family]]* is an undercurrent of [[family|family life]] — the quiet, ongoing self-denial that parents and siblings undertake for one another. The novel does not celebrate sacrifice as noble suffering but presents it as a fact of familial [[love-and-courtship|love]], woven into the daily choices that define relationships. Poole's treatment is unsentimental: sacrifice is real, costly, and rarely recognized in its own time. It is what families run on, and what the novel's structure — moving from generation to generation — is designed to make visible.

## Manifestations

Sacrifice takes three forms in the novel. As **parental devotion**: [[edith-sidney|Edith Gale]] embodies this theme most fully. After her husband's death, "How [[edith-sidney|Edith]] had slaved and sacrificed, how bravely she had" (028-093) becomes a silent testimony to the cost of family devotion — the word "slaved" emphasizing the relentlessness of her [[work-and-vocation|labor]]. The sentence breaks off, as if the description exceeds what language can capture. As **[[work-and-vocation|vocation]]al commitment**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s commitment to her school project carries a similar weight: "I'd sacrifice anything — everything — [[education|schools]]" (035-107), demonstrating the all-or-nothing quality of genuine sacrifice. The ellipsis between "everything" and "[[education|schools]]" suggests the breadth of what she is willing to give up — and the hierarchy that places her [[work-and-vocation|work]] above all else. As **generational transfer**: sacrifice in the novel is ultimately about what one generation gives up so the next can have more. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s lifelong [[work-and-vocation|labor]], [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s domestic self-denial, [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[work-and-vocation|vocation]]al dedication — all are forms of investment in futures the givers will not see.

The novel does not ask whether sacrifice is worth it. It simply shows that it is what [[love-and-courtship|love]] looks like when it takes material form.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]**: Sacrifice as domestic [[work-and-vocation|labor]] — the "slaving" that keeps [[family|family life]] running.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Sacrifice as [[work-and-vocation|vocation]] — giving up personal comfort for the [[work-and-vocation|work]] she believes in.
- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: Sacrifice as fatherhood — a lifetime of [[work-and-vocation|work]] whose rewards are deferred to [[ediths-children|the next generation]].
