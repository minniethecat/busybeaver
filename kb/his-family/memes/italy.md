# Italy

## Overview

Italy appears in *His Family* as one of the European destinations that draw wealthy Americans across the Atlantic — a place of art, history, and a pace of life that contrasts with New York's frantic energy. It is part of the European world that Laura and Harold Sloane inhabit after their marriage, and its presence in the novel signals the cultural aspirations of the American upper class. Italy is the Old World — cultivated, beautiful, and distant from the concerns of the Gale family.

## Description in the Novel

References to Italy are brief and allusive — the novel assumes its readers know what Italy represents: the Renaissance, beauty, a slower tempo of life. For Laura and Harold, Italy is one stop on a European itinerary that signals their arrival in the world of wealth and leisure. For the novel itself, Italy is a point of contrast — the ancient against the modern, the aesthetic against the practical, the leisurely against the driven.

## Scenes and Events

- Laura and Harold's European travels, of which Italy is a part
- The cultural associations that Italy carries for educated Americans of the era
- The contrast between Italy's ancient continuity and New York's constant change

## Thematic Significance

Italy represents the cultural and aesthetic values that the novel's American characters admire but cannot fully inhabit. It is the world of art and leisure that stands outside the novel's central drama of family, work, and change — a reminder that other ways of living exist, even if they are not available to Roger or Deborah.
