# Remorse

**Remorse** is a recurrent emotional undercurrent in Ernest Poole's *His Family*, surfacing at key moments of loss, separation, and familial reckoning. Characters experience remorse not as abstract guilt but as a visceral pang tied to specific relationships and irreversible choices.

## In the Novel

Roger Gale feels a "pang of deep remorse" (003-092) when contemplating his daughter [[laura-sloane|Laura]] in love — an early signal that his paternal oversight has blind spots. The emotion returns more devastatingly after Laura's death, when those left behind experience "anguish, sobbing, alone. And remorse came" (028-095). In the novel's later chapters, Roger and his surviving daughters draw close "as though in remorse" (037-010), the shared weight of loss reshaping their bonds.

## The Architecture of Remorse

Remorse in *His Family* is structured around three distinct moments: anticipation, crisis, and aftermath. The "pang of deep remorse" Roger feels early in the novel (003-092) is anticipatory — a premonition that his relationship with Laura will be a source of future regret. The remorse that follows Laura's death (028-095) is the crisis point, raw and isolating. The drawing-together of the surviving family members "as though in remorse" (037-010) represents the aftermath — remorse transformed from a private anguish into a shared condition that binds the family closer. This tripartite structure mirrors the novel's broader concern with how [[death]] reshapes family relationships: not by erasing conflict, but by recasting it in the light of irreversible loss.

## Themes

Remorse in *His Family* is tied to the theme of [[change]] — the recognition that time has carried relationships past the point of repair. Unlike guilt, which implies moral failure, remorse in Poole's treatment is existential: it arises from the simple, painful awareness that one did not love enough, or act soon enough, while the opportunity remained. The novel does not offer easy consolation for remorse; rather, it suggests that remorse can become a form of connection — a shared acknowledgment of loss that, paradoxically, draws the surviving family members closer together.
