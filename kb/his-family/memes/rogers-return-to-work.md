# Roger Gale's Return to Work

## Overview

Roger's return to his office after his prolonged illness is a quiet but significant
event in the novel's final movement. It signals that he has survived — not just
physically but psychologically — and that the structures of his life (business,
routine, professional identity) are still standing. Roger, who was "crazy to get
back to his office again" (001-204) even in normal times, had been too sick to
work; his return is therefore a measure of recovery and resilience.

## Context and Build-up

Roger's relationship to his office had always been central to his identity. He
"rose early and was in his office by nine o'clock" (008-040), working from a
"dark creaky edifice down" (008-049) in the financial district. His illness
interrupted this lifelong rhythm — an existential crisis as much as a physical
one, since Roger measured himself by his work. Johnny Geer, who had been "back in
Roger's office" and proven "a great help" (022-043), kept the business running
during his absence. Johnny's visits during Roger's convalescence served as
bridges back to professional life.

## The Event

The return itself is understated in the narrative — Roger simply walks back into
his office and resumes his place. But the novel has prepared us to understand its
weight. After "her father returned from his office" had been the daily rhythm of
the household (013-087), his absence was an unnatural silence. His return
restores that rhythm. The office survived his absence — a continuity that mirrors
the continuity Roger hopes for in his family. "Upon his return to his office he"
(024-075) felt something "almost of guilt" — the guilt of the survivor, perhaps,
or the guilt of a man who had briefly let go of the world.

## Consequences

The return completes the illness arc and sets the stage for the novel's final
movement toward resolution. Roger is diminished but not defeated, changed but
still himself. His professional life — the constant thread running through
decades of family change — proves durable. He has work to do, and that work
keeps him tethered to life.
