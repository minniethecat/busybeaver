## Overview

The **[[medicine|doctor]]** in *His [[family|Family]]* is a figure of [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al authority whose limits the novel quietly tests. [[bruce|Bruce]] Cunningham embodies the modern medical professional, trained and credentialed, yet the novel shows medicine encountering situations that exceed its powers — especially in [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s prolonged [[disease|illness]], when "the [[medicine|doctor]]s" (041-056) [[work-and-vocation|work]] "far into the next night" without the outcome anyone [[dream|hope]]s for. The [[medicine|doctor]] appears as a family member, a cultural type, and a symbol of the new [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al [[social-class|class]] reshaping [[america|American]] society in the early twentieth century.

## The Doctor as Professional and Family Member

[[bruce|Bruce]]'s medical identity is established early and reinforced throughout the novel. When a child is born, "the [[medicine|doctor]] who had brought [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s child" (006-173) enters the narrative as a figure of life-giving authority. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s school engages "[[deborahs-school|the school]] [[medicine|doctor]], the nurse" (010-104) as institutional fixtures, reflecting the [[progress|Progress]]ive Era's [[religion|faith]] in [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al expertise as an instrument of social improvement. Even in casual conversation, medical authority is something one can invoke or borrow: "I learned that from the [[medicine|doctor]]" (011-129), a [[nature|character]] quips, treating the [[medicine|doctor]]'s word as portable wisdom.

But the novel also registers the [[medicine|doctor]]'s limitations. "In the morning the [[medicine|doctor]] [[poverty|want]]ed to pull up the shades" (006-077) — a small domestic detail that suggests the tension between medical advice and family preference. The [[medicine|doctor]]'s instructions are not always followed; the [[home|household]] has its own rhythms that resist [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al intervention. A [[nature|character]] observes drily, "You know, people fool [[medicine|doctor]]s" (017-027), acknowledging that patients can be opaque to even trained observers, capable of concealing symptoms or fabricating them.

## The Limits of Medical Authority

The novel does not mock [[medicine|doctor]]s — [[bruce|Bruce]] is a sympathetic figure — but it consistently shows the gap between medical confidence and actual outcomes. When someone urgently calls, "You'd better have a [[medicine|doctor]]!" (012-062), the exclamation carries both [[religion|faith]] in medical intervention and [[fear|anxiety]] about its necessity. The need for a [[medicine|doctor]] signals a crisis beyond lay management. Yet the novel also registers the [[medicine|doctor]]'s fallibility: "that [[new-york-city|New York]] [[medicine|doctor]] had been right" (038-132) is said in a context that implies other [[medicine|doctor]]s have been wrong. And a [[nature|character]] tells himself bluntly, "These [[medicine|doctor]]s fool themselves often" (039-039).

During [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s final [[disease|illness]], the [[medicine|doctor]] becomes a recurring but limited presence. "When the [[medicine|doctor]] arrived she took him upstairs" (035-050), and the formal politeness — "Come right in here, [[medicine|doctor]], won't you? I [[poverty|want]] to hear" (035-056) — masks the family's anxious dependency. "[[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[medicine|doctor]] had put her to bed and installed" (041-042) a regimen, but the installation of medical authority coincides with [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s withdrawal from the active life that defined her. The "hard faces from time to time of [[medicine|doctor]]s, nurses, servants" (041-122) that pass through [[the-hospital|the sickroom]] blur together — the [[medicine|doctor]] is one figure among many in the caregiving ensemble, central but not sufficient.

## The Doctor's Professional Ecosystem

The novel's [[medicine|doctor]]s exist within a broader medical ecosystem. There is "[[deborahs-school|the school]] [[medicine|doctor]]" (010-104) — institutional medicine, preventive and bureaucratic. There is the attending physician at a birth (006-173) — medicine at its most triumphant. And there is the [[medicine|doctor]] called in for a crisis who may or may not be able to help. A telling comparison emerges with the druggist: when a [[nature|character]] "went to the druggist on her block, and after listening to his advice she bought some patent medicine" (015-087), the druggist functions as a cut-rate [[medicine|doctor]], offering medical advice without medical credentials.

This spectrum of medical authority — from the corner druggist to the trained physician to the specialists who attend [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] — maps the uneven landscape of early twentieth-century [[america|American]] [[medicine|health]]care. The novel is too subtle to moralize about this unevenness, but it records it with documentary precision.
