# Family

> "For the story of his family, from Roger's point of view at least, a long uneven narrative, with prolonged periods of peace and again with events piling one on the other." (008-016)

## The Central Motif

"His Family" announces its subject in its title. The novel traces the life of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], a widower in late 19th- and early 20th-century [[new-york-city|New York]], as he watches his three daughters — [[edith-sidney|Edith]], [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], and [[laura-sloane|Laura]] — diverge from one another and from the world he knows. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s deepest attachment is also his deepest [[fear|anxiety]], for his family is both sanctuary and proving ground: the place where his values are tested by generational change (001-070).

The novel opens with [[roger-gale|Roger]] at sixty, alone in [[the-gale-home|the family home]] on the lower west side of [[new-york-city|New York]], reflecting on a life shaped by family: first his marriage to [[judith-gale|Judith]], who died young leaving him with three small daughters, then the long years of raising them, and now the unsettling present in which his daughters have become strangers to him and to one another. Though [[roger-gale|Roger]] built his identity around "his family," the family itself has splintered — "separation, as the family of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] divided and went different ways" (006-010).

## Three Daughters, Three Paths

Each of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s three daughters embodies a distinct response to family [[duty|obligation]], and their divergent paths form the novel's emotional architecture.

**[[edith-sidney|Edith Gale Sidney]]** (eldest) embraces family as biological and domestic continuity. She marries young, raises five [[childhood|children]] in the countryside, and becomes the keeper of the family's domestic traditions. From [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s perspective, "new lives in his family" arrive through [[ediths-children|Edith's children]] (004-202). She is practical, [[tradition|convention]]al, and rooted — the daughter who most resembles the world [[roger-gale|Roger]] understands.

**[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]** (middle daughter) transforms family [[duty|obligation]] into public service. Rejecting a traditional marriage to [[bruce|Bruce]], she founds a school for immigrant [[childhood|children]] in the tenement districts of [[new-york-city|New York City]]. Her conception of family expands outward: "Down in my school we've a family of seven hundred [[childhood|children]]" (009-030). What [[roger-gale|Roger]] calls "[[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s big family" (010-044) becomes both his pride and his bewilderment — a family that includes [[new-york-city|the city]]'s poor.

**[[laura-sloane|Laura Gale Sloane]]** (youngest) flees family for social ambition and modernity. She marries into wealth, travels to [[europe|Europe]], and embodies the restless energy of the new century. She is the daughter who most confounds [[roger-gale|Roger]]: "wild as a hawk, gadding about to parties" (002-130). Yet her departure from the family circle is itself a family act — [[laura-sloane|Laura]] defines herself in reaction to the home she leaves behind.

## Domestic Space and Memory

The [[the-gale-home|Gale family home]] on the lower west side of [[new-york-city|New York]] serves as the novel's central gathering place — the [[home|house]] where [[judith-gale|Judith]]'s family "had lived long in [[new-york-city|New York]]" before her death (001-094). The home is where [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s memory lives, where his deepest [[memory|recollection]] "was the story of his family" (001-149).

Yet the home also records absence. After [[judith-gales-death|Judith Gale's death]], the [[home|house]] becomes a museum of [[death|loss]], and the daughters' departures hollow it further. By the novel's middle, "his family had shifted its center" (021-105) — the physical [[home|house]] remains, but its gravitational pull weakens as each daughter establishes her own orbit. [[deborah-and-rogers-reconciliation|Deborah and Roger's reconciliation]] late in the novel returns the home briefly to its role as family anchor, but on different terms.

## Family as Inheritance

The novel consistently frames family as an inheritance — not of wealth, but of [[nature|temperament]] and accumulated moral weight. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] descends from "many [[generational-change|generations]] of stolid folk" in the [[new-hampshire-farm|New Hampshire]] mountains, where "his family had lived for many [[generational-change|generations]]" (001-070). This lineage gives [[roger-gale|Roger]] his solidity, his stubbornness, and his conviction that a family should hold together.

The question the novel poses is whether this inheritance can survive modernity. [[ediths-children|Edith's children]] represent biological continuity — "to himself and his family was living on in [[edith-sidney|Edith]]" (006-169) — but [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s "family" of school[[childhood|children]] and [[laura-sloane|Laura]]'s cosmopolitan marriage suggest that inheritance takes new forms in a changing world. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s final [[peace|acceptance]], late in the novel, is that his family has not been lost but transformed.

## The Title's Irony

The possessive "His" in the novel's title carries deliberate weight. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] sees the family as his — his creation, his [[duty|responsibility]], his story. But the novel chronicles his slow reckoning with the fact that a family is not a possession. Each daughter claims her own version of family, and each version is legitimate. The novel's arc traces [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s movement from ownership to [[peace|acceptance]].
