## Overview

Pride in *His [[family|Family]]* operates as a subtle emotional register — rarely declared but frequently felt. For [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], pride is a quiet satisfaction in what he has built: his home, [[family|his family]], his standing. It is not boastful but deeply felt, a "little glow" that surfaces in moments of recognition. For his [[childhood|children]], pride takes different forms: [[bruce|Bruce]]'s pride in material possessions, [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s easily wounded pride in her maternal role. Across the novel, pride functions as both a source of warmth and a point of vulnerability.

## Manifestations

[[roger-gale|Roger]]'s pride is rooted in the tangible: "[[roger-gale|Roger]] took pride in showing it" (005-170) — "it" being his home, the physical manifestation of a lifetime's [[work-and-vocation|work]]. In quieter moments, he feels "a little glow of pride. Yes, this daughter of his wa[s]" (028-051), the unfinished sentence suggesting an emotion too tender to name fully. The narrator elsewhere notes "a slight glow of pride" (030-062) and that [[roger-gale|Roger]] "dwelt on this little glow of pride" (037-030), the repetition of "little glow" marking pride as a modest, almost shy emotion — [[nature|character]]istic of [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s reserve.

For [[ediths-children|the next generation]], pride is more complicated. [[bruce|Bruce]]'s "new car, which was the pride and joy of" (013-199) [[youth|the young]] man, represents the material pride of a new era. [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s pride is more fragile: when something comes "there to wound her pride, [[edith-sidney|Edith]] quickly showed herself" (034-308) — pride here is a defensive reflex, easily injured. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s own pride operates differently from his [[childhood|children]]'s: it is "pride, but not to his daughter's an[swer]" (026-087), suggesting a generational gap in how pride is expressed and understood. [[edith-sidney|Edith]] "prided" herself on certain qualities (006-162), but the novel quietly questions whether such pride in traditional roles can survive the changes sweeping through the family.
