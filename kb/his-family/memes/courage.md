# Courage

## Overview

Courage in *His [[family|Family]]* is not the dramatic heroism of battlefields but the intimate bravery required within [[family|family life]] — mustering resolve for difficult conversations, facing discouragement, and persisting through personal trials. The novel presents courage as a domestic virtue, exercised in drawing rooms and quiet talks rather than public arenas. This is consistent with the novel's broader concern: how ordinary people face the daily demands of life, [[death|loss]], and change without the consolations of heroic narrative. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s courage, like everything else about him, is quiet and cumulative — a matter of persistence rather than grand gestures.

## Manifestations

Courage takes three distinguishable forms in the novel. As **mustered resolve**: [[george-sidney|George]] Gale embodies this form of courage when he prepares to share important news with his father: "Mustering his courage he led up to it by speaking of..." (003-067). The verb *mustering* is significant — it suggests courage is assembled deliberately, called up from reserves rather than spontaneously discovered. As **persistence through discouragement**: [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] confronts "heavy discouragements" (009-092) in his [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al and [[family|family life]], yet continues forward. Other [[nature|character]]s face setbacks without visible collapse — "[they] did not appear in the least discouraged" (017-160) — and one insists rejection "Did not discourage me in the least" (017-165). The novel treats courage as the capacity to hear "no" and continue, unshaken. As **quiet endurance of fear**: "Repeatedly he fought off his fears" (024-079) describes a form of courage that is invisible, repetitive, and unglamorous — a daily discipline rather than a single decisive act. Even when "a cold [[fear|dread]] of this season" grips her, one [[nature|character]] "bravely kept it to herself" (030-077), locating courage in concealment and self-containment.

The novel's treatment of courage is gendered. Men are expected to be courageous in public ways; [[womens-roles|women]]'s courage is exercised in domestic endurance. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s description as "a brave little woman" (026-094) captures this diminution — courage acknowledged but qualified, as if bravery in a woman is inherently surprising and therefore smaller.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: His courage is persistence — facing "discouragements" in business and family without breaking.
- **[[george-sidney|George]] Gale**: The courage of difficult conversations — "mustering" resolve before speaking to his father.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Courage as [[work-and-vocation|vocation]]al commitment. She faces opposition to her school and persists, described as "brave" in a way that both honors and diminishes her.
- **[[bruce|Bruce]]**: Courage after [[the-great-war|the war]] — facing a life diminished by injury and [[death|loss]] without complaint.
