# Deborah and Roger's Reconciliation

## Overview

The reconciliation between Roger and Deborah is the novel's emotional climax — the moment when the central conflict of *His Family* reaches its resolution. Roger has spent the novel resisting Deborah's independence — her school, her "family of my own," her relationship with Bruce — and Deborah has spent it asserting her right to a life beyond her father's expectations. Their reconciliation is not a single scene but a gradual process, accelerated by Roger's illness, in which Roger comes to see that Deborah's definition of family is not a rejection of his but an expansion of it.

## Context and Build-up

The conflict between Roger and Deborah is the novel's deepest emotional current. She is the daughter most like him — stubborn, principled, committed — and their battles over her independence are the most painful because they matter the most. Roger's visit to "a few of the class-rooms" (010-095) is an early step, but his illness is the real catalyst: in the hospital, dependent on Deborah's care, he begins to see what she has been building.

## The Event

The reconciliation unfolds through small gestures and quiet conversations rather than a single dramatic scene. Roger's acceptance of Johnny Geer — Deborah's student — into his office and his life is one measure of the change. His willingness to let Deborah define her own future is another. By the novel's end, Roger has arrived at a hard-won peace with his daughter's choices.

## Consequences

The reconciliation completes Roger's character arc: from grief-stricken patriarch to a man capable of accepting that his daughters' lives will not follow his script. It also validates Deborah's vision of family — not as a rejection of blood ties but as their generous extension.
