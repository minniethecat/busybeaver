# Founding of Deborah's School

## Overview

The founding of Deborah Gale's tenement school is the event that redefines the novel's central concept — family. When Deborah establishes "the big school in the tenements" (002-025), she is doing more than opening a classroom: she is creating what she will later call "a family of my own." The school's founding is the alternative to the traditional family that Roger is trying to preserve — not a rejection of family but an expansion of its boundaries to include those who need it most.

## Context and Build-up

After her mother's death, Deborah "had stayed at home with her father when Laura had gone away to school" (001-151), filling the domestic role that Judith's death had vacated. But she was not content with domesticity alone. The tenement districts of New York — crowded with immigrant families, hungry children, and untapped potential — called to her. She began with "little things" (001-151), small acts of teaching and organizing, and these grew into a network of classrooms.

The educational establishment did not support her. "The principal of her school" — meaning the head of a conventional school — "had made little impression" (002-036) on Deborah. She was building something new, and the old institutions did not recognize it.

## The Event

The school's founding is not depicted as a single dramatic moment but as an accumulation — "little things" becoming a school, a school becoming a movement. Deborah found space in a tenement building, recruited students from the neighborhood's immigrant families, and built a curriculum around the children's actual needs. Her sister Edith's mockery — "her latest and most modern fad, to run a school where every child shall sit with a rat in its lap" (007-081) — captures the conventional skepticism Deborah faced.

## Consequences

The school transforms Deborah from dutiful daughter to independent woman with a vocation. It becomes the center of her emotional life and the locus of the alternative family she builds — students like Johnny Geer, colleagues like Allan Baird, the wider community of the tenement district. It also becomes the primary source of conflict with Roger, who sees her commitment to the school as a threat to her commitment to him.

## Thematic Significance

The school's founding embodies the novel's progressive vision: that family can be chosen as well as inherited, that service to the community is a legitimate form of filial devotion, and that a woman's life can be defined by more than marriage and motherhood.
