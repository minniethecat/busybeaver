## Identity

Madge Deering is a minor but thematically important character in *His Family*, introduced as [[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]'s close friend. She is a widow living in Morristown, New Jersey, with four young daughters. Her husband died when she was twenty-nine, leaving her to raise the girls alone (001-210)--(001-212). Despite this tragedy, Madge is described as resilient and determined, a woman who refused to be defeated by circumstance. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] had known Madge for years and "liked her for all that" (001-211) -- a grudging admiration that speaks both to Madge's appeal and to Roger's complicated feelings about modern women.

> Naturally smart and able, Madge was always running to town, keeping up with all her friends and with every new fad and movement there, although she made fun of most of them. Twice she had taken her girls abroad. (001-214)--(001-217)

## Character and Worldliness

Madge represents a modern, independent woman -- the kind [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] wishes his daughter Edith could become. Roger contrasts Madge's vitality with Edith's more withdrawn nature, reflecting on how Madge has grown in "strength and breadth" since the death of her husband (021-052)--(021-055). This phrase -- "strength and breadth" -- is significant: Madge has not merely survived loss but expanded, deepened, become more fully herself through it.

She is portrayed as worldly and socially engaged. "Naturally smart and able, Madge was always running to town, keeping up with all her friends and with every new fad and movement there, although she made fun of most of them" (001-214)--(001-216). This detail reveals her independence of mind -- she is curious about the new, engaged with it, but not swept away by it. She is also dauntlessly facing the challenges of raising four inquisitive daughters in their teens (004-186)--(004-189).

## Role in the Novel

Madge serves as a foil to [[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]. Where Edith is domestic and cautious, Madge is adventurous and outward-facing. Her worldliness and resilience throw Edith's anxieties and retreat from life into sharper relief. Roger constantly measures Edith against Madge, and Edith herself seems aware of the comparison.

In Chapter XXI, Madge calls Edith to arrange a visit: "Madge Deering called me up to-night" (021-050), Edith tells Roger in "a tone of weariness." The weariness suggests that Madge's vitality, while admirable, can also be exhausting -- or perhaps that Edith feels the weight of the comparison. When Roger says approvingly, "I'm glad," and adds, "You and Madge -- " before pausing (021-056), the unfinished sentence speaks volumes about his hopes for his daughter.

In Chapter XXII, after [[bruce|Bruce]]'s health crisis, Madge offers to accompany Edith and the children to the mountains and see them settled there (022-005)--(022-007). A brief talk with Madge "relieved his mind still further" (022-007). Her practical competence is a comfort in a family that often seems paralyzed by indecision and anxiety.

> What a recovery she had made from the tragedy of years ago. How alert and wide-awake she seemed. If Edith could only grow like that. (022-008)--(022-009)

## Resilience and Recovery

Madge's story is fundamentally one of recovery. Widowed at twenty-nine with four daughters, she might have retreated into grief or dependency. Instead, she built a life of energy, independence, and engagement. Roger's admiration for her is tinged with something like envy on his daughter's behalf -- if Madge can do it, why cannot Edith?

Her resilience is shown not in grand gestures but in the texture of daily life: running to town, keeping up with movements, taking her girls abroad twice, facing inquisitive teenagers "dauntlessly" (004-186)--(004-189). She has refused to let tragedy define her, and in doing so has become the novel's exemplar of what a woman alone could achieve in early 20th-century America.

## Relationships

- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]** -- Close friend; Madge is a confidante and, in Roger's eyes, a role model. Edith's weariness when Madge calls (021-050) suggests a complex dynamic: affection mixed with the exhausting awareness of being measured against a standard she cannot meet
- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]** -- Roger admires Madge's resilience and wishes his daughters shared it. He has known her for years and "liked her for all that" (001-211)
- **Four daughters** -- Unnamed in the novel, but their inquisitiveness is noted as "the most terrific questions" Madge faces dauntlessly (004-186)--(004-189)
- **[[bruce|Bruce]]** -- Her offer to help Edith and the children after Bruce's illness is a practical expression of her loyalty to the family

## Themes

Madge embodies the theme of adaptability and resilience in the face of personal tragedy. She also represents the changing role of women in early 20th-century America -- a widow who, rather than retreating from the world, actively engages with it and raises her daughters with similar independence. As a foil to Edith, she sharpens the novel's central question about the next generation: will they shrink from life or embrace it? Madge has chosen embracement.
