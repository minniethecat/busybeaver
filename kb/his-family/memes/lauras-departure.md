# Laura's Departure for Europe

## Overview

Laura's departure for Europe with Harold Sloane is the event that most clearly
signals her separation from the Gale family. After the wedding, she and Harold
travel, socialize, and inhabit a world of "sumptuous young women" (003-181) that
Roger can neither enter nor fully comprehend. The departure is geographic but also
emotional: Laura is leaving not just the city but the family's gravitational field.
"Laura and her husband had returned from abroad in October" (016-005) — the word
"returned" reminding us that she had been away, and that her presence in the family
was now intermittent at best.

## Context and Build-up

Laura's marriage to Harold was always also an escape — from the middle-class home,
from her father's expectations, from the pull of family obligation. Harold, who
"knew Europe as a group" (005-108) of fashionable destinations, represented a world
of wealth and mobility that the Gales could not share. The departure for Europe is
the fulfillment of that trajectory: Harold's resources enable a life of travel and
leisure that sets Laura apart from her sisters, who remained anchored in New York.

## The Event

The departure is a threshold — Laura steps onto a ship and into a different life.
The novel registers it through the family's responses: Roger's quiet hurt, Edith's
practical concern, Deborah's understanding. Laura herself is exhilarated yet
anxious — "only a few weeks ahead" (023-146) captures the emotional ambiguity of
someone who has chosen distance but cannot quite see what lies beyond it. The
timing — "Laura and Harold returned from abroad" (025-022) at an unexpected moment
later in the novel — underscores how unpredictable her presence in the family has
become. She comes and goes on her own schedule, no longer bound by the rhythms of
the Gale household.

## Consequences

Laura's absence from the family's later crises — especially Roger's illness — is
the measure of her departure's cost. She has chosen distance, and distance means
absence precisely when she is most needed. Her return visits, however, also
introduce new elements to the family dynamic: on one return "from abroad had
brought as a" (031-006) gift or presence that shifted the household atmosphere.
Laura becomes a visitor in her own family, perpetually arriving and departing,
never quite belonging.
