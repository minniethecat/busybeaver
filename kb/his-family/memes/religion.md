# Religion

## Overview

Religion in *His [[family|Family]]* is less a doctrine than a question — one that each [[nature|character]] answers differently. For [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s generation, religion was inherited [[tradition|convention]]: church attendance, moral propriety, a general Christian frame[[work-and-vocation|work]] that did not require examination. But his daughters' generation faces the question directly. "How about your religion?" [[roger-gale|Roger]] blurts out to a young reformer (007-233), revealing his own unease with the subject. The novel traces religion's transformation from institutional Christianity to a more diffuse but urgent spirituality of social [[law|justice]] — what one [[nature|character]] calls "[[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s religion of brotherhood, democracy" (040-087).

## Manifestations

Religion appears in three modes. As **inherited [[tradition|convention]]**: the churches of old [[new-york-city|New York]], the "old religion" of the Christians [[roger-gale|Roger]] remembers (011-219), which provided social structure but demanded little personal engagement. As **moral crisis**: the moment when inherited faith proves inadequate — as when [[roger-gale|Roger]] contemplates death, poverty, and the suffering he sees in [[new-york-city|the city]], finding no ready answers in [[tradition|convention]]al piety. As **social gospel**: the emerging [[modernity|progress]]ive Christianity that animates [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[work-and-vocation|work]] — a faith expressed not in creeds but in [[education|schools]], clinics, and "the religion of brotherhood" that crosses [[social-class|class]] and ethnic lines.

[[allan-baird|Allan Baird]], the [[medicine|doctor]]-reformer, articulates the [[dream|hope]] that "at last you will have a religion" — not the old faith but a new one, born of engagement with suffering (011-218). The novel treats this transformation sympathetically but without triumphalism: the new religion of social service is genuine, but the old religion's consolations are not scorned.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]** represents the older generation's unexamined faith — Christianity as cultural inheritance, not personal conviction. His unease when religion is directly discussed reveals the gap between [[tradition|convention]] and belief.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]** embodies the new religion of service. Her [[education|schools]] are her church; her "brotherhood" is her creed. She practices a faith that [[roger-gale|Roger]] can respect even if he cannot share it.
- **[[allan-baird|Allan Baird]]** is the novel's clearest voice for [[modernity|progress]]ive Christianity — a faith reimagined as social medicine, healing bodies and communities rather than saving souls.
