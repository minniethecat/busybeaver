## Overview

Business is central to the world of *His [[family|Family]]*, both as the [[work-and-vocation|profession]] of the protagonist [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] and as a thematic force that shapes the social landscape of the novel. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s business — an innovative newspaper clipping bureau — represents the entrepreneurial energy of early twentieth-century [[america|America]], while the broader world of commerce and finance defines the social hierarchy in which his daughters move.

## Role in the Novel

[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s business [[work-and-vocation|career]] is introduced early in the novel. He "had ventured into the business world, clerking now at this" (001-085) establishment and that, eventually founding a novel enterprise: "a bureau of news clippings, a business new and unheard of but" (001-088) for its time. This innovation captures the spirit of [[america|American]] entrepreneurship — identifying a need and building an enterprise to fill it. The business grows rapidly: "the business widening fast had revealed" (001-090) new opportunities and challenges.

The demands of commerce are not without cost. The novel notes that "there had come reverses in business" (001-115) alongside domestic tragedies — "and at home one baby" (001-115) — linking the [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al and personal spheres. [[roger-gale|Roger]] becomes "absorbed in his growing business and in his" (001-117) expanding responsibilities, a tension that runs throughout the novel as he struggles to balance his commercial life with [[family|his family]] [[duty|obligation]]s.

Business also figures in the lives of [[ediths-children|the next generation]]. [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] represents a newer, more aggressive form of business — finance and speculation rather than the patient building of an enterprise. [[bruce|Bruce]] pursues architecture as a [[work-and-vocation|profession]], a creative form of business that stands between [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s commercial world and [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s idealistic reform [[work-and-vocation|work]].

## Thematic Significance

Business in the novel serves as a lens through which questions of ambition, success, and masculinity are examined. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s identity is deeply tied to his [[work-and-vocation|work]] — when his business falters, so does his sense of self. The contrast between [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s entrepreneurial generation and [[harold-sloane|Harold]]'s financial generation mirrors the broader shift from nineteenth-century capitalism to twentieth-century finance. Business is presented neither as villain nor hero but as the inescapable medium through which the [[nature|character]]s must navigate their lives and relationships.
