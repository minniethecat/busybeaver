# Marriage

## Overview

Marriage is the institution around which *His [[family|Family]]* organizes its exploration of [[love-and-courtship|love]], duty, and [[womens-roles|women]]'s lives in early 20th-century [[america|America]]. The novel opens in marriage's aftermath — [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], long widowed, remembers [[judith-gale|Judith]] and their shared life as the emotional baseline against which all subsequent events are measured. "Then had come his marriage," the narrative reflects (001-094), framing [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s union with [[judith-gale|Judith]] as both origin story and lost paradise, the event that made [[family|his family]] possible and whose ending set the novel's central drama in motion.

## Manifestations

Marriage takes three distinct forms across the novel. As **remembered ideal**: [[roger-gale|Roger]] and [[judith-gale|Judith]]'s marriage is the novel's gold standard — a union of deep partnership in which "their [[love-and-courtship|love]] had grown rich in sympathies" (001-111). It stands as both model and impossible benchmark for their daughters. As **social contract**: [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s marriage to [[george-sidney|George Sidney]] and [[laura-sloane|Laura]]'s to [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] represent different strategies of marital alliance — one domestic and practical, the other ambitious and socially mobile. "For marriage is a serious thing," the novel observes (003-174), and each daughter's choice reveals her values. As **rejected option**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s decision not to marry — or at least not to marry [[tradition|convention]]ally — is the novel's most radical statement about [[womens-roles|women]]'s changing relationship to the institution. She has her "big family" of students instead.

The novel is sophisticated about marriage's emotional texture. It does not sentimentalize ([[roger-gale|Roger]]'s loneliness is real) nor condemn ([[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s domesticity has [[pride|dignity]]). It simply shows, across four [[womens-roles|women]]'s lives, how marriage shapes and constrains, fulfills and disappoints.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger]] and [[judith-gale|Judith Gale]]**: The remembered marriage that haunts the novel — "the heart and center" of the family's identity (007-145).
- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]**: Marriage as continuity. Her union with [[george-sidney|George]] produces five [[childhood|children]] and anchors the family line.
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]**: Marriage as [[social-class|social mobility]]. Her choice of [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] takes her out of the family orbit and into a different [[social-class|class]].
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Marriage declined. Her [[freedom|independence]] is the novel's counterpoint to a world in which marriage is [[womens-roles|women]]'s expected destiny.
