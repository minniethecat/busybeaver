## Overview

**Science** in *His [[family|Family]]* appears both as a metaphor for systematic understanding and as a marker of the modern intellectual world encroaching on traditional [[family|family life]]. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], a businessman of an older generation, invokes science to describe what he has learned — and what he fears his [[childhood|children]] are leaving behind. The concept threads through the novel in three registers: as Roger's personal framework for understanding family, as a cultural signifier of the [[modernity|modern age]], and as a practical tool applied to the management of urban [[home|households]].

## Manifestations

[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] frames family life itself in scientific terms, declaring "our family isn't a job; it's a science, and I've got my degree" (006-119). This reveals his conviction that understanding and managing family relationships requires systematic knowledge, not mere instinct. The broader intellectual culture of the era surfaces in descriptions of a public library filled with "poetry, history, books of all the sciences" (011-180), where even a volume of Darwin sits prominently on the desk having been read so heavily that "it is a case for the board of health" (011-181). Later, the concept takes a practical turn with "Domestic science, naturally — I have a woman who does nothing else" (034-217), suggesting the application of systematic knowledge to [[home|household]] management — a theme aligned with the novel's interest in changing [[womens-roles|gender roles]] and [[work-and-vocation|professionalization]]. Science in the novel represents the modern impulse to systematize what was once intuitive, paralleling the broader societal shifts the Gale family navigates.

## Science and Education

[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]'s school embodies the progressive educational ideals of the era, which [[edith-sloane|Edith]] dismissively calls "her latest and most modern fad, to run a school where every child shall sit with a rat in its lap or a goat, and do just what he pleases" (007-081). Yet the school represents a serious commitment to applying modern principles of [[education]] to the lives of immigrant children. Roger himself recognizes its value, reflecting that "here was something solid, a good education for every child" (009-119). The [[education|school]] becomes a nexus where scientific and progressive ideas meet practical social reform, and where [[allan-baird|Doctor Baird]] — a man of medicine — becomes a frequent presence, his talk ranging over "clinics, adenoids, children's teeth, epidemics and the new education" (017-077).

## Science and Modern Thought

The tension between scientific rationality and older ways of thinking runs throughout the novel. [[roger-gale|Roger]] wrestles with his daughters' embrace of modern ideas, exclaiming to [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], "God knows I've tried to be modern" (036-209) and calling himself "downright modern" (036-210). Yet he simultaneously challenges her, asking "how in God's name can you modern women ever marry anyone?" (036-213) and accusing her of twisting "everything modern I've ever said — to turn it and twist it and use it against me" (036-220). [[laura-sloane|Laura]] too is drawn into the orbit of modern thought: she "can play with modern ideas and keep her head while she's doing it" (007-071), while Roger fears she catches "vaguely at such ideas" (007-073) without understanding their consequences. [[allan-baird|Allan Baird]], as a doctor, embodies scientific [[work-and-vocation|professionalism]] in a changing world, and [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] is accused of being "nothing but mind and reason" (031-212) — the epitome of the scientific temperament applied to personal life.
