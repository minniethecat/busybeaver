# Work and Vocation

## Overview

Work defines the men and [[womens-roles|women]] of *His [[family|Family]]* as much as family does. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s identity is inseparable from his small business — its fortunes rise and fall with his own sense of worth. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[education|teaching]] is not a job but a calling, the work through which she discovers who she is. [[bruce|Bruce]]'s architecture and [[allan-baird|Allan Baird]]'s medicine are similarly forms of self-definition. The novel is unusual among early 20th-century [[america|American]] fiction in the attention it pays to the texture of work: what people do, how they feel about it, and what it means for their sense of a life well lived. Roger reflects that "here was growth, he had worked at it day and night" (001-089), revealing how thoroughly his sense of self is bound up in his labor.

## Manifestations

Work in the novel takes three forms. As **livelihood**: [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s business — the source of family income — is under constant pressure from a changing economy. The novel notes that "the start she had given him, his work downtown, his income" (005-085) formed the foundation of family life. His anxieties about money are anxieties about work's reliability, about whether honest labor still guarantees a decent life. Roger is warned that he is "working too hard" (001-198), yet he cannot stop — work is inseparable from his sense of purpose.

As **identity**: for [[roger-gale|Roger]], work is who he is. A "self-made" man in the [[america|American]] mold, he measures his worth by his ability to provide. When business falters, his identity falters with it. The novel describes him as a man "who had had to work for it hard" (006-181), investing labor with moral significance. [[bruce|Bruce]] is similarly defined by his work: "Bruce was working at his desk, with an old" (006-111) intensity that marks architecture as more than a profession — it is a form of self-expression.

As **vocation**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[education|teaching]] and [[bruce|Bruce]]'s architecture represent work chosen for its own sake — callings rather than mere jobs. Their work has moral and aesthetic dimensions beyond the economic. Roger "pictured her work that night, her class-room" (002-033), imagining Deborah's teaching with a mixture of pride and bewilderment. Deborah's work is described as something she had "such a good time" (002-027) doing, a fulfillment that transcends mere employment.

The novel is attentive to the gender politics of work. [[roger-gale|Roger]] takes for granted that he will work and his daughters will not. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s decision to have a vocation is thus the novel's most radical gesture — a woman claiming what men have always had: work as identity, not merely as necessity. Yet Roger has "a deep aversion" (002-084) to showing interest in her work, exposing the gender barrier that Deborah must navigate.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: Work as duty and identity. His business is his contribution to the family, and its struggles are his deepest source of [[fear|anxiety]]. "For this kind of work was his life" (006-178) — work is not what Roger does but what he is.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Work as vocation. Her [[education|schools]] are not a job but a calling — the center around which her life is organized. She represents the novel's argument that work can be a form of service and self-discovery.
- **[[bruce|Bruce]]**: Work as art and service. Architecture gives form to [[new-york-city|the city]]; war gives it meaning and then destroys it. His desk scenes convey the intensity of creative labor.
- **[[allan-baird|Allan Baird]]**: Work as social medicine. His medical practice in [[tenement-district|the tenements]] is the male counterpart to [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s educational work.

## The Texture of Labor

What distinguishes Poole's treatment of work is its concreteness. The novel does not merely declare that Roger works hard — it shows him at his desk, in his office, worrying about clients. It does not merely state that Deborah is a teacher — it evokes "her class-room" (002-033) and the daily rhythms of school life. This attention to the material details of work — the desks, the offices, the classrooms, the late nights — gives the novel's meditation on vocation a lived quality that abstract discussions of "the meaning of work" cannot match. Roger's habit of bringing "work home from the office" (001-219) — and Edith's gentle disapproval of it — captures the way work permeates the boundaries between public and private life.
