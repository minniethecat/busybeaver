# The Park

## Overview

The park is the city's green respite — a public space where the classes mix and the pace of life slows. In a novel preoccupied with the transformation of New York, the park represents what endures: trees rather than buildings, open space rather than construction, nature rather than commerce. It is where Edith's children play, where Roger walks, where the city's residents encounter one another outside the hierarchies of business and domestic life.

## Description in the Novel

The park is described through its effect on those who enter it — the quiet, the light, the sense of escape from the "roaring" city. It is one of the older city's surviving features, a place that resists the logic of development and growth. The novel's references to the park are brief but consistent: it is a recurring point of reference for characters seeking fresh air, exercise, or a moment of stillness.

## Scenes and Events

- Family outings to the park — a ritual of leisure
- Roger's solitary walks through its paths
- Children playing — the continuity of life amid urban change
- The park as common ground where social differences are temporarily suspended

## Thematic Significance

The park represents the older city's commitment to public goods — space that belongs to everyone and no one. In a novel about privatization (of family, of wealth, of attention), the park is a reminder that some things are held in common. It is the spatial expression of the novel's democratic impulse.
