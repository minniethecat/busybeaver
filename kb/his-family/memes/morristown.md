## Overview

Morristown, New Jersey, appears in *His Family* as the home of [[madge-deering|Madge Deering]], Edith Sidney's widowed friend. Located outside of [[new-york-city|New York City]], Morristown represents a quieter, more rural alternative to urban life — a place where a widow can raise her daughters in "a simple place" away from the pressures of the city.

## Description in the Novel

Madge Deering "was living out in Morristown" (001-210) when she is first introduced in the narrative. The town is portrayed as a practical and appealing destination — Madge urges the Sidneys to "get that place in Morristown" (004-236), promising that "We'll want you out there" often. It is described approvingly as "a simple place like Morristown" (006-148), the simplicity being a virtue rather than a limitation. The town's distance from the city does not isolate Madge; rather, it anchors her independent life as a mother of four daughters, "out in Morristown with four daughters to bring" (021-053) up.

## Scenes and Events

Morristown is referenced rather than directly depicted in the novel. It serves as a desirable destination — a place of retreat from the city where family life can unfold at a gentler pace. Madge Deering's residence there is a marker of her independence and her choice to raise her daughters outside the urban environment.

## Associated Characters

## Thematic Significance

Morristown embodies the rural alternative to urban life that runs as a subsidiary theme through the novel. Like the [[new-hampshire-farm|New Hampshire farm]], it represents a place of simplicity and retreat — a counterpoint to the crowded, changing streets of New York. For Madge Deering, it is the setting for her independent widowhood and capable motherhood, a place where a woman can manage her own life and raise her daughters on her own terms.
