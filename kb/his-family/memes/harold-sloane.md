# Harold Sloane

## Overview

Harold Sloane is the wealthy young man who marries Laura Gale, taking her from the family's modest middle-class home into a world of European travel, fashionable society, and inherited ease. He enters the novel through Laura's careful introduction — "Do you remember Harold Sloane?" she asks her father "a little strangely" (003-083) — and Roger's immediate suspicion: "What's the matter with Sloane?" (004-048). Harold represents everything Roger is not: unearned wealth, social confidence, a life that does not require struggle.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Harold's courtship of Laura is one of the novel's key plot threads. Roger's initial resistance — he "heartily damned this fellow Sloane for breaking into the family" (003-125) — gives way to grudging acceptance as he realizes he cannot stop the match. Harold is never presented as a villain; he is simply from a different world, one that Roger cannot enter and Laura eagerly embraces.

After the marriage, Harold and Laura travel extensively, and Laura becomes increasingly distant from her family. Harold's wealth enables her escape, but the novel is subtle about whether this is liberation or loss. He is a decent but shallow figure — kind to Laura, polite to Roger, but fundamentally unable to understand the emotional gravity of the family he has helped to disperse.

## Relationships

- **Laura Sloane** (wife): A marriage that represents social mobility for Laura and a desirable prize for Harold. Their compatibility is social rather than deep.
- **Roger Gale** (father-in-law): Mutual incomprehension. Roger sees Harold as a symbol of the forces pulling his family apart; Harold sees Roger as a relic of a passing world.
- **Deborah Gale** (sister-in-law): Barely interacting — they inhabit different moral universes.
