## Overview

America is the backdrop against which the entire narrative of *His Family* unfolds. More than a mere setting, it functions as a thematic presence — a nation in flux during the early twentieth century, absorbing waves of immigration and undergoing rapid social transformation. The novel repeatedly invokes "American" identity as a question its characters must answer for themselves.

## Description in the Novel

The novel opens with a reflection on the changing face of the nation, observing places "where American faces were still to be seen" (001-012), immediately establishing the tension between old-stock identity and the immigrant present. Roger Gale, the protagonist, asks himself pointedly, "was he not an American?" (005-032) — a question that frames his struggle to understand his place in a changing society. The narrative voice traces the forces "reaching away into every phase of American life" (001-091), suggesting the interconnectedness of national character and individual destiny. The novel also depicts America as a destination: "What new recruits to America had brought them here" (005-174), capturing the immigrant experience that reshapes the city and nation Roger inhabits.

## Scenes and Events

America as a concept surfaces throughout the novel rather than in a single location. Roger's reflections on his own American identity recur in moments of personal crisis. The [[the-great-war|Great War]] brings questions of national loyalty and sacrifice to the forefront, as America's role in world affairs becomes a point of contention among the characters. The contrast between America and [[europe|Europe]] — particularly in the experiences of characters who travel abroad — highlights the distinctiveness of the American social experiment.

## Associated Characters

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]** — His identity as an American is a recurring question in his internal monologue (005-032)
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]** — Her social aspirations and European travels embody the American pursuit of cosmopolitan sophistication
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]** — Her reform work engages directly with the American immigrant experience and social justice

## Thematic Significance

America in the novel represents both promise and anxiety. It is the nation built by immigrants — the "new recruits" who came seeking opportunity — and also the nation whose older Anglo-Saxon stock watches that transformation with ambivalence. The tension between these Americas — one rooted in New England tradition, the other in the polyglot energy of the city — mirrors the generational conflicts within the Gale family itself.
