## Overview

Woman suffrage in *His [[family|Family]]* appears as both a specific political cause and a symbol of the broader transformation of [[womens-roles|women]]'s roles in early twentieth-century [[america|America]]. The campaign for the vote runs parallel to the Gale daughters' personal struggles for autonomy. For [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], suffrage is connected to her educational mission; for [[roger-gale|Roger]], it is one more bewildering feature of the modern world his daughters inhabit. The novel embeds the suffrage question within domestic conversations, showing how a national political movement registered in the intimate space of the family.

## Manifestations

The suffrage campaign enters the novel through [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]. "There's the suffrage campaign," she answers (014-249), when [[roger-gale|Roger]] questions her activities. His response is [[nature|character]]istically blunt: "Now what the devil has suffrage to do with your [[education|schools]]?" (014-251). The question reveals [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s inability to connect political rights for [[womens-roles|women]] with his daughter's educational [[work-and-vocation|work]] — he sees separate domains where [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] sees a unified project of [[womens-roles|women]]'s [[progress|advancement]]. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] and her circle also debate a formal "question: 'Resolved that woman suffrage has [[work-and-vocation|work]]ed in'" (015-033), showing that suffrage was not simply accepted but actively debated among the novel's [[nature|character]]s. The narrator notes "suffrage speeches they had made" (008-087), placing [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] among the activist [[womens-roles|women]] who took to public platforms to argue for the vote.
