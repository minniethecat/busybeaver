# Success

## Overview

Success in *His [[family|Family]]* operates as a double-edged value — outwardly celebrated but inwardly questioned. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] built a prosperous business and takes "quiet relish" in his standing (006-181), yet the novel persistently undermines the sufficiency of material achievement alone. Success in business does not guarantee success in [[family|family life]], and the tension between these two domains drives much of the narrative's emotional weight. Poole's treatment is [[nature|character]]istically nuanced: success is real, its rewards are genuine, but its costs are equally real and often borne by others.

## Manifestations

Success appears across three domains, each with its own logic. As **business achievement**: [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] embodies the successful man of affairs. The narrator describes him as "a successful man of affairs" who "had not been spoiled by success" (006-182), [[work-and-vocation|work]]ing harder than ever. His success is a foundation, not a destination — something achieved and then maintained through continuous effort. As **educational impact**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s school project also succeeds — her meeting is called "a huge success" (029-004) — showing that success can take forms beyond the commercial. As **agricultural accomplishment**: [[george-sidney|George]] achieves "a huge success of [[new-hampshire-farm|the farm]]" (037-196), extending the concept across business, education, and agriculture. The novel distributes success across its [[nature|character]]s, refusing to locate it in any single domain or any single person.

Yet success is often paired with [[death|loss]]. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] wins her battle for [[womens-roles|women]]'s rights (018-019) but at the cost of family closeness. The novel suggests that genuine success must reckon with what it displaces — that every achievement has a shadow cost, visible or not, borne by the achiever or by someone else.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: Success as a man of affairs — achieved, maintained, and ultimately questioned.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Success in education — measured not in money but in lives changed.
- **[[george-sidney|George]] Gale**: Success on [[new-hampshire-farm|the farm]] — quieter, less visible, but equally real.
