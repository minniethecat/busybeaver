## Overview

Paris appears in *His Family* as a symbol of European sophistication and cosmopolitan experience. References to the city cluster around two narrative threads: Deborah Gale's recollections of her youthful travels through Europe and Laura Sloane's social aspirations, which draw upon European cultural capital as a marker of status.

## Description in the Novel

The city is mentioned alongside other European capitals as part of Deborah's remembered itinerary: she "lived in London and Paris, Munich and Rome, always in queer old lodgings" (005-105), a description that evokes a bohemian, independent youth far removed from the conventional domesticity expected of women in her era. When Deborah's European past is recounted to the younger generation, it sparks fascination — "'Aunt Deborah's trip to Paris!'" becomes an exclamation of wonder (006-053). The younger characters press for stories: "She has told about Paris lots of" times (006-060), and the simple mention of the name — "Paris." — carries its own evocative weight (006-062).

## Scenes and Events

Paris itself is never depicted directly in the novel; it exists as a remembered and aspired-to place. Deborah's recollections of her time there form part of the backstory that shapes her character as a woman of broader experience than her sisters. For Laura, Paris represents the world of fashion and sophistication to which she aspires — a world her husband [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] can provide access to through wealth and travel.

## Associated Characters

- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]** — Lived in Paris during her youthful European travels (005-105)
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]** — Aspires to the cosmopolitan world Paris represents
- **[[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]]** — His wealth enables the European travel that includes Paris

## Thematic Significance

Paris functions in the novel as a marker of worldliness and independence. For Deborah, it represents the freedom of her youth — a time when she lived outside the constraints of conventional American womanhood. For Laura, it represents the social achievement of European travel, a sign of having arrived in the world of wealth and sophistication that her marriage to Harold has opened. The city thus embodies competing visions of what it means to be cosmopolitan: Deborah's bohemian independence versus Laura's fashionable consumption.
