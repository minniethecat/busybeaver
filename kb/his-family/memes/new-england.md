## Overview

New England serves as the symbolic ancestral ground of the Gale family's American identity. Roger Gale's origins in the region shape his values and his understanding of what it means to be American. Throughout the novel, New England is invoked as a touchstone of tradition, moral seriousness, and the older America that is being transformed by immigration and urbanization.

## Description in the Novel

Roger's connection to New England is described as deeply formative: a voice in the narrative rises "like a voice from the heart of New England where he had been born and" (007-236) raised. The landscape itself has shaped the region's people — "New England had grown very" (014-204) particular in its character, suggesting a regional identity formed by generations of stolid, reserved folk. Roger reflects that he "had been raised in New England" (032-152), and this upbringing informs his responses to the rapid changes he witnesses in [[new-york-city|New York City]].

The novel traces the values of "generations of stolid folk in New England" who "made" (036-196) the regional character — a character of moral earnestness, emotional reserve, and a certain suspicion of novelty. A character pointedly remarks, "that this is still New England. Every new idea we have we wa" (038-069) — the incomplete sentence suggesting the regional instinct to pause, to scrutinize, before embracing change.

## Scenes and Events

New England appears in the novel primarily through Roger's memories and through the values he brings to his family life. The region is not depicted in direct scenes but is a constant presence in Roger's character and worldview.

## Thematic Significance

New England in the novel stands for the older America — the America of the Puritan conscience, of self-reliance and moral seriousness — against which the newer, immigrant America of the cities is measured. Roger's New England heritage is both a source of strength and a limitation: it gives him his moral compass but also makes it difficult for him to embrace the changes sweeping through his family and his city. The tension between New England tradition and urban modernity is one of the novel's central thematic oppositions.
