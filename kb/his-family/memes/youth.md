## Overview

Youth is a central thematic concern of *His [[family|Family]]*, embodied in the younger generation of Gales and their contemporaries. The novel is fundamentally about the transmission of life and values from one generation to the next, and [[childhood|youth]] represents both the promise of renewal and the threat of discontinuity. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s struggle to understand his daughters is, at its core, a struggle with [[childhood|youth]] itself — with its energies, its demands, and its inevitable departure from the patterns of [[memory|the past]]. The novel opens with an image of [[new-york-city|New York]] as a city "of [[business|enterprise]], adventure, [[childhood|youth]]" (001-007), establishing the identification between the young and the transforming city that will run throughout the narrative.

## Role in the Novel

The tension between [[childhood|youth]] and age structures the novel's central conflicts. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s daughters — [[edith-sidney|Edith]], [[laura-sloane|Laura]], and [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] — each represent different [[childhood|youth]] trajectories: domestic tradition, social ambition, and idealistic reform. [[roger-gale|Roger]], rooted in the values of his [[new-england|New England]] upbringing, finds each path troubling in its own way. The young do not merely grow up; they grow *away*, into a world [[roger-gale|Roger]] does not fully recognize.

The novel also depicts [[childhood|youth]] at the margins of the family. [[johnny-geer|Johnny Geer]], [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s student, represents the immigrant [[childhood|youth]] that her school serves — [[childhood|children]] whose futures depend on the educational opportunities the older generation provides. [[elizabeth-sidney|Elizabeth Sidney]] and her brother represent the youngest generation, the grand[[childhood|children]] whose lives extend the family story into an unseen future.

Youth in the novel is [[nature|character]]ized by its questioning of established patterns. The young ask why things must be as they are. They embrace new ideas, new freedoms, new ways of living. For [[roger-gale|Roger]], this is both bewildering and — in his more generous moments — a source of pride. His family, after all, is "His [[family|Family]]," and the energy of its [[childhood|youth]] is part of his legacy.

## Key Manifestations

### The City and the Young

Poole repeatedly associates [[childhood|youth]] with [[new-york-city|New York]] itself. The city is described as a place of "[[business|enterprise]], adventure, [[childhood|youth]]" (001-007), and its relentless energy — "the [[childhood|youth]], the wild" vitality of urban life (030-047) — mirrors the unsettling dynamism of the younger generation. Both [[new-york-city|the city]] and the young are [[nature|character]]ized by forward motion, by the refusal to stay still, by an appetite for the new that dis[[music|concert]]s the old. This parallel between [[modernity|urban transformation]] and generational change is one of the novel's most persistent structural devices.

### Youth as Memory

For [[roger-gale|Roger]], [[childhood|youth]] is also a memory — something he once possessed and has lost. The novel draws repeated contrasts between the [[childhood|youth]] of its older [[nature|character]]s and the [[childhood|youth]] they now observe in their [[childhood|children]]. "They had reminded him of his [[childhood|youth]]" (003-018): the young provoke in [[roger-gale|Roger]] not only [[fear|anxiety]] but nostalgia, a recognition that the gulf between [[generational-change|generations]] is crossed by the common experience of having once been young. This double vision — seeing [[childhood|youth]] from both within and without — gives the novel's treatment of age its particular poignancy.

### The Hungry Army

One of the novel's most striking images describes the young as the "hungry army of [[childhood|youth]]" (004-165), pressing forward with an appetite that is both vital and threatening. The metaphor captures the ambivalence at the heart of the novel's treatment of generational succession: [[childhood|youth]] is life, but it is also replacement. The young do not simply inherit the world; they take it, and in taking it, they push the old aside. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s ultimate [[success|achievement]] is to accept this process not as defeat but as the natural order — the continuation of family across time.

## Thematic Significance

Youth in *His [[family|Family]]* embodies the central paradox of [[motherhood|parenthood]]: to raise [[childhood|children]] is to prepare them for a world you will not inhabit. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s journey is toward accepting this truth — that his [[childhood|children]]'s [[childhood|youth]] is not a rejection of him but the fulfillment of what family means. The novel treats [[childhood|youth]] not sentimentally but as a force of nature, like [[new-york-city|the city]] itself, that transforms everything it touches and cannot be stopped. In the novel's final image — the young "trooping by, singing" (043-092) on a balmy evening — Poole offers [[childhood|youth]] as both elegy and affirmation: the old pass, the young continue, and in their continuation the family endures.
