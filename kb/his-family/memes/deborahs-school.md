# Deborah's School

## Overview

Deborah Gale's school is the most important non-domestic space in *His Family* — a network of classrooms in New York's tenement districts where immigrant children receive an education that is as much about community as about curriculum. Dismissed by Edith as "her latest and most modern fad, to run a school where every child shall sit with a rat in its lap" (002-070), the school is in fact the novel's most tangible image of positive change. It is where Deborah builds what she calls "a family of my own" — a community bound not by blood but by shared purpose.

## Description in the Novel

The school occupies several rooms in a tenement building — "the big school in the tenements" (002-025) — and serves the neighborhood's immigrant families. It is not a charitable institution in the conventional sense; the novel emphasizes its practical, hands-on character. "The principal of her school had made little impression" (002-036) on the educational establishment, but the school's influence extends in unexpected directions: "her school had long curious arms" (002-042) reaching even into the mayor's office.

The school is described with affection and a touch of humor. Deborah's students are "hungry boys" (011-138), and her methods are unconventional — but the novel treats the school as genuinely transformative work, not a rich woman's hobby. It is, in Deborah's framing, her response to the question that haunts the novel: what is a family, and how can it be sustained?

## Thematic Significance

The school is Deborah's alternative to the traditional family that Roger is trying to preserve. Where Roger sees family as blood and home, Deborah sees it as care and community. The school is the physical instantiation of this vision — a place where the boundaries of family are deliberately expanded to include those who need it.
