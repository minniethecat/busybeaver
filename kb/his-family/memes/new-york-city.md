# New York City

> "He was thinking of a young New York, the New York of his own young manhood." (001-003)

## The Young City

[[new-york-city|New York City]] is more than the setting of *His Family* — it is an active force, a character in its own right whose explosive growth parallels and accelerates the dissolution of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s traditional family structure. The novel opens with Roger contemplating not the old New York but "a young New York, the New York of his own young manhood" (001-003), and this pairing of personal and urban biography persists throughout.

Roger arrived in the city at seventeen: "At seventeen he had come to New York. There had followed hard struggle" (001-077). His life is inseparable from the city's growth — both have been shaped by decades of relentless change, and both face an uncertain future as the 20th century accelerates.

## Old and New

The novel draws a sharp distinction between old New York and new. The old city is represented by the Gale family home on the lower west side, by Roger's established business routines, and by the social codes of a mercantile class rooted in the 19th century. The new city is represented by [[downtown-new-york|downtown transformation]], by the immigrant populations flooding into the [[tenement-district|tenement districts]], and by the restless ambition of characters like [[laura-sloane|Laura]] and [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]].

[[edith-sidney|Edith]] feels this division most acutely: she is caught "between old and new. She felt New York" (004-181) — the city is not merely a place she inhabits but a pressure she experiences, a force that makes demands on her identity. The city's expansion is a physical fact — the growth of buildings, the spread of neighborhoods, the constant arrival of new populations — and an emotional one.

## The City as Marketplace

Roger's New York is above all a commercial city. His [[rogers-office|office]], his business partnerships, and his daily rhythms are shaped by the city's economic metabolism. "Neighbors in New York are such cats!" Edith complains (006-078), capturing the atomization that urban commerce produces — the city is efficient but not warm.

Yet the novel also presents New York as a city of builders. [[bruce|Bruce]], the young architect whom [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] refuses to marry, is one of those "who are building New York — actually doing it" (007-096). The city is not merely a backdrop but a project, constantly being remade by the energy of its inhabitants. Roger admires Bruce's constructive energy even as he mistrusts the world it is building.

## Immigrant New York

[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]'s school brings the novel into the immigrant neighborhoods that Roger's generation views from a distance. "Small Jews and Italians" (002-034) populate Deborah's school, and through her work the novel registers the demographic transformation reshaping the city. New York in the 1910s was absorbing the largest wave of immigration in American history, and the novel captures this through the lens of Deborah's expanding sense of family obligation.

Roger's discomfort with this new city is generational. He is "not of old New York" (001-001) — not the Knickerbocker aristocracy — but he is also not of the immigrant city. He occupies a middle ground: the self-made businessman whose world is being eclipsed from both above and below.

## The City as Future

The novel's final vision of New York is ambivalent. The city's growth — "outstripping even the growth of New York" (001-263) — is both magnificent and terrifying. The city that Roger knew as a young man is gone, replaced by something larger, louder, and less legible. But the novel does not condemn this change. It simply records it, through Roger's watchful, weary, ultimately accepting gaze. New York is the future, and the future belongs to the young.
