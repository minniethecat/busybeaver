# Hannah

## Overview

Hannah is the Irish nurse in Edith Sidney's household — a background figure whose presence illuminates the novel's treatment of class, immigration, and domestic service. Described as "the devoted Irish girl" (026-104), she is one of the novel's few working-class characters given individual attention. Her name and nationality are specified; her feelings are registered; her place in the household is debated. She represents the invisible labor that undergirds middle-class family life, and her brief crisis — when Edith considers letting her go — reveals how deeply the family depends on her.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Hannah's most dramatic moment comes early: she "had slapped little Tad" (004-196), and Edith is shocked — she had "never known Hannah to do it before" (004-197). The slap is both a transgression and a humanizing detail: Hannah is not a saint of service but a person with limits. The incident passes, and Hannah remains.

Her deeper role emerges when Edith considers dismissing her. "Let Hannah go? Oh, father!" (026-068) — Edith's horrified response reveals Hannah's importance. Edith has "a talk with Hannah, the devoted Irish girl who had been with her so long" (026-104), and when Hannah is told the change "will not be for long," she "brightened a little" (026-110). The "intricate schedule, with Hannah and school both" (026-114) that organizes the household cannot function without her. Later, Deborah wishes she "could have Hannah, too" (027-187), and Roger himself asks to "ask Hannah to put a cot in my room" (032-025) when he is ill.

## Relationships

- **Edith Sidney**: Employer and the character most invested in keeping Hannah. Their relationship is professional but affectionate — mutual dependence rather than friendship.
- **Tad**: The child Hannah slapped — the moment reveals both her limits and her humanity.
- **Roger Gale**: In his illness, Hannah becomes part of his care, a testament to how the "family" extends to those who serve it.
