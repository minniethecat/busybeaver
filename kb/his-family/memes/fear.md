## Overview

Fear in *His [[family|Family]]* operates on multiple scales: the private anxieties of a father watching [[family|his family]] disperse, and the collective dread of a world plunging into war. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] fears [[loss|losing]] his daughters to the modern world they inhabit; his daughters fear the constraints of the life he represents. The novel traces how private fears are swallowed by the "fearful season" of [[war|the Great War]] (006-215), which reorders all personal anxieties into a new, darker register.

## Manifestations

[[roger-gale|Roger]]'s fears are quiet but persistent. He "had feared trouble there, for he knew" (014-091) that the currents pulling [[family|his family]] apart were beyond his control. When he contemplates what lies ahead, the narrator notes he moves "into what he did not fear" (014-281), a curious formulation suggesting that what he does fear remains unspoken — perhaps the dissolution of the family itself. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] observes to her father that people "are so fearfully wrapt up in each" (016-041), identifying the intensity of [[family|family bonds]] as itself a source of anxiety.

The novel's treatment of fear reaches its broadest scope with [[the-great-war|the war]]: "a brief but fearful season was to be utterly" (006-215) transformative. [[edith-sidney|Edith]] is "fearfully worried" (006-027) about events beyond her control. The narrator notes [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s "fearlessness in facing the ordeal" (001-228) of his wife's death, establishing early that fear and courage are not opposites but companions. [[bruce|Bruce]] is described as being "under fearful tension" (001-242), and the [[home|household]] operates under various "fearful" pressures. Yet there is defiance too: "'No fear!'" (007-302) cries one [[nature|character]], and [[roger-gale|Roger]] "could smile at his former fears" (008-008) when [[laura-sloane|Laura]] proves more resilient than expected.
