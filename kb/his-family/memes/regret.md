## Overview

Regret threads through *His [[family|Family]]* as a quiet but persistent note, surfacing at moments when characters confront the gap between what they chose and what might have been. Rather than melodramatic remorse, the novel presents regret as the natural shadow of a life fully lived — a signal that choices have mattered and consequences have been real.

## Manifestations

[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] dismisses regrets "with only a slight frown and a movement of his heavy shoulders" (001-109), yet the very mention reveals their presence. When [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] leaves home, a "note of keen regret" (007-258) enters her departure. The old [[home|house]] itself, sold and left behind, brings [[roger-gale|Roger]] "a pang of regret" (008-006), though these feelings "soon died away" (008-014). John expresses "keen regret" (022-084) over the changing family dynamics, and lets out "a little sigh of regret" (022-110). [[edith-sidney|Edith]] feels "affection and regret" (026-014) looking at her father's aging face. Roger glances at John the [[courage|cripple]] and feels "a little pang of regret" (037-041), wondering what will become of him when the business closes. The pattern is clear: regret in *His [[family|Family]]* is inseparable from [[love-and-courtship|love]] — one cannot care deeply about people and places without occasionally [[grief|mourning]] [[loss|what is lost]] to time.

## Remorse and Parental Failure

Beyond fleeting regret, the novel probes deeper forms of self-reproach. When [[laura-gale|Laura]] announces her marriage, [[roger-gale|Roger]] feels "a rush of tenderness and a pang of deep remorse" (003-092) — not guilt over anything he has done, but over what he has not: "What did he know of the affair? What had he ever done for her? Too late! He had begun too late!" (003-093–094). This is remorse born of parental [[duty|neglect]], the sudden realization that his daughter's life has passed beyond his reach. Later, after [[edith-sidney|Edith]] has been sobbing upstairs following her husband's [[death]], "remorse came over him" (028-095–096), as Roger recognizes how his sympathies had been unfairly tilted toward [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s grand vision and away from Edith's quieter struggles. [[scn28-remorse|Remorse]] in these scenes functions less as moral condemnation than as a corrective — an emotional [[change|recalibration]] that restores proper perspective on what each daughter has endured.

## Regret and Forward Motion

Significantly, Poole's characters do not dwell in regret. [[roger-gale|Roger]] "dismissed regrets like these with only a slight reluctance" (001-109); after the house sale brings a pang of regret, those feelings "soon died away" (008-014). [[edith-sidney|Edith]], looking back on years devoted to her children, declares, "Not that I regret it, but — well, we're going to blossom out" (019-026–027). The novel treats regret as a passing sensation, not a permanent state — a brief backward glance before characters resume moving forward. Even the pang of regret Roger feels for John (037-041) is immediately followed by his reversing course: the house is not sold, John is brought into the firm, and the family's fortunes pivot toward renewal. Regret in *His [[family|Family]]* is the [[nature|psychological]] cost of action and inaction alike, but it is never the last word.
