## Overview

Loneliness in *His [[family|Family]]* is rarely about physical solitude. Instead, it emerges most sharply within the family circle — a parent surrounded by [[childhood|children]] who no longer need him, a daughter isolated by her convictions, a wife separated by [[death|grief]]. The novel portrays loneliness as a paradox of intimacy: those closest to us can be the ones we feel most distant from.

## Roger Gale: The Loneliness of the Patriarch

[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s loneliness is the novel's most sustained exploration of isolation. It begins with his wife Judith's [[death|dying]] — he feels "himself terribly alone, with eternity around him, his wife slipping far" (001-141). In the years that follow, he endures "nights by the thousand spent alone" (001-148), a solitude that becomes "a gray level road in his life" (001-148). The novel's narrator describes "those bleak lonely years" (005-024) after Judith's death, framing Roger's widowerhood as a prolonged isolation.

Yet Roger's loneliness persists even as his household fills with people. Amid the crises of managing his daughters' lives, he experiences "a pang of utter loneliness amid these vexing problems" (007-116) — a loneliness that strikes not in an empty [[home|house]] but precisely when he is most surrounded by [[family|family]] obligations. The routine isolation is at times wrenchingly mundane: after losing his temper with his daughters, Roger "went down and ate his breakfast alone" (007-269). A later passage captures the domestic vacuum after Laura's marriage, as Deborah asks him "you aren't going to find it too lonely here, with Laura gone?" (007-360).

Roger's loneliness intensifies with [[aging|age]]. Walking the streets of New York after a confrontation with his daughters, "he felt desperately alone" (003-179), aware that while his children were "dancing" (003-179) to the rhythms of a new generation, he belonged to a world that was passing. Even in the countryside he "spent much of his time alone" (014-142), taking long solitary rides. His loneliness is existential: the town itself "seemed small and lonely, a little glow in the infinite dark" (029-161), reflecting his own sense of diminishment against the vastness of [[time|time]].

## Deborah Gale: Independence and Its Costs

[[deborah-gale|Deborah]] embodies a different register of loneliness — one rooted in the cost of her fierce independence. She admits candidly, "I see — and so I'm lonely for a family of my own. But you're forgetting my school" (009-020), revealing that her professional calling substitutes for, but does not fully replace, the family life she sees others building. In a moment of vulnerability she confides, "I'm afraid of being alone to-night" (009-027), acknowledging that her self-sufficiency has limits.

Deborah's loneliness is intertwined with struggle. Behind her "beaming smile" the narrator reveals Roger "would feel the pain and loneliness, and the angry grit which was fighting it down" (015-101). Her isolation is combative rather than passive — she fights it rather than succumbing. Yet Roger observes that even Deborah, for all her strength, can be "lonely, as though she were feeling her age" (016-175). As she ages, she confronts the loneliness of the unmarried life: "on the other [side], a lonely life which will soon grow old" (036-193). In the novel's final chapters, Roger peers into Deborah's future and "thought he saw a lonely old age, childless, loveless, cynical" (043-159), a vision that haunts him even as he tries to reassure himself that "whatever happened" (043-160) she would find meaning.

## Edith Sidney: Grief and Domestic Isolation

[[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s loneliness is specifically maternal — the isolation of a woman who has lost a child. After the [[death|death]] of her daughter, "darkness" settles "over her spirit, together with loneliness and fatigue" (026-174). Her loneliness is so profound that [[roger-gale|Roger]] contemplates practical interventions to alleviate "her loneliness. How about reading aloud to her?" (028-108). The suggestion of reading aloud — an act of shared presence rather than conversation — underscores how deeply Edith has retreated into her grief, unable to reach across the void to those who remain.

Edith's isolation is compounded by the [[home|domestic]] sphere itself. After her loss, she is absorbed into the family household, yet her grief isolates her within it. The "rooms were empty and still" (037-118) — the physical house mirrors the emotional vacancy at its center.

## Existential and Generational Loneliness

Beyond individual characters, the novel explores loneliness as a condition of [[modernity]]. The young express a specifically modern form of isolation: "You feel too free. And you feel scared and lonely. You look up at the stars. There are millions. You are only a speck of dust" (011-225). This passage articulates a loneliness born not of loss but of existential freedom — the vertigo of a universe emptied of traditional meaning.

The generation gap produces its own isolation. Roger, confronting the "city of his children's lives" (007-117), finds himself a stranger in the very world his offspring inhabit. The old man "left alone and scared to death lest he should be 'put away'" (011-118) represents a universal fear — the dread of being discarded by the generation one has raised. In the novel's closing vision, a "stark lonely figure, a woman in a graveyard cutting the grass on family graves" (043-177) serves as the ultimate image of generational loneliness: the survivor tending to a [[family]] that exists now only in memory.
