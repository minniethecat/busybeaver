# Roger Gale's Evening Walks

## Overview

Roger Gale's evening walks are the novel's most persistent motif — a recurring event that, taken together, form a ritual of observation and reflection. "It was time to go home" (001-193) — but before he goes home, Roger walks, and in walking he sees the city transforming around him. These walks are the narrative's way of registering the passage of time and the pressure of change: each walk reveals another building replaced, another landmark lost, another block of the old New York consumed by the new.

## Context and Build-up

Roger walks because the city draws him — it is where he built his life, and it is where he can think. His walks are solitary, after work, before the family dinner or the club. They are a habit grounded in his character: the man who "discovered, even in those early years" the pleasures of physical exertion in the New Hampshire mountains (001-074) now walks the city streets with the same absorbed attention.

## The Event

Each walk is a small event, but cumulatively they form the novel's most sustained meditation on change. Roger notices what has been torn down and what has risen in its place. He registers the new populations — immigrants, the wealthy, the workers — who are reshaping the city's character. The walks are the novel's documentary impulse: through Roger's eyes, we see the city that is being lost and the city that is being built.

## Consequences

The walks deepen Roger's alienation from the world his daughters inhabit. He walks through a city that increasingly belongs to them — to Deborah's tenement districts, to Laura's fashionable neighborhoods — and his status as observer rather than participant grows more pronounced as the novel proceeds.

## Thematic Significance

The walks embody the novel's method: change is registered through observation, not declaration. Roger does not announce that the city is changing; he walks and sees it.
