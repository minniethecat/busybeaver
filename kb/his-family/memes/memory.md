## Overview

Memory in *His [[family|Family]]* is the mechanism by which the past remains alive in the present. For [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], memory is not passive recollection but an active, sometimes painful, connection to what has been lost — above all, to his late wife [[judith-gale|Judith]]. The novel opens with [[roger-gale|Roger]] reflecting that "memory now was the story of [[family|his family]]" (001-149), establishing memory as both the narrative frame and the emotional core of the novel. Memory bridges the gap between the living and the dead, and between [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s generation and his [[childhood|children]]'s.

## Manifestations

The novel's opening chapter establishes memory as its organizing principle. [[roger-gale|Roger]] sits thinking, "memory stirring in his mind" (001-291), and "groped for the memory she had stirred" (001-306) — the "she" being [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], whose presence triggers a flood of recollections about the family's past. When listening to his daughter's voice, "often as he listened the memory came of her voice as a girl" (002-008), collapsing the distance between the adult daughter before him and the child she once was.

[[music|Music]] becomes a vehicle for memory when "over this memory of a song rose now the surging music of Tschaikovsky's" (004-128), layering personal recollection over present aesthetic experience. The deepest statement of memory's significance comes from [[roger-gale|Roger]] himself: "This is my memory living on in you, my" (014-284) — spoken to one of his daughters, framing memory as inheritance, as the part of a parent that survives in the child. In quieter moments, [[roger-gale|Roger]] "recalled a memory of a dusk far back in his boy[hood]" (014-294), and "from far back in his childhood a memory flashed into his mind" (025-088), showing how memory reaches beyond the family story into the deepest layers of personal identity.
