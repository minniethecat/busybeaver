# Progress

## Overview

Progress in *His [[family|Family]]* is not a simple good. The novel, set in the early twentieth century, charts the advance of modernity through [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[reform|school reform]]s, [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s changing [[new-york-city|New York]], and even the advance of disease. Poole presents [[modernity|progress]] as a force that cannot be stopped, only reckoned with. Unlike the triumphant narratives of [[america|American]] [[modernity|progress]] that dominated the era's public rhetoric, the novel's treatment is deeply ambivalent: [[modernity|progress]] builds and destroys, liberates and disorients, heals and kills.

## Manifestations

The novel deploys the language of [[modernity|progress]] across three registers, each complicating the others. As **inexorable [[death|mortality]]**: the novel opens with a reference to "the slow but inexorable [[modernity|progress]]" (001-124) of [[judith-gale|Judith Gale]]'s fatal ailment — immediately associating [[modernity|progress]] with [[death|loss]] rather than improvement. This framing sets the tone for everything that follows. As **social improvement**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s school embodies "[[modernity|progress]]ive elements" but faces constant struggle against "confusions" (039-125). The school's [[work-and-vocation|work]] is real but fragile — [[modernity|progress]] that must be defended daily against institutional inertia and social indifference. As **personal decline**: [[roger-gale|Roger]], aging, "seldom did he notice the [[modernity|progress]] of his" own condition (039-113). At its close, the novel describes [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s disease as "[[modernity|progress]]ing fast" (043-004), linking [[modernity|progress]] inexorably to [[death|mortality]].

The verbal echo across these instances — "[[modernity|progress]]" applied alike to [[reform|social reform]], personal aging, and fatal disease — is Poole's most pointed commentary. Progress is not one thing; it is simply the direction time moves. Whether that movement is good or bad depends on what is [[modernity|progress]]ing and for whom.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Progress as [[work-and-vocation|vocation]] — her school is the novel's most concrete instance of [[modernity|progress]] as improvement.
- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: Progress as [[death|loss]] — what advances in his lifetime is also what takes everything away.
- **[[allan-baird|Allan Baird]]**: Medical [[modernity|progress]], with its promises and its limits, embodied in a [[medicine|doctor]] who sees both.
