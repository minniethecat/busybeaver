# Duty

## Overview

Duty is the moral gravity that shapes [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s life. As a widower with three daughters, a small businessman in a changing city, and a man of [[tradition|traditional values]] confronting modernity, [[roger-gale|Roger]] navigates the world through a frame[[work-and-vocation|work]] of obligation — to his [[childhood|children]], to the memory of his wife [[judith-gale|Judith]], and to his own sense of what a father should be. "It's your duty to talk to her," [[edith-sidney|Edith]] insists, invoking the word that organizes their shared moral vocabulary (007-110). [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s reply reveals both his adherence to and his resentment of this frame[[work-and-vocation|work]]: "I know my duties without being told" (007-111).

## Manifestations

Duty operates in three spheres in the novel. As **parental obligation**: [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s sense that he must guide — and sometimes control — his daughters' choices, from [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s marriage to [[laura-sloane|Laura]]'s social life to [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s un[[tradition|convention]]al school. As **marital fidelity**: the living presence of [[judith-gale|Judith]]'s wishes, which [[roger-gale|Roger]] and the daughters invoke to settle arguments and justify decisions. As **social responsibility**: most evident in [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s commitment to the poor [[childhood|children]] of [[tenement-district|the immigrant quarter]], which represents a new kind of duty — chosen rather than inherited, a "duty in life" that becomes "your happiness" (018-044).

The novel traces a [[generational-change|generational shift]] in how duty is understood. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s duty is backward-looking — it honors what was, what [[judith-gale|Judith]] would have [[poverty|want]]ed. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s duty is forward-looking — it builds new institutions for those who have nothing. Between them, [[edith-sidney|Edith]] represents duty as social [[tradition|convention]]: the obligation to marry well and preserve appearances.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]** feels duty most keenly and is most constrained by it. His internal conflicts — whether to intervene in his daughters' lives, whether to accept or resist change — are framed as questions of duty.
- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]** is the novel's chief enforcer of duty-as-[[tradition|convention]]. "It's your duty, it seems to me," she tells her father (007-053), wielding the word as social authority.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]** represents duty transformed into [[work-and-vocation|vocation]]. Her school is a "duty in life" freely chosen, not inherited — a model of obligation that [[roger-gale|Roger]] eventually comes to admire.
