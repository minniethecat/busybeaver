## Overview

Sarah is a domestic servant employed in the Gale household. Though she appears only briefly in the novel, her presence reflects the reality of middle-class domestic life in early twentieth-century New York — where servants were an integral but largely invisible part of the household. She is addressed directly by family members and carries out the routine tasks that sustain the family's daily life.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Sarah appears in several domestic scenes that illustrate her role within the Gale household. She is addressed by name in the course of household management: "Oh, Sarah," [[edith-sidney|Edith]] says while directing household affairs (003-038). Her duties bring her into contact with various family members, and she receives instructions directly — as when [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] says to her, "You stay" (012-071), adding her to the small group present during a family moment.

Sarah's role in the novel is emblematic rather than individual. Like [[hannah|Hannah]], another servant in the household, she represents the working-class presence that supports the Gale family's domestic life. She receives orders, carries out tasks, and remains in the background of the family's story — visible enough to be named, but not developed as a character with her own arc or perspective. Her presence serves as a reminder of the class structure within which the novel's central family drama unfolds.

## Relationships

Sarah's primary relationship is with her employers in [[the-gale-home|the Gale household]]. She interacts with [[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]], who directs the domestic affairs of the home (003-038), and with [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], who addresses her directly on at least one occasion (012-071). Her position parallels that of [[hannah|Hannah]], another servant in the household, and together they represent the domestic working class that made possible the comfortable middle-class life the Gales enjoyed.
