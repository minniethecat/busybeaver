## Overview

Gossip in *His Family* operates as a quiet but pervasive force — the unwritten social text that characters read, contribute to, and attempt to manage. It circulates through families, neighborhoods, and professional circles, carrying judgment and shaping reputation. In a novel where so much goes unsaid, what is said *about* people matters deeply. Poole treats talk not as mere background noise but as a structural element of the novel's social world — the medium through which community is both built and policed.

## Gossip as Social Bond and Weapon

In the rural respite of the New Hampshire farm, gossip serves as a benign connective tissue. A character asks for the news and receives "the gossip" (013-017) — a harmless exchange that binds community through shared knowledge. On the farm, "they cooked and sewed and gossiped" (015-032), the three verbs placed in easy parallel, as if gossip were as natural and necessary as cooking and sewing. But gossip also carries a harder edge in the urban sphere. Roger Gale, as a lawyer, is intimately familiar with scandal: "he knew about scandal, he handled it in his office each day" (032-110). The rumor that threatens a client's reputation is not idle talk but a destructive force that can unravel lives.

The Gale household itself is a theater of talk. The sisters are described as "gossiping in Deborah's room or in her own dainty chamber chatting" (003-030) — the easy slippage between "gossiping" and "chatting" suggesting how thin the line is between harmless conversation and the circulation of judgments. Poole's characters are "chattering like magpies in the genial old house" (001-113), the bird metaphor capturing both the energy and the mindlessness of domestic talk.

## The Desire for Quiet

Characters in the novel repeatedly express a wish for discretion. "They both want it quiet, without any scandal" (032-109) — the line captures a fundamental tension between the private desire to be left alone and the public reality of being talked about. A professional reputation, built "little by little" (029-093), can be undone by talk. Deborah's school generates its own ecosystem of talk: Roger "heard the daily gossip of that" (039-121), observing the hum of conversation that surrounds his daughter's institution. Even institutions built on ideals are not immune to the murmur of opinion.

## Whisper and Silence

The novel is full of whispers — "he heard whispers, eager voices" (007-280) — that register the presence of talk too delicate or too dangerous to be spoken aloud. At Christmas, there are "conspiracies which were whispered about" (030-080) the house, the word "conspiracies" lending a playful but pointed weight to the secret conversations that structure family life. In a household where direct communication often fails, whispers become the alternative channel — the way information travels when no one will say things plainly. Gossip, in this light, is not merely a social force but an emotional one: the sound of a family trying to understand itself without quite daring to speak.

## Gossip and the Gale Family

Within the family, gossip takes the form of observation and commentary — the sisters discussing each other's choices, the father quietly noting the shifts in his household. The novel is suffused with the awareness that family life is conducted before an audience, that every decision is subject to the scrutiny of those who claim to love you best. In a family where silence often prevails, gossip fills the spaces that direct communication leaves empty.
