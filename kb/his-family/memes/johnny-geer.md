# Johnny Geer

## Overview

Johnny Geer is one of Deborah Gale's students — a "cripple boy" (015-059) from her tenement school who enters Roger Gale's life and becomes, in Deborah's words, part of her "family." He is "the hungriest of them all — hungry to get on in the world" (011-138), and his trajectory from tenement schoolboy to Roger's office worker and trusted companion embodies the novel's vision of social mobility through education and personal kindness. He is also the character through whom Roger begins to understand what Deborah means when she speaks of her "family of her own" — a community bound not by blood but by care.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Johnny first appears through Deborah's school, one of the "hungry boys" for whom she builds her classes (011-138). He is marked by his physical disability — the novel consistently refers to him as "the cripple" or "the cripple boy" — but his disability is never treated as a limitation on his ambition or his worth. Roger first remembers "Johnny Geer, the cripple boy whom he had liked" (012-035), and this initial liking deepens into genuine affection.

When Roger falls ill, Johnny becomes "[h]is most frequent visitor" (015-059), working in Roger's office by day and spending his free hours with the recovering man. Deborah proposes that Johnny come stay and "use one of our empty rooms" (015-132), further integrating him into the household. By the novel's later chapters, Johnny is "back in Roger's office" and described as "a great help" (022-043). Roger's gruff affection — "Hello, Johnny. How are you?" (022-051) — and his concern for Johnny's health — "I want you to take a good solid month off" (022-122) — reveal how thoroughly the boy has become family.

## Relationships

- **Deborah Gale**: Teacher, mentor, and the person who first brought Johnny into the Gale orbit. She considers him "one of _her_ family" (028-174), and the possessive emphasis on "her" underscores how seriously she takes the claim.
- **Roger Gale**: The most significant relationship. Roger's acceptance of Johnny — from initial liking to protective concern — parallels his gradual acceptance of Deborah's broader definition of family. Johnny is the bridge: through him, Roger experiences firsthand what Deborah means when she says she has "a family of my own."
