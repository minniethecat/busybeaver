# Ambition

## Overview

**Ambition** in *His [[family|Family]]* is portrayed as a natural and powerful force in [[youth|the young]]er generation, one that both impresses and unsettles the older [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]. The novel traces how personal ambitions reshape family relationships, pulling [[childhood|children]] away from traditional roles into new forms of public and [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al life.

## Manifestations

Ambition appears most vividly in the [[nature|character]] of [[george-sidney|George]], [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s son, whose "great ambition" is shared in intimate conversation: "together about his great ambition. He [[poverty|want]]ed to enter the Fire Department" (009-039). The word *great* signals the seriousness with which the novel treats [[childhood|youth]]ful aspiration — ambition is not a passing fancy but a defining drive. The concept extends beyond individual goals to encompass "their intimate dreams and ambitions" (027-084), suggesting a shared inner landscape where personal [[dream|hope]]s shape identity. By the novel's later chapters, ambition has become woven into the fabric of institutional life — "its varied personalities, ambitions and conspiracies" (039-124) — showing how individual drive scales into organizational culture. Throughout, the novel treats ambition as a force of modernity, pulling [[youth|the young]]er Gales away from their father's world and into their own.

Ambition is not confined to a single generation or social station. The young tenement boy [[john]] confides to Roger, in a moment of striking vulnerability amid the city's rushing turmoil, "I want to be an architect" (015-112). His declaration — offered while gazing up at "a mighty tower" (015-107) — captures ambition as an upward-looking force, a refusal to accept one's present station. The novel places this aspiration alongside the more privileged ambitions of the Gale [[children]], suggesting that ambition cuts across class lines as a universal human drive. Similarly, [[george-sidney|George]] later pivots from the Fire Department to agricultural ambition, confiding to Roger "that he meant to be a farmer. He wanted to go in the autumn to the State Agricultural College" (023-093). George "was planning out his career" and "wrestling with a book entitled 'Our New Mother Earth'" (023-091, 023-092), embodying ambition as an iterative, evolving process — one that adapts as the young discover new possibilities.

## Ambition and the Generational Divide

The novel positions ambition as a fault line between generations. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], an old man rooted in the [[new-york-city|New York]] of his youth, finds himself alternately admiring and bewildered by his children's drive. When [[edith-sidney|Edith]] dismisses George's farming plans as "just a stage he's going through" (023-097) and Elizabeth's aspiration of "becoming a doctor" as a "crazy idea" (023-098, 023-099), Roger erupts in [[defense]] of the young: "They won't get over it! Their dreams are parts of something new! Something I'm quite vague about — but some of it has come to stay! You're losing all your chances — just as I did years ago! You'll never know" (023-101, 023-102, 023-103). Roger's passionate outburst transforms him from an observer of ambition into its unexpected champion. His confession — "just as I did years ago" — reveals that he, too, once harbored dreams he failed to pursue, casting his defense of his [[grandchildren]]'s ambitions as both vicarious redemption and a warning to Edith.

Roger's recognition of ambition in the young is repeatedly tinged with [[nostalgia]] and [[loss]]. Watching his grandchildren, he observes how "their young minds had turned happily back into themselves, into their own engrossing lives, their plans, their intimate dreams and ambitions" (027-083, 027-084). The word "engrossing" emphasizes the self-contained world that each child carries, sealed off from adult comprehension — ambition as an interior sanctuary. Roger's own creative drive, by contrast, lives on only indirectly through his daughter: "And now here was Deborah fulfilling a part of him in herself" (009-132). Where the young pursue their ambitions openly, Roger's have long since gone underground, surfacing only in vicarious form.

## Public vs. Private Ambition

The novel distinguishes between two modes of ambition. The first is private and individual — [[george-sidney|George]]'s desire to join the Fire Department, [[john]]'s architectural [[dream]], Elizabeth's wish to become a [[medicine|doctor]]. The second is public and institutional, exemplified by [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] and her school. Deborah's ambition is not for herself but for a cause: the education and uplift of [[tenement-district|the tenement]] children. Through [[deborahs-school|the school]], ambition scales from the personal to the systemic. [[roger-gale|Roger]] comes to perceive the school as "a world in itself, with its politics, its many jarring factions, its jealousies, dissensions, its varied personalities, ambitions and conspiracies" (039-122, 039-123, 039-124). Here, ambition is no longer a solitary flame but a contested social force — "jarring factions" competing for influence within an institution that serves "everybody's children" (039-138).

This public ambition is at once more potent and more consuming than its private counterpart. Deborah's work immerses her in "the savage struggle for life; and there were many climaxes of dreams and aspirations, of loves and bitter jealousies" (041-023, 041-024). Ambition in this context merges with [[duty]]: it is not what one wants for oneself but what one owes to the [[community]]. Through Deborah, the novel argues that the highest form of ambition is the one that outlasts the individual: "work, dreams and aspirations. How bright it seemed" (043-204). Even on his deathbed, [[roger-gale|Roger]] glimpses this continuity of ambition across generations — his daughter's work carrying forward into the lives of children yet unborn.

## Ambition as Life-Force

Throughout the novel, ambition is consistently linked to vitality and renewal. In the [[new-york-city|city]]'s [[tenement-district|tenement]] streets, Roger perceives "the vital throbbing of a tremendous joyousness, of gaiety, fresh hopes and dreams, of leaping young emotions like deep buried bubbling springs bursting up resistlessly to renew the fevered life of the town" (010-136, 010-137, 010-138, 010-139). Ambition is not merely intellectual — it is a physical, "leaping" and "bubbling" force that "renews" the urban world. The metaphor of underground springs suggests that ambition is both natural and inexhaustible, welling up wherever [[youth]] and [[community]] converge.

This life-force persists beyond individual achievement. In his final moments, Roger sees "children, lives of children; work, dreams and aspirations" (043-204) merging into a single vision. The novel's closing line — "And his dreams were of children" (044-001) — enlarges the concept: ambition is no longer the property of any one person but the inheritance of the [[family]] as a whole, passed from [[parent]] to child across [[generational-change|generations]] as the engine of human continuity.
