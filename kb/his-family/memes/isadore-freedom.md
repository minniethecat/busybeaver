## Identity

Isadore Freedom is a minor but symbolically potent character in *His Family*, introduced in Chapter XI as a young Russian-Polish immigrant who has embraced the promise of American freedom. He came from Russian Poland and, upon arriving in [[new-york-city|New York]], legally changed his name to "Freedom" because it was "the first American word he learned over there" (011-148)--(011-149). The name change cost him seven dollars out of the nine he possessed (011-149)--(011-151). Deborah introduces him with deep admiration: "Isadore Freedom," said Deborah, "is the beginning of something tremendous" (011-147).

## Character and Physical Description

At approximately twenty-one years old, Isadore is described as "hardly more than a boy," short of frame but large of limb, with wide stooping shoulders, reddish hollows in his dark cheeks, and an "enormous smile" (011-168)--(011-172). There is "a springiness in his step, vigor and warmth in the grip of his hand" that impresses [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] upon their first meeting (011-170)--(011-172).

Deborah describes him vividly: "Isadore is a flame, a kind of a torch" (011-151). This metaphor captures his intense, almost consuming dedication to learning and to the ideal of freedom itself. When he speaks of his library's books, his enthusiasm is unbounded: "They read all, all! Look at this Darwin on my desk" (011-181), he cries, showing visitors the collection with proprietorial pride. Yet there is also vulnerability beneath his fervor; when the visit ends, "do," Isadore ended huskily (011-233), overcome with emotion. The narrator notes a "suspicious brightness" in Isadore's eyes as he looks at Deborah happily (011-235).

## Education and Vocation

Isadore's story is one of self-improvement through education. He began working in a sweatshop while attending [[deborahs-school|Deborah's school]] five nights a week. After classes ended at ten, he would go to a basement cafe, drink one glass of Russian tea, and study until closing at one in the morning. He slept, as a rule, four hours a night (011-154)--(011-159). This punishing schedule -- work, school, and study compressing sleep to the barest minimum -- speaks to an almost monastic devotion to self-cultivation.

By the time he appears in the novel, Isadore has become a librarian earning fifteen dollars a week. Having "all the money he needs," he has dedicated his life to "education in freedom" (011-159)--(011-162). His trajectory from sweatshop worker to librarian is both a personal triumph and a testament to Deborah's educational mission.

## Meeting Roger

When [[roger-gale|Roger]] and [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] visit Isadore at his small public library on a ghetto street, Isadore leaped up from his desk and came toward them with outstretched hands (011-166)--(011-167). He shows Roger the library's collection with pride, and as they walk through the streets afterward, the narrator records how "joyous cries of greeting met Isadore from every side" (011-202) -- he is clearly a figure of warmth and importance in his community.

After Isadore has left them, on the street outside, "Deborah turned" to Roger, her expression conveying the depth of her feeling for what Isadore represents (011-239). Roger later reflects on the encounter. He was glad he had met that boy, and so he felt toward Deborah's whole terrific family. Confused and deafening as it was, there was something inspiring in it all (012-003)--(012-005).

Yet Roger also reveals his own ambivalence and limits. Privately, he speculates that in five years Isadore "will either be a great man or in jail" (012-018), dismissing his idealism as "wild oats" (012-021). He wonders "what a gem could be made of Isadore with a little careful polishing" (012-012) -- a patronizing thought that reveals the gap between Roger's generation and the raw, unpolished energy of the new America that Isadore embodies.

## Symbolic Significance

Isadore Freedom is more than a character; he is an embodiment of the novel's deepest themes. His name is a declaration, his life a demonstration. Through him, Poole explores what "freedom" means in practice -- not merely the absence of oppression, but an active, lifelong commitment to growth, learning, and community. His four hours of sleep, his seven-dollar name change, his Darwin on the desk -- each detail builds the portrait of a man for whom freedom is not a birthright but a daily discipline.

In a novel structured around the question of what the older generation passes on to the younger, Isadore is the counterpoint to the Gale children. He has inherited nothing -- no wealth, no name, no social position -- and yet he has built a life of meaning through sheer force of will. He is the beginning of something tremendous, as Deborah says, and the novel leaves open the question of what that something will become.

## Themes

Isadore Freedom embodies the novel's themes of immigration, the American dream, and education as a path to liberation. His character represents the transformative power of [[deborahs-school|Deborah's educational mission]] and serves as a living example of the ideals she is working to realize. Through him, the novel explores what "freedom" means in practice -- not just a name, but a lifelong commitment. He also serves as a test of Roger's capacity for understanding: can the old New Englander truly appreciate the new American that Isadore represents?
