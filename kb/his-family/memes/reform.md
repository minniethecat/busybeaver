# Reform

## Overview

Reform in *His [[family|Family]]* is embodied most fully in [[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]'s school — an institution that represents the [[modernity|progressive]] conviction that social problems can be solved through organized effort. The novel distinguishes between reckless reform and practical action: [[roger-gale|Roger]] "had no use for wild reforms, but here was something solid, a good education for every child" (009-118). This careful endorsement of reform — skeptical of grand schemes but committed to concrete improvement — reflects the novel's broader attitude toward social change: [[dream|hopeful]] but measured, aware of the "heavy complexity of it all" (010-091) that reformers must navigate.

The novel situates reform within the context of a rapidly changing America. The very city around the characters pulses with transformation: "silence could be felt the throes of change; the very atmosphere seemed charged with drastic things impending" (001-293). Reform is not presented as an ideological abstraction but as a necessary response to the pressures of [[immigration|immigration]], urbanization, and mass education — the forces that [[roger-gale|Roger]] confronts when he realizes that the public [[education|schools]] alone contain eight hundred thousand children (010-119).

## Manifestations

Reform operates through two principal channels in the novel.

As **educational reform**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s night school is the novel's central reform project — a direct, practical effort to improve [[childhood|children]]'s lives through education. The school is not theoretical; it is described through its daily operations, its curriculum, and its effects on individual students. Reform here is person-to-person [[work-and-vocation|work]], not abstract policy.

As **institutional reform**: the novel traces the "ramifications here which wound out through the police and the [[law|courts]] to reformatories, distant cells" (010-089) — the net[[work-and-vocation|work]] of institutions that shape lives in ways reformers can only partially affect.

The school itself is a "strange astounding" (010-102) institution with "long curious arms" (002-042) that reach from Carnegie Hall to the mayor's private office. Its scope is immense: Deborah battles "rags and filth and garbage, with vermin, darkness and disease" (010-111), while the city grows relentlessly — "the people of [[tenement-district|the tenements]] breeding, breeding, breeding, and packing the schools to bursting" (010-122). Faced with this scale, [[roger-gale|Roger]] despairs that "what could any teacher be but a mere cog in a machine, ponderous, impersonal, blind, grinding out future New Yorkers?" (010-124). Yet the novel resists this despair: [[roger-gale|Roger]] is ultimately "disturbed and thrilled" by the "vital throbbing of a tremendous joyousness, of gaiety, fresh hopes and dreams" (010-136–137) that pulses through [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s work.

[[roger-gale|Roger]]'s description of [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] as "a radical school reformer" who is also "the most conservative woman I know" (017-191) captures the novel's nuanced view of reform: it is at once radical in its ambition and conservative in its methods, grounded in the concrete reality of [[childhood|children]]'s lives rather than ideological abstraction. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] is "still but a pioneer in a jungle, endlessly groping and trying new things" (010-117) — reform, in this vision, is never finished.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Reform as [[work-and-vocation|vocation]]. Her [[education|schools]] are practical, hands-on projects that improve lives one child at a time. She is both "radical" and "conservative" — radical in her commitment to change, conservative in her methods of patient attention.
- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: The skeptical observer who comes to respect reform when it is grounded in real [[work-and-vocation|work]] rather than rhetoric. He himself "had had his feeling for humanity in the large" (009-121), having "run a boys' club at a little mission school" (009-123) — so his endorsement of [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s work carries the weight of shared experience, not just paternal pride.
- **[[allan-baird|Allan Baird]]**: Medical reform through his [[work-and-vocation|work]] in [[tenement-district|the tenements]] — the counterpart to [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s educational reform. [[allan-baird|Baird]]'s hospital plans represent reform institutionalized at scale.
- **[[laura-gale|Laura Gale]]**: The fashionable radical. Her embrace of reform as social currency — approving of [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s "scheme of wholesale motherhood" (016-082) because it "would give 'a perfectly glorious jolt' to the old-fashioned [[home|home]]" (016-083) — illustrates how the rhetoric of reform can be co-opted by those who have no stake in its practical consequences. She represents reform as performance rather than [[work-and-vocation|vocation]].

## The Reform Temperament

The novel distinguishes between the true reformer's temperament and superficial enthusiasm for new ideas. Edith complains that [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s "head is so full of new ideas" (004-233) that she may find the [[home|house]] a burden, and Bruce laments that she "sets herself like an angry old hen against all Deborah's new ideas" (027-027). But the novel exposes these accusations as projections: when [[roger-gale|Roger]] observes [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] closely with her school [[childhood|children]], he finds that despite being much with the children, she "apparently had no new ideas at all. She seemed to be only listening" (014-092–093).

Reform, for [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], is not about imposing abstractions but about attentive presence.

The contrast with fashionable radicalism is sharp. [[laura-gale|Laura]] "gloried in it, as though this 'radical' sister of hers were a distinct social asset among her giddy friends uptown" (016-076–077), and her set had acquired "an appetite for men and women with ideas — the more 'radical,' the better" (016-079). [[roger-gale|Roger]] himself observes that "the way Laura used this word at times made Roger's blood run cold" (016-080). True reform, the novel insists, is not a dinner-party credential; it is the daily labor of [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] in the [[tenement-district|tenements]].

## Limits of Reform

The novel is honest about what reform cannot do. The [[law|legal]] and institutional apparatus of reform — "reformatories, distant cells" (010-089) and "that electric chair" (010-090) — represents a darker current of social control that runs parallel to [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s humane efforts. The school itself, for all [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s devotion, can feel like "a mere cog in a machine" (010-124) given the scale of need. And [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s early impression that [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s school was merely a place of "strange, dirty little urchins" (002-129) in "a vast foul region" (002-128) reminds us that reform must continually overcome the [[social-class|class]] prejudices and fears of those it seeks to persuade.

Reform in *His [[family|Family]]* is thus characterized not by triumph but by persistence. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] thinks "of all that we're so sure to do in the many, many years ahead" (004-163) — the reformer's horizon extends beyond any single victory. The school is both a concrete achievement and a symbol of unfinished [[work-and-vocation|work]], a bridge between the "new America" (008-065) that is arriving and the old order that is passing.
