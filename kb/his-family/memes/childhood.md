## Overview

Childhood in *His [[family|Family]]* functions as both a personal memory and a public concern. For [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], flashes of his own childhood provide emotional reference points for understanding the present. For [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], childhood becomes a [[work-and-vocation|professional]] mission — the education and care of other people's children. The novel traces childhood from private, nostalgic [[memory|recollection]] to a subject of [[reform|social reform]], mirroring the [[progress|Progressive]] Era's growing interest in child welfare.

## Childhood as Memory

[[roger-gale|Roger]]'s childhood memories surface unexpectedly, triggered by present circumstances. "From far back in his childhood a memory flashed into his mind" (025-088), the verb "flashed" capturing the involuntary, vivid quality of these [[memory|recollection]]s. Earlier, [[roger-gale|Roger]] had "thought of childhood as" (010-130) something idyllic and protected, a conception challenged by the urban poverty he witnesses.

Roger's own New Hampshire farm upbringing shapes his understanding of what childhood should be — open country, physical labor, family closeness. His ancestors were "many generations of stolid folk" whose endurance was forged in childhood, and he measures his grandchildren's city childhood against this rural ideal. The tension between memory and reality runs through his view of the next generation.

## Children and the Family

The novel portrays children as the living continuation of family identity. [[edith-sidney|Edith]] "had four children, and was soon to have another" (001-040), and this biological continuity is for Roger the most legible form of family permanence. Judith, his late wife, had told him: "you will live on in our children's lives" (001-138), a promise that consoles and haunts him. The phrase "what children all of us are" (001-133) suggests that adults remain children in their needs and vulnerabilities — a theme Poole develops throughout the novel.

Roger observes his daughters and realizes "inwardly they were children still, each groping for her happiness" (001-176). This recognition of perpetual childhood — the idea that maturity is a surface over enduring childlike needs — is one of the novel's deepest insights about human nature.

## Childhood as Social Concern

[[deborah-gale|Deborah]] transforms childhood from a private family matter into a public mission. Her school for immigrant children represents the Progressive Era conviction that society has a duty to all children, not only those born into comfort. "Down in my school we've a family of seven hundred children" (009-030), she tells Roger, and the number shocks him — a "family" far larger than his own.

## The Next Generation

The novel's closing chapters return to childhood as a symbol of continuity. "Through babyhood and childhood, through ignorance and filth" (042-102) traces a life's trajectory from its earliest stages through hardship. In the final chapter, "childhood. John, too, had come into the" (044-011) links [[ediths-children|the next generation]] to the family story. John, [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s son, represents childhood not as memory but as promise — the living continuation of [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s lineage.
