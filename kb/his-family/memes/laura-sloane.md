# Laura Sloane

## Overview

Laura Sloane (née Gale) is the middle daughter of Roger Gale and the one who moves farthest from the family's modest middle-class origins. Described as "a joyous creature" upon her return from school (001-154), Laura is the beauty of the family — an object of admiration, desire, and Roger's anxious concern. Her marriage to Harold Sloane represents the novel's clearest instance of social mobility through marriage, and her subsequent distance from the family — both geographical (European travel) and emotional — embodies the centrifugal forces pulling the Gale family apart.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Laura is the daughter Roger understands least. Where Edith is practical and Deborah is idealistic, Laura is aesthetic — she lives in the world of appearances, fashion, and social performance. The novel does not condemn this; it simply observes it, noting how Laura's path represents one of the few routes available to ambitious women of her era. Her courtship with Harold Sloane is treated as a genuine emotional event — "only a few weeks ahead" of her wedding, she is described as caught between excitement and something like panic (023-146).

After her marriage, Laura becomes increasingly distant from the family. She and Harold travel, socialize, and inhabit a world of "sumptuous young women" (003-181) that Roger can neither enter nor fully comprehend. Her arc raises the question the novel cannot quite answer: whether social mobility through marriage is liberation or exile.

## Relationships

- **Roger Gale** (father): The most distant of the father-daughter relationships. Roger loves Laura but does not understand her, and she does not seek his understanding.
- **Harold Sloane** (husband): A wealthy man who represents everything Roger is not — inherited money, social ease, a life of leisure. Laura's marriage to him is both achievement and departure.
- **Edith Sidney** (sister): Laura's foil — Edith anchors the family while Laura escapes it.
- **Deborah Gale** (sister): Laura's opposite — Deborah builds institutions while Laura inhabits them.
- **Judith Gale** (mother, deceased): Laura's memory of her mother is less central to her identity than it is for Edith or Deborah.
