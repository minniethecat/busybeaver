## Overview

**Parenthood** in *His Family* is the defining role of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s life. The novel examines what it means to be a parent — the [[duty|obligations]], the [[love]], the watching and waiting — as Roger navigates his daughters' adult lives while reflecting on his own legacy. Parenthood extends beyond the biological; [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s relationship with the children of [[deborahs-school|her school]] represents a form of public parenthood that the novel treats with deep seriousness. Roger confronts the stakes of fatherhood most sharply in moments of recognition — "you've got a girl of your own growing up" (007-315) — the words landing with the force of sudden clarity.

## Roger as Father

As a father of daughters now grown, Roger occupies a distinct position — no longer the authority figure of their childhood but not yet ready to be set aside. He watches [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s social climbing and [[laura-sloane|Laura]]'s restless independence with a mixture of concern and quiet admiration. The novel suggests that parenthood in adulthood means learning when to speak and when to remain silent, a lesson Roger struggles with throughout. Within each daughter he senses "the deep glad growing certainty" (005-205) of a life taking shape beyond his direction — a certainty that is at once the fulfillment of his parenting and its quiet undoing. The paradox is central to the novel's vision: the more successfully a parent loves, the more completely the child departs into a world the parent cannot enter.

## Public Parenthood

The tenement world offers a broader vision of parenthood. Roger observes how "children and parents all took" (015-039) part in the communal life of the neighborhood, blurring the lines between private and public care. The novel also explores the weight of motherhood through Deborah's work — "here was motherhood of the genuine kind" (028-090), a form of parenthood measured not by sentiment but by tireless service. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s school extends this ethos, making her a parent figure to dozens of children who are not her own. Through Deborah, the novel argues that meaningful parenthood extends beyond bloodlines into the [[community]]. Roger's own observations confirm this — watching tenement life, he notes that "here was motherhood of the genuine kind" (005-145), a form of care measured not by sentiment but by tireless daily presence.
