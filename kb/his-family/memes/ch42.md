(042-001) Of that greater family, one member had been in [[the-gale-home|the house]] all through the

(042-002) month which had just gone by. But he had been so quiet, so carefully

(042-003) unobtrusive, that he had been scarcely noticed. Very early each morning,

(042-004) day after day, John had gone outside for his breakfast and thence to the

(042-005) office where he himself had handled the business as well as he could, only

(042-006) coming to [[roger-gale|Roger]] at night now and then with some matter he could not settle

(042-007) alone, but always stoutly declaring that he needed no other assistance.

(042-008) "Don't come, Mr. Gale," he had urged. "You look worn out. You'll be sick

(042-009) yourself if you ain't careful. And anyhow, if you hang around you'll be

(042-010) here whenever she wants you."

(042-011) Early in [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[disease|illness]], John had offered to give up his room for the

(042-012) use of one of the nurses.

(042-013) "That's mighty thoughtful of you, [[johnny-geer|Johnny]]," [[allan-baird|Allan]] had responded. "But we've

(042-014) got plenty of room as it is. Just you stick around. We [[poverty|want]] you here."

(042-015) "All right, Doc. If there's any little thing, you know--answering the

(042-016) 'phone at night or anything else that I can do--"

(042-017) "Thank you, so; I'll let you know. But in the meantime go to bed."

(042-018) From that day on, John had taken not only his breakfast but his supper,

(042-019) too, outside, and no one had noticed his absence. Coming in late, he had

(042-020) hobbled silently up to his room, stopping to listen at Deborah's door. He

(042-021) had kept so completely out of the way, it was not till the baby was three

(042-022) weeks old, and past its second crisis, that Deborah thought to ask for

(042-023) John. When he came to her bed, she smiled up at him with the baby in her

(042-024) arms.

(042-025) "I thought we'd see him together," she said. John stood on his crutches

(042-026) staring down. And as Deborah watched him, all at once her look grew intent.

(042-027) "Johnny," she said softly, "go over there, will you, and turn up the light,

(042-028) so we can see him better."

(042-029) And when this was done, though she still talked smilingly of the child,

(042-030) again and again she glanced up at John's face, at the strange self-absorbed

(042-031) expression, stern and sad and wistful, there. When he had gone the tears

(042-032) came in her eyes. And Deborah sent for her husband.

(042-033) * * * * *

(042-034) The next day, at [[rogers-office|the office]], John came into Roger's room. Roger had been at

(042-035) [[work-and-vocation|work]] several days and they had already cleared up their affairs.

(042-036) "Here's something," said John gruffly, "that I wish you'd put away

(042-037) somewhere."

(042-038) And he handed to his partner a small blue leather album, filled with the

(042-039) newspaper clippings dealing with Deborah's illness. On the front page was

(042-040) one with her picture and a long record of her service to the [[childhood|children]] of

(042-041) [[new-york-city|New York]].

(042-042) "She wouldn't want to see it now," John continued awkwardly. "But I thought

(042-043) maybe later on the boy would like to have it. What do you think?" he

(042-044) inquired. Roger gave him a kindly glance.

(042-045) "I think he will. It's a fine thing to keep." And he handed it back. "But I

(042-046) guess you'd better put it away, and give it to her later yourself."

(042-047) John shifted his weight on his crutches, so quickly that Roger looked up in

(042-048) alarm:

(042-049) "Look here! You're not well!" He saw now that the face of the cripple was

(042-050) white and the sweat was glistening on his brow. John gave a harsh little

(042-051) nervous laugh.

(042-052) "Oh, it's nothing much, partner," he replied. "That's another thing I

(042-053) wanted to tell you. I've had some queer pains lately--new ones!" He caught

(042-054) his breath.

(042-055) "Why didn't you tell me, you young fool?"

(042-056) "You had your own troubles, didn't you?" John spoke with difficulty. "But

(042-057) I'll be all right, I guess! All I need is a few days off!"

(042-058) Roger had pressed a button, and his stenographer came in.

(042-059) "Call a taxi," he said sharply. "And, John, you go right over there and lie

(042-060) down. I'm going to take you [[family|home]] at once!"

(042-061) "I've got a better scheme," said John, setting his determined jaws. The

(042-062) sweat was pouring down his cheeks. "It may be a week--but there's just a

(042-063) chance it--may be a little worse than that! So I've got a room in a

(042-064) [[the-hospital|hospital]]! See? Be better all round!" He swayed forward.

(042-065) "Johnny!" Roger caught him just in time, and the boy lay senseless in his

(042-066) arms.

(042-067) * * * * *

(042-068) At home, a few hours later, Allan came with another [[doctor|physician]] down from

(042-069) John's small bedroom. He saw his colleague to the door and then came in to

(042-070) Roger.

(042-071) "I'm afraid Johnny has come to the end."

(042-072) For a moment Roger stared at him.

(042-073) "Has, eh," he answered huskily. "You're absolutely sure he has? There's

(042-074) nothing--nothing on earth we can do?"

(042-075) "Nothing more than we're doing now."

(042-076) "He has fooled you fellows before, you know--"

(042-077) "Not this time."

(042-078) "How long will it be?"

(042-079) "Days or hours--I don't know."

(042-080) "He mustn't suffer!"

(042-081) "I'll see to that." Roger rose and walked the floor.

(042-082) "It was the last month did it, of course--"

(042-083) "Yes--"

(042-084) "I blame myself for that."

(042-085) "I wouldn't," said Allan gently. "You've done a good deal for Johnny Geer."

(042-086) "He has done a good deal for this family! Can Deborah see him?"

(042-087) "I wish she could."

(042-088) "Better stretch a point for her, hadn't you? She's been a kind of a mother

(042-089) to John."

(042-090) "I know. But she can't leave her bed."

(042-091) "Then you won't tell her?"

(042-092) "I think she knows. She talked to me about him last night."

(042-093) "That's it, a mother!" Roger cried. "She was watching! We were blind!" He

(042-094) came back to his chair and dropped into it.

(042-095) "Does John know this himself?" he asked.

(042-096) "He suspects it, I think," said Allan.

(042-097) "Then go and tell him, will you, that he's going to get well. And after

(042-098) you've done it I'll see him myself. I've got something in mind I want to

(042-099) think out."

(042-100) After Allan had left the room, Roger sat thinking about John. He thought of

(042-101) John's birth and his drunken mother, the accident and his struggle for

(042-102) life, through babyhood and childhood, through ignorance and filth and pain,

(042-103) through din and clamor and hunger, fear; of the long fierce fight which

(042-104) John had made not to be "put away" in some big institution, of his battle

(042-105) to keep up his head, to be somebody, make a career for himself. He thought

(042-106) of John's becoming one of Deborah's big family, only one of thousands, but

(042-107) it seemed now to Roger that John had stood out from them all, as the figure

(042-108) best embodying that great fierce hunger for a full life, and as the link

(042-109) connecting, the one who slowly year by year had emerged from her greater

(042-110) family and come into her small one. And last of all he thought of John as

(042-111) his own companion, his only one, in the immense adventure on which he was

(042-112) so soon to embark.

(042-113) A few moments later he stood by John's bed.

(042-114) "Pretty hard, Johnny?" he gently asked.

(042-115) "Oh, not so bad as it might be, I guess--"

(042-116) "You'll soon feel better, they tell me, boy." John shut his eyes.

(042-117) "Yes," he muttered.

(042-118) "Can you stand my talking, just a minute?"

(042-119) "Sure I can," John whispered. "I'm not suffering any now. He's given me

(042-120) something to put me to sleep. What is it you want to talk about? [[business|Business]]?"

(042-121) "Not exactly, partner. It's about the family. You've got so you're almost

(042-122) one of us. I guess you know us pretty well."

(042-123) "I guess I do. It's meant a lot to me, Mr. Gale--"

(042-124) "But I'll tell you what you don't know, John," Roger went on slowly. "I had

(042-125) a son in the family once, and he died when he was three months old. That

(042-126) was a long time ago--and I never had another, you see--to take his

(042-127) place--till you came along." There fell a breathless silence. "And I've

(042-128) been thinking lately," Roger added steadily. "I haven't long to live, you

(042-129) know. And I've been wondering whether--you'd like to come into the

(042-130) family--take my name. Do you understand?"

(042-131) John said nothing. His eyes were still closed. But presently, groping over

(042-132) the bed, he found Roger's hand and clutched it tight. After this, from time

(042-133) to time his throat contracted sharply. Tears welled from under his eyelids.

(042-134) Then gradually, as the merciful drug which Allan had given did its work,

(042-135) his clutch relaxed and he began breathing deep and hard. But still for some

(042-136) time longer Roger sat quietly by his side.

(042-137) The next night he was there again. [[death|Death]] had come to the huddled form on

(042-138) the bed, but there had been no relaxing. With the head thrown rigidly far

(042-139) back and all the features tense and hard, it was a fighting figure still,

(042-140) a figure of stern protest against the world's injustice. But Roger was not

(042-141) thinking of this, but of the discovery he had made, that in their talk of

(042-142) the night before John had understood him--completely. For upon a piece of

(042-143) paper which Allan had given the lad that day, these words had been

(042-144) painfully inscribed:

(042-145) "This is my last will and testament. I am in my right mind--I know what I

(042-146) am doing--though nobody else does--nobody is here. To my partner Roger Gale

(042-147) I leave my share in our business. And to my teacher Deborah Baird I leave

(042-148) my crutches for her school."
