## Overview

**Time** in *His Family* is both a structural device and a thematic preoccupation. The novel opens with a clock: "the great bell slowly boomed the time away" (001-301), signaling that the old world is passing. Throughout the narrative, [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] measures his life against the clock — aware that "there is still time, but you have not" (001-190) much left.

## Time and Mortality

For Roger, time is inseparable from [[aging]] and [[mortality-and-death|death]]. At "ten o'clock" (001-193), the hour to go home, the novel repeatedly marks time as a countdown — not toward any particular event, but toward the end of Roger's life. Yet time also offers renewal: his grandchildren's lives represent a future beyond his own, where "times had changed" (003-158) and would continue changing.

## Time and the City

The novel uses [[new-york-city|New York City]] itself as a clock. The city's constant transformation — buildings rising and falling, streets changing character — registers the passage of historical time alongside the personal time of the Gale family. Time in the city is accelerated, compressed, making Roger's sixty years feel like centuries. The "great bell" that opens the novel tolls not just for Roger but for an entire era of the city's history. The "hurrying crowds" (001-006) that fill the opening paragraph establish the urban tempo against which all personal time is measured — a city always in motion, always leaving the past behind.
