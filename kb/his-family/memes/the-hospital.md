# The Hospital

## Overview

The hospital appears in *His Family*'s later chapters as the site of Roger Gale's illness and recovery — a space where the novel's domestic drama is stripped to its essentials. Here, the questions that have driven the entire novel — what holds a family together, what legacy a parent leaves, who cares and who does not — are tested in the crucible of sickness and possible death. "He watched the nurse in her hospital room" (010-099), and in watching, Roger begins to see what care looks like: not the abstract duty of family tradition but the concrete, daily work of tending to a body.

## Description in the Novel

The hospital is rendered through sensory detail — the nurse's movements, the doctor's visits, the quality of light in the room. It is a place of vulnerability: Roger, who has spent the novel trying to hold his family together through force of will, is now dependent on others. The "trained nurse" (010-099) becomes a significant figure; the "school doctor, the nurse and the visiting nurses" (010-104) form a network of care that extends beyond the family.

The hospital also becomes a site of reconciliation. It is here that Roger's daughters gather, where old grievances are set aside, where the family's deeper bonds are reaffirmed. The hospital strips away the social performances that have complicated their relationships and leaves only the question that matters: who will be here?

## Scenes and Events

- Roger's admission and the family's response
- His daughters' visits — Edith's practicality, Deborah's devotion, Laura's distance
- The slow process of recovery and the reordering of relationships
- The novel's final movement toward acceptance and peace

## Thematic Significance

The hospital is where the novel's abstract themes become concrete. Family is not an idea here; it is the person who sits by the bed. Duty is not a principle; it is the hand that adjusts the pillow. In the hospital, *His Family* earns its emotional weight.
