# The Church

## Overview

The church in *His Family* is a background institution — present but not central, invoked but not dramatized. It represents the traditional moral framework that Roger Gale's generation takes for granted and that his daughters are beginning to question or reinterpret. The novel is notably secular for a work of its era: its deepest moral questions are worked out not in church but at home, not through prayer but through Deborah's service and Roger's slow awakening. The church appears as another institution caught between old and new, its authority eroding as the city transforms around it.

## Description in the Novel

References to church are scattered and often oblique — a funeral, a wedding, a mention of attendance. The novel's religious sensibility is less institutional than personal: Roger's sense of duty, Deborah's sense of calling, and Judith's dying wish all carry spiritual weight without requiring ecclesiastical sanction. The church is where traditional rituals happen, but the novel's own spiritual center is elsewhere — in the family home, in the tenement school, in the work of caring for others.

## Scenes and Events

- Judith Gale's funeral, conducted in the church's rituals
- Roger's occasional, unexamined church attendance
- The contrast between institutional religion and Deborah's practical morality
- The novel's broader secular-humanist vision of social good

## Thematic Significance

The church's quiet presence in the novel reflects a world where religious authority is waning but still part of the social landscape. The novel does not attack religion; it simply finds its moral energy elsewhere — in family obligation, social reform, and the bonds of care that Deborah builds in her school.
