# Washington Square

## Overview

Washington Square is one of the few named New York landmarks in *His Family*, and its appearance carries symbolic weight. A remnant of the old city — the New York of gracious homes and quiet streets that Roger remembers from his early years — the Square stands in contrast to the "young New York, the gigantic roaring town" (001-001) that the novel chronicles. It represents the continuity that Roger values and the past that the modern city is erasing, block by block, building by building.

## Description in the Novel

The Square is never described in detail — the novel's references are glancing, as if the place is so firmly lodged in the imagination that description would be redundant. It is the kind of place where old New Yorkers walk, where families once lived, where the city's elegant past is still visible in the facades of the surrounding buildings. For Roger, who walks the city nightly, the Square is a landmark of memory — a place that anchors him in a city that is slipping away.

## Scenes and Events

- Roger's evening walks, which may take him through or past the Square
- The Square as emblem of the old New York that Roger mourns
- Its quiet presence as counterpoint to the "roaring" growth elsewhere

## Thematic Significance

Washington Square embodies the novel's central spatial tension: the old city versus the new. It is a place of memory and grace in a novel about transformation and loss. Its understated presence in the narrative — the novel does not need to describe it because it is already understood — reflects the taken-for-granted world that Roger is losing.
