# Judith Gale

## Overview

Judith Gale is the most important character in *His Family* who never appears on stage. Roger's wife and the mother of his three daughters, she died years before the novel opens, but her presence is felt on every page. "Before that, Judith his wife had been here" (001-060) — the phrase captures both her absence and her enduring influence. Her death is the wound from which the novel grows, and her dying wish — "for keeping the family together" (007-145) — is the command that Roger spends the rest of his life trying to obey.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Judith exists only in memory, but memory in this novel is vivid and consequential. We learn that she came from a New York family that "had lived long in New York" (001-094), that her marriage to Roger was a deep partnership, and that her death shattered the world she had built. "Terrible weeks" followed her dying, and for Roger "death had come to his soul" (001-147) in the aftermath.

Her influence operates through what others remember and invoke. Roger measures every decision against what Judith would have wanted. Edith and Deborah argue by citing their mother's example. Even Laura, the most independent daughter, carries the weight of Judith's legacy. The novel's central drama — whether the family can hold together as Judith wished — is defined by her absence.

## Relationships

- **Roger Gale** (husband): The great love of Roger's life, and the loss that defines him. "Their love had grown rich in sympathies" (001-111).
- **Deborah Gale** (daughter): The daughter who most directly carries Judith's spirit — not through imitation but through transformation.
- **Edith Sidney** (daughter): The daughter who preserves Judith's domestic legacy through her own household.
- **Laura Sloane** (daughter): The daughter most distant from Judith's example — and most haunted by it.

## Themes

Judith embodies the novel's central question: how does a family persist after its center is gone? Her dying wish provides the novel's moral framework, but the story refuses to make that framework simple. Keeping the family together proves impossible in any literal sense; the question becomes whether the family can survive in some transformed way.
