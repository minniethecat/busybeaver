## Overview

Bridgeport, Connecticut, appears in *His Family* as the home of a wealthy widowed aunt who factors into the Sidney family's social and financial calculations. Located within traveling distance of [[new-york-city|New York City]], it represents the extended family network that stretches beyond the city into the surrounding region.

## Description in the Novel

Bridgeport enters the narrative through its wealthy resident — a "widowed aunt in Bridgeport who adores him" (004-081), the "him" referring to [[george-sidney|George Sidney]]. This aunt is a figure of some financial significance to the family; George speaks "briefly of his rich aunt in Bridgeport" (005-084) in a tone that suggests both acknowledgment of her wealth and a certain dismissiveness. When she visits the city, the family makes a point of going to "the Ritz to meet the aunt from Bridgeport" (006-003), indicating both her social standing and the effort the family makes to accommodate her. When Roger returns "to Bridgeport" (007-309) after a visit, he retreats "back in his study" to reflect on the encounter.

## Scenes and Events

Bridgeport serves as an off-stage location from which the wealthy aunt exercises her influence. Her arrival in New York is a minor social event that draws the family to the Ritz (006-003).

## Associated Characters

- **[[george-sidney|George Sidney]]** — Connected through his wealthy widowed aunt who lives in Bridgeport (004-081), (005-084)
- **Aunt from Bridgeport** — A wealthy widow who adores George and visits the city (006-003)

## Thematic Significance

Bridgeport represents the network of family wealth and obligation that extends beyond the immediate New York setting. The wealthy aunt is a reminder that the Sidneys are embedded in a larger family system, with all the expectations and dependencies that implies. The small effort of meeting her at the Ritz captures the delicate social negotiations that family wealth entails.
