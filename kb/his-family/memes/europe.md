# Europe

## Overview

Europe in *His Family* serves two distinct and opposed functions. First, it is the destination of the wealthy — the place where Laura and Harold Sloane travel, socialize, and distance themselves from the Gale family's middle-class life. Harold "knew Europe as a group of fashionable shops" (005-108), and Laura's letters from abroad signal her increasing remove from the family's orbit. But Europe is also the site of war — the "adventurers from all over Europe" (025-082), the "schools by thousands all over Europe closing down" (024-037), and the "wide zones of Europe already blackened and laid waste" (027-139) that bring the novel's later chapters into the shadow of the Great War.

## Description in the Novel

Europe is never directly depicted — it exists in the novel as a point of reference, a destination, and a threat. Laura and Harold's European travels are described through what they leave behind and through the letters and reports that reach New York. "Somebody over in Europe had worked on it long and lovingly" (005-155) — the phrase captures both Europe's cultural prestige and its distance from the Gales' world.

When war breaks out in Europe, the novel's tone darkens. The "women in Europe these days" (026-098) that Edith mentions are suffering losses the Gales can barely imagine. Europe becomes the site of the modern catastrophe that the novel's domestic drama has been building toward — the external confirmation of all Roger's anxieties about change.

## Scenes and Events

- Laura and Harold's departure for Europe — her progressive separation from the family
- Harold's European shopping and fashion knowledge as markers of his class
- The outbreak of war and its impact on Bruce, a veteran
- Deborah's awareness of the war's implications for her work and her worldview

## Thematic Significance

Europe represents the world beyond America — both the cosmopolitan escape that wealth enables and the catastrophe that modernity produces. The novel's movement from domestic drama to global war is mediated through Europe, which becomes the symbolic space where family and history converge.
