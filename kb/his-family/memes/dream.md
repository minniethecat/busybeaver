## Overview

**Dreams** in *His [[family|Family]]* exist in productive tension with reality. The novel traces the arc from [[childhood|youth]]ful dreaming — "genial friendships and dreams and explorations, a search for [[love-and-courtship|romance]]" (001-079) — through the sobering encounter with life's limits: "this [[love-and-courtship|love]] had not been quite *all* he had dreamed, this woman" (001-106). For [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] especially, dreams represent the inner life that the demands of family and business slowly but inexorably encroach upon. The novel's treatment of dreams is neither cynical nor sentimental; it acknowledges disappointment while honoring the persistence of the dreaming impulse across a lifetime.

## Youthful Dreams and Their Disappointments

The novel opens with [[roger-gale|Roger]] as a young man arriving in [[new-york-city|New York]], full of "dreams and explorations, a search for [[love-and-courtship|romance]], the strange glory of [[love-and-courtship|love]]" (001-078, 001-079). These early dreams carry the expansive energy of [[childhood|youth]] and ambition — [[new-york-city|the city]] itself is a landscape of possibility. But Poole is careful to show the disappointment that shadows [[ambition|aspiration]]: there "had come disappointments, too, sudden severe little checks from his wife" (001-104, 001-105), and the realization settles in that "this [[love-and-courtship|love]] had not been quite *all* he had dreamed" (001-106). The qualification — "not quite *all*" — is [[nature|character]]istic of Poole's restraint: the disappointment is real but not total, a narrowing rather than a betrayal.

The younger generation inherits this dreaming impulse in collective form. The narrator describes "tremendous joyousness, of gaiety, fresh hopes and dreams, of leaping young" (010-137) energy — dreams as a generational possession, something held in common rather than in private [[loneliness|solitude]]. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s school is a dream made tangible, transforming "dreams of something" into institutional reality: "Their dreams are parts of something" (023-101), [[roger-gale|Roger]] cries, recognizing that [[youth|the young]]er generation's [[ambition|aspiration]]s have a constructive, outward-turning quality his own never quite achieved.

## The Geography of Reverie

[[roger-gale|Roger]]'s dreams in later life take a distinctive geographical form. Though he "had never been [[europe|abroad]] himself but had always dreamed of going there" (005-103), he sustains an imaginative travel-life nourished by maps and reading. He "had dreamed of long delicious" wanderings through "quaint crooked streets" (005-106), and these dreams provide an interior refuge from business pressures. The dream of [[europe|Europe]] — never realized, never abandoned — becomes a private counterpoint to the practical businessman's life he has actually lived.

These geographical dreams are interrupted by domestic reality with almost comic abruptness: a shout comes "in on his dream, as she shouted to her son below" (014-169), collapsing the distance between inner vision and [[home|household]] noise. The novel captures the fragility of reverie with precision — dreams are not protected spaces but vulnerable ones, always at risk of being broken into.

## Dream as Memory and Anticipation

In the novel's quieter passages, dreaming shades into memory. A [[nature|character]] gets "a dreamy look, oh, a very dreamy look" coming "into his eyes" (006-246, 006-247) — not dreaming forward into the future but backward into [[memory|the past]], as [[memory|recollection]] takes on the quality of reverie. The novel also captures dreams as anticipation: [[nature|character]]s are described "dreaming still of happiness, quite" (033-028), the trailing "quite" suggesting that happiness remains just out of reach, the object of an ongoing and perhaps unending dream. And in its most fleeting register, a moment of perception simply fades: "Like a dream it faded all away, and with a" (029-166) — the sentence itself trailing off, miming the evaporation it describes.
