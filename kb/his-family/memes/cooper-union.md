# Cooper Union

Cooper Union (formally the Cooper Union for the Advancement of Science and Art) is a landmark
educational institution and public meeting hall in New York City, featured in Ernest Poole's
novel *His Family* (1917) as a venue for political speeches, labor gatherings, and social
reform events.

## Role in the Novel

Cooper Union appears primarily in the context of public meetings and political oratory.
The venue is described as "packed to the doors" (027-209) and "packed to the walls, with an
overflow meeting out on the street" (029-005), emphasizing the intense public engagement
with the social and political causes of the era. The hall serves as a symbol of popular
democracy and civic participation in early 20th-century New York.

## Significance

In the novel, Cooper Union represents several intersecting themes:
- **Public Discourse**: The space where ordinary citizens gather to hear speeches and
  participate in democratic debate
- **Social Reform**: Associated with labor movements, women's suffrage meetings, and
  progressive causes of the period
- **Contrast with Domesticity**: One passage contrasts genuine motherhood with "orating in
  Cooper Union" (028-090), highlighting the tension between public activism and private
  domestic life that runs through the novel
