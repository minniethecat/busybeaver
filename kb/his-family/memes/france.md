## Overview

France appears in *His Family* in multiple contexts: as a destination of cultural pilgrimage, as the site of George Sidney's philanthropic barn-building project, and most significantly, as a theater of [[the-great-war|the Great War]] that disrupts the lives of the Gale family. Its presence in the novel reflects the interconnectedness of American and European experience in the early twentieth century.

## Description in the Novel

France is first mentioned in connection with George Sidney's rural philanthropy: he is building a barn "in a little village over in France" (006-057), a project that his family greets with affectionate exasperation — "go ahead with your barn in France" (006-065). This episode, humorous in tone, connects the domestic world of the Gales to a broader transatlantic awareness.

The nation takes on a more somber resonance as the war approaches. The narrative notes that "Germany, Italy, Russia, France and even England, all were in" (024-013) the gathering storm. Roger Gale reflects on what "it must be like that now in France" (025-096), the distant war becoming an object of anxious contemplation. The work of "American surgeons in France" (027-062) is noted, signaling the American engagement with the European conflict. This wartime context transforms France from a place of picturesque philanthropy into ground zero of a world-changing catastrophe.

## Thematic Significance

France embodies two distinct thematic functions in the novel. Before the war, it represents the Old World that Americans visit for culture and charity — a beneficiary of American goodwill. During and after the war, it becomes a symbol of the devastation that modernity can unleash, a warning that the forces transforming America are not merely domestic but global. The contrast between George's quaint barn-building and the wartime suffering in France encapsulates the novel's movement from innocence to experience.
