# Social Class

## Overview

Class is the invisible architecture of *His [[family|Family]]*. The novel moves across the full spectrum of early 20th-century [[new-york-city|New York]] society: from the immigrant tenements where [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] teaches to the fashionable hotels where [[laura-sloane|Laura]] socializes, from [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s modest middle-class [[home|household]] to the world of "rich looking chaps with beards" glimpsed on Fifth Avenue (003-164). Poole's novel won the first Pulitzer Prize in part because it captured, with documentary precision, how class shaped every dimension of urban [[america|American]] life.

## Manifestations

Class operates in three key dimensions in the novel. As **geography**: the physical distance between the "garish daylight" of the poor neighborhoods — "here were poverty and ugliness" (010-006) — and the elegant homes of [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s business clients maps social hierarchy onto urban space. As **[[ambition|aspiration]]**: [[lauras-wedding|Laura's marriage]] to [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] represents a leap upward; [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s business struggles represent the fragility of middle-class status. As **conscience**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[work-and-vocation|work]] in "five [[education|schools]]" where "the poverty was rapidly becoming worse" (027-128) makes class suffering visible — and makes the Gale family's relative comfort a subject of implicit moral scrutiny.

The novel is particularly attentive to the **psychology** of class. [[roger-gale|Roger]] is neither rich nor poor, but his position feels precarious. He worries about his business, about his daughters' futures, about slipping. His resentment of [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] is partly personal and partly class resentment — a self-made man's suspicion of inherited wealth.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]** occupies the anxious middle. He has enough to be comfortable but not enough to be secure. His worldview is shaped by the Protestant ethic of [[work-and-vocation|work]] and thrift.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]** crosses class boundaries daily. She moves between her father's comfortable home and the "poverty and vice and crime" (010-135) of the neighborhoods she serves, embo[[death|dying]] a vision of class reconciliation through education and care.
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]** (née Gale) aspires upward. Her marriage to [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] is the novel's clearest instance of class mobility through marriage — and the emotional costs that mobility exacts.
- **[[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]]** represents inherited wealth and its casual assumptions. He embodies the class position that [[roger-gale|Roger]] resents and [[laura-sloane|Laura]] desires.
