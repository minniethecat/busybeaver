# Laura's Wedding

> "The kind of wedding Laura liked was a thing that reached from the church to the house and down into the very kitchen." (007-010)

## A Wedding as Social Theater

[[lauras-wedding|Laura Gale's wedding]] to [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] is the novel's most extended domestic set piece and the event that most fully exposes the tensions between old and new, family obligation and social ambition. Laura, the youngest and most restless of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s daughters, transforms the family home into a stage for her aspirations: "the kind of wedding Laura likes" (007-010) is a production, reaching from church to house to kitchen.

The wedding preparations consume multiple chapters as the [[the-gale-home|Gale home]] is "transformed into a shop where the wedding presents were displayed" (007-019). The domestic space becomes commercial; the family gathering becomes a social event. Roger watches with a mixture of bemusement and hurt as his house is taken over by strangers and his daughter recedes into bridal formality.

## Generational Collision

Laura's wedding crystallizes the novel's generational conflict. She wants "a church wedding, bridesmaids, ushers" (004-082) — the full apparatus of modern social display. Roger, whose values were formed in an earlier era, finds the spectacle alien: "She needs her home for her wedding" (006-012), and the home that was built for family life is being repurposed for social performance.

The tension is not simply between father and daughter but between two conceptions of what a wedding means. For Laura, the wedding is a social event — a declaration of arrival. For Roger, it should be a family event — a private transition witnessed by those who matter. "The kind of wedding which mother would have wanted" (007-038) hovers as an unspoken counterpoint: the wedding Judith would have arranged, if she had lived, would have been different.

## Roger's Dispossession

The wedding is one of the novel's most vivid instances of Roger's sense of dispossession. "I don't want to talk of the wedding, child, but of what's coming after" (007-207), he tells Laura, trying to shift the conversation from spectacle to substance. But Laura is already gone — already a Sloane in spirit if not yet in name. The wedding formalizes a departure that has been underway since the novel's opening.

Roger's discomfort peaks at the ceremony itself, where he must perform the role of father of the bride while feeling that the role has been hollowed out. "When the wedding was over he would take out his" (007-087) — the incomplete sentence captures his anticipation of relief. The wedding is something to be survived, not celebrated.

## Family Reconfiguration

The wedding also reconfigures the remaining family. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] watches her sister's display with characteristic skepticism. [[edith-sidney|Edith]], as the eldest, feels the weight of precedent: "the oldest sister to put off the wedding two or three weeks" (007-035) is a request that reveals how weddings reverberate through sibling relationships.

After the wedding, the Gale home has one fewer permanent resident, and the family's center of gravity shifts. Laura's marriage to Harold Sloane aligns her with wealth and modernity, moving her orbit away from the lower west side. The wedding is an ending disguised as a beginning — a celebration that masks a loss.

## References

Laura's wedding is developed primarily in Chapters 4 through 7. Key passages include 004-082 (church wedding plans), 004-224 to 004-231 (wedding planning), 006-012 (needing the home for the wedding), 007-002 to 007-019 (home transformed into wedding shop), 007-035 to 007-038 (sibling tensions and mother's memory), and 007-207 (Roger's plea to talk of what comes after). Poole's treatment of the wedding is unsentimental and socially observant: the rituals are described in their material detail, and the emotions are registered through what characters do not say as much as what they do.
