(021-001) [[roger-gale|Roger]] went through the next two days in a kind of a stupor. He remembered

(021-002) holding [[edith-sidney|Edith]] and feeling her shudder as though from a chill. He remembered

(021-003) being stopped in the hall by [[george-sidney|George]] who had dressed himself with care in

(021-004) his first suit with long trousers. "I just wanted you to remember," the boy

(021-005) whispered solemnly, "that I'm nearly sixteen and I'll be here. He said to

(021-006) stand by her and I will." The rest of that ghastly time was a blank,

(021-007) punctuated by small quiet orders which Roger obeyed. Thank God, [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] was

(021-008) there, and she was attending to everything.

(021-009) But when at last it was over, and Roger had spent the next day in his

(021-010) office, had found it impossible to [[work-and-vocation|work]] and so had gone [[family|home]] early, Deborah

(021-011) came to him in his room.

(021-012) "Now we must have a talk," she said. "[[allan-baird|Allan]] has gone through [[bruce|Bruce]]'s

(021-013) affairs, and there are still debts to be settled, it seems."

(021-014) "How much do they come to, Deborah?"

(021-015) "[[About]] five thousand dollars," she said. And for a moment neither spoke. "I

(021-016) wish I could help you out," she went on, "but I have nothing saved and

(021-017) neither has Allan. We've both kept using our money [[downtown-new-york|downtown]]--except just

(021-018) enough for the trip [[europe|abroad]]--and we'll need almost all of that to settle for

(021-019) [[judith-gales-funeral|the funeral]]."

(021-020) "I can manage," Roger said, and again there was a silence.

(021-021) "Edith will have to come here to live," Deborah said presently. Her

(021-022) father's heavy face grew stern.

(021-023) "I'd thought of that," he answered. "But it will be hard on her,

(021-024) Deborah--"

(021-025) "I know it will--but I don't see anything else to be done." The deep quiet

(021-026) voice of his daughter grew sweet with pity as she spoke. "At least we can

(021-027) try to make it a little easier for her. You can take her up to the

(021-028) mountains and I can close her apartment. But of course she won't agree to

(021-029) it unless she knows how matters stand." Deborah waited a little. "Don't you

(021-030) think you're the best one to tell her?"

(021-031) "Yes," said Roger, after a pause.

(021-032) "Then suppose we go to her. I'm sleeping up there for the next few nights."

(021-033) * * * * *

(021-034) They found Edith in her living room. She had sent the nurse out, put the

(021-035) [[childhood|children]] to bed, and left alone with nothing to do she had sat facing her

(021-036) first night. Her light soft hair was disheveled, her pretty features pale

(021-037) and set. But the moment Roger entered he saw that she had herself in hand.

(021-038) "Well, father," she said steadily. "You'd better tell me about our affairs.

(021-039) _My_ affairs," she corrected herself. When he had explained, she was silent

(021-040) a moment, and then in a voice harsh, bitter, abrupt, "That will be hard on

(021-041) the children," she said. On an impulse he started to take her hand, but she

(021-042) drew a little away from him.

(021-043) "The children, my dear," he said huskily, "will be taken care of always."

(021-044) "Yes." And again she was silent. "I've been thinking I'd like to go up to

(021-045) [[new-hampshire-farm|the mountains]]--right away," she continued.

(021-046) "Just our idea," he told her. "Deborah will arrange it at once."

(021-047) "That's good of Deborah," she replied. And after another pause: "But take

(021-048) her home with you--will you? I'd rather not have her here to-night."

(021-049) "I think she'd better stay, my dear."

(021-050) "All right." In a tone of weariness. "[[madge-deering|Madge Deering]] called me up to-night.

(021-051) She's coming in town to-morrow, and she means to stay till I go."

(021-052) "I'm glad," he said approvingly. Madge had been a widow for years. Living

(021-053) out in [[morristown|Morristown]] with four daughters to bring up, she had determinedly

(021-054) fought her way and had not only regained her hold but had even grown in

(021-055) strength and breadth since the death of her husband long ago. "I'm glad,"

(021-056) he said. "You and Madge--" he paused.

(021-057) "Yes, we'll have a good deal in common," Edith finished out his thought.

(021-058) "You look tired, dad. Hadn't you better go home now?" she suggested after a

(021-059) moment.

(021-060) "Yes," said Roger, rising. "Good-night, my child. Remember."

(021-061) In the outer hallway he found Deborah with [[laura-sloane|Laura]]. Laura had been here

(021-062) several times. She was getting Edith's [[grief|mourning]].

(021-063) "There's a [[love-and-courtship|love]] of a hat at Thurn's," she was saying softly, "if only we

(021-064) can get her to wear it. It's just her type." And Laura drew an anxious

(021-065) breath. "Anything," she added, "to escape that hideous heavy crepe."

(021-066) Roger slightly raised his brows. He noticed a faint delicious perfume that

(021-067) irritated him suddenly. But glancing again at his daughter, trim, fresh and

(021-068) so immaculate, the joy of life barely concealed in her eyes, he stopped and

(021-069) talked and smiled at her, as Deborah was doing, enjoying her beauty and her

(021-070) youth, her love and all her happiness. And though they spoke of her sister,

(021-071) she knew they were thinking of herself, and that it was quite right they

(021-072) should, for it gave them a little relief from their gloom. She was honestly

(021-073) sorry for Edith, but she was sorrier still for Bruce, who she knew had

(021-074) always liked her more than he would have cared to say. She was sorrier for

(021-075) Bruce because, while Edith had lost only her husband, Bruce had lost his

(021-076) very life. And life meant so much to Laura, these days, the glowing,

(021-077) coursing, vibrant life of her warm beautiful body. She was thinking of that

(021-078) as she stood in the hall.

(021-079) * * * * *

(021-080) In the evening, at home in his study, Roger heard a slight knock at the

(021-081) door. He looked up and saw John.

(021-082) "May I come in, Mr. Gale, for a minute?"

(021-083) "Yes, my boy." John hobbled in.

(021-084) "Only a minute." His voice was embarrassed. "Just two or three things I

(021-085) thought of," he said. "The first was about your son-in-law. You see, I was

(021-086) his stenographer--and while I was in [[rogers-office|his office]]--this morning helping

(021-087) [[doctor|Doctor]] Baird--I found a good deal I can do there still--about things no one

(021-088) remembers but me. So I'll stay there awhile, if it's all right. Only--" he

(021-089) paused--"without any pay. See what I mean?"

(021-090) "Yes, I see," said Roger. "And you'd better stay--in that way if you like."

(021-091) "Thanks," said John. "Then about his wife and family. You're to take them

(021-092) up to the mountains, I hear--and--well, before this happened you asked _me_

(021-093) up this summer. But I guess I'd better not."

(021-094) "I don't think you'd be in the way, my boy."

(021-095) "I'd rather stay here, if you don't mind. When I'm through in your

(021-096) son-in-law's office I thought I might go back to yours. I could send you

(021-097) your mail every two or three days."

(021-098) "I'd like that, John--it will be a great help."

(021-099) "All right, Mr. Gale." John stopped at the door. "And Miss Deborah," he

(021-100) ventured. "Is she to get married just the same?"

(021-101) "Oh, yes, I think so--later on."

(021-102) "Good-night, sir."

(021-103) And John went out of the room.

(021-104) When _would_ Deborah be married? It came over Roger, when he was alone, how

(021-105) his family had shifted its center. Deborah would have come here to live,

(021-106) to love and be happy, a mother perhaps, but now she must find a home of her

(021-107) own. In her place would come Edith with her children. All would center on

(021-108) her in her [[death|grief]].

(021-109) And for no cause! Just a trick of chance, a street accident! And Roger grew

(021-110) bitter and rebelled. Bruce was not the one of the family to die. Bruce, so

(021-111) shrewd and vigorous, so vital, the practical man of affairs. Bruce had been

(021-112) going the pace that kills--yes, Roger had often thought of it. But that had

(021-113) nothing to do with this! If Bruce had died at fifty, say, as a result of

(021-114) the life he had chosen, the fierce exhausting city which he had loved as a

(021-115) man will love drink, then at least there would have been some sense of

(021-116) fairness in it all! If the town had let him alone till his time! But to be

(021-117) knocked down by an automobile! The devilish irony of it! No

(021-118) reason--nothing! Just hideous luck!

(021-119) Well, life was like that. As for Edith and her children, he would be glad

(021-120) to have them here. Only, it would be different, [[the-gale-home|the house]] would have to

(021-121) change again. He was sorry, too, for Deborah. No [[marriage|wedding]] trip as she had

(021-122) planned, no home awaiting her return.

(021-123) So his mind went over his family.

(021-124) But suddenly such thoughts fell away as trivial and of small account. For

(021-125) these people would still be alive. And Bruce was dead, and Roger was old.

(021-126) So he thought about Bruce and about himself, and all his children grew

(021-127) remote. "You will live on in our children's lives." Was there no other

(021-128) immortality? The clock ticked on the mantle and beside it "The Thinker"

(021-129) brooded down. And Roger looked up unafraid, but grim and gravely wondering.
