## Overview

Servants in *His [[family|Family]]* are a near-invisible but structurally essential presence. They cook, clean, and maintain the [[the-gale-home|Gale house]]hold, enabling the family's [[home|domestic life]] while remaining largely unnamed and unindividuated. Their presence marks the Gales' [[social-class|class]] position — comfortable but not ostentatiously wealthy — and their occasional mentions in the narrative reveal economic pressures and shifts in [[home|household]] fortune. The servants function as a quiet index of the family's financial and emotional state.

## Manifestations

The servants appear in the novel primarily through economic references. [[roger-gale|Roger]] remarks grimly, "Turn off the servants instead of the" (024-152) — his unfinished sentence suggesting a painful economy measure he would rather avoid. The fragment "for the servants" (026-046) similarly hints at [[home|household]] decisions made with the staff in mind. In a moment of financial strain, [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s sister asks pointedly, "Have the servants been paid?" (034-184), a question that cuts through domestic pretense to the material reality of maintaining a [[home|household]]. In the novel's later chapters, reference is made "from time to time of [[medicine|doctor]]s, nurses, servants" (041-122), grouping domestic [[work-and-vocation|work]]ers with [[doctor|medical profession]]als as necessary attendants to [[family|family life]], particularly in times of [[disease|illness]]. The servants' near-invisibility in the narrative — they are mentioned but rarely named or given voice — itself constitutes a quiet commentary on [[social-class|class]] relations in early twentieth-century [[america|America]].
