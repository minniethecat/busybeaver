# Home

## Overview

In *His [[family|Family]]*, "home" is more than a house — it is the emotional center of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s world and the novel's chief symbol of continuity amid sweeping urban and social change. The Gale [[family|family home]], on a quiet street in the lower west side of [[new-york-city|New York]], is introduced as part of "a city of houses, separate homes, of quiet streets with rustling trees" (001-014). This domestic space, presided over by [[roger-gale|Roger]] after his wife [[judith-gales-death|Judith's death]], serves as the physical manifestation of [[family|family bonds]] — a place where all three daughters return, even as their lives pull them in different directions.

## Manifestations

The novel treats home across three registers. First, as a **physical place**: the old house, the [[new-hampshire-farm|New Hampshire]] summer home that "he had kept as a summer place" (001-201), the rooms where [[childhood|children]] are born and marriages planned. Second, as **emotional anchor**: [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s deep need to keep his daughters near, expressed in his insistence that [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] and [[laura-sloane|Laura]] "stay at home" and his resentment when they leave. Third, as a **threatened ideal**: the encroaching city — "tier upon tier of modern homes, all overhanging [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s house as though presently to crush it down" (002-122) — embodies the pressure of [[modernity|urban transformation]] on traditional domestic life.

[[deborah-gale|Deborah]], who "had stayed at home with her father" after [[laura-sloane|Laura]] went away to school (001-151), becomes the keeper of domestic continuity. But the novel refuses to make home merely nostalgic. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s gradual [[peace|acceptance]] that home must change — that his daughters must build homes of their own — is one of the novel's central emotional arcs.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]** sees home as a sacred trust from [[judith-gale|Judith]]: the place where her spirit lingers and where the family must be kept together. His business struggles, his walks through the changing city, and his evening rituals all center on returning home.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]** redefines home by extending it outward — her school in [[tenement-district|the immigrant quarter]] becomes a kind of enlarged home, what she calls "a [[deborahs-family-of-her-own|family of my own]]" (009-020). She both honors and transforms the concept of home that [[roger-gale|Roger]] cherishes.
- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]** embodies home as biological continuity: her household overflows with [[childhood|children]], creating [[ediths-children|the next generation]] of the family within domestic walls. [[roger-gale|Roger]] sees "new lives in [[family|his family]]" through her (004-202).
