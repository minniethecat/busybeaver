# Tradition

## Overview

Tradition is [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s native language — the set of inherited assumptions about family, [[work-and-vocation|work]], gender, and morality that he absorbed from "many [[generational-change|generations]] of stolid folk in [[new-england|New England]]" (036-196) and that the modern world is rapidly rendering obsolete. The novel does not treat tradition as simply good or bad; it is the ground [[roger-gale|Roger]] stands on, the frame[[work-and-vocation|work]] through which he makes sense of his life. But it is also a constraint — on his daughters, on his own capacity for change, on [[new-york-city|the city]] around him that will not stand still.

## Manifestations

Tradition operates through three channels in the novel. As **family structure**: the patriarchal [[home|household]], the duty of [[childhood|children]] to parents, the expectation that daughters will marry and sons will [[work-and-vocation|work]] — all of which the novel's events challenge and reshape. As **moral frame[[work-and-vocation|work]]**: the Protestant ethic of [[work-and-vocation|work]], thrift, and [[duty|responsibility]] that [[roger-gale|Roger]] lives by and that [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] transforms into a secular gospel of social service. As **physical inheritance**: the old [[home|house]], [[new-hampshire-farm|the New Hampshire home]]stead, the furniture and rituals that carry the weight of "countless [[generational-change|generations]] reaching back" (044-047).

The novel traces tradition's erosion without celebrating it. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s world is genuinely valuable — its solidity, its decency, its sense of [[duty|obligation]] — and its passing is genuinely mourned. But the novel also insists that tradition must bend or break: the daughters cannot live their mother's life, and [[new-york-city|the city]] cannot remain the small-scale world of [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s [[childhood|youth]].

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: Tradition's living representative. His values are genuine and his world is coherent — but he is the last of his kind.
- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]**: Tradition's domestic guardian. She preserves the [[home|household]], the [[childhood|children]], the rituals — but even she cannot keep the world outside from pressing in.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Tradition's transformer. She takes her father's ethic of duty and remakes it as a calling to serve the poor. She honors tradition by changing it.
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]**: Tradition's deserter. She leaves the family orbit for a world of wealth and fashion that has little use for the old ways.
