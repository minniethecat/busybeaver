# Roger Gale's Office

## Overview

Roger Gale's office is the counterpart to his home — the place where he is not a father but a businessman, not a widower but a man of affairs. "Bruce was still in his office" (001-194) and Roger was "crazy to get back to his office again" (001-204) — the office exerts a pull that competes with the pull of home. It is the site of Roger's public self, the domain where his competence is unquestioned and his authority is real. Johnny Geer works there; Bruce visits; Roger's business partners gather. It is, in its way, as central to his identity as the Gale home.

## Description in the Novel

The office is downtown, near the commercial heart of the city. Roger "set up an office of his own and was doing splendidly" (001-241) — it represents his self-made success, the enterprise he built from nothing. The office is also where the pressures of the business world converge: "I've been in their office all day" (001-267), and the work follows him home — he tries not to "take work home from the office" (001-219) but cannot always separate the two worlds.

After Roger's illness, Johnny Geer is "back in Roger's office" and proves "a great help" (022-043), extending the office's significance into the novel's later chapters. The continuity of the office — it survives Roger's absence and welcomes his return — mirrors the continuity Roger hopes for in his family.

## Thematic Significance

The office represents the world of work and masculine identity that the novel takes seriously but does not romanticize. It is where Roger is powerful, and it is also where he is worn down. The tension between office and home — the pull of work against the pull of family — is one of the novel's central dynamics.
