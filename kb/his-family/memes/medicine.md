## Overview

**Medicine** in *His [[family|Family]]* appears in both [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al and folk forms, reflecting the early twentieth-century world in which doctors coexist with patent remedies and home care. The novel uses medical scenes to illuminate [[nature|character]] — who seeks help, who dismisses it, and what kind of "medicine" each person ultimately needs. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] himself invokes medicine metaphorically, telling [[edith-sidney|Edith]] that her [[childhood|children]] are "all the medicine you need" (013-028), a line that captures both the era's casual attitude toward remedies and the novel's deeper claim that family connection is the most reliable cure for human suffering.

## The Spectrum of Medical Care

The novel presents a broad spectrum of medical practice, from informal to [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al. At one end, a [[nature|character]] simply "went to the druggist on her block, and after listening to his advice she bought some patent medicine, a steel jacket and some crutches, and thought she'd done her" (015-087) — a scene that captures the casual, unregulated medical culture of the era. The druggist serves as a quasi-medical authority, and the patient's self-satisfaction at having "done her" duty to her own health is both comic and revealing.

At the other end of the spectrum, [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al doctors attend to serious [[disease|illness]]. "When the doctor arrived she took him upstairs" (035-050) — the formality of the moment, the doctor being "taken" to the patient, signals a different order of medical encounter. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s long [[disease|illness]] draws sustained medical attention, with family providing "the many meals, the medicines and the endless task of keeping her" (035-016). The juxtaposition of "medicines" with "meals" and "the endless task of keeping her" makes clear that medical treatment is embedded in a broader caregiving economy, most of it performed by [[womens-roles|women]].

## Medicine as Metaphor

The novel's most striking use of medicine is metaphorical. A [[nature|character]] declares, "I've got to take this. It's my medicine" (032-249), referring not to a physical remedy but to a difficult moral or emotional necessity. This usage transforms medicine from a material substance into a figure for any bitter but necessary experience. The metaphor [[work-and-vocation|work]]s because the novel has already established a world in which literal medicines are everywhere — patent remedies, doctor's prescriptions, home care — making the metaphorical extension feel natural rather than forced.

[[roger-gale|Roger]]'s earlier comment that his grand[[childhood|children]] are "all the medicine you need" (013-028) participates in the same metaphorical register. It is a warm line, but it also subtly diminishes the authority of [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al medicine, substituting emotional bonds for clinical intervention. The novel never resolves the tension between these two models of healing — the medical and the relational — but keeps them in productive dialogue throughout.

## Medicine and Social Class

The novel's medical scenes also register [[social-class|class distinctions]]. The [[nature|character]] who buys "patent medicine" from the druggist (015-087) belongs to a different social stratum than those attended by private [[doctor|physician]]s. Access to medical care is uneven, and the novel quietly documents this stratification without moralizing. The druggist's advice is affordable and accessible; the doctor's visit is a marker of middle-[[social-class|class]] respectability. In this, *His [[family|Family]]* captures a transitional moment in [[america|American]] medicine — between the folk remedies of the nineteenth century and the [[work-and-vocation|profession]]alized, institution-based medicine of the twentieth.
