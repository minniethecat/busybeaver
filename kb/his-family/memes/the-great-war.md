# The Great War

## Overview

World War I enters *His Family* as an external force that transforms the novel's domestic world. The "schools by thousands all over Europe closing down" (024-037), the "adventurers from all over Europe" (025-082), and the "wide zones of Europe already blackened and laid waste" (027-139) bring the novel's themes of change and destruction to their fullest expression. The war is the modern catastrophe that Roger has been fearing in miniature — the proof that the forces reshaping his city and his family can also destroy a continent.

## Context and Build-up

Before the war, Europe in the novel was a place of leisure and culture — Harold Sloane's shopping districts, Laura's travel destinations. The war transforms this picture utterly. The outbreak is reported through newspapers and conversations, a distant thunder that grows louder. Bruce, a veteran of earlier conflicts, understands what is coming before the others do. Deborah watches with the alertness of someone whose students' families will be directly affected.

## The Event

The war is rendered through its effects rather than its battles. The novel is not a war story — it is a novel about how war reaches into domestic life. "Think of the women in Europe these days!" (026-098) Edith exclaims, and the war becomes a lens through which the novel examines its own themes of loss, duty, and endurance. Bruce's experience — only alluded to, never directly described — is the war's personal face in the novel: a builder who has seen destruction on an industrial scale.

## Consequences

The war reshapes the novel's moral landscape:
- It validates Roger's anxieties about modernity — the forces he feared were not imaginary
- It gives new urgency to Deborah's work — building schools in a world capable of such destruction
- It shadows Bruce's character — the architect who has seen what modern warfare can do
- It places the Gale family's domestic struggles in a global context of real catastrophe

## Thematic Significance

The war is the novel's historical grounding — the reminder that the family drama is not happening in isolation but in a world capable of immense violence. It transforms the novel's central question from "Can Roger hold his family together?" to "Can anything hold together in a world like this?"
