## Overview

Tad is the youngest child of [[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]] and a minor but recurring character in Ernest Poole's "His Family" (1917). Referred to affectionately as "little Tad" throughout the novel (004-196), he appears across multiple chapters as part of the next generation of the Gale family. His cheerful, innocent perspective provides contrast to the novel's themes of aging and generational change.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Tad first appears as a young child under the care of an Irish nurse named Hannah, who is reported to have "slapped little Tad that afternoon" (004-196). He is later described as "the chubby" member of the family, alongside his brothers Bob and George (006-039). Tad's presence is frequently associated with domestic scenes and childhood innocence: at a family supper, "the questions were popping from every side, while little Tad beamed from one to the other" (006-067), and he is shown to find such gatherings "all so wonderful, to be having supper" with the extended family (006-068).

Throughout the later chapters, Tad appears in brief domestic vignettes. Edith is seen "mending a pair of rompers for little Tad" (028-002), and his droll behavior is noted as a source of parental amusement (028-047). During Bruce's illness, the cold is passed "to Bruce and Tad" (035-009), suggesting Tad's continued presence in the family household. In one of the novel's final domestic scenes, Tad is mentioned alongside Bob as part of the household preparing for Betsy's departure to school (038-143).

## Relationships

Tad is the youngest son of [[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]] and the brother of Bob and George, Edith's other children. He is the grandson of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], the novel's protagonist, though direct interactions between Tad and Roger are not prominently featured. Tad belongs to the third generation of the Gale family, representing the continuity of family life that Roger observes with both pride and wistfulness as he ages.
