# George Sidney

## Overview

George Sidney is Edith Gale's husband and the father of her five children. He is present in the novel more as a fact than as a fully realized character — a decent, conventional man who provides for his family and serves as a stable presence in the Gale family's extended orbit. The novel does not explore his inner life in depth, but his role is structurally important: he represents the kind of marriage and domesticity that Edith has chosen, and that her sisters have not.

## Life and Role in the Novel

George appears primarily through Edith's household — at family gatherings, in conversations about the children, in the background of scenes centered on Roger or Deborah. He is described as having brought home a book "the other night" that Edith mentions (002-061), and he participates in the family's rituals and rhythms. His marriage to Edith is not dramatized as a romance; it is presented as a settled fact, the kind of stable union that anchors the family's continuity.

His role in the novel is to embody the ordinary — not in a dismissive sense, but in the sense that most lives are ordinary, and the novel's realism depends on rendering that ordinariness with accuracy and respect.

## Relationships

- **Edith Sidney** (wife): A stable, conventional marriage built on shared domestic values and the raising of children.
- **Roger Gale** (father-in-law): A respectful, unremarkable relationship — Roger sees in George the kind of steady provider he himself once was.
- **Deborah Gale** (sister-in-law): Distant — George's conventional world has little intersection with Deborah's progressive one.
