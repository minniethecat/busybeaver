# Disease

## Overview

Disease in *His [[family|Family]]* operates at multiple levels: as the literal illness that claims individual lives, as a metaphor for the moral condition of the modern city, and as the final bodily decline that brings [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s story to its close. The novel is attentive to both the medical realities of early twentieth-century disease — Pott's disease, tuberculosis, pneumonia — and to what disease reveals about the society in which it occurs. [[allan-baird|Allan Baird]]'s medical [[work-and-vocation|work]] in [[tenement-district|the tenements]] and [[bruce|Bruce]]'s war injuries make disease a point where individual suffering and social structure intersect.

## Manifestations

Disease appears in three registers.

As **literal affliction**: [[johnny-geer|John Geer]]'s Pott's disease — "It's Pott's disease, and it's gone too far. John is eighteen. He may live" (015-080) — represents the randomness of bodily catastrophe; the diagnosis is later confirmed when Deborah states simply, "He has tuberculosis" (028-122), while [[roger-gale|Roger]] himself shouts back, "John has Pott's Disease" (028-123), revealing the generational conflict in how disease is understood and named.

As **urban metaphor**: [[bruce|Bruce]] describes the "disease which has its grip upon this whole infernal town" (013-031), linking physical illness to the moral and social sickness of [[new-york-city|the city]]. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s school [[work-and-vocation|work]] is described in contrast to "vermin, darkness and disease" (010-112) and "turmoil, filth, disease and death" (010-134) — the conditions she fights against.

As **final decline**: [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s own disease in the novel's closing chapters is described as gentle but progressive — "His disease, although progressing fast, gave him barely any pain" (043-004) — a decline that mirrors the quiet end of a generation.

The [[medicine|doctor]] [[allan-baird|Allan Baird]] recognizes disease as partly social: "Do you know what's the matter with kids these days? It's the twentieth century. It's a disease" (006-138). This diagnosis — that [[modernity|modern life]] itself is pathological — runs through the novel as a counterpoint to its attention to specific medical conditions.

In the tenement clinic, Baird confronts the structural dimension: "Where a sick or pregnant mother" (017-066) needs care, the medical establishment is absent, forcing private charity to fill the gap. The novel places disease within a larger frame that includes "epidemics and the new education" (017-077), suggesting that disease, education, and social reform are inseparable domains.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[johnny-geer|John Geer]]**: The young man with Pott's disease whose case becomes a point of conflict about medical knowledge and paternal authority. His condition is named twice, once by Baird (015-080) and once in confrontation between Deborah and Roger (028-122, 028-123), marking the father's resistance to accepting the diagnosis.
- **[[allan-baird|Allan Baird]]**: The doctor who navigates between medical science and social understanding of disease. He treats both wealthy patients uptown and tenement families, and his diagnostic vocabulary — Pott's disease, tuberculosis — exists alongside a broader social analysis of health.
- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: His own fatal disease frames the novel's end (043-004), giving his reflections a quality of final summation. His robust health in earlier life — "his rugged health had buoyed him up" (001-078) — makes the decline more poignant.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Her own illness (013-002) becomes a turning point in her relationship with her father. She later confronts disease professionally, through her school's doctor and visiting nurse (010-104), and personally, when she cares for Bruce's sick child (035-050, 035-071).
- **[[bruce|Bruce]]**: His description of the city's "disease" (013-031) makes explicit the metaphorical register. His war experience adds another layer — the sick and dying on "battle-fields, the shrieks of the dying" (026-149) — linking urban disease to the larger violence of the age.
- **[[edith-gale|Edith Gale]]**: In her "feverish state" (006-085) during childbirth-related crisis, she exemplifies the novel's pattern of illness as an occasion for family reckoning.

## Disease and the City

The tenements provide the most concentrated vision of disease in the novel. Deborah's school is built in a neighborhood of "rotten old tenement buildings with rags and filth" (010-111), where the battle against disease is waged physically, room by room. A visiting nurse tour reveals "a sick man" lying in a crowded tenement (011-046), and the workers speak matter-of-factly of neighbors who are dying: "He can't hear you anyhow. He's dying. He's been dying weeks" (011-063). In this world, disease is not exceptional but constant — a feature of the urban landscape rather than a disruption.

The novel contrasts this with the sanitized sickness of the middle class home. When Deborah falls ill, the response is orderly: "a trained nurse was soaping the windows" (006-075), a doctor is summoned, and the household organizes around her recovery. During her illness, [[roger-gale|Roger]] "was learning more" about his daughter (013-165), suggesting that illness in the novel's domestic sphere creates unexpected opportunities for intimacy and understanding. Even so, the city's fever seeps in: Deborah's face appears "flushed and feverish" (013-014), mirroring the larger "feverish city life" (013-168) that surrounds her.

## Children and Vulnerability

Children are the novel's most visible victims of disease. The tenement children suffer from conditions that Baird itemizes: "adenoids, children's teeth, epidemics" (017-077). The vulnerability is starkly dramatized when "the child fell sick, within a few hours its small" life was threatened (041-099), a passage that collapses the distance between health and death into a matter of hours. Deborah's own experience with sickness deepens when she confronts Bruce's baby: "If I had a baby as sick as that, what would I do?" (035-084), and later, "If I had a child like that — as sick as" (035-106) — her fragmentary thoughts revealing the terror that childhood disease inspires even in a seasoned school reformer.

At the same time, recovery is possible. By the novel's later movement, "sick children were up and about" (036-052), and Deborah moves among them with a competence that the novel explicitly links to her own hard-won experience with illness. Disease, in this narrative arc, becomes not only a threat but a teacher — the condition that draws characters into deeper engagement with the life around them.

## Disease as Metaphor and Social Critique

Beyond the literal afflictions, disease functions in the novel as a sustained critique of [[modernity]]. Bruce's accusation that a "disease" grips "this whole infernal town" (013-031) recasts city life itself as pathological. The city's energies are described repeatedly in fever terms — "the fevered life of the town" (010-139), "feverish city life" (013-168), "feverish rush for money" (034-015) — suggesting that the metropolitan economy itself produces symptoms analogous to illness. Roger, raised on the farm where "there ain't a sick animal on the whole farm" (014-033), embodies an older, healthier relation to work and the land that the city has supplanted.

The novel does not offer easy cures. When a friend claims "he has found a cure" (013-269), Bruce's reply is swift and contemptuous: "Damn his cure! You come to us!" (013-270). This rejection of quick fixes aligns with the novel's broader pragmatism: disease must be fought not through panaceas but through the slow institutional work of schools, clinics, visiting nurses, and changed living conditions. That work is Deborah's, and the novel suggests it is never finished — only carried forward into the next generation.
