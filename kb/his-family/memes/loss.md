# Loss

## Overview

Loss is the condition from which *His [[family|Family]]* begins and the thread that runs through every [[nature|character]]'s story. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s [[death|loss]] of his wife [[judith-gale|Judith]] — and with her, his [[religion|faith]] in God — establishes the novel's emotional key: "in the end it had been wrenched away, and with it he had lost all [[dream|hope]] that either for [[judith-gale|Judith]] or himself was there any existence beyond the grave" (001-145). From this originating [[death|loss]], the novel traces how each [[nature|character]] lives with what they have lost: [[edith-sidney|Edith]] loses her husband, [[bruce|Bruce]] loses his capacity for life, [[laura-sloane|Laura]] loses her illusions, and [[roger-gale|Roger]] loses first his [[religion|faith]], then his [[rogers-illness|health]], and finally his life.

The novel treats loss not as a single event but as a cumulative condition. Roger's early loss of Judith defines the decades that follow: "So [[death]] had come to Roger's soul. He had been deaf and blind to his children. Nights by the thousand spent alone" (001-147). His inner life becomes "a gray level road in his [[memory]]" (001-149), shaped entirely by what is absent rather than what remains.

## Manifestations

Loss takes distinct forms across the novel. As **[[death|loss]] of [[religion|faith]]**: [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s [[death|loss]] of religious [[religion|belief]] is presented as the deepest rupture — "ever since his wife had died and he had lost his [[religion|faith]] in God" (004-138). This [[death|loss]] leaves him without the consolations that had shaped his [[new-england|New England]] upbringing. The passage continues with Roger's existential question: "What was the meaning of it all if life were nothing but a start, and there were nothing but the grave?" (004-139). His loss of faith is not merely intellectual but is bound inextricably to the loss of Judith — the two are a single wound.

As **[[death|loss]] of [[love-and-courtship|loved]] ones**: [[edith-sidney|Edith]] loses her husband [[george-sidney|George]], and this is the loss the world can see and acknowledge. But [[bruce|Bruce]]'s [[death|loss]]es are described as more profound — "while [[edith-sidney|Edith]] had lost only her husband, [[bruce|Bruce]] had lost his life" (021-075) — the [[death|loss]] of purpose and vitality that the modern city inflicts. The novel's language here is striking: Bruce "had been going the pace that kills" (021-113), and though his actual death is from a street accident, the narrative frames it as a life already being drained. Roger's bitter reflection sharpens this: "If [[bruce|Bruce]] had died at fifty, say, as a result of the life he had chosen, the fierce exhausting city which he had loved as a man will love drink, then at least there would have been some sense of fairness in it all!" (021-113).

As **[[death|loss]] of certainty**: [[roger-gale|Roger]] at the novel's climax confronts having "forever lost his [[religion|faith]] in the sanity and steadiness of the great mind of humanity" (028-015) — a [[death|loss]] of the foundational [[religion|belief]] in human [[modernity|progress]] that had organized his worldview. The war shatters Roger's assumption that "mankind" was "mature" (028-016), and he is left with the conviction that humanity is "still only a precocious child, with a terrible precocity" (028-017).

The novel attends to the physical signs of [[death|loss]]: "his tall, stooping, massive frame had lost its look of ruggedness" (026-161). Loss is not merely an emotional state but something that marks the body and alters a person's presence in the world. Deborah, watching her father age, calculates the practical consequences: "Suppose, suppose.... Was his life insured, she wondered" (026-163) — the daughter's accounting of loss in the ledger of survival.

## Loss and Mourning

Mourning in the novel is both a public ritual and a private negotiation. When [[edith-sidney|Edith]] becomes a widow, the material culture of grief is immediate: "She was getting Edith's mourning" (021-062), and [[laura-sloane|Laura]] frets over the aesthetics — "There's a love of a hat at Thurn's... if only we can get her to wear it" (021-063). But Laura's shallowness serves a dramatic purpose: her preoccupation with the surfaces of grief contrasts with [[deborahs-school|Deborah]]'s attempt to create a home around Edith's sorrow. "All would center on her in her grief" (021-108), the narrative notes, and Roger rails against the senselessness — "And for no cause! Just a trick of chance, a street accident!" (021-111).

Later, [[laura-sloane|Laura]] returns from abroad with "a mourning gown from Paris, a most alluring creation" (031-007), which Edith finds "simply indecent, insulting" (031-008). The gulf between the sisters is measured by their competing ideas of what mourning should look like. Laura treats it as a fashion; Edith experiences it as a violation.

At the novel's close, Roger's dying vision gathers the mourners: "he saw women mourning now, and Edith with her children turned to him her anxious eyes" (044-027). But the vision dissolves: "already she had gone" (044-028). The final mourning belongs to everyone, and to no one.

## Loss and Consolation

The novel is attentive to what might, and what cannot, console. Early in the narrative, the conventional religious answer — "You will live on in our children's lives" (004-140) — is offered but left ringing hollow. Roger finds no comfort in generational continuity; the loss of Judith is too absolute.

Yet the novel also traces the possibility that loss can be transformed. In a pivotal passage, Roger, sitting in the mountain landscape of his boyhood, feels the old certainty lift: "And the old haunting certainty, that her soul had died with her body, was gone. There came to him the feeling that he and his wife would meet again" (014-149). The passage is careful not to claim this as resolved faith — it poses the question: "Why did this hope come back to him? Was it all from the glory of the sun? Or was it from the presence... of those many other mortals, folk of his own flesh and blood, who at their deaths had gone to their graves to put on immortality? Or was this deepening faith in Roger simply a sign of his growing old age?" (014-151). The novel allows each of these explanations to coexist.

At the end, Roger arrives at a state beyond loss: "boundless faith in life resplendent, conquering death, and serenely sweeping him on — into what he did not fear" (014-281). The grammar is telling — "into what he did not fear" is the opposite of knowing; it is trust without certainty. The novel's final word on loss is not a recovery but a surrender.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]**: Loss defines his arc — from the [[death|loss]] of his wife and [[religion|faith]] at the novel's start to the final [[death|loss]] of his own life.
- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]**: Loss of her husband is described as almost manageable — a [[death|loss]] within the expected order of things.
- **[[bruce|Bruce]]**: His [[death|loss]]es are deeper and less named — of purpose, of future, of the self he was before [[the-great-war|the war]].
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]**: Loss of the illusions that had sustained her social ambition.
- **[[deborahs-school|Deborah]]**: Hers is the loss not of a spouse but of a settled future. After Bruce's death, "the whole life of his family had shifted its center" (021-107), and Deborah must find a home of her own instead of the life she had imagined.
- **[[judith-gale|Judith Gale]]**: Though dead before the novel begins, Judith is the absent center around whom every other loss is organized.
