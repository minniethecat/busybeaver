## Overview

War, and specifically the Great War ([[the-great-war|World War I]]), looms over the later chapters of *His [[family|Family]]* as a world-changing event that reaches into the domestic sphere of the Gale family. The war represents the violent entrance of modernity into the world of tradition and family that [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] has spent his life building, and it forces each [[nature|character]] to reckon with questions of duty, sacrifice, and the fragility of civilization. Unlike the slow transformations of immigration and [[urban-transformation|urbanization]] that the novel traces across decades, [[the-great-war|the war]] arrives as a rupture — sudden, total, and irreversible.

## Role in the Novel

The war enters the narrative gradually, at first as distant news from [[europe|Europe]]. The novel captures the strange suspended quality of the early war years in [[america|America]] — the conflict is happening elsewhere, yet it penetrates daily life through newspapers, conversations, and the anxious awareness that neutrality cannot last. [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]] asks [[roger-gale|Roger]] bluntly, "How has [[the-great-war|the war]] hit you, sir?" (025-066), a question that links the global catastrophe to the personal concerns of the family. Characters discuss "how they had eluded [[the-great-war|the war]]" (025-024) — a recognition that even in neutral [[america|America]], the conflict is inescapable.

For [[youth|the young]]er generation, [[the-great-war|the war]] represents both duty and danger. [[bruce|Bruce]], as a veteran, carries the physical and psychological marks of his service. The novel alludes to "splendid Breton soldier boys just going into action" (025-044), an image that captures both the romanticism with which [[the-great-war|the war]] was initially viewed and the terrible waste that would follow. [[bruce|Bruce]]'s return — wounded, changed, struggling to resume his [[work-and-vocation|career]] as an architect — is one of the novel's most concrete depictions of [[the-great-war|the war]]'s human cost.

The war accelerates the changes already underway in [[america|American]] society — the loosening of old constraints, the questioning of [[tradition|traditional values]], and the emergence of a harder, more cynical worldview. For [[roger-gale|Roger]], [[the-great-war|the war]] is the ultimate demonstration that the world he knew is passing away, replaced by forces he cannot control or fully understand. His daughters' generation inhabits a post-war world whose assumptions about duty, nation, and family differ fundamentally from his own.

## Key Manifestations

### The War and Business

[[roger-gale|Roger]]'s [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al life as the head of a firm is not insulated from [[the-great-war|the war]]'s effects. The economic disruptions, the shift in markets, and the broader uncertainty that [[the-great-war|the war]] creates all register in the novel's treatment of [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s [[work-and-vocation|work]]. The war reveals the interdependence of domestic prosperity and global stability — a connection that the novel's earlier chapters, focused on local and familial concerns, had only hinted at.

### Bruce's Wounds

[[bruce|Bruce]] functions in the novel as the embodied consequence of [[the-great-war|the war]]. His injuries are not merely physical; [[the-great-war|the war]] has altered his sense of possibility and his place in the world. His relationship with the Gale family — and particularly with [[roger-gale|Roger]] — is shadowed by the shared knowledge of what he has endured. The novel's treatment of [[bruce|Bruce]] avoids sentimentality: his recovery is partial, his future uncertain, his heroism unremarked except in the quiet persistence with which he continues to [[work-and-vocation|work]] and to [[love-and-courtship|love]].

### The End of an Era

The war serves a structural function in the novel: it marks the definitive end of the nineteenth-century world into which [[roger-gale|Roger]] was born. The steady verities of his [[childhood|youth]] — [[religion|faith]] in [[modernity|progress]], confidence in [[america|America]]'s exceptional destiny, the equation of [[work-and-vocation|work]] with virtue — do not survive [[the-great-war|the war]] intact. What remains, the novel suggests, is family: the one institution durable enough to outlast even the collapse of civilizations.

## Thematic Significance

The war in *His [[family|Family]]* functions as the culmination of the forces of change that the novel has been tracing. If immigration and [[urban-transformation|urbanization]] represent the slow transformation of [[america|American]] life, [[the-great-war|the war]] represents its sudden, violent acceleration. It is the event that definitively separates the [[generational-change|generations]]: [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s world of steady [[work-and-vocation|work]] and family [[duty|obligation]] cannot survive [[the-great-war|the war]]'s revelation of modern destructiveness. The war also sharpens the novel's questions about what it means to be an [[america|American]] in a world where national boundaries have become battle lines.
