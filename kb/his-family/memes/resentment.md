## Overview

Resentment in *His Family* is rarely explosive. It simmers beneath the surface of domestic life — a sullen bitterness that characters feel but seldom name. The novel traces resentment's quiet damage: how it calcifies into permanent estrangement, how it passes between generations, and how the very closeness of family makes its injuries most acute.

## Bitterness in Marriage and Loss

The novel registers bitterness most sharply in the aftermath of loss. Allan Baird's "voice had a ring of bitterness" (015-094) — a brief notation that gestures toward a deep well of hurt. After the death of his wife, a character reflects that "to do this was not easy at first, for his bitterness still rankled" (034-041), suggesting that grief and resentment are intertwined, that the dead can leave grievances as well as sorrow.

The bitterness of compromised marriage surfaces repeatedly: one character speaks of marriage "with a curious bitterness" (031-185), acknowledging that the institution itself can breed the resentments it is supposed to heal. The novel is attuned to the gap between the promise of domestic life and its disappointments, and bitterness is the emotional residue of that gap.

## Sullen Resistance and Domestic Unrest

Within the Gale household, resentment takes quieter forms. A character is described "watching him with a look of sullen bitterness" (014-163) — the word "sullen" capturing the particular quality of resentment that does not speak but does not forgive. The "old cook's growing resentment made it far from easy" (026-140) — a domestic detail that reveals how resentment, like housework, is cumulative and invisible until it reaches a breaking point.

## Social and Class Resentment

Beyond the domestic sphere, the novel registers resentment along class lines. "And I know some people who will be glad enough to take him in" (029-039) — a remark shot through with the bitterness of those who feel overlooked or undervalued. The changing social order of early twentieth-century New York creates new hierarchies, and those who lose status in the reshuffling — like Roger Gale — carry a resentment that is as much about class as it is about family.

The neighborhood's transformation from genteel enclave to immigrant district generates its own forms of resentment among longtime residents. Poole treats this bitterness not as admirable but as comprehensible — the natural response of people who built a world only to watch it become unrecognizable.

## The Generational Dimension

Roger Gale's own resentments — at his daughters' independence, at the changing world that has left him behind — are the novel's most sustained exploration of the theme. His bitterness is the bitterness of a man who built a world only to watch it be dismantled. The novel suggests that resentment is the shadow emotion of love: the more deeply one cares, the more acutely one can be wounded by the objects of that care.

Roger's resentment is amplified by his awareness that his own perspective is becoming obsolete. He cannot command his daughters as a patriarch of an earlier generation might have done, and his bitterness is in part a response to this powerlessness. The novel neither condemns nor excuses this — it simply observes, with the clarity of realism, how resentment operates as a quiet corrosive in family life.
