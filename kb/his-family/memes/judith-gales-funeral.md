# Judith Gale's Funeral

## Overview

Judith Gale's funeral is the ceremonial event that formalizes the loss around which the novel is built. It is referenced rather than narrated — the novel opens after the funeral has already occurred — but its presence is felt throughout. The funeral is the moment when Roger's private grief becomes public, when the family's new configuration (father and three daughters, without a mother) is publicly established, and when the question that drives the novel — how will this family survive its loss? — is first posed.

## Context and Build-up

Judith's illness and death were the subject of "terrible weeks" that preceded the funeral. The family's life before her death — the home she made, the relationships she nurtured — was shattered, and the funeral was the ritual that acknowledged the shattering.

## The Event

The funeral was conducted in the church, with the traditional rites and the assembled community. Roger's grief was profound — afterward, "death had come to his soul" (001-147) — and the funeral marked a threshold he could not cross back over. The daughters, still young, absorbed the loss in different ways: Edith through increased responsibility, Laura through withdrawal, Deborah through devotion.

## Consequences

The funeral is the narrative's point of origin. Everything that follows is a response to this loss, and Roger's subsequent efforts to "keep the family together" are an attempt to honor the promise he made before the funeral — perhaps at the funeral itself — to Judith.

## Thematic Significance

The funeral is the novel's primal scene of loss, establishing death as the antagonist against which family is the only defense.
