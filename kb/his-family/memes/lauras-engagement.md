# Laura Gale's Engagement

## Overview

Laura's engagement to Harold Sloane is the novel's central romantic plot — a courtship that Roger views with suspicion and Laura pursues with determination. Harold is wealthy, smooth, and from a different social world, and Roger "heartily damned this fellow Sloane for breaking into the family" (003-125). But Laura is of age, in love, and unwilling to let her father's reservations define her future. The engagement is the battleground on which the novel's generational conflict is most directly fought: who decides whom a daughter marries?

## Context and Build-up

Laura introduces Harold carefully — "Do you remember Harold Sloane?" she asks "a little strangely" (003-083). The strangeness betrays her awareness that this introduction will not be simple. Roger's response is immediate suspicion: "What's the matter with Sloane?" (004-048). The answer, unspoken but understood, is that Sloane represents everything Roger distrusts: inherited wealth, social confidence, a life that does not require struggle.

## The Event

The engagement period is marked by Roger's anxious scrutiny and Laura's quiet determination. "Only a few weeks ahead" of the wedding (023-146), Laura is caught between excitement and something like panic — the recognition that marriage will separate her from the family in ways she can only partly anticipate.

## Consequences

The engagement establishes the pattern of Roger's diminishing authority. He cannot prevent the marriage, and his inability to do so is a milestone in the novel's chronicle of patriarchal decline.
