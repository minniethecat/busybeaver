# Women's Roles

## Overview

*His [[family|Family]]* is, in significant measure, a novel about what it meant to be a woman in a time of rapid social transformation. [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s three daughters are not merely [[nature|character]]s in his story — they are three distinct answers to the question of what women can and should do with their lives. "To the eye they were grown women all," the novel observes of the three sisters (001-175), yet beneath the surface each represents a different response to the era's changing gender expectations. The novel traces these trajectories with a documentary precision rare in [[america|American]] fiction of its period.

## Manifestations

The novel maps women's roles across three life paths. **Domesticity**: [[edith-sidney|Edith]] embodies the traditional role of wife and mother, her [[home|household]] overflowing with [[childhood|children]]. Her life is circumscribed but dignified — she runs her home with competence and authority. **Social ambition**: [[laura-sloane|Laura]] pursues the glamour of wealth and fashion, "sumptuous young women smiled out upon the streets" from their limousines (003-181). Her path represents a modern version of a very old female strategy — marriage as mobility. **Vocation**: [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] rejects both domesticity and social climbing for a life of independent [[work-and-vocation|work]], her school a calling that gives her an identity beyond wifehood or motherhood.

The novel does not judge among these paths. Each has its costs and rewards. [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s domesticity brings [[childhood|children]] but constricts; [[laura-sloane|Laura]]'s ascent brings luxury but distances her from family; [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[freedom|independence]] brings purpose but loneliness. The novel's [[success|achievement]] is to make all three paths legible — to show that the question of women's roles admits no single answer.

## Characters and Perspectives

- **[[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]**: Women's traditional role as wife, mother, [[home|household]] manager. Her life is the baseline against which her sisters' departures are measured.
- **[[laura-sloane|Laura Sloane]]**: Women's role as social asset and aesthetic object — beautiful, desired, and restless.
- **[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]**: Women's emerging role as independent [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al and [[reform|social reform]]er — "a modern woman" (017-112) who defines herself through [[work-and-vocation|work]] rather than marriage.
- **[[judith-gale|Judith Gale]]** (in memory): The idealized wife and mother whose example both inspires and constrains her daughters.
