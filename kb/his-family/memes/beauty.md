## Overview

**Beauty** in *His [[family|Family]]* is closely associated with [[childhood|youth]] and vitality, particularly embodied in [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]'s daughters. It is a quality that [[roger-gale|Roger]] observes with mingled pride and melancholy -- beautiful because it is vivid, yet tinged with the awareness that it belongs to a generation moving beyond him. The novel presents beauty not as a static attribute but as a force that is at once life-affirming and elegiac, always seen through the gaze of someone conscious of time's passage. From the "shapely arms and shoulders" and "shimmering satins" of the opera house of Roger's youth (001-098) to the "purple gleaming pavement" under Laura's limousine (043-154), beauty traces an arc from remembered glamour to present radiance and, finally, to the intimations of loss that shadow every gleaming surface.

## Manifestations

The novel treats beauty as a life-affirming force. [[edith-sidney|Edith]] is described with "her beauty" as a defining trait (001-099), while [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] is appreciated through her father's loving gaze: "enjoying her beauty" (021-069). Later, [[roger-gale|Roger]] reflects on a younger woman with the thought "What an ardent gleaming beauty she was" (025-058), linking beauty to energy and [[love-and-courtship|passion]] -- an "ardent gleaming" quality that radiates outward.

The concept extends beyond physical appearance: beauty encompasses "life, its beauty, all it means" (004-160), suggesting a philosophical dimension where beauty stands for everything worth living for. Beauty in the novel is never static -- it is always seen through the lens of someone aware of life's transience.

Each of Roger's daughters embodies a different register of beauty. [[laura-sloane|Laura]] is the family's acknowledged beauty, "a joyous creature" whose radiant attractiveness is inseparable from her social ambition (001-154). Roger observes her with the thought "What an ardent gleaming beauty she was" as she returns from Europe "with bright new plumage fresh from the shops of the Rue de la Paix" (025-058). Her beauty is worldly, performed, and expensive -- an aesthetic asset she deploys in her ascent. [[edith-sidney|Edith]] is beautiful in a more domestic mode: "fresh and smiling, blooming with health" on the New Hampshire farm (014-144), her "pretty florid face" growing "flushed and radiant" at the prospect of Laura's wedding (004-217). [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], by contrast, is repeatedly described as *not* beautiful in the conventional sense -- she is defined by voice, work, and moral intensity rather than physical charm. Her beauty emerges in moments of transformation, as when she dresses for an evening at home and her niece [[betsy|Betsy]] exclaims, "Why, Auntie, how lovely you look!" (027-101), and Betsy later "thought, 'How beautiful she is'" while watching her aunt with admiration (027-149).

## Beauty and Transience

Beauty in *His Family* is inseparable from the awareness of its impermanence. Roger's earliest memory of beauty -- the opera house of his youth, with its "shimmering satins, dainty gloves and little jewelled slippers, shapely arms and shoulders, vivacious movements, nods and smiles" (001-098) -- is already framed as something lost, belonging to a vanished New York. This pattern recurs throughout the novel: beauty is most intensely perceived at the moment it is slipping away. When Roger watches Laura's "gay young daughter speeding up the Avenue, the purple gleaming pavement reflecting studded lines of lights" (043-154), he knows he will never see her again, and the gleaming beauty of the scene is heightened by its finality.

Even the natural world participates in this logic: the "clear beautiful afternoon toward the end of May" (038-001) that opens the chapter of Roger's last journey to the farm is beautiful precisely because it is fleeting -- "the orchards were pink and white on the hills" in a display that will not last.

This linkage of beauty to mortality finds its fullest expression in Deborah's speech at the concert: "I was thinking of hungry people -- millions of them, now, this minute -- not only here but in so many places -- concerts, movies, libraries. Hungry, oh, for everything -- life, its beauty, all it means" (004-160). Here beauty is the object of a universal hunger, the thing that makes life worth living, and yet it is defined by its absence as much as its presence. Deborah's insight is that beauty must be *given* -- through schools, institutions, the work of culture -- because it is not equally available, and its scarcity is a form of deprivation.

## Physical Beauty and Its Limits

The novel draws a sustained contrast between physical beauty and the deeper attractiveness of character. [[laura-sloane|Laura]] possesses "the glowing, coursing, vibrant life of her warm beautiful body" (021-077), and her mother Judith before her was described as having "eyes black, with gleaming lights; her lips warm and rich, alive" (003-015). This physical beauty is powerful but morally ambiguous: Laura's radiance coexists with selfishness, and Roger is troubled by the "intimate gleaming manner" (016-035) of her glances with her husband.

Physical beauty, the novel suggests, can be a force of disruption as much as delight.

Against this, Poole sets a beauty of character that has little to do with appearance. [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s work in the school brings her into contact with a "kind beautiful teacher" (010-074) whose beauty resides in her compassion. The "lovely old farm in a little village over in France" (006-057) is lovely not for its grandeur but for its simplicity. The New Hampshire mountains offer beauty in "the slate and granite crags in lovely pink and purple" (014-145) -- a beauty of endurance rather than glamour, the same mountains where young Roger "had discovered, even in those early years, that life was fresh, abundant, new" (001-073). This natural beauty is the novel's moral counterweight to Laura's Parisian glamour: one promises renewal, the other consumption.

## The Gleaming City

Beauty is not only a human attribute in *His Family*; it is a quality of the city itself. New York appears throughout the novel as a place of "gleaming" surfaces: the "gleaming pinnacle" of the tower whose great bell "slowly boomed the time away" (001-300), the "dark rolling torrent of cars, lights gleaming by the" hundreds (003-167), the "purple gleaming pavement" of the Avenue (043-154). This urban gleam is modern, electrical, and impersonal -- a beauty of surfaces that reflects the characters' own preoccupation with appearances. Yet it is also sublime: the city's scale and energy produce a kind of beauty that dwarfs individual lives even as it frames them. Roger, dying, watches the gleaming city from his window and sees in it both the world he helped build and the world that will continue without him.
