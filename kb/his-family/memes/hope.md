# Hope

## Overview

Hope in *His [[family|Family]]* is the counterweight to [[death|loss]]. As [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] confronts the deaths of those he [[love-and-courtship|love]]s and the transformation of the world he knows, [[dream|hope]] — often embodied in [[ediths-children|the next generation]] — provides the emotional resource that allows him to continue. The novel treats [[dream|hope]] not as naive optimism but as a hard-won orientation toward the future, earned through suffering and chosen in defiance of despair. Throughout the novel, Poole places [[dream|hope]] in tension with [[death|grief]], using the family's forward momentum to answer the retrospective pull of memory and [[grief|mourning]] (011-214).

Hope manifests most clearly in the novel's treatment of [[childhood|children]] and the future they represent. [[roger-gale|Roger]], having lost his wife [[judith-gale|Judith Gale]] and watched his daughters grow away from him, finds a renewed sense of purpose in the family's continuation. The presence of grand[[childhood|children]] — [[edith-sidney|Edith]]'s [[childhood|children]], including [[elizabeth-sidney|Elizabeth Sidney]] — offers a vision of life beyond his own. The novel returns repeatedly to the image of [[ediths-children|the next generation]] as the vessel for unrealized [[dream|hope]]s (001-146), a theme that culminates in the final gathering.

[[deborah-gale|Deborah Gale]]'s school is itself an institution built on [[dream|hope]] — the conviction that educating immigrant [[childhood|children]] can transform lives and communities (009-082). Her [[work-and-vocation|work]] represents an active [[dream|hope]], one that requires effort and sacrifice rather than passive waiting. In her long conversations with [[roger-gale|Roger]], [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] articulates a vision of [[progress|social progress]] that gives her life direction and meaning even as her personal circumstances grow more difficult.

[[bruce|Bruce]], too, despite his war wounds and [[work-and-vocation|profession]]al struggles, embodies a form of [[dream|hope]] through his commitment to building and creating as an architect. His resilience — returning to [[work-and-vocation|work]] after injury, pursuing his craft in a changing [[new-york-city|New York]] — reflects the novel's broader argument that [[dream|hope]] is inseparable from purposeful action.

The novel does not end with resolution but with a quiet [[peace|acceptance]] that looks forward. In the final chapter, the family gathers — a scene that affirms continuity and the possibility of renewal even after [[death|loss]]. The quiet domestic rituals that close the novel are themselves an expression of [[dream|hope]]: the [[religion|belief]] that ordinary life, sustained across [[generational-change|generations]], is meaning enough.

## Key Manifestations

### Hope and Youth

The novel's most vibrant expressions of [[dream|hope]] accompany its depictions of [[youth|young people]]. Poole describes the "tremendous joyousness, of gaiety, fresh [[dream|hope]]s and [[dream|dreams]], of leaping young" life that surrounds [[youth|the young]]er generation (010-137). This energy stands in deliberate contrast to [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s more somber, retrospective consciousness, creating a tension between forward-looking [[childhood|youth]] and backward-looking age that drives much of the novel's emotional architecture.

### Hope Against Despair

Poole does not present [[dream|hope]] as an easy emotion. It surfaces most powerfully in moments of crisis — when "all your [[dream|hope]]s are sinking low" (011-214), [[nature|character]]s must choose whether to persist. The novel's treatment of [[dream|hope]] is notable for its refusal to separate it from the experience of suffering. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s [[dream|hope]] for his daughters' futures coexists with his [[death|grief]] for his wife [[judith-gale|Judith]]; [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s [[dream|hope]] for her school coexists with her physical exhaustion and financial strain. The novel suggests that [[dream|hope]] is not the absence of despair but the decision to act in its presence.

### Bruce's Return

[[bruce|Bruce]]'s trajectory in the novel — returning wounded from war, struggling to reestablish his [[work-and-vocation|career]], yet ultimately finding purpose in his [[work-and-vocation|work]] and family — offers one of the novel's most concrete narratives of [[dream|hope]] realized through perseverance (006-037). His story, like [[deborah-gale|Deborah]]'s, grounds the novel's thematic treatment of [[dream|hope]] in the specific material circumstances of its [[nature|character]]s' lives.

## Thematic Significance

Hope in the novel is intimately tied to the theme of generational change. The older generation's [[dream|hope]] is invested in [[youth|the young]]; [[youth|the young]] carry [[dream|hope]] forward into an uncertain future. Poole presents [[dream|hope]] not as a philosophical abstraction but as a practical necessity of [[family|family life]] — the force that keeps parents [[work-and-vocation|work]]ing, [[childhood|children]] growing, and communities cohering despite the forces that pull them apart. In this sense, [[dream|hope]] is the novel's answer to the questions of [[death|mortality]] and change that haunt its protagonist.

The novel's title — *His [[family|Family]]* — itself suggests this orientation. [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s identity is defined not by his individual [[success|achievement]]s but by his place within a lineage that extends both backward and forward in time. Hope, in this context, is the emotional correlate of family continuity: the feeling that corresponds to the fact that life goes on.
