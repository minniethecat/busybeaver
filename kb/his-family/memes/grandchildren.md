# Grandchildren

The **grandchildren** in *His Family* represent the next generation — the inheritors of the Gale legacy and, for [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], a source of hope amid the dissolution of his immediate family. They embody the novel's concern with what endures across generational lines.

## Roger's Grandson

Roger's grandson is "Roger's favorite grandson" (002-058), introduced early in the novel as a point of warmth in the aging patriarch's life. When Roger is with "his small grandson Roger felt a current of warm" (006-071) affection that contrasts with the tensions of his relationships with his adult daughters. The grandson also serves a narrative function: Roger "pictured George, his grandson, at this moment driving down" (014-021) the road, a projection of the future into which Roger himself will not travel. In a moment of characteristic directness, the grandson asks, "Do you see it?" (014-036), and later Roger "abruptly he turned to his grandson" (014-188), the gesture suggesting both connection and the recognition of time passing. In the novel's closing pages, Roger "saw his small grandson growing up" (043-201), a final image that frames the grandchild as the vessel of continuity — the Gale name and values will live on, even if Roger will not.

## Symbolic Significance

The grandchildren function less as fully realized characters than as symbols of continuity and renewal. Roger's relationship with his grandson offers him a version of fatherhood uncomplicated by the ideological and generational conflicts that define his relationships with his adult daughters. While [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], [[edith-sidney|Edith]], and [[laura-sloane|Laura]] challenge Roger's worldview, his grandson simply receives his affection — and in doing so, provides Roger with an emotional refuge. The novel's treatment of grandchildren also reflects the broader theme of [[generational-change]]: each generation inherits from the last, but also transforms what it receives.
