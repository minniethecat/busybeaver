## Identity

Doctor Lake is a minor character in *His Family*, referred to as "the big child specialist" whose care [[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]]'s children had been under for years (028-144)--(028-145). He serves as a medical authority figure in the novel, particularly in moments of family health crises. Although he appears on stage only briefly in Chapter XXXV, his influence is felt across multiple chapters as the Gale family navigates the illnesses of its youngest members.

## Medical Authority

Doctor Lake's pronouncements carry significant weight in the Gale family. In Chapter XXVIII, when [[johnny-geer|Johnny Geer]] is ill, Edith invokes Doctor Lake's opinion to argue that the boy should be removed from the house, citing "very serious risk" for every day he remains (028-144)--(028-147), (028-160)--(028-161). This recommendation creates tension between Edith and [[roger-gale|Roger]], who "heartily damned Doctor Lake" (028-147). The force of Roger's reaction reveals how much authority Lake commands -- enough to challenge Roger's own judgment about what is best for his household.

Edith's reliance on Doctor Lake's opinion also illuminates her character: she is a mother who trusts medical expertise over instinct, who would rather risk offending her father than disregard a specialist's warning. When she tells Roger, "Then I'm telling you that Doctor Lake said there was very serious risk" (028-160), she is wielding Lake's authority as a shield.

> "Doctor Lake didn't think I was. ... I talked to him to-day on the telephone, and he said we should get John out of the house." (028-144)--(028-146)

## In Family Crisis

Doctor Lake reappears in Chapter XXXV during a critical health crisis involving [[bruce|Bruce]], Edith's youngest child. When Bruce develops pneumonia with a temperature of 105.4 degrees, Edith calls Doctor Lake despite her earlier hesitation -- she had previously avoided calling him for reasons left implicit but suggestive of cost, pride, or some tension (035-013), (035-026)--(035-027).

The delay in calling him becomes a source of regret, as both Roger and Edith acknowledge they "should have had Lake long before" (035-034)--(035-035). This admission carries the weight of parental guilt -- the fear that a timely call might have made the difference.

When Lake finally arrives, he enters with professional gravity: "'Very well, sir.' And Lake entered the room, with Edith" (035-057). The narrator notes that in Lake's eyes "sometimes showed an ironic light" (035-060), a subtle characterization that hints at a man who has seen enough suffering to maintain a certain detachment. Later, Deborah mentions to Roger, "She had Lake to-night" (035-092), and the narrative notes that Roger "explained to him, of course, about Lake" (035-168), suggesting that the doctor's involvement and his findings become a significant topic of family discussion after Bruce's death.

> "I've called Doctor Lake," she said. "I've just taken Bruce's temperature. It's a hundred and five and two fifths." (035-026)--(035-027)

## Professional Persona

Doctor Lake is never fully characterized as an individual -- he has no backstory, no family, no personal details. He exists purely in his professional capacity. This is not a flaw in characterization but a deliberate choice: Lake represents the impersonal authority of modern medicine, which enters the intimate space of the family at moments of crisis, delivers its verdict, and departs. His "ironic light" (035-060) suggests a man who knows the limits of his power but continues to exercise it nonetheless.

The fact that he is consulted by telephone (028-144) rather than through house calls underscores the novel's theme of modernization -- the doctor is no longer the family friend who drops by for tea, but a specialist reachable by the new technology of the telephone, summoned in emergencies.

## Significance

Doctor Lake represents the growing authority of medical professionals in early 20th-century family life. His role as the family's trusted child specialist -- consulted by telephone and deferred to in moments of crisis -- reflects the modernization of healthcare and the displacement of traditional home remedies by professional medicine. In a novel about generational change, Lake embodies the new, impersonal expertise that was reshaping how families understood and responded to illness.
