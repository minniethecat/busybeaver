## Overview

**Change** is a central theme in *His Family*, registered most acutely by [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], an aging man who finds himself caught between the [[new-york-city|New York]] of his youth and a rapidly transforming world. The novel opens with the city itself "in the throes of change" (001-293), establishing transformation as both setting and theme.

## Change in the City

The physical transformation of [[new-york-city|New York]] mirrors the social and familial changes Roger confronts. Walking through familiar streets, he observes how "twinkling all was changed" (010-012), the city's relentless growth erasing the landmarks of his past. Yet some things persist — Roger notes "how unchanged and yet how new" (014-074) certain places remain, capturing the novel's ambivalence toward change: it is neither wholly destructive nor wholly renewing, but a complex mix of loss and possibility.

## Change in the Family

The most dramatic change Roger confronts is in his daughters. The "change in Edith was something tremendous" (004-213), marking her transformation from his little girl into a society woman with her own ambitions and judgments. Similarly, [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] "had undergone a striking change" (005-145) as she found purpose in her schoolwork, redirecting her energies from family concern to public service. For Roger, these changes in his children register as both loss and quiet pride.
