# Roger Gale

> "Roger Gale was a rugged heavy man not quite sixty years of age, with broad, massive features already deeply furrowed." (001-026)

## Character and Origins

[[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] is the narrative center of Ernest Poole's *His Family*, and the novel is fundamentally the story of his consciousness — his memories, his anxieties, his slow reckoning with a world that will not stay still. Born on a farm in the New Hampshire mountains, "this deep zest in Roger Gale had been bred in his boyhood" among pines and open country (001-069). His ancestors were "many generations of stolid folk" whose endurance formed his character before he ever came to the city.

At the novel's opening, Roger is a widower of nearly sixty, his wife [[judith-gale|Judith]] years dead, his three daughters grown and increasingly foreign to him. The novel traces his arc from grief-stricken withdrawal — "so death had come to Roger's soul" (001-147) — through incremental re-engagement with his daughters' lives, his city's transformation, and his own mortality. He is a 19th-century man confronting the 20th century, and the confrontation reshapes him.

## The Widower's Gaze

Roger's perspective structures the novel. We see the city, his daughters, and the changing times entirely through his eyes — and his eyes are those of a man who has loved and lost deeply. "The memory now was the story of his family" (001-149): Roger's consciousness is a museum of his wife Judith, and every present event echoes against what she would have thought or felt.

This widower's gaze gives the novel its distinctive emotional register. Roger is neither sentimental nor cynical; he is watchful, wry, and increasingly aware of his own irrelevance. When Laura arrives home with rouged lips, he is "startled" — "for his daughter's lips were rouged" (003-017) — and the shock is not moral outrage but generational vertigo. His child has become a stranger.

## The Father and His Daughters

Each of Roger's relationships with his three daughters reveals a different facet of his character:

**[[edith-sidney|Edith]]**, the eldest, is the daughter Roger most easily understands. She marries, raises children, and represents biological and domestic continuity. Through Edith, Roger sees "new lives in his family" (004-202), and her practicality mirrors his own. She is the daughter who requires the least translation.

**[[deborah-gale|Deborah]]**, the middle daughter who stays at home longest, is Roger's mirror and his mystery. She shares his stubbornness and his moral seriousness, but directs them toward public service rather than private accumulation. Her "big family" — the school she founds for immigrant children — baffles Roger even as it commands his grudging admiration. "Down in my school we've a family of seven hundred children" (009-030), Deborah tells him, and Roger cannot decide whether this is a rejection of his family or its expansion. Their [[deborah-and-rogers-reconciliation|eventual reconciliation]] is the novel's emotional climax.

**[[laura-sloane|Laura]]**, the youngest, is the daughter Roger understands least. Her social ambition, her marriage to [[harold-sloane|Harold Sloane]], and her restless modernity are all foreign to his values. She is "wild as a hawk, gadding about to parties" (002-130), and Roger's incomprehension is tinged with a father's hurt. Yet her departure — to Europe, to society, to a life far from his — is the fact he must ultimately accept.

## Business and the City

Roger is a businessman, and his working life grounds the novel in material reality. His office, his partners, and his commercial instincts are part of his identity — the competence that defines him as much as his fatherhood. The novel shows him at work: concerned about his business, navigating the commercial culture of [[new-york-city|New York City]], and watching the city transform around him.

[[rogers-office|Roger's office]] represents the professional self that exists alongside the domestic self. He is a man of two worlds — the counting-house and the family dinner table — and the novel suggests that his wholeness depends on maintaining both. His [[rogers-illness|serious illness]] mid-novel threatens this equilibrium, forcing a reckoning with his own fragility.

## The Final Acceptance

Roger's character arc completes in the novel's closing chapters. His "final acceptance" is not a conversion — he does not become a modern man — but a reconciliation with limits. "Oh, Roger Gale," says [[allan-baird|Allan Baird]] softly (037-158), and the naming is significant: Roger has become, by the end, a man whom others see clearly, stripped of his own defenses. His final vision encompasses "innumerable lives, the many generations, of his family" — a widening of the possessive "his" into something more generous.

The novel's title, *His Family*, announces that everything we see is filtered through Roger's consciousness. His growth is the novel's growth: from ownership to acceptance, from grief to peace, from the narrow family of blood to a broader family of human connection.
