## Overview

Elizabeth Sidney is the young daughter of [[edith-sidney|Edith]] and [[george-sidney|George Sidney]], and a granddaughter of [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]]. As a member of the novel's second generation, she appears in several domestic scenes that illustrate the continuance of family life beyond the central conflicts of Roger's daughters. She is described as "blonde and short" (006-042), and her presence in the narrative grounds the story in the rhythms of a growing family.

## Life and Role in the Novel

Elizabeth appears primarily in domestic settings, often alongside her parents. At a family dinner early in the novel, she sits at the table with the adults, "blonde and short and rather" (006-042), and is noted as being "neither quick nor resourceful" when put on the spot (006-048). Despite her limited role, her presence grows as the novel progresses: by the later chapters she has ideas of her own — what her family calls "her crazy idea" (023-098) — and she shows an eagerness to participate in conversations (027-118).

As a young woman, Elizabeth takes on responsibilities in the household. During a period of difficulty, her mother [[edith-sidney|Edith]] "kept Elizabeth home from school to help" (035-015), suggesting both the family's financial pressures and Elizabeth's growing role. In the novel's final chapter, she appears among the family gathered together — "[[bruce|Bruce]] came in with [[edith-sidney|Edith]], and [[george-sidney|George]] and young Elizabeth" (044-006) — representing the continuity of family across generations, a central theme of the novel.

## Relationships

Elizabeth's primary relationships are within the immediate Sidney household. Her mother [[edith-sidney|Edith Sidney]] is the dominant figure in her life, managing both her education and her role in the household (035-015). Her father [[george-sidney|George Sidney]] appears alongside her in several domestic scenes — they "tramped down and came back at dusk" together (024-003). She also has a younger brother, "little Bob" (027-177), with whom she shares the experience of being sent "back in the schools where they belong" (027-177). Her relationship with her grandfather [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]] is glimpsed through family gatherings, most notably in the final chapter (044-006).
