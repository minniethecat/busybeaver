## Overview

Music in *His [[family|Family]]* functions as a marker of generational and cultural change. For [[roger-gale|Roger Gale]], music is an unfamiliar language — something he observes from the outside, unable to grasp its emotional pull. For his daughter [[deborah-gale|Deborah]], by contrast, music is a channel of profound feeling and a symbol of the new world her father struggles to understand. The novel's treatment of music crystallizes the broader theme of an older generation confronting the emotional and aesthetic landscape of modernity.

## Manifestations

The novel's most extended treatment of music occurs during the symphony concert in Chapter IV (004-099–004-167). [[roger-gale|Roger]] attends reluctantly, having "refused before" similar invitations out of a "liking for music" that was, in truth, indifference (004-099). At the concert, [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] whispers to him, "See what the music does to them" (004-114), drawing his attention to the audience's rapt absorption. The "surging music of Tschaikovsky's" (004-128) fills the hall with what the narrator describes as "tumultuous music" stirring "hungers" in its listeners (004-129). [[roger-gale|Roger]] watches [[deborah-gale|Deborah]] even after the music ceases, noting that she remains "still under the spell of the music" (004-157).

Elsewhere, music appears as a social marker: [[bruce|Bruce]] attends a "Broadway musical show" (005-011), and the Astor Roof is noted for its "musical" entertainments (006-187). The novel's minor [[nature|character]]s include "musicians" among the lecturers and actors of [[new-york-city|the city]]'s cultural life (008-083). These references place music within the broader tapestry of modern urban experience that [[roger-gale|Roger]]'s [[childhood|children]] embrace and that he observes with quiet bewilderment.
