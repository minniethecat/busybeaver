# Forgiveness

**Forgiveness** in *His Family* operates as both a personal plea and a familial imperative — characters ask for it, offer it, and withhold it in ways that define the emotional architecture of the Gale household. It is closely tied to the novel's exploration of [[duty]], [[family-themes|family]], and the strains of generational change.

## In the Novel

The language of forgiveness appears at moments of interpersonal crisis. [[edith-sidney|Edith]] asks her father, "You'll forgive my — preaching to you, dad?" (024-181), revealing her awareness that her moral earnestness strains their relationship. After Laura's death, the surviving sisters reach toward reconciliation: "Look here, Laura, if I've been hard, forgive me — please — and let me help" (031-179). A more pragmatic view comes from a minor character who urges, "Let her show him she's ready to forgive! That's only Christian, ain't it?" (032-099). In the novel's later chapters, "a note of good-humored forgiveness crept into" (033-016) the conversation between Roger and his daughter, suggesting that forgiveness, when it arrives, is less a dramatic event than a gradual softening.

## Key Moments

Forgiveness in the novel is intertwined with [[death]] and loss. Laura's death is the pivotal event that makes forgiveness urgent — before her death, the family operated on unspoken resentments; afterward, the survivors must either forgive or be consumed by [[remorse]]. Edith's marriage to [[bruce|Bruce]] and Deborah's devotion to her school both create distance from Roger, and forgiveness becomes the mechanism by which those distances are bridged, however imperfectly. The novel's treatment of forgiveness is notably unsentimental: it is not a resolution but a process, one that continues into the final chapters as Roger and his surviving daughters "drew close" (037-010) in shared grief.

## Themes

Forgiveness in Poole's treatment is not easy or automatic. It must be actively sought, and it often comes too late — or not at all. The novel suggests that the capacity to forgive is tied to the capacity to understand the pressures that [[modernity]] and [[change]] have placed on family relationships. The Christian language of forgiveness invoked by characters ("That's only Christian," 032-099) sits alongside a more secular, pragmatic understanding that forgiveness is necessary for family survival rather than for moral purity.
