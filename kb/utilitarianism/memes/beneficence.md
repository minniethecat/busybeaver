## Definition

**Beneficence** in Mill's utilitarian ethics is the moral duty to promote the well-being of others through positive action, as distinct from the negative duty not to harm. In his analysis of justice (Chapter V), Mill classifies beneficence as an "imperfect obligation" — one that society may rightly encourage but cannot justly compel in particular instances. Unlike duties of justice, which correlate with assignable rights, "in the case of charity or beneficence, which we are indeed bound to practise, no one has a right to our charity in particular" [005-016]. Beneficence is morally required in the general disposition but not enforceable in specific acts.

## Key Arguments

- **Imperfect versus perfect obligations**: Mill draws a sharp distinction between justice and beneficence: "justice involves something which it is not only right to do, and wrong not to do, but which some individual person can claim from us as his moral right. No one has a moral right to our generosity or beneficence" [005-017]. Justice is a perfect obligation (enforceable, with correlative rights); beneficence is an imperfect obligation (meritorious but not compellable).

- **The duty of positive beneficence**: Mill acknowledges that society has been "inculcating on each other the duty of positive beneficence" [005-035] throughout history. The general practice of beneficence is clearly conducive to the general happiness, and "they have an unmistakable interest in" promoting it. The duty is real, even though its application to particular cases cannot be precisely specified.

- **The enforcement boundary**: Mill argues that the distinction between justice and beneficence tracks what society may legitimately enforce. "We do not call anything wrong unless we mean to imply that a person ought to be punished in some way for doing it" [005-014]. Since we do not punish someone for failing to be charitable in a specific instance — only for a general failure of benevolence — beneficence inhabits the moral space between the obligatory and the supererogatory.

- **Beneficence and utility**: The utilitarian foundation of beneficence is straightforward: acts of beneficence promote the general happiness. But utility alone cannot determine when beneficence should be enforced, because "reasons of prudence, or the interest of other people, may militate against actually exacting it" [005-016] in particular cases.

## Related Concepts

- [[duty]] — beneficence is an imperfect duty: morally required in general but not compellable in particular
- [[obligation]] — the binding force of beneficence is moral suasion, not legal compulsion
- [[rights]] — unlike justice, beneficence does not correlate with any assignable individual right
