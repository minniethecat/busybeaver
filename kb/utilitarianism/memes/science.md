## Definition

For Mill, **science** — particularly the "science of morals" — denotes the systematic, inductive study of ethical principles modeled on the natural sciences. Mill argues that ethics can and should be a science: its first principles are derived from observation and experience, its particular truths precede and support its general theory, and its method is inductive rather than intuitive [001-004].

## Key Arguments

### Ethics as an Inductive Science

Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by situating ethics among "the first principles of all the sciences" [001-002]. He observes that even in the most advanced sciences, practitioners continue to debate fundamentals, and that "the particular truths precede the general theory" rather than being deduced from it [001-003]. This observation serves his larger argument: that the absence of consensus on ethical first principles does not disqualify ethics from being a science.

### The Science of Morals

Mill explicitly endorses the "inductive" school's claim "that there is a science of morals" [001-004]. For the inductivist, moral judgments — "right and wrong, as well as truth and falsehood" — are "questions of observation and experience" [001-004]. This epistemic stance grounds utilitarian ethics in empirical investigation of what actually conduces to happiness, rather than in a priori intuition.

### Elementary Notions and Systematic Progress

Like any mature science, moral science must clarify "the elementary notions with which the science is conversant; and their relations to one another" [001-003]. Mill sees his project as contributing to this clarification: by defining utility, happiness, justice, and their interrelations, he aims to advance moral science toward the systematic rigor of the natural sciences.

### The Practical Dimension

Unlike purely theoretical sciences, moral science has an irreducibly practical dimension: its aim is not merely to understand conduct but to guide it. Mill notes that moralists seldom "attempt to make out a list of the a priori principles which are to serve as the premises of the science," and "still more rarely do they make any effort to reduce those various principles to one first principle" [001-005]. His principle of utility is offered as precisely this unifying first principle.
