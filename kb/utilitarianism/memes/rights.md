## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **rights** are claims that individuals possess, grounded in the principle of utility. A right is something "which society ought to defend me in the possession of" ([002-025]). Rights derive their force not from abstract or natural law but from the general happiness they protect — when a right is violated, the harm extends beyond the individual to the security of all.

## Key Arguments

- **Utility as the foundation of rights.** Mill argues that having a right is to have something whose protection is justified by "the general utility" ([002-025]). Rights are not self-evident or God-given; they are social constructs that serve the greatest happiness.

- **The conflict-resolution function.** When rights clash, utility provides the "standard to which conflicting rights and duties can be referred" ([002-037]). No right is absolute — each must be weighed against others by reference to the overall happiness produced.

- **Legal rights and their enforcement.** The distinction between legal and moral rights is central. Legal rights are enforced by law and "penal sanction" ([005-005]), whereas moral rights are upheld by social opinion and conscience. Impartiality in adjudicating rights claims is "of course obligatory" ([005-008]).

- **Equal protection of rights.** The principle of "giving equal protection to the rights of all" is maintained by those who believe in the greatest happiness principle ([005-009]). Equality before the law and in the distribution of rights is a direct corollary of utilitarian impartiality.
