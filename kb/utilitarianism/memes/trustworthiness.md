# Trustworthiness

**Trustworthiness** — the quality of being reliable in assertion and faithful in obligation — operates in Mill's thought on two levels: as an epistemic virtue grounding moral knowledge, and as a practical requirement of social morality. Without trustworthiness, neither philosophical reasoning nor the bonds of social duty can be sustained.

## Epistemic Trustworthiness

Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by addressing the epistemological foundations of ethics. He argues that the first principles of conduct can be known through rational inquiry "without impairing at all, the trustworthiness of the conclusions of those sciences" [001-002]. Just as the trustworthiness of scientific knowledge survives philosophical scrutiny of its first principles, so too does moral knowledge. The reliability of our moral judgments does not require an unquestioned axiom.

## Trustworthiness of Human Assertion

Mill acknowledges that moral discourse depends on the credibility of human testimony. He warns against anything that works "towards weakening the trustworthiness of human assertion, which is... the principal test of truth" [002-031]. When the sincerity of moral claims is undermined — by hypocrisy, self-deception, or corruption — the very fabric of ethical reasoning is damaged.

## Trust and Social Obligation

In the practical sphere, trustworthiness manifests in the fulfilment of commitments. Mill notes the gravity of betraying trust: "he who betrays a friend that trusts him, is guilty of" a serious moral wrong [002-024]. This breach is not merely a private failing but a violation of the expectations on which social cooperation depends. The magistrate, too, must be trustworthy: Mill observes that society must have reason for "trusting the magistrate with so unlimited" a power [005-013].
