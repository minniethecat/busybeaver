# Judgment

**Judgment**, in Mill's utilitarian framework, refers to the act of applying general moral principles to particular cases. It is the bridge between abstract ethical theory and concrete moral decision-making.

Mill distinguishes sharply between the *source* of moral principles and the *faculty* of moral judgment. The former is a matter of ethical theory; the latter a practical capacity honed by experience. This distinction allows him to defend the principle of utility as the ultimate standard while acknowledging that competent practical judgment requires more than deductive application of a formula.

## General Principles and Particular Cases

Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by observing that both the intuitive and inductive schools "agree that the morality of individual action is not a question of direct perception, but of the application of a law to an individual case" [001-004].

Moral judgment, on this shared view, is not a perceptual act but an act of *application* — subsuming a particular action under a general rule. The dispute between schools concerns only the *origin* of the general rules, not the structure of judgment itself.

This shared commitment places judgment at the center of ethical practice. Even if one accepts the principle of utility as the ultimate standard, the work of moral life consists largely in judging which actions promote utility in concrete circumstances.

## Competent Judges

Mill's theory of qualitative hedonism relies on the concept of the **competent judge** — someone qualified by experience to compare different pleasures. He appeals to "the judgment of those who are qualified by knowledge of both" [002-010] to determine which pleasures are higher.

The competent judge is not a moral expert in the sense of possessing special theoretical knowledge, but someone with the relevant *experience* — someone who has actually felt both kinds of pleasure and can therefore compare them.

Mill takes this further: "From this verdict of the only competent judges I apprehend there can be no appeal" [002-010]. The judgment of the experienced carries an authority that cannot be overturned by abstract argument. This is a pragmatic commitment — the only evidence we can have about the relative quality of pleasures is the testimony of those who know them.

## Judgment and Feeling

In a crucial passage, Mill argues that the preference for higher pleasures is not a matter of pure reason but of "the feelings and judgment of the experienced" [002-011]. When people who know both intellectual and bodily pleasures prefer the intellectual, even knowing it brings less immediate gratification, this settled preference constitutes the evidence for qualitative superiority.

This appeal to feeling as a component of judgment is significant. Mill does not picture moral judgment as dispassionate calculation; it involves the trained sensibilities of the judging person, much as aesthetic judgment requires developed taste.

## Fallibility of Judgment

Mill acknowledges that judgment can err. He observes that "wrong judgments are occasionally suggested" [005-002] even by principles that are in general sound. The fallibility of judgment does not, however, undermine the enterprise of moral reasoning — it only cautions against dogmatic certainty.

He also notes that the judgment that someone has a right to something "admits of several exceptions" [005-005], indicating that rights-claims require nuanced judgment rather than mechanical application.
