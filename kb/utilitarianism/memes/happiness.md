# Happiness

**Happiness** is the ultimate end of utilitarian ethics — the *summum bonum* that serves as the criterion for all moral judgment. In Mill's formulation, happiness is defined as "pleasure and the absence of pain" and unhappiness as "pain and the privation of pleasure" [002-003]. The doctrine that actions are "right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" forms the core of the utilitarian standard [002-003].

## The Greatest Happiness Principle

Mill's **Greatest Happiness Principle** (also called the **Principle of Utility**) holds that "actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003]. This principle takes its name from Bentham, who, as Mill notes, "latterly called it the greatest happiness principle" [001-006].

Crucially, the utilitarian standard is "not the agent's own greatest happiness, but the greatest amount of happiness altogether" [002-011]. This makes utilitarianism an **impartial** and **altruistic** doctrine: the agent must weigh their own happiness equally with that of every other affected person.

## Higher and Lower Pleasures

Mill's most celebrated contribution to utilitarian theory is his distinction between **higher** and **lower** pleasures — a qualitative, not merely quantitative, ranking of satisfactions:

> Human beings have faculties more elevated than the animal appetites, and, when once made conscious of them, do not regard anything as happiness which does not include their gratification. [002-005]

Mill argues that the pleasures of "the intellect, of the feelings and imagination, and of the moral sentiments" possess "a much higher value as pleasures than to those of mere sensation" [002-005]. This distinction answers the charge that utilitarianism is "a doctrine worthy only of swine" by showing that human happiness involves qualitatively superior satisfactions [002-005].

The famous verdict of the "competent judges" — those who have experienced both higher and lower pleasures — gives us the standard for ranking:

> It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied. [002-006]

## The Sense of Dignity

Mill identifies a **sense of dignity** as essential to human happiness. This sense is "so essential a part of the happiness of those in whom it is strong that nothing which conflicts with it could be, otherwise than momentarily, an object of desire to them" [002-008].

A being of higher faculties "requires more to make him happy, is capable probably of more acute suffering," yet "he can never really wish to sink into what he feels to be a lower grade of existence" [002-008].

## Happiness as an Attainable End

Against objectors who claim happiness is unattainable, Mill defends a **moderate and realistic** conception:

> The happiness which they meant was not a life of rapture, but moments of such, in an existence made up of few and transitory pains, many and various pleasures, with a decided predominance of the active over the passive, and having as the foundation of the whole not to expect more from life than it is capable of bestowing. [002-013]

The "main constituents of a satisfied life" are **tranquillity** and **excitement**, either of which "by itself is often found sufficient for the purpose" [002-013]. These two are "in natural alliance" — the prolongation of either being "a preparation for, and exciting a wish for, the other" [002-013].

## Self-Sacrifice and Happiness

Mill addresses the paradox of **self-sacrifice**: how can utilitarianism, which makes happiness the end, praise those who sacrifice their own happiness? His answer is that self-sacrifice is noble only when it serves the happiness of others:

> A sacrifice which does not increase, nor tend to increase, the sum total of happiness it considers as wasted. The only self-renunciation which it applauds is devotion to the happiness, or to some of the means of happiness, of others. [002-015]
