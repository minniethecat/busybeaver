## Definition

In Mill's treatment, **uncertainty** in the moral domain is an epistemic condition arising from the absence of an acknowledged first principle. Mill observes that "similar confusion and uncertainty, and in some cases similar discordance" [001-002] exist in ethics as in the speculative sciences, treating uncertainty not as a reason for skepticism but as a normal stage in the development of any science — one that is resolved through the adoption of a unifying first principle.

## Key Arguments

### Uncertainty and the Need for a First Principle

Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by diagnosing the state of moral philosophy: the lack of an acknowledged first principle has left ethical reasoning in a condition of "confusion and uncertainty" [001-002]. This diagnosis serves as the argumentative motivation for his entire project: the principle of utility is offered as the remedy to moral uncertainty, the Archimedean point from which systematic ethical reasoning can proceed.

### Uncertainty About Duration

In a more psychologically inflected passage, Mill notes that rational persons do not allow "the uncertainty of their duration" to prevent them from pursuing happiness [002-021]. The uncertainty of life's span is a fact of the human condition, but it does not paralyze action; similarly, the uncertainties inherent in moral reasoning do not justify abandoning the project of ethical inquiry. Pragmatic engagement with uncertainty — proceeding on the best available evidence while acknowledging its limits — is the appropriate response.

### Uncertainty and the Inductive Method

Mill's inductive approach to ethics treats uncertainty as a transitional state. Just as the natural sciences progressed from uncertainty to systematic knowledge through the accumulation and generalization of particular observations, so too can moral science overcome its current uncertainties through the patient application of inductive method. The principle of utility is both the product of this inductive process and the instrument for its further refinement.
