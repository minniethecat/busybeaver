# Affection

**Affection**, in Mill's utilitarian ethics, designates the personal and social bonds — love, attachment, fellow-feeling — that constitute essential ingredients of a satisfied human life and serve as a foundation for the internal sanction of morality. Mill treats affection as both a private good (a constituent of individual happiness) and a bridge to the social feelings on which utilitarian morality depends.

## Affection as a Constituent of Happiness

In surveying the main elements of a satisfied life, Mill identifies two principal constituents: tranquillity and excitement. But he immediately notes that these are not exhaustive. Affection supplies a third dimension of well-being — the attachments that give life meaning beyond bare contentment. Those "who leave after them objects of personal affection, and especially those who have also cultivated a fellow-feeling with the collective interests of mankind" retain a source of value that does not dwindle as selfish interests terminate [002-016].

## The Cultivation of Fellow-Feeling

Mill denies that "any human being should be a selfish egoist, devoid of every feeling or care but those which center in his own miserable individuality" [002-017]. He insists that private affections can be genuine and sincere, and that something "far superior" to selfish egoism is attainable through moral cultivation. This conviction underlies his argument that the utilitarian standard, far from encouraging selfishness, requires the agent to extend concern beyond the self.

## Affection and the Sanction of Utility

The principle of utility draws its binding force, in part, from the same psychological mechanisms that sustain personal affection. Mill argues that "the principle of utility either has, or there is no reason why it might not have, all the sanctions which" belong to any other moral system [003-003]. Among these sanctions, the social feelings — sympathy, affection for others, and the desire to be in unity with fellow creatures — provide the internal motivation for moral conduct that external sanctions alone cannot supply.
