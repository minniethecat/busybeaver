## Definition

In Mill's moral psychology, **approbation** and its opposite, **disapprobation**, are the attitudes of moral approval and disapproval that constitute one of the most powerful informal sanctions on conduct. Approbation is not merely a private sentiment but a social force: it is through the expression of "our own and the public disapprobation" [005-013] that moral norms are enforced independently of legal penalty. Mill treats approbation as a central mechanism by which the principle of utility is brought to bear on individual conduct — what receives approbation is, ultimately, what promotes the general happiness.

## Key Arguments

- **Approbation and the standard of right**: Mill confronts the objection that "happiness is not the standard of approbation and disapprobation" [004-003] because people desire things other than happiness, such as virtue. His reply is that this objection misunderstands utilitarianism: the doctrine "maintains not only that virtue is to be desired, but that it is to be desired disinterestedly, for itself" [004-003]. Approbation attaches to virtue because virtue has become part of happiness, not as a rival to it.

- **Disapprobation as the mark of injustice**: Mill identifies "that particular epithet of disapprobation" [005-003] as what distinguishes the judgment of injustice from mere inexpediency. When an action is judged unjust, it attracts a "peculiar character and intensity" [005-003] of disapprobation that goes beyond ordinary moral disapproval. This special sentiment of disapprobation is what gives justice its "superior binding force" [005-003] compared to other moral considerations.

- **Public disapprobation as sanction**: Mill describes how, when legal enforcement of justice would be inexpedient, "we lament the impossibility, we consider the impunity given to injustice as an evil, and strive to make amends for it by bringing a strong expression of our own and the public disapprobation to bear upon the offender" [005-013]. Public disapprobation thus functions as a substitute for legal penalty — an essential mechanism for enforcing morality where law cannot reach.
