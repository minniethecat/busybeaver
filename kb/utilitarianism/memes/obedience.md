## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **obedience** denotes the conformity of action to moral principle, whether that principle is the ultimate principle of utility or the secondary moral rules derived from it. Obedience is not blind submission but reasoned compliance: Mill distinguishes between "direct obedience to principle" [002-024] as the proper motive of duty, and the limited "licence of disobedience" [005-006] justified only when a law or rule is itself unjust.

## Key Arguments

### Obedience to Principle as the Motive of Duty

Mill insists that right action must be performed from "the motive of duty, and in direct obedience to principle" rather than from inclination, habit, or fear of sanction alone [002-024]. This Kantian-sounding formulation is given a utilitarian grounding: obedience to principle is justified because principles encode the accumulated experience of what tends to promote happiness. To act from duty is to act on the best available knowledge of consequences.

### The Limits of Obedience: Unjust Laws

Mill carefully circumscribes the duty of obedience. He argues that we "should confine the licence of disobedience to the case of unjust laws" [005-006], implying that while obedience is the default moral posture, it is not unconditional. When a law violates the principle of utility — particularly the requirements of justice — disobedience may be justified. The burden of proof, however, rests on the disobeyer to show that the law is genuinely unjust.

### Obedience Waived for Competing Considerations

Mill acknowledges that even "the claims of justice are waved, in obedience to other considerations" [005-007] in certain circumstances. Justice, though the most stringent part of morality, is not absolute — it may be overridden when obedience to a different moral demand (such as the prevention of greater harm) requires it. This reflects the utilitarian commitment to balancing competing claims rather than treating any single rule as inviolable.

### Obedience as the Rule, Disobedience as the Exception

Mill asserts that if "obedience to them were not the rule, and disobedience the exception," the "peace among human beings" would be impossible [005-035]. The stability of society depends on widespread conformity to moral and legal rules; habitual disobedience, even to imperfect rules, threatens the security that is the "most vital of all interests." Utilitarian calculation therefore strongly favors obedience as the default, with disobedience reserved for clear cases of injustice.

### Punishment for Disobedience

The enforceability of rules depends on the expectation that "disobedience to them" will be punished [005-028]. The penal sanction — legal, social, or internal — attaches to disobedience, reinforcing the rule's binding force. This external incentive structure supports the internal motive of duty, together ensuring sufficient compliance for social order.
