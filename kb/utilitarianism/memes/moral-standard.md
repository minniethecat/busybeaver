# Moral Standard

A **moral standard** is the criterion by which right and wrong are distinguished — the measure against which actions, motives, and characters are judged. For Mill, the utilitarian moral standard is the principle of utility: "actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003]. He argues that any moral standard must be able to answer three questions: what is its content, what is its sanction, and what are the motives to obey it.

## The Utilitarian Standard Defined

Mill gives his clearest statement of the utilitarian moral standard early in the essay: "The creed which accepts as the foundation of morals, Utility, or the Greatest Happiness Principle, holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003]. By happiness, Mill means "pleasure and the absence of pain"; by unhappiness, "pain and the privation of pleasure" [002-003]. This standard applies not only to individual acts but to rules, institutions, and ways of life.

## The Question Every Standard Must Answer

Mill identifies a universal question that must be asked of any proposed moral standard: "What is its sanction? what are the motives to obey it? or, more specifically, what is the source of its obligation? whence does it derive its binding force?" [003-001]. This question is not peculiar to utilitarianism — "it really arises in regard to all standards" [003-001]. A moral standard without an account of its sanction would be merely advisory, lacking the binding force that distinguishes morality from mere preference.

## Comparison with Other Standards

Mill contrasts the utilitarian standard with alternative moral standards — particularly those of the intuitionist school, which treat moral rules as self-evident *a priori* truths. The advantage of the utilitarian standard, he argues, is that it provides a single, measurable criterion: the tendency of actions to promote or diminish happiness. Other standards, lacking such a common measure, cannot resolve conflicts between competing duties without appealing — often covertly — to utility itself.

## The Internal Sanction

Mill identifies the ultimate sanction of the utilitarian moral standard as "the conscientious feelings of mankind" [003-005] — the internal feeling of duty that is cultivated through education and social life. This sanction, he argues, is "the same as of all other moral standards" [003-005]: it is the subjective conviction of obligation, grounded in the social feelings of humanity and strengthened by habituation.
