## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **suffering** is the negative dimension of sentient experience whose minimization is the direct object of the principle of utility. Mill treats suffering — whether in the form of "direct suffering" or "the privation of some means of happiness" [005-036] — as the primary evil that morality exists to prevent and remedy. The capacity for suffering grounds both the scope of moral consideration and the urgency of the claims of justice.

## Key Arguments

### The Capacity for Acute Suffering

Mill observes that human beings are "capable probably of more acute suffering" than other creatures, and are "certainly accessible to it at more points" [002-007]. This heightened vulnerability gives human well-being its special moral urgency: a being capable of deeper suffering has a stronger claim to protection. The qualitative dimension of suffering — not merely its intensity but the range of experiences through which it can arise — informs the utilitarian calculus.

### Poverty as Preventable Suffering

Mill identifies poverty as a paradigmatic form of socially caused suffering. He asserts that "poverty, in any sense implying suffering, may be completely extinguished by the wisdom of society" [002-018], arguing that the material conditions of human misery are neither natural nor inevitable. This claim places the elimination of poverty-induced suffering among the most urgent demands of utilitarian social policy.

### Suffering and Proportionate Punishment

In the context of justice, Mill invokes a proportionality principle: punishment should inflict "any amount of suffering beyond the least that will suffice" to deter the offence [005-030]. This constraint reflects the utilitarian recognition that suffering is intrinsically bad — even when inflicted on wrongdoers — and must be minimized even in the service of deterrence and social defense.

### Suffering and the Sentiment of Justice

Mill locates the psychological origin of justice in the "animal desire to repel or retaliate a hurt or damage to oneself or to those with whom one sympathizes" [005-037]. The perception of suffering — "either in the person suffering, or in a sympathizing spectator" [005-037] — triggers the moral sentiment that underlies justice. Suffering is thus both the object of moral concern and the psychological trigger of the moral response.
