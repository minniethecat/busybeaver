# Moral Law

The concept of **moral law** — a binding rule or principle claiming authority over human conduct — is central to debates about the foundation of ethics.

Mill engages with this concept critically, contrasting the utilitarian view, in which all moral laws derive their authority from the principle of utility, with intuitionist systems in which moral laws are treated as independently authoritative.

## The Intuitionist Claim

The intuitionist school of ethics holds that moral laws are known by a direct rational intuition — they are self-evident *a priori* truths requiring no further justification.

Mill observes that the intuitive and inductive schools "both agree that the morality of individual action is not a question of direct perception" but rather of general principles [001-004].

Across different ethical traditions, there is broad agreement on "the same moral laws" [001-004]; what distinguishes them is their account of the *source* and *evidence* of those laws.

## Mill's Critique of Independent Moral Laws

Mill's central objection is to the claim that moral laws possess independent authority.

In other ethical systems, he notes, "the moral laws all claiming independent authority" serve as ultimate justifications [002-037]. But when these laws conflict, there is no higher principle to adjudicate between them.

The utilitarian system, by contrast, traces all moral laws to a single source: "utility is the ultimate source of moral obligations" [002-037]. When duties conflict, the principle of utility provides a common standard for resolution.

## The Convergence Argument

Mill goes beyond critique to offer a constructive argument. He observes that intuitionists now agree "that the intuitive perception is of principles of morality, and not of the details" [003-008].

If only general principles are intuitively known, the question becomes which principle. Mill suggests that "if there is any principle of morals which is intuitively obligatory, I should say it must be" the regard for the pleasures and pains of others [003-008].

If so, "the intuitive ethics would coincide with the utilitarian, and there would be no further quarrel between them" [003-008]. The apparent conflict between intuitionism and utilitarianism dissolves once both are seen to point toward the same fundamental principle.

## The Role of Secondary Principles

Mill does not deny the practical importance of moral laws. He insists they function as secondary principles — the accumulated moral wisdom of human experience — and that in ordinary cases they are the appropriate guide.

The appeal to first principles is necessary only when these secondary principles conflict. The utilitarian framework thus accommodates the practical force of moral laws while denying their metaphysical independence.
