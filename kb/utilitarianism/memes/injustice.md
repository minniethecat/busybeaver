## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **injustice** is defined by contrast with justice: it is the quality attributed in common to all modes of conduct designated as unjust, distinguished from actions that are merely disapproved without that particular epithet. Mill investigates injustice methodically by surveying "the various modes of action, and arrangements of human affairs, which are classed, by universal or widely spread opinion, as Just or as Unjust" [[005-005]]. The concept of injustice is inseparable from the idea of violated rights — whether legal rights or moral rights — and from the notion that the perpetrator ought to be punished [[005-006], [005-014]].

## Key Arguments

- **Injustice as the violation of rights**: Mill identifies the first and most definite form of injustice as depriving a person of liberty, property, or any other thing belonging to them by law. However, he immediately acknowledges exceptions: the deprived person may have forfeited their legal rights, or the law itself may be a bad law, raising the question of whether violating bad law constitutes injustice. This leads Mill to the concept of moral rights — when a law is thought unjust, it is "being so in the same way in which a breach of law is unjust, namely, by infringing somebody's right" [[005-005], [005-006]].

- **The origin of the injustice sentiment**: Mill traces the sentiment of injustice historically to the violation of law, specifically "violations of such laws as ought to exist, including such as ought to exist, but do not." Among the Greeks and Romans, who recognized that laws were made by humans and could be bad, "the sentiment of injustice came to be attached, not to all violations of law, but only to violations of such laws as ought to exist" [[005-012]].

- **The desire for punishment**: A defining mark of injustice is the impulse to see it punished. "It would always give us pleasure, and chime in with our feelings of fitness, that acts which we deem unjust should be punished." Even when punishment by tribunals is deemed inexpedient, "we consider the impunity given to injustice as an evil, and strive to make amends for it by bringing a strong expression of our own and the public disapprobation to bear upon the offender" [[005-013]].

- **Penal sanction and the essence of wrong**: Mill observes that "the idea of penal sanction, which is the essence of law, enters not only into the conception of injustice, but into that of any kind of wrong. We do not call anything wrong, unless we mean to imply that a person ought to be punished in some way or other for doing it." This insight places injustice on a continuum with moral wrong in general, distinguished by the specific character of the rights violated [[005-014]].

- **Injustice and inequality**: Mill notes that the perception of injustice is closely tied to views about expediency. "Those who think that utility requires distinctions of rank, do not consider it unjust that riches and social privileges should be unequally dispensed; but those who think this inequality inexpedient, think it unjust also." The judgment of what counts as injustice thus varies with one's understanding of what serves the general welfare [[005-010]].

## Related Concepts

- [[justice]] — the positive counterpart; injustice is defined by contrast with justice, as "justice, like many other moral attributes, is best defined by its opposite"
- [[rights]] — injustice consists in the violation of legal or moral rights; the concept of a right is central to distinguishing injustice from mere inexpediency
- [[punishment]] — the sentiment of injustice carries an inherent impulse toward punishment of the offender
- [[equality]] — perceptions of injustice are frequently tied to perceived violations of equality, though Mill shows that equality judgments themselves vary with views about utility
- [[law]] — historically, the concept of injustice originated from the violation of law, and the idea of legal constraint remains "the generating idea of the notion of justice"
