## Definition

**Right**, in Mill's utilitarian ethics, designates actions that tend to promote happiness and are therefore morally justified. Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by noting that the question of "the criterion of right and wrong" has been a central problem of philosophy from its dawn [[001-001]]. Against intuitionist theories that treat rightness as a *sui generis* property, Mill holds that "actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [[002-003]]. Rightness is thus not an intrinsic property of actions but a function of their consequences.

## Key Arguments

- **The test of right and wrong**: Mill insists that any theory of right and wrong must provide a determinate criterion — a "test" — by which actions can be evaluated. The principle of utility is proposed as this test, replacing the vague appeals to intuition or common sense that Mill finds in rival theories [[001-003]].
- **Right in conduct versus right in motive**: Mill carefully distinguishes between the rightness of an action and the praiseworthiness of the agent's motive. An action may be right (conducive to happiness) even when performed from selfish motives, and conversely, a well-intentioned action may be wrong if it produces harm [[002-022], [002-024]].
- **The right to happiness**: Responding to critics who ask "What right hast thou to be happy?", Mill argues that every human being possesses an equal claim to happiness — a claim grounded not in abstract right but in the empirical fact that happiness is the ultimate object of desire [[002-013], [002-021]].
