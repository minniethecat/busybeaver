## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian theory of justice, **property** is a socially protected interest whose secure possession is among the most vital conditions of human welfare. Property rights are not natural or pre-social entitlements but conventional arrangements justified by their contribution to general utility — specifically, by the security they provide for individuals to plan, invest, and enjoy the fruits of their labour without fear of arbitrary deprivation.

## Key Arguments

### Property as a Protected Interest

Mill ranks the protection of property alongside the protection of life and liberty as a core demand of justice. He notes that "to deprive any one of liberty, his property, or any other thing which belongs to him" is universally classed as an injustice [005-004]. The violation of property rights constitutes injustice because it attacks the security without which "nothing is safe for the future" — security being, in Mill's analysis, "the most vital of all interests."

### The Impulse to Violate Property

Mill acknowledges the natural temptation "to meddle with the property or life of another" [002-033] when it serves one's interests. The institution of property — reinforced by legal sanction and moral sentiment — exists precisely to counteract this impulse. Without such institutional protection, the stronger would prey upon the weaker, and the conditions of peaceful cooperation would dissolve.

### Unequal Property and Social Justice

Mill addresses the objection that property protections preserve unjust inequalities. He acknowledges that the existing distribution of property provides unequal "protection of their property" [005-033] to the rich and the poor. However, he distinguishes between the institution of property (which is justified by utility) and particular distributions of property (which may be unjust and subject to reform). The utilitarian can critique existing property arrangements without rejecting property as such.
