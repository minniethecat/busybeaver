# Utilitarian Principle

The **utilitarian principle** — also called the **principle of utility** or the **greatest happiness principle** — is the foundational moral standard of utilitarian ethics. It holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness. Mill presents this principle as the first principle of morality, from which all secondary moral rules derive their authority.

## Definition and Terminology

Mill identifies the principle of utility with "the principle of utility, or as Bentham latterly called it, the greatest happiness principle" [001-006]. It serves as the ultimate standard: "actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [001-006]. The principle judges actions solely by their consequences for human welfare, making it the ground of all moral obligation and the test of all moral rules.

## The Principle as First Principle

Mill treats the utilitarian principle as the first principle of morality — the ultimate axiom from which all other moral truths derive. "Whatever we adopt as the fundamental principle of morality, we require subordinate principles to apply it by" [002-035]. The principle of utility functions as the test of these subordinate principles: a secondary rule (such as the prohibition on lying) is valid only insofar as its observance tends to promote the general happiness. The principle is not applied directly to every action; rather, "the corollaries from the principle of utility, like the precepts of every practical art, admit of indefinite improvement" [002-034].

## Working with the Principle

Mill acknowledges that deriving practical conclusions from the principle requires care. He notes that "the Epicureans... were by no means faultless in drawing out their scheme of consequences from the utilitarian principle" [002-005], suggesting that correct application of the principle demands more than a superficial calculation of pleasures and pains. The principle must recognise qualitative distinctions among pleasures, the role of secondary rules, and the social dimensions of happiness. Mill argues that "the utilitarian principle has already the benefit" of the growing consensus that moral rules must be judged by their tendency to promote human welfare [003-008].

## The Principle's Scope

"if the principle of utility is good for anything, it must be good for settling... the limits" of moral rules [002-032]. The principle governs not only private conduct but also legislation, social policy, and the structure of institutions. Its scope extends to "the whole sentient creation" [002-011] — all beings capable of pleasure and pain fall within its purview.
