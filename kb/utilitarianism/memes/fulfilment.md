## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **fulfilment** denotes the realisation or satisfaction of moral obligations and the expectations derived from them. Mill uses the concept in two related senses: first, the fulfilment of the principle of utility through dutiful action; second, the fulfilment of the obligations of justice, which he treats as a necessary condition of social life. Fulfilment is not merely a psychological state but a normative standard — actions are judged by whether they fulfil the requirements of the greatest happiness principle [[004-010], [005-038]].

## Key Arguments

- **Fulfilment of the utilitarian principle**: Mill argues that individuals persist in acting on the principle of utility even when they fail at other times, suggesting that its fulfilment represents the steady orientation of moral conduct. The expectation of deriving happiness from the fulfilment of moral principles is itself part of the motivation for ethical behaviour [[004-010]].
- **Fulfilment in the context of justice**: Mill draws a critical distinction between the fulfilment of justice and cases that merely appear to involve justice. Some actions that seem intuitively just are not properly cases of fulfilling justice at all, but belong to other moral categories — a distinction essential to Mill's effort to define the proper scope of justice [[005-007]].
- **Fulfilment as a necessary condition of justice obligations**: For Mill, the fulfilment of obligations is not optional but "a necessary condition" of the obligations of justice themselves. Justice demands fulfilment because without it the social fabric — grounded in the security of expectations — would collapse [[005-038]].
