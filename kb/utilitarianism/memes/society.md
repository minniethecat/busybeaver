# Society

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **society** plays a dual role: it is both the context in which moral character is shaped, and the domain across which the consequences of actions must be evaluated. The "society into which" individuals are thrown influences whether their higher capacities flourish or wither [002-010].

## Society and Moral Progress

Mill envisions social progress as the gradual removal of preventable evils through collective wisdom. He argues that "most of the great positive evils of the world are in themselves removable, and will, if human affairs continue to improve, be in the end reduced within narrow limits" [002-018]. Poverty, he suggests, "may be completely extinguished by the wisdom of society, combined with the good sense and providence of individuals."

## The Social Feelings as Sanction

The ultimate sanction of utilitarian morality rests on a "natural basis of sentiment" — the social feelings of mankind [003-010]. As society develops, the recognition that individual interests are bound up with the general happiness becomes "an attribute which it would be impossible for the individual to dispense with." These social feelings give utilitarian morality its binding force, making the general happiness "recognized as the ethical standard."
