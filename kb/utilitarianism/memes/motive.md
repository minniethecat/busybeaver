# Motive

**Motive** refers to the psychological impulse or reason that moves a person to act. In Mill's utilitarian framework, the motive of an action is sharply distinguished from its moral quality: an action's rightness depends on its consequences, not on the motive from which it was done. "The motive has nothing to do with the morality of the action, though much with the worth of the agent" [002-023].

## Motive vs. Morality of Action

Mill draws a clean separation between the evaluation of an act and the evaluation of the agent. An action performed from a selfish motive may be morally right if it produces good consequences, while an action from a benevolent motive may be wrong if it produces harm. The motive matters for judging **character**, not for judging the **act itself** [002-024].

## The Sanction Question

A central question of utilitarian ethics is: "what are the motives to obey it?" [003-001]. Mill identifies two classes of motives — **external** (hope of favour, fear of displeasure from others or from God) and **internal** (conscience, the feeling of duty). External motives operate through "abundant interested motives" [003-010], while the internal sanction is "the essence of Conscience" [003-005].

## The Habituation of Motives

Mill observes that actions originally done from a specific motive can, through habit, be performed without it. "Men originally did from a motive of some sort, they continue to do from habit" [004-010]. This insight connects motive to character formation: repeated right action, whatever the initial motive, builds a disposition towards virtue.
