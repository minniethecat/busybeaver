# Creed

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **creed** refers to a foundational moral doctrine — a comprehensive statement of the ultimate principle governing right and wrong. Mill uses the term to articulate and defend the utilitarian position while contrasting it with rival ethical systems.

The language of "creed" is deliberate. It signals that utilitarianism is not merely a decision procedure or policy preference but a substantive ethical commitment — a *doctrine* about what makes actions right, analogous in scope and seriousness to a religious or ideological creed.

## The Utilitarian Creed

Mill's canonical formulation appears early in Chapter 2: "The creed which accepts as the foundation of morals, Utility, or the Greatest Happiness Principle, holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003].

This passage functions as a creed in the classical sense — a concise, affirmative statement of doctrine. It names the principle (Utility), specifies its content (promotion of happiness), and states the criterion of rightness (consequences for happiness).

Mill's choice of "creed" rather than "theory" or "doctrine" reflects his view that ethics, while rational, also involves commitment. A moral creed is not merely assented to intellectually; it is adopted as a practical guide.

## Competing Creeds

Mill contrasts the utilitarian creed with rival ethical frameworks. He asks pointedly: "Is utility the only creed which is able to furnish us with excuses for evil?" [002-036]. The rhetorical question highlights a strength of utilitarianism — its principled basis for resolving moral dilemmas in which some harm is unavoidable.

Other creeds, Mill suggests, lack a consistent resource for adjudicating hard cases. They may condemn certain actions absolutely, but when conflicting duties arise, they offer no higher principle for resolution. "Under every creed, at the opening thus made, self-preference slips in" [002-037] — without a unified standard, private interest fills the gap left by doctrinal ambiguity.

## Creed and Moral Motivation

Mill is attentive to the motivational dimension of moral creeds. A creed is not merely a theoretical position; it shapes practical deliberation and character. He observes that under competing moral systems, "the moral laws all claiming independent authority" [002-037] create confusion about what one actually ought to do.

The utilitarian creed resolves this by tracing all obligations to a single source. By unifying moral demands under one principle, it provides both theoretical simplicity and practical clarity — a single standard against which competing claims can be measured.
