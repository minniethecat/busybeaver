# Utilitarian School

The **Utilitarian School** (also known as **Classical Utilitarianism** or **Philosophical Radicalism**) is the philosophical movement inaugurated by **Jeremy Bentham** and elaborated by **John Stuart Mill** that holds utility — the greatest happiness of the greatest number — as the foundation of morality and the criterion of right action. Mill's *Utilitarianism* (1863) is the school's definitive manifesto.

## Core Tenets

The utilitarian school is defined by several interconnected doctrines:

1. **The Principle of Utility**: "Actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003].

2. **Hedonism about Value**: "Pleasure and freedom from pain are the only things desirable as ends" [002-004].

3. **Impartiality**: The utilitarian standard is "not the agent's own greatest happiness, but the greatest amount of happiness altogether" [002-011].

4. **Consequentialism**: The moral quality of an act is determined solely by its consequences for the general happiness.

5. **The Qualitative Distinction**: Mill's distinctive contribution — "some kinds of pleasure are more desirable and more valuable than others" [002-005] — distinguishes the school from Bentham's purely quantitative hedonism.

## Mill's View

Mill presents the utilitarian school as the culmination of an ethical tradition stretching from antiquity to modernity. He traces its lineage from "Epicurus to Bentham," positioning himself as the tradition's most sophisticated exponent [002-002].

Mill acknowledges the school's historical debts while asserting its superiority. Against the *a priori* moralists, he argues that "to those *a priori* moralists who deem it necessary to argue at all, utilitarian arguments are indispensable" [001-006]. The school's adversaries, he contends, cannot avoid appealing to the very principle they reject.

Mill also claims credit for the school's name: he is "the first person who brought the word utilitarian into use," adopting it from a passing expression and establishing it as the designation for "the recognition of utility as a standard" [002-001fn].

## Historical Context

The utilitarian school emerged from the **Enlightenment** and the reforming spirit of early 19th-century Britain. Bentham and his followers — the **Philosophical Radicals** — applied utilitarian principles to legal reform, political economy, and social policy. They advocated for:

- **Legal reform**: Rationalizing the common law system on utilitarian grounds
- **Democratic reform**: Extending the franchise and promoting representative government
- **Penal reform**: Designing punishment to deter crime while minimizing suffering
- **Economic policy**: Evaluating laws by their tendency to promote the general welfare

Mill's *Utilitarianism* (1863) was written at a time when the doctrine faced vocal opposition from intuitionist and deontological moralists. The work aimed to clarify what utilitarianism actually asserts — distinguishing it from caricatures — and to provide a rational grounding for the principle of utility.
