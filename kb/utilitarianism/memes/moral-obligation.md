# Moral Obligation

**Moral obligation** is the binding force that makes an action morally required rather than merely desirable. Mill treats it as a central concept in utilitarian ethics, analysing its source, its varieties, and its connection to the idea of justice. For Mill, the ultimate ground of moral obligation is utility — the general happiness — though he acknowledges that secondary principles mediate most everyday obligations.

## The Utilitarian Source of Obligation

Mill locates the foundation of moral obligation in the principle of utility itself. Utility is "the ultimate source of moral obligations" [002-037]: all specific duties derive their binding force from their tendency to promote the general happiness. There can be "no other ground of moral obligation than the general happiness" [003-003]. The sanctions that enforce obligation — external sanctions of social favour and disfavour, and the internal sanction of conscience — all ultimately rest on the human desire for happiness and aversion to pain [003-003].

## Secondary Principles and Practical Reasoning

Although utility is the ultimate source, Mill denies that every moral decision requires direct appeal to first principles. "There is no case of moral obligation in which some secondary principle is not involved" [002-038]. These secondary principles — rules against lying, stealing, and other common wrongs — are the accumulated wisdom of human experience and serve as the practical guide for everyday moral reasoning.

## Perfect and Imperfect Obligation

Mill draws an important distinction between two kinds of moral obligation. **Duties of perfect obligation** are those "in virtue of which a correlative right resides in some person or persons" [005-016]. These correspond to the domain of justice: an individual can claim performance as their moral right. **Duties of imperfect obligation** are "those moral obligations which do not give birth to any right" [005-016], such as charity or beneficence — we are bound to practice them, but not toward any definite person or at any prescribed time. The presence of a correlative right "constitutes the specific difference between justice, and generosity or beneficence" [005-017].

## The Idea of Moral Obligation

Mill reflects on the psychological character of obligation. By a "tendency of the human mind," the idea of moral obligation acquires "a sort of mystical character" [003-005], leading people to believe that it can attach only to certain objects by a supposed mysterious law. Mill regards this as an illusion born of the complexity of moral feeling, which draws from fear, religious sentiment, self-esteem, and the desire for others' esteem.
