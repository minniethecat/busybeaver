# Honour

**Honour** in Mill's utilitarian ethics denotes the esteem and recognition accorded to those who cultivate noble character and act for the general happiness. Mill famously declares "All honour to those who... for themselves" [[002-020]], affirming that self-cultivation of virtue deserves moral recognition even within a consequentialist framework. Honour is not an end in itself but a social sentiment that reinforces the pursuit of moral excellence.

## Honour and Noble Character

Mill connects honour to the cultivation of a "noble character" [[002-011]]. He raises the question whether such a character "is always the happier for its nobleness" [[002-011]], and answers that the "cultivation of nobleness of character" has value that extends beyond the individual: "even if each individual were" only indirectly benefited, society as a whole gains from the presence of noble exemplars [[002-012]]. Honour thus functions as a social mechanism for encouraging the development of character traits that serve the general good.

## Honour as a Sanction

Within the utilitarian framework of sanctions, honour operates as a form of external sanction — the approval and esteem of others. Mill's discussion of the moral feelings includes the "pain" attendant on violating the standard of right [[003-006]], and honour provides the positive counterpart: the pleasure of being held in esteem by one's community. This social dimension makes honour a practical reinforcement of moral conduct.
