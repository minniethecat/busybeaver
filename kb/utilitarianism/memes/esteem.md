## Definition

In Mill's moral psychology, **esteem** refers both to the regard we feel for ourselves (self-esteem) and the positive regard we seek from others (desire of the esteem of others). Mill identifies these as fundamental components of the internal sanction of morality — part of the complex "mass of feeling" that constitutes conscience. The desire for the esteem of others, together with self-esteem, forms part of the "extreme complication" of associations that give moral obligation its binding force [003-005].

## Key Arguments

- **Esteem as a component of the internal sanction**: Mill lists "self-esteem" and "desire of the esteem of others" alongside fear, religious feeling, and self-abasement as elements that make up the psychological phenomenon of conscience [003-005]. The binding force of morality "consists in the existence of a mass of feeling which must be broken through in order to do what violates our standard of right" [003-005], and esteem-based feelings are integral to this mass.

- **The social dimension of esteem**: The desire for the esteem of others links the internal and external sanctions of morality. External sanctions operate through "the hope of favour and the fear of displeasure from our fellow creatures" [003-003]. Esteem functions as a powerful social motive — people "desire and commend all conduct in others towards themselves, by which they think their happiness is promoted" [003-003], reinforcing utilitarian norms through mutual approbation.

- **Public expression of esteem and disapprobation**: Where legal enforcement is inexpedient, society enforces moral norms through "a strong expression of our own and the public disapprobation" [005-013]. The withholding and conferral of esteem are key mechanisms by which the principle of utility shapes conduct independently of law.

- **Self-esteem and integrity**: Self-esteem is the internalized form of this dynamic. The cultivated conscience feels "a pain, more or less intense, attendant on violation of duty" [003-005] even without detection. Self-esteem makes moral integrity possible — the person of principle values their own good opinion, which can be lost only by their own wrongdoing.
