## Definition

**Intention** in Mill's utilitarian ethics refers to the purpose or end for which an action is performed, distinguished from both the motive (the feeling that prompts the action) and the consequences (the effects the action produces). Mill discusses intention primarily in two contexts: the distinction between intention and motive in moral assessment, and the role of intention in the formation of virtuous character through the cultivation of will.

## Key Arguments

- **Intention and moral assessment**: Mill distinguishes between the role of intention and the role of consequences in moral judgment. While consequences determine the rightness or wrongness of an act, a person's intention is relevant to the evaluation of their character. The general intention of an agent — whether they habitually aim at the general good — is what marks them as virtuous or vicious, independent of the outcome of any particular act.

- **Intention versus expediency**: Mill warns against confusing utilitarian calculation with mere expediency, which "generally means that which is expedient for the particular interest of the agent himself" [002-031]. Intentions directed at merely personal or temporary advantage, without regard for general rules, are not truly utilitarian. "The Expedient, in this sense, instead of being the same thing as the useful, is a branch of the hurtful" [002-031].

- **Intention and the cultivation of virtue**: In his analysis of the proof of the principle of utility, Mill discusses how virtuous action, initially performed for its consequences, can come to be intended for its own sake. The "person of confirmed virtue" acts with a "general intention" that is "not in contradiction to the general intention prevailing at other times, but in fulfilment of it" [004-011]. This describes the psychological process by which intending the general happiness becomes a stable disposition.

- **The will-intention relationship**: Mill observes that "will, in the beginning, is entirely produced by desire" [004-011], but through habituation, one may come to "will from habit what we no longer desire for itself, or desire only because we will it" [004-011]. Intention thus occupies a middle ground between fleeting desire and settled disposition — it is the direction of the will toward an end, shaped by both present desire and past habituation.
