## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **distinction** operates as a dual methodological concept: it refers both to the qualitative discriminations that ground utilitarian ethics (the distinction between higher and lower pleasures, between justice and expediency) and to the conceptual boundaries that separate competing ethical theories. Mill is acutely aware that the failure to draw proper distinctions is the source of most anti-utilitarian objections, and his argumentative strategy consistently relies on clarifying distinctions that critics have blurred. The term appears as a "watchword of sectarian distinction" in the reception of utilitarian ideas, but Mill works to transform it into a rigorous philosophical tool [[002-003]].

## Key Arguments

- **The distinction between higher and lower pleasures**: Mill's most famous contribution to utilitarian theory is the qualitative distinction among pleasures. He argues that "it is quite compatible with the principle of utility to recognize the fact, that some kinds of pleasure are more desirable and more valuable than others." This distinction is grounded in the actual preferences of those who have experienced both kinds — "of two pleasures, if there be one to which all or almost all who have experience of both give a decided preference, that is the more desirable pleasure" [[002-003]].

- **The distinction between right and wrong**: Mill examines the psychological basis of the distinction between right and wrong, asking what makes this distinction different from mere like and dislike. "With every one who regards the distinction between right and wrong in a proper light," the utilitarian standard of happiness provides the criterion. The distinction is not a matter of intuition but of experience and rational judgment [[002-027]].

- **The distinction between will and desire**: In Chapter IV, Mill draws a crucial psychological distinction: "The distinction between will and desire thus understood is an authentic and highly important psychological fact." Will, he argues, is desire transformed by habit — what begins as a desire for pleasure can, through association, become a settled disposition to act that no longer requires the conscious anticipation of pleasure. This distinction is central to Mill's proof that virtue can be desired for its own sake [[004-011]].

- **The distinction between justice and morality**: Mill identifies the penal sanction as "the real turning point of the distinction between morality and simple expediency." The mark of a moral duty — as opposed to something merely desirable — is that "a person may rightfully be compelled to fulfil it." Within morality, justice is further distinguished by the presence of correlative rights: "wherever there is a right, the case is one of justice, and not of the virtue of beneficence" [[005-014], [005-016]].

- **The distinction between justice and expediency**: "There is no doubt that this distinction lies at the bottom of the notions of right and wrong." Mill identifies the mental link that separates justice from the general field of expediency by examining the sentiments associated with each. The sentiment of justice carries a unique imperative force — a sense that the demands of justice may be exacted from persons as one exacts a debt — that mere expediency lacks [[005-015], [005-017]].
