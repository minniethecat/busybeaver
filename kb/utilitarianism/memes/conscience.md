## Definition

**Conscience** in Mill's utilitarian ethics is the "internal sanction" of the principle of utility — "a feeling in our own mind" that restrains action through the pain attendant on violation of duty [003-007]. Mill rejects the intuitionist view of conscience as an innate moral faculty, arguing instead that conscience is cultivated through education, social feeling, and association. It is "not innate, but acquired," yet nonetheless "natural" to human beings as a product of their social development [003-009].

## Key Arguments

- **Conscience as internal sanction**: Mill distinguishes between external sanctions (law, public opinion, divine punishment) and the internal sanction of conscience. The latter is "a pain, more or less, attendant on violation of duty," felt even in the absence of external consequences. The conscientious person refrains from wrongdoing not from fear of punishment but from "the reproaches of his own conscience" [005-014].

- **The cultivated, not innate, origin of conscience**: Mill argues that "there is this basis of powerful natural sentiment" that can be "nurtured and cultivated" by education and social institutions [003-009]. Conscience is not a mysterious faculty implanted by nature but develops through association: the mind links harmful actions with the pain of disapprobation, producing a genuinely binding moral feeling. "No person of feeling and conscience would be selfish and base" even if they could escape detection [002-007].

- **The binding force of morality**: For Mill, conscience answers the question "What is the source of obligation?" Without it, moral principles would be abstractions with no motivational force. "The ultimate sanction of all morality" is "a subjective feeling in our own mind" — our conscience gives moral rules their "binding efficacy" [003-004].

- **Conscience and self-deception**: Mill acknowledges that conscience can be evaded through rationalization. He speaks of "the means of cheating our own conscience" [002-036], recognizing that self-deception is a persistent danger in moral life. The utilitarian standard can guard against this by providing an objective criterion against which conscience's deliverances may be calibrated.
