# Feeling

In Mill's moral psychology, **feeling** (or sentiment) serves as the subjective foundation of ethical judgment. Critics of utilitarianism, Mill notes, include "some of the most estimable in feeling and purpose," who find the doctrine's apparent reduction of morality to pleasure "utterly mean and grovelling" [002-004]. Mill's response is not to dismiss feeling but to show that utilitarian morality properly understood engages the noblest human feelings.

## The Role of Feeling in Moral Judgment

Mill appeals to feeling as a source of evidence about value. "No person of feeling and conscience would be selfish and base" even if assured that a life of lower pleasures were more satisfying [002-007]. This appeal to the sensibilities of the cultivated person is central to Mill's argument for the superiority of higher pleasures: the competent judge is one whose feelings have been refined by experience and reflection.

## Fellow-Feeling and the Social Bond

The cultivation of "a fellow-feeling with the collective interests of mankind" is, for Mill, essential to a satisfying life [002-016]. Those who develop this fellow-feeling "retain as lively an interest in life on the eve of death as in the vigour of youth and health." The "feelings and judgment of the experienced" provide the only standard for comparing pleasures and pains [002-011].
