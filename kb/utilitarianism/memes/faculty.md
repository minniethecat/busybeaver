# Faculty

The **faculty** — particularly the **moral faculty** — is a key concept in Mill's analysis of how human beings come to know and apply moral principles. Mill critically examines the "popular theory of a natural faculty, a sense or instinct, informing us of right and wrong" [001-003], and develops an alternative account grounded in experience, cultivation, and the principle of utility.

## The Intuitive Faculty Theory

Mill opens his discussion by engaging with the intuitionist claim that human beings possess an innate moral sense. According to this view, "our moral faculty" supplies us with direct apprehension of moral truths, independent of reasoning about consequences [001-004]. Mill rejects this account as insufficient: appealing to a mysterious "natural faculty" [001-003] explains nothing and leaves moral disagreement unresolved.

## The Rational Faculty

Mill distinguishes the moral faculty from the **rational faculty**. Moral judgments fall "within the cognizance of the rational faculty" [001-008], meaning that moral principles are subject to rational scrutiny and must be justified by appeal to evidence — specifically, evidence about the consequences of actions on human happiness. The rational faculty "does not pretend to pronounce on the worth of the premises but to frame its conclusions from them" [001-008].

## Cultivation of the Moral Faculty

In Chapter III, Mill argues that "the moral faculty, if not a part of our nature, is a natural outgrowth from it" [003-009]. Like other human capacities — speech, reason, aesthetic sensibility — the moral faculty is a **potential** that requires cultivation. It develops through education, social interaction, and the accumulated experience of what conduces to human well-being. Mill's account allows for moral progress: as our understanding of happiness evolves, so too does the refinement of our moral faculty.

## Faculty and the Standard of Utility

The moral faculty is not, for Mill, the source of moral principles but rather the **instrument** through which we apply them. The principle of utility provides the standard; the moral faculty — shaped by experience and reason — applies that standard to particular cases. This division resolves the tension between the need for a fixed moral criterion and the flexibility required for practical judgment.
