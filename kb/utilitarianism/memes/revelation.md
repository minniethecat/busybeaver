## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **revelation** — particularly the Christian revelation — is examined in relation to the foundations of ethics. Mill argues that a utilitarian who believes in a perfectly good and wise God "necessarily believes that whatever God has thought fit to reveal on the subject of morals must fulfil the requirements of utility in a supreme degree" [[002-030]]. The concept also appears in Mill's epistemological critique: he warns against the tendency to treat subjective feelings as a "revelation of some objective reality" without rational warrant [[005-002]].

## Key Arguments

- **Revelation and utility are compatible**: Mill contends that revealed ethics and utilitarian ethics need not conflict. A believer in divine goodness must hold that divine revelation on moral matters aligns with the principle of utility. "Whatever aid religion, either natural or revealed, can afford to ethical investigation is as open to the utilitarian moralist as to any other" [[002-030]].
- **Revelation as guidance, not code**: Mill endorses the view that the Christian revelation was intended "to inform the hearts and minds of mankind with a spirit which should enable them to find for themselves what is right," rather than to provide a comprehensive code of conduct. This interpretation leaves room for "a doctrine of ethics, carefully followed out, to interpret to us the will of God" [[002-030]].
- **The false appearance of revelation in moral feeling**: In his analysis of justice, Mill cautions that "mankind are always predisposed to believe that any subjective feeling, not otherwise accounted for, is a revelation of some objective reality" [[005-002]]. This tendency can lead to treating the feeling of justice as an ultimate criterion rather than subjecting it to rational scrutiny.
