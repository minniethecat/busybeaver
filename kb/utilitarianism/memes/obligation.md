## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian theory, **obligation** (or moral duty) is a requirement to act or refrain from acting, grounded ultimately in the principle of utility. Mill seeks to establish a "common ground of obligation" ([001-005]) that replaces intuitionist appeals to self-evident moral rules. An action is obligatory when it produces the greatest happiness of all affected, and when the failure to perform it would rightfully incur some form of sanction — legal, social, or internal.

## Key Arguments

- **Utility provides the source of moral obligation.** Mill rejects the idea that obligations are self-evident or apprehended by a "moral sense." Instead, "the source of moral obligation" is the general happiness ([001-006]): we are obligated to act in ways that promote the greatest good, and this ground is prior to all secondary moral rules.

- **Obligation is tied to sanction.** The binding force of obligation comes from the sanctions attached to non-performance. The "ground of the obligation to abstain" from harmful acts is the utility calculation showing that the act causes more pain than the alternative ([002-025]). Without sanction, obligation would be merely advisory.

- **Conflicting obligations require utility as arbiter.** Mill acknowledges "equivocal cases of conflicting obligation" as "the real difficulties" in ethics ([002-037]). When duties clash (e.g., truth-telling vs. preventing harm), only the utilitarian calculus can resolve which obligation takes priority. Secondary principles help navigate these cases but never override the ultimate appeal to consequences.

- **Perfect vs. imperfect obligations.** Following the jurisprudential tradition, Mill distinguishes perfect obligations — those correlative to a specific right held by another person — from imperfect obligations, which are genuine duties but do not correspond to any assignable right ([005-007]). Justice involves perfect obligations; benevolence involves imperfect ones.
