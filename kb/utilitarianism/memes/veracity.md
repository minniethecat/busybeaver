## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **veracity** — the disposition to tell the truth and refrain from deception — is among the most important of the moral virtues, not because truth-telling is an absolute duty but because the general observance of veracity is indispensable to social trust and therefore to the general happiness. Mill treats veracity as a paradigmatic case of a moral rule whose utility is so overwhelming that "the feeling on the subject of veracity is one of the most useful" elements of the moral sentiments [[002-031]].

## Key Arguments

- **Veracity as a pillar of social trust**: Mill argues that any deviation from truth, even unintentional, "does that much towards weakening reliance on veracity." The cumulative effect of individual lapses in truthfulness erodes the foundation of social cooperation, making veracity a matter of collective as well as individual concern [[002-031], [002-032]].
- **The exception problem**: Mill acknowledges the long-standing moral debate over whether lying is ever permissible. Within the utilitarian framework, the question is not whether truth-telling is intrinsically good but whether the consequences of deception — particularly the erosion of trust — can ever be outweighed by countervailing benefits. Mill's analysis suggests that such cases exist but that the bar is extremely high, given the "most useful" nature of the veracity sentiment [[002-031]].
- **Veracity and the classification of moral judgments**: Mill notes that judgments about "truth and falsehood" are "questions of observation and experience," placing veracity within the empirical domain alongside other matters of fact. This classification is significant because it rejects the intuitionist claim that moral truths (including the duty of veracity) are known *a priori* [[001-004]].
