# Doctrine

**Doctrine**, in Mill's *Utilitarianism*, refers to a systematic body of ethical teaching — a structured theory of morality that provides both a foundational principle and derivative rules for practical guidance. Mill presents "the utilitarian doctrine" as such a system and defends it against competing ethical doctrines of his time.

## Doctrine and Science of Ethics

Mill draws an analogy between ethical doctrine and scientific theory: "the detailed doctrines of a science are not usually deduced from, nor depend for their evidence upon, what are called its first principles" [001-002]. Just as scientific knowledge advances through the accumulation of detailed doctrines, moral philosophy progresses through the refinement of its systematic teachings. Mill surveys "past and present ethical doctrine" to situate utilitarianism within the tradition [001-005].

## The Utilitarian Doctrine Defined

The core of the utilitarian doctrine is "that happiness is desirable, and the only thing desirable, as an end" [004-001]. From this central claim, the entire doctrinal structure unfolds: happiness as the ultimate end, the Greatest Happiness Principle as the criterion of right action, and secondary moral rules as the practical guides to conduct. Mill insists that "we need a doctrine of ethics, carefully followed out" to supply "an acknowledged standard" [002-030].

## Common Objections to the Doctrine

Mill catalogues several "reproaches" against the utilitarian doctrine. Some critics deride it as "a doctrine worthy only of swine" [002-004], reducing all human pursuits to base pleasures. Others object that the doctrine "denies that people desire virtue" [004-003]. Still others charge that "all doctrines which recognize as a fact in morals the existence of conflicting considerations" render practical guidance impossible [002-036]. Mill systematically answers each objection, arguing that the utilitarian doctrine accommodates higher pleasures, explains the desire for virtue, and provides a method for resolving apparent conflicts.

## Derivative Doctrines

Mill distinguishes between the primary doctrine and "secondary or derivative doctrines" [005-039]. The principle of utility is the foundational doctrine; specific moral rules — concerning justice, rights, veracity, and so forth — are derived doctrines that have proven their utility through long human experience. These derivative doctrines are not deduced logically from the first principle but emerge as "corollaries from the principle of utility" [005-039].
