## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian theory, **sanction** refers to the motivating force that compels obedience to a moral standard. The question "What is its sanction?" ([003-001]) asks: what are the motives to obey the principle of utility? Mill distinguishes two categories: **external sanctions** — the hope of favour and fear of displeasure from others or from God — and the **internal sanction**, a "feeling in our own mind" ([003-004]), namely conscience, which creates pain when duty is violated.

## Key Arguments

- **External sanctions include social, religious, and natural pressures.** Mill notes that "there is this basis of powerful natural sentiment" underlying external sanctions ([003-004]). These include the desire for social approval, fear of divine punishment, and the natural consequences of actions.

- **The internal sanction is the feeling of duty.** This "disinterested" feeling, cultivated through education and social life, "constitutes it" — meaning conscience is the ultimate internal motivator ([003-005]). When fully developed, the internal sanction makes violating moral rules psychologically painful.

- **The ultimate sanction of all morality is the general happiness.** Mill argues that the "ultimate sanction, therefore, of all morality" is the "conscientious feelings of mankind" directed at the greatest happiness principle ([003-005]). The sanctions of the utilitarian standard are no weaker than those of any other moral system ([003-003]).

- **Education and social cultivation strengthen sanctions.** Mill emphasizes that the internal sanction "will not exist in the mind unless it be cultivated" ([003-007]), and advocates "making sufficient use of the external sanctions and of the force of early impressions" ([003-009]) to build moral character.
