# Jeremy Bentham

**Jeremy Bentham** (1748–1832) was an English philosopher, jurist, and social reformer regarded as the founder of modern **utilitarianism**. Mill refers to Bentham throughout *Utilitarianism* as the originator of the doctrine's central formulation — the **Greatest Happiness Principle** — and as the philosopher who gave the utilitarian tradition its name and core commitments.

## Mill's Reference

Mill credits Bentham with the definitive articulation of the utilitarian standard. The "principle of utility, or as Bentham latterly called it, the greatest happiness principle" serves as the foundation of Mill's own ethical theory [001-006]. Mill traces the lineage of utilitarian thought "from Epicurus to Bentham," positioning Bentham as the modern inheritor of an ancient tradition that grounded ethics in pleasure and the avoidance of pain [002-002].

Mill notes that he himself "brought the word utilitarian into use," adopting it "from a passing expression in Mr. Galt's *Annals of the Parish*" — but the doctrine the word names is Bentham's [002-001fn].

## Key Contributions

### The Greatest Happiness Principle

Bentham's central insight is that the moral quality of actions is determined by their tendency to promote or diminish **happiness**. Mill adopts this principle as the foundation of his own ethical system: "actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003].

### The Principle of Equality

Bentham's dictum — **"everybody to count for one, nobody for more than one"** — encapsulates the egalitarian core of utilitarian ethics [005-039]. Mill endorses this principle as fundamental to justice, arguing that "equal amounts of happiness are equally desirable, whether felt by the same or by different persons" [005-039].

### Legal and Social Reform

Beyond philosophy, Bentham applied utilitarian principles to law and social policy. Mill references Bentham's work on justice and rights, noting that Bentham recognized certain "conditions" as essential to the general happiness — conditions that ground our conception of moral rights [005-041].

## Relationship to Utilitarianism

Bentham is the **founding figure** of the utilitarian tradition. Mill's *Utilitarianism* is in large part an elaboration and defense of Bentham's core doctrine, though Mill diverges from Bentham in significant ways:

| Aspect | Bentham | Mill |
|--------|---------|------|
| Pleasure metric | Quantitative (hedonic calculus) | Qualitative (higher/lower pleasures) |
| Foundation | Empirical, psychological | Empirical + appeal to "competent judges" |
| Scope | Individual + legal reform | Individual + collective happiness |
| Approach to justice | Derives rights from utility | Rights grounded in "general happiness" |

Mill acknowledges Bentham's quantitative approach was incomplete, noting that "utilitarian writers in general have placed the superiority of mental over bodily pleasures chiefly in the greater permanency, safety, uncostliness, etc." rather than in their "intrinsic nature" [002-005]. Mill's qualitative distinction between higher and lower pleasures is his most significant departure from Bentham.
