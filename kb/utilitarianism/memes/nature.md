# Nature

**Nature**, and especially **human nature**, provides the psychological foundation for Mill's utilitarian ethics. Against critics who "represent human nature in a degrading light" — as incapable of disinterested moral motivation — Mill constructs an account of human nature that makes the pursuit of the general happiness both possible and compelling [002-004].

## The Social Feelings as Natural

Mill argues that the social feelings of mankind — sympathy, conscience, the desire for unity with others — are "a fact in human nature" [003-006]. The sentiment of justice is "as completely a part of our nature, as the horror of crime is in any one" [003-002]. These feelings are not artificial impositions on an otherwise selfish nature; they are "a natural outgrowth" of the social condition — spontaneous developments that emerge from human beings living together [003-009].

## A Powerful Principle

Far from being a weak or fragile tendency, the social feelings constitute "a powerful principle in human nature, and happily one of those which are capable of great increase through cultivation" [003-010]. This is crucial for Mill: if the social feelings are both natural and cultivable, then the utilitarian project of extending moral concern to the general happiness has a firm psychological footing.

## The Provision of Nature

Mill finds in nature a providential mechanism: "were there not this provision of nature, by which things, originally indifferent, become pleasurable through association," moral motivation would be psychologically impossible [004-007]. The association of virtue with pleasure — and vice with pain — is not a rational insight but a natural process, built into the constitution of the human mind. This "provision of nature" does the work that reason alone cannot.

## Animal Nature and Human Good

Mill acknowledges that the capacity for self-preservation and the desire to avoid harm are "common to all animal nature; for every animal tries to hurt those who have hurt it" [005-019]. But he distinguishes this animal impulse from the distinctively human sentiment of justice, which includes the capacity for impartiality — the willingness to apply rules equally even when one's own interests are at stake. Human nature is animal nature refined by sympathy and reason.
