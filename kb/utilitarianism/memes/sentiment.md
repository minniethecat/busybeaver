# Sentiment

**Sentiment** is the emotional component of moral judgment — the feeling of approval or disapproval that accompanies our perceptions of right and wrong. For Mill, moral sentiments are not innate but are **acquired through experience and association**, yet they can become as powerful and binding as if they were natural. The "sentiment of justice" is the paradigm case.

## The Sentiment of Justice

The sentiment of justice has a "peculiar character and intensity" [005-004] that distinguishes it from other moral feelings. It includes both the **desire to punish** those who harm others and the **feeling of personal violation** when one's own rights are infringed. This sentiment can "gather round it" strong emotional force, making justice feel more compelling than mere expediency [005-006].

## Origin of Moral Sentiment

Mill rejects the view that moral sentiments are innate intuitions. "The sentiment itself does not arise from anything which would commonly be thought of as the idea of expediency" [005-018] — it has a natural origin in the human capacity for sympathy and the instinct of self-defense. The sentiment evolves from "the primitive and spontaneous sentiment of justice as the *lex talionis*" [005-030] into a moralized feeling that incorporates considerations of general welfare.

## Sentiment and the Sanction of Morality

The moral sentiments serve as the **internal sanction** of utilitarian ethics. Mill identifies "a natural basis of sentiment for utilitarian morality" [003-010] in the social feelings of mankind — the desire to be in unity with our fellow creatures. This sentiment can be cultivated until it becomes "as deeply rooted in our character" as any innate principle [003-002].

## Rule and Sentiment

Justice requires both "a rule of conduct, and a sentiment which sanctions the rule" [005-022]. The rule identifies what justice demands; the sentiment provides the motivational force to obey it. The distinctive character of justice arises from the combination of a rational rule and an emotionally powerful sentiment [005-034].
