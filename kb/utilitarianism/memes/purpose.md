## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **purpose** denotes the end or object for which an action, rule, or institution exists — and, critically, the standard by which it is to be judged. Mill uses the concept of purpose polemically to distinguish the utilitarian understanding of morality from competing theories. Against intuitionists who treat moral rules as self-evident ends in themselves, Mill insists that every moral standard has a purpose, and that purpose is the promotion of happiness. "It is not my present purpose to criticize these thinkers," Mill writes of the intuitionist school, but his entire argument in *Utilitarianism* is structured around demonstrating that utility is the only coherent purpose for a system of morals [[001-006], [002-004]].

## Key Arguments

- **The rational purpose of human life**: Mill explicitly denies that any form of existence "cannot be the rational purpose of human life and action" simply because it is attainable. The objection that happiness is unattainable — and therefore an inadequate purpose — is met with Mill's empirical claim that the constituents of a satisfying existence are within reach. The purpose of moral philosophy is not to posit an unreachable ideal but to guide conduct toward an attainable good [[002-013]].

- **Purpose and the standard of morality**: Mill argues against "the grosser misconception of the purpose of a standard of morality" — the notion that a moral standard must provide a mechanical decision procedure for every case. The purpose of the utilitarian standard is to supply "a test of right and wrong," not to eliminate the need for judgment in applying it. This clarification is essential to Mill's response to critics who charge that utilitarianism cannot guide action in complex situations [[002-026]].

- **Divine purpose and utilitarian ethics**: Mill addresses the theological variant of utilitarian thinking by noting that those who believe God designed human nature with happiness as "His purpose in their creation" are themselves utilitarians of a sort. They hold that "utility is not only the test of what is right, but the very reason why it is right" — the purpose of divine creation being human happiness. This argument strategically aligns utilitarian morality with religious sentiment [[002-029]].

- **The purpose of sanctions**: Mill describes how "the whole course of human education and cultivation are bent to the purpose" of strengthening the internal sanction of conscience. The purpose of moral education is to associate the general happiness so firmly with individual feeling that the two become inseparable in the agent's motivational structure. This is the psychological mechanism by which purpose — the abstract end of morality — becomes an effective motive [[003-004]].

- **Temporary purpose versus enduring rule**: Mill distinguishes between actions that serve "some immediate object, some temporary purpose" and those governed by rules of justice whose purpose is the enduring protection of vital interests. A violation of justice "violates a rule which is of the utmost importance to the general welfare," even if the immediate purpose seems beneficial. This distinction grounds Mill's differentiation of justice from the remainder of morality [[002-031]].

## Related Concepts

- [[utility]] — the ultimate purpose of all moral action and the criterion by which all subordinate purposes are judged
- [[happiness]] — the substantive end that gives moral purpose its content; the purpose of morality is the promotion of the general happiness
- [[teleology]] — the philosophical framework in which purpose (telos) is the fundamental explanatory and normative concept
- [[sanction]] — the motivational mechanisms whose purpose is to align individual conduct with the general good
- [[justice]] — that branch of morality whose purpose is the protection of the most vital human interests

## See Also
