## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, a **disposition** is an enduring tendency of character — a mental and moral inclination that disposes an agent toward certain kinds of actions. Mill distinguishes between actions as the primary objects of moral evaluation and dispositions as secondary: a right action does not necessarily indicate a virtuous character, and "actions which are blameable, often proceed from qualities entitled to praise" [002-027]. Utilitarians evaluate dispositions not intrinsically but by their predominant tendency to produce good or bad conduct, "resolutely refusing to consider any mental disposition as good, of which the predominant tendency is to produce bad conduct" [002-027].

## Key Arguments

- **Dispositions evaluated by consequences**: Mill holds that "actions and dispositions are only virtuous because they promote another end than virtue" [004-003]. This is the core utilitarian position on character: a disposition is morally good not in itself but because of its tendency to promote happiness. However, Mill immediately adds that virtue, once established as a means to the ultimate end, may become desired for itself through association — "they not only place virtue at the very head of the things which are good as means to the ultimate end, but they also recognize as a psychological fact the possibility of its being, to the individual, a good in itself" [004-003].

- **The disposition to believe in objective morality**: Mill observes "a disposition to believe that a person who sees in moral obligation a transcendental fact, an objective reality belonging to the province of 'Things in themselves,' is likely to be more obedient to it than one who believes it to be entirely subjective, having its seat in human consciousness only" [003-006]. He rejects this disposition, arguing that the actual motivating force of moral feeling is "exactly measured by its strength" regardless of one's ontological beliefs about its origin.

- **Disposition vs. action in moral judgment**: Mill acknowledges the complexity of evaluating character. When an action is blameable but proceeds from praiseworthy qualities, "it modifies their estimation, not certainly of the act, but of the agent" [002-027]. This distinction between act-evaluation and agent-evaluation allows utilitarianism to account for the moral relevance of character without abandoning consequentialism.
