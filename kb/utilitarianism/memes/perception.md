# Perception

**Perception**, in Mill's moral philosophy, refers to the immediate apprehension or discernment of moral truths. Mill distinguishes between the *perception* of general moral principles and their *application* to particular cases, a distinction central to his critique of intuitionist ethics.

## Moral Perception and General Principles

Mill argues that the moral faculty "supplies us only with the general principles of moral judgments" and must be looked to "for the abstract doctrines of morality, not for perception of it in the concrete" [001-004]. Both the intuitive and inductive schools agree that "the morality of an individual action is not a question of direct perception, but of the application of a law to an individual case" [001-004]. The real dispute is not whether morality requires principles, but what the source and evidence of those principles are.

## Intuitive Perception versus Induction

Mill observes that "the philosophic supporters of that theory are now agreed that the intuitive perception is of principles of morality, and not of the details" [003-008]. The intuitionist school claims that moral principles are known *a priori*, requiring no evidence beyond the meaning of the terms. Mill's objection is not that intuition plays no role in moral cognition, but that appeals to intuition alone cannot resolve disputes between competing principles — a standard external to intuition is required.

## Perception and the Idea of Justice

In Chapter V, Mill notes that "the powerful sentiment, and apparently clear perception, which that word recalls with a rapidity and certainty resembling an instinct" has led many to regard justice as an inherent quality in things, independent of utility [005-001]. This apparent perceptual clarity is, for Mill, an illusion produced by the strength of the sentiment — the perception of justice feels immediate but is in fact shaped by the long association of justice with vital social interests.
