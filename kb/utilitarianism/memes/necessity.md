## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **necessity** denotes the condition under which a moral rule, obligation, or course of action becomes indispensable — not by metaphysical decree but by the accumulated force of social experience and the demands of human well-being. Mill draws a critical distinction between *physical necessity* (the laws of nature) and *moral necessity* (rules that have proven indispensable to social existence), arguing that the latter acquires its binding character through the principle of utility rather than through any *a priori* categorical imperative [[005-025]].

## Key Arguments

- **Moral necessity as analogous to physical necessity**: Mill observes that certain moral rules become so indispensable that "indispensability becomes a moral necessity, analogous to physical," not because they are logically inevitable but because their consistent enforcement is required for the general happiness [[005-025]].
- **No inherent necessity in human nature**: Mill explicitly rejects the claim that there is an "inherent necessity that any human being should be" a particular way. Human character is shaped by circumstances and education, not by a fixed essential nature, which has significant implications for moral responsibility and the possibility of moral improvement [[002-017]].
- **The necessity of impartiality in moral judgment**: Mill argues that humans are "under a necessity of conceiving themselves as" impartial spectators when making moral judgments, suggesting that the perspective of the disinterested observer is not optional but required by the structure of moral reasoning itself [[003-011]].
- **Necessity and the foundations of justice**: In Chapter V, Mill examines whether the rules of justice possess a distinctive kind of necessity. He argues that what we call the "primary necessity of social existence" — rules protecting security and basic welfare — constitutes the most indispensable part of morality [[005-042]].
