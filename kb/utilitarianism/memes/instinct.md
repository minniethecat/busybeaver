# Instinct

The concept of **instinct** in Mill's *Utilitarianism* appears primarily in discussions of moral psychology — particularly in arguments about whether moral judgment is instinctive or rational, and whether the sentiment of justice has an instinctual basis.

Mill grants that moral responses often have the *appearance* of instinct, but argues that this does not settle their normative status. Instinct provides raw motivational material; reason and the principle of utility determine whether that material should be endorsed or restrained.

## Instinct and Moral Perception

Mill opens Chapter 1 by situating his inquiry against a background of competing accounts. Some thinkers treat moral knowledge as arising from "a natural faculty, a sense or instinct, informing us of right and wrong" [001-003].

This instinctive model treats moral cognition as analogous to sense perception — immediate, involuntary, and not requiring inference. Mill acknowledges the phenomenological force of this view even as he subjects it to critical scrutiny.

## The Instinctive Appearance of Justice

Mill's most sustained discussion of instinct appears in Chapter 5, where he examines the sentiment of justice. He notes that the judgment of justice often arises with a "rapidity and certainty resembling an instinct" [005-001] — it feels immediate and compelling, as though hardwired.

However, Mill insists that this resemblance is misleading. "The notion of justice might be a peculiar instinct, and might yet require, like our other instincts, to be controlled and enlightened by a higher reason" [005-002]. The fact that a response feels instinctive does not make it authoritative.

He draws an analogy: just as the instinct of self-preservation must sometimes yield to moral considerations, so the "instinct" of justice must be evaluated against the principle of utility rather than taken as its own justification.

## The Impulse of Self-Defence

Mill identifies specific natural impulses that feed into the sentiment of justice. Chief among them is "the impulse of Self-defence" [005-018], which he describes as one of the animal impulses that "either are or resemble instincts" [005-018].

This impulse — the urge to retaliate against harm — provides the raw emotional energy behind the demand for punishment. But Mill is careful to distinguish the impulse itself from the moralized sentiment of justice, which requires the additional element of sympathy extending beyond the self to include all who may be harmed.

## Instinct and Moral Development

Throughout, Mill treats instinct as a developmental starting point rather than a normative destination. Instincts are "controlled and enlightened" by reason [005-002]; they provide the motivational material that moral education shapes into genuinely ethical dispositions.

This account coheres with Mill's broader naturalism about the moral faculty — it grows out of human nature but is not reducible to it, requiring cultivation and rational reflection to achieve its proper form.
