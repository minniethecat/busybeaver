## Definition

In Mill's moral psychology, the **constitution** of human nature refers to the general laws and structures of human emotion, cognition, and character that underlie moral phenomena. It is the explanatory substrate to which Mill appeals when accounting for both the formation of moral habits and the origin of the sentiment of justice [005-004].

Mill's use of "constitution" is deliberately naturalistic: rather than treating moral sentiments as transcendental facts or special provisions of Nature, he seeks to explain them as products of "the general laws of our emotional constitution" operating under specifiable social conditions [005-004].

## Key Arguments

- **Habit and the constitution**: Like "all other parts of our constitution," the moral faculties are "amenable to habit" — they can be shaped, strengthened, or enfeebled by the conditions in which they develop. This makes moral character a matter of cultivation rather than fixed endowment [004-011].

- **The constitution as an explanatory basis**: To determine whether the sentiment of justice is "inexplicable" and requires "a special provision of Nature," Mill proposes to examine "whether this particular attribute or combination of attributes would be capable of gathering round it a sentiment of that peculiar character and intensity by virtue of the general laws of our emotional constitution" [005-004].

- **Constitution and reform**: Because the constitution is malleable through habit and social conditions, Mill's analysis opens the door to moral progress: institutions and education can shape the emotional constitution toward greater sympathy and justice.
