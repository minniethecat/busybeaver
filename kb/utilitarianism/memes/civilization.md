## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian philosophy, **civilization** denotes the advanced state of social development characterized by moral progress, intellectual culture, and the refinement of human sympathies. Civilization is not merely a material or technological condition but encompasses the moral and emotional advancement of humanity. Mill argues that "civilization, virtue, everything on which" human happiness depends is promoted through the progressive cultivation of social feelings [002-032].

## Key Arguments

- **Civilization and moral progress**: The advance of civilization is inseparable from moral improvement. Mill asserts that "the influences of advancing civilization" transform the social state, making it "at once" more cooperative and more morally demanding [003-010]. As civilization develops, human relations become less violent and more governed by sympathy.

- **Universal moral consideration**: "In all states of civilization, every person, except an absolute" monarch, is born into a social position that entails obligations to others [003-011]. Civilization thus expands the circle of moral concern beyond immediate kin and tribe.

- **Deepening moral feeling**: As "civilization goes on," the connection between individual well-being and collective welfare "is felt to be more and more" natural and necessary [003-012]. Civilized life progressively strengthens the social feelings that Mill identifies as the foundation of utilitarian morality.

- **Civilization, virtue, and happiness**: Mill links civilization directly to both virtue and happiness, treating them as mutually reinforcing elements of human progress [002-032]. The advancement of civilization is itself a utilitarian good.
