## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **progress** refers to the gradual historical and intellectual development of moral understanding, both in ethical theory and in the practical moral sentiments of humanity. Mill situates his own work within this progressive narrative, observing that "the little progress which has been made in the development" [001-001] of a systematic first principle of ethics testifies to the difficulty of the enterprise, even as the utilitarian doctrine represents an advance beyond intuitionist and theological approaches.

## Key Arguments

- **Progress in ethical theory**: Mill treats the emergence of utilitarian ethics as part of a broader "progressive state of the human mind" [002-034] in which moral codes previously grounded in custom, religion, or intuition are subjected to rational scrutiny. He predicts "definite improvement" [002-034] as the utilitarian standard displaces these less systematic alternatives, arguing that a single, clear criterion of right and wrong is itself a mark of moral progress.

- **Progress in the moral sentiments**: Beyond the history of ethical doctrines, Mill traces the "origin and progressive" [005-013] development of the moral sentiments themselves — particularly the sentiment of justice. He describes how the natural feeling of resentment becomes progressively moralized through expanding social sympathy, transforming raw retributive impulse into principled concern for the general good. This is a historical and psychological process, not a sudden conversion.

- **The limits of progress**: Mill acknowledges that moral progress is incomplete and vulnerable. The "little progress" and lingering attachment to intuitionist and a priori methods are evidence that the rationalization of ethics is still underway. Progress is not guaranteed — it requires deliberate cultivation of the moral faculties through education and social institutions that reinforce the association between individual happiness and the general good.
