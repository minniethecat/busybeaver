## Definition

**Retaliation** (or the retaliatory impulse) is, in Mill's theory of justice, the natural psychological foundation from which the sentiment of justice develops. Mill identifies the "natural feeling of retaliation or vengeance, rendered by intellect and sympathy applicable to those injuries... which wound us through, or in common with, society at large" [005-020] as the raw material of justice. This natural impulse is not itself moral, but becomes so when it is "moralized by the social feeling" and subordinated to the general good.

## Key Arguments

- **Retaliation as the animal root of justice**: Mill argues that the desire to punish wrongdoing has a pre-moral, instinctual origin. "It is natural to resent, and to repel or retaliate, any harm done or attempted against ourselves, or against those with whom we sympathize" [005-019]. This retaliatory impulse is "common to all animal nature; for every animal tries to hurt those who have hurt... itself or its young" [005-019]. Justice is not a pure construction of reason but has its roots in this natural sentiment.

- **The moralization of retaliation**: The natural feeling of retaliation "in itself, has nothing moral in it; what is moral is, the exclusive subordination of it to the social sympathies, so as to wait on and obey their call" [005-020]. Without this moralization, the impulse "would make us resent indiscriminately whatever any one does that is disagreeable to us" [005-020]. Moralized retaliation is selective: it directs itself only at harms to the common good, not at merely personal grievances.

- **Retaliation and the social feeling**: The transformation of retaliation from a merely personal impulse into a moral sentiment depends on the capacity of "intellect and sympathy" to extend the scope of concern beyond oneself. A just person is characterized by "resenting a hurt to society, though not otherwise a hurt to themselves, and not resenting a hurt to themselves, however painful, unless it be of the kind which society has a common interest with them in the repression of" [005-020].

- **The intensity of retaliation and the importance of security**: Mill explains that the peculiar energy of the retaliatory sentiment — "the thirst for retaliation" — "derives its intensity, as well as its moral justification, from the extraordinarily important and impressive kind of utility which is concerned" [005-024]. The interest at stake is security, "to every one's feelings the most vital of all interests" [005-024]. The intensity of the retaliatory feeling is proportional to the importance of what it protects.
