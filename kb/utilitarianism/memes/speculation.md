# Speculation

**Speculation**, as Mill uses the term, refers to systematic philosophical inquiry into the foundations of ethics. The opening sentence of *Utilitarianism* laments "the backward state in which speculation on the most important subjects still lingers" [001-001], framing the entire work as a contribution to speculative ethics — the rational investigation of the ultimate criterion of right and wrong.

## The State of Ethical Speculation

Mill opens his treatise by observing the "little progress which has been made in the decision of the controversy respecting the criterion of right and wrong" [001-001]. From "the dawn of philosophy the question concerning the *summum bonum*" has been "the main problem in speculative thought," yet after two thousand years "the same discussions continue" [001-001]. This diagnosis motivates Mill's project: to clarify the utilitarian doctrine and advance the speculative enterprise toward resolution.

## Speculation and the Role of First Principles

A key insight of Mill's approach to speculation is that first principles in ethics, as in science, are typically the *last* results of inquiry, not the first. "The truths which are ultimately accepted as the first principles of a science are really the last results of metaphysical analysis" [001-002]. Applied to ethics, this means speculation cannot simply announce a supreme principle and deduce moral rules from it; rather, the principle of utility gains its authority through the gradual convergence of moral judgment upon it as a common standard.

## Kant and the History of Speculation

Mill situates his own work within the broader history of philosophical speculation, noting that Kant's system "will long remain one of the landmarks in the history of philosophical speculation" [001-006]. Yet he criticizes Kant for failing to derive actual moral duties from the categorical imperative without surreptitiously relying on utilitarian considerations. The history of speculation thus reveals, for Mill, the indispensability of the utilitarian standard.

## Speculation and the Idea of Justice

"In all ages of speculation," Mill writes, "one of the strongest obstacles to the reception of the doctrine that Utility or Happiness is the criterion of right and wrong has been drawn from the idea of Justice" [005-001]. The apparent self-evidence of justice has consistently resisted utilitarian reduction, making it the central challenge for utilitarian speculation from Socrates onward.
