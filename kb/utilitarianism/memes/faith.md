## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **faith** — in the sense of "good faith" or fidelity to engagements — is treated as a core obligation of justice. To break faith with another person is "confessedly unjust," whether by violating an express promise or by disappointing expectations knowingly raised [[005-007]].

Faith-keeping belongs to the class of perfect obligations — duties that correlate with a definite right residing in an assignable person [[005-016]].

## Key Arguments

- **Faith as a requirement of justice**: Mill identifies the breach of faith as one of the paradigm cases of injustice. Alongside depriving a person of a possession and treating a person worse than they deserve, breaking faith constitutes a wrong done to "some assignable person who is wronged" [[005-016]].

- **Faith and expectation**: The obligation to keep faith is grounded in the expectations our conduct creates in others. Mill specifies that the obligation holds "at least if we have raised those expectations knowingly and voluntarily" [[005-007]].

- **Not an absolute obligation**: Like other obligations of justice, faith-keeping may be overruled "by a stronger obligation of justice on the other side," or by conduct of the other party that absolves us of our obligation to them [[005-007]].
