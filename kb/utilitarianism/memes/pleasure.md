# Pleasure

**Pleasure** is the central positive component of happiness in utilitarian ethics. Mill defines happiness as "pleasure and the absence of pain" [002-003]. But Mill's most distinctive contribution is his distinction between **higher** and **lower** pleasures — a qualitative ranking that goes beyond Bentham's purely quantitative hedonic calculus.

## Higher and Lower Pleasures

Mill argues that "some kinds of pleasure are more desirable and more valuable than others" [002-005]. The pleasures of "the intellect, of the feelings and imagination, and of the moral sentiments" possess "a much higher value as pleasures than to those of mere sensation" [002-005].

The criterion for ranking pleasures is the **verdict of competent judges** — those who have experienced both kinds. "Of two pleasures, if there be one to which all or almost all who have experience of both give a decided preference, irrespective of any feeling of moral obligation to prefer it, that is the more desirable pleasure" [002-005].

> It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied. [002-006]

## Pleasure as the Sole Intrinsic Good

Mill endorses a **hedonistic theory of value**: "pleasure and freedom from pain are the only things desirable as ends" [002-004]. All other goods — virtue, knowledge, beauty, love — are desirable either for the pleasure they inherently contain or as means to the promotion of pleasure and the prevention of pain.
