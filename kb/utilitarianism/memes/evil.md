# Evil

**Evil** in Mill's utilitarian philosophy refers to harm or suffering inflicted upon sentient beings — the opposite of the good that the principle of utility aims to maximize. Mill analyzes evil both as a practical reality of the human condition and as a concept central to the definition of [[justice]].

## The Positive Evils of the World

Mill acknowledges that "most of the great positive evils of the world are in themselves removable" [002-018], rejecting the pessimistic view that suffering is inevitable and beyond human remedy. This optimism is grounded in the utilitarian conviction that human effort, guided by the principle of utility, can progressively eliminate the sources of misery. The anxiety "concerning the evils of life" can be mitigated through "a happy existence" and mental cultivation [002-021].

The practical stakes are high: if evils are removable, then moral philosophy has a genuine task — to identify and eliminate them. Mill criticizes those who "furnish us with excuses for evil doing, and means of cheating our own consciences" [002-036] by treating moral questions as insoluble.

## Evil and the Definition of Justice

The concept of evil is fundamental to Mill's analysis of justice. Justice centrally concerns cases where one person inflicts a positive evil upon another: to "inflict upon a person an evil" [005-006] without justification is the paradigm of injustice. To "obtain that (whether good or evil) which he deserves" [005-007] is the concern of distributive justice.

Mill identifies the most serious forms of evil as "acts of wrongful aggression, or wrongful exercise of power over some one" [005-036]. The disappointment of legitimate expectations also ranks "among human evils and wrongs" as one of the gravest [005-037].

## Evil for Evil: Retribution

Mill identifies the principle of **retribution** — "evil for evil" [005-036] — as a deeply rooted human sentiment closely connected to the feeling of justice. The natural desire to return harm for harm is the primitive origin of the justice sentiment.

However, Mill is careful to distinguish the natural feeling from the moral principle. Punishment is only justified when it serves "to prevent evil to others" [005-027]. The retributive impulse must be constrained by the general good: the "just principle of evil for evil" must be prevented "from being perverted to the infliction of evil without compensation" [005-038]. Left unchecked, retribution becomes mere vengeance.

## Immunity from Evil

Justice secures for each person an essential immunity from evil inflicted by others. The whole value of social existence depends on "all our immunity from evil" [005-024] being protected. Mill argues that the security of each person's interests is "the most vital of all interests" [005-025], and the rules of justice are the means by which society guarantees this security.

Mill notes that justice serves the dual function of "repressing evil by evil" while simultaneously promoting "good as well" [005-039]. The justified punishment of wrongdoers both deters future harm and affirms the moral order.
