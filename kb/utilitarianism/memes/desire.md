# Desire

**Desire** is a fundamental psychological state of wanting or seeking some object. In Mill's "proof" of the principle of utility (Chapter IV), desire plays the central evidentiary role: the only proof that something is desirable is that people **actually desire it**. "The sole evidence it is possible to produce that anything is desirable, is that people do actually desire it" [004-002].

## Desire as Evidence in the Proof of Utility

Mill's argument that happiness is the only thing desirable as an end rests on the claim that "people do actually desire it" [004-002]. He acknowledges that people desire things other than happiness — virtue, money, power, fame — but argues that these are originally means to happiness that "come to be desired for themselves" through association [004-005]. In the same way that money, originally desired for what it can buy, becomes an object of desire in itself, so do other goods.

## Desire vs. Will

Mill draws an important distinction between desire and will. "Will is a different thing from desire" [004-010]. A person of confirmed virtue may will the right action even when they no longer feel the original desire for it. "The distinction between will and desire thus understood is an authentic and highly important psychological fact" [004-011]. Habit transforms what was once desired into what is willed.

## The Objects of Desire

Mill insists that "there is in reality nothing desired except happiness" [004-008]. Whatever else is desired — virtue, knowledge, beauty — is desired either as part of happiness or as a means to it. The apparent diversity of human desires reduces, under analysis, to variations in the means by which people pursue the single end of happiness.
