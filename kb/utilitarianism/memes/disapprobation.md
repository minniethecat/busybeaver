## Definition

**Disapprobation** is the attitude of moral disapproval that, together with [[approbation]], constitutes the social sanction by which moral norms are enforced independently of legal penalty. Mill treats disapprobation as a crucial mechanism in utilitarian ethics: the fear of public disapprobation is one of the most powerful external [[sanction|sanctions]] motivating moral conduct [003-004], and the peculiar intensity of disapprobation attached to injustice distinguishes it from mere inexpediency [005-003].

## Key Arguments

- **Disapprobation and the standard of utility**: Mill confronts the objection that "happiness is not the standard of approbation and disapprobation" because people desire virtue for its own sake [004-003]. His response is that the utilitarian doctrine "maintains not only that virtue is to be desired, but that it is to be desired disinterestedly, for itself" — virtue has become part of [[happiness]], not an alternative to it. Hence happiness remains the ultimate standard of both approbation and disapprobation.

- **Disapprobation as the distinguishing mark of injustice**: Mill identifies that particular "epithet of disapprobation" as what sets judgments of injustice apart from those of mere inexpediency [005-003]. While many actions are disapproved as imprudent or unwise, only those that attract the special intensity of moral disapprobation are deemed unjust. This superior binding force of [[justice]] derives from the peculiar character of the disapprobation it evokes.

- **Public disapprobation as an external sanction**: When legal enforcement would be inexpedient, "we lament the impossibility, we consider the impunity given to injustice as an evil, and strive to make amends for it by bringing a strong expression of our own and the public disapprobation to bear upon the offender" [005-013]. Public disapprobation thus substitutes for legal punishment, forming a key part of what Mill calls the external sanctions [003-004] — the "fear of displeasure from our fellow creatures."

## Related Concepts

- [[approbation]] — the positive counterpart; together with disapprobation, these form the binary mechanism of social moral evaluation
- [[sanction]] — disapprobation is the core of the external social sanction, motivating compliance through fear of others' disapproval
- [[conscience]] — the internal sanction; when disapprobation is internalized through education and association, it becomes the pain of conscience
- [[justice]] — distinguished from other moral concepts precisely by the peculiar intensity of disapprobation it attracts
