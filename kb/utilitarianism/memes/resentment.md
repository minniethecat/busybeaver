## Definition

In Mill's theory of justice, **resentment** is the natural, pre-moral emotional impulse from which the sentiment of justice originates. It is an instinctive response to harm — "to feel resentment merely because we have suffered" [005-021] — that becomes progressively moralized when extended beyond self-interest to encompass harms suffered by others and by society as a whole. The sentiment of justice is "simply the natural feeling of resentment, moralized by being made coexistent with the demands of social good" [005-043].

## Key Arguments

- **Resentment as the raw material of justice**: Mill locates the psychological origin of justice not in abstract reason but in a powerful natural emotion. He observes that among all violations of moral rules, "none excite more resentment, either in the person suffering, or in a sympathizing spectator" [005-037] than injustices. This raw emotional intensity explains why justice carries a force and urgency that other moral obligations lack — it is rooted in an impulse more primitive and compelling than rational calculation.

- **The moralization of resentment**: Mere natural resentment is not yet justice — it is "the reverse of commendable" [005-021] when felt simply because one has been hurt. The transformation from raw resentment to moral sentiment requires two elements: first, the extension of resentment to harms suffered by others through sympathy, and second, its subordination to the general good. Only when resentment is "moralized by being made coexistent with the demands of social good" [005-043] does it become the sentiment of justice proper.

- **Resentment and the desire for punishment**: The natural connection between resentment and the impulse to retaliate explains why justice, uniquely among moral obligations, carries the concept of deserved punishment. The desire "to make the offender suffer" is the unrefined form of what becomes, when moralized through the principle of utility, the proportionate and impartial administration of punishment that justice requires.
