# Good

The **Good** (*summum bonum* or "highest good") is the ultimate object of ethical inquiry — that which is desirable in itself and towards which all other goods are means. Mill identifies the good with **happiness** (pleasure and the absence of pain), arguing that "whatever can be proved to be good must be so by being shown to be a means to something admitted to be good without proof" [001-007].

## The Argument for Happiness as the Good

Mill's argument in Chapter IV proceeds in two steps. First, he argues that **happiness is desirable** — the sole thing desirable as an end. Second, he argues that **the general happiness is the moral standard** — that each person's happiness is equally valuable.

The claim that "all desirable things are desirable either for the pleasure inherent in themselves, or as means to the promotion of pleasure and the prevention of pain" [002-004] is Mill's statement of **hedonism about value**. Things like virtue, knowledge, and beauty are good because they are either sources of pleasure or means to it.

## Instrumental vs. Intrinsic Good

Mill distinguishes between things that are good **as ends** and things that are good **as means**. "Whatever else is good is not so as an end but as a mean" — only happiness is intrinsically good [001-008]. This position anchors utilitarianism as a monistic theory of value: there is one fundamental good, and all other goods derive their value from their relationship to it.
