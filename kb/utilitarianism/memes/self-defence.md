## Definition

In Mill's analysis of justice, **self-defence** is the most basic of the natural impulses that underlie the sentiment of justice — the instinctive reaction against harm threatened or inflicted upon oneself. Mill treats this impulse as the raw material from which the moral concept of justice is constructed: self-defence, when combined with intelligence and the capacity for sympathy, is transformed from a merely animal reaction into a reasoned moral claim that society has an obligation to protect [[005-019], [005-036]].

## Key Arguments

- **Self-defence as the primitive root of justice**: Mill identifies the impulse of self-defence as the biological foundation on which the entire edifice of justice is built. The "impulses of self-defence, of defence of others, and of vengeance" are the natural sentiments that, when disciplined by rational intelligence and enlarged by sympathy, give rise to the distinctively moral demand that wrongdoers be punished and victims protected [[005-036]].

- **From instinct to moral principle**: The transition from mere self-defensive impulse to the moral concept of justice depends on two distinctly human capacities. First, "the superiority of intelligence" enables the individual to recognise that the harm they instinctively resist is genuinely injurious to their welfare. Second, "the power of sympathizing with human beings" extends the concern from one's own defence to the defence of all persons against similar harms, transforming a self-regarding impulse into a general moral rule [[005-019]].

- **Self-defence and the limits of moral obligation**: Mill's analysis implies that self-defence occupies a distinctive position in the hierarchy of moral claims. As the most basic and urgent of the interests that justice protects, the right of self-defence against direct attack is among the least contestable of moral permissions. Even a utilitarian framework that subjects all rules to the test of general utility recognises that the impulse to defend oneself against aggression is so fundamental that any moral system failing to accommodate it would be psychologically unsustainable [[005-019]].

- **Defence of others as moral extension**: Mill explicitly groups "defence of others" alongside self-defence as one of the natural impulses that contribute to the sentiment of justice. This pairing is significant: it shows that the moral concern for protecting others is not a separate, later development but is rooted in the same instinctive foundation as self-protection. Sympathy provides the bridge from self-regarding to other-regarding defence [[005-036]].

## Related Concepts

- [[justice]] — the moral domain within which the impulse of self-defence is refined into a system of rights and obligations
- [[security]] — the broader interest, recognised by Mill as "the most vital of all interests," that self-defence serves to protect
- [[vengeance]] — the natural impulse closely related to self-defence, which Mill identifies as the animal root of the desire to punish wrongdoers
- [[sympathy]] — the capacity that transforms the self-regarding impulse of defence into a general concern for the protection of all
