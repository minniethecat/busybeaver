# Metaphysics

In Mill's usage, **metaphysics** refers to the foundational analysis of the ultimate concepts and principles that underlie a science. Mill employs the term both to describe the grounding of ethical theory in facts of human nature and to critique rival philosophical approaches that claim *a priori* access to moral truth.

## Metaphysical Analysis and First Principles

Mill observes that "the truths which are ultimately accepted as the first principles of a science, are really the last results of metaphysical analysis" [001-002]. This claim has direct implications for ethics: the first principles of morality are not given *a priori* but are reached through the analysis of experience. Mill compares the relation of first principles to a science to "roots to a tree, which may perform their office equally well though they be never dug down to and exposed to light" [001-002]. The utilitarian principle, on this view, is the product of metaphysical reflection on moral experience, not a self-evident axiom.

## The Moral Faculty and Its Objects

Mill argues that "our moral faculty, according to all those of its interpreters who are entitled to the name of thinkers, supplies us only with the general principles of moral judgments; it is a branch of our reason, not of our sensitive faculty" [001-004]. This rationalist conception of moral cognition distinguishes Mill's view from intuitionist theories that treat moral perception as analogous to sensory perception. The moral faculty does not directly discern right and wrong in particular cases; it supplies the general principles from which particular judgments are derived.

## Desire and Metaphysical Necessity

In his proof of the principle of utility, Mill makes a striking metaphysical claim: "to desire anything, except in proportion as the idea of it is pleasant, is a physical and metaphysical impossibility" [004-009]. The inseparability of desire and pleasure is not, for Mill, a contingent psychological observation but a conceptual truth about the nature of desiring. This claim anchors the utilitarian argument that happiness is the only thing desirable as an end.
