## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **liberty** refers to the sphere of individual freedom of action — the capacity to pursue one's own happiness without undue interference from others. Mill identifies the "love of liberty and personal independence" as one of the elemental human drives that enter into the composition of happiness. However, he also acknowledges that liberty must be subject to "some restriction" for the sake of social cooperation, and that it is unjust to deprive anyone of liberty without sufficient warrant grounded in the principle of utility [[002-007], [005-006]].

## Key Arguments

- **Liberty as a constituent of happiness**: Mill includes the "love of liberty and personal independence" alongside the love of power and the love of excitement as fundamental human motivations. A utilitarian ethics that ignores the value human beings place on liberty would be incomplete — liberty is not merely instrumental to happiness but an integral part of what makes a life worth living [[002-007]].
- **The denial of liberty as a harm**: Mill recognises that when a person "is denied the liberty to use the sources of happiness" within their reach, they suffer a genuine loss. The deprivation of liberty constitutes one of the clearest cases of harm that the principle of utility is designed to prevent, forming a direct link between liberty and the harm principle [[002-017]].
- **Justice as the guardian of liberty**: Mill argues that it is "unjust to deprive any one of liberty, his property, or any other thing which belongs to him" unless some stronger claim of justice overrides. This places liberty among the protected interests that justice secures — a position Mill develops by connecting the natural sentiment of resentment at lost liberty with the evolved moral sentiment of justice [[005-004]].
- **The necessity of restricting natural liberty**: Mill acknowledges that justice requires "some restriction on the natural liberty of mankind" — a restriction justified not by abstract right but by its necessity for social existence. The utilitarian grounding of liberty means that its limits are drawn by reference to the consequences for general happiness, not by appeal to inviolable natural rights [[005-006]].
