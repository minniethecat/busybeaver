## Definition

The **criterion of right and wrong** is the central epistemological question of Mill's *Utilitarianism*. From the opening sentence of Chapter I, Mill frames the work as an inquiry into "the controversy respecting the criterion of right and wrong" [001-001]. The criterion is the standard or test by which actions, rules, and dispositions are judged morally right or wrong. Mill's answer — the Greatest Happiness Principle — holds that utility is the "ultimate criterion of conduct" [005-001].

## Key Arguments

- **The need for a criterion**: Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by observing that after two millennia of ethical philosophy, there remains no consensus on "the criterion of right and wrong" [001-001]. The lack of an agreed criterion produces endless moral disagreement and undermines the authority of moral rules.

- **The criterion must be ultimate**: Mill insists that the criterion of morality cannot be derived from something more fundamental, for otherwise that prior principle would be the true criterion. "If utility is the ultimate criterion, it must be because it is itself the test, not because something else proves it to be so" [004-002].

- **The criterion and the summum bonum**: Mill argues that the criterion of morality must be identical with the ultimate good. "If happiness is the sole end of human action, it necessarily follows that it must be the criterion of morality, since a part is included in the whole" [004-009]. The criterion of right action is whatever promotes the summum bonum.

- **Rival criteria**: Mill critiques alternative criteria proposed by intuitionist philosophers, who "acknowledge them as an ultimate criterion of conduct" [005-002] without providing rational justification. He argues that intuitionism merely reifies custom and prejudice as moral truths.

- **Critique of justice as criterion**: In Chapter V, Mill acknowledges that justice is often advanced as a rival criterion to utility. However, he argues that justice "is not the ultimate criterion" but rather a set of rules "which give to other maxims of justice their binding force" through their connection to utility [005-006].

## Related Concepts

- [[utility]] — Mill's proposed criterion of right and wrong
- [[justice]] — often advanced as a rival criterion but subordinated to utility
- [[maxim]] — subsidiary maxims of justice are validated by the ultimate criterion of utility
- [[principle]] — the first principle or criterion from which secondary moral rules are derived
- [[proof]] — Mill attempts to prove that utility is the sole criterion of morality (Ch. IV)
- [[happiness]] — the criterion must track the ultimate end of human action
