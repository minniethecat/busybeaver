# Opinion

**Opinion** plays a multifaceted role in Mill's utilitarian philosophy, functioning as a source of moral knowledge, a social **sanction** that enforces conduct, and a potential source of error when unexamined. Mill distinguishes between the "sanction of opinion" (social disapproval as a deterrent) and opinion as the deliverance of informed judgment — notably the "opinion of the competent judges."

## Opinion as Source of Moral Principles

Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by noting that the controversy over the criterion of right and wrong has divided philosophers not only over the standard itself but over the very source of moral knowledge:

> According to the one opinion, the principles of morals are evident *a priori*… according to the other doctrine, right and wrong, as well as truth and falsehood, are questions of observation and experience [001-004]

This frames the epistemological question at the heart of ethics: is moral knowledge intuitive or empirical? Mill's own position is that the utilitarian standard is arrived at through the same empirical methods that govern all other knowledge.

## The Sanction of Opinion

One of the most important functions of opinion in Mill's system is as a **sanction** — a source of motivation for moral conduct. Mill identifies the "sanction of opinion" alongside law and conscience as a key mechanism of moral enforcement:

> The hope of favour and the fear of displeasure from our fellow creatures… the sanction of opinion [002-028]

Society's approval and disapproval exert a powerful influence on conduct, and Mill notes that turning "the sanctions of opinion against such violations" is a crucial function of moral education [002-028]. Opinion, "enforced by law and opinion" [002-034], is constitutive of the moral environment in which individuals develop their character.

## Education, Opinion, and Moral Progress

Mill is keenly aware that opinion can be both a force for moral improvement and a source of stagnation. He observes that "education and opinion have consecrated" certain moral beliefs, making them appear self-evident when they may in fact be mere prejudice [003-001]. The task of moral philosophy is to subject such opinions to rational scrutiny.

At the same time, Mill is optimistic about the progressive direction of opinion. He describes how institutions and opinion once "directed… as it once was in the worst periods of history" have been gradually improved through rational debate [003-013]. Differences of opinion, far from being an obstacle, drive moral and intellectual progress.

## Opinion and the Competent Judges

Mill's reliance on the "opinion of the competent judges" is central to his argument for qualitative hedonism. Those who have experienced both higher and lower pleasures, and who give a "decided preference" to the higher, constitute the tribunal whose opinion settles questions of value:

> According to the utilitarian opinion, the end of human action is necessarily also the standard of morality [002-012]

But not all opinions are equal. The "opinion of the qualities of the person" [002-026] who renders the judgment matters — only those with the relevant experience are qualified to rank pleasures.

## Opinion and Justice

In his analysis of justice, Mill notes how certain actions are designated "by universal or widely spread opinion, as Just or as Unjust" [005-004]. But he immediately adds the crucial qualification: "opinions will differ" [005-005] as to the precise boundaries. This tension between the universality of the justice sentiment and the variability of opinions about its application is a central theme of Chapter V.
