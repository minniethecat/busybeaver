## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **intuition** refers to the epistemological claim that moral principles are known directly, without recourse to experience or observation of consequences. Mill identifies the intuitionist school of ethics as the principal rival to the utilitarian approach: whereas intuitionists hold that moral truths are apprehended *a priori*, utilitarians insist that morality must be grounded in the observable tendency of actions to promote happiness [[001-008]]. Mill treats intuitionism not as a complete alternative to utility but as a method that, when properly examined, either collapses into utility or relies on unexamined custom [[001-004]].

## Key Arguments

- **Intuition versus experience**: Mill argues that the intuitionist position fails to provide any criterion for resolving moral disagreements. When two intuitionists disagree about a moral principle, there is no method of adjudication beyond appeal to yet another intuition. The utilitarian standard, by contrast, offers an empirical test grounded in the general happiness [[001-004]].
- **Intuitive perception of moral principles**: Mill acknowledges that many philosophers agree "the intuitive perception is of principles of morality" rather than of particular cases. However, he contends that even this concession does not establish intuition as an independent source of moral knowledge, since the content of such principles invariably reflects the influence of experience and social custom upon the mind [[003-008]].
- **The way of intuition as insufficient method**: Mill observes that those who approach morality "solely in the way of intuition" cannot give a reasoned account of why one moral rule should prevail over another. Considerations "may be presented" to the intuitionist, but without a common standard like utility, moral reasoning reduces to assertion and counter-assertion [[001-008]].
