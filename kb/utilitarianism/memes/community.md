## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **community** denotes the social whole whose interests are the object of utilitarian moral calculation. Mill uses the concept in two key contexts: the distributive question of how social goods should be shared, and the question of whether individuals can apprehend a "community of interest" with the larger society [[005-010], [005-019]].

## Key Arguments

### Social feelings of mankind
Mill grounds the utilitarian moral sentiment in "the social feelings of mankind" — the desire to be in unity with fellow creatures. Strengthening social ties enlarges the sense of community beyond the self [[003-010]].

### Community and distributive justice
Mill considers claims about how "the produce of the labour of the community should be shared" as implicating justice. Distribution engages justice because it concerns what the individual may claim from the community [[005-010]].

### Community of interest as moral achievement
The capacity to apprehend "a community of interest between himself and" society marks moral development. The cultivated individual recognises that their welfare is bound up with the welfare of others [[005-019]].

### Reciprocity between individual and community
Justice requires both what the community should give to each person and what each person owes to the community. This reciprocity — "Each from his own" — is the core of utilitarian social ethics [[005-032]].
