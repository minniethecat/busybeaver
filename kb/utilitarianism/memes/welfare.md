# Welfare

**Welfare** in Mill's utilitarian ethics designates the well-being of persons — the state of affairs that the principle of utility aims to maximise. Mill speaks of "practically consulting the welfare of others" [[003-012]] as a core moral practice, and treats welfare as the ultimate test of social arrangements. The term encompasses both material and psychological dimensions of human flourishing.

## Welfare and the General Good

Mill equates welfare with the "general good" [[002-022]], the aggregate well-being of all persons affected by an action or policy. Conduct "opposed to the general good" is condemned, but Mill also recognises that "a direct impulse to promote the general good" [[002-022]] exists as a natural human motivation, grounded in the social feelings. Welfare is thus both the criterion of right action and an object of human concern.

## Welfare and Social Institutions

The promotion of welfare is not solely a matter of individual conduct. Mill notes that the "support of all present social well-being" depends on institutional arrangements, and that "the insufficiency" [[002-031]] of those arrangements can undermine welfare even when individuals act rightly. Certain conditions — such as the "maxims" protecting freedom — are "more vital to human well-being than any maxims, however important" [[005-034]] that govern particular cases. This hierarchical conception places basic liberties at the foundation of welfare.

## Welfare and Impartiality

The utilitarian commitment to welfare requires impartiality: each person's welfare counts equally. Mill's formula that each is to count for one and no one for more than one applies directly to the assessment of welfare. This distinguishes the utilitarian conception of welfare from partial or egoistic notions that prioritise the welfare of a particular individual or group.
