# Generalization

**Generalization** is Mill's term for the process of deriving secondary moral rules from the fundamental principle of utility. Drawing on his broader empiricist philosophy, Mill treats moral generalizations as empirically grounded rules of thumb that bridge the gap between the abstract utility principle and the concrete decisions of practical ethics.

## Intermediate Generalizations in Ethics

Mill warns against the temptation "to pass over the intermediate generalizations entirely, and endeavour to test each individual action directly by the first principle" [002-034]. Such a procedure would be impractical: without secondary rules derived from accumulated experience, moral reasoning would need to calculate the utility of every action from scratch. Intermediate generalizations — rules like "do not lie" or "keep your promises" — are not self-evident truths but empirically grounded summaries of what has tended to promote happiness across human history.

## Empirical Generalization from Experience

Mill describes moral rules as "empirical generalizations from the observed" [005-040]. Just as scientific laws are generalized from particular observations, moral rules are generalized from the observed tendencies of certain kinds of actions to produce happiness or unhappiness. These generalizations are fallible and revisable — they can be refined as experience accumulates — but they are indispensable for practical moral reasoning.

## The Limits of Generalization

Mill acknowledges that we should not be "altogether to be bound by the generalizations from specific experience" [005-041]. When established secondary rules conflict, or when novel circumstances render a generalization inapplicable, the moral agent must fall back on the first principle of utility directly. The relationship between the principle of utility and its derivative generalizations is thus not deductive but heuristic: the generalizations guide action reliably in ordinary cases, but the ultimate standard remains the tendency to promote happiness.
