# Moral Sense

The concept of a **moral sense** — an innate faculty for perceiving right and wrong — represents a major dividing line between intuitionist and utilitarian moral theories.

Mill engages with the moral sense tradition critically, acknowledging that moral perception *appears* instinctive, while arguing that the moral faculty is a rational capacity shaped by experience and cultivation rather than a direct quasi-perceptual intuition.

## The Intuitionist Account

The intuitionist tradition, with roots in Shaftesbury and Hutcheson, holds that human beings possess a "natural faculty, a sense or instinct, informing us of right and wrong" [001-003]. On this view, moral truths are perceived immediately, much as the senses perceive colors or sounds.

This "moral sense" is treated as analogous to the external senses — it directly apprehends moral qualities without the mediation of reasoning. The claim is that just as we cannot help but see a red object as red, we cannot help but perceive cruelty as wrong.

## Mill's Rationalist Response

Mill rejects the perceptual analogy. He observes that "our moral faculty, according to all those of its interpreters who are entitled to the name of thinkers, supplies us only with the general principles of moral judgments; it is a branch of our reason, not of our sensitive faculty" [001-004].

The crucial distinction is between principle and perception. The moral faculty does not deliver concrete moral verdicts directly; it provides the general principles from which particular judgments must be derived by application. This makes morality a rational, not sensory, enterprise.

Even the intuitionist school, Mill notes, ultimately agrees that "the morality of individual action is not a question of direct perception, but of the application of a law to an individual case" [001-004]. The dispute is therefore not about whether moral judgment involves reasoning, but about the *source* of the principles that reasoning applies.

## The Moral Faculty as Acquired Capacity

In Chapter 3, Mill develops a naturalistic account of moral development. He argues that "the moral faculty, if not a part of our nature, is a natural outgrowth from it; capable, in a certain small degree, of springing up spontaneously, and susceptible of being brought by cultivation to a high degree of development" [003-009].

This position navigates between two extremes. Against strict innatists, Mill denies that the moral faculty is a ready-made "part of our nature" with fixed deliverances. Against radical empiricists who would treat morality as wholly artificial, he insists that it grows *from* human nature, with a natural basis in the social feelings.

The moral faculty is thus an acquired capacity — learned, but not arbitrary; cultivated, but not fabricated. This account preserves the felt authority of moral judgment while allowing for its development and correction through experience.
