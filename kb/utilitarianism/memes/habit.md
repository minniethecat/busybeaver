# Habit

**Habit** in Mill's *Utilitarianism* refers to the settled disposition that shapes human conduct — a pattern of action so ingrained that it operates without conscious deliberation. Mill distinguishes between the **motive** of an action and its **moral character**, noting that "the great majority of good actions are intended not for the benefit of the world, but for that of individuals," and that these arise from "habitual motives of action" rather than from a constant calculation of utility [002-023].

## Habit and the Will

Mill draws a close connection between habit and the will. In the case of an "habitual purpose," action flows not from a fresh act of willing each time but from a settled character: "in the case of an habitual purpose, instead of willing the act because we desire it, we desire it because we will it" [004-010]. Habit thus transforms willing from a deliberate decision into a stable disposition — a process central to Mill's account of **virtue** formation.

## Volition and Habituation

The transition from deliberate choice to automatic response is key: "volition which has become habitual, and is put in operation by the force of habit" is no longer experienced as a conscious choice [004-011]. Mill goes further, arguing that "that which is the result of habit" occupies a distinct category in moral psychology — it is neither purely rational calculation nor blind instinct, but a cultivated disposition that serves utility by making moral conduct reliable [004-012].

## Habit in the Moral Life

For Mill, the moral life depends on habituation: we come to desire virtue not by constant reckoning of consequences, but by forming the habit of acting virtuously. This is why "those on which they habitually and with full assurance rely" become the stable foundation of moral conduct [005-037]. The social feelings of mankind — sympathy, conscience, the sense of justice — are all, in Mill's view, habits that have been cultivated by civilisation and that make utilitarian morality psychologically possible.
