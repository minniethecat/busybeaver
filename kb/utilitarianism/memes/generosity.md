# Generosity

**Generosity** — the disposition to give freely to others beyond what justice requires — is distinguished by Mill from the domain of perfect obligation. In his analysis of justice, Mill draws a crucial boundary: generosity belongs to the sphere of **beneficence**, not justice, and carries a different kind of moral force.

## Generosity Distinguished from Justice

Mill's key distinction appears in Chapter V of *Utilitarianism*: there is a "difference between justice, and generosity or beneficence. Justice involves something which" is not merely desirable but morally mandatory [005-017]. Generosity, by contrast, is praiseworthy but not compulsory. We may admire the generous person without condemning the person who merely meets the demands of justice.

## The Duty of Beneficence

Mill acknowledges that beneficence is not entirely optional. "In the case of charity or beneficence, which we are indeed bound to practise," there exists a genuine duty — yet this duty differs from the duty of justice [005-016]. The obligation to be beneficent is an **imperfect duty**: no specific person can claim a right to any particular act of generosity, even though everyone has a general duty to contribute to the welfare of others.

## Positive Beneficence and Social Interest

Mill notes that "the duty of positive beneficence" is something in which all members of society "have an unmistakable interest" [005-035]. A society in which generosity flourishes is one in which the general happiness is more fully realised. Generosity fills the space beyond what justice can compel — the voluntary contribution that transforms a merely orderly society into a genuinely humane one.
