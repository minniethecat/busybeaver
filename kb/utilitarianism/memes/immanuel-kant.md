# Immanuel Kant

**Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804) was a German philosopher whose system of **deontological ethics** stands as the principal rival to utilitarian moral theory. In *Utilitarianism*, Mill engages Kant as the most formidable representative of the *a priori* moral tradition — and uses Kant's own reasoning to demonstrate the indispensability of utilitarian considerations.

## Mill's Reference

Mill describes Kant as a "remarkable man, whose system of thought will long remain one of the landmarks in the history of philosophical speculation" [001-006]. This acknowledgment of Kant's stature makes Mill's subsequent critique all the more pointed: Kant's ethical system, despite its grandeur, fails on its own terms.

## Mill's Critique of the Categorical Imperative

Kant's **categorical imperative** — "So act that the rule on which thou actest would admit of being adopted as a law by all rational beings" — is presented as "a universal first principle as the origin and ground of moral obligation" [001-006]. Mill subjects this principle to a devastating critique:

> When he begins to deduce from this precept any of the actual duties of morality, he fails, almost grotesquely, to show that there would be any contradiction, any logical (not to say physical) impossibility, in the adoption by all rational beings of the most outrageously immoral rules of conduct. All he shows is that the consequences of their universal adoption would be such as no one would choose to incur. [001-006]

Mill's point is that Kant's supposedly *a priori* principle **smuggles in utilitarian reasoning through the back door**. What makes an immoral rule "outrageous" when universalized is not a logical contradiction but the badness of its **consequences** — which is precisely what the utilitarian standard measures.

## Kant's Implicit Utilitarianism

Mill returns to Kant in Chapter V (On the Connection Between Justice and Utility), reinforcing his earlier argument:

> When Kant propounded as the fundamental principle of morals, "So act that thy rule of conduct might be adopted as a law by all rational beings," he virtually acknowledged that the collective interest of mankind, as a practical test of morality, must be present to the mind of the agent when deciding on the morality of an act. [005-021]

The categorical imperative, Mill argues, is effectively a **disguised appeal to the general happiness**. To ask whether a rule could be willed as universal law is to ask whether its universal adoption would serve the collective interest — which is the utilitarian standard.

## Key Contributions

### The Categorical Imperative

Kant's central ethical principle holds that moral duties are **unconditional** — they bind all rational beings regardless of desires, goals, or consequences. The imperative takes several formulations, the most famous being the "universal law" formulation that Mill critiques [001-006].

### Deontological Ethics

Kant founded the **deontological** tradition in ethics, which judges actions by their conformity to moral rules rather than by their consequences. This stands in direct opposition to utilitarianism's **consequentialist** framework.

### The Kingdom of Ends

Kant's vision of a "kingdom of ends" — a community of rational beings treating each other always as ends and never merely as means — has affinities with Mill's impartiality requirement, though the two philosophers ground this requirement differently.

## Relationship to Utilitarianism

Kant is the **principal philosophical opponent** of the utilitarian tradition. Mill's engagement with Kant serves a dual purpose:

1. **Negative**: To demonstrate that *a priori* moral systems cannot generate concrete duties without covertly appealing to consequences — i.e., to utility.
2. **Positive**: To suggest that Kant's own commitment to the "collective interest of mankind" aligns him, perhaps unwittingly, with the utilitarian standard.
