## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **belief** refers to the cognitive component of moral judgment — the conviction that certain actions are morally right or wrong. Mill distinguishes between beliefs grounded in experience and utility from those purportedly derived from *a priori* intuition. He argues that moral beliefs have been "vitiated" by the influence of non-utilitarian doctrines, but that the ultimate foundation of correct moral beliefs lies in the principle of utility [[001-005]].

## Key Arguments

- **Belief and the theological sanction**: Mill examines whether moral obligation requires belief in a deity. He contends that even without "belief in a Providence," the psychological and social sanctions of utility remain effective [[002-029], [003-013]].
- **Belief in Duty as objective reality**: The strength of the feeling of duty is often taken as evidence of its objective character. Mill questions whether this belief derives from the "transcendental origin" of moral obligation or from the accumulated associations of utility [[003-006], [003-008]].
- **Belief and moral knowledge**: Mill draws a parallel between moral beliefs and other forms of knowledge, suggesting that beliefs about right and wrong should be subject to the same empirical scrutiny as beliefs about matters of fact [[005-018]].
