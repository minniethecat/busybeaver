# Rule

**Rule** in Mill's *Utilitarianism* serves as the intermediary between abstract principle and concrete action. Moral rules are not ultimate axioms but "rules of action" derived from experience — they guide conduct when direct calculation of utility is impractical. Mill opens the work by noting that all ethical theories presuppose "rules of action" for the sake of some end, and the task of philosophy is to identify "the one principle, or the rule for deciding between the various principles" [001-003][001-005].

## The Directive Rule of Human Conduct

Mill explicitly frames utility as "the directive rule of human conduct" — not merely a criterion of rightness after the fact, but a practical guide for decision-making [002-011]. This distinguishes Mill's utilitarianism from act-by-act calculation: the principle of utility provides a "rule" that governs whole classes of actions, allowing moral agents to act on settled norms rather than constantly computing consequences.

## Moral Rules for the Multitude

Mill recognises that moral rules serve a social function. He describes "the rules of morality for the multitude" as those that have been "laid down" through the accumulated experience of mankind [002-034]. These rules — concerning honesty, fidelity, justice — are not arbitrary conventions; they encode the long-tested generalisations of human experience about which kinds of conduct tend to promote happiness. Their authority derives from their track record of utility.

## The Golden Rule and Rational Universality

Mill invokes the golden rule of Jesus — "do as you would be done by" — as the "complete spirit of the ethics of utility" [002-022]. He frames the utilitarian ideal as shaping "our conduct by a rule which all rational beings might adopt" [005-022]. This convergence of utility with the ideal of impartial, universalisable rules anticipates the **rule-utilitarian** reading of Mill: what makes an action right is its conformity to a rule whose general observance maximises happiness.

## Rules and Exceptions

Mill is sensitive to the problem of exceptions. Moral rules are not absolute — they can be "overruled by a stronger obligation" [005-007]. However, he warns against the human tendency to treat one's own case as exceptional: "it is not unfrequently that a person feels an exception to moral rules, and, when under temptation, thinks it justifiable" [002-036]. The proper response is not to abandon rules but to formulate them with sufficient precision that genuine exceptions are rare.
