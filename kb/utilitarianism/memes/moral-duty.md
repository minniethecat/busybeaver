# Moral Duty

**Moral duty** is the obligation to perform or abstain from certain actions, grounded in the utilitarian principle that the action tends to promote or diminish the general happiness. In Mill's framework, duty is distinguished from mere desirability: a duty is an action that a person may "be compelled to fulfil" — it is a matter of moral requirement, not merely of recommendation. The concept of duty provides the structure within which utilitarian morality translates the general goal of happiness into concrete obligations.

## The Rule of Duty

Duty, for Mill, operates primarily through rules rather than through case-by-case calculation. The "rule of duty" serves as the practical guide for conduct: an action is morally permissible if "the rule of duty does not condemn them" [002-024]. This rule-based structure reflects Mill's recognition that direct appeal to the principle of utility in every decision would be impractical and prone to error. Instead, secondary principles — themselves tested by their conformity to the general happiness — define the content of duty.

## The Feeling of Duty

Mill devotes significant attention to the psychological dimension of duty. The "feeling of duty" [002-023] is the subjective experience of moral compulsion — the sense that certain actions must be done regardless of inclination. This feeling is reinforced by the "internal sanction of duty" — conscience — which Mill describes as "a feeling in our own mind" [003-004] that attaches pain to the violation of duty. Whether this feeling is innate or acquired, Mill argues that when "associated with utility" [003-009] it acquires the full force of moral obligation.

## The Limits of Duty

Not every good action is a duty. Mill distinguishes between actions that are morally required and those that are merely desirable or praiseworthy: "we do not call it his duty" [005-014] when an action, though beneficial, falls outside the domain of moral compulsion. For example, certain acts of charity, while virtuous, are not "regarded as a duty in itself" [005-008] but rather as instances of beneficence that go beyond what can be demanded. The line between duty and supererogation is drawn by the presence or absence of a correlative right, and by whether society can reasonably compel performance.

## The Foundation of Duty

Mill insists that "this great moral duty rests upon a still deeper foundation" [005-039] than mere social convention or intuition — namely, the principle of utility itself. All duties ultimately derive their binding force from their tendency to promote the general happiness, and any purported duty that cannot be justified on utilitarian grounds must be re-examined.
