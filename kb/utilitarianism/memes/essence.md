## Definition

In Mill's usage, **essence** denotes the defining or constitutive character of a concept — that which makes a thing what it is and distinguishes it from all else. The term appears at critical junctures in Mill's argument: in relation to the essence of general happiness as a moral criterion, the essence of law as penal sanction, the contested essence of justice as equality, and the essential elements identified in the idea of justice. Mill's analytical method characteristically seeks to distinguish the accidental from the essential in moral concepts, clarifying their boundaries by isolating their defining features [[003-004], [005-034]].

## Key Arguments

- **The essence of morality as general happiness**: Mill describes the internal sanction of utilitarian morality as dependent on "the general happiness is the essence, or even only the criterion, of good conduct." Whether happiness is treated as the defining nature of morality or merely as its evaluative standard, it functions as the organizing center of ethical life — the point around which moral sentiments, social sympathies, and educational practices converge [[003-004]].

- **Equality as the contested essence of justice**: Mill observes that equality "often enters as a component part both into the conception of justice and into the practice of it, and, in the eyes of many persons, constitutes its essence." However, he immediately notes the variability of this judgment: "the notion of justice varies in different persons, and always conforms in its variations to their notion of utility." What is taken as essential to justice is, in practice, determined by each person's understanding of what serves the general welfare [[005-009]].

- **Penal sanction as the essence of law**: In his analysis of the foundations of moral obligation, Mill identifies "the idea of penal sanction, which is the essence of law" as a concept that "enters not only into the conception of injustice, but into that of any kind of wrong." This essential connection between law and punishment provides the conceptual bridge by which the legal origins of the notion of justice expand into the broader domain of moral obligation. The essence of law — the threat of sanction — becomes the essence of moral duty itself [[005-014]].

- **The essential elements of the idea of justice**: Mill's concluding analysis identifies several features "which we have found to be of the essence of the idea of justice": the desire to punish those who have done harm, the knowledge that harm has been done to a definite individual or individuals, and the concept of a right residing in some person or persons. These essential elements together constitute the distinctive character of justice — what separates it from generosity, beneficence, and other moral virtues that lack the element of enforceable obligation to determinate individuals [[005-034]].
