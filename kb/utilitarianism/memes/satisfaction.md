## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian philosophy, **satisfaction** refers to the subjective experience of having one's desires or expectations met. Mill distinguishes between the complete satisfaction of desires — which he treats as a psychological ideal rarely attained — and the more modest satisfactions accessible in ordinary life. Satisfaction functions as both a component of happiness and a criterion for evaluating the success of moral arrangements. Mill notes that the "general feeling of satisfaction" within a community bears witness to the practical adequacy of its justice principles [[002-007], [005-030]].

## Key Arguments

- **Satisfaction and the completeness of happiness**: Mill acknowledges that no human being can achieve "the most complete satisfaction of all the desires" they share with other sentient beings. This candid recognition of human limitation grounds his argument that utilitarian happiness is not about perpetual bliss but about a life rich in the satisfactions actually accessible to beings of our nature [[002-007]].
- **Satisfaction and the sources of tranquillity**: Mill argues that a cultivated mind can find "in tranquillity the sources of satisfaction accessible to him, without concerning himself" with the uncertainties of a wider world. The internal sources of satisfaction — intellectual activity, moral sentiment, aesthetic appreciation — are more reliable than external goods [[002-021], [005-031]].
- **Satisfaction and primitive desire**: The satisfaction derived from moral conduct becomes associated with the satisfaction of "our primitive desires" through the psychological mechanism of association. What begins as a means to satisfying basic drives becomes itself a direct source of satisfaction [[004-007]].
