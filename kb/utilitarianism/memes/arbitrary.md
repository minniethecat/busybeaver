## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **arbitrary** describes a choice, distinction, or principle that lacks a rational basis and rests instead on mere preference, impulse, or convention. Mill's project is fundamentally anti-arbitrary: he seeks to establish a non-arbitrary foundation for morality by grounding it in the principle of utility, which provides a determinate criterion — the tendency to promote happiness — against which moral claims can be tested.

## Key Arguments

- **Against arbitrary acceptance of moral principles**: Mill insists that the acceptance or rejection of the utilitarian principle "must not depend on blind impulse or arbitrary choice" [[001-008]]. Moral philosophy demands reasons, and the principle of utility offers a rational basis for moral judgment that distinguishes it from mere custom or intuition. Without a determinate standard, all moral claims become arbitrary.

- **The vulnerability of artificial morality to analysis**: Mill warns that moral associations created purely by social conditioning are fragile in the face of rational inquiry. When "intellectual culture goes on," such associations "yield by degrees to the dissolving force of analysis" [[003-009]]. If the feeling of duty rests on an arbitrary basis rather than on natural human sentiment, it will not withstand rational scrutiny. This is why the utilitarian foundation must be grounded in human nature rather than convention.

- **Arbitrariness in theories of justice**: In the final chapter, Mill illustrates the problem of arbitrariness through conflicting theories of punishment. Different justifications — deterrence, retribution, proportionality — each appear "unanswerable" from their own perspective, yet "any choice between them, on grounds of justice, must be perfectly arbitrary" [[005-032]] without an ultimate standard to adjudicate. The principle of utility resolves this arbitrariness by providing the common criterion that these competing theories lack.
