# Devotion

**Devotion** — the disinterested dedication of oneself to the good of others — is a moral ideal that Mill places at the centre of utilitarian ethics. For Mill, the "morality of self-devotion" is not the exclusive property of any one philosophical school but "a possession which belongs by as good a right to them as either to the Stoic or to the Christian" [002-021]. What matters is that devotion serves the general happiness.

## Disinterested Devotion and the Moral Faculty

Mill locates devotion within the broader category of **disinterested** moral feeling. The moral faculty, he argues, operates with a "limit of disinterested devotion" — a capacity to act for others without regard to personal interest [003-004]. This disinterestedness "is always in the mind itself" [003-007]: it is a quality of the agent's disposition, not merely of outward action.

## Devotion as an Intrinsic Good

In Mill's analysis, disinterested devotion can become something desired for its own sake. Virtue, including devotion to others, "is to be desired disinterestedly, for itself" [004-003]. Through habituation, "in those who love it disinterestedly it has become so, and is desired and cherished... as a part of their happiness" [004-004]. Devotion thus transforms from an instrumental good into a constituent of personal well-being.

## Self-Devotion and the General Happiness

Mill insists that self-devotion must be directed toward a genuine end. It is not valuable merely as self-denial; it must contribute to the happiness of others. The utilitarian standard evaluates devotion by its consequences — does it increase the sum total of well-being? Mere self-sacrifice without this end would be morally empty.
