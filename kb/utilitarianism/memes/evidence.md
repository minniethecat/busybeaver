## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian system, **evidence** refers to the rational grounds on which moral claims — including the principle of utility itself — are justified. Mill distinguishes sharply between the *source* from which a moral principle derives its authority and the *evidence* that supports it as true. The question of evidence is central to his argument that ethics, like natural science, is a matter of observation, experience, and inductive reasoning, not of *a priori* intuition [[001-004]].

## Key Arguments

- **Evidence as the dividing line between ethical schools**: Mill frames the entire debate between intuitionism and utilitarianism as a disagreement about evidence. Intuitionists claim that moral principles carry "their evidence in themselves, and are independent of experience," while the inductive school — Mill's own — holds that moral knowledge depends entirely upon evidence drawn from observation and the general progress of human knowledge [[001-004], [005-026]].

- **The sole evidence for desirability**: In a celebrated passage, Mill argues that "the sole evidence it is possible to produce that anything is desirable is that people do actually desire it." This claim applies the method of empirical proof to the ultimate end of conduct — happiness. The parallel between visible objects and desirable ends is not a logical fallacy (as critics have charged) but an assertion that desirability is established by the same inductive method that establishes all matters of fact [[004-002]].

- **Evidence and moral adjudication**: Mill insists that all moral questions — including the comparison of competing obligations and the adjudication of conflicts between secondary principles — are "questions of observation and experience," to be settled "upon evidence." There is no special moral faculty that bypasses the ordinary process of weighing empirical considerations, just as there is no *a priori* access to scientific truths [[004-009]].

- **Etymology is not evidence**: Mill warns against mistaking the historical origin of a word for evidence about the concept it now denotes. "Etymology is slight evidence of what the idea now signifies," he observes in the context of justice — a methodological caution that applies across his entire philosophical method and reflects his commitment to conceptual analysis grounded in present usage rather than historical accident [[005-011]].

- **Degrees of evidence in moral science**: Mill acknowledges that moral philosophy cannot match the precision of mathematics; the "kind and degree of evidence which constitutes scientific proof" varies across domains. Ethics operates with the evidence appropriate to its subject matter — human conduct and character — which admits of probability and generalisation rather than demonstration [[005-041]].
