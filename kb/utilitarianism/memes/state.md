# State of Society

Mill uses the concept of a **state of society** — the prevailing condition of social and moral development — as a crucial contextual variable in ethical reasoning. Moral obligations, the strength of sanctions, and the content of justice all depend, in part, on the state of society in which they operate.

## The Backward State

Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by noting "the backward state in which speculation on the" foundations of morals remains, despite centuries of philosophical effort [001-001]. This backwardness is not merely intellectual; it reflects a broader condition in which moral theory has failed to achieve the systematic clarity found in other domains of inquiry.

The backward state of moral philosophy is significant because it means that even basic questions — what makes an action right, how moral terms are defined — remain contested in ways that the natural sciences have long since resolved.

## The Social State as Natural and Necessary

In Chapter 3, Mill develops a naturalistic account of social development. "The social state is at once so natural, so necessary, and so habitual to man, that" it requires no artificial justification [003-010]. Human beings are by nature social creatures, and the social state is the context in which moral capacities develop.

"And since in all states of civilization, every person" is embedded in a network of social relationships, the moral sanctions that operate through these relationships — public opinion, sympathy, the desire for esteem — are available in every society, though with varying force [003-011].

## Progress and the Improving State

Mill's optimism about moral progress is grounded in the possibility of social improvement. "In an improving state of the human mind, the influences" that shape moral character grow stronger and more enlightened [003-013]. Education, political reform, and the accumulation of experience all contribute to the gradual elevation of the social state.

The contrast between "the comparatively early state of human advancement in which" we find ourselves and the higher states toward which we may aspire is itself a moral motivator [003-014]. The utilitarian standard provides the direction for this improvement: toward conditions that better promote the general happiness.
