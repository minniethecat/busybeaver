# Interest

**Interest** is a foundational concept in Mill's utilitarian ethics, operating at both the individual and collective levels. Mill's theory requires that individuals learn to align their personal interests with the "collective interests of mankind" [002-021], a process he regards as essential to moral motivation and social cohesion.

## Self-Interest and the Collective Interest

Mill acknowledges that self-interest is a powerful human motive but insists that utilitarian morality requires looking beyond it. Private utility concerns "the interest or happiness of some few persons" [002-025], whereas the utilitarian standard demands consideration of the general happiness. The transition from self-interest to collective concern is facilitated by "the strongest motives both of interest and of sympathy" [003-012], which together demonstrate to each person that their own well-being is inseparable from that of others.

## Interest as the Foundation of Moral Obligation

Mill grounds moral obligation in the recognition that "the interests of all are to be consulted" [003-011]. The utilitarian principle requires that every person's interests count equally in the moral calculus. The "consideration due to the interests of our fellow creatures" [003-008] is not a matter of mere altruism but a rational recognition that each individual's happiness forms part of the aggregate that the principle of utility seeks to maximize.

## The Interest of Security

In Mill's analysis of justice, the "interest involved is that of security, to every one's feelings the most vital of all interests" [005-024]. Security is the most fundamental interest because without it, all other goods become precarious. This explains, Mill argues, why the rules of justice — which protect security — carry a special stringency and emotional force that distinguishes them from other moral rules.

## Common Interest and the Social Feelings

Mill argues that the utilitarian standard operates through "the importance, to the common interest of mankind, of maintaining inviolate" the sentiment of justice [005-006]. The "sense of apprehending a community of interest between himself and the human society of which he forms a part" [005-019] is, for Mill, the psychological foundation that makes utilitarian morality possible: once this community of interest is felt, each person can regard the general happiness as a good to be pursued.
