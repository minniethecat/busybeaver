## Definition

**Security** is, in Mill's utilitarian theory of justice, the most vital of all human interests — the foundational good upon which all other goods depend. Mill argues that "security no human being can possibly do without; on it we depend for all our immunity from evil, and for the whole value of all and every good, beyond the passing moment" [005-024]. Unlike most other benefits, which are needed by some but not by others, security is a universal and non-substitutable condition of well-being, giving the duties that protect it — the duties of justice — their uniquely stringent and compelling character.

## Key Arguments

- **Security as the ground of justice**: Mill locates the special force of justice in the extraordinary importance of security. The "interest involved is that of security, to every one's feelings the most vital of all interests" [005-024]. While "all other earthly benefits are needed by one person, not needed by another; and many of them can, if necessary, be cheerfully foregone, or replaced by something else; but security no human being can possibly do without" [005-024]. This universal indispensability explains why justice, which protects security, is "incomparably the most sacred and binding part of all morality."

- **The link between security and retaliation**: The natural sentiment underlying justice is the "thirst for retaliation; and this thirst derives its intensity, as well as its moral justification, from the extraordinarily important and impressive kind of utility which is concerned" [005-024]. The instinct to retaliate is moralized and channeled by the recognition that threats to security are threats to the very conditions of social existence.

- **Security and the social instinct**: Mill traces the human concern for security to the capacity to apprehend "a community of interest between himself and the human society of which he forms a part, such that any conduct which threatens the security of the society generally, is threatening to his own, and calls forth his instinct... of self-defence" [005-019]. This extension of self-protective concern from the individual to society is, for Mill, a product of superior intelligence and sympathy — the cognitive and affective foundations of justice.

- **Security and rights**: Because security is universally indispensable, the obligations that protect it correlate with genuine moral rights — claims that "society ought to defend me in the possession of" [005-024]. The institution of rights is the social mechanism by which security is guaranteed to each individual, making rights "the most sacred and binding part of all morality" precisely because they secure the conditions of all other goods.
