## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **well-being** designates the comprehensive welfare of human beings — encompassing both the material and social conditions necessary for happiness. It is the ultimate end that moral rules serve, and the most vital rules are those whose observance is indispensable to its preservation [005-034].

Mill treats human well-being not as a single homogeneous state but as a complex condition sustained by multiple interdependent social practices, among which veracity occupies a foundational position [002-031].

## Key Arguments

- **Veracity as well-being's foundation**: The trustworthiness of human assertion is "the principal support of all present social well-being," and the insufficiency of that trust does more harm than any other single factor to "keep back civilization, virtue, everything on which human happiness on the largest scale depends" [002-031].

- **Well-being and the hierarchy of moral rules**: Moral rules that protect the "essentials of human well-being" — particularly those forbidding harm and interference with freedom — are of "more absolute obligation than any other rules for the guidance of life" [005-034].

- **Social and individual dimensions**: Well-being has both individual (freedom from harm, personal happiness) and social (trust, civilization, virtue) dimensions, and the latter are sustained by rules whose general observance is itself a condition of well-being.
