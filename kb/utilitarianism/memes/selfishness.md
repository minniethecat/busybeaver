## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **selfishness** denotes the tendency to prioritise one's own interests at the expense of others. Mill treats selfishness as a major obstacle to both personal happiness and social well-being, identifying it as one of the principal causes of life's unsatisfactoriness. However, he distinguishes selfishness from legitimate self-interest, arguing that the utilitarian principle — which requires each agent to count their own happiness as exactly equal to that of any other person — provides the rational corrective to selfish partiality [[002-009], [002-016]].

## Key Arguments

- **Selfishness and the decay of character**: Mill warns that those who lack sufficient mental cultivation or social sympathy tend to "sink into indolence and selfishness" as they age. The antidote to this decline is the cultivation of higher pleasures and the strengthening of sympathetic ties to others [[002-009]].
- **Selfishness as a cause of unhappiness**: Mill identifies selfishness as second only to the privations of "youth and health" as a principal cause of life's unsatisfactory character. He argues that those who live exclusively for their own gratification find that the sources of pleasure diminish over time, while those who cultivate concern for others discover an inexhaustible source of satisfaction [[002-016]].
- **The limits of selfishness as a moral rule**: Mill observes that "a rule even of utter selfishness could not possibly be adopted" as a universal maxim because human beings are constitutively social. Even the egoist depends upon the cooperation of others, and this interdependence forces a recognition that pure selfishness is practically as well as morally untenable [[005-021]].
