## Definition

In Mill's moral psychology, **sensibility** (or "passive sensibility") is the capacity to be affected — the receptive dimension of the mind that registers pleasure and pain, desire and aversion. Mill pairs it in opposition to volition (the active faculty): "Will, the active phenomenon, is a different thing from desire, the state of passive sensibility" [004-010]. Sensibility is thus the foundational layer of utilitarian psychology — it is through sensibility that the原始 data of pleasure and pain enter the mind, providing the raw material from which desires, and eventually volitions, are formed.

## Key Arguments

- **Sensibility as the origin of desire**: Mill's psychological model traces all motivation to sensibility. Desires are not innate ideas but responses of the sensitive faculty to experienced pleasures and pains. This empiricist grounding is essential to the utilitarian project: if all desire originates in sensibility to pleasure and pain, then pleasure and pain are the only things desirable as ends, which Mill takes to establish the principle of utility.

- **The decay of sensibility and its implications**: Mill acknowledges that "passive sensibilities" [004-010] may diminish over time through changes in character or circumstance. A person of confirmed virtue may "persist in acting on [their purposes], even though these pleasures are much diminished, by changes in his character or decay of his passive sensibilities" [004-010]. This fact — that habituation can outlast the sensibility that gave rise to it — explains both the stability of virtuous character and the possibility of actions persisting beyond their original utility.

- **Sensibility and the cultivation of moral feeling**: Just as the will can be cultivated through habit, sensibility itself is educable. Mill's broader argument implies that moral education must attend to the refinement of sensibility — training individuals to feel pleasure and pain at the right objects — since sensibility is the root from which all moral motivation grows. The utilitarian's task is not merely to calculate consequences but to cultivate the sensibilities that make the general happiness an object of personal concern.
