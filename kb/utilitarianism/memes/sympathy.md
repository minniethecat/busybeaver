## Definition

**Sympathy** in Mill's utilitarian ethics is the capacity to share in the feelings of others — the "social feelings of mankind" that form the psychological foundation of both the internal sanction of morality and the sentiment of justice. Mill identifies sympathy as one of the "powerful natural sentiments" that, when cultivated by education and social institutions, constitute the binding force of utilitarian morality [003-003]. It is through sympathy that individual happiness becomes connected to the happiness of all, transforming the Greatest Happiness Principle from an abstract doctrine into a lived moral motive.

## Key Arguments

- **Sympathy and the internal sanction**: Mill argues that the internal sanction of conscience is built upon a foundation of "social feelings of mankind — the desire to be in unity with our fellow-creatures" [003-003]. Sympathy is the raw material from which the moral sentiments are formed: "associations, derived from sympathy, from love, and still more from fear" [003-004] produce the binding power of moral rules.

- **Sympathy versus interest**: Mill acknowledges that both "motives both of interest and of sympathy" [003-012] contribute to moral motivation. However, he argues that the social state itself makes sympathy increasingly powerful: as civilization advances, human beings come to feel "that entircness of sympathy with all others" that makes cooperation indispensable [003-014].

- **Sympathy and the justice sentiment**: In his analysis of justice (Chapter V), Mill identifies sympathy as a constitutive element of the emotion of injustice. The feeling that an act is unjust arises partly from "the impulse of self-defence, and the feeling of sympathy" [005-018]. A human being, by virtue of his "superior range of sympathy" [005-019], is capable of feeling wrong when others are harmed — even strangers and distant persons.

- **The enlarged sympathy**: Mill envisions moral progress as the expanding circle of sympathy. "The human capacity of enlarged sympathy" [005-022] grows through education and social development, extending moral concern beyond the tribe to all humanity. This expansion of sympathy is the psychological mechanism by which utilitarianism's universalistic ideal can become a practical moral force.
