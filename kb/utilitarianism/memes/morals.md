## Definition

In Mill's usage, **morals** is a practical art directed at determining the rules and principles of right conduct.

Mill distinguishes morals from theoretical sciences: principles function as "roots to a tree, which may perform their office equally well though they be never dug down to and exposed to light" [001-003].

## Key Arguments

- **Morals as a practical art**: The purpose of moral inquiry is to guide conduct. Mill criticizes those who "assume the ordinary precepts of morals as of *a priori* authority" without examining their underlying principle [001-005].

- **The foundation of morals is utility**: "Utility, or the Greatest Happiness Principle" is the foundation of morals [002-003]. Every system of morals implicitly relies on utility, even when claiming *a priori* derivation.

- **Standard versus motives**: Utility provides the standard for judging right and wrong. Individual actions may be motivated by habit or sentiment. "The multiplication of happiness is, according to the utilitarian ethics, the object of virtue" [002-025].

- **Sanctions of morals**: Morals derive binding force from external sanctions (social disapproval, law), internal sanctions (conscience), and religious sanctions. Conscience is "the ultimate sanction of the principle of utility" [003-003].

- **Morals and justice**: Justice is a particularly important class of moral rules protecting security, "the most vital of all interests" [005-039]. Its special intensity reflects the importance of security, not a separate moral foundation.
