## Definition

**Duty** in Mill's utilitarian ethics refers to actions that are morally obligatory — those that a person may "rightly be compelled to perform" [005-014]. Mill distinguishes duty from the broader class of morally desirable actions: while all right actions contribute to utility, only those that society may legitimately enforce through sanctions constitute duties proper. "We do not call anything wrong unless we mean to imply that a person ought to be punished in some way for doing it" [005-014].

## Key Arguments

- **Duty and the internal sanction**: Mill identifies the "internal sanction of duty" as "a feeling in our own mind; a pain, more or less, attendant on violation of duty" [003-004]. This subjective feeling of obligation is, for Mill, the product of education, association, and the "social feelings of mankind," not an innate moral sense.

- **Duty distinguished from expediency**: Mill argues that "it is not a necessary condition to the acceptance of the utilitarian standard that every action shall be done from a feeling of duty" [002-023]. Actions that promote utility are right regardless of the motive; duty concerns what may be enforced, not what motives are praiseworthy.

- **Duty and justice**: In Chapter V, Mill develops the relationship between duty and [[justice]], arguing that justice concerns "a rule of conduct, and a sentiment which sanctions the rule" [005-014]. Duties of justice are distinguished from other moral duties by the presence of a correlative right held by some assignable person.

- **Perfect and imperfect obligations**: Mill distinguishes between duties of perfect obligation (which correlate to rights and may be enforced) and imperfect obligations (such as beneficence, which are meritorious but not compulsory in particular instances). "Reasons of prudence, or the interest of other people, may militate against actually exacting it" [005-014].

- **The duty of beneficence**: Mill notes that society has been "inculcating on each other the duty of positive beneficence" [005-035], acknowledging that while particular acts of beneficence may not be compelled, the general disposition is a moral duty.

## Related Concepts

- [[obligation]] — a related term; Mill sometimes uses obligation interchangeably with duty
- [[justice]] — duties of justice constitute the most stringent class of moral obligations
- [[sanction]] — duty is enforced through internal (conscience) and external sanctions
- [[conscience]] — the internal sanction experienced as pain when duty is violated
- [[rights]] — duties of perfect obligation correlate with assignable rights
- [[expediency]] — contrasted with duty; expedient actions promote utility but are not obligatory
- [[beneficence]] — an imperfect duty: meritorious but not compellable in particular cases
