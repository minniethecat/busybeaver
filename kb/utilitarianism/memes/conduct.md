# Conduct

**Conduct** — human action considered under moral evaluation — is the primary object of ethical judgment in Mill's utilitarian framework. The "directive rule of human conduct," according to Mill, is the principle of utility: actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness [002-011]. Conduct is judged not by the agent's motives but by its consequences.

## The Criterion of Right Conduct

Mill establishes the Greatest Happiness Principle as the ultimate standard: "the end and precepts for human conduct, by the observance of which an existence such as has been described might be secured" [002-012]. This standard serves "as the directive rule of human conduct" — not as a direct algorithm for every decision, but as the foundational principle from which secondary moral rules derive [002-011].

## Conduct and Consequences

A central tenet of utilitarian ethics is that "right in conduct is not the agent's own happiness, but that of all concerned" [002-022]. This distinguishes utilitarian conduct from egoistic action: the moral agent must be "strictly impartial as a disinterested and benevolent spectator" [002-022]. Mill insists that "rules of conduct cannot be so framed as to require no exceptions" [002-036], acknowledging that secondary rules must be applied with practical wisdom.

## Habit and Conduct

Mill emphasizes the role of **habit** in shaping moral conduct. "Both in feeling and in conduct, habit is the only thing which imparts certainty" [004-012]. The cultivation of habitual virtue through education and social influence is, for Mill, essential to making utilitarian conduct second nature — a point developed in his discussion of the **moral faculty** and its cultivation.

## Conduct as the Object of Judgment

Mill argues that conduct is the ultimate object of moral evaluation: utility provides the means "by which to judge of all human conduct" [004-009]. While we may have moral feelings about a person's character or motives, it is only through their outward conduct that moral rules are applied. The effects of "any line of conduct on the general happiness" [002-033] are what determine its moral worth.
