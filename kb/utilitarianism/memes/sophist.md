# Sophist

The **Sophists** were itinerant teachers of rhetoric and argumentation in ancient Greece, most famously represented by Protagoras. In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, the term appears as a foil: Mill frames the utilitarian position as the Socratic alternative to "the popular morality of the so-called Sophist" [001-002], thereby aligning utilitarian ethics with the philosophical seriousness of Socrates against the rhetorical facility of Sophistic argument.

## Mill's Use of the Term

Mill's reference to the Sophists is strategic and historical. He opens the treatise by evoking the scene of "the youth Socrates" listening to "the old Protagoras" and asserting "the theory of utilitarianism against the popular morality of the so-called Sophist" [001-002]. This framing achieves two purposes: it situates the debate over moral foundations in the deepest tradition of Western philosophy, and it pre-emptively dismisses the opponents of utilitarianism as occupying the Sophistic role — purveyors of popular prejudice rather than rigorous reasoning.

## Sophistry as False Reasoning

Mill uses the term "sophistry" to characterise arguments that rest on superficial or deceptive grounds. Certain moral objections to utilitarianism, he suggests, "rest on little better than sophistry" [002-037] — they appear plausible but lack genuine philosophical foundation. The charge of sophistry is Mill's way of asserting that his opponents' arguments, like those of the ancient Sophists, substitute rhetorical effect for logical substance.

## Philosophical Significance

By casting utilitarianism as the Socratic position and its critics as Sophists, Mill draws on one of philosophy's founding narratives: the triumph of reasoned inquiry over rhetorical manipulation. This rhetorical strategy underscores his broader claim that utilitarian morality, far from being a novel or narrowly modern doctrine, represents the mature development of a philosophical tradition reaching back to Socrates himself.
