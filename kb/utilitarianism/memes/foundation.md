## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, the **foundation** of morality is the ultimate ground or basis upon which ethical judgments rest. Mill identifies the question of the foundation of morality — "what is the *summum bonum*?" — as "the main problem in speculative thought" throughout the history of philosophy [[001-001]]. The foundation is not a deductive premise from which moral rules are logically derived, but the underlying principle that gives moral rules their binding authority.

## Key Arguments

- **The historical search for a foundation**: From "the dawn of philosophy," thinkers have sought a single criterion of right and wrong. Mill observes that after two thousand years, "neither thinkers nor mankind at large seem nearer to being unanimous on the subject" [[001-001]]. This persistent disagreement reveals the difficulty of establishing a foundation that commands universal assent.

- **Utility as the foundation of morals**: Mill states the utilitarian creed explicitly: "The creed which accepts as the foundation of morals, Utility, or the Greatest Happiness Principle, holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [[002-003]]. The foundation is thus not an abstract principle but one grounded in the actual experience of pleasure and pain.

- **The social feelings as a firm foundation**: Mill locates the ultimate foundation of utilitarian morality in human nature itself — "the social feelings of mankind; the desire to be in unity with our fellow creatures" [[003-010]]. This natural sympathy provides a "firm foundation" that does not depend on artificial sanctions or theological authority. Even if utility "had no foundation in human nature," the sanctions of education and opinion could still sustain it [[003-009]].

- **The deeper foundation of justice**: In the concluding chapter, Mill argues that the duty of equal regard for all rests "upon a still deeper foundation, being a direct emanation from the first principle of morals, and not a mere logical corollary from secondary or derivative doctrines" [[005-039]]. This distinguishes the foundational principle from the secondary rules that derive from it.
