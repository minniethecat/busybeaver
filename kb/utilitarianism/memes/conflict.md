## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **conflict** refers to the collision between competing moral obligations or principles that cannot all be simultaneously satisfied. Mill recognises that "there exists no moral system under which there do not arise unequivocal cases of conflicting obligation" [[002-037]]. The existence of such conflicts is not a defect peculiar to utilitarianism but an unavoidable feature of applied ethics.

## Key Arguments

- **Conflicting considerations as a fact of morals**: Mill insists that the recognition of "conflicting considerations" is a hallmark of any serious ethical doctrine — "all doctrines do, that have been believed by sane persons" [[002-036]]. The question is not whether conflicts arise but how they are resolved. A moral system without a determinate standard of right and wrong has no principled way to adjudicate them.

- **The challenge of conflicting obligations**: In cases of genuine conflict, the agent must determine which obligation takes priority. Mill argues that these conflicts "are overcome practically with greater or with less success according to the intellect and virtue of the individual" [[002-037]]. This acknowledges that moral conflict resolution depends partly on judgment and character, not merely on rule-application.

- **Conflict between individual and collective aims**: Mill addresses the potential conflict between the individual and society, noting that a person "still needs to be conscious that his real aim and theirs do not conflict; that he is not opposing himself to what they really wish for, namely their own good, but is furthering it" [[003-015]]. This concern for alignment between personal conduct and the general good is central to utilitarian morality.

- **Conflicting conceptions of justice**: In the domain of punishment, Mill observes how "many conflicting conceptions of justice come to light in discussing the proper apportionment of punishments to offences" [[005-029]]. Different theories of punishment — retribution, deterrence, rehabilitation — each appeal to justice but yield incompatible prescriptions, illustrating the need for an ultimate standard.
