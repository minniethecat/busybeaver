# Expediency

**Expediency** (*simple expediency*) refers to the consideration of what is merely useful or convenient in a particular case, without reference to general rules or overriding moral principles. Mill distinguishes expediency sharply from both utility and justice, warning against the confusion that "commonly attaches to simple expediency" [005-003].

## Expediency vs. Utility

While utility is the general principle that actions are right insofar as they promote happiness, expediency is often invoked to justify exceptions to rules for short-term advantage. Mill cautions that many people associate utility with "a rule of such transcendant expediency" that they dismiss it as not truly expedient [002-032]. The confusion between utility and expediency is one of the most persistent objections to utilitarianism.

## Expediency vs. Justice

The crucial distinction for Mill is between "morality and simple expediency" [005-014]. Justice involves rules backed by a particularly intense sentiment, while mere expediency lacks this moral force. Cases of justice are always cases of expediency (they promote welfare), but not all cases of expediency rise to the level of justice: "the difference is in the peculiar sentiment which attaches to the former" [005-043].

## Expediency and Inequality

In Chapter V, Mill notes that some justify inequality "on grounds of expediency" [005-005], and that communists "consider it unjust that the produce of the labour of the community should be shared on any other principle than that of exact equality" — but Mill treats this as a matter of "differences of opinion about expediency" [005-010] rather than a settled question of justice.
