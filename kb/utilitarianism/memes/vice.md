# Vice

**Vice** in Mill's utilitarian framework is the negative counterpart to [[virtue]] — a disposition or pattern of conduct that tends to produce unhappiness rather than happiness. Mill treats vice as a real psychological phenomenon, not merely the abstract absence of virtue, and analyzes its relationship to pleasure, desire, and human development.

## Vice and the Absence of Virtue

Mill explicitly pairs vice with virtue as objects of desire that are as real as pleasure and the absence of pain:

> They desire, for example, virtue, and the absence of vice no less really than pleasure and the absence of pain [004-003]

This formulation establishes that the desire for virtue — and the aversion to vice — are not derivative or instrumental but have become "a thing desirable in itself" through association with pleasure [004-004]. The moral agent comes to value virtuous conduct intrinsically and to recoil from vicious conduct in the same way.

## Indolence as Vice

Mill identifies one specific form of vice: **indolence**, or habitual laziness. He writes of those "in whom indolence amounts to a vice" [002-015], noting that such persons do not desire excitement or active engagement with life. This connects vice to the cultivation of character — a person who sinks into "indolence and selfishness" [002-009] fails to develop the higher faculties, which Mill regards as essential to human happiness.

## Vice and the Utilitarian Calculus

Within the utilitarian framework, vice is wrong because it tends to produce unhappiness. Just as actions are "right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003], vicious dispositions are condemned because they systematically diminish well-being. The utilitarian assessment of vice is thus consequentialist: a trait is vicious to the extent that its cultivation and expression reduce the sum total of happiness.
