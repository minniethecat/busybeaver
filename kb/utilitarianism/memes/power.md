## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **power** operates in two distinct but interrelated registers: as an object of human desire — one of the "great objects of human life" alongside fame and wealth — and as a social force that shapes moral conduct through sanctions, law, and the influence of public opinion. Mill traces the natural attraction of power to the instrumental access it provides to other goods, warning that power exercised without moral constraint becomes a threat to justice and general happiness [[004-005], [005-013]].

## Key Arguments

- **Power as object of desire**: Mill places power alongside fame and fortune as one of the primary objects of human striving. However, he argues that power's allure lies not in any intrinsic value but in "the immense aid it gives to the attainment of our other wishes" — a psychological observation consistent with the utilitarian claim that all desires ultimately trace back to pleasure and the avoidance of pain [[004-005], [004-006]].

- **The psychological power of moral sentiment**: The internal sanction of conscience — "a feeling in our own mind" — possesses "great power" to influence conduct even in the absence of external enforcement. Mill argues that this psychological power, when cultivated through education and habit, is "sufficiently strong to act as a restraint upon conduct" and forms the most reliable foundation for moral motivation [[003-003], [003-006]].

- **Power as an attribute of morality itself**: Mill rejects the notion of morality as "despotically imposed by the power of society," instead conceiving of moral obligation as arising from the rational recognition of general utility. The binding force of moral rules does not depend on sovereign command but on the reasoned conviction that they serve the common good — a position that distinguishes utilitarian ethics from both theological and authoritarian accounts of morality [[003-015]].

- **Power and the problem of justice**: In the domain of justice, Mill identifies the "wrongful exercise of power" as a primary category of injustice. The sentiment of justice — "so powerful" that it appears intuitively self-evident — is, in Mill's analysis, a composite of the natural impulse to retaliate against harm and the rational recognition that society has an obligation to protect individuals from the abuse of power. This dual analysis allows Mill to explain both the felt urgency of justice claims and their rational foundation in utility [[005-001], [005-025], [005-036]].

- **The limits of social power**: A recurring theme in Mill's political philosophy is the danger of unlimited social authority over individuals. Even when exercised in the name of morality, "so unlimited an amount of power over individuals" risks becoming a greater evil than the conduct it seeks to regulate. The principle of liberty thus functions as a necessary constraint on the legitimate exercise of collective power [[005-013]].

## Related Concepts

- [[authority]] — the legitimate exercise of power within a recognized moral or political framework
- [[obligation]] — the binding claim that moral rules, supported by social power, impose on individuals
- [[justice]] — the domain of enforceable moral claims, centrally concerned with protecting individuals from the abuse of power
- [[sanction]] — the source of moral motivation, including the "powerful natural sentiment" that underlies conscience
- [[liberty]] — the principle that limits the scope of legitimate social power over the individual
