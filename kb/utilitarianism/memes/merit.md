## Definition

In Mill's analysis of justice, **merit** (or desert) is the principle that each person should obtain what they deserve — good for good conduct, evil for evil conduct. It constitutes, in Mill's words, "perhaps the clearest and most emphatic form in which the idea of justice is conceived by the general mind" [005-031].

Merit is not presented by Mill as a primitive or self-evident concept. Rather, it is a derivative principle of justice whose binding force is explained by reference to utility — specifically, to the social interest in maintaining the connection between conduct and outcome that sustains both security and reciprocity.

## Key Arguments

- **Merit as a principle of justice**: The idea "that each person should obtain that (whether good or evil) which he deserves; and unjust that he should obtain a good, or be made to undergo an evil, which he does not deserve" is one of the fundamental maxims that the sentiment of justice enforces [005-031].

- **The exception for unmerited evil**: Even the sacred rule of veracity admits of exception "when the withholding of some fact … would save an individual (especially an individual other than oneself) from great and unmerited evil" — showing that desert considerations can override even the most stringent moral rules [002-032].

- **Merit and social justice**: Mill notes the controversy over whether superior talent deserves superior remuneration: those arguing against claim the less favored deserve "compensation … for this unmerited inequality of advantages," while proponents argue superior service earns greater reward [005-031].
