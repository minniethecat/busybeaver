## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **courage** is a moral virtue whose value derives not from any intrinsic quality but from its contribution to the general happiness. Courage — the capacity to act rightly in the face of fear, opposition, or personal cost — exemplifies the utilitarian understanding of virtue as an instrumental good: it is desirable not as an end in itself, but as a means to "the promotion of the general happiness" [[002-026], [002-019]].

## Key Arguments

- **Courage as a component of moral character**: Mill describes the moral ideal in terms that implicitly require courage — the "amiable, brave, or benevolent" person whose character produces good effects. Courage here is not a standalone excellence but a disposition that enables other virtues to be exercised effectively [[002-026]].
- **Virtue as a means to happiness**: Following the utilitarian principle, courage — like all virtues — is originally cultivated as a means to happiness. Mill explains that virtue becomes desired "for its own sake" only through psychological association, but its ultimate justification remains its contribution to well-being [[004-008]].
- **The person of confirmed virtue**: Mill's concept of the "person of confirmed virtue" describes an individual whose moral dispositions — including courage — have become so deeply ingrained that acting rightly has become habitual and pleasurable. Such a person does not need to summon courage for each moral decision; it has become part of their character [[004-010]].
- **Moral courage versus physical courage**: Within utilitarian ethics, moral courage — the willingness to uphold principles against social pressure — holds particular significance, as the sanctions of public opinion can be more powerful deterrents to right action than physical danger.
