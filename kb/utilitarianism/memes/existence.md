## Definition

In Mill's utilitarianism, **existence** is the fundamental substrate of all moral evaluation — it is the condition of sentient beings whose happiness or suffering constitutes the subject matter of ethics. Mill invokes existence in two distinct but related senses: as the qualitative mode of life available to beings with different capacities (a "manner of existence"), and as the bare fact of sentience that grounds all moral claims. The utility principle derives its normative force from the fact that each person's "sentient existence" matters — that happiness is desirable and suffering undesirable for every being capable of experiencing them [[002-023]].

## Key Arguments

- **Higher and lower modes of existence**: Mill's qualitative hedonism rests on the claim that beings with developed faculties prefer "the manner of existence which employs their higher faculties." A person with cultivated capacities "can never really wish to sink into what he feels to be a lower grade of existence," even if the lower existence promises greater immediate satisfaction. This preference is the empirical basis for distinguishing higher from lower pleasures — it is grounded in the actual choices of competent judges who have experienced both modes of existence [[002-006], [002-007]].

- **An enviable existence is attainable**: Mill argues that the principal sources of unhappiness — poverty, disease, and the unkindness of others — can be overcome through social progress. "An existence such as has been described might be, even now, the lot of many," and with continued improvement it could become accessible to all. The recognized "non-existence of an acknowledged first principle" of ethics has been a major obstacle to moral progress, but the attainability of a satisfying existence under utilitarian principles is Mill's central optimistic claim [[002-017], [001-005]].

- **The moral relevance of sentient existence**: Mill extends the scope of utilitarian concern to "every human being's sentient existence," grounding the principle of equal consideration in the universal capacity for suffering and enjoyment. The demands of critics who would limit utilitarian calculation to "the swine's pleasure" miss this qualitative dimension entirely — the utilitarian standard properly understood refers to the existence appropriate to human beings with developed faculties [[002-023]].

- **Existence of conflicting considerations as a moral fact**: Mill acknowledges "the existence of conflicting considerations" as an inescapable feature of moral life. The utilitarian standard does not eliminate moral conflict but provides the framework for resolving it — by determining which course of action tends to produce the greatest balance of happiness over unhappiness. This recognition distinguishes Mill's approach from naive forms of consequentialism that deny the reality of moral dilemmas [[002-036]].

- **The existence of moral feeling as a social fact**: The binding force of utilitarian morality depends on "the existence of a mass of feeling which must be broken through" before any individual can violate the general good. This social-psychological foundation — the internal sanction of conscience shaped by education and sympathy — is what transforms the principle of utility from an abstract standard into a living moral force [[003-005]].

## Related Concepts

- [[happiness]] — the quality of existence that utilitarianism seeks to maximize; the ultimate end to which all sentient existence aspires
- [[pleasure]] — the experiential component of existence that distinguishes higher from lower modes of life
- [[quality]] — Mill's key modification of Benthamite hedonism, distinguishing modes of existence by the quality, not merely quantity, of pleasure they afford
- [[progress]] — the historical process by which an enviable existence becomes attainable for all, through the reduction of poverty, disease, and social cruelty
