## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **estimation** refers to the process by which the value of pleasures, actions, and principles of justice is assessed.

Mill uses the term for: estimation of pleasures by competent judges [002-006]; estimation of persons through moral judgment [002-027]; and estimation of justice versus expediency [005-037].

## Key Arguments

- **Quality affects estimation of pleasures**: Against Bentham, Mill insists quality must be considered "in estimating all other things" [002-006]. The preference of competent judges provides evidence for qualitative rankings.

- **Estimation of justice versus expediency**: Justice concerns that "which places the Just, in human estimation, above the simply Expedient" [005-037]. A violated right — a disappointed legitimate expectation — marks an act as unjust.

- **Maxims as instruments of estimation**: "Most of the maxims of justice current in the world" are "simply instrumental to carrying into effect the principles of justice" [005-038]. Impartiality and equality are practical rules, not self-evident truths.

- **Estimation of agents versus acts**: Knowledge of motives "modifies their estimation, not certainly of the act, but of the agent" [002-027]. Act-estimation is governed by utility; agent-estimation considers motive and character.
