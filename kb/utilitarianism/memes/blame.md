## Definition

**Blame** is the moral attitude of holding an agent responsible for wrongful conduct. In Mill's analysis, blameworthiness is closely tied to the utilitarian conception of right and wrong: actions are blameable when they tend to produce harm and the agent could reasonably have been expected to act otherwise. Mill notes the important distinction that "actions which are blameable, often proceed from qualities which are themselves praiseworthy" — a recognition of the complexity of moral psychology [[002-027]].

## Key Arguments

- **Blame and moral obligation**: Mill distinguishes between mere inexpediency and genuine moral wrongfulness. We do not blame someone for every suboptimal choice; rather, blame is reserved for violations of moral obligation — actions that society has a right to punish or sanction [[005-014]].
- **Blame and the justice sentiment**: The impulse to blame is connected to the natural feeling of retaliation that Mill identifies as the psychological root of justice. The desire to see wrongdoers punished is a primitive sentiment, refined by reason and sympathy into the moral concept of blame [[005-021]].
- **Blameable laws and civil disobedience**: Mill argues that a law "judged to be bad, may blamelessly be disobeyed" when it fails to serve the general good. This principle distinguishes between legal obligation and moral obligation, grounding the right of conscientious disobedience [[005-006], [005-008]].
