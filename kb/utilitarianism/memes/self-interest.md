## Definition

**Self-interest** in Mill's utilitarian framework designates the pursuit of one's own well-being, understood not as narrow selfishness but as "intelligent self-interest" — a rational assessment of what truly conduces to one's long-term happiness [[005-022]]. Mill distinguishes sharply between self-interest and the moral principle of utility: the latter asks each person to consider the general happiness impartially, not merely their own. Self-interest is a psychological fact about human motivation; utility is a normative principle about what ought to be pursued.

## Key Arguments

- **Intelligent versus narrow self-interest**: Mill contrasts the "human conception of intelligent self-interest" with mere selfish impulse. Enlightened self-interest recognizes that one's own happiness is intertwined with the happiness of others, partly through the social feelings that Mill identifies as the natural basis of utilitarian morality.
- **Self-interest and the sanctions of utility**: The external sanctions of utility — the approval and disapproval of others, legal penalties, and divine will — all operate by making it in an agent's self-interest to act morally. The internal sanction (conscience) also connects self-interest to morality by making moral violation painful to the agent.
- **Self-interest and impartiality**: The principle of utility requires each person to count their own happiness for no more than it is worth — "as strictly impartial as a disinterested and benevolent spectator." This demand for impartiality is in tension with the natural priority each person gives to their own interest, a tension that Mill's theory of the social feelings is designed to resolve.
