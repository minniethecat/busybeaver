## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **excitement** figures as a key dimension of human happiness and motivation. Mill identifies the "love of excitement" as one of the elemental human drives that "do really enter into and compose" the fabric of moral psychology. He treats excitement alongside tranquillity as one of the two fundamental ingredients of a satisfying life, arguing that a proper understanding of happiness must account for both [[002-008], [002-015]].

## Key Arguments

- **Excitement as a component of happiness**: Mill acknowledges that the pursuit of excitement — whether through the "love of power," intellectual stimulation, or aesthetic experience — is a genuine and legitimate source of human satisfaction. It is not a mere passion to be suppressed, but an element of the good life that utilitarianism must recognise and accommodate [[002-008]].
- **The continuity of pleasurable excitement**: Mill considers the objection that human happiness is unattainable because "continuity of pleasurable excitement" is impossible. He responds by distinguishing between continuous high excitement — which is neither possible nor desirable — and a life that balances excitement with tranquillity. The highest form of existence is not perpetual stimulation but a rhythm of engagement and repose [[002-014]].
- **Tranquillity and excitement as complementary goods**: Mill presents "tranquillity, and excitement" as the two poles of human well-being: "With much tranquillity, many" people find moderate excitement sufficient for happiness, while those capable of higher pleasures can find excitement in intellectual and moral pursuits without sacrificing inner calm [[002-015]].
