## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **virtue** is not an end in itself but a means to happiness. Initially desired only for the pleasure or utility it brings, virtue can become desired "for its own sake" through psychological association ([004-008]). Mill denies that virtue is inherently superior to happiness — rather, it is one of the "ingredients of happiness" ([004-003]), cultivated because virtuous conduct reliably produces the greatest good.

## Key Arguments

- **Virtue is originally desired as a means to happiness.** Mill observes that people "desire, for example, virtue, and the absence of vice no less really than pleasure and the absence of pain" ([004-003]). But this desire develops from experience: virtuous actions produce happiness, so virtue itself becomes associated with pleasure.

- **Psychological association transforms means into ends.** Through habituation, virtue transitions from instrumental good to intrinsic good. "Those who desire virtue for its own sake, desire it either because the consciousness of it is a pleasure, or because the consciousness of being without it is a pain" ([004-008]). The distinction between means and ends dissolves in human psychology.

- **Confirmed virtue is a stable character trait.** Mill describes "a person of confirmed virtue" ([004-010]) as someone whose desire for virtue is so ingrained that acting virtuously has become part of their nature. Such a person "pursues deliberate objects" consistently with moral principle ([004-011]).

- **The utilitarian objection answered.** Critics claim utilitarianism cannot account for the value of virtue, but Mill replies that "its end is not happiness but virtue, which is better than happiness" is a misunderstanding ([002-019]). Utilitarianism does value virtue — as an essential component and instrument of happiness, not as a rival to it.
