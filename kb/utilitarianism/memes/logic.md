## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **logic** refers to the structure of valid reasoning in ethics. Mill distinguishes between logical entailment and the mistaken view that moral principles can be proven by logic alone without appeal to experience [[001-007]].

Mill insists that justice is "not a mere logical corollary" from secondary principles. It must be grounded in the principle of utility itself [[005-039]].

## Key Arguments

### Limits of logical proof
Mill asks whether ultimate ends admit of logical proof. Questions of ultimate ends are "not amenable to direct proof" in the way mathematical propositions are. To suppose otherwise would involve a "logical impossibility" [[001-007]].

### Justice and first principles
Mill warns against treating justice as "a mere logical corollary from secondary" moral rules. Proper moral logic traces every secondary maxim back to the foundational principle [[005-039]].

### Logic and demonstrative reasoning
First principles in ethics are "incapable of proof by reasoning" in the strict demonstrative sense. The logical structure of ethics must be anchored in experiential evidence [[004-001]].
