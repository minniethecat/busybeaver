## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **authority** refers to the binding force or normative power that moral rules and social institutions exercise over individuals. Mill identifies two competing views of moral authority: the intuitionist school, which regards moral precepts as possessing *a priori* authority, and the utilitarian school, which grounds authority in the principle of utility and the observable consequences of actions [[001-004]]. Mill argues that any moral rule's claim to authority must ultimately be justified by its contribution to the general happiness [[001-006]].

## Key Arguments

- **Authority of conscience**: Mill examines the claim that conscience possesses an inherent authority independent of experience. He argues that the feeling of obligation, while psychologically powerful, does not by itself establish the objective authority of any particular moral rule — the test of utility remains the ultimate criterion [[001-005]].
- **Competent authority and law reform**: In discussing justice, Mill notes that laws can be "altered by competent authority" when they fail to serve the general good. This applies the utilitarian principle to institutional authority: legitimate authority is that which can be justified by its tendency to promote happiness [[005-005]].
- **Authority and justice**: Mill identifies the distinctive character of justice as the claim to a special kind of authority — one that overrides other moral considerations. He traces the sources of this authority to the sentiment of justice, which combines the desire for self-protection with the feeling of sympathy [[005-027], [005-029]].
