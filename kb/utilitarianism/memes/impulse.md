# Impulse

**Impulse** in Mill's moral philosophy denotes a spontaneous motivational force — an urge or inclination that arises prior to and independently of rational deliberation.

Mill's treatment of impulse is characteristically nuanced. He denies that impulse can serve as an ultimate moral justification (against intuitionists who treat certain impulses as self-validating), while simultaneously recognizing that the raw material of morality consists in natural human impulses shaped by cultivation and sympathy.

## Impulse and the Need for a First Principle

In Chapter 1, Mill argues that without an ultimate standard, moral decision "must depend on blind impulse or arbitrary choice" [001-008]. This is a foundational claim: the alternative to principled utilitarian reasoning is not another rational system but *non-rational* motivation.

This framing puts pressure on intuitionist rivals. If they cannot supply a "first principle" to adjudicate between conflicting intuitions, the default arbiter becomes impulse — a result no serious moral philosophy can accept. The principle of utility earns its place by being the only alternative to governance by unreflective impulse.

## The Direct Impulse to Promote the General Good

Mill makes the striking claim that "a direct impulse to promote the general good may exist" [002-022] as one of the sanctions of the principle of utility. This impulse, when present, operates as an internal motivational force — the person feels moved to act for the general welfare without needing to calculate or deliberate.

This impulse is not, however, the *justification* for the utilitarian principle. It is a *sanction* — something that motivates compliance, not something that proves correctness. The distinction parallels Mill's treatment of conscience: both are powerful internal forces whose authority derives from the principle of utility, not from their own felt urgency.

## Impulse and the Justice Sentiment

Mill's most detailed treatment of impulse appears in Chapter 5, where he identifies "the impulse of Self-defence" [005-018] as a fundamental component of the sentiment of justice. This impulse — the urge to retaliate against harm — is one of the animal drives that "either are or resemble instincts" [005-018].

Mill also identifies "the impulses of self-defence, of defence of others" [005-036] as motivational forces underlying the enforcement of moral rules. These impulses provide the emotional energy behind punishment and moral condemnation.

However, Mill insists that impulse alone does not constitute justice. The *sentiment* of justice includes but goes beyond the impulse: it adds the element of sympathy, extending the concern from oneself to all sentient beings who may be harmed.

## Impulse and Moral Education

The broader implication of Mill's account is that moral education consists in part in shaping impulse — strengthening those impulses that tend toward the general happiness and restraining those that tend away from it. Impulse is the raw material; moral principle and social sympathy are the shaping forces.
