# Religion

**Religion**, in Mill's *Utilitarianism*, is discussed primarily in relation to the sanctions of morality and the social cultivation of ethical feeling. Mill treats religion as one of the external forces that can reinforce moral rules, while also arguing that utilitarian morality can function without dependence on religious belief.

## Religion and the Sanctions of Utility

Mill contends that the utilitarian morality can draw support from "whatever aid religion, either natural or revealed, can afford" [002-030]. The promise of divine reward and punishment represents a powerful external sanction that can reinforce the motives for obeying utilitarian moral rules. However, Mill is careful to note that the utilitarian standard does not *require* religious belief; the principle of utility stands on its own rational foundation and is binding on believers and non-believers alike.

## The Religion of Humanity

In a striking passage, Mill envisages a secular analogue: the utilitarian spirit, he suggests, could be cultivated to such intensity that "the feeling of unity to be taught as a religion, and the whole force of education" devoted to it [003-013]. This "religion of humanity" would harness the same psychological mechanisms that traditional religions employ — emotional attachment, social reinforcement, early education — but direct them toward the general happiness rather than divine obedience.

## Religion as a Foreshadowing

Mill goes further, suggesting that the power "ever exercised by any religion may be but a type and foretaste" of what utilitarian moral education could achieve [003-014]. He warns, however, that the danger of this power "is not that it should be insufficient, but that it should be so excessive as to interfere unduly with human freedom and individuality" [003-014]. The utilitarian's respect for liberty thus tempers even the project of moral cultivation.
