## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian philosophy, **independence** denotes the capacity for autonomous moral judgment and action, cultivated through habit and character formation. Mill references "the love of liberty and personal independence" as a powerful motivating force in human nature [002-007]. Independence is not merely a political ideal but a moral-psychological achievement—a cultivated disposition that enables individuals to act on principle rather than mere inclination.

## Key Arguments

- **Independence as cultivated habit**: Mill emphasizes that independence is acquired through practice and habituation. The capacity for moral autonomy is "cultivated into this habitual independence," meaning it is not innate but developed through repeated exercise [004-012]. This aligns with Mill's broader theory of character formation through habituation.

- **Independence and liberty**: The "love of liberty and personal independence" is presented as one of the deeply rooted human sentiments to which moral appeals can be made [002-007]. Mill sees the desire for independence as a natural and laudable feature of human psychology that supports moral agency.

- **Moral autonomy and virtue**: Habitual independence is essential for genuine virtue. Without independence of mind, an individual cannot truly act from principle or apply the utilitarian standard autonomously. Independence is thus a precondition for responsible moral agency.

- **Independence in social context**: While independence is an individual achievement, it operates within a social framework. Mill's account balances personal autonomy with the recognition that moral agents are embedded in networks of social feeling and obligation.
