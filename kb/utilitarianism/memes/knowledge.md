## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **knowledge** serves a dual role: as the epistemic foundation for moral science and as the practical capacity by which competent judges discern qualitative differences among pleasures. Mill grounds ethical knowledge in experience and observation rather than a priori intuition, treating it as an inductive science whose first principles are derived from the particulars of human conduct [001-003].

## Key Arguments

### Knowledge as the Basis of Moral Science

Mill insists that ethics must proceed like the natural sciences, building from observed particulars toward general principles. He criticizes intuitive moralists for failing to acknowledge that "the particular truths precede the general theory" [001-003], and notes that the very possibility of a "science of morals" depends on the inductive method [001-004]. The lack of an "acknowledged first principle" has left ethics in a condition "more unlike what might have been expected" compared to other sciences [001-005].

### Knowledge and the Competent Judge

The qualitative hedonism central to Mill's theory depends on the "judgment of those who are qualified by knowledge of both" higher and lower pleasures [002-010]. Only those acquainted with both types of experience can serve as competent judges, making knowledge the gatekeeper of qualitative discrimination. This epistemic requirement elevates knowledge from a mere intellectual virtue to a constitutive element of utilitarian evaluation.

### Knowledge of Consequences and Justice

Mill connects knowledge to the operation of justice through the "knowledge or belief that there is some definite individual or individuals to whom harm has been done" [005-018]. The moral sentiment of justice is activated when we know who has been wronged, linking epistemic states directly to moral response. Without such knowledge, the desire to punish a wrongdoer lacks its proper object.

### First Premises and the Limits of Knowledge

In a significant epistemological concession, Mill acknowledges that "the first premises of our knowledge" cannot be proved by reasoning, "as well as to those of our conduct" [004-001]. The principle of utility, like the axioms of mathematics, is a first premise accepted on the basis of reflective consideration rather than deductive proof. This parallel between theoretical and practical knowledge places utilitarian ethics on the same epistemic footing as other sciences.
