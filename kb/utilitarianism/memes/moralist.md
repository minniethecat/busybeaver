# Moralist

The term **moralist** in Mill's usage refers both to the moral philosopher who constructs ethical theory and to the practical moral agent who applies moral principles in judgment and action. Mill distinguishes two broad camps — the *a priori* or intuitionist moralists and the utilitarian moralists — and uses their disagreements to illuminate the central questions of ethical methodology.

## The Two Schools of Moralists

Mill identifies a fundamental division among moral philosophers. On one side stand "those *a priori* moralists who deem it necessary to argue" from self-evident first principles [001-006]. These intuitionist moralists claim that moral truths are known by direct rational insight, requiring no derivation from experience.

On the other side stand the utilitarian moralists, who ground their system in the principle of utility — the greatest happiness of the greatest number. Mill notes that "utilitarian moralists have gone beyond almost all others in affirming that the motive has nothing to do with the morality of the action" [002-024], emphasizing consequences over intentions.

## The Moralist's Tools

Mill argues that the utilitarian moralist is no worse equipped than any other for navigating ethical complexity. "It is as open to the utilitarian moralist as to any other. He can use it" — referring to the common resources of moral reasoning available to all schools [002-030].

The challenges that confront moralists — conflicting obligations, uncertain consequences, the gap between principle and application — are universal difficulties, acknowledged "by all moralists; the chief of which is when" two or more moral rules appear to conflict [002-032]. No ethical theory escapes these problems; the utilitarian moralist at least addresses them with a clear criterion of judgment.

## The Moralist's Role

In practice, the moralist's role extends beyond theory. Mill describes how "a moralist attempts, as some have done, to" guide conduct by articulating principles and applying them to cases [005-017]. The moralist and the legislator share a common concern with estimating the tendencies of actions — "happiness in the estimation of the moralist and the legislator, involves" an assessment of consequences for all affected parties [005-040].

The utilitarian moralist's distinctive contribution is the insistence that moral evaluation be governed by a single, transparent standard: the promotion of happiness. This makes the moralist's task empirical rather than merely speculative.
