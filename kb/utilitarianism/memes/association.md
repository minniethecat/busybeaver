## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian psychology, **association** is the mental mechanism by which ideas, feelings, and desires originally connected to one object become transferred to another through repeated conjunction. This principle — drawn from the empiricist associationist tradition of Hartley and James Mill — is central to Mill's account of how moral sentiments develop from non-moral origins, how virtue comes to be desired for its own sake, and how the "complete web of corroborative association" [003-012] sustains moral motivation against the forces of egoism and habit.

## Key Arguments

- **Association and the origin of moral sentiments**: Mill argues that moral feelings, however compelling they appear, are not innate. They are "incrusted over with collateral associations, derived from sympathy, from love, and still more from fear" [003-004]. The conscience is not a special faculty but an accretion of associations — an "artificially created" [003-009] sentiment that, once formed, acquires all the force of a natural feeling. This demystifies morality without diminishing its authority.

- **The fragility of moral association**: Mill acknowledges a serious difficulty for associationist ethics: if moral associations are artificially cultivated, "it might well happen that this association also, even after it had been formed, might be worn away by contrary influences" [003-010]. He meets this challenge by arguing that education and social institutions can so strengthen moral associations that they become practically indissoluble, woven into "the complete web of corroborative association" [003-012] that sustains moral conduct.

- **Association and the desire for virtue**: In Chapter IV, Mill invokes association to explain how virtue, originally desired only as a means to happiness, becomes desired for its own sake. Things initially "indifferent" become valued "by association with what it is a means to" [004-005]. The "strong association thus generated between them and the original end" [004-006] transforms instrumental goods into intrinsic goods. Virtue is thus "a good to the agent, not merely as a means, but in itself" [004-012], through the same associative process that generates all non-innate desires.

- **Association and social unity**: The utilitarian project of aligning individual happiness with the general good depends on the mechanism of association. Mill envisions an "indissoluble association between his own happiness and the good of the whole" [002-022] in every individual, reinforced by education and social institutions. Association is thus the psychological foundation of utilitarian social ethics — the bridge between self-interest and impartial benevolence.
