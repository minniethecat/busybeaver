## Definition

In Mill's moral psychology, **volition** is the active phenomenon of willing, which he carefully distinguishes from desire, the "state of passive sensibility" [004-010]. Volition is the executive faculty — the power by which an agent translates desire into action. Though volition "originally [is] an offshoot" from desire, Mill argues that through the power of habit it may "take root and detach itself from the parent stock; so much so that in the case of an habitual purpose, instead of willing the thing because we desire it, we often desire it only because we will it" [004-010].

## Key Arguments

- **The will-desire distinction**: Mill insists that "Will, the active phenomenon, is a different thing from desire, the state of passive sensibility" [004-010]. This distinction is essential to his proof of the principle of utility in Chapter IV. Critics object that people act from will, not from desire for pleasure; Mill responds that this objection confuses the origin of will (which is always in desire) with its mature operation (which may be independent of contemporaneous desire through habituation).

- **Conscious and habitual volition**: Mill distinguishes three cases of volition. Sometimes action is "done unconsciously, the consciousness coming only after the action"; at other times action occurs "with conscious volition" [004-010]; and in a third case, action proceeds from "volition which has become habitual, and is put in operation by the force of habit, in opposition perhaps to the deliberate preference" [004-011]. This tripartite analysis shows Mill's recognition that human action cannot be reduced to a simple model of desire-plus-deliberation.

- **Habitual volition and virtue**: The case of "the person of confirmed virtue" [004-011] represents the highest development of volition, where "the habitual act of will in the individual instance is not in contradiction to the general intention prevailing at other times, but in fulfilment of it" [004-011]. The virtuous person wills the right action from habit, without needing to summon the desire for it on each occasion. This analysis supports Mill's claim that "will, in the beginning, is entirely produced by desire" [004-011] — even the most autonomous virtuous will traces back to the original association of virtue with pleasure.
