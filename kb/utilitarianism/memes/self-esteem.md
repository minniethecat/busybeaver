## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **self-esteem** appears among the internal sanctions of morality — the subjective feelings that motivate obedience to moral rules. Mill lists "self-esteem, desire of the esteem of others, and occasionally even self-abasement" alongside fear, religious feeling, and the recollections of childhood as constituents of the "mass of feeling" that gives moral obligation its binding force [[003-005]].

## Key Arguments

- **Self-esteem as an internal sanction**: Mill identifies self-esteem as one element in the complex psychological foundation of conscience. The binding force of moral obligation "consists in the existence of a mass of feeling which must be broken through in order to do what violates our standard of right" [[003-005]]. Self-esteem contributes to this mass: violating one's own moral standard threatens one's regard for oneself.
- **Self-esteem and the esteem of others**: Mill links self-esteem with the "desire of the esteem of others," suggesting that our regard for ourselves is intimately connected with our sense of how we are regarded by others. This dual orientation — inward toward self-evaluation and outward toward social recognition — gives moral feeling its characteristic complexity [[003-005]].
- **The sanction of conscience**: Whatever theory one holds of the origin of conscience, "this is what essentially constitutes it": a mass of feeling, including self-esteem, that produces remorse when violated. Mill argues that this account of the internal sanction presents "nothing embarrassing" for the utilitarian standard, since the sanction of utility is "the same as of all other moral standards — the conscientious feelings of mankind" [[003-005]].
