## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian theory, **government** is the institution of political authority whose legitimacy and operations must be evaluated by the principle of utility. Government plays a central role in the administration of justice—particularly impartiality, protection of rights, and the distribution of social burdens. Mill treats government not as an end in itself but as an instrument whose moral justification depends on its contribution to the general happiness [005-010].

## Key Arguments

- **Impartiality as a governmental duty**: Mill identifies impartiality as a core requirement of just government, exemplified by "impartiality in the selection among candidates for a government employment" [005-009]. The demand for equal treatment without favoritism is a defining feature of justice administered through government institutions.

- **Government as necessary for justice**: "Whoever thinks that government is necessary, sees no injustice" in the taxation and compulsory services that government imposes [005-010]. The necessity of government for social order justifies certain impositions that would otherwise be considered infringements on liberty.

- **Government and the protection of rights**: The institution of law and government provides protection that is "afforded to and is equally" necessary for all members of society [005-032]. Government secures the legal rights that constitute the framework within which individuals pursue happiness.

- **Security and inequality**: In the absence of law and government, the vulnerable suffer disproportionately—Mill notes that the weak are worse off "in the absence of law or government, than the poor, and indeed would" be even more exposed [005-033]. Government thus serves an equalizing and protective function.
