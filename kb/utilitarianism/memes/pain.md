## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **pain** is one of the two sovereign masters of human action, paired with [[pleasure]] as the foundation of the Greatest Happiness Principle. Mill defines happiness as "pleasure and the absence of pain" and unhappiness as "pain and the privation of pleasure" [002-003]. Pain serves as the negative pole of utilitarian calculation — actions are judged right insofar as they tend to minimize or prevent pain [002-004].

## Key Arguments

- **Pain as the measure of wrongness**: Mill argues that the "theory of life" upon which utilitarianism is built holds that "pleasure and freedom from pain are the only things desirable as ends" [002-004]. The prevention of pain is thus co-equal with the promotion of pleasure as a moral aim.

- **Qualitative distinction among pains**: Mill acknowledges that pains differ in intensity — "the acuter of two pains" [002-011] — and implicitly in quality, consistent with his broader qualitative hedonism. Not all pains are commensurable by duration and intensity alone.

- **Pain and moral sanction**: Mill identifies the "internal sanction of duty" as "a feeling in our own mind; a pain, more or less, attendant on violation of duty" [003-004]. This connects pain to the psychological mechanism of conscience, making it central to moral motivation.

- **Pain and sympathy**: The utilitarian standard requires "regard to the pleasures and pains of others" [003-008], extending pain beyond self-interest to a universal moral calculus. The cultivation of social feeling requires sensitivity to the pain of others.

- **Pain in the proof of utility**: In Chapter IV, Mill discusses how things originally desired as means to pleasure or exemption from pain come to be desired as ends through association [004-007, 004-008, 004-009].
