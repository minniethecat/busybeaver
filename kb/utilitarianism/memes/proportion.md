## Definition

In Mill's utilitarianism, **proportion** serves as the fundamental metric of moral evaluation: "actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness." This proportional principle establishes utility as a quantitative measure, where the moral quality of an action is assessed by its tendency toward happiness or unhappiness — not as an absolute binary but as a matter of degree. Mill extends the concept of proportion from moral judgment to the analysis of human motivation, the structure of sanctions, and the theory of just punishment [[002-003], [001-008]].

## Key Arguments

- **The Greatest Happiness Principle as proportional calculus**: Mill's formulation of the utilitarian standard is explicitly proportional. Actions are judged not by whether they produce happiness or unhappiness, but by the degree to which they tend toward one or the other. This proportional framework allows utilitarianism to handle the complexity of real moral situations, where actions often produce mixed consequences and must be evaluated by their preponderant tendency [[002-003], [002-008]].

- **Pleasure as a matter of proportion, not just quantity**: In his treatment of higher and lower pleasures, Mill introduces a qualitative dimension to the proportional calculus. Human beings with developed faculties prefer pleasures that engage their higher capacities, even when those pleasures come in "no means in exact proportion to their higher faculties." The proportion between pleasure quality and the exertion required to attain it is a central problem in Mill's qualitative hedonism [[002-008], [002-015]].

- **Proportion in the internal sanction**: The binding force of conscience, Mill argues, operates "in proportion to the sensitiveness and thoughtfulness of the individual." The moral sentiments that sustain utilitarian conduct vary in strength across individuals and societies, growing in proportion to education, sympathy, and the recognition of general utility. This proportional model of moral development explains both individual variation and the possibility of moral progress [[003-015]].

- **Proportionality in punishment and justice**: In his analysis of justice, Mill endorses the maxim "that the punishment should be proportioned to the offence" as a widely-recognized principle of justice. He acknowledges that proportionality in punishment is among those precepts of justice that command near-universal assent, though he roots this principle not in retributive intuition but in the utilitarian requirement that sanctions be calibrated to serve the general welfare [[005-030], [005-038]].

- **Desire proportioned to the idea of pleasure**: Mill's psychological hedonism asserts that "to desire anything, except in proportion as the idea of it is pleasant, is a physical and metaphysical impossibility." This proportional claim — that desire is always and only for pleasure or the means to it — forms the empirical foundation of his proof of the principle of utility in Chapter IV [[004-009]].
