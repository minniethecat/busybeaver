## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **vengeance** is identified as the natural impulse that underlies the desire to punish — one of the primitive elements of the sentiment of justice. Mill conceives the sentiment of justice, "in that one of its elements which consists of the desire to punish," as "the natural feeling of retaliation or vengeance, rendered by intellect and sympathy applicable to those injuries... which wound us through, or in common with, society at large" [[005-020]]. Vengeance is thus not itself moral, but becomes so when subordinated to the social sympathies.

## Key Arguments

- **Vengeance as a natural, pre-moral impulse**: Mill is clear that the feeling of vengeance "in itself, has nothing moral in it; what is moral is the exclusive subordination of it to the social sympathies, so as to wait on and obey their call" [[005-020]]. The transformation from natural impulse to moral sentiment is accomplished by redirecting vengeance away from personal grievances and toward injuries to the common good.
- **Moralization by social feeling**: Without moralization, "the natural feeling would make us resent indiscriminately whatever any one does that is disagreeable to us." When "moralized by the social feeling," however, vengeance "only acts in the directions conformable to the general good" [[005-020]]. The just person resents a hurt to society even when not personally affected, and refrains from resenting a personal hurt unless society shares an interest in its repression.
- **Vengeance among the enforcing impulses of justice**: Mill lists vengeance alongside self-defence and defence of others as impulses "called forth against" those who violate the primary moralities. These impulses "enjoin the punishment of those who violate them," making vengeance one of the psychological forces that sustain the system of justice [[005-036]].
