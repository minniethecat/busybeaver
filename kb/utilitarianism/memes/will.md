# Will

**Will** occupies a distinctive place in Mill's moral psychology as the faculty that bridges desire and action, and as the mechanism through which virtue, originally pursued as a means to happiness, becomes an end desired for its own sake. Mill's treatment of will in Chapter IV of *Utilitarianism* seeks to answer a central objection: if virtue is only valued as a means to happiness, does this not degrade it?

## Will and the Transformation of Means into Ends

Mill observes a psychological process by which things originally willed as means become willed as ends. Through habituation, "what was once desired as an instrument for the attainment of happiness, has come to be desired for its own sake" — a transformation that applies par excellence to virtue. The will, originally directed toward happiness, learns to find satisfaction in virtuous action itself. This is not a departure from the principle of utility but a refinement of it: "the will to be virtuous" becomes "a part of happiness" [004-010].

## The Cultivation of Will

The will is not fixed by nature but is shaped by "the appliances of education and general cultivation" [003-004]. Mill envisions a society in which education directs the will toward the general happiness — "education and opinion" so shape individual character that each person develops "an indissoluble association between his own happiness and the good of the whole" [002-022]. The cultivated will is the psychological foundation of the utilitarian moral motive.

## Will vs. Desire

Mill distinguishes will from desire: things may be willed without being desired for the pleasure they bring, and things may be desired without being actively willed. The person of developed character wills the right not because they continuously desire it but because willing the right has become habitual — a "confirmed will" [004-010]. This distinction is crucial for understanding how utilitarian ethics can accommodate the phenomenon of duty: one may will the general happiness even when immediate desire pulls in another direction.
