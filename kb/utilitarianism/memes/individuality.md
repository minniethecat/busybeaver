# Individuality

**Individuality** — the distinctive character and agency of each person — holds a complex place in Mill's utilitarian ethics. Mill rejects the view that utilitarianism absorbs the individual into the collective; instead, he insists that "the morality of individual action is not a question of direct perception" [001-004] but must be judged by reference to a shared standard while respecting the distinctness of persons.

## Individual Happiness and the Higher Self

Mill argues that a person properly cultivated does not merely pursue his "own miserable individuality" but seeks "something far superior to this" [002-017]. The noble character "prizes more than his individual happiness" certain goods — the welfare of others, the pursuit of knowledge, the exercise of virtue [002-019]. Individuality is not repudiated by utilitarianism but elevated: the individual finds fulfilment in identifying with interests wider than his own.

## The Limits of Individual Action

The utilitarian framework constrains individual action through the principle of impartiality. Each individual is one among many, and no one's happiness counts for more than another's. Mill speaks of acting "within the limits imposed by the conscientious regard" for the good of all [002-021]. Individuality is bounded by the equal claim of every other individual to the conditions of well-being.

## Freedom and Individuality

Mill cautions against arrangements that interfere "unduly with human freedom and individuality" [003-014]. The cultivation of individual character requires a sphere of personal liberty — though Mill also insists this freedom must be compatible with the general good. Individuality, properly understood, flourishes when the social order protects space for personal development while channelling individual energies toward the common welfare.
