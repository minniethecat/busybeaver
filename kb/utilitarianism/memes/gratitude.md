## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **gratitude** is treated as one of the fundamental moral sentiments that underlie the concept of justice. Mill argues that the sentiment of justice has its psychological origin in the natural impulse of retaliation, but that this impulse is moralised by the addition of two sentiments: sympathy and gratitude. A person who has received a benefit feels gratitude toward the benefactor, and this sentiment — when generalised through sympathy — becomes the basis for the moral judgment that benefits ought to be reciprocated [[005-017]].

## Key Arguments

- **Gratitude and the sentiment of justice**: Mill classifies cases involving gratitude as falling within the domain of justice. When a person does a service and the recipient later fails to return the benefit, the disappointed expectation of gratitude generates a sense of injustice. This is one of the ways in which the natural retaliatory impulse is transformed into a moral sentiment [[005-017]].
- **Gratitude and the quality of pleasure**: In discussing the qualitative dimensions of pleasure, Mill notes that certain modes of existence are "the most grateful to the feelings," apart from their purely quantitative intensity. This use of "grateful" denotes aesthetic and moral agreeableness rather than interpersonal obligation, but it illustrates the broader role of affective approval in Mill's moral psychology [[002-010]].
