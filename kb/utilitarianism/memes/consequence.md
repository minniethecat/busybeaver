## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **consequences** are the effects or outcomes of actions by which their moral rightness or wrongness is judged. The principle of utility holds that "actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" — making consequences, rather than motives or rules, the sole criterion of moral evaluation. Mill insists that a "test of right and wrong must be the means... of ascertaining what is right or wrong, and not a consequence of having already ascertained it" [001-003], positioning the assessment of consequences as logically prior to moral judgment.

## Key Arguments

- **Consequences as the criterion of morality**: Mill argues that all ethical systems ultimately appeal to consequences. When Kant attempts to derive duties from the categorical imperative, "all he shows is that the consequences of their universal adoption would be such as no one would choose to incur" [001-007]. The appeal to consequences is inescapable even for deontological theories.

- **Consequences of actions versus qualities of persons**: Against the objection that utilitarianism "renders men cold and unsympathizing" by focusing solely on "the dry and hard consideration of the consequences of actions" [002-026], Mill responds that the evaluation of actions and the evaluation of persons are distinct enterprises. "These considerations are relevant, not to the estimation of actions, but of persons" [002-026] — consequences determine rightness, while character and motive are judged separately.

- **Consequentialist reasoning and rules**: Mill acknowledges that in particular cases, the consequences of a single act may appear beneficial while the act violates a generally useful rule. But a rational agent must recognize "that the action is of a class which, if practised generally, would be generally injurious" [002-025]. This recognition of rule-consequences, rather than act-consequences alone, grounds the obligation to follow moral rules even when immediate outcomes tempt otherwise.

- **The scope of consequentialist evaluation**: The utilitarian standard requires considering the consequences for all affected, not merely the agent. On "occasions on which any person... has it in his power to do this on an extended scale... are but exceptional; and on these occasions alone is he called on to consider public utility" [002-025]. In ordinary actions, the relevant consequences are those affecting "the interest or happiness of some few persons" [002-025].

- **Justice grounded in consequentialist reasoning**: Mill holds that justice is "grounded on utility" and is "the chief part, and incomparably the most sacred and binding part, of all morality" [005-034] precisely because its consequences for human well-being are so profound. The "most sublime contempt for the consequences of actions as an element in their morality" [005-034] is, for Mill, a delusion — consequences are the very substance of moral reasoning.
