## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **impartiality** is the principle that equal consideration must be given to the interests of all persons affected by an action. It means "being solely influenced by the considerations which ought to influence the particular case in hand" ([005-008]). As a core component of justice, impartiality demands that no one's happiness counts for more than anyone else's in the utilitarian calculus.

## Key Arguments

- **Impartiality is a requirement of justice.** Mill classifies impartiality as one of the "judicial virtues" and an "obligation of justice" ([005-038]). In legal and moral deliberation, favouring one party over another without relevant grounds violates the basic demands of just treatment.

- **Impartiality and equality are closely allied.** Mill observes that impartiality is "nearly allied to the idea of equality" ([005-009]). Both principles require that like cases be treated alike, and that differences in treatment be justified by relevant differences in the cases, not by arbitrary preference.

- **The utilitarian grounding of impartiality.** The requirement of "perfect impartiality between us" is derived from the greatest happiness principle itself ([005-040]). If each person's happiness counts equally in the utilitarian sum, then the agent must weigh everyone's interests impartially — one's own happiness carries no special weight.

- **Impartiality resists self-preference.** The impartiality requirement challenges the natural human tendency to give disproportionate weight to one's own interests or those of family and friends. Mill acknowledges this tension but maintains that moral judgment requires rising above it, especially "where rights are concerned" ([005-008]).
