# Theology

**Theology** in Mill's treatment of ethics refers to the religious or divine-command foundation that rival moral theories claim for themselves. Mill opens the essay by contrasting the utilitarian approach with systems "full of fictions as English law, and of mysteries as theology" [[001-002]], signalling his view that theological ethics relies on claims inaccessible to rational scrutiny. Yet Mill does not dismiss religion outright; he treats it as a potential ally of utilitarian morality.

## Theology and Ethical Investigation

Mill grants that "whatever aid religion, either natural or revealed, can afford to ethical investigation" [[002-030]] is available to the utilitarian as much as to any other moral theorist. The utilitarian can appeal to divine will as a sanction for moral conduct, provided that the content of that will is understood in terms of the general happiness. In this, Mill follows the theological utilitarian tradition of thinkers like William Paley.

## Providence and Moral Motivation

The belief in Providence plays a supporting role in Mill's account of moral motivation. The "feeling of unity" taught "as a religion" [[003-013]] can reinforce the social feelings that underpin utilitarian morality. Mill observes that "even without the aid of belief in a Providence, both the psychological power" [[003-013]] of moral sanctions is considerable, but he acknowledges that religious belief can strengthen these motives. The religious sanction, he argues, "may be but a type and foretaste" [[003-014]] of the moral influence that a fully developed utilitarian culture could achieve.

## Theology as a Sanction

Within Mill's taxonomy of sanctions, the religious sanction is one of the external sanctions that support morality. It operates through the expectation of divine reward and punishment. Mill treats this as a legitimate but secondary support: the internal sanction of conscience and the external sanction of social opinion are, in his view, more fundamental and more reliable.
