## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **contempt** appears in two distinct registers. The first is the contempt directed by critics against utilitarian ethics — the charge that the doctrine is "worthy only of swine" [002-004], a degrading view of human nature. The second, more philosophically significant, is the contempt professed by certain moralists for the consequences of actions as a criterion of right and wrong — a stance Mill rejects as inconsistent with any coherent account of justice.

## Key Arguments

- **Contempt for utilitarian hedonism**: Mill recounts how Epicurean philosophy was "at a very early period, contemptuously likened" [002-004] to a doctrine fit only for swine. He answers this contempt by arguing that it is the critics, not the utilitarians, who degrade human nature: "the accusation supposes human beings to be capable of no pleasures except those of which swine are capable" [002-004]. The contempt rests on a failure to recognize the qualitative distinction among pleasures that is central to Mill's utilitarianism.

- **The contemptuous challenge to happiness**: A second class of objectors "contemptuously ask, What right hast thou to be happy?" [002-013], asserting that happiness is unattainable and that noble beings have learned the lesson of renunciation. Mill responds that this contempt for happiness as an end is based on an exaggerated notion of what happiness entails — not "a continuity of highly pleasurable excitement" but a life of "moments of such, in an existence made up of few and transitory pains, many and various pleasures" [002-013].

- **Contempt for consequences in theories of justice**: Mill identifies those "who profess the most sublime contempt for the consequences of actions as an element in their morality" [005-034] and argues that this stance is ultimately incoherent. While he acknowledges that justice carries a special sentiment distinct from simple expediency, he insists that "the justice which is grounded on utility [is] the chief part, and incomparably the most sacred and binding part, of all morality" [005-034]. Contempt for consequences cannot survive a careful analysis of what justice actually requires.
