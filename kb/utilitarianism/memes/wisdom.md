## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **wisdom** is the capacity to apply knowledge and experience to the pursuit of the general happiness — a practical intellectual virtue that bridges moral theory and effective action. Unlike the classical conception of wisdom as contemplative excellence, Mill's treatment situates wisdom within the social project of moral improvement: it is the "wisdom of society" that extinguishes harmful dispositions and cultivates beneficial ones [[002-018]].

## Key Arguments

- **The wisdom of society as a moral force**: Mill invokes "the wisdom of society, combined with the" forces of education and opinion, as the mechanism through which individual character is shaped toward benevolence. Wisdom here operates not merely at the individual level but as a collective capacity embedded in social institutions [[002-018]].
- **Wisdom and the religious sanction**: In discussing the theological dimension of moral obligation, Mill references "the perfect goodness and wisdom of God" as the traditional ground of divine command ethics. He does not endorse this view but acknowledges it as the framework within which many conceive of moral wisdom [[002-030]].
- **Intellect and virtue combined**: Mill describes moral progress as a function of "the intellect and virtue of the individual," suggesting that wisdom — the union of intellectual understanding and moral character — is the prerequisite for effective ethical agency. Neither intellect without virtue nor virtue without intellect suffices for right action [[002-037]].
- **Wisdom in the history of ethics**: Mill situates his project within the long tradition of ethical inquiry that has "occupied the most gifted intellects," implicitly positioning utilitarian philosophy as the culmination of the accumulated moral wisdom of humanity [[001-001]].
