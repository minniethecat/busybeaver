## Definition

**Preference** in Mill's utilitarian ethics serves a dual role. In his qualitative hedonism, the "decided preference" of competent judges — those who have experienced both higher and lower pleasures — provides the test by which one pleasure is judged superior in quality to another [002-006]. In his theory of justice, preference acquires a negative valence: to "show favour or preference to one person over another" [005-008] violates the principle of impartiality that is central to the utilitarian conception of justice. Preference thus marks the boundary between legitimate qualitative judgment and illegitimate partiality.

## Key Arguments

- **Preference as qualitative test**: Mill argues that "of two pleasures, if there be one to which all or almost all who have experience of both give a decided preference, irrespective of any feeling of moral obligation to prefer it, that is the more desirable pleasure" [002-006]. The competent judge's preference is the operational criterion for distinguishing higher from lower pleasures without appealing to an external standard.

- **Higher pleasures and the dignity preference**: Mill observes that "few human creatures would consent to be changed into any of the lower animals for a promise of the fullest allowance of a beast's pleasures" [002-009]. This preference for distinctly human pleasures — intellectual, moral, aesthetic — over merely animal satisfactions reveals a qualitative hierarchy grounded in human nature.

- **Preference in the proof of utility**: In Chapter IV, Mill discusses "the deliberate preference" [004-011] as a form of evidence for what is desirable. If competent judges, after deliberation, prefer one end to another, this preference constitutes evidence that the preferred end is genuinely more desirable — analogous to the way visual perception constitutes evidence of what is visible.

- **Preference as bias — the justice problem**: When preference becomes partiality — showing "favour or preference to one person over another" [005-008] — it violates justice. Mill argues that "utility alone can decide the preference" [005-032] when competing claims arise, and utility demands impartial consideration of all affected interests.
