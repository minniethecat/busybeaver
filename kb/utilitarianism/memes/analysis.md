## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **analysis** denotes the intellectual process of breaking down complex moral concepts into their constituent elements. Mill employs analysis both as a descriptive tool — explaining how moral principles are discovered — and as a critical force that subjects received moral beliefs to rational scrutiny. The "truths which are ultimately accepted as the first principles of a science, are really the last results of metaphysical analysis" [[001-002]].

## Key Arguments

- **Analysis as the path to first principles**: Mill argues that in ethics, as in the natural sciences, abstract principles are not the starting point but the endpoint of inquiry. The detailed doctrines of a science are not "deduced from" first principles; rather, first principles emerge as "the last results of metaphysical analysis" of particular truths [[001-002]]. Moral knowledge proceeds from the concrete to the abstract.

- **The dissolving force of analysis**: Mill acknowledges a tension in the role of analysis. On the one hand, analysis is essential to philosophical progress; on the other, "moral associations which are wholly of artificial creation, when intellectual culture goes on, yield by degrees to the dissolving force of analysis" [[003-009]]. This creates a risk: if the feeling of duty associated with the principle of utility is not grounded in natural sentiment, analysis may weaken it. However, this very danger underscores the importance of grounding morality in human nature rather than artifice.

- **Analysis of the notion of justice**: In his concluding chapter, Mill undertakes an extended analysis of justice, dissecting the concept into its component elements — the desire to punish, the notion of a definite right, the sentiment of impartiality. He submits his analysis to the reader's judgment: "If the preceding analysis, or something resembling it, be not the correct account of the notion of justice" [[005-025]], then justice must be considered independent of utility — a position he argues against at length.
