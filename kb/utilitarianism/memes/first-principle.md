# First Principle

A **first principle** is the ultimate ground or fundamental axiom from which all other propositions in a system are derived. In Mill's utilitarian ethics, the first principle is the principle of utility — the greatest happiness principle — which serves as the common ground of all moral obligation. Mill argues that every ethical system requires such a foundation, whether acknowledged or not.

## The Need for a First Principle in Ethics

Mill opens his essay by observing that moral philosophers rarely "make any effort to reduce those various principles to one first principle, or common ground of obligation" [001-005]. They either assume the ordinary precepts of morals as having *a priori* authority, or they lay down some generality "much less obviously authoritative than the maxims themselves" [001-005]. Mill insists that "there ought either to be some one fundamental principle or law at the root of all morality" or a means of deciding between competing principles [001-005]. Without a first principle, moral reasoning lacks a common standard.

## First Principles and Proof

Mill acknowledges a crucial limitation: first principles cannot be proved by reasoning in the ordinary sense. "To be incapable of proof by reasoning is common to all first principles; to the first premises of our knowledge, as well as to those of our conduct" [004-001]. This does not distinguish ethics from other sciences — even mathematics has disputed first principles [001-002]. What can be offered is not deductive proof but considerations "capable of determining the intellect either to give or withhold its assent" [004-001].

## Secondary Principles as Mediators

Mill distinguishes between the first principle and the secondary principles that guide everyday moral reasoning. It would be "a strange notion" that having a first principle means "referring every individual action directly to the first principle" [002-034]. In practice, secondary principles — rules like truth-telling and fairness — mediate between the ultimate standard and particular decisions. Only in cases of conflicting obligations is it "requisite that first principles should be appealed to" [002-038].

## The Utilitarian First Principle

For Mill, the first principle of morals is the principle of utility. This principle implies "the equal claim of everybody to happiness in the estimation of the moralist and the legislator" [005-040], which in turn "involves an equal claim to all the means of happiness" subject to the inevitable conditions of human life [005-040]. From this first principle, rules of justice such as impartiality follow "as corollaries" [005-039] — "not a mere logical inference" but a direct emanation grounded in the equal consideration of all.
