# Experience

**Experience** is the epistemological foundation of Mill's utilitarian ethics. Mill insists that moral questions are ultimately "questions of observation and experience" — not matters of *a priori* intuition or pure reason [001-004]. The principle of utility, like any scientific generalisation, must be tested against the evidence of human life.

## The Test of the Experienced

Mill's celebrated argument for the superiority of higher pleasures relies on the testimony of "the experienced" — those who have "experience of both" kinds of pleasure and can therefore give a "decided preference" [002-011]. The judgment of competent judges is not a democratic vote but an appeal to the qualitative evidence that only experience can provide. This gives Mill's hedonism its distinctively empiricist character: the good is not deduced from first principles but discovered through reflective experience.

## The Accumulated Experience of Mankind

Moral rules, for Mill, are "general conclusions from the experience of human life" — the accumulated wisdom of generations distilled into precepts [002-036]. Mill argues that this body of empirical knowledge is "as high a probability as can be derived from the experience of human life" and that to disregard it is to abandon the only reliable guide we have. The utilitarian does not reinvent morality from scratch each morning; she draws upon "the experience of human life" as recorded in common-sense morality.

## Experience and Proof

Mill's proof of the principle of utility in Chapter IV is explicitly experiential. He maintains that "the sole evidence it is possible to produce that anything is desirable is that people do actually desire it" [004-002]. The proof appeals to "the other sources of our experience" — not to logical deduction but to the introspective evidence of what human beings in fact find desirable [004-002]. This makes the proof a "question of fact and experience, dependent, like all similar questions, on the evidence of observation" [004-009].

## Experience and Moral Learning

Mill sees "mankind" as having been "learning by experience the tendencies of actions" over the course of civilisation [002-033]. Moral knowledge is cumulative — each generation adds to the stock of empirical generalisations about what conduces to happiness. This progressive view of moral epistemology contrasts sharply with intuitionist accounts that treat moral knowledge as fixed and self-evident.
