# Morality

**Morality** is the central subject of Mill's *Utilitarianism*, which opens with the question of "the foundation of morality" [001-001]. For Mill, morality is not a set of arbitrary rules but a system grounded in a single fundamental principle — the **principle of utility**, or the Greatest Happiness Principle — from which all moral obligations derive.

## The Foundation of Morality

Mill observes that philosophers have long sought "one fundamental principle or law at the root of all morality" [001-005]. The utilitarian answer is that this root principle is the promotion of happiness: actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse. The "theory of morality is grounded" on the fact that pleasure and pain are the ultimate motivators of human conduct [002-003].

## Morality and the Standard of Right Action

A key function of morality is to provide a **standard** by which actions are judged. Mill argues that "the purpose of a standard of morality" is to supply a rule for determining right and wrong conduct [002-026]. Utilitarians "look on the morality of actions as measured by the utilitarian standard" — their tendency to promote or diminish the general happiness [002-027]. When "different standards of morality" conflict, Mill contends that utility is the ultimate arbiter [002-028].

## The Sanction of Morality

In Chapter III, Mill addresses the question of what binds us to obey moral rules — the **sanction** of morality. He argues that the "sanction of all morality" consists of both external motives (hope of favour, fear of displeasure) and internal ones (conscience, the feeling of duty) [003-005]. The utilitarian morality, Mill contends, possesses all the same sanctions as any other moral system [003-001].

## Morality vs. Expediency

Mill draws an important distinction between **morality** and mere **expediency**. Not every action that promotes utility is a matter of moral obligation; some are matters of prudence or simple expediency. The "distinction between morality and simple expediency" lies in the kind and degree of obligation involved [005-014]. What "marks off morality in general from the remaining provinces of expediency" is the presence of sanctions — particularly the internal sanction of conscience [005-015].
