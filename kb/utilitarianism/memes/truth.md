## Definition

In Mill's ethical epistemology, **truth** in the moral domain is analogous to truth in the mathematical and natural sciences: it is discovered through observation and experience, confirmed inductively, and applicable to the guidance of conduct. Mill treats "truth and falsehood" as parallel to "right and wrong" — both are "questions of observation and experience" [001-004], rejecting the intuitionist claim that moral truths are known a priori.

## Key Arguments

### Moral Truth as Inductive Truth

Mill positions moral truth squarely within the empirical tradition. "The truths which are ultimately accepted as the first principles of a science," he writes, are "the last result of metaphysical analysis practiced on the elementary notions with which the science is conversant" [001-002]. Moral truths are not self-evident axioms but hard-won conclusions of systematic inquiry into human conduct and its consequences.

### Truth and Falsehood in Ethics

By asserting that "right and wrong, as well as truth and falsehood, are questions of observation and experience" [001-004], Mill collapses the traditional distinction between factual and normative inquiry. There is no special faculty of moral intuition delivering moral truths; the same inductive faculties that discover scientific truths also discover moral ones. This is a foundational commitment of utilitarian naturalism.

### Deviation from Truth

Mill acknowledges that even "unintentional deviation from truth" can weaken "the trustworthiness of human assertion" [002-031], treating truthfulness as a secondary moral rule that supports the broader utilitarian framework. Truth-telling is not an absolute duty but a generally reliable practice whose systematic violation would undermine social cooperation and therefore happiness.

### Truth and Penal Sanction

Mill identifies the internal dimension of moral obligation with the recognition of a truth: "the idea of penal sanction... is the essence of law," and by extension, "the idea of penal sanction... enters into the meaning of obligation in general" [005-014]. To grasp a moral truth is to recognize that violation warrants sanction — whether external (legal, social) or internal (conscience).

### The Truths of Arithmetic

Mill uses mathematical truth as a limiting case to illuminate moral truth. He notes that "the truths of arithmetic are applicable to the valuation of happiness, as to all other measurable quantities" [005-040], suggesting that moral reasoning, like arithmetic, operates on truths that are universally applicable once their terms are clearly defined.
