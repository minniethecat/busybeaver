## Definition

A **maxim** in Mill's usage refers to a general rule or principle of conduct that guides moral judgment. Mill distinguishes between the ultimate criterion of morality (utility) and the secondary maxims (such as those of [[justice]]) that are validated by their connection to that criterion. Maxims are "the common groundwork of those maxims some generality which is much recommended by the teachers of virtue" [001-005] — practical rules derived from experience and confirmed by the principle of utility.

## Key Arguments

- **Multiplicity of maxims**: Mill argues that justice involves not "some one rule, principle, or maxim, but many, which do not always coincide in their dictates" [005-026]. Moral life requires navigating conflicts among maxims, and the ultimate appeal is to utility.

- **Maxims of justice**: Mill surveys "most of the maxims of justice current in the world" [005-038] and shows how each derives its binding force from social utility. Maxims such as *volenti non fit injuria* ("to one who consents, no injury is done") and the principle of impartiality are not self-evident truths but practical rules tested by their contribution to well-being.

- **Maxims as intermediate generalizations**: Maxims occupy a middle position between the abstract principle of utility and particular moral judgments. They are "more important to human well-being than any maxims" [005-034] that lack connection to the ultimate standard, because they provide practical guidance without requiring case-by-case calculation.

- **Limits and exceptions**: Mill acknowledges that maxioms must be qualified: "those limits ought to be the same" [005-040] as required by the principle of utility. No maxim is absolute — each may be overruled when a more important social duty or greater utility is at stake [005-042].

- **Maxims and Kant**: Mill critiques Kant's attempt to derive moral maxims from the categorical imperative alone. He argues that Kant's maxims covertly rely on utilitarian reasoning to be plausible, since the categorical imperative "fails, almost grotesquely, to deduce any of the actual duties of morality" without considering consequences.
