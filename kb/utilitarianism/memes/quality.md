## Definition

**Quality** in Mill's utilitarian ethics refers to the qualitative dimension of pleasures — the claim that some pleasures are intrinsically more valuable than others, independent of their quantity or intensity. Mill famously asserts that "in estimating all other things, quality is considered as well as quantity," and it would be "absurd" to suppose that "the estimation of pleasures should be supposed to depend on quantity alone" [002-006]. This qualitative hedonism is Mill's most significant departure from Bentham's purely quantitative calculus.

## Key Arguments

- **The criterion of competent judges**: Mill's test for qualitative superiority is the "decided preference" of those who have experienced both pleasures. "Of two pleasures, if there be one to which all or almost all who have experience of both give a decided preference, irrespective of any feeling of moral obligation to prefer it, that is the more desirable pleasure" [002-006]. This transforms the evaluation of pleasures from a matter of mere sensation into a question for reflective judgment.

- **Higher and lower pleasures**: Mill argues that "few human creatures would consent to be changed into any of the lower animals for a promise of the fullest allowance of a beast's pleasures" [002-009]. No intelligent human being would trade intellectual, moral, and aesthetic pleasures for merely animal satisfactions — even if the latter were more abundant. This preference reveals a genuine qualitative distinction.

- **The dignity argument**: Mill grounds the qualitative hierarchy in human dignity: "It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied" [002-010]. This famous passage is not a rejection of hedonism but a redefinition: the higher pleasures are more valuable because they engage distinctively human faculties.

- **Quality and the criterion of right and wrong**: The qualitative dimension resists formal codification. When asked "what is the quality" that distinguishes justice from other moral claims, Mill examines the sentiment itself — looking for "whether there is any quality" [005-003] that is always present — rather than appealing to an a priori definition.
