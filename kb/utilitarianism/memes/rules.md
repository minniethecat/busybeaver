# Moral Rules

**Moral rules**, also referred to as secondary principles, are the intermediate generalizations that bridge the gap between the abstract principle of utility and the concrete decisions of daily moral life. Mill argues that these rules are indispensable: we cannot calculate the consequences of every action from scratch, so we rely on rules distilled from accumulated human experience.

## Rules as Secondary Principles

Mill recognizes that morality cannot operate without rules. Actions are performed "for the sake of some end, and rules of action, it seems natural to" suppose, must be derived from that end [001-003]. The principle of utility supplies the ultimate end — happiness — and moral rules are the tested means to that end.

The alternative, Mill warns, is chaos. Without settled rules, morality degenerates into "the most outrageously immoral rules of conduct" justified by ad hoc reasoning [001-007]. A coherent set of moral rules, grounded in utility, is the only defense against arbitrary moral judgment.

## Rules for the Multitude

Mill draws an important distinction between the rules that govern ordinary moral life and the refined judgments possible in exceptional cases. The rules of morality function as guidelines "for the multitude, and" for the common run of human affairs [002-034]. They are not absolute — exceptional circumstances may warrant departing from them — but they carry the weight of accumulated wisdom.

This qualified rule-utilitarianism acknowledges that "an exception to moral rules, and, when under temptation," individuals may rationalize self-serving departures from principle [002-036]. The rules thus serve a critical psychological function: they provide stability against the distortions of self-interest.

## Rules of Justice

The most stringent moral rules are those of justice. Mill observes that "one of the three builds upon rules of justice confessedly true" — justice rules are distinguished by their special force and the legitimacy of punishment for their violation [005-027].

These rules "concern the essentials" of human well-being — security, liberty, and the basic conditions of social life [005-034]. Because they protect interests so fundamental that no society can function without them, the rules of justice carry a weight and urgency that ordinary moral rules do not.
