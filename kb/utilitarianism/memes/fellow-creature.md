# Fellow Creature

The concept of **fellow creatures** is Mill's term for the other human beings to whom we owe moral duties. It expresses the fundamental utilitarian conviction that every person's happiness counts equally in the moral calculus, and that the recognition of others as fellow creatures — beings with interests as real as one's own — is the psychological foundation of social morality. The phrase recurs throughout Mill's text, linking the impartiality of the utilitarian standard to the natural human capacity for sympathy and solidarity.

## The Basis of Moral Community

Mill grounds the scope of moral concern in the recognition of shared sentience. The interests of "our fellow creatures" are "due" moral consideration [003-008] not because of any special relationship or contract, but simply because they, like us, are capable of happiness and suffering. This recognition is the bridge from self-interest to the general good: the utilitarian must regard "the rest of his fellow creatures" not as "struggling rivals" [003-014] for scarce goods, but as participants in a shared project of mutual welfare.

## Unity with Fellow Creatures

The desire "to be in unity with our fellow creatures" [003-010] is, Mill argues, already a powerful force in human psychology. This social feeling — the sense of connection and shared fate — is "already a powerful principle in human nature" [003-010] and is strengthened by the progress of civilisation. As society advances, people increasingly recognise their interdependence and the impossibility of flourishing in isolation from others' welfare.

## Fellow Creatures and Justice

The concept of fellow creatures is central to Mill's theory of justice. The most stringent obligations — those of justice — are owed to other persons as "fellow creatures" with valid claims. The opinion of "his fellow creatures" [005-014] enforces the obligations of justice, while the most fundamental duties involve acknowledging that "most exertions are due to our fellow creatures" [005-017]. The claim "we have on our fellow creatures to join in making safe for us the very groundwork of our existence" [005-025] expresses the reciprocal character of justice: each person's security depends on the cooperation of others.

## Practical Implications

The fellow-creature concept has concrete moral implications. Mill uses it to illustrate the impartiality demanded by the utilitarian standard: "He who saves a fellow creature from drowning does what is morally right, whether his motive be duty or the hope of being paid" [002-024], because the action promotes the happiness of a being whose welfare counts equally. The standard judges actions by their effect on all affected fellow creatures, not by the agent's particular loyalties or sentiments.
