## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **prudence** refers to the practical wisdom by which an agent pursues their own long-term well-being, as distinct from morality which governs conduct affecting the interests of others. Mill notes that "experience all the prudence, as well as all the morality of life, is dependent" on the principle of utility [002-033], positioning prudence and morality as parallel domains both answerable to the same standard of happiness.

## Key Arguments

- **Prudence versus morality**: Mill draws a clear line between prudential and moral obligations. When considering whether an act is a duty, "reasons of prudence, or the interest of other people" [005-014] may each count, but only the latter constitutes a moral duty proper. Prudential considerations, while grounded in the same utilitarian logic, lack the social sanction and punitive dimension that distinguishes moral obligation.

- **Self-regarding conduct**: The domain of prudence coincides with what Mill calls "self-regarding" action. He describes how individuals form their sentiments, "whether self-regarding or sympathetic" [005-019], suggesting a psychological continuum from purely prudential to fully moral concerns. Prudence occupies the self-regarding end of this spectrum — conduct affecting only the agent's own interests.

- **Prudential instruction and moral exhortation**: Mill distinguishes prudential advice from moral command. People may give "prudential instruction or exhortation" [005-035] to one another, but such instruction lacks the obligatory force and sanction attached to moral rules. This distinction preserves the special character of justice and morality while acknowledging prudence as a legitimate, utility-based guide to personal conduct.
