## Definition

**Desert** (or moral desert) is, in Mill's analysis of justice, the principle that persons should receive good or evil in proportion to their conduct — that it is "just that each person should obtain that (whether good or evil) which he deserves; and unjust that he should obtain a good, or be made to undergo an evil, which he does not deserve" [005-007]. Mill identifies desert as "the clearest and most emphatic form in which the idea of justice is conceived by the general mind" [005-007], and his analysis probes the relationship between desert-based justice and the utilitarian principle.

## Key Arguments

- **Desert as a core element of justice**: Mill ranks desert among the principal components of the concept of justice. "Speaking in a general way, a person is understood to deserve good if he does right, evil if he does wrong; and in a more particular sense, to deserve good from those to whom he does or has done good, and evil from those to whom he does or has done evil" [005-007]. This reciprocal structure — good returned for good, evil for evil — is fundamental to ordinary moral thinking.

- **Impartiality and desert**: Mill argues that impartiality, in certain institutional contexts, is itself a requirement of desert. In judicial or educational settings, impartiality means "being solely influenced by desert" [005-008] — treating persons according to what they merit rather than according to favor or preference. A tribunal "must be impartial, because it is bound to award, without regard to any other consideration, a disputed object to the one of two parties who has the right to it" [005-008].

- **Desert and the Greatest-Happiness Principle**: Mill reconciles desert with utility through the principle of equal consideration. The principle of utility requires that "one person's happiness, supposed equal in degree... is counted for exactly as much as another's" [005-039]. From this, it follows that "we should treat all equally well... who have deserved equally well of us, and that society should treat all equally well who have deserved equally well of it" [005-039]. Desert, properly understood, is a corollary of the Greatest-Happiness Principle, not a competing standard.

- **The limits of desert**: Mill notes that desert does not exhaust morality. "The precept of returning good for evil has never been regarded as a case of the fulfilment of justice, but as one in which the claims of justice are waved, in obedience to other considerations" [005-007]. This suggests that while desert is central to justice, there are moral considerations — such as mercy or beneficence — that may override desert-based claims.
