# Ethics

**Ethics**, or moral philosophy, is the systematic inquiry into the nature of right and wrong conduct. Mill's *Utilitarianism* is a contribution to this inquiry, proposing the principle of utility as the foundational criterion of ethics. Mill engages with the long history of ethical thought while arguing that the search for a "first principle" has left ethics in a state of persistent controversy [001-005].

## The Task of Ethics

Mill defines the task of ethics clearly: "It is the business of ethics to tell us what are our duties, or by what test we may know them" [002-023]. This dual function — identifying both the content of moral obligation and the criterion by which it is recognized — distinguishes ethics as a practical discipline, not merely a theoretical one. No "ethical standard" can claim legitimacy unless it provides workable guidance for human life [002-026].

## The Need for a First Principle

A recurring theme in Mill's argument is that ethics has suffered from the absence of an acknowledged first principle. "The search for an acknowledged first principle has made ethics not" a settled science [001-005]. Mill contends that "we need a doctrine of ethics, carefully followed out, to interpret" the secondary rules that guide everyday moral judgment [002-030]. The principle of utility fills this role: it supplies the ultimate standard by which all subordinate ethical rules are justified.

## Intuitive vs. Inductive Ethics

Mill draws a crucial methodological distinction between **intuitive** and **inductive** approaches to ethics. The intuitive school holds that moral principles are known *a priori*, independent of experience. The inductive school — to which Mill belongs — maintains that ethical knowledge arises from observation of the consequences of actions. "If so, the intuitive ethics would coincide with the utilitarian" [003-008], Mill suggests, since both ultimately appeal to human well-being as the ground of moral judgment.

## Ethics and Progress

Mill envisions ethics as a progressive science. In the concluding chapter, he references the view "that in ethics, as in all other branches of scientific study, the truths of the science are not to be discovered by looking out for a single principle" [005-041]. While Mill disagrees with the rejection of a unifying principle, he acknowledges that ethical knowledge advances through the gradual accumulation and refinement of doctrine — much as the natural sciences progress.
