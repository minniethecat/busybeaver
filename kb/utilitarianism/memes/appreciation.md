## Definition

In Mill's philosophy, **appreciation** refers to the developed capacity to recognize and properly value something — whether a moral theory, a higher pleasure, or an intrinsic good. It is both an intellectual act (understanding) and an evaluative one (estimating worth), and Mill employs it at two critical junctures in his argument [001-007].

For the utilitarian theory itself, appreciation requires understanding the doctrine correctly — a precondition Mill insists upon before any critique can be valid [001-007]. For qualitative hedonism, appreciation is the faculty exercised by competent judges who have experienced both higher and lower pleasures [002-009].

## Key Arguments

- **Appreciation of the theory**: Mill opens the work by declaring his aim "to contribute something towards the understanding and appreciation of the Utilitarian or Happiness theory," distinguishing genuine appreciation from the "ignorant blunder" of critics who fail to grasp what utility means [001-007].

- **Appreciation of higher pleasures**: The capacity for "a full appreciation of the intrinsic superiority of the higher" pleasures distinguishes the competent judge. Men may choose lower pleasures despite knowing them inferior, but this does not negate their capacity for appreciation — it reflects "infirmity of character" rather than a failure of discernment [002-009].

- **Appreciation and education**: Appreciation is not an innate faculty but a developed one. The capacity for nobler feelings requires cultivation; where it is absent, this reflects lack of opportunity rather than absence of the underlying capacity.
