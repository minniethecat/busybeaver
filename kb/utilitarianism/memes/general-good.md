# General Good

The **general good**, in Mill's utilitarian ethics, is the aggregate well-being of all persons affected by an action — the standard against which conduct is judged right or wrong.

## Impartiality and the General Good

Mill states the core requirement of utilitarian impartiality: "As between his own happiness and that of others, utilitarianism requires him to be as strictly impartial as a disinterested and benevolent spectator" [002-022]. He identifies this standard with the golden rule of Jesus of Nazareth.

## The Proof of the General Good

Mill grounds the general good in each person's desire for their own happiness. "No reason can be given why the general happiness is desirable, except that each person, so far as he believes it to be attainable, desires his own happiness" [004-002]. This yields the conclusion that the general happiness is a good to the aggregate of all persons.

## The Sentiment of Justice and the General Good

Mill traces the sentiment of justice to the impulse to defend the collective. The individual attaches himself to "the collective idea of his tribe, his country, or mankind" such that any act hurtful to them raises his sympathy and urges resistance [005-020]. Conduct "opposed to the general good" elicits a "direct impulse" of resistance [002-022].
