# Reason

**Reason** is, in Mill's moral epistemology, the faculty through which ethical principles are analyzed, justified, and applied. Mill inherits from the empiricist tradition a conception of reason as an acquired human capacity — "it is natural to man to speak, to reason, to build cities, to cultivate the ground, though these are acquired faculties" [003-008] — and he deploys it to ground utilitarian ethics in experience while respecting the limits of rational demonstration.

## Reason as the Moral Faculty

Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by situating ethics within the domain of reason rather than sentiment: the question of the criterion of right and wrong is "a branch of our reason, not of our sensitive faculty" [001-004]. This aligns utilitarianism with the rationalist ambition of providing a principled foundation for moral judgment, even as Mill anchors that foundation in the empirical fact of human desire for happiness. Reason does not supply the ultimate end — happiness — but it organizes, applies, and defends the principle that happiness is the standard of right action.

## The Limits of Reasoning

A central claim of Chapter IV is that "to be incapable of proof by reasoning is common to all first principles" [004-001]. The principle of utility, as a first principle of conduct, cannot be deduced from more basic premises — just as the deliverances of the senses cannot be proved to correspond to reality. The "only proof" that happiness is desirable is "that people do actually desire it" [004-002] — an appeal to experience, not a deductive argument. "No reason can be given why the general happiness is desirable, except that each person... desires his own happiness" [004-002]. This limitation is not a defect of utilitarianism but a feature of all ultimate normative commitments.

## Reason and Justice

In his analysis of justice (Ch. V), Mill shows reason operating as the arbiter among competing claims. When asked why society ought to defend individual rights, Mill answers: "I can give him no other reason than general utility" [005-022]. Reason traces the sentiment of justice back to its utilitarian foundation in the interest of security. Yet reason also tempers the primitive impulse toward retaliation: it is reason that transforms the "animal element" of the thirst for revenge into the measured, impartial administration of justice.
