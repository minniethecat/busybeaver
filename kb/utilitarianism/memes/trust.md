## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **trust** is the fabric of social cooperation — the reliance that individuals place in one another's honesty, fidelity, and goodwill, without which the general happiness cannot be secured. Trust is not treated as an intrinsic good but as a social condition whose maintenance is so overwhelmingly useful that the moral rules protecting it — veracity, fidelity to promises, respect for the security of persons — rank among the most stringent obligations in the utilitarian calculus [[002-031]].

## Key Arguments

- **Betrayal of trust as a distinct wrong**: Mill identifies the betrayal of trust as a paradigmatic form of moral injury. "He who betrays the friend that trusts him" is "guilty of a crime" not merely because of the harm caused but because the act strikes at the foundation of voluntary human association. The special wrongness of betrayal derives from the exploitation of the very confidence that makes cooperation possible [[002-024]].

- **Veracity as the guardian of trust**: Truth-telling is among the most important moral virtues precisely because every deviation from it — even when unintentional — "does that much towards weakening the trustworthiness of human assertion." The cumulative erosion of trust through individual lapses in veracity represents a diffuse but real harm to the general welfare, one that utilitarian reasoning must take fully into account [[002-031]].

- **Trust and the foundations of knowledge**: Mill extends the concept of trustworthiness beyond the moral domain into epistemology. The "trustworthiness of the conclusions" of moral science depends on the soundness of the method by which they are reached. Just as social trust is the precondition of cooperation, epistemic trust — confidence in the reliability of inductive methods — is the precondition of moral knowledge [[001-002]].

- **The limits of trust in political authority**: Mill warns against placing excessive trust in political power. The concern that citizens might "with reason be afraid of trusting the magistrate with so unlimited an amount of power over individuals" reflects his broader liberal conviction that institutional safeguards, not personal trust, must protect individual liberty. Trust in persons is a fragile and fallible basis for political order [[005-013]].
