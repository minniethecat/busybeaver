# Utility

**Utility** (or the **Principle of Utility**) is the foundational moral standard of utilitarian ethics. Mill defines it as the principle that "holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003]. Bentham originally called it "the greatest happiness principle" [001-006].

## Clarifying the Term

Mill opens Chapter II with a crucial clarification: utility is not opposed to pleasure. "A passing remark is all that needs be given to the ignorant blunder of supposing that those who stand up for utility as the test of right and wrong use the term in that restricted and merely colloquial sense in which utility is opposed to pleasure" [002-001]. In the utilitarian tradition, every writer "from Epicurus to Bentham who maintained the theory of utility meant by it, not something to be contradistinguished from pleasure, but pleasure itself, together with exemption from pain" [002-002].

## Utility as a Standard, Not a Motive

Mill distinguishes between utility as a **standard of right action** and utility as a **motive**. One need not always act from a conscious desire to maximize the general happiness; it is sufficient that one's actions conform to rules whose general observance promotes happiness.

## The Proof of Utility

In Chapter IV, Mill addresses the epistemic status of the principle of utility. He argues that "questions of ultimate ends are not amenable to direct proof" — one cannot prove that happiness is desirable in the way one proves a theorem. However, Mill offers "considerations capable of determining the intellect either to give or withhold its assent to the doctrine; and this is equivalent to proof" [001-007].
