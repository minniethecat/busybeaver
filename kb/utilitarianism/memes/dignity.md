## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, the **sense of dignity** is a distinctively human faculty — "a sense of dignity, which all human beings possess in one form or other" [002-008] — that serves as the psychological foundation for the qualitative distinction between higher and lower pleasures. It is not an abstract moral principle but an empirically observable feature of human nature: those who have experienced both higher and lower pleasures consistently prefer the former, even at the cost of greater discontent, because their sense of dignity will not permit them to sink to a lower grade of existence.

## Key Arguments

- **Dignity and the qualitative hedonism**: Mill introduces the sense of dignity to explain why competent judges prefer "higher" intellectual and moral pleasures over merely sensual ones. Even when a being of "higher faculties requires more to make him happy" and is "capable probably of more acute suffering" [002-005], the sense of dignity ensures that "he can never really wish to sink into what he feels to be a lower grade of existence" [002-007]. Dignity thus functions as a psychological veto against purely quantitative pleasure-maximization.

- **Dignity as a universal human capacity**: Mill insists that the sense of dignity is not confined to the intellectual elite. It belongs to "all human beings" in some form, though he acknowledges that in some persons it is "a very powerful feeling," while in others it may be weaker or more easily overcome [002-008]. The universal possession of this capacity grounds the claim that qualitative hedonism applies to all moral agents, not just a cultivated minority.

- **Dignity and the refusal of contentment**: The paradox Mill identifies is that creatures with a stronger sense of dignity are often less contented than those without it, yet "it is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied" [002-007]. Dignity makes the refusal of base contentment not merely permissible but expressive of a higher form of happiness.
