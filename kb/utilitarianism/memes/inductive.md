# Inductive Method

The **inductive method** is the approach to moral inquiry that grounds ethical knowledge in observation, experience, and the empirical study of human consequences. In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, the inductive school is contrasted with the intuitive school: both agree that morality consists in the application of general laws to particular cases, but they disagree on how those laws are known. Mill aligns himself with this inductive tradition, treating the principle of utility as an empirically grounded standard rather than a self-evident axiom.

## Definition

According to Mill, the inductive school of ethics holds that "right and wrong, as well as truth and falsehood, are questions of observation and experience" [001-004]. The inductive method thus treats moral inquiry as an empirical science, grounding judgments of right and wrong in observed facts about human happiness and suffering. Unlike the intuitive school, which derives moral principles from self-evident *a priori* axioms, the inductive school derives its principles from the experienced consequences of human action.

## Key Arguments

**1. Induction as the foundation of moral knowledge.** Mill identifies two rival methodological schools in moral philosophy: the intuitive and the inductive. The intuitive school holds that moral principles are known *a priori*, requiring nothing to command assent "except that the meaning of the terms be understood." The inductive school, by contrast, derives moral knowledge from the observed tendencies of actions to produce happiness or unhappiness. Both schools affirm that "there is a science of morals" [001-004], but they differ fundamentally on the source and evidence of moral laws. The inductive approach treats moral inquiry as continuous with scientific inquiry, subjecting ethical claims to the same empirical standards that govern knowledge of the natural world.

**2. The accumulation of moral knowledge through experience.** A central tenet of the inductive method is that moral knowledge accumulates over time through the collective experience of humanity. Mill argues that "mankind have been learning by experience the tendencies of actions; on which experience all the prudence, as well as all the morality of life, are dependent" [002-033]. This cumulative process yields what Mill calls "general conclusions from the experience of human life" [002-036] — secondary moral rules that encapsulate the practical wisdom of generations. These rules, such as prohibitions against lying, theft, and violence, are not arbitrary decrees but empirically grounded generalizations about what conduct promotes human well-being.

**3. The consilience of induction and deduction in ethical method.** Mill devotes careful attention to the relationship between inductive and deductive reasoning in ethics. He acknowledges the view that moral truths are "completely attainable only by deducing, from the laws of life and the conditions of existence, what kinds of action necessarily tend to produce happiness" [005-040]. At the same time, he warns against "relying too exclusively upon such deductions, and declining altogether to be bound by the generalizations from specific experience" [005-041]. Mill's own position is that ethical method requires a synthesis of both approaches: "the consilience of the results of both these processes, each corroborating and verifying the other, is requisite to give to any general proposition the kind and degree of evidence which constitutes scientific proof" [005-041]. The inductive method thus does not exclude deductive reasoning but insists that all ethical deductions must ultimately be validated against empirical observation.
