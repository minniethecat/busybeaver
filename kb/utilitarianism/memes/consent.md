## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **consent** operates as a criterion of value in the theory of higher pleasures and as a condition of legitimate obligation in the theory of justice. In the qualitative hedonism of Chapter II, the "decided preference" of competent judges — those who have experienced both higher and lower pleasures — serves as the test of qualitative superiority. This preference constitutes a form of informed consent that grounds the distinction between mere quantity of pleasure and genuine quality. In the analysis of justice, consent functions as a limiting condition: actions done with the consent of the affected party are generally not considered unjust, and obligations arise in part from the consent individuals give to the social and legal order [[002-006], [005-029]].

## Key Arguments

- **Consent as the test of qualitative superiority**: Mill's famous assertion that "few human creatures would consent to be changed into any of the lower animals" for a promise of the fullest allowance of beastly pleasures establishes consent — in the form of the considered preference of competent judges — as the decisive criterion for ranking pleasures by quality rather than quantity. The "unwillingness to consent" to a degraded existence reveals a fundamental fact about human valuation that purely quantitative hedonism cannot capture [[002-006]].

- **The competent judge's refusal to consent**: Extending the consent test, Mill claims that "no intelligent human being would consent to be a fool, no instructed person would consent to be an ignoramus." This negative formulation — what a competent judge would not consent to — provides the boundary between higher and lower pleasures. The refusal of consent reveals a qualitative dispreference that is irreducible to the mere calculus of pleasure quantities, establishing Mill's departure from Bentham's purely quantitative hedonism [[002-007]].

- **Consent and the limits of justice**: In Chapter V, Mill identifies consent as a key factor in determining whether an act is unjust. An action "done with the consent of the person who is supposed to be injured by it" is generally not counted among the violations of justice, because the voluntary acceptance of a burden or risk removes the element of wrongful harm that characterizes injustice. This principle has important implications for contract, punishment, and the scope of individual liberty [[005-029]].

- **Consent to law and the basis of political obligation**: Mill grounds the obligation to obey the law partly in the fact that individuals have "engaged to obey the laws, and consented to be punished for any disobedience." This consensual model of political obligation — though Mill acknowledges its limits for those who have not explicitly consented — provides a utilitarian alternative to theories that ground obligation in divine command or abstract natural right [[005-028]].

- **Consent and the limits of sacrifice**: Mill sharply distinguishes between voluntary self-sacrifice and sacrifice imposed without consent. While utilitarianism may require or permit an individual to make sacrifices for the general good, it does not permit society to "take from him a sacrifice, without his consent, for other people's benefit." The requirement of consent — or at minimum, the demonstration that the sacrifice serves general utility — protects individuals from being used merely as means to others' ends [[005-027]].

## Related Concepts

- [[liberty]] — the principle that limits the scope of legitimate interference without individual consent
- [[justice]] — the domain of enforceable obligations; consent is one criterion for determining what counts as just
- [[obligation]] — the binding moral claim that arises in part from individual consent to social and legal arrangements
- [[pleasure]] — the qualitative ranking of which depends on the consent-based preferences of competent judges
- [[rights]] — the moral claims that individuals hold, which cannot be overridden without their consent except for the general good
