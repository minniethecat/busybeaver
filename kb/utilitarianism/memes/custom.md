# Custom

**Custom** represents, in Mill's analysis, both a psychological obstacle to rational morality and a vehicle for moral progress. Mill recognizes that customary morality exerts enormous influence over human conduct — but he argues that the principle of utility provides the only standard by which customs can be critically evaluated.

## Custom as Epistemic Obstacle

Mill observes that people typically rest their moral beliefs on received opinion "on which [one] has not been accustomed to rest" any deeper justification [003-001]. When a moral standard lacks the "halo" that custom throws around familiar principles, it appears paradoxically less authoritative — "the superstructure seems to stand better without, than with, what is represented as its foundation" [003-002]. This explains why the utilitarian principle, despite grounding everyday moral rules, strikes many as less compelling than the rules themselves.

## Custom and Moral Progress

Yet custom is not static. Mill traces "the entire history of social improvement" as "a series of transitions, by which one custom or institution after another, from being a supposed primary necessity of social existence, has passed into the rank of an universally stigmatized injustice and tyranny" [005-042]. The feeling of justice itself — often mistaken for a *sui generis* instinct — is in part shaped by custom [005-003]. The principle of utility provides the critical leverage needed to distinguish customs worth preserving from those that should be abandoned.
