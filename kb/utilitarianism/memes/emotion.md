# Emotion

In Mill's ethical psychology, **emotion** — particularly the moral emotions — mediates between abstract principle and the felt urgency of moral obligation.

## The Emotional Constitution and Moral Sentiment

Mill asks how a given attribute of conduct "would be capable of gathering round it a sentiment of that peculiar character and intensity" that marks moral judgment [005-004].

The answer lies in the general laws of our emotional constitution, the shared dispositions that render certain objects apt to provoke approbation or indignation. The sentiment of justice is a compound emotion, built from the desire to punish harm and sympathetic identification with the collective.

## The Moral Faculty and Its Emotional Basis

Mill argues that the moral faculty "is a branch of our reason, not of our sensitive faculty" [001-004]. Reason supplies the principles of morality; emotion supplies the motive force that moves agents to act on them.

## Emotion and the Internal Sanction

Moral motivation draws on emotions from diverse sources: "from fear; from all the forms of religious feeling; from the recollections of childhood and of all our past life; from self-esteem, desire of the esteem of others, and occasionally even self-abasement" [003-009]. The internal sanction is a composite of many emotions, woven together by habituation and social cultivation.
