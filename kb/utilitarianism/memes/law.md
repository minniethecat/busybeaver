# Law

**Law** in Mill's *Utilitarianism* operates at two levels: as a metaphor for the binding force of moral obligation, and as the concrete institution of legal systems whose ultimate justification is utility. Mill identifies "one fundamental principle or law at the root of all morality" as the central question of ethics — and argues that this principle is utility [001-005].

## Moral Law and Divine Command

Mill confronts the theological view that "the will of God" is "the supreme law of morals." His reply is characteristically utilitarian: even if God wills moral rules, the utilitarian must ask whether God wills them because they are right, or whether they are right because God wills them. In either case, Mill argues, "a utilitarian may answer that" the principle of utility provides the standard by which even divine commands must be evaluated [002-030]. The moral law derives its authority from its contribution to happiness, not from its source.

## Law, Justice, and Punishment

The concept of law is inseparable from justice. Mill traces the etymology: *justum* derives from *jussum*, "that which has been ordered" — the link between law and right conduct is ancient and linguistic [005-011]. The "essence of law" includes not merely the idea of a rule but also "the idea of a sanction" — a penalty attached to violation — and this "enters not only into the conception of justice, but into that of law" itself [005-014].

## The Subjection to Law

Mill notes that "the sentiment of submission to law" is one of the psychological foundations of the sense of justice [005-006]. We feel anger at law-breakers not merely because they cause harm but because they violate a rule to which all are expected to submit. This sentiment of submission — to law and to "the ordinances of law" — transforms individual resentment into the impartial demand for justice.

## Bad Laws and Moral Critique

Mill recognises that "other rights may be a bad law" — legal validity does not entail moral legitimacy [005-005]. A law that fails to serve utility is a law that ought to be changed. This is the critical edge of utilitarian jurisprudence: the standard of utility provides a benchmark against which existing laws can be measured and found wanting. The moral law (utility) sits in judgment over the legal law (statute).
