# Greatest Happiness Principle

The **greatest happiness principle** is Mill's name for the fundamental axiom of utilitarian ethics — the principle that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, and wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness. Mill adopts the term from Bentham, who "latterly called it the greatest happiness principle" [001-006], and makes it the foundation of his entire moral system.

## The Standard: Not One's Own Happiness

Mill is explicit that the utilitarian standard is not the agent's own greatest happiness. Rather, it is "the greatest amount of happiness altogether" [002-011] — the happiness of all sentient beings affected by an action, impartially considered. This is what distinguishes utilitarianism from mere egoism: the agent is required to be "as strictly impartial as a disinterested and benevolent spectator" [002-011].

## The Practical Objection and Mill's Reply

A common objection to the greatest happiness principle is that "there is not time, previous to action, for calculating and weighing the effects of any line of conduct on the general happiness" [002-033]. Mill dismisses this as a misunderstanding. Human beings do not need to calculate from scratch on every occasion: "there has been ample time, namely the whole past duration of the human species" [002-033] to learn which kinds of actions tend to promote or diminish happiness. The accumulated experience of mankind provides ready-made rules — secondary principles — that guide ordinary moral decision-making.

## The Sanction of the Greatest Happiness Morality

Mill argues that the greatest happiness morality possesses all the sanctions available to any ethical system. The external sanctions — "the hope of favour and the fear of displeasure from our fellow creatures or from the Ruler of the Universe" [003-003] — are available to utilitarianism as much as to any rival doctrine. The internal sanction — the feeling of conscientious duty — is cultivated through education and social life, and Mill maintains that the desire to promote the general happiness can become "an attribute which it would not be without" [003-015] — a natural and spontaneous motive.

## Proof and the General Happiness

The sole evidence that the general happiness is desirable, Mill argues, is "that people do actually desire it" [004-002]. Just as the proof that something is visible is that people see it, and the proof that a sound is audible is that people hear it, so the evidence that happiness is an end is that people in fact pursue it. This analogical argument — not a deductive proof but a consideration addressed to the intellect — anchors the greatest happiness principle as the ultimate standard of utilitarian ethics.
