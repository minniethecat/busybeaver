## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **expectations** are the anticipations of conduct or outcome that individuals form based on social practice, explicit promise, or tacit encouragement. Mill identifies the disappointment of legitimate expectations as a core form of injury that gives the sentiment of justice its characteristic intensity [005-037].

Mill grounds the concept in the distinction between authorized expectations — those protected by recognition of individual rights — and mere hopes. To violate another's legitimate expectations is, he argues, to inflict a "real hurt" [005-007].

## Key Arguments

- **Expectations and rights**: The violation of "the legitimate and authorized expectations of any one else" constitutes an infringement of right, distinguishing justice from other departments of morality [002-025].

- **Expectation and religious sanction**: Even belief in divine judgment operates on conduct only through the "expectation of actual reward and punishment," which works through subjective religious feeling rather than objective duty [003-006].

- **Disappointed expectations as injustice**: It is "confessedly unjust to break faith with any one: to violate an engagement, either express or implied, or disappoint expectations raised by our own conduct" — at least when those expectations were raised knowingly and voluntarily [005-007].

- **The naturalness of expectation**: The expectation of reciprocal benefit is among "the most natural and reasonable of expectations," one which a person "must at least tacitly have encouraged, otherwise the benefits would seldom have been conferred" [005-037].
