# Deduction

**Deduction** is, in Mill's *Utilitarianism*, the method of reasoning from general principles to particular conclusions. Both the intuitive and inductive schools "hold equally that morality must be deduced from principles" [001-004]; their disagreement concerns the source of the premises, not the deductive form of moral reasoning.

## Definition

In Mill's usage, deduction is the process by which general moral principles are applied to individual cases. He observes that "the morality of an individual action is not a question of direct perception, but of the application of a law to an individual case" [001-004]. Mill also uses the term methodologically to describe Bentham's practice of deriving expected consequences from "the laws of human nature and the universal conditions of human life" [005-041].

## Key Arguments

**1. Morality Requires Deduction from Principles.** Both the intuitive and inductive schools agree that moral judgment involves deducing particular duties from general principles [001-004]. This shared commitment to deduction is what makes ethics a science.

**2. Formal Deduction Alone Cannot Generate Moral Content.** Mill's critique of Kant illustrates this limit. When Kant "begins to deduce from this precept any of the actual duties of morality, he fails, almost grotesquely, to show that there would be any contradiction, any logical (not to say physical) impossibility, in the adoption by all rational beings of the most outrageously immoral rules of conduct" [001-007]. Deduction from a purely formal principle yields no determinate duties without empirical premises.

**3. Deduction and Empirical Generalization Are Complementary.** Mill responds to Herbert Spencer's view that moral knowledge is "completely attainable only by deducing, from the laws of life and the conditions of existence, what kinds of action necessarily tend to produce happiness" [005-041]. While Mill acknowledges that Bentham freely applied deduction to "the effect of actions on happiness from the laws of human nature" [005-041], he warns against "relying too exclusively upon such deductions, and declining altogether to be bound by the generalizations from specific experience" [005-041]. Mill's conclusion is that "the consilience of the results of both these processes, each corroborating and verifying the other, is requisite to give to any general proposition the kind and degree of evidence which constitutes scientific proof" [005-041].

**4. The First Principle Is Not a Mere Logical Corollary.** The Greatest Happiness Principle is "a direct emanation from the first principle of morals, and not a mere logical corollary from secondary or derivative doctrines" [005-039]. Equality of consideration is part of the foundation, not a derivative theorem.
