## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **unity** denotes the bond of shared feeling and common interest among human beings that serves as the natural foundation of utilitarian morality. Mill describes this as "the desire to be in unity with our fellow creatures," a powerful principle already present in human nature and capable of being strengthened through education and social institutions [[003-010]].

## Key Arguments

- **Unity as the firm foundation**: Mill identifies the social feelings — the desire to be in unity with others — as the "firm foundation" of utilitarian morality. This natural sympathy is already "a powerful principle in human nature, and happily one of those which tend to become stronger, even without express inculcation, from the influences of advancing civilisation" [[003-010]].

- **The growing feeling of unity**: In an improving state of society, "the influences are constantly on the increase, which tend to generate in each individual a feeling of unity with all the rest" [[003-013]]. Mill envisions that if this feeling of unity were "taught as a religion, and the whole force of education, of institutions, and of opinion, directed... to make every person grow up from infancy surrounded on all sides both by the profession and the practice of it," it would become as powerful as any religious conviction [[003-013]].

- **Unity and distributive justice**: The concept of unity has practical implications for distributive justice. Mill observes that different schools of thought — such as those who favour strict equality versus those who favour proportionate reward — disagree about what social unity demands in practice, while all appeal to some notion of just distribution [[005-010]].

- **Community of interest**: A human being "is capable of apprehending a community of interest between himself and the human society of which he forms a part" [[005-019]]. This capacity for recognising shared interests transforms individual self-interest into a broader concern for the collective good, bridging the gap between personal and general happiness.
