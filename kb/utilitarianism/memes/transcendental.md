## Definition

The **transcendental** in Mill refers to moral theories that locate the origin of obligation outside human experience — in a "transcendental law, having no connexion with utility or with happiness" [002-030].

Mill identifies this with intuitionist and Kantian theories, which hold that moral truths are objective realities apprehended by a special faculty.

## Key Arguments

- **Transcendental justification is unnecessary**: Moral sanctions operate through internal feelings fully explainable by associationist psychology. The transcendental moralist claims the sanction "will not exist in the mind unless it is believed to have its root out of the mind" [003-007]. Mill rejects this.

- **Intuitionism cannot resolve moral disagreement**: Even among intuitionists, "the intuitive perception is of principles of morality, and not of the details" [003-008]. The transcendental faculty delivers only abstract principles, not practical guidance.

- **Transcendental belief does not guarantee conduct**: Conscience is not "in any perceptible degree present in all of us; but this, unhappily, is a fact admitted by those who believe the most strenuously in their transcendental origin" [003-009].

- **The utilitarian alternative**: Mill grounds moral obligation in the natural desire for happiness and the social feelings binding individuals to the collective good [002-030].
