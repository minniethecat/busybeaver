# Freedom

**Freedom** appears in Mill's *Utilitarianism* in two distinct senses: as **freedom from pain** — a component of happiness itself — and as **individual liberty** — a sphere of action whose protection is among the most vital requirements of justice. Mill's commitment to freedom is grounded in utilitarian reasoning, not in abstract rights.

## Freedom from Pain

Mill defines happiness as "pleasure and the absence of pain" — unhappiness as "pain and the privation of pleasure" [002-003]. This makes freedom from pain a constitutive element of well-being, not merely an instrumental good. The Greatest Happiness Principle requires minimizing pain and maximizing pleasure across all sentient beings.

## Freedom as a Requirement of Justice

The rules that protect individual freedom are "more vital to human well-being than any maxims" of policy [005-034]. These rules — prohibiting interference with each other's freedom — are "the main element in determining the whole of the social feelings of mankind" [005-035]. Their observance "alone preserves peace among human beings." Mill warns, however, that social sanctions can become "so excessive as to interfere unduly with human freedom and individuality" [003-014].
