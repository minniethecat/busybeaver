# Moral

**Moral** (or **morality**) is the foundational concern of ethics — the question of what constitutes right and wrong conduct. In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, the search for the criterion of morality is the central problem that has occupied philosophers "from the dawn of philosophy" [001-001]. Mill argues that the question concerning the *summum bonum*, or the **foundation of morality**, is "the main problem in speculative thought" and has divided thinkers into "sects and schools, carrying on a vigorous warfare against one another" [001-001].

## The Foundation of Morality

Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by surveying the state of moral philosophy. Despite over two thousand years of discussion, "neither thinkers nor mankind at large seem nearer to being unanimous on the subject than when the youth Socrates listened to the old Protagoras" [001-002]. Mill identifies two competing schools: the **intuitive school**, which holds that "the principles of morals are evident *a priori*," and the **inductive school**, which treats right and wrong as "questions of observation and experience" [001-004].

Both schools, Mill observes, agree that "morality must be deduced from principles" [001-004]. Yet the intuitive school "seldom attempt to make out a list of the *a priori* principles" and "still more rarely do they make any effort to reduce those various principles to one first principle, or common ground of obligation" [001-005]. The failure to establish a single fundamental principle has, in Mill's view, left ethics without a proper foundation.

## The Moral Faculty

Mill critically examines the notion of an innate **moral faculty** — a "sense or instinct, informing us of right and wrong" [001-003]. He notes that even believers in moral instinct "who have any pretensions to philosophy have been obliged to abandon the idea that it discerns what is right or wrong in the particular case in hand, as our other senses discern the sight or sound actually present" [001-004].

Instead, the moral faculty is understood as "a branch of our reason, not of our sensitive faculty," supplying "only the general principles of moral judgments" [001-004]. This aligns with Mill's broader argument: morality is not a matter of direct perception but of the "application of a law to an individual case" [001-004].

## The Tacit Influence of Utility

A key historical claim Mill advances is that the **principle of utility** has exercised a "tacit influence" on moral beliefs even among those who explicitly reject it:

> Although the non-existence of an acknowledged first principle has made ethics not so much a guide as a consecration of men's actual sentiments, still, as men's sentiments, both of favour and aversion, are greatly influenced by what they suppose to be the effects of things upon their happiness, the principle of utility, or as Bentham latterly called it, the greatest happiness principle, has had a large share in forming the moral doctrines of those who most scornfully reject its authority. [001-005]

Mill goes further, asserting that "to those *a priori* moralists who deem it necessary to argue at all, utilitarian arguments are indispensable" [001-005].

## Kant's Failure

Mill uses Kant's **categorical imperative** as an illustration of the deficiency of non-utilitarian moral systems. Kant lays down a "universal first principle as the origin and ground of moral obligation": *So act that the rule on which thou actest would admit of being adopted as a law by all rational beings* [001-005].

Yet, Mill argues, when Kant "begins to deduce from this precept any of the actual duties of morality, he fails, almost grotesquely, to show that there would be any contradiction, any logical (not to say physical) impossibility, in the adoption by all rational beings of the most outrageously immoral rules of conduct" [001-005]. All Kant shows is that "the consequences of their universal adoption would be such as no one would choose to incur" [001-005] — which, Mill implies, is a tacit appeal to utilitarian reasoning.

## Quality in Moral Pleasures

In Chapter II, Mill extends his analysis to the **quality** of moral sentiments. He argues that the pleasures of "the intellect, of the feelings and imagination, and of the moral sentiments" possess "a much higher value as pleasures than to those of mere sensation" [002-005]. This qualitative distinction is central to Mill's defense against the charge that utilitarianism is a "doctrine worthy only of swine" [002-005].

The capacity for moral feeling is essential to human dignity: "It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied" [002-006].

## Moral Standard and Impartiality

The utilitarian moral standard demands strict impartiality. "As between his own happiness and that of others, utilitarianism requires an agent to be as strictly impartial as a disinterested and benevolent spectator" [002-011]. This principle, Mill argues, is embodied in "the golden rule of Jesus of Nazareth" — to do as one would be done by — which he calls "the complete spirit of the ethics of utility" [002-011].
