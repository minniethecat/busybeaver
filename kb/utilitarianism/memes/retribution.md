## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **retribution** designates the principle that wrongdoers should suffer evil in return for the evil they have inflicted — captured in the ancient maxim of *lex talionis*, "an eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth." Mill observes that although this principle has been "generally abandoned in Europe as a practical maxim," there persists "in most minds, a secret hankering after it" [[005-030]]. Retribution, or "evil for evil," is described as "closely connected with the sentiment of justice, and is universally included in the idea" [[005-036]].

## Key Arguments

- **Retribution as a natural sentiment**: When retribution "accidentally falls on an offender in that precise shape, the general feeling of satisfaction evinced bears witness how natural is the sentiment to which this repayment in kind is acceptable" [[005-030]]. This natural appeal, however, does not make it a sufficient standard of justice.
- **Retribution versus proportionality**: Mill contrasts the retributive impulse with the principle that punishment "should be proportioned to the offence," measured by moral guilt. He notes the tension between those who hold proportionality as the test of justice in punishment and those who maintain that only deterrence — "what amount of punishment is necessary to deter from the offence" — is relevant [[005-030]].
- **Retribution and the impulses of justice**: In his analysis of the sentiment of justice, Mill identifies retribution as arising alongside "the impulses of self-defence, of defence of others, and of vengeance," all of which are "called forth against" violators of the primary moralities [[005-036]]. Retribution thus occupies a place among the natural impulses that, when moralized by social sympathy, constitute the sentiment of justice.
