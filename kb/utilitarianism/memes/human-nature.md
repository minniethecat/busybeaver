## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian philosophy, **human nature** functions as both the empirical foundation of moral theory and the ultimate constraint on ethical possibility. Rejecting the "degrading" caricature that utilitarianism reduces human motivation to base self-interest, Mill argues that human nature includes capacities for disinterested devotion, sympathetic concern for others, and the cultivation of higher faculties — all of which make the utilitarian standard of general happiness a psychologically attainable ideal. The laws of human nature, understood through experience and self-observation, provide the factual premises from which utilitarian conclusions about moral obligation and the good life are derived [[002-004], [003-004]].

## Key Arguments

- **Against the "degrading" view**: Mill's critics charge that utilitarianism "represents human nature in a degrading light" by reducing all motivation to pleasure-seeking. Mill counters that this objection confuses the utilitarian account of what people actually desire with a low estimate of what they are capable of desiring. Human nature, properly understood, includes a rich range of faculties — intellectual, aesthetic, and sympathetic — whose exercise constitutes the higher pleasures that distinguish utilitarian ethics from mere hedonism [[002-004], [002-023]].

- **Human nature and disinterested devotion**: Mill insists that utilitarianism demands and human nature permits "all that the capacities of human nature admit of disinterested devotion" to the general good. The moral sentiments — conscience, sympathy, and the feeling of unity with others — are "a fact in human nature," not artificial impositions. Their reality is confirmed by experience and observation, and their cultivation through education and social life is the central task of utilitarian moral development [[003-004], [003-006]].

- **Habit as a "powerful principle in human nature"**: The formation of moral habits, Mill argues, is a powerful and pervasive force in human psychology. Once established, habits of virtuous conduct become "a power... in human nature" that operates with the reliability of instinct, making moral action both easier and more consistent over time. This emphasis on habituation places Mill's moral psychology in continuity with Aristotle's ethics, though Mill grounds the value of habit in its contribution to general utility [[003-010]].

- **The psychological foundation of the proof of utility**: In Chapter IV, Mill's argument that happiness is the only thing desirable as an end rests on the claim that "human nature is so constituted as to desire nothing which is not either a part of happiness or a means to happiness." This psychological claim — that the structure of human desire is inherently happiness-directed — provides the factual premise for the utilitarian principle. If human nature were different, the normative force of the utilitarian standard would be correspondingly altered [[004-008]].

- **Laws of human nature as the basis of ethical knowledge**: Mill grounds the science of morals in "the laws of human nature and the universal conditions of human life." Just as the physical sciences derive their principles from natural laws, ethics must derive its normative conclusions from empirical truths about human psychology, sociality, and the conditions under which happiness is attainable. This naturalistic methodology distinguishes utilitarian ethics from intuitionist and theological alternatives [[005-041]].
