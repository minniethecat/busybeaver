## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **certainty** refers to the degree of confidence attainable in moral knowledge.

Mill contrasts moral certainty with the demonstrative certainty of mathematics, observing that "similar confusion and uncertainty" exist about first principles in all sciences [001-002]. Yet this does not impair practical reliability.

## Key Arguments

- **Moral uncertainty parallels scientific uncertainty**: All sciences suffer confusion about foundational principles without impairing practical reliability. Morals are no different [001-002].

- **Certainty through association and habit**: The sentiment of justice acquires apparent certainty through rapid association: "the mind recalls with a rapidity and certainty resembling an instinct" [005-001].

- **Desire as the engine of moral certainty**: Virtue is instilled "by making the person desire virtue — by making him think of it in a pleasurable light, or of its absence in a painful one" [004-012].
