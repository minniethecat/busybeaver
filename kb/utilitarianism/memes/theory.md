# Ethical Theory

The concept of an **ethical theory** — a systematic account of the foundations and structure of morality — is the organizing framework of Mill's *Utilitarianism*. Mill presents utilitarianism as a comprehensive theory that answers three questions: what is the criterion of right action, how is that criterion justified, and how does it connect to the actual practice of moral judgment.

## Theory Against Intuition

Mill frames his project as a defense of utilitarian theory against its critics. He recounts "a real conversation" in which "the theory of utilitarianism against the" intuitionist alternative was debated [001-002]. The fundamental choice in ethical theory is between systems that ground morality in an observable end — happiness — and those that appeal to unverifiable moral intuitions.

The methodological contrast is stark. "Particular truths precede the general theory, the contrary might be expected" in ethics as in other sciences — practical moral knowledge comes first, and theoretical systematization follows [001-003]. The utilitarian claims that his theory merely makes explicit the criterion that ordinary moral judgment already implicitly applies.

## The Theory of Life

Mill identifies a deeper dimension of utilitarian theory: it presupposes a "theory of life" — a conception of what makes a life worth living. "Now, such a theory of life excites in many minds" deep resistance, because it appears to reduce human existence to the pursuit of pleasure [002-004].

Yet Mill insists that "there is no known Epicurean theory of life which does not assign" to the pleasures of the intellect, the feelings, and the moral sentiments a far higher value than to those of mere sensation [002-005]. The utilitarian theory of life is not hedonism in the vulgar sense, but a qualitative hedonism that recognizes the superiority of distinctively human pleasures.

## Theory and Practice

Mill's deep concern is the relationship between theory and practice. He worries that unless ethical theory is firmly established, moral practice will drift. If "proposes to itself were not, in theory and in practice, acknowledged" as the proper standard, moral judgment loses its anchor [004-002].

The gap between theory and practice — between what the utilitarian standard demands and what ordinary moral motivation can supply — is a permanent challenge. The theory must be demanding enough to guide genuine improvement, yet realistic enough to engage the actual motives of human beings.
