# Wrong

**Wrong** is, alongside [[right]], one of the two fundamental categories of moral judgment in Mill's utilitarian philosophy. Actions are "wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness" [002-003]. Mill distinguishes between wrong in the broad utilitarian sense and wrong in the narrower, more stringent sense connected to [[justice]].

## Wrong as the Reverse of Happiness

The utilitarian definition of wrong is symmetrical with the definition of right:

> Actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness [002-003]

This consequentialist framework makes wrongness a matter of degree rather than an absolute property. An action is wrong to the extent that its consequences diminish the sum total of happiness. Mill explicitly rejects the "transcendental" or "metaphysical" conception of wrong as a mysterious quality inherent in actions independently of their effects.

The utilitarian standard has been criticized for reducing "the distinction between right and wrong" to a calculation of consequences [002-026]. Mill responds by arguing that the complexity of this calculation — requiring consideration of "all the circumstances" [002-035] — is a feature of moral judgment, not a defect of utilitarianism.

## Wrong and Justice: The Stricter Sense

Mill draws a crucial distinction between wrong in the broad sense and wrong in the sense connected to justice. Not everything morally wrong is a matter of justice:

> We do not call anything wrong unless we mean to imply that a person ought to be punished in some way or other for doing it [005-014]

This connects wrongness to the idea of **sanction**: calling an action wrong implies that the agent deserves blame, punishment, or at least the disapproval of conscience. Mill elaborates:

> We call any conduct wrong… which we think ought to be punished, whether by law, by the opinion of mankind, or by the reproaches of conscience [005-015]

## The Two Elements of Wrongful Conduct

Mill identifies two essential elements in the concept of wrongful conduct toward others:

> A wrong done, and some assignable person who is wronged [005-016]

This distinguishes wrongs that violate **perfect obligations** — duties owed to specific persons who have corresponding rights — from wrongs that violate **imperfect obligations**, where no assignable individual can claim redress. Injustice is the subclass of wrongs where a specific person is harmed and has a claim to protection.

## Wrong and Moral Obligation

The gradation from wrong to obligation is another key distinction. Mill notes that there are "things which it is not only a right to do, and wrong not to do, but which some individual person can claim from us as his moral right" [005-017]. This defines the most stringent moral category: a wrong that violates a duty of justice — something one is not merely wrong to omit, but which another person has a right to demand.

## Wrongful Aggression

Mill identifies "wrongful aggression, or wrongful exercise of power over some one" as the paradigm of injustice [005-036]. Wrongful interference with each other's freedom is one of the primary concerns of justice: the rules of justice "must never forget to include wrongful interference with each other's individual freedom" [005-034].
