# Proof

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **proof** in ethics does not mean deductive demonstration. Mill explicitly acknowledges that "questions of ultimate ends do not admit of proof, in the ordinary acceptation of the term" — this is true of all first principles, including those of knowledge and conduct [004-001]. Instead, Mill appeals to a broader conception of proof grounded in observation and experience.

## The Nature of Ethical Proof

Mill argues that the only proof capable of establishing an ethical first principle is analogous to proving that something is visible: "The only proof capable of being given that an object is visible, is that people actually see it" [004-002]. Similarly, the "sole evidence it is possible to produce that anything is desirable, is that people do actually desire it." This shift from visibility to desirability is Mill's central argumentative strategy — the principle of utility is proven by showing that happiness is what all people ultimately desire.

## Proof Beyond Deduction

The question of the utilitarian standard is "not a subject of what is commonly understood by proof" [001-008]. Mill distinguishes between the narrow sense of proof as logical deduction, and a "larger meaning of the word proof" in which ultimate normative questions are "as amenable to it as any other of the disputed questions of philosophy." The proof consists in showing that the utilitarian principle already implicitly governs practical reasoning about right and wrong [001-007].
