## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **intellect** refers to the higher cognitive and rational faculties whose pleasures are qualitatively superior to mere bodily sensations. Mill opens the treatise by noting that the fundamental question of moral philosophy "has occupied the most gifted intellects, and divided them into sects" [001-001], positioning intellectual inquiry as central to ethical reasoning. The "pleasures of the intellect" form the cornerstone of his qualitative hedonism, where he argues they are preferred by those competent to judge both intellectual and sensual pleasures [002-005].

## Key Arguments

- **Qualitative superiority**: Mill asserts that the pleasures of the intellect, feelings, and imagination possess a higher quality than those of mere sensation, such that "they would not resign" intellectual pleasures for any quantity of lower pleasures [002-005]. This qualitative distinction separates utilitarian ethics from Bentham's purely quantitative hedonism.

- **Intellectual cultivation and character**: Those who cultivate their intellectual tastes find life more fulfilling. Mill warns that individuals "lose their intellectual tastes" through neglect, diminishing their capacity for higher enjoyments [002-010]. The development of intellect is therefore essential to human flourishing.

- **Intellect and moral progress**: The "intellectual culture" of society advances alongside civilization, gradually improving moral understanding and social relations [003-009]. As intellectual culture grows, moral sentiments become more refined.

- **Practical role in justice**: Mill acknowledges that "intellect and sympathy" work together in the application of justice, tempering the natural desire for retaliation and rendering punishment proportionate [005-020]. Intellectual judgment is indispensable to equitable moral reasoning.
