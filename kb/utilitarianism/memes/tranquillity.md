## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian analysis of happiness, **tranquillity** is one of the two fundamental constituents of a pleasurable life — the other being excitement. Mill defines tranquillity not as mere absence of disturbance but as a positive state of composed satisfaction, the condition in which a person can "cultivate in tranquillity the sources of satisfaction" that make life worth living [[002-015], [002-021]].

## Key Arguments

- **Tranquillity and excitement as complementary poles**: Mill identifies these two elements as jointly sufficient for a happy life: "tranquillity, and excitement." He observes that "with much tranquillity, many" persons find contentment, while others require a greater degree of stimulation. The balance between these two varies by individual temperament and circumstances, but neither alone is adequate for lasting happiness [[002-015]].
- **The cultivation of tranquillity**: Mill draws on historical example — the practice of the Roman Empire — to illustrate that tranquillity is not a passive default but an active achievement requiring "cultivation." The "sources of satisfaction" that sustain tranquillity must be deliberately developed and protected from the erosive effects of excessive pursuit of excitement [[002-021]].
- **Tranquillity versus mere contentment**: Mill distinguishes genuine tranquillity from the mere absence of striving. The "continuity of pleasurable excitement" is, he notes, unsustainable — the alternation between excitement and tranquillity reflects the natural rhythm of human psychology. Tranquillity provides the baseline of well-being against which the peaks of excitement are measured [[002-014]].
