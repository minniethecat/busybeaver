# Punishment

**Punishment** is, in Mill's utilitarian theory, the practical expression of the sentiment of justice. The desire that wrongdoers suffer punishment is, alongside the impulse of self-defense and sympathy for victims, one of the natural components from which the sentiment of justice is composed. "The sentiment of justice... is a desire that punishment may be suffered by those who inflict harm" [005-022].

## Punishment as the Core of Justice

Mill traces the concept of justice to the primitive impulse of retaliation — returning "evil for evil" [005-022]. Over time, this natural impulse is moralized through two transformations: it is extended to include harms done to society at large (not only to oneself), and it is regulated by the principle of utility so that punishment is inflicted only when it serves the general good. "There are cases... in which it means, being solely influenced by consideration for the public interest" [005-009] — the administration of punishment must be guided by social utility, not private vengeance.

## The Justification of Punishment

The utilitarian justification of punishment rests on its social consequences. Punishment deters future wrongdoing, protects society, and expresses the community's condemnation of harmful acts. "The legitimate infliction of punishment" [005-029] is tied to the recognition that certain rules are so essential to human welfare that their violation warrants a coercive response. But Mill insists that punishment must be "proportioned to the offence" [005-038] — a maxim that serves the utilitarian purpose of preventing excessive suffering and maintaining proportionality between the penalty and the social harm.

## Punishment and the Sanctions

Punishment operates as an **external sanction** in Mill's taxonomy of moral motives. Alongside "the expectation of actual reward and punishment" [003-004] — whether physical, moral, or religious — punishment provides one of the "abundant interested motives" [003-006] for compliance with moral rules. But Mill also looks forward to a state of moral progress in which "the appliances of education and general cultivation" [003-004] reduce the need for punitive sanctions by internalizing the disposition to act rightly.
