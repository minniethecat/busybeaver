## Definition

In Mill's utilitarianism, **universal** refers to the scope and applicability of moral principles across all persons and contexts. Mill deploys "universal" in three interconnected senses: the universal first principle from which all moral rules derive [001-006]; the test of universal adoption by which proposed rules are evaluated [001-007]; and the universal conditions of human life that constrain and ground ethical reasoning [005-041].

## Key Arguments

### The Universal First Principle

Mill's project in *Utilitarianism* is to identify and defend a "universal first principle as the origin and ground of moral obligation" [001-006]. Unlike intuitionists who posit multiple irreducible moral truths, Mill argues that all moral rules trace back to the single principle of utility — the greatest happiness principle — which is universal in the sense that it applies to all moral agents and all actions without exception.

### Universal Adoption as a Test of Rules

Mill deploys a universalization test reminiscent of Kant's categorical imperative: proposed moral rules should be evaluated by considering "the consequences of their universal adoption" [001-007]. A rule whose universal observance would produce net harm fails as a moral rule. This consequentialist universalization differs from Kant's in that the criterion is happiness produced, not logical contradiction.

### Universal Conditions of Human Life

Mill grounds the content of justice in "the universal conditions of human life" and "the laws of human nature" [005-041]. Security — the most vital of all interests — is universally required by all persons in all societies, which explains why justice (the protection of security) commands such strong and universal sentiment. What is universally necessary for human flourishing becomes the substance of moral obligation.

### Universal Admission and Moral Consensus

Mill frequently appeals to what is "universally admitted" or acknowledged "by universal or widely spread opinion" [005-006] as evidence for moral distinctions. The near-universal condemnation of certain acts (such as singling out an individual for punishment without justification) provides inductive evidence that justice has a real, universally recognized core [005-027].
