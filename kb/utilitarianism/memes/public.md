# Public Good

The **public good** — the welfare of the community taken as a whole — is the ultimate standard by which utilitarian ethics judges actions, institutions, and policies. Mill treats the public good not as a metaphysical entity but as the aggregate of the interests of all individuals who compose the public.

## The Public Good as Utilitarian Standard

Mill grounds the utilitarian conception of public good in the social feelings. "To those who have neither public nor private affections, the" very possibility of moral motivation disappears [002-015]. The capacity to care about the public good is what makes moral life possible — without it, there is no bridge from self-interest to the interest of others.

He further insists that sincere devotion to the public good is psychologically real: "sincere in the public good, are possible, though" rare and demanding of cultivation [002-017]. Mill's naturalism requires that the public affections be explicable as developments of human nature, not impositions upon it.

## The Public Benefactor

Mill introduces the figure of the public benefactor — one who acts consistently for the good of others — as a moral ideal. To be "a public benefactor, are but exceptional" cases, requiring unusual strength of character and favorable circumstances [002-025]. The public benefactor exemplifies the highest reach of utilitarian morality, where private interest yields to the general happiness.

Yet Mill is realistic about the limits of this ideal. In an imperfect world, complete devotion to the public good cannot be expected of everyone, and the moral system must accommodate the ordinary range of human motivation.

## Public Interest and Justice

The public interest provides the foundation for justice itself. Mill argues that considerations of justice are ultimately "enforced by consideration for the public interest; as in making a selection" between competing claims, the public good is the decisive criterion [005-009].

Public disapprobation — the community's moral judgment expressed collectively — functions as a sanction that enforces the rules necessary for social life. "The public disapprobation to bear upon" wrongdoers is the mechanism by which the community protects its shared interests [005-013].
