# Principle

A **first principle** in ethics is the fundamental ground from which all moral duties and judgments derive their authority. Mill argues in Chapter I that ethics lacks an "acknowledged first principle" — a deficiency that "has made ethics not so much a guide as a consecration of men's actual sentiments" [001-005]. The utilitarian project is to supply this missing foundation.

## The Need for a First Principle

Mill observes that while the sciences have managed to progress despite disagreement about their "first principles," a practical art like morals cannot afford the same indeterminacy. "All action is for the sake of some end, and rules of action must take their whole character and colour from the end to which they are subservient" [001-003]. Without a clear criterion, moral reasoning risks circularity.

Mill criticizes the intuitive moralists who "seldom attempt to make out a list of the *a priori* principles" and "still more rarely do they make any effort to reduce those various principles to one first principle, or common ground of obligation" [001-005]. The alternative to a unified first principle is either ethical **pluralism** (multiple irreducible principles that may conflict) or **relativism**.

## Utility as the First Principle

Mill proposes the **principle of utility** — the greatest happiness principle — as the needed first principle of morals. "The principle of utility, or as Bentham latterly called it, the greatest happiness principle, has had a large share in forming the moral doctrines even of those who most scornfully reject its authority" [001-006]. The claim is both normative (utility *ought* to be the first principle) and descriptive (utility *already tacitly* operates as the first principle in much moral reasoning).

## Principles in Moral Judgment

Mill distinguishes between **general principles** and their **application to particular cases**. "Our moral faculty supplies us only with the general principles of moral judgments; it is a branch of our reason, not of our sensitive faculty" [001-004]. The morality of an individual action is "not a question of direct perception, but of the application of a law to an individual case" [001-004].
