## Definition

In Mill's moral psychology, **temptation** denotes the inclination to act against one's better judgment — to prefer a lower, nearer pleasure over a higher, more distant one, or to make a self-serving exception to a moral rule one generally endorses. Temptation is the primary mechanism by which moral knowledge fails to translate into moral conduct, and its management is central to the utilitarian account of character formation and the function of secondary moral rules.

## Key Arguments

### Temptation and the Preference for Lower Pleasures

Mill acknowledges that persons may, "under the influence of temptation, postpone" higher pleasures "to the lower" [002-009]. This concession is essential to his qualitative hedonism: the fact that people sometimes choose lower pleasures does not refute the superiority of higher ones, because such choices occur under the distorting influence of temptation rather than from sober judgment. The competent judge, free from temptation's sway, reliably prefers the higher.

### Temptation and Self-Serving Exceptions

In discussing the function of moral rules, Mill notes that the agent "when under temptation, will see an utility in the breach of a rule, greater than he will see in its observance" [002-036]. Temptation operates by presenting a particular case as exceptional, obscuring the long-term and general consequences of rule violation. Secondary moral rules provide a necessary bulwark against this cognitive distortion: they encode the accumulated wisdom that apparent exceptions are rarely genuine.

### The Conquerability of Temptation

Mill describes the moral disposition as "still feeble, conquerable by temptation, and not to be fully relied on" until it has been strengthened through habituation and education [004-011]. The cultivation of virtue is, in part, the progressive reduction of temptation's power. A fully formed moral character is one in which the desire to do right has become so firmly established that temptation ceases to be a serious threat to conduct.

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]] — the stable disposition that resists temptation
- [[character]] — the pattern of conduct shaped by repeated victories or defeats against temptation
- [[habit]] — the mechanism by which resistance to temptation is automated
- [[duty]] — the principle to which one appeals against temptation's promptings
- [[rules]] — the secondary principles that prevent temptation-driven exceptions
- [[will]] — the faculty whose weakness temptation exploits
