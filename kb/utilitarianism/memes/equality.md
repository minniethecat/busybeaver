# Equality

**Equality** is, in Mill's analysis of justice, "nearly allied to the idea of impartiality" and "often enters as a component part both into the conception of justice and into the practice of it" [005-009]. It is one of the core maxims that give content to the sentiment of justice, yet Mill shows that equality, like other justice concepts, derives its binding force from the principle of utility rather than from any independent *a priori* status.

## Equality and Expediency

Mill argues that the application of equality is consistently shaped by considerations of utility. "Each person maintains that equality is the dictate of justice, except where he thinks that expediency requires inequality" [005-009]. This observation reveals that equality is not a self-evident absolute: people appeal to equality when it serves perceived social utility, and justify departures from it on the same grounds. Those who support vast inequalities in rights nonetheless maintain that "the rights of the slave, such as they are, ought to be as sacred as those of the master" [005-010] — a hollow equality that reveals the dependence of the principle on underlying judgments of expediency.

## The Right to Equality of Treatment

"All persons are deemed to have a right to equality of treatment, except when some recognised social expediency requires the reverse" [005-041]. When social inequalities "have ceased to be considered expedient, they assume the character not of simple inexpediency, but of injustice" [005-041]. Mill thus accounts for both the moral force of equality claims and their historical variability: as societies revise their judgments of what is expedient, the domain of equality expands.

## Equality and the Greatest Happiness Principle

Bentham's dictum — "everybody to count for one, nobody for more than one" — provides the utilitarian foundation for equality. The maxims of "equality and impartiality" are, Mill writes, "included among the precepts of justice" and hold "exalted rank, among human obligations" [005-038]. This is because impartial calculation of the general happiness requires that each person's happiness be weighed equally — a procedural equality built into the very structure of utilitarian reasoning.
