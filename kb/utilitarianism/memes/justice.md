# Justice

**Justice** is a central concept in Mill's *Utilitarianism*, treated extensively in Chapter V ("On the Connection Between Justice and Utility"). Mill argues that the **sentiment of justice** — the powerful feeling that certain actions are unjust and deserve punishment — is not a mysterious *a priori* instinct but is grounded in the principle of utility [005-002].

## Is Justice a Peculiar Instinct?

Mill asks whether "the feeling itself, of justice and injustice, is *sui generis*" — a unique, irreducible moral sentiment — or whether it can be derived from more basic concerns [005-003]. His answer is that justice is **derivative**, not primitive. The feeling of justice arises from the combination of two natural impulses: the **desire to punish** those who cause harm, and the **sympathy** we feel for the victims of harm.

## Forms of Justice

Mill surveys the various "forms in which the notions of justice and injustice present themselves" [005-005]:

1. **Legal rights**: It is unjust to deprive someone of their legal rights
2. **Moral rights**: It is unjust to withhold what someone has a moral right to
3. **Desert**: It is just that each person receives what they deserve
4. **Faith-keeping**: It is unjust to break promises or violate engagements
5. **Impartiality**: It is unjust to show favour or preference in matters where impartiality is required
6. **Equality**: It is unjust to treat people unequally without sufficient reason

## The Connection to Utility

The unifying thread across all forms of justice is **utility** — specifically, the utility of **security**. "Security," Mill writes, "no human being can possibly do without; on it we depend for all our immunity from evil." Justice is the name we give to the most stringent utilitarian obligations — those rules whose observance is essential to the general happiness. "Utilitarianism seldom has the justice to acknowledge" its own role in grounding justice [002-021].
