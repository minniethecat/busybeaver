# Character

**Character** is the enduring set of dispositions, habits, and tendencies that constitute a person's moral identity. For Mill, character is central to ethics because it is the primary object of moral evaluation: an act may be right or wrong, but character determines the **worth of the agent**. "The best proof of a good character is good actions" [002-027].

## Character and Happiness

Mill argues that the cultivation of noble character contributes directly to happiness — both one's own and that of others. Even "if each individual were only benefited by the nobleness of others," the cultivation of nobleness of character would still be a contribution to general happiness [002-012]. But Mill goes further, maintaining that "a noble character is always the happier for its nobleness" [002-011].

## Infirmity of Character

A recurrent concern in Mill's ethics is "infirmity of character" — the gap between knowing what is right and doing it. "Men from infirmity of character, make their knowledge of what is better yield to the temptation of inferior pleasure" [002-009]. This weakness explains why even those who accept utilitarian principles may fail to act on them.

## Character Formation

Mill emphasizes that character is formed through education, social influence, and repeated action. The "earnestness and thoughtfulness of the character" [003-015] depends on cultivation. Because character can change — "diminished, by changes in his character or decay of his passive sensibilities" [004-010] — moral education becomes a central utilitarian concern.
