# Self-Sacrifice

**Self-sacrifice** — the willing surrender of one's own good for the sake of others — is a central and carefully qualified concept in Mill's utilitarian ethics. Mill honours the nobility of self-sacrifice while insisting that it derives its value solely from its contribution to the general happiness. Sacrifice without beneficial consequence is morally empty.

## The Instrumental Value of Sacrifice

Mill's treatment of self-sacrifice is uncompromisingly utilitarian. He rejects the view that sacrifice is good in itself: "A sacrifice which does not increase, nor tend to increase, the sum total of happiness, is wasted" [002-021]. The hero or martyr is not admired for suffering as such, but for suffering in a cause that advances the well-being of others. Mill pointedly asks: "this self-sacrifice must be for some end: it is not its own end" [002-019].

## The Nobility of Self-Sacrifice

Despite his instrumental account, Mill accords genuine honour to self-sacrifice. He speaks of "all honour to those who" abjure their own happiness for the sake of others [002-020]. The capacity for self-sacrifice is, for Mill, a mark of the higher human faculties — a proof that human beings are capable of being moved by goods beyond their own private pleasure. "Whoever supposes that this sacrifice of the superior being, since it... does not increase happiness, is to be condemned" misunderstands the utilitarian position [002-008].

## The Limits of Self-Sacrifice

Mill recognizes a dangerous edge to the ideal of self-sacrifice. He warns against "making him a sacrifice, without his consent, for other people's benefit" [005-027]. Self-sacrifice must be voluntary to be morally admirable; coerced sacrifice is injustice, not virtue. The distinction between freely chosen devotion and imposed sacrifice is essential to the utilitarian defence of individual rights.
