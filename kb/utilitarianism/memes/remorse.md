## Definition

**Remorse** is, in Mill's utilitarian ethics, the retrospective pain of conscience that follows the deliberate violation of a moral standard. In Chapter III of *Utilitarianism*, Mill identifies remorse as the characteristic experience of moral failure: the "mass of feeling" that a person "will probably have to be encountered afterwards" when they have broken through their standard of right [003-005]. Remorse is not an arbitrary or mysterious phenomenon but the felt manifestation of the internal sanction of morality — the emotional cost of wrongdoing that gives moral rules their binding force.

## Key Arguments

- **Remorse as the substance of conscience**: Mill argues that whatever theory one holds about the nature or origin of conscience, the capacity to feel remorse after wrongdoing "is what essentially constitutes it" [003-005]. Conscience is not a transcendent faculty or an innate moral compass; it is the organized mass of feeling whose prospective sting deters violation and whose retrospective sting is remorse. This analysis demystifies conscience by grounding it in a natural psychological phenomenon — the pain attendant on violating one's own standard of right.

- **Remorse and the binding force of morality**: The binding force of moral obligation "consists in the existence of a mass of feeling which must be broken through" to do wrong [003-005]. The anticipation of remorse functions as a prospective deterrent, while remorse itself is the retrospective sanction that confirms the reality of moral obligation. Without the capacity for remorse, moral rules would lack motivational hold on those who possess what Mill calls "conscientious feelings."

- **Remorse as the final sanction**: In Chapter V, Mill develops a three-tiered account of punishment for wrongdoing: legal punishment, the opinion of fellow creatures, and finally "the reproaches of his own conscience" [005-014]. Remorse occupies the innermost tier — it is the sanction that operates when no external penalty is possible or expedient. Mill treats the capacity for self-reproach as the mark that distinguishes moral wrong from mere imprudence: "We do not call anything wrong, unless we mean to imply that a person ought to be punished in some way or other for doing it" [005-014], and where law and public opinion cannot reach, conscience — experienced as remorse — supplies the penalty.

- **Remorse and the cultivation of character**: While Mill does not directly argue that remorse should be cultivated, his account implies that the capacity for remorse is strengthened through the same processes of education, association, and social feeling that cultivate [[conscience]] itself. The person of well-developed moral character feels remorse more keenly, and this heightened sensitivity serves as a more effective internal check against wrongdoing.

## Related Concepts

- [[conscience]] — remorse is the retrospective operation of conscience; the two are conceptually inseparable in Mill's account
- [[sanction]] — remorse is the internal sanction of morality, operating when external sanctions (law, opinion) are absent or insufficient
- [[punishment]] — remorse is a form of self-inflicted punishment, the innermost tier of Mill's three-tiered account of penal sanction
- [[approbation]] — the social counterpart to the internal feeling of remorse; public disapprobation is the external analogue of private self-reproach
- [[sentiment]] — remorse is a moral sentiment of the retrospective kind, arising from the violation of internalized moral standards
