## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian ethics, **humanity** refers both to the collective human species as the ultimate object of moral concern and to the sentiment of active benevolence toward all human beings. The "service of humanity" functions as the highest moral ideal in Mill's system—a secular replacement for religious devotion. Mill critiques those who set too low a moral standard "as being too high for humanity," arguing instead that humanity is capable of far greater moral achievement than commonly assumed [002-023].

## Key Arguments

- **Service to humanity as ultimate sanction**: Mill describes "the service of humanity" as a powerful motivating force that can operate "even without the aid of belief" in religious doctrine [003-013]. This reflects his project of grounding morality in secular social feelings rather than divine command. The desire to serve humanity provides the strongest internal sanction for utilitarian conduct.

- **Humanity's moral capacity**: Against critics who claim utilitarian standards are unrealistically demanding, Mill insists they are not "too high for humanity" [002-023]. He argues that human nature, properly cultivated through education and social feeling, is fully capable of being motivated by the general happiness.

- **Humanity and social feeling**: The sentiment of humanity is closely tied to "man's feelings of humanity and of social expediency" [005-033], which Mill sees as natural extensions of human sympathy. These feelings, when cultivated, produce a powerful inclination toward justice and benevolence.

- **Humanity as a secular religion**: Mill positions the service of humanity as functionally equivalent to religious devotion—a unifying object of reverence and dedication that can inspire moral behavior across society, independent of theological commitments.
