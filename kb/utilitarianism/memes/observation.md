## Definition

In Mill's utilitarian methodology, **observation** — together with experience — constitutes the empirical foundation of moral knowledge. Mill explicitly aligns ethics with the empirical sciences, asserting that the fundamental questions of moral theory are "questions of observation and experience" rather than of a priori intuition. In the proof of the principle of utility, Mill appeals to "self-consciousness and self-observation, assisted by observation of others" as the only admissible evidence for claims about what human beings desire. Observation thus serves as the methodological bridge between descriptive psychology and normative ethics in Mill's system [[001-004], [004-009]].

## Key Arguments

- **Observation as the foundation of moral science**: Mill opens *Utilitarianism* by framing the dispute between the intuitive and inductive schools of ethics as a question of method. The intuitionists claim moral principles are known a priori; the inductivists, among whom Mill counts himself, hold that "both schools" ultimately appeal to "observation and experience" even if the intuitionists deny it. By establishing observation as the common ground of ethical inquiry, Mill positions utilitarianism not as a sectarian doctrine but as the rigorous application of empirical method to moral questions [[001-004]].

- **Self-observation in the proof of utility**: In Chapter IV, Mill argues that the sole admissible evidence for what is desirable is what people "do actually desire." Access to this evidence requires "self-consciousness and self-observation" — the introspective examination of one's own motivational states — supplemented by "observation of others" — the empirical study of human conduct. The proof of utility is thus an observational argument: it does not deduce the principle from abstract premises but induces it from the observed facts of human psychology. If people are observed to desire happiness for its own sake and all other things as means to it, then happiness satisfies the only test of desirability that the nature of the case permits [[004-009]].

- **Observation and the limits of ethical demonstration**: Mill's empiricism entails a frank acknowledgment of the limits of ethical knowledge. Because the phenomena of desire, will, and moral motivation are "complex" and resist precise experimental control, observation in ethics yields probabilistic conclusions rather than demonstrative certainty. Mill does not claim that the utilitarian principle can be proved in the manner of a mathematical theorem; he claims only that "the considerations which have now been adduced" — grounded in observation — are sufficient to warrant rational acceptance of the principle [[004-009]].
