## Definition

In Mill's *Utilitarianism*, **abstraction** denotes the mental operation of isolating a concept from its concrete instances. Mill uses the term in two distinct but related senses: critically, to describe the error of treating moral principles as detached from experience; and constructively, to designate the highest standard of utilitarian morality, which must be "abstract" enough to serve as a universal criterion while remaining empirically grounded [[003-010], [005-039]].

## Key Arguments

- **Voluntary abstraction and moral impartiality**: Mill argues that the capacity for impartial moral judgment requires an "effort of voluntary abstraction," by which the agent conceives of their own interests as no more important than those of any other person. This abstraction is not a flight from reality but a disciplined cognitive act that enables the universal standpoint characteristic of utilitarian reasoning [[003-010]].
- **Abstract doctrines versus concrete application**: Mill distinguishes between the "abstract doctrines of morality," which philosophy must supply, and the "concrete" application of those doctrines in particular cases. He insists that while the ultimate standard — the principle of utility — is abstract, it is not an "abstract idea" in the pejorative sense, but a "concrete whole" composed of particular pleasures and pains [[001-004], [004-006]].
- **The highest abstract standard**: In the final chapter, Mill identifies the principle of utility as "the highest abstract standard of social and distributive justice." This abstractness is not a defect; it is what enables the standard to adjudicate conflicts between more specific maxims of justice [[005-039]].

## Related Concepts

- [[utility]] — the "highest abstract standard" that Mill defends as the foundational principle of ethics
- [[principle]] — the general moral rules that occupy an intermediate position between abstract utility and concrete action
- [[justice]] — the domain in which abstract standards and concrete rules of right most frequently conflict
- [[logic]] — the art of reasoning from abstract principles to concrete conclusions, essential to applied ethics
