# Value

In Mill's utilitarian framework, **value** operates on two registers: the *hedonic* value of pleasures, ranked qualitatively by competent judges, and the *moral* value of rights and obligations, grounded in general utility. The concept bridges Mill's qualitative hedonism and his theory of justice.

## The Qualitative Distinction of Value

Mill's central innovation in hedonistic theory is the claim that pleasures differ not merely in quantity but in *value* — a qualitative ranking that competent judges can reliably discern. "Human beings have faculties more elevated than the animal appetites, and, when once made conscious of them, do not regard anything as happiness which does not include their gratification" [002-005]. The higher pleasures — those of intellect, moral sentiment, and imagination — possess a value that no quantity of lower pleasure can outweigh.

## Personal Affection and Enduring Value

Mill draws a contrast between the transient value of selfish interests and the enduring value of personal attachments. Selfish interests "dwindle in value as the time approaches when all selfish interests must be terminated by death" [002-016]. In contrast, those who cultivate objects of personal affection and fellow-feeling with mankind retain value that survives the contemplation of mortality. This distinction reinforces the utilitarian case for extending concern beyond the self.

## Value and Rights

On Mill's account, a right is something "which society ought to defend me in the possession of" [005-024]. When asked why society ought to do so, Mill answers: "I can give him no other reason than general utility." The value of a right is thus instrumental, grounded in its contribution to the general good — but this instrumental grounding does not diminish the stringency of the obligation to respect it.

## Equal Value and Justice

Mill applies the concept of equal value to questions of distributive justice. In discussing taxation, he notes that certain principles of justice invoke the claim that all persons are "of equal value to all" [005-033]. This commitment to equal consideration — each person counting for one, none for more than one — is the distributive corollary of the utilitarian standard.
