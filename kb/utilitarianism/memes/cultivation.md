## Definition

**Cultivation**, in Mill's utilitarian philosophy, refers to the deliberate development of character, intellect, and moral sensibility through education, habit, and social influence. Mill uses the term to denote both the "general cultivation of nobleness of character" [002-012] as a social ideal, and the process by which individuals acquire the dispositions necessary for utilitarian moral life — including the "cultivation in ourselves of a sensitive feeling" for the good of others [002-031].

## Key Arguments

- **Cultivation and the quality of pleasure**: Mill argues that a "cultivated mind" is capable of experiencing higher pleasures inaccessible to the uncultivated. "A cultivated mind — I mean a mind that has knowledge and experience — finds sources of interest wherever it turns" [002-016]. This connects cultivation to Mill's qualitative hedonism: cultivation determines not just the quantity but the *kind* of pleasures one can enjoy.

- **Cultivation of nobleness**: Mill advocates for "the general cultivation of nobleness of character" [002-012] as an end in itself, while acknowledging it is also a means to the greatest happiness. The utilitarian standard "can only attain its end by the general cultivation of nobleness of character" — noble individuals contribute more to general happiness and find satisfaction in doing so.

- **Cultivation and the moral sanctions**: Mill identifies cultivation as central to the formation of conscience. Through "education and general cultivation," individuals are "bent to the purpose" of moral conduct [003-004]. The internal sanction of duty is not innate but developed through cultivation of the social feelings.

- **Cultivation to a high degree**: Mill holds that the social feelings are "susceptible of being brought by cultivation to a high degree of development" [003-009], contradicting the view that human nature is fixed. This optimism about moral progress through cultivation underlies Mill's reformist politics.

- **Cultivation of sensitivity**: Mill emphasizes "the cultivation in ourselves of a sensitive feeling" for the interests of others as essential to utilitarian morality. Without cultivated sympathy, the principle of utility would lack motivational force.
