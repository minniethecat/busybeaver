## Overview

Mr. Honyman is the Tappitt family's attorney in Baslehurst, serving as a legal adviser to Mr. Tappitt during the dispute over the brewery partnership with Luke Rowan. His role bridges the personal and commercial dimensions of the novel's central conflict, translating the Tappitts' domestic grievances into legal terms while also representing the interests of Rowan as the late Mr. Bungall's heir.

## Biography

Mr. Honyman maintains a legal practice in Baslehurst with a "little back room" [022-006] where he receives clients. He is consulted by Mrs. Tappitt, who brings her marital grievances into his office, and later serves as an intermediary between Tappitt and Rowan during the brewery negotiations.

Honyman's advice consistently favours settlement: he tells Mrs. Tappitt that her husband "should give" regarding the business arrangement [022-043], and later communicates Rowan's position to Tappitt. His clerk is noted for revelling "in thinly-covered" hints about the brewery transfer to Rowan [030-002], suggesting the legal office becomes a conduit for town gossip.

When Rowan presses for a final resolution, Honyman conveys to Tappitt that Rowan is prepared to proceed with the legal transfer, noting that Tappitt is "quite in time" to respond [028-017].

## Relationships

### Mr. Tappitt

Honyman serves as Tappitt's legal counsel, advising him on the terms of the brewery partnership dissolution. Their relationship is professional, though Honyman appears to maintain independence of judgment—he does not simply echo Tappitt's preferences but advises according to the legal merits of Rowan's claim.

### Mrs. Tappitt

Mrs. Tappitt independently seeks Honyman's counsel, bringing "the story of her wrongs" to his office [022-026]. She quotes him to her daughters as an authority, asking "do you know what Mr. Honyman says about it?" [022-042]. Honyman receives her diplomatically but does not endorse her more emotional positions.

### Luke Rowan

As the late Mr. Bungall's representative, Rowan instructs Honyman to proceed with the legal formalities of the brewery transfer. Honyman acts on Rowan's instructions [028-003] while maintaining his existing relationship with Tappitt, embodying the professional lawyer's duty to serve conflicting parties with impartiality.

## Key Scenes

- **Mrs. Tappitt's Consultation** (Chapter 22) — Mrs. Tappitt brings her grievances to Honyman's back room [022-006], seeking validation for her position against Rowan.
- **Advice to the Tappitt Daughters** (Chapter 22) — Honyman's opinion that Tappitt "should give" becomes a talking point within the Tappitt household [022-043].
- **Conveying Rowan's Position** (Chapter 28) — Honyman informs Mrs. Tappitt of Rowan's instructions, describing the proposed arrangement as "a great blessing" [028-005].
- **The Final Message to Tappitt** (Chapter 28) — Honyman delivers Rowan's ultimatum: Tappitt is "quite in time" to accept the terms [028-017].

## Character Analysis

Mr. Honyman functions as the novel's figure of professional legal reason amid the emotionally charged brewery dispute. Unlike the Tappitts, who treat the conflict as a personal affront, Honyman frames it in terms of legal rights and obligations—the late Mr. Bungall's will, Rowan's inheritance, and Tappitt's partnership terms. His modest "little back room" [022-006] suggests a small-town solicitor of limited ambition but competent practice, and his willingness to serve both sides of the dispute reflects Trollope's interest in the lawyer as an agent of orderly settlement rather than adversarial combat.
