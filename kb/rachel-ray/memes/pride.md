# Pride

## Overview

Pride in Anthony Trollope's *Rachel Ray* is a moral Rorschach test—the same quality that sustains a character's dignity in one context becomes destructive vanity in another. Luke Rowan asserts "his pride, the truest pride, that he was a gentleman"(011-028); Rachel Ray's "female pride"(018-003) protects her from appearing desperate; Mr. Tappitt's "pride in his anger"(010-066) blinds him to reasonable accommodation. Trollope, steeped in the Christian ethical tradition that ranked pride first among the deadly sins yet admired proper self-respect, refuses to resolve the tension. Instead, he traces pride through the novel's social and romantic conflicts, distinguishing between the pride that sustains integrity and the pride that destroys relationships.

## Context

The Victorian period inherited a complex moral vocabulary around pride. Christian teaching condemned it as the original sin—the pride of Lucifer, the root of all vice—while secular culture increasingly valorized self-reliance, ambition, and what Samuel Smiles called "self-help." The gentlemanly ideal incorporated both strands: a gentleman was expected to display proper pride in his name and honor while avoiding the vulgarity of boasting. For women, the calculus was different—pride was permissible as "modesty" or "self-respect" but condemned as "vanity" or "forwardness" when it exceeded narrow bounds.

## In the Novel

### Pride and Gentility

Luke Rowan's self-conception is built on pride: "he had once declared to himself his pride, the truest pride, that he was a gentleman"(011-028). The narrator's qualifier—"perhaps not the truest pride"—introduces doubt about whether pride can ever be wholly "true." Rowan's pride sustains him in his conflict with Tappitt and his courtship of Rachel, but it also threatens to become the "conceited, prone to think that his own opinion was better than the opinion of any other person"(004-053) quality that the narrator attributes to young men of his type.

### Feminine Pride

Rachel Ray's pride is of a different character—defensive rather than assertive. "Her pride had supported her"(020-002) during the period of estrangement from Luke, and "her female pride told her that it was well for her to show herself superior to any such jealousy"(018-003). This feminine pride is performative: it must be displayed without appearing to be displayed, a paradox that Trollope explores with sympathetic irony.

### Pride as Obstacle

Mr. Tappitt's "pride in his anger"(010-066) exemplifies pride's destructive potential. Unable to accept Luke Rowan as a partner in the brewery, Tappitt's wounded pride escalates a business dispute into a personal feud that threatens his family's prosperity. The novel suggests that pride becomes sinful not when it asserts worth but when it refuses reconciliation.

## Key Passages

> "He had once declared to himself his pride, the truest pride, that he was a gentleman."(011-028)

The narrator's layered qualifiers—"once," "to himself," "perhaps not the truest"—frame pride as inherently self-deceiving, a quality one claims rather than possesses.

> "Her pride had supported her, and had been sufficiently strong to carry her through the misery of that day."(020-002)

Rachel's pride is presented here as a survival mechanism—not vanity but emotional armor that enables her to endure romantic disappointment with dignity.

> "Full of energy and hope and pride. But he was conceited, prone to think that his own opinion was better."(004-053)

The semicolon that separates "pride" from "conceited" marks the boundary Trollope is most interested in—the invisible line where proper self-regard becomes improper self-regard.

> "Mr. Tappitt felt a pride in his anger, and was almost happy in the energy of his resentment."(010-066)

Tappitt's "pride in his anger" is the novel's most vivid portrait of pride as self-destructive pleasure—the brewer enjoying his own rage as a form of self-validation.

## Related Characters

- **Luke Rowan** — The young brewer whose "truest pride" as a gentleman both sustains and complicates his courtship
- **Rachel Ray** — The heroine whose feminine pride protects her dignity in romantic uncertainty
- **Mr. Tappitt** — The established brewer whose wounded pride drives the novel's business conflict
- **Mrs. Prime (Dorothea)** — The religious sister whose spiritual pride masks worldly resentment
- **Mr. Comfort** — The vicar whose professional pride is tempered by genuine good nature
