# Mr. Comfort

## Overview

Mr. Comfort is one of Trollope's most characteristic clerical creations: a man of the church who is also thoroughly a man of the world. As vicar of Baslehurst, he ministers to the spiritual needs of his parish; as a trained lawyer, he understands the worldly mechanisms of property and marriage settlements. This dual expertise makes him the novel's natural mediator—he is called upon to counsel Mrs. Ray about Rachel's courtship, to advise on the brewery dispute, and to represent the community's preference for peace over litigation. His Christianity is of the moderate, pragmatic sort that Trollope clearly prefers to both the severity of Evangelicalism and the laxity of mere social conformity.

## Biography

### The Lawyer-Clergyman

Mr. Comfort's unusual combination of vocations—"he had been a lawyer before he took orders"—makes him uniquely suited to the entangled problems of Baslehurst. He understands that marriage is both a sacrament and a contract, that the brewery is both a commercial enterprise and a community institution. His advice is always toward accommodation rather than confrontation, reflecting a theology that sees human happiness as a legitimate spiritual good.

### Counsel to Mrs. Ray

When Dorothea's opposition threatens to crush Rachel's hopes, Mr. Comfort provides the theological counterweight. He assures Mrs. Ray that marriage is a good thing, that young people should marry, and that maternal anxiety should not be mistaken for spiritual discernment. His advice is "asked after Luke Rowan's worldly means"(005-042)—practical as well as pastoral.

### The Brewery Dispute

In the Tappitt-Rowan conflict, Mr. Comfort advocates settlement. He represents the community's interest in a functioning brewery and peaceful relations, and his legal training allows him to speak authoritatively about the merits of Rowan's claim. His mediation is not entirely successful—the dispute runs its course—but his role establishes that the community expects reasonable compromise.

## Relationships

### Mrs. Ray
Mr. Comfort is the spiritual authority Mrs. Ray can trust precisely because he does not demand unreasonable spiritual rigor. His gentleness with her timidity is a pastoral kindness.

### Rachel Ray
Though his direct interactions with Rachel are minimal, his counsel to her mother is decisive for her fate.

### Mr. Tappitt and Luke Rowan
As mediator, Mr. Comfort occupies the middle ground in the brewery conflict, respected by both sides if fully trusted by neither.

### Mrs. Prime
The implicit contrast between Mr. Comfort's moderate Anglicanism and Mrs. Prime's severe Evangelicalism is one of the novel's theological arguments. Comfort's religion makes room for dancing; Mrs. Prime's does not.

## Key Scenes

- **Counsel to Mrs. Ray (Chapter V)** — Mr. Comfort advises on the propriety of Rachel's courtship
- **The Brewery Mediation (Chapters XIII, XVII)** — Attempts to broker peace between Tappitt and Rowan

## Character Analysis

Mr. Comfort embodies Trollope's ideal of the Anglican clergyman: learned without being pedantic, pious without being severe, worldly-wise without being cynical. His legal training is not incidental but essential to his character—it represents the integration of spiritual and practical wisdom that Trollope believed the Church of England at its best provided. In the novel's moral economy, Mr. Comfort is the reasonable center against which the extremes of Mrs. Prime's rigidity and the Tappitts' worldliness can be measured.
