## Overview

Mr. Griggs is a minor comic character who appears at the Tappitt ball as an inept suitor. Distinguished by his "singular arrangement of jewellery" [007-060], he pursues the younger female guests with an awkwardness that provides moments of social comedy and serves as a foil to the novel's more serious romantic plots.

## Biography

Mr. Griggs makes his sole significant appearance at the Tappitts' ball, where he immediately marks himself as a figure of fun. He "desires the honour" [007-046] of dancing with one of the young ladies and proceeds through the evening with a combination of eagerness and ineptitude that alienates his intended partners.

His jewellery attracts particular notice and mockery [007-060], and his dancing style is described in terms that suggest physical awkwardness. When he approaches a young woman, he does so "with a very low bow" [008-078] that fails to impress. By the end of the evening, his attentions are regarded as something to escape—one character refers to "that horrid Mr. Griggs" [008-131], and a young woman is congratulated on her "escape" from him [008-103].

## Relationships

### The Tappitt Ball Guests

Griggs functions primarily as a social foil: his awkwardness throws into relief the grace of the more eligible young men and the social competence of characters like Mr. Cornbury and Luke Rowan. His misadventures provide a comic subplot within the ball sequence.

### The Younger Women

Griggs directs his attentions toward the younger unmarried women at the ball, all of whom seek to evade him. His pursuit and their evasion create a pattern of comic chase that runs through the ball chapters.

## Key Scenes

- **The First Approach** (Chapter 7) — Griggs requests a dance, described as desiring "the honour of your hand" [007-046].
- **The Jewellery** (Chapter 7) — The narrator notes the "singular arrangement of Mr. Griggs' jewellery" [007-060], marking him as a figure of sartorial excess.
- **The Low Bow** (Chapter 8) — Griggs approaches with excessive formality [008-078] and stands "transfixed" [008-087] when his advance fails.
- **The Escape** (Chapter 8) — A young woman is congratulated on avoiding him: "What an escape you had of Mr. Griggs, my dear!" [008-103].

## Character Analysis

Mr. Griggs belongs to the tradition of the comic suitor in Victorian fiction—the man whose romantic aspirations far exceed his social competence. His jewellery, his bowing, and his persistence all mark him as a figure of mild satire, but Trollope handles him without cruelty. Griggs is ridiculous rather than villainous, and his function is to leaven the ball sequence with comedy while providing a contrast to the genuine romantic interest generated by characters like Luke Rowan. His brief appearance and rapid dismissal from the narrative suggest he exists purely for this contrastive purpose.
