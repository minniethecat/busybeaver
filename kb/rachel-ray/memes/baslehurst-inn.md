# Baslehurst Inn

## Overview

The Baslehurst inn is a space of masculine sociability and commercial conversation that provides the novel with a counterpoint to the domestic interiors of Bragg's End and the brewery house. In the Victorian novel, the inn is a traditional site of plotting, drinking, and informal negotiation—a place where men of different classes can meet on terms of temporary equality. Luke Rowan's presence at the inn signals his integration into Baslehurst's masculine commercial world, and conversations held there advance the novel's business subplot.

## In the Novel

### Commercial Sociability

The inn serves beer, of course—and in a town whose economy is built on brewing, the quality of the beer served at the inn is a matter of commercial as much as personal interest. Conversations about the brewery, the partnership, and the Tappitt-Rowan dispute find a natural home in the inn's public rooms.

### Class and Gender

Like all Victorian inns, this one is implicitly male space. Rachel Ray would never enter it; Mrs. Tappitt would never be seen there. The inn's exclusion of women is part of the novel's mapping of gendered space: the cottage, the brewery house, and the ballroom are female domains (however contested); the inn, the counting-house, and the brewery itself are male territory.
