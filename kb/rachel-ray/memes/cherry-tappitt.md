# Cherry Tappitt

## Overview

Cherry Tappitt is the youngest of the brewer's three daughters and the one who most clearly possesses an independent moral sense. It is Cherry who first predicts that Luke Rowan "would fall in love with Rachel Ray directly he saw her"(003-011), and it is Cherry who "was very brave, and still stood up for him before her mother and sisters"(017-007) when the family feud has driven everyone else to hostility. In a novel that anatomizes the varieties of female character—the weak, the severe, the scheming, the kindly—Cherry represents the quiet virtue of loyalty, and her independence from her mother's machinations makes her one of the novel's most sympathetic minor figures.

## Biography

### The Youngest Daughter

As the youngest of three sisters, Cherry occupies a position of relative freedom within the Tappitt household. Her mother's ambitions are focused on Augusta, the tallest and therefore "the beauty"(003-011); Cherry is left to develop her own judgment without the pressure of being the family's primary matrimonial asset.

### The Rowan Introduction

It is Cherry who makes the introductions that set the plot in motion. She promises to introduce Luke to "the beauties of the neighbourhood"(003-011) and specifically to Rachel Ray. Her open-heartedness is the catalyst for the central romance, and the fact that she never regrets this—even when her family turns hostile—speaks to her integrity.

### Loyal Opposition

When the brewery feud escalates, Cherry alone maintains her allegiance to Rowan. "She was very brave, and still stood up for him before her mother and sisters;—but even Cherry did not dare to say a word in his favour before her father"(017-007). This paragraph is Cherry's finest moment: brave enough to defy her mother and sisters, prudent enough not to provoke her enraged father. It is a portrait of moral courage operating within realistic limits.

## Relationships

### Luke Rowan
Cherry's loyalty to Luke is personal rather than romantic—she introduced him to Rachel, she wishes them well, and she refuses to join the family campaign against him. Her friendship represents the possibility of goodwill across factional lines.

### Rachel Ray
Cherry's matchmaking—if her good-natured prediction can be called that—changes Rachel's life. She bears Rachel no resentment for succeeding where the Tappitt sisters (in their mother's eyes) have failed.

### Mrs. Tappitt
The relationship between Cherry and her mother is one of quiet resistance. Cherry does not openly defy Mrs. Tappitt, but she consistently refuses to adopt her mother's hostilities, maintaining a private moral space within the family.

### Augusta and Martha Tappitt
Cherry's older sisters share their mother's resentment of Rachel; Cherry's loyalty to her own judgment isolates her within the family circle.

## Key Scenes

- **The Prediction (Chapter III)** — Cherry declares her conviction that Luke will fall in love with Rachel Ray
- **Standing Alone (Chapter XVII)** — Cherry "was very brave, and still stood up for him"(017-007) against the family consensus

## Character Analysis

Cherry Tappitt is a minor character who embodies one of the novel's central moral values: the courage to maintain personal loyalty in the face of collective hostility. She is not a rebel—she does not openly defy her father or publicly break with her mother—but within the limits of her position as the youngest daughter of a provincial brewer, she does what she can. Trollope's portrait of Cherry is an argument that moral independence need not be dramatic to be real.
