# Mr. Sturt

## Overview

Mr. Sturt is the tenant farmer whose homestead stands at the edge of Bragg's End green, immediately adjacent to Mrs. Ray's cottage. He appears in the novel only through brief report and neighbourly exchange, yet his farm forms the physical and social anchor of the little community in which Rachel Ray grows up. When Rachel's happiness with Luke Rowan hangs in the balance, it is Mr. Sturt's quiet approbation—"Mr. Sturt had thought it very right"(021-032)—that speaks for the common-sense moral judgment of the rural world Trollope so carefully constructs.

## Biography

### The Bragg's End Tenant

Mr. Sturt occupies the "house and homestead of Farmer Sturt"(001-011) that stands back from the green at Bragg's End, close upon the wooden bridge over the little stream. He is a tenant on the Cornbury estate, as Rachel later learns: "Mr. Sturt was a tenant on the Cornbury estate"(023-005). His farmyard sits directly opposite Mrs. Ray's cottage, so close that Rachel "could see over into the gloom of Mr. Sturt's farmyard across it"(020-060).

### Neighbour to the Rays

The Sturt household is woven into the daily fabric of Mrs. Ray's life. She frequently crosses the green to visit Mrs. Sturt—for "a little thick cream"(002-018), for conversation, for refuge when she wishes to be "out of the way"(019-004). The farm kitchen becomes a second domestic space for the Bragg's End women.

### A Judgement of Approval

Mr. Sturt's most significant contribution to the novel's moral architecture comes in a single reported sentence. When the question of Rachel's attachment to Luke Rowan is under discussion, "Mr. Sturt had thought it very right"(021-032). Coming from a man of steady rural character, this endorsement carries weight. It represents the judgment of the honest, unpretentious working world—a counterweight to the snobbery Rachel encounters elsewhere.

### The Evening News

On the evening when news of Rachel's situation reaches Bragg's End, it is Mr. Sturt's voice that sounds across the green, first heard "by the voice of Mr. Sturt on the same evening; and Mrs. Ray"(025-003) receives the tidings. He is, in this small way, a bearer of community news—part of the informal network that binds Bragg's End together.

## Relationships

### Mrs. Sturt
The Sturts appear as a pair, their farmhouse a centre of neighbourly warmth. Mrs. Ray's regular visits to Mrs. Sturt suggest a household of kindness and practical hospitality.

### Mrs. Ray
As the widow's nearest neighbour, Mr. Sturt forms part of the small protective circle around Bragg's End cottage. The easy access between the two households provides Mrs. Ray with both practical help (cream, refuge) and social connection.

### Rachel Ray
Rachel's childhood has been lived in the shadow of the Sturt farm. When she looks across from her window, the farmyard is her horizon; when she needs to absent herself, Mrs. Sturt's kitchen is her destination. Mr. Sturt's approval of her attachment to Luke Rowan is a small but significant vote of confidence from the world she knows best.

## Key Scenes

- **The Farm at Bragg's End (Chapter I)** — The "house and homestead of Farmer Sturt"(001-011) is established as the physical anchor of the green
- **A Vote of Confidence (Chapter XXI)** — "Mr. Sturt had thought it very right"(021-032) that Rachel should love Luke Rowan
- **News Across the Green (Chapter XXV)** — Mr. Sturt's voice brings the evening's news to Mrs. Ray

## Character Analysis

Mr. Sturt is scarcely more than a name in the novel, yet his presence serves an important structural purpose. He represents the stable, unpretentious rural world—the world of tenant farmers and neighbourly goodwill—against which the novel's dramas of social climbing, romantic intrigue, and religious severity are measured. His single reported judgment ("thought it very right") functions as a moral benchmark: if the honest farmer approves, the reader is encouraged to approve as well. Trollope, who always valued the virtues of the English countryside, places Mr. Sturt at the geographical and moral centre of Bragg's End.
