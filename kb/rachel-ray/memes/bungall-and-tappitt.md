# Bungall & Tappitt

## Overview

Bungall & Tappitt is the brewing firm at the commercial center of Baslehurst and the business whose disputed succession provides the novel's secondary plot. Founded as a partnership between the now-deceased Mr. Bungall and the living Mr. Tappitt, the firm passes into controversy when Bungall's will leaves a share to his great-nephew Luke Rowan. "He had left away from Mr. Tappitt all that he could leave"(003-009)—the narrator's precise legal language captures the core dispute. Bungall & Tappitt is more than a business; it is the economic foundation of the Tappitt family's social position and the arena in which generational and commercial values clash.

## In the Novel

### The Founding and the Will

Old Mr. Bungall built the firm in partnership with Tappitt. Upon Bungall's death, his will directed that a share of the business pass to Luke Rowan—"not, however, in this way that he had left away from Mr. Tappitt all that he could leave"(003-009). The will's legal language governs the dispute, but its spirit is clear: Bungall intended Rowan to have a stake.

### The Partnership Dispute

Mr. Tappitt has managed the firm as sole proprietor since Bungall's death and regards Rowan's claim as an outrage—"Luke Rowan should never be partner of his"(003-010). The dispute is fought through lawyer's letters, the reluctant erection of a joint name-board, confrontations, and ultimately the poker incident. The firm's governance is at stake: will it remain a personal domain or become a modern partnership?

### Resolution

The firm's future is resolved by Rowan's eventual triumph—not through litigation but through Tappitt's reluctant retirement. The name Bungall & Tappitt presumably continues, but with Rowan as the active partner, representing a new generation of commercial professionalism.
