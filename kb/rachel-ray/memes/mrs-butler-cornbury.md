# Mrs. Butler Cornbury

## Overview

Mrs. Butler Cornbury is the nearest thing to a fairy godmother that Trollope's realist universe permits. The wife of the local squire and the social apex of Baslehurst society, she takes up Rachel Ray with an impulsive generosity that transforms the younger woman's fortunes. Her championship at Mrs. Tappitt's ball—where "she talked her young friend up to the top of the tree"(008-002)—is the novel's most decisive act of social intervention. Unlike the scheming Mrs. Tappitt or the severe Mrs. Prime, Mrs. Cornbury acts from genuine kindness without ulterior motive, embodying Trollope's conviction that the landed gentry have a moral function in provincial life: to recognize and protect merit regardless of social origin.

## Biography

### The Cornbury Connection

Mrs. Butler Cornbury enters the narrative as a friend of the Tappitt family through her acquaintance with Augusta. However, "Mrs. Cornbury would not like to meet Rachel Ray; but she is going to bring her to the ball"(006-036)—the phrasing reveals Mrs. Cornbury's independence: she is willing to act outside the expectations of the Tappitt circle when her own judgment prompts her.

### The Ball

At Mrs. Tappitt's ball, Mrs. Cornbury's patronage is decisive. She "began the work of chaperon and carried it on heartily"(008-002), lending Rachel the social legitimacy that neutralizes Mrs. Tappitt's hostility. Her approval signals to all of Baslehurst that Rachel Ray is a young woman worth knowing—a judgment the narrative itself endorses.

### Later Appearances

Mrs. Cornbury continues to appear as a benevolent background presence, her initial act of patronage having set the social conditions for Rachel's eventual happiness. She represents the positive potential of the country-house system: a landed class that uses its influence not to exclude but to welcome.

## Relationships

### Rachel Ray
Mrs. Cornbury's relationship with Rachel is one of pure patronage—she recognizes Rachel's quality and decides to "talk her young friend up"(008-002) without any expectation of return. This unsolicited kindness is the novel's clearest demonstration that social power can be used benevolently.

### Mrs. Tappitt
The implicit contrast between the two matriarchs—Mrs. Cornbury's generous use of influence versus Mrs. Tappitt's scheming—is one of the novel's quiet moral arguments.

### Walter Cornbury
Her husband, the squire, provides the institutional weight behind her social influence, though he remains largely offstage.

## Key Scenes

- **The Ball (Chapter VIII)** — Mrs. Cornbury's "powerful influence"(008-002) transforms Rachel's social standing in a single evening

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Butler Cornbury is a minor character with a major structural function. In a novel about the difficulty of crossing social boundaries—between the brewery and the cottage, the Evangelical and the worldly, the poor and the comfortable—Mrs. Cornbury represents the possibility of benevolent social mobility. Her patronage of Rachel is not an act of condescension but of recognition: she sees that Rachel deserves better than the Tappitts' gossip, and she has the power to make it so. Trollope, who believed in the moral potential of the landed gentry, gives Mrs. Cornbury the role of validating the novel's heroine to the world.
