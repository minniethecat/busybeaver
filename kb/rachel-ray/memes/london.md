# London

## Overview

London is the great offstage presence in *Rachel Ray*—never directly depicted, but constantly invoked as the source of Luke Rowan's manners, his mother's worldly wisdom, and the metropolitan standard against which Baslehurst's provincialism is measured. Luke comes from London; his mother and sister still live there; and the possibility that Rachel might one day go there as Luke's wife is one of the novel's unspoken horizons. For Trollope, who divided his own life between London literary society and rural retreat, the relationship between metropolis and province was a lifelong preoccupation.

## In the Novel

### The Metropolitan Standard

Luke Rowan's London origin is part of his appeal and his alienness. He brings to Baslehurst assumptions about commercial practice and personal conduct that differ from the town's settled ways. His insistence on good beer instead of bad, his directness in courtship, and his willingness to assert his legal rights all mark him as a metropolitan rather than a provincial type.

### Mrs. Rowan's World

Luke's mother remains in London, and her opposition to his marriage to Rachel—"do not speak against her if you can say nothing worse of her than that she is poor"(013-038)—is the voice of metropolitan prudence. London, in this formulation, is where mothers worry about their sons' financial futures; Baslehurst is where sons fall in love regardless.

### An Unseen Horizon

Rachel never visits London in the course of the novel, but her marriage to Luke implies a possible future there. The city represents the larger life that marriage opens up—a life beyond the cottage, beyond Baslehurst, beyond the known world of Devonshire.
