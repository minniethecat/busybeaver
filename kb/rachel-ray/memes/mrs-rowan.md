# Mrs. Rowan

## Overview

Mrs. Rowan is the voice of worldly prudence in the novel's matrimonial negotiations. As Luke's mother, she has his interests at heart; as a London widow of limited means, she knows the importance of a financially advantageous marriage. Her opposition to Luke's attachment to Rachel Ray—"she would say nothing worse of her than that she is poor"(013-038)—is perhaps the most reasonable objection raised against the match, and it is precisely its reasonableness that makes it a genuine test of Luke's commitment. Trollope treats Mrs. Rowan with the respect due to a mother's concern, even as the novel's momentum favors romantic choice over financial calculation.

## Biography

### The London Widow

Mrs. Rowan lives in London, far from Baslehurst, and her perspective is shaped by metropolitan assumptions about marriage and money. She has raised Luke and Mary without a husband's support, and her financial realism is hard-won. When she learns that Luke intends to marry a portionless Devonshire girl, her maternal alarm is understandable.

### The Test of Luke's Resolve

Luke's response to his mother's objections—"Rachel Ray I do intend to marry—if she will have me"(013-038)—is the novel's clearest statement of his commitment. Mrs. Rowan's opposition serves the narrative function of testing that commitment against the most sympathetic form of resistance: a mother's practical concern for her son's welfare.

## Relationships

### Luke Rowan
The central relationship. Mrs. Rowan's maternal concern and Luke's romantic determination create a tension that is resolved by Luke's firmness and, ultimately, by the novel's endorsement of love over calculation.

### Rachel Ray
Mrs. Rowan never meets Rachel in the novel's primary action, but her judgment—that Rachel is "poor" and therefore unsuitable—hangs over the courtship as a reminder that love must contend with the world's practical wisdom.

### Mary Rowan
Mrs. Rowan's daughter, who accompanies Luke to Baslehurst and provides a more sympathetic perspective on her brother's choice.

## Key Scenes

- **The Maternal Interview (Chapter XIII)** — Luke declares his intentions to his mother
- **The Ball (Chapter VII)** — Mary Rowan attends in her mother's place, bridging London and Baslehurst

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Rowan is a minor character whose function is to give the novel's romantic plot a credible antagonist. She is not a villain—her concern for Luke's financial future is legitimate—but the novel asks us to see that her prudence, however reasonable, is insufficient as a guide to life. The triumph of Luke's love over his mother's objections is not a triumph over wickedness but over the kind of worldly wisdom that Trollope himself, as a practical man of affairs, understood and respected.
