## Overview

Mr. Sharpit is an attorney who becomes involved in the brewery dispute between Mr. Tappitt and Luke Rowan. Operating from the Dragon Inn rather than a respectable office, he represents a less reputable strain of provincial legal practice—a "dangerous companion" [027-075] whose methods unsettle even his client.

## Biography

Mr. Sharpit first appears when Mr. Tappitt, seeking to strengthen his legal position against Rowan, arranges to meet him at the Dragon Inn [027-005]. Sharpit operates from the pub rather than from chambers in the Close, signalling his position on the margins of respectable Baslehurst legal practice.

At the Dragon, Sharpit brings together various parties "in order that they might be instructed" [027-061] in the facts of the case. Over gin and bitters, he and Tappitt discuss strategy [027-066], but Tappitt finds himself "not altogether pleased by Mr. Sharpit's manner" [027-068]. An idea crosses Tappitt's mind that Sharpit is "a very dangerous companion" [027-075], suggesting that the attorney's approach—aggressive, perhaps unscrupulous—exceeds what Tappitt is comfortable with.

Sharpit's function in the narrative is brief but significant: he represents the temptation of sharp practice, the lure of winning by any means, which Tappitt ultimately recoils from. In this sense, Sharpit serves as a moral test that Tappitt, whatever his other failings, manages to pass.

## Relationships

### Mr. Tappitt

Sharpit is engaged by Tappitt as legal counsel but the relationship quickly sours. Tappitt is discomfited by Sharpit's manner and methods, and the association ends with Tappitt recognizing the danger of the attorney's approach [027-075]. The episode suggests that Tappitt, for all his stubbornness in the brewery dispute, retains a core of respectability that recoils from outright sharp practice.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Sharpit belongs to a recognizable Victorian type: the marginally respectable attorney who operates from the pub rather than the chambers, offering his clients the promise of victory through methods that hover on the edge of propriety. His name—Sharpit—is one of Trollope's more transparently allegorical Choices, signalling the sharp practice he represents. His brief appearance in the novel serves a double function: he provides local colour in the legal-commercial subplot, and he acts as a moral foil, allowing Tappitt to demonstrate that even a stubborn and self-interested man may draw a line between legitimate self-assertion and questionable practice.
