# The Wedding of Rachel Ray and Luke Rowan

## Overview

The wedding of Rachel Ray and Luke Rowan is the novel's implied conclusion—an event that the entire narrative has pointed toward but that Trollope, with characteristic restraint, does not directly depict. Victorian novels typically end with weddings, but Trollope's endings are often notably brisk: the marriage is announced, the characters are disposed of, and the narrator withdraws. The wedding exists as a narrative fact rather than a narrated scene—a choice that reflects Trollope's conviction that what matters is not the ceremony but the lives that precede and follow it.

## In the Novel

### The Unwritten Scene

The wedding is not depicted. The novel moves from the reconciliation directly to closure, with the marriage treated as an accomplished fact rather than a dramatic event. This absence is significant: Trollope's interest is in the process of courtship (with all its complications of family, money, religion, and gossip) rather than in its ceremonial conclusion.

### The Implied Future

The wedding points forward to a life beyond the novel's boundaries. Rachel will leave Bragg's End—the cottage that has contained her—and enter a new world as Luke's wife. The implied future is one of moderate happiness: not the ecstasy of romance but the steady contentment of a well-made match between two decent young people.
