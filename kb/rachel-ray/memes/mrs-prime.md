# Mrs. Prime

## Overview

Mrs. Prime—Dorothea—is the most formidable presence in the Bragg's End household, and arguably the most complex religious character in the novel. "Black, and stiff, and stern, in widow's weeds"(001-005), she has returned to her mother's cottage after the death of her husband, bringing with her both a widow's portion that has improved the family's material circumstances and an Evangelical severity that has transformed its spiritual atmosphere. She is "nothing hypocritical"(001-007)—her faith is genuine—but her certainty about the line between righteousness and worldliness makes her an unintentional tyrant. Trollope's treatment of Mrs. Prime is notably fair: she is misguided but never malicious, and her religious seriousness, however oppressive, reflects a real moral commitment.

## Biography

### Marriage and Widowhood

Dorothea married a man older than herself, a marriage that brought financial security if not romantic fulfillment. Her widowhood came early, and she returned to Bragg's End "in widow's weeds"(001-005), the visual emblem of her permanent identification with grief and renunciation. Her widow's portion brought "accession of wealth to the family"(001-010), giving her a material authority within the household that reinforces her spiritual authority.

### Evangelical Piety

Mrs. Prime's religion is of the rigorous Evangelical variety that dominated much of mid-Victorian English Protestantism. She regards the world as a snare, pleasure as temptation, and the sermons of Mr. Prong as the proper spiritual diet for the household. "There was nothing hypocritical about Mrs. Prime, nor did she make any attempt to appear to be better than she was"(001-007)—but her conviction that she knows what is right leaves no room for others' judgment.

### Conflict with Rachel

The novel's central domestic conflict pits Mrs. Prime against her younger sister. She sees Rachel's natural impulses—evening walks, dancing, love of Luke Rowan—as backsliding toward worldliness, and "would foretell hard things for her sister, denouncing her as fashioning herself wilfully in the world's ways"(001-014). Her opposition to Luke Rowan on spiritual grounds is sincere, but the reader is led to question whether it is also tinged with a less admirable impulse: the determination of the strong will to dominate the weak.

### The Mr. Prong Subplot

Mrs. Prime's religious seriousness attracts the attention of Mr. Prong, the curate whose views align with her own. His interest in her—spiritual and possibly marital—creates a subplot that tests Mrs. Prime's own consistency. Her eventual refusal of Mr. Prong suggests that beneath her severity lies a more complex understanding of herself and of human relationships than her religious pronouncements admit.

## Relationships

### Rachel Ray
The central relationship of the novel is the struggle between the two sisters. Mrs. Prime genuinely believes she is saving Rachel's soul; Rachel genuinely believes her sister is smothering her. Trollope's achievement is to make both perspectives intelligible without fully endorsing either.

### Mrs. Ray
Mrs. Prime's dominance over her mother is almost absolute. The mother "had allowed herself to be fastened"(001-007) to Dorothea's wall, and the metaphor of the clinging plant describes their relationship as much as it describes Mrs. Ray's general nature.

### Mr. Prong
The Evangelical curate who admires Mrs. Prime and whose sermons she admires. Their relationship, and Mrs. Prime's eventual rejection of it, reveals the limits of her religious ideology as a guide to lived experience.

### Luke Rowan
Mrs. Prime regards Luke as the embodiment of worldly temptation and opposes Rachel's attachment to him with all the force of her moral authority.

## Key Scenes

- **First Appearance (Chapter I)** — Mrs. Prime's introduction establishes her as the dominant force in the household from the opening pages
- **Confrontations with Rachel (Chapters I, V, XII)** — The sisters' arguments about worldliness, dancing, and Luke Rowan recur throughout the novel
- **The Mr. Prong Subplot (Chapters IX–XI)** — Mrs. Prime's own romantic possibility tests her principles against her feelings

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Prime is Trollope's most nuanced portrait of Evangelical piety in the novel. She is "a harder taskmaster"(001-006) than the Ten Commandments themselves, yet Trollope insists that "there was nothing hypocritical about"(001-007) her. Her tragedy—and it is a quiet tragedy—is that her genuine virtue is harnessed to a theology that leaves no room for ordinary human happiness. The novel does not ask us to condemn her, only to see that her certainty is itself a form of pride. Mrs. Prime's function in the novel is to represent the claim of religious duty in its most uncompromising form, against which Rachel's quieter claim of natural affection must make its way.
