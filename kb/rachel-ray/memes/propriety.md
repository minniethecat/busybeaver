# Propriety

## Overview

Propriety in *Rachel Ray* is the invisible architecture of social life in Baslehurst—a code of conduct that governs what may be said, done, and felt in public. The word and its antithesis, "impropriety," appear at every level of the narrative, from Mrs. Ray's unquestioning deference to clerical authority to the scandalized whispers that follow Rachel Ray's churchyard walks with Luke Rowan. Trollope treats propriety with characteristic ambivalence: it is both a necessary social lubricant and a potential prison, especially for women. The novel's central romantic plot is, in large measure, a negotiation between the demands of propriety and the claims of genuine feeling.

## Context

Victorian propriety was codified in an extensive literature of conduct manuals, sermons, and social commentary. For middle-class women especially, "propriety" encompassed dress, speech, reading matter, and above all, regulation of contact with unmarried men. A young woman walking unchaperoned with a gentleman, as Rachel does in the churchyard, was a breach serious enough to generate community-wide gossip. Trollope, who portrayed the clergy with insider knowledge and social convention with a reformer's eye, uses propriety as a lens through which to examine the gap between external compliance and internal integrity.

## In the Novel

### Propriety and the Clergy

Mrs. Ray's piety is inseparable from her adherence to propriety: "Mrs. Ray never questioned the propriety of her clergyman's life, nor did she doubt his doctrine"(001-009). For her, the clerical office guarantees proper conduct—a trust that the novel gently undermines through the contrast between Mr. Comfort's genuine decency and Mr. Prong's self-serving religiosity.

### Propriety and Courtship

The novel's central romantic crisis is framed as a question of propriety. When Rachel walks with Luke Rowan in the churchyard, the question immediately arises "as to the propriety or impropriety of the proceeding"(002-040). Rachel herself "had been guilty of no great impropriety"(003-016), but the appearance of impropriety is enough to unsettle her mother and outrage her sister. The Tappitt sisters police Rachel's conduct precisely because courtship propriety determines marriageability.

### Propriety and Self-Deception

"People will talk"(009-042), Luke Rowan warns, but the novel suggests that excessive concern with propriety can become a form of hypocrisy. Mrs. Prime exercises "the utmost rigour of self-denying propriety"(003-003) while nursing resentments. The narrator observes that "she hardly believed in any such propriety herself"(004-031)—propriety performed rather than felt becomes indistinguishable from falsehood.

## Key Passages

> "Mrs. Ray never questioned the propriety of her clergyman's life, nor did she doubt his doctrine."(001-009)

This sentence establishes propriety as an extension of religious faith—a trust in institutional authority that the novel proceeds to complicate.

> "A question would be raised as to the propriety or impropriety of the proceeding."(002-040)

The passive construction captures how propriety functions as a community judgment—an impersonal force that individuals must anticipate and accommodate.

> "She hardly believed in any such propriety herself, and she knew that he did not."(004-031)

The narrator's exposure of Mrs. Prime's inner skepticism about the very propriety she enforces is one of the novel's most psychologically acute moments.

> "She had been guilty of no great impropriety—unless, indeed, she had been guilty in allowing herself to think of Luke Rowan."(003-016)

The narrator's afterthought—the admission that even thinking of a man might constitute impropriety—captures the suffocating reach of Victorian social codes.

## Related Characters

- **Rachel Ray** — The heroine, whose churchyard walks test the boundaries of propriety
- **Mrs. Prime (Dorothea)** — The enforcer of propriety who "hardly believed in any such propriety herself"(004-031)
- **Mrs. Ray** — The mother whose instinctive propriety conflicts with maternal love
- **Luke Rowan** — The suitor who must navigate propriety's demands while asserting "a gentleman has his rights"(008-084)
- **Mrs. Tappitt** — The brewmistress who polices Rachel's conduct as a proxy for class anxiety
