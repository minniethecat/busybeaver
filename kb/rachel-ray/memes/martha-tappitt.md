## Overview

**Martha Tappitt** is the eldest of the [[three-tappitt-sisters|three Tappitt sisters]] of [[baslehurst|Baslehurst]], daughters of [[bungall-and-tappitt|Bungall and Tappitt]], the town's oldest-established brewery firm. Within the novel's moral architecture, Martha serves as the voice of pragmatic justice and domestic honesty — a counterweight to her younger sister Cherry's romantic impulsiveness and her father's blustering tyranny. She is characterised chiefly by the narrator's direct judgment that "of all living female human beings Martha is the most honest and just" [007-060], a verdict that positions her as a moral touchstone within the Tappitt household and the broader Baslehurst community.

## Biography

### Family Background and Position

Martha Tappitt is the eldest of three daughters of [[mr-tappitt|Thomas Tappitt]], the proprietor of the [[bungall-and-tappitt-brewery|Bungall and Tappitt brewery]] in Baslehurst. The Tappitt sisters — Martha, Cherry, and Augusta — are introduced early in the narrative as fixtures of Baslehurst society, their social standing tied to the brewery's commercial prominence and their father's long reign over it since old Mr. Bungall's death. Martha, "who was nearly thirty years of age" [003-033], occupies a transitional position: too old to be counted among the marriageable young ladies in the same manner as her younger sisters, yet still a participant in the town's social rituals.

### The Luke Rowan Affair

When [[luke-rowan|Luke Rowan]] arrives in Baslehurst claiming a partnership interest in the brewery through his late father's connection to old Mrs. Bungall, the Tappitt household is thrown into conflict. Martha's response to Rowan is notably ambivalent. Unlike Cherry, who is immediately captivated by Rowan's charms, Martha "shook her head" [006-029] at the prospect of social entanglement. Her "dislike to him — or rather, her distrust of him" [017-007] proves prescient: Martha senses earlier than her family that Rowan's presence threatens not merely the brewery's commercial stability but the Tappitt family's domestic peace.

Yet Martha is not inflexible. When evidence mounts in Rowan's favour — particularly through his courtship of [[rachel-ray|Rachel Ray]] — she acknowledges her misjudgment with characteristic honesty. She is the one who later admits the Tappitt sisters "had been very foolish" [020-048] in their treatment of Rachel, a concession that demonstrates her capacity for self-correction.

### Resolution

By the novel's close, Martha's role as the Tappitt family's moral compass is affirmed. Her "sense of justice" [005-018] carries the day in family deliberations, and her sagacious assessment of the lawsuit threat against Rowan — recognising it as bluster rather than genuine legal peril — proves correct. She remains at the brewery, her fate as the eldest unmarried daughter rendered with the quiet dignity that characterises all her appearances.

## Relationships

### Cherry Tappitt

Martha's relationship with her younger sister [[cherry-tappitt|Cherry]] is defined by the tension between Martha's seriousness and Cherry's eagerness. When Cherry's romantic enthusiasm threatens to overwhelm family judgment, it is Martha who restrains her: "Don't be so foolish," she tells Cherry during a pivotal encounter on the road [020-040]. Yet their bond is genuine; Martha's corrections are those of protective elder sisterhood, not rivalry.

### Mr. Tappitt

With her father [[mr-tappitt|Thomas Tappitt]], Martha maintains a relationship of respectful but clear-eyed honesty. She does not share Cherry's tendency to flatter their father's pretensions, nor does she openly defy him. Her approach is one of quiet influence — her "sense of justice" [005-018] operating as a stabilising force within the brewery household.

### Rachel Ray

Though Martha initially participates in the Tappitt family's social snubbing of [[rachel-ray|Rachel Ray]], she is the first to recognise the injustice of this treatment. Her acknowledgment that the family "had been very foolish" [020-048] about Rachel marks a turning point in the social dynamics of Baslehurst's younger set, and her eventual support for Rachel and Luke's union aligns with her broader commitment to fairness.

### Luke Rowan

Martha's attitude toward [[luke-rowan|Luke Rowan]] evolves from distrust to qualified acceptance. Her initial suspicion — that "it's more than the brewery business" [017-040] — proves perceptive: Rowan's interest extends beyond commerce to romance and social position. Martha's eventual recognition of Rowan's genuine feelings for Rachel Ray allows her to reconcile her distrust with the facts.

## Character Analysis

Martha Tappitt occupies a distinctive position in the novel's moral dramaturgy. In a narrative world where female characters are frequently measured by their romantic eligibility, Trollope makes Martha exceptional: she is "nearly thirty," unmarried, and yet presented not as a figure of pathos but as the Tappitt household's most reliable moral agent. The narrator's superlative — "of all living female human beings Martha is the most honest and just" [007-060] — is remarkable for its absolute quality, a judgment rarely bestowed so unequivocally on any character in the Trollope canon.

Martha functions structurally as a foil to two contrasting modes of feminine behaviour represented within the novel. Against Cherry's romantic impulsiveness, Martha embodies deliberation and self-restraint. Against [[mrs-prime|Mrs. Prime]]'s rigid evangelical severity, Martha represents a secular, practical morality grounded in domestic fairness rather than doctrinal certainty. She is, in effect, the novel's model of ethical common sense — a character whose judgments the reader is trained to trust even when they run counter to the sympathies invited by the central romance plot.

The trajectory of Martha's attitude toward Rachel Ray — from social exclusion to honest self-reproach — mirrors in miniature the novel's broader thematic concern with the gap between social performance and moral substance. Her admission that "we had been very foolish" [020-048] is more than a plot point; it is the novel's quiet vindication of the principle that justice, even when belated, is the proper work of community life.
