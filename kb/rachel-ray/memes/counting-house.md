# The Counting-house

## Overview

The counting-house is the administrative nerve center of Bungall & Tappitt's brewery—a space of ledgers, contracts, and commercial authority that represents the formal, legal dimension of the brewing trade. It is where Mr. Tappitt keeps his accounts, where Luke Rowan's claim to partnership is scrutinized in documentary form, and where the novel's business plot is transacted in the language of figures rather than feelings. In the gendered geography of Victorian commerce, the counting-house is emphatically male space, and its exclusion of women is complete—neither Mrs. Tappitt nor Rachel Ray would have reason or permission to enter it.

## In the Novel

### The Site of Commercial Authority

The counting-house is where Tappitt's authority is most tangible—and where it is most threatened. Rowan's legal claim is a matter of documents, and documents live in the counting-house. The space thus becomes a proxy for the contested partnership: who controls the counting-house controls the brewery.

### Masculine Space

Like the inn, the counting-house is part of the novel's masculine geography. Its exclusion of women is not a matter of explicit prohibition but of implicit social convention. The business of the brewery—as distinct from the social life of the brewery house—is men's business, conducted in men's spaces, on men's terms.
