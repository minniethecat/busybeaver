# Mrs. Tappitt

## Overview

Mrs. Tappitt is the novel's most energetic comic antagonist—a woman whose determination to secure advantageous marriages for her three daughters drives much of the social plot. She is, in Trollope's characteristic formulation, a schemer who lacks the talent for successful scheming: she "could scheme for her child, but she could not teach her child to scheme"(003-011). Her hostility to Rachel Ray—a rival for Luke Rowan's attention who has the temerity to be prettier than her own daughters—is the engine of Baslehurst gossip, and her manipulations provide the novel with some of its most satisfying moments of comic comeuppance. Yet Trollope never reduces her to mere caricature; she is a mother whose ambition for her children, however misguided in its methods, springs from a recognizable maternal love.

## Biography

### The Brewer's Wife

Margaret Tappitt has risen to local prominence through marriage to the proprietor of Bungall & Tappitt's brewery. Her social position is comfortable but not secure—she is acutely conscious that the Tappitts are trade, not gentry, and her social aspirations for her daughters reflect this anxiety. She wants for Augusta, Martha, and Cherry what she cannot quite claim for herself.

### The Rowan Gambit

When Luke Rowan arrives as a potential heir to the brewery, Mrs. Tappitt immediately identifies him as a matrimonial prospect. She hopes that one of her daughters—preferably Augusta, the tallest and therefore "in her mother's eyes, the beauty"(003-011)—will catch his attention. When Rowan instead shows interest in Rachel Ray, Mrs. Tappitt's disappointment curdles into active hostility. She cannot openly oppose the match, but she can—and does—spread the kind of gossip that makes Rachel's life difficult.

### The Ball

Mrs. Tappitt's ball is her finest social production and her greatest defeat. She plans it as a showcase for her daughters; instead, it becomes the occasion for Rachel Ray's social triumph and Mrs. Butler Cornbury's decisive intervention on Rachel's behalf. The spectacle of a mother's plans going awry is handled with Trollope's characteristic comic sympathy.

### Comic Antagonist

Mrs. Tappitt's machinations consistently fail in ways that reveal her limitations without destroying her dignity. She is outmaneuvered by Mrs. Cornbury, ignored by Rowan, and ultimately unable to prevent either the brewery's transfer or Rachel's triumph. Yet she remains a figure of comedy rather than villainy—her schemes are too transparent and her motives too human to provoke real animus.

## Relationships

### Mr. Tappitt
The marriage of the Tappitts is one of the novel's quiet portraits of domestic partnership. Mrs. Tappitt is the stronger will in domestic matters, while Mr. Tappitt governs the brewery. Their mutual loyalty, even as their plans fail, gives their household a solidity that contrasts with the fragility of Bragg's End.

### Augusta, Martha, and Cherry Tappitt
Mrs. Tappitt's relationship with her daughters defines her. She wants for them what she believes they deserve; her tragedy—a comic tragedy—is that she cannot see them clearly enough to understand what they actually need. Cherry's quiet independence escapes her notice entirely.

### Rachel Ray
The antagonist relationship that drives the social plot. Mrs. Tappitt's dislike of Rachel is partly tactical (Rachel threatens her matrimonial plans) and partly temperamental (she resents the natural grace that her own daughters lack).

### Luke Rowan
Mrs. Tappitt's initial welcome of Rowan as a potential son-in-law turns to bitter resentment when he chooses Rachel. Her subsequent hostility is comic in its transparency but genuine in its feeling.

## Key Scenes

- **The Scheme Revealed (Chapter III)** — Mrs. Tappitt's attempt to keep Rachel away from Rowan: "Rachel Ray is all very well, but she is not the person whom you should show off to a stranger"(003-011)
- **The Ball (Chapters VII–VIII)** — Mrs. Tappitt's social ambitions reach their apex and collapse simultaneously
- **The Gossip Campaign (Chapter X)** — Mrs. Tappitt's efforts to damage Rachel's reputation: "such a fuss with that girl Rachel Ray"(010-004)

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Tappitt is a study in the comedy of maternal ambition. Trollope understands that her scheming is not evidence of a bad heart but of a limited imagination—she can conceive of no higher good for her daughters than the kind of marriage she herself made, and she pursues that good with an energy that is admirable in its way. Her failure is not moral but intellectual: she cannot see that the world is more various than her categories allow. The novel's treatment of Mrs. Tappitt is ultimately generous—she is ridiculous but never despicable, and her defeat carries no sting of humiliation because Trollope has made her too human to hate.
