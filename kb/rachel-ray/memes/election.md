# Election

## Overview

A parliamentary election at Baslehurst serves as a significant subplot in *Rachel Ray*, occupying chapters XVII and XXIV–XXV of the novel. The election contest between Butler Cornbury and his opponent provides Trollope with an opportunity to explore political partisanship in a provincial town while entangling his romantic protagonists in the machinery of local politics.

## Context

Mid-Victorian parliamentary elections were public, boisterous affairs conducted before the Ballot Act of 1872. Voting was open—neighbors knew how neighbors voted—making electoral choice a matter of public declaration with social and commercial consequences. The Second Reform Act (1867) had not yet been passed, so the electorate was limited to propertied men.

## In the Novel

### Election as Commercial Pressure

The brewing business of Tappitt and Bungall becomes entangled with the election when Luke Rowan's political allegiance shifts. Butler Cornbury's agent expects the brewery's support, but "Mr. Tappitt's vote for the election—that was gone!" (017-094) when Rowan exerts his influence. This intersection of commerce and politics is characteristic of Trollope's realism.

### Election as Social Drama

On the day of the election, "Luke Rowan was to be seen standing at the corner of the street"(024-038), actively participating in the electoral machinery. The election chapters depict the town mobilized: bands playing, crowds gathering, and the public performance of political allegiance. "The chances of the election formed, of course, the chief subject of conversation"(018-031) throughout Baslehurst.

### Election as Moral Test

The election subplot tests characters' integrity. Mrs. Prime's religious politics lead her to oppose Butler Cornbury's party: "Isn't the Protestant religion of your country your party?"(017-071) she demands, conflating religious and political identity. The novel gently satirizes such absolutism while acknowledging the sincerity of conviction.

## Key Passages

> "It may be remembered that an election was impending over the town of Baslehurst."(017-027)

This transitional sentence inaugurates the election subplot that will dominate several later chapters.

> "Mr. Tappitt's vote for the election;—that was gone!"(017-094)

The brewery's political allegiance shifts with Luke Rowan's ascendancy, illustrating how commercial interests shape political expression.

> "The news of the election of Butler Cornbury reached the cottage at Bragg's End."(025-003)

The election's resolution arrives at the novel's domestic center, connecting the public political drama to the private romantic plot.

## Related Characters

- **Luke Rowan** — The young brewer whose political activity antagonizes Tappitt
- **Mr. Tappitt** — The old brewer whose vote is lost to Rowan's influence
- **Butler Cornbury** — The successful parliamentary candidate
- **Mrs. Prime** — Rachel's sister, whose religious politics color her view of the election
