# Three Tappitt Sisters

The "three Tappitt sisters" are a collective reference to the daughters of Mr. Tappitt, the brewer of Baslehurst: **Martha Tappitt**, **Cherry Tappitt**, and the youngest Miss Tappitt. Together they define the domestic and social atmosphere of the Tappitt household at the brewery.

## The Sisters

### Martha Tappitt

Martha is the eldest daughter, "the queen of the evening" during the Tappitt ball [010-080]. She is described as "handsome" and serves as the principal hostess alongside her mother [010-082]. Martha is the most socially prominent of the three sisters, and her marriage prospects are a subject of open discussion between Mr. and Mrs. Tappitt [010-095].

### Cherry Tappitt

Cherry is the middle daughter, distinguished by her "round, good-humoured face" and a temperament described as "placidity" [010-081]. Unlike Martha, Cherry lacks the social ambition that drives her elder sister, content to participate in family gatherings without seeking prominence [010-090]. Her name, more than any other character's, invites the reader's attention to the novel's deliberate use of meaningful naming.

### The Youngest Miss Tappitt

The youngest Tappitt daughter remains unnamed in the text, referred to only as part of the collective. Her presence at the ball is noted alongside her sisters [010-080], but she receives no individual characterization, functioning as a marker of the household's completeness rather than as an independent character.

## Role in the Novel

The Tappitt sisters serve several narrative functions:

- **Domestic backdrop**: They anchor the brewery as a site of family life, contrasting with the commercial activity conducted by their father.
- **Social gauge**: Their collective presence at the ball, at church, and at the dinner table signals the respectability of the Tappitt household.
- **Foil to the Ray sisters**: The Tappitt sisters' more prosperous circumstances — the ball, the brewery's standing — set off the Rays' more modest domestic arrangements at Bragg's End.
- **Marriage economy**: Martha's discussed eligibility and Cherry's quiet contentment represent two distinct positions within the novel's broader treatment of courtship and marriage.

## Structure in the Household

The three sisters, together with Mrs. Tappitt, form a female quartet that manages the brewery's domestic sphere. Mr. Tappitt's authority as brewer is tempered by the presence of his wife and daughters, whose social activities — the ball, attendance at church, the management of visitors — define the household's public face [010-075]. The sisters are "all smiles and good humour" when the brewery's social reputation requires it, a performance of domestic harmony that Mr. Tappitt's commercial anxieties cannot disrupt [010-080].
