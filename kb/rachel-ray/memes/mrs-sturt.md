## Overview

Mrs. Sturt is the wife of a tenant farmer on the Cornbury estate and the Rays' nearest neighbour at Bragg's End. Warm-hearted, plain-spoken, and rich in folk wisdom, she serves as a maternal confidante to Rachel Ray, offering emotional comfort and practical advice during the crises of Rachel's courtship by Luke Rowan. Her farm kitchen becomes a refuge where Rachel can speak freely away from her mother's anxious scrutiny.

## Biography

Mrs. Sturt lives with her husband on a farm whose yard abuts the lane leading to Bragg's End. The Sturts are tenants of the Cornbury estate [023-005], placing them within the social orbit of the novel's gentry while remaining firmly of the farming class.

She first appears as a neighbourly presence, exchanging a few minutes' conversation with Mrs. Ray [002-060]. Her role deepens as Rachel begins to confide in her: Rachel crosses the green to Mrs. Sturt's farm "so as to be out of the way" [019-004] when Mr. Comfort calls on her mother with news of the Dorcas meeting allegations. In the large front kitchen of the farmhouse [019-062], Mrs. Sturt offers Rachel direct, unsentimental counsel.

Her most significant intervention comes when she warns Rachel against clerical interference in matters of the heart: "Don't let ere a parson be making you believe as you're too bad for anything" [019-068]. This homespun wisdom counters the evangelical severity of Mrs. Prime and provides Rachel with an alternative moral framework rooted in natural feeling rather than doctrinal anxiety.

Mrs. Sturt also serves as a conduit for news: it is from her that Mrs. Ray first learns of the rumours circulating about Rachel [023-005]. Yet she delivers such tidings with "friendly tact" [023-015], and Mrs. Cornbury, visiting Mrs. Sturt's "little parlour" [023-006], finds an ally in the farmer's wife.

## Relationships

### Rachel Ray

Mrs. Sturt's most important relationship is with Rachel, for whom she provides a refuge from the emotional pressures of the Ray household. Her kitchen becomes a space of honest talk, and her advice—earthy, practical, and free of religious severity—offers Rachel an alternative to both her mother's anxiety and her sister's evangelical judgements.

### Mrs. Ray

The two neighbour women maintain cordial relations, exchanging visits and small kindnesses such as the gift of clotted cream [021-002]. Mrs. Sturt shows "friendly tact" [023-015] when conveying sensitive news, demonstrating social intelligence that transcends her rustic station.

### Mrs. Butler Cornbury

Mrs. Cornbury visits Mrs. Sturt's parlour and finds common ground with her [023-006], a connection that bridges the social distance between the gentry and the tenant farming class. Their alliance in supporting Rachel suggests that practical kindness transcends social rank in Trollope's moral universe.

### Mr. Sturt

Her husband is mentioned as a tenant farmer [023-005] who shares her practical outlook on life. He "had thought it very right" [021-032] that Rachel should have access to the pony carriage, suggesting the Sturts operate as a united front of neighbourly goodwill.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Sturt represents the moral value Trollope invests in plain country people. She is uneducated in the formal sense—her speech is rendered in dialect ("thou's off," "ere a parson")—yet she possesses an emotional intelligence and moral clarity that the novel's more genteel characters often lack. Her advice to Rachel, warning against clerical interference in matters of love, articulates one of the novel's central thematic positions: that natural affection and common sense should guide the heart, not religious severity or social calculation. As a tenant farmer's wife, she also anchors the novel's social world in the agricultural economy that underpins the Cornbury estate, reminding readers that the romantic plot unfolds against a backdrop of rural labour and neighbourly interdependence.
