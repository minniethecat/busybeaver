# Baslehurst Church

## Overview

The parish church of Baslehurst is the novel's spiritual center, presided over by Mr. Comfort's moderate Anglicanism and attended by the full cross-section of provincial society. Sunday services bring together the brewery family, the Bragg's End household, the Cornbury gentry, and the town's humbler residents in a ritual of communal visibility that is as much social as spiritual. For Rachel Ray, church attendance is a duty monitored by Mrs. Prime's severe eye; for Mrs. Prime, the quality of sermons is an index of spiritual health; for the gossips of Baslehurst, who sits where and who talks to whom after the service provides material for the week's conversation.

## In the Novel

### Social Theater

The church is where Baslehurst sees and is seen. The Ray family pew, the Tappitt family pew, and the Cornbury pew map the town's social hierarchy onto sacred space. Rachel's "yawn in church"(001-014) is a small sin that Mrs. Prime regards as evidence of spiritual backsliding, and the length of the second Sunday sermon is a matter of domestic complaint.

### Competing Theologies

The church represents moderate Anglicanism in contrast to the Evangelical severity of Mr. Prong's chapel. Mr. Comfort's sermons are presumably reasonable; Mr. Prong's are fiery. Mrs. Prime's preference for the latter is a theological choice that has domestic consequences—she would impose Mr. Prong's severity on the entire household.

### The Churchyard Stile

The churchyard adjacent to the church is the setting for Rachel and Luke's first private conversation—a secular encounter in sacred space. Luke waits at the stile; Rachel approaches; "she left Luke Rowan still standing there"(004-052) and flees. The proximity of church to churchyard, of sacred ritual to romantic possibility, is characteristic of Trollope's sense that human life is not easily divided into spiritual and worldly compartments.
