## Overview

**Mr. Hart** is a wealthy clothier from Hound Street in [[baslehurst|Baslehurst]], described as "the great clothier from Hound Street" [017-027]. He stands as the Liberal candidate for Parliament in the Baslehurst election, and his campaign becomes intertwined with the novel's central brewery dispute, drawing [[mr-tappitt|Mr. Tappitt]] into the political sphere and providing a backdrop of public life against which the domestic drama unfolds.

## Biography

### The Cloth Merchant

Mr. Hart is established as a man of commercial substance — "the great clothier from Hound Street" [017-027] — whose wealth and standing make him a natural candidate for parliamentary representation. His business success places him among Baslehurst's leading citizens, and his political ambitions reflect the Victorian pattern of successful merchants entering public life.

### The Parliamentary Candidate

Mr. Hart's candidacy for Parliament is a significant plot element in the novel's later chapters. He stands as a Liberal, and his campaign becomes a focal point for the town's political energies. "Mr. Hart is a liberal, and I mean to speak out my mind openly" [017-085], declares one supporter, capturing the partisan spirit of the contest.

The election brings Mr. Hart into direct contact with the Tappitt family. [[mr-tappitt|Mr. Tappitt]] is drawn into the campaign through Mr. Hart's personal appeal: "Mr. Hart in person had called upon him" [017-029]. The brewery proprietor's support becomes a matter of political consequence, and the election provides a public dimension to the private negotiations over the brewery's future.

### Election Day and Aftermath

On the morning of the election, "Mr. Hart was declared by his own committee to have been returned" [024-067], though the result proves less decisive than his supporters had hoped. He is ultimately "the candidate who was beaten" [027-043], his defeat a minor key resolution that parallels the novel's broader theme of ambitions tempered by reality.

## Relationships

### Mr. Tappitt

Mr. Hart's relationship with [[mr-tappitt|Thomas Tappitt]] is primarily political. The brewer's support is courted by the Hart campaign, and "in no stronger way could Mr. Hart have bound to him the Tappitt interest" [024-004] than through the political alliance they form. Mr. Tappitt's wife, meanwhile, becomes one of Hart's "more enthusiastic" supporters [025-002].

### The Liberal Interest

Mr. Hart represents the Liberal interest in Baslehurst, opposed by the Conservative faction associated with the established brewery order. His campaign engages characters across the social spectrum, from the farmers dining at the ordinary to the lawyers and clergymen who shape local opinion.
