# Jealousy

## Overview

Jealousy in *Rachel Ray* is the dark underside of provincial sociability—the emotion that converts competition into conflict and turns neighborliness into surveillance. The novel's central antagonist, the Tappitt family, is motivated by what Luke Rowan calls "mean, paltry jealousy"(013-054)—resentment of a younger, more capable man who threatens their brewing dynasty and their social standing. But jealousy is not confined to the Tappitts; it circulates through Baslehurst as gossip, suspicion, and the small resentments that accumulate in a community where everyone knows everyone else's business. Trollope, a novelist of social nuance rather than melodramatic passion, treats jealousy not as grand operatic emotion but as a quotidian moral failing—the petty vice that does more damage than spectacular sin.

## Context

Victorian moralists treated jealousy as a species of envy, a violation of the Tenth Commandment's prohibition against coveting. But the 19th-century novel gave jealousy a richer psychological treatment than theology allowed—from the sexual jealousy of *Othello* to the social jealousy of *Vanity Fair*. Trollope's innovation is to treat jealousy largely in economic and social rather than sexual terms. His characters are jealous of business position, social standing, and romantic advantage in ways that reflect the competitive anxieties of a commercial society.

## In the Novel

### Commercial Jealousy

The novel's most sustained portrait of jealousy is the Tappitt family's resentment of Luke Rowan. Mr. Tappitt, threatened by Rowan's claim to a partnership in the brewery, "accused them of jealousy, meanness, selfishness, and all the sins brought into the world by the love of money"(013-060). This is jealousy as economic territoriality—the elder generation's fear of displacement by the young. The Miss Tappitts' jealousy of Rachel Ray is an extension of the same logic: if Luke Rowan is to triumph in the brewery, his romantic choice of Rachel over a Tappitt daughter compounds the family's commercial defeat.

### Jealousy and Gender

The novel distinguishes between male and female jealousy. Luke Rowan's anger at the Tappitts is framed as righteous indignation against "jealousy, meanness, selfishness"(013-060)—he positions himself above jealousy even as he reacts to it. Rachel Ray's response to romantic uncertainty is, by contrast, to suppress any appearance of jealousy: "her female pride told her that it was well for her to show herself superior to any such jealousy"(018-003). The double standard is clear—men may confront jealousy directly; women must perform its absence.

### Jealousy as Plot Engine

"If the Tappitts in their jealousy were striving to rob Rachel Ray of her lover"(017-093), the narrator observes, jealousy becomes a narrative force that drives the romantic plot toward its crisis. The Tappitts' jealousy of Rachel's position with Luke Rowan generates the obstacles that test the lovers' constancy, making jealousy, paradoxically, the engine that ultimately produces the happy ending.

## Key Passages

> "It is mean, paltry jealousy, and nothing else."(013-054)

Luke Rowan's accusation cuts to the novel's moral center—jealousy is not a grand passion but a mean and petty emotion, beneath the dignity of those who feel it.

> "He accused them of jealousy, meanness, selfishness, and all the sins brought into the world by the love of money."(013-060)

The narrator's catalog links jealousy to a family of economic sins, locating its origin in the competitive commercial world that sustains Baslehurst.

> "If the Tappitts in their jealousy were striving to rob Rachel Ray of her lover."(017-093)

This conditional formulation—"if"—captures the novel's treatment of jealousy as both real in its effects and uncertain in its attribution.

> "Her female pride told her that it was well for her to show herself superior to any such jealousy."(018-003)

Rachel's internal negotiation—feeling jealousy but refusing to display it—reveals the gendered economy of emotion that governs Victorian courtship.

## Related Characters

- **Mr. Tappitt** — The brewer whose commercial jealousy of Luke Rowan drives the novel's business plot
- **The Miss Tappitts** — The three daughters whose romantic jealousy of Rachel Ray generates the novel's social conflict
- **Luke Rowan** — The target of Tappitt jealousy, whose righteous indignation masks his own pride
- **Rachel Ray** — The heroine who must navigate being the object of jealousy without expressing it herself
- **Mrs. Tappitt** — The brewmistress whose maternal ambition shades into jealousy of the Rays
