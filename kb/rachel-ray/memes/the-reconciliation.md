# The Reconciliation

## Overview

The reconciliation between Rachel Ray and Luke Rowan is the novel's emotional conclusion—the moment when the barriers that have kept them apart (the brewery dispute, Mrs. Prime's religious opposition, Mrs. Ray's maternal anxiety, the gossip of Baslehurst) are finally overcome. Trollope handles the reconciliation with characteristic restraint: there is no dramatic scene, no public declaration, no villain's comeuppance. Instead, the obstacles simply dissolve, and what has been true between Rachel and Luke since the stile in Chapter IV is finally allowed to become true in the world. The reconciliation is a quiet triumph—the vindication of steady affection over all the forces that have opposed it.

## In the Novel

### The Resolution of Obstacles

Each obstacle to the union is resolved in turn. The brewery dispute is settled, removing the commercial barrier. Mrs. Ray grants her permission, removing the maternal barrier. Mrs. Prime's opposition is neutralized by events rather than argument—she discovers, through her own experience with Mr. Prong, that the heart cannot be wholly governed by theological principle. The gossip of Baslehurst simply exhausts itself.

### Trollope's Quiet Ending

The reconciliation exemplifies Trollope's preference for realistic resolution over dramatic climax. There is no scene in which Rachel and Luke fall into each other's arms; instead, the narrator simply reports that they are to be married, that the obstacles have been cleared, and that the novel's work is done. This quietness is a moral choice: in Trollope's world, happiness does not require drama.
