# Ambition

## Overview

Ambition in Anthony Trollope's *Rachel Ray* is distributed unevenly across the social landscape of Baslehurst. Luke Rowan's ambition to reform the brewery and win Rachel Ray; Mrs. Prime's ambition to expand her religious influence; Mrs. Ray's modest ambition for her daughter's happiness; Mr. Prong's ambition to "stand and be fumigated"(016-025) in ecclesiastical preferment—each character's ambition reveals their place in the social order and their relationship to its constraints. Trollope, a lifelong civil servant who rose through the Post Office while writing novels before dawn, understood ambition as both a necessary virtue and a moral hazard. His treatment of the theme is characteristically balanced: ambition is praised when it serves honest industry and condemned when it becomes social climbing or spiritual pride.

## Context

The 1860s were a period of expanding opportunity—and expanding anxiety about opportunity. The 1854 Northcote-Trevelyan Report had opened the civil service to competitive examination; the 1857 Matrimonial Causes Act had altered the landscape of marriage; and industrial commerce created new paths to wealth outside the traditional professions. Samuel Smiles's *Self-Help* (1859) celebrated ambition as a moral force, while critics warned that unchecked ambition eroded social stability and personal integrity. For women, ambition was particularly fraught—admirable in the domestic sphere, suspect when it reached beyond it.

## In the Novel

### Commercial Ambition

Luke Rowan's ambition centers on the brewery: "it was his ambition to teach them"(011-083) (the Rays about beer), and more broadly to transform Bungall & Tappitt's into a modern commercial enterprise. His ambition is presented sympathetically—it is allied with competence and honesty—but it also generates conflict with Mr. Tappitt, whose own ambition is defensive and territorial. The narrator notes that "as to that ambition of putting down the consumption of spirits"(010-064), Luke's reforming zeal may exceed his practical reach.

### Social Ambition

Mrs. Tappitt's "mother's ambition that her daughter should have the chance of dancing with an honourable"(008-112) represents the novel's treatment of social climbing. This maternal ambition is understandable but slightly ridiculous—the Cornburys are too far above the Tappitts for a dance to mean what Mrs. Tappitt hopes. Mrs. Prime's "greatest ambition was to improve the worldly position"(007-002) of her religious protégés, an ambition that conflates spiritual and social advancement in a way the novel gently satirizes.

### Ambition and Gender

The novel is particularly attentive to how ambition is gendered. Rachel Ray's ambitions are domestic and relational—to marry the man she loves—while her sister's ambitions, channeled through religion, reach toward public influence. "That was her ambition"(009-051): Mrs. Prime's desire to be the leading religious woman in her district is both impressive and suspect, ambition disguised as service.

## Key Passages

> "Her greatest ambition was to improve the worldly position of the people in her district."(007-002)

The narrator's description of Mrs. Prime conflates spiritual mission with worldly ambition, revealing the hybrid nature of Victorian religious activism.

> "A mother's ambition that her daughter should have the chance of dancing with an honourable."(008-112)

Mrs. Tappitt's ambition is at once touching and absurd—the narrator's tone captures the pathos of social aspiration in a rigidly stratified society.

> "That was her ambition. When Mr. Prong told her that she was the woman best fitted to stand beside him, she believed him."(009-051)

Mrs. Prime's ambition finds its object in Mr. Prong's proposal, but the narrator's ironic juxtaposition—ambition, then credulity—undermines the dignity of both.

> "As to that ambition of putting down the consumption of spirits by good beer, he was sanguine."(010-064)

Luke Rowan's reforming ambition is presented with the narrator's characteristic blend of sympathy and gentle skepticism—noble in intent, perhaps unrealistic in execution.

## Related Characters

- **Luke Rowan** — The reforming brewer whose commercial ambition drives the plot
- **Mrs. Prime (Dorothea)** — The religious activist whose ambition masquerades as service
- **Mrs. Tappitt** — The brewmistress whose social ambition for her daughters is both comic and poignant
- **Mr. Prong** — The curate whose ecclesiastical ambition is undisguised self-interest
- **Rachel Ray** — The heroine, whose ambitions are modest but fiercely defended
