# Bungall & Tappitt's Brewery

## Overview

Bungall & Tappitt's brewery is more than a place of business—it is the economic engine of Baslehurst and the stage for the novel's commercial subplot. Founded by the partnership of the late Mr. Bungall and the living Mr. Tappitt, the brewery represents established provincial commerce at its most comfortable and complacent. When Luke Rowan arrives to claim the share willed to him by his great-uncle Bungall, the brewery becomes a contested space: old against young, custom against innovation, "bad beer"(004-052) against good. Trollope, who understood the romance of commerce as well as the romance of love, makes the brewery a site of genuine drama—the poker-wielding rage of Mr. Tappitt is as vivid as any scene of courtship.

## In the Novel

### The Commercial Foundation

The brewery is the source of the Tappitt family's prosperity and social standing. Its product—"the very name of beer that makes money"(003-007)—is both a literal commodity and a metaphor for the honest trade that Trollope respects. Mr. Tappitt has governed the concern as his personal domain since old Bungall's death, and his identity is inseparable from it.

### The Partnership Dispute

The novel's central business conflict is fought within the brewery's walls. Old Bungall's will left a share to Luke Rowan—"he had left away from Mr. Tappitt all that he could leave"(003-009)—and Rowan's arrival to claim it disrupts Tappitt's comfortable routine. The name-board that Tappitt reluctantly erected—"Mr. Tappitt had put up a board with the joint names"(003-008)—is a visual emblem of the contested partnership. The conflict escalates through legal threats, confrontations, and ultimately to physical menace when "Mr. Tappitt had been driven to forget himself, and to take a poker in his hand as a weapon of violence"(017-007).

### Social Center

The brewery is also a social space. Mrs. Tappitt's ball is held under its auspices, transforming the commercial premises into a ballroom where the town's social dramas play out. The brewery house where the Tappitt daughters grow up is both home and stage, and Cherry's quiet resistance to her mother's schemes is the brewery's most appealing domestic product.

### Luke's Vision

Rowan's perspective on the brewery represents a new generation of commercial professionalism. His thought about "the practicability of making good beer instead of bad"(004-052) is both a technical observation and a moral position: commerce should be conducted with integrity, and inherited custom is no excuse for poor quality.

## Key Scenes

- **The Name-Board (Chapter III)** — Tappitt's reluctant concession to legal reality
- **The Confrontation (Chapter XVII)** — Tappitt "had been driven to forget himself, and to take a poker in his hand as a weapon of violence"
- **Mrs. Tappitt's Ball (Chapters VII–VIII)** — The brewery transformed into a social arena
- **The Resolution (Chapter XXVIII)** — Tappitt's quiet retirement from the business

## Related Characters

- **Mr. Tappitt** — The established brewer whose identity is the brewery
- **Luke Rowan** — The young claimant whose modern methods challenge the old order
- **Mr. Bungall** — The deceased founder whose will sets the plot in motion
- **Mrs. Tappitt** — The brewer's wife who uses the brewery's social capital for her daughters' advancement
- **Cherry Tappitt** — The youngest daughter, the brewery's most independent spirit
