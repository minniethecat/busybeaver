# Mrs. Tappitt's Ball

## Overview

Mrs. Tappitt's ball is the novel's grand set-piece—a social event that occupies two chapters and transforms the novel's emotional landscape. Planned by Mrs. Tappitt as a showcase for her three daughters, it instead becomes the occasion for Rachel Ray's social triumph through the patronage of Mrs. Butler Cornbury. "It came to be voted by public acclamation that Rachel Ray was the belle of the evening"(008-002). The ball crystallizes the novel's central conflicts: Mrs. Tappitt's scheming versus Mrs. Cornbury's generosity, provincial anxiety versus metropolitan confidence, and Rachel's private feelings versus the public scrutiny of Baslehurst society.

## In the Novel

### The Setting

The ball is held at the brewery, transforming the commercial premises into a social arena. The brewery house's rooms are opened, musicians are hired, and the town assembles. The transformation of working space into dancing space is itself a metaphor for the novel's interest in the permeable boundary between commerce and society.

### Rachel's Triumph

Rachel arrives under Mrs. Cornbury's protection and immediately becomes the center of attention. She dances three times with Luke Rowan—a public declaration of preference that scandalizes the Tappitts. Her card fills with partners; her beauty is acknowledged; and even those who resent her cannot deny that she has carried the evening.

### Luke's Courtship

For Luke Rowan, the ball is an opportunity to claim Rachel publicly. He dances with her three times, claims her hand for the final dance, and "found himself confronted by Luke Rowan and his sister"(007-024) in a strategic family introduction. His behavior at the ball is the novel's most overt statement of his intentions before his formal declaration.

### The Gossip Aftermath

The ball's consequences ripple through subsequent chapters. "On the following morning the party was of course discussed"(010-004)—the Tappitt household processes its disappointment, and the town's gossip networks amplify Mrs. Tappitt's resentment. The ball is the peak of Rachel's social visibility and the source of her subsequent troubles.
