# Love

## Overview

Love in Anthony Trollope's *Rachel Ray* (1863) operates across multiple registers—romantic attraction between Rachel Ray and Luke Rowan, maternal affection between Mrs. Ray and her daughters, and the austere religious devotion of Mrs. Prime. Trollope presents love not as an abstract ideal but as a practical force that shapes decisions, tests characters, and ultimately must be reconciled with the demands of social propriety and economic reality. The novel's courtship plot, set against the backdrop of Baslehurst's brewing trade and church politics, examines whether romantic love can survive the scrutiny of family, community, and conscience.

## Context

The Victorian novel tradition placed romantic love at the center of its marriage plots, but Trollope was distinctive in treating love as one element among many in the calculus of marriage—alongside money, social standing, and family approval. Writing in the 1860s, when companionate marriage was becoming the middle-class ideal, Trollope neither dismisses romantic love as frivolous nor elevates it above practical considerations. His characters must navigate between the sentimental and the pragmatic, and the novel's happy ending depends on reconciling the two.

## In the Novel

### Romantic Love

The central love story of *Rachel Ray* is the courtship between Rachel Ray and Luke Rowan. From their first meeting beneath the churchyard elms, Trollope establishes the attraction as mutual and immediate: "she saw Luke Rowan, and he saw her"(002-079). Luke's interest is first expressed as aesthetic appreciation—"he said to himself that she was very lovely—more lovely than beautiful"(004-052)—but quickly deepens into genuine attachment. For Rachel, the experience is transformative: "there had as yet been no word spoken of him as a lover; but Rachel told her mother that she thought she liked Mr. Rowan very much"(004-046).

### Maternal and Familial Love

Mrs. Ray's love for her daughters is the emotional foundation of the Ray household. Her widowhood has not diminished her capacity for affection: "she had another whom she loved and caressed, and I may declare that the young girl under her protection was the dearer to her of the two"(001-013). Yet maternal love in the novel is also a source of anxiety—Mrs. Ray's fear for Rachel's happiness leads her to waver between supporting and opposing the match with Luke Rowan.

### Love versus Duty

The conflict between love and duty is embodied in the character of Mrs. Prime. Her "love of power"(001-010) is entangled with religious severity, and her "love" for Mr. Prong—"that sort of love which is bred by a desire to make things seem to be as they are not"(004-049)—contrasts sharply with the genuine romantic feeling between Rachel and Luke. The novel suggests that love, when divorced from natural human affection, becomes a form of self-deception.

## Key Passages

> "Mrs. Ray had another whom she loved and caressed, and I may declare that the young girl under her protection was the dearer to her of the two."(001-013)

Trollope's narrator establishes the depth of maternal love as the emotional center of the Ray household, even as it competes with Mrs. Ray's dependence on her elder daughter.

> "There had as yet been no word spoken of him as a lover; but Rachel told her mother that she thought she liked Mr. Rowan very much."(004-046)

This understated confession marks the turning point in Rachel's emotional journey—the moment when private feeling begins to become acknowledged attachment.

> "If you ask her to love you, she will love you."(004-025)

Spoken of Rachel, this observation captures Trollope's view of love as responsive and natural—a capacity for affection that, once awakened, transforms the lover's entire orientation toward the beloved.

> "Mrs. Prime did love him—with that sort of love which is bred by a desire to make things seem to be as they are not."(004-049)

The narrator's dissection of Mrs. Prime's self-deceiving "love" for Mr. Prong offers the novel's sharpest contrast to genuine romantic feeling, linking false love with religious hypocrisy.

## Related Characters

- **Rachel Ray** — The novel's heroine, whose capacity for romantic love awakens through her courtship with Luke Rowan
- **Luke Rowan** — The young brewer whose "almost in love"(006-040) status with Rachel evolves into determined commitment
- **Mrs. Ray** — Rachel's mother, whose maternal love is the emotional bedrock of the novel, even when it falters
- **Mrs. Prime (Dorothea)** — Rachel's sister, whose religious severity and self-deceiving "love" for Mr. Prong represent love's perversion
- **Mr. Prong** — The clergyman who offers Mrs. Prime a loveless proposal, embodying the novel's critique of marriage without affection
