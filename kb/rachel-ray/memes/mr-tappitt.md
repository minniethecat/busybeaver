# Mr. Tappitt

## Overview

Mr. Tappitt is the comic antagonist of Trollope's business plot—the established Baslehurst brewer whose comfortable world is disrupted by Luke Rowan's arrival. The "sole surviving partner"(001-027) of the Bungall & Tappitt brewery after old Mr. Bungall's death, Tappitt has governed the concern as his personal domain and regards Rowan's legal claim as an outrage. He is neither a villain nor a fool: he is a man of settled habits confronted with a modernity he cannot comprehend and a legal claim he cannot defeat. Trollope treats his predicament with characteristic sympathy for the conservative temperament, even as the narrative momentum favors the young challenger.

## Biography

### The Brewing Trade

Mr. Tappitt has dedicated his life to Bungall & Tappitt's brewery. The concern is prosperous if unglamorous—"the very name of beer that makes money"(003-007)—and Tappitt takes pride in his product even as his methods have grown complacent. His identity is inseparable from the brewery; when Luke Rowan threatens his control, the threat feels existential.

### The Rowan Challenge

When old Mr. Bungall died, his will left a share of the brewery to his great-nephew Luke Rowan. Tappitt's response is immediate and visceral: "Luke Rowan should never be partner of his"(003-010). He had "put up a board with the joint names"(003-008) only under legal compulsion, and he regards Rowan's claim as an injustice. The dispute escalates through lawyer's letters, confrontations, and ultimately to the point where Tappitt "had been driven to forget himself, and to take a poker in his hand as a weapon of violence"(017-007). This moment—the solid citizen reduced to threatened violence by wounded pride—is the novel's most vivid image of how commercial disputes destabilize character.

### Domestic Life

Tappitt's domestic world centers on his wife and three daughters. He is a fond but ineffectual father, leaving the management of his daughters' marital prospects to Mrs. Tappitt. His household is comfortable and conventional, and his daughters' loyalty to him—even Cherry's, who secretly sympathizes with Rowan—testifies to his decency as a father even when his conduct as a businessman is indefensible.

### Resolution

The novel's commercial resolution is pragmatic rather than punitive. Tappitt is not ruined; he is bought out. His exit from the brewery is not glorious, but it is dignified, and he remains a respected figure in Baslehurst. Trollope allows him the grace of a quiet retirement rather than a dramatic fall.

## Relationships

### Luke Rowan
The antagonist relationship at the novel's commercial heart. Tappitt's resistance to Rowan is equal parts legal principle, personal pride, and generational conservatism. He cannot see Rowan as anything but an interloper, and his inability to negotiate a compromise extends the conflict beyond reason.

### Mrs. Tappitt
Mrs. Tappitt is the stronger personality in the marriage, scheming for her daughters' social advancement with an energy her husband reserves for the brewery. Tappitt's deference to her in domestic matters is part of the novel's comic texture.

### Augusta, Martha, and Cherry Tappitt
Tappitt's three daughters represent his domestic legacy. His fondness for them is genuine, though his understanding of their individual characters—particularly Cherry's independence—is limited.

### Mr. Comfort
The vicar serves as a mediator in the brewery dispute, representing the community's preference for peace over litigation. Tappitt's respect for Mr. Comfort's opinion is a sign of his fundamental respectability.

## Key Scenes

- **The Brewery Board (Chapter III)** — Tappitt's resentment of the name-board bearing Rowan's name beside his own
- **The Confrontation (Chapter XVII)** — Tappitt "had been driven to forget himself, and to take a poker in his hand as a weapon of violence"(017-007), the dispute's nadir
- **The Resolution (Chapter XXVIII)** — Tappitt's quiet exit from the brewery, handled by Trollope without humiliation

## Character Analysis

Mr. Tappitt is one of Trollope's most sympathetic portrayals of conservative resistance to change. He is wrong about nearly everything—about the brewery, about Rowan, about his own legal position—yet he is never contemptible. His attachment to the brewery is the attachment of a lifetime's work; his resentment of Rowan is the resentment of a man who has done his duty as he understood it and is now told that his understanding was incomplete. Trollope's treatment of Tappitt embodies his characteristic moral vision: right and wrong are real categories, but they rarely map neatly onto the people we like and dislike. Tappitt's defeat is necessary, but it is not a victory for anyone worth cheering.
