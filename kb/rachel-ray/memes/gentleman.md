# Gentleman

## Overview

The term "gentleman" in Anthony Trollope's *Rachel Ray* functions as both a social designation and a moral claim—a word that characters deploy to assert their own standing, evaluate potential suitors, and police the boundaries of respectable society. Luke Rowan insists on being recognized as a gentleman even as he enters the commercial world of brewing; Mrs. Prime judges him by the standard of "a serious young man"(012-025); and the narrator observes that a gentleman "has his rights"(008-084) in courtship. Trollope, acutely sensitive to the gradations of Victorian class, uses the contested meaning of gentility to explore the tension between inherited status and earned character.

## Context

The Victorian ideal of the gentleman underwent significant transformation during the 19th century. The older, aristocratic definition—a gentleman as a man of independent means who did not work for a living—was increasingly challenged by a middle-class ideal that emphasized moral conduct, education, and professional achievement. The Reform Bills of 1832 and 1867, the expansion of the professions, and the rise of commercial wealth all contributed to the instability of the term. Trollope, who moved between the literary, professional, and club worlds of London, was uniquely positioned to observe the gentleman as both an aspirational identity and a site of social anxiety.

## In the Novel

### Gentleman by Birth or Character

The novel presents competing definitions of the gentleman. For the established families of Baslehurst, gentility is a matter of birth: "It was admitted at no private gentleman's table"(003-007)—the passive construction revealing gentility as something recognized rather than achieved. Yet Luke Rowan can claim to be a gentleman even though he works in trade, asserting "his pride, the truest pride, that he was a gentleman"(011-028).

### The Gentleman as Suitor

The question of whether Luke Rowan is a proper gentleman bears directly on his suitability as Rachel's husband. "Could any serious young man," Mrs. Prime wonders, be "manifestly of that class which was esteemed by Mrs. Prime"(012-025)—where "class" and moral seriousness are conflated. Luke's behavior toward Rachel is scrutinized for gentility: "A gentleman has his rights you know, Mrs. Ray"(008-084), he asserts, claiming the privilege of direct courtship.

### Gentleman and Trade

The novel is particularly interesting in its treatment of the gentleman in trade. Brewing is commerce, not a profession, yet Luke Rowan's education and bearing mark him as a gentleman. Mr. Tappitt, by contrast, is "not a gentleman"(006-045) despite his commercial success—the distinction resting on manner, education, and sensibility rather than income. Trollope suggests that gentility is a performance as much as an inheritance.

## Key Passages

> "It was admitted at no private gentleman's table."(003-007)

The narrator describes the humble status of Baslehurst beer—excluded from the tables of the gentry—highlighting the connection between consumption, commerce, and social hierarchy.

> "Her husband was a gentleman, and a great favorite with the old lady."(003-014)

Martha's description of Luke Rowan's father establishes gentility as a heritable quality, passed from father to son and recognized by the community.

> "He was not a gentleman. May I not call it a necessary incident to the position which he had to fill?"(006-045)

The narrator's qualified defense of Mr. Tappitt's lack of gentility—treating it as a function of his commercial role rather than a moral failing—reveals Trollope's nuanced class analysis.

> "He had once declared to himself his pride, the truest pride, that he was a gentleman."(011-028)

Luke Rowan's inward declaration of gentility as a matter of pride captures the aspirational dimension of the concept—gentility claimed rather than inherited.

## Related Characters

- **Luke Rowan** — The young brewer who asserts his status as a gentleman through conduct and pride
- **Mr. Tappitt** — The established brewer who, despite commercial success, is "not a gentleman"
- **Mr. Butler Cornbury** — The aristocratic connection who embodies gentility by birth
- **Mrs. Prime (Dorothea)** — The moral arbiter who judges suitors by the standard of "serious" gentility
- **Rachel Ray** — The heroine whose romantic choice involves an implicit judgment about what constitutes a gentleman
