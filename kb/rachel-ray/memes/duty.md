# Duty

## Overview

Duty in *Rachel Ray* functions as both a genuine moral compass and a source of self-deception. The novel traces how characters invoke duty to justify their actions—sometimes nobly, sometimes self-servingly. Mrs. Prime's severe widowhood, Rachel's obedience to her mother, and Luke Rowan's business ethics all operate under the language of duty, but the novel subtly questions whether duty always aligns with what is right.

## Context

Victorian moral discourse elevated duty to a cardinal virtue. The evangelical revival emphasized personal accountability before God, while the broader culture stressed filial obedience, professional integrity, and social obligation. Trollope, writing for a middle-class readership, was attentive to how these ideals played out in ordinary life—often revealing the gap between professed duty and actual motivation.

## In the Novel

### Duty and Self-Deception

Mrs. Prime embodies duty turned rigid. Her widow's weeds, her Dorcas society work, and her opposition to Rachel's marriage are all framed as duty—but the narrator suggests that "her peculiar ideas of duty required her to militate against her nature." Her fault was that "she had taught herself to believe that cheerfulness was a sin."(001-007) Duty becomes a vehicle for control rather than genuine virtue.

### Duty and Romantic Choice

Rachel faces competing duties: to her mother's wishes, to her sister's religious standards, and to her own heart. The novel suggests that true duty must accommodate human feeling—Mrs. Ray, for all her timidity, ultimately sides with Rachel's happiness against Mrs. Prime's prohibitions.

### Duty and Business

Luke Rowan's sense of duty to his late father's investment drives his contest with Tappitt. His insistence on examining the brewery's books is framed as a duty to his inheritance—but it also serves his personal ambition.

## Key Passages

> "She had taught herself to believe that cheerfulness was a sin."(001-007)

The narrator's diagnosis of Mrs. Prime reveals how duty can curdle into joylessness.
