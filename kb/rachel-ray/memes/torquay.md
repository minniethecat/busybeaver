## Overview

**Torquay** is a seaside resort town on the Devonshire coast that functions in the novel as a symbol of social aspiration, retirement, and escape from the industrial grime of [[baslehurst|Baslehurst]]. For [[mrs-tappitt|Mrs. Tappitt]], Torquay represents the fulfilment of a long-cherished dream: to leave the [[bungall-and-tappitt-brewery|brewery]] behind and establish herself in a "genteel place" [022-096] with a marine villa and the refined society that accompanies it. The Tappitt family's eventual retirement to Montpellier Villa, Torquay, provides the novel's closing image of settled domestic contentment — a retirement earned through the surrender of commercial ambition and the acceptance of changed circumstances.

## Description

### The Resort

Torquay is presented in the novel as a place of leisure and social display, distinguished by its balls, assemblies, and marine villas. It is the kind of place where a retired brewer's family might reinvent itself, shedding the associations of trade for the more ambiguous status of the genteel. "Torquay would be delicious," [[cherry-tappitt|Cherry Tappitt]] declares [022-053], her adjective capturing the blend of pleasure and pretension that the resort represents.

The town's geographical position on the Devonshire coast places it at some distance from Baslehurst — a distance that is as much social as it is physical. To move to Torquay is to leave behind not only the brewery's "nasty dirty smoke" [027-050] but also the intricate web of local obligations, rivalries, and gossip that defines life in a small market town.

### Montpellier Villa

The Tappitt family's specific destination is Montpellier Villa, which they inspect before the move and find "will suit us delightfully" [029-078]. The villa's name — Montpellier — carries its own freight of genteel association, borrowed from the French Mediterranean, and represents the aspirational nomenclature characteristic of Victorian seaside development. Within three months of the novel's main action, the Tappitts are "settled in the Torquay villa" [030-002], the speed of the transition underscoring the decisiveness with which Mrs. Tappitt pursues her domestic ambition.

## Significance

Torquay's significance in the novel operates on multiple levels. Most immediately, it is the object of Mrs. Tappitt's social ambition — the reward she extracts from her husband's commercial surrender. Her repeated refrain about the "nasty dirty smoke" [027-050] of the brewery positions Torquay as the antithesis of industrial Baslehurst: clean where the brewery is dirty, genteel where the brewery is commercial, leisurely where the brewery demands constant labour.

But Torquay also serves a structural function in the novel's resolution. The Tappitts' removal to the coast clears the social field in Baslehurst, allowing [[rachel-ray|Rachel Ray]] and [[luke-rowan|Luke Rowan]] to establish their married life without the friction of the old brewery regime. The move is thus both a personal reward for the Tappitts and a narrative necessity for the protagonists. The final chapter's image of the Tappitts "settled pleasantly at Torquay" [030-007] is the novel's benediction on a family that has learned, however reluctantly, to accept diminished circumstances with grace.

Torquay also gestures beyond the novel's immediate world to the broader Victorian phenomenon of seaside retirement — the emergence of coastal resorts as sites where the commercial classes could transmute the profits of trade into the appearance of gentility. Mrs. Tappitt's marine villa is both a genuine domestic achievement and a quiet satiric comment on the social ambitions it represents.
