# Marriage

## Overview

Marriage in Anthony Trollope's *Rachel Ray* (1863) is not merely a romantic conclusion but a central social and economic institution that drives the novel's plot. Set in the Devonshire town of Baslehurst, the novel examines marriage through multiple lenses—as a woman's essential "prop"(001-002), as a family negotiation, and as a test of moral character. The theme pervades the narrative from the opening character sketch of Mrs. Ray, a widow who "cannot grow alone as standard trees," to the final resolution of Rachel Ray's engagement.

## Context

In Victorian England, marriage was the defining event in a middle-class woman's life. The 1857 Matrimonial Causes Act had recently reformed divorce law, but social expectations remained rigid. A woman's economic security, social identity, and moral standing were all mediated through marriage. Trollope, writing in the early 1860s, presents these realities without overt polemic, allowing his characters to embody different responses—from Mrs. Prime's ascetic widowhood to Mrs. Ray's gentle dependence, to Rachel's hopeful romanticism.

## In the Novel

### Marriage as Necessity

The novel's opening meditation establishes Trollope's view of marriage as both natural and socially constructed. "Of most women it may be said that it would be well for them that they should marry—as indeed of most men also, seeing that man and wife will each lend the other strength, and yet in lending lose none; but to the women of whom I now speak some kind of marriage is quite indispensable."(001-002) Mrs. Ray, widowed young, transferred her dependence to her elder daughter Dorothea: "Mrs. Ray immediately married herself to her eldest child."(001-004)

### Marriage as Social Negotiation

The brewing family of the Tappitts has three marriageable daughters, and Mrs. Tappitt's "ideas on this matter were rather ambitious."(003-010) When Luke Rowan's attraction to Rachel Ray becomes apparent, the question of whether it would be a good match occupies multiple chapters. Mrs. Ray's sisterly friend Mrs. Sturt warns that "marriage is not to be thought of in a moment"(019-028), while the brewmistress Mrs. Tappitt considers a match between Rachel and Luke "beneath" his station.(013-025)

### Marriage and Character

For Rachel herself, marriage to Luke Rowan represents both romantic fulfillment and moral testing. She must weigh her own feelings against her mother's fears and her sister's religious scruples. Luke declares his intentions plainly, treating "his marriage as a thing settled"(021-018), but the obstacles—financial, familial, and religious—require both characters to demonstrate constancy.

## Key Passages

> "There are women who cannot grow alone as standard trees;—for whom the support and warmth of some wall, some paling, some post, is absolutely necessary."(001-002)

Trollope's botanical metaphor establishes the novel's central thesis about marriage as structural support for women in a society that offers them few independent roles.

> "A woman in want of a wall against which to nail herself will swear conjugal obedience sometimes to her cook, sometimes to her grandchild, sometimes to her lawyer."(001-002)

The author's wry observation extends the marriage metaphor to all forms of female dependence, suggesting that the instinct to attach is universal even when the object is unsuitable.

> "When anything so serious as marriage was to be considered, it was necessary that there should be no deceit or double-dealing in the matter."(013-059)

This passage reflects the moral seriousness with which Trollope treats the institution—marriage requires honesty and transparency from all parties.

## Related Characters

- **Rachel Ray** — The novel's protagonist, whose marriage prospects form the central plot
- **Luke Rowan** — The young brewer whose courtship of Rachel drives the romantic narrative
- **Mrs. Ray** — Rachel's widowed mother, whose own marital history shapes her anxieties
- **Mrs. Prime (Dorothea)** — Rachel's sister, a young widow whose religious severity opposes the match
- **Mr. Prong** — The clergyman who proposes to Mrs. Prime, offering a stark contrast to romantic love (023-021)
