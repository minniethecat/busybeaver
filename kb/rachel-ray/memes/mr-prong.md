# Mr. Prong

## Overview

Mr. Prong is the curate whose Evangelical severity makes him the natural spiritual ally of Mrs. Prime—and, unexpectedly, her potential suitor. His sermons are of the rigorous sort that Dorothea admires, and his theology aligns perfectly with her own conviction that the world is a snare. Yet Trollope's treatment of Mr. Prong is characteristically even-handed: he is not a hypocrite or a villain, merely a man whose genuine piety has been narrowed by a theology that cannot accommodate ordinary human happiness. His interest in Mrs. Prime—whether spiritual, marital, or both—creates a subplot that tests her own consistency and reveals the limits of Evangelical rigor as a guide to lived experience.

## Biography

### The Evangelical Curate

Mr. Prong preaches the kind of sermons that Mrs. Prime considers proper spiritual food: sermons about sin, judgment, and the dangers of worldliness. His ministry appeals to the serious-minded minority within the parish, and his influence over Mrs. Prime is a function of their shared theological convictions.

### Interest in Mrs. Prime

Mr. Prong's attention to Mrs. Prime goes beyond pastoral concern. He sees in her a kindred spirit—a woman whose religious seriousness matches his own—and the possibility of a match between them hovers over several chapters. This subplot serves a double function: it provides comic relief (the spectacle of two severe people discovering that marriage requires more than shared theology) and it tests Mrs. Prime's own principles. Can she, who has opposed Rachel's romance on spiritual grounds, accept a suitor whose only recommendation is spiritual compatibility?

### Resolution

Mrs. Prime's eventual refusal of Mr. Prong is one of the novel's quiet revelations. She discovers—perhaps to her own surprise—that she does not wish to marry a man she does not love, however sound his theology. This discovery aligns her, against her will, with the very romantic principles she has denounced in Rachel.

## Relationships

### Mrs. Prime
The central relationship. Mr. Prong admires Mrs. Prime's piety; she admires his preaching. Their mutual admiration, however, proves an insufficient foundation for marriage, and Mrs. Prime's refusal exposes the gap between her theology of renunciation and her actual human needs.

### Rachel Ray
Though they have little direct interaction, Mr. Prong represents the religious culture that threatens Rachel's happiness. His sermons are the spiritual diet that Mrs. Prime would impose on the entire household.

### Mr. Comfort
The contrast between Mr. Prong's severity and Mr. Comfort's moderation represents the novel's internal debate about the nature of Anglicanism.

## Key Scenes

- **The Sermons (Chapters V, IX)** — Mr. Prong's preaching establishes his theological character
- **The Courtship Subplot (Chapters IX–XI)** — Mrs. Prime's encounter with the possibility of remarriage

## Character Analysis

Mr. Prong is a minor figure who punches above his narrative weight. His function is to embody Evangelicalism in its most uncompromising form, and through him Trollope explores what happens when religious principle collides with ordinary human desire. Mr. Prong is not a villain—Trollope is too fair for that—but his inability to see that marriage might require something beyond doctrinal agreement makes him a figure of pathos as much as satire. His rejection by Mrs. Prime is the novel's quiet argument that even the most rigorous theology cannot wholly suppress the human heart.
