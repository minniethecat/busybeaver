# Rachel Ray

## Overview

Rachel Ray is the nineteen-year-old heroine at the center of Trollope's novel. The daughter of a timid widow and the younger sister of the fiercely Evangelical Mrs. Prime, Rachel inhabits the quiet world of Bragg's End cottage yet possesses a "capacity for sustained purpose"(001-014) that sets her apart from her mother's weakness and her sister's severity. Over the course of the novel, she must navigate the competing claims of family duty, religious scrutiny, and her own awakening heart when Luke Rowan enters her life. Trollope draws her as neither saint nor rebel—she commits the small sins of "a vain walk into Baslehurst on a summer evening"(001-014) while remaining fundamentally decent, and her moral growth consists precisely in learning to trust her own judgment rather than submitting to the certainties of others.

## Biography

### Early Life and Family Situation

Rachel Ray grew up at Bragg's End, the cottage where her widowed mother settled after Mr. Ray's death. Her father had been "a steady, sober, hardworking, earnest man, well esteemed in the world"(001-004), a lawyer in Exeter whose death left the family in modest circumstances. Rachel was raised alongside her much older sister Dorothea—now Mrs. Prime, a childless widow who has returned to live with them—under a regime of strict Evangelical observance. The household is dominated by Mrs. Prime's religious seriousness: "as long as she had been subject to Dorothea she had never been allowed to do a thing that she liked"(001-013).

### First Meeting with Luke Rowan

Rachel meets Luke Rowan through the Tappitt sisters at the brewery. Cherry Tappitt, with characteristic good humor, "declared her conviction that he would fall in love with Rachel Ray directly he saw her"(003-011). When they first walk together at the brewery, Rachel is drawn into conversation with the young man from London, and their encounter at the stile in the churchyard becomes the novel's first intimate moment. There, "she left Luke Rowan still standing there"(004-052) and fled home, her thoughts in confusion.

### Courtship and Opposition

The courtship between Rachel and Luke unfolds under the shadow of multiple oppositions. Mrs. Prime regards Luke as a worldly temptation. Mrs. Tappitt, angered that Luke has not chosen one of her own daughters, spreads malicious gossip: "If there isn't Rachel Ray going to waltz with Walter Cornbury!"(007-014). At Mrs. Tappitt's ball, however, Rachel is declared "the belle of the evening"(008-002) through the patronage of Mrs. Butler Cornbury. Luke's direct declaration—"Rachel Ray I do intend to marry—if she will have me"(013-038)—cuts through the intrigue, but Rachel must wait through a long period of estrangement while the brewery partnership is contested.

### Resolution

Rachel's emotional trial is the novel's central arc: she must learn to distinguish her own feelings from the voices of her mother, her sister, and the gossips of Baslehurst. When she finally "received from her mother permission to regard Luke Rowan as her lover"(012-081), it marks not only a romantic triumph but a moral one—the quiet girl of Bragg's End has learned to stand her ground.

## Relationships

### Mrs. Ray
Rachel's mother is "a woman who cannot grow alone as a standard tree"(001-002)—weak, loving, and perpetually torn between her two daughters. Rachel's relationship with her mother is tender but complicated by Mrs. Ray's inability to offer firm guidance. When Rachel needs permission to love Luke Rowan, Mrs. Ray's "poor vacillating words" prove inadequate, and Rachel must essentially give herself that permission.

### Mrs. Prime (Dorothea)
The widowed elder sister is Rachel's foil and antagonist within the household. "Black, and stiff, and stern"(001-005), Dorothea embodies the rigor of Evangelical religion and regards Rachel's impulses toward pleasure as dangerous backsliding. She "foretells hard things for her sister, denouncing her as fashioning herself wilfully in the world's ways"(001-014). Their conflict is the domestic engine of the plot.

### Luke Rowan
The young brewer from London who becomes Rachel's suitor. Their relationship develops through the novel's set pieces—the churchyard stile, the ball, the letter—and represents Rachel's passage from girlhood to adult agency. Luke's steadiness and directness provide the counterweight to the evasions and gossip that surround Rachel.

### Mrs. Butler Cornbury
The squire's wife who becomes Rachel's most effective patron. Her championship at the ball—"she talked her young friend up to the top of the tree"(008-002)—gives Rachel the social standing to withstand the Tappitts' hostility.

### The Tappitt Sisters
Augusta, Martha, and Cherry Tappitt are Rachel's initial bridge to Luke Rowan and later her rivals. Cherry alone remains loyal, still "standing up for him before her mother and sisters"(017-007) even after the family feud.

## Key Scenes

- **The Churchyard Stile (Chapter IV)** — Rachel's first private conversation with Luke Rowan, in which he calls her by her Christian name and she "had allowed his doing so to pass by without notice"(004-052).
- **Mrs. Tappitt's Ball (Chapters VII–VIII)** — Rachel is elevated to "belle of the evening"(008-002) and dances three times with Luke Rowan, scandalizing Baslehurst society.
- **The Love-Letter (Chapter XVI)** — "Rachel Ray's First Love-Letter"(016-001) marks the formal beginning of their acknowledged attachment.
- **Permission Granted (Chapter XII)** — After long maternal hesitation, Rachel "received from her mother permission to regard Luke Rowan as her lover"(012-081).

## Character Analysis

Rachel Ray represents Trollope's ideal of young womanhood: she is neither the saintly martyr of didactic fiction nor the rebellious heroine of sensation novels. Her distinguishing trait is a "capacity for sustained purpose"(001-014) visible in her brow from the first pages. This capacity must grow against the grain of her upbringing—against her mother's timidity, her sister's authoritarian piety, and the prying surveillance of Baslehurst gossip. Rachel's moral triumph is not that she chooses love over duty, but that she learns to distinguish between the two when they have been deliberately confused by those who claim to know better. Her final emergence is quiet, domestic, and thoroughly Trollopian: she becomes a wife, not a rebel.
