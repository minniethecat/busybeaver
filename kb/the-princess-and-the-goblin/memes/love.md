# Love

Love in *The Princess and the Goblin* is not framed as romantic passion but as a protective, nurturing force that binds the royal family across generations. It is expressed through the great-grandmother's care, the king's devotion, and the thread that guides and guards Irene on her journeys.

## The Great-Grandmother's Love

The old lady's love for Irene is the novel's central emotional force. When Irene visits, the great-grandmother rubs ointment on her with "the sweetest odour filled the room — like that of roses and lilies" (011-041). She gives Irene the magical thread (015-078) and the ring (015-090), tangible tokens of an invisible bond. Her love is both tender and demanding — she tests Irene's courage with the rose fire, asking gently, "You're not afraid of the rose — are you?" (015-021).

## The King's Love

King Papa's love for Irene is expressed in physical tenderness and protective concern. "He wore a narrow circle of gold set with jewels around his helmet" (010-004), and when he visits, his presence fills the palace with joy. The king's love is also practical — he stations guards to watch "outside the house every night, walking round and round it from sunset to sunrise" (010-033), protecting his daughter while he is away.

## Love Tested by Disbelief

Love in the novel is tested when stories are not believed. Lootie "was not comfortable in her mind — not that she suspected the least truth in Irene's story, but that she loved her dearly, and was vexed with herself for having been cross to her" (004-034). Lootie's love for Irene is genuine but limited — she cannot believe in the grandmother, and this disbelief creates a barrier between them. True love, the novel suggests, requires trust in the beloved's word even when it strains credulity.

## The Goblin Queen's Love Story

The goblin world has its own distorted version of love. Curdie overhears the goblins discussing their royal lineage: "I suppose he fell in love with her" (008-022), followed by the reaction "Oh, horrid! How could the king have fallen in love with her?" (008-038). The goblin queen with her six toes is described as the object of a king's love — a grotesque inversion of the royal love that binds Irene's family. Where the human royal family's love is luminous and protective, the goblins' royal marriage is a source of shame and deformity.

## Love and the Thread

The magical thread is the material embodiment of the great-grandmother's love, "tempered in the rose-fire and tied to her opal ring" (020-010). It guides Irene through darkness and danger, never failing. The thread suggests that love is not merely a feeling but an active force that leads, protects, and restores.

## Curdie's Devotion

Curdie's feelings for Irene grow from protective instinct to something deeper. He risks his life to save her from the goblins, and by the novel's end, his "love" has become a cornerstone of the kingdom's future. The king's "beautiful smile spread like sunshine over his face" (017-005) when he sees Curdie with Irene, suggesting his approval of the bond forming between them.

## Lovely Places and Dreams

The word "lovely" recurs throughout the novel as a marker of beauty touched by goodness. The garden is "a very lovely place" (010-024). Irene dreams "in the midst of the loveliest dreams — of summer seas and moonlight and mossy springs and great murmuring trees" (011-065) after visiting her grandmother. The grandmother's room is "a lovely haven to reach from the darkness and fear" (015-005). In MacDonald's vocabulary, the lovely is that which participates in love.
