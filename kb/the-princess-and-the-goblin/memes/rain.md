# Rain

Rain in *The Princess and the Goblin* marks the boundary between the protected indoor world and the wildness beyond. It is rain that keeps Irene indoors, setting the stage for her first journey up the tower stair; it is rain that makes "a great trampling noise" on the roof [002-005], dramatizing the isolation of the palace.

## Rain and Confinement

The novel opens with rain. Irene is confined to the nursery because of bad weather: "she could not of course go out" [002-003]. The rain turns the palace into an enclosed world where Irene must explore inward rather than outward. The sound of rain — "gathering itself together into raindrops, and pouring down on the roof" [002-003] — creates the atmosphere of a world under siege.

## Rain and Discovery

It is during rain that Irene first encounters the hidden passages. Running from the nursery, she enters a labyrinth of doors, and the rain's sound — "the sounds of the rain" [002-005] — echoes through the corridors, disorienting and guiding her. The rain drives her exploration, and in her wandering she finds the tower stair and, eventually, her great-grandmother.

## Rain as Companion

Rain reappears at moments of emotional significance. When "the rain fell, and drenched" Irene [011-002] on her night journey to the tower, it is part of the trial she must endure. The "rain made a great trampling noise" [002-005] as if the elements themselves were active participants in the story.

## Symbolic Meaning

Rain in the novel represents the world's wildness pressing against domestic safety. It is the force that drives characters inward — both literally, into the palace, and metaphorically, into self-discovery. Water cleanses as well as confines.
