# Stair

The staircase in *The Princess and the Goblin* is a central architectural and symbolic feature — "a curious old stair of worm-eaten oak" [002-004] that connects the nursery level of the palace to the great-grandmother's tower room. Every ascent of the stair marks a step in Irene's spiritual journey toward wisdom and courage.

## The Tower Stair

The stair that leads to the great-grandmother's tower is hidden and difficult to find. After her first visit, Irene "could not find the stair of the tower" [005-011] on subsequent attempts, suggesting that the stair is not always accessible. When she does find it, the journey is transformative: "she did not come on a stair that went up, but she came upon one that went down" [005-013] — a paradox that hints at the stair's magical nature.

## Ascent and Descent

Climbing the stair is associated with revelation. "As she went slowly up the stair, not quite sure that she was not dreaming" [011-005], Irene passes from the ordinary world into the moonlit realm of the great-grandmother. The stair she climbs "stair after stair" [011-006] is a journey of persistence. Later, during the goblin crisis, Irene "ran up the stairs at once" [014-009] to find safety.

## The Stair as Threshold

The foot of the stair marks a boundary. It is "just outside the nursery door" [014-008], connecting the domestic space to the tower. When the king visits, he ascends "the old stair" [017-013] to visit his own grandmother. Even Curdie, the doubting miner, eventually "went up the old staircase, and a new thought struck him" [021-106], showing that the stair transforms all who climb it.
