# Danger

Danger in *The Princess and the Goblin* is a constant presence, taking multiple forms: the goblins' schemes against the human world above, the physical perils of the mountain and its mines, and the moral dangers of pride and disbelief.

## Goblin Threats

The goblins' plans present the most systematic danger in the novel. Curdie discovers "the latter plan of the goblins was to inundate the mine by breaking outlets for the water accumulated in the natural reservoirs of the mountain" [009-023]. Later, the goblins resolve to "carry out their second plan upon the failure of the first" [029-002], demonstrating their persistent malice. The mine is therefore "in the greatest danger" [029-002] from goblin flooding.

## Physical Perils Underground

Curdie faces direct physical danger in the goblin tunnels. After crawling through a hole in the wall, he recognizes "there was not much danger of meeting any goblins" but "there was considerable danger of his taking a wrong turning, for he had now no light" [009-020]. The underground darkness itself is a threat, capable of trapping him in the labyrinth.

## Danger on the Mountain

The mountain itself poses serious risks. During the storm, "it was not only out of the question for the princess to go down the mountain, but most dangerous" [029-012] due to the swollen streams. The house itself "appeared in danger of falling, and every moment the torrent was increasing" [031-019]. The wind threatens to "blow him off the mountain" [029-005] as Curdie struggles upward.

## Irene's Encounters with Danger

Irene's adventures repeatedly put her in harm's way. Running from what she believes to be a goblin creature, "she ran on, for she fancied the horrible creature just behind her" [014-009]. Her great-great-grandmother gives her a magical thread to protect her: "If ever you find yourself in any danger—such, for example, as you were in this same night—you must take off your ring and put it under the pillow of your bed" [015-078].

## Protection from Danger

The novel contrasts danger with multiple forms of protection. The king leaves "six of his attendants behind him" with orders to watch the house every night, while Curdie and the men-at-arms guard against goblin incursions. Irene's grandmother assures Curdie, "My grandmother is in no danger" [031-027], indicating that some beings transcend mortal peril. The magical thread and ring provide supernatural safeguards.
