# Story

The idea of story in *The Princess and the Goblin* operates on multiple levels: the narrator's framing of the tale, the stories characters tell one another, and the question of who will be believed when extraordinary events are recounted.

## The Narrator's Framing

The narrator establishes a direct relationship with the reader from the beginning, marking the tale as a story consciously told: "at the time my story begins was about eight years old" [001-003]. The narrator repeats this framing: "I have said the Princess Irene was about eight years old when my story begins. And this is how it begins" [002-002]. These self-conscious gestures invite the reader to share in the act of storytelling.

## Stories Told and Retold

Characters within the novel repeatedly recount their adventures to others, creating nested narratives. Curdie's mother asks him to "tell us the whole story from beginning to end, just as it all happened" [023-003] after his escape from the goblins. During their walk together, Curdie "gave her a full account, not only of the character and habits of the goblins, so far as he knew them, but of his own adventures" [021-070]. Storytelling is how knowledge passes between characters and how experiences are validated.

## The Problem of Belief

A central tension in the novel is whether stories of the extraordinary will be believed. The narrator notes that Lootie "did not suspect the least truth in Irene's story" [004-034] about her grandmother, treating it as childish fantasy. Curdie himself doubts Irene's account of her grandmother until he sees her with his own eyes. The narrator observes that "nobody who met us would believe a word we said to them" [022-026], acknowledging the difficulty of convincing others of miraculous events.

## Stories of Goblins

The goblins have their own stories and traditions, passed down through songs and oral history. Curdie learns their "character and habits" [021-070] partly through listening at their gatherings. The goblins' stories of their royal lineage and their grievances against the "sun-people" shape their motivations and actions.

## The Story's Continuation

The novel ends by looking forward to further storytelling: "The rest of the history of The Princess and Curdie must be kept for another volume" [032-006]. This metafictional gesture reminds the reader that story is never truly finished — it continues beyond the page.
