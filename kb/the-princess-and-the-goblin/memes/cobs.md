# Cobs

"Cobs" is the word used by [[miners|the miners]] throughout [[The Princess and the Goblin]] to refer to [[goblins|the goblins]]. Introduced early in the novel by [[peter]] as a piece of local dialect — "The 'cobs,' as we call them" (006-089) — the term carries the weight of a shared working vocabulary, marking the miners' pragmatic, unromantic relationship with the creatures who share their mountain.

## Nature and Definition

The term "cobs" is the miners' own. It appears nowhere in the novel's narrative voice except in reported speech or free indirect discourse aligned with the mining community's perspective. The narrator and the gentry — [[irene]], [[lootie]], [[king-papa]] — use "goblins." The miners use "cobs." This linguistic divide reflects a broader social division in the novel: the miners live in daily proximity to the goblin threat and have developed their own language for it, while the castle-dwellers maintain the more formal, legendary term.

The word "cobs" carries a diminutive, familiar quality — it is shorter, more informal, and perhaps slightly dismissive compared to "goblins." For men who confront goblins in their daily work, who fight them off with rhyming verses and stamping feet, the familiar name may serve to reduce the creatures' power in their imaginations. At the same time, the term testifies to the miners' firsthand knowledge: they call the goblins "cobs" because they have earned the right to name what they face.

When [[curdie]] is underground, he constantly uses "cobs" in his internal monologue: "dodged the cobs" (012-005), "afraid of the cobs" (018-003), "the cobs' creatures" (018-003). This internalized usage shows how thoroughly the miners' dialect shapes Curdie's worldview — he thinks of the goblins in the language of his community even when alone in the dark.

## Narrative Role

The term "cobs" appears at crucial moments of the novel's plot, always in dialogue spoken by or through the mining community:

> "The cobs are coming!" he said. "They don't seem to know their way, but they're evidently hunting for the house" (027-003).

This warning, delivered by [[curdie]] to [[irene]] and [[lootie]], is the novel's most dramatic point-of-no-return — the moment the goblin threat breaches the castle's walls. That Curdie uses "cobs" here, in a moment of urgency directed at the princess, shows how natural the term is to him; he does not code-switch even in crisis.

When the goblins actually enter the house, the narration preserves Curdie's perspective: "The cobs were in the house!" (027-007). The exclamation reads as free indirect discourse — Curdie's thought rendered in his own dialect.

## Significance

The term "cobs" exemplifies MacDonald's attention to the linguistic texture of social class. In a novel that is, at one level, about the alliance between a princess and a miner's son, the fact that two words exist for the same enemy underlines the social distance that must be bridged. When [[irene]] calls them "cobs" in conversation with [[curdie]] — "But the cobs have been into your house" (028-021) — she is speaking his language, crossing the class boundary that separates the castle from the cottage. This linguistic accommodation mirrors the broader theme of mutual understanding and cooperation that resolves the novel's central conflicts.

The miners' coinage also reflects their practical relationship with the goblins. For the castle-dwellers, goblins are creatures of legend and fear. For the miners, cobs are a fact of working life — dangerous, but manageable with the right tools (rhymes, a pickaxe, knowledge of their soft feet). The name encodes this pragmatic attitude.
