# Music

Song and rhythm serve as a protective force in *The Princess and the Goblin*, functioning as both a practical defense against goblins and a marker of the novel's moral order. Curdie's singing, the miners' work-songs, and the grandmother's mysterious melody represent three registers of musical power.

## Nature / Definition

Music in the novel is not merely entertainment but a weapon and a spiritual force. The goblins have a physiological aversion to singing and rhythm — "They can't bear singing, and they can't stand that song" (006-029). Curdie exploits this weakness, using his songs and rhymes as a goblin-repellent during his underground explorations (019-024)(019-031). At the other end of the spectrum, the great-great-grandmother sings "a strange sweet song" of mysterious origin (022-060).

## Narrative Role

Curdie's singing is his most reliable tool in goblin territory. When working late in the mines, he sings rhymes about the goblins to protect himself (006-027)(006-029). He teaches this technique to others, noting that "if anyone gets frightened for want of me, they have only to sing" (006-041). During his capture by the goblins, Curdie uses improvised verses to mock and repel his captors (019-024)(019-031). The grandmother's song, by contrast, serves a different function — it signals her otherworldly nature and the novel's spiritual dimension (022-060).

## Significance

MacDonald uses music to map the novel's moral cosmos. Curdie's rough working-class rhymes repel goblins because goblins represent disorder and malice — forces that cannot coexist with rhythm and harmony. The grandmother's transcendent song represents the highest register of musical order, associated with divine beauty. Music thus functions as a spectrum from practical folk wisdom to supernatural grace.
