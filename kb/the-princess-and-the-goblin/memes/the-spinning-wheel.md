The **spinning wheel** is the defining object of Irene's [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|great-great-grandmother]]. It is present in every scene in the old lady's tower room, its constant hum providing the audible signature of her presence. From it comes the magical thread that guides Irene through darkness.

## First Encounter

When Irene first discovers the tower room, the spinning wheel is the first thing she sees. The old lady "sat spinning" (003-002), and the room itself contains "nothing but the spinning-wheel and the chair beside it" (003-021). The grandmother lives by her spinning — Irene's nurse later dismissively refers to her as "an old woman who made her bread by her spinning" (003-021). The simplicity of the scene — an old woman and her wheel — belies the supernatural power concentrated there.

## The Hum of the Wheel

The sound of the spinning wheel is Irene's guide. When she seeks her grandmother's room, she listens for its hum. The moment of recognition is joyful:

> There it was! it was the hum of the spinning-wheel! What a diligent grandmother to work both day and night!
>
> (011-008)

Conversely, when the wheel is silent, Irene knows something is wrong. After her night on the mountain, "there was no sound of the spinning-wheel. She grew frightened once more" (015-003). Later, when Curdie accompanies her, she "could she hear any sound of the spinning-wheel, and once more her heart sank" (022-002). The hum is a sonic beacon — its presence means safety, its absence means danger or concealment.

## The Magical Thread

The spinning wheel produces the magical thread. The grandmother spins "in the moonlight," and the thread shines "like — what shall I say it was like? It was not white enough for silver" (011-029). This thread, once wound into a ball and tempered in the rose fire, becomes Irene's guide — an invisible line of connection between grandmother and child that leads Irene through goblin tunnels and back to safety.

## A Symbol of Creation

The spinning wheel carries rich symbolic weight. In classical mythology, the Fates spin the thread of life. In MacDonald's Christian imagination, the grandmother's spinning suggests divine creation — patient, continuous, purposeful. She spins while Irene sleeps, while the world outside goes about its business, weaving something that will be needed later. The wheel represents a power that works quietly, persistently, and for the good of those who trust it.
