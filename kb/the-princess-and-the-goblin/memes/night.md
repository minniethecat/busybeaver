# Night

Night in [[The Princess and the Goblin]] is a threshold time — when the ordinary world of [[the-palace|the palace]] gives way to the hidden realm of [[goblins|the goblins]] and the mysterious presence of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the great-grandmother]]. It is simultaneously a time of danger and a time of wonder, when [[stars]] become visible and the old lady works at her [[the-grandmothers-lamp|spinning wheel]] (001-005, 011-033).

## The Goblins' Hour

Night is the [[goblins|goblins]]' domain. They "never came out but at night, and then seldom showed themselves in any numbers" (001-005). Their hammers and pickaxes can be heard beneath [[The Princess and the Goblin|the mountain]] after dark (008-061), and [[curdie|Curdie]] stays up alone through the night to listen to their schemes (007-008).

> The goblins held their meetings and planned their attacks at night (008-056).

The darkness gives the goblins courage, but it also makes them vulnerable — [[curdie|Curdie]] uses the cover of night to spy on them, and their nocturnal habits define them as creatures opposed to the [[sun|sunlight]].

## The Grandmother's Night Work

[[irenes-great-great-grandmother|The old lady]] spins at her wheel both day and night. When [[irene|Irene]] asks if she works all the time, she receives a precise answer:

> "I don't work every night — only [[moon|moonlit]] nights, and then no longer than the moon shines upon my wheel" (011-033).

Her lamp "never goes out, night or day" (011-053), and it is on a moonlit night that [[irene|Irene]] first finds her way to [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower room]] (011-004). The grandmother's night work is not labor but creation — spinning something beautiful while the world sleeps.

## Night as Revelation

For [[irene|Irene]], night becomes a time of revelation. Her first ascent to [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]] happens in darkness lit only by [[moon|moonlight]]:

> It was a lovely cloudless night, with the sky all out in full-blown stars — not one missing (011-002).

[[curdie|Curdie]], emerging from the [[goblins|goblin]] tunnels, remarks on how "pleasant the night air felt" (009-025) — suggesting that night above ground is wholesome compared to the darkness below. The novel distinguishes between the natural night (beautiful, star-filled, grandmother-blessed) and the unnatural darkness of the goblin underworld.

## Irene's Starry Eyes

[[irene|Irene]] herself is associated with the night sky. Her eyes are described as "like two bits of night sky, each with a star dissolved in it" (001-003), connecting the princess to the beauty and mystery of the nocturnal world from the novel's opening pages.

> She was a glorious sight in that lovely room, and the fire of roses, and the great lamp that sent its light through the whole universe (020-008).

## Significance

Night in MacDonald's novel is not merely a setting — it is a spiritual condition. The goblins hide in darkness but are blind to true light. [[irene|Irene]] walks through darkness but is guided by [[the-magical-thread|an invisible thread]]. The night reveals different things to different eyes: to the [[goblins|goblin]], a time to attack; to [[irene|Irene]], a time to discover; to [[curdie|Curdie]], a time to work. MacDonald suggests that [[courage-and-faith|faith]] transforms night from enemy to companion.
