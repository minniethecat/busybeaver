# Silver

Silver in *The Princess and the Goblin* appears as a recurring motif associated with the great-grandmother, the moon, and all that is pure and luminous. Unlike gold (associated with the king), silver belongs to the old lady's realm — her basin, her moon, her hair, and the thread she spins.

## The Silver Basin

The great-grandmother's silver basin is one of the first objects Irene encounters in the tower room. The old lady "stopped her wheel, and rose, and, going out of the room, returned with a little silver basin and a soft white towel" (003-020) and washes Irene's hands and face. Irene notes "how straight and tall she was" as the old lady "carried away the basin and towel" (003-021), the silver vessel marking the dignity of its bearer. The basin returns in a later visit: "she got a large silver basin, and having poured some water into it" (011-050), she washes Irene again — the silver basin becoming a recurring instrument of purification.

## Silvery Hair

The great-grandmother's hair is consistently described as silvery. Irene tells Lootie about "the old lady, you know, with the long hair of silver" (010-013). During a moonlit visit, Irene feels "a great longing woke up in her heart to try once more whether she could not find the old lady with the silvery hair" (011-005). When Irene finds her, she sits "in the middle of the moonlight" with "her silver" (011-010) — the hair blending with the moonbeams so that it is unclear where moonlight ends and silver hair begins.

## Silver Thread and Casket

The thread the grandmother spins "in the moonlight it shone like—what shall I say it was like? It was not white enough for silver" (011-029), yet its silvery quality is unmistakable. The grandmother also possesses "a curious silver casket" (011-041) from which she takes Irene's magical ring. These silver objects — basin, casket, thread — form a constellation of magical artifacts associated with the grandmother's power.

## Silver in the King's World

Silver also appears in the human realm. The king's beard has "silvery lines" (010-006) running through it, marking his age and wisdom. Curdie, a miner's son, works the rock for silver ore — the precious metal that connects the underground world of labour to the above-ground world of value and beauty.

## Silver and the Goblins

The goblins have a complicated relationship with silver. They are miners who know the value of precious metals — Curdie mentions "silver" among the treasures of the mountain (006-026). But silver also represents the "sun-people" they despise. Their queen's toes have "webs between them" and they prefer the dark places where "no light of the sun ever came" (007-006).

## The Silvery Voice

The grandmother's voice itself is described as silvery. When Irene reaches her door, she hears "the silvery voice of her grandmother" (015-002) calling her name. The adjective connects voice and moonlight and metal — the grandmother's speech is as pure and clear as the silver basin in which she washes Irene's face.

## Symbolic Meaning

Silver represents the feminine principle of purity and wisdom in contrast to gold's masculine authority. The great-grandmother — ancient, wise, and powerful — works in silver light, uses silver vessels, and creates threads of silver. She embodies a power that is older and deeper than the king's gold crown, and her silver artifacts mediate between the everyday world and the realm of magic and moon.
