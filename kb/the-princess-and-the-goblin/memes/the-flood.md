# The Flood

**The Flood** is the climactic disaster event in [[The Princess and the Goblin]] when the [[goblins]], upon the failure of their first plan to kidnap [[irene|Princess Irene]], execute their second scheme — releasing underground reservoirs to inundate [[the-mines|the king's mines]] and drown the miners. The water instead finds an unexpected outlet through the goblins' own tunnel into [[the-palace|the king's house]], ultimately flooding the goblin realm and destroying them.

## The Goblins' Second Scheme

The goblins' flood plan was conceived as a backup to their primary scheme of [[the-goblins-plot|abducting Princess Irene]]. [[curdie|Curdie]] had learned of this design while spying underground:

> He had all at once remembered the resolution of the goblins to carry out their second plan upon the failure of the first. No doubt they were already busy, and the mine was therefore in the greatest danger of being flooded and rendered useless — not to speak of the lives of the miners.
>
> (029-002)

The goblins' reasoning, as Curdie later explained to [[king-papa|the king]], was that the miners were of more importance to the upper world than they actually were (031-018).

## The Night of the Storm

The flood was unleashed during a tremendous storm. As Curdie and [[peter|Peter]] emerged from the mine, they found the mountain engulfed in tempest:

> The thunder was bellowing, and the lightning lancing out of a huge black cloud which lay above it and hung down its edges of thick mist over its sides. The lightning was breaking out of the mountain, too, and flashing up into the cloud. From the state of the brooks, now swollen into raging torrents, it was evident that the storm had been storming all day.
>
> (029-005)

The storm above ground mirrored the catastrophe below — as the goblins breached the underground reservoirs, nature itself seemed to conspire in the destruction.

## The Flood's Path

The goblins had let loose all the underground reservoirs and streams, expecting the water to run down into the mine, which was lower than their part of the mountain. They believed they had broken a passage through into it. However, as Curdie discovered while listening with his ear to the floor of the king's hall, the readiest outlet the water could find was the very tunnel the goblins had dug toward the king's house (031-018).

When the water burst forth, it came with tremendous force:

> Before he had reached the gate, the noise had grown to a great thundering roar, and the ground trembled beneath their feet, and before the last of them had crossed the court, out after them from the great hall door came a huge rush of turbid water, and almost swept them away.
>
> (031-014)

The king stood with [[irene|Irene]] in his arms, looking back with amazement on the issuing torrent, which glimmered fierce and foamy through the night (031-016).

## Rescue of the Horses

[[curdie|Curdie]] led the men-at-arms over the garden wall to the stables, where they found the horses in terror as the water rose rapidly around them. With no other way to get them out, Curdie mounted the king's white charger and led the way through the torrent, bringing all the horses safely to rising ground (031-023).

## The Goblins' Demise

The flood turned against its creators. [[curdie|Curdie]] returned with the news that dead goblins were tossing about in the current through the house:

> They had been caught in their own snare; instead of the mine they had flooded their own country, whence they were now swept up drowned.
>
> (031-032)

The goblins' own engineering had become the instrument of their destruction. The miners, whom the goblins had sought to destroy, were unharmed, as the solid wall [[peter|Peter]] and his fellows had built kept the water from entering the mine proper.

## Aftermath

The flood permanently ended the goblin threat. The waters eventually subsided, and the underground goblin realm was rendered uninhabitable. The [[goblins]] who survived the deluge were scattered and never again posed a danger to the kingdom.

The flood thus served as a narrative mechanism of poetic justice — the goblins' malice returning upon their own heads, while the innocent were preserved through the foresight of [[curdie|Curdie]] and the miners' hard work.
