# Goblins

The goblins are a subterranean race dwelling in the [[the-cave|cavern]]s and tunnels beneath [[the-mountain|the mountains]], near [[king-papa|the king]]'s [[the-palace|palace]]. Formerly surface-dwelling humans, they were driven underground and have evolved into grotesque, vengeful creatures who hate the people of the sunlit world, particularly their music and [[singing-as-protection|singing]] (001-005).

## Origin and Nature

The goblins once lived on the surface as ordinary humans, but for mysterious reasons they were driven underground generations ago. Over time, they adapted to subterranean life, growing physically strange and morally twisted. MacDonald describes them as dwelling in the "hollow places underneath" [[the-mountain|the mountains]] — "huge [[the-cave|cavern]]s, and winding ways" connected by natural passages and their own excavations (001-004).

They are not merely folklore but a real, organized society with a [[king-papa|king]], a queen, a [[the-palace|palace]], and a court (018-004, 026-011). Their physical appearance is variously described as grotesque, with some having hardened, toeless feet and others being described as animal-like in their features.

## Society and Culture

The goblins have a structured society with clear hierarchy:

- **[[the-goblin-king|The Goblin King]]**: Ruler of the goblins, who plots to kidnap [[irene|Princess Irene]] and marry her to his son (027-023)
- **The Goblin Queen**: Resides in the [[the-goblin-palace|goblin palace]], with a bodyguard and attendants (027-023)
- **The Goblin Court**: A hierarchical system with courtiers and servants (018-037)

They gather in [[the-hall|the great hall]] of their [[the-goblin-palace|underground palace]], where the Goblin King holds court and councils are convened to plan their campaigns against the surface world (009-006, 019-002).

The goblins work primarily at night, tunneling and expanding their underground domain (007-006). They have a deep antipathy toward the surface-dwelling humans, but their hatred is concentrated on specific targets:

- They cannot bear **[[singing-as-protection|singing]]** and music, which physically pains them (006-029)
- They [[fear]] the miners, especially [[curdie|Curdie]], who knows their weaknesses
- They were once "[[king-papa|the king]]'s subjects" but have since rebelled, owing allegiance to no one (017-016)

## Creatures

The goblins keep strange hybrid animals as pets and work-creatures, descended from surface animals that were taken underground generations ago:

> "The goblins, as Curdie had discovered, were much more animal than human in their nature; and their creatures shared in the degeneration." (013-006)

These creatures — called "[[the-goblins-creatures|cobs]]" or "[[the-goblins-creatures|cobbles]]" — are mentioned as physically warped and used by the goblins for labor and defense (013-004–013-006).

## Physical Traits and Weaknesses

Goblins are described as having several distinctive physical vulnerabilities:

- Their feet are soft and toeless — a major weakness that [[curdie|Curdie]] exploits by kic[[king-papa|king]] their feet in combat (001-005)
- They dislike [[water]], and [[the-palace|the palace]]'s [[water]] system is used against them
- They are physically strong but vulnerable to direct attacks on their feet

## Plot Against the Humans

The goblins' central plot drives the novel's conflict: they plan to flood [[the-mines|the king's mines]], kidnap [[irene|Princess Irene]], and marry her to the goblin prince Harelip (026-011). This plan is ultimately thwarted by [[curdie|Curdie]]'s resourcefulness (using his knowledge of goblin anatomy and mining), [[irene|Irene]]'s [[courage-and-faith|courage]] following the magical [[the-magical-thread|thread]], and the flooding of [[the-cave|the goblin caves]] — a divine judgment that washes their realm away (031-018).

## Significance

The goblins represent more than simple villains. MacDonald uses them to explore themes of degeneration, the consequences of exile from light and goodness, and the nature of evil. Their physical grotesqueness mirrors their moral condition — "much more animal than human" (013-006). They serve as a foil to the human characters, illustrating through contrast the values of [[courage-and-faith|courage]], [[courage-and-faith|faith]], and love that the protagonists embody.
