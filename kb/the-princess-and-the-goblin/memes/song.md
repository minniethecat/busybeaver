# Song

Song is a recurring motif in "The Princess and the Goblin," functioning as both a literal protective weapon against [[goblins|the goblins]] and a vehicle for spiritual expression. From [[curdie|Curdie]]'s defiant miner rhymes to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s strange sweet melody, song marks the boundary between the human and goblin worlds — a boundary that goblins cannot cross.

## Nature / Definition

Song in the novel is not mere entertainment — it is an active force with real effects. [[curdie|Curdie]]'s songs and rhymes cause [[goblins|the goblins]] physical distress, driving them away when other weapons fail. The goblins' inability to bear song is linked to their own voicelessness:

> "They can't bear singing, and they can't stand that song. They can't sing themselves, for they have no more voice than crows." (006-029)

This is the key to song's protective power: it is a uniquely human capacity that [[goblins|goblins]] lack. Song is a marker of creative intelligence, of language shaped by rhythm and feeling — precisely the faculties that the degraded, animalistic [[goblins|goblins]] have lost. Song protects because it asserts what [[goblins|goblins]] are not and cannot be.

> "They won't always mind that song even, down there. And if anyone should sing it at them, I don't believe it would do much good." (006-041)

Curdie's qualification — that the song may not always work, especially deep underground — keeps the magic grounded and believable. The song's power is proportional to the singer's courage and proximity to the sunlit world. It is not a spell that works mechanically; it is an expression of human spirit that [[goblins|goblins]] find unbearable.

## Curdie's Battle Songs

[[curdie|Curdie]] wields song as his primary weapon. His first goblin encounter turns on a counting rhyme chanted as he works underground:

> "Thirteen, fourteen, fifteen" — the chanting rhythm disturbs and drives off the goblin creatures (006-027–006-029).

In the goblin palace, he taunts them with improvised verse: "Once there was a goblin living in a hole" (019-024). When they protest, "returned Curdie, and began singing again: 'Go to bed, Goblin, do'" (019-031). Song is how Curdie arms himself when physical weapons are insufficient — it is inseparable from his courage.

During the climactic battle, Curdie is described as "stamping and slashing and singing" (027-010), becoming "a small incarnate whirlwind" of song and violence (027-011). Song and combat are fused — he fights with his pick in one hand and his voice as his shield.

## The Goblins' Counter-Song

The [[goblins|goblins]] have their own crude musical tradition, which serves as a dark parody of [[curdie|Curdie]]'s songs. In the tunnels, a goblin voice breaks out: "Jabber, bother, smash! You'll have them in a crash!" (021-003). [[irene|Irene]] mistakes it for Curdie: "Why, you were singing loud!" (021-007).

The goblin song is rhythmic and rhyming but violent and chaotic — a corrupted mirror of human song. It suggests that [[goblins|goblins]] retain a vestigial musical instinct, but it has been warped into something destructive rather than creative.

## The Grandmother's Healing Song

In contrast to [[curdie|Curdie]]'s martial songs, [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s singing is numinous and restorative. [[irene|Irene]] hears "the voice of the lady, singing a strange sweet song, of which she could distinguish no word" (022-060). This song transcends language — it is pure comfort, pure presence, the sound of divine love made audible.

Where Curdie's songs repel evil, the grandmother's songs draw the good toward safety. Both are protective, but one operates by driving away darkness and the other by calling toward light.

The absence of song is as meaningful as its presence. When Curdie is angry with [[irene|Irene]] for doubting him, he walks "neither whistling nor singing" (023-002) — the silence signals his broken trust. Song is the sound of a whole, courageous spirit; its absence marks division and doubt.

## Significance

Song in the novel represents the creative, spiritual dimension of humanity — the God-given capacity to shape language into beauty and rhythm. The [[goblins|goblins]] have lost this capacity through their degradation, and their inability to bear it reveals what they have become. Song is both the mark of human dignity and the weapon that defends it.

The two modes of song — Curdie's martial rhymes and the grandmother's wordless melody — suggest that song operates on multiple planes. On the earthly plane, it is a practical defense against evil. On the spiritual plane, it is the language of love that needs no words. Together, they assert that the human voice, raised in courage or in love, is the ultimate repudiation of everything [[goblins|the goblins]] represent.
