# Fear

Fear is a dominant emotional force in "The Princess and the Goblin," operating both as the [[goblins]]' primary weapon against the human characters and as a test of [[courage-and-faith|courage]] that [[king-papa|Irene]] and [[peter|Curdie]] must overcome. It is depicted as a force that can paralyze or be transcended through [[courage-and-faith|faith]] and action.

## Nature / Definition

Fear pervades the novel from its earliest chapters. The [[goblins|goblin]]s are described as [[irene|Irene]]'s primary source of fear:

> Formerly the goblins were her only fear (006-110)

But fear extends beyond [[goblins|the goblins]] to encompass the unknown, the dark, and the supernatural. The novel presents fear as a natural human response that can be overcome — not through its absence, but through [[trust]] in a higher power. When [[irene|Irene]] follows the [[the-magical-thread|magical thread]], her fear vanishes:

> Her fear vanished; once more she was confident and safe (021-002)

## Irene's Encounters with Fear

[[irene|Irene]]'s journey through the novel charts a progression from being controlled by fear to maste[[the-ring|ring]] it. Her early encounter in [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s chamber shows fear being overpowered by [[curiosity]]:

> curiosity was stronger than her fear, and she opened the door very softly (003-002)

When she flees from the [[the-goblins-creatures|long-legged cat creature]]:

> she ran, unable to think for fear, and ready to run anywhere (014-008)

But after reaching safety, her perspective transforms:

> she would fear nothing, not even the terrible creature (014-009)

The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]'s chamber offers complete refuge:

> she only knew there was no fear in her, and everything was so beautiful and restful (015-039)

## Fear and the Goblins

The [[goblins|goblin]]s explicitly weaponize fear. The [[king-papa]]'s [[men-at-arms]] fear supernatural [[goblins|goblin]] reprisal:

> they feared giving an alarm. They, therefore, said nothing (026-010)

Yet [[curdie|Curdie]]'s approach to fear is different — he confronts it head-on. After attac[[king-papa|king]] [[goblins|the goblins]]:

> he would have no fear of them; and if it were not for fear of their feet (019-048)

- [[light|The Grandmother]] — her presence eliminates fear entirely

## Significance

Fear in MacDonald's novel functions as a spiritual metaphor. Those who [[trust]] in [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] are freed from fear ([[irene|Irene]] following [[the-magical-thread|the thread]]), while those who rely only on themselves remain vulnerable to it. The progression from fear to fearlessness mirrors the spiritual journey from doubt to [[courage-and-faith|faith]].
