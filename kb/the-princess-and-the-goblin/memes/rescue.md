# Rescue

Rescue is the central narrative action of *The Princess and the Goblin*, structuring the novel's climax and establishing the bond between [[Irene]] and [[Curdie]]. The novel presents rescue not as a one-way heroic act but as a reciprocal pattern: Irene rescues Curdie from the goblin cave, and Curdie later rescues Irene and Lootie from the goblin attack. This mutual rescue establishes the partnership at the heart of the story and embodies the novel's theme that courage and faith are meant to be shared, not hoarded.

## Nature / Definition

Rescue in the novel is an act of crossing boundaries. Irene crosses from the safety of the surface world into the darkness of the [[goblins|goblin]] caves to rescue Curdie. Curdie crosses from the world of miners and practical action into the world of faith, accepting rescue from a thread he cannot see. Each rescue requires the rescuer to leave their own sphere and enter the other's.

The first rescue is set up by the thread. Irene follows [[the-magical-thread|her grandmother's thread]] "in total darkness" (020-008) until she finds Curdie imprisoned behind a wall of stone. The rescue is not violent — Irene does not fight goblins or break down doors. She comes, she finds, she frees. The power of rescue lies not in force but in presence and guidance.

> Irene too had to tell a long story — of how she had come to his rescue. (021-070)

Curdie's rescue of Irene and Lootie (Ch. 27) is the counterpart. When the goblins attack the house, Curdie "sprung to Lootie's rescue" (027-022), using physical force where Irene had used spiritual guidance. The parallelism is deliberate: Irene's rescue was quiet, following a thread; Curdie's is loud, stamping goblin feet. Together they demonstrate that rescue takes different forms depending on the rescuer's gifts.

> Having thus rescued the servants, they set about... (027-024)

The reciprocal structure is completed in the novel's denouement. Curdie "determined to find and rescue the princess as she had found and rescued him" (027-025). Rescue creates a debt, but it is a debt of love, not of obligation — Curdie rescues Irene not because he owes her but because her rescue of him has transformed him into the kind of person who rescues others.

The early rescue in Chapter 6 (006-104) foreshadows the pattern: Curdie comes to Irene's aid against the goblin creatures. But this is a minor rescue, a skirmish. The great rescues are yet to come — Irene's descent into the caves, and Curdie's defense of the house. The escalation from minor to major rescue tracks the growth of both characters.

> He determined to find and rescue the princess as she had found and rescued him. (027-025)

## Significance

Rescue is the novel's primary argument for community. Irene does not defeat the goblins alone; Curdie does not escape the cave alone. Each needs the other, and each is needed by the other. The goblins, by contrast, have no culture of rescue — when the flood comes, they are left "cowering without courage to attempt their escape" (027-018), because no one rescues anyone in goblin society.

The reciprocity of rescue also transforms the characters. Before Irene rescues him, Curdie cannot see the thread and does not fully believe in the grandmother. After being rescued, he is changed — not because he now sees the thread, but because he has experienced the reality of the grandmother's guidance through Irene's actions. Rescue is the novel's mechanism for transmitting faith: Irene's faith rescues Curdie physically, and that physical rescue opens the door to his spiritual transformation.

The grandmother orchestrates the rescues but does not perform them herself. She provides the thread, the lamp, the ring — the tools of rescue. But Irene must walk the dark passage, and Curdie must fight the goblins. The novel's theology of rescue is one of equipped agency: divine power provides, but human courage acts.
