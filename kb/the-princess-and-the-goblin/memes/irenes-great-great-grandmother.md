# Irene's Great-Great-Grandmother

A mysterious, ancient, and beautiful woman who dwells in a hidden [[the-grandmothers-tower|tower room]] of [[king-papa|the king]]'s [[the-palace|palace]]. She is simultaneously very old — [[irene|Irene]]'s great-great-grandmother — and perpetually young, appea[[the-ring|ring]] as a woman of twenty-three with golden hair or as an ancient figure with snow-white hair, depending on when and how [[irene|Irene]] sees her (003-002, 015-007).

## Role in the Story

The grandmother is the spiritual center of the novel. She is the source of the magical gifts that guide and protect [[irene|Irene]]: the [[the-magical-thread|invisible thread]], [[the-ring|the ring]], [[the-fire-roses|the fire roses]], and [[the-grandmothers-lamp|the lamp]]. She lives in a [[the-grandmothers-tower|tower room]] that most of [[the-palace|the palace]] inhabitants cannot find or do not believe exists, accessible only to those with [[courage-and-faith|faith]].

She first appears to [[irene|Irene]] when [[irene|the princess]], lost and crying, climbs [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]] stair and discovers her spinning (003-002–003-032). From that moment, the grandmother becomes [[irene|Irene]]'s protector and guide, appea[[the-ring|ring]] at critical moments to offer comfort, cleansing, and direction.

## Appearance and Traits

The grandmother's appearance shifts between two forms:

- As the ancient spinner: white hair "almost as snow," wise eyes, a sweet but old voice, dressed simply at her spinning-wheel (003-002–003-004)
- As the eternal beauty: golden hair "streaming like a cataract," dressed in pale blue velvet, face of a woman of three-and-twenty (015-007)

She is both maternal and mysterious. She never forces [[irene|Irene]] to believe but invites [[courage-and-faith|faith]] gently. Her room is filled with magical [[light]], moon-like and star-like, and her pigeons come and go as they please (003-073).

## Key Relationships

- [[irene|Princess Irene]] — her great-great-granddaughter; the grandmother guides, protects, and cleanses her
- [[curdie|Curdie]] — unable to see the grandmother at first, he eventually feels [[the-magical-thread|the thread]] she spun
- [[king-papa|The King]] — her son, who meets her at the novel's climax
- The pigeons — her messengers and companions, guided home by her [[the-grandmothers-lamp|lamp]]

## Significance and Themes

The grandmother is widely interpreted as a representation of divine wisdom or God in MacDonald's Christian allegory. She is ancient beyond reckoning yet eternally youthful; she is both within the world ([[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]] in [[the-palace|the palace]]) and beyond it (most cannot find or see her); she guides without controlling, protects without removing danger, and asks only for [[courage-and-faith|faith]].

Her spinning of [[the-magical-thread|the thread]] evokes the classical image of the Fates or the Norns, but MacDonald transforms this into a Christian figure of providence — one who weaves not destiny but guidance, and who promises to hold the other end of every [[the-magical-thread|thread]].
