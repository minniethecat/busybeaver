# Joan Peterson

Joan [[peter|Peter]]son is the mother of [[peter|Curdie]] and wife of [[peter|Peter the miner]] in "The Princess and the Goblin." Though a minor character in terms of page time, she plays a crucial narrative role: her quiet wisdom and [[courage-and-faith|faith]] enable [[curdie|Curdie]] to accept the reality of [[light|the grandmother]] after his skeptical first encounter.

## Role in the Story

[[curdie|Curdie]]'s mother is introduced as a woman of deep, understated wisdom:

> Peterson was such a nice good mother! All mothers are nice and good, but Mrs. Peterson was better than most (012-004)

When [[curdie|Curdie]] returns from his failed encounter with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] — unable to see her — it is his mother who interprets his experience and restores his [[courage-and-faith|faith]]:

> At last Curdie's mother spoke (023-009)

She does not dismiss his story but reframes it:

> "There are things other people can't see, Curdie," said his mother very gravely. "You must accept that." (023-018)

> "I wanted to tell you," replied his mother. "But first, I may as well mention that I believe every word you say." (023-022)

## Appearance and Traits

[[curdie|Curdie]]'s mother is characterized by her quietness and the weight her words carry:

> the less his mother said, the more Curdie believed she had to say (012-007)

This economy of speech gives her an almost oracular quality — when she does speak, [[curdie|Curdie]] listens. Her wisdom is practical and grounded, rooted in a miner's wife's life of hard work and family care.

She is perceptive — when [[curdie|Curdie]] tells of emerging in [[king-papa|the king]]'s garden, she immediately grasps the significance:

> "Oh, Curdie," cried his mother, "then the princess must have been in the mountain!" (023-042)

## Key Relationships

- [[peter|Curdie]] — her son, whose [[courage-and-faith|faith]] she guides and restores
- [[peter|Peter the miner]] — her husband (mentioned in Ch. 12, 23)
- [[light|The Grandmother]] — she seems to know of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s existence independently

## Significance

Joan [[peter|Peter]]son represents the novel's ideal of quiet, grounded [[courage-and-faith|faith]]. Unlike [[curdie|Curdie]], who must see to believe, his mother accepts the reality of the invisible without evidence. She embodies what [[curdie|Curdie]] must learn — that some things are true whether one can perceive them or not.
