# Door

Doors in *The Princess and the Goblin* are not merely architectural features; they are symbolic thresholds marking the boundary between the known and the unknown. Every important journey in the novel begins with the opening of a door, and every encounter with the magical requires crossing one.

## The Passages of Doors

Irene's first adventure begins when she opens a door and enters a labyrinth of passages: "It was full of doors on each side. There were so many doors!" [002-005]. Running through these passages, she finds "Nothing but passages and doors everywhere!" [002-006] — a geography of endless thresholds that symbolizes her expanding world.

## The Great-Grandmother's Door

The old lady's door is found at "a little square place, with three doors, two opposite each other, and one opposite the top of the stair" [003-002]. The princess taps "gently at the door" [011-008] and enters to find the moonlit room. This door is special — the old lady says, "nobody could find the room except I pleased" [011-055], making the door both a physical and magical barrier.

## Doors of Escape and Return

When threatened by goblin creatures, Irene finds the nursery door a refuge: "just outside the nursery door, she imagined the creature running after her" [014-008]. Yet "if she were but indoors again, she would fear nothing" [014-009] — the door marks the threshold between danger and safety.

## Doors Underground

The goblins too have their doors. Curdie watches "the door of Glump's new abode" [009-020], and the goblins seek "to keep the door of communication open" [009-023] between their underground world and the palace above. These doors represent the goblins' conspiracy and their desire to breach the boundary between worlds.

## Symbolic Meaning

Doors embody the novel's central tension between enclosure and freedom, safety and exploration. Characters who open doors — Irene, Curdie — grow in courage and understanding. Characters who fear doors — Lootie — remain trapped in the nursery.
