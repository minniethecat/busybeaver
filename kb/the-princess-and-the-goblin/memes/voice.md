**Voice** is a significant narrative motif in *The Princess and the Goblin*. Throughout the story, the quality and character of voices serve as a reliable indicator of moral nature — gentle, musical voices belong to the good, while harsh, growling voices belong to the goblins. The motif reaches its deepest expression in the voice of Irene's [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|great-great-grandmother]], which guides both Irene and Curdie through darkness.

## The Grandmother's Voice

Irene's great-great-grandmother speaks with a distinctive voice that changes over the course of the story. When Irene first meets her, the voice is described as "a sweet, but old and rather shaky voice, which mingled very pleasantly with the continued hum of her wheel" (003-004). By later encounters, the voice has transformed — becoming purely "silvery" (015-002), "sweet" and musical (011-009, 022-003).

The grandmother's voice has a quality that transcends ordinary sound. On one occasion Irene hears it "so plainly through the door" that she wonders at it (015-005). At a pivotal moment, Curdie hears the grandmother's voice singing "a strange sweet song" from somewhere he cannot locate (022-060), marking his first direct perception of her presence.

## Curdie's Song and Goblin Voices

Curdie's method of repelling goblins relies directly on voice. He discovers that goblins cannot bear singing, and his improvised rhymes make them scatter in terror:

> "They can't bear singing, and they can't stand that song. They can't sing themselves, for they have no more voice than a crow; and they don't like other people to sing."
>
> (006-029)

The goblins' own voices are characterised as harsh and animalistic — a "hoarse indistinct voice" (006-019), "rough and deeper" tones (008-004), and low growls "as inarticulate as if the goblin's tongue had been a sausage" (008-057). Their conversation is punctuated with bursts of "coarse tittering" (006-019).

## The Goblin Council

In the goblin palace hall, the motif of voice reaches a comedic peak. The goblin king speaks "in a slow and stately voice" (009-011), mimicking courtly formality while his subjects respond in chorus — "Glump," answered several voices (009-010). The juxtaposition of attempted dignity with brutish names and growling voices highlights the story's satire of pretension.

## Voice as Truth and Trust

The motif builds toward a thematic conclusion: voice is connected to truth. When the goblins lie, their voices betray them — they are hoarse, indistinct, and false. When the grandmother speaks, her voice is sweet and true. Irene's own voice trembles with fright (006-042), but this honest fear stands in contrast to goblin deception. The grandmother's voice becomes a guiding thread, audible even through stone walls, revealing that truth can be heard if one has the ears — and the faith — to listen.
