# Secret

A secret in The Princess and the Goblin is a piece of knowledge deliberately concealed or selectively shared, used by George MacDonald as both a plot mechanism and a moral test. Secrets circulate through every level of the novel's world — among [[goblins]], between [[irene]] and [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]], between [[curdie]]'s family and the miners — and each secret shapes the loyalties and betrayals that drive the story forward.

## Nature and Definition

The secret is fundamentally an asymmetrical distribution of knowledge. One character knows what another does not, and this gap creates narrative tension. MacDonald uses secrets not merely for suspense but as instruments of moral education: what a character does with a secret — whether they keep it faithfully, reveal it rashly, or use it for harm — reveals their essential nature.

The goblins' world is structured around secrets. Their plot against [[irene]] and the kingdom is a secret they keep from the surface-dwellers, discussed in their underground councils. The goblin queen's physical deformity is likewise a secret, known only to a few:

> I must first tell you a secret. I once saw the queen's feet (008-031).

Secrets among the goblins are weapons: withheld knowledge that enables their schemes and protects their vulnerabilities. By contrast, the secrets shared between [[irene]] and [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]] are gifts — knowledge given in love that deepens trust rather than enabling harm.

## Secrets of the Grandmother

[[irenes-great-great-grandmother|The grandmother]]'s most important secrets are shared with [[irene]] alone. The existence of the magical thread, the nature of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s tower, and the identity of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] herself are all secrets that [[irene]] must learn to keep. [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|The grandmother]] tells her:

> I will tell you a secret — if that light were to go out you would fancy yourself lying in a bare garret (011-055).

This secret — that the magical beauty of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s room depends entirely on the rose-fire's light — teaches [[irene]] that what seems solid and permanent may in fact be contingent on a hidden source. It is a secret about the nature of reality itself, and [[irene]] receives it in trust.

## Secrets Among the Miners

The human community also trades in secrets for protection. [[curdie-mother|Curdie's mother]] is brought into "the secret" of the goblin threat because she can be trusted:

> they had taken Mrs. Peterson into the secret, for they knew mother could hold her tongue (012-002).

This practical secret binds the human community together against a common enemy. The miners share intelligence about goblin movements in confidence, and this network of shared secrets proves essential to the kingdom's defense.

The goblins themselves understand the power of secrecy in warfare. [[curdie]] overhears their plans only by hiding and listening — by piercing their secret. The goblin council speaks openly only because they believe themselves unwatched, never imagining a human could penetrate:

> he even to imagine in his most secret thoughts? (009-018)

And at the novel's climax, the goblins regroup for:

> another try after their secret (021-088)

— the secret being their hidden plot, which must be protected at all costs.

## Significance

The secret operates as a moral device in the novel. Characters who keep secrets faithfully — [[irene]] with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]], [[curdie]] with his father, [[curdie-mother|Mrs. Peterson]] with the mining community — are rewarded with deeper relationships and practical victories. Characters who cannot be trusted with secrets — those who would blab or betray — are excluded from the inner circles of knowledge and power. MacDonald thus uses secrets to build a moral hierarchy: the trustworthy are admitted to knowledge, and knowledge enables effective action against [[goblins|evil]].

The secret also structures the novel's theology. [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|The grandmother]]'s existence is itself a kind of secret — visible only to those who have [[courage-and-faith|faith]], inexplicable to those who do not. The deepest secrets in the novel are not plots or plans but realities: the grandmother is real, the thread leads true, the light protects. These are secrets only in the sense that they are hidden from the faithless, available to all who will trust and climb the [[stair|stairs]].
