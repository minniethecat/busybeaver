The **globe of light** is one of the most powerful supernatural manifestations in *The Princess and the Goblin*. It appears at moments of crisis as a visible sign of Irene's [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|great-great-grandmother]]'s protective power, emanating light in the darkness and releasing the white bird that guides and reassures the protagonists.

## The Globe in the Sky

The globe first appears during the floods. Irene, Curdie, and the king witness it hovering "somewhere about the top of the king's house, a great globe of light" (031-025). The light shines against the darkness of the storm and flood, a beacon visible to all three characters simultaneously — marking a rare moment when the grandmother's magic is perceived by more than one person at once.

## The White Bird

The globe's most dramatic manifestation comes when the king, Curdie, and Irene face the subterranean waters. As they turn their eyes toward the globe:

> The same moment there shot from it a white bird, which, descending with outstretched wings, made one circle round the king and Curdie and the princess, and then glided up again. The light and the pigeon vanished together.
>
> (031-029)

Irene immediately understands the meaning: "you see my grandmother knows all about it, and isn't frightened" (031-029). The globe and bird together constitute a visible theophany — a manifestation of the grandmother's awareness and power.

## Earlier Appearances

Before its dramatic appearance at the flood, the globe is foreshadowed. Irene, running uphill at night, sees her grandmother's lamp which "guided her pigeons home through the darkest night" — this lamp is a smaller, constant version of the great globe, always burning in the tower (014-009). The lamp and the globe are two manifestations of the same light — one steady and domestic, the other spectacular and public.

## The Globe as Divine Light

The globe of light functions as a symbol of divine providence in MacDonald's Christian-influenced imagination. It appears when human effort is exhausted — when Curdie cannot dig further, when the waters rise, when all seems lost. The light does not solve the problem directly; instead, it signals presence, awareness, and protection. Irene's confidence in the light models the faith that the novel's religious subtext encourages: trust in an unseen benevolent power that manifests itself at the right moment.
