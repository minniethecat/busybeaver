# Moon

In [[The Princess and the Goblin]], the moon serves as both a natural phenomenon and a supernatural symbol closely associated with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]. Her [[the-grandmothers-lamp|lamp]] and its [[light]] are repeatedly compared to moonlight, and she tells [[irene|Irene]] she spins only on moonlit nights (011-033).

## The Moon and the Grandmother

The most significant connection between the moon and the supernatural occurs in the grandmother's tower. [[irene|Irene]] mistakes the [[the-grandmothers-lamp|grandmother's lamp]] for the moon rising:

> At first she thought the rising moon had left her place, and drawn near to the very foot of the old tower (014-009).

This association is deliberate. [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|The grandmother]] explains that her moon serves as a beacon for her messenger birds:

> "When I want a particular bird to deliver a message, they always see my moon and know where to fly to" (011-053).

## Moonlight as Guiding Light

[[irene|Irene]]'s nighttime journey up [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]] is described as following moonlight through the dark passages:

> The moon was shining brightly into the window (011-004).

Even when there is no natural moon in the dark stairwell, [[irene|Irene]] finds [[light|moonlight]] guiding her — the grandmother's supernatural lamp filling the same symbolic role:

> In the dark, for there was no moon there. Yes! it was! it was the light of the grandmother's lamp (011-008).

## Significance and Themes

The moon in MacDonald's novel is not merely atmospheric — it is a sign of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s active presence in the world. Its light bridges the natural and [[supernatural]], visible to those with [[courage-and-faith|faith]] (like [[irene|Irene]]) and glimpsed even by skeptics (like [[curdie|Curdie]]). The moon's consistent association with night, guidance, and hidden beauty reinforces the novel's theme that the divine operates in ways the world cannot see.
