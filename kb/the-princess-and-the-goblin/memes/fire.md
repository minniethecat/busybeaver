Fire appears in the novel in three contrasting forms: the grandmother's enchanted rose fire, the goblins' subterranean torches, and the ordinary hearth fire of home. Each type of fire reveals something about the world it illuminates and the beings who tend it (015-006, 009-023, 018-003).

## Nature / Definition

Fire in the novel is not a single concept but a spectrum. At one end is the grandmother's "rose fire" — a fire "which burned in the shapes of roses, red and white" (015-007). It is magical, beautiful, and safe: the grandmother can put her hand into it (015-067). At the other end is the goblins' fire — "the explosive firedamp" of the mines (009-023) and the flickering light that reveals their gatherings (018-003). Between them lies ordinary domestic fire — Curdie's forge, the hearth where his mother waits (028-003).

> "I've lighted a fire for you, Irene: you're cold and wet. Come and warm yourself." (015-006)

### The Rose Fire

The rose fire is the grandmother's element. It burns in sculptural forms: "a fire which burned in the shapes of roses, red and white" (015-007). It does not consume — the grandmother "put her hand in the fire, brought out the ball, glimmering as it went" (015-067). The fire-opal, the novel's other great magical object, is drawn from this fire (015-050, 015-063). Irene sits "gazing, now at the rose fire, now at the starry walls" (015-039), the fire becoming a focus of wonder and safety in the grandmother's tower.

> The lady put her hand in the fire, brought out the ball, glimmering as it went. (015-067)

### Goblin Fire

Goblin fire is the antithesis of the rose fire. It flickers, smokes, and vanishes into darkness: "the smoke of which vanished in the darkness" (018-004). Where the rose fire is steady and beautiful, goblin fire is unstable and treacherous — Curdie nearly falls into it during combat (018-037). The goblins need fire for light but cannot master it: "they had left the fire burning" (019-047) as they fled, their domain never fully illuminated.

### Domestic Fire

Between these two poles sits the ordinary fire of home. Curdie's mother sits "by the fire" with Irene in her arms (028-003), and the king repeatedly tries "to light the fire which had been drowned by the overflow of the mine" (029-008). Domestic fire is neither magical nor threatening — it is simply human, the fire of warmth and welcome.

## Narrative Role

The grandmother's rose fire marks her tower as a space of magic and safety — Irene warms herself by it, and the fire-opal is drawn from it (015-050). Goblin fire, by contrast, marks their realm as one of flickering half-light, where "the light flickered as if from a fire behind" stone walls (018-003). Curdie observes goblins around their fire (018-003), and later almost falls into it in combat (018-037). Fire also appears in the forge, where Curdie works as a miner — the practical fire of human industry.

The fire motif recurs at key emotional moments. After the escape, Irene and Curdie find themselves by "the last embers of the expiring fire" (021-040) — the dying fire marking the end of their ordeal. In the grandmother's tower during Curdie's visit, "the lady, who was seated by a fire of red roses mingled with white" (022-004) receives him, and the rose fire remains invisible to him until the grandmother reveals it: "Don't you see the lovely fire of roses — white ones amongst the red?" (022-017).

## Significance

Fire in the novel maps onto the story's moral geography. The rose fire is associated with the grandmother — warm, beautiful, nurturing, and governed by a higher law. Goblin fire is associated with the underground — flickering, smoky, treacherous, subject to the chaos of the goblin world. The domestic fire of hearth and forge occupies the middle ground: ordinary, human, vulnerable. That the grandmother controls fire without being burned signals her transcendence of natural law, while the goblins' inability to master fire (it smokes, flickers, and one nearly falls into it) signals their incomplete dominion over nature.

The rose fire is also identified with life and restoration. In the final healing scene, the grandmother "went to the fire. Putting in her hand, she drew forth something" (022-050) — the fire-opal once more, used to restore. Fire in the grandmother's hands is generative, not destructive. It gives rather than consumes.
