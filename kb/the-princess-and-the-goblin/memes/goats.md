# Goats

Mountain goats appear as part of the [[miners]]' pastoral life on [[The Princess and the Goblin|the mountain]] and serve as a simile for [[curdie|Curdie]]'s natural agility and connection to the terrain (017-016, 028-002).

## Goats and the Miners

The [[miners]] keep goats alongside their mining work — a detail that grounds the mountain community in a realistic pastoral economy:

> The goats belonged to the miners mostly — a few of them to Curdie's mother (017-016).

The goats represent the above-ground, daylight life of the mountain folk, contrasting with the subterranean [[goblins|goblins]] who keep grotesque creatures in darkness.

## Curdie the Goat

[[curdie|Curdie]]'s physical prowess is explicitly compared to a goat's. After escaping from [[goblins|the goblins]], he climbs:

> Like one of its own goats, he went up the mountain (028-002).

This goat-like agility is not merely athletic — it is essential to [[curdie|Curdie]]'s role as protector. His ability to traverse the mountain, to find [[goblins|goblin]] tunnels, and to carry warnings between [[the-palace|the palace]] and the [[miners|mine]] depends on his sure-footedness in dangerous terrain.

## Significance

The goats contribute to MacDonald's broader pattern of associating goodness with honest labour and natural life. Unlike the [[goblins|goblins]]' subterranean creatures, goats are useful, beautiful, and free — part of the mountain's healthy ecology that the [[miners]] steward.
