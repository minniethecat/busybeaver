# Stream

A recurring natural element in *The Princess and the Goblin* — from the "great brown streams tumbling down everywhere" on the mountainside to the underground water channels threading through goblin tunnels. Streams serve as pathways, obstacles, companions, and the ultimate means of victory over the [[goblins]] (006-004, 013-006, 031-018).

## Nature / Definition

Streams flow at every level of the mountain, from the rain-swollen torrents on the surface to the "tiny stream of water running down" through underground passages (009-022). They are the mountain's circulatory system, carrying water from the heights to the depths. After storms, the streams become fierce and dangerous; in fair weather, they are gentle and charming, "jabber[ing] and prattl[ing] down the road" (020-007).

The mountain's hydrology is complex: natural reservoirs feed underground streams, which the goblins have accidentally tapped:

> The goblins, as Curdie had discovered, were mining on a new plan — they had broken into the channel of a small stream (013-006).

This accidental breach in the stream channel is the germ of the goblins' own destruction. The water they hoped to use as a weapon against the miners instead becomes the means by which the entire goblin realm is flooded.

## Narrative Role

Streams appear throughout the novel as guides, challenges, and agents of plot resolution. For [[irene|Irene]]'s first mountain walk, a stream forms "a watery arch" over ferns, falling into "a great rock basin that was filled" with "crystal clear water" (006-005). Later, a stream leads her and Lootie directly to [[curdie|Curdie]]: "until they came to a huge rock, where a little stream rushed out. They followed its course" (007-005).

Underground, a stream becomes [[irene|Irene]]'s literal guide. Following [[the-magical-thread|the magical thread]], she moves alongside a stream that seems almost like a living companion:

> By and by she came to a little stream that jabbered and prattled down the road (020-007).

The stream's sound reassures her in the dark, and she ultimately escapes by following its channel:

> She danced and clapped her hands with delight as she followed by the channel of the little stream (021-094).

## The Flood: Stream as Weapon

The streams' most dramatic narrative function is their role in the goblins' defeat. The goblins have been "mining on a new plan" — deliberately "breaking outlets for the water accumulated in the natural reservoirs" (009-023), intending to flood the mines. But [[curdie|Curdie]] discovers "the tunnel they had made to the king's house" and realizes that the streams can be redirected to destroy the goblins instead (031-018).

The flood becomes an act of poetic justice: the goblins who sought to destroy the miners with water are themselves swept away. The novel ends with "dead goblins tossing about in the currents" (031-032) as the mountain's waters reclaim the hollow places the goblins had usurped. The streams, which have been characters in their own right throughout the story, become the final agents of resolution.

## Significance

MacDonald's streams are not merely scenery — they embody a theology of nature in which the natural world actively cooperates with divine purpose. The stream that guides Irene, the stream that Curdie braves for his mother, and the flood that destroys evil all suggest a creation that is alive and responsive. Water, in MacDonald's symbolic vocabulary, is simultaneously beautiful (the "watery arch" of 006-005), sustaining, and capable of justice.

The streams' triple function — beauty, guidance, judgment — mirrors the grandmother's own character: gentle with [[irene|Irene]] and those who trust her, yet a force of reckoning for those who oppose her order. The goblins' fatal mistake is trying to weaponize water against the natural order; the water instead obeys its deeper purpose.
