# The Goblin Queen

The Goblin Queen is the royal consort of the [[goblins|Goblin King]] and a figure of authority in the [[goblins|goblin]] realm. She is distinguished from common [[goblins|goblin]]s by her stone shoes — a mark of royalty — and participates in the [[goblins|goblin]] council's [[the-goblins-plot|plot]] against the human inhabitants of the [[the-mountain|mountain]].

## Identity and Status

The [[goblins|goblin]] queen is first mentioned indirectly through a conversation between [[curdie|Curdie]] and a [[goblins|goblin]] family. The [[goblins|goblin]] explains that unlike common [[goblins|goblin]]s who go barefoot, the queen wears shoes:

> "The queen does." (008-018)

This is explicitly a mark of distinction:

> "for distinction. The first queen, you see—I mean the king's first wife—wore shoes." (008-019)

The queen's feet are a subject of [[goblins|goblin]] lore and [[curiosity]]:

> "I once saw the queen's feet." (008-031)

The description of her feet is grotesque:

> "the ends of her feet were wonderfully broad and could spread themselves out like a fan" (008-037)

## Properties

The [[goblins|goblin]] queen shares the distinctive [[goblin-feet|goblin foot]] anatomy — soft, vulnerable, and normally hidden by stone shoes. Her stone shoes are the primary marker of her royal status, setting her apart from barefoot common [[goblins|goblin]]s.

## Role in the Plot

The queen appears in the climactic scenes. Du[[the-ring|ring]] [[curdie|Curdie]]'s confrontation underground:

> While the queen and her party had encountered difficulties (027-018)

She is present with [[king-papa|the king]] at the [[the-goblin-palace|goblin court]]:

> Curdie recognized as the king and queen of the goblins (021-051)

In the novel's resolution, her fate is recorded:

> they found a multitude of dead goblins — among the rest the queen, with her feet crushed by her own stone shoes (032-004)

Her death — crushed by the very shoes that marked her royal status — is a fitting ironic punishment that echoes [[goblins|the goblins]]' vulnerability: the feet they are so ashamed of become her undoing.

## Significance

The Goblin Queen embodies [[goblins|the goblins]]' central irony: their obsession with hiding their perceived weakness (their feet) leads to their destruction. The queen's stone shoes — her mark of distinction — become the instrument of her death. MacDonald uses her story as an illustration of how pride in cove[[the-ring|ring]] weakness ultimately fails.
