# Underground

The underground in [[The Princess and the Goblin]] is both a literal geography — the network of mines, natural caverns, and goblin dwellings beneath [[the-mountain]] — and a moral landscape. It is the realm where [[curdie]] and [[miners|the miners]] work honorably, where [[goblins|the goblins]] scheme in darkness, and where the line between courage and fear is drawn.

## Nature and Definition

The underground world is characterized by perpetual darkness, labyrinthine passages, and the absence of all natural light. Even the miners, who spend their days there, cannot distinguish night from day below ground: "you could not tell night from day down there, except from feeling tired and sleepy; for no light of the sun ever came into those gloomy regions" (007-006). The temperature is warm but oppressive — "it is very warm underground" (007-006) — creating an environment that is physically taxing even for hardened workers.

The underground is divided between two competing domains. [[miners|The miners]] work the mountain's veins of precious ore in excavated "gangs" — narrow passages carved from living rock. [[goblins|The goblins]] occupy the deeper, natural caverns and the abandoned tunnels beyond the mine's boundary, constantly digging new chambers and passages of their own. The barrier between these two domains is thin and porous: the miners' excavations have broken through into goblin territory, and the goblins themselves tunnel ceaselessly toward the surface, threatening both mine and kingdom.

The goblins' underground existence is presented as both cause and consequence of their degeneration. Having retreated below ground generations ago to escape the king's taxes, they have "greatly altered in the course of generations; and no wonder, seeing they lived away from the sun, in cold and wet and dark places" (001-005). Their physical grotesqueness and moral corruption are explicitly linked to their subterranean habitat.

## Narrative Role

The underground is the novel's primary arena of conflict. It is where [[curdie]] works, spies on [[goblins|the goblins]], and fights them. It is where [[irene]] descends — guided by [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]]'s invisible thread — to rescue [[curdie]] from the goblin cave (020-010). And the novel's climax involves the strategic manipulation of the underground itself: [[miners|the miners]] turn the goblins' own flooding scheme against them by sealing passages and redirecting underground reservoirs (031-018).

For [[curdie]], the underground is a place of both labor and reconnaissance. He navigates it with a ball of string anchored to his pickaxe, descending nightly into goblin territory to discover their plans. His comfort in the dark — "he was not afraid of the cobs, he was afraid of not finding his way out" (018-003) — distinguishes him from other miners who refuse overnight work.

## Significance

MacDonald uses the underground as more than a setting — it is a physical metaphor for spiritual and moral states. The darkness of the goblins' realm corresponds to their spiritual darkness: they are creatures who cannot bear light, who scheme in secret, and whose cunning increases as their form degenerates. The miners, by contrast, bring light (their headlamps) and song (their rhyming verses) into the darkness, transforming the underground into a place of honest labor. [[curdie]]'s ability to navigate the underground without fear — armed only with a pickaxe, a ball of string, and his wits — represents the triumph of human courage and practical skill over the terrors of the unknown.
