# Morning

Morning in *The Princess and the Goblin* is the time of return, restoration, and new understanding. After the perils and revelations of night, morning brings the ordinary world back into focus — breakfast, conversation, and the confirmation that the extraordinary events of darkness were real.

## Morning Rituals

The day begins with domestic care. The great-grandmother asks Irene, "And what would you have for breakfast this morning?" [003-064], serving bread and milk in the tower room. Morning is a time of sharing and sustenance. When Irene wakes in her own bed after visiting the tower, morning confirms that her journey was not merely a dream.

## Morning After Danger

After Curdie's night in the goblin tunnels, morning brings safety and respite. The daylight drives the goblins back underground. "The sun was not yet up, but some of the first rays had reached the mountain tops" [020-005] when Irene begins her dawn journey following the thread. Morning light transforms the frightening landscape into something beautiful: "everything was so fresh and cool and lively" [020-006].

## Morning as Return

The novel's pattern is nocturnal adventure followed by morning return. The great-grandmother's thread guides Irene back before sunrise — her adventures occupy the liminal space between night and day. Morning represents the ordinary world's resilience, its capacity to absorb the magical and carry on.
