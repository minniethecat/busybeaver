# The Petersons' Cottage

The [[peter|Peter]]sons' cottage is the family home of [[peter|Curdie]], his mother [[curdie-mother|Joan Peterson]], and his father [[peter|Peter the miner]]. It sits on [[the-mountain|the mountains]]ide above the [[the-mines|mines]] and represents honest poverty, domestic warmth, and the simple goodness of the mining folk in "The Princess and the Goblin."

## Location and Description

The cottage is situated on [[the-mountain|the mountains]]ide, accessible by a path that turns off the main [[the-mountain|mountain]] road:

> "It's on the other side of those rocks the path turns off to my father's." (006-080)

It is a modest dwelling — a "poor cottage on the high hillside" (012-004) — yet it is described with warmth and affection:

> a little heaven in that poor cottage on the high hillside (012-004)

The cottage is small enough that [[curdie|Curdie]] can compare it to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s [[the-grandmothers-tower|tower chamber]]:

> "like the one in mother's cottage, only big enough to take the whole of our cottage" (022-022)

## Narrative Appearances

The cottage appears at key moments in [[curdie|Curdie]]'s story:

1. **[[curdie|Curdie]]'s return from adventure** (Ch. 7): After his first encounter with [[irene|Irene]]:

   > Curdie went home whistling (007-002)

2. **The mother's wisdom** (Ch. 23): [[curdie|Curdie]] tells his mother of his experience with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] inside the cottage:

   > as well as the floor of our own cottage (023-022)

3. **[[curdie|Curdie]]'s recovery** (Ch. 28–29): After being wounded, [[curdie|Curdie]] is brought home:

   > finding him home to his mother's cottage (028-002)

   > he darted out of the cottage (028-046)

   The cottage is threatened by flood [[water]]s:

   > two roaring and dangerous streams, united again in front of the cottage (029-005)

4. **[[irene|Irene]]'s perspective** (Ch. 29): Even [[irene|the princess]] appreciates cottage life:

   > "how nice it must be to live in a cottage on the mountain!" (029-009)

## Narrative Role

The cottage embodies the novel's positive portrayal of honest poverty. Unlike the grand [[the-palace|palace]], it is a place of simple goodness — the home of the [[courage-and-faith|faith]]ful, hardwor[[king-papa|king]] [[peter|Peter]]son family. [[curdie|Curdie]]'s origins in this humble cottage, rather than in noble chambers, underscore the novel's democratic spiritual vision: worth comes from character, not station.
