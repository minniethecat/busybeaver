A recurring gesture of affection and blessing throughout *The Princess and the Goblin*, where kisses mark pivotal emotional moments and challenge social conventions about who may touch whom (004-034, 006-083, 011-050). The ungiven kiss to Curdie becomes a deferred promise that the novel's entire plot works to fulfill.

## Nature / Definition

Kisses in the novel serve multiple functions: as maternal comfort (the nurse kissing Irene, 004-038), as royal blessing (the grandmother kissing Irene's forehead, 011-050), as filial love (the king kissing Irene, 017-012), and as a social boundary-crossing that Lootie fiercely polices: "A princess mustn't give kisses. It's not at all proper" (006-096). The tension between natural affection and social propriety makes the kiss a charged gesture throughout.

## Narrative Role

### The Unkept Promise

The kiss drama unfolds around Irene's promise to kiss Curdie. When Curdie rescues Irene from the goblin creature, she declares spontaneously: "I'll give you a kiss when we get home" (006-083). The promise is made in gratitude, without calculation. But Lootie intervenes at once:

> "A princess mustn't give kisses. It's not at all proper." (006-096)

Irene protests: "Lootie, I promised him a kiss" (006-101). Lootie's prohibition is absolute — "not after sunset, or to let her kiss a miner-boy" (006-104) — establishing the class barrier that the novel's events must overcome. Curdie accepts graciously: "You mustn't kiss me tonight. But you shan't break your word. You shall kiss me another time" (006-105). The kiss becomes a promise deferred, hovering over every subsequent encounter between Irene and Curdie.

### The Grandmother's Kisses

In contrast to Lootie's prohibition, the grandmother's kisses are freely, lovingly given. She "drew her towards her, and kissed her on the forehead and the cheek" (011-050). At Irene's bedside, the old lady "kissed the forehead which lay in her bosom" (011-064). When Irene returns in tears, the grandmother draws "the child to her bosom, and, kissing the tear-stained face" (015-015) — her kisses heal rather than police.

The grandmother also kisses Curdie, bridging the class gap that Lootie enforces: "she smiled, and held up her mouth to kiss her" — kissing both redeemed children (028-018).

### The Fulfilled Promise

The novel brings the kiss arc to completion in its final chapter. Irene reminds her father of the promise: "And I promised him a kiss when we got home, but Lootie said I mustn't" (030-014). The king — not Lootie — now adjudicates:

> "There, give Curdie a kiss." (030-015)

Irene "put her arms round Curdie's neck, and kissed him on the mouth" (030-017). The kiss that Lootie forbade is now commanded by the king. The whole novel has worked toward this moment — the making-right of a simple, loving gesture that class convention had interrupted.

## Significance

The kiss is the novel's most intimate gesture and its sharpest social critique. Lootie's prohibition — "A princess mustn't give kisses" — is not presented as wise but as a failure of love. The grandmother, the king, and Irene herself all recognize that love should not be constrained by class. Irene's promise is made spontaneously, from gratitude, before social rules have time to intervene; the entire novel's adventure works to restore the natural order that Lootie's class-consciousness had interrupted.

The goblins even mock the kiss: "He actually wanted to kiss the princess" (025-023), the goblin reports of Curdie, treating it as scandal — ironic, given that the goblins themselves are the novel's true threat. The ungiven kiss appears to the goblin mind as something shameful, when in fact it is the most natural and good thing in the story.

The kiss story also reveals Lootie's character. She "resolved to watch her carefully from that time onwards" (006-110) — her prohibition leads not to love but to surveillance. Lootie is not a villain; she is a conscientious enforcer of a bad rule. The novel's resolution shows that the king, the ultimate authority, stands with love, not with propriety.
