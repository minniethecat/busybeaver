# The Passage

The system of corridors, galleries, and narrow ways running through [[king-papa|the king]]'s house and deep into [[the-mountain|the mountain]] — the connective architecture that threads the novel's three-tiered world together and provides the setting for journeys both physical and spiritual (001-004, 002-005).

## Variety and Scope

MacDonald's passages span the full vertical range of the story. The goblin domain opens into "long galleries and passages running off" from great pits (001-004). Higher up, [[king-papa|the king]]'s house contains "passage after passage" (005-011) of bewildering complexity. Between these extremes, narrow connecting ways thread through the mountain's interior.

The passages vary dramatically in character. Some are grand goblin galleries:

> None of the galleries he saw were unworthy of note (009-002).

Others are cramped and treacherous:

> Through one narrow passage after another, over lumps of rock... (020-008).

This variety of scale and character makes the passages more than a single location — they are a medium of travel, a geography of connection that defines the relationship between all the novel's fixed places.

## Passages as Labyrinth

The passages' primary narrative function is disorientation. [[irene|Irene]]'s first major adventure begins when she runs into a passage:

> Into this she ran. It was full of doors on each side... Nothing but passages and doors everywhere! She threw herself on the floor and burst into tears (002-005–002-006).

Later, she "ran through passage after passage and could not find" her way (005-011). The passages are a labyrinth in which the unguided wanderer is helpless — but this very helplessness becomes the precondition for discovering [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]. Getting lost in the passages is the necessary first step toward being found.

## Passages as Connection

For all their disorienting quality, the passages are also the means of connection. When [[irene|Irene]] follows [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother's]] [[the-magical-thread|thread]], the same passages that once confused her become navigable:

> It led her through certain narrow passages towards the kitchen, turning here and there (020-002).

The passages connect the [[tower|tower]] to the [[the-kitchen|kitchen]], upper rooms to [[the-cellar|cellar]], human house to goblin [[the-cave|caves]]. They are the world's circulatory system, and [[irene|Irene]] discovers that what seemed a maze is actually a network — if only one has a guide.

## Goblin Passages

The goblin-built passages have a different character from the human ones. When the goblins excavate, "a passage was broken through" (009-023) — a violent phrase that distinguishes goblin tunneling from the natural or human-built ways. The goblins' passages are acts of will and invasion, most dramatically in the [[the-tunnel|tunnel]] that breaches "into the king's wine cellar" (026-010) through "a passage" (031-018) deliberately aimed at the human world.

[[curdie|Curdie]], a miner's son, navigates these goblin passages with professional competence, following the goblins "through one narrow passage after another" (021-045) and tracking their movements by sound and vibration.

## Significance

The passages embody a fundamental truth of MacDonald's fictional world: everything is connected, but those connections are not obvious. The mountain is not solid but threaded with ways through. Getting lost is a necessary prelude to being found. And the passages that terrify when walked alone become the very means of rescue when walked with a guide — whether that guide is [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother's]] [[the-magical-thread|thread]] or [[curdie|Curdie's]] miner's knowledge of the rock.

The passages also connect the novel's three realms — divine (tower), human (palace, kitchen), and demonic (goblin caves). They are morally neutral infrastructure, capable of carrying threat or salvation depending on who travels them and with what guidance.
