# Crown

In [[The Princess and the Goblin]], the crown appears as a marker of royal identity for both [[king-papa|King Papa]] and [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]. More significantly, the grandmother's crown reveals her true nature — she appears youthful and radiant when wearing it, distinguishing her from ordinary mortals (015-093, 015-094).

## Irene's Questions About the Crown

[[irene|Irene]]'s curiosity about crowns runs through the early chapters. When [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]] first asks about her [[king-papa|father]]'s kingdom, [[irene|Irene]] defensively asks:

> "Where is your crown, then?" (003-049).

Later, [[irene|Irene]] tells [[lootie|her nurse]] that [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] keeps her crown in her bedroom:

> "And she keeps her crown in her bedroom" (004-015).

[[lootie|Lootie]] dismisses this as childish fantasy, but [[irene|Irene]] persists:

> "I don't think my papa wears his crown for a nightcap. Does he, nurse?" (004-017).

## The Grandmother's Crown

The most significant crown appearance occurs in [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]] when [[irene|Irene]] sees [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]] transformed:

> "Is it because you have your crown on that you look so young?" (015-093).

[[irenes-great-great-grandmother|The grandmother]] replies:

> "It is partly because I was thinking this evening that I put my crown on. And I thought you would like to see me with it" (015-094).

The crown here is not merely ornamental — it reveals [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s true, eternal nature. Without it, she appears aged; with it, she radiates youth and majesty.

## The Goblin Crown Prince

In contrast, the [[goblins|goblin]] court has its own crown prince — a figure of ridicule rather than majesty. He speaks in broken sentences and is the first to flee danger, subverting the royal dignity the crown should represent (018-005, 019-023).

## Significance and Themes

The crown symbolizes legitimate authority — both temporal ([[king-papa|the king]]) and spiritual ([[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]). MacDonald draws a sharp contrast between true royalty and the goblins' parody of it. The grandmother's crown, which transforms her from old to young, suggests that divine authority is not about power but about the revelation of hidden glory.
