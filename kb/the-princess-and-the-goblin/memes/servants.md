# The Servants

The collective household staff who tend [[king-papa|the king]]'s house — nurses, cooks, housekeepers, and men-at-arms — forming the human backdrop against which [[irene|Irene]]'s extraordinary adventures unfold (005-013, 024-002).

## Composition and Roles

The servants include several distinct figures and groups. [[lootie|Lootie]] the [[nurse|nurse]] is the most prominent individual servant, responsible for [[irene|Irene]]'s daily care. The [[cook]] presides over the [[the-kitchen|kitchen]] (026-004). The housekeeper oversees domestic order. The "gentlemen-at-arms" (024-002) function as the house's military guard. Together they form a microcosm of the kingdom's social order within the king's household.

> The room was full of women-servants; and the gentlemen-at-arms, who had followed the king (024-002).

This gathering of servants occurs at a moment of crisis — the goblin invasion — showing the household rallied and ready.

## Servants and Irene

[[irene|Irene]] occupies an ambiguous position among the servants. She is the princess, yet she is also a child. She was "a great favourite with the servants," and "there was a general rush" to her when she appeared in the [[the-kitchen|kitchen]] during her first wandering (005-013). She sometimes visits "the servants' hall or the kitchen, where she was not princess merely" (011-002), suggesting that among servants she experiences a version of ordinary childhood, free from the formality of her station.

However, the servants are also a constraint. [[lootie|Lootie]]'s "terror" (006-026) and the servants' "strict orders never to... [speak] of the grandmother" (006-026) make them obstacles to [[irene|Irene]]'s access to what lies above and below. The servants embody the ordinary world's disbelief in the extraordinary.

## The Servants and the Goblin Crisis

In the novel's climactic sequence, the servants appear in two contrasting modes. When [[curdie|Curdie]] is suspected of being a burglar, "the servants crowded in to see the villain" (025-022), displaying the credulous, mob-like quality of the household staff. Yet later, when the goblins' plot is revealed, the servants rally to action:

> The crowd of servants made eager way for him (024-024).

And at the story's resolution:

> The cook rushed to the kitchen and the servants to their work (030-018).

The servants, despite their limitations of perception, remain loyal to [[king-papa|the king]] and ready to serve when the true danger is understood.

## Gossip and Doubt

The servants are carriers of rumor and skepticism. [[lootie|Lootie]] is described as "foolishly whispering to the servants — sometimes that the princess had... seen a ghost" (025-003). This whispering undermines [[irene|Irene]]'s credibility, making her account of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] seem like childish fantasy. The servants function as a chorus of worldly skepticism, their doubt providing the narrative friction that makes [[irene|Irene]]'s faith meaningful.

> One of the servants was having a chat together (026-002).

These domestic scenes — servants talking among themselves — create the texture of ordinary life against which the goblin threat and the grandmother's magic stand out.

## Significance

The servants are the novel's representation of ordinary humanity — decent, hardworking, loyal to their king, but limited in vision. They cannot see [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]], cannot find the [[tower|tower stair]], and dismiss what they do not understand as fantasy or madness. Yet they are not villains; they form the supporting structure of the household, and when danger materializes in a form they can recognize (goblins in the [[the-cellar|cellar]]), they respond with courage.

The servants' limited perception serves to elevate [[irene|Irene]]'s special access without condemning the ordinary. The servants are not wrong to be ordinary; they are simply not called to the same kind of seeing as [[irene|Irene]].
