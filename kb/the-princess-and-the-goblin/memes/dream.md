# Dream

Dream in *The Princess and the Goblin* serves as the primary skeptical framework through which Irene's supernatural experiences are questioned. Repeatedly, characters (especially Lootie) dismiss Irene's encounters with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her great-great-grandmother]] as dreams, forcing Irene to defend the reality of what she has seen (004-028, 005-014). The grandmother herself addresses the accusation head-on, giving Irene rational arguments against the "it was only a dream" dismissal (011-012). The novel thus uses "dream" not as an irrational escape but as a test of faith — a lens through which characters either recognize or reject truth.

## Nature / Definition

The concept of dream functions as a boundary-marker in the novel's epistemology. Those who lack faith — Lootie, the nurse — relegate Irene's experiences to the category of dream, a dismissal that lets them avoid the implications of a real supernatural presence in the world. The novel systematically undermines this dismissal. When Lootie tells Irene "You've dreamt it, then, child" (004-028), Irene immediately counters: "No, I didn't dream it. I went upstairs, and I looked all about" (004-029).

The grandmother later provides a deeper analysis. She tells Irene: "I will give you one reason that will help to keep you from thinking it was a dream: if you had dreamed it, you would not have been able to find your way back" (011-012). This pragmatic test — that dream-experiences cannot be reliably revisited — gives Irene a rational defense against the skepticism of others.

> If you had dreamed it, you would not have been able to find your way back. (011-012)

The novel does not deny that Irene dreams. She does — "she fell asleep and dreamed a good many dreams" (011-003). But these ordinary dreams are distinguished from her encounters with the grandmother, which have a consistency, continuity, and consequence that dreams lack. When Irene feels "not quite sure that she was not dreaming" (011-005), the ambiguity is presented as a test rather than a defect.

## Narrative Role with Key Appearances

Dream discourse punctuates the novel at critical moments, always surfacing when Irene must decide whether to trust her perceptions despite outside skepticism.

The grandmother's rebuke in Chapter 15 is particularly pointed: "you made up your mind that I was a dream, and no real great-great-grandmother" (015-100). This frames the dream-dismissal as a moral choice rather than an innocent mistake — a willful decision to diminish reality rather than stretch one's understanding to accommodate the extraordinary.

> You made up your mind that I was a dream, and no real great-great-grandmother. (015-100)

Curdie's experience in the tower inverts the pattern. When he awakens in the grandmother's chamber, he feels "only walking in the story of a dream" (020-008) — but here the dream-feeling signals genuine wonder rather than dismissal. For Curdie, reality has become so extraordinary that it feels like dream; for Lootie, the extraordinary is dismissed as dream precisely to avoid its reality.

## Significance

The dream motif articulates the novel's central epistemological claim: what appears to be mere dream may be the truest reality, and what passes for "common sense" may be a refusal to see. Irene's willingness to be thought a dreamer — to endure the condescension of Lootie and the skepticism of Curdie — is itself a form of [[courage|courage]]. She does not need others to validate her experience because the experience validates itself through its effects: the thread leads her, the grandmother heals her, the ring guides her.

The novel never argues that all dreams are real or that skepticism is always wrong. Rather, it insists that the dream-accusation can be a lazy refusal to investigate. "If I were to say I must have been dreaming," Irene remarks in one passage, "I don't know that I should believe myself" (023-020). The point is not that dreams are true, but that some experiences dismissed as dreams are true — and that distinguishing the two requires faith, courage, and the willingness to go upstairs and look.
