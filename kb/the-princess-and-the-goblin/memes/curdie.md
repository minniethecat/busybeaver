# Curdie

A young miner who works in [[the-mines|the king's mines]] beneath the [[the-mountain|mountain]]. Brave, resourceful, and quick-witted, Curdie discovers [[goblins|the goblins]]' plot against [[the-palace|the palace]] and uses his knowledge of mining and [[goblins|goblin]] anatomy to fight them. His journey from worldly skepticism to [[courage-and-faith|faith]] in [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s reality parallels [[irene|Irene]]'s growth (006-026–006-030).

## Role in the Story

Curdie is the novel's action hero. While [[irene|Irene]] represents [[courage-and-faith|faith]] and spiritual [[courage-and-faith|courage]], Curdie represents practical [[courage-and-faith|courage]] and worldly skill. He works underground, knows [[goblins|the goblins]]' tunnels, understands their language, and fights them using his knowledge of their physical weakness — their soft, toeless feet (001-005).

He discovers [[goblins|the goblins]]' [[the-goblins-plot|flood plot]] by overhea[[the-ring|ring]] them while wor[[king-papa|king]] at night (007-006–007-007). His reports to [[the-palace|the palace]] are initially dismissed, but he persists. When he is captured and imprisoned by [[goblins|the goblins]], [[irene|Irene]] follows [[the-magical-thread|the thread]] to rescue him — the moment when [[courage-and-faith|faith]] and practical [[courage-and-faith|courage]] converge (020-002–021-095).

## Appearance and Traits

Curdie has "eyes as dark as the [[the-mines|mines]] in which he worked and as sparkling as the crystals of their rocks" (006-030). He is physically strong from mining work and quick-thinking — he improvises rhyming verses to repel [[goblins|goblin]]s, turning his [[fear]] into creative defiance (006-026–006-029).

His primary flaw is skepticism: he cannot initially believe [[irene|Irene]]'s account of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]. His growth involves learning that not everything real can be seen:

> "You don't think I'm doubting my own mother?" (023-029)

## Significance and Themes

Curdie represents natural virtue — [[courage-and-faith|courage]], loyalty, intelligence — that needs to be completed by supernatural [[courage-and-faith|faith]]. His arc from skeptic to believer mirrors the novel's intended effect on its reader. He is a model of what a boy can achieve through [[bravery]] and persistence, but MacDonald shows that even Curdie's considerable abilities are insufficient without [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s [[the-magical-thread|thread]] — a theological point rendered as compelling adventure.
