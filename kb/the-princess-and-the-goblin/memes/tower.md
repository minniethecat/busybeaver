# The Tower

A secluded upper chamber at the top of a narrow, concealed stair in [[king-papa|the king]]'s house, where [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|Irene's great-great-grandmother]] keeps her spinning wheel and dovecot, accessible only to those who find their way upward (005-002, 014-009).

## Location and Architecture

The tower is part of [[king-papa|the king]]'s house on [[the-mountain|the mountain]], but it is set apart from the inhabited rooms below. [[irene|Irene]] first stumbles upon it while lost in the upper passages, and later struggles to find it again deliberately. The key architectural feature is the "little tower stair" (014-010) — a narrow, winding staircase that separates the tower from the ordinary house. The stair's location is not obvious, and [[lootie|Lootie]], standing only a few rooms away, fails to find it (005-011), suggesting that accessing the tower requires something more than mere spatial navigation.

The tower room itself is described as a place of light and spinning, where [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] keeps white [[doves|pigeons]] that feed on eggs she cooks herself (005-002). The room radiates a quality of eternal presence, with the grandmother appearing sometimes young, sometimes old, as if the tower stands outside ordinary time.

[[irene|Irene]]'s relationship to the tower follows a pattern of finding, losing, and seeking. Her first discovery is accidental — she is running from [[nurse|her nurse]] and the rain and takes a wrong turn. When she brings [[lootie|Lootie]] back, the stair has vanished. The tower then becomes an object of desire and doubt: Irene searches "the many doors" and "passage after passage" (014-002), sometimes despairing that "after all, [it] might lead to no tower" (014-008). Her eventual rediscovery coincides with a deepening of her relationship with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]], suggesting that access to the tower depends on a readiness of spirit.

## Description in the Text

The tower is present in the narrative less through physical description than through the effect it has on those who reach it. When [[irene|Irene]] climbs with [[curdie|Curdie]]:

> Irene growing happier and happier as they ascended (022-002).

The ascent itself transforms. The narrator introduces the tower early with a pointed contrast:

> ...the rain, but the lady in the tower; and the first question that... (005-002).

This juxtaposition positions the tower as the opposite of bad weather — it is shelter, but of a higher order than mere physical protection.

## The Tower as the World's Vertical Axis

The tower sits at the midpoint of the novel's vertical architecture. Above is the open sky and [[stars|starlight]]; below are the [[the-cellar|cellar]], the [[the-tunnel|tunnel]], and the [[goblins|goblin]] depths. The tower is neither the sunlit surface nor the lightless underground, but a liminal space — high yet interior, luminous yet enclosed. [[irene|Irene]] must climb to reach it, but the climb is through the interior of [[king-papa|the king]]'s own house, not up the mountain's exterior face.

This vertical placement mirrors the story's moral geography. [[goblins|The goblins]] burrow downward, away from light and toward darkness. [[irene|Irene]] climbs upward, toward [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] and her spinning wheel — the source of the [[the-magical-thread|magical thread]] that guides Irene through darkness.

## Significance

The tower is the story's most concentrated image of the inaccessible made accessible. It is invisible to [[lootie|Lootie]] and the [[servants]], who cannot find the stair even when they search nearby rooms (005-011). It is invisible to [[curdie|Curdie]] at first — when he climbs the stair, he sees only "a bare garret" (022-002), unable to perceive [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] or her room. This selective visibility makes the tower a test of perception: what you see in the tower depends on what you bring to it.

The tower also provides the novel's most concrete image of homecoming — not to the ordinary home of the nursery and [[lootie|the nurse]], but to a deeper, stranger home where time bends and a grandmother figure spins the thread that will guide the child through danger.
