# Stairs

The stairs in The Princess and the Goblin are the vertical passageways of [[the-hall|the king's house]], physical structures that also function as thresholds between worlds. The most significant of these is "a curious old stair of worm-eaten oak" (002-004) — the staircase that connects [[the-nursery|the nursery]] level to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s tower and marks the path of [[irene|Irene]]'s spiritual journey from childhood innocence to courageous faith.

## Nature and Definition

The stairs are first encountered when [[irene|Irene]], wandering through [[the-hall|the palace]], discovers them at the foot of the tower. They are old, made of "worm-eaten oak" (002-004), suggesting great age and linking [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s presence in the house to its deepest history. The stairs are the vertical axis of the house — the only path upward to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] and the only path back down to the ordinary world.

The stairs are not always accessible. After her first visit to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]], [[irene|Irene]] searches repeatedly and:

> could not find the stair of the tower (005-011).

When she does find the stair again, it behaves paradoxically:

> she did not come on a stair that went up, but she came upon one that went down (005-013).

This inversion — finding the upward stair by going down — signals that the stairs operate by a logic that transcends ordinary architecture. They are not merely a physical passage but a spiritual threshold that cannot be found by determination alone.

## Ascent as Transformation

[[irene|Irene]]'s relationship with the stairs changes as she matures. Her first ascent is hesitant, full of wonder and disorientation. Her later ascents are more confident. And when danger threatens — when [[goblins|goblins]] and their creatures are abroad in the house — [[irene|Irene]] runs "up the stairs at once" (014-009) without hesitation, seeking the safety of her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]'s tower.

The stairs also connect the domestic world below to the mysterious realm of "upstairs" — a word that recurs in goblin conversation as a term for the human world. The goblins refer to "the country upstairs" (008-075) and note that "all the men, and women too, upstairs wear shoes" (008-039). For [[goblins|the goblins]], the stairs represent the inaccessible surface — the world of light and air that their deformed feet cannot tolerate.

## The Stairs as Connective Architecture

The staircase is a junction between three worlds: the domestic ([[the-nursery|nursery]], [[the-hall|hall]], [[the-kitchen|kitchen]]), the celestial ([[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s tower), and the subterranean ([[the-mines|the mines]], reached by descending stairs and ladders). [[curdie]]'s journey takes him "down rough stairs, and even sometimes a ladder" (020-008), and he eventually reaches "the bottom of all the stairs" (022-032) — the deepest point, where the human world borders the goblin domain. Each set of stairs marks a boundary between realms.

## Significance

The stairs embody George MacDonald's vision of spiritual growth as a journey taken one step at a time. [[irene|Irene]] does not fly or teleport to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s chamber — she climbs. The physical effort of ascent, the darkened passages between landings, the uncertainty of finding the right stair — all of these map onto the experience of faith as MacDonald understood it: gradual, effortful, and rewarded with revelation at the top.
