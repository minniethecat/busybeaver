# Trust

Trust is a central virtue in "The Princess and the Goblin," forming the foundation of [[king-papa|Irene]]'s relationship with her [[light|grandmother]] and serving as the quality that [[peter|Curdie]] must develop before he can perceive [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s true nature.

## Nature / Definition

Trust is presented in the novel as a relational virtue — it exists between persons, not as an abstract quality. [[irene|Irene]]'s trust in her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] is the engine of her safety and growth. The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] explicitly affirms this:

> "You may, my child. And I trust you will always think it your duty." (015-092)

The [[the-goblin-king|goblin king]]'s reference to a "trusty subject" (009-011) suggests that trust operates in political as well as personal realms, though [[goblins|the goblins]]' version is hollow — based on [[fear]] and self-interest rather than love.

## Trust Tested

Trust is tested most explicitly through [[peter|Curdie]]. When [[irene|Irene]] asks [[curdie|Curdie]] to accompany her to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]], he hesitates:

> "I don't feel quite sure whether I can trust you," said Curdie. (025-016)

[[curdie|Curdie]]'s inability to trust [[irene|Irene]]'s account of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] reflects his rationalist mindset — he trusts only what he can verify with his senses. His journey involves learning to trust what he cannot see.

The [[goblin-queen|Goblin Queen]] represents false trust:

> She trusted in her shoes: they were of iron (018-037)

> the group which, in trusting in the queen and her shoes, had been exterminated (027-014)

Trust placed in the wrong object — in iron shoes rather than in [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s guidance — leads to destruction.

## Narrative Role

Trust operates as a moral litmus test in the novel:
- [[irene|Irene]] trusts [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] → she is guided and protected
- [[curdie|Curdie]] initially cannot trust → he cannot see [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]
- The [[goblins|goblin]]s trust in iron shoes → they are crushed

The novel suggests that trust in divine/higher goodness ([[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]) yields salvation, while trust in material defenses (iron shoes) or in oneself alone leads to destruction.
