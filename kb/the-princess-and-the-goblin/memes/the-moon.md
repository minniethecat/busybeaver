# The Moon

The moon in "The Princess and the Goblin" is a celestial symbol closely associated with [[light|the grandmother]], appea[[the-ring|ring]] both as natural [[light|moonlight]] and as [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s own magical "moon" — a [[the-grandmothers-lamp|lamp]] that guides her [[the-pigeons|pigeons]] and signals her watchful presence.

## Nature / Definition

The moon appears in two distinct forms in the novel: the natural moon in the night sky, and [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s personal "moon" — the luminous globe that hangs in her chamber:

> The moon was shining brightly into the room (011-004)

> "Why don't you put out your moon?" asked the princess. (011-052)

The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] explains the purpose of her moon:

> "If any of my pigeons are out on a message, they always see my moon and know where to fly to." (011-053)

This identifies [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s moon as a homing beacon — a signal visible only to her messengers, distinct from the natural moon but sha[[the-ring|ring]] its guiding function.

## The Grandmother and Moonlight

The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] has a special relationship with [[light|moonlight]], spinning only on moonlit nights:

> "I don't work every night — only moonlit nights, and then no longer than the moon shines upon my wheel." (011-033)

The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]'s spinning wheel in the [[light|moonlight]] creates an ethereal image:

> There was the moonlight streaming in at the window, and the spinning-wheel shining in the moonlight (011-029)

[[irene|Irene]] experiences dreamy visions that blend [[light|moonlight]] with beauty:

> of summer seas and moonlight and mossy springs and great murmurs of wind (011-065)

## Narrative Role

The moon functions on multiple levels:

1. **Symbol of guidance**: The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]'s moon guides her pigeons home, just as [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] herself guides [[irene|Irene]] and [[curdie|Curdie]]. On cloudy nights when the moon is obscured (025-006), the natural world mirrors states of spiritual [[darkness]].

2. **Magical boundary**: The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] spins only in [[light|moonlight]] — a detail that marks her work as operating at the boundary between the natural and the supernatural.

3. **Atmospheric device**: Moonlight scenes (the gambolling creatures in Ch. 13, [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s chamber in Ch. 11) create the novel's most vivid and dreamlike imagery.
