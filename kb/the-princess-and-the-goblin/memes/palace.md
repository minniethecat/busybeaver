# Palace

The palace is the great house of [[king-papa|the king]], built upon one of [[the-mountain|the mountain]]'s slopes. It serves as the home of [[irene|Princess Irene]], the seat of human authority, and the symbolic center of the ordered, sunlit world that stands in opposition to the [[goblins|goblins]]' underground domain.

## Nature / Definition

The palace is introduced in the novel's opening paragraph as the royal residence "built upon one of the mountains" in a country "full of mountains and valleys" (001-002). It is described as half castle, half farmhouse — a structure that combines royal dignity with domestic practicality. This hybrid nature reflects [[king-papa|the king]]'s character: a monarch who is also an accessible, loving father.

The palace contains multiple distinct spaces: the [[irene|princess]]'s [[the-nursery|nursery]] and living quarters, the [[the-hall|great hall]] where [[king-papa|the king]] holds court, the servants' quarters, and — most significantly — a largely forgotten [[the-tower|tower]] at its top where [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] dwells. The tower is physically connected to the palace but spiritually apart, accessible only to those who believe.

> When she came to the top, she found herself in a little square place, with three doors. (003-002)

The palace sits above ground, bathed in sunlight, directly contrasting with the [[the-goblin-palace|goblin palace]] — a former reservoir converted into an underground hall (009-006). The vertical opposition of palace above and goblin realm below structures the novel's spatial and moral geography.

## Narrative Role

The palace serves as the narrative's home base — the place from which [[irene|Irene]] ventures into danger and to which she returns. Its rooms, walls, and hidden staircases are the geography of her daily life, and its organizational logic (who knows which rooms exist; who believes what about the tower) mirrors the novel's theme of belief and perception.

The palace is not merely backdrop — it is the object of [[goblins|the goblins]]' plot. The goblins scheme to flood the mines and break through into the palace (008-056–008-062). Their palace, by contrast, is a grotesque inversion: a former reservoir turned court hall, whose mineral beauty is admired even as its inhabitants are repulsive.

> The goblin palace was nothing but an old water reservoir, now the great palace hall of the goblins. It rose high above their heads, and the light of the fire showed it full of beautiful shapes — columns and arches — all of natural rock. (009-006)

The palace is also where the novel's central validation occurs. At the climax, [[king-papa|the king]] returns to the palace and meets [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] there — the moment when the palace's hidden upper realm is recognized by the palace's earthly ruler (030-012).

## Significance

The palace embodies the principle of legitimate order. It is the visible seat of [[king-papa|the king]]'s authority, standing in sunlight on the mountain slope, while [[goblins|the goblins]] rule in darkness underground. This spatial hierarchy — above and below, light and dark — maps onto the novel's moral framework.

The tower within the palace is particularly significant. It represents a truth that exists within the ordinary world but is invisible to those who lack faith. The servants dismiss the tower as empty; [[irene|Irene]] alone knows it houses her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]. MacDonald uses the palace's architecture to argue that the divine is present within the everyday — you need only climb the stairs to find it.
