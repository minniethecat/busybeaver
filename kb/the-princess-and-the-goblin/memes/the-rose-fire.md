# The Rose Fire

The rose fire in *The Princess and the Goblin* is a supernatural element in the great-grandmother's tower room — a fire made of burning roses that serves as a source of light, heat, and magical transformation. It is central to the great-grandmother's power and to Irene's initiation into the deeper mysteries of her lineage.

## Appearance

The rose fire is first described as "a huge bouquet of red roses on a low stand against the wall" [015-007]. When Irene approaches, she finds the roses are burning: "one of the burning roses" [015-018] is taken up by the great-grandmother. The fire consists of "red roses mingled with white" [022-004], and the old lady tends it as a domestic hearth.

## Function

The rose fire serves multiple purposes:
- **Purification**: It burns away impurity and fear. When Irene recoils from its heat, the old lady tests her courage.
- **Tempering**: The thread that guides Irene is "tempered in the rose-fire" [020-010], giving it its magical properties.
- **Illumination**: The fire lights the great-grandmother's room with a warm, living glow.
- **Portent**: When Irene visits, she sits "gazing, now at the rose fire, now at the starry walls" [015-039], as if reading meaning in the flames.

## Fire as Test

The old lady asks Irene, "You're not afraid of the rose — are you?" [015-021] as she holds the flaming rose toward her. When Irene cannot bear it, the lady says "it is too hot for you yet" [015-023] and throws the rose away, suggesting that the fire tests spiritual readiness. Later, when Curdie sees the thread, he exclaims, "just like a fiery rose!" [016-012], recognizing its origin.

## Symbolic Meaning

The rose fire unites two opposing symbols — the fragile beauty of the rose and the destructive power of fire — into a single image of transformative love. It is beauty that burns away falsehood, and warmth that does not consume the innocent.
