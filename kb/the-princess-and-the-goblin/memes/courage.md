A defining virtue in *The Princess and the Goblin*, demonstrated by multiple characters in the face of physical danger, supernatural mystery, and the darkness of the underground world (007-006, 009-022, 020-002). The novel distinguishes between two kinds of courage: practical courage (Curdie's mining skill and physical bravery) and spiritual courage (Irene's trust in the invisible thread).

## Nature / Definition

Courage in the novel is not reckless bravery but the willingness to act despite fear. The "more courageous" miners dare the darkness (007-006). Curdie's "great confidence in his boy's courage and resources" drives him into goblin territory (007-009). Had he not been "very courageous, he must have been very anxious" when trapped in the mines (009-022). The novel insists that courage is tested, not assumed — it is revealed in the moment of danger, not claimed beforehand.

> Had he not been very courageous, he must have been very anxious. (009-022)

### Irene's Courage

Irene's courage is spiritual, not physical. It revives through beauty: "as often as she looked at the lovely thing, her courage revived" (014-009). It comes from memory of the grandmother: "the thought gave her such courage that she stopped to put on her shoes" before venturing into the tunnels (020-002). And it is sustained by the thread — "another thing which helped to keep up her courage was that, as often as she uncovered her ring, it gave out a lovely light" (021-002). Irene's courage is relational: it depends on the grandmother, the ring, the thread. It is not self-generated but received.

> The thought gave her such courage that she stopped to put on her shoes. (020-002)

### Curdie's Courage

Curdie's courage is practical and rooted in skill. When goblin creatures attack, he stamps feet and sings — his courage is active, physical, and defiant. His "great confidence" (007-009) comes from years of mining experience. But Curdie's courage is importantly incomplete: he cannot perceive the thread. His practical bravery must be completed by trust in Irene's different kind of knowledge, a trust that is itself an act of courage for a boy who relies on what he can see and measure.

### False Courage

The novel also notes how courage can be borrowed from unworthy sources. Of the goblin king and queen, MacDonald writes: "the king and queen gave them courage" (018-037) — but this is the courage of a mob, not of individual virtue. The goblin councillors are "encouraged by this mark of favour" from their superiors (018-016), motivated by ambition rather than conviction. The novel distinguishes between courage that comes from within (or from above) and courage that is merely the absence of dissent in a crowd.

## Narrative Role

Courage is tested in every major episode. Irene must find courage to leave her room at night and follow the thread. Curdie's courage is tested in the goblin halls and mines. Lootie's lack of courage — her refusal to believe Irene's stories about the grandmother — is a cautionary counter-example. Even Curdie's mother demonstrates a quieter courage, joining "in the praises of his courage and energy" (030-010) while serving as the family's emotional anchor.

## Significance

Courage in the novel is not the absence of fear but the presence of trust — in the grandmother, in the thread, in one's own resources. Curdie's courage is practical and rooted in skill; Irene's is spiritual and rooted in faith. Together they model two kinds of courage: the courage to act (Curdie stamping goblin feet) and the courage to believe (Irene following an invisible thread). The novel suggests that true courage requires both.

The grandmother's gift-list is instructive: "strength and beauty and mirth and courage and clear eyes and strong painless hands" (015-096). Courage sits in the middle of these gifts, flanked by mirth and clear sight — suggesting that courage is neither grim endurance nor blind risk, but a kind of clear-eyed joy. The courageous person sees truly and acts joyfully, not in spite of danger but in trust that danger is not the final reality.

The goblins' "courage" by contrast — the mob courage given by the king and queen — proves worthless: when the flood comes, they are left "cowering without courage to attempt their escape" (027-018). Courage borrowed from power rather than trust collapses when power fails.
