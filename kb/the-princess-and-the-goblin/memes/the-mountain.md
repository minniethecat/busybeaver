# The Mountain

The great mountain that serves as the primary setting of the novel. On its surface stands [[king-papa|the king]]'s [[the-palace|palace]]; deep within its [[the-cave|cavern]]s and hollow places live [[goblins|the goblins]]. The mountain is both a physical location and a symbolic landscape representing the layers of reality — sunlit surface, hidden passages, and subterranean [[darkness]] (001-002, 001-004).

## Location and Geography

The mountain rises over a great country full of mountains and valleys, and [[king-papa|the king]]'s [[the-palace|palace]] is built upon one of its slopes (001-002). It is a place of dramatic weather — mist, rain, and cloud constantly sweep its heights, creating an atmosphere of mystery and isolation:

> "Suddenly the shadow of a great mountain peak came up from behind, and shot in front of them. The long, wasting, thin, ragged garments of the cloud swept on, and the mountain was visible from base to summit." (006-005)

The mountain has two distinct realms: the sunlit surface world of humans, and the dark underground domain of [[goblins|the goblins]].

## The Underground Domain

Beneath the mountain's surface lies a vast network of natural and excavated spaces:

> "These mountains were full of hollow places underneath; huge caverns, and winding ways, some with water running through them, and some shining with many-coloured lamps of precious stones." (001-004)

The [[goblins|goblin]]s live in these subterranean [[the-cave|cavern]]s, where they were driven generations ago (001-005). The underground includes:
- Natural [[the-cave|cavern]]s and tunnels formed over geological time
- The [[goblins|goblin]]s' excavated galleries — their homes, [[the-palace|palace]], and mine-workings
- Passages connecting different levels, some known to [[goblins|the goblins]], some to the miners
- A great natural reservoir that later floods [[goblins|the goblins]]' domain

## Surface Features

The mountain's surface is characterized by:
- The [[the-palace|king's palace]] on its slopes
- Steep, winding roads exposed to the elements
- Streams and [[water]]falls that descend its sides
- Wild areas where shepherds' and miners' families live

## Symbolic Meaning

The mountain operates as a vertical moral geography. The higher one goes, the closer to light, goodness, and [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s tower. The deeper one descends, the closer to [[goblins|the goblins]], [[darkness]], and danger. [[irene|Irene]]'s journey follows this pattern: she discovers her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] in the high tower, and later descends into [[darkness]] following [[the-magical-thread|the thread]] to save [[curdie|Curdie]].

The mountain's dual nature — beautiful and dangerous, shelte[[the-ring|ring]] both [[the-palace|palace]] and [[goblins|goblin]] lair — mirrors the novel's theme that the world contains both light and [[darkness]], and navigating between them requires [[courage-and-faith|faith]] and [[courage-and-faith|courage]].
