# Rock

Rock in *The Princess and the Goblin* is the substance that defines the boundary between the human world above and the goblin world below. It is what miners like Curdie and his father work upon, what goblins live within, and what both separates and connects the two realms.

## The Mountain's Substance

The entire landscape is built upon rock. Curdie's father works as a miner, breaking rock underground. The goblins live in "a natural opening under a huge rock, where a little stream rushed out" (007-005) and work to expand their domain within the mountain. They sing: "Thus we rive the rocks, Force the goblin locks" (006-026), celebrating their mastery of the underground world.

## Rock in the Mine

Curdie works the rock for silver, "throwing all the ore he had disengaged on one side behind him, to be ready for carrying out in the morning" (008-002). His eyes are "as dark as the mines in which he worked and as sparkling as the crystals in their rocks" (006-030), the rock's mineral wealth reflected in the miner's own appearance. When he spies on the goblins, Curdie "kept nibbling away with his fingers at the edges of the hole" because "here the rock had been very much shattered with the blasting" (008-078).

## Rock as Barrier and Passage

Rocks serve both as barriers and gateways. Irene and Curdie must navigate "where it narrowed between rocks so that only one could pass at a time" (006-078). The goblins' palace is hidden within the rock, and their water-flooding plan requires breaking through rock barriers. Curdie climbs "along the walls of rock" (009-022), risking his life on the mountain's face. He hears "something like the rush of a river inside the rock" (009-005), the mountain's hidden waterways threatening to burst through.

## The Garden Rock

In the king's garden, "where the rocks came through in great masses" (010-024), nature and domesticity meet. "Against one of these rocks was a garden seat, shadowed from the afternoon sun by the overhanging of the rock itself" (010-025). There was "a little winding path up to the top of the rock, and on top another seat" (010-025). A stream emerges "merrily out of an opening in the rock" (010-032). Here rock is not threat but foundation for contemplation and rest.

## The Misperceived Rock

Irene mistakes a rock formation for a goblin, calling out: "Look, look, Lootie! Don't you see that funny man peeping over the rock?" (006-013). But "when they came nearer, the princess saw it was only a lump of the rock itself that she had taken for a man" (006-014). This moment of misperception blurs the line between inert stone and living threat, prefiguring the goblin creatures that Curdie later fights — one of which "ran straight up one of the rocks like a huge spider" (006-085).

## Symbolic Meaning

Rock embodies the novel's interest in what lies beneath the surface — literally and metaphorically. The goblins' attempt to breach the rock into the palace (018-037) represents the uprising of the hidden, while Curdie's mining represents honest labour that reveals the treasures within the mountain. The rock is both the goblins' home and their prison, both wealth and danger.
