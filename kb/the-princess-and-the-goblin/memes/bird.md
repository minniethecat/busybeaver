The **bird** — most often appearing as a pigeon or dove — is a recurring motif in *The Princess and the Goblin*. Birds are intimately connected to Irene's [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|great-great-grandmother]], serving as her messengers, her food source, and symbols of her protective presence.

## Birds in the Great-Great-Grandmother's Tower

Irene first encounters the birds during her initial visit to her great-great-grandmother's tower room. The old lady keeps a multitude of pigeons — mostly white but of all colours — that come and go freely through an opening in the wall. She collects their eggs for food from a wall of pigeonholes:

> The lady took hold of a little loop of string in the wall at the side of the door and, lifting a shutter, showed a great many pigeonholes with nests, some with young ones and some with eggs in them. The birds came in at the other side, and she took out the eggs on this side.
>
> (003-082)

The pigeons feed themselves — the old lady explicitly notes that she can eat their eggs only because they have wings to fly and gather their own food (003-079).

## Pigeons as Messengers

The great-great-grandmother's pigeons are not merely poultry. They serve as her couriers, bringing her the rare spiderweb she uses for her spinning from a forest across the great sea (011-031). In the darkest night, the birds navigate by the old lady's moon — her lamp that never goes out — and always know where to fly to (011-053).

## The White Bird from the Globe of Light

The most dramatic appearance of a bird occurs near the story's climax. As the king, Curdie, and Irene face the subterranean waters, a white bird emerges from the great globe of light:

> The same moment there shot from it a white bird, which, descending with outstretched wings, made one circle round the king and Curdie and the princess, and then glided up again. The light and the pigeon vanished together.
>
> (031-029)

Irene recognizes this as a sign of her grandmother's awareness and protection, declaring that her grandmother "knows all about it, and isn't frightened" (031-029).

## The Snow-White Pigeon

Earlier in the story, a snow-white pigeon visits Irene while she is with her father the king, flying in through an open window and settling on her head. When the king reaches for it, the pigeon flies away, its whiteness flashing once in the sun before vanishing — a moment that prompts the king to gaze at his daughter with a knowing half-smile (010-003).

## Symbolic Significance

Throughout the narrative, birds — particularly white pigeons — function as visible signs of the great-great-grandmother's invisible care. They appear at key moments: when Irene needs confirmation of the old lady's reality, and at the climax when divine-seeming protection manifests as a white bird from light. The motif reinforces the story's theme that help comes from unexpected sources, and that what seems ordinary (a pigeon, a spiderweb) may be part of a larger benevolent design.
