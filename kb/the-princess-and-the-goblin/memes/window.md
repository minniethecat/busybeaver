# Window

Windows in *The Princess and the Goblin* are frames through which characters glimpse the world beyond their immediate enclosure. They separate inside from outside, human from goblin, safety from danger — yet they also admit light, air, and vision.

## The Pigeon at the Window

One of the novel's most magical moments occurs at a window. During the king's visit, "a snow-white pigeon flew in at an open window and settled upon Irene's head" (010-018). Irene "broke into a merry laugh, cowered a little, and put up her hands to her head" (010-018). When the king reaches for it, the pigeon "spread its wings and flew again through the open window, when its whiteness made one flash in the sun and vanished" (010-020). The open window becomes a portal between the ordinary and the supernatural — the pigeon enters from somewhere beyond, delivers its silent message, and returns to mystery.

## Windows and Danger

The window is also a point of vulnerability. When Lootie opens it, she creates an entry for threat: "The room feels close: I will open the window a little. The evening is mild: it won't hurt you" (014-005). But while she is gone, Irene sees "a pair of eyes" (014-007) at the window — the long-legged cat creature that has followed her. Irene later confesses to her grandmother: "I ought to have run up to you at once when the long-legged cat came in at the window, instead of running out on the mountain" (015-011). The window, opened for comfort, becomes a breach in the house's defenses.

## Locked Windows

After the goblin creatures are seen, the captain of the guards gives "orders that from this night the front door and all the windows on the ground floor should be locked" (016-011). The window shifts from being a source of light and air to being a potential entry point that must be secured against the darkness. The locked window symbolizes the human world's attempt to maintain a boundary against goblin intrusion.

## The Grandmother's Moon-Window

The great-grandmother's tower room contains a supernatural window — the moon itself. Irene, looking at the sky through her grandmother's magic, asks "Then how was it? I don't see a window all round" (015-033), unable to comprehend how she can see the moon and stars from within a tower room. The grandmother's "window" is not glass but magic — a vision that transcends architecture.

## The Mica Window

Underground, Curdie discovers goblin dwellings with "the mica window" (020-009) — panes of translucent mineral that serve as windows in the goblin world. These mineral windows are a grotesque imitation of human architecture, showing how the goblins, despite their hatred of the surface, cannot escape the forms of the world they left.

## Curdie's Surveillance

Curdie first observes the goblins from his window. He wakes "in the middle of the night" and hears "curious noises outside" (007-003), and from his window sees the goblin gathering. The window serves as a safe vantage point — a place to observe danger without being exposed to it. It is the threshold between the domestic safety of home and the goblin-haunted night.
