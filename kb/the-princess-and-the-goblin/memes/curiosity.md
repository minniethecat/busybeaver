# Curiosity

Curiosity is a defining character trait of [[king-papa|Princess Irene]] in "The Princess and the Goblin." It is the impulse that repeatedly drives her to explore, discover, and ultimately find her [[light|grandmother]], functioning as a virtuous form of inquisitiveness that overcomes [[fear]].

## Nature / Definition

Curiosity first appears as a narrative driver when [[irene|Irene]] hears a mysterious humming sound that leads her to explore:

> she began to hear a curious humming sound. Could it be the rain? (003-002)

Significantly, curiosity proves stronger than [[fear]] in this initial encounter:

> curiosity was stronger than her fear, and she opened the door very softly (003-002)

This pattern — curiosity overpowe[[the-ring|ring]] [[fear]] — recurs throughout the novel and is essential to [[irene|Irene]]'s character arc. Her curiosity leads her up the mysterious staircase where she discovers [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s chamber.

## The Old Staircase

The physical object most associated with curiosity is the "curious old stair":

> which opened at the foot of a curious old stair of worm-eaten oak (002-004)

The adjective "curious" attaches to many mysterious objects in [[irene|Irene]]'s world — a curious cap [[curdie|Curdie]] wears (006-030, 006-044), curious noises at night (007-003), a curious brooch (011-003), a curious silver casket (011-041). This pattern establishes the world as one in which curiosity is rewarded with discovery.

The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] herself smiles at [[irene|Irene]]'s curiosity:

> "Well!" said the old lady with a curious smile. "Mind you tell her all about it." (003-086)

## Narrative Role

Curiosity functions as:
1. **Plot engine**: [[irene|Irene]]'s discoveries — [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]], the pigeons, the magical [[the-magical-thread|thread]] — all begin with curiosity
2. **Character virtue**: Unlike [[lootie|the nurse]] [[lootie|Lootie]], who [[fear]]s the unknown, [[irene|Irene]]'s curiosity is portrayed as healthy and rewarded
3. **Theological metaphor**: Curiosity about the divine (see[[king-papa|king]] [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]) is shown as proper and virtuous
