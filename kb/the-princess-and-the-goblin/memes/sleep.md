# Sleep

A recurring state in *The Princess and the Goblin* that marks transitions between ordinary and extraordinary experience. Characters fall asleep in one world and wake in another; dreams of the grandmother blur into waking encounters; and the choice of where to sleep — in one's own bed or in the grandmother's tower — becomes a test of trust and belonging (011-044, 015-089).

## Nature / Definition

Sleep in the novel is never merely biological rest. It is a threshold state — a passage between the ordinary world of nurseries and mines, and the extraordinary world of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]], [[the-grandmothers-tower|her tower]], and its wonders. When characters sleep, the boundaries between reality and the supernatural grow thin.

[[irene|Irene]]'s experience is exemplary. On her first night after visiting the grandmother, "although she fell asleep and dreamed a good many dreams, she remembered them all" (011-003). The distinction between dream and reality is deliberately blurred. The grandmother's tower is real — the reader knows this because Irene brings back tangible objects — yet the tower's reality has the quality of a dream, accessed through passages that others cannot find.

The grandmother herself invites Irene into this ambiguity:

> "Would you like to sleep with me?" (011-044).

Irene's response — "I am very young. You won't mind sleeping with such a very young woman?" (011-049) — shows that sleeping with the grandmother is a significant act, not a casual arrangement. It implies intimacy, trust, and a willingness to enter the grandmother's world completely.

## Sleep and Trust

The choice of where to sleep becomes a test of faith. Irene asks:

> "Mightn't I stay and sleep with you tonight, grandmother?" (015-089).

The grandmother's response is not recorded, but the question itself is significant. Irene wants to remain in the tower — to sleep where the lamp never goes out, where [[pigeon|pigeons]] nest, where the spinning wheel turns. She wants to make the extraordinary world her resting place, not merely a place to visit.

[[curdie|Curdie]], by contrast, experiences sleep as a practical necessity. After his exhausting work spying on the goblins: "after a good sleep, though a short one, he always felt fit for anything" (012-005). Sleep restores him for action. His relationship to sleep is functional, not numinous — until he is healed in the grandmother's tower and wakes to a transformed understanding.

## Dreams and Waking

The novel repeatedly uses the dream-waking boundary to question what is real. Curdie, after seeing the grandmother's light:

> "If you were to say I must have been dreaming, I don't know that I should be able to satisfy you" (023-020).

This acknowledgment — that the grandmother's reality cannot be proven to skeptics — is central to the novel's epistemology. Sleep, dream, and waking are not sharply separated; they overlap, and the truth of the grandmother's existence is found in that overlap.

The nurse "came to sleep through" the extraordinary events (020-003) — her sleep represents a failure of perception, a spiritual blindness that prevents her from seeing what is real. Characters who sleep through wonders miss them; characters who dream of the grandmother are closer to truth than those who are merely awake.

## Significance

Sleep in *The Princess and the Goblin* carries a double meaning. On one hand, it represents vulnerability and spiritual blindness — those who sleep through wonders miss them, and the goblins exploit the sleeping hours for their schemes. On the other hand, sleep with the grandmother — in her tower, under her light — is an act of supreme trust and the deepest mode of belonging. Irene wants to sleep in the tower because she wants the extraordinary to be her ordinary.

This duality reflects MacDonald's broader theological vision. The spiritual life is not a matter of being perpetually awake in the literal sense, but of being awake to the right reality. The nurse is literally awake yet spiritually blind; Irene, dreaming of the grandmother, is closer to truth. Sleep — the right kind of sleep, in the right place, with the right company — becomes a form of communion.
