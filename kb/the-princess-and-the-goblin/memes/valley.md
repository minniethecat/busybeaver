# The Valley

The valleys that lie among the mountains of the novel's setting, forming the inhabited lowlands beneath [[king-papa|the king]]'s [[the-mountain|mountain]] [[the-palace|palace]]. The valleys represent the wider human world — the roads, villages, and pastoral landscapes that connect the palace to the kingdom beyond.

## Geography and Setting

The novel opens with a panoramic description of the mountainous country:

> "His palace was built upon one of the mountains, over a great country full of mountains and valleys." (001-002)

The valleys are the spaces between the peaks — lower, more accessible terrain where ordinary life unfolds. Unlike the [[the-mountain|mountain]] slopes with their dramatic weather and dangerous heights, the valleys are places of cultivation, travel, and settled habitation.

One particular valley is described as a quiet, uninhabited hollow where [[irene|Irene]] and [[lootie|Lootie]] wander during an outing:

> "They had been running down into a little valley in which there was no house to be seen." (006-025)

This "little valley" represents the boundary between safety and danger — it is here, far enough from the [[the-palace|palace]] to be exposed, that [[irene|Irene]] and [[lootie|Lootie]] first encounter the [[goblins|goblin]] creatures after dark.

## Travel and Connection

The valleys serve as the kingdom's arteries of travel. The road that climbs the [[the-mountain|mountain]] to the [[the-palace|palace]] begins in the valley:

> "Curdie was coming up the road from the valley to meet them." (006-026)

[[curdie|Curdie]]'s approach from the valley marks him as a child of the wider world, not the isolated palace — he comes from [[curdie-cottage|the cottage]] on the [[the-mountain|mountain]] slope, connected to the valley road below. Conversely, at the novel's climax, the flood waters roar downward:

> "While the torrent went roaring down the road into the valley beneath." (031-014)

The valley is both origin and destination — a place of departure and return.

## Significance

The valleys complete the novel's vertical geography. If the [[the-mountain|mountain]] peak houses the divine presence of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] and the underground houses [[goblins|the goblins]], the valleys represent the middle ground of ordinary human existence — farms, roads, villages, and the king himself riding through the countryside. When the flood sweeps down "into the valley beneath" (031-014), it symbolizes the purging of evil flowing out of the [[the-mountain|mountain]] and into the wider world, where it will be dispersed and neutralized.

In the larger structure of the story, the valley is the world that [[irene|Irene]] protects without leaving [[the-mountain|the mountain]] — the kingdom that depends on the palace's security but remains unaware of the [[goblins|goblin]] threat beneath its peaceful surface.
