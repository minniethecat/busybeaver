Voice and song function as a form of supernatural protection in the novel. Curdie's improvised rhymes drive goblins away, while the grandmother's "strange sweet song" brings healing and comfort (006-026, 022-060). Singing runs through the entire book: from Irene's girlhood merriment (005-013) to Curdie's underground battles (027-002–027-011), song marks the boundary between the human and the goblin worlds.

## Nature / Definition

Singing in the novel is not mere entertainment — it is an active force. Curdie's miner songs and rhymes cause goblins physical distress: "They can't bear singing, and they can't stand that song" (006-029). His singing is spontaneous and defiant — he composes verses on the spot to repel goblin attacks. The grandmother's singing, by contrast, is ethereal: "a strange sweet song, of which Irene could not understand a word" (022-060). Both types of song transcend ordinary communication and operate in a realm between speech and magic.

### Curdie's Battle Songs

Curdie wields song as a weapon. His first goblin encounter turns on a rhyme chanted as he works:

> "Thirteen, fourteen, fifteen" — chanted at the goblin creatures (006-027–006-029)

In the goblin palace, he taunts them with verse: "Once there was a goblin living in a hole" (019-024). When they protest the insult, "returned Curdie, and began singing again: 'Go to bed, Goblin, do'" (019-031). During the climactic battle, Curdie is "stamping and slashing and singing" (027-010), becoming "a small incarnate whirlwind" of song and violence (027-011). Song is inseparable from his courage — it is how he arms himself when weapons are insufficient.

### The Goblins' Counter-Song

The goblins have their own crude musical tradition. In the tunnels, "a voice broke out singing: 'Jabber, bother, smash! You'll have them in a crash!'" (021-003). Irene, who heard it, mistakes it for Curdie's voice: "Why, you were singing loud!" (021-007). The goblin song is a dark parody of Curdie's — rhythmic and rhyming, but violent and chaotic where Curdie's is orderly and defiant.

### The Grandmother's Healing Song

At the highest register is the grandmother's song. After the escape, when Irene and Curdie are brought to her tower, "came the voice of the lady, singing a strange sweet song, of which Irene could not understand a word" (022-060). The song transcends language itself — it is pure music, incomprehensible but effective, healing the exhausted children. This is the novel's highest form of song: beautiful, mysterious, and restorative.

## Narrative Role

The trajectory of singing in the novel moves from innocence to warfare to healing. Irene begins "singing merrily before she reached the bottom" (005-013). Curdie transforms from a boy who goes "whistling and singing" into the mine (027-002) to a warrior whose song is weaponized. The grandmother's song at the end completes the arc — song returns to its peaceful, restorative function.

## Significance

Song in the novel represents the human creative spirit arrayed against goblin malice. The goblins "can't bear singing" (006-029) because it embodies everything they are not: joyful, free, creative, and orderly. Curdie's ability to improvise rhymes in the face of danger is a form of courage that requires no weapon — he arms himself with words.

The three registers of song — Irene's innocent merriment, Curdie's battle rhymes, the grandmother's healing song — trace a spiritual ascent. The novel suggests that music is a language that bridges the human and the divine. The grandmother's "strange sweet song" that Irene "could not understand a word" of (022-060) speaks beyond words: it is the voice of a reality that reason cannot parse but that the heart recognizes.

The goblins' counter-song is also significant — it shows that the goblins *can* create, but their creation bends toward destruction. Where Curdie's rhyme is playful and defiant, the goblin chant is violent and threatening. Song, like fire, reads the moral character of its singer.
