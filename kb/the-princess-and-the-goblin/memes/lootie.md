# Lootie

[[irene|Princess Irene]]'s nurse and primary caretaker in [[king-papa|the king]]'s absence. Though well-meaning and devoted to [[irene|Irene]]'s physical safety, Lootie embodies worldly skepticism — she dismisses [[irene|Irene]]'s account of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] as fantasy and represents the conventional thin[[king-papa|king]] that [[courage-and-faith|faith]] must overcome (003-085, 004-005, 004-039).

## Role in the Story

Lootie's role is to provide contrast. She is not a villain — she genuinely cares for [[irene|Irene]] — but she cannot see beyond the ordinary world. When [[irene|Irene]] tells her about the beautiful [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] in [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]], Lootie dismisses it:

> She treats Irene's story as childish imagination rather than truth, telling her she must have dreamed it (004-039).

This dis[[courage-and-faith|belief]] is thematically important. Lootie represents the voice of reason unilluminated by [[courage-and-faith|faith]] — the perspective that [[curdie|Curdie]] will later share before his own transformation. Her failure to believe [[irene|Irene]] prefigures the novel's central c[[the-hall|hall]]enge: that [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] is real but can only be perceived by those with [[courage-and-faith|faith]].

## Appearance and Traits

Lootie is a devoted nurse who has raised [[irene|Irene]] in the queen's absence. She is attentive to [[irene|Irene]]'s physical needs and protective of her safety — her [[fear]] of [[goblins|the goblins]] is genuine and reasonable given the danger (006-110). Her limitation is spiritual rather than moral: she cannot conceive of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s supernatural reality.

## Significance and Themes

Lootie is not a villain but a representative of the limitations of conventional wisdom. She loves [[irene|Irene]] and keeps her safe by worldly standards, but she cannot help [[irene|Irene]] in the spiritual dimension where the novel's real battles are fought. Her dis[[courage-and-faith|belief]] is echoed by [[curdie|Curdie]] and by [[the-palace|the palace]] guards — all good people who simply cannot see what [[irene|Irene]] sees.

MacDonald uses Lootie to make a theological point: worldly care, however sincere, is insufficient without [[courage-and-faith|faith]]. The novel does not condemn Lootie but gently shows her limitation, inviting the reader to move beyond it.
