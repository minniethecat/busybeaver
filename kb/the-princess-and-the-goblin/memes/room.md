# Room

Rooms in *The Princess and the Goblin* are charged spaces where the ordinary becomes extraordinary. The novel's architecture of rooms — the nursery, the housekeeper's room, the great-grandmother's tower chamber — maps a geography of growing awareness, each room revealing more of the hidden world.

## The Nursery

The nursery is Irene's primary room, where she is cared for by Lootie. Yet it is not entirely safe: "as soon as the nurse goes out of the room" [002-003], Irene begins her explorations. The nursery represents the known world, but also its limitations — "there were so many doors" [002-005] leading to unexplored passages beyond it.

## The Great-Grandmother's Room

The old lady's tower room is the most significant space in the novel. It is an "empty room" where she sits "spin-spinning all day long" [004-013], yet when Irene enters it, it becomes "the loveliest room she had ever seen in her life" [011-040]. The room changes according to the visitor — when the king visits, it appears different. "The sweetest odour filled the room — like that of roses and lilies" [011-041], and a moon hangs within it, "shining brightly into the room" [011-004].

## Rooms as Portals

Rooms in the novel often function as thresholds. The old lady "opened the door opposite the stair" to show Irene a "little square place, with three doors, two opposite each other" [003-002], [003-073]. When Irene searches for Lootie, she passes "through the long avenue of silent rooms" [014-009]. Each room is a waystation on a larger journey.

## The Goblins' Rooms

The goblins too have their rooms — the "palace hall of the goblins" that "rose to a tremendous height" [009-006], and Glump's "new abode" [009-020]. Their underground rooms contrast with the palace rooms above: cramped, dark, and full of conspiracy.
