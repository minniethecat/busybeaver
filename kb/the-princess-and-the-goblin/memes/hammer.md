# Hammer

The hammer in [[The Princess and the Goblin]] serves a dual role: it is one of the [[miners|miners']] essential tools for excavating [[the-mountain]]'s precious ore, and its distinctive sound — a tap-tapping that echoes through solid rock — becomes the novel's primary acoustic signal of underground labor and, more ominously, of [[goblins|goblin]] activity.

## Nature and Definition

The hammer appears first in [[curdie]]'s miners' song, a working chant that celebrates the tools and rhythms of the mining life:

> "Ring! dod! bang! Go the hammers' clang! Hit and turn and bore! Whizz and puff and roar! Thus we rive the rocks, Force the goblin locks. — See the shining ore!" (006-026).

The hammer is here presented as an instrument of productive force — it "rives the rocks" and "forces the goblin locks," directly opposing goblin obstruction. The song's percussive language ("Ring! dod! bang!") mimics the sound of the hammer itself, making the verse an onomatopoeic expression of miner identity. The hammer, in this framing, is not merely a tool but a symbol of the miners' way of life: rhythmic, forceful, honest, and triumphant.

In the mine itself, the hammer's sound is the constant accompaniment to labor. The mine echoes with "the sounds of their companions burrowing away in all directions in the inside of the great mountain — some boring holes in the rock in order to blow it up with gunpowder, others shovelling the broken ore into baskets to be carried to the mouth of the mine, others hitting away with their pickaxes" (007-005). The hammer is part of an orchestrated chorus of industrial activity. Even in the loneliest gang, a miner "would hear only a tap-tapping, no louder than that of a woodpecker, for the sound would come from a great distance off through the solid mountain rock" (007-005).

## Narrative Role

The hammer's most significant narrative function is as a point of uncanny doubling. The goblins, too, use hammers — but their hammering is heard at night, the miners' nighttime, which is the goblins' day. The "constant sound of the goblin hammers and pickaxes at night" (008-061) reveals that they are "making new houses for themselves, to which they might retreat when the miners should threaten to break into their dwellings" (008-061). The same sound — tap-tapping — signifies two opposed civilizations: one building, one expanding; one creating wealth, the other digging schemes. The miners who stay overnight in the mine hear these nocturnal hammerings and know them for what they are: the sound of the goblin counter-world going about its business beneath their feet.

When [[curdie]] fights the goblins in their own court, he deliberately turns his pickaxe to use its blunt, hammer-like end, "being as gentle as courageous and not wishing to kill any of them" (018-037). The hammer-end of the tool serves as a non-lethal weapon — the miner's tool can be used to subdue without killing, distinguishing Curdie's violence from the goblins' malice.

## Significance

The hammer's tap-tapping is the novel's most persistent auditory motif, and it carries a specific thematic weight. The sound of hammers is the sound of civilization — of human beings shaping their environment through work. But in the novel's underground, this sound is contested. The goblins' hammers mimic the miners' hammers, and the miners who hear tapping at night are hearing their own activity echoed back in distorted form. This acoustic doubling suggests a fundamental question the novel poses: what distinguishes the miners' work from the goblins'? The answer, the novel insists, lies not in the tool or its sound but in the purpose: the miners work for honest sustenance and the service of [[king-papa|their king]], while the goblins work for conquest and revenge.

The hammer also features in the novel's broader celebration of labor. Alongside the [[pickaxe]] and the shovel, it represents the dignity of manual work — a recurring MacDonald theme. Curdie's song, with its exuberant "Ring! dod! bang! Go the hammers' clang!", turns the noise of mining into music, transforming the brute physicality of hammering rock into an act of joy.
