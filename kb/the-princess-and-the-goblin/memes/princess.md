# Princess

Princesshood in [[The Princess and the Goblin]] is a moral identity rather than merely a social status. Through [[irene]], George MacDonald develops a vision of royalty that is defined by courage, obedience, and a specific kind of trusting faith — qualities available in principle to anyone but brought into sharp focus by the expectations placed upon a princess.

## Nature and Definition

The novel opens by establishing [[irene]]'s royal status as a fundamental fact of the narrative world: "There was once a little princess whose father was king over a great country full of mountains and valleys" (001-002). Yet from the outset, Irene's princesshood is tested, qualified, and deepened by experience. She is not born knowing how to be a princess; she must learn it. The novel opens with her lost, frightened, and crying — "She did not cry long, however, for she was as brave as could be expected of a princess of her age" (002-007) — establishing bravery as the first duty of princesshood.

The narrator frequently invokes Irene's royal identity as an explanatory principle for her behavior. When she enters [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s room without hesitation, the narrator comments: "That the princess was a real princess you might see now quite plainly; for she didn't hang on to the handle of the door, and stare without moving, as I have known some do who ought to have been princesses but were only rather vulgar little girls" (003-006). Here, "real princess" is defined not by birth but by conduct — a real princess follows instructions, trusts, and acts.

True princesshood in the novel involves a specific form of obedience — not to arbitrary rules but to the wisdom of higher authority. When [[irene]] follows [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]]'s invisible thread into the darkness of the goblin tunnels, she performs the quintessential act of royal faith: trusting in a guidance she cannot see because she trusts the one who provided it. This obedience is not passivity but the active courage required to walk into danger on the strength of a promise.

## The Two Kingships

The novel contrasts two models of royalty. [[king-papa|King Papa]] represents temporal authority — legitimate, benevolent, but limited to the surface world, and largely absent from the story. [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|The grandmother]] represents a higher, spiritual kingship that operates through love, guidance, and invisible thread rather than through soldiers and decrees. [[irene]] is the princess of both realms: daughter of the king in the visible world, great-great-granddaughter (and thus heir) of the grandmother in the invisible one. Her princesshood requires her to navigate both allegiances — to be a dutiful daughter of the king while also being the faithful follower of the grandmother.

The goblins offer a dark parody of royalty. The [[goblin-king|Goblin King]] rules through fear and schemes of conquest; the [[goblin-queen]] clings to her granite shoes as a "royal privilege" (019-041). Their grotesque court — with its stolen gems and fire-lit cavern — is a distorted mirror of King Papa's court, suggesting what royalty becomes when divorced from the moral virtues that the novel defines as its true foundation.

## Narrative Role

The concept of princesshood is tested most severely in the novel by the goblins' plan to abduct Irene and marry her to Harelip, the goblin prince. This plan, introduced in the goblins' court speeches, represents an assault on the very idea of royal dignity — the goblins seek to absorb the human royal line into their own, believing that "peace would be secured for a generation at least by the pledge the prince would hold for the good behaviour of her relatives" (023-047). The plan fails not only through force of arms but through the inherent incompatibility the novel asserts between true royalty and goblin degradation. A princess who follows her grandmother's thread cannot be made a goblin bride.

## Significance

MacDonald's concept of princesshood is a form of spiritual pedagogy. Being a princess, in the novel's terms, means being capable of a particular kind of trust — the trust that enables Irene to follow an invisible thread, to believe in a grandmother others cannot see, and to descend into darkness on the strength of that belief alone. This is not naive credulity; it is the reasoned faith of someone who has tested her grandmother's guidance and found it reliable.

The novel also uses princesshood to explore the relationship between social hierarchy and moral worth. Irene is a princess by birth, but she earns the title repeatedly through her actions. Curdie, a miner's son, proves himself equally noble through courage and loyalty. The novel's resolution — in which king, princess, and miners cooperate to defeat the goblins — suggests that true royalty is not exclusive but inclusive: the health of the kingdom depends on the alliance of all its people, and the princess's role is to be the living link between the throne and the cottage.
