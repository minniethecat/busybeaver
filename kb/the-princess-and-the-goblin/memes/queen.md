# Queen

Queenship in "The Princess and the Goblin" appears in two distinct forms: the human queen — [[irene|Irene]]'s deceased mother, the "queen-mamma" — and the goblin queen, the living consort of the [[the-goblin-king|Goblin King]]. These two queens represent opposing models of royal femininity: one associated with memory, love, and legacy, the other with malice, vanity, and active villainy.

## Nature / Definition

Queenship in MacDonald's world exists in the shadow of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]], who is the true sovereign. The human queen — Irene's mother — is absent from the narrative, having died before the story begins. She is invoked only through objects: the [[the-ring|ring]] that [[king-papa|the king]] gives to [[irene|Irene]] was "your queen-mamma's once" (017-005), and Irene's identity as princess implies her mother's queenship.

The goblin queen, by contrast, is vividly present. She appears alongside the goblin king at the goblin court, wears stone shoes as a mark of distinction, and participates actively in the goblins' plot against the surface world. She is described as fierce, vain, and easily provoked to malice.

> "It was your queen-mamma's once," he said. (017-005)

The human queen exists as an absence that shapes the present. Irene never knew her mother, and the novel never provides her name or story. Yet the ring she wore connects Irene to a lineage of royal womanhood that culminates in the grandmother. The goblin queen, meanwhile, is all too present — a figure of active, unapologetic cruelty.

## The Goblin Queen

The goblin queen is the more fully realized queen in the novel. She is distinguished from common goblins by her footwear:

> "The queen does." (008-018)

The stone shoes are explicitly a mark of rank: "for distinction. The first queen, you see — I mean the king's first wife — wore shoes" (008-019). This detail is simultaneously comic and grotesque — goblin royalty is distinguished by covering the very feet that mark their species' deformity.

In the goblin court scenes, the queen is an active participant in governance. When [[curdie|Curdie]] is captured, the queen leads the interrogation alongside the king. She laughs triumphantly at Curdie's defiance, threatens him, and joins in the decision-making about his fate (018-010–018-019). Unlike the silent, absent human queen, the goblin queen has voice and agency — but she uses them for cruelty.

The goblin queen's narrative arc mirrors the goblins' trajectory: from plotting in the underground court to participating in the ill-fated attack on the surface world. Her death in the flood (032-004) is the culmination of the goblins' defeat — the queen who wore stone shoes is destroyed by the water that floods the mines.

## Significance

The two-queen structure reinforces the novel's moral dualism. The human queen represents a femininity grounded in love, memory, and generative legacy — she is absent but her love persists through the ring and through [[irene|Irene]]. The goblin queen represents femininity corrupted by malice and vanity — she is present, vocal, and aggressive, but her authority is hollow and ultimately self-destructive.

The grandmother transcends both models. When [[irene|Irene]] asks if the grandmother is a queen, she answers simply: "Yes, I am a queen" (003-048). But her queenship is of a different order entirely — not political but spiritual, not inherited but inherent. The grandmother is the true queen, the one from whom all other female authority in the novel derives.
