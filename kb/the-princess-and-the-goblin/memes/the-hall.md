# The Goblin Hall

The great hall of the [[the-goblin-palace|goblin palace]] is the central chamber of [[goblins|the goblins]]' subterranean realm beneath the [[the-mountain|mountain]]. A vast oval [[the-cave|cavern]] once filled with [[water]], it now serves as the throne room and assembly hall where the [[the-goblin-king|Goblin King]] holds court, plots against the surface [[king-papa|king]]dom, and receives his subjects.

## Location

The hall lies deep within the [[the-mountain|mountain]], accessible through a network of winding tunnels and passages that [[goblins|the goblins]] have excavated over generations. It sits at the end of a long avenue that leads upward from [[goblins|the goblins]]' outer [[the-cave|cave]]s, and its position — far from any surface entrance — reflects [[goblins|the goblins]]' retreat from daylight and their preference for the closed, dark spaces of the underground.

The hall is adjacent to other [[goblins|goblin]] chambers, including smaller family [[the-cave|cave]]s and the royal apartments. [[curdie|Curdie]] discovers the hall while following [[goblins|the goblins]] deeper into the [[the-mountain|mountain]] du[[the-ring|ring]] one of his nighttime spying expeditions (009-002).

## Description

MacDonald describes the hall as a natural [[the-cave|cavern]] repurposed by [[goblins|goblin]] craft:

> It was a magnificent cavern, of an oval shape, once probably a huge natural reservoir of water, now the great palace hall of the goblins (009-006).

The hall's dimensions are vast, its ceiling high above the floor. The upper walls are lined with a projecting ledge upon which [[king-papa|the king]]'s throne is elevated, allowing him to look down upon his assembled subjects. The [[goblins|goblin]]s have furnished the hall with grotesque furniture — chairs with seven legs, tables of uneven height — that reflects both their craftsmanship and their essential deformity:

> The sides of the cave were full of shining minerals like those of the palace hall (018-004).

The hall is lit by torches and [[the-grandmothers-lamp|lamp]]s, but the light is smoky and dim compared to daylight, casting distorted shadows that mirror [[goblins|the goblins]]' own misshapen forms. When the torches go out, the [[darkness]] is absolute — "morning would b[[the-ring|ring]] no light into these regions" (009-022).

The hall serves as the setting for nearly every major [[goblins|goblin]] scene. It is where [[goblins|the goblins]]' collective schemes are hatched and where their [[king-papa|king]] exercises his authority. [[curdie|Curdie]]'s repeated visits to the hall — first as spy, then as prisoner — form the novel's central arc of underground adventure.

## Significance

The goblin hall functions as the dark mirror of [[king-papa|the king]]'s [[the-palace|palace]] above ground. Where the surface [[king-papa|king]]dom is governed by love, sunlight, and legitimate authority ([[king-papa|the King]]), the hall is ruled by resentment, torchlight, and the Goblin King's tyranny. The hall's origin as a natural reservoir — a place that once held [[water]] — is significant: [[water]] in MacDonald's symbolic vocabulary represents purification and divine judgment, and the hall's eventual destruction by flood (Ch. 31) restores it, in a sense, to its original purpose.

The hall also dramatizes [[goblins|the goblins]]' fundamental limitation. For all their cunning and numbers, they remain confined to a single underground chamber, plotting endlessly but never able to break into the world of light. Their hall is both their seat of power and their prison.
