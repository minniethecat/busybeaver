# Cave

Cave in *The Princess and the Goblin* refers to the individual chambers and dwellings within the broader subterranean network of [[the-cave|the goblin caves]]. While the full cave system is a vast labyrinth spanning the interior of [[the-mountain|the mountain]], individual caves function as homes, meeting-places, and prison cells — each a distinct spatial unit with its own narrative significance.

## Nature / Definition

Caves are the goblins' architecture. The novel describes "huge caverns, and winding ways" beneath the mountain (001-004), but it also names specific caves: the outer caves where goblin families live, the great cavern where the goblin court assembles, and the smaller caves where captives are held. Caves are both natural formations and adapted dwellings — "caves, that is, occupied by goblin families" (018-003).

> Hollow places underneath; huge caverns, and winding ways, some with water dripping down, others full of water. (001-004)

The cave ecology is shaped by the absence of natural light. Goblin caves are lit by torches and firelight — "the fire was blazing, and casting a glare into the great darkness of the huge cavern" (021-041). The walls are "full of shining minerals" (018-004), and the spaces vary from intimate family dwellings to the "magnificent cavern, of an oval shape" that serves as the royal hall (009-006). Caves are at once geological features, domestic spaces, and political theaters.

The goblins' cave-dwelling reflects their nature. Unlike houses on the surface — built upward into light and air — caves are dug inward and downward into [[darkness]]. Goblin architecture is an architecture of retreat, a civilization built away from the sun.

The progression of cave scenes traces a descent: from the outer family caves (Ch. 9, 12, 18) to the magnificent royal cavern (Ch. 9) to the deeper cavern where Curdie is imprisoned (Ch. 20–21). Each layer of cave represents a deeper level of goblin society — and a greater danger.

> The sides of the cave were full of shining minerals. (018-004)

The cave where Irene finds Curdie is a prison-cave — a space of captivity that becomes, through Irene's arrival, a space of liberation. When Irene enters "a cavern, where glowed the red embers of a fire" (020-009), the cave shifts from goblin territory to a threshold of rescue. The physical space does not change, but its meaning is transformed by Irene's act of faith.

## Significance

Caves in the novel are not merely settings but characters in their own right. They echo, amplify, and threaten. "The shouting and roaring that filled the cave would have appalled Curdie" (018-037) — the cave itself becomes an instrument of intimidation, magnifying goblin voices into a terrifying chorus. When "the cave was quite still" (019-046), the silence is as meaningful as the noise.

MacDonald uses the scale of caves to underscore his themes. The "huge cavern" (021-041) dwarfs individual goblins, making them seem small even in their own domain. The "magnificent cavern, of an oval shape" (009-006), once a natural reservoir, suggests that the goblins inhabit spaces not made for them — spaces that, as the flood demonstrates, will eventually reject them. Caves are lent, not owned. The mountain reclaims them at the end.

The cave also provides the spatial logic for the novel's most important encounter. Irene finds Curdie in a cave within a cave — a prison within the labyrinth. Her rescue is not a battle but a navigation. She does not fight her way in; she follows her way in. The cave, which seems designed to prevent rescue, becomes instead the stage on which rescue is performed.
