# Stars

Stars appear throughout [[The Princess and the Goblin]] as a decorative and symbolic motif, adorning both [[the-nursery|the nursery]] ceiling and [[the-grandmothers-tower|the grandmother's tower]]. They connect the domestic world of [[irene|Irene]]'s childhood with the supernatural realm of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]].

## Stars on the Nursery Ceiling

The novel opens with a striking image: [[irene|Irene]]'s nursery ceiling painted blue with stars:

> The ceiling of her nursery was blue, with stars in it, as like the sky as the painter could make it (001-003).

This detail establishes [[irene|Irene]]'s world as one where the celestial touches the everyday — a visual foreshadowing of her encounters with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]].

## Stars in the Grandmother's Tower

When [[irene|Irene]] reaches [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]], she finds the same motif elevated to supernatural beauty. The walls are blue with "silver stars" (015-005), and [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s spinning wheel glitters with "what looked like stars of silver" (011-040). The room itself seems alive:

> The wall opened and gathered again and shut out the stars; the wall gathered again and opened, and the stars shone in once more (015-040).

[[curdie|Curdie]] later sees the same room in vision:

> A room, with blue walls and silver stars, and no end of things in it (023-015).

## The Grandmother's Eyes

MacDonald extends the star imagery to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] herself. [[irene|Irene]] perceives her eyes as containing "two melted stars" (003-008) — a description that identifies [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] as a celestial being inhabiting earthly form.

## Stars in Irene's Speech

Asked what she saw in [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]], [[irene|Irene]] answers simply:

> "The sky, and the [[moon]] and the stars" (015-028).

For [[irene|Irene]], [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s room is not merely decorated with stars — it contains them.

## Significance and Themes

The star motif weaves together the domestic and the divine. What begins as nursery decoration becomes, in [[the-grandmothers-tower|the tower]], a living reality. MacDonald suggests that the beauty children perceive in their ordinary surroundings (a painted ceiling) is a shadow of greater beauty waiting to be discovered through [[courage-and-faith|faith]].
