# Peter

Peter Peterson is a miner in [[king-papa|the king]]'s service and the father of [[curdie|Curdie]], the novel's young hero. Though a supporting character with relatively few scenes, Peter embodies the quiet virtues of diligence, foresight, and paternal care that MacDonald elevates as the foundation of a well-ordered society.

## Role in the Story

Peter's narrative function is to provide a baseline of responsible adulthood against which both [[goblins|the goblins]]' malice and the children's [[courage-and-faith|courage]] can be measured. He works in the [[the-mines|mines]] by day, participates in the miners' vigil against [[goblins|goblin]] incursions by night, and provides his family with a home whose warmth and stability stand in stark contrast to [[goblins|the goblins]]' chaotic underground existence.

When [[curdie|Curdie]] stays behind in the [[the-mines|mines]] to spy on [[goblins|the goblins]], Peter is among the men who remain with him, though his primary concern is always protective:

> "I can't stay with you," said Peter; "but I want to go and pay the men" (007-010).

His relationship with his son is marked by mutual respect. When [[curdie|Curdie]] returns injured from his ordeal, Peter's response captures both paternal tenderness and the pragmatic stoicism of a wor[[king-papa|king]] man:

> "I love you, indeed, my boy. I am sure you deserve some" (023-035).

## Foresight and Industry

Peter's most consequential trait is his foresight. Before the [[goblins|goblin]] threat fully materializes, he has already begun stockpiling provisions in a secure cottage on the [[the-mountain|mountain]]:

> There the foresight of Peter had already collected a great deal of store (029-003).

This preparation proves critical when [[goblins|the goblins]]' flood threatens the [[the-mountain|mountain]]. Peter's cottage becomes a refuge for [[irene|Irene]], [[curdie|Curdie]], and eventually [[king-papa|the king]] himself. MacDonald presents Peter's foresight not as heroic brilliance but as the natural fruit of steady, conscientious labor — a miner's habit of prepa[[the-ring|ring]] for what lies beneath the surface, applied to the safety of his family and [[king-papa|king]]dom.

## Appearance and Traits

Peter is described through his actions rather than his physical appearance. He is hardwor[[king-papa|king]], reliable, and quietly authoritative. He does not seek recognition — when [[king-papa|the king]] summons him after the flood, it is in acknowledgment of service already rendered, not reward for ambition. The novel's final chapters show Peter participating in the rebuilding of [[king-papa|the king]]dom's defenses (032-004), his mining expertise now directed toward permanent protection rather than mere extraction.

## Key Relationships

- [[curdie|Curdie]] — his son, whose [[courage-and-faith|courage]] and integrity reflect Peter's own values
- Peter's wife — [[curdie|Curdie]]'s mother, who shares Peter's quiet strength and hospitality
- [[irene|Princess Irene]] — [[irene|the princess]] who finds refuge in Peter's cottage
- The [[king-papa|king]] — Peter's sovereign, who comes to [[trust]] the miner family

## Significance and Themes

Peter represents the dignity of labor and the heroism of ordinary goodness. In a novel filled with magical [[the-ring|ring]]s, [[the-magical-thread|invisible thread]]s, and ancient [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]s, Peter's contribution — stockpiling food, reinforcing a cottage, raising an honest son — may seem unremarkable. But MacDonald's theology insists that such quiet [[courage-and-faith|faith]]fulness is precisely the material out of which salvation is built. Peter does not see [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] or feel [[the-magical-thread|the thread]]; he simply does his work, loves his family, and prepares for danger. And in doing so, he helps save a [[king-papa|king]]dom.

His name — Peter, the rock — may be an intentional echo of the biblical Peter, the disciple whose [[courage-and-faith|faith]] was both flawed and foundational. Peter Peterson is the rock upon which the novel's vision of community rests: the unglamorous, essential solidity of a good man doing his duty.
