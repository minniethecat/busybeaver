# The Goblin Cat

**The Goblin Cat** (also called the **long-legged cat**) is one of the grotesque creatures belonging to the [[goblins]] in [[The Princess and the Goblin]]. It appears at [[irene|Princess Irene]]'s nursery window, terrifying her and precipitating her first recorded flight onto [[the-mountain|the mountain]]. The creature is an example of the misshapen animals bred by the goblins in their subterranean realm.

## Appearance and Behaviour

The creature is described as a "cat with the long legs" (016-004) — a feline form grotesquely distorted, like all goblin creatures. The goblins' domesticated animals were descended from normal surface animals that had been taken underground generations ago, and had become "grotesque and misshapen" from living in the unnatural conditions of the goblin caves (013-004). The long-legged cat is among the most frightening of these creatures, capable of entering through windows and terrorizing the household.

## Encounter with Irene

[[irene|Irene]] first encounters the creature when it comes in through her nursery window:

> "I ought to have run up to you at once when the long-legged cat came in at the window, instead of running out on the mountain and making myself such a fright."
>
> (015-011)

In her panic, Irene fled out onto [[the-mountain|the mountain]] and lost her way, an adventure that ultimately led her to discover her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|great-great-grandmother]]'s tower and the magical world within it. The cat's appearance thus paradoxically enables Irene's first significant encounter with her grandmother.

## Aftermath and Reactions

When Irene tells [[lootie|Lootie]] about the cat, her nurse initially dismisses it. Lootie was "on the point of saying that the long-legged cat must have been a twilight fancy of the princess's" (016-009). However, the memory of the real horrors the household had experienced — and the reprimand [[king-papa|the king]] had given her — prevented her from voicing her disbelief. Lootie herself "had a strong suspicion that the cat was a goblin; for she knew nothing of the difference between the goblins and their creatures: she counted them all just goblins" (016-009).

The gentlemen-at-arms, by contrast, "remembered, with no little horror, just such a creature amongst those they had surprised at their gambols upon the princess's lawn" (016-010). Their corroborating memory validated Irene's account and underscored the real danger posed by the goblin creatures.

## Irene's Transformation

A significant aspect of Irene's encounter with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|her grandmother]] is the way it transforms her fear. After sitting in the grandmother's chamber, surrounded by the [[the-fire-roses|rose-fire]] and silver light:

> If all the long-legged cats in the world had come rushing at her then she would not have been afraid of them for a moment.
>
> (015-039)

This passage marks a key moment in Irene's spiritual development — under her grandmother's protection, the creatures that once terrified her lose their power.

## The Goblins' Creatures

The cat belongs to the broader category of goblin creatures — animals that the goblins kept and bred underground:

> The goblins had a kind of sheep of their own — very queer creatures, which they drove out to feed at night, and the other goblin creatures were wise enough to keep good watch over them, for they knew they should have their bones by and by.
>
> (017-016)

The goblins counted wild mountain goats as theirs as well, and lived partly upon them. The creatures, like their goblin masters, hated daylight and verse, and shared the goblins' aversion to the surface world.
