# The King

[[irene|Princess Irene]]'s father, referred to throughout the novel as "king-papa," is the benevolent ruler of the surface kingdom that sits atop the [[the-mountain|mountain]] where [[goblins|the goblins]] dwell. Though he appears only intermittently, his authority and love provide the framework of order and protection within which [[irene|Irene]]'s adventures unfold — and his eventual recognition of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] validates the novel's central claim that [[courage-and-faith|faith]] perceives what worldly wisdom cannot.

## Role in the Story

The king is an absent-but-present figure. He rides through his kingdom attending to affairs of state, returning to [[the-palace|the palace]] only periodically. Yet his love for [[irene|Irene]] is never in doubt. When he arrives at [[the-palace|the palace]], [[irene|Irene]] runs to him joyfully:

> She clapped her hands right joyfully, "my king-papa is come" (030-003).

His narrative function is twofold. As a father, he embodies the love and protection that [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] extends spiritually — he is the earthly counterpart to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s heavenly care. As a king, he represents legitimate authority, contrasting with the [[the-goblin-king|Goblin King's]] tyrannical rule underground.

The king also serves as the novel's key witness to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s reality. When he sees the [[the-ring|ring]] on [[irene|Irene]]'s finger, he is reassured, though he does not fully understand its origin (017-013). And at the novel's climax, he meets [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] herself:

> "And there is his mother, king-papa!" she said. "See — there. She is coming down to meet you" (030-012).

## Appearance and Traits

MacDonald gives the king no elaborate physical description, but his bea[[the-ring|ring]] and character emerge through action. He rides a white horse (010-004), suggesting purity and nobility. He is tender with [[irene|Irene]], patient with her questions, and dignified in his authority. [[irene|Irene]]'s repeated address of "king-papa" fuses the formal ("king") with the intimate ("papa"), captu[[the-ring|ring]] his dual role as both sovereign and father.

His most notable trait is his willingness to believe. Unlike [[lootie|Lootie]], who dismisses [[irene|Irene]]'s stories as fantasy, the king listens seriously and does not mock. When [[irene|Irene]] asks:

> "Please, king-papa," she said, "will you tell me where you got that beautiful grandmother of mine?" (017-004),

the king's response is measured and respectful. He does not claim to understand [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]], but neither does he deny her existence.

## Key Relationships

- [[irene|Princess Irene]] — his beloved daughter, whose [[courage-and-faith|faith]] in [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] he comes to share
- [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|Irene's Great-Great-Grandmother]] — his own mother, whose existence he only fully confirms at the novel's climax
- [[curdie|Curdie]] — a miner's son whom the king comes to [[trust]] and honor
- [[the-goblin-king|The Goblin King]] — his subterranean counterpart and antagonist, representing corrupted kingship
- [[peter|Peter]] — [[peter|Curdie's father]], a loyal miner in the king's service

## Significance and Themes

The king embodies legitimate, loving authority — the kind of kingship that MacDonald, as a Christian writer, associates with divine fatherhood. His governance is protective, not exploitative; he [[the-mines|mines]] the [[the-mountain|mountain]] for its wealth but does not, like [[goblins|the goblins]], scheme to destroy those above. His reunion with his own mother ([[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]) at the story's end completes the novel's vision of a restored family order: [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]], father, and child united in love, with the [[courage-and-faith|faith]]ful servant [[curdie|Curdie]] brought into the household.

The king's journey from unknowing acceptance (he does not deny [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] but cannot confirm her) to direct encounter (he sees her with his own eyes) mirrors the novel's larger spiritual arc. He is the adult who learns to see what the child has seen all along.
