# Cob

The term used by the miners of the mountain to refer to goblins. "Cob" is a colloquial name reflecting the goblins' association with stone-work, hammering, and their misshapen physical nature.

## Nature / Definition

Curdie explains the term to Irene: "The 'cobs,' as we call them" [006-089]. The word connects to the Old English and dialect word for a lump or rounded mass, fitting the goblins' grotesque physical forms. It also evokes "cobble" — the sound of hammering and stone-working that constantly emanates from the goblin caves [006-028]. The miners use "cobs" as their everyday term for the creatures, distinguishing their practical working-class perspective from the more formal "goblins."

## Narrative Role

The term "cobs" appears primarily in dialogue and narration from Curdie's perspective, marking his miner's worldview. When Curdie describes "the home-life of the cobs in the various caves they called houses" [012-005], the familiar term underscores his intimate knowledge of goblin society. In moments of crisis, the word signals urgency — "The cobs are coming!" [027-003] conveys immediate danger more viscerally than "goblins" would.

## Significance

The dual naming — "goblins" in the narrator's voice and "cobs" in the miners' speech — reflects MacDonald's skill at differentiating social registers. The miners' term is earthy, practical, and devoid of the supernatural connotations of "goblin." It reduces the creatures to a workplace hazard, consistent with the miners' pragmatic relationship to the underground world.
