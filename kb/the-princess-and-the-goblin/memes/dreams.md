# Dreams

Dreams in "The Princess and the Goblin" serve as a central thematic device through which the boundary between reality and imagination is explored. The question of whether [[king-papa|Irene]]'s encounters with her [[light|grandmother]] are dreams or real experiences recurs throughout the novel, testing both [[irene|Irene]]'s [[courage-and-faith|faith]] and the reader's perception.

## Nature / Definition

The dream/reality tension begins early, when [[lootie|Irene's nurse]] dismisses her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] encounter:

> "You've dreamt it, then, child." (004-028)

[[irene|Irene]] firmly denies this:

> "No, I didn't dream it. I went upstairs, and I looked all about." (004-029)

This pattern — others dismissing [[irene|Irene]]'s experiences as dreams while she insists on their reality — becomes the novel's central epistemological question. Even [[irene|Irene]] herself wavers:

> "Then it must all be a dream," said Irene. "I half thought so." (010-017)

But [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] actively works to prove her reality:

> "If you hadn't come to think I was a dream... I will give you one reason to believe I am not a dream." (011-012)

## Symbolic Role

The dream motif is intimately tied to the theme of [[courage-and-faith|faith]]. [[irene|Irene]] must believe in her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]'s reality even when evidence is thin:

> she was not quite sure that she was not dreaming, suddenly a great longing seized her (011-005)

The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] gives [[irene|Irene]] a physical token (the [[the-ring|ring]]) precisely so she can distinguish dream from reality:

> "and then say it had been all a dream." (011-018)

[[curdie|Curdie]] also experiences dream-visions that prove significant — he dreams of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] before meeting her:

> He thought he was ascending on a ladder... At length in his dream he came to a door (027-002, 027-006)

> Curdie remembered his dream, and was silent, thinking (028-027)

## Significance

The dream motif in MacDonald's novel serves a theological purpose. Like [[courage-and-faith|faith]] itself, [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s reality cannot be proven by ordinary means — those who have not encountered her dismiss the experience as imagination. The progression from doubt to certainty that [[irene|Irene]] undergoes mirrors a spiritual journey of [[courage-and-faith|faith]]. The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]'s statement captures this:

> "You may be sure I will do all I can to help you believe I am anything but a dream." (011-062)
