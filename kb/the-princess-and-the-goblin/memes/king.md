# King

The concept of kingship in "The Princess and the Goblin" is embodied primarily through [[king-papa|Irene's father]], the benevolent ruler of the surface kingdom. Kingship in the novel is defined by the fusion of authority with love — a model of governance that stands in deliberate contrast to the tyrannical rule of the [[the-goblin-king|Goblin King]] underground.

## Nature / Definition

Kingship in MacDonald's world is not merely political — it is paternal and, ultimately, theological. The king is introduced in the novel's first sentence as the father of the "little princess" and the ruler "over a great country full of mountains and valleys" (001-002). His rule is defined by protection: he laid "what they thought to be a spell" — an annual tribute — to keep the goblins at bay (001-005).

> The princess, whose name was Irene, was born there, but she was sent to be brought up by country people in a large house, half castle, half farmhouse, on the side of another mountain, about halfway between its base and its peak. Her father was the king. (001-002)

The king's authority is legitimate because it is exercised for the good of his subjects, not for self-aggrandizement. He rides out to attend to the affairs of his kingdom, leaving [[irene|Irene]] at the palace; he returns periodically to visit her. His absences are not neglect but duty, and his returns are moments of joy and reunion.

## Narrative Role

Though the king appears only intermittently in the narrative, his presence is felt throughout. [[irene|Irene]]'s repeated address — "king-papa" — fuses the formal ("king") with the intimate ("papa"), encapsulating MacDonald's vision of kingship as fatherhood writ large.

The king's most significant narrative function is as the novel's key witness. When he sees the ring on [[irene|Irene]]'s finger, he is reassured, though he does not fully understand its origin (017-013). At the climax, he meets [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] directly — the moment that validates [[irene|Irene]]'s faith and demonstrates that earthly authority and heavenly wisdom are ultimately aligned (030-012).

> She clapped her hands right joyfully, "my king-papa is come." (030-003)

## Two Kings: Contrasted Models

The novel deliberately contrasts two models of kingship. The human king rules with love and duty from his sunlit palace on the mountain slope. The [[the-goblin-king|Goblin King]] rules through fear and force from his underground hall. Where the human king's authority rests on care for his subjects, the goblin king's authority rests on power and intimidation. Where the human king welcomes [[curdie|Curdie]] and recognizes [[irene|Irene]]'s truth, the goblin king imprisons those who challenge him.

This contrast extends to family. The human king's relationship with [[irene|Irene]] is one of tender love; the goblin king's relationship with [[harelip|Harelip]], his son, is transactional and cruel. MacDonald uses the two kings to argue that legitimate authority is inseparable from love — a ruler who does not love is a tyrant, no matter his title.

## Significance

Kingship in the novel is ultimately a type of divine fatherhood. The king's authority is earthly, but it participates in a larger order. [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|The grandmother]], as the king's mother, stands above even the king — suggesting that royal authority is nested within a higher, more ancient, and more powerful maternal-feminine divine order.

When the king meets his mother at the novel's climax (030-012–030-014), the hierarchy is made explicit: the king bows to the queen-mother. Kingship is real and good, but it is not ultimate. The true sovereign is the grandmother, whose power is not political but spiritual.
