# The Kitchen

The kitchen and servants' quarters of [[king-papa|the king]]'s [[the-palace|palace]], a separate social domain from the royal apartments where [[irene|Princess Irene]] lives. It appears as a liminal space through which [[irene|Irene]] passes on adventures, a place where the usual hierarchies are at once present and permeable.

## Location and Social Nature

The kitchen is located in the lower part of the [[the-palace|palace]], reached through narrow passages. [[irene|Irene]] first stumbles upon it by accident during her explorations:

> "After walking for some distance, looking this way and that, and sometimes stopping to listen, for she thought she heard some noise, and then going on again, without really knowing whither she was going, she found herself in the kitchen." (005-013)

The kitchen is connected to the servants' hall, forming a distinct social sphere within the palace. Although [[irene|Irene]] is not strictly "allowed" there, the boundary is porous:

> "Although she was not allowed to go into the servants' hall or the kitchen, where she was not princess, but only a little girl." (011-002)

## The Cook and Her Cat

The kitchen is the domain of the [[cook]], a figure of domestic authority whose great black cat becomes an obstacle during [[irene|Irene]]'s night journey following [[the-magical-thread|the thread]]:

> "The cook's great black cat, pursued by the housemaid's terrier, had bounced against her bedroom door." (020-003)

The kitchen thus represents the bustling, practical life of the palace — the world of cooking, cleaning, and daily work that continues beneath [[irene|Irene]]'s more rarefied existence and sometimes intrudes upon it.

## Significance

The kitchen embodies the novel's democratic spirit. It is a place where [[irene|Irene]] is "not princess, but only a little girl" (011-002), reminding readers that beneath all social rank lies common humanity. The kitchen also serves a narrative function as a transitional space — [[irene|Irene]] passes near it on her way to adventures, crossing from the ordered upper palace into the unknown [[the-passage|passage]]s and stairways.

At the novel's climax, when the flood threatens, the kitchen becomes a place of urgent action, with the cook and servants rallying to their posts — the palace's ordinary life mobilized against extraordinary danger (030-018).
