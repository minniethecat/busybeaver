# Sunrise

Sunrise in [[The Princess and the Goblin]] marks moments of new perception and natural beauty. The dawn world MacDonald describes is fresh and uncorrupted — a stark contrast to the underground realm of [[goblins|the goblins]] and the dangers of night (020-006).

## Irene's First Sunrise

[[irene|Irene]] experiences sunrise for the first time on a mountain walk:

> She had never in her life been out before sunrise, and everything was so fresh (020-006).

This moment of wonder is characteristic of MacDonald's treatment of nature — the created world reveals beauty to those who encounter it with innocence. For [[irene|Irene]], sunrise is a discovery as real as [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s [[the-grandmothers-tower|tower]].

## The Sun in the Novel

The sun appears as a benevolent presence throughout the book. [[irene|Irene]] anthropomorphises it playfully:

> "See, see, Lootie! The sun has had his face washed" (006-003).

This childlike vision of the sun as a friendly being, washed clean by rain, reflects MacDonald's theology — the natural world is not neutral but actively good, a gift from a loving creator.

## Sunset to Sunrise

The guards who patrol [[the-palace|the palace]] watch "round it from [[sunset]] to sunrise" (010-033), establishing the rhythm of day and night as the novel's temporal framework. [[goblins|The goblins]], who "live away from the sun, in cold and wet and dark places" (001-005), are defined by their opposition to daylight — sunrise is their banishment.
