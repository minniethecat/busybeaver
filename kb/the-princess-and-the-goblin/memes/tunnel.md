# Tunnel

An underground passage engineered by the [[goblins]] to breach the boundary between their subterranean realm and the [[the-palace|king's house]] above. Unlike natural cave formations or mine shafts, the tunnel is a calculated act of aggression — a deliberate invasion route that drives the novel's climactic conflict and its resolution (013-006, 031-018).

## Nature / Definition

The goblins are miners by nature, constantly excavating the mountain's interior. Their tunnels are not random but purposeful. The tunnel in question is a specific, targeted excavation: the goblins are "mining on a new plan" (013-006), cutting a passage toward the human world above.

> The goblins, as Curdie had discovered, were mining on a new plan — they had broken into the channel of a small stream, but the break being in the top of the channel, the water ran down only when it happened to be above the level of the opening (013-006).

This accidental breach of the stream channel is the fatal flaw in the goblins' engineering. Their tunnel intersects natural waterways that they cannot control. What they intend as an invasion route becomes a conduit for their destruction.

The tunnel's destination is later confirmed: it was "the tunnel they had made to the king's house" (031-018). This is not general mining; it is an act of territorial aggression, aimed directly at the seat of human authority.

## Narrative Role

The tunnel functions as a plot device at two critical points in the novel. First, [[curdie|Curdie]] discovers the goblins' new mining plan during his scouting mission (013-006). The discovery puts him in danger — the goblins capture him — but the knowledge he gains proves essential.

Second, the tunnel becomes the key to the goblins' defeat. When [[curdie|Curdie]] explains the situation to the king:

> "It is, Your Majesty," said Curdie; and proceeded to tell him about the second scheme of the goblins (031-018).

[[curdie|Curdie]] realizes that the tunnel, which the goblins dug as an invasion route, can be used against them. By flooding the underground reservoirs into the tunnels, the human defenders can destroy the goblin realm entirely.

The final mention of tunnels in the novel underscores the goblins' irredeemable nature. Even after the flood destroys most of them:

> Setting all hands to the work, tunnelling here and building there (032-004).

The survivors immediately resume their underground engineering, suggesting that tunnelling is not merely a tactic for them but an expression of their fundamental nature — creatures who reshape the earth to their own dark purposes.

## Significance

The tunnel represents the goblins' principal mode of action: patient, subterranean, and ultimately self-defeating. Their engineering skill is undeniable — they carve passages through solid rock, plan invasions with strategic precision. But their work always contains the seed of its own failure. By breaking into the stream channel, they set in motion the very flood that destroys them.

This pattern of self-defeating malice runs throughout MacDonald's theology: evil, in attempting to extend its reach, invariably creates the conditions for its own undoing. The tunnel is the physical embodiment of this principle. The goblins dig their own grave, and [[curdie|Curdie]] simply opens the sluice.
