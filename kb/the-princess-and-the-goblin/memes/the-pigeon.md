# The Pigeon

**The Pigeons** are a flock of magical white birds kept by [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|Irene's great-great-grandmother]] in her hidden tower chamber. They serve multiple roles: providing sustenance for the grandmother, acting as messengers, and bringing materials from faraway lands for the grandmother's weaving.

## Description

When [[irene|Irene]] first climbs to the top of the grandmother's tower, she discovers the pigeons:

> When she reached the top, she found herself in a little square place, with three doors, one opposite to the stair and one on each side. She stood looking about her, and in a minute or two a multitude of the loveliest pigeons, mostly white, but of all colours, came flying in from outside.
>
> (003-073)

The tower features many pigeonholes with nests, "some with young ones, some with eggs" (003-082). The pigeons come and go freely through openings in the tower, and the grandmother explains that they "feed themselves" (003-079).

## Provider of Sustenance

The grandmother subsists on pigeons' eggs. This detail becomes a point of curiosity in the household below. [[lootie|Lootie]] tells Irene's father, [[king-papa|the king]], "And she lives upon pigeons' eggs" (004-011). The other servants repeat this, and it becomes common knowledge:

> Everyone in the house, it was evident, seeing she lived on pigeons' eggs, and cooked them herself, that she was a real, actual princess.
>
> (005-002)

Irene herself becomes curious about the taste of pigeons' eggs and asks her nurse about them (005-004–005-005).

## The White Pigeon as Messenger

A particularly significant pigeon appears during Irene's visit when [[king-papa|the king]] is present:

> At that moment a snow-white pigeon flew in at an open window and alighted on the old lady's shoulder.
>
> (010-018)

When [[king-papa|the king]] tries to take the pigeon, it evades him — "but it spread its wings and flew back to the old lady" (010-020). The pigeon's loyalty to the grandmother alone suggests it recognizes only her authority, reinforcing the grandmother's mysterious power.

## Weavers of the Thread

The pigeons serve a crucial practical function in the grandmother's work. They bring her spider-web material from distant lands, which she uses to spin [[the-magical-thread|the invisible thread]]:

> The old lady responded that the spider-webs came from over the great sea — of a particular kind. "My pigeons bring it me from over the great sea."
>
> (011-031)

This detail links the pigeons to [[the-magical-thread|the magical thread]] that guides [[irene|Irene]] throughout the novel — the very thread spun from material gathered by these birds connects the grandmother to her great-great-granddaughter across all distances.

## Significance

The pigeons represent the quiet, unassuming magic that pervades the grandmother's world. They are ordinary in appearance — white pigeons do not seem fantastical — yet they cross oceans to bring exotic materials, recognize only the grandmother, and sustain her without visible effort. Like many elements of the grandmother's realm, they hint at powers far beyond the ordinary while presenting a surface of homely simplicity.

The pigeons also serve as a subtle link between the grandmother's tower and the wider world beyond the mountain. While much of the novel is confined to the mountain, the mines, and the palace, the pigeons' flights across "the great sea" remind the reader that the grandmother's concerns and connections extend far beyond the immediate setting.
