# Sky

The sky in *The Princess and the Goblin* is a defining feature of the surface world, marking the boundary between the human realm above and the goblin realm below. It is associated with light, freedom, beauty, and the natural order that goblins have rejected.

## Sky as the Surface World

The sky is what goblins lack and what defines the human world. The goblins once "lived above ground, and were very like other people" [001-005] but were driven underground. Their existence beneath the mountain means they never see the sky — a condition that shapes their ugliness and malice. The novel sets the open sky against the "subterranean caverns" [001-005] as opposing principles.

## Irene's Vision of the Sky

Irene's most striking encounter with the sky comes through her grandmother's magic. Looking through her grandmother's window, she sees "the sky, and the moon and the stars" [015-028] — and is struck by its depth: "It looked as if there was no bottom to it" [015-028]. This vision comes after she has been lost in dark passages, making the contrast between enclosed darkness and infinite sky particularly powerful.

## Sky and Weather

The sky shapes the novel's atmosphere through weather. The story opens on "one very wet day, when the mountain was covered with mist" [002-003], and Irene and Lootie walk "up the mountain; for the road was so hard and steep that the water could not rest upon it" [006-004]. On the morning Irene follows her thread to Curdie, "the sky was mottled with small clouds" [020-004] and "the wind blew deliciously over the mountainside" [020-004], the open sky offering freedom after her night fears.

## The Goblin Rejection of Sky

The goblins' hatred of the surface world is fundamentally a rejection of sky and sunlight. They sing of mining through rock and scheme to flood the mines, but never aspire to walk beneath the open sky. Their realm is enclosed, their light artificial. The sky represents everything they have lost and everything they now oppose.

## Symbolic Meaning

The sky embodies openness, visibility, and divine order. It is the realm of stars that Irene sees through her grandmother's magic, the source of purifying rain, and the canopy beneath which all human action takes place. The novel's spatial imagination — above ground / below ground — depends on the sky as the ultimate reference point for the world of light and goodness.
