# Hope

Hope in *The Princess and the Goblin* is not passive optimism but an active trust that sustains characters through danger and uncertainty. It is embodied in the magical thread, in Irene's confidence in her great-grandmother, and in the expectation of rescue that keeps Curdie alive in the goblins' prison.

## Hope and the Thread

The magical thread is the tangible form of hope. When Irene follows it, "she had to go down rough stairs, and even sometimes a ladder" [020-008], yet she continues because the thread promises a destination. The great-grandmother says the thread will "lead her straight" [020-002], and this promise — this hope — is what allows Irene to walk through darkness.

## Hope in Darkness

When Curdie is trapped in the goblin caves, hope sustains him. He believes Irene will bring help, and "a beautiful smile spread like sunshine over his face" [017-005] when he thinks of her. Hope is not certainty — it is the willingness to act as if rescue will come, even when the evidence is thin.

## Tested Hope

Irene's hope is tested when she cannot find the tower stair on subsequent attempts. She "could not find the stair of the tower" [005-011], and her nurse Lootie dismisses her story as a dream. Yet she persists, hoping that the great-grandmother is real — and this hope is eventually rewarded with proof. The novel suggests that genuine hope survives doubt and disbelief.
