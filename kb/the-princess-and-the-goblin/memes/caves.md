# Caves

The natural and excavated hollow spaces within the mountains that serve as the goblins' dwellings and territory. These caves form an underground network beneath the surface world of the king's house and the miners' workings.

## Nature / Definition

The mountains around the king's house are "full of hollow places underneath; huge caverns" (001-004). The goblins have adapted these natural formations into their dwellings — "caves, that is, occupied by goblin families" (018-003). The goblins live in these caves as their domestic spaces, described as "the life of the cobs in the various caves they called houses" (012-005). The miners' excavations connect to the goblins' natural cave network, creating a shared underground geography.

## Narrative Role

The caves form the primary setting for the goblin society and for Curdie's underground adventures. When Curdie works late in the mine, he overhears the goblins' plans through the cave walls (008-061). He later traverses the goblin caves, passing "one after another he passed goblin houses" (018-003), discovering their scheme against the king's house. The caves also contain the goblin palace with its grand hall and the king's throne "hollowed out of a huge block of green copper ore" (009-007).

## Significance

The caves represent the hidden, subterranean world that exists beneath everyday life — a classic MacDonald theme of invisible realities. The goblins' cave-dwelling existence contrasts with the surface world of the king's house, creating a vertical cosmology that maps onto the novel's moral geography: light and goodness above, darkness and malice below.
