# Pickaxe

[[curdie]]'s pickaxe in [[The Princess and the Goblin]] is the tool that defines his identity as a [[miners|miner]], serves as his primary defense against [[goblins|cobs]], and functions as the anchor for his explorations of the goblin tunnels. It is the physical embodiment of his working-class heroism — a common tool elevated to extraordinary significance through its wielder's courage and resourcefulness.

## Nature and Definition

The pickaxe is introduced in the context of the miners' daily labor. When [[curdie]] and his father [[peter]] enter the mine, they join their companions who are "soon hard at work with their pickaxes and shovels and hammers" (007-005). The pickaxe is one of several tools that define the miner's trade, used for striking rock, dislodging ore, and boring into the mountain. It is a tool of production and honest labor — the opposite of the goblins' tools, which are used for secret tunneling and malicious scheming.

But the pickaxe quickly transcends its mundane function. When [[curdie]] begins his reconnaissance missions into goblin territory, the pickaxe becomes an anchor: "The end of this string he fastened to his pickaxe, which figured no bad anchor" (012-005). As Curdie unrolls his ball of string through the goblins' labyrinthine passages, the pickaxe left at the mine entrance holds the other end, providing the only guarantee of his safe return. The pickaxe-as-anchor symbolizes the miner's world as a fixed point of safety and sanity from which Curdie ventures into chaos.

In combat, the pickaxe transforms again into a weapon. When Curdie is ambushed by [[goblins|the cobs']] creatures in the dark, "his hand fell upon his pickaxe, and before the horrid beasts could do him any serious harm, he was laying about with it right and left" (018-003). The narrator explicitly frames it as a battle-axe: "He stood for a little, weighing his battle-axe in his hand as if it had been the most precious lump of metal — but indeed no lump of gold itself could have been so precious at the time as that common tool" (018-003). This remarkable passage dignifies the laborer's tool above gold itself, a clear statement of MacDonald's thematic priorities.

## Narrative Role

The pickaxe is present at nearly every major turn of Curdie's story. It anchors his string-guided navigation. It serves as his weapon against goblin creatures, goblin soldiers, and the goblin king and queen. It is lost and recovered — the goblin queen's creatures drag it away while Curdie is unconscious, leading him into the heart of the goblin court (018-003). Its recovery, when Curdie finds it "lying by the side of the queen" in the royal bedchamber (021-051), marks the moment he regains his agency and escapes.

Crucially, when Curdie faces the goblin queen's granite shoes, "his only chance with her would have been to attack the granite shoes with his pickaxe, but before he could think of that she had caught him up" (018-037). The pickaxe is the tool that might have won the fight — its absence at the critical moment underscores its irreplaceable value.

## Significance

The pickaxe is the clearest symbol in the novel of MacDonald's elevation of honest labor to heroic dignity. Curdie is not a knight with a sword; he is a miner with a pickaxe, and his tool is shown to be equal to — or better than — any conventional weapon. The narrator's explicit comparison of the pickaxe to a battle-axe, and his assertion that gold itself could not be more precious, is a direct thematic statement: the tools of productive work are nobler than the treasures of kings. This aligns with the novel's broader democratic vision, in which a miner's son and a princess must cooperate as equals to defeat the goblin threat.

The pickaxe also embodies the novel's fusion of the domestic and the heroic. It is a tool of subsistence — Curdie uses it to earn his family's bread — that doubles as a tool of survival and triumph. In this, it parallels [[irene]]'s thread from her grandmother: a seemingly ordinary object (miner's tool / sewing thread) that becomes, in the hands of the faithful and courageous, an instrument of deliverance.
