# Mining

The act of digging into [[The Princess and the Goblin|the mountain]] connects the two opposing communities in the novel. The human [[miners]] dig for honest livelihood, while [[goblins|the goblins]] mine to expand their underground domain and threaten [[the-palace|the palace]] above (013-006).

## Human Mining

[[king-papa|The king]]'s [[miners]], led by [[peter]], work the mountain for precious metals. Their work is honorable and productive — it supports their families and serves [[king-papa|their king]]. The miners' practical knowledge of tunnels and rock formations becomes crucial to the plot (001-004).

## Goblin Mining

[[goblins|The goblins]] mine not for wealth but for conquest. [[curdie|Curdie]] discovers their advance:

> Curdie was satisfied that the goblins were mining in the direction of the palace (023-058).

He calculates their trajectory by listening underground:

> "I know something of what direction the cobs are mining" (023-038).

The goblins work "both day and night" (013-006), their relentless digging driving the novel's tension toward the climax.

## The Conflict of Mines

The novel's central conflict is, literally, a conflict over underground space. Both [[miners|humans]] and [[goblins|goblins]] dig into the same mountain, and their tunnels inevitably intersect. The resolution comes when [[peter|the miners]], warned by [[curdie]], flood the goblin tunnels — turning the goblins' own mining ambition against them (032-005).
