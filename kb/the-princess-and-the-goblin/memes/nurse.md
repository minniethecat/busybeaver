# Nurse

Irene's nursemaid and primary caretaker at the king's house, known affectionately as "Lootie" by the young princess. A well-meaning but practical-minded woman whose disbelief in Irene's stories of her great-great-grandmother creates narrative tension and highlights the novel's theme of faith versus skepticism.

## Role in the Story

The nurse serves as Irene's guardian and companion in the king's house, responsible for the princess's daily care and supervision. Her narrative function is as a foil to Irene's imaginative and spiritual experiences: where Irene readily believes in the supernatural grandmother, the nurse consistently dismisses these accounts as childish fantasy [004-024][004-026]. This skepticism persists even after the nurse herself is rescued by Curdie during the goblin encounter on the mountain, revealing her fundamentally pragmatic nature [006-009][006-016].

## Appearance and Traits

The nurse is characterized by her emotional expressiveness and protective instincts. She is quick to tears when frightened [006-009], easily vexed when Irene contradicts her worldview [004-026], and equally swift to affection when moved [004-038]. Despite her skepticism toward Irene's stories, she genuinely loves the child, as shown by her terror during the goblin encounter and her determination to protect Irene at all costs [006-022][006-055].

## Significance and Themes

The nurse embodies the theme of mundane perception versus spiritual or imaginative truth. Her persistent disbelief in the grandmother — despite Irene's detailed accounts — parallels the broader tension in MacDonald's work between visible reality and invisible truth. As MacDonald writes through the narrator, the nurse represents the limitations of a worldview confined to ordinary experience [004-034].
