# Hill

The hills in *The Princess and the Goblin* are the slopes and inclines of the mountain landscape that characters must navigate. They shape action — making journeys difficult, facilitating danger, and forming part of the novel's rugged terrain.

## The Mountain's Slopes

The great house sits on the mountain slopes, and nearly every journey involves going up or down hill. When Irene and Lootie walk out, "the road was so hard and steep that the water could not rest upon it" [006-004], the hill defining the landscape. Irene falls "with a crash" on "a hard downhill road" [006-016] while fleeing from what she believes to be a goblin, the slope itself becoming a hazard.

## The Goblin Entrance

The goblins' domain is entered through the hillside: "They entered the hill by a natural opening under a huge rock, where a little stream rushed out" [007-005]. The hill is the threshold between worlds — its rocky face conceals the passages that lead down into the goblin kingdom. The stream that "sped away down the hill through the garden" [010-032] marks another connection between underground water and surface landscape.

## Uphill Struggles

Running uphill represents effort and danger in the novel. Irene, fleeing a suspected goblin creature, is "soon out of breath with running uphill" [014-009] but keeps going, driven by fear. In contrast, Curdie hurries "up the hill without meeting a single goblin on the way" [009-025] after escaping the underground, the uphill climb marking his return to safety and fresh air.

## The King's Approach

The hill serves as a stage for the king's arrival. Irene, playing on the lawn, hears "the distant blast of a bugle" [010-003] and watches her father's party descend the mountain. The hill connects the wider kingdom to the great house, and the king's horsemen navigate its slopes.

## The Hill as Home

The hills are also domestic space. Curdie's mother's cottage sits on the mountainside, and "the goats belonged to the miners mostly—a few of them to Curdie's mother" [017-016], grazing on the hills. The hill is not merely an obstacle but a place where life — human and animal — is lived.
