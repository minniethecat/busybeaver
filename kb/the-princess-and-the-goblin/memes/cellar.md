# The Cellar

A subterranean storage room beneath [[king-papa|the king]]'s house, situated over a huge block within the mountain's foundation, and the point where the [[goblins|goblin]]-dug [[the-tunnel|tunnel]] breaks through into the human world — the literal threshold of invasion (026-009, 027-017).

## Location and Physical Setting

The cellar lies beneath [[king-papa|the king]]'s house, directly above the mountain's bedrock. The text describes "a huge block which lay under the cellars of the house, within the line of the foundations" (026-009). This geological detail is structurally crucial: the goblins have tunneled upward and outward, but the massive foundation block presents a barrier they must circumvent or break through.

The cellar is identified specifically as "the king's wine cellar" (026-010), a detail that gives the space material texture — it holds valuable stores, casks of wine, the provisions of the household. When [[curdie|Curdie]] and the [[king-papa|king]]'s party arrive there:

> ...the wine cellar, and regardless of its huge vats... (026-015).

The cellar is a working domestic space, not a dungeon or vault, which makes its transformation into a battlefield all the more striking.

## The Goblin Incursion

The cellar is the destination of the goblin invasion route. [[goblins|The goblins]], tunneling from their underground [[the-cave|caves]], "came out in the king's wine cellar" (026-010). This is the culmination of their long-plotting scheme — to bypass the surface defenses and emerge directly inside [[king-papa|the king]]'s own house.

> It was full of goblins... (027-017).

When [[curdie|Curdie]] and the household forces reach the cellar, they find it swarming with goblins. The scene that follows is the novel's climax: combat in a confined underground space, with goblins stampeding and the household forces pressing their advantage. The cellar's physical nature — enclosed, below ground, filled with heavy casks — shapes the battle that takes place within it.

## Curdie's Navigation

[[curdie|Curdie]]'s knowledge of the underground proves decisive. He tracks the goblins "up on rafters, and down to the cellars" (027-010) and leads the way through the house to intercept them. When their path leads "through the wine cellar" (027-018), he understands the tactical situation and makes a critical decision — not to follow the fleeing goblins back down their tunnel, but to secure the cellar and cut off their retreat.

> He was turning away for the cellar to follow the goblins... (028-002).

The cellar thus becomes both the point of incursion and the point where the goblin threat is decisively met and turned back.

## The Cellar and the Foundation

The cellar's position — sitting directly on "a huge block which lay under the cellars" (026-009) — is structurally significant. The block is part of the mountain's bedrock, the solid foundation on which [[king-papa|the king]]'s house stands. The goblins' tunneling comes "not many feet below the cellar" (030-007), undermining this foundation. The cellar is thus the thin floor between civilization and the chaos beneath, and the goblins' breach is an architectural violation as much as a military one.

## The Cellar in the Novel's Vertical Scheme

In the novel's moral geography, the cellar occupies the lowest point of the human world — deeper than [[the-kitchen|the kitchen]], far below the [[tower|tower]], directly above the goblin [[the-cave|caves]]. It is the dark inverse of the tower: where the tower houses [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] in light and spinning, the cellar houses wine casks and becomes a chamber of [[goblins|goblin]] violence. The cellar and the tower are the vertical extremes of [[king-papa|the king]]'s house, defining the full range of the story's inhabited space.

After the invasion is repelled, the goblins' tunnel into the cellar is "securely built up" and "greatly widened" (032-004) — sealed and fortified, turning a vulnerability into a permanent strong point. The cellar's story ends not in destruction but in reinforcement: a breach discovered, a threat met, a foundation made stronger.

## Significance

The cellar is the novel's most physical location of danger — not the atmospheric dread of the goblin [[the-cave|caves]] or the spiritual test of the [[tower|tower stair]], but the immediate, tangible threat of enemies pouring through a hole in the floor. It is where the abstract becomes concrete: the goblin menace is no longer a distant rumor or a song heard underground but a horde occupying [[king-papa|the king]]'s own storeroom.

The cellar's transformation from wine storage to battlefield to sealed stronghold mirrors the novel's larger arc: ordinary life, disrupted by danger, restored through courage and faith — but permanently marked by the knowledge of what lies beneath.
