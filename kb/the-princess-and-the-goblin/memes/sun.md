# Sun

The sun in *The Princess and the Goblin* is a defining boundary between the two worlds of the novel. It illuminates the human realm of the palace, garden, and mountain slopes, while the goblins — who "lived away from the sun, in cold and wet and dark places" (001-005) — are creatures of the sunless underground. The sun symbolizes the divine order that the goblins reject.

## The Surface World's Light

For Irene, the sun is a source of joy and beauty. When "the sun broke out so gloriously" (006-002) after rain, she exclaims, "See, see, Lootie! The sun has had his face washed. Look how bright he is!" (006-003). She personifies the sun as a living presence with a face that can be washed clean by rain. The "open air and the sunlight" (006-030) are essential for growing things, including Curdie who "was about twelve years old" and benefitted from working outdoors like "even vegetables."

## Sunset as Boundary

Sunset marks the boundary of safety and obedience in the novel. Lootie is under "express orders to be out with the princess one moment after the sun was down" (006-009), and when they stray too far up the mountain, "the sun was getting low" (006-005) becomes a source of panic. The king considers it serious to "let the princess be out after sunset" (006-104). The goblins begin to emerge as the sun sets, and the locked gates after sunset represent the division between the safe daylight world and the perilous night.

## Goblins and the Sun

The goblins are defined by their separation from the sun. They are "born of a sun-mother" (018-017) — a goblin explanation for any physical deformity — but they "chose to be busy at night" (019-004) out of aversion to "sun-people" (019-007). The goblins' contempt for surface-dwellers is expressed through this term: they call humans "sun-creatures" (019-022) and find something about them "very troublesome" (019-019).

## The King's Arrival in Sunlight

The king's visit comes on "one splendid sunshiny day" (010-003) when Irene hears "the distant blast of a bugle" (010-003). The sun illuminates the king's golden crown and white horse, making his entrance a blaze of light. When the white pigeon Irene holds spreads its wings, "its whiteness made one flash in the sun and vanished" (010-020) — a moment of supernatural brightness in which the bird seems to become pure light.

## Sunrises and New Beginnings

Irene has never "been out before sunrise" (020-006) when she follows the thread at dawn. In the early morning, "everything was so fresh and cool and lively" (020-006), the sunrise marking not just a new day but a new stage in Irene's journey. The thread itself "looks just like a spider's web" in the sunlight (021-077) — visible only in the light, invisible in darkness — making the sun the condition under which magical guidance can be perceived by others.

## Sun and the Mine

Even in the underground mine, the sun's absence is felt. The miners work where "no light of the sun ever came" (007-006), and the contrast with the surface world defines the mine as a liminal space between the human and goblin realms. Curdie, whose eyes are "as sparkling as the crystals in their rocks" (006-030), brings something of the sun's brightness underground with him.
