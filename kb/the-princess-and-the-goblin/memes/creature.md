# Creature

The misshapen domesticated animals kept by the goblins in their underground realm. These "creatures" are descended from surface animals that followed the goblins underground generations ago, becoming grotesque and deformed through their subterranean existence.

## Nature / Definition

The goblin creatures are the domesticated animals of the underground world. MacDonald explains that "the original stocks of these horrible creatures were very much the same as the animals now seen on the surface of the earth" [013-004], but centuries of underground living have transformed them. They are described as "creatures — but so grotesque and misshapen" [013-002], with each generation producing stranger and more hideous forms [013-005].

## Narrative Role

The creatures serve as mounts and companions for the goblins. The goblin queen's creature appears as a bizarre riding animal, and the goblins use their creatures for transport within the cave network. They also represent the corrupting influence of the underground: living away from the sun and surface has physically degraded these animals, mirroring the goblins' own moral and physical degeneration.

## Significance

The creatures embody MacDonald's theme of degeneration through separation from the natural order. Their physical grotesquerie is a direct consequence of living underground, just as the goblins' moral corruption stems from their rejection of the surface world and its values. The creatures function as a vivid visual metaphor for what happens when beings are cut off from light and nature.
