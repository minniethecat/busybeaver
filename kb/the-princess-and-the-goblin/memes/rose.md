# Rose

A recurring floral motif in *The Princess and the Goblin*, appearing in multiple forms: the supernatural fire of roses in the grandmother's tower, the rose-coloured furnishings, and the wild primroses on the mountain. The rose serves as a sensory marker of the grandmother's presence and a symbol of divine love and beauty.

## Nature / Definition

The rose in MacDonald's novel is not merely a flower but a multi-sensory symbol associated with the great-great-grandmother's domain. It manifests primarily as the "rose fire" — a supernatural fire made of burning roses that provides light and warmth without consuming (015-007)(015-018). The grandmother's chamber also features rose-coloured velvet curtains and a rose-coloured counterpane (011-040)(022-019). In the natural world, primroses grow in the mountain hollows (017-014)(020-004).

## Properties

The rose fire possesses unique supernatural properties. It burns with intense heat yet does not destroy — when the grandmother throws a rose from the fire, it is described as "too hot for you yet" rather than deadly (015-023). Irene's magical thread is later tempered in the rose-fire, gaining the properties that allow it to guide her through the darkness of the mountain passages (020-010). The fire also emits a distinctive fragrance "like that of roses and lilies" that fills the room (011-041).

## Narrative Role

The rose motif is tightly bound to Irene's encounters with her grandmother. The rose fire first appears when Irene visits the grandmother's tower chamber, marking the space as otherworldly (015-007). The grandmother uses a burning rose to demonstrate her nature to Irene, passing it across her hand and fingers unharmed (015-018). Later, the grandmother tempers Irene's magical thread in the rose-fire, connecting the rose motif to the thread that guides Irene through the mountain's darkness (020-010).

## Significance

The rose is a traditional Christian symbol of divine love, and MacDonald deploys it consistently to mark the grandmother's benevolent supernatural presence. The rose fire that burns without consuming echoes the biblical burning bush, while the fragrance of roses signals the grandmother's nearness to Irene. The primroses on the mountain extend the floral motif into the natural world, suggesting that divine beauty permeates all realms of the novel's universe.
