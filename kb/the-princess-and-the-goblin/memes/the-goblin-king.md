# The Goblin King

The sovereign of the subterranean [[goblins|goblin race]] and the primary antagonist of the novel. He rules from an [[the-goblin-palace|underground palace]] and commands [[goblins|the goblins]]' secret operations — the tunneling, the flooding plan, and the attempted abduction of [[irene|Princess Irene]] (018-004, 026-011, 027-023).

## Role in the Story

The goblin king is the architect of the central conflict. He orchestrates [[goblins|the goblins]]' long-term plan: to excavate beneath [[the-palace|the palace]], flood [[the-mines|the king's mines]], and kidnap [[irene|Princess Irene]] to marry her to his son, the [[goblins|goblin]] prince (026-011). This plan unites the novel's scattered threats into a single, coordinated scheme.

His authority over [[goblins|the goblins]] is absolute. When his orders are c[[the-hall|hall]]enged, his rage is [[fear]]some. Yet he is also cunning — the plan is methodical and patient, executed over many nights of secret labor.

## Appearance and Traits

The goblin king is described with a mixture of the grotesque and the regal. He presides over a court of [[goblins|goblin]]s around a fire (018-004) and appears in battle "roa[[the-ring|ring]] like a tiger" at the head of his bodyguard (027-023). His physical form shares [[goblins|the goblins]]' characteristic deformities — soft, vulnerable feet and animal-like features — but he is distinguished by his intelligence and the force of his will.

## Key Relationships

- [[the-goblin-queen|The Goblin Queen]] — his consort, who shares his underground court
- Harelip — his son, the [[goblins|goblin]] prince he intends to marry to [[irene|Irene]]
- [[goblins]] — his subjects, who [[fear]] and obey him
- [[curdie|Curdie]] — his nemesis, who repeatedly thwarts his plans

## Significance and Themes

The goblin king represents tyrannical evil — organized, patient, and malicious. Unlike [[goblins|the goblins]] collectively (who are more pitiable than terrifying — degenerated through no choice of their own), [[king-papa|the king]] is evil by will. He plots, schemes, and commands with conscious intent.

His defeat in the novel's climax — by [[water]], the element he planned to weaponize — is an ironic reversal typical of MacDonald's moral framework: evil defeats itself through its own machinations.
