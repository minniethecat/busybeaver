# Roses

Roses and primroses are recurring botanical symbols in the novel, consistently associated with [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|Irene's great-great-grandmother]], her magical tower chamber, and the sensory experience of the supernatural breaking into the ordinary world.

## Nature and Symbolism

Roses appear most prominently as the "fire of roses" — a magical fire that burns in [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s chamber, composed of actual roses that glow with warmth and light without being consumed:

> "She saw a fire of roses—a heap of roses in the middle of the floor. And the roses were burning. She had never seen anything more beautiful." (015-007)

The roses are both literal flowers and symbolic emblems. [[irene|Irene]] reaches into the fire and handles one of the burning roses without harm, suggesting that in her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]]'s presence, the ordinary laws of nature are suspended:

> "She took, as she thought, a rose from between her fingers, one of the burning roses, passed it once and again and a third time down her arm from shoulder to wrist, and the pain was gone." (015-018)

The scent of roses pervades the grandmother's chamber and follows [[irene|Irene]] into the ordinary world:

> "It smells of all the roses in the world, I think." (022-062)

## Primroses in the Wild

Primroses appear outdoors, in the natural world of the [[the-mountain|mountain]], and carry a similar association with kindness and innocence. [[irene|Irene]] finds them in the hollows during her outings:

> "In the summer hollows there were lovely primroses, and not so many that she ever picked one without feeling that was a pity." (017-014)

The [[goblins|goblins]] recognize [[irene|Irene]] as "the little girl I saw talking to the primroses a few weeks ago" (023-029), suggesting that her gentle relationship with the flowers marks her as special — a child who exists in harmony with the natural world.

## Significance

The roses are the clearest sensory proof of the grandmother's reality — they are seen, smelled, and felt. When [[curdie|Curdie]] finally perceives them in Chapter 22, his skepticism is challenged by direct experience. The primroses extend this symbolism into the outdoor world, linking the grandmother's supernatural grace with the natural beauty of creation.

> "Don't you smell the roses? It was my grandmother healed you." (028-036)
