# Moonlight

In [[The Princess and the Goblin]], moonlight is not mere natural illumination but a supernatural signal intimately tied to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]. It is the only light under which she spins, the beacon by which her messenger pigeons navigate, and the medium through which [[irene]] first glimpses the grandmother's true, ageless beauty.

## Nature and Definition

Moonlight in the novel bridges the natural and supernatural worlds. The grandmother's spinning is explicitly governed by lunar cycles — she works only on moonlit nights, and only so long as the moon shines upon her wheel:

> "I don't work every night—only moonlit nights, and then no longer than the moon shines upon my wheel. I shan't work much longer tonight" (011-033).

The moonlight itself seems to merge with the grandmother's own radiance. When [[irene]] enters the tower room, the grandmother's silvery hair becomes indistinguishable from the light streaming in, so that "you could not have told which was which" (011-010). This fusion suggests that the grandmother is herself a source of the light she appears merely to work under.

The grandmother's "moon" — a globe of silvery light hanging from the dome of her bedchamber — is a permanent, never-extinguished lamp that serves as a beacon for her pigeons:

> "In the darkest night, if any of my pigeons are out on a message, they always see my moon and know where to fly to" (011-053).

When [[irene]] calls it simply "your moon," she blurs the line between the celestial body and the grandmother's personal possession, reinforcing the sense that the grandmother commands or embodies this light.

## Narrative Role

Moonlight is structurally central to the novel's most important moments. The grandmother first reveals her true, golden-haired form under the full moon (015-007). The goblins are driven back not by sunlight but by a silvery globe of light that descends upon [[curdie-mother|Curdie's mother]] when she is surrounded by cobs on the mountain path (023-025). And [[irene]]'s second visit to the tower — the visit that establishes their recurring relationship — is undertaken by following the moonlight up the old staircase (011-008).

## Significance

Moonlight in the novel functions as a sign of divine presence and protection. It is the light of revelation — under it, [[irene]] sees what is invisible to others ([[lootie|her nurse]] cannot see the grandmother at all in daylight). It is also the light of order and rhythm: the grandmother spins by the moon's schedule, spinning the thread of [[irene]]'s life and guidance. The goblins, creatures of absolute darkness, are repelled by moonlight and its silvery kin, suggesting their incompatibility with any light, even the gentler, reflected light of the moon rather than the harshness of the sun. This positions moonlight as a specifically maternal, feminine, and nurturing form of divine illumination — appropriate to a novel in which the grandmother, not the king, is the primary supernatural guardian.
