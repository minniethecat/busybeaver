# Singing as Protection

Singing and improvised rhyming verses serve as a practical defense against [[goblins|goblin]]s, who physically cannot bear the sound of human music. [[curdie|Curdie]] the [[curdie|miner-boy]] discovers and exploits this weakness, using song as both weapon and shield throughout his encounters with the subterranean creatures (006-026–006-030).

## Discovery

[[curdie|Curdie]] discovers [[goblins|the goblins]]' vulnerability to song early in the novel. While wor[[king-papa|king]] in the [[the-mines|mines]], he notices that his singing and whistling provoke a violent reaction from [[goblins|the goblins]]:

> "They can't bear singing, and they can't stand that song of yours." (006-029)

The [[goblins|goblin]]s' reaction is not merely dislike but physical pain — they recoil, grimace, and retreat when faced with human music. This makes singing a uniquely effective non-violent weapon.

## Properties

The protective power of singing has specific characteristics:

- It must be **genuine** rather than [[fear]]ful — [[courage-and-faith|courage]] expressed through song
- **Rhyming verses** seem especially potent, perhaps because of their structured, ordered nature versus [[goblins|goblin]] chaos
- The effect is **immediate** — [[goblins|goblin]]s react as soon as singing begins
- It works as a **group defense** — miners singing together can keep [[goblins|goblin]]s at bay

## The Grandmother's Song

The [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|grandmother]] also uses song, but hers is of a different order entirely. When she sings, it is "a strange sweet song" that seems to operate on a cosmic rather than merely defensive level (022-060). Her singing suggests that music in the novel operates on a spectrum — from [[curdie|Curdie]]'s folk rhymes that merely repel, to [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]]'s supernatural song that transforms.

## Significance

Singing represents the power of order, beauty, and [[courage-and-faith|courage]] over chaos and [[darkness]]. The [[goblins|goblin]]s — creatures of the dark, of "animal" nature rather than human — cannot tolerate the structured beauty of music. This aligns with MacDonald's Christian worldview: song as an expression of the divine order that evil cannot endure.

[[curdie|Curdie]]'s improvised rhymes also demonstrate his quick wit and [[courage-and-faith|courage]]. He does not merely sing pre-learned [[songs]] but creates verses on the spot, turning his [[fear]] into creative defiance — a model of how the novel suggests one should face danger.
