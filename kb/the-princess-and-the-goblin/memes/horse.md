# Horse

Horses in *The Princess and the Goblin* appear exclusively in connection with the king and his men-at-arms. They are creatures of the surface world, symbols of royal authority, mobility, and the swift response to danger that characterizes the human kingdom in contrast to the goblin underworld.

## The King's White Horse

The king rides a distinctive white horse that marks his royal status. When Irene first sees her father approaching, "he rode a white horse and was taller than any of the men with him" [010-004]. The king wears "a narrow circle of gold set with jewels around his helmet" [010-004], and the white horse beneath him completes the image of majesty. Irene immediately recognizes her father and "ran to the side of the white horse and held up her arms" [010-005].

## The King's Bond with His Horse

The king has a notable connection with his horse, speaking to it directly as a trusted companion. After greeting Irene, "he spoke to his white horse, and the great beautiful creature walked away over the grass" [010-006], demonstrating both the horse's training and the mutual understanding between king and mount. When the king departs, "he rode away upon his great white horse" [010-033], the adjective "great" emphasizing the horse's stature as befitting a king.

## Horses in the Return Visit

When the king returns in spring, he again arrives on horseback: "the king rode through its budding valleys to see his little daughter" [017-002]. His horse is part of a larger mounted company — "all the people of the house about him" gather as he sits "on his horse" [030-006] after Curdie carries Irene to safety.

## Horses in Crisis

The flood crisis at the end of the novel reveals the practical importance of horses. The king urgently declares, "We must set out at once. But how to get at the horses!" [031-020]. Curdie leads the men-at-arms to the stables where "they found their horses in terror; the water was rising fast around them" [031-023]. The horses' fear mirrors the danger, and their rescue is essential to the party's escape. The king's fastest horse is also noted when a servant is dispatched "on the fastest horse in the stable, to tell your king-papa" [024-024] of Irene's illness.

## Symbolic Meaning

Horses represent the reach and speed of the human kingdom above ground. Unlike goblins, who move through tunnels, the king and his men travel openly on horseback across the landscape. Horses connect the worlds — bringing the king to Irene, carrying messages, and enabling rescue. The absence of any goblin horses underscores the subterranean creatures' separation from surface life.
