# The Irene–Curdie–Goblins Triangle

The three most-referenced entities in the wiki — [[irene|Princess Irene]] (70 backlinks), [[curdie|Curdie]] (63 backlinks), and [[goblins|the goblins]] (59 backlinks) — form the narrative triangle that drives *The Princess and the Goblin*. Their relationships define the novel's central conflicts: light against darkness, [[courage-and-faith|faith]] against [[fear]], and surface against underground.

## Irene and Curdie: Faith Meets Action

[[irene|Irene]] and [[curdie|Curdie]] represent complementary virtues. Irene embodies [[courage-and-faith|faith]] — she follows [[the-magical-thread|an invisible thread]] through [[darkness]] because she [[trust|trusts]] her [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|great-great-grandmother]] (020-002–021-002). Curdie embodies action — he works in [[the-mines|the mines]], fights [[goblins|goblins]] with rhymes, and navigates [[the-cave|underground tunnels]] by instinct and courage (006-026–006-029).

Their relationship passes through three phases:
1. **Skepticism**: Curdie dismisses Irene's account of [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] as a dream (004-039)
2. **Mutual rescue**: Irene rescues Curdie from the [[goblins|goblins]]' prison using [[the-magical-thread|the thread]] (020-002–021-095); later Curdie protects Irene in the [[the-mines|mines]]
3. **Shared faith**: Curdie comes to believe in [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] and [[the-magical-thread|the thread]], uniting action with faith

## Curdie and the Goblins: The Underground War

[[curdie|Curdie]] and [[goblins|the goblins]] are antagonists locked in an asymmetric war. The goblins vastly outnumber Curdie, but he possesses what they lack: [[singing-as-protection|creative defiance]]. His improvised rhyming verses exploit the goblins' vulnerability to [[songs|music and verse]] (006-026–006-029), a folkloric weakness MacDonald weaves into the narrative.

The goblins' plot — to flood [[the-mines|the mines]] and abduct [[irene|Irene]] for their [[the-goblin-king|goblin king]]'s son — puts Curdie in the position of defender. His knowledge of [[the-mines|mining]] and [[the-underground|underground passages]] makes him uniquely capable of opposing them.

## Irene and the Goblins: The Prize and the Threat

[[irene|Irene]] is the goblins' intended victim but also their ultimate undoing. The [[goblins|goblins]] plan to kidnap her as a bride for [[the-goblin-king|Harelip]], the goblin prince (019-002–019-010). Yet it is Irene who, following [[the-magical-thread|the thread]], exposes their plans and helps defeat them.

Where Curdie fights goblins directly, Irene transcends them — her [[courage-and-faith|faith]] in [[the-magical-thread|the thread]] leads her through goblin territory without engaging on their terms.

## Thematic Resolution

The triangle resolves in the novel's climax: Irene follows [[the-magical-thread|the thread]] to [[king-papa|the king]], Curdie fights through the [[the-mines|mines]] to protect her, and the [[goblins|goblins]] are defeated by [[water|flood]] — the very element they planned to use as a weapon (031-002–032-001).

MacDonald's message is embedded in this structure: [[courage-and-faith|faith]] (Irene), courage (Curdie), and [[fear|malice]] (the goblins) are not abstract qualities but forces embodied in characters whose relationships drive the story toward its conclusion.
