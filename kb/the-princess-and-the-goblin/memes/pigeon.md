# Pigeon

A flock of pigeons — mostly white — that inhabit [[the-grandmothers-tower|the grandmother's tower]], providing eggs for her sustenance, serving as her messengers through the darkest night, and brooding over gifts she keeps warm. The pigeons are the first creatures [[irene|Irene]] encounters in her grandmother's domain, marking the tower as a space of peace and gentle care (003-073, 011-053).

## Nature / Definition

The pigeons are described as multicolored, predominantly white:

> A multitude of the loveliest pigeons, mostly white, but of all colours, nestled about the room (003-073).

They are self-sustaining creatures. When [[irene|Irene]] asks how they get their food, [[irenes-great-great-grandmother|the grandmother]] explains:

> "The pigeons feed themselves. They've got wings now and then — you shall see" (003-079).

When Irene asks why they need to fly, the grandmother gives an elegantly simple answer:

> "Just so. If they couldn't fly, I couldn't eat their eggs" (003-080).

This exchange establishes the pigeons as part of a self-contained, self-sustaining domestic economy. They do not need to be fed or tended by the grandmother — they come and go freely, returning to their "pigeonholes with nests, some with young ones, some with eggs" (003-082).

## Messenger Pigeons

The pigeons' most significant narrative function is as messengers. The grandmother explains that even in darkness, her pigeons can find their way home:

> In the darkest night, if any of my pigeons are out on a message, they always find their way home (011-053).

This capability aligns the pigeons with [[the-grandmothers-lamp|the grandmother's lamp]] — a beacon that "which guided her pigeons home through the darkest night" (014-009). The lamp and pigeons form a system: the light guides, the birds carry messages. Neither [[irene|Irene]] nor the reader ever witnesses the pigeons actually delivering a message, but the grandmother's claim establishes them as an intelligence network, a gentle counterpart to the goblins' underground spies.

A snow-white pigeon makes a dramatic appearance in chapter X, flying in at an open window while [[irene|Irene]] is with her nurse:

> At that moment a snow-white pigeon flew in at an open window (010-018).

When [[curdie|Curdie]] reaches out to catch it, "it spread its wings and flew away" (010-020). The pigeon's ability to evade capture while making its presence known suggests it is on a mission — perhaps checking on Irene, or carrying unseen intelligence back to the grandmother.

## Brooding Pigeons as Incubators

The pigeons serve a third function: as living incubators. The grandmother keeps an object warm "under one of my brooding pigeons" (015-038). This detail links the pigeons to the novel's themes of warmth, nurture, and protection. They are not merely utilitarian creatures but participants in the grandmother's economy of care — their body heat preserving what must be kept alive.

## Significance

The pigeons embody the grandmother's mode of presence in the world: gentle, indirect, self-sustaining. They do not attack — they fly. They do not demand care — they feed themselves. Yet they are not passive. They carry messages, provide nourishment (through their eggs), and offer warmth through their brooding bodies. They are, in miniature, what the grandmother herself is at scale: a quiet sustaining force that needs no acknowledgment from the world it sustains.

The contrast with the goblins is striking. The goblins tunnel and hammer and dig; their presence in the mountain is violent and extractive. The pigeons fly and coo and lay eggs; their presence in the tower is generative. Where goblins reshape the earth by force, pigeons participate in its order by nature.
