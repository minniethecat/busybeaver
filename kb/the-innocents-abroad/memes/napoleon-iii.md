# Napoleon III

Emperor of the French (1852–1870), encountered by Mark Twain during the Paris visit of 1867. Napoleon III is one of the most extensively characterized figures in *The Innocents Abroad* — Twain renders him through a remarkable blend of satirical portraiture, political analysis, and grudging admiration.

## Overview

Napoleon III, born Charles-Louis Napoleon Bonaparte, was the nephew of Napoleon I and ruled France as Emperor during Twain's 1867 visit. Twain first glimpses him at a military review at the Arc de l'Etoile alongside the Sultan of Turkey, Abdul Aziz (013-040, 013-041). The encounter produces some of the book's most vivid character sketches, as Twain contrasts the two rulers — one representing European modernity and energy, the other representing oriental despotism and indolence (013-045, 013-050). Over the course of multiple chapters, Twain returns to Napoleon III repeatedly, building a composite portrait that mixes caustic observation of his physical appearance and cunning with genuine respect for his administrative achievements and personal resilience.

## In the Travelogue

Twain and his companions first hear of Napoleon III through the Old Travelers — "those delightful parrots" who claim to "know more about the country than Louis Napoleon knows now or ever will know" (012-014). The real encounter comes in Paris, when Twain learns that the Emperor and the Sultan of Turkey are about to review twenty-five thousand troops at the Arc de l'Etoile (013-040). Twain immediately abandons the International Exposition, declaring he "had a greater anxiety to see these men than I could have had to see twenty expositions" (013-040).

At the review, Twain describes Napoleon III with unsparing physical detail: "a long-bodied, short-legged man, fiercely moustached, old, wrinkled, with eyes half closed, and such a deep, crafty, scheming expression about them" (013-043). He watches the Emperor bow "ever so gently" to the crowd, observing everything "with his cat eyes from under his depressed hat brim, as if to discover any sign that those cheers were not heartfelt and cordial" (013-043). This portrait of suspicion and calculation is paired with a rhapsodic passage tracing Napoleon III's improbable rise: from exile and obscurity in America — where he "associated with the common herd" and "ran foot races for a wager" — to his time as a London policeman, the failed Strasbourg coup, imprisonment at Ham, and his eventual ascent as President and then Emperor through the coup d'etat of 1851 (013-046). Twain exclaims: "Who talks of the marvels of fiction? Who speaks of the wonders of romance?" (013-046).

Later, Twain sees the Emperor again in the Bois de Boulogne, where Napoleon's carriage procession "outshone them all" amid dukes, duchesses, and elaborate liveries (014-024). At Versailles, Twain reflects on Napoleonic "strategy" and notes the Emperor's boulevard-building project in Paris — straight thoroughfares designed so "a cannon ball could traverse from end to end without meeting an obstruction more irresistible than the flesh and bones of men" (016-008). These broad avenues were Napoleon III's deliberate answer to the barricade-building revolutionaries of the Faubourg St. Antoine (016-008).

In Italy, Twain sees Napoleon III's influence everywhere. The grand turnpikes, palatial railway depots, and handsome boulevards of Florence and other Italian cities are, to Twain, imitative of "the genius of Louis Napoleon" — though he notes that unlike Italy, France under Napoleon III has "the wherewithal to back up his projects" (025-004). Much later, in Yalta, Twain compares Czar Alexander II favorably to Napoleon III, noting that the Russian Emperor has "none of that cunning in his eye that all of us noticed in Louis Napoleon's" (037-009).

## Historical Context

Charles-Louis Napoleon Bonaparte (1808–1873) was the nephew of Napoleon I and spent much of his early life in exile. After two failed coup attempts — the Strasbourg affair of 1836 and the Boulogne landing of 1840 — he was imprisoned at the fortress of Ham, from which he escaped in 1846. Following the Revolution of 1848, he was elected President of the French Second Republic, and in December 1851 he staged a coup d'etat, proclaiming himself Emperor Napoleon III the following year. His reign saw the extensive modernization of Paris under Baron Haussmann, the construction of railways across France, and an aggressive foreign policy that included the Crimean War and the disastrous French intervention in Mexico, which ended with the execution of Emperor Maximilian I in 1867 — an event Twain alludes to with sharp feeling (016-008).

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's judgment of Napoleon III is notably ambivalent. He cannot help but admire the man's "nerve, his calm self-reliance, his shrewd good sense" (016-008), calling him "the brilliant adventurer" and "the genius of Energy, Persistence, Enterprise" (013-050). The passage at (013-046) is one of the book's most extraordinary set-pieces — a sustained celebration of Napoleon III's improbable rise from obscurity to imperial power, rendered in a breathless series of contrasting clauses that build toward near-biblical cadence.

Yet Twain's admiration is always qualified. He cannot feel "friendly toward my quondam fellow-American, Napoleon III" because of the Maximilian affair — "when in fancy I see his credulous victim, Maximilian, lying stark and stiff in Mexico" (016-008). The physical description at (013-043) emphasizes craftiness and suspicion rather than nobility. And the contrast drawn between Napoleon III and Alexander II at Yalta — the latter's eye lacking the "cunning" that all the travelers noticed in Louis Napoleon's (037-009) — confirms that Twain saw the French Emperor as fundamentally untrustworthy. Napoleon III is, for Twain, a man of extraordinary gifts used in the service of ambition rather than principle.
