# Rome

Capital of Italy and the ancient Roman Empire, visited by Twain and his party in Chapters 25 through 28 — a city whose monumental accumulation of history, art, and relics provoked in Twain both genuine wonder and a comic exhaustion with the obligations of tourism.

## Overview

Rome presented Twain with a problem of scale. Unlike Venice, whose romantic decay could be captured in moonlit gondola rides, or Paris, whose modernity offset its historical weight, Rome was an unmanageable density of things one was obligated to see. "What is there in Rome for me to see that others have not seen before me? What is there for me to touch that others have not touched?" (026-003). The question was not merely rhetorical — it expressed a genuine crisis of tourist fatigue.

Yet Twain's Rome chapters contain some of his richest description. The Coliseum, St. Peter's, the catacombs, the relic economy, the hierarchy of saints — all are rendered with the characteristic blend of awe and skepticism that defines the travelogue. His conclusion was characteristically honest: "The surest way to stop writing about Rome is to stop. I wished to write a real 'guidebook' chapter on this fascinating city, but I could not do it, because I have felt all the time like a boy in a candy shop — there was everything to choose, and yet no choice" (028-042).

## Arrival and First Impressions

The party reached Rome from Civita Vecchia, the ancient port city: "We shall go by rail to Civita and from thence to Rome, and by rail to Naples" (024-024). The approach was marked by Twain's own bureaucratic frustrations — "I was going to get my passport vised for Rome in Florence, and then they would not let me come in the front gate" (025-024), and his sardonic observation that the Italian authorities regarded his passport with suspicion "among the criminal archives of Rome" (025-026).

His first extended meditation on Rome came through a remarkable conceit: the idea that one could "scarcely throw a stone across at Rome" from a neighboring point, and yet the view encompassed everything. From that vantage, he wrote, "you can see every notable object in Rome, from the Castle of St. Angelo to the Coliseum" (026-012). The party's dragoman claimed that St. Peter himself "started away from Rome by the Appian Way" from this very spot (026-013), a claim Twain records with his usual deadpan.

## Description and Notable Features

### The Coliseum and Ancient Rome

The Coliseum received perhaps Twain's most concentrated attention among the ancient monuments. "The Coliseum was the theatre of Rome, and Rome was mistress of the world" (026-017). The structure was not merely a ruin but a symbol of the empire's moral character: "In ancient Rome they combined religious duty with pleasure" (026-016). The arena where Christians were martyred and gladiators fought became, in Twain's account, the emblem of everything magnificent and terrible about the civilization that built it.

Surrounding the Coliseum, the entire fabric of ancient Rome lay in fragments. Even the Pantheon, "the pagan temple of all the gods," had been "saved by being consecrated as a Christian church" (026-015). Twain was struck by the paradox: the temples of the old gods survived precisely because they had been repurposed. "The beautiful Pantheon, the noblest of the monuments of ancient Rome," owed its preservation to the very religion it had opposed (026-015).

### St. Peter's Basilica

St. Peter's dominated Twain's Rome as it dominated the city's skyline. The basilica was "second only to St. Peter's at Rome" — a phrase that appeared even when describing other great churches, as if all ecclesiastical architecture existed on a scale whose endpoint was fixed in the Vatican (018-029). Twain described the interior with genuine awe, noting the markers on the floor showing the comparative lengths of other great cathedrals: "the greatest depth of St. Peter's at Rome — which is to say that each side chapel in St. Peter's is as large as most ordinary churches" (058-027).

Yet the experience of visiting was itself overwhelming. "At St. Peter's, at Rome, it is almost impossible to see it, because one is too familiar with it already" (054-003). The building's fame preceded it so completely that the reality could only disappoint — a theme Twain developed across his Roman account.

### The Catacombs

Beneath the city, the catacombs provided Twain with a chilling glimpse of early Christian Rome. He noted that there were "three hundred and sixty catacombs under Rome, each with its maze of narrow passages" (027-078). The underground tunnels, where Christians had buried their dead and worshipped in secret during the persecutions, were a reminder that the splendor of St. Peter's had been built on centuries of hidden suffering.

The catacomb visit also provided material for Twain's skepticism about relics. Bones were everywhere attributed to saints, and the sheer quantity strained credulity — a theme he developed across the Italian chapters.

### The Relic Economy

Rome was the global capital of the relic trade, and Twain documented the system with meticulous irony. "The Holy Personages rank thus in Rome," he wrote, before enumerating a precise hierarchy: "First — The Redeemer. Second — The Virgin. Third — The Apostles. Fourth — The Martyrs..." (028-033). The ranking was not merely theological but commercial — different classes of saint commanded different levels of veneration and, implicitly, different prices for their relics.

He noted with amusement that there were "no 'Christ's Churches' in Rome, and no 'Churches of the Holy Ghost'" — the naming conventions were themselves part of the city's elaborate taxonomy of holiness (028-040). Day after day and night after night, the party trudged "among the crumbling wonders of Rome" (028-041), accumulating a kind of saint-fatigue that Twain rendered comic rather than reverent.

## Historical Significance

Rome's significance in Twain's narrative operates on multiple levels. It is the capital of classical antiquity, the seat of the Catholic Church, and the repository of Western art. Twain is aware that he stands at the intersection of all three traditions, and the weight is almost too much.

He reflects that "Rome was mistress of the world" and that her empire extended "through Greece Rome, and through Rome the world" (058-034). The aqueducts he saw reminded him of what he had "seen about Rome" elsewhere, connecting the imperial infrastructure to the landscapes of the Holy Land (041-009). Even the guide who showed the party through the Coliseum "talked of the grand old days of Rome well nigh two thousand years ago" and "painted the scenes of that old time" with vividness (028-015).

The sixth-century plague that "visited Rome" becomes a set-piece for Twain's ambivalence: a fearful pestilence supposedly stayed by the appearance of St. Michael sheathing his sword atop Hadrian's tomb, now the Castle of St. Angelo (027-086). The legend is recorded with the same deadpan as the biblical assertions — Twain reports the tradition without endorsing it.

## Daily Life and Local Color

Twain's Rome is less concerned with contemporary daily life than with the burden of the past. The present-day city barely appears except as the scaffolding on which the ancient monuments are displayed. The guides lead, the tourists follow, the saints multiply, and the relics accumulate — this is the rhythm of the Roman sojourn.

One vivid local episode concerns a noblewoman who visited St. Calasanctius: "whose house in Rome he visited; he visited only the houses of the poor" (027-082). And Twain notes that "when the French troops came to Rome, and when Pius VII was carried away," the political upheavals of the modern era had done nothing to diminish the city's ecclesiastical character (027-083).

The artists of Rome come in for characteristic deflation: "The sublime history of Rome remains unpainted! They paint St. Sebastian stuck full of arrows, and call it a historical piece" (028-025). Twain's exasperation with the endless repetition of martyrological subjects — in a city whose actual history was so vast — speaks to his preference for narrative vitality over devotional convention.

## Twain's Verdict

Twain left Rome without a settled opinion, which he freely admitted: "I wished to write a real 'guidebook' chapter on this fascinating city, but I could not do it, because I have felt all the time like a boy in a candy shop — there was everything to choose, and yet no choice" (028-042). This is perhaps the most honest verdict in the entire travelogue: the admission that Rome was simply too much.

He found the ancient ruins genuinely moving, the Coliseum magnificent, and St. Peter's overwhelming. But the sheer accumulation of things one was supposed to admire, the endless parade of saints and martyrs and relics, produced a reaction that was as much comic as reverent. Rome was the city that finally made him stop writing about cities.

> "The surest way to stop writing about Rome is to stop." (028-042)
