# Milan

Northern Italian city and capital of Lombardy, reached by rail from Genoa as the second major stop of the excursion party's Italian tour — dominated in Twain's narrative by the Cathedral, which he declared "the princeliest creation that ever brain of man conceived."

## Overview

Milan entered Twain's consciousness before he ever saw it. The excursion program listed as a waypoint from Genoa, and the party had been "dying to see the renowned cathedral" long before the train drew near (018-005). Twain's account of Milan is heavily weighted toward its two great artistic treasures — the Cathedral and Leonardo's Last Supper — but it also contains some of his funniest vignettes: the jewel-encrusted corpse of St. Charles Borromeo, the bathhouse with no soap, and the startling observation that Milan had the most luxuriant crop of dwarfs in Italy.

## The Cathedral

The approach to Milan was dominated by one thought. "Toward dusk we drew near Milan and caught glimpses of the city and the blue mountain peaks beyond. But we were not caring for these things — they did not interest us in the least. We were in a fever of impatience; we were dying to see the renowned cathedral!" (018-005). Twain was certain he would recognize it anywhere: "We needed no one to point it out — we did not wish anyone to point it out — we would recognize it even in the desert of the great Sahara" (018-005).

The Cathedral rewarded every expectation: "Howsoever you look at the great cathedral, it is noble, it is beautiful! Wherever you stand in Milan or within seven miles of Milan, it is visible and when it is visible, no other object can chain your whole attention" (018-009). "It is the first thing you look for when you rise in the morning, and the last your lingering gaze rests upon at night. Surely it must be the princeliest creation that ever brain of man conceived" (018-009).

Twain's account of the Cathedral's architecture and ornamentation runs for several pages through Chapter 18, describing its "marble wilderness of graceful spires" and its thousands of statues. "They say that the Cathedral of Milan is second only to St. Peter's at Rome. I cannot understand how it can be second to anything made by human hands" (018-015). The bas-reliefs and statues adorning the facade were designed by "Raphael, Michelangelo, Canova — giants like these gave birth to the designs, and their own pupils carved them" (018-010).

## St. Charles Borromeo and the Treasury

Beneath the Cathedral, in the crypt under the grand altar, Twain encountered one of his most macabre spectacles — the preserved body of St. Charles Borromeo, Bishop of Milan. The saint was "a warmhearted, unselfish man; a man whose whole life was given to succoring the poor, encouraging the fainthearted, visiting the sick," who moved among the plague-stricken in Milan "brave where all others were cowards, full of compassion where pity had been crushed out of all other breasts" (018-019).

The tomb was opened by a priest turning a windlass. The sarcophagus split lengthwise, and "the lower part sank down and disclosed a coffin of rock crystal as clear as the atmosphere. Within lay the body, robed in costly habiliments covered with gold embroidery and starred with scintillating gems. The decaying head was black with age, the dry skin was drawn tight to the bones, the eyes were gone, there was a hole in the temple and another in the cheek, and the skinny lips were parted as in a ghastly smile!" (018-020). Over the dreadful face "hung a crown sown thick with flashing brilliants; and upon the breast lay crosses and croziers of solid gold that were splendid with emeralds and diamonds" (018-020).

Twain calculated the furnishings of the dead saint's chamber "weighed six millions of francs in ounces and carats alone, without a penny thrown into the account for the costly workmanship bestowed upon them" (018-022). The cathedral treasury was even more astonishing: "There were Virgins and bishops there, above their natural size, made of solid silver, each worth, by weight, from eight hundred thousand to two millions of francs... bas-reliefs that weighed six hundred pounds, carved in solid silver; croziers and crosses, and candlesticks six and eight feet high, all of virgin gold, and brilliant with precious stones" (018-022). The total value, "by simple weight, without counting workmanship, were valued at fifty millions of francs!" (018-022). Twain quipped: "If I could get the custody of them for a while, I fear me the market price of silver bishops would advance shortly, on account of their exceeding scarcity in the Cathedral of Milan" (018-022).

The relic collection included "two of St. Paul's fingers, and one of St. Peter's; a bone of Judas Iscariot, (it was black), and also bones of all the other disciples; a handkerchief in which the Saviour had left the impression of his face" (018-026). "Once a year all these holy relics are carried in procession through the streets of Milan" (018-026).

## The Last Supper

In "an ancient tumbledown ruin of a church," Twain found "the mournful wreck of the most celebrated painting in the world — The Last Supper, by Leonardo da Vinci" (019-031). The encounter was marred by a placard of "wretched English" describing the refectory rules (019-031). Twain's reaction to the famous painting is characteristically deflationary: the party "are not infallible judges of pictures," and the chief impression is of decay and diminishment.

Yet the painting moved him to a broader reflection on the Italian art experience. After viewing the Ambrosian Library, with its manuscript of Virgil annotated by Petrarch and drawings by "Michelangelo (these Italians call him Mickel Angelo)" (019-007), Twain developed his theory that copies always look better than originals: "I do not know what the picture was, but by the time we had been in Milan half a day, I had formed the opinion that a copy of an ancient picture was invariably a great deal prettier to look at than the picture itself" (019-035).

## Daily Life and the Bathhouse

Twain's bath in Milan became one of his finest comic set pieces. "We have had a bath in Milan, in a public bathhouse. They were going to put all three of us in one bathtub, but we objected. Each of us had an Italian farm on his back. We could have felt affluent if we had been officially surveyed and fenced in" (019-015). After undressing, "we discovered that haunting atrocity that has embittered our lives in so many cities and villages of Italy and France — there was no soap" (019-015). A woman nearly walked in on them, leading to a frantic exchange through the door.

The Roman amphitheatre, modernized by the Milanese for "more peaceful recreations," served as "a race track, and at other seasons they flood it with water and have spirited yachting regattas there" (019-009). The guide's English was so bad that "he would hardly try so hazardous an experiment as the telling of a falsehood, when it is all he can do to speak the truth in English without getting the lockjaw" (019-009).

Outside the city, the party drove "two miles out of Milan to 'see ze echo,' as the guide expressed it" (019-067). Troops of peasant girls "hooted at us, shouted at us, made all manner of game of us, and entirely delighted me. My long-cherished judgment was confirmed. I always did think those frowsy, romantic, unwashed peasant girls I had read so much about in poetry were a glaring fraud" (019-067).

## Dwarfs and Departure

In a memorable passage from Chapter 33, Twain ranks European cities by their physical deformities: "If you want dwarfs — I mean just a few dwarfs for a curiosity — go to Genoa. If you wish to buy them by the gross, for retail, go to Milan. There are plenty of dwarfs all over Italy, but it did seem to me that in Milan the crop was luxuriant" (033-013). The passage continues through a comic tour of cripples across the continent, culminating in Constantinople where the "truly gifted flourish."

The departure from Milan was by rail toward Lake Como: "We left Milan by rail. The Cathedral six or seven miles behind us; vast, dreamy, bluish, snow-clad mountains twenty miles in front of us — these were the accented points in the scenery. The more immediate scenery consisted of fields and farmhouses outside the car and a monster-headed dwarf and a moustached woman inside it. These latter were not show-people. Alas, deformity and female beards are too common in Italy to attract attention" (020-002). In his final retrospective, Twain remembered "Milan again, and her stately Cathedral with its marble wilderness of graceful spires" (062-005).
