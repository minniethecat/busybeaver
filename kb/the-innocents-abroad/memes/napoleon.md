# Napoleon

French Emperor Napoleon Bonaparte and his nephew Napoleon III loom large in Mark Twain's *The Innocents Abroad*, serving as recurring touchstones for Twain's commentary on French power, vanity, and the layered history of the places he visits on his 1867 grand tour.

## Overview

Napoleon appears throughout *The Innocents Abroad* not as a single coherent portrait but as a dual presence: the legendary Napoleon I, whose military conquests and imperial ambition left monuments and scars across Europe, and the reigning Napoleon III, whom Twain encounters in contemporary Paris and treats with characteristic irreverence. Twain's Napoleon is simultaneously a figure of historical awe — the man who was crowned by the Pope, whose bones rest at Les Invalides — and a target of democratic American skepticism. The narrative moves between these registers freely, sometimes within the same paragraph, reflecting Twain's broader ambivalence toward Old World aristocracy and the cult of the conqueror.

Twain's treatment of Napoleon is part of the book's larger project of debunking European grandeur through American eyes. Where European guidebooks genuflect, Twain cracks jokes. Where history books solemnly recount Napoleon's campaigns, Twain notes that Napoleon's horses kicked the legs off ancient statues [019-034]. This irreverent lens — treating emperors as men and monuments as curiosities — is one of the book's most distinctive and influential comic strategies.

## In the Travelogue

Twain first encounters Napoleon in Paris, where the emperor's presence is inescapable. At the Tuileries and the Place Vendome, he sees "the Napoleon Column" rising above the city [015-041]. During a grand review at the Bois de Boulogne, Twain catches "a glimpse of the Emperor Napoleon and the Sultan of Turkey" together [013-001], a moment of imperial theater that he observes with the sharp eye of a foreign correspondent.

The review scene provides Twain with one of his most vivid set-pieces. Napoleon III appears "in military uniform — a long-bodied, short-legged man" who is "the representative of the highest modern civilization, progress, and refinement" [013-045] [013-046]. The irony is unmistakable: Twain's praise is delivered through gritted teeth, and the portrait that emerges is of a man whose power rests on image management rather than genuine greatness. "Does the Emperor Napoleon live here now, Ferguson?" Twain asks his companion at a grand edifice, the question itself a deflation of imperial pretension [013-024].

At Fontainebleau, Twain deepens his engagement with Napoleonic history. The palace is "filled, as it is, with souvenirs of Napoleon the First, and three dead kings" [016-005], a phrase that casually demotes monarchs to museum pieces. He notes that "Louis Napoleon has taken care of all that" — repairing the damage of past revolutions — but the tone suggests grudging acknowledgment rather than admiration [016-008]. The "wonderful park — Paradise Lost — Napoleonic strategy" at Fontainebleau becomes a metaphor for the gap between imperial ambition and human reality [016-001].

In Milan, Twain encounters further evidence of Napoleon's material impact on Europe. At the Brera gallery, he observes a painting "so smoked and discolored by time, and Napoleon's horses kicked the legs off most of the antique statues" [019-034]. This casual aside — lumping time's decay with Napoleonic vandalism — typifies Twain's leveling wit.

The journey's later stages continue to reference Napoleon. In Venice, Twain notes a physical resemblance between a local figure and "Louis Napoleon" [023-015]. In the Holy Land, even at a distance from European politics, Napoleon remains a reference point: a guide estimates the cost of horses as "two Napoleons — eight dollars" [047-026], the emperor's very name having become currency.

## Historical Context

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) rose from Corsican obscurity to become Emperor of the French, conquering much of Europe before his defeat at Waterloo and exile to Saint Helena. His nephew, Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte (1808–1873), was elected President of France in 1848 and proclaimed himself Emperor Napoleon III in 1852. At the time of Twain's visit in 1867, Napoleon III was in the final years of his reign — the Franco-Prussian War and the collapse of the Second Empire lay just three years ahead.

Twain's American readers in 1869 would have been intimately familiar with both Napoleons. Napoleon I represented the old European order that the United States had defined itself against; Napoleon III was a contemporary figure whose Mexican intervention had challenged the Monroe Doctrine and whose reign embodied the autocratic tendencies that American democracy defined itself against. Twain's mockery thus had a political edge that his original audience would have immediately recognized.

The Tuileries Palace, the Napoleon Column in the Place Vendome, and Les Invalides (where Napoleon I's tomb rests) were among the most visited sites in Paris. Twain's descriptions of these monuments reflect the standard tourist itinerary of the period, but his commentary consistently undermines the reverent tone expected by European guidebooks.

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's portrait of Napoleon III is one of the most sustained pieces of political satire in *The Innocents Abroad*. At the Paris review, he writes:

> "Napoleon III, the representative of the highest modern civilization, progress, and refinement." [013-046]

The sentence reads as praise but functions as indictment. Twain surrounds the emperor with "American shoddy-wers" surrounding "a king, the coronation of two Napoleons, the christening of the young prince" [014-002], reducing imperial ceremony to a fashion show for status-seeking tourists.

Twain is particularly sharp about the gap between Napoleon III's reputation and his actual character. He calls him "the brilliant adventurer, Napoleon III, the genius of Energy, Persistence, Enterprise" [013-050], piling up capitalist virtues that sit uneasily alongside the hereditary claims of monarchy. The word "adventurer" is carefully chosen — in nineteenth-century usage it carried connotations of opportunism and moral flexibility.

Yet Twain's treatment of Napoleon I is more complex. At Fontainebleau, amidst the relics of the First Empire, Twain's tone shifts toward something approaching historical reflection. The "souvenirs of Napoleon the First, and three dead kings" [016-005] evoke a melancholy grandeur that the living Napoleon III cannot match. For Twain, Napoleon I belongs to history and can be treated with a measure of respect; Napoleon III belongs to the present and deserves only satire.

That said, Twain never entirely surrenders his comic skepticism. The observation that "Napoleon's horses kicked the legs off" ancient statues in Milan [019-034] is a miniature masterpiece of Twain's method: take a fact, strip it of reverence, and present it with deadpan precision. The reader is left unsure whether to laugh at the vandalism or at the very idea of caring about it — which is exactly where Twain wants us.
