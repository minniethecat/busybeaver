# Leonardo da Vinci

Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519) was the archetypal Renaissance man — painter, sculptor, engineer, anatomist, and inventor. In *The Innocents Abroad*, he appears in two distinct and contradictory guises: as the creator of *The Last Supper*, one of the few Old Master paintings Twain admits to genuinely admiring, and as "Count Leonardo," a fictionalized cruel nobleman in an extended comic improvisation. This doubleness captures Twain's complex response to artistic authority: respect for evident genius, combined with irreverent impulse to mock the pretensions that accumulate around famous names.

## Overview

Twain encounters Leonardo's most famous mural — *The Last Supper*, painted on the refectory wall of Santa Maria delle Grazie in Milan — and finds himself, against his better judgment, impressed. This is a rare concession for Twain, who spent much of the Italian tour mocking the cult of Old Masters. But Leonardo also becomes material for an extended comic fantasy. In a passage of pure improvisation, Twain invents "Count Leonardo," a cruel and drunken nobleman who lords over a castle and nearly executes a troupe of traveling players before divine intervention saves them. The historical Leonardo and the fictional Count share nothing but a name and a setting, yet the juxtaposition is revealing: Twain cannot help but make stories, even from the most solemn of cultural monuments.

## The Last Supper

In Milan, Twain visits the refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie to see Leonardo's deteriorating masterpiece. He calls it "the first painting in the world — *The Last Supper*, by Leonardo da Vinci" (019-031) — a striking statement from a writer who had spent much of the preceding chapters mocking precisely this kind of superlative. Twain admits, "We are not infallible judges of painting, but it is very possible it may be the first" (019-031), a characteristic qualification that leaves room for doubt even as it registers genuine emotion.

He then makes an even more remarkable admission: "I was wanting to outdo this creation of da Vinci's. I suppose painters will go on copying it as long as any of the original is left visible" (019-035). The passage places Leonardo in a category apart. Twain had complained that artists everywhere copy Raphaels and Michelangelos (019-035), but with Leonardo, the copying seems to him not a symptom of artistic stagnation but a tribute to an achievement so singular that imitation is the only adequate response. He also notes the Italian spelling convention — "Leonardo da Vinci. (They spell it Vinci and pronounce it Vinchy)" (019-007) — a detail that places the reader in the experience of foreign travel, where even the names of world-famous artists must be re-learned in local pronunciation.

## Count Leonardo: A Comic Improvisation

In an extended comic passage, Twain invents a Gothic melodrama set in Leonardo's castle. "Count Leonardo" is a drunken, brutal nobleman who "lords it in the castle" and "wrestles with his lusts" (021-025). When a troupe of mountebanks arrives and performs for him, the Count orders one of them executed on a whim: "Priest, to thy work!" (021-041), commanding a friar to hear the condemned man's confession. At the last moment, a divine hand "struck the brutal Leonardo's weapon from his grasp" (021-044), saving the victim from execution.

This improvised story has no basis in Leonardo da Vinci's biography — it reads as pure Twainian invention, a parody of Gothic romance set in an Italian castle. The juxtaposition is deliberate: having just paid real tribute to *The Last Supper*, Twain deflates the reverence by turning "Leonardo" into a stock villain from pulp fiction. A minor character's exclamation — "A Leonardo! 'tare an ouns!'" (021-046) — completes the satire, reducing the great name to a comic oath. The Count Leonardo passage is Twain insisting, in his most characteristic mode, that he will not be cowed by fame — he will joke with it, mock it, and reshape it for his own comic purposes.

## Historical Context

Leonardo da Vinci painted *The Last Supper* between 1495 and 1498 on the refectory wall of the Dominican convent of Santa Maria delle Grazie in Milan. Unlike traditional fresco, Leonardo used an experimental technique of oil and tempera on a dry wall, which began deteriorating almost immediately. By 1867, when Twain visited, the painting was already substantially decayed — a ghost of its original self, barely legible through centuries of flaking, damp, and ill-advised restorations. The fragmentary state of *The Last Supper* was part of its mystique for 19th-century visitors, and Twain's acknowledgment that "as long as any of the original is left visible" (019-035) it would be copied reflects the painting's precarious material condition as much as its artistic stature.

## Twain's Assessment

Leonardo da Vinci is the rare Old Master whom Twain treats with something approaching genuine admiration. The *Last Supper* passage (019-031) contains none of the sarcasm Twain directs at Michelangelo's *Last Judgment* or Raphael's *Transfiguration*. The admission that he "was wanting to outdo this creation of da Vinci's" (019-035) is as close as Twain comes in the entire travelogue to confessing that a work of art moved him.

Yet the Count Leonardo improvisation (021-021-046) complicates the picture. Having granted Leonardo his respect, Twain immediately exercises his right to play — to take the name, strip it of its aura, and use it as material for low comedy. This is not dismissal; it is appropriation. Twain's Leonardo is a figure he can both honor and mock, and the tension between those responses is what makes the portrait alive.
