## Overview

Few figures populate the pages of *The Innocents Abroad* as persistently as beggars. From the Azores to Bethlehem, Twain and his fellow pilgrims are pursued by what he describes as entire populations whose "ct, education, and profession" is begging. Twain's treatment of beggars oscillates between comic exasperation and genuine social observation, forming one of the book's most consistent motifs.

Italy occupies a special place in Twain's taxonomy of mendicancy: "For every beggar in America, Italy can show a hundred" [025-010]. The observation is more than casual hyperbole — it reflects Twain's growing awareness that European poverty operates on a scale unfamiliar to his American readers.

## Italy: The Epicenter of Professional Begging

Naples provides Twain with his most vivid beggar encounters. He describes an urban landscape where "beggars, Princes and Bishops, jostle each other" [030-007], and he notes with grim humor the competitive nature of the trade:

> "A beggar in Naples who can show a foot which has neither been chopped off by a street car nor crushed by a building, is the one who gets the sympathy." [033-013]

The passage reflects Twain's growing cynicism — beggars, he implies, fabricate or exaggerate their injuries to maximize donations. He concludes with characteristic bite: "Ah, a beggar has to have exceedingly good judgment" [033-014].

In Rome, organized religious mendicancy takes center stage: "An abbot and a dozen beggarly friars is all we have" [021-030]. The phrase "beggarly friars" collapses the distance between sanctioned religious poverty and street-level begging — a typical Twain rhetorical move.

## The Holy Land: Beggars as a Feature of Pilgrimage

By the time Twain reaches Palestine, beggars have become not merely an annoyance but a defining feature of the Holy Land experience. At the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, beggars are described as permanent fixtures:

> "Amidst the usual assemblage of beggars, one sees on his left a few old men" [053-007]

The biblical landscape is overrun: "Bethlehem and its troops of beggars and relic-peddlers" [055-045] pursue pilgrims relentlessly:

> "Importunate swarms of beggars and peddlers who hang in strings to them" [055-046]

Twain's use of military language — "troops," "regiment," "swarms" — frames the beggars as an occupying force, transforming sacred sites into commercial battlegrounds.

## Beyond Comedy: The Social Observation

Beneath the humor, Twain occasionally drops into genuine sociological observation. In Damascus, he notes a contrast:

> "Drivers, guides, peddlers and beggars — but in Damascus they so hate a Christian, they will not even beg of him." [044-013]

The observation is striking: begging, in Twain's analysis, requires a baseline of civility that Damascus lacks. And of the Bedouins near the Sea of Galilee, he writes with almost anthropological precision: they are "all abject beggars by nature, instinct and education" [048-003], collapsing nature and nurture into a single damning assessment.

## The American Contrast

Twain's beggar commentary consistently invokes an implicit American standard. The "beggarly handful of fishing-smacks" [033-003] near Athens, the "trembling beggars" in contrast to "the lord of a mighty empire" [047-019] — these juxtapositions reflect Twain's conviction that American prosperity and self-reliance stand in opposition to Old World dependency. Whether this conviction is fair or merely chauvinistic, it is one of the book's organizing principles.
