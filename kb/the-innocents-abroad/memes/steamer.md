# Steamer

A steam-powered vessel — the dominant mode of long-distance travel in the 1860s, carrying Mark Twain and his fellow "pilgrims" across the Atlantic and around the Mediterranean during their five-month excursion.

## Overview

The steamer was the engine of nineteenth-century travel, and in *The Innocents Abroad*, steamers of various sizes frame the entire journey. The great excursion steamer *Quaker City* carried Twain and some sixty passengers from New York to Europe and the Holy Land, while smaller local steamers ferried them across lakes, along coastlines, and between Mediterranean ports. These vessels were both practical conveyance and social microcosm — floating hotels where Americans of different classes ate, prayed, danced, and grumbled together for months at sea.

Twain's narrative gives the steamer a double role: it is both the vehicle of the voyage and a stage for the human comedy he observes. The ship's cramped quarters, its formal dinners, its deck promenades, and its storm-tossed miseries are described with the same sharp eye he trains on cathedrals and ruins. The steamer, in Twain's telling, is as much a character in the book as any guide or fellow-passenger.

## In Twain's Narrative

The *Quaker City* dominates the early chapters, where Twain introduces the "pleasure excursion" and its participants with wry affection. From the moment they "steamed down to the foot of the harbor" [002-019], the ship becomes the pilgrims' floating home. The steamer reappears throughout the journey — as the "little monthly steamer" that brings news to the Azores [006-003], as the "small steamer bound for the venerable Moorish town" off Tangier [007-034], and as the "little French steamer" that carries them to Civita Vecchia [024-024]. On Lake Como, they "voyaged by steamer down the Lago di Lecco" [021-002]; on Lake Geneva, they "chartered a little steamer and went out" [030-014].

These smaller vessels, Twain notes, were a far cry from the comforts of the *Quaker City*: on the French steamer, there were "only half a dozen" passengers [025-019], and the contrast between the great excursion steamer's formal service — "knives and forks, soup-plates" [041-014] — and the rough practicality of local Mediterranean boats runs through the travelogue as a quiet theme.

## Cultural and Historical Context

By the 1860s, steam-powered vessels had revolutionized transatlantic travel. What had been a weeks-long sailing voyage under uncertain winds became a scheduled service of ten to twelve days. The great steamship lines — Cunard, Collins, Inman — competed for passengers, and the "pleasure excursion" that Twain joined was part of a new phenomenon: middle-class American tourism to Europe and the Holy Land.

The *Quaker City* itself was a side-wheel steamer, a type common in American waters but already being superseded by screw-propeller ships on the Atlantic. The smaller steamers that plied Mediterranean and European lake routes were often paddle-wheelers of modest size — "little steamers" by the standards of the ocean-going vessels. For many of the pilgrims, the experience of these local boats, with their mixed crowds of locals and travelers, was as novel as the ancient sites they visited.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain treats the steamer with characteristic ambivalence. He mocks the pretensions of "a steamship at that!" [004-034] and catalogs the discomforts of seasickness and cramped quarters with unsparing detail. Yet there is genuine affection in his descriptions of deck life — the promenades, the moonlight quarterdeck [003-018], the dances "on the upper deck, under the awnings" [004-024].

> "We steamed down to the foot of the harbor, and came to anchor." [002-019]

The steamer, for Twain, is the great leveler. On its decks, "youth and beauty" mingle with "old age and infirmity" [001-002]; the ship's routines — meals, prayers, mock trials — reduce everyone to the same human condition. His descriptions of the smaller steamers are sharper, more sardonic: the "little French steamer" with its indifferent service, the "small steamer" that lurches through rough Mediterranean seas. Twain clearly preferred the *Quaker City*'s familiar discomforts to the uncertainties of foreign boats.
