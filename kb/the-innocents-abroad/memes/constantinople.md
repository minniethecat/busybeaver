# Constantinople

Capital of the Ottoman Empire, straddling the Bosporus between Europe and Asia, visited by the Quaker City excursionists in Chapters 33 through 34 — a city whose exotic costumes, slandered dogs, magnificent mosques, and bewildering bazaars produced some of Twain's most vivid descriptive writing.

## Overview

Constantinople had been on the excursion program from the very beginning, listed alongside "Paris" and "Jerusalem" as one of the great destinations of the voyage (001-010). The party approached it from the Black Sea, and Twain's description of the arrival catches the full theatricality of the moment: the mosques and minarets rising "above the battlements of Constantinople" (022-003).

Twain's Constantinople is a city of sensory extremes. The costumes are "fantastic," the dogs are everywhere, the beggars are "monsters," and the bazaar merchants lie as a matter of course. Yet beneath the satire runs genuine admiration for the city's beauty — particularly the view from the Bosporus — and for the magnificent Hagia Sophia, "the chief lion of Constantinople" (033-015). The chapters balance exoticism with humor, documenting a world that seemed to the American travelers almost impossibly foreign.

The city was the easternmost point of the Mediterranean tour proper, and the departure point for the Black Sea excursion to Sebastopol and Odessa. It was also where the party left behind "a dozen passengers" (035-002) and picked up the warnings about brigands in Syria that would color the Holy Land journey.

## Arrival and First Impressions

The ship anchored before Constantinople, and Twain's first view of the city from the water was spectacular. "We shall remember Constantinople and the Bosporus — the colossal magnificence of the city" (062-007). The panorama encompassed Scutari and other suburbs: "This great city contains a million inhabitants, but so narrow are its streets, and so crowded together are its houses, that from the anchorage it looks as if you could touch it with your hand" (033-008).

The approach from the Bosporus was, for Twain, one of the high points of the entire voyage. The Golden Horn, the domes and minarets, the cypress trees of the cemeteries, and the ceaseless boat traffic combined into a prospect he ranked among the world's finest. The city appeared magnificent from a distance — a judgment that would, as with so many of Twain's destinations, be tested by close contact.

## Description and Notable Features

### Hagia Sophia

Hagia Sophia — "St. Sophia" in Twain's usage — was the undisputed crown jewel of the Constantinople visit. "St. Sophia is the chief lion of Constantinople. You must get a firman and hire a military guard to see it" (033-015). The bureaucratic hurdles only heightened the anticipation, and the mosque did not disappoint.

Twain described its interior in terms of immense scale: the vast dome floating above, the galleries, the columns pillaged from older temples across the empire. "It is situated in the centre of Constantinople. You go down a flight of stone steps" (033-022), entering a space that had been successively a Christian cathedral, a mosque, and then a mosque again. The layering of religious history — the Christian mosaics plastered over, the Arabic inscriptions, the mihrab oriented toward Mecca — embodied the city's own palimpsest identity.

The building was so dominant in Twain's imagination that he used it as a yardstick for other great structures. When describing later sites, he referred back to St. Sophia as a benchmark — it was one of the few buildings in the travelogue that he treated with undiluted respect.

### The Bosporus

The Bosporus itself was as much an attraction as anything on its shores. The strait separating Europe from Asia, lined with palaces and villas and gardens, presented what Twain considered one of the loveliest waterways in the world. The ceaseless traffic of caiques and ferries, the dolphins playing in the current, the changing light on the water — all contributed to an impression of vitality that offset the squalor of the streets behind the waterfront.

"We shall remember Constantinople and the Bosporus," Twain wrote in his concluding reflections — the pairing of city and strait suggesting that neither could be fully appreciated without the other (062-007).

### The Bazaar

The Grand Bazaar was where Twain's comic instincts found their fullest expression in Constantinople. The merchants were, by his account, congenital liars: "They do not buy and sell long in Constantinople till they lie and cheat like the rest of the world" (034-007). But the observation was more anthropological than moralistic — Twain recognized the bazaar as a cultural institution in which bargaining was a performance art.

The bazaar also connected Constantinople to the broader trade networks of the Levant. Twain noted that the city had long carried on "an extensive commerce with Constantinople and Syria" (017-022), and the goods on display — carpets, silks, spices, jewelry — testified to the empire's reach.

## Historical Significance

Constantinople represented, for Twain, the eastern terminus of European civilization — the point where familiar Christian traditions gave way to the Islamic world. He noted that "the expedition sent to Jerusalem to seize it got into trouble" with the Byzantine authorities (025-013), and the city's history as the capital of the Eastern Roman Empire formed the unspoken backdrop to his account.

The Ottoman present interested him more than the Byzantine past. The "Georgian girls" still sold "in Constantinople by their parents, but not publicly" (034-003) — a detail that shocked his American sensibilities. The city was connected by telegraph to the Syrian towns: "At Constantinople everybody fell to telegraphing" ahead to arrange dragomen and transport for the Holy Land journey (041-005). The modern technology coexisted uneasily with the ancient customs, a juxtaposition Twain found endlessly fascinating.

His discussion of the newspaper industry captured this contradiction perfectly. "The newspaper business had its birth in Constantinople about a year ago, and was a compound of the Greek and French languages" (034-017). "The newspaper business has its inconveniences in Constantinople. Two Greek papers and one French are published there" (034-019). The very idea of newspapers in a city of mosques and minarets struck Twain as mildly absurd, a sign of westernization that had not quite taken.

## Daily Life and Local Color

The dogs of Constantinople are perhaps the single most memorable feature of Twain's account. "Darting in and out of the alleys... are the famed dogs of Constantinople; drifting noiselessly about as if they had no more than a passing interest in the world" (033-011). The dogs were a civic institution — ownerless, ubiquitous, organized into neighborhood packs that defended their territories with ferocity.

Twain devoted an extended passage to correcting the "slandered" reputation of these animals: "The celebrated dogs of Constantinople have been misrepresented — slandered. It has been said they break up the Sabbath tranquility unmercifully" (034-008). In fact, he argued, they were models of civic order — and besides, "the fleas of Constantinople" preferred dogs "to a wider range on a healthier basis" (034-009). A "steam-engine" was noisy compared to "a Constantinople dog" (034-012).

The beggars of Constantinople rivaled those of Naples in Twain's grotesque catalog. "Monsters, both, go straight to Constantinople. A beggar in Naples who can stand on his head will make a living, but in Constantinople they go a step further" (033-013). He described "a blue-faced man, who had no arms" and other spectacles with a mixture of horror and fascination (033-014).

The costumes of the city enchanted him: "Fantastic fashions" filled the streets, the "endless white rag of Constantinople wrapped round and round their heads" (045-004), turbans and fezzes and flowing robes that made every street scene a pageant. Yet this was also a city where "a sailor had jumped overboard at Constantinople one night, and was seen no more" (060-015) — the exotic surface concealed darker currents.

## Twain's Verdict

Constantinople received one of Twain's most unequivocal endorsements, placed at the climax of his retrospective catalog of the voyage's highlights. "We shall remember Constantinople and the Bosporus — the colossal magnificence of the city" stands as the opening of a litany that includes "bauble-like southern Italy" and "sacred Jerusalem" and "Damascus, the 'Pearl of the East'" (062-007). Constantinople earned its place at the head of this list.

The city was strange, dirty, and deceptive — but it was also beautiful, vital, and utterly unlike anything the Americans had seen before. Twain's appreciation of Constantinople was anchored in its visual magnificence as seen from the water, the splendor of St. Sophia, and the sheer exoticism of its street life. If Paris was sophisticated and Rome was overwhelming, Constantinople was genuinely alien — and Twain cherished it for that very reason.

> "We shall remember Constantinople and the Bosporus — the colossal magnificence of the city." (062-007)
