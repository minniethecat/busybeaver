# Mosque

The Islamic house of worship — termed "mosques" by Christian travelers — that Mark Twain encountered from Tangier to Cairo, whose soaring minarets, glittering domes, and ritual exclusions mark the boundaries between the Western tourist and the Muslim world in *The Innocents Abroad*.

## Overview

Mosques appear throughout Twain's travelogue as both architectural marvels and cultural barriers. From the "fine Moorish mosque, with tall tower" in Tangier (009-002) to the "great mosque of Mehemet Ali" in Cairo (058-033), these buildings punctuate the Quaker City itinerary at every eastern port. They define skylines — the "great globes of the mosques, and the countless minarets" rising above Constantinople (033-008) — and they mark the limits of the Christian tourist's access, since "so dire a profanation is it for a Christian dog to set foot upon the sacred threshold of a Moorish mosque, that no amount of purification can ever make it fit for the faithful to pray in again" (009-005).

Twain's treatment of mosques alternates between genuine architectural admiration and satirical distance. He is awed by Hagia Sophia, fascinated by the Umayyad Mosque in Damascus, and charmed by the "little mosque" in the Holy Land. But he also treats Muslim worship with comic condescension, describing Turkish worshippers performing "gymnastics" (033-017) and noting the absurdity of asses bearing stones to build sacred spaces (009-004). The mosque, in Twain's hands, becomes a lens through which the American traveler's uneasy encounter with Islam is refracted — part genuine wonder, part cultural incomprehension, and part comic deflation.

## Role in the Travelogue

### Tangier: The Unseen Interior

The party's first encounter with a mosque comes in Tangier, Morocco, in Chapter 9. Twain describes "a fine Moorish mosque, with tall tower, rich with clusters of marble columns" (009-002), but neither he nor any member of the party can enter. The prohibition is absolute: no Christian may set foot inside a Moorish mosque, on pain of rendering it permanently defiled. This exclusion sets the tone for the entire eastern journey — the mosque as a space that is visible, magnificent, and off-limits.

The Tangier mosque also provides Twain with material for his characteristic deflationary humor. The clock in the tower "got out of order" and "the Moors of Tangier have endured their clock without protest from that day to this" (009-003), the implication being that the Moors lack the ingenuity or initiative to repair it. And when a new mosque is built, "asses bear the stones and the mortar, and almost all the material, up the steep hill" (009-004) — the comic image of donkeys hauling sacred building materials up a precipice undercuts the solemnity of the enterprise. Blucher, one of Twain's fellow travelers, attempts to ride his horse into a mosque and is stopped — a narrow escape from "dire profanation" (009-005).

### Constantinople: Hagia Sophia and the Great Mosques

Constantinople, reached in Chapter 33, offers Twain his most sustained encounter with mosque architecture. The city's skyline is dominated by "the great globes of the mosques, and the countless minarets that everywhere pointed out of the mass of building" (033-008). Hagia Sophia — "St. Sophia" — is "the chief lion of Constantinople" and the one mosque whose interior Twain describes in detail, having obtained a firman and military guard (033-015).

Hagia Sophia's history as a Christian cathedral "turned into a mosque, without much alteration" (033-016) fascinates Twain. The building embodies the layered religious history of the East: Byzantine mosaics plastered over, Arabic inscriptions added, the mihrab oriented toward Mecca where the Christian altar once stood. Inside, Twain watches "ragged Turks" bowing and kissing the earth, "muttering prayers the while, and keeping up their gymnastics till they ought to have been tired, if they were not" (033-017). The word "gymnastics" reduces Muslim ritual prostration to calisthenics, encapsulating Twain's blend of ethnographic curiosity and irreverent distance.

### Damascus: The Great Mosque and Its Precincts

The Umayyad Mosque — the Great Mosque of Damascus — appears in Chapters 54 and is the most elaborately described mosque after Hagia Sophia. "The inside of the great mosque is very showy with variegated marble walls" (054-016), Twain writes, noting the ornamental richness that distinguishes it from the plainer mosques of North Africa. "Just outside the mosque is a miniature temple" (054-017), a subsidiary structure whose purpose Twain does not fully explain but whose presence adds to the architectural density of the sacred precinct.

This mosque also carries the weight of recent violence. Twain's visit to Damascus occurs only six years after the massacre of 1861, in which thousands of Christians were killed, many near the mosque. The juxtaposition of architectural beauty and sectarian bloodshed — left largely implicit in Twain's text — shadows the Damascus account and lends the mosque a darker significance than its "showy" interior might suggest.

### The Holy Land: A Modest Shrine

In the Holy Land, Twain encounters a "little mosque" that "stands upon the spot which tradition assigns as the place where the disciples rested" (051-019). The diminutive descriptor — "little mosque" — is characteristic. Unlike the grand imperial mosques of Constantinople or Damascus, this is a modest structure, notable primarily for its location rather than its architecture. It testifies to the Muslim presence even in the Christian holy places, a reminder that the land Twain's pilgrims have come to claim as theirs is shared — and contested — territory.

### Cairo: Mehemet Ali's Monument

The final major mosque in the travelogue is "the great mosque of Mehemet Ali" in Cairo (058-033). Twain declines to describe it — "I shall not describe the great mosque of Mehemet Ali, whose entire walls are built of alabaster" (058-033) — a rhetorical gesture that both acknowledges the building's magnificence and withholds it, as if the accumulated sights of the voyage have exhausted his descriptive capacity. The alabaster walls, mentioned in the very act of refusing description, linger in the reader's imagination precisely because Twain declines to elaborate.

### Spain: The Memory of Mosques

Even in Christian Spain, the memory of mosques persists. Twain notes that "the cities of Spain, are great mosques and cathedrals" (040-013), a compressed observation that collapses centuries of religious transformation into a single line. The mosques of Córdoba and Granada — converted into cathedrals after the Reconquista — testify to the Islamic civilization that once dominated the Iberian Peninsula and whose architectural fingerprints remain visible everywhere.

## Historical Context

The mosques Twain encountered in 1867 belonged to the late Ottoman period, when Islamic religious architecture had reached its mature form after more than a millennium of development. The great imperial mosques of Constantinople — Süleymaniye, Sultan Ahmed, and Hagia Sophia — were the work of the 16th and 17th centuries, built by Ottoman sultans to rival and surpass the Christian churches they had conquered.

The legal status of non-Muslims in mosque precincts varied across the Ottoman and Moorish worlds. In Morocco, the prohibition was absolute — no Christian could enter any mosque. In the Ottoman Empire, a firman (imperial permit) could be obtained, as Twain did for Hagia Sophia, but the process was cumbersome and the access partial. "You must get a firman and hire a military guard to see it" (033-015), Twain notes — the bureaucratic hurdles serving both to control foreign visitors and to maintain the sanctity of the space.

The mosque in the 19th-century Islamic world was more than a place of prayer. It was a center of education (madrasas attached to mosques), a court of law (qadis dispensing justice in mosque precincts), a hospice for the poor, and a symbol of communal identity. Twain's reduction of the mosque to architecture and ritual — dome, minaret, prostration — reflects the limitations of the tourist's gaze, which registers surfaces and behaviors but rarely penetrates to the institutional and social functions beneath.

For Twain's American readers, the mosque was an exotic object — part of the Oriental furniture of the Levant, familiar from Romantic poetry and travel literature but rarely encountered at first hand. Twain's descriptions both satisfy and subvert these expectations, offering the requisite exoticism while puncturing it with comic irreverence.

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's attitude toward mosques is characterized by the same dual consciousness he brings to all religious subjects in the travelogue. He is genuinely moved by architectural magnificence — Hagia Sophia receives his unqualified admiration, and the silhouette of Constantinople's mosques from the Bosporus is one of the voyage's lasting images. The great mosque of Damascus, "very showy with variegated marble walls" (054-016), earns descriptive attention even as the word "showy" hints at a faintly disapproved ostentation.

But his treatment of Muslim worship inside mosques is consistently condescending. The prostration of worshippers is "gymnastics" (033-017); the ablutions at the fountain before prayer are "a thing not relished by the Moorish bystanders" when observed by Christian tourists (009-013); and the muezzin's call summons "faithful vagabonds" (038-011). The mosque, for Twain, is a beautiful building filled with strange rituals performed by people he cannot quite take seriously — except when he can.

The prohibition against Christian entry into mosques provokes Twain's characteristic mix of indignation and amusement. "Ye know, also, that when mosques are builded, asses bear the stones and the mortar" (009-004) — the biblical cadence parodies sacred language while reducing mosque construction to donkey labor. The equation of Christian exclusion with Moorish superstition flatters his Protestant readers while acknowledging, however grudgingly, the sincerity of Muslim religious conviction.

Yet Twain's approach to mosques is not uniformly dismissive. The "little mosque" in the Holy Land (051-019) is treated without satire — a simple statement of fact that accords the structure a dignity it does not need to earn. And his refusal to describe Mehemet Ali's alabaster mosque, while characteristically perverse, implies a recognition that some sights exceed description — a rare concession from a writer who rarely admits to being at a loss for words.

> "He goes to his mosque about noon on his Sabbath, as he was in Mecca, and there he removes his shoes at the door, performs his ablutions, makes his salaams, pressing his forehead to the pavement time and again, says his prayers, and goes back to his work." (009-013)
