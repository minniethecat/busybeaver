# Merchant

A person engaged in trade, buying and selling goods — a figure Mark Twain encountered in every port of his 1867 voyage and whose diverse manifestations across cultures provided rich material for his ethnographic and satirical observations.

## Overview

Merchants populate the pages of *The Innocents Abroad* in remarkable variety: Moorish traders in Tangier, goose-merchants in Paris, money-changers in Constantinople, and the bazaar merchants of Damascus who, to Twain's astonishment, "did not hold us up" for baksheesh [044-013]. Each encounter with commerce reveals something about the culture Twain is visiting — its economic organization, its social customs, its attitude toward foreigners, and its place in the larger world of 19th-century trade.

For Twain, the merchant is also a literary device. The gap between American commercial straightforwardness and the elaborate bargaining rituals of the Middle East, the contrast between the "merchant fleets" of Venice's glorious past and its tourist-driven present, and the comic figure of the overly aggressive vendor — all serve his larger themes of cultural encounter and American innocence abroad.

## In Twain's Narrative

Merchants first appear in Tangier, where Twain describes "sashed, and trousered Moorish merchants from Fez" amid the city's exotic street life [007-024]. The description is characteristic: Twain catalogs the visual details of dress and behavior while maintaining his American observer's detachment. In Gibraltar, he notes the "Muhammadan merchant" going about his business in a "civilized land" [008-014].

The theme deepens in Italy and the Middle East. In Pompeii, Twain walks "down the long street of the merchants" and imagines the commercial life of the ancient city [031-009]. In Venice, he reflects on the city's former "dominion of the seas" when its "merchant fleets whitened the remotest oceans" [022-002] — a historical perspective that adds depth to his travelogue. In Paris, the comic figure of the goose-merchant provides one of the book's memorable comic vignettes [033-012].

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, the Mediterranean merchant occupied a rapidly changing economic role. The old trading networks that had connected Europe, North Africa, and the Levant for centuries were being reshaped by steam navigation, European colonialism, and the expansion of industrial capitalism. Twain's observations capture both the surviving traditions of the bazaar and the encroaching standardization of global commerce. The "Ishmaelitish merchants" of the Bible [047-016] and the modern shopkeepers of Paris [026-017] represent poles of a commercial continuum that Twain navigates with characteristic wit.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's treatment of merchants reveals both his American commercial sensibility — he respects honest dealing and despises sharp practice — and his ethnographer's eye for the revealing detail. He is particularly attentive to the psychology of bargaining, the rituals of buying and selling, and the power dynamics between local merchants and foreign tourists.

> "The merchants in the bazaars did not hold us up..." [044-013]

This simple observation about Damascus carries a world of implication: elsewhere, tourists are routinely exploited; here, remarkably, they are not. The merchant, in Twain's hands, becomes a barometer of a culture's commercial morality.
