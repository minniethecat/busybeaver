# Pilgrimage

The sacred journey to a holy site for religious devotion — a practice that Mark Twain simultaneously participates in, observes, and satirizes throughout his 1867 voyage to Europe and the Holy Land aboard the *Quaker City*.

## Overview

Pilgrimage — the journey undertaken for religious purposes to a sacred destination — is the central organizing metaphor of *The Innocents Abroad*. The sixty-one passengers of the *Quaker City* call themselves "pilgrims" from the opening chapters, and Twain uses this designation to frame the entire voyage as a modern pilgrimage: part genuine religious devotion, part commercial tourism, and part comic misadventure. The tension between these registers — sacred and secular, reverent and irreverent — generates much of the book's humor and its enduring insight into the American encounter with the Old World.

Twain's pilgrims are not medieval penitents walking barefoot to Canterbury. They are Protestant Americans from New England, the South, and the Mississippi Valley [004-002], traveling by steamship and railway, armed with guidebooks and Baedekers, measuring sacred sites against American standards of comfort. Yet the language of pilgrimage clings to them persistently. Twain refers to the voyage as "this wearisome pilgrimage" [057-002], declares "the long pilgrimage was ended" [056-007], and concludes with the valedictory "So ends the pilgrimage" [056-009]. The frame is deliberate: by treating a commercial excursion as a pilgrimage, Twain exposes the gap between the ideal of sacred travel and the reality of Gilded Age tourism.

The concept of pilgrimage extends beyond the Christian Holy Land. Twain also observes and reports on the Islamic pilgrimage to Mecca — the Hajj — through the Moors of Tangier, noting the social status conferred by the title Hadji [009-015] and the Emperor's absurd decree restricting the pilgrimage to "bloated aristocrats who were worth a hundred dollars in specie" [009-016]. This comparative perspective deepens Twain's treatment: pilgrimage, whether Christian or Muslim, is shown to be entangled with economics, social climbing, and bureaucratic absurdity.

## In Twain's Narrative

The pilgrimage frame is established early, with the passengers packing "substantial clothing to use in rough pilgrimizing in the Holy Land" [001-008]. By Chapter 4, the pilgrims are "becoming domesticated" [004-001] aboard ship, their identities solidifying. Twain, ever the detached observer, notes that "I doubt if there is a single pilgrim of all that host but can show a hundred minor bruises received on the trip" [004-006], establishing the comic register that will define the pilgrimage: these are travelers who suffer for their sanctity, but their suffering is comically minor.

Even before reaching the Holy Land, pilgrimage language saturates the journey. Twain describes "a dismal fifty-hour pilgrimage between New York and St. Louis" [012-007], secularizing the term for comic effect. He notes that "many stricken lovers make pilgrimages to this shrine" at the Alhambra-like court in the Azores [015-005]. And at Fontainebleau, he declares: "It was worth a pilgrimage to see" [016-003]. The word, by the time the ship reaches the Mediterranean, has become Twain's flexible instrument: it can be reverent, ironic, hyperbolic, or deflating, depending on context.

The Holy Land chapters intensify the pilgrimage theme. Twain describes "one portion of this Holy Land pilgrimage" with reference to the transportation arrangements [041-005], while the pilgrims carry "Tent Life in the Holy Land" and "The Land and the Book" as their "outside aids in this pilgrimage" [049-002]. The guidebooks become a kind of secular scripture, and the pilgrimage becomes a checklist of sacred sites to be verified against their descriptions.

At Damascus, Twain observes the green turban of the Hadji — "the honored sign that my lord has made the pilgrimage to Mecca" [044-013] — and reflects on the comparative prestige of Muslim pilgrimage. The Moorish pilgrims he encountered in Tangier, who "never wash, either on land or sea" during their months-long journey [009-015], present a mirror image of the Quaker City pilgrims: equally devout, equally absurd, equally human. The Emperor's decree that "no man should make the pilgrimage save bloated aristocrats who were worth a hundred dollars in specie" [009-016] is promptly circumvented by a Jewish money-changer who lends pilgrims the required sum just long enough to qualify — a vignette that Twain deploys to illustrate the universal gap between pilgrimage ideals and economic realities.

The Jordan River episode crystallizes the pilgrimage motif. The travelers had "promised themselves all along that they would cross the Jordan where the Israelites crossed it when they entered Canaan from their long pilgrimage in the desert" [055-020]. This conflation of the biblical Israelites' pilgrimage with their own tourist excursion is both comic and genuinely moving. The modern pilgrims wade into the Jordan at dawn [055-018], participating in a tradition that stretches back millennia — while also taking photographs and chipping off souvenir fragments.

The pilgrims' approach to pilgrimage as checklist completion reaches its apogee in Chapter 55: "Perfectly secure now, against failing to accomplish any detail of the pilgrimage, they felt like drawing in advance upon the holiday soon to be placed to their credit" [055-003]. The language of credit and debit — pilgrimage as a transaction in which devotion is deposited and satisfaction withdrawn — captures Twain's satirical vision of American sacred tourism.

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, pilgrimage was undergoing a profound transformation. The medieval model — a penitential journey undertaken at great personal risk and expense — was giving way to modern religious tourism, facilitated by steamships, railways, and the infrastructure of the Grand Tour. Thomas Cook had begun organizing package tours to the Holy Land in the 1860s, and the *Quaker City* excursion, though organized by Henry Ward Beecher's Plymouth Church, was part of this broader trend toward democratized sacred travel.

For Twain's American Protestant readers, pilgrimage carried complex connotations. On one hand, pilgrimage was a Catholic practice — associated with relics, indulgences, and what Protestants regarded as superstitious ritualism. On the other, travel to the Holy Land was a deeply Protestant aspiration, a chance to walk where Jesus walked and to verify the Bible against the landscape. Twain navigates this tension throughout the book, mocking Catholic "fantastic pilgrimizing" [006-001] while also expressing genuine emotion at sites like the Sea of Galilee. The Protestant pilgrims aboard the *Quaker City* are caught between their desire for authentic religious experience and their suspicion of the institutional apparatus — monks, shrines, and relics — that has grown up around pilgrimage over centuries.

The Islamic pilgrimage to Mecca — the Hajj — provides an important counterpoint. Twain's description of the Moorish Hadjis in Tangier [009-015, 009-016] is one of the book's most sustained engagements with non-Christian religious practice. His tone is characteristically double: he mocks the pilgrims' filth and penury — they "never wash" during a journey of "five to seven months" [009-015] — while also acknowledging the genuine social prestige that the title Hadji confers. The Emperor's decree restricting pilgrimage to the wealthy [009-016] becomes an occasion for Twain to satirize both Islamic bureaucracy and, by implication, the class dimensions of all sacred travel. The parallels between the Hadji's green turban in Damascus [044-013] and the Quaker City pilgrims' own badges of status — their guidebooks, their American citizenship, their Protestant confidence — are left for the reader to draw.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's treatment of pilgrimage is fundamentally satirical but not nihilistic. He never denies that the journey to the Holy Land is meaningful; he simply insists on recording what it actually feels like — the heat, the dust, the bad horses, the grasping guides, the disappointments and occasional transcendences. His honesty about the gap between expectation and reality is what makes the book, in its own way, a genuinely religious document: it records a pilgrimage undertaken in good faith, by people who are often ridiculous but also capable of genuine awe.

His most characteristic move is to use pilgrimage language simultaneously as praise and critique. When he writes that "At this rate it is a glorious privilege to be a pilgrim to the Holy Land" [041-016], the "at this rate" — camping in miserable conditions, riding broken-down horses — transforms the statement from piety into irony. The privilege is real, but it is purchased at a cost the pilgrims did not anticipate.

The closing chapters deploy pilgrimage language in a register of exhausted relief. Twain writes:

> "The long pilgrimage was ended, and somehow we seemed to feel glad of it." [056-007]

The "somehow" carries the weight of the entire journey — the accumulated exhaustion, disappointment, and also the genuine satisfaction of completion. Two paragraphs later, he makes the closure definitive:

> "So ends the pilgrimage." [056-009]

The two sentences, appearing within paragraphs of each other at the close of Chapter 56, form a diptych of closure. The first admits relief; the second performs a ritual conclusion. Neither sentence offers the pious reflection that a conventional pilgrimage narrative would require.

Back aboard the *Quaker City*, Twain expands on the relief: "No amount of money could have purchased for us, in a strange vessel and among unfamiliar faces, the perfect satisfaction and the sense of being at home again which we experienced when we stepped on board the 'Quaker City,' — our own ship — after this wearisome pilgrimage" [057-002]. Home, not Jerusalem, is the true destination of this pilgrimage. The sacred journey has been completed not in triumph but in exhaustion, and the pilgrims' relief at returning to their floating America is the book's most honest religious statement.

In the final chapters, Twain's valedictory for the *New York Herald* — framed as "a kind word for the Hadjis" because "Hadjis are people who have made the pilgrimage" [061-001] — completes the circle. Twain, the skeptical observer, has become a Hadji himself. The pilgrimage has claimed him despite his resistance, and his final act is to write "fulsomely complimentary" words [061-001] for his fellow pilgrims — a gesture of solidarity that is partly ironic, partly sincere, and entirely characteristic of the book's double vision.

Reflecting from the distance of the writing desk, Twain casts the entire experience in retrospective terms: "it has been a long time since this notable pilgrimage was ended; and as I sit here..." [062-001]. The pilgrimage has become a story — the very book the reader holds — and the act of narration is revealed as the final stage of the journey.
