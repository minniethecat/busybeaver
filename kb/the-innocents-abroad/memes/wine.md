# Wine

An alcoholic beverage made from fermented grapes, central to European dining, religious practice, and social life — a recurring motif in Mark Twain's observations of continental culture during his 1867 voyage.

## Overview

Wine flows through the pages of *The Innocents Abroad* as both a literal refreshment and a marker of cultural difference between Protestant America and Catholic Europe. Twain's narrative is punctuated by wine — at meals, in churches, on excursions, in the leisurely rhythms of European cafe life. For the American traveler accustomed to a culture of temperance, the ubiquity of wine in Mediterranean society was a source of amusement, occasional indulgence, and sharp satirical commentary.

The beverage appears in multiple contexts throughout the travelogue: as a commercial product with its own economic history (Madeira, Asti, Burgundy), as an element of Catholic ritual and monastic life, as a marker of sophistication or its absence among Twain's fellow travelers, and as a simple pleasure of travel. Twain's treatment of wine reveals his own conflicted relationship with European culture — drawn to its pleasures but ever the skeptical American observer.

## In Twain's Narrative

Wine accompanies Twain from the earliest pages of the book. In the Azores, he learns that a blight "killed all the vines fifteen years ago," destroying the island's once-famous wine industry [006-003]. At dinner aboard the Quaker City and in port restaurants, his companions — particularly the pretentious "Interrogation Point" — make elaborate shows of ordering wine, which Twain skewers mercilessly: "'I never dine without wine, sir' (which was a pitiful falsehood)" [011-004][011-005].

In France, Twain describes "rich Burgundian wines" consumed at a Lyonnais restaurant [012-011] and notes the cafe culture where Parisians sit "sipping wine and coffee" on the sidewalks [012-018]. In Italy, he mentions the famous "Asti" wines [017-026] and the gardens of Genoa where visitors take "wine and ices" [019-011]. Even amid the ruins of Pompeii, he reflects on the wine and oil that once filled the shattered amphorae [031-009].

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, wine was deeply woven into the fabric of European life in ways unfamiliar to most Americans. In Catholic countries, wine was both a daily beverage — often safer than water — and a sacramental element of the Mass. The wine trade was a major economic force, with regions like Bordeaux, Burgundy, and Asti producing wines that commanded international markets. The Madeira wine industry had recently been devastated by the phylloxera epidemic, a fact Twain records in his Azores observations.

For American Protestants, wine occupied an ambiguous position. The temperance movement was gaining strength in the United States, and many of Twain's readers would have viewed habitual wine-drinking as a moral failing. Twain navigates this tension carefully — he reports on European wine culture with curiosity and occasional pleasure, but always maintains a degree of ironic distance that reassures his American audience.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's treatment of wine reveals the interplay between his genuine enjoyment of European pleasures and his role as cultural satirist. He is willing to participate — "we took wine and ices in a fine garden" [019-011] and "tested the wines of the country" [012-028] — but he refuses to adopt the pretensions he sees around him. His most pointed mockery is reserved for Americans who affect European wine connoisseurship:

> "'Eleven bottles of wine, at 1,200 reis, 13,200 reis!'" — Twain reproduces the bill from the Azores with comic emphasis on the wine expenditure [005-017]

The bill's inclusion is a masterstroke of satirical journalism: the numbers speak for themselves, and the wine expense looms large amid the modest costs of the meal.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- **Azores wine bill**: The famously itemized dinner receipt showing eleven bottles of wine at 13,200 reis [005-017][005-022]
- **Madeira's ruined vineyards**: The disease that killed the island's wine industry [006-003]
- **French Burgundy**: Dining in style at a Lyons restaurant [012-011]
- **Parisian cafe culture**: Sidewalk wine-drinking as a spectacle of leisure [012-018]
- **Italian wine-tasting**: "Testing the wines of the country" with fellow travelers [012-028]
- **Asti wines**: Mentioned in connection with the monks of northern Italy [017-026]
