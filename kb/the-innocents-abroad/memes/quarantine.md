# Quarantine

The maritime quarantine protocols enforced at Mediterranean ports in the 1860s — ostensibly public-health measures that the *Quaker City* excursionists encounter as arbitrary, theatrical, and maddeningly inconvenient barriers to their sightseeing ambitions.

## Overview

In the mid-19th century, Mediterranean port cities routinely imposed quarantine — isolation periods for arriving vessels — as a holdover from centuries of plague prevention. Ships suspected of carrying disease were forbidden from landing passengers or cargo for days or weeks. For the *Quaker City* pilgrims, quarantine represented neither a genuine health safeguard nor a rational administrative procedure, but an absurd bureaucratic ritual that existed to be circumvented. Twain treats quarantine as one of the great comic obstacles of the voyage: a system so manifestly senseless that violating it becomes not a crime but an imperative of common sense.

The quarantine episodes form a recurring motif across the second half of the travelogue. They appear at four distinct ports — Leghorn, Naples, Athens, and Malaga/Cadiz — each encounter more brazen than the last, culminating in Twain's frank declaration that "we abused the man who invented quarantine" (060-010). The concept illuminates a broader theme in *The Innocents Abroad*: the collision between American impatience and Old World bureaucracy.

## In Twain's Narrative

The first hint of trouble comes at Leghorn, where the chapter synopsis records the ship as "an object of grave suspicion" and notes "Threats of quarantine" (024-001). The passengers learn "that we shall probably be quarantined at Naples" (024-024), and Twain and a few companions preemptively abandon ship, taking a French steamer to Civita and then rail to Rome and Naples, because "they do not quarantine the cars, no matter where they got their passengers from" (024-024). The absurdity is already apparent: the very same travelers are dangerous on a ship but harmless on a train.

When the *Quaker City* arrives at Naples, the quarantine is enforced with full rigor. The ship lies "in the harbor of Naples — quarantined" for well over a week (029-002). Twain, having arrived by rail, is free to roam while his shipmates are imprisoned. He describes the scene with characteristic sadistic humor:

> "Of course no one is allowed to go on board the ship, or come ashore from her. She is a prison, now. The passengers probably spend the long, blazing days looking out from under the awnings at Vesuvius and the beautiful city — and in swearing. Think of ten days of this sort of pastime!" (029-002)

Twain and his rail-traveling companions torment the quarantined passengers by rowing out to the ship and describing "how splendid the city is; and how much better the hotel fare is here than anywhere else in Europe; and how cool it is; and what frozen continents of ice cream there are" (029-002). He adds, with deadpan understatement: "This tranquilizes them" (029-002).

The most dramatic quarantine episode occurs at Athens. The commandant of the Piraeus delivers an ultimatum: the ship must either depart immediately or anchor outside the harbor "and remain imprisoned in our ship, under rigid quarantine, for eleven days" (032-016). Twice as long as the Naples detention. The news is devastating: "To lie a whole day in sight of the Acropolis, and yet be obliged to go away without visiting Athens! Disappointment was hardly a strong enough word to describe the circumstances" (032-016).

Twain's response is to break the law. At eleven o'clock at night, he and three companions steal ashore in a small boat (032-019). As they pick their way over the rocky hillsides, Twain dwells on the grim legal consequences:

> "My immediate comrade and I talked in an undertone about quarantine laws and their penalties, but we found nothing cheering in the subject. I was posted. Only a few days before, I was talking with our captain, and he mentioned the case of a man who swam ashore from a quarantined ship somewhere, and got imprisoned six months for it; and when he was in Genoa a few years ago, a captain of a quarantined ship went in his boat to a departing ship, which was already outside of the harbor, and put a letter on board to be taken to his family, and the authorities imprisoned him three months for it, and then conducted him and his ship fairly to sea, and warned him never to show himself in that port again while he lived." (032-019)

Yet this litany of punishments, rather than deterring the adventurers, merely gives "a sort of dismal interest to our quarantine-breaking expedition" (032-019). The travelers proceed to wander thirteen miles through the Greek countryside, steal a gallon of grapes from a vineyard in full moonlight, and eventually dodge a police boat "on the lookout for any quarantine-breakers that might chance to be abroad" (032-040) before slipping back aboard ship at dawn.

## Cultural and Historical Context

Quarantine — from the Italian *quaranta giorni*, meaning forty days — originated in 14th-century Venice as a plague-control measure requiring ships to wait offshore before landing. By the 1860s, when Twain sailed the Mediterranean, the system was an entrenched feature of port administration, though its scientific rationale had eroded. The quarantine laws Twain confronted were relics of an older epidemiological worldview, enforced with bureaucratic rigidity even when no actual disease was present.

For American travelers of the post-Civil War era, quarantine represented everything maddening about the Old World: arbitrary authority, sluggish officialdom, and elaborate protocols that served no discernible practical purpose. Twain's generation, raised on frontier individualism and pragmatic problem-solving, regarded such regulations not as prudent caution but as comic obstructions — something to be outwitted rather than respected.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain never treats quarantine as a legitimate public-health measure. From his first mention of it at Leghorn to the final episode off the Spanish coast, quarantine is presented as bureaucratic theater — an exercise in official vanity that bears no relationship to genuine health concerns. His attitude is not political outrage but comic contempt: quarantine is one more absurdity in a world full of them, and the correct response is to laugh at it and then find a way around it.

The democratic mass-meeting becomes the pilgrims' instrument for processing their quarantine frustrations. In Chapter 60, Twain reports that "we abused the man who invented quarantine, we held half a dozen mass-meetings and crammed them full of interrupted speeches, motions that fell stillborn, amendments that came to nought and resolutions that died from sheer exhaustion in trying to get before the house" (060-010). The self-parody is unmistakable: the Americans respond to bureaucratic obstruction by generating yet more bureaucracy — a legislative farce that produces nothing but catharsis.

Notably, Twain never questions whether quarantine might sometimes be necessary. His framing is consistently that of the inconvenienced tourist for whom the loss of a day's sightseeing is a genuine tragedy. This very narrowness of perspective is part of the book's comic method: the reader is invited to share Twain's indignant bafflement that anyone would prioritize public health over the immediate gratification of American travelers.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- **Leghorn (Chapter 24)**: The ship falls under "grave suspicion"; passengers preemptively abandon ship to reach Naples by French steamer and rail, exploiting the loophole that trains are exempt from quarantine (024-001, 024-024).
- **Naples (Chapter 29)**: The *Quaker City* is detained for over a week. Twain's group, having arrived by rail, tortures the imprisoned passengers with vivid descriptions of the pleasures they are missing ashore (029-001, 029-002).
- **Piraeus/Athens (Chapter 32)**: The pilgrims face an 11-day quarantine. Twain and three others sneak ashore at midnight, dodge police boats, wander through the Greek countryside stealing grapes, and return at dawn — a full-scale quarantine blockade run (032-016, 032-019, 032-035, 032-040).
- **Malaga (Chapter 59)**: Anchored off the cathedral city, "they would not let us land. Quarantine." Four run the blockade and spend seven delightful days in Seville, Cordova, and Cadiz (059-009).
- **Off Lisbon (Chapter 60)**: The passengers vote in mass-meeting to skip Lisbon entirely because "we must surely be quarantined there" (060-003). The final quarantine episode ends in communal abuse of "the man who invented quarantine" and a series of comically futile mass-meetings (060-010).
