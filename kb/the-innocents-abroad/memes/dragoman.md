# Dragoman

The dragoman was the interpreter, guide, and logistical manager hired by Western travelers to navigate the Ottoman Empire — a figure who, in Twain's Holy Land journey, becomes both indispensable facilitator and object of persistent satire, blurring the line between service and exploitation.

## Overview

In the 19th-century Levant, the dragoman (from the Arabic *tarjuman*, meaning interpreter) functioned as the essential intermediary between European and American travelers and the complex social, linguistic, and commercial world of the Ottoman Empire. A dragoman arranged transportation, negotiated with local authorities, managed servants and pack animals, selected campsites, prepared meals, and served as cultural translator. For the *Quaker City* pilgrims undertaking the "long trip" through Syria and Palestine, the dragoman was not merely a hired guide but the architect of their entire overland experience.

Twain's dragoman — a man named Abraham, whom the pilgrims nickname "Ferguson" — embodies both the practical necessity and the moral ambiguity of this role. Part concierge, part con man, part commissar of the caravan, Abraham represents the commercial mediation of a journey the pilgrims had imagined as a purely spiritual undertaking. Through him, Twain explores the uncomfortable reality that even the holiest pilgrimage runs on money, bargaining, and the self-interest of those who profit from sacred tourism.

## In Twain's Narrative

The dragoman makes his first appearance in Chapter 41, when the pilgrims have arrived at Beirut and are frantically organizing their overland expedition. Twain describes the "shrill note of preparation — Distress about dragomans and transportation" (041-001) that has consumed the party since Constantinople. Their anxiety is palpable: "we knew very well that Palestine was a country which did not do a large passenger business, and every man we came across who knew anything about it gave us to understand that not half of our party would be able to get dragomen and animals" (041-005). The party's desperation — telegraphing consuls, willing to accept "horses, jackasses, cameleopards, kangaroos — anything" (041-005) — sets the stage for the dragoman's power over them.

The collective telegraphing works too well. By the time the pilgrims reach Beirut, "we found the place full of dragomen and their outfits" (041-006), as the entire region had become convinced that "the whole population of the Province of America" was descending upon the Holy Land.

At three o'clock, "Abraham, the dragoman, marshaled them before us" (041-009) — the horses that is, a wretched collection of one-eyed, tailless, limping, sore-backed animals that constitute the pilgrims' mounts for the journey. The display, Twain drily notes, "was exactly according to the guidebook" (041-011).

Yet Abraham quickly reveals his competence at the practical arts of travel. That evening, to the pilgrims' astonishment, he produces a camp of unprecedented luxury: "five stately circus tents" (041-013) furnished with iron bedsteads, mattresses, "snow-white sheets" (041-013), pewter pitchers, carpets, and a dedicated dining tent with "tablecloth and napkins whose whiteness and whose fineness laughed to scorn the things we were used to in the great excursion steamer" (041-014). The dinner — roast mutton, roast chicken, roast goose, pudding, delicious grapes — exceeds anything they had eaten in weeks. And yet:

> "that polite dragoman, Abraham, came bowing in and apologizing for the whole affair, on account of the unavoidable confusion of getting under way for a very long trip, and promising to do a great deal better in future!" (041-014)

The irony is total: the dragoman's groveling apology for magnificence reveals the gap between the service promised and the service delivered, a gap that will define the pilgrims' relationship with Abraham throughout the journey.

The dragoman's authority extends to all aspects of camp life. "When the dragoman's bell rang at half-past five this morning and the cry went abroad of 'Ten minutes to dress for breakfast!' I heard both" (042-004), Twain reports with surprise, noting that he had not heard the ship's breakfast gong for a month. The dragoman's bell — a "genuine, simon-pure bell" (041-014) — replaces the ship's routines with camp discipline.

But the dragoman is also a source of frustration and suspected manipulation. At Ain Mellahah, the party stops after "a very short day's run, but the dragoman does not want to go further, and has invented a plausible lie about the country beyond this being infested by ferocious Arabs, who would make sleeping in their midst a dangerous pastime" (046-014). Twain's skepticism about these "ferocious Arabs" — whom he has already sized up as frauds — extends to the dragoman who deploys them as an excuse. The dragoman's control over the itinerary is not always aligned with the pilgrims' wishes or the truth.

This becomes explicit during the disastrous episode at the Sea of Galilee, when the pilgrims attempt to hire a boat. Ferguson (as Twain clarifies: "By Ferguson I mean our dragoman Abraham, of course" (045-028)) serves as interpreter in the negotiations. When the boatmen demand two Napoleons — eight dollars — the pilgrims counter with one, and the boat immediately "was twenty paces from the shore, and speeding away like a frightened thing" (047-029). The pilgrims then offer the full price, "and pilgrims and dragoman shouted themselves hoarse with pleadings to the retreating boatmen to come back" (047-030) — but to no avail. The dragoman, for all his authority, cannot undo a lost bargain.

## The Dragoman and the Economy of Pilgrimage

Beyond camp management, the dragoman functions as the pilgrims' chief financial agent. At Nazareth, a moment crystallizes the economic web in which Abraham operates:

> "The dragoman had paid his master, but that counted as nothing — if you hire a man to sneeze for you, here, and another man chooses to help him, you have got to pay both. They do nothing whatever without pay." (050-004)

This lament appears when the theatrical Arab "guard" — whose services the pilgrims never wanted and whose weapons are rusted relics (049-010) — arrives to collect baksheesh from both the dragoman and the pilgrims directly. The dragoman's payment to the guard's sheik-master means nothing; the guard himself must be paid. Twain's exasperation glides into theological commentary: "How it must have surprised these people to hear the way of salvation offered to them 'without money and without price'" (050-004). The dragoman, in this reading, is part of a culture in which nothing is given freely — the antithesis of Christian grace.

The dragoman's role as scheduling authority produces another comic moment in the journey to the Jordan. "At two in the morning they routed us out of bed — another piece of unwarranted cruelty — another stupid effort of our dragoman to get ahead of a rival" (055-015). The dragoman's competitive instincts — racing against other parties — override the pilgrims' comfort, and their predawn departure becomes one more grievance in the long litany of the Holy Land journey.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's treatment of Abraham/Ferguson is characteristically double-edged. He never denies the dragoman's competence — the camp is luxurious, the food excellent, the logistics of moving eight men, twenty-six mules, and nineteen servants through a harsh landscape are managed with professional precision. Abraham apologizes for magnificence, which is either humility or performance, but the magnificence itself is real.

Yet Twain also detects and satirizes the dragoman's manipulations: the invented dangers that justify short travel days (046-014), the competitive sprinting that drags pilgrims from bed at two in the morning (055-015), the economic arrangements that never quite protect the pilgrims from further demands for payment (050-004). The dragoman is at once servant and master — bowing apologetically while controlling every aspect of the pilgrims' experience.

The nickname "Ferguson" is itself revealing. By giving Abraham an absurd Anglo-Saxon alias, the pilgrims domesticate and mock him, refusing the dignity of his actual name. "By Ferguson I mean our dragoman Abraham, of course" (045-028) — the "of course" acknowledging that the joke is so well-established it requires no explanation. The dragoman, for all his power over their daily lives, remains an outsider and an object of humor.

Twain's deepest insight about the dragoman is structural. The dragoman is not a rogue but a system: a necessary evil produced by the gap between Western travelers and Eastern realities. Without him, the pilgrims could not function. With him, they are perpetually managed, manipulated, and mildly betrayed. The dragoman is the pilgrim's shadow — the commercial logic that the sacred journey pretends to transcend but can never escape.
