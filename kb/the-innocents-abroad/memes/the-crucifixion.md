# The Crucifixion

The Crucifixion of Jesus Christ, traditionally dated to circa 30--33 AD at Golgotha (the Place of the Skull) outside Jerusalem, is the central redemptive event of Christianity and the gravitational center of Mark Twain's Holy Land pilgrimage in *The Innocents Abroad*. Twain's encounter with the sites associated with the Crucifixion -- particularly within the Church of the Holy Sepulchre -- produces some of the most emotionally complex and intellectually revealing passages in the entire travelogue.

## Overview

The Crucifixion overshadows every other event and destination in Twain's Holy Land itinerary. From the moment the Quaker City pilgrims first sight Jerusalem -- "walls still stand that witnessed the spectacle of the Crucifixion" [052-024] -- through days of exhaustive touring within the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, the execution of Jesus on Calvary serves as both a historical touchstone and a test of the pilgrims' faith. Twain's account oscillates between reverence for the genuine historical gravity of the event and caustic skepticism toward the layers of ecclesiastical embellishment that have accumulated around its commemoration.

Twain approaches the Crucifixion not as a theological abstraction but as a concrete historical occurrence whose physical location can -- unlike the tomb of Jesus or the site of the Annunciation -- be identified with reasonable certainty. The public nature of the execution, the notoriety of the condemned man, and the catastrophic natural phenomena reported to have accompanied it (the earthquake, the rending of the Temple veil, the preternatural darkness) all contribute to Twain's conviction that the site could not have been lost to memory in the three centuries before Helena's church-building campaign [053-032]. This intellectual conviction, combined with the sensory overload of the Greek and Latin chapels built upon the rock of Calvary, gives his account of the Crucifixion sites a tonal complexity unmatched elsewhere in the book.

## Background

The Crucifixion of Jesus of Nazareth occurred during the Roman governorship of Pontius Pilate (AD 26--36), at the conclusion of a sequence of events that included Jesus's triumphal entry into Jerusalem, his arrest at Gethsemane, trials before the Sanhedrin and Pilate, scourging, and the forced procession along the Via Dolorosa to Golgotha. According to the Gospel accounts, Jesus was crucified between two thieves, mocked by soldiers and passersby, and died after approximately six hours on the cross. The Gospels record supernatural occurrences accompanying his death: a three-hour darkness from noon, an earthquake that split rocks, and the tearing of the Temple veil.

By the fourth century, the precise locations of these events had become the object of intense pious inquiry. St. Helena, mother of the Emperor Constantine, traveled to Palestine and, according to tradition, discovered the True Cross along with the crosses of the two thieves, the crown of thorns, and the nails of the Crucifixion [053-011]. Helena's discovery inaugurated a tradition of church-building on the site that continues to the present day, enshrining the rock of Calvary and the tomb of Jesus under the single great dome of the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. Twain observes that "the place of the Crucifixion, and, in fact, every other place intimately connected with that tremendous event, are ingeniously massed together and covered by one roof" [053-006].

## The Event

The Crucifixion was, in Roman practice, a deliberately public and degrading form of execution reserved for slaves, rebels, and non-citizens. The condemned man was scourged, compelled to carry the crossbeam to the execution site, then nailed or bound to the cross and left to die of asphyxiation, exposure, or shock. In the case of Jesus, the Gospels describe soldiers dividing his garments, the posting of a titulus reading "This is the King of the Jews," and the presence of a small group of followers -- including his mother Mary, Mary Magdalene, and the disciple John -- at the foot of the cross.

Twain's narrative, while never recounting the biblical Crucifixion story in full, gathers around the physical remnants and commemorative sites of the event. He visits the place where the soldiers divided the raiment [053-024], the spot where the Roman centurion stood guard [053-022], the rock-bearing the earthquake fissure said to have opened at the moment of Christ's death [053-034], and the Chapel of the Mocking, where Christ was crowned with thorns and struck by the soldiers before the execution [053-029]. Each of these sites, preserved (or invented) within the sprawling complex of the Holy Sepulchre, represents a distinct node in the Passion narrative.

The chapter heading for Chapter 53 enumerates the sites in a breathless summary that captures both the density of commemoration and the almost bureaucratic thoroughness with which the monks have catalogued every conceivable detail: "The Stone of Unction -- The grave of Jesus -- Places of the apparition -- The finding of the three crosses -- The legend -- Monkish impostures -- The Pillar of Flagellation -- The place of a relic -- 'The bonds of Christ' -- 'The center of the Earth' -- Place whence the dust was taken of which Adam was made -- Grave of Adam -- The martyred soldier -- The copper plate that was on the cross -- The good St. Helena -- Place of the division of the garments -- St. Dimas, the penitent thief -- The late Emperor Maximilian's contribution -- Grotto wherein the crosses were found, and the nails, and the crown of thorns -- Chapel of the Mocking -- Tomb of Melchizedek -- Graves of two renowned crusaders -- *The place of the Crucifixion*" [053-001].

## Twain's Account and Commentary

Twain's visit to the place of the Crucifixion is carefully deferred by his guides, who present it last among the many sites within the Church of the Holy Sepulchre -- "it is the crowning glory of the place" [053-032]. This structural decision in the tour narrative mirrors the event's theological primacy, and Twain's response to it is markedly different from his reaction to the other holy sites he has inspected. Throughout the church, he has maintained a posture of genial skepticism, noting how the Stone of Unction was concealed under marble to prevent pilgrims from chipping pieces off [053-007], and how the baton that lets a pilgrim feel the Pillar of Flagellation through a screen is treated as proof positive of authenticity [053-012].

At Calvary, however, the tone shifts. Twain articulates his reasoning with uncharacteristic earnestness:

> "The place of the Crucifixion affects him differently. He fully believes that he is looking upon the very spot where the Savior gave up his life. He remembers that Christ was very celebrated, long before he came to Jerusalem; he knows that his fame was so great that crowds followed him all the time; he is aware that his entry into the city produced a stirring sensation, and that his reception was a kind of ovation; he can not overlook the fact that when he was crucified there were very many in Jerusalem who believed that he was the true Son of God. To publicly execute such a personage was sufficient in itself to make the locality of the execution a memorable place for ages; added to this, the storm, the darkness, the earthquake, the rending of the vail of the Temple, and the untimely waking of the dead, were events calculated to fix the execution and the scene of it in the memory of even the most thoughtless witness." [053-032]

This passage is Twain's most sustained argument for the authenticity of any holy site in Jerusalem, and it operates on entirely rational, historical grounds rather than theological presupposition. The public nature of crucifixion, the pre-existing fame of Jesus, and the spectacular natural phenomena reported by the Gospels all militate against the loss of the location from collective memory. Twain argues that oral transmission across three hundred years -- "fathers would tell their sons about the strange affair, and point out the spot; the sons would transmit the story to their children" [053-032] -- is entirely plausible for so significant and singular an event. He contrasts this with the burial site, where "not half a dozen persons knew where they buried the Saviour, perhaps, and a burial is not a startling event, anyhow" [053-032].

And yet, even as Twain grants the authenticity of the location, he cannot suppress his discomfort with the ecclesiastical packaging in which it has been delivered to him. He observes the ornate altar, the jewel-encrusted icons, the gilt and diamond-studded depictions, and remarks dryly:

> "When one stands where the Saviour was crucified, he finds it all he can do to keep it strictly before his mind that Christ was not crucified in a Catholic Church. He must remind himself every now and then that the great event transpired in the open air, and not in a gloomy, candle-lighted cell in a little corner of a vast church, upstairs -- a small cell all bejeweled and bespangled with flashy ornamentation, in execrable taste." [053-033]

This is quintessential Twain: the collision of sublime historical truth with the gaudy apparatus of institutional religion produces a dissonance that he renders with precision and wit. The same tension runs through his account of the Roman soldier's altar. Tradition holds that the centurion who supervised the Crucifixion, witnessing the earthquake and darkness, exclaimed "Surely this was the Son of God!" and was subsequently beheaded on that very spot by the Temple priests for his blasphemous confession [053-022]. The altar commemorating this soldier stands in full view of the place of Crucifixion, and Twain recounts the story with genuine narrative force -- the vail rent, the rock of Golgotha split asunder, "the artillery of heaven thundered, and in the baleful glare of the lightnings the shrouded dead flitted about the streets of Jerusalem" [053-022] -- even as the reader suspects he does not fully credit the tradition.

The earthquake that the Gospels associate with the Crucifixion is given tangible form in the church itself: "a rift in the living rock made by the earthquake at the time of the Crucifixion, and an extension of which he had seen before in the wall of one of the grottoes below" [053-034]. Twain returns to this geological feature repeatedly, as if the crack in the rock offers a physical anchor for belief that the more elaborate shrines cannot provide.

## Aftermath and Significance

The Crucifixion reverberates through Twain's narrative far beyond the walls of the Holy Sepulchre. In Constantinople, he observes Turkish women moving through the dim bazaar aisles and imagines they look "as the shrouded dead must have looked when they walked forth from their graves amid the storms and thunders and earthquakes that burst upon Calvary that awful night of the Crucifixion" [033-011]. This image -- the risen dead walking the streets of Jerusalem in the storm-lashed darkness -- is one of the most vivid in the entire book and demonstrates how the event haunts Twain's imagination even when he is many hundreds of miles from Jerusalem.

The most famous legend to emerge from the Crucifixion, as Twain relates it, is the story of the Wandering Jew. On the Via Dolorosa, the guide points out "the veritable house where the unhappy wretch once lived who has been celebrated in song and story for more than eighteen hundred years as the Wandering Jew" [054-006]. According to the tradition, this man stood in his doorway on the day of the Crucifixion, watched the struggling mob approach with the condemned Saviour, and when Jesus would have rested a moment, pushed him rudely away and said, "Move on!" The Lord's reply -- "Move on, thou, likewise" -- condemned the man to wander the earth without rest until the Second Coming [054-006]. Twain's handling of the legend is characteristically layered: he recounts it with apparent credulity, swelling the tale with accounts of the Wanderer's futile attempts to die in the sack of Jerusalem by Titus, in Muhammad's Arabian campaigns, and in the Crusades, only to undercut it all at the end by revealing that the Wanderer has left his mark on a wall in the form of what appears to be a modern tourist's graffiti: "S. T. -- 1860 -- X." [054-009].

St. Helena's discovery of the True Cross, approximately three hundred years after the Crucifixion, marks the beginning of organized Christian pilgrimage to the site [053-011]. Twain recounts the legend of how the three crosses were tested one by one upon a noble lady of Jerusalem who lay gravely ill: the first cross caused her to scream and swoon, the second threw her into convulsions requiring six strong men to restrain her, and the third restored her instantly to health. Twain's tone hovers exquisitely between credulity and irony: "When we listen to evidence like this, we cannot but believe. We would be ashamed to doubt, and properly, too. Even the very part of Jerusalem where this all occurred is there yet. So there is really no room for doubt" [053-011].

In the long sweep of history, the Crucifixion and the sites associated with it have been a casus belli for centuries of conflict. Twain notes that "the most gallant knights that ever wielded sword wasted their lives away in a struggle to seize it and hold it sacred from infidel pollution," and remarks that even "in our own day a war, that cost millions of treasure and rivers of blood, was fought because two rival nations claimed the sole right to put a new dome upon it" [053-035]. The reverence for the place of the Crucifixion -- and the willingness of men to kill and die for control of it -- is for Twain a paradox that sits uneasily alongside the identity of the man who died there, "the meek and lowly, the mild and gentle, Prince of Peace" [053-035].
