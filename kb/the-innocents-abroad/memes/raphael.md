# Raphael

Raphael Sanzio (1483–1520) was one of the three towering figures of the Italian High Renaissance, alongside Michelangelo and Leonardo da Vinci. In *The Innocents Abroad*, Raphael's works appear repeatedly in the galleries and churches of Italy — and Twain treats them not with reverence but with the irreverence of a man who refuses to be told what to admire.

## Overview

Twain encounters Raphael's paintings throughout his Italian tour: in Venice, in Florence, and most memorably in Rome, where the *Transfiguration* hangs in the Vatican. But unlike the dutiful tourists who filled their journals with rapturous descriptions of Raphael's genius, Twain approaches the Renaissance master with profound skepticism. His critique is not really about Raphael himself — it is an assault on the convention of obligatory admiration, the cultural pressure to swoon before Old Masters whether one genuinely responds to them or not. Raphael becomes, in Twain's hands, a symbol of everything fraudulent about the Grand Tour aesthetic.

## In the Travelogue

Raphael first enters the narrative as one of the artistic giants whose works dominate Italian collections, grouped with Michelangelo and Canova as figures whose "statue is a study in itself" (018-010). In Venice, Twain lists Raphael among "the world's idols" — "Titian, Tintoretto, Paul Veronese, Raphael — none other than the world's idols" (025-013) — and immediately launches into a satirical assault on their paintings. He complains that Raphael "pictured such infernal villains" that the artist deserved to starve (025-014), a deliberately outrageous judgment designed to scandalize conventional taste.

In Rome, Twain visits Santa Croce and notes the tomb of "Michelangelo, Raphael and Machiavelli" (024-009) with passing interest — the great names of the Italian Renaissance interred together, their fame a burden to every tourist who must dutifully visit. The climax of Twain's engagement with Raphael comes at the Vatican, where he sees "the Transfiguration, by Raphael, and some other things" (028-022) and confesses that he "contemplated with a bat" (028-022) — meaning he was unmoved. He then imagines buying "a small trifle of a Raphael" (028-029) but finds the price too steep, a joke that reduces the master's work to a souvenir.

## Historical Context

Raphael Sanzio died at 37, yet left a body of work — including the *School of Athens*, the *Sistine Madonna*, the *Transfiguration*, and countless Madonnas and portraits — that defined the High Renaissance ideal of harmony, grace, and balanced composition. For 19th-century travelers on the Grand Tour, Raphael represented the pinnacle of artistic achievement, and guidebooks of the period devoted pages of breathless prose to his works. Twain's hostility to Raphael was thus not merely personal taste; it was a calculated provocation aimed at the entire apparatus of cultural tourism — the guidebooks, the lecturers, the fellow travelers who had learned their opinions by rote.

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's treatment of Raphael is of a piece with his larger critique of Old Master worship. He lumps "all the Michelangelos, the Raphaels, the Guidos and the other old masters" (028-025) into a single category of obligatory admiration, and his complaint is consistent: these paintings depict suffering, villainy, or religious ecstasy in ways that American viewers of 1867 find alien and off-putting. "Wherever you find a Raphael, a Rubens, a Michelangelo, a Carracci, or a Da Vinci (and we see them every day), you find artists copying them" (019-035) — the copying proves the fame, but Twain implies it also proves the stagnation. The art world has become a closed circuit of imitation, and Raphael, for all his genius, is the figurehead of a system Twain finds intellectually dishonest.
