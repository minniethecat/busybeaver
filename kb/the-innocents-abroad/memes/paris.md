# Paris

French capital and seat of the 1867 International Exposition, the first major European stop of the Quaker City excursion after crossing the Atlantic, visited in Chapters 12 through 15.

## Overview

Paris was Twain's introduction to continental Europe, and it set the pattern for much of what followed: wonder at the scale and grandeur of the Old World, exasperation with local guides, and a willingness to puncture pretension with humor. The party arrived at the height of the 1867 International Exposition, when Napoleon III had just completed his sweeping reconstruction of the city. "Everybody was going to the famous Paris Exposition — I, too, was going to the Paris Exposition" (002-005).

Twain's Paris is a city of contradictions — splendid avenues and bewildering guides, the magnificent Arc de Triomphe and the gruesome relics of the Revolution, fashionable restaurants and the "antipodes" of the Morgue and the sewers. Over twelve days, the party attempted to "do" Paris thoroughly, a task Twain concluded was impossible: "We have gone to bed every night perfectly satisfied, and ready to say 'We have seen Paris!' — only to find that when morning came and we prepared for a fresh start, there was always a dozen of its celebrated things had been left over" (013-039).

## Arrival and First Impressions

The journey to Paris from Marseilles was already an education in European sensibilities. En route, Twain was interrogated by fellow passengers about American travel habits: "Are you going through Spain to Paris?" That question was bandied about the ship day and night for two weeks, reminding him of a man who, condemned to the gallows, was asked on the steps "how he liked his head" (007-012). The train itself became a site of social comedy — the other three passengers refused to sleep, suspecting Twain to be a murderer, until "the mystery was cleared up" (012-011).

The arrival itself was sublime: "At last, lo, we stood in magnificent Paris!" (012-016). Twain describes "speeding through the streets of Paris and delightfully recognizing certain names and places with which books had long ago made us familiar" (012-017). His first adventure was the palatial barbershop — a "gilded and frescoed" establishment where he wished to recline "at full length, with my heels four feet higher than my head, in that thoroughly restful attitude" (012-021). The experience went poorly: "I never desired to dream of palatial Parisian barbershops anymore" (012-026). The day closed with the party puzzling "over French 'guides to Paris'" and talking "disjointedly in a foreign tongue" with the landlord about supper (012-031).

## Description and Notable Features

### The International Exposition

The 1867 International Exposition dominated Twain's Paris visit. The universal attraction drew "such multitudes of Englishmen and Americans to Paris that it would be next to impossible to find lodging" — the party was warned about the difficulty before ever setting sail (013-002). The grounds contained the "curious things the world loves to look upon," from the "gorgeous East" to France's own "wonderful works in painting, sculpture, and the like" (014-009). Twain resolved that there would be "no well-informed people in the ship" to explain the exposition's purpose, and that when he returned he would be the only one aboard who "really understood the Parisian Exposition" (013-006).

Beyond the exposition, Napoleon III's rebuilding of the city commanded Twain's admiration: "He has rebuilt Paris and has partly rebuilt every city in the State. He levelled the buildings in great blocks, and began over. The narrow streets were widened; stately avenues were devised" (013-048). The transformation was so total that "the man who had known Paris thirty years ago could not recognize the city today" (013-048).

### The Morgue and the Sewers

After exhausting the fine-art circuit, Twain sought what he called the "antipodes" of Parisian refinement: the Morgue and the sewers (014-009, 015-001). The Morgue — "a sort of exhibition of citizens who have met with sudden deaths" — provided material for his darkest humor. He describes the scene: "I stood and looked in the same place for an hour, expecting one to start up and stare" (014-018). The sewers, by contrast, he found genuinely impressive: clean, well-lit, and navigated by boat. "We traveled several leagues through these dismal canals, and there was not a moment when we could not see to read a newspaper" (014-022).

### The Cathedral of Notre Dame and Relics

Twain visited Notre Dame but was withheld from rapture by his growing relic-fatigue. He noted the church's connection to Abelard and Heloise — "a canon of the cathedral of Paris" (015-009) — and recounted the tragedy in detail, including Abelard founding "a school of rhetoric in Paris" and the brutal revenge taken by Heloise's uncle Fulbert. "The last hope of seeing the only woman he ever loved had failed, and a voluntary hermit, he entered the walls of an Abbey and gave up the world" (015-018). Yet Twain's verdict on the church was cool: like all the great cathedrals he visited, it was a storehouse of relics and legends he could not quite credit. He was more interested in the human story.

### The Arc de Triomphe and Napoleon

Standing under the Arc de Triomphe, Twain reflected on Napoleon I: "Here in brilliant Paris, under this majestic Arch of Triumph, the first Napoleon's funeral pageant paused with his dead body on its way from St. Helena to its final resting place" (013-045). Twain was moved by the historical weight of the spot, though characteristically he undercut the solemnity by adding he could "scarcely compute" the cost of Napoleon III's rebuilding — the juxtaposition of grand history and modern expense being essential to his Parisian account.

## Historical Significance

Twain is acutely aware that Paris is the capital not just of France but of European fashion and culture. Driving through the Bois de Boulogne, he observes "drives, and paths, and parks" filled with thousands of people — "some of them dressed in the very latest Paris fashions" (017-007). When they reached Genoa, the contrast was explicit: "And besides, as in Paris, we had a guide" (017-012), a phrase that sums up his experience of European tourism as a guided ordeal.

He mentions that the crown of thorns "is in Sainte Chapelle, in Paris, and part of one also in Notre Dame" (017-017), linking Paris to the broader European economy of relics. And he notes that the great sculptures of antiquity could be "set up in the galleries of Paris for the worship of the world" (017-027), acknowledging the city's status as arbiter of artistic taste.

The historical layers are dense: in the very same chapter that describes the Exposition, Twain recounts Abelard's twelfth-century tragedy at the Cathedral of Paris, the French Revolution relics, and the Napoleon dynasty's architectural transformation of the city — all within a few pages, as if to demonstrate why Paris cannot be "done" in twelve days.

## Daily Life and Local Color

Twain's comic adventures in Paris begin with the barbershop and never really end. The French guide becomes a character in his own right — the embodiment of all that was exasperating about European tourism. Describing the guide's patter, Twain writes that he "expected to have a guide named Henri de Montmorency, or Armand de la Chartreuse, or something that would sound grand in a letter to the village newspaper," but was instead saddled with "a hideous being" who "had lost his health, his equanimity, and his hair" (013-010).

The guide's relentless salesmanship provided material for extended comic set-pieces: "'Ah, here is ze finest silk magazin in Paris — ze most celebrate'" (013-019). The chapter-length account of what "Americans fare at the hands of the Paris guides and what sort of people Paris guides are" (013-038) became a template for all subsequent guide-bashing across Italy and the Holy Land.

The restaurant culture fascinated Twain: "In Paris we often saw in shop windows the sign 'English Spoken Here'" (015-027), along with the phrase he found most charming: "Ici on parle Francais" — here French is spoken. And his final paragraph before leaving Paris sums up his ambivalence: "Enough of Paris for the present. We have done our whole duty by it. We have seen the Tuileries, the Madeleine, the Bourse, the Luxembourg, the Palais Royal, the Vendome Column, the Hotel des Invalides, the Pantheon, the Garden of Plants, the Jardin Mabille, the Grand Opera, the Circus, the Academy of Medicine, the Louvre, the Champs Elysees... — all these we have seen, and seen with our own eyes, and I have described them all in a letter to my mother" (015-041).

## Twain's Verdict

Twain left Paris without delivering a definitive judgment, which is itself a judgment. The city was too vast, too dense with history and culture, to be captured in twelve days or twelve chapters. His final word before departing for Versailles and Genoa is characteristically deflationary: "We shall see Paris only for a little while as we ride into the station of the Lyons railway, and then — we are off for Italy!" (015-048).

Yet his admiration for Napoleon III's rebuilt city — the sewers, the broad avenues, the Bois de Boulogne — was genuine. What annoyed him was the tourist experience, not the city itself. Paris becomes in his narrative the prototype of European sophistication that the Americans must learn to navigate: a magnificent place whose guides would skin you alive if you let them.

> "He has rebuilt Paris and has partly rebuilt every city in the State." (013-048)
