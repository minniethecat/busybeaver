# Music

The art of organized sound — encountered by Mark Twain in forms ranging from martial bands and cathedral organs to the amateur performances of his fellow Quaker City passengers, and consistently used as a lens for cultural observation and comic relief.

## Overview

Music forms a constant backdrop to Twain's narrative in *The Innocents Abroad*, threading through the voyage from the Quaker City's improvised shipboard concerts to the grand military bands of European capitals. Twain's treatment of music reveals the breadth of his observational gifts: he is equally attentive to the sublime (vesper bells drifting over Lake Como, a Paris military band stirring patriotic fervor) and the ridiculous (the ship's "distressing choir-music," a chaotic ball in Paris where the music "struck up" to general pandemonium).

For a travel writer, music serves as a particularly useful barometer of cultural difference. The military marches of Imperial France, the operatic traditions of Italy, the church organs of Rome — each musical encounter tells Twain something about the society that produces it, and each becomes an opportunity for the kind of vivid, sensory description that distinguishes his best travel writing.

## In Twain's Narrative

Music enters the book on the very first page, as Twain describes the Quaker City passengers arriving with "light musical instruments for amusement in the ship" [001-008]. This sets the tone for the shipboard culture that follows: amateur performances on "parlor-organ," accordion, and clarinet, which together "made good music, too, what there was of it" — a characteristic Twain half-compliment [004-005][004-028]. Other passengers, he drily notes, "said openly that they wished we had left our distressing choir-music at home" [004-032].

Ashore, the musical landscape changes dramatically. In Paris, Twain is captivated by the "fine military bands" performing for crowds in the great squares [007-037]. At the Paris Opera ball, he describes a scene of near-chaos: "Shouts, laughter, furious music, a bewildering chaos of darting and lumbering couples" [014-020]. The contrast with the serene "music of the vesper bells" drifting over Lake Como could hardly be starker [020-012].

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, music occupied a central place in European public life. Military bands were a fixture of urban culture, performing regularly in parks and squares as expressions of national pride and civic order — particularly in France under Napoleon III. Opera houses like La Scala in Milan and the Paris Opera were not merely entertainment venues but symbols of cultural prestige and social hierarchy. Church music, from Gregorian chant to elaborate polyphonic Mass settings, remained at the heart of Catholic worship.

For American travelers, European musical culture could be both awe-inspiring and alienating. The formality of the opera, the ubiquity of martial music, the elaborate church organ traditions — all differed markedly from the more informal musical culture of the United States. Twain's reactions capture this cultural gap while also revealing his genuine responsiveness to music's emotional power.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain approaches music with the same irreverent honesty he brings to all his subjects. He is unafraid to admit when music moves him — as with the military bands of Paris or the vesper bells of Como — and equally unafraid to mock what strikes him as pretentious or incompetent. His description of the shipboard choir is a masterpiece of affectionate satire: he acknowledges their good intentions even as he records the complaints they provoked.

> "Twenty sets formed, the music struck up, and then—I placed my hands on my hips and laughed until the tears ran down." [014-018]

This passage, describing the Paris Opera ball, captures Twain's essential approach to music as spectacle: he is a willing participant who never quite loses his outsider's perspective.
