# Turkish Baths and Public Bathing

Throughout his five-month voyage across Europe and the Holy Land, Twain confronts a wide spectrum of bathing customs--from the public bathhouses of Milan to the celebrated but deeply disappointing Turkish bath of Constantinople, and from the soothing warm springs of Tiberias to the bizarre buoyancy of the Dead Sea. Bathing becomes a recurring site of cultural comedy, physical discomfort, and deflated expectations (034-023).

## Overview

Bathing in "The Innocents Abroad" serves as more than a matter of personal hygiene. It is a lens through which Twain examines the gap between the romantic promises of Orientalist travel literature and the grimy realities of the Middle East in the 1860s. The bath--whether European or Ottoman--repeatedly emerges as a setting where Twain's expectations, shaped by years of reading, collide with experiences that are far less luxurious, far more awkward, and sometimes outright hostile (034-035). The motif also reveals Twain's broader critique of the European and Middle Eastern failure to supply what he considers the bare essentials of civilized life, most pointedly soap (019-015) (011-002).

## In Twain's Narrative

Twain's encounters with baths and bathing span the full geographical range of his journey. The first notable episode occurs in Paris and Marseilles, where the pilgrims discover--to their enduring irritation--that French hotels do not stock soap in guest rooms (011-002). This shortage becomes a running joke that follows the party across Europe. In Milan, the situation reaches its comic peak when the three men rent a public bathhouse and discover, after undressing, that there is no soap whatsoever--a crisis that requires an emissary to be dispatched "far up town" to procure some (019-015). Twain dryly concludes that the English, knowing how to travel comfortably, carry their own soap, while other foreigners "do not use the article" (019-024).

The most famous and extended bath episode is the Turkish bath in Constantinople, which Twain approaches with years of accumulated fantasy. Travel books had filled his imagination with images of marble chambers, spiced Eastern fragrances, and languorous attendants fanning bathers on divans of eider down (034-023). The reality is the precise opposite: a vast, barnlike courtyard of unpainted railings, rickety chairs, and cadaverous attendants who "shed no entrancing odors--just the contrary" (034-024). The scrubbing, drenching, and steam--far from the voluptuous ritual Twain anticipated--become an ordeal he compares to being pared down with a jack-plane (034-031).

In the Holy Land, bathing takes on additional layers of meaning. Twain bathes in the sources of the Jordan at Banias, an experience so icy that he blames it for his subsequent cholera (045-015). At the Dead Sea, the famous buoyancy makes for a "funny bath" where swimmers bob uncontrollably on the surface and come out coated in salt crystals "till we shone like icicles" (055-027). The ancient warm baths near Tiberias, mentioned by Pliny, Twain perversely refuses to visit precisely because Pliny has already claimed them (049-003).

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the mid-nineteenth century, the Turkish bath, or hammam, was one of the most celebrated institutions of the Ottoman Empire among Western travelers. Victorian Britain had even imported the practice, with Turkish bath establishments opening in London and other cities during the 1850s and 1860s. Travelogues routinely described the hammam as a site of Oriental luxury: steam rooms, marble slabs, vigorous massage and scrubbing by attendants, followed by periods of repose with coffee and tobacco. Twain's own fantasy, built from "incendiary books of travel," accurately reflects the tropes of contemporary Orientalist writing (034-023).

The public bathhouse in Europe served a different function. In cities like Milan, public baths were practical institutions catering to residents who lacked private facilities. The absence of soap in these establishments, which so vexed Twain and his companions, reflected genuine cultural differences in bathing practices--many southern Europeans in the 1860s relied on water alone or used soap far more sparingly than Americans of the period (019-024). Similarly, bathing openly in the Nile or the Jordan was an everyday practice in the eastern Mediterranean, one that initially shocked the American pilgrims before becoming unremarkable through familiarity (058-003).

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward bathing customs oscillates between comic indignation at inconvenience and genuine disillusionment with romantic expectations. The Turkish bath episode in particular exemplifies what would become a signature Twain move: the systematic demolition of a cherished fantasy by the blunt force of reality. As he concludes:

> "Here endeth my experience of the celebrated Turkish bath, and here also endeth my dream of the bliss the mortal revels in who passes through it. It is a malignant swindle." (034-035)

Yet his complaint is not simply that the bath was unpleasant--it is that the literary tradition that built up his expectations was fraudulent. He directs his anger not at the bath attendants themselves but at the "incendiary books of travel" and the traveling writers who "invest it with a charm of poetry" (034-035). The bath becomes a parable about the unreliability of received wisdom and the gap between the curated, romanticized East of European imagination and the raw, unwashed East of direct experience.

The soap crisis, by contrast, is played for pure comedy. Dan's multilingual outburst--"Soap, you know--soap... I don't know how you Irish spell it, but I want it"--and the Doctor's mock-eloquent discharge of Italian ("Here, cospetto! corpo di Bacco! Sacramento! Solferino!--Soap, you son of a gun!") turn a mundane inconvenience into vaudeville (019-023). Twain treats soap less as a hygienic commodity than as a marker of civilization itself, and its absence becomes a recurring indictment of European backwardness.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- **Marseilles soap shortage**: The pilgrims cannot find soap in their hotel and must ring for it each time they wash, leading Twain to observe that the French manufacture soap but never use it themselves (011-002).
- **Milan bathhouse**: The party rents three tubs at a public bath only to discover there is no soap; a search of the city ensues (019-015).
- **Turkish bath, Constantinople**: The anticlimactic centerpiece--a barnlike establishment of unpainted wood and cadaverous attendants, culminating in a scrubbing so vigorous Twain jokes he needs a jack-plane (034-024)-(034-031).
- **Bathing in the sources of the Jordan**: A bone-chilling dip below the castle of Banias, which Twain believes causes his cholera (045-015).
- **The Dead Sea**: A "funny bath" of impossible buoyancy where swimmers cannot sink, concluding with a crust of salt that requires vigorous toweling (055-027).
- **Bathing in the Nile**: Stark-naked men of "superb build" bathe openly, a custom that initially startles but soon ceases to occasion remark (058-003).
