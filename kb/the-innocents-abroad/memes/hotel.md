# Hotels

The lodgings where the American pilgrims sleep, dine, complain, and observe local customs — a recurring setting in *The Innocents Abroad* that Twain uses to contrast European comfort with Middle Eastern privation, and to stage some of his most memorable comic set-pieces about the absurdities of foreign travel.

## Overview

Hotels in Twain's travelogue function as far more than places to sleep. They are microcosms of the cultures they inhabit — the polished professionalism of French waiters, the soap-free indifference of Italian innkeepers, the unexpected luxury of a Damascene courtyard, and the comic squalor of an American hotel Twain recalls for comparison. For the *Quaker City* pilgrims, hotels become a reliable source of bafflement, irritation, and occasionally delight. Each hotel stop reveals something about the country: its standards of cleanliness, its attitude toward foreigners, its culinary pretensions, and the gap between guidebook promise and lived reality.

Twain tracks hotels obsessively across the entire journey. In Europe, they represent civilization with quirky local defects (the perpetual absence of soap). In the Holy Land, luxury evaporates — the party sleeps in tents and endures khans so primitive that Twain declares he "would lie as well as at a hotel" in the open air (041-008). By Egypt, the contrast reaches its zenith: the famously terrible Shepherd's Hotel in Cairo, which Twain pairs with an extended, hilariously unflattering portrait of the Benton House back in America (057-014).

## In Twain's Narrative

The hotel motif appears at the very first landfall. In Marseilles, the pilgrims arrive at the Grand Hotel du Louvre et de la Paix and are required to fill out elaborate registration forms — "who we were, where we were born, what our occupations were, the place we came from last, whether we were married or single" (010-025) — a bureaucratic ritual that Twain presents as equal parts police surveillance and French hospitality. The same chapter features the party comically failing to distinguish the French *douain* (customhouse) from a hotel, a linguistic blunder that establishes the hotel as a site of confusion from the start (010-025).

In Genoa and Milan, Twain turns his attention to the hotel staff. The French and Italian waiters astonish him with their quiet competence: "a really polite hotel waiter who isn't an idiot" is, he declares, "the strangest curiosity yet" (011-002). He marvels at the European custom of driving "right into the central court of the hotel, in the midst of a fragrant circle of vines and flowers" (011-002), a detail that contrasts pointedly with American hotel practices. The hotel thus becomes a vehicle for serious cultural observation: these are not merely amusing foreign quirks but evidence of a more civilized, leisurely mode of life that Twain genuinely envies.

At Lake Como, the hotel provides the setting for an idyllic interlude. "Our hotel sits at the water's edge," Twain writes, and the evenings there — swimming in the lake, sailing "among the reflections of the stars," listening to "the soft melody of flutes and guitars" from passing gondolas (020-007) — represent the high-water mark of European comfort before the journey turns eastward into hardship.

The transition comes in Syria, where hotel culture undergoes a radical transformation. The Damascus hotel, entered "through a kind of a hole in the wall," surprises the weary pilgrims with its unexpected luxury: "a great flagged court, with flowers and citron trees about us, and a huge tank in the centre that was receiving the waters of many pipes" (044-011). Inside, the rooms feature broad divans, spring mattresses, and — most remarkably — carpets, a comfort so rare that Twain declares "if there is anything drearier than the tomb-like, stone-paved parlors and bedrooms of Europe and Asia, I do not know what it is" (044-011). The running water in marble tanks evokes paradise itself: "nothing, in this scorching, desolate land could look so refreshing as this pure water flashing in the lamplight" (044-011).

But Damascus also reveals the limits of hotel luxury. Twain suspects the ornamental tank between the rooms is used for drinking water — after he has already "dipped my baking head far down into its cool depths" (044-012). His revenge on the landlord's poodle, whom he dunks to the bottom of the same tank, completes the scene's comic arc: the hotel is both sanctuary and stage for petty grievance.

The hotel motif reaches its satirical climax in Cairo, where Shepherd's Hotel — "the worst on earth except the one I stopped at once in a small town in the United States" (057-013) — triggers Twain's longest and most elaborate hotel anecdote. The extended description of the Benton House, with its "two inches of sallow, sorrowful, consumptive tallow candle" (057-014), its crestfallen bed, its broken-nosed pitcher, and its magnificently resourceful porter who steals a lamp and an armful of eclectic books, ranks among Twain's finest comic writing in the entire book. The anecdote is a deliberate structural inversion: the American hotel, remembered from back home, is worse than anything the East can offer.

## Cultural and Historical Context

Nineteenth-century European hotels were undergoing rapid transformation. The Grand Hotel model — large, purpose-built establishments offering uniform luxury — had emerged in the 1850s, epitomized by the Grand Hotel du Louvre in Paris (opened 1855, the first in the world to use elevators). Twain's party stayed at its Marseilles namesake, the Grand Hotel du Louvre et de la Paix (010-025). These establishments catered to the new class of middle-class tourists created by steam travel, offering table d'hote dining, English-speaking staff, and the elaborate registration rituals Twain mocks.

In the Ottoman East, accommodations were far more varied. A traveler might stay in a khan (a walled courtyard inn with rooms for travelers and stables for animals), a monastery guesthouse, or — as Twain's party often did — in tents, camping "in the mountains of Lebanon" (041-002) and across the length of Palestine. The Damascus hotel that so impresses Twain (044-011) was almost certainly a converted merchant's house in the old city, adapted to accommodate the growing trickle of Western pilgrims and tourists following the Crimean War (1853-1856), which had opened the region to increased European and American visitation.

The soap motif — the pilgrims must "ring for soap every time we wash" (011-002), must send out for it when bathing (019-024), must include it in hotel bills alongside "candles and other nonsense" (019-025) — reflects a genuine cultural difference. Most European hotels of the period did not provide soap as a standard amenity; English travelers were known to carry their own. Twain transforms this practical inconvenience into a running joke about continental hygiene and American expectations.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's treatment of hotels operates on two registers simultaneously. On the surface, he is a comic complainer — the American tourist perpetually dissatisfied with foreign accommodations, whether it's the missing soap of Europe (011-002, 019-024, 019-025) or the primitive conditions of Syria (041-008). The running jokes about soap, about the impossibility of sleeping in "tomb-like, stone-paved parlors and bedrooms" (044-011), about the ridiculous English of hotel advertisements (019-028–019-030), all serve this persona.

Beneath the comedy, however, runs a current of genuine cultural appreciation. Twain is charmed by the European custom of dining at leisure, of smoking in "cool chambers" after the table d'hote (011-003). He envies the "comfort" that is "the main charm of life in Europe" (019-013), and the hotel is precisely where he observes it most intimately. His praise for the Damascus hotel — "all this luxury was as grateful to systems and senses worn out with an exhausting day's travel, as it was unexpected" (044-011) — is sincere, not ironic. The hotel here becomes a measure of civilization itself, and Twain's recognition of splendor where he least expects it is part of his broader argument against guidebook prejudice.

The Egypt sequence provides the deepest insight. By pairing Shepherd's Hotel with the Benton House (057-013–057-014), Twain collapses the East-West hierarchy on which the entire travel narrative ostensibly rests. The worst hotel in the world is not in Cairo — it is in America. The anecdote is a masterstroke of comic self-deprecation that reorients the reader's perspective: Twain's complaints about foreign hotels are not the smug superiority of the American abroad but the universal lament of the traveler, wherever he finds himself.
