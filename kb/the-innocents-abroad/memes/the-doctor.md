# The Doctor

A fellow passenger aboard the *Quaker City* excursion, the Doctor is one of Twain's unnamed but frequently appearing travel companions. His real name is never given; he is referred to as "the Doctor" or "Dr. B."

He is part of Twain's inner circle alongside Dan and Jack (the author himself). He is distinguished by his deadpan humor, his willingness to play the fool, and his self-appointed role as a French interpreter.

## Overview

The Doctor first appears at Gibraltar, joining the author and Dan to purchase kid gloves before attending the theater (007-037). He becomes one of the most frequently mentioned members of Twain's traveling party thereafter.

His unnamed status reflects Twain's narrative approach — while historical figures like [[michelangelo]] and [[napoleon-iii]] are given full names, ordinary Americans aboard ship are often known only by nicknames, titles, or initials.

The Doctor's defining trait is his ability to keep a straight face while playing the fool. Twain says he is the designated guide-tormentor: "the Doctor asks the questions, generally, because he can keep his countenance, and look more like an inspired idiot, and throw more imbecility into the tone of his voice than any man that lives" (027-023).

This makes him the indispensable straight man in the travelers' comedy routine.

## In the Travelogue

### Early Adventures

The Doctor first appears during the glove-buying episode in Gibraltar. After Twain destroys kid gloves under a shop attendant's flattery, the Doctor joins Dan in mocking him. Speaking "to the moon," the Doctor deadpans: "it is always easy to tell when a gentleman is used to putting on kid gloves" (007-048). This establishes a teasing dynamic that persists through the voyage.

### The French Language Debacle

One of the Doctor's most extended comic sequences occurs in Marseilles, where he attempts to serve as the party's French interpreter. When the group hires a waterman, the Doctor declares the man is going to *allez* to the *douain* — which he translates as going to the hotel. In fact, the *douain* is the customhouse, a humiliation the group silently absorbs (010-024).

At their first cafe, the Doctor's French fails again. He asks "*Avez-vous du vin?*" with elaborate distinctness, but the dame looks increasingly perplexed. Twain remarks, "Doctor, there is a flaw in your pronunciation somewhere" (010-031). The punchline lands when the Frenchwoman says: "Bless you, why didn't you speak English before? I don't know anything about your plagued French!" (010-034).

### In Paris: The Guide and Billiards

In Paris, the Doctor becomes the primary antagonist of the guide Ferguson. When Ferguson stops the carriage at silk stores hoping for commissions, the Doctor delivers serenely sarcastic rebukes. As they pull up to yet another silk merchant, the Doctor says: "At last! How imposing the Louvre is, and yet how small! How exquisitely fashioned!" — knowing it is not the museum (013-028).

Twain notes that "the Doctor was always serene, always smooth-voiced" (013-027), even when the party grew angry. The Doctor also joins Twain in a disastrous billiard game, where "Dan was to mark while the Doctor and I played" (012-027). Neither scores in an hour, and they abandon the game in disgust.

### Tormenting the Guides

The Doctor's most celebrated role comes in Italy. The party feigns stupidity to deflate their guides. At every statue the Doctor asks, "By Michelangelo?" (027-014). In the Roman Forum: "Michelangelo?" — the guide explains it predates him by a thousand years (027-016). At an Egyptian obelisk, he asks again, and the guide cries, "Zis is *two* thousan' year before he is born!" (027-019).

The performance culminates at the Vatican. Before a three-thousand-year-old Egyptian mummy, the Doctor asks if the gentleman's parents are still living (027-055). When told the mummy is long dead, he turns savagely on the guide: "what do you mean by such conduct as this! Playing us for Chinamen... if you've got a nice *fresh* corpse, fetch him out!" (027-073).

### Venice and the Barber

In Venice, the Doctor participates in a prank shave. He and Dan praise the barber's gentle touch — "Doctor, it is perfect luxury. The ship's barber isn't anything to him" (023-042) — luring Twain into the chair for a brutal shave. Twain finds "Dan and the Doctor both wiping blood off their faces and laughing" (023-045), confirming their complicity.

### Medical Practice in Syria

In the Holy Land, the Doctor briefly becomes famous in his actual profession. When local Arabs discover a physician is among them, they flock for treatment. "As soon as the tribe found out that we had a doctor in our party, they began to flock in from all quarters" (045-024).

Twain describes the scene with wry biblical parallels: "What reverent and what worshiping looks they bent upon that dread, mysterious power, the Doctor!" (045-024). He distributed medicine until his supply ran out, and "his reputation is mighty in Galilee this day" (045-026). "Dr. B." also diagnosed Twain's cholera as caused by bathing in the Abana River (045-015).

## Character and Role

The Doctor fills a distinct niche within the traveling party. Where Jack (Twain) narrates and Dan provides blunt commentary, the Doctor contributes a quiet, self-possessed deadpan. Twain says he is "always serene, always smooth-voiced" (013-027), contrasting humorously with the genuine frustration of his targets.

The Doctor also serves as a straight man, taking his share of mockery in the kid-glove incident and playing along with the Venice barber prank. His medical profession rarely matters. When it does — treating Syrian villagers — it serves Twain's commentary. The "poor human Doctor" (045-025) becomes a secular echo of the healing miracles that drew crowds to Galilee.
