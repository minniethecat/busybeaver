# Jericho

An ancient biblical city in the Jordan Valley, conquered by Joshua and visited by Mark Twain's excursion party as a waypoint on the journey to the Dead Sea — a site that appears in the narrative both as a literal destination and as the comedic namesake of Twain's famously eccentric horse.

## Overview

Jericho occupies a dual role in *The Innocents Abroad*. It is, first, one of the most historically significant places on the Holy Land itinerary — the city whose walls fell to Joshua's trumpets and upon which a biblical curse still rests. Twain's party, like countless pilgrims before them, made the arduous journey from Jerusalem down through the Judean wilderness to see the ancient ruin and continue onward to the Jordan River and the Dead Sea. The excursion, proposed to shake the party out of creeping lethargy during their Jerusalem stay, promised adventure among the lawless Bedouins and the stark desert landscapes of the Jordan Valley [055-003].

But Jericho also provides one of the book's most sustained comic episodes through an unexpected surrogate: Twain's horse. Early in the overland journey through Syria and Lebanon, Twain acquired a peculiar mare which he named "Jericho," and the creature's absurd behavior — shying at telegraph poles, kicking at flies on its own head, and biting its rider's legs — becomes a running joke that spans multiple chapters [042-008]. By the time the party actually approached the biblical Jericho, the horse named Jericho had already been traded away, leaving only the name to reverberate through the narrative [045-027].

The intersection of these two Jerichos — the biblical ruin and the ridiculous horse — exemplifies Twain's method throughout the travelogue: a willingness to puncture sacred solemnity with everyday absurdity, while still acknowledging, in his more reflective moments, the genuine weight of history and the melancholy of a land laboring under an ancient curse [056-014].

## Arrival and First Impressions

The journey to Jericho was conceived as a remedy for creeping demoralization. After exhausting most of Jerusalem's holy sites, the excursion party had grown lazy — sleeping late, lingering at dinner, and lounging on divans swapping travel gossip. "A diversion must be tried, or demoralization would ensue. The Jordan, Jericho and the Dead Sea were suggested" [055-003]. The proposal ignited immediate enthusiasm, stirring the nomadic instinct that Twain believed civilization had never fully eradicated from the human soul [055-003].

The caravan departed Jerusalem at nine in the morning, but the expedition was immediately shadowed by alarming rumors. Word circulated that lawless Bedouins in the Jordan Valley had defeated a troop of Turkish cavalry, killed several men, and were besieging a Turkish garrison in an old fort near Jericho [055-005]. Another party of excursionists was said to have fled back to Jerusalem under cover of darkness. Faced with these reports, the pilgrims adopted a pose of defiant courage — while quietly maneuvering for position at the rear of the procession [055-006].

The route descended through Bethany, past the traditional house and tomb of Lazarus, then plunged into "a close, flaming, rugged, desolate defile, where no living creature could enjoy life, except, perhaps, a salamander" [055-014]. This was the wilderness where John the Baptist had preached — though Twain drily noted that John "never could have got his locusts and wild honey here" [055-014]. The landscape offered none of the romance that Sunday-school education had promised.

## Description and Notable Features

The approach to Jericho was defined less by the city itself than by the terrain surrounding it — a "dreary, repulsive, horrible solitude" through which the party crept at a pace so slow that "it did seem as if we never would get to Jericho" [055-006]. Twain's notoriously slow horse, and the calamitous state of everyone's suddenly malfunctioning saddles, became a running source of delay and dark comedy. Every rider repeatedly dismounted to make adjustments, and Twain found that no matter how he tried to stay safely in the rear, his horse "was forever turning up in the lead" [055-006].

When the party finally reached the site of ancient Jericho, the reality of the famous ruin proved distinctly underwhelming. Twain's assessment is characteristically blunt:

> "Ancient Jericho is not very picturesque as a ruin. When Joshua marched around it seven times, some three thousand years ago, and blew it down with his trumpet, he did the work so well and so completely that he hardly left enough of the city to cast a shadow." [055-014]

The thoroughness of Joshua's destruction meant there was simply very little left to see. Yet Jericho occupied what Twain acknowledged as "one of the very best locations for a town we have seen in all Palestine" [055-014] — a paradox explained by the enduring power of the biblical curse pronounced against its rebuilding. One king, Twain noted, had made the attempt in defiance of the curse and "was stricken sorely for his presumption" [055-014]. The site, blessed by geography but condemned by scripture, would remain forever unoccupied.

Nearby, the party visited the fountain that the prophet Elisha had sweetened — a spring that, Twain observed with characteristic dryness, "is sweet yet" [055-014]. The presence of this small miracle of potable water in the midst of the desolation provided one of the few comforting notes in an otherwise harsh landscape.

### The Horse Named Jericho

No account of Jericho in *The Innocents Abroad* would be complete without the horse. Acquired in Beirut for the overland journey, the mare named Jericho was, by Twain's account, "the most spirited horse on earth" — if shying at everything in sight could be considered spirit [042-008]. Her peculiarities were lovingly catalogued:

| Quality | Twain's Observation |
|---------|---------------------|
| Shying | "He shies at everything he comes across, with the utmost impartiality" [042-008] |
| Telegraph poles | "He appears to have a mortal dread of telegraph poles, especially" [042-008] |
| Falling symmetry | "I never fall off twice in succession on the same side" [042-008] |
| Haystack courage | "He walked up to that with an intrepidity and a recklessness that were astonishing" [042-008] |
| Tail deficiency | "His tail has been chopped off... he has to fight the flies with his heels" [042-008] |
| Sociability | "He reaches around and bites my legs too" [042-008] |

Twain suspected the Arab owner had gravely misunderstood the animal's character — presenting it as a fiery, untamed steed when in reality it "only looked like he wanted to lean up against something and think" [042-008]. The horse's career with Twain was brief. By the time the party reached Banias, at the sources of the Jordan, "Jericho and I have parted company" [045-027]. Its replacement, named "Baalbec" because "he is such a magnificent ruin," proved an equally unpromising mount — blind, toothless, with one hind leg bending the wrong way and the other "as straight and stiff as a tent-pole" [045-027].

## Historical Significance

Jericho stands as one of the most ancient continuously inhabited sites in the world, and Twain's narrative draws its historical resonance almost entirely from the Old Testament. The city occupies a central place in the conquest narrative of the Book of Joshua. After Moses died on Mount Pisgah and the forty years of desert wandering ended, Joshua assumed command and launched the military campaign that would secure Canaan for the Israelites:

> "Then Joshua began his terrible raid, and from Jericho clear to this Baal-Gad, he swept the land like the Genius of Destruction. He slaughtered the people, laid waste their soil, and razed their cities to the ground. He wasted thirty-one kings also." [042-015]

The geography of Joshua's campaign, which Twain traced from the heights of Lebanon looking down across the valley toward Baalbec (the biblical Baal-Gad), made the scale and ferocity of the conquest vividly real to him. Jericho was the starting point — the first Canaanite city to fall, its walls collapsing not through siege engines but through the ritual circling and trumpet blasts that have made the story one of the most enduring in Western culture.

The curse upon Jericho emerges as a theme that particularly fascinates Twain. The biblical prohibition against rebuilding the city (Joshua 6:26) had, in his telling, proven remarkably durable across three millennia. While other cities had risen and fallen, been rebuilt and renamed, Jericho's "site will always remain unoccupied" [055-014] — a living monument to the power of an ancient malediction. This observation feeds into the larger theme of the Palestinian landscape that Twain develops in his concluding chapters: a land that "sits in sackcloth and ashes" under "the spell of a curse that has withered its fields and fettered its energies" [056-014].

Jericho also occupies a notable cameo in Twain's critique of pilgrim literature. Observing his fellow travelers' tendency to parrot the sentiments of guidebook authors rather than form their own impressions, Twain predicted: "I can almost tell, in set phrase, what they will say when they see Tabor, Nazareth, Jericho and Jerusalem — because I have the books they will 'smouch' their ideas from" [048-024]. The pilgrims' Jericho, he implied, would be William C. Grimes's Jericho, not the real one.

## Daily Life and Local Color

The journey to Jericho brought the excursion party into its closest encounter with the Bedouin presence that haunted the collective imagination of Holy Land travelers. The Arab guards supplied for the expedition — "two gorgeous young Arab sheiks, with cargoes of swords, guns, pistols and daggers on board" [055-006] — were positioned at the front of the column, while reinforcements of "cadaverous Arabs, in shirts and bare legs" were sent ahead "to brandish rusty guns, and shout and brag, and carry on like lunatics" [055-006] and thereby scare off any genuine marauders.

Twain, however, had by this point grown deeply skeptical of the entire Bedouin-threat apparatus. He dryly observed that "on our whole trip we saw no Bedouins, and had no more use for an Arab guard than we could have had for patent leather boots and white kid gloves" [055-006]. The attacks reported by other pilgrim parties, he insinuated, were theatrical performances staged by the guards themselves — the supposed Bedouins and their supposed defenders meeting afterward "in full view of the pilgrims, after the battle, and took lunch, divided the baksheesh extorted in the season of danger, and then accompanied the cavalcade home to the city" [055-006]. The entire Arab guard system, Twain concluded, was "one which is created by the Sheiks and the Bedouins together, for mutual profit" [055-006].

The desert road to Jericho also provided the setting for Twain's comic meditation on cowardice and bravado. When the cry of "Bedouins!" went up, "every man shrunk up and disappeared in his clothes like a mud-turtle" [055-006]. Twain's first impulse was to "dash forward and destroy the Bedouins," his second to "dash to the rear to see if there were any coming in that direction" — and he "acted on the latter impulse. So did all the others" [055-006]. In the safety of retrospect, each pilgrim concocted ever more elaborate fantasies of what he would have done to any Bedouin foolish enough to attack. The wild-eyed pilgrim rhapsodist, when pressed, delivered the most memorable threat: "Eat him!" [055-006].

The overnight portion of the journey was no less miserable. Roused at two in the morning by the dragoman's competitive zeal, the party rode through the chill darkness toward the Jordan, nodding in their saddles and waking with a start to find the procession vanished into the gloom. The whispered order — "Close up — close up! Bedouins lurk here, everywhere!" — sent "an exquisite shudder" shivering along every spine [055-014].

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's verdict on Jericho must be assembled from scattered fragments, for the city itself scarcely detained him. The ruin was too meager, the surroundings too harsh, and the party's attention was fixed on reaching the Jordan and the Dead Sea beyond. Yet Jericho becomes, in the culminating rhetoric of the book, a symbol of Palestine's desolation.

In the penultimate chapter, surveying the entire pilgrimage from the vantage point of Jaffa and the waiting ship, Twain delivers his most famous judgment on the Holy Land. The passage gathers Jericho into a litany of decline:

> "Jericho the accursed, lies a moldering ruin, today, even as Joshua's miracle left it more than three thousand years ago; Bethlehem and Bethany, in their poverty and their humiliation, have nothing about them now to remind one that they once knew the high honor of the Saviour's presence." [056-014]

The image of Jericho "moldering" under an ancient curse encapsulates Twain's central argument about Palestine: that the land has been frozen in a state of divinely ordained decay, its famous places reduced to shadows of their biblical selves. The curse that Joshua pronounced had proven, in Twain's reading, more enduring than any city wall or temple.

Yet the comic counterpoint provided by the horse named Jericho prevents the portrait from becoming merely grim. The mare's absurd behavior — shying at everything except haystacks, kicking at flies on her own head, biting her rider — humanizes the Jericho theme and demonstrates Twain's refusal to remain in a posture of unrelieved reverence. The horse "Jericho" is ridiculous; the city Jericho is melancholy; and somewhere in the gap between them lies Twain's distinctive sensibility, capable of holding both attitudes in suspension without resolving either.

The expedition's actual departure from the Jericho region was marked not by lingering farewells but by exhaustion. After bathing in the Jordan (discovering it was "not any wider than Broadway in New York" [055-014]) and floating in the Dead Sea (finding that "we could not sink" and emerging "coated with salt till we shone like icicles" [055-014]), the party dragged itself through the "hideous afternoon's ride" to the convent of Mars Saba. "Such roasting heat, such oppressive solitude, and such dismal desolation can not surely exist elsewhere on earth," Twain wrote. "And such fatigue!" [055-014].
