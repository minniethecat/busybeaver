# Michelangelo

Italian Renaissance sculptor, painter, architect, and poet, transformed by Mark Twain into a comic symbol of art-fatigue — the man the guides blame for designing every church, palace, and landscape feature in Italy.

## Overview

Michelangelo Buonarroti enters *The Innocents Abroad* not as a solemn subject of art appreciation but as the target of one of Twain's most sustained comic campaigns. In Milan, Twain's party sees drawings by the master at the Ambrosian Library and learns the local pronunciation — "these Italians call him Mickel Angelo" (019-007). In Florence, they "weep over the tombs of Michelangelo, Raphael and Machiavelli" at Santa Croce (024-009). But it is in Rome that Michelangelo becomes an absurd omnipresence, as guides attribute to him the design of everything from St. Peter's to the Coliseum to the Appian Way.

Twain's treatment of Michelangelo is a masterclass in comic exaggeration. He begins by professing genuine admiration: "I used to worship the mighty genius of Michelangelo — that man who was great in poetry, painting, sculpture, architecture — great in everything he undertook" (027-010). This sets up the punchline: the traveler, force-fed Michelangelo at every stop, rebels against the monotony of greatness. The comedy is not at Michelangelo's expense but at the expense of the guidebook culture and the tourist industry that flattens all wonder into a numbing catalog of names.

## In the Travelogue

The Michelangelo joke begins in Milan, where the travelers visit the Ambrosian Library. There, amid manuscripts of Virgil annotated by Petrarch and a letter of Lucrezia Borgia, "we saw some drawings by Michelangelo (these Italians call him Mickel Angelo), and Leonardo da Vinci" (019-007). The tone is already droll — Twain "reserves opinion" of the sketches, a deadpan deflation.

At the Milan Cathedral, Michelangelo's stature as a Renaissance titan is acknowledged straightforwardly: bas-reliefs and statues adorn the vast marble facade, and "Raphael, Michelangelo, Canova — giants like these gave birth to the designs, and their own pupils carved them" (018-010). No joke yet — just the standard guidebook litany.

The comic escalation begins in earnest in Rome. Chapter 27 opens with Twain's mock-confession that he has avoided the cliche "butchered to make a Roman holiday," then pivots: "In this connection I wish to say one word about Michelangelo Buonarotti" (027-010). What follows is the book's most famous rant: "I do not want Michelangelo for breakfast — for luncheon — for dinner — for tea — for supper — for between meals." Every Italian city, Twain complains, credits Michelangelo with its splendors: "In Genoa, he designed everything; in Milan he or his pupils designed everything; he designed the Lake of Como" (027-010). In Florence, they are shown the stone where Michelangelo used to sit and admire his own work. In Rome, the absurdity peaks: "He designed St. Peter's; he designed the Pope; he designed the Pantheon, the uniform of the Pope's soldiers, the Tiber, the Vatican, the Coliseum, the Capitol" — and Dan, a fellow traveler, delivers the comic coup de grace: "Lump the whole thing! say that the Creator made Italy from designs by Michelangelo!" (027-010).

Twain's party devises a revenge on the guides: the "imbecility and idiotic questions" game (027-012). At every monument, Dr. asks "By Michelangelo?" — even before the ancient Roman Forum ("No — thousan' year before he is born," 027-016) and the Egyptian obelisk ("Oh, mon dieu, genteelmen! Zis is two thousan' year before he is born!" 027-019). The guide, worn down by this running joke, "dreads to show us anything at all" (027-020).

In the Capuchin Convent, viewing chapels decorated entirely with human bones, Twain dryly observes that the intricate designs "were by Michelangelo, I think" (028-003), a callback to the omnipresent attribution. Even in reflecting broadly on Italian art, Michelangelo is lumped into the weary traveler's litany alongside Raphael and da Vinci, with the observation that copies always look better than the originals (019-035).

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's stance on Michelangelo is, beneath the comedy, one of genuine respect. He opens his Chapter 27 monologue with an earnest salute: "I used to worship the mighty genius of Michelangelo — that man who was great in poetry, painting, sculpture, architecture — great in everything he undertook" (027-010). The joke is never that Michelangelo was not a genius. It is that the tourist experience, with its relentless guide-led cataloging, turns even the sublime into the monotonous.

The note of relief is comic but also honest: "I never felt so fervently thankful, so soothed, so tranquil, so filled with a blessed peace, as I did yesterday when I learned that Michelangelo was dead" (027-011). Twain means this literally — the man has been dead for three centuries, and learning this fact provides a moment of absurd comfort to the over-stimulated traveler.

As a sightseeing strategy, the Michelangelo joke is effective: "Relief for overtasked eyes and brain from study and sightseeing is necessary, or we shall become idiotic sure enough. Therefore this guide must continue to suffer" (027-020). The comedy is cathartic — a protest against the numbing conventions of the Grand Tour.
