# Infidels and Outcasts

The web of exclusionary labels — "infidel," "Christian dogs," and "Franks" — that Twain and his fellow American excursionists encounter throughout the Ottoman domains, and the ironic reversal they experience: having departed a nation where Christianity is the dominant culture, they suddenly find themselves the despised religious minority, treated as unclean outsiders at every turn.

## Overview

Throughout *The Innocents Abroad*, Twain repeatedly records the terms by which Muslims of the Ottoman world designate Christians. These range from the theological — "infidel" — to the visceral — "Christian dog" — to the geopolitical — "Frank." Taken together, they form a taxonomy of exclusion that structures every encounter between the American pilgrims and the inhabitants of North Africa, Turkey, Syria, and Palestine. For Twain, this experience is at once humiliating and darkly comic: the Americans, who left a country where Protestant Christianity was the default and dominant identity, suddenly find themselves the object of the very religious contempt they are accustomed to seeing directed at others.

The concept runs deeper than mere name-calling. The status of "infidel" carries practical consequences. Mosques are off-limits (008-002). Water sources may be denied (047-021). Food prepared for Christians is considered polluted (044-025). And in Damascus, the hatred is so intense that even beggars refuse Christian alms (044-013). For a people who pride themselves on being the democratic, open-handed representatives of a modern nation, to be treated as ritually unclean is a profound and disorienting shock — one that Twain anatomizes with characteristic irony.

## In Twain's Narrative

The theme appears at the very first African port of call. In Tangier, Twain notes with wonder that Moorish dwellings admit no outsider: "within their sacred walls no Christian dog can enter" (008-002). The phrase is not his own but the reported speech of the Moors — a phrase he finds so striking that he returns to it in the next chapter when Blucher nearly rides his mule into a mosque: "so dire a profanation is it for a Christian dog to set foot upon the sacred threshold of a Moorish mosque that no amount of purification can ever make it fit for the faithful to pray in again" (009-002). The danger is not theoretical: "the time has been, and not many years ago, either, when a Christian would have been most ruthlessly slaughtered if captured in a mosque" (009-002).

The Tangier episode also introduces the grotesque realism that accompanies this status. When a mosque clock breaks, the Moors, lacking a Muslim clockmaker, debate how to proceed. The solution, as a tribal elder proposes, is brutally literal: "send the Christian dog on all fours, and barefoot, into the holy place to mend the clock, and let him go as an ass!" (009-004). The Christian clockmaker enters the mosque stripped of his humanity — reduced to the status of a beast of burden. Twain presents this without explicit commentary, letting the image speak for itself.

As the pilgrimage moves east, the language shifts. In Constantinople, the crowds of the city are described as "the grand mass of the infidel horde" wearing "the fiery red skullcap they call a fez" (033-010). Here Twain, for the first and perhaps only time in the book, turns the term back on its users: the Turks themselves become "the infidel horde" when viewed through Christian eyes. The reversal is fleeting but significant — it acknowledges that "infidel" is a matter of perspective, that the label flows both ways.

The theme darkens in the Holy Land. At Joseph's Pit, the Arab guards "venerated a place made sacred by their ancestor's imprisonment too much to be willing to see Christian dogs drink from it" (047-021). The pilgrims must rig a rope from rags to draw water — a small indignity that stands for the larger pattern. In Damascus, the hostility reaches its peak. Twain observes that the Damascenes "so hate the very sight of a foreign Christian that they want no intercourse whatever with him" (044-013). Women hide their eyes from "our contaminating Christian vision"; merchants scowl silently instead of hawking their wares; and "the beggars actually passed us by without demanding baksheesh" (044-013) — the ultimate proof of fanaticism, since everywhere else in the Ottoman world, beggars would swarm any Westerner.

The Damascus chapter culminates in a historical memory that gives the abstract label its most concrete and horrific meaning. In 1861, a massacre of Christians swept through the city and the surrounding regions: "twenty-five thousand more Christians were massacred and their possessions laid waste" (044-024). The Muslim victors "would not defile their hands by burying the 'infidel dogs'" (044-024), leaving bodies to rot in the streets. The slur is no longer comic. Twain, rarely given to open political advocacy, breaks character: "I never disliked a Chinaman as I do these degraded Turks and Arabs, and when Russia is ready to war with them again, I hope England and France will not find it good breeding or good judgment to interfere" (044-025).

## The Irony of Reversal

The deepest thematic thread in Twain's treatment of religious exclusion is the irony of role reversal. The Americans aboard the *Quaker City* come from a nation where Christianity — specifically Protestant Christianity — is the cultural default. They are accustomed to being the religious majority, the arbiters of what is normal and acceptable. In the Ottoman world, they are the minority, the polluted, the outsiders whose very touch defiles.

Twain does not dwell on this irony explicitly, but it pervades his reportage. When he notes that the Moors of Tangier "do smile upon a Christian in a way which is in the last degree comforting" (008-004), the humor lies in the inversion: an American man, presumably accustomed to bestowing or withholding approval, finds himself grateful for a smile from a "bronzed Moor." When the pilgrims are told that their drinking from a goatskin has polluted it, requiring the water to be "filtered through a rag" (044-025) before a Muslim may drink, the indignity is compounded by the pilgrims' helplessness — there is no appeal to equality or common humanity that carries any weight in this context.

Twain's response to this reversal is not self-pity but a kind of ethnographic candor. He reports what happens, lets the indignities accumulate, and only occasionally lets his anger show. The result is a portrait of what it feels like to be the despised minority — an experience rare for 19th-century Americans and rarer still in American travel writing of the period.

## Cultural and Historical Context

The term "infidel" (from Latin *infidelis*, unfaithful) was the standard Islamic designation for non-Muslims, particularly Christians and Jews, in the Ottoman Empire. While Christians and Jews were recognized as *dhimmis* — "People of the Book" entitled to certain protections — they remained second-class subjects, subject to special taxes, legal disabilities, and social segregation. The designation "Frank" was a legacy of the Crusades, when Western Europeans were collectively known as "Franks" regardless of nationality.

The 1860–1861 massacres Twain references were a real and recent event. Sectarian violence between Druze and Maronite Christians in Mount Lebanon had spread to Damascus, where Muslim mobs killed thousands of Christians over several days in July 1860. The European powers intervened diplomatically, and the Ottoman authorities eventually restored order, but not before an estimated 25,000 Christians had been killed across the region. For Twain, writing in 1869, these events were less than a decade in the past — a living memory that gave the epithet "infidel dogs" its murderous historical weight.

The pilgrims' shock at being treated as religious outsiders also reflects a specifically American innocence. The United States in the 1860s had no significant Muslim population and no colonial presence in the Islamic world. Most of Twain's fellow travelers had likely never experienced religious discrimination of any kind. To go from that world to one where your religion makes you untouchable was, for them, a genuinely novel and disorienting experience.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's treatment of this theme is characteristically layered. On the surface, he mocks the absurdity of it all — the clockmaker crawling on all fours, the narghile mouthpiece "that remained on that brass mouthpiece" with "the taste of a thousand infidel tongues" (034-027), the goatskin water filtered through a rag. The comedy of the reversed situation is never far from the surface.

But beneath the comedy runs a current of genuine anger. The massacre passage in Damascus is the clearest expression: Twain drops the ironic mask and writes as an outraged Western observer, invoking Russia's military power as a hoped-for corrective. This is not Twain's finest moment as a writer — the passage reads more like a newspaper editorial than literature — but it shows how deeply the experience of being a religious outcast affected him.

His most interesting move is the one he makes least often: turning the label back on its users. When he calls the Constantinople crowds "the infidel horde" (033-010), he reminds the reader that "infidel" is a perspective, not a fact. And when he describes the narghile mouthpiece as carrying "the taste of a thousand infidel tongues" (034-027), the word has been so thoroughly reclaimed that it describes Muslims and Christians alike — anyone who has put their lips to the communal pipe. In these moments, Twain gestures toward the relativism that will characterize his later, more mature work.
