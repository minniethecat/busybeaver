# Venice

Italian lagoon city, once the powerful maritime Republic of Venice, visited by the Quaker City excursionists as part of their Italian tour in Chapters 22 through 24.

## Overview

Venice was, for Twain, the site of his most dramatic contrast between romantic expectation and sardonic reality. Arriving by rail from Milan via Verona and Padua, the party entered a city whose name had conjured images of princely cavaliers and singing gondoliers — and immediately found themselves in a "hearse" of a gondola with a "mangy, barefooted guttersnipe" at the oar (022-004). Yet within hours, the moonlight transformed everything, and Venice became "complete" — the city of poetry and romance restored (022-006).

Twain's Venice is a city of dualities: by day, "decayed, forlorn, poverty-stricken, and commerceless"; by moonlight, crowned "once more with the grandeur that was hers five hundred years ago" (022-012). This pattern — initial disappointment yielding to genuine wonder — structures the entire Venetian sojourn. Twain finds himself genuinely moved by the city's melancholy grandeur, even as he continues to mock its pretensions and its guides.

## Arrival and First Impressions

The party reached Venice at eight in the evening, having traveled by rail from Milan. Twain's first encounter with the famed gondola was a brutal deflation of romantic expectations: "And this was the storied gondola of Venice! — the fairy boat in which the princely cavaliers of the olden time were wont to cleave the waters of the moonlit canals" (022-004). The reality was "an inky, rusty old canoe with a sable hearse-body clapped on to the middle of it," and the gondolier a "mangy, barefooted guttersnipe" — when he began to sing, Twain threatened to throw him overboard (022-005).

But the disillusionment proved premature. Sweeping out into the Grand Canal, Twain found the city transformed under "the mellow moonlight": "Right from the water's edge rose long lines of stately palaces of marble; gondolas were gliding swiftly hither and thither... Music came floating over the waters — Venice was complete" (022-006). The arrival coincided with a grand fete honoring a cholera-checking saint, and "all Venice was abroad on the water" (022-007). Twain describes two thousand gondolas adorned with colored lanterns — "like a vast garden of many-colored flowers" (022-007).

## Historical Decline

Twain opens his Venetian account with a sober reflection on the Republic's fall. For fourteen hundred years Venice was "a haughty, invincible, magnificent Republic" whose navies "well nigh held dominion of the seas" and whose "merchant fleets whitened the remotest oceans with their sails" (022-002). Six hundred years ago she was "the Autocrat of Commerce" and the great "distributing-house" of Oriental trade. But Twain finds her "fallen a prey to poverty, neglect and melancholy decay" — her piers deserted, her warehouses empty, her glory departed (022-002).

> "She that in her palmy days commanded the commerce of a hemisphere and made the weal or woe of nations with a beck of her puissant finger, is become the humblest among the peoples of the earth — a peddler of glass beads for women, and trifling toys and trinkets for schoolgirls and children." (022-002)

This elegy is leavened by characteristic Twain humor. He notes that the city "must be a paradise for cripples, for verily a man has no use for legs here" (022-010), and observes that by day the place looks "like an overflowed Arkansas town" (022-011). The Senate's decree requiring all gondolas to be painted black — originally a rebuke to ostentatious plebeians — now gives the city the "color of mourning. Venice mourns" (023-003).

## Government and the Doges

The Ducal Palace provides Twain with material for a sustained meditation on Venetian political terror. The portraits of the Doges lining the Senate Chamber are marred by a single "black square" — the erased portrait of Marino Faliero, the conspirator beheaded five hundred years earlier (022-015). The "terrible Lions' Mouths" at the head of the Giant's Staircase — anonymous accusation boxes whose "heads were gone (knocked off by the French during their occupation of Venice)" — prompt Twain's most detailed account of the Republic's machinery of fear (022-016).

He describes a system of universal surveillance: fifteen hundred Patricians governed the Republic, three hundred Senators were chosen from them, and from the Senators came the Doge and the Council of Ten. The Ten secretly selected a Council of Three — all government spies under surveillance themselves. "Men spoke in whispers in Venice, and no man trusted his neighbor — not always his own brother" (022-016). The Council of Three met masked and robed in scarlet — "a nod to the executioner was sufficient" (022-016). Twain's visit to the Council of Three chamber and the prison, with its "infernal den" frescoes depicting nothing but "pictures of death and dreadful suffering," provides the darkest passages in the Venetian account (022-019).

The Bridge of Sighs itself — "a bridge that is a covered tunnel — you can not be seen when you walk in it" — partitioned light-sentenced prisoners from those doomed to "lingering misery and utter oblivion" in dungeons below water level (022-020). The prison cells, "without light, air, books; naked, unshaven, uncombed, covered with vermin," inspire one of Twain's most somber passages: the prisoner's "useless tongue forgetting its office, with none to speak to" (022-020).

## Art and Churches

The Cathedral of St. Mark, "built entirely of precious marbles, brought from the Orient," receives Twain's characteristic deflation: he "could not go into ecstasies over its coarse mosaics, its unlovely Byzantine architecture, or its five hundred curious interior columns" (022-022). Everything was "worn out — every block of stone was smooth and almost shapeless with the polishing hands and shoulders of loungers" (022-022). Yet the church's hoary traditions and its legend of St. Mark's bones — stolen from Alexandria in vessels of lard to bypass Muslim inspectors — clearly fascinate him. The belief held that if the saint's remains were ever removed, "the ancient city would vanish like a dream, and its foundations be buried forever in the unremembering sea" (022-024).

Among the Venetian churches, Twain singles out Santa Maria dei Frari, where lie the body of Canova and the heart of Titian, who died during a plague that killed fifty thousand — "to him alone the state permitted a public funeral in all that season of terror and death" (023-017). The state archives, housed in the conventual buildings, are noted as containing millions of documents — "The secret history of Venice for a thousand years is here — its plots, its hidden trials, its assassinations" (023-020).

Twain's growing art-fatigue culminates in Venice. He notes that there are "twelve hundred pictures by Palma the Younger in Venice and fifteen hundred by Tintoretto" and concludes with disarming honesty: "to me it seemed that when I had seen one of these martyrs I had seen them all" (023-022). The guide system, however, provides one bright spot: an educated Black guide born in South Carolina who speaks four languages, "dresses better than any of us," and is "daintily polite" — "Negroes are deemed as good as white people, in Venice, and so this man feels no desire to go back to his native land. His judgment is correct" (023-033).

## Daily Life and Local Color

Twain relishes the oddity of Venetian daily existence. "We see little girls and boys go out in gondolas with their nurses, for an airing. We see staid families, with prayerbook and beads, enter the gondola dressed in their Sunday best, and float away to church" (023-011). At midnight, the crowd leaves the theatre, and "we see them separate here and there, and disappear up divergent streets... And over all broods that mysterious stillness, that stealthy quiet, that befits so well this old dreaming Venice" (023-011).

He observes the descendants of the ancient doges "airing their nobility in fashionable French attire in the Grand Square of St. Mark, and eating ices and drinking cheap wines, instead of wearing gallant coats of mail and destroying fleets and armies as their great ancestors did" (023-049). The Bronze Horses of St. Mark's are noted with characteristic quip: "Venice may well cherish them, for they are the only horses she ever had. It is said there are hundreds of people in this curious city who never have seen a living horse in their lives" (023-049).
