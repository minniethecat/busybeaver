# The Oracle

An older passenger aboard the *Quaker City* whom Twain and his companions nickname "the Oracle" — a pompous, well-meaning humbug whose habit of reading guidebooks aloud and mixing up facts makes him one of the book's most memorable comic figures.

## Overview

The Oracle is introduced in Chapter VII as one of "one or two people among us who are sometimes an annoyance" — though Twain quickly clarifies that he does not count the Oracle among that number (007-025). The Oracle is an older man, referred to variously as the Oracle and "the old Doctor" (017-026), who travels with the *Quaker City* excursion through Europe and the Holy Land. He is not to be confused with the other Doctor aboard — a younger physician who is part of Twain's intimate circle and a very different comic presence.

The Oracle represents one of Twain's sharpest satires on the problem of secondhand knowledge. He reads a chapter in the guidebook, mixes the facts up with his bad memory, and then presents the resulting confusion as original wisdom (007-025). What makes him comic rather than merely annoying is his serene self-confidence: he genuinely believes in his own authority, even when inventing authors and spouting scientific nonsense. Twain treats him with a kind of affectionate exasperation that suggests he finds the Oracle more amusing than contemptible.

## In the Travelogue

The Oracle makes his formal debut in Chapter VII, off the coast of Gibraltar. Twain describes him as "an innocent old ass who eats for four and looks wiser than the whole Academy of France would have any right to look, and never uses a one-syllable word when he can think of a longer one, and never by any possible chance knows the meaning of any long word he uses or ever gets it in the right place" (007-025). Despite this, the Oracle "will serenely venture an opinion on the most abstruse subject and back it up complacently with quotations from authors who never existed" (007-025). When cornered, he will slide to the other side of the argument, claim he had been there all along, and then deploy his opponent's own arguments back at them — "only with the big words all tangled" (007-025).

Twain illustrates the Oracle's methods with a specific anecdote: at breakfast, the Oracle points out the window at the African coast and declares a hill to be one of the "Pillows of Herkewls" with "the ultimate one alongside of it" (007-025). When corrected that the Pillars of Hercules are not both on the same side of the strait, the Oracle invents a catalogue of nonexistent authorities: "Old Gibbons don't say nothing about it — just shirks it complete — Gibbons always done that when he got stuck — but there is Rolampton, what does he say? Why, he says that they was both on the same side, and Trinculian, and Sobaster, and Syraccus, and Langomarganbl—" (007-025). Twain's narrator capitulates: "Oh, that will do — that's enough. If you have got your hand in for inventing authors and testimony, I have nothing more to say" (007-025).

Despite his absurdity, Twain admits: "We don't mind the Oracle. We rather like him. We can tolerate the Oracle very easily" (007-030). It is the Poet Lariat and the "Interrogation Point" — a naive young passenger who believes everything he is told — who truly distress the company.

In Chapter X, the Oracle appears again during a Mediterranean sunset. Twain describes "that serene, that inspired, that overpowering humbug" scorning the dinner gong to admire the view (010-003). The Oracle then delivers a magnificently nonsensical opinion:

> "Well, that's gorgis, ain't it! They don't have none of them things in our parts, *do* they? I consider that them effects is on account of the superior refragability, as you may say, of the sun's diramic combination with the lymphatic forces of the perihelion of Jubiter. What should you think?" (010-003)

Dan tells him to go to bed, Jack tells him not to bother with "dictionary bosh," and the Poet Lariat replies with a "barbarous rhyme" before going below (010-003, 010-008). Undeterred, the Oracle declares that "them fellows have all tackled the old Oracle, as they say, but the old man's most too many for 'em" (010-008). He then launches into a complaint about poets, invoking another set of invented authorities — "Gibbons, and Hippocratus, and Sarcophagus, and all them old ancient philosophers" — before Twain himself cuts him off: "Doctor, you are going to invent authorities now and I'll leave you, too" (010-008).

In Genoa, Twain notes that they are not tired of the "Asti" wines, "which that old Doctor (whom we call the Oracle), with customary felicity in the matter of getting everything wrong, misterms 'nasty'" (017-026). This is one more example of the Oracle's defining trait: his reliable unreliability.

Chapter XXXII provides the Oracle's most famous blunder. Passing through the Straits of Messina at two in the morning, the Oracle steps out on deck "with his eternal spyglass" to see Scylla and Charybdis (032-004). When asked why he cares about this location, he declares: "I wish to see *all* the places that's mentioned in the Bible" (032-004). Informed that Scylla and Charybdis is not a biblical reference, he asks what place it is, and upon being told, exclaims: "Scylla and Cha — confound it, I thought it was Sodom and Gomorrah!" (032-004). He closes his spyglass and goes below.

Twain adds a wry postscript: "The above is the ship story. Its plausibility is marred a little by the fact that the Oracle was not a biblical student, and did not spend much of his time instructing himself about Scriptural localities" (032-011). He also notes that the Oracle lately complains that "the only beverage in the ship that is passable, is the butter" — giving him "the credit of getting one long word in the right place, anyhow, for once in his life" (032-011). In Rome, Twain reports that the Oracle said "the Pope was a noble-looking old man, but he never *did* think much of his Iliad" (032-011).

## Character and Role

The Oracle embodies Twain's critique of guidebook-dependent tourism. Every passenger aboard the *Quaker City* is, to some degree, reliant on secondhand knowledge — guidebooks, legends, received opinions. The Oracle takes this dependence to its absurd extreme: he reads, misremembers, and then presents the garbled result as original authority. His comic function is to exaggerate a universal tourist failing into visibility.

The Oracle is also a study in verbal pretension. His love of long words — "refragability," "diramic," "lymphatic forces of the perihelion of Jubiter" — is Twain's parody of pseudoscientific jargon, the kind of language that sounds impressive but means nothing (010-003). Yet significantly, the Oracle deploys these words not with cynical intent but with genuine self-satisfaction. He believes in his own learning, and this innocence is what redeems him from being merely obnoxious.

The nickname "Oracle" is itself satirical. An oracle speaks divine truth; this Oracle reliably gets everything wrong. But there is a hint of affection in the nickname too — the passengers have created a shared joke around him, and shared jokes are a form of community. As Twain says: "We don't mind the Oracle. We rather like him" (007-030).
