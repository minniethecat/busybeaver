# Damascus

Ancient Syrian capital, claimed by tradition to be the oldest continuously inhabited city in the world, visited by the Quaker City excursionists in Chapters 44 through 45 — a city Twain declared more beautiful from its mountain approach than any he had ever seen, and whose associations with St. Paul, the Garden of Eden, and the River Abana made it the crowning jewel of the eastern tour.

## Overview

Damascus was, for Twain, the great surprise of the Holy Land excursion. After the disappointments of Jerusalem — the squalor, the commercialized piety, the oppressive weight of biblical expectation — Damascus offered something unexpected: sheer beauty. The approach from the heights, the vast green garden in which the city lay embedded, the river that gave it life — all combined to produce a response of unforced admiration unique in the travelogue.

Twain's Damascus is a city defined by superlatives. It is the "oldest city on Earth" (044-001), predating even Abraham. The garden surrounding it was, by local tradition, the Garden of Eden. The approach from the mountain was one of the great panoramic prospects of the world. And the city's beauty at sunset, watched from Muhammad's legendary lookout perch, moved Twain to lyricism. He called it "the Pearl of the East" and meant it (044-008).

Yet even here, Twain's comic instincts did not desert him. The Damascus streetcars were an abomination. The city's confidence in its own uniqueness was absurd. And the bout of cholera that confined him to his room for twenty-four hours — contracted, he suspected, from the River Abana — became the occasion for one of his most characteristic blends of suffering and humor.

## Arrival and First Impressions

The journey to Damascus was itself an ordeal. "It was a three days' journey to Damascus. It was necessary that we should travel with haste" (043-011). The party had been "prostrated with the hard rides from Baalbec to Damascus" (045-028), and "Dan's horse and Jack's" had been pushed to their limits. But the approach compensated for everything.

"A little after six o'clock in the morning we were in the saddle, riding toward Damascus" (044-002). The climb to the overlook point was deliberate: Twain wanted to see the city as the Prophet Muhammad was said to have seen it. "Muhammad's lookout perch, over Damascus" provided the vantage — and the view stopped him cold (044-003). "Damascus is beautiful from the mountain. It is beautiful enough to make the Arab admirer break out into poetry, and the traveler into description" (044-005).

He described the city as "an immense garden" with the city at its center, the whole embedded in the desert "like an emerald set in brass" (044-005). The Abana River, descending from the Anti-Lebanon mountains, fed the orchards and fountains that made the city possible. Twain was so moved that he declared: "If I were to go to Damascus again, I would camp on Muhammad's hill about a week, and then go away without ever having touched the city" (044-006) — the view was better than the reality.

## Description and Notable Features

### The World's Oldest City

Twain embraced the local claim that Damascus was the oldest continuously inhabited city on Earth. "Damascus dates back anterior to the days of Abraham, and is the oldest city in the world" (044-009). The assertion, which he attributes to tradition rather than archaeology, becomes the foundation for a remarkable temporal panorama. Damascus had seen "the foundations of Baalbec, and Thebes, and Ephesus laid" (044-009); it had watched empires rise and fall. "She saw Rome built; she saw it overshadow the world with its power; she saw it wither and pass away" (044-009).

The city's antiquity was not merely a matter of dates but of accumulated association. Abraham himself had passed through Damascus — "when the Pyramids of Egypt were young, Damascus a village, Abraham a prattling infant" (024-019). The temporal perspective, compressing millennia into a single sentence, was one of Twain's most effective rhetorical devices.

### The Conversion of St. Paul

No biblical association was more vivid in Damascus than the conversion of St. Paul. "As he journeyed, he came near Damascus, and suddenly there shined round about him a light from heaven" (044-016). The story, from Acts 9, was fundamental to Christian history — and Damascus was its stage.

Twain traced the geography of the conversion with unusual care. "Three or four hours out from Damascus we passed the spot where Saul fell from his horse" (045-008) (though he acknowledged the horse was not in the biblical account). The house of Ananias, "a citizen of Damascus, named Ananias," to whom the Lord said "Arise, and go into the street which is called Straight" (044-019), was still pointed out by guides. And the wall from which the disciples "let Paul down over the Damascus wall at dead of night — for he had to escape and flee to Jerusalem" (044-023) was among the city's most prized biblical sites.

Twain recorded the traditions without the corrosive skepticism he had applied to European relics. Perhaps the physical beauty of Damascus predisposed him to generosity; perhaps the biblical associations, so intimately tied to the landscape, felt more authentic than bones in crystal reliquaries.

### The Rivers of Damascus

The Abana — "River of Damascus" — was the city's lifeblood. Twain believed it was this river "that gave me the cholera" (045-015), but the infection did not diminish his appreciation. The contrast with the dusty Holy Land was explicit: Damascus had water in abundance, and water was life. The gardens, the fountains, the orchards — all depended on the river that descended from the mountains.

Naaman the Syrian, the biblical leper healed by Elisha, had famously preferred the rivers of Damascus to the Jordan: "Are not Abana and Pharpar, rivers of Damascus, better than all the waters of Israel?" Twain cited "Naaman's ancient dwelling in Damascus" (044-027) approvingly, sharing Naaman's evident preference for Damascus water.

### The Great Mosque and Christian Massacre Site

The Umayyad Mosque — the Great Mosque of Damascus — was the city's most important Islamic monument. But Twain's visit was shadowed by recent history. The mosque stood near the site where "Christians who were massacred in Damascus in 1861 by the Turks" had died (044-024). The massacre, which had occurred only six years before Twain's visit, had killed thousands and drawn international condemnation. Twain's reference is brief but pointed — a reminder that the "Pearl of the East" had its dark currents.

## Historical Significance

Damascus was, for Twain, the great counter-example to Jerusalem. Where Jerusalem was small and squalid, Damascus was vast and beautiful. Where Jerusalem's sacredness was claustrophobic, Damascus wore its antiquity lightly. The city's historical significance lay not in any single event but in its sheer duration — the fact that it had been continuously inhabited since before recorded history.

The biblical associations were rich. Abraham had been here. Saul became Paul here. The "caravan route from Damascus to Jerusalem and Egypt" (049-014) was the great highway of the biblical world. The "caravans" that passed through Damascus connected it to Baghdad, Mecca, and the Mediterranean ports. Twain noted that the city "had an order from Constantinople" to look out for the American party (041-002), and that "at Constantinople everybody fell to telegraphing" to Damascus to arrange dragomen (041-005) — the city was part of an Ottoman administrative network that stretched from the Bosporus to the Holy Land.

## Daily Life and Local Color

The Damascus streetcars provided Twain with one of his most memorable comic set-pieces. "I do not like riding in the Damascus streetcars" (044-014) — the understatement was characteristic. The cars were crowded, the horses were ill-tempered, the drivers were reckless, and the whole experience was a comic ordeal. The streetcars became a symbol of modernity's incursion into the ancient city — and a target for Twain's deflationary humor.

The bazaar culture of Damascus was, by Twain's account, even more aggressive than Constantinople's. "At night, for baksheesh, except Damascus" — the city was notorious even among the famously grasping towns of the Levant (044-010). "Damascus, with its four thousand years of experience in fleecing travelers," had perfected the art (044-010). Twain notes that his party attracted "a vast procession of men and boys" who expected tips for simply walking alongside them.

The donkeys and horses of the Damascus journey provided material for extended comedy. The "gallop, as is the fashion at Damascus" (057-007), was a reckless style of riding that Twain considered suicidal. The contrast between the sublime approach and the comic reality of the city — the streetcars, the beggars, the galloping donkeys — was Damascus in miniature.

Yet the city's self-regard amused him most: "In Damascus they think there are no such people as Damascus people in all the world" (044-026). The civic pride of Damascenes, their conviction that their city was uniquely blessed, was both absurd and, in view of the city's genuine beauty, partly justified.

## Twain's Verdict

Damascus received Twain's most affectionate endorsement of any city in the travelogue. "We shall remember... Damascus, the 'Pearl of the East,' the oldest city on earth, the one city in all the world, perhaps, where the Turkish traveler is safe from extortion" (062-007). The praise — "the Pearl of the East" — was not ironic. Twain meant it.

The view from the mountain, the gardens, the rivers, the biblical associations, and (paradoxically) even the comic ordeals of the streetcars and the baksheesh — all contributed to a portrait of a city that had won Twain's heart. The cholera, too, became part of the story, a badge of authentic experience in a place that rewarded the traveler's suffering with moments of transcendent beauty.

> "If I were to go to Damascus again, I would camp on Muhammad's hill about a week, and then go away without ever having touched the city." (044-006)
