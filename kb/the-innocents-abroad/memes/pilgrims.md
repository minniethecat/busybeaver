# Pilgrims

The self-adopted designation of the American excursionists aboard the *Quaker City* — a term Twain deploys with layered irony throughout the travelogue, using it to mock his fellow travelers' pretensions, to celebrate their democratic energy, and to reflect on what it means to be an American abroad in the 1860s.

## Overview

From the opening chapters, the passengers of the *Quaker City* are referred to as "pilgrims" — a label that simultaneously evokes the religious pilgrims of the Middle Ages and the commercial tourists of the Gilded Age. The term is Twain's masterstroke: it dignifies the journey as a sacred undertaking while slyly undercutting that dignity with every mention of the pilgrims' very American obsession with schedules, bargains, and creature comforts.

The pilgrims are not a monolithic group. Twain distinguishes among the "true" pilgrims — the devout Protestants for whom the Holy Land is a genuine spiritual destination — and the sightseers, adventurers, and skeptics who make up the majority. Yet the collective noun sticks, and through it Twain builds one of the book's fundamental comic tensions: the gap between the pilgrimage ideal and the tourist reality.

## In Twain's Narrative

The pilgrims are introduced early in the voyage. In Chapter 4, Twain describes them "becoming domesticated" (004-001) aboard ship, observing that the strict schedule of bells and meals has tamed them: "longer half-past six to these pilgrims from New England, the South, and the Mississippi Valley" (004-002). The diversity of origins underscores their Americanness — they are a cross-section of the reunited nation, now projected onto the Old World.

Twain wryly notes that "I doubt if there is a single pilgrim of all that host but can show a hundred minor bruises received on the trip" (004-006), establishing the pilgrims as simultaneously resilient and perpetually aggrieved. Their relationship to charity becomes an early satirical target: "the executive officer said the pilgrims had no charity" (004-033).

Speaking collectively of them, Twain writes that "somehow our pilgrims suggest that expression" — "a tribe" (007-024), capturing both their insularity and their primitive energy as they descend upon the ancient ports. Yet he also singles out individuals: "Speaking of our pilgrims reminds me that we have one on board who is an excellent bird" (007-025) — a term of amused affection.

The pilgrim identity intensifies as the ship approaches the Holy Land. In Chapter 41, Twain describes "angry pilgrims" and the "shrill note of preparation" as the party readies for the overland journey (041-001). The chapter heading captures the emotional state: "Vandalism prohibited—Angry pilgrims—Approaching Holy Land!" He then delivers his most direct reflection:

> "It was a glorious privilege to be a pilgrim to the Holy Land" (041-016)

The statement reads as sincere until one recalls the pages of complaints, bargaining, and humbug that surround it. This is Twain's method: affirm the ideal while documenting the reality.

The Holy Land chapters intensify the pilgrims' role. Twain describes their enthusiasm at the Sea of Galilee (047-001), their competitive "pilgrimizing" at sacred sites, and their eventual disillusionment. He writes: "How the pilgrims abused each other! Each said..." (047-032), capturing the fractiousness that genuine pilgrimage provokes.

## The Pilgrim as American Archetype

Twain's pilgrims embody distinctly American traits projected onto the ancient world. They are democratic — "the pilgrims took down portions of the front" of an ancient house as souvenirs (048-004), treating antiquity as a buffet. They are practical — measuring sacred sites against American standards of comfort. And they are incorrigibly optimistic — "the incorrigible pilgrims have come in with their pockets full of specimens" (045-016), collecting the Holy Land piece by piece like a geological survey.

Twain's own position is complex. He is both pilgrim and anti-pilgrim, participant and observer, American and cosmopolitan. When he writes "I will say here, in plain terms, that if I have seemed to be ill-natured when I talk about our pilgrims" (047-034), he is offering a quasi-apology that itself performs the pilgrim's self-consciousness.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward the pilgrims oscillates between genuine affection, satirical distance, and moments of shared awe. His characteristic move is to mock the pilgrims' behavior while defending them against European condescension:

> "graceless ladies and uncouth pilgrims, by rude travesties upon water-colors" (038-006)

The European critics are themselves ridiculous; the pilgrims' gracelessness is at least honest. This double-edged defense — bad but authentic — recurs throughout the book and becomes Twain's signature stance: the democratic vulgarity of Americans is preferable to European pretense.

He is also willing to concede when pilgrim enthusiasm is legitimate. At Galilee: "the pilgrim enthusiasts of our party... the eager footsteps of the pilgrims... stood upon the shore" (047-023–047-024) — the prose here drops its irony and records genuine emotion.

Ultimately, the pilgrim is Twain's most successful comic invention: a figure who encompasses the contradictions of American identity in the Gilded Age, at once sacred and profane, earnest and ridiculous, and always, unmistakably, from somewhere west of the Atlantic.
