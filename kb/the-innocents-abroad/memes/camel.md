# Camels

Camels are a recurring motif in *The Innocents Abroad*, serving simultaneously as essential transportation in the Holy Land, an iconic marker of the "Oriental" exoticism Twain's readers expected, and the target of the author's characteristic comic exaggeration.

## Overview

Throughout the Eastern chapters of the travelogue, camel trains appear as one of the most persistent and visually striking elements of Twain's Eastern landscape. From the decks of the *Quaker City* steaming through the Dardanelles (033-006) to the narrow bazaars of Smyrna (038-019) and the storied plains of the Holy Land (045-006), camels are ever-present: laden with cargo, striding in single file, or kneeling at ancient wells. Twain deploys the camel as both a genuine ethnographic observation and a comic prop, using its imposing size, peculiar appearance, and famously intractable demeanor to generate humor and to deflate the romantic expectations of armchair travelers.

## In Twain's Narrative

Twain's first distant sighting of camels comes from the ship, where passengers glimpse "a train of camels" on the shores of the Dardanelles through field glasses (033-006). But his first close encounter occurs at Smyrna, where he describes camels as "very much larger than the scrawny specimens one sees in the menagerie" and paints a vivid picture of a camel train laden with spices marching through the narrow alleys of the bazaar among "porters with their burdens, money-changers, lamp-merchants" and "portly cross-legged Turks" -- a scene he calls "a genuine revelation of the Orient" (038-019).

In the Syrian countryside, camel trains become so frequent that Twain pauses to define the animal for his readers in characteristically humorous terms: "When he is down on all his knees, flat on his breast to receive his load, he looks something like a goose swimming; and when he is upright he looks like an ostrich with an extra set of legs" (042-007). He further notes their "immense, flat, forked cushions of feet, that make a track in the dust like a pie with a slice cut out of it" and observes that their diet is so indiscriminate that "they would eat a tombstone if they could bite it" (042-007).

The camel becomes a physical obstacle on the narrow trail to Nazareth, where Twain's party meets "all the camel trains and jackass caravans between Jericho and Jacksonville in that particular place and nowhere else" (050-003). He describes the camel as a creature that "would not turn out for a king," stalking serenely along "bringing his cushioned stilts forward with the long, regular swing of a pendulum" (050-003). The comic highlight of this passage is the camel bite: "A camel did this for one of the boys, who was drooping over his saddle in a brown study. He glanced up and saw the majestic apparition hovering above him, and made frantic efforts to get out of the way, but the camel reached out and bit him on the shoulder before he accomplished it" (050-003).

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, the camel was an indispensable beast of burden across the Ottoman Empire and the Holy Land. Camel caravans transported goods between Damascus, Jerusalem, Cairo, and the Arabian interior. For Twain's American audience, who had never traveled to the Middle East, camels belonged firmly to the realm of Bible illustrations and *The Arabian Nights* -- creatures of romance and Scripture rather than everyday reality. Twain exploits this gap between expectation and experience throughout his narrative.

The camel was also freighted with biblical associations. Twain notes the Midianite invasion described in the Book of Judges, where "both they and their camels were without number, as the sand by the seaside for multitude" (051-030). At the Temple Mount in Jerusalem, Twain reflects that the "precious remains of Solomon's Temple" call up pictures of "camels laden with spices and treasure -- beautiful slaves, presents for Solomon's harem" (054-018), connecting camels to the popular image of Solomon and the Queen of Sheba. Even John the Baptist's ascetic costume of "camel's hair about his loins" earns a passing reference (055-008).

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's treatment of camels oscillates between genuine wonder and relentless mockery. On the one hand, he acknowledges the camel train as an authentic element of the Eastern tableau: "The same long trains of stately camels go and come -- the same impressive religious solemnity and silence rest upon the desert" (045-006). On the other, he cannot resist puncturing the romantic picture. After describing a well scene that looked like the "grand Oriental picture which I had worshiped a thousand times in soft, rich steel engravings," he inventories its actual miseries, including the "stench of camels" and the suggestion that "a couple of tons of powder placed under the party and touched off would heighten the effect" (051-020).

His most devastating verdict follows shortly after: "I cannot be imposed upon any more by that picture of the Queen of Sheba visiting Solomon. I shall say to myself, You look fine, Madam but your feet are not clean and you smell like a camel" (051-021). The camel thus becomes a vehicle for Twain's larger project of demystifying Orientalist fantasy through firsthand comic reportage.

Even the camel's celebrated speed is treated with amused skepticism. During a camel-versus-horse race outside Baalbec, the camel "scampered by them without any very great effort" (043-004), which Twain presents as a surprise to the American horsemen. And from Muhammad's hill overlooking Damascus, the caravan of camels below appears merely as "creeping mites" on a yellow desert (044-006).
