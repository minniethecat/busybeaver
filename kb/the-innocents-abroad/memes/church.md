# Church

The Catholic Church as an institutional presence, architectural landmark, and cultural force encountered by Mark Twain throughout his European and Holy Land pilgrimage — a ubiquitous tourist destination, custodian of relics, and frequent target of his anti-Catholic satire.

## Overview

In *The Innocents Abroad*, the church — both as a physical building and as an institutional power — is one of the most pervasive elements of Twain's European experience. From Genoa to Jerusalem, the excursionists spend an extraordinary portion of their itinerary visiting churches, cathedrals, and chapels, absorbing their architecture, their art, and their relics. Twain himself remarks on the sheer density of ecclesiastical structures with characteristic understatement.

The church serves multiple functions in the narrative. As a building, it is the primary tourist attraction of every European city — the repository of paintings, sculptures, frescoes, and architectural marvels that constitute the Old World's cultural inheritance. As an institution, it is the custodian of the relics and legends that Twain treats with alternating amusement and contempt. And as a cultural force, it represents everything that Twain's Protestant American sensibility finds alien: priestcraft, superstition, and the commercialization of the sacred.

Yet Twain's treatment of the church is not simply hostile. He is capable of genuine awe before ecclesiastical architecture — the Milan Cathedral leaves him reaching for superlatives, and St. Peter's so overpowers him that he declines to describe it at all. The tension between his satirical skepticism and his capacity for wonder gives his church commentary its distinctive texture.

## Role in the Travelogue

The church dominates the European portion of the pilgrimage. Twain observes that "the people in these old lands seem to make churches their specialty," and notes with bemusement that "I think there is a church every three or four hundred yards all over town" in Genoa. The streets are "sprinkled from end to end with shovel-hatted, long-robed, well-fed priests, and the church bells by dozens are pealing all the day long" (017-013).

This ecclesiastical saturation produces an itinerary that is heavily weighted toward church visits. From Notre Dame in Paris and St. Denis to the cathedrals of Genoa, Milan, and Venice, the pilgrims move from one church to the next. Twain catalogs these visits with a mixture of genuine interest and growing fatigue. "I have not been to church so often in a long time as I have in the last few weeks," he confesses (017-013).

The churches function as museums before museums existed in the modern sense. They house the art that travelers come to see — paintings by Rubens, Titian, Guido, and Paul Veronese (017-011) — and the architecture that defines the European cityscape. In Milan, the Cathedral becomes the obsessive focus of Twain's descriptive powers:

> "Howsoever you look at the great cathedral, it is noble, it is beautiful! Wherever you stand in Milan, or within seven miles of Milan, it is visible — and when it is visible, no other object can chain your whole attention." (018-009)

The Cathedral of San Lorenzo in Genoa is described as "vast, and has colonnades of noble pillars, and a great organ, and the customary pomp of gilded moldings, pictures, frescoed ceilings" (017-014). The Church of the Annunciation, built by a single family, is "a wilderness of beautiful columns, of statues, gilded moldings, and pictures almost countless" (017-018).

The churches are also the primary distribution points for the relics that Twain finds so absurd. "We find a piece of the true cross in every old church we go into, and some of the nails that held it together" (017-017). The sheer repetition of identical relics across different churches becomes one of his chief satirical themes.

In Rome, St. Peter's overwhelms him to the point of descriptive surrender. After detailing its dimensions — it covers five acres, the dome is 438 feet high, the interior height is 438 feet — he concludes: "I prefer not to describe St. Peter's. It has been done before" (026-013). This rare restraint testifies to the building's genuinely intimidating scale.

In the Holy Land, the Church of the Holy Sepulchre presents the most complex case. Built over the sites of the Crucifixion and the tomb of Christ, it is simultaneously the most sacred Christian site in the world and, in Twain's view, a monument to monkish fraud. "With all its claptrap sideshows and unseemly impostures of every kind, it is still grand, revered, venerable — for a god died there" (053-035).

## Historical Context

In the 1860s, the Catholic Church was still the dominant institutional presence in Italy and much of southern Europe. The Pope ruled the Papal States as a temporal monarch, and the church's influence permeated every aspect of social and cultural life. The unification of Italy, which would strip the Papacy of its territorial possessions, was underway but incomplete when Twain visited in 1867.

For American Protestant visitors like Twain and the Quaker City pilgrims, the Catholic Church represented a foreign and faintly suspect institution. The Protestant Reformation had defined American religious identity in opposition to Rome, and 19th-century anti-Catholic sentiment remained strong. The spectacle of ornate cathedrals, robed priests, and relic veneration struck American visitors as both fascinating and vaguely idolatrous.

The physical churches themselves represented the accumulated wealth of centuries. Twain is keenly aware of this economic dimension. He observes that "there are thousands of churches in Italy, each with untold millions of treasure stowed away in its vaults" and contrasts this with the poverty visible in the streets (025-006). He further notes that "all the churches in an ordinary American city put together could hardly buy the jeweled frippery in one of her hundred cathedrals," comparing the ecclesiastical wealth of Italy unfavorably with the practical modesty of American Protestant worship (025-010).

This economic critique reaches its sharpest formulation in his observation about the church's relationship with wealth: "the fathers of the church waxed fat upon his bounty" (021-023). The Catholic Church, in Twain's rendering, is an institution that has perfected the art of extracting wealth from the faithful while offering dubious spiritual returns.

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's commentary on the church operates on a spectrum from genuine admiration to withering satire. The architecture can move him — the Milan Cathedral is "noble," "beautiful," and "a vision! — a miracle!" (018-009, 018-005). St. Peter's is so immense that it breaks his descriptive machinery. The interior of the Milan Cathedral, with "its long rows of fluted columns, like huge masses of alabaster," earns unironic praise (018-013).

Yet the satire is never far behind. The relic economy — "a piece of the true cross in every old church," enough nails for "a keg" (017-017) — exposes the institutional credulity that Twain finds absurd. The "fathers of the church waxed fat" (021-023) on the donations of the faithful, and the accumulated wealth locked in church treasuries stands in stark contrast to the poverty of the Italian people. Twain poses a pointed question:

> "What is this church, compared with the needs of the people? All the churches in an ordinary American city put together could hardly buy the jeweled frippery in one of her hundred cathedrals." (025-010)

He extends this critique to the church's ceremonial extravagance. The bejeweled corpse of St. Charles Borromeo in the crypt of Milan Cathedral — "robed in costly habiliments covered with gold embroidery and starred with scintillating gems" — prompts one of the book's most somber reflections on the vanity of ecclesiastical display in the presence of death (018-020).

Twain also deploys the church as a measure of cultural difference between Old World and New. The "congregational singing at church and prayers was not of a superior order" at the Protestant services aboard the Quaker City (004-028), but this modest American worship is implicitly contrasted with the "magnificence and misery" (025-010) of Italian Catholicism. For Twain, the simplicity of American Protestantism is not merely a religious preference but a civilizational virtue.

In the Holy Land, the Greek and Latin churches appear less as objects of satire and more as elements of a shared Christian inheritance that has decayed into competitive fraud. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre, divided among rival denominations, becomes a stage for "black-robed, animal-looking Italian monks, with candles in their hands, who were chanting something in Latin" while "they perform everywhere — all over the vast building, and at all hours" (053-010). The ancient church at Smyrna, one of the seven apocalyptic churches of Revelation, is "an ancient tumbledown ruin of a church" — the mournful wreck of a once-great Christian community (019-031).
