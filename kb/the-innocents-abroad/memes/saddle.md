# Saddles and Riding

The experience of riding horses, donkeys, and mules through the Holy Land and the Near East -- a persistent physical ordeal that Twain transforms into a comic leitmotif exposing the gap between romantic expectation and painful reality.

## Overview

Throughout *The Innocents Abroad*, the act of riding serves as both literal transportation and a satirical device. Twain and his fellow pilgrims spend countless hours mounted on animals of spectacularly poor quality, fitted with saddles that are variously described as "peculiar," "shapeless," and "ragged spoons." The saddle becomes a metonym for the entire Eastern travel experience: it promises adventure but delivers only chafing, exhaustion, and bitter comedy.

The riding motif runs from the pilgrims' first comic encounter with donkeys in the Azores to their final mad dash on Egyptian donkeys near the Pyramids. In between, Twain catalogs the miseries of Syrian saddles, the villainy of the horses, and the physical toll of riding from dawn until midnight across the punishing terrain of Palestine. The saddle, in Twain's hands, is the place where the romance of Oriental travel collides most painfully with its reality.

## In Twain's Narrative

The pilgrims' first experience with Eastern riding comes in the Azores, where they mount "a squad of little donkeys ready saddled for use" with "a sort of sawbuck with a small mattress on it" (006-008). The result is a chaotic stampede through the streets of Horta, with Blucher repeatedly thrown from his donkey and a pile-up that leaves the cavalcade in a heap (006-011). Twain nonetheless concedes it was "a fresh, new, exhilarating sensation, this donkey riding, and worth a hundred worn and threadbare home pleasures" (006-012).

By the time the expedition reaches Syria, the novelty has worn thin. Preparing for the Holy Land, the pilgrims engage in "a critical inspection of saddles and bridles that had never yet touched horses" (041-003). The horses themselves, when revealed, are "the hardest lot I ever did come across" -- one-eyed, rabbittailed, limping, with sore backs (041-009). Twain selects his horse, characteristically, "because I thought I saw him shy, and I thought that a horse that had spirit enough to shy was not to be despised" (041-011).

The donkey expedition to Ephesus proves equally disastrous. The saddles are "made very high in order that the rider's feet might not drag the ground," but "there were no bridles -- nothing but a single rope, tied to the bit. It was purely ornamental, for the donkey cared nothing for it" (040-004). The ladies ride astride "because they could not stay on the shapeless saddles sidewise," and the scene descends into farce as "every now and then a broad umbrella would suddenly go down out of the cavalcade, announcing to all that one more pilgrim had bitten the dust" (040-005).

## Physical Toll and Comic Exaggeration

Twain's most vivid treatment of riding as physical torment comes during the forced march to Damascus. The pilgrims insist on pressing ahead to honor the Sabbath, and Twain bitterly records riding "in a ragged spoon of a saddle that slips fore-and-aft, and 'thort-ships,' and every way, and on a horse that is tired and lame, and yet must be whipped and spurred with hardly a moment's cessation all day long" (043-015). He notes that they had been "in the saddle nineteen hours, and the horses were cruelly tired" on another occasion (052-013).

The comic high point of the riding motif may be the pilgrims' dash toward the Dead Sea, when rumors of Bedouin attacks send everyone scrambling for the rear of the procession. Twain writes: "I had a notoriously slow horse, but somehow I could not keep him in the rear, to save my neck. He was forever turning up in the lead. In such cases I trembled a little, and got down to fix my saddle. But it was not of any use. The others all got down to fix their saddles, too. I never saw such a time with saddles" (055-006). The universal saddle-fixing becomes a transparently comic pretense for cowardice.

At the journey's midpoint, Twain pauses to reflect: "In the saddle -- abroad on the plains -- sleeping in beds bounded only by the horizon: fancy was at work with these things in a moment" (055-003). Even here, the romance is undercut by knowing irony, for the reality of saddle life is exhaustion, boils, and pain.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward riding evolves from amused curiosity to weary exasperation to mature comic detachment. The key vehicle for his critique is the gap between literary romanticism and physical fact. He had once, as a boy, "longed to be an Arab of the desert and have a beautiful mare" and imagined himself heroically refusing a fortune for her (045-029). But experience destroys the fantasy:

> "If these Arabs be like the other Arabs, their love for their beautiful mares is a fraud. These of my acquaintance have no love for their horses, no sentiment of pity for them, and no knowledge of how to treat them or care for them. The Syrian saddle-blanket is a quilted mattress two or three inches thick. It is never removed from the horse, day or night. It gets full of dirt and hair, and becomes soaked with sweat. It is bound to breed sores." (045-030)

This passage encapsulates Twain's method: deflate the romantic cliche with relentless physical detail. The "dreadful saddle-boils" that cover the horses' backs become a central image, and Twain's swapping of horses "not on account of his personal appearance, but because I have not seen his back" is a grim joke at his own expense (045-028).

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, Middle Eastern travel meant riding. Railways were few, roads were poor, and the principal means of overland transport remained the horse, donkey, mule, and camel. European and American travelers arrived with romantic preconceptions shaped by the Arabian Nights, Sir Walter Scott, and the flood of sentimental travelogues from writers like William C. Grimes (whom Twain relentlessly satirizes).

The Syrian saddle Twain describes -- "a quilted mattress two or three inches thick" never removed from the horse -- was an authentic piece of equipment whose design horrified Western observers accustomed to English saddlery. The "frog-shaped things" encountered in Ephesus and Smyrna reappear in Egypt on the most stylish donkeys (058-002), suggesting these were the standard pattern throughout the Ottoman Levant.
