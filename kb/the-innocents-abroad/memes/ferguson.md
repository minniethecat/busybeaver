# Ferguson

"Ferguson" is not a single person in *The Innocents Abroad* but a generic label applied by the Quaker City pilgrims to every guide, dragoman, and interpreter they employ across Europe and the Holy Land. The name originates with their Paris guide, A. Billfinger, and becomes a running joke that extends from France through Italy, Turkey, Syria, and Palestine. Ferguson is the quintessential comic foil — a combination of the pilgrims' linguistic laziness, their irreverent humor, and their suspicion of all hired intermediaries.

## Overview

The Ferguson identity begins in Paris when the travelers hire a guide whose real name — A. Billfinger — strikes them as impossibly unromantic. "Call him Ferguson," says Dan, and the rechristening is immediate and permanent (013-012). This Paris Ferguson sets the template: a figure at once essential and exasperating, whose hunger for commissions and drink becomes a source of sustained comedy throughout Chapter 13.

What makes Ferguson remarkable in the book is the extension of the name to guides everywhere. "All guides are Fergusons to us. We can not master their dreadful foreign names," Twain explains (035-003). The Ottoman guide Faraway Moses — a celebrated local personality — is "called Ferguson, just as we had done with all other guides," to his "smothered exasperation" (035-003). The Roman guide is "our Roman Ferguson," described as "the most patient, unsuspecting, long-suffering subject we have had yet" (027-077). In Syria, the dragoman Abraham is "our Ferguson" (045-028), and a Greek who volunteers to show them around Beirut is regarded as "a choice specimen of a Greek 'Ferguson'" in the chapter heading (041-001).

The Ferguson concept thus becomes Twain's vehicle for satirizing the entire institution of the European and Near Eastern guide — their commissions, their rehearsed patter, their dubious facts, and the mutual dependence of tourist and tout.

## In the Travelogue

### The Paris Ferguson (A. Billfinger)

The original Ferguson is hired at the Grand Hotel du Louvre. A "plainly dressed" man with "a noticeable air of neatness," he presents himself with studied deference, producing a calling card identifying him as **A. Billfinger, Guide to Paris, France, Germany, Spain, etc., etc.** (013-007). His "perfect" opening speech charms the party immediately — "We were more than charmed — we were overjoyed. We hired him at once" (013-006).

The charm wears off quickly. Ferguson reveals two "discrepancies": an insatiable appetite and a relentless determination to steer the party into commission-paying shops.

> "As long as we had that fellow after that, he was always hungry; he was always thirsty. He came early; he stayed late; he could not pass a restaurant; he looked with a lecherous eye upon every wine shop." (013-016)

The silk-shop episode is the chapter's comic centerpiece. After Ferguson learns Dan wants to buy silk dress patterns, he diverts the carriage to a silk store, claiming, "I suppose ze gentleman say he wish to buy some silk" — to which the Doctor delivers the definitive rebuke:

> "You are not required to 'suppose' things for the party, Ferguson. We do not wish to tax your energies too much." (013-022)

The Doctor's weapon against Ferguson is deadpan misdirection. At each silk-store stop, he pretends to admire "the palace of the Louvre," forcing Ferguson to correct him (013-024)(013-028)(013-032). The contest ends in a draw: the party misses the Louvre entirely, but "Ferguson sold not a solitary silk dress pattern" (013-037).

### Ferguson as Relicmonger

Beyond commerce, Ferguson is a purveyor of dubious information about relics. At Notre Dame, he tells the party that a silver cross belonging to an archbishop killed in the 1848 insurrection was thrown into the Seine and lay in the mud for fifteen years — until an angel appeared to a priest and told him where to dive for it. Twain notes dryly: "These people have a somewhat singular taste in the matter of relics" (014-005).

In the Bois de Boulogne, Ferguson points out the tree struck by a bullet in an assassination attempt on the Russian Czar. Twain observes that in America such a tree would be forgotten in five years, "but it will be treasured here. The guides will point it out to visitors for the next eight hundred years" (014-025).

In the Vatican, Ferguson appraises a newly excavated statue of Jupiter at "A hundred thousand dollars!" and explains the Pope's system of paying discoverers half the assessed value — though Twain adds, "I do not know whether Ferguson always tells the truth or not, but I suppose he does" (028-029).

### The Roman Ferguson

The Italian guide — "our Roman Ferguson" — endures the full force of the pilgrims' anti-guide strategy. The party has perfected the technique of feigning stupidity to torment guides, and the Doctor deploys it relentlessly:

> "Ah — Ferguson — what — what did you say was the name of the party who wrote this?" (027-029)

The Roman Ferguson is shown Christopher Columbus's handwriting, his bust, and an Egyptian mummy, and each time the Doctor asks absurd questions — whether Columbus died of measles (027-053), whether his parents are still living (027-055), and of the mummy: "Is — is he dead?" (027-070). The mummy exchange climaxes with the Doctor's mock outrage:

> "Playing us for Chinamen because we are strangers and trying to learn! Trying to impose your vile secondhand carcasses on *us*! ... if you've got a nice *fresh* corpse, fetch him out!" (027-073)

Yet Twain's affection for this guide is evident. He writes: "Our Roman Ferguson is the most patient, unsuspecting, long-suffering subject we have had yet. We shall be sorry to part with him" (027-077). The guide later describes them to the hotel landlord as "lunatics" — which Twain accepts as "a very good thing for a guide to say" (027-074).

### Ferguson in the Holy Land

In Syria and Palestine, "Ferguson" becomes the standard name for the party's dragoman and his assistants. The dragoman Abraham is called "our Ferguson" (045-028). His lieutenant, Mohammed, is "our Ferguson's lieutenant" (045-028). At the Sea of Galilee, Ferguson negotiates the boat fare:

> "How much? — ask him how much, Ferguson!" (047-025)
> *Ferguson* — (interpreting) — "He says two Napoleons — eight dollars." (047-026)

The pilgrims' instantaneous deflation at the price — "Too much! — we'll give him one!" (047-028) — and the boatmen's immediate departure is one of the Holy Land section's finest comic moments.

At Joseph's Pit, where local Arabs refuse to let "Christian dogs" drink, it is Ferguson who "tied rags and handkerchiefs together till he made a rope long enough to lower a vessel to the bottom" so the party can drink (047-021). Near the end of the pilgrimage, Ferguson's connections in the region are demonstrated when "a wild Arab in charge of a camel train recognized an old friend in Ferguson, and they ran and fell upon each other's necks and kissed each other's grimy, bearded faces upon both cheeks" (051-022) — a moment that teaches Twain the meaning of the biblical "kiss of welcome."

During the long desert marches, Ferguson's name becomes shorthand for the endlessness of the journey: "Ferguson, how many *more* million miles have we got to creep under this awful sun before we camp?" (057-002).

### Ferguson as Generic Term for Guides

The Ferguson joke reaches its conceptual peak when the celebrated Turkish guide Faraway Moses — a "recognized celebrity" whose name appears in Murray's guidebooks — is subjected to the same treatment:

> "Therefore, ignoring this fellow's brilliant fame, and ignoring the fanciful name he takes such pride in, we called him Ferguson, just as we had done with all other guides. It has kept him in a state of smothered exasperation all the time." (035-003)

Similarly, a Greek who offers to show the party around Beirut is regarded as "a choice specimen of a Greek 'Ferguson'" (041-001), and when he subsequently asks for payment, the U.S. Consul is surprised — the young man's family is reportedly worth $150,000 (041-007).

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's treatment of Ferguson is complex. On one level, Ferguson is a con man — a parasite on the tourist economy whose livelihood depends on commissions, inflated prices, and fabricated stories. Twain devotes Chapter 13 partly to "abusing that accomplished knave Billfinger" (013-038). But the abuse is performed with relish, not moral outrage.

On another level, Twain implicates the tourists themselves. The pilgrims hired Ferguson without asking his price — they were seduced by surface polish (013-006). They bring their own absurd expectations, wanting guides named "Henri de Montmorency" rather than Billfinger (013-010). And their mockery of guides — the "imbecility and idiotic questions" (027-012) — is itself a kind of bad behavior, however entertaining.

The Ferguson name also serves as a democratizing joke. By calling every guide Ferguson, regardless of local fame or self-importance, the pilgrims assert their refusal to be impressed. Faraway Moses may be in the guidebooks, but to the Quaker City party he is just another Ferguson. The joke is both a democratic leveling and a deliberate provincialism — a refusal to engage with foreign identity on its own terms.
