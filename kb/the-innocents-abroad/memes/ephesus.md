# Ephesus

Ancient Ionian Greek city on the coast of Asia Minor, one of the Seven Churches of Revelation, reached by rail from Smyrna in Chapter 40 — a ruined metropolis whose desolation Twain found both haunting and absurd, and whose marble fragments scattered across the plain testified to a magnificence "older than tradition itself."

## Overview

Ephesus was, for Twain, the most purely archaeological stop of the entire expedition. Unlike the Italian cities where living populations still inhabited the ancient streets, Ephesus was utterly deserted — a "world of precious relics, a wilderness of marred and mutilated gems" (040-013). The excursion program had promised "opportunity of visiting Ephesus, fifty miles distant by rail" from Smyrna (001-003), and the Superintendent of the railway "put a train at our disposal, and did us the further kindness of accompanying us to Ephesus" (040-002). Sixty donkeys were loaded into freight cars "for we had much ground to go over" (040-002).

The visit produced some of Twain's most evocative landscape description, his legend of the Seven Sleepers (a parody of medieval saints' tales), and the comic indignity of having their plundered relics confiscated by an Ottoman official — an incident that caused "a sensation" among the party (041-002).

## Arrival and First Impressions

The journey to Ephesus was itself a remarkable transition: "It runs almost to the very gates of Ephesus — a town great in all ages of the world — a city familiar to readers of the Bible, and one which was as old as the very hills when the disciples of Christ preached in its streets" (039-016). The railroad brought the modern world to the edge of antiquity: "The idea of a locomotive tearing through such a place as this, and waking the phantoms of its old days of romance out of their dreams of dead and gone centuries, is curious enough" (039-016).

At "ancient Ayassalook, in the midst of a forbidding desert," the party came upon "long lines of ruined aqueducts, and other remnants of architectural grandeur, that told us plainly enough we were nearing what had been a metropolis, once" (040-003). They mounted donkeys and proceeded to the theatre, where they "all stood in the vast theatre of ancient Ephesus — the stone-benched amphitheatre I mean — and had our picture taken. We looked as proper there as we would look anywhere, I suppose. We do not embellish the general desolation of a desert much" (040-006).

## The Ruins and Their History

From the hilltop prison traditionally attributed to St. Paul — "a gray ruin of ponderous blocks of marble" — Twain described the panorama: "Behind you is the sea; in front is a level green valley, (a marsh, in fact), extending far away among the mountains; to the right of the front view is the old citadel of Ayassalook, on a high hill; the ruined Mosque of the Sultan Selim stands near it in the plain, (this is built over the grave of St. John, and was formerly Christian Church)" (040-009). The hill of Pion clustered with ruins, the mountain of Coressus opposite — "The scene is a pretty one, and yet desolate — for in that wide plain no man can live, and in it is no human habitation" (040-009).

The Temple of Diana, one of the Seven Wonders of the World, was the city's supreme achievement. Twain reminded readers that "this place once stood Ephesus, the proudest city of ancient times, and whose Temple of Diana was so noble in design, and so exquisite of workmanship, that it ranked high in the list of the Seven Wonders of the World" (040-008). But nothing of the temple remained visible.

Twain's reflection on the city's storied past is one of his most evocatively written. "It is incredible to reflect that things as familiar all over the world today as household words, belong in the history and in the shadowy legends of this silent, mournful solitude. We speak of Apollo and of Diana — they were born here" (040-010). Homer, Alexander the Great, Hannibal, Antony and Cleopatra, Paul the Apostle, John, Mary Magdalene, and the Virgin Mary — all had connections to the city. "It makes me feel as old as these dreary hills to look down upon these moss-hung ruins, this historic desolation" (040-010).

The marble fragments littering the plain prompted genuine awe: "Go where you will about these broad plains, you find the most exquisitely sculptured marble fragments scattered thick among the dust and weeds; and protruding from the ground, or lying prone upon it, are beautiful fluted columns of porphyry and all precious marbles; and at every step you find elegantly carved capitals and massive bases, and polished tablets engraved with Greek inscriptions" (040-013). The scale was astonishing: at Constantinople, Pisa, and in Spain, "great mosques and cathedrals, whose grandest columns came from the temples and palaces of Ephesus, and yet one has only to scratch the ground here to match them" (040-013). Twain concluded: "We shall never know what magnificence is, until this imperial city is laid bare to the sun" (040-013).

## The Sculpture and the Builders

The finest sculpture the party had yet seen was one lying in the old theatre — the very theatre where St. Paul's "riot" with the silversmiths had occurred. "It is only the headless body of a man, clad in a coat of mail, with a Medusa head upon the breastplate, but we feel persuaded that such dignity and such majesty were never thrown into a form of stone before" (040-014).

The builders of antiquity left Twain in awe: "The massive arches of some of these ruins rest upon piers that are fifteen feet square and built entirely of solid blocks of marble, some of which are as large as a Saratoga trunk, and some the size of a boardinghouse sofa. They are not shells or shafts of stone filled inside with rubbish, but the whole pier is a mass of solid masonry" (040-015). "They have braved the storms and sieges of three thousand years, and have been shaken by many an earthquake, but still they stand" (040-015). An English Company was preparing to excavate Ephesus — "and then!" (040-015).

## The Seven Churches and Prophecy

Twain had already reflected on Ephesus in the context of the Seven Churches prophecy when in Smyrna (Chapter 38). He noted that "the 'candlestick' has been removed from Ephesus. Her light has been put out. Pilgrims, always prone to find prophecies in the Bible, and often where none exist, speak cheerfully and complacently of poor, ruined Ephesus as the victim of prophecy" (038-012).

But Twain was skeptical: "And yet there is no sentence that promises, without due qualification, the destruction of the city" (038-012). The other verses of Revelation were "singularly complimentary to Ephesus" (038-014). "The threat is qualified. There is no history to show that she did not repent. But the cruelest habit the modern prophecy-savans have, is that one of coolly and arbitrarily fitting the prophetic shirt on to the wrong man" (038-014). Drawing on St. Paul's own words — "If after the manner of men I have fought with beasts at Ephesus" (040-011) — Twain pointed out the absurdity of confusing warnings to churches with predictions about cities.

## The Legend of the Seven Sleepers

In what is perhaps the longest set-piece of parody in the entire book, Twain retells the medieval legend of the Seven Sleepers of Ephesus in his own comic idiom. The seven Christian youths, fleeing the persecution of "the good King Maximilianus," embarked on a five-year crime spree before returning to their cave on Mount Pion (040-018). There they consumed six bottles each of "curious liquors" and slept for two centuries (040-019).

When they awoke, "their raiment was all gone," and they wrapped themselves in leaves (040-020). Returning to Ephesus, nothing was recognizable. "Johannes said, It hardly seems like Ephesus" (040-021). The punchline follows their discovery that they have slept two hundred years: "Alas, that we drank of the curious liquors... Behold, the jig is up — let us die" (040-024). Twain concludes with his comic names: "Johannes Smithianus, Trumps, Gift, High, and Low, Jack, and The Game" — and labels on the bottles reading "Rumpunch, Jinsling, Egnog" (040-024). He deadpans: "Such is the story of the Seven Sleepers, (with slight variations), and I know it is true, because I have seen the cave myself" (040-025).

## Relic Confiscation

The party's souvenir-hunting ended in humiliation. "We brought not a relic from Ephesus!" (041-002). After gathering "fragments of sculptured marbles and breaking ornaments from the interior work of the Mosques; and after bringing them at a cost of infinite trouble and fatigue, five miles on muleback to the railway depot, a government officer compelled all who had such things to disgorge!" (041-002). The order came from Constantinople, and worse — "a principal sufferer discovered that the imperial order was enclosed in an envelope bearing the seal of the British Embassy at Constantinople" (041-002). The party was incensed that the British legation "simply intimated that we were a sort of gentlemen and ladies who would bear watching!" (041-002). Twain, however, conceded the justice: the English Company excavating Ephesus "need to be protected, and deserve to be" (041-002).
