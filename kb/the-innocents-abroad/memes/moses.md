# Moses

The name Moses appears in *The Innocents Abroad* in two sharply contrasting incarnations: "Faraway Moses," the celebrated Turkish guide of Constantinople, and Moses S. Beach, a prominent American newspaper publisher traveling with Twain's party. Twain never discusses the biblical Moses in sustained detail, but the Exodus narrative haunts the Holy Land chapters, with the living guide and the newspaperman both measured — implicitly or explicitly — against the prophetic archetype whose name they share.

## Overview

The biblical Moses is the shadow presence behind Twain's Holy Land narrative. When the party follows "the way the children of Israel journeyed on, with Moses at the head of the general government" (042-013), they are retracing the Exodus route, and every biblical site evokes the lawgiver. But Twain's direct attention falls on two contemporary Moseses. Faraway Moses is the wily Turkish guide in Constantinople whose fame — carefully cultivated through visitors' notebooks — has made him "a made man" (035-003). Moses S. Beach of the *New York Sun* is a fellow passenger whose presence Twain notes with characteristic journalistic interest. Between them, these two Moses figures embody the extremes of travel in Twain's world: the exotic local operator and the familiar American type, both navigating foreign terrain for their own purposes.

## Faraway Moses

Faraway Moses appears in the Constantinople chapters as a celebrated Turkish guide whose reputation has been built through the endorsements of travelers. Twain introduces him with the chapter heading "Faraway Moses — Melancholy Sebastopol" (035-001), signaling the guide's importance to the Constantinople experience. "All our notebooks have mentioned Faraway Moses' name, and he is a made man" (035-003) — the guide had perfected the art of seducing tourists into his shop with coffee and flattery. Twain describes him as an "illustrated Turkish guide" who "will seduce them into buying" (035-002), a figure whose commercial success rested on the same foundations as his own travel writing: the credulity and enthusiasm of touring Americans.

## Moses S. Beach

Moses Sperry Beach (1822–1892) was the owner and publisher of the *New York Sun*, one of the most widely read newspapers in 19th-century America. Twain mentions him simply as "Mr. Moses S. Beach, of the *New York Sun*" (057-012), placing him among the notable passengers on the *Quaker City* excursion. Beach was a significant figure in American journalism — his father, Moses Yale Beach, had founded the *Sun*, and the younger Beach continued its tradition of sensational, mass-appeal reporting. Twain, himself a working journalist filing dispatches that would become this very book, was traveling in company with a fellow newspaperman whose reach vastly exceeded his own.

## The Biblical Shadow

Though Twain does not devote sustained passages to the biblical Moses, the Exodus narrative supplies the organizing framework for the Holy Land journey. The party follows the route of the Israelites with "Moses at the head of the general government" (042-013). In Egypt, Twain notes the spot "where Pharaoh's daughter found Moses in the bulrushes" (058-006), and the chapter heading promises "Moses in the bulrushes — Place where found" (058-001). These biblical references create a dissonance that Twain exploits: the journey of American tourists on horseback, managed by a mercenary dragoman, retracing the steps of a prophet who spoke with God, is a structural irony that suffuses the entire Holy Land section.

## Twain's Assessment

Twain treats Faraway Moses with the same amused detachment he applies to all the commercial operators of the East — a man whose fame is as manufactured as the souvenirs he sells. The phrase "a made man" (035-003) captures the mechanism: reputation, in the Ottoman tourism economy, was not earned but constructed. Moses S. Beach receives no particular judgment, merely a mention — yet the juxtaposition of a newspaper publisher named Moses traveling through the land of the biblical Moses is the kind of coincidence Twain never needed to underline. As for the prophet himself, Twain's treatment is indirect but unmistakable: the "suffering Moses" invoked in exasperation over exorbitant prices (005-018) collapses the distance between the biblical figure and the everyday frustrations of a tourist being overcharged.
