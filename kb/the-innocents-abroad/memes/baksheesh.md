# Baksheesh

The practice of soliciting tips, alms, and petty bribes from foreign travelers — a ubiquitous feature of travel in the Ottoman Middle East that Twain encounters relentlessly and satirizes as one of the defining irritations of his pilgrimage.

## Overview

Baksheesh is the Turkish/Arabic-derived term for a tip, gratuity, or charitable gift that permeated every layer of society in the 19th-century Ottoman Empire. In *The Innocents Abroad*, Twain uses the word — and the practice it describes — as a leitmotif for the region's poverty, opportunism, and the transactional nature of every human encounter a Western traveler experiences. From the streets of Damascus to the summit of the Great Pyramid, the cry of "Baksheesh!" follows the pilgrims like a refrain, becoming one of the book's most memorable running jokes.

The concept illuminates a deeper cultural collision: the American pilgrims, with their Protestant work ethic and notions of honest commerce, confront a world where nothing is given freely and every interaction is an occasion for outstretched hands.

## In Twain's Narrative

The word "baksheesh" first appears in earnest when the pilgrims reach Syria. In Damascus, Twain notes with grim irony that the beggars — unlike everywhere else — "passed us by without demanding baksheesh" (044-013), which he presents not as relief but as evidence of the city's fanatical hostility toward Christians: even professional beggars would rather refuse coin than suffer the pollution of Christian contact.

The theme intensifies in Jerusalem, where baksheesh becomes the "eternal" sound and the "sole language" of the city:

> "the eternal 'baksheesh.' To see the numbers of maimed, malformed and diseased humanity that throng the holy places and obstruct the gates, one might suppose that the ancient days had come again, and that the angel of the Lord was expected to descend at any moment to stir the pool of Bethesda" (053-005)

Every sacred site extracts its toll. At the tomb of Lazarus, nothing can be seen "but baksheesh can purchase a sight" (052-004). At the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, the guardians and guides "persecuted us all the time for baksheesh" (054-028). Twain observes that baksheesh is as integral to the Holy Land pilgrimage experience as prayer:

> "Beggars, cripples and monks compass you about, and make you think only of baksheesh when you would rather think of something more in keeping with the character of the spot." (055-042)

The theme reaches its comic peak in Egypt, where the party ascends the Great Pyramid:

> "We suffered torture no pen can describe from the hungry appeals for baksheesh that gleamed from Arab eyes and poured incessantly from Arab lips. Why try to call up the traditions of vanished Egyptian grandeur... Why try to think at all? The thing was impossible." (058-015)

At the pyramid, the Arabs' baksheesh demands become an elaborate con. The party "received ragged Arabs by couples, dozens and platoons, and paid them baksheesh for services they swore and proved by each other that they had rendered, but which we had not been aware of before" (058-023). Each newly paid group simply circled to the rear and reappeared with "a newly-invented delinquent list for liquidation" (058-024).

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the Ottoman Empire of the 1860s, baksheesh functioned as an informal economy parallel to official wages. Guides, guards, gatekeepers, and self-appointed "protectors" at archaeological and religious sites received little or no salary, surviving entirely on gratuities from visitors. The system was so entrenched that refusing to pay could result in genuine obstruction or withheld access.

Twain, however, finds the practice indistinguishable from begging and extortion. He presents Damascus as the one place where baksheesh culture breaks down — but only because the Damascenes are too fanatically anti-Christian to accept money from infidels, a backhanded compliment that reveals his exasperation with the system everywhere else.

The practice also intersects with Twain's broader critique of religious tourism in the Holy Land. The commercialization of sacred sites — where monks and guides demand payment at every altar and tomb — becomes for Twain a dark comedy of piety for sale.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward baksheesh moves from annoyance to dark humor to something approaching despair. His characteristic strategy is to exaggerate the absurdity:

> "the most infernal chorus: 'Howajji, baksheesh! howajji, baksheesh! howajji, baksheesh!'" (048-003)

He pays baksheesh "out to sore-eyed children and brown, unsightly, deformed and leprous-looking creatures, male and female, in all the damned thoroughfares" (048-004), the profanity signaling his genuine anger beneath the comic surface.

Yet he also finds moments of grudging cultural appreciation. The lepers in Naaman's house in Damascus "hold up their hands and beg for baksheesh when a stranger enters" (044-026), which Twain presents not as an indictment of the sufferers but as a tableau of biblical continuity — Naaman was a leper three thousand years ago, and lepers beg at his house still.

The bathos reaches its apex in Egypt, where baksheesh becomes not merely annoying but philosophically corrosive: "One must bring his meditations cut and dried, or else cut and dry them afterward" (058-015). The ceaseless demand for money makes genuine reflection impossible — a confession that baksheesh, more than any other hardship of Eastern travel, defeats the pilgrim's capacity for wonder.
