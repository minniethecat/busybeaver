# Antiquity

The ancient past, particularly the civilizations of Greece, Rome, and biblical Israel — a constant presence in *The Innocents Abroad* that Twain approaches with a characteristic mix of genuine awe and comic deflation.

## Overview

Antiquity is the gravitational center around which much of Twain's travelogue orbits. The Quaker City's itinerary was designed to take its passengers through the ruins of the ancient world: Pompeii, the Acropolis, Ephesus, the temples of Baalbek, the streets of Jerusalem. For 19th-century Americans, these sites represented the fountainhead of Western civilization — the physical remains of the classical and biblical worlds that had shaped their religion, their politics, and their cultural imagination.

Twain's response to antiquity is complex and often contradictory. He is capable of genuine reverence before the monuments of the past, yet he refuses to be intimidated into piety. He mocks the exaggerated claims of guides and the credulity of his fellow pilgrims, yet he is himself moved by the "hoary antiquity" of places like Damascus [044-009] and the "remote antiquity" of the Holy Land [058-029]. His treatment of antiquity reveals both the educated American's cultural heritage and the frontier democrat's suspicion of old-world pretensions.

## In Twain's Narrative

Twain introduces the theme early, describing Tangier as "a cradle of antiquity" and noting that the city suits "a venerable antiquity like this" [008-001][008-005]. In Italy, he is struck by the "vision of gray antiquity" presented by ancient sites [015-003]. Venice makes him feel connected to "the peoples of a remote antiquity" [023-021].

The Holy Land intensifies the theme. In Damascus, Twain invokes the "mists of a hoary antiquity" [044-009], while the landscape itself seems to him "of such high antiquity that no man knows who built it" [045-011]. The ruins of Ephesus prompt reflection on the builders of antiquity: "What soldiers they were, these men of antiquity!" [040-015]. At the journey's end, looking back, he writes of "the horizon of remote antiquity" toward which his thoughts turn [058-029].

## Cultural and Historical Context

For educated Americans of the 1860s, classical antiquity was a living cultural presence. Latin and Greek were central to the curriculum; the Bible was read in light of its ancient Near Eastern contexts; the American republic understood itself in conscious relationship to the Roman republic. Travel to the ancient sites of the Mediterranean was therefore not merely tourism but a kind of cultural pilgrimage — a return to the sources of Western identity.

At the same time, the discipline of archaeology was transforming the understanding of antiquity. Excavations at Pompeii, Ephesus, and throughout the Holy Land were producing new knowledge that sometimes confirmed and sometimes challenged traditional accounts. Twain's travelogue reflects both the traditional reverence for classical and biblical sites and a modern, skeptical sensibility informed by new discoveries.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's relationship to antiquity is perhaps best captured in his phrase "signs of a hoary antiquity, but with nothing touching or beautiful about it" [033-018]. He respects the ancient world but refuses to be sentimental about it. He is more interested in what antiquity reveals about human nature — ambition, credulity, artistic genius, violence — than in antiquity as a source of cultural authority.

> "Everywhere were signs of a hoary antiquity, but with nothing touching or beautiful about it." [033-018]

This honest assessment — the refusal to pretend to emotions he does not feel — is characteristic of Twain's approach throughout the book.
