# Mountain

An elevated landform — encountered throughout *The Innocents Abroad* as Twain journeys from the volcanic Azores to the Alpine lakes, the hills of Italy, and the rugged terrain of the Holy Land.

## Overview

Mountains provide the vertical dimension of Twain's travelogue — the grand backdrop against which cities, lakes, and ruins are set. They appear in every phase of the journey: the "mountain of mud" that is Flores in the Azores [005-010], the "snow-clad mountains" rising above Lake Como [020-002], the "imposing mountain scenery that ennobles the landscape" of northern Italy [021-062], and the "barren hills" of the Holy Land [033-004]. Mountains, in Twain's telling, are both aesthetic objects — things to be admired — and physical obstacles — things to be crossed, often with considerable discomfort.

Twain's treatment of mountains reveals his characteristic blend of romantic sensibility and comic deflation. He can write lyrically about "dreamy, bluish, snow-clad mountains" [020-002] in one paragraph and complain about a mountain road that jolts his spine in the next. The mountain, like the sea, is a reality that refuses to conform to the guidebook's idealized descriptions — and Twain loves it for that.

## In Twain's Narrative

Mountains enter the story early, in the Azores: Flores is "only a mountain of mud standing up out of the dull mists of the sea" [005-010], and "the mountains on some of the islands are very high" [006-017]. The landscape grows more dramatic as the *Quaker City* enters the Mediterranean: "Ape's Hill," a "grand old mountain with summit streaked with gray lava" above Gibraltar [007-009], and the "swarthy Riffians from the mountains" of Morocco [008-003].

The Alpine lakes provide Twain's most sustained mountain description. Lake Como is "shut in by great mountains" [020-010], its shores dotted with villages that "look so snug and cozy" on the mountain sides [020-012]. The party voyages "through wild mountain scenery" [021-002] on Lake Lecco, and Twain is genuinely moved by the "imposing mountain scenery that ennobles the landscape" [021-062].

In the Holy Land, mountains take on a different character — less picturesque, more severe. The "barren hills" around Athens [033-004] prefigure the rougher terrain to come. Twain crosses "the plains and deserts and mountains of the West" in memory [012-007], comparing the biblical landscape to the American frontier. The mountains of the Holy Land are not Alpine postcards but harsh, dusty places where biblical history happened.

## Cultural and Historical Context

Nineteenth-century travelers were steeped in the aesthetic categories of the sublime and the picturesque, and mountains were the quintessential sublime landscape. The Romantics — Byron, Shelley, Ruskin — had trained the European and American eye to see mountains as emblems of nature's grandeur and the divine. Twain's mountain descriptions participate in this tradition even as they undercut it. He registers the sublimity of Alpine scenery but refuses to be overwhelmed by it; his humor is a defense against the tyranny of the picturesque.

The Holy Land mountains presented a different challenge. Biblical associations gave these hills immense significance for the pilgrims, but their actual appearance — barren, rocky, monotonous — disappointed expectations shaped by Sunday-school illustrations. Twain's honesty about this gap between image and reality is one of the book's lasting contributions to travel literature.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's mountain descriptions reveal a genuine susceptibility to landscape beauty beneath the comic surface. His Lake Como passages, in particular, approach the lyrical:

> "We voyaged by steamer down the Lago di Lecco, through wild mountain scenery, and by hamlets and villages perched on rocky ledges..." [021-002]

Yet he never lets the lyricism stand unchallenged for long. A mountain is beautiful, yes — but the road over it is terrible, the inn at its foot is flea-ridden, and the guidebook's description bears no resemblance to the actual experience. This doubleness — admiration and deflation — is Twain's signature mode.
