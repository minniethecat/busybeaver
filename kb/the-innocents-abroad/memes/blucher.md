# Blucher

A young American from the Far West aboard the *Quaker City* excursion, Blucher is one of Twain's most frequently cited fellow travelers — a figure of guileless enthusiasm, comic misfortune, and shameless souvenir fabrication who embodies the innocent abroad at his most endearing and absurd.

## Overview

Blucher enters the narrative before the ship even sails. Twain describes spending time with him in New York during the month preceding departure: "He was confiding, good-natured, unsophisticated, companionable; but he was not a man to set the river on fire" (002-005). This assessment — affectionate but underwhelming — sets the tone for every subsequent appearance. Blucher is never the hero of any anecdote, but he is the human interest that makes the anecdotes memorable.

Throughout the voyage, Blucher functions as a comic barometer of the pilgrim experience. His bafflement at ship time, his disastrous restaurant bill in the Azores, his donkey-riding catastrophes, his near-fatal trespass at a Moorish mosque, his appalling French, and above all his magnificent indifference to truth in the labeling of souvenirs — together these episodes form a portrait of American innocence confronting the Old World with more enthusiasm than competence.

## In the Travelogue

### Before Departure

Twain and Blucher spend the month before sailing together in New York, where Blucher develops "the most extraordinary notions about this European exodus and came at last to consider the whole nation as packing up for emigration to France" (002-005). His signature moment comes in a Broadway store where, unable to make change for a handkerchief, he tells the clerk he will pay in Paris. When the clerk replies that he is not going to Paris, Blucher is so affronted that he walks out without a word, later pronouncing: "It was a lie — that is my opinion of it!" (002-014)

### Ship Time and the Watch

On the Atlantic crossing, Blucher — "from the Far West and on his first voyage" (005-003) — is tormented by the constantly changing ship time. His new $150 watch from Illinois cannot keep pace with the eastward-gaining vessel. He pushes the regulator "clear around" to no avail (005-006), convinced the watch is defective. The captain finally explains the mystery of ship time, and Twain notes drily: "This young man asked a great many questions about seasickness before we left, and wanted to know what its characteristics were and how he was to tell when he had it. He found out" (005-007).

### The Azores Dinner

Blucher's most famous episode occurs at Fayal in the Azores. Grateful to be on solid land, he invites nine companions to a grand banquet at the principal hotel. When the bill arrives — "Total, twenty-one thousand seven hundred reis!" — Blucher is devastated, convinced he has been ruined (005-018). He offers the landlord $150 and declares: "I'll swim in blood before I'll pay a cent more" (005-020). The landlord, consulting an American, translates the bill into dollars — $21.70 — and "Happiness reigned once more in Blucher's dinner party" (005-023). The episode perfectly captures both Blucher's generous spirit and his comic ignorance of foreign exchange.

### Donkey Misadventures

In the Azores, Blucher proves incapable of managing a donkey. "Blucher could do nothing at all with his donkey" (006-010). The beast scrapes him against walls, darts into its birthplace, and scrapes him off at the doorway. After remounting, Blucher warns the muleteer: "Now, that's enough, you know; you go slow hereafter" (006-010) — but the Portuguese driver, understanding nothing, shouts "Sekki-yah!" and the donkey bolts again. "He turned a corner suddenly, and Blucher went over his head" (006-011). Twain adds that Blucher's attempts to swear were drowned out by his donkey's braying — "every time he opened his mouth his animal did so also and let off a series of brays that drowned all other sounds" (006-011).

### The Moorish Mosque

At Tangier, Blucher nearly causes an international incident. He starts to ride his mule directly into the open doorway of a Moorish mosque before being stopped by shouts. Twain explains that "so dire a profanation is it for a Christian dog to set foot upon the sacred threshold of a Moorish mosque that no amount of purification can ever make it fit for the faithful to pray in again" (009-002). The chapter heading calls him "that heedless Blucher," and Twain grimly notes: "Had Blucher succeeded in entering the place, he would no doubt have been chased through the town and stoned" (009-002). A later anecdote about a Christian clock-mender forced to enter a mosque on all fours prompts Twain to observe: "Therefore, if Blucher ever sees the inside of a mosque, he will have to cast aside his humanity and go in his natural character" (009-005).

### The Soap Letter

In Paris, Blucher composes a magnificent complaint letter to the landlord — a macaronic collision of English and French demanding soap:

> "Monsieur le Landlord — Sir: Pourquoi don't you mettez some savon in your bedchambers? Est-ce que vous pensez I will steal it? ... Savon is a necessary de la vie to anybody but a Frenchman, et je l'aurai hors de cet hotel or make trouble. You hear me. Allons." (019-026)

Twain remonstrates that the landlord will never understand it, "but Blucher said he guessed the old man could read the French of it and average the rest" (019-027). Twain adds: "Blucher's French is bad enough, but it is not much worse than the English one finds in advertisements all over Italy every day" (019-028).

### Disappearance

By the time the ship reaches Genoa, Blucher has gone missing. Twain lists the absent companions: "'Moult' was in England, Jack in Switzerland, Charley in Spain. Blucher was gone, none could tell where" (017-003). His unexplained absence adds to his mystique — he is simply the kind of traveler who might turn up anywhere, or nowhere.

### The Souvenir Collector

Blucher's most sustained comic trait is his astonishing approach to souvenir-collecting. At Sebastopol, Twain catches him labeling relics with complete disregard for truth. A sack of battlefield fragments yields a specimen marked "Fragment of a Russian General" — which turns out to be "nothing but a couple of teeth and part of the jawbone of a horse" (035-011). Confronted, Blucher's invariable reply is: "Go slow — the old woman won't know any different" (035-013), referring to his aunt back home.

Twain catalogs further offenses: "I have found him breaking a stone in two, and labeling half of it 'Chunk busted from the pulpit of Demosthenes,' and the other half 'Darnick from the Tomb of Abelard and Heloise'" (035-014). He has distributed pebbles from the ship's shore as relics from Mars Hill where St. Paul preached, and "he says he never expects to run out of mementoes of St. Paul as long as he is in reach of a sandbank" (035-016).

### The Russian Visit

At Yalta, Blucher makes his final memorable appearance. Discussing whether the Grand Duke's breakfast was the Duke's idea, "Blucher said he believed Baker's boy had suggested it to his Imperial Highness" (037-019). When the Governor-General visits the ship, "we all liked him, especially Blucher. When he went away, Blucher invited him to come again and fetch his carpet along" (037-029).

### The Syrian Horses

At Beirut, when the dragoman parades a collection of wretched horses before the party, "It was fearful. Blucher shook his head and said: 'That dragon is going to get himself into trouble fetching these old crates out of the hospital the way they are, unless he has got a permit'" (041-009).

## Character and Role

Blucher represents a specific American type: the western innocent, generous to a fault, enthusiastic beyond his competence, and cheerfully indifferent to Old World decorum. His naivete is not stupidity — his assessment of the Beirut horses as "old crates out of the hospital" is sharp and accurate — but a kind of radical sincerity that refuses to be intimidated by foreign surroundings.

Twain's treatment of Blucher is consistently affectionate. Unlike the Oracle, who is mocked for pomposity, or the pilgrims collectively, who are satirized for hypocrisy, Blucher is comic without being contemptible. His souvenir fabrications are presented not as fraud but as a harmless, almost charming indifference to historical accuracy — an impulse Twain himself admits sharing: "I notice that all travelers supply deficiencies in their collections in the same way" (035-016).
