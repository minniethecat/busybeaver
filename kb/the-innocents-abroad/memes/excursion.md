# The Excursion

The Quaker City Pleasure Excursion was a six-month packaged voyage to Europe and the Holy Land, conceived and sold by Captain Charles C. Duncan in 1867 as a bold commercial venture — a "picnic on a gigantic scale" (001-002) that would carry select American travelers through the Mediterranean and mark the beginning of organized transatlantic tourism for the American middle class.

## Overview

The excursion was an unprecedented enterprise for its time. Advertised as the "Grand Holy Land Pleasure Excursion," it offered 150 cabin passengers a five-month itinerary spanning the Azores, Gibraltar, France, Italy, Greece, Turkey, the Holy Land, and Egypt, all for a fixed price of $1,250 in currency (001-004). The venture was masterminded by Captain Charles C. Duncan, who operated from an office at 117 Wall Street in New York, and was facilitated by a committee system that reviewed applications, selected the steamer, and managed the ship's finances (001-004).

For months before departure, "the great pleasure excursion to Europe and the Holy Land was chatted about in the newspapers everywhere in America and discussed at countless firesides" (001-002). Twain himself noted that the project "compelled that interest which attractive novelties always command," for "its like had not been thought of before" (001-002). The excursion was, in effect, the prototype of the modern package tour — combining transportation, accommodations, meals, and guided sightseeing into a single purchase, long before Thomas Cook made the model famous.

## In Twain's Narrative

Twain represents the excursion from its earliest conception to its nostalgic afterglow. In Chapter 1, he reproduces the full promotional program — a document so seductive that "who could read the program of the excursion without longing to make one of the party?" (001-003). The program promised everything from the Paris Exposition to Garibaldi's home at Caprera, from Vesuvius to the Holy Land, with the assurance that "the ship will at all times be a home" (001-004). Twain confesses that "human nature could not withstand these bewildering temptations" and that he "hurried to the treasurer's office and deposited my ten percent" (001-005).

The departure scene in Chapter 2 immediately undercuts the promotional rhetoric. The pier was "crowded with carriages and men," and "groups of excursionists, arrayed in unattractive traveling costumes, were moping about in a drizzling rain and looking as droopy and woebegone as so many molting chickens" (002-017). Twain dryly observes: "It was a pleasure excursion — there was no gainsaying that, because the program said so — it was so nominated in the bond — but it surely hadn't the general aspect of one" (002-017). This gap between the advertised ideal and the mundane reality becomes the book's central comic engine.

The shipboard culture that developed aboard the *Quaker City* was nothing like the frolic Twain had imagined. In his valedictory article for the *New York Herald*, reprinted in Chapter 61, he reflected that "three-fourths of the *Quaker City*'s passengers were between forty and seventy years of age" and that "the pleasure ship was a synagogue, and the pleasure trip was a funeral excursion without a corpse" (061-002). Dancing was attempted on only three evenings and "even this melancholy orgy was voted to be sinful, and dancing was discontinued" (061-002). The advertised title — "The Grand Holy Land Pleasure Excursion" — was, Twain suggested, a misnomer; "'The Grand Holy Land Funeral Procession' would have been better — much better" (061-002).

Despite the satire, the excursion delivered on its geographic promises. The ship did sail to the Azores, Gibraltar, Marseilles, Genoa, Leghorn, Naples, Athens, Constantinople, Smyrna, Beirut, Joppa, Alexandria, and numerous other ports, and the passengers did visit Paris, Rome, Jerusalem, and the Pyramids. Twain acknowledged in his conclusion that "the expedition accomplished all that its programme promised that it should accomplish, and we ought all to be satisfied with the management of the matter, certainly" (061-002). The structure of the excursion — its fixed schedule, its committee governance, its blend of the sacred and the commercial — gave Twain both a narrative framework and an endless source of material.

## Cultural and Historical Context

The excursion sailed in the summer of 1867, just two years after the end of the American Civil War. The nation was reunifying, and for the first time a substantial American middle class had both the means and the desire to travel abroad. Twain captured the moment in Chapter 2: "Everybody was going to Europe — I, too, was going to Europe. Everybody was going to the famous Paris Exposition" (002-005). Steam technology had made transatlantic travel reliable, and the Paris Exposition of 1867 provided a glamorous destination. The excursion was part of a broader "European exodus" (002-005) that saw steamship lines carrying Americans out of the country "at the rate of four or five thousand a week" (002-005).

The excursion's hybrid identity — part religious pilgrimage, part secular tourism — reflected the cultural tensions of its era. The supplementary program instructed passengers to bring Bibles and books on the Holy Land (001-008), and the advertised title foregrounded the religious destination. Yet the marketing also promised pleasure, novelty, and social prestige. The "select company" rhetoric (001-004) appealed to status-conscious Americans who wanted to distinguish themselves from ordinary travelers, even as Twain lampooned the pretensions of the committee that reviewed applications (001-005). The excursion was, in many ways, an exercise in American self-fashioning — an attempt to export national identity across the Atlantic and measure it against the Old World.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward the excursion is characteristically double-edged. He delights in its absurdities while remaining fundamentally grateful for the experience. His satire targets not the concept of the excursion itself but the gap between its pretensions and its realities. When Captain Duncan appears on deck, Twain notes simply: "It was Captain Duncan, chief of the expedition" (003-021), and by Chapter 10, at the Fourth of July celebration, he reports that "Captain Duncan made a good speech" (010-015) — an earnest compliment amid the irony.

The most revealing assessment comes in the final chapter, where Twain reflects: "the mass of my memories of the excursion have grown more and more pleasant as the years have passed" and confesses that "if the *Quaker City* were weighing her anchor to sail away on the very same cruise again, nothing could gratify me more than to be a passenger" (062-001). This retrospective warmth does not cancel the satire but complicates it: the excursion was both ridiculous and genuinely enriching, and Twain knew it.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- The promotional program (001-004), reproduced in full, detailing every port of call and promising "a select company" with a physician, library, and musical instruments aboard
- The departure in the rain (002-017), where excursionists looked like "molting chickens" and the promised battery of guns was silent because "the ammunition was out"
- The supplementary program (001-008) instructing passengers to bring "light musical instruments for amusement," saddles for Syrian travel, green spectacles, umbrellas, and veils for Egypt
- The Fourth of July celebration (010-002) at sea, one of the few moments when shipboard life rose to the occasion
- The valedictory article (061-002) in which Twain calls the excursion "a funeral excursion without a corpse" but concludes by wishing everyone well
- The closing reflection (062-001) where Twain admits he would happily do the whole thing again
