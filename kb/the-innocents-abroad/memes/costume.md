Costume serves as a persistent visual motif in *The Innocents Abroad*, providing Twain with an endless source of descriptive pleasure and comic observation. From the "fanciful costumes" of cathedral guards to the "grotesque costumes" of Middle Eastern streets, clothing becomes a shorthand for cultural difference — and for the theatricality of travel itself [006-007].

Twain's eye for costume is evident from the voyage's first days. In the Azores, he remarks on "the customs and costumes of twenty curious peoples" as one of the promised attractions of the journey [001-002]. The Quaker City had been advertised as an excursion that would expose its passengers to a panorama of human variety, and costume — visible, legible, entertaining — was the most immediate marker of that variety.

## European Costumes

In Europe, Twain is particularly drawn to the theatrical costuming he encounters at every turn. The Swiss Guard at the Vatican, "dressed in the fanciful costumes of two centuries ago," strikes him as a living anachronism — men whose clothing belongs more to opera than to modern life [006-007]. At Fontainebleau, "patrician ladies in stunning costumes of centuries ago" populate the historical tableaux [017-011]. The French court of centuries past is preserved in its wardrobe.

The Paris Exposition dazzles Twain with "hundreds of people in gay costumes" who "walked or ran or danced" through the pavilions [016-002]. Here costume is pure spectacle — national identity performed for an international audience. Twain is a willing audience, never tiring of cataloging what people wear.

## Eastern Costumes

It is in the East, however, that costume becomes a consuming interest for Twain. Constantinople presents "barbarians in all sorts of weird costumes, all more or less ragged" [008-004]. The streets of the Ottoman capital become a living theater: "red and blue, and undress costumes of snowy white, and also the unmistakable uniforms of rank" create a kaleidoscopic effect that Twain struggles to capture in prose [007-024].

In Damascus, "the fearful and wonderful costumes from the back country" supply endless entertainment [036-005]. Twain describes "a fancy-looking negro in Turkish costume" and "an Arab" preceding travelers [038-019]. The bazaars of Smyrna and the streets of Jerusalem offer similar visual feasts.

## Twain as Costumed Figure

Twain is not merely an observer of costume but himself a costumed figure in the eyes of locals. In Italy, "these people laughed at my costume" — the American tourist, dressed in practical travel clothes, becomes an object of amusement to those he has been scrutinizing [026-006]. The reversal is characteristic of Twain's humor: the observer becomes the observed, the costume critic becomes the costumed.

Aboard the Quaker City, the amateur theatricals produce "impromptu theatrical costumes" for "extravagant farces," turning the ship itself into a costume drama [025-019]. The pilgrims become players — a role that, in Twain's telling, they never fully abandon throughout the journey.
