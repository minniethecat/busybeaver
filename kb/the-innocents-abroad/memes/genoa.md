# Genoa

Italian maritime republic and port city, the first Italian landfall of the Quaker City excursion, celebrated by Mark Twain for its palaces, its beautiful women, its churches, and its silver filigree — and mocked for its guides, its peeling frescoes, and its cave-like streets.

## Overview

Genoa was the first Italian city Twain's party set foot on, arriving "early in the bright summer morning" from Marseilles. The city rose "out of the sea and flung back the sunlight from her hundred palaces" (017-004). The expedition's itinerary had promised the excursionists "an opportunity to look over this, the 'magnificent city of palaces'" and to "visit the birthplace of Columbus, twelve miles off, over a beautiful road built by Napoleon I" (001-003). For Twain, Genoa became a city of superlatives — the most beautiful women in Europe, the most churches per square yard, and the most irritating guides in Italy.

## Arrival and First Impressions

The approach from the sea was spectacular: "the stately city of Genoa rose up out of the sea and flung back the sunlight from her hundred palaces" (017-004). But it was not the architecture that captured Twain's attention. It was the women. "I would like to remain here. I had rather not go any further. There may be prettier women in Europe, but I doubt it" (017-006). With Twain's characteristic hyperbole, he claims the population of Genoa is "120,000; two-thirds of these are women, I think, and at least two-thirds of the women are beautiful" (017-006).

The Genoese women, he notes, are "as dressy and as tasteful and as graceful as they could possibly be without being angels. However, angels are not very dressy, I believe. At least the angels in pictures are not — they wear nothing but wings" (017-006). The young women were "robed in a cloud of white from head to foot," wearing only "a filmy sort of veil, which falls down their backs like a white mist" (017-006).

The evening promenade provided even more evidence. The Genoese had "a pleasant fashion of promenading in a large park on the top of a hill in the center of the city, from six till nine in the evening, and then eating ices in a neighboring garden" (017-007). Twain saw two thousand persons, "chiefly young ladies and gentlemen," dressed "in the very latest Paris fashions," with ladies' robes glinting "among the trees like so many snowflakes" (017-007). He concluded: "I never saw such a freshet of loveliness before. I did not see how a man of only ordinary decision of character could marry here, because before he could get his mind made up he would fall in love with somebody else" (017-007).

## Palaces and Architecture

Genoa's epithets — "The Superb" and the "City of Palaces" — drew Twain's scrutiny. He acknowledged the palaces were "sumptuous inside, but they are very rusty without and make no pretensions to architectural magnificence" (017-010). "'Genoa the Superb' would be a felicitous title if it referred to the women" (017-010). The interior visits to "immense thick-walled piles, with great stone staircases, tesselated marble pavements" and "grand salons hung with pictures by Rubens, Guido, Titian, Paul Veronese" were undermined by the fact that "the folks were all out in the country for the summer" (017-011). The silent, empty halls with their "undertaker-looking servant" who "stood stiff and stark and unsmiling in his petrified livery" left Twain "praying that the roof would fall in on these dispiriting flunkies" (017-011).

The exteriors, however, provided rich comic material. The palaces, "solid marble palaces though they be, are in many cases of a dull pinkish color, outside, and from pavement to eaves are pictured with Genoese battle scenes, with monstrous Jupiters and Cupids, and with familiar illustrations from Grecian mythology" (017-020). Where paint had peeled, "the effect is not happy. A noseless Cupid or a Jupiter with an eye out or a Venus with a fly-blister on her breast, are not attractive features in a picture" (017-020). Twain compared the frescoed walls to "the tall van, plastered with fanciful bills and posters, that follows the bandwagon of a circus about a country village" (017-020).

Yet he was awed by the sheer scale of Genoese construction: "I can not conceive of such a thing as Genoa in ruins. Such massive arches, such ponderous substructions as support these towering broad-winged edifices, we have seldom seen before; and surely the great blocks of stone of which these edifices are built can never decay; walls that are as thick as an ordinary American doorway is high cannot crumble" (017-021).

## Historical Significance

Twain provides a capsule history of Genoa's medieval power. The republics of Genoa and Pisa "were very powerful in the Middle Ages. Their ships filled the Mediterranean, and they carried on an extensive commerce with Constantinople and Syria" (017-022). The Saracens captured and pillaged Genoa nine hundred years before, but Genoa and Pisa allied and "besieged the Saracen colonies in Sardinia and the Balearic Isles with an obstinacy that maintained its pristine vigor and held to its purpose for forty long years" (017-022). The victories were divided "equably among their great patrician families," and "descendants of some of those proud families still inhabit the palaces of Genoa, and trace in their own features a resemblance to the grim knights whose portraits hang in their stately halls" (017-022).

But that was the past. "Genoa's greatness has degenerated into an unostentatious commerce in velvets and silver filagree-work. They say that each European town has its specialty. These filagree things are Genoa's specialty" (017-024). The silver smiths take "silver ingots and work them up into all manner of graceful and beautiful forms," creating "bunches of flowers, from flakes and wires of silver, that counterfeit the delicate creations the frost weaves upon a windowpane" (017-024). A miniature silver temple, with "fluted columns, whose Corinthian capitals and rich entablatures, whose spire, statues, bells, and ornate lavishness of sculpture were wrought in polished silver," is described as "a wonder of beauty" (017-024).

## Churches and the Cathedral of San Lorenzo

Genoa, Twain discovered, had a church density unmatched elsewhere: "I think there is a church every three or four hundred yards all over town. The streets are sprinkled from end to end with shovel-hatted, long-robed, well-fed priests, and the church bells by dozens are pealing all the day long, nearly" (017-013). Friars, "with shaven head, long, coarse robe, rope girdle and beads," looked like "consummate famine-breeders. They are all fat and serene" (017-013).

The Cathedral of San Lorenzo was "about as notable a building as we have found in Genoa. It is vast, and has colonnades of noble pillars, and a great organ, and the customary pomp of gilded moldings, pictures, frescoed ceilings, and so forth" (017-014). The guides claimed half the cathedral "was a Jewish synagogue before the Saviour was born, and that no alteration had been made in it since that time. We doubted the statement, but did it reluctantly. We would much rather have believed it" (017-014).

The Chapel of St. John the Baptist was a particular target of Twain's irreverence. Women were "only allowed to enter it on one day in the year, on account of the animosity they still cherish against the sex because of the murder of the Saint to gratify a caprice of Herodias" (017-015). The marble chest supposedly containing St. John's ashes was wrapped with a chain that "had confined him when he was in prison. We did not desire to disbelieve these statements, and yet we could not feel certain that they were correct — partly because we could have broken that chain, and so could St. John, and partly because we had seen St. John's ashes before, in another church. We could not bring ourselves to think St. John had two sets of ashes" (017-015).

## The Guide and Christopher Columbus

The Genoese guide provided some of Twain's best comic material. Claiming to be "the most gifted linguist in Genoa, as far as English was concerned," he declared "only two persons in the city beside himself could talk the language at all" (017-012). The guide "showed us the birthplace of Christopher Columbus, and after we had reflected in silent awe before it for fifteen minutes, he said it was not the birthplace of Columbus, but of Columbus' grandmother!" (017-012). When challenged, "he only shrugged his shoulders and answered in barbarous Italian" (017-012). In a later reflection, Twain noted: "The guides in Genoa are delighted to secure an American party, because Americans so much wonder, and deal so much in sentiment and emotion before any relic of Columbus. Our guide there fidgeted about as if he had swallowed a spring mattress" (027-024).

## Night Streets and Final Impressions

Twain's last image of Genoa is of its nocturnal character: "When we have been prowling at midnight through the gloomy crevices they call streets, where no footfalls but ours were echoing, where only ourselves were abroad, and lights appeared only at long intervals and at a distance, and mysteriously disappeared again" — the memory of a cave he knew at home was always in his mind, "with its lofty passages, its silence and solitude, its shrouding gloom, its sepulchral echoes, its flitting lights, and more than all, its sudden revelations of branching crevices and corridors where we least expected them" (017-025).

The final stop was the cemetery — "a burial place intended to accommodate 60,000 bodies" — a "vast marble collonaded corridor" far more beautiful than the palaces, with "monuments, tombs, and sculptured figures that are exquisitely wrought and are full of grace and beauty" (017-027). "They are new and snowy; every outline is perfect, every feature guiltless of mutilation, flaw, or blemish; and therefore, to us these far-reaching ranks of bewitching forms are a hundred fold more lovely than the damaged and dingy statuary they have saved from the wreck of ancient art" (017-027).

Twain would later recall the garden of Count Pallavicini, near Genoa, as a "mimic land of enchantment" where one passes through a yellow-tinted glass pane to see "the faintest, softest, richest picture that ever graced the dream of a dying Saint" (049-019). Through that contrived window, looking back at the old "city of palaces," "everything is golden-rich, and mellow, and dreamy as a vision of Paradise" (049-019).
