# Christopher Columbus

Christopher Columbus (1451--1506) was the Genoese navigator whose 1492 voyage inaugurated European contact with the Americas. In *The Innocents Abroad*, Twain encounters Columbus through his birthplace, relics, and reputation as an icon of discovery---while also subjecting the tourist industry around his memory to irreverent wit.

## Overview

The travelers first encounter Columbus as a chronological benchmark. In the Azores, Twain notes the islands were "one hundred years old when Columbus discovered America" (006-003). Later in Tangier, a crumbling wall "was old when Columbus discovered America" (008-005), making Columbus's achievement the pivot between ancient and modern worlds.

Columbus becomes a major presence when the party reaches Genoa. There the guide, "the most gifted linguist in Genoa" (017-012), first claims to show Columbus's birthplace, only to reverse himself and declare it the home of his grandmother instead. The moment typifies Twain's satire of gullible tourists and unreliable guides.

## In the Travelogue

Twain's fullest engagement with Columbus occurs in Rome, where he meditates on the nature of discovery itself. In a rhapsodic passage, he places Columbus among the great discoverers: "Columbus, in the Pinta's shrouds, when he swung his hat above a fabled sea and gazed abroad upon an unknown world!" (026-002). For Twain, the defining human joy is discovery---and Columbus stands as its archetype.

The travelers visit Genoa's municipal palace to view Columbus's handwriting. The guide, "full of animation---full of impatience" (027-024), presents it: "I show you ze letter writing by Christopher Colombo!" (027-025). The Doctor examines it and says, "Why, I have seen boys in America only fourteen years old that could write better than that" (027-035), leaving the guide shaken up (027-038).

The guide then offers "a beautiful bust---for it was beautiful" (027-040) of Columbus. The party asks absurd questions: "Well, what did *he* do?" (027-045), "Is---is he dead?" (027-047), and "Ah---which is the bust and which is the pedestal?" (027-057). Genoa's guides "are delighted to secure an American party" (027-024) because Americans are so susceptible to awe before Columbus's relics.

In Venice, Twain returns to Columbus in a serious vein while criticizing the monotony of Old Master martyr paintings. He wishes Titian had painted "Columbus returning in chains from the discovery of a world" (023-022) rather than yet another saint---suggesting that Twain, beneath the satire, regards Columbus as a genuinely worthy subject for great art.

## Historical Context

Columbus was born in the Republic of Genoa in 1451 and went to sea at an early age. After years of seeking sponsorship, he secured backing from Ferdinand and Isabella for a westward voyage, departing in August 1492 with the Nina, the Pinta, and the Santa Maria. His landfall in the Bahamas on October 12, 1492, initiated European colonization of the Americas.

Columbus made four voyages in total. His later years were marked by political difficulties: he was arrested and returned to Spain in chains in 1500, the very scene Twain wished Titian had painted. He died in Valladolid in 1506. By Twain's era, Columbus had become an American folk hero, and Genoa proudly claimed him as a native son whose relics were prized tourist attractions.

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's treatment of Columbus is characteristically layered. In the discovery passage of Chapter 26, Twain's admiration is genuine and unironic---Columbus is "one of the men who have really *lived*---who have actually comprehended what pleasure is---who have crowded long lifetimes of ecstasy into a single moment" (026-002). This is one of the book's rare passages of unguarded enthusiasm.

Yet confronting the physical relics---the handwriting, the bust---Twain's skepticism reasserts itself. The relics become a focal point for his satire of European guides and American tourists who "deal so much in sentiment and emotion before any relic of Columbus" (027-024). Twain's method is not to deny Columbus's greatness but to puncture performative veneration.

This dual perspective---reverence for the achievement, mockery of its commodification---is consistent with Twain's treatment of sacred subjects throughout *The Innocents Abroad*. He can admire the man who "swung his hat above a fabled sea" while simultaneously tormenting the guide who expects him to swoon over a letter in the municipal palace.
