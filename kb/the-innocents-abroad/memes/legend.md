# Legend

A traditional story, often of religious or semi-historical origin, transmitted through oral or written tradition — a category of narrative that Mark Twain encountered constantly from local guides and church authorities, and which he subjected to his characteristic blend of curiosity and satirical skepticism.

## Overview

Legends are perhaps the single most frequently contested category of information in *The Innocents Abroad*. At nearly every important religious site, Twain and his fellow pilgrims are presented with stories — of miracles, martyrdoms, relics, and holy events — that the local guides and clergy offer as established fact. Twain's response to these narratives ranges from polite credulity to open mockery, and his treatment of legend forms one of the book's most persistent themes: the tension between faith, historical evidence, and what a skeptical 19th-century American is willing to believe.

The word "legend" itself recurs with telling frequency. Twain uses it as a framing device for stories he finds dubious, a polite way of saying "this is probably not true." His driver's tale of the Countess of Cellere is introduced as "a legend," and by the end Twain can only exclaim, "Splendid legend — splendid lie — drive on" [021-060].

## In Twain's Narrative

Twain's first extended encounter with legend-as-tourist-content comes in Genoa, where a guide regales the party with a "tiresome repetition of a legend" about Christopher Columbus [007-013][007-019]. The experience sets a pattern: tour guides throughout Italy and the Holy Land narrate miraculous stories that strain the credulity of Twain and his companions. In Rome, the guide tells the "legend of Ara Coeli," the story of a bleeding heart painting [027-001]. In Milan, Twain encounters the "legend of Brother Thomas" with its picturesque horrors [028-001].

The most sustained treatment of legend comes in the Holy Land chapters. At the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, Twain recounts "the legend" of the finding of the three crosses, noting with dry understatement that the story is "well authenticated in many legends that are among the most trustworthy" — a sentence whose irony is unmistakable [053-011][053-014]. The "legend of St. Veronica's handkerchief" receives similar treatment [054-001], as does the story of the Seven Sleepers at Ephesus [040-001][040-026].

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 19th century, European travel was saturated with legend. Guidebooks of the period — particularly those covering Catholic pilgrimage sites — presented miraculous stories alongside historical facts, often making no clear distinction between the two. For Protestant travelers, this blending of legend and history was one of the most striking features of Catholic Europe, and responses ranged from genuine fascination to reformist outrage.

The critical study of hagiography and religious legend was also advancing during this period. The Bollandist scholars had been systematically examining saints' lives for centuries, and the 19th century saw the rise of modern historical-critical methods that would transform the study of religious texts. Twain, though no scholar, instinctively applies a similar skepticism — he wants evidence, not assertion, and he is merciless toward those who cannot provide it.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward legend is more nuanced than simple mockery. He is genuinely interested in the stories themselves — he retells them at length, often with evident narrative pleasure — and he recognizes their cultural and emotional power. The "shadowy legends" of Ephesus move him [040-010], and even the most dubious Catholic legends receive more curiosity than contempt.

His real target is not legend itself but the credulity that treats legend as fact. When a guide presents a story as unquestionably true, Twain's skepticism activates. When a legend is offered as legend — a story that may or may not be true, but which has been told for centuries — he is willing to listen with genuine interest:

> "According to the legend, this great discovery elicited a shout of joy..." [053-011]

The phrase "according to the legend" is Twain's diplomatic formula: it reports the story without endorsing it, leaving the reader free to draw their own conclusions.
