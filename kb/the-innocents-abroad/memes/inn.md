Hotels and inns serve as way stations through *The Innocents Abroad*, providing Twain with recurring opportunities for domestic observation, social comedy, and cultural comparison. The inn is where the traveler rests, eats, and writes — and where the differences between nations are measured in bedsheets, breakfasts, and the competence of waiters [019-025].

Twain's treatment of hotels follows a consistent pattern: arrival, negotiation, assessment of accommodations, and — above all — evaluation of the food. "At every hotel we stop at we always have to make out that the bill-of-fare is in French," he complains, describing the ritual of deciphering menus as a minor trial of travel [019-025]. The hotel dinner becomes a site of linguistic anxiety and culinary adventure.

## European Hotels

The European hotels range from grand establishments to peculiar local inns. In Paris, Twain stays at a hotel where the "handsome little chapel" attached to the building prompts a wry observation about French Catholicism [019-030]. In Genoa, "the hotel we live in belonged to one of the great patrician families" — a former palace converted to tourist lodging, its massive architecture unchanged by its new commercial purpose [017-023].

The Italian hotels present a particular set of challenges. "We secured rooms at the hotel, or rather, we had three beds put into one room," Twain writes of one stop, and the sardonic correction captures the casual overcrowding of mid-nineteenth-century tourist accommodation [012-018]. Yet even here, Twain finds amusement: "a really polite hotel waiter who isn't an idiot" is a rarity worth recording, and the Italian hotel waiter earns a rare compliment [011-002].

In Venice, "Our hotel sits at the water's edge" — the location is perfect, the gondolas pull up to the steps, and the city's unique geography transforms the hotel itself into a kind of theater box overlooking the Grand Canal [020-007].

## The Hotel Register

The hotel register becomes a source of sustained comedy for Twain. In Venice, he copies out the mangled English of fellow travelers who have attempted to write their addresses in the register: "We laugh at Englishmen when they write their address in dilapidated French in foreign hotel registers!" [023-016]. The register is a palimpsest of national vanity — every traveler trying, and mostly failing, to pass as cosmopolitan.

## Holy Land Accommodations

In the Holy Land, the inn gives way to the encampment. The comfortable European hotel, with its printed menu and its chapel, is replaced by tents and rough shelters. Twain does not dwell on this loss — the Holy Land is approached as pilgrimage, not tourism — but the contrast between the "excellent dinner at the principal hotel" of earlier chapters and the Spartan conditions of Palestine camp life measures the distance the journey has traveled [005-014].

> "The hotel fare is better here than anywhere else in Syria," Twain allows of Damascus, and the remark is freighted with relief — a return, however brief, to the comfort that the European tourist has learned to expect [029-002].
