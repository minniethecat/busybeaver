# Disease

Physical illness and its manifestations — from the cholera fears that shadowed 19th-century travelers to the biblical diseases Twain encountered in the Holy Land, forming a dark counterpoint to the book's more comic observations.

## Overview

Disease threads through *The Innocents Abroad* as a persistent undercurrent to the voyage's more celebrated adventures. Twain's narrative acknowledges, often in passing but with telling detail, the ever-present reality of illness in 19th-century travel: the quarantines that regulated Mediterranean ports, the cholera that haunted the Middle East, the leprosy and other afflictions visible in the streets of Damascus and Jerusalem, and the agricultural blights that could destroy an island's economy.

Twain's treatment of disease ranges from the practical (the Azores wine blight as economic history) to the theological (the biblical healing narratives as sites of Protestant skepticism toward Catholic miracle claims). Throughout, his characteristic blend of reportage and irony serves him well: disease is a subject that rewards the clear-eyed observer, and Twain's refusal to sentimentalize suffering gives his disease passages a documentary power that complements the book's more celebrated comic set-pieces.

## In Twain's Narrative

Disease first enters the narrative in the Azores, where Twain reports that a blight "killed all the vines fifteen years ago," destroying the island's Madeira wine industry [006-003]. This early mention establishes disease not just as a human affliction but as an economic and ecological force.

In the Holy Land, the theme intensifies. Twain describes the "maimed, malformed and diseased humanity that throng the holy places" of Jerusalem, creating a stark contrast between the sacred associations of the sites and the suffering of their modern visitors [053-005]. In Damascus, he writes with unflinching directness about leprosy: no one can "appreciate the horror of this disease until he looks upon it in all its ghastliness" [044-027].

The New Testament healing narratives provide a framework for Twain's observations. He describes the pool of Bethesda, "where the swine ran down into the sea," and notes how the sick gathered at sacred sites hoping for cures [047-025]. At the same time, he remains the skeptical Protestant observer, contrasting the biblical accounts with what he sees as contemporary Catholic superstition surrounding healing relics and shrines.

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, disease was a constant companion of travel. Cholera pandemics had swept through Europe and the Middle East repeatedly during the 19th century, and quarantine stations were a routine feature of Mediterranean ports. Travelers to the Holy Land were warned about the prevalence of leprosy, typhus, and other diseases associated with poverty and poor sanitation. The germ theory of disease was still controversial — Louis Pasteur's definitive experiments were only a few years in the past — and many travelers carried a mix of scientific and superstitious beliefs about illness.

The ruined vineyards of Madeira, which Twain mentions, reflected a real ecological disaster: the phylloxera epidemic that devastated European vineyards in the late 19th century. The disease, caused by a North American insect to which European vines had no resistance, would eventually transform the global wine industry.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's approach to disease is characteristically unsentimental. He does not avert his gaze from suffering, nor does he indulge in the kind of pious reflection that many 19th-century travel writers brought to scenes of affliction. His description of leprosy in Damascus is notably direct, even harsh: it is a horror, and he says so plainly. This honesty is of a piece with his broader rejection of the romantic conventions of travel writing.

At the same time, Twain is not indifferent. His account of the diseased and disabled pilgrims at Jerusalem's holy sites carries an implicit critique of a system that exploits the desperate:

> "He healed the centurion's servant..." [047-035]
