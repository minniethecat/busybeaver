# Customs and Inspections

The customs inspections and baggage searches that greet the *Quaker City* pilgrims at every port of call — bureaucratic rituals Twain treats as comic theater, casting customs officials as pompous antagonists whose examinations range from the perfunctory to the absurd, and whose procedures serve the vanity of officialdom far more than any genuine regulatory purpose.

## Overview

In the port cities of 1860s Europe and the Mediterranean, customs inspection was an unavoidable ritual of arrival. Travelers' baggage was opened, searched, and (in theory) scrutinized for contraband goods. Passports were examined and visas stamped. For many visitors, the customhouse was the first point of contact with a foreign country — and for Twain, it was the first opportunity for satire. The customs encounters in *The Innocents Abroad* form a recurring comic motif: officials who barely glance at satchels, inspectors who cannot understand the jokes they confiscate, and a system that makes crossing a border feel like a trial without a crime.

Twain's treatment of customs aligns with his broader satirical method: European bureaucracy is not evil, merely ridiculous. The pilgrims are never seriously harmed by customs procedures, only inconvenienced, bewildered, and occasionally robbed of a joke or a souvenir. But in accumulating these minor humiliations, Twain constructs a portrait of the Old World as a place where official power exists to assert itself, not to serve.

## In Twain's Narrative

The pilgrims' first customs encounter occurs at Marseilles. Twain, Dan, and the Doctor contract with a boatman who they (mis)understand to be taking them to their hotel. The Doctor confidently identifies the destination: "he is going to *allez* to the *douain*. Means he is going to the hotel" (010-025). The mistake is soon revealed: "It was easy to remember then that the *douain* was the customhouse and not the hotel" (010-026). Yet the inspection itself proves anticlimactic:

> "With winning French politeness the officers merely opened and closed our satchels, declined to examine our passports, and sent us on our way." (010-026)

The comic deflation is complete: the dreaded customs procedure turns out to be a perfunctory gesture, the officials so indifferent they will not even look at the passports they are presumably paid to inspect. Twain withholds the obvious comment — that this politeness renders the entire customs apparatus pointless — allowing the absurdity to speak for itself.

A more elaborate customs comedy unfolds at Civita Vecchia, when the pilgrims arrive with baggage to be searched at the railway depot. An Italian officer discovers one of Twain's written jokes among his effects and subjects it to elaborate scrutiny: "They took one of my ablest jokes and read it over carefully twice and then read it backwards. But it was too deep for them" (025-024). The scene escalates into bureaucratic farce:

> "At length a veteran officer spelled it over deliberately and shook his head three or four times and said that in his opinion it was seditious." (025-025)

Twain attempts to clarify, but "the more I explained the more they could not understand it, and when they desisted at last, I could not even understand it myself" (025-025). The joke is confiscated as an "incendiary document, leveled at the government" (025-026), and Twain imagines it will be filed away among "the criminal archives of Rome" as "a mysterious infernal machine which would have blown up like a mine and scattered the good Pope all around" (025-026). The passage transforms a routine baggage search into a parable about the collision between American humor and European officialdom: the inspectors are not malicious, merely incapable of recognizing a joke — and that very incapacity becomes the joke.

At Ephesus, the customs dynamic inverts — here it is not what the pilgrims bring in but what they attempt to take out. The party has "gathered up fragments of sculptured marbles and breaking ornaments from the interior work of the Mosques" and transported them "at a cost of infinite trouble and fatigue, five miles on muleback to the railway depot" (041-002). But a government officer with "an order from Constantinople to *look out for our party*" (041-002) compels everyone to "disgorge" their relics. Twain's response is characteristically perverse: "I never resist a temptation to plunder a stranger's premises without feeling insufferably vain about it" (041-002). He takes pride in the official rebuke as a kind of credential — to be worth watching is a mark of distinction.

The satirical linkage of Michelangelo to customs regulations — Twain complains that the ubiquitous Renaissance master "designed the piers of Leghorn and the custom house regulations of Civita Vecchia" (027-010) — further reduces customs bureaucracy to the status of a tourist nuisance, no more significant than the endless parade of Michelangelo attributions that weary the pilgrims across Italy.

## Cultural and Historical Context

In mid-19th century Europe, customs inspection was a serious administrative function deeply tied to state revenue. France, Italy, and the Ottoman Empire all maintained elaborate customs apparatuses, and tariffs on imported goods constituted a significant portion of government income. Passport systems — requiring travelers to obtain visas at each border — were still in force across much of the continent, a relic of the police-state mentality of the post-Napoleonic restoration.

For American travelers of Twain's era, customs represented a collision between two conceptions of government. The United States had no internal passport controls and minimal customs barriers between states. The pilgrims arrived in Europe expecting to move freely; what they encountered was a patchwork of inspection stations, document checks, and baggage searches that treated them as potential smugglers. Their resentment was not political philosophy but practical frustration — every hour spent at a customhouse was an hour lost from sightseeing.

The passport system provided a particular target for Twain's satire. In France, the customs officers at Marseilles "declined to examine our passports" (010-026) — an abdication of duty that exposes the theatrical nature of the whole arrangement. At Civita Vecchia, by contrast, the passport regime proves formidable: "They keep up the passport system here, but so they do in Turkey. This shows that the Papal States are as far advanced as Turkey" (025-024). The deadpan comparison of papal governance to Ottoman rule is Twain at his most subversive, reducing both civilizations to equals in bureaucratic absurdity.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain approaches customs with the same irreverent humor he brings to guides, hotels, and quarantine. He never mounts a principled critique of border controls or trade policy; his objection is aesthetic and personal. Customs officials are comic foils because they take seriously what Twain considers trivial — the contents of a satchel, the political implications of a joke, the shards of ancient marble that no living person cares about. The officials' solemnity in performing meaningless inspections supplies the humor; Twain's pose of innocent victimhood supplies the narrative charm.

Yet beneath the comedy lies a consistent American faith in the superiority of practical common sense over bureaucratic formality. When the French officers "merely opened and closed our satchels," when the Italian inspectors "spelled it over deliberately" and returned a verdict of sedition, Twain is asking an implicit question: what purpose does any of this serve? The customs apparatus, in his treatment, exists not to prevent smuggling or collect revenue but to give employment to men in uniform and to remind travelers that they have entered a world where authority is arbitrary.

The most revealing passage may be his response to the Ephesus confiscation. Rather than protesting the injustice of being singled out, Twain acknowledges it was "a wise, a just, and a well-deserved rebuke" (041-002). He concedes that "the same precautions would have been taken against *any* travelers" (041-002) and that the English excavation company "can not afford to run the risk of having their hospitality abused by travelers, especially since travelers are such notorious scorners of honest behavior" (041-002). This rare moment of self-awareness — admitting that the pilgrims' relic-plundering genuinely warranted official intervention — gives the customs satire a more complex moral texture.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- **Marseilles (Chapter 10)**: The pilgrims' first customs encounter. The Doctor mistakes the *douain* (customhouse) for the hotel. The French officers open and close satchels without actually inspecting them and decline to examine passports — polite and utterly ineffectual (010-025, 010-026).
- **Civita Vecchia (Chapter 25)**: Baggage inspection at the railway depot. An Italian officer confiscates one of Twain's jokes as an "incendiary document" after reading it forwards and backwards without comprehension. Twain imagines it entering the Papal criminal archives as a potential infernal machine (025-024, 025-025, 025-026).
- **Civita Vecchia Passports (Chapter 25)**: Twain's passport is examined by a policeman on the wharf, who then confiscates it for twelve hours. Twain speculates the officials "thought I wanted to take the town" (025-024).
- **Ephesus (Chapter 41)**: A government officer with orders from Constantinople compels the pilgrims to surrender the sculptural fragments they have pried from Ephesus's mosques, transported five miles by mule (041-002).
- **Michelangelo's Custom House (Chapter 27)**: Twain's list of things Michelangelo supposedly designed includes "the custom house regulations of Civita Vecchia" (027-010) — reducing customs bureaucracy to yet another wearisome Italian attribution.
