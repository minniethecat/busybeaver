# Procession

A formal, organized group of people moving in a ceremonial manner, typically for religious, civic, or celebratory purposes — a common spectacle encountered by Mark Twain throughout his 1867 Mediterranean and Holy Land voyage.

## Overview

Processions appear throughout *The Innocents Abroad* as one of Twain's most frequently observed public spectacles. From the grand religious ceremonies of Catholic Europe to the solemn pilgrimages of the Holy Land, processions offered Twain a window into the rituals, social hierarchies, and theatricality of the cultures he visited. He describes them with his characteristic mix of genuine fascination and satirical detachment, often noting the gap between the solemn intent and the chaotic reality of these public displays.

In the context of 19th-century travel, processions were both an attraction for foreign visitors and a routine expression of communal identity. For Twain, they served as moments of vivid description and cultural commentary, revealing as much about the onlookers — including himself and his fellow pilgrims — as about the participants.

## In Twain's Narrative

Twain encounters processions in nearly every major stop on the Quaker City itinerary. In Milan, he witnesses the carrying of holy relics through the streets during a religious festival [018-026]. At Rome, he describes penitents forming a procession to the Church of the Holy Cross, observing the solemn ritual with his usual eye for detail [027-086]. In Venice, processions pass by water rather than land, with gondolas and decorated boats replacing marching feet [030-007]. In Genoa, he notes the elaborate church ceremonies involving sacred vessels used in "the great public processions" [014-005].

Beyond religious contexts, Twain also describes secular processions — parades of sleighs in Russia [016-005], military displays in Paris [007-037], and the motley cavalcade of tourists and locals that the Quaker City party itself formed as it moved through foreign towns. Even the trip's departure from New York is framed as a kind of procession [001-008].

## Cultural and Historical Context

In 1860s Europe, religious processions remained central to Catholic public life, particularly in Italy, France, and Spain. These events often involved the display of relics, statues of saints, and elaborate vestments, accompanied by music, incense, and the participation of clergy and lay confraternities. For Protestant American travelers like Twain and his shipmates, these displays could seem exotic, theatrical, or even superstitious — reactions that color Twain's descriptions throughout the book.

Civic processions were equally important in 19th-century Europe. Military parades, royal entries, and patriotic demonstrations served as displays of state power and public unity. Twain's narrative captures both the pageantry and the human comedy of these events, particularly in the contrast between grand historical associations and mundane modern realities.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward processions is characteristically ambivalent. He is genuinely moved by the spectacle and historical resonance of some processions, particularly those connected to ancient Rome or the Holy Land:

> "Perhaps, when the triumphal processions of the Emperors moved over it, the ground was better kept." [026-012]

Yet he never misses an opportunity to puncture solemnity with irreverent observation. The "motliest and the worst mixed procession" he describes in Venice captures his comic sensibility [030-007]. He is fascinated by the logistics and the human element — who marches, who watches, what goes wrong — as much as by the symbolic meaning.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- **Religious procession, Milan**: Holy relics carried through the streets during a festival [018-026]
- **Penitential procession, Rome**: A general procession formed to do penance at the Church of the Holy Cross [027-086]
- **Annual procession, Genoa**: Grand procession of priests, citizens, soldiers, and civic societies [029-008]
- **Fantastic procession, Asia Minor**: A "long procession" of travelers on donkeys, described as "fantastic" and comical [040-001][040-005]
- **Sleigh procession, Russia**: "A procession of those quaint sleighs" in the snow [016-005]
- **Military parade, Paris**: Crowds gathering to hear martial music and watch soldiers march [007-037]
