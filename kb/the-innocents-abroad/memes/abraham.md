# Abraham

Abraham was the dragoman — the contracted interpreter, guide, and logistical manager — who led Mark Twain's party of pilgrims through the Holy Land in 1867. He appears throughout the Palestine and Syria chapters as an indispensable yet exasperating figure whose politeness masked a calculating commercial operator.

## Overview

Abraham the dragoman was the central organizing figure for the Holy Land expedition, responsible for horses, tents, provisions, routes, and negotiations with local tribes and officials. Twain portrays him with a mixture of grudging respect and satirical exasperation: a man of unfailing courtesy who could explain away any delay or inconvenience with a bow and an apology, yet who always seemed to have his own interests carefully protected. In the complex world of Ottoman Palestine, where every village demanded its own tribute and every road had its hazards, Abraham was both the pilgrims' shield and their exploiter — a figure Twain treats as a necessary evil of Eastern travel.

## In the Travelogue

Abraham first appears at 3 p.m. on the day of departure from Damascus, marshaling the horses for the long journey through Palestine (041-009). Twain immediately establishes the dragoman's dual nature: polite to a fault but fundamentally self-interested. When delays occurred, Abraham would come "bowing in and apologizing" for the inconvenience (041-014) — a performance of deference that Twain clearly viewed with skepticism.

The dragoman's control over the expedition was near-absolute. He decided when the bell rang in the morning (half-past five) (042-004) and where the party would stop for the night, often declaring a halt when they had traveled "a very short day's run" because "the dragoman does not want to go further" (046-014). When the pilgrims wanted fresh horses, Abraham claimed there were none to be had; when Jack's horse died, the dragoman blamed him for it (045-028). Through it all, Abraham maintained his unshakable composure, smiling and shaking his head at complaints (049-009). The pilgrims and dragoman occasionally united in common cause, as when they "shouted themselves hoarse" together at uncooperative locals (047-030).

## Historical Context

A dragoman in the 19th-century Ottoman Empire was far more than a translator. The role encompassed negotiating contracts with local sheikhs, arranging protection money (tribute) for safe passage through tribal territories, provisioning the expedition, and managing the complex logistics of desert travel. Dragomans typically held a near-monopoly position once a party was committed to the road, since travelers had no independent means of securing horses, guides, or safe conduct. Twain's party had contracted with Abraham to furnish "everything" (041-008), making them entirely dependent on his services — a dependency the dragoman was not shy about exploiting.

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's portrait of Abraham oscillates between satire and something approaching affection. He calls the dragoman "polite" with unmistakable irony (041-014), yet also acknowledges his competence. When describing the well at which the party halted — "of Abraham's time, no doubt" — Twain wryly notes, "I mean our dragoman Abraham, of course" (045-028), conflating the dragoman with his biblical namesake in a joke that both mocks and elevates him.

The dragoman's most memorable quality was his imperviousness to criticism. "These appeals were vain — the dragoman only smiled and shook his head" (049-009). He "laughed — not at the facetiousness" of the pilgrims' jokes but at their powerlessness (049-008). Yet for all his frustration, Twain never suggests the journey could have been managed without him. Abraham was the gatekeeper of the Holy Land, and like the biblical patriarch whose name he bore, he presided over a pilgrimage through a wilderness of his own creation.
