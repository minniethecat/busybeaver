# Valley

A low-lying area between hills or mountains — encountered by Twain in the Italian lake country and most memorably in the Holy Land, where the valleys of the Jordan and the surrounding terrain carry the weight of biblical history.

## Overview

In *The Innocents Abroad*, valleys are the soft counterpoints to mountains — places of greenery, settlement, and, in the Holy Land, intense historical significance. The "verdant slopes and valleys" that frame Lake Como [020-014] represent the European picturesque at its finest: cultivated, charming, shot through with Italian sunlight. But Twain's valleys also include the harsher landscapes of the Holy Land, where biblical battles were fought and ancient cities once stood.

The valley in Twain's travelogue carries a dual identity. In Italy and Switzerland, valleys are part of the touristic sublime — scenery to be admired from a steamer deck or a hotel window. In the Holy Land, valleys become archaeological and theological sites: the Jordan Valley, the Valley of Elah (where David fought Goliath), the valley near Ephesus. Twain moves between aesthetic appreciation and historical imagination, sometimes in the same paragraph.

## In Twain's Narrative

Valleys appear most prominently during the European lake tour and the extended Holy Land journey. On Lake Como, "domes and verdant slopes and valleys" bar the view [020-014], creating the layered landscape that travelers had crossed an ocean to see. Near Athens, villages "nestle in the valleys or roost upon the lofty perpendicular cliffs" [032-012] — a pastoral vision of Greek life.

But the valleys that matter most to Twain are in the Holy Land. The Jordan Valley — "a level, narrow valley" — becomes the scene of biblical reenactment, as the pilgrims imagine the Israelites crossing into the Promised Land [030-020]. The "green valley, five or six miles wide" near the Sea of Galilee [046-007] is where "He made this valley, so quiet now, a reeking slaughter-pen" [046-018] — the site of ancient battles. The valley near Ephesus frames the "level green valley (a marsh, in fact)" [040-009], where Twain's characteristic deflation — the parenthetical "a marsh, in fact" — undercuts the biblical sublime.

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the biblical imagination that shaped the pilgrims' expectations, valleys were charged sites. The Valley of Jehoshaphat, the Vale of Siddim, the Valley of Elah — these names resonated with Sunday-school associations. For the American Protestants aboard the *Quaker City*, seeing these valleys in person was a form of religious verification, proof that the Bible was geography as well as scripture.

Twain understood the power of these associations but refused to be bound by them. His valleys are real places: dusty, hot, and often smaller than the imagination had painted them. The Jordan, for instance, turns out to be a narrow, muddy stream, and its valley, though "level" and "narrow," is hardly the grand panorama of biblical illustration. Twain records the discrepancy without cynicism — he is genuinely interested in the gap between expectation and reality.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's valley descriptions are among his most evocative landscape passages. He has a painter's eye for the layered composition of Italian lake scenery — "domes and verdant slopes and valleys" — and a historian's awareness of what happened in the Holy Land's "green valley, five or six miles wide." The mix of aesthetic response and historical imagination makes these passages richer than his mountain descriptions, which tend toward the purely visual.

> "He made this valley, so quiet now, a reeking slaughter-pen." [046-018]

This sentence, from the Holy Land chapters, captures Twain's method: the valley's present quiet is thrown against its violent past, producing a frisson that is neither piety nor mockery but something in between — a traveler's honest response to a landscape saturated with meaning he can neither fully accept nor dismiss.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- **Lake Como valleys** — "domes and verdant slopes and valleys" framing the lake [020-014]
- **The Jordan Valley** — "a level, narrow valley" at the Jordan River [030-020]
- **Athenian valleys** — villages that "nestle in the valleys" beneath the Acropolis [032-012]
- **Valley near Ephesus** — "a level green valley, (a marsh, in fact)" [040-009]
- **The Zeb Dana valley** — crossed by the pilgrims near Damascus [044-003]
- **The Valley of Elah** — where David fought Goliath, visited during the Holy Land tour
- **Valley near the Sea of Galilee** — "a green valley, five or six miles wide" [046-007]; the site of biblical battles [046-018]
- **Valley behind Pion** — near Ephesus, "stretched abroad, far over the valley" [040-021]
- **Messengers crossing valleys** — "over mountains — valleys — uninhabited deserts" [037-010]
