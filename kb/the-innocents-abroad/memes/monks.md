# Monks

Catholic monks in *The Innocents Abroad* are the robed religious figures who populate the churches, convents, and holy sites Mark Twain visits from Italy to the Holy Land. They function simultaneously as guides to sacred places, custodians of dubious relics and legends, and unwitting embodiments of the superstition and priestcraft that Twain's Protestant American sensibility finds alternately amusing, grotesque, and fraudulent.

## Overview

Throughout his journey, Twain encounters monks in every Catholic country and at nearly every sacred site. They appear as Capuchin friars in Rome displaying chambers decorated entirely with the bones of their deceased brethren, as Latin monks in Nazareth who present grotto after grotto as the authentic dwelling-places of the Holy Family, and as black-robed Italian monks endlessly chanting in the gloomy aisles of Jerusalem's Church of the Holy Sepulchre. For Twain, the monk is a type: industrious in the preservation of relics and legends, serenely untroubled by the incredibility of the traditions he presents, and entirely at ease within a system of belief that the author regards as an elaborate fraud.

The monks serve a practical narrative function as well. They are the gatekeepers of the holy places, the storytellers who provide Twain with the material for his most sustained satire of Catholic piety and relic-veneration. Without the monks' legends — of the True Cross discovered by St. Helena, of the chapel built around the exact centre of the earth, of Godfrey of Bouillon's enchanted sword — much of the book's richest comic material would not exist.

## In Twain's Narrative

Twain's first mention of monks comes in Genoa, where he observes "coarse-robed monks" among the daily street life (017-026). In Genoa's churches he also notes "friars of orders gray, with shaven head, long, coarse robe, rope girdle and beads" — but adds that "these worthies suffer in the flesh and do penance all their lives, I suppose, but they look like consummate famine-breeders. They are all fat and serene" (017-013). The irony — ascetics who appear well-fed — establishes the satirical tone that will persist throughout.

The most extended interaction with a monk occurs in Rome at the Capuchin Convent (028-002 to 028-021). There, a "good-natured monk" guides Twain through underground chambers where the bones of four thousand deceased brothers have been arranged into arches, pyramids, and elaborate frescoes of vertebrae, kneecaps, and toe-nails. The monk takes "a high pride in his curious show" (028-003) and narrates, with businesslike detachment, the tragic love story of Brother Thomas while pointing to the various fragments of his skeleton. When asked if all the brethren expect to lie in this ossuary, the monk answers quietly, "We must all lie here at last" (028-018). Twain is struck by the monk's utter lack of distress at this prospect — "he even looked as if he were thinking, with complacent vanity, that his own skull would look well on top of the heap" (028-019).

In Nazareth, Twain confronts what he calls "these gifted Latin monks" who "never do anything by halves" (050-007). At the Convent of the Annunciation, they present not only the grotto where Mary received the angel's message, but also "the Virgin's Kitchen, and even her sitting-room, where she and Joseph watched the infant Saviour play with Hebrew toys." Twain finds this proliferation of grottoes — the Virgin's dwelling in Nazareth, her hiding-place in Bethlehem, the Saviour's birthplace, the site of Herod's slaughter of the innocents, all conveniently preserved in living rock — plainly fraudulent. Yet he acknowledges a pragmatic admiration: "It is an imposture — this grotto stuff — but it is one that all men ought to thank the Catholics for" because it preserves the memory of sacred localities for future generations (050-007). The monks, he concedes, "know how to drive a stake through a pleasant tradition that will hold it to its place forever."

In Jerusalem, monks are ubiquitous in the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. Twain describes "black-robed, animal-looking Italian monks, with candles in their hands, who were chanting something in Latin" (053-010), and notes that "they perform everywhere — all over the vast building, and at all hours" (053-010). At the end of his survey of the church's holy sites, he concludes that most of them — the Sepulchre itself, the place where Mary stood, the spot of the angel's appearance — are "imaginary holy places created by the monks" (053-032). He reserves genuine belief only for the location of the Crucifixion.

The monks also appear as guardians of the extravagant legends that Twain recounts with deadpan irony. Discussing Godfrey of Bouillon's sword, he writes that "these statements are all well authenticated in many legends that are among the most trustworthy legends the good old Catholic monks preserve" (053-014). The monks call the grotto where St. Helena found the crosses the "Chapel of the Invention of the Cross," a name Twain notes "is unfortunate, because it leads the ignorant to imagine that a tacit acknowledgment is thus made that the tradition that Helena found the true Cross here is a fiction — an invention" (053-027). At Ramleh, the pilgrims "stayed all night with the good monks" (056-006) before their final ride to Jaffa.

At Jerusalem, he also notes that beggars, cripples and monks together "compass you about, and make you pay for seeing" the holy sites (055-042), placing monks alongside the other figures of persistent demand that characterize the pilgrim's experience.

## Twain's Attitude

Twain's treatment of monks is consistently satirical but never purely hostile. His skepticism is directed at the system of belief and the traffic in relics that the monks represent, rather than at the monks as individuals. The Capuchin friar who guides him through the bone crypt is described as "good-natured" (028-003), and Twain acknowledges the genuine artistry involved in the ossuary's construction. The monks at Ramleh who give the pilgrims lodging are simply "good monks" (056-006).

> "It is an imposture — this grotto stuff — but it is one that all men ought to thank the Catholics for. Wherever they ferret out a lost locality made holy by some Scriptural event, they straightway build a massive — almost imperishable — church there, and preserve the memory of that locality for the gratification of future generations." (050-007)

This passage encapsulates Twain's ambivalence. He knows the grottoes are fake, yet he respects the monks' dedication to preserving traditions that give pilgrims a tangible connection to sacred history. The satire lands not on the monks' personal failings but on the absurdity of the legends they maintain and the credulity they require of the faithful.

Twain also uses the monks to sharpen his contrast between Catholic and Protestant approaches to holy sites. He writes that "the world owes the Catholics its good will even for the happy rascality of hewing out these bogus grottoes," because "if it had been left to Protestants to do this most worthy work, we would not even know where Jerusalem is today" (050-007). The monks' industrious fraud, in Twain's view, has at least served a preservative function that Protestant neglect would not have achieved.

## Related Concepts

The monk is closely linked to several of Twain's recurring themes. The veneration of [[relics]] — fragments of the True Cross appearing in every church, enough nails of the cross "to make a keg," Veronica's handkerchief in multiple cathedrals simultaneously — is presented largely through the monks' custodianship. The practice of [[baksheesh]] blends with the monks' role when they demand payment for showing sacred sites. And the entire system of holy-site tourism, so central to the pilgrimage, depends on the monks' willingness to identify, authenticate, and display the locations of biblical events.

The monks also embody the broader theme of priestcraft that Twain identifies in Italy: "We were in the heart and home of priest craft — of a happy, cheerful, contented ignorance, superstition, degradation, poverty, indolence, and everlasting unaspiring worthlessness" (021-004). The monks are not singled out for this judgment — it applies to the entire Catholic religious establishment — but they are its most visible and ubiquitous representatives throughout the journey.
