# Relics

The veneration, proliferation, and commodification of holy and historical relics — a defining feature of Catholic and Orthodox devotional culture that Twain encounters, scrutinizes, and mercilessly satirizes throughout his pilgrimage across Europe and the Holy Land.

## Overview

Throughout *The Innocents Abroad*, relics form one of the most persistent themes of Twain's cultural commentary. In nearly every old church and holy site the pilgrims visit, they are shown fragments of the True Cross, nails from the Crucifixion, bones of saints, and artifacts of biblical history.

These objects are treated by their clerical custodians with the utmost reverence, yet Twain quickly notices a pattern: the supply of certain relics far exceeds what any reasonable accounting of the originals could support.

Twain's treatment of relics operates on two levels. On the surface, he catalogues the absurdity of the relic trade — the multiple skulls of saints, the "keg of nails" from the True Cross, and the several handkerchiefs of St. Veronica scattered across European cathedrals.

Beneath the humor lies a sharper critique: relics represent the institutionalized credulity that Twain, as a Protestant American, finds both baffling and faintly scandalous about Old World Catholicism. The relic economy — in which pilgrims pay to venerate, and guides collect fees to display — becomes a synecdoche for everything the excursionists find alien.

## In Twain's Narrative: Europe

Relics appear from the earliest stages of the European tour. At Notre Dame in Paris, the pilgrims are shown "some nails of the true cross, a fragment of the cross itself, a part of the crown of thorns" — objects whose authenticity Twain questions by noting they "had already seen a large piece of the true cross in a church in the Azores" (014-005).

He adds that Ferguson told them of a silver cross recovered from the Seine on an angel's instructions, observing dryly that these people "have a somewhat singular taste in the matter of relics" (014-005). The implication is clear: relic culture is not reverence but a peculiar form of credulous collecting.

The theme intensifies in Italy. At the Cathedral of San Lorenzo in Genoa, Twain visits the Chapel of St. John the Baptist, which contains a marble chest said to hold the saint's ashes. But he "had seen St. John's ashes before, in another church" and "could not bring ourselves to think St. John had two sets of ashes" (017-015).

The portrait of the Madonna painted by St. Luke also appears here, and Twain cannot resist noting "the Apostle's modesty in never once mentioning in his writings that he could paint" (017-016). The accumulating absurdities culminate in a direct protest from the narrator:

> "But isn't this relic matter a little overdone? We find a piece of the true cross in every old church we go into, and some of the nails that held it together. I would not like to be positive, but I think we have seen as much as a keg of these nails. Then there is the crown of thorns; they have part of one in Sainte Chapelle, in Paris, and part of one also in Notre Dame. And as for bones of St. Denis, I feel certain we have seen enough of them to duplicate him if necessary." (017-017)

At the Milan Cathedral, the relic display reaches its most theatrical extreme. The priests show "two of St. Paul's fingers, and one of St. Peter's; a bone of Judas Iscariot, (it was black), and also bones of all the other disciples; a handkerchief in which the Saviour had left the impression of his face" (018-026).

Among the most precious items are "a stone from the Holy Sepulchre, part of the crown of thorns, (they have a whole one at Notre Dame), a fragment of the purple robe worn by the Saviour, a nail from the Cross, and a picture of the Virgin and Child painted by the veritable hand of St. Luke. This is the second of St. Luke's Virgins we have seen" (018-026).

The parenthetical aside about Notre Dame having a complete crown of thorns is classic Twainian deadpan. These relics, the priests add, are carried in procession through the streets of Milan once a year.

In Venice, Twain encounters a different kind of relic — civic rather than ecclesiastical. At St. Mark's Cathedral, "under the altar repose the ashes of St. Mark — and Matthew, Luke and John, too, for all I know. Venice reveres those relics above all things earthly" (022-023).

He recounts the legend of how Venetian merchants stole St. Mark's bones from Alexandria, smuggling them past Muslim guards in vessels of lard.

## In the Holy Land: The Church of the Holy Sepulchre

In the Holy Land, the relic theme deepens. At the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, the pilgrims encounter the most sacred ground in Christendom, yet they find it saturated with dubious relics. The Stone of Unction, where the Saviour's body was prepared for burial, is concealed beneath a marble slab because "pilgrims were too much given to chipping off pieces of it to carry home" (053-007).

The priests show a fragment of the Pillar of Flagellation — though "we could not see it, because it was dark inside the screen" — and the verification method consists of thrusting a stick through a hole (053-012). At the Chapel of St. Helena, they learn that the saint-empress discovered not only the True Cross but also the crown of thorns, the nails, and both thieves' crosses.

Twain notes sardonically that the chapel is called the "Chapel of the Invention of the Cross" — "a name which is unfortunate, because it leads the ignorant to imagine that a tacit acknowledgment is thus made that the tradition that Helena found the true Cross here is a fiction — an invention" (053-027).

The copper plate from the cross bearing Pilate's inscription, "THIS IS THE KING OF THE JEWS," is, Twain observes wryly, "in one of the churches in Rome, now. Anyone can see it there" (053-023). He expands on Helena's remarkable archaeological productivity:

> "She traveled all over Palestine, and was always fortunate. Whenever the good old enthusiast found a thing mentioned in her Bible, Old or New, she would go and search for that thing, and never stop until she found it. If it was Adam, she would find Adam; if it was the Ark, she would find the Ark; if it was Goliath, or Joshua, she would find them." (053-023)

Yet Twain reserves his most affecting relic encounter for Godfrey of Bouillon's sword, "the plain old sword of that stout Crusader, Godfrey of Bulloigne — King Godfrey of Jerusalem." He writes:

> "No blade in Christendom wields such enchantment as this — no blade of all that rust in the ancestral halls of Europe is able to invoke such visions of romance in the brain of him who looks upon it — none that can prate of such chivalric deeds or tell such brave tales of the warrior days of old." (053-014)

The sword stirs within him "every memory of the Holy Wars that has been sleeping in his brain for years" — a relic that moves him, in stark contrast to the ecclesiastical trumpery that leaves him cold.

## In the Holy Land: Turkish Sacred Relics

In Jerusalem, Twain discovers that relic veneration is not confined to Christianity. At the Dome of the Rock, "the Turks have their sacred relics, like the Catholics. The guide showed us the veritable armor worn by the great son-in-law and successor of Muhammad, and also the buckler of Muhammad's uncle" (054-016).

The great iron railing around the sacred rock is ornamented with "a thousand rags tied to its open work," which "are to remind Muhammad not to forget the worshipers who placed them there" (054-016). Twain notes the parallel with equanimity, suggesting that relic culture is a universal human impulse rather than a specifically Catholic failing.

## The Pilgrims as Relic-Hunters

The pilgrims themselves become avid relic-hunters, and Twain's satire turns inward. At Sebastopol, "there was nothing else to do, and so everybody went to hunting relics. They have stocked the ship with them" — cannon balls, broken ramrods, fragments of shell, "iron enough to freight a sloop" (035-011).

One passenger, Blucher, becomes the embodiment of absurd relic-mongering. Twain finds him labeling a horse's jawbone "Fragment of a Russian General" and explaining: "Go slow — the old woman won't know any different" (035-012 to 035-013).

Blucher has "turned his stateroom into a museum of worthless trumpery" and is caught "breaking a stone in two, and labeling half of it 'Chunk busted from the pulpit of Demosthenes,' and the other half 'Darnick from the Tomb of Abelard and Heloise'" (035-014).

He hands out pebbles from the sea shore, claiming they came from Mars Hill where St. Paul preached, and "says he never expects to run out of mementoes of St. Paul as long as he is in reach of a sandbank" (035-016).

Twain concludes: "I notice that all travelers supply deficiencies in their collections in the same way. I shall never have any confidence in such things again while I live" (035-016).

In Egypt, the relic-hunting impulse reaches its low point. One pilgrim "had crawled up there and was trying to break a 'specimen' from the face of this the most majestic creation the hand of man has wrought" — the Sphinx itself (058-031).

Twain calls these relic-hunters "well meaning reptiles" and notes with satisfaction that "Egyptian granite that has defied the storms and earthquakes of all time has nothing to fear from the tack-hammers of ignorant excursionists" (058-031).

At Cleopatra's Needle in Alexandria, another relic-hunter borrowed a sledge hammer but still "tried Pompey's Pillar, and this baffled him" (057-009).

Even Twain himself is not immune. After gathering specimens at Ephesus — where "it is a world of precious relics, a wilderness of marred and mutilated gems" (040-013) — he ruefully reports: "We brought not a relic from Ephesus!" (041-002). The exclamation mark captures both the absurdity and the genuine disappointment.

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, relic veneration was an established feature of Catholic and Orthodox practice. The Council of Trent had reaffirmed the legitimacy of relic veneration against Protestant objections, and the practice remained integral to popular piety. Churches competed to acquire and display prestigious relics, which drew pilgrims and their donations.

The distinction between genuine artifacts, pious forgeries, and objects of sincere but misplaced belief was often impossible to draw, and few within the institutional church had any incentive to draw it. The relic economy was too lucrative.

Twain and his fellow passengers were overwhelmingly American Protestants, for whom the Catholic cult of relics represented everything they had been taught to reject: superstition, priestcraft, and the commercialization of the sacred. Their journey was a sustained encounter with a religious culture they regarded with a mixture of fascination and bewilderment.

The 19th century also witnessed a parallel secular relic culture: the collecting of historical mementos, battlefield souvenirs, and archaeological specimens. Twain's pilgrims, for all their scorn of Catholic relics, enthusiastically participate in this secular version.

The impulse to possess a tangible fragment of the past, Twain suggests, is universal; only the objects and justifications differ.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward relics is predominantly satirical, but it is not monolithic. He operates along a spectrum from amused skepticism to outright contempt, with occasional flashes of genuine reverence.

At one extreme is the deadpan cataloguing of impossible multiplicities — the multiple sets of St. John's ashes, the keg of True Cross nails, the several St. Luke Madonnas. The humor derives from the collision between the priests' solemn claims and simple arithmetic.

At the opposite extreme is the bejeweled corpse of St. Charles Borromeo in the crypt of Milan Cathedral. The body, "robed in costly habiliments covered with gold embroidery and starred with scintillating gems," with "a crown sown thick with flashing brilliants" hanging over "this dreadful face, its dust and decay and its mocking grin," prompts one of the book's most serious passages.

Twain reflects: "How poor, and cheap, and trivial these gewgaws seemed in presence of the solemnity, the grandeur, the awful majesty of Death!" (018-020 to 018-021).

Between these poles lies a complex middle ground. Twain is genuinely moved by Godfrey's sword, a relic whose authenticity he does not question. He is surprisingly awed by the Church of the Holy Sepulchre itself — "with all its claptrap sideshows and unseemly impostures of every kind, it is still grand, revered, venerable — for a god died there" (053-035).

And at Mount Tabor, he confesses a strange nostalgia for the very relics he has been mocking: "A Catholic church is nothing to me that has no relics" (049-020). This unexpected admission reveals that relic culture has come to define what sacred space means to him on this journey.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- **Notre Dame, Paris** (014-005): Nails of the True Cross, fragment of the cross, part of the crown of thorns, the bloody robe of the Archbishop of Paris, and a silver cross recovered from the Seine by angelic guidance.
- **San Lorenzo, Genoa** (017-015 to 017-016): Ashes of St. John the Baptist (second set encountered) and a portrait of the Madonna by St. Luke (second example).
- **Milan Cathedral** (018-026): Two of St. Paul's fingers, one of St. Peter's, a bone of Judas Iscariot (black), bones of all disciples, the Saviour's handkerchief, a stone from the Holy Sepulchre, a nail from the Cross, and a third St. Luke Virgin.
- **St. Mark's, Venice** (022-023): Ashes of St. Mark, smuggled from Alexandria in vessels of lard.
- **Church of the Holy Sepulchre, Jerusalem** (053-007 to 053-027): The Stone of Unction, the Pillar of Flagellation, the copper plate from the Cross, Godfrey's sword, the centre of the earth column, the dust from which Adam was made, and the tomb of Adam.
- **Dome of the Rock, Jerusalem** (054-016): Armor of Muhammad's son-in-law, buckler of Muhammad's uncle, and hundreds of rag-tokens tied to the railing.
- **Sebastopol** (035-011 to 035-016): The pilgrims' secular relic-hunting — cannonballs, bone fragments, and Blucher's fabricated "relics" labeled for his aunt.
- **Ephesus** (040-013): A "world of precious relics, a wilderness of marred and mutilated gems" — sculptured marble fragments scattered across the plains.
- **Alexandria** (057-009): A relic-hunter with a sledge hammer attempting to break fragments from Cleopatra's Needle and Pompey's Pillar.
- **The Sphinx** (058-031): A "well meaning reptile" with a hammer attempting to break a specimen from the face of the Sphinx.
