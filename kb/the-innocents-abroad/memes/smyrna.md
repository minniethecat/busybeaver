# Smyrna

Ancient seaport of Asia Minor (modern Izmir, Turkey), the Quaker City's first notable Asiatic landfall and the gateway through which Mark Twain introduces his readers to the realities — and disappointments — of "Oriental" travel.

## Overview

Smyrna was the excursion party's first encounter with Asia, arriving after the return from Constantinople and the Black Sea excursion. Twain had been anticipating the Orient since the voyage began, listing "Smyrna" alongside "Constantinople" and "Jerusalem" in the book's opening pages (001-005, 001-010). The city did not disappoint as an introduction to the East, but it did systematically dismantle every romantic preconception the travelers had brought with them.

Twain devotes two full chapters (38 and 39) to Smyrna, making it one of the more extensively described stops on the voyage. His treatment moves through several registers: comic debunking of guidebook fantasies, theological satire directed at prophecy-mongering pilgrims, genuine ethnographic observation of street life and local custom, and moments of almost lyrical nostalgia for the imagined Orient of his childhood reading. The result is a portrait that captures both the squalor and the strange magic of the city — the "combination of Mohammedan stenches" and the camel trains that "cast you back at once into your forgotten boyhood" and the world of *The Arabian Nights* (038-011, 038-019).

Smyrna also serves a structural purpose in the narrative. It is the hinge between the European tour and the Holy Land pilgrimage that follows. After Smyrna comes Ephesus, then Beirut, then the long overland journey through Syria and Palestine. Twain uses Smyrna to reset reader expectations — to announce that the comic mode that worked for Paris and Italy will be applied with equal vigor to the sacred geography of the East.

## Arrival and First Impressions

The approach to Smyrna was framed by the sailors' extended mockery of the pilgrims' pretensions. All the way from Constantinople, the crew had been burlesquing the Address to the Emperor of Russia, with a third cook crowned in a "resplendent tin basin" playing the Czar while the "watch below" impersonated "graceless ladies and uncouth pilgrims" (038-001). Twain, as a member of the committee that drafted the Address, felt the sting personally: "I never was so tired of any one phrase as the sailors made me of the opening sentence of the Address to the Emperor of Russia" (038-001).

The ship passed between Lemnos and Mytilene, the islands appearing as "mere bulky shapes, with the softening mists of distance upon them — whales in a fog, as it were" (038-003). Then, holding course southward, the pilgrims "began to 'read up' celebrated Smyrna" (038-003) — the quotation marks around "read up" signaling Twain's skepticism about the usefulness of guidebook preparation.

What they found was not the fabled city of Orientalist fantasy but a densely packed Ottoman port. Twain's first extended description of Smyrna is one of the most concentrated passages of sensory reportage in the entire book — a single long paragraph that catalogs the city's features in an accelerating rhythm that builds toward comic climax:

> "That is to say, its Muslim houses are heavy and dark, and as comfortless as so many tombs; its streets are crooked, rudely and roughly paved, and as narrow as an ordinary staircase; the streets uniformly carry a man to any other place than the one he wants to go to, and surprise him by landing him in the most unexpected localities; business is chiefly carried on in great covered bazaars, celled like a honeycomb with innumerable shops no larger than a common closet, and the whole hive cut up into a maze of alleys about wide enough to accommodate a laden camel, and well calculated to confuse a stranger and eventually lose him; everywhere there is dirt, everywhere there are fleas, everywhere there are lean, brokenhearted dogs..." (038-011)

## Description and Notable Features

### The Bazaars and Streets

The commercial life of Smyrna centered on its covered bazaars, which Twain describes as a "hive cut up into a maze of alleys" (038-011). The workshops opened directly onto the streets, the workmen visible to passersby. Every alley was "thronged with people" in a "wild masquerade of extravagant costumes" (038-011). The sensory assault was total: "all manner of sounds assail the ear, and over them all rings out the muezzin's cry from some tall minaret, calling the faithful vagabonds to prayer" (038-011).

The city was ethnically stratified. "A portion of the city is pretty exclusively Turkish; the Jews have a quarter to themselves; the Franks another quarter; so, also, with the Armenians" (038-016). Twain was particularly taken with the Armenian quarter, where the houses were "large, clean, airy, handsomely paved with black and white squares of marble" and featured central courtyards with "luxuriant flower-gardens and sparkling fountains" (038-016).

### The Armenian Girls

Twain's account of the Armenian women of Smyrna is one of the warmest personal passages in the travelogue. He describes them as "all comely of countenance, and exceedingly neat and cleanly; they look as if they were just out of a bandbox" (038-016). His praise escalates to a characteristically Twainian confession:

> "Some of the young ladies — many of them, I may say — are even very beautiful; they average a shade better than American girls — which treasonable words I pray may be forgiven me." (038-016)

What particularly charmed him was their sociability. Unlike the veiled and inaccessible women of other Oriental locales, the Armenian girls "will smile back when a stranger smiles at them, bow back when he bows, and talk back if he speaks to them. No introduction is required" (038-016). Twain recounts an hour-long chat with a pretty Armenian girl — he speaking English, she speaking Greek or Armenian — and finding that mutual incomprehension was "not much of a drawback" (038-016).

### Camels

At Smyrna the travelers got their first close view of camels, having previously only glimpsed them through field glasses in the Dardanelles. Twain's description captures both the physical scale of the animals and their romantic associations:

> "To see a camel train laden with the spices of Arabia and the rare fabrics of Persia come marching through the narrow alleys of the bazaar, among porters with their burdens, money-changers, lamp-merchants, Al-naschars in the glassware business, portly cross-legged Turks smoking the famous narghile; and the crowds drifting to and fro in the fanciful costumes of the East, is a genuine revelation of the Orient." (038-019)

The camels were "very much larger than the scrawny specimens one sees in the menagerie," striding in single file with a "fancy-looking negro in Turkish costume, or an Arab, preceding them on a little donkey" (038-019). For Twain, the camel trains completed the picture — they transported him from the disappointing reality of modern Smyrna into the realm of childhood imagination.

### The Citadel and the Six Smyrnas

The "lions of Smyrna" — the local term for tourist attractions — consisted of three sites: the ruined citadel on Mount Pagus, the site of one of the Seven Apocalyptic Churches, and the tomb of the martyr Polycarp (039-001, 039-002). Riding through the town on donkeys, the party could "see marks of the six Smyrnas that have existed here and been burned up by fire or knocked down by earthquakes" (039-006). The evidence was everywhere embedded in the modern city:

| Layer | Destruction | Evidence Visible |
|-------|-------------|------------------|
| First Smyrna | Fire or earthquake | Excavated building-stone buried for ages |
| Second Smyrna | Fire or earthquake | Broken pillars in modern walls |
| Third Smyrna | Fire or earthquake | Sculpted marble fragments |
| Fourth Smyrna | Fire or earthquake | Capitals reused as building material |
| Fifth Smyrna | Fire or earthquake | Hills rent asunder, exposed foundations |
| Sixth (Modern) Smyrna | Surviving | "Mean houses and walls" incorporating ancient spoils |

Twain notes with characteristic irony that the hills and rocks "are rent asunder in places, excavations expose great blocks of building-stone that have lain buried for ages, and all the mean houses and walls of modern Smyrna along the way are spotted white with broken pillars, capitals and fragments of sculptured marble that once adorned the lordly palaces that were the glory of the city in the olden time" (039-006).

### The Oyster Mine Mystery

Halfway up the citadel hill, five hundred feet above the sea, Twain discovered what he called "three veins of oyster shells, just as we have seen quartz veins exposed in the cutting of a road in Nevada or Montana" (039-009). The parallel was irresistible to the former silver prospector. The veins were "about eighteen inches thick and two or three feet apart," slanting downward, a "well-defined lead by itself, and without a spur" (039-009).

Twain's mock-serious investigation of how oyster shells came to be embedded in a hillside five hundred feet above the sea is one of the set-piece comic passages of the Smyrna chapters. He rejects the restaurant theory (no champagne corks), the earthquake theory (the crockery is inexplicable), the Noah's Ark theory (eight people could not have eaten that many oysters), and finally arrives at the "slender theory that the oysters climbed up there of their own accord" — only to dismiss that too, since "an oyster does not take any interest in scenery — he scorns it" (039-009). His conclusion is the same as the guidebooks': "They are there, but how they got there is a mystery" (039-009).

## Historical Significance

Smyrna's historical importance in Twain's account rests on three pillars: its biblical associations, its repeated destruction and reconstruction, and its place in early Christian martyrology.

### The Crown of Life

Smyrna is one of the seven churches of Asia addressed in the Book of Revelation. The biblical text promises the church at Smyrna a "crown of life" if they remain "faithful unto death." Twain devotes a substantial theological satire to the way pilgrims and "prophecy-savans" have misinterpreted this promise, applying it to the city rather than the church. "No crown of life is promised to the town of Smyrna and its commerce, but to the handful of Christians who formed its 'church'" (038-014). He argues that the pilgrims' interpretation is not only wrong but damaging — it "provokes worldly men into using light conversation concerning sacred subjects" (038-016).

Twain's indignation is genuine beneath the comic surface. He despises what he sees as a lazy hermeneutic that fits "the prophetic shirt on to the wrong man" without "regard to rhyme or reason" (038-014). The fact that Ephesus — a sister church also addressed in Revelation — lies in ruins while Smyrna thrives is taken by pilgrims as proof of fulfilled prophecy, but Twain points out that the biblical threat against Ephesus was explicitly conditional: "or else I will come unto thee quickly, and will remove thy candlestick out of his place, except thou repent" (038-014). The qualification is crucial and routinely ignored.

Smyrna has been "utterly destroyed six times" (038-016). If the crown of life were an insurance policy, Twain observes, the city "would have had an opportunity to collect on it the first time she fell" (038-016). The theological point is that cities rise and fall by natural and historical causes, not by divine micromanagement of prophetic schedules.

### The Martyr Polycarp

Polycarp, the second-century bishop of Smyrna, was burned at the stake and stabbed when the fire miraculously failed to consume him — one of the most celebrated martyrdoms of the early church. Twain and his party visited Polycarp's tomb and the traditional site of his martyrdom (039-001, 039-002). Twain's account is notably brief and uncharacteristically restrained — he records the visit without satire, perhaps recognizing that Polycarp's suffering was genuine in a way that the "prophecy-savant" speculations were not.

### The Seven Churches

The party also visited what was claimed to be the site of the original Christian congregation addressed in Revelation. They were shown a small Greek church "built upon the ancient site" and given wax candles as souvenirs — one of which promptly melted down the back of Twain's neck in the Smyrna sun, leaving him with "nothing left but the wick, and it is a sorry and a wilted-looking wick at that" (039-002).

Twain and several companions argued that the "church" mentioned in the Bible "meant a party of Christians, and not a building" and that the impoverished and persecuted early Christians would neither have been able to afford nor dared to build a church edifice in open daylight (039-002). The "elders of the ship's family" overruled them — and were later embarrassed to discover they had been taken to the wrong site: "the accepted site is in the city" (039-002).

## Daily Life and Local Color

Smyrna gave Twain his first sustained exposure to the textures of Ottoman urban life, and his reportage is detailed and appreciative despite the comic frame. The ethnographic inventory of the bazaar — "porters with their burdens, money-changers, lamp-merchants, Al-naschars in the glassware business, portly cross-legged Turks smoking the famous narghile" (038-019) — is crowded with specific occupational types, each observed with a journalist's eye.

The costumes of Smyrna were "picturesque and fanciful" but, as Twain would later note, "not so varied as at Constantinople" (041-007). What distinguished Smyrna in Twain's account was less the visual spectacle than the quality of social interaction — the accessibility of the Armenian quarter, the street-level commerce, the donkey rides up to Mount Pagus. Smyrna was a city where the travelers could walk and talk and get lost in the bazaar, a more intimate encounter than the formalized tourist circuits of European capitals.

The Millerite episode provided a bizarre footnote. Three years before Twain's visit, a group of American-style millenarians in Smyrna had set a date for the end of the world and gathered on the citadel hill to await their ascension. A violent thunderstorm broke out below them while they remained dry on the mountain, "looking down upon the fearful storm going on below, and really believed that their proposed destruction of the world was proving a grand success" (039-013, 039-014). The event was "a thing unprecedented in Smyrna at that time of the year" (039-014).

The railway to Ephesus — "a strange thing to think of" in "the dreamy realm of the Orient — in the fabled land of *The Arabian Nights*" (039-014) — represented the intrusion of modernity into the ancient landscape. Built by an English company, it was "well built and well conducted" but carried only "eight hundred pounds of figs" in freight its first year (039-014). Twain found the image of a locomotive "tearing through such a place as this, and waking the phantoms of its old days of romance out of their dreams of dead and gone centuries" to be "curious enough" (039-014).

## Twain's Verdict

Twain's judgment on Smyrna is characteristically double-edged. The city is simultaneously a disappointment and a revelation. The "oriental splendor" promised by romantic literature turns out to be a "fraud" (038-001) — a combination of stenches, filth, and discomfort. Yet the very same streets that offend his American nose also deliver the "genuine revelation of the Orient" that no guidebook could convey (038-019).

The theological satire is among the sharpest in the book. Twain's takedown of prophecy-mongering pilgrims has real intellectual substance beneath the humor — he is making a case for reading biblical texts in their historical and literary context rather than as a codebook for predicting the rise and fall of cities. "These things put arguments into the mouth of infidelity" (038-016), he warns, and the warning is not ironic. Bad interpretation, in Twain's view, does more damage to faith than honest skepticism ever could.

Yet for all his debunking, Smyrna works its magic on Twain. The passage about the camel train transforming the bazaar into a scene from *The Arabian Nights* is not satire — it is genuine nostalgia for the imaginative world of childhood reading. "Again your companions are princes, your lord is the Caliph Haroun Al Raschid, and your servants are terrific giants and genii that come with smoke and lightning and thunder" (038-019). Twain the cynical traveler and Twain the dreamy boy coexist in the Smyrna chapters, and their coexistence is what makes the portrait rich.

The departure from Smyrna was charged with anticipation. The party sailed "in the wildest spirit of expectancy, for the chief feature, the grand goal of the expedition, was near at hand — we were approaching the Holy Land!" (041-003). At Smyrna, "more telegraphing was done" to secure dragomen and transportation for the Palestinian journey (041-005). The city was, in narrative terms, the last secular stop before the sacred geography that would dominate the remainder of the book.

In his final retrospective catalog of the voyage's memorable sights, Twain would remember "Oriental Smyrna" alongside "sacred Jerusalem" and "Damascus, the Garden of Eden" (062-007) — an acknowledgment that, for all his mockery, the city had earned its place among the defining experiences of the journey.
