# Jerusalem

The Holy City, sacred to Christians, Jews, and Muslims, reached by the Quaker City party after a grueling overland journey from Damascus in Chapter 52 — a place where Twain's romantic expectations collided with the reality of a small, impoverished, and disease-ridden city, yet where the weight of biblical history proved impossible to dismiss.

## Overview

Jerusalem was, by Twain's own admission, the emotional and narrative climax of the entire voyage. The excursion program had promised it from the beginning, listing "Jerusalem" alongside "Constantinople" and "Smyrna" as the great destinations of the voyage (001-010). The approach through the Judean hills, the first sight of the city walls, and the descent into the crowded streets — all are rendered with a gravity notably absent from Twain's Italian chapters.

Yet Jerusalem also provoked some of Twain's sharpest satire. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre, the most sacred site in Christendom, became in his account a theater of sectarian squabbling and commercialized piety. The pilgrims who "wept when they saw Jerusalem" and "wept when they lay in the stable at Bethlehem" struck him as performing an expected emotion rather than experiencing a genuine one (050-038). The city was a paradox: sacred beyond measure and squalid beyond belief.

Twain spent several days exploring Jerusalem and its environs, visiting the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, the Mosque of Omar, the Wailing Wall, the Mount of Olives, the Garden of Gethsemane, and Bethlehem. What emerged was not reverence but a kind of exhausted fascination — an acknowledgment that the city's associations were more powerful than its physical reality.

## Arrival and First Impressions

The approach to Jerusalem was an exercise in delayed gratification. The party had "hurried on" through the Judean hills, "impatient" to see the holy city, but "still no Jerusalem came in sight" (052-018). Twain describes the mounting anticipation: they "swept around the base of a hill," and there "on the commanding eminence" — still nothing. The pilgrims grew restive.

Then, suddenly: "A sinner swung his hat on high! Jerusalem!" (052-019). The moment was theatrical, and Twain knew it. But the reality that followed was bracing. "Jerusalem numbers only fourteen thousand people" (052-020) — not the great metropolis of scripture but a small provincial town. The streets were narrow, crooked, and filthy. "To reproduce a Jerusalem street, it would only be necessary to take a narrow alley, fill it with cobblestones, and then crowd it with Turks, Arabs, Jews, Greeks, Latins, Armenians, and Copts" (053-003).

The population was "composed of Muslims, Jews, Greeks, Latins, Armenians, Copts, Abyssinians, Syrian Maronites, and a few Protestants" (053-005) — a catalog that emphasized Jerusalem's role as a crossroads of faiths, all coexisting in mutual suspicion. Twain observed this diversity with anthropological detachment, noting the costumes, the languages, and the constant jostling for position at the holy sites.

## Description and Notable Features

### The Church of the Holy Sepulchre

No site in the travelogue receives more sustained attention than the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, the traditional site of Christ's crucifixion and burial. Twain's account runs for pages, documenting every chapel, every relic, every competing claim of the various Christian denominations that shared custody of the building.

His tone is skeptical throughout. He notes the "noble lady" who "lay very ill in Jerusalem" and was cured by a miraculous dream — a story he recounts with the same deadpan he applied to the Venetian lion's miraculous bleeding (053-011). The "stream that was heard beyond the Damascus Gate" flowing from the church was another legend he recorded without endorsing (053-011).

Yet beneath the skepticism ran a current of genuine historical imagination. He describes the "first Christian Kings of Jerusalem" who "once lay buried by that sacred spot" (053-030), and reflects that the sepulchre had been venerated "long before he came to Jerusalem" (053-032). The church was a palimpsest of faith, politics, and commerce — and Twain documented every layer.

### The Mosque of Omar and the Temple Mount

The Mosque of Omar, occupying the Temple Mount, was the great Islamic monument of Jerusalem. Twain described it in relation to the city's layout: "The Mosque of Omar, the Damascus Gate, the Mount of Olives, the Valley of Jehoshaphat" — all were part of the panoramic view from the city walls (052-021). The mosque was, he noted, built on the site of Solomon's Temple, the holiest place in Judaism, and its golden dome was the most visible landmark in the city.

He observed that the Mosques "occupy a fourth part of Jerusalem. They are upon Mount Moriah" (054-011), linking the Islamic present to the biblical past through the geography of Abraham's near-sacrifice of Isaac. The Temple platform was itself a concentration of sacred history: "The angel stood and threatened Jerusalem, and David persuaded him to spare the city" at this very spot (054-013).

### The Walls and Gates

Jerusalem's walls fascinated Twain as both military architecture and symbolic boundary. The party "walked entirely around the city outside the walls" (053-002), a circuit that Twain estimated could be done in an hour. The Damascus Gate, "the ancient and the famed Damascus Gate," was the point of entry for caravans from the north and the starting point of the road to Nablus and Galilee (052-024). The party later "marched out at the stately Damascus gate" when departing for Jericho and the Jordan (056-002).

The walls enclosed a city whose density surprised him. "Every window" seemed to tell a story. The stones themselves were freighted with prophecy: Twain noted that one of "the very stones of Jerusalem" was the subject of Christ's prediction that not one stone would be left upon another — a prophecy that history had, by Twain's account, partly fulfilled but left incomplete (054-005).

### The Mount of Olives and Environs

The Mount of Olives provided the essential panoramic view that Twain considered indispensable to understanding Jerusalem. "From the Mount of Olives" one could see "Jerusalem, the Dead Sea and the mountains of Moab" — a prospect that encompassed the entire geography of the Gospels (054-030). The Garden of Gethsemane at its foot, with its ancient olive trees, was another site where biblical memory overwhelmed present reality.

Twain's ascent of the Mount of Olives became one of the "pleasant memories of Jerusalem, memories we shall call up with satisfaction" (054-031) — a rare moment of unforced appreciation in a city that had otherwise exhausted him.

## Historical Significance

Jerusalem's significance in the travelogue is theological as much as historical. Twain is interested in the city as the stage on which the central events of Christianity were said to have occurred, and he brings to bear the same evidentiary skepticism he applied to European relics.

The biblical associations are omnipresent. The city's "very stones" were those Christ "mentioned when he said not one stone should be left upon another" (054-005). The Pool of Siloam, "under Jerusalem somewhere," was the site of one of Christ's healing miracles (054-027). The tradition that "when Titus sacked Jerusalem and slaughtered eleven hundred thousand Jews" was part of the city's tragic history (054-006). The Crusader kingdom, with "Godfrey reigning in Jerusalem" (021-017), represented a Christian interlude in the city's long Islamic history.

Twain also records the miraculous traditions with his characteristic ambivalence. Every "fiftieth year" a miracle was supposed to occur at the Holy Sepulchre, and the pilgrims "must never fail to report in Jerusalem" (054-007) — the obligation to witness miracles was itself a kind of tourist duty.

## Daily Life and Local Color

The daily life of Jerusalem was, for Twain, a spectacle of poverty and piety. The streets were narrow, the houses cramped, the residents — of every faith — competing for the custom of pilgrims. The "baksheesh" culture that Twain had documented across the Levant reached its apotheosis here: every holy site had its guardians, each expecting payment.

The pilgrims themselves were a subject of fascinated observation. Twain describes the emotional performances at the holy places with an irony that stops just short of cruelty: "I wept, when I saw Jerusalem, I wept when I lay in the stable at Bethlehem" (050-038) — the repetition of "I wept" suggesting a ritual rather than a spontaneous response. Yet he acknowledged that the associations were genuinely moving, and his own account of the city's sites is neither dismissive nor contemptuous.

The diversity of Jerusalem's population — "Muslims, Jews, Greeks, Latins, Armenians, Copts, Abyssinians, Syrian Maronites, and a few Protestants" (053-005) — created a street life unlike anything in Europe. Costumes, languages, and competing claims to sanctity filled every corner of the city, and Twain absorbed it all with the eye of a reporter who had by now seen enough of the world to appreciate the unique density of Jerusalem's human fabric.

## Twain's Verdict

Jerusalem left Twain exhausted but not unmoved. The city had been the goal of his crusade — "the goal of our crusade, renowned Jerusalem" (052-016) — and the reality, however diminished from the biblical ideal, could not be written off. The final statement on Jerusalem comes not in Twain's own words but through the catalog of memories he assembles in the concluding chapter: "We shall remember... sacred Jerusalem" (062-007) — the adjective "sacred" doing more work than any extended evaluation could.

He found the city small, dirty, and commercialized in its piety. But he also found it genuinely evocative — a place where geography and scripture converged in ways that no amount of skepticism could entirely dissolve. "The thoughts Jerusalem suggests are full of poetry, sublimity, and more than all, dignity" (052-023). That sentence, unadorned by irony, may be the truest verdict Twain ever delivered.
