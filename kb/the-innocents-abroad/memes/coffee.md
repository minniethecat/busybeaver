# Coffee and Social Rituals

The social and cultural role of coffee-drinking across Europe and the Ottoman Empire, as observed and satirized by Mark Twain during the 1867 Quaker City excursion — a practice that serves as a barometer of cultural difference, a setting for public life, and an occasion for Twain's characteristic deflation of romantic expectations.

## Overview

Coffee in *The Innocents Abroad* is more than a beverage; it is a recurring marker of cultural ritual and social contrast. Twain encounters coffee throughout the journey — as the concluding course of elaborate French table d'hote dinners, as a fixture of Parisian sidewalk cafe life, and most memorably, as the "world-renowned Turkish coffee that poets have sung so rapturously for many generations" (034-034). At every turn, the practice of coffee-drinking becomes a lens through which Twain observes the habits, pretensions, and everyday textures of foreign societies, always measuring them against American norms.

Coffee appears in two principal registers in the travelogue. In France and continental Europe, it is part of the civilized ritual of dining and street life — an emblem of order, leisure, and sophistication that Twain generally admires, even as he notes its foreignness. In the Ottoman East, however, coffee-drinking becomes entangled with the "Oriental splendor" that Twain systematically debunks. Turkish coffee, celebrated in Romantic travel literature as an exotic luxury, is subjected to Twain's satirical scrutiny and found to be one more "malignant swindle" (034-035). The trajectory from Parisian cafe to Constantinople bathhouse traces an arc of disillusionment that is central to the book's larger project of deflating Orientalist fantasy.

## In Twain's Narrative

Coffee first appears as the final course of the French table d'hote, where the elaborate sequence of soup, fish, roast beef, vegetables, chicken, salad, dessert, figs, and "finally coffee" (011-003) marks the leisurely cadence of European dining. The coffee course is inseparable from the ritual of lingering at table — "we must sit long in the cool chambers and smoke" (011-003) — establishing coffee as part of a civilized rhythm entirely foreign to American haste.

In Paris, coffee is woven into the fabric of public street life. Twain observes "two hundred people sat at little tables on the sidewalk, sipping wine and coffee" (012-018) amid the "conflagration of gaslight everywhere" (012-018), painting coffee as a key ingredient of the Parisian spectacle. The same pattern recurs at the Jardin Mabille in Asnieres, where crowds sit "in the open air in front of the flagstaff and the temple, drinking wine and coffee or smoking" (014-013). Coffee here is a social lubricant, a marker of public leisure and conviviality.

The most extended treatment of coffee comes in Constantinople, where Twain's expectations — shaped by Orientalist travel literature — collide with reality. He had dreamed of a Turkish bath culminating in "delicious coffee" (034-023) sipped while reclining on a divan, lulled by incense and fountains. Instead, he finds the actual Turkish coffee served in a bare, coop-like room to be "another fraud" (034-034). His description is memorably savage: "The cup is small, it is smeared with grounds; the coffee is black, thick, unsavory of smell, and execrable in taste. The bottom of the cup has a muddy sediment in it half an inch deep. This goes down your throat, and portions of it lodge by the way, and produce a tickling aggravation that keeps you barking and coughing for an hour" (034-034).

A subtler coffee moment occurs during the expedition in the mountains of Lebanon, where Twain calls for "a second cup of coffee" (042-006) at a breakfast of mutton chops, fried chicken, omelettes, and fried potatoes — all consumed beneath a tent canopy overlooking a "noble panorama of mountain, sea and hazy valley" (042-005). Here coffee is unremarkable, part of a satisfying meal earned by hard riding, a brief moment where coffee simply works as it should.

At Mount Tabor, Twain notes in passing that its Greek Convent has good coffee — "the coffee there is good, but never a splinter of the true cross or bone of a hallowed saint to arrest the idle thoughts of worldlings" (049-020) — a wry inversion that values the coffee above the absent [[relics]].

The comic climax of coffee in the book comes not in the Orient but aboard ship, where the coffee has degraded over three weeks "till at last it had ceased to be coffee altogether and had assumed the nature of mere discolored water" (060-003). When a passenger (revealed to be Twain himself) complains to Captain Duncan, the Captain tastes it and delivers the devastating verdict: "It *is* inferior — for *coffee* — but it is pretty fair *tea*" (060-006). The episode, in which Twain "had made an egregious ass of himself before the whole ship" (060-007), closes the coffee motif with characteristic self-deprecation, even as it reinforces the larger theme of the shipboard coffee's steady decline.

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, coffeehouses were well-established institutions across both Europe and the Ottoman Empire, but they functioned quite differently. French cafes were centers of social and intellectual life, frequented by all classes, and increasingly associated with the spectacle of modern urban existence that so fascinated visitors like Twain. The Parisian sidewalk cafe, where one might linger for hours over a single cup, represented a form of public leisure that had no real equivalent in mid-19th-century America.

Turkish coffee, prepared by boiling finely ground beans in a cezve (a small copper pot) and served unfiltered in tiny cups, was a ritual central to Ottoman hospitality and social life. The thick, muddy, intensely strong brew was a deliberate contrast to European filtered preparations. What Twain experienced as "execrable" (034-034) was not a failure of execution but an authentic expression of a different coffee tradition — one that European Romantic writers had, in Twain's view, absurdly glamorized. His reaction reveals as much about his own expectations and palate as it does about the beverage itself.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's treatment of coffee is quintessentially in his mode: he approaches the subject with a mix of genuine curiosity, willingness to be pleased, and razor-sharp readiness to deflate pretension. When coffee fits into a pattern of civilized enjoyment he respects — the French table d'hote, the Parisian sidewalk cafe — he reports it with matter-of-fact appreciation. When coffee is freighted with the exaggerated expectations produced by "incendiary books of travel" (034-024), he takes savage delight in exposing the gap between fantasy and reality.

> "Of all the unchristian beverages that ever passed my lips, Turkish coffee is the worst." (034-034)

The line is pure Twain: hyperbolic, judgmental, and funny, but also revealing. The word "unchristian" is a deliberate provocation, collapsing religious and gustatory categories for comic effect. Yet the passage also hints at a genuine disappointment — Twain had truly wanted to enjoy the Oriental luxury he had read about, and his anger at the coffee is fueled by the larger anger of a tourist who feels swindled by the travel writers who preceded him.

His self-deprecating shipboard coffee episode (060-003 through 060-007) rounds out the theme by turning the satirical lens on himself. Having spent much of the book mocking foreign coffee customs, Twain ends up the butt of his own coffee complaint, mistaking tea-weak coffee for an injustice when it was simply his own confusion. It is a neat narrative symmetry that underscores the book's larger project: the education of an American traveler through repeated encounters with his own assumptions.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- **French table d'hote ritual** — Coffee as the unhurried conclusion of an elaborate multi-course meal in Marseilles, accompanied by smoking and newspaper reading (011-003)
- **Parisian sidewalk cafe life** — Two hundred Parisians sipping wine and coffee at outdoor tables on the Rue de Rivoli at lamplighting, part of the glittering street spectacle (012-018)
- **Garden entertainment at Asnieres** — Crowds drinking wine and coffee in the open air before the Blondin tightrope performance (014-013)
- **The Turkish bath debacle** — Twain's comprehensive rejection of Turkish coffee as "another fraud" after the bath attendant's service, the cup "smeared with grounds" and the sediment "half an inch deep" (034-034)
- **Mountain breakfast in Lebanon** — A satisfying second cup of coffee amid an epic panorama, earned by hard riding (042-006)
- **Mount Tabor's Greek Convent** — Good coffee but no holy [[relics]], a joke at the expense of Catholic relic-collecting (049-020)
- **The shipboard coffee crisis** — Three weeks of declining quality culminating in Twain's mistaken complaint to Captain Duncan (060-003 through 060-007)
