# Muhammad

Founder of Islam and prophet to Muslims worldwide. Twain repeatedly references Muhammad throughout his Holy Land journey — particularly at the hill overlooking Damascus where tradition says the Prophet turned away from earthly paradise, and at the Mosque of Omar in Jerusalem where footprints and relics anchor sacred tradition.

## Overview

Muhammad (c. 570–632 CE) appears in *The Innocents Abroad* not as a character but as a pervasive presence whose legacy shapes every landscape Twain traverses in Syria and Palestine. The Prophet's name is invoked in mosques, tombs, stone footprints, suspended rocks, and local customs that govern how Mohammedans eat, drink, pray, and regard foreign Christians. Twain is characteristically irreverent — he mocks relics, scoffs at traditions, and occasionally concedes that "the Prophet was wise" — but he records the details with an ethnographer's thoroughness, making Muhammad one of the most frequently referenced historical figures in the travelogue.

Twain's Muhammad is refracted through the lens of mid-nineteenth-century American Protestant skepticism. He finds Islamic traditions outlandish yet fascinating, and he treats them with the same satirical scrutiny he applies to Catholic relic veneration. His accounts preserve vivid snapshots of Ottoman-era Islamic practice as observed by a foreign traveler who was equal parts amused, offended, and captivated.

## In the Travelogue

Muhammad first enters the narrative indirectly, through the customs of Tangier. Twain notes that "the religion of Muhammad causes its devotees to abhor swine's flesh" (022-024) and describes how Mohammedan pilgrims who complete the hajj earn the title Hadji and lifelong prestige (009-015). He observes Tangier's three-Sabbath rhythm, remarking that "the Muhammadans' comes on Friday" (009-013) and records the shrewd practice by which pilgrims borrow a hundred dollars to meet the financial qualification for the pilgrimage, then repay it before the ship departs (009-016).

The most extended treatment of Muhammad comes in Chapter 44, when Twain arrives at the hilltop overlooking Damascus. He writes: "I think I have read about four hundred times that when Muhammad was a simple camel-driver he reached this point and looked down upon Damascus for the first time, and then made a certain renowned remark. He said man could enter only one paradise; he preferred to go to the one above" (044-004). Twain finds the view so magnificent that he declares: "If I were to go to Damascus again, I would camp on Muhammad's hill about a week, and then go away. There is no need to go inside the walls. The Prophet was wise without knowing it when he decided not to go down into the paradise of Damascus" (044-006).

In Damascus itself, the party visits "the tomb of Muhammad's children" (044-024) amid a city Twain describes as "the most fanatical Mohammedan purgatory out of Arabia" (044-013), noting the prevalence of green turbans worn by those who have made the pilgrimage to Mecca.

At the Mosque of Omar in Jerusalem (Chapter 54), Twain catalogs Muhammad-related relics with characteristic irony. He reports that "Muhammad was well acquainted with this stone. From it he ascended to heaven" and that the stone tried to follow him — only the angel Gabriel's grip prevented it, leaving "prints of his monstrous fingers, two inches deep" in the rock (054-013). Twain notes the tradition that "in the place on it where Muhammad stood, he left his footprints in the solid stone" and drily estimates "he wore about eighteens" (054-014). The guide explains that Muhammad personally lifts souls from perdition to heaven by the hair, which explains the Mohammedan custom of leaving a scalp-lock: "a good Mohammedan would consider himself doomed to stay with the damned forever if he were to lose his scalp-lock and die before it grew again" (054-014). Twain's verdict: "The most of them that I have seen ought to stay with the damned, anyhow, without reference to how they were barbered" (054-014).

The mosque also displays "the veritable armor worn by the great son-in-law and successor of Muhammad, and also the buckler of Muhammad's uncle" (054-016), and worshipers tie rags to the iron railing "to remind Muhammad not to forget the worshipers who placed them there" (054-016). A stone column projecting from the Temple wall marks where "the Muslims believe Muhammad will sit astride of it when he comes to judge the world" (054-030).

The Wandering Jew legend includes a chapter in which the accursed wanderer "followed Muhammad when he carried destruction to the cities of Arabia" five hundred years after the Crucifixion, hoping to win a traitor's death — but even Muhammad's conquests could not grant him oblivion (054-006).

## Historical Context

Muhammad ibn Abdullah was born in Mecca around 570 CE and began receiving revelations at age forty that would become the Quran. Forced to flee Mecca for Medina in 622 (the Hijra, which marks year one of the Islamic calendar), he returned as a military and spiritual conqueror in 630, unifying the Arabian peninsula under Islam before his death in 632. Within a century of his passing, Muslim rule extended from Spain to Central Asia.

By Twain's time in 1867, the Ottoman Empire — the seat of the Sunni caliphate — had ruled Syria and Palestine for over three centuries. Damascus was a major center of Islamic learning and pilgrimage, second only to Cairo in the Arab provinces. The 1860 massacre of Christians in Damascus referenced in Twain's account (044-024) had occurred just seven years before his visit, and the tensions between Muslim populations and European travelers were acute. Twain's observations of Mohammedan customs — dietary restrictions (008-014, 022-024), ablution practices (009-013), veiling of women (044-013), and the taboo on Christians entering mosques (009-002, 054-011) — reflect the lived reality of sharia-influenced Ottoman society as encountered by a Western outsider.

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's treatment of Muhammad and Islam is consistently satirical but not monolithic. When the Prophet's instinct aligns with Twain's own aesthetic judgment — as at the Damascus overlook — he grants a backhanded compliment: "The Prophet was wise without knowing it" (044-006). More often, Twain deploys the relics and traditions as material for the same skeptical comedy he directs at Catholic shrines and guidebook pieties. The suspended rock of Omar, the eighteen-inch footprints, the scalp-lock doctrine — all are delivered with straight-faced mockery that exposes what Twain sees as the credulity of the faithful.

Yet Twain's contempt is directed less at Muhammad personally than at the degraded state of contemporary Islam as he perceives it. He describes Damascenes as "the ugliest, wickedest looking villains we have seen" (044-013) and rails against Mohammedans who "would not defile their hands by burying the infidel dogs" after the Christian massacre (044-024). His commentary reveals the cultural supremacism of a nineteenth-century American Protestant abroad, but it also preserves, in granular detail, the texture of Ottoman-era Islamic observance — the green turbans of the hadjis, the food taboos, the ablution fountains, the call to prayer — that a more reverent observer might have skipped.
