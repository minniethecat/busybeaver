# Baalbec

Ancient temple complex and ruined city in the Beqaa Valley of modern Lebanon, reached by the excursion party on horseback and tent from Beirut as the first major stop of the "long trip" through Syria and Palestine — the site of the colossal Temple of the Sun whose construction, Twain concluded, must have been the work of "a race of gods or of giants."

## Overview

Baalbec was the first great ruin of the Holy Land expedition, encountered after difficult overland travel from Beirut. The party had arranged "horses, pack animals, and tents" to journey to "Baalbec, Damascus, the Sea of Tiberias, and thence southward" to Jerusalem (041-008). Already from miles away, "through the glasses," they could discern "the faint outlines of the wonderful ruins of Baalbec, the supposed Baal-Gad of Scripture" (042-012). Twain found the place architecturally overwhelming — a triumph of ancient engineering that dwarfed anything in the modern world.

## Historical Mystery

Unlike the Italian cities with their documented histories, Baalbec presented an enigma. "It has stood there for thousands of years, the wonder and admiration of travelers; but who built it, or when it was built, are questions that may never be answered" (043-005). Twain was certain of one thing: "Such grandeur of design, and such grace of execution, as one sees in the temples of Baalbec, have not been equaled or even approached in any work of men's hands that has been built within twenty centuries past" (043-005).

The temples — "the great Temple of the Sun, the Temple of Jupiter, and several smaller temples" — were "clustered together in the midst of one of these miserable Syrian villages, and look strangely enough in such plebeian company" (043-006). The contrast between the sublime ruins and the squalid village that surrounded them was one of the starkest juxtapositions of the entire expedition.

In a later retrospective, Twain placed Baalbec among the world's most ancient cities, noting that Damascus "saw the foundations of Baalbec, and Thebes, and Ephesus laid; she saw these villages grow into mighty cities, and amaze the world with their grandeur — and she has lived to see them desolate, deserted, and given over to the owls and the bats" (044-009).

## The Temple of the Sun

The Great Temple was immense: "nearly three hundred feet long and one hundred and sixty feet wide. It had fifty-four columns around it, but only six are standing now — the others lie broken at its base, a confused and picturesque heap" (043-006). The six surviving columns, with "their bases, Corinthian capitals and entablature — and six more shapely columns do not exist" — rose to a combined height of ninety feet with the entablature (043-006). "A prodigious altitude for shafts of stone to reach, truly — and yet one only thinks of their beauty and symmetry when looking at them; the pillars look slender and delicate, the entablature, with its elaborate sculpture, looks like rich stucco-work" (043-006).

The optical illusion of scale was one of Twain's central observations. When gazing upward, the columns seemed almost fragile. But "when you have gazed aloft till your eyes are weary, you glance at the great fragments of pillars among which you are standing, and find that they are eight feet through; and with them lie beautiful capitals apparently as large as a small cottage; and also single slabs of stone, superbly sculptured, that are four or five feet thick, and would completely cover the floor of any ordinary parlor. You wonder where these monstrous things came from, and it takes some little time to satisfy yourself that the airy and graceful fabric that towers above your head is made up of their mates. It seems too preposterous" (043-006).

## The Megalithic Platform

The foundation platform of the Great Temple surpassed even the columns in its monumentality. These substructions were "built upon massive substructions that might support a world, almost; the materials used are blocks of stone as large as an omnibus — very few, if any of them, are smaller than a carpenter's tool chest — and these substructions are traversed by tunnels of masonry through which a train of cars might pass" (043-006).

Twain devoted particular attention to the largest stones: "I can not conceive how those immense blocks of stone were ever hauled from the quarries, or how they were ever raised to the dizzy heights they occupy in the temples" (043-008). One stretch of platform, "two hundred feet long, is composed of blocks of stone as large, and some of them larger, than a streetcar" (043-008). But these were dwarfed by three stones in another section: "In combined length these three stones stretch nearly two hundred feet; they are thirteen feet square; two of them are sixty-four feet long each, and the third is sixty-nine. They are built into the massive wall some twenty feet above the ground. They are there, but how they got there is the question. I have seen the hull of a steamboat that was smaller than one of those stones" (043-008).

The conclusion was inescapable: "A race of gods or of giants must have inhabited Baalbec many a century ago. Men like the men of our day could hardly rear such temples as these" (043-008).

## The Quarry Stone

The visit to the quarry, "about a quarter of a mile off, and down hill," revealed an even larger stone — the famous "Stone of the Pregnant Woman," though Twain does not use that name. "In a great pit lay the mate of the largest stone in the ruins. It lay there just as the giants of that old forgotten time had left it when they were called hence — just as they had left it, to remain for thousands of years, an eloquent rebuke unto such as are prone to think slightingly of the men who lived before them" (043-009).

The dimensions were staggering: "a solid mass fourteen feet by seventeen, and but a few inches less than seventy feet long! Two buggies could be driven abreast of each other, on its surface, from one end of it to the other, and leave room enough for a man or two to walk on either side" (043-009). The quarry stone, squared and ready but never moved, became Twain's symbol for the lost capacities of the ancient builders.

## Graffiti and Vandalism

From awe, Twain pivoted to one of his most characteristic irritations: tourist graffiti. "One might swear that all the John Smiths and George Wilkinsons, and all the other pitiful nobodies between Kingdom Come and Baalbec would inscribe their poor little names upon the walls of Baalbec's magnificent ruins, and would add the town, the county and the State they came from — and swearing thus, be infallibly correct" (043-010). His contempt was unvarnished: "It is a pity some great ruin does not fall in and flatten out some of these reptiles, and scare their kind out of ever giving their names to fame upon any walls or monuments again, forever" (043-010).

The vandalism extended to his own party. In a later chapter, Twain lamented: "The incorrigible pilgrims have come in with their pockets full of specimens broken from the ruins. I wish this vandalism could be stopped. They broke off fragments from Noah's tomb; from the exquisite sculptures of the temples of Baalbec... Heaven protect the Sepulchre when this tribe invades Jerusalem!" (045-016).

## The Horse Named Baalbec

In a comic coda, Twain immortalized Baalbec in an unexpected way. Acquiring a wretched horse on the journey from Baalbec to Damascus — "One of his hind legs bends the wrong way, and the other one is as straight and stiff as a tent-pole. Most of his teeth are gone, and he is as blind as bat" — Twain "finally concluded to call him Baalbec, because he is such a magnificent ruin" (045-027). The joke persisted: "Look at poor cropped and dilapidated 'Baalbec,' and weep for the sentiment that has been wasted upon the Selims of romance!" (045-030).

This naming was one of Twain's most effective comic devices — applying the language of architectural appreciation to a broken-down horse, and thereby implicitly comparing the noble ruin to a decrepit animal. Both were magnificent in conception, both had seen better days, and both were being ill-used by the Arabs of the present.
