# Donkeys

The small, obstinate, and perpetually galloping donkeys on which the pilgrims traversed the Holy Land — a mode of transport that Twain transforms from practical necessity into a sustained comic meditation on dignity, discomfort, and the American body in foreign space.

## Overview

If the *Quaker City* was the pilgrims' floating home, the donkey was their terrestrial destiny. From Damascus to Jerusalem to the Pyramids, the pilgrims mounted these "diminutive animals" and submitted to the mercies of donkey-boys who drove them at relentless speed through narrow streets and rocky paths. Twain's descriptions of donkey-riding constitute some of the book's most vivid physical comedy: the pilgrims' long legs dangling to the ground, their dignity shattered by each stumble, their bodies battered by hours in the saddle.

## In Twain's Narrative

The Damascus donkeys provide the template. Twain describes riding through the city's narrow streets: "The narrow streets swarmed like a hive with men and women in strange Oriental costumes, and our small donkeys knocked them right and left as we plowed through them" (044-014). The donkey-boys are merciless: "These persecutors run after the animals, shouting and goading them for hours together; they keep the donkey in a gallop always" (044-014). The result is chaos: "The donkeys fell down and spilt us over their heads occasionally, but there was nothing for it but to mount and hurry on again."

The Holy Land chapters intensify the theme. Donkeys become the vehicle — literally — for every comic humiliation the pilgrimage can devise. Tall Americans on tiny animals, urged on by shouting boys, careening through streets unchanged since biblical times: the image is both ridiculous and strangely apt. Pilgrims on donkeys are pilgrims indeed — not the dignified pilgrims of Renaissance paintings, but pilgrims in the biblical sense: dusty, tired, and absurd.

## Cultural Context

The donkey was the universal transport of the Ottoman Middle East — cheap, hardy, and available everywhere. For the locals, it was normal; for the Americans, accustomed to carriages and streetcars, it was a daily assault on their dignity. The donkey thus becomes a measure of cultural distance: what is mundane for one culture is mortifying for another.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's donkey episodes are among his most purely comic passages. He never moralizes about donkeys — he simply describes what happens when you put an American on one. The comedy is physical, immediate, and universal. Everyone who has ridden a donkey knows the feeling of helplessness Twain captures: the animal going where it wants, at whatever pace it chooses, while the rider clings on and hopes for the best.

> "I do not like riding in the Damascus streetcars" (044-014)

The deadpan comparison of donkey transport to streetcars is classic Twain: the modern infrastructure of one culture becomes the comic vehicle of another.
