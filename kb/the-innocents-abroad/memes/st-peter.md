# St. Peter

St. Peter the Apostle — fisherman, disciple, and the rock upon which the Church was built — appears in [[the-innocents-abroad]] primarily through the vast basilica that bears his name. Twain visits [[rome]] repeatedly during his Mediterranean [[pilgrimage]] and finds St. Peter's both overwhelming in its scale and irresistible as a target for his irreverent wit. The apostle himself surfaces in relics, statuary, and the constant invocation of his authority by the Catholic establishment that Twain so gleefully satirizes.

## In the Travelogue

Twain encounters St. Peter's legacy throughout his Roman sojourn and beyond. The basilica named for the apostle becomes a recurring benchmark — every other grand church in Europe is measured against it. At Milan, he notes that "the Cathedral of Milan is second only to St. Peter's at Rome" [018-029], establishing the basilica as the unquestioned summit of ecclesiastical architecture. Throughout the journey, comparisons to St. Peter's serve as shorthand for ultimate scale: the Sphinx is described in relation to it, and even the dirt of the Church of the Nativity in [[jerusalem]] is said to be inferior because "it is said to be more wonderful than St. Peter's, but its dirt is much more so" [033-017].

The apostle himself appears in the narrative through the relics that Twain catalogues with characteristic skepticism. Among the holy fragments preserved in European churches, he records "one of St. Peter's" fingers alongside other sacred bones [018-026]. In Roman sculpture, a group "represents the Saviour, St. Peter, Pope Leo, St. Silvester, Constantine and Charlemagne" [028-032], placing the apostle in the grand pageant of Church history that the statuary celebrates.

## Historical Context

St. Peter, originally named Simon, was a Galilean fisherman called by Jesus to become a "fisher of men." According to the Gospels, Jesus declared him the rock upon which the Church would be founded and gave him the keys to the kingdom of heaven — a passage that underpins the papacy's claim to apostolic succession. Tradition holds that Peter traveled to Rome, led the Christian community there, and was martyred under Nero, crucified upside down at his own request because he felt unworthy to die in the same manner as Christ.

The Basilica of St. Peter in Vatican City stands over what the Church believes to be the apostle's tomb. Construction of the current Renaissance basilica began in 1506 under Pope Julius II and spanned over a century, employing the talents of Bramante, Raphael, Michelangelo, and Bernini. Michelangelo, whom Twain discusses at length in his travelogue, contributed the iconic dome. As Twain notes with characteristic understatement, "he designed St. Peter's" [027-010] — an achievement that, in Twain's telling, ranks alongside designing the Pope and perhaps the entire Roman enterprise.

## The Basilica's Grandeur

Twain devotes sustained attention to the overwhelming physical presence of the basilica. He visited it repeatedly, declaring "I have been to the monster Church of St. Peter, frequently. I knew its dimensions" [026-008]. The sheer scale defeats ordinary description: "St. Peter's is bulky. Its height and size would represent two of the Washington Capitol set one on top of the other" [026-009].

The perspective from within produces one of Twain's most vivid spatial observations:

> "To stand in the door of St. Peter's and look at men down toward its further extremity, two blocks away, has a diminishing effect on those men — they look smaller than full-grown men should look." [026-010]

From the dome, the vast panorama of Rome unfolds: "From the dome of St. Peter's one can see every notable object in Rome" [026-012]. And yet, after all this buildup, Twain delivers a characteristically perverse refusal:

> "I prefer not to describe St. Peter's. It has been done before." [026-013]

The basilica remains a touchstone throughout the journey. From a distance, "nothing can utterly blot St. Peter's from sight and leave it swelling above the level plain" [055-003]. And in a final, memorable tribute, Twain writes that "we shall remember St. Peter's: not as one sees it when he walks in it, but as a picture in the mind, framed in the blue Italian sky" [062-006].

## Relics and Sacred Objects

The basilica houses a collection of relics that Twain catalogues with barely concealed amusement. Among the most notable are architectural fragments with biblical provenance. He reports that "they have twelve small pillars, in St. Peter's, which came from Solomon's Temple" [026-011] — a claim that places fragments of ancient Jerusalem within the walls of Renaissance Rome.

The apostolic relics extend beyond architecture to the physical remains of saints. The fragment of St. Peter's finger [018-026] belongs to a sprawling inventory of sacred bones that Twain encounters across Europe — a collection that includes, in the same passage, fragments of St. Paul and even a bone of Judas Iscariot. The commercial dimension of relic veneration does not escape him: access to holy sites comes at a price, and "at St. Peter's, at Rome, it is almost impossible to see anything without paying for it" [054-003].

## Twain's Assessment

Twain's treatment of St. Peter's is a study in productive contradiction. He clearly regards the basilica as genuinely magnificent — he returns to it repeatedly, measures other monuments against it, and records its dimensions with care. His final word on the subject, that it will be remembered as "a picture in the mind, framed in the blue Italian sky" [062-006], is among the most lyrical passages in the entire travelogue.

Yet the irreverence never stays suppressed for long. The "monster Church" [026-008] is too vast, too wealthy, too entangled with the commercial machinery of relics and indulgences to escape Twain's satirical impulse entirely. His refusal to describe the building — "I prefer not to describe St. Peter's. It has been done before" [026-013] — is simultaneously a joke at the reader's expense, a jab at the convention of travel writing, and a tacit admission that language cannot capture the experience. It is pure Twain: refusing to play the game even as he plays it masterfully.

St. Peter the apostle, in contrast, receives almost no direct commentary from Twain beyond the relics and art that invoke him. Twain's quarrel is not with the Galilean fisherman but with the institutional apparatus that claims his authority — the papacy, the cathedral-building enterprise, and the relic industry that monetizes fragments of the sacred dead.
