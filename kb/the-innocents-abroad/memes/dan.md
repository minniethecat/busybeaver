# Dan

A passenger aboard the *Quaker City* excursion and one of Mark Twain's closest companions throughout the voyage, appearing alongside "the Doctor" and "Jack" in numerous comic episodes across Europe and the Holy Land.

## Overview

Dan is one of the core trio of Twain's intimate traveling companions on the *Quaker City* voyage, along with the Doctor (the ship's surgeon) and Jack. He appears in many of the book's most memorable comic set pieces, from the glove-buying debacle in Gibraltar to the barber-shop prank in Venice. Twain portrays Dan as a plain-spoken, practical man who often cuts through the pretense of his companions with dry, laconic wit. His name is frequently paired with Jack's -- "Dan and Jack" -- suggesting they form a recognizable duo within the excursion party.

Dan's role in the narrative is that of the straight-talking everyman. While the Doctor and Twain himself engage in elaborate displays of (often mangled) Continental erudition, Dan provides the blunt American perspective, unashamed of his ignorance of foreign languages and customs. Twain clearly likes him, and Dan's deadpan observations consistently serve as the comic puncturing of European pretension and his companions' affectations.

## In the Travelogue

Dan first appears in Chapter 7, during the stop at Gibraltar, where he accompanies Twain and the ship's surgeon to buy kid gloves before the theater (007-037). When Twain is flattered into buying gloves that are far too small and rips them to shreds attempting to put them on, it is Dan who delivers the first deadpan mockery: "Some gentlemen don't know how to put on kid gloves at all, but some do" (007-047). He then soliloquizes with the Doctor about the "grace" that "only comes with long, very long practice" (007-051), cementing one of the book's early running jokes.

During the Fourth of July celebrations at sea in Chapter 10, Dan delivers the memorable dismissal of the Oracle's pompous pseudo-scientific blather: "Oh, go to bed!" (010-005). When the Oracle protests, Jack observes, "Dan don't never stand any chance in an argument with me" (010-006), establishing Dan's impatience with pretension as a consistent character note.

In Marseilles, Dan's practical impatience with his companions' broken French provides ongoing comedy. When the Doctor and Twain try to communicate with a boatman in elaborate but incomprehensible French, Dan says simply, "Oh, go to the pier, you old fool -- that's where we want to go!" (010-021). When his companions insist on using French and the boatman brings them to the *douain* (customhouse) instead of the hotel, the Doctor claims "he is going to the hotel" -- and Dan's name becomes shorthand for the uncultivated but correct observer (010-025).

In Paris, Dan's practicality again asserts itself. When the party hires a guide with the absurdly un-French name "Billfinger," Dan cuts through his companions' romantic fantasies: "Call him Ferguson" (013-012). When Ferguson (formerly Billfinger) keeps steering the party toward silk shops in hopes of commissions, it is Dan who finally snaps: "Confound the idiot! I don't want to see any silks today, and I won't look at them. Drive on" (013-034).

At the Zoological Gardens in Marseilles, Dan and the Doctor share genuine laughter at a comical bird they nickname "The Pilgrim" -- Twain notes that "such natural and such enjoyable laughter had not been heard among our excursionists since our ship sailed away from America" (011-008). Dan quips that "All he wants now is a Plymouth Collection" (011-009), showing his familiarity with American religious culture.

In Milan, Dan features in the bathtub scene at a public bathhouse. While Twain and the Doctor argue over whether to call for soap in Italian or French, Dan's voice "rose on the air: 'Oh, bring some soap, why don't you!'" (019-018), spelling it out letter by letter in increasing exasperation (019-021). The Doctor rebukes him: "Dan, how often have we told you that these foreigners cannot understand English?" (019-023).

In Venice, Dan participates in one of the book's most elaborate pranks. A barber gives both him and the Doctor smooth shaves, and the two conspire to lure Twain into the chair with their praises: "Dan, this is the easiest shave I have had since we left the ship" (023-036); "Dan, it is perfect luxury" (023-042). When Twain finally succumbs and is brutally scraped, he finds "Dan and the Doctor were both wiping blood off their faces and laughing" (023-045). They had endured torturous shaves themselves purely to set the trap.

At Pompeii -- in the chapter Twain ironically titled to show he would not use the phrase "butchered to make a Roman holiday" -- Dan's exasperation with the constant Michelangelo attribution reaches its peak in Rome. "Dan said the other day to the guide, 'Enough, enough, enough! Say no more! Lump the whole thing! say that the Creator made Italy from designs by Michelangelo!'" (027-010).

In the Holy Land, Dan appears as part of the riding party through Syria and Palestine. Twain notes that on the hard ride from Baalbec to Damascus, "Dan's horse and Jack's were so crippled we had to leave them behind" (045-028). On the narrow bridle-path descending from Jerusalem, "Jack was caught two or three times, and Dan and Moult as often" by passing camels and freight (056-003).

In Egypt, Dan and Jack select the most colorfully painted donkeys for the ride to the Pyramids, chosen "because they brought back Italian reminiscences of the 'old masters'" (058-002). At the Great Pyramid, Twain bets repeatedly against an Arab runner, borrowing money to keep wagering. The scene climaxes with Twain saying to Dan, "Lend me a dollar -- I can beat this game, yet" (058-018), and later declaring, "I will stay right here and risk money on you as long as Dan has got a cent" (058-019).

Dan and Jack also accompany Twain on a walk at the Pyramids where they are besieged by beggars, adopting a "new code -- it was millions for defense, but not a cent for baksheesh" (058-024).

## Twain's Assessment

> "It was good to hear Dan and the Doctor laugh -- such natural and such enjoyable laughter had not been heard among our excursionists since our ship sailed away from America." (011-008)

Twain consistently paints Dan as a man of plain speech and common sense. In a book filled with elaborate satire, pretentious guides, and pompous fellow travelers, Dan's role is the American democrat who refuses to be impressed by European airs. His deadpan mockery of Twain's glove-buying vanity (007-047, 007-051), his blunt dismissal of the Oracle's intellectual pretensions (010-005), and his exasperation with foreign languages (010-021, 019-021) all mark him as the voice of unvarnished American practicality.

Yet Twain also shows genuine affection for Dan. The two are clearly close -- Dan is present in almost every private excursion, from Tangier to the Pyramids. The barber-shop prank in Venice (023-045) reveals a relationship comfortable enough for elaborate practical jokes, and Twain consistently records Dan's wry observations as the sensible counterpoint to the absurdities around them.
