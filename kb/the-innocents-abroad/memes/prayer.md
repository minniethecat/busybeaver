# Prayer

Prayer in *The Innocents Abroad* encompasses the full spectrum of religious observance Twain witnessed during the 1867 voyage: Protestant prayer meetings aboard the *Quaker City*, Muslim ritual prayer in Tangier and Constantinople, Catholic devotions at Roman and Venetian shrines, and the pilgrims' fervent worship in the Holy Land.

## Overview

Prayer serves as one of the travelogue's most revealing cultural lenses. Aboard the *Quaker City*, it is scheduled routine — an institution as much social as spiritual, conducted with the same clockwork predictability as meals and dominoes.

In the Mediterranean ports, by contrast, Twain encounters prayer as an embodied, sensory practice. Muslims bow and press foreheads to pavement (009-013), Catholic families float to church by gondola with prayerbooks and beads (023-011), and saints spend whole nights in prayer in underground catacombs (027-079).

The juxtaposition highlights the tension between American Protestant restraint and Old World theatrical devotion.

Yet prayer is also a target of Twain's irreverent humor. The ship's executive officer grumbles that the pilgrims' prayers for fair winds defy both logic and Christian charity. A fair wind for the eastbound *Quaker City* is a head wind for a thousand westbound vessels (004-033/034). This double vision — prayer as genuine spiritual practice and as absurd human presumption — runs throughout the book.

## In Twain's Narrative

The prayer theme enters on the first Saturday night. A gong sounds, and Twain calls prayer meeting "an appropriate relief" after a dismal day of storm and seasickness (002-020). The next day, still stormbound, "we had repetitions of church and prayer-meetings" (003-002). The wry "of course" signals ambivalence: prayer is compulsory, not optional.

The nightly devotions become shipboard ritual. At seven, "the gong sounded and a large majority of the party repaired to the after cabin... for prayers" (004-005). The "unregenerated" called it the "Synagogue." The service was brief: two hymns and a short prayer (004-005). The singing, Twain adds, "was not of a superior order of architecture" (004-028).

The most pointed critique comes from the executive officer. The pilgrims, he says, pray for fair winds "when they know as well as I do that this is the only ship going east this time of the year" (004-034). His verdict: "It ain't good sense, it ain't good reason, it ain't good Christianity, it ain't common human charity. Avast with such nonsense!" (004-034).

Prayer also intrudes on the Holy Land experience. At evening prayers in the pilgrims' tent, a leader reads from Scripture about "the voice of the turtle being heard in the land" (047-011). Young Jack, taking this literally, spends an hour watching a mud-turtle, waiting for it to sing. He concludes the whole thing is "a fraud" when the turtle falls asleep instead.

## Prayer in Muslim Contexts

Twain's encounters with Muslim prayer provide vivid descriptions. In Tangier, the Moor goes to his mosque, removes his shoes, performs ablutions, "makes his salaams, pressing his forehead to the pavement time and again, says his prayers, and goes back to his work" (009-013). The matter-of-fact rhythm — work, prayer, work — suggests respect for devotion integrated into daily life.

The sanctity of the mosque is absolute. When Blucher tries to ride into one, he is stopped: "so dire a profanation is it for a Christian dog to set foot upon the sacred threshold" that no purification can ever restore it for prayer (009-002). Even the pilgrims' glimpse of worshippers at the fountains "was a thing not relished by the Moorish bystanders."

At St. Sophia, Twain's tone turns dismissive. He watches "ragged Turks" bowing and kissing the earth, "muttering prayers the while, and keeping up their gymnastics till they ought to have been tired, if they were not" (033-017). "Gymnastics" reduces prayer to calisthenics; "if they were not" implies disbelief that genuine devotion could sustain such exertion.

In Smyrna, Twain captures the Muslim urban soundscape: "over them all rings out the muezzin's cry from some tall minaret, calling the faithful vagabonds to prayer" (038-011). The pairing "faithful vagabonds" is characteristic — simultaneously honoring the call and undercutting the called.

## Catholic Devotional Practice

Catholic prayer receives Twain's most ambivalent treatment. In Venice, he observes "staid families, with prayerbook and beads, enter the gondola dressed in their Sunday best, and float away to church" (023-011). The scene is rendered without satire — picturesque, almost affectionate.

In Rome, the catacombs were sites of intense devotion. St. Charles Borromeo spent whole nights in prayer there (027-079). Legend held that St. Philip Neri's heart was "so inflamed with divine love as to burst his ribs" (027-080); Twain dryly wonders "what Philip had for dinner" (027-081). Catacomb walls are inscribed with "prayers, or sentences expressive of Christian hopes" (027-078).

Twain also visits the Church of Ara Coeli, quoting the legend of Pope Gregory leading a penitential procession to end a plague (027-086). His willingness to reproduce the liturgical Latin suggests fascination with Catholic ritual, even as the skeptical framing keeps the reader at a distance.

## The Hermit's Prayer

The most extreme prayer Twain encounters is at Mars Saba in the Judean desert. The founder achieved sainthood through self-torture, a diet of bread and water, total withdrawal from society, and "his constant prayer and saintly contemplation of a skull" (055-033).

The phrase captures the paradox: the hermits' devotion is admirable in its intensity yet grotesque in its materiality. They pray over death's-heads, sealed away from all human contact, including women, for life (055-033).

## Twain's Perspective

Twain approaches prayer with the double consciousness he brings to all religious subjects. He participates in shipboard meetings and never mocks the act of prayer itself. But he is relentlessly attentive to the human comedy: the bad singing, the self-serving weather petitions, the pilgrim who expects turtles to sing on cue.

> "The congregational singing at church and prayers was not of a superior order of architecture." (004-028)

When Twain turns to Muslim and Catholic devotion, his language shifts between ethnographic curiosity and pointed skepticism. He watches Muslim prayer with the eye of a tourist — noting the "gymnastics," the ablutions, the muezzin's cry — but rarely with the eye of a fellow worshipper. Catholic vigils, relics, and miracle stories provoke his most extended meditations on faith and credulity.

Yet a thread of respect runs beneath the satire. Venetian families with their prayerbooks, the Moor at his ablutions, the hermits of Mars Saba — these are people for whom prayer is woven into the fabric of life, not segmented into fifteen-minute intervals. Twain may not share their faith, but he records its expressions with an attention that borders on reverence.

## Examples in the Travelogue

- The *Quaker City*'s nightly prayer meetings: "two hymns from the Plymouth Collection and a short prayer, and seldom occupied more than fifteen minutes" (004-005)
- Muslim prayer in Tangier: "pressing his forehead to the pavement time and again, says his prayers, and goes back to his work" (009-013)
- Venetian families going to church "with prayerbook and beads" by gondola (023-011)
- St. Charles Borromeo spending "whole nights in prayer" in the catacombs (027-079)
- Turkish worshippers in St. Sophia bowing and kissing the earth, "muttering prayers the while" (033-017)
- The muezzin's call in Smyrna "calling the faithful vagabonds to prayer" (038-011)
- Evening prayers in the pilgrims' tent, where a scriptural passage about singing turtles causes confusion (047-011)
- Mars Saba hermits: "constant prayer and saintly contemplation of a skull" (055-033)
