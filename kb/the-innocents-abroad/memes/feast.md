# Feast

A large, celebratory meal — a recurring motif in *The Innocents Abroad* that reveals Twain's interest in the social rituals, culinary novelties, and occasional absurdities of dining across cultures.

## Overview

Feasting in *The Innocents Abroad* encompasses far more than the consumption of food. For Twain, meals are social theater — occasions for cultural observation, comic anecdote, and the revelation of character. From the communal dining aboard the Quaker City to the exotic fare of Damascus, from the formal banquets of Europe to the simple sustenance of the Holy Land, Twain uses the shared table as a privileged vantage point on the cultures he visits.

The theme also carries religious overtones throughout the travelogue. The "feast of St. Mark" in Venice [027-086], the biblical feasts referenced in Jerusalem, and the contrast between Catholic feast-day celebrations and Protestant American simplicity all provide Twain with material for his characteristic blend of curiosity and irreverence.

## In Twain's Narrative

Feasting appears early in the book, with Twain reporting that one of his fellow passengers "wanted to give a feast" aboard the Quaker City [005-014]. The shipboard meals become a running theme — sometimes convivial, sometimes trying — and they set a baseline of American expectations against which foreign dining customs are measured.

In Damascus, Twain describes feasting his "eyes upon the earthly paradise" of the view from the city walls [044-004]. In Ephesus, he recounts the legend of Christians who "entered into it and feasted" in a cave before their martyrdom [040-019]. In the Holy Land, he notes with pleasure that at last there are "no feasting flies" — a small mercy that speaks to the hardships of desert travel [051-020].

The trip's culmination includes a feast of fresh dates and other pleasant provisions [058-033], a fitting conclusion to a journey in which the quality of one's meals was often the difference between a good day and a miserable one.

## Cultural and Historical Context

In the 1860s, dining customs varied enormously across the regions Twain visited. French and Italian cuisine were already regarded as the pinnacle of European gastronomy, while Middle Eastern food was exotic and unfamiliar to most American travelers. Religious feast days structured the calendar of Catholic Europe, with elaborate culinary traditions attached to each observance. For Protestant Americans, the contrast between fasting and feasting traditions — and the perceived indulgence of Catholic feast culture — was a recurring source of cultural commentary.

## Twain's Perspective

Twain's attitude toward feasting is characteristically American: he appreciates abundance but distrusts ostentation, enjoys novelty but grows weary of unfamiliar food, and is always ready to puncture pretension with comic observation. His ideal meal seems to involve simple, plentiful food eaten in good company — a standard that was met more often in the informal settings of the voyage than in the formal banquets of Europe.

> "I shall not tell how we feasted on fresh dates and enjoyed the refreshing watermelons..." [058-033]
