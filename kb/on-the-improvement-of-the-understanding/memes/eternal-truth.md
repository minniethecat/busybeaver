# Eternal Truth

An eternal truth (*aeterna veritas*) in Spinoza's *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione* is a proposition "which, being positive, could never become negative" (017-001) — a truth whose negation is impossible and whose nature implies necessary existence. Eternal truths constitute the "fixed and eternal things" and their laws from which the essences of all particular mutable things must be derived (015-008).

## Definition

Spinoza defines eternal truth through a contrast with contingent existence:

"By an eternal truth, I mean that which being positive could never become negative. Thus it is a primary and eternal truth that God exists, but it is not an eternal truth that Adam thinks. That the Chimaera does not exist is an eternal truth, that Adam does not think is not so." (017-001)

Key characteristics:

- **Necessity of being**: An eternal truth is one whose contrary is impossible — it "could never become negative" (017-001)
- **Connection to essence**: "If the nature of the thing known implies necessary existence, we cannot possibly be in error with regard to its existence" (010-004). Eternal truths are those whose essence entails existence.
- **Distinction from mutable things**: The existence of particular mutable things "has no connection with their essence, or... is not an eternal truth" (015-007). Their existence or non-existence depends on external causes, not on their nature.
- **Immunity to fiction**: "Fiction cannot be concerned with eternal truths" (009-005). One cannot feign that an eternal truth is otherwise, because the mind, knowing the essence, cannot conceive the contrary.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Eternal truths play a foundational role in the method's progression from inadequate to adequate knowledge:

1. **As the proper objects of intellect**: The understanding's highest activity is the cognition of eternal things and their laws. "The essences of particular mutable things are not to be gathered from their series or order of existence... This inmost essence must be sought solely from fixed and eternal things, and from the laws, inscribed (so to speak) in those things as in their true codes" (015-008).

2. **As a constraint on fiction**: Eternal truths mark the boundary of what can be feigned. "If the existence of a conceived thing is in itself an eternal truth, fiction can have no part in it" (010-002). The mind cannot entertain fictions about what it clearly and distinctly perceives to be necessarily true.

3. **As the goal of definition**: Perfect definitions must capture the "inmost essence of a thing" (015-001), and this essence, for eternal things, is inseparable from necessary existence. The progression from definitions of abstracta (like the circle) to definitions of "physical beings and realities" is a progression toward eternal truths.

4. **As the foundation of knowledge**: Before seeking knowledge of particular mutable things, we must first acquire "a sufficient knowledge of eternal things and their infallible laws" (015-009). The knowledge of eternal things provides the deductive basis from which knowledge of particulars can be derived.

5. **As the object of method's foundation**: The "foundation which must direct our thoughts" is "the knowledge of that which constitutes the reality of truth, and the knowledge of the understanding, its properties, and powers" (016-002). This foundation, once acquired, provides "a path whereby the intellect, according to its capacity, may attain the knowledge of eternal things."

## Key Passages

> "By an eternal truth, I mean that which being positive could never become negative. Thus it is a primary and eternal truth that God exists, but it is not an eternal truth that Adam thinks." (017-001)

> "Fiction cannot be concerned with eternal truths." (009-005)

> "If the existence of a conceived thing is in itself an eternal truth, fiction can have no part in it; but if the existence of the conceived be not an eternal truth, we have only to be careful such existence be compared to the thing's essence, and to consider the order of nature." (010-002)

> "By the series of causes and real entities, I do not here mean the series of particular and mutable things, but only the series of fixed and eternal things." (015-007)

> "This inmost essence must be sought solely from fixed and eternal things, and from the laws, inscribed (so to speak) in those things as in their true codes, according to which all particular things take place and are arranged." (015-008)

## Notes

The concept of eternal truth in the *Tractatus* occupies a middle ground between the scholastic doctrine of *aeternae veritates* as ideas in the divine mind and Spinoza's mature doctrine in the *Ethics*, where "the eyes of the mind, by which it sees and observes things, are the demonstrations themselves" (*Ethics* V, Proposition 23, Scholium). In the *Tractatus*, eternal truths are both ontological (they describe the fixed structure of reality) and epistemological (they constrain what can be thought and feigned).

The endnote definition at 017-001 is deceptively simple. "That which being positive could never become negative" means more than logical necessity — it means that the truth in question follows from the nature of the thing itself, not from contingent external causes. "God exists" is an eternal truth because God's essence involves existence; "Adam thinks" is not, because Adam's existence depends on causes outside his essence. This distinction between *essentia* and *existentia* — and the claim that only the former yields eternal truths — is foundational for Spinoza's entire method.
