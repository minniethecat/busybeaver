# Proof

Proof (*demonstratio*) is the deductive chain that extends certain knowledge from a foundational true idea to its consequences. Spinoza models proof on Euclidean geometry, where each proposition is demonstrated from prior propositions, ultimately grounded in self-evident axioms (005-006).

## Definition

Spinoza treats proof as a structured chain of reasoning:

- **Proof by deduction**: "to be certain that our starting-point is really a true idea, we need proof. This first course of reasoning must be supported by a second, the second by a third, and so on" (008-001). Proof is serial — each link validates the next.
- **Proof vs. intuition**: Some truths require no proof — "when once it is understood, manifests itself, we have need only of an example without further proof" (017-019). The foundation itself is unproved (it is self-evident).
- **Mathematical model**: Euclid's demonstration that certain numbers are proportionals "from the nature and property of proportion" (005-006) exemplifies genuine proof.

Proof, then, is not mere argumentation but a **necessary inference** from the essence of the thing.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Proof serves three functions:

1. **Validation**: Proof confirms that a derived idea genuinely follows from a true idea, preventing the intrusion of fictitious or false ideas.
2. **Extension**: Through proof, the mind extends its knowledge from what is immediately known (the foundation) to what is mediately known (remote consequences).
3. **Correction**: The requirement of proof exposes errors — if a claimed truth cannot be demonstrated from a true idea, it is not knowledge but opinion.

Spinoza contrasts genuine proof with the arbitrary rules "given without proof by their masters" (005-005), which tradesmen memorize but do not understand. Genuine proof produces understanding, not mere compliance.

## Key Passages

> "Mathematicians, however, know by the proof of the nineteenth proposition of the seventh book of Euclid, what numbers are proportionals, namely, from the nature and property of proportion it follows that the product of the first and fourth will be equal to the product of the second and third." (005-006)

> "Tradesmen will at once tell us that they know what is required to find the fourth number, for they have not yet forgotten the rule which was given to them arbitrarily without proof by their masters." (005-005)

> "When once it is understood, manifests itself, we have need only of an example without further proof." (017-019)

## Notes

The Euclidean model of proof pervades Spinoza's thinking. In the *Ethics*, he will adopt the full *more geometrico* apparatus of definitions, axioms, propositions, demonstrations, and scholia. The *Tractatus* already shows this commitment: genuine knowledge is demonstrated knowledge. Yet Spinoza also recognizes that the foundation itself cannot be proved — it must be directly intuited. This creates a two-tier epistemology: intuitive grasp of foundations + deductive proof of consequences.
