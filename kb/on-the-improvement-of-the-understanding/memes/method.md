# Method

Method (*methodus*) is the centerpiece of Spinoza's *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione*. It is defined as reflective knowledge — "the idea of an idea" (007-006) — and constitutes the mind's ordered procedure for distinguishing truth from error and for acquiring new knowledge. Method is not a set of external rules but the mind's innate capacity for self-correction, operating from the standard of a true idea already possessed.

## Definition

Spinoza gives method a precise definition through several formulations:

"Method is nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea" (007-006). This means method is thinking about thinking — it operates on ideas already present, not directly on things. Crucially, "there can be no method without a pre-existent idea" (007-006): one cannot devise a method without already having at least one true idea to serve as its standard.

Method is "the discernment of a true idea, by distinguishing it from other perceptions, and by investigating its nature" (007-005). Its goal is practical: "that we may so train our mind that it may, by a given standard, comprehend whatsoever is intelligible" (007-005).

## What Method Is Not

Spinoza carefully distinguishes method from:
- "Reasoning in the search for causes" — method is not causal inquiry itself (007-005)
- "The comprehension of the causes of things" — method is not metaphysics (007-005)
- "Seeking for the signs of truth after the acquisition of the idea" — the true method "teaches us the order in which we should seek for truth itself" (007-004)

## The Self-Amplifying Character of Method

A crucial feature is method's self-reinforcing nature: "the more things the mind knows, the better does it understand its own strength and the order of nature" (007-008). As the mind acquires true ideas, it simultaneously gains new instruments for further inquiry — method grows more powerful through its own exercise.

## Key Passages

> "Method is nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea; and... there can be no method without a pre-existent idea." (007-006)

> "That will be a good method which shows us how the mind should be directed, according to the standard of the given true idea." (007-006)

> "Method is not identical with reasoning in the search for causes, still less is it the comprehension of the causes of things: it is the discernment of a true idea, by distinguishing it from other perceptions." (007-005)

> "The more things the mind knows, the better does it understand its own strength and the order of nature." (007-008)

## Notes

Spinoza's conception of method as "reflective knowledge" (cognitio reflexiva) owes a debt to Descartes' *Regulae ad Directionem Ingenii*, but Spinoza radicalizes it: method is not a set of rules applied externally but the mind's self-knowledge operating through ideas. The idea of method as "idea ideae" (idea of an idea) directly anticipates the parallelistic psychology of *Ethics* Part II.
