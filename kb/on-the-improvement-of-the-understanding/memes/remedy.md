# Remedy

The remedy (*remedium*) is the central metaphor of the *Tractatus*'s opening: the method of improving the understanding is the cure for a mind sick with false goods. Spinoza portrays himself as "a sick man struggling with a deadly disease" who "is compelled to seek a remedy with all his strength, inasmuch as his whole hope lies therein" (002-005).

## Definition

The remedy-concept has three dimensions:

- **Medical metaphor**: The mind is ill — absorbed in riches, fame, and sensual pleasure. These ordinary objects "not only bring no remedy that tends to preserve our being, but even act as hindrances, causing the death not seldom of those who possess them" (002-005). The search for a remedy IS the search for the method.

- **The remedy is method**: "One thing was evident, namely, that while my mind was employed with these thoughts it turned away from its former objects of desire" (002-009). The very act of seeking the remedy is already therapeutic — the mind begins to heal as it turns toward the true good.

- **Doubt as disease**: "We have now to investigate the doubtful idea — that is, to inquire what can cause us to doubt, and how doubt may be removed" (011-001). The method removes doubt as medicine removes disease.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The remedy metaphor establishes the **existential urgency** of the method:

1. **Motivation**: The treatise is not an academic exercise but a matter of life and death for the mind. The sick man "sees that death will surely be upon him unless a remedy be found" (002-005).

2. **Diagnosis**: The illness is attachment to perishable goods. The remedy redirects love toward "a thing eternal and infinite" (002-008).

3. **Therapeutic process**: The method itself is the remedy — not a single dose but a regimen. Each step of the method (distinguishing true from false ideas, forming good definitions) is part of the ongoing cure.

The remedy is the *ethical* dimension of method: knowledge is not merely cognitive but therapeutic, restoring the mind to its proper object and condition.

## Key Passages

> "I thus perceived that I was in a state of great peril, and I compelled myself to seek with all my strength for a remedy, however uncertain it might be; as a sick man struggling with a deadly disease, when he sees that death will surely be upon him unless a remedy be found, is compelled to seek a remedy with all his strength, inasmuch as his whole hope lies therein." (002-005)

> "All the objects pursued by the multitude not only bring no remedy that tends to preserve our being, but even act as hindrances, causing the death not seldom of those who possess them." (002-005)

> "One thing was evident, namely, that while my mind was employed with these thoughts it turned away from its former objects of desire, and seriously considered the search for a new principle; this state of things was a great comfort to me, for I perceived that the evils were not such as to resist all remedies." (002-009)

## Notes

The medical metaphor has Stoic roots: the passions are diseases of the soul, philosophy is the therapy. Spinoza inherits this tradition through Descartes and the Dutch context where medical discourse modeled scientific method.

Spinoza's innovation is to fuse the medical and the methodological: the *methodus medendi intellectus* is at once a logical procedure (geometrical deduction) and a spiritual therapy (redirection of desire). To think clearly IS to be healthy; to be absorbed in confused ideas IS to be sick.
