# Riches

In Spinoza's *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione*, riches (Latin *divitiae*) are one of the three "ordinary objects of desire" that men mistakenly pursue as ultimate ends. Alongside fame and sensual pleasure, the pursuit of riches exemplifies the kind of perishable good that distracts the mind from the search for the true and final good — a good that is eternal, infinite, and communicable to all.

## Definition

Spinoza analyzes riches as a paradigmatic case of a misdirected desire:

- **An ordinary object of desire**: Riches are listed among "the benefits which are acquired through fame and riches" (001-002), the pursuit of which occupies men's minds and prevents them from seeking a higher good.
- **Absorbing and distracting**: "The pursuit of honors and riches is likewise very absorbing" (002-002) — the effort required to acquire wealth consumes mental energy that could be directed toward intellectual improvement.
- **Not followed by contentment**: "The attainment of riches and fame is not followed as in the case of sensual pleasure by repentance" (002-003), but this absence of immediate dissatisfaction makes riches more insidious: the pursuer never recognizes the futility of the pursuit.
- **A source of extreme attachment**: Spinoza observes "men who have undergone great risks, even to death, for the sake of their riches" (002-006), illustrating the depth of attachment that perishable goods can inspire.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Riches function in the treatise as a foil for the true good:

1. **Diagnosis of misdirected desire**: Spinoza begins the treatise by recounting his personal experience of recognizing that riches (along with fame and pleasure) could not deliver the happiness they promised. This autobiographical opening establishes the psychological barrier that the method must overcome.

2. **The distinction in how riches are pursued**: In the Endnotes, Spinoza clarifies that he means "distinguishing riches according as they are pursued for their own sake, or as means to higher things" (017-001). Riches are not inherently evil — they become an obstacle only when pursued as ends rather than as means.

3. **The reorientation of love**: Spinoza acknowledges that "I could not forthwith lay aside all love of riches, sensual enjoyment, and fame" (002-008) — the reorientation of desire from perishable to eternal goods is a gradual process, not an instantaneous conversion.

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of riches reflects both Stoic and medieval Jewish ethical traditions. The three ordinary objects of desire — riches, fame, sensual pleasure — echo the traditional Christian classification of the three temptations (wealth, power, lust), but Spinoza's analysis is distinctively philosophical rather than moralistic. Riches are not condemned as sinful; they are analyzed as *irrational objects of desire* — things that promise happiness but cannot deliver it because they are perishable, competitive (what one person gains, another loses), and external (dependent on fortune rather than on the mind's own activity). The Endnotes clarification (017-001) is important: Spinoza does not advocate ascetic renunciation but rather the proper ordering of goods, where riches serve as means to the higher end of intellectual improvement.
