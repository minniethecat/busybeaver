# Eternity

The mode of existence proper to fixed things and the laws governing them, as distinct
from the temporal series of singular, changing existences.

## Definition

In the TIE, "eternity" (*aeternitas*) characterizes the fixed and eternal things that
are the proper objects of the intellect. Unlike singular changing things that exist
in a temporal series, eternal things "are all by nature simultaneous" (015-007) and
their laws are "inscribed in these things as in their true codes" (015-008).

Eternal truths are a related concept: "fiction cannot be concerned with eternal truths"
(009-005); they are necessary and cannot be feigned otherwise. The distinction between
eternal and temporal is Methodological: the intellect seeks knowledge of eternal things,
while imagination is confined to the temporal series.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The method distinguishes two kinds of objects: the "series of singular, changing things"
and the "series of fixed and eternal things" (015-007). The intellect's task is to
grasp the latter: "It would be impossible for human weakness to grasp the series of
singular, changing things" (015-007), but fixed and eternal things, being fewer and
governed by universal laws, are within the intellect's reach.

Knowledge of eternal things is pursued "solely from fixed and eternal things, and from
the laws inscribed in these things" (015-008), not from the temporal series of existence
(015-009). This is the method of definition and deduction that Spinoza advocates.

Love of the eternal also has an ethical dimension: "love towards a thing eternal and
infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy" (002-008).

## Key Passages

> Love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is
> itself unmingled with any sadness. (002-008)

> All things which come to pass, come to pass according to the eternal order and
> fixed laws of nature. (003-001)

> It would be impossible for human weakness to grasp the series of singular, changing
> things... but the series of fixed and eternal things... may be grasped by the human
> mind. (015-007)

> The laws of these things... are inscribed in these things as in their true codes.
> (015-008)

## Notes

"Eternity" in the TIE has methodological rather than theological force. It designates
the type of knowledge the method aims at: knowledge of necessary, universal truths
rather than contingent, singular facts. This maps onto the *Ethics* distinction between
knowledge *sub specie aeternitatis* (under the form of eternity) and knowledge *sub
specie durationis* (under the form of duration).
