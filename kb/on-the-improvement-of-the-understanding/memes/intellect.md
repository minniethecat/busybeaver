# Intellect

The intellect (*intellectus*) is, in Spinoza's *Tractatus*, the active, truth-oriented faculty of the mind. It is the power by which the mind forms true ideas, distinguishes them from false and fictitious ones, and, through the method of reflective knowledge, builds upon its innate instruments to ascend toward complete wisdom (006-007, 007-007).

## Definition

Spinoza does not define *intellectus* formally in the treatise — like *mens*, it is treated as a given capacity whose nature is progressively revealed through method. Its essential features emerge from its contrast with imagination and its role in method:

- The intellect is **active and self-augmenting**: "the intellect, by its native strength, makes for itself intellectual instruments, whereby it acquires strength for performing other intellectual operations" (006-007)
- The intellect is **the seat of true ideas**: a true idea existing in us is "a natural instrument" that the intellect apprehends, enabling it to "understand the difference existing between itself and all other perceptions" (007-007)
- The intellect is **distinct from imagination**: confusion arises "while making no distinction between the imagination and the intellect," leading us to think "that what we more readily imagine is clearer to us" (013-005)
- The intellect is **not passive**: unlike imagination, which is affected by particular bodily objects (012-002), the intellect operates according to its own laws (013-001)

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The intellect is the faculty *through which* method operates. Spinoza's account of its role unfolds in four stages:

**1. The intellect's native strength (006-006, 006-007)**: Spinoza rejects the infinite regress argument against method — the idea that a method for finding truth would itself require a prior method, and so on to infinity. Using the analogy of material tool-making — where men "at first made use of the instruments supplied by nature" and "gradually mounted from the simplest operations to the making of tools" — he argues that the intellect, "by its native strength," possesses innate "intellectual instruments" with which it begins the process of inquiry (006-007).

**2. The nature of method (007-005, 007-006)**: Method is not the search for causes or the comprehension of causes; it is "the discernment of a true idea, by distinguishing it from other perceptions" (007-005). Method is "reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea" — and since there can be no idea of an idea without a pre-existent idea, "there can be no method without a pre-existent idea" (007-006). A good method "shows us how the mind should be directed, according to the standard of the given true idea" (007-006).

**3. The intellect's progressive ascent (007-007)**: "In proportion as it acquires new ideas, the mind simultaneously acquires fresh instruments for pursuing its inquiries further." The true idea existing in us functions as "a natural instrument," and when the mind apprehends this idea, it understands the difference between itself and all other perceptions — "in this, one part of the method consists."

**4. The intellect and fiction (010-002, 010-005)**: The intellect's clarity is the criterion for distinguishing truth from fiction. Confused ideas "compounded of different confused perceptions" give rise to false ideas, but these cannot be confused with the intellect's own clear perceptions once the mind is properly trained (010-005).

## Key Passages

> "The intellect, by its native strength, makes for itself intellectual instruments, whereby it acquires strength for performing other intellectual operations, and from these operations again fresh instruments, or the power of pushing its investigations further, and thus gradually proceeds till it reaches the summit of wisdom." (006-007)

> "Method is nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea; and … there can be no method without a pre-existent idea." (007-006)

> "In proportion as it acquires new ideas, the mind simultaneously acquires fresh instruments for pursuing its inquiries further." (007-007)

> "Sometimes, while making no distinction between the imagination and the intellect, we think that what we more readily imagine is clearer to us; and also we think that what we imagine we understand." (013-005)

## Notes

Spinoza's account of intellect in the *Tractatus* is primarily *methodological* rather than metaphysical. The hammer-and-tool analogy (006-006, 006-007) is one of the most celebrated passages in early modern epistemology: it argues that the intellect possesses innate truth-criteria — "natural instruments" — that enable it to begin inquiry without falling into the skeptical infinite regress. This argument combines a rationalist commitment to innate ideas with a constructivist account of intellectual progress: the intellect builds its tools as it goes, each true idea becoming an instrument for acquiring further true ideas.

The distinction between intellect and imagination (013-005) is not merely a taxonomic refinement but the practical core of Spinoza's method: most philosophical errors arise precisely from mistaking what is easily imagined for what is clearly conceived.
