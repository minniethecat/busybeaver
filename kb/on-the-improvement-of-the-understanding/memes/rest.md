# Rest

Rest (*quies*) appears in the *Tractatus* as one of Spinoza's key examples for explaining the nature of falsity. Along with motion, it is a simple idea — an idea that "cannot be other than true" (010-006) — yet when combined incorrectly with a conception, it generates falsity.

## Definition

Rest is the absence or privation of motion. As a simple idea, it is true in itself: "simple ideas cannot be other than true — e.g., the simple idea of a semicircle, of motion, of rest, of quantity, &c." (010-006).

Falsity arises only in composition: "falsity consists only in this, that something is affirmed of a thing, which is not contained in the conception we have formed of that thing, as motion or rest of a semicircle" (010-006). The idea "rest" is not false; the judgment "a semicircle is at rest" is false because rest is not contained in the conception of a semicircle.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The rest/motion example serves a precise methodological function in Spinoza's analysis of falsity. It demonstrates that:

1. **Simple ideas are inherently true** — they correspond to genuine features of reality.
2. **Falsity is a problem of composition** — error occurs when the mind affirms a connection not present in the essences of things.
3. **Falsity is privative** — it is the absence of adequate knowledge, not a positive force. We err because we do not fully grasp the essence.

## Key Passages

> "Falsity consists only in this, that something is affirmed of a thing, which is not contained in the conception we have formed of that thing, as motion or rest of a semicircle. Whence it follows that simple ideas cannot be other than true — e.g., the simple idea of a semicircle, of motion, of rest, of quantity, &c." (010-006)

## Notes

The rest/motion pair is Spinoza's most frequently recurring example for epistemological points. Its power lies in simplicity: everyone immediately grasps that a semicircle is neither in motion nor at rest — these are categories that simply do not apply. By choosing an example where the error is obvious, Spinoza isolates the *structure* of falsity (miscomposition) from its *content*, making the analysis generalizable to more subtle cases of error.

In the *Ethics*, rest and motion take on greater metaphysical significance in the physical digression (E2p13s), where bodies are defined by their ratios of motion and rest. The *Tractatus* usage is purely epistemological.
