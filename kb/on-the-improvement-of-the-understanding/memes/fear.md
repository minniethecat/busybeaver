# Fear

In Spinoza's *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione*, fear appears in two interconnected roles: as one of the emotional disturbances that arise from love of perishable things, and as a methodological obstacle — the fear of forming hypotheses or of falling into error that paralyzes the intellect and prevents it from proceeding toward adequate knowledge.

## Definition

Spinoza treats fear under two aspects throughout the treatise:

- **Fear as emotional disturbance**: "When a thing is not loved, no quarrels will arise concerning it — no sadness will be felt if it perishes — no envy if it is possessed by another — **no fear**, no hatred, in short no disturbances of the mind" (002-007). Fear is presented as one of the emotional perturbations that accompany attachment to perishable objects — riches, fame, sensual pleasure — and that vanish when the mind turns toward eternal and infinite things.
- **Fear of error as methodological paralysis**: Spinoza repeatedly assures the reader that "we need, therefore, be in no fear of forming hypotheses" (009-013) and that "we need have no fear of confusing with true ideas" (010-002) because the method itself provides the criterion for distinguishing true from false ideas.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Fear is significant for Spinoza's method in two ways:

1. **Eliminating the fear of error**: A major obstacle to inquiry is the fear of making mistakes. Spinoza addresses this by providing a clear criterion — the true idea as "the standard of truth" (norma veritatis) — which allows the inquirer to "form as many simple ideas as we please, without any fear of error" (010-009). Because simple ideas are necessarily true, there is no risk in forming them freely.

2. **Fear as symptom of attachment to perishable goods**: The emotional fear that accompanies love of riches, fame, and pleasure (002-007) is a sign that the mind is oriented toward the wrong objects. The method's goal — directing the mind toward love of the eternal and infinite — eliminates this kind of fear by eliminating its cause: attachment to what can be lost.

## Key Passages

> "None of the objects of my fears contained in themselves anything either good or bad, except in so far as the mind is affected by them" (001-001)

> "No fear, no hatred, in short no disturbances of the mind" (002-007)

> "We need, therefore, be in no fear of forming hypotheses, so long as we admit nothing as a conclusion which is not clearly and distinctly perceived" (009-013)

> "Wherefore we form as many simple ideas as we please, without any fear of error" (010-009)

> "In respect to the idea of the primary elements... no confusion need be feared" (010-013)

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of fear in the *Tractatus* is more epistemological than psychological. While he acknowledges fear as an emotional disturbance (alongside hatred, envy, and sadness), his primary interest is in the fear of error — the intellectual timidity that prevents the inquirer from forming ideas and proceeding with investigation. His solution is distinctively rationalist: rather than counseling caution or skepticism, he insists that the inquirer can proceed boldly because the method itself guarantees that simple ideas are true and that deductions from them are reliable. This confidence in method is a hallmark of Spinoza's rationalism and contrasts sharply with Descartes' methodological doubt.
