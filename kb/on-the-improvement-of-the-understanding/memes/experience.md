# Experience

Experience (*experientia vaga*), or "mere experience," is the third of Spinoza's four modes of perception — knowledge derived from unclassified sensory occurrences that have happened to take place, without the intellect having ordered them into a causal understanding (005-001).

It is the practical, everyday mode of knowing through which "nearly all the practical knowledge of life" is acquired (005-002).

## Definition

Spinoza defines mere experience as "perception arising from experience not yet classified by the intellect, and only so called because the given event has happened to take place, and we have no contradictory fact to set against it, so that it therefore remains unassailed in our minds" (005-001).

Key characteristics:

- **Unclassified by intellect**: Experience in this mode is raw — it has not been ordered by reason or reduced to essential knowledge (005-001)
- **Practical in scope**: It yields knowledge such as "oil has the property of feeding fire, and water of extinguishing it," and "a dog is a barking animal, man a rational animal" (005-002)
- **Inadequate for essence**: While experience can produce practical rules, it cannot reveal the essence of things — "we shall never discover anything in natural phenomena by its means, except accidental properties, which are never clearly understood, unless the essence of the things in question be known first" (006-006)
- **Subject to doubt**: Experience-based knowledge is vulnerable to correction when we acquire knowledge of causes — for "if, after doubting, we acquire a true knowledge of the senses, and how things at a distance are represented through their instrumentality, doubt is again removed" (011-002)

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Experience occupies an ambivalent position in Spinoza's epistemological method. On one hand, it is the very starting point of the treatise — the opening sentence declares: "After experience had taught me that all the usual surroundings of social life are vain and futile" (001-001). It is experience that prompts the search for the true good.

On the other hand, experience is explicitly rejected as a method for attaining adequate knowledge:

1. **As a mode of perception**: Experience is ranked third among the four modes, above hearsay but below reason and intuitive knowledge (005-001 through 005-004). Its deliverances are "very uncertain and indefinite" (006-006).

2. **As a tool-making analogy**: Spinoza's famous hammer analogy illustrates the relationship between innate instruments and acquired ones. Just as "men at first made use of the instruments supplied by nature to accomplish very easy pieces of workmanship, laboriously and imperfectly" (006-007), so experience provides the raw material from which the intellect forges better instruments. But one cannot remain at the level of raw experience.

3. **As a source of error**: When the mind's "experience is multiplex" and its understanding small, "its power of coining fictions" grows larger (009-009). Experience unguided by intellect produces fictions and false ideas. Spinoza warns that we must "separate such perceptions from all those which arise from hearsay or unclassified experience" (010-002).

4. **As a stepping stone**: Despite its limitations, experience is not discarded. The artisan's progression from crude natural tools to complex mechanisms (006-007) models the intellect's progression — experience provides the initial foothold without which method could not begin.

## Key Passages

> "After experience had taught me that all the usual surroundings of social life are vain and futile... I finally resolved to inquire whether there might be some real good." (001-001)

> "Perception arising from mere experience — that is, from experience not yet classified by the intellect, and only so called because the given event has happened to take place, and we have no contradictory fact to set against it." (005-001)

> "By mere experience I know that I shall die, for this I can affirm from having seen that others like myself have died... In the same way I know that a dog is a barking animal, man a rational animal, and in fact nearly all the practical knowledge of life." (005-002)

> "As men at first made use of the instruments supplied by nature to accomplish very easy pieces of workmanship, laboriously and imperfectly, and then... gradually mounted from the simplest operations to the making of tools... So, in like manner, the intellect, by its native strength, makes for itself intellectual instruments." (006-007)

> "In proportion as the mind's understanding is smaller, and its experience multiplex, so will its power of coining fictions be larger." (009-009)

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of experience reflects his rationalist commitment: experience can motivate inquiry and provide practical guidance, but it cannot yield adequate ideas. The opening sentence of the treatise — often noted by commentators — makes experience the catalyst for philosophical reflection while simultaneously marking its insufficiency. The very fact that experience teaches the vanity of ordinary objects demonstrates experience's capacity for self-transcendence: it can reveal its own limits and point toward the need for a higher mode of knowledge.

The distinction between *experientia vaga* (mere, unclassified experience) and ordered experience (experiment under fixed rules, as described at 015-009) is crucial but only sketched in the *Tractatus*. The latter, discussed briefly in the context of aids for knowing particular things, anticipates the experimental method that would later be systematized by the natural sciences.
