# True Good

The fixed, eternal good that serves as the proper end of human striving, distinguished
from the perishable goods pursued by the multitude.

## Definition

Spinoza defines the true good (*verum bonum*) relative to the end it serves: "everything
which will serve as such means a true good" (003-002). Unlike ordinary goods (riches, fame,
 sensual pleasure) which are "uncertain by reason of their own nature" (002-004), the true
good is "a fixed good" (002-004) — certain in its nature and not subject to the fluctuations
of fortune.

The terms "good" and "evil" are relative: "the same thing may be called both good and bad
according to the relations in view" (003-001). The true good is defined by its relation
to the supreme human perfection.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The quest for the true good is the starting point of Spinoza's entire philosophical project.
Faced with the insufficiency of ordinary objects of desire (002-001), he resolves to seek
"a good not uncertain in its nature" (002-004). This search leads to the method for
improving the understanding.

The **chief good** (*summum bonum*) is "the knowledge of the union existing between the
mind and the whole of nature" (003-002). All true goods are means to this end. The
method itself is a true good because it enables the mind to attain this knowledge.

Spinoza's resolution is practical: "all our actions and thoughts must be directed to this
one end" (003-005). Sciences that do not serve this end are to be "rejected as useless"
(003-005).

## Key Passages

> For the ordinary surroundings of life which are esteemed by men to be the highest good,
> may be classed under the three heads — Riches, Fame, and the Pleasures of Sense: with
> these three the mind is so absorbed that it has little power to reflect on any different
> good. (002-001)

> I should be leaving a good, uncertain by reason of its own nature... for the sake of a
> good not uncertain in its nature (for I sought for a fixed good). (002-004)

> Love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself
> unmingled with any sadness. (002-008)

> Everything which will serve as such means a true good. The chief good is that he should
> arrive... at the knowledge of the union existing between the mind and the whole of
> nature. (003-002)

## Notes

The distinction between ordinary goods and the true good structures the opening argument
of the TIE (§§1–17). Spinoza's approach is therapeutic: he diagnoses the mind's sickness
(attachment to perishable goods) and prescribes the remedy (love of the eternal). The
true good thus functions both as a criterion for evaluating pursuits and as a motivational
principle for undertaking the philosophical life.
