# Will and Understanding

Spinoza holds that the will (*voluntas*) and the understanding (*intellectus*) are not separate faculties but one and the same. Every idea involves an act of affirmation; therefore willing and understanding coincide. This identity is foundational to his theory of error and certainty.

## Definition

According to Spinoza, the will is nothing other than the mind's power of affirming or denying ideas. Since every idea inherently involves affirmation (to perceive an idea is, in some sense, to affirm it), the will cannot be distinguished from the understanding. As he notes in the *Tractatus*, the mind's capacity for understanding determines the scope of its affirmations: "in proportion as the mind's understanding is smaller... so will its power of coining fictions be larger" (058).

## Key Passages

> "The natural order demands that I should here recapitulate all the modes of perception, which I have hitherto employed for affirming or denying anything with certainty, so that I may choose the best, and at the same time begin to know my own powers and the nature which I wish to perfect." (026)

> "We can see that it is before all things necessary for us to deduce all our ideas from physical things — that is, from real entities, proceeding, as far as may be, according to the series of causes, from one real entity to another real entity." (099)

## Notes

The identity of will and understanding is more explicitly argued in the *Ethics* (E2p49). In the *Tractatus*, it appears as an implicit principle underlying the discussion of fictitious and false ideas.
