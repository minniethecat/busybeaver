# Cause

A cause (*causa*) is, for Spinoza, that through which a thing is understood. Knowledge through causes — specifically through the proximate cause — distinguishes genuine intellectual cognition from the lower modes of perception, and the ability to define a thing through its proximate cause is the mark of the highest form of knowledge.

## Definition

Spinoza distinguishes several senses of "cause" across the treatise:

- **Proximate cause** (*causa proxima*): the immediate condition through which a thing is understood. To define a thing properly "it must be explained through its proximate cause" (009-002). A definition that does not enable us to deduce all properties of the thing from it is inadequate.
- **External causes**: those on which the existence of contingent things depends. If a thing's "existence depends on external causes, then we must follow the same method" of seeking causes to understand it (010-004).
- **Primary causes** (*causae primae*): the understanding of things "through their primary causes" is what distinguishes true ideas from false ones — false ideas arise when we lack access to primary causes and construct fictions instead (010-007).
- **Unknown causes**: when the nature of a thing "depends on causes unknown to us," we feign or imagine rather than understand (009-004). Fiction and falsity arise precisely where causal knowledge is absent.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The concept of cause is methodologically central in three ways:

1. **Definition through proximate cause**: The second part of method treats "good definitions" as its means (015-001), and good definitions are those that state the proximate cause. A definition must "be such that all the properties of the thing, when regarded simply in itself and not in conjunction with others, can be deduced from it" — this is the causal criterion of definitional adequacy.

2. **Cause as the mark of the fourth mode of perception**: The lowest modes of perception — hearsay and vague experience — do not involve causal knowledge. Only the fourth mode "apprehends the adequate essence of a thing without danger of error" (006-005), and this involves understanding through the thing's proximate cause. When we perceive an effect and infer its cause, we have only the third mode: "Perception arising when the essence of one thing is inferred from another thing... such as when we perceive that the mind is united to the body, and that their union is the cause of the given sensation" (005-003).

3. **Cause in the validation of ideas**: A true idea can be distinguished from false ideas in part by considering "the causes and manner" of the idea's production (010-002). False ideas involve "no causes present to us, wherefrom... we can understand how the thing has come to pass" (010-003). The presence or absence of causal understanding is thus a criterion for distinguishing true from fictitious and false ideas.

## Key Passages

> "A definition, if it is to be called perfect, must explain the innermost essence of a thing, and must take care not to substitute for this any of its properties. To illustrate my meaning... it must be explained through its proximate cause." (009-002)

> "When the mind has only partial knowledge of a thing... and is not aware of the means and the causes whereby the event took place, we speak of fiction." (009-013–014)

> "If the existence of a thing depends on external causes, then we must follow the same method." (010-004)

> "We... acknowledge the object as its cause, but must depend on the actual perception for our knowledge of the effect." (010-008)

## Notes

Spinoza's emphasis on proximate cause in definition reflects his rejection of scholastic definition-by-genus-and-differentia. For Spinoza, a definition must be genetic — it must show how the thing comes to be, not merely what category it falls under. This causal theory of definition connects the *Tractatus* to the *Ethics*, where the genetic definition of God as *causa sui* grounds the entire system. The doctrine of the proximate cause also underlies Spinoza's critique of abstraction and universals: concepts formed by abstracting common properties from particulars lack causal content and therefore cannot yield genuine knowledge.
