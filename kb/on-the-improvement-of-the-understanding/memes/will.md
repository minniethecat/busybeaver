# Will

Will (*voluntas*) plays a dual role in Spinoza's *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione*. It is simultaneously the source of error — asserting or denying beyond what the intellect clearly perceives — and the motivating force behind the entire project of improving the [[understanding]]. For Spinoza, the reorientation of the will toward the [[highest-good]] is the first and indispensable step of philosophical method.

## Definition

Spinoza does not provide a single formal definition of will in the *Tractatus*, but its character emerges across several contexts:

| Context | Role of Will | Key Passage |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Ethical motivation** | The drive that seeks the highest good, overriding attachment to ordinary objects of desire | 003-001 |
| **Source of error** | Will affirms or denies beyond clear perception, generating false ideas | 009-010 |
| **Methodological discipline** | The trained will defers judgment until the intellect attains clear and distinct ideas | 006-002 |

In each context, will is the active, striving dimension of the mind — the power that drives inquiry forward but must be disciplined by method.

## Will and the Search for the Highest Good

The *Tractatus* opens with Spinoza's personal account of reorienting his will. He describes the "ordinary objects of men's desires" — wealth, honor, sensual pleasure — as uncertain by nature and as obstacles to the search for the true good:

> "The love of a thing which is eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unmingled with any sadness, wherefore it is greatly to be desired and sought for with all our strength." (003-001)

The will's reorientation is the turning point of the treatise. Spinoza does not advocate suppressing desire but redirecting it: "they [riches, honor, pleasure] be sought as means, they will be under restraint" (002-009). The [[will]] itself is not the problem — its object is.

## Will as Source of Error

Spinoza locates one source of false ideas in the will's tendency to affirm or deny before the intellect has attained clarity:

> "The mind, when it pays attention to a fictitious thing which is in its nature false, so as to ponder over it and understand it, and from it to deduce in due order what ought to be deduced, will easily see its falsity." (009-010)

The will that rushes to judgment produces [[false-idea]]; the will that is trained by [[method]] waits for the intellect to achieve [[certainty]]. This discipline is not a denial of will but its perfection: "the will is free, but only in so far as it is determined by adequate ideas" — a thesis that anticipates the *Ethics*.

## Will and Certainty

Spinoza draws a crucial distinction between psychological certainty (a feeling of conviction produced by the will) and epistemological certainty (grounded in the nature of the idea itself):

> "No one can know the nature of the highest certainty, unless he have an adequate idea or the objective essence of some thing." (007-003)

The will cannot substitute for knowledge. True [[certainty]] is not a matter of subjective confidence but of objective adequacy — the idea carries its own warrant, and the will merely assents to what the intellect already sees.

## Will in the Structure of Method

The method Spinoza proposes is simultaneously intellectual and volitional. The intellect provides the order of ideas; the will provides the sustained attention and desire that drive inquiry forward. Together they constitute the "instruments of the intellect" (007-005) — not external tools but innate powers of the mind, refined through reflective practice. The [[understanding]] and the will are distinct faculties but inseparable in the actual work of philosophical [[method]].
