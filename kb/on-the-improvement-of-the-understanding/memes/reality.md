# Reality

Reality (*realitas*) in Spinoza's *Tractatus* denotes positive being — the actual existence and essence of a thing. Crucially, reality is not confined to the world of objects: ideas themselves possess reality, and the reality of a true idea is the foundation of certainty (007-003).

## Definition

Spinoza distinguishes two orders of reality:

- **Formal reality** (the world of things): The man Peter is something real — he exists as a concrete individual in the world of objects (007-002).
- **Subjective/objective reality** (the world of thought): "The true idea of Peter is the reality of Peter represented subjectively, and is in itself something real, and quite distinct from the actual Peter" (007-002). An idea is not a mere image — it has its own positive being.

The relationship between the two orders is isomorphism: "the idea in the world of thought is in the same case as its correlate in the world of reality" (007-009). This structural identity is the ground of Spinoza's epistemology: if an idea corresponds to reality, it can be the premise for valid inferences.

Conversely, "the more existence is conceived generally, the more is it conceived confusedly" (009-006). Reality understood abstractly — as "being in general" — is a source of confusion. Clarity demands grasping the particular reality of each thing.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Reality serves three essential functions in the method:

1. **Standard of truth**: "That which constitutes the reality of truth" is the intrinsic nature of a true idea, not its correspondence to an external object (010-004). An architect's true idea of a building is true regardless of whether the building ever exists (010-004).

2. **Foundation of certainty**: "Certainty is nothing else than the subjective essence of a thing" (007-003). To possess a true idea is to possess the reality of the thing in thought — and this possession IS certainty.

3. **Source of inferential power**: "There is in ideas something real, whereby the true are distinguished from the false" (010-005). This reality in ideas is what enables the mind to deduce further truths — it is the engine of methodical progress.

## Key Passages

> "The man Peter is something real; the true idea of Peter is the reality of Peter represented subjectively, and is in itself something real, and quite distinct from the actual Peter." (007-002)

> "Hence it is clear that certainty is nothing else than the subjective essence of a thing: in other words, the mode in which we perceive an actual reality is certainty." (007-003)

> "The idea in the world of thought is in the same case as its correlate in the world of reality." (007-009)

> "As regards that which constitutes the reality of truth, it is certain that a true idea is distinguished from a false one, not so much by its extrinsic object as by its intrinsic nature." (010-004)

## Notes

Spinoza's concept of reality as intrinsic to ideas is a radical departure from the correspondence theory of truth. A true idea does not "match" reality — it IS reality, but in the order of thought. This is the germ of the *Ethics*'s parallelism: "The order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things" (E2p7). The *Tractatus* already affirms that ideas and things are two isomorphic orders of one and the same reality.
