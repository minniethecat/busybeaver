# Mode (of Perception)

A distinct way or manner in which the mind perceives or knows its objects, classified
by Spinoza into four modes of increasing adequacy.

## Definition

In the TIE, a mode of perception (*modus percipiendi*) is a distinct way in which the
mind apprehends knowledge. Spinoza reduces all modes to four kinds (005-001):
1. Hearsay or arbitrary convention
2. Vague experience (*experientia vaga*)
3. Inference from another thing (reasoning from effect to cause)
4. Direct intuition of the essence of a thing (*scientia intuitiva*)

The term is also used more broadly for "the mode in which we perceive an actual thing"
(007-003), emphasizing the *manner* of cognition rather than its content.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Classifying the modes of perception is methodologically prior to all other steps: "in
order that from these modes of perception the best may be selected, it is necessary
that we briefly enumerate" them (006-001). The method's first task is to determine
"the mode of perception best adapted to aid us in attaining to our perfection" (008-007).

The fourth mode alone "apprehends the adequate essence of a thing without danger of
error" (006-005). The lower modes are progressively eliminated:
- Mode 1 (hearsay): wholly uncertain
- Mode 2 (vague experience): cannot give adequate ideas (006-003)
- Mode 3 (reasoning): can be certain but depends on prior knowledge (006-004)
- Mode 4 (intuition): the paradigm of adequate knowledge

## Key Passages

## Notes

"Mode" in the TIE differs from the metaphysical sense in Spinoza's *Ethics* (where modes
are affections of substance). Here, *modus* retains its methodological meaning: a way or
manner of cognizing. The TIE's modal classification anticipates the three kinds of
knowledge in *Ethics* 2p40s2 (imagination, reason, intuitive science).
