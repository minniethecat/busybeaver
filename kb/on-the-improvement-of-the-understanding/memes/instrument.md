# Instrument

Intellectual instruments (*instrumenta intellectus*) are the true ideas that the mind possesses as "native" or "natural" tools, by means of which it acquires further true ideas, and from those still further instruments, in a self-augmenting progression toward the "summit of wisdom" (006-007). The instrument concept is the linchpin of Spinoza's method — it explains how knowledge can begin without infinite regress and how it can grow without external input.

## Definition

Spinoza introduces intellectual instruments through an analogy with material tool-making:

"But as men at first made use of the instruments supplied by nature to accomplish very easy pieces of workmanship, laboriously and imperfectly, and then, when these were finished, wrought other things more difficult with less labour and greater perfection; and so gradually mounted from the simplest operations to the making of tools, and from the making of tools to the making of more complex tools... So, in like manner, the intellect, by its native strength, makes for itself intellectual instruments, whereby it acquires strength for performing other intellectual operations, and from these operations again fresh instruments, or the power of pushing its investigations further, and thus gradually proceeds till it reaches the summit of wisdom." (006-007)

Key characteristics:

- **Innate starting point**: The intellect possesses "native strength" (*vis nativa*) and "natural instruments" — true ideas present in the mind from the beginning (006-007, 007-007)
- **Self-augmenting**: "In proportion as it acquires new ideas, the mind simultaneously acquires fresh instruments for pursuing its inquiries further" (007-007)
- **Interconnected**: Ideas serve as instruments because they are connected — "those things which are connected with others — as all things that exist in nature — will be understood by the mind, and their subjective essences will maintain the same mutual relations as their objective realities — that is to say, we shall infer from these ideas other ideas... and thus our instruments for proceeding with our investigation will increase" (007-009)
- **The true idea as the first instrument**: "A true idea must necessarily first of all exist in us as a natural instrument; and... when this idea is apprehended by the mind, it enables us to understand the difference existing between itself and all other perceptions" (007-007)

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The instrument concept solves the problem of infinite regress that threatens any method for finding truth:

1. **The regress problem**: To discover the best method of finding truth, do we need another method to discover that method, and a third for the second, "and so on to infinity"? (006-006). Spinoza answers that method does not need to be discovered by a prior method — the mind already possesses true ideas as natural instruments.

2. **The tool-making analogy**: Just as the first humans made crude tools with their bare hands, then used those tools to make better tools (006-007), so the intellect uses its innate true ideas to form more true ideas. The analogy resolves the apparent paradox: how can we seek truth without already possessing it? We *do* already possess it, in the form of the true idea that serves as the mind's native instrument.

3. **The method as reflective knowledge**: Method is "nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea" (007-006). Given a true idea, method teaches the mind how to use it as a standard for distinguishing true from false perceptions and for directing further inquiry (007-006).

4. **Progressive perfection**: The method becomes more perfect "in proportion as the mind attains to the comprehension of a greater number of objects," and "will be absolutely perfect when the mind gains a knowledge of the absolutely perfect being" (007-007). The instruments improve with use.

5. **The second part of method**: Having established that the mind possesses instruments, the second part addresses the "means" for their proper use — good definitions (015-001), which are "the cardinal point of all this second part of method" (015-001). The definition provides the formal starting point from which the intellect's instruments can operate.

## Notes

The instrument concept is one of Spinoza's most original contributions to method. It anticipates several themes of later epistemology: the bootstrapping problem (how does inquiry begin without prior knowledge?), the concept of innate ideas as functional rather than contentful (the true idea is an *instrument*, not a proposition), and the self-correcting character of scientific inquiry (instruments improve with use).

The tool analogy at 006-007 is carefully crafted. Spinoza distinguishes between the *making* of tools and their *use*: the hammer is both a product of prior tools and an instrument for making further tools. Similarly, a true idea is both the *result* of prior intellectual activity and an *instrument* for further inquiry. This dual character — product and instrument — is what makes the progression self-sustaining.

The connection to definition (015-001) is critical but often overlooked: instruments alone are insufficient; they require a starting point, and "the true method of discovery is to form thoughts from some given definition." The definition of the understanding itself becomes the ultimate instrument (016-003).
