# Love

Love appears in Spinoza's *Tractatus* in two contrasting forms: the ordinary love directed at transient objects of desire, and the "love towards a thing eternal and infinite" (002-008) that constitutes part of the true and final good. The distinction between these two forms of love is central to Spinoza's ethical argument.

## Definition

Spinoza does not provide a single formal definition of love, but characterizes it through its objects. Ordinary love is defined by "the quality of the object which we love" (002-007) — its nature is determined by what it is directed toward. Intellectual love (*amor intellectualis*) is suggested by the phrase "love towards a thing eternal and infinite" (002-008), and love is listed among affects "referred to thought, such as love, joy, &c." (016-003).

Key characteristics:

- **Object-dependent**: The nature of love is determined by its object — "the quality of the object which we love" (002-007) shapes the character of the love itself
- **Ordinary love is transient**: When directed at finite, perishable objects (wealth, fame, pleasure), love is inherently unstable — "When a thing is not loved, no quarrels will arise concerning it" (002-007), implying that attachment to ordinary objects is a source of conflict and disturbance
- **Love of the eternal is transformative**: "Love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is unmingled with any sadness" (002-008) — this love alone provides stable fulfillment
- **Affect referred to thought**: Love is classified among affects "referred to thought" (016-003), distinguishing it from merely bodily passions

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Love is central to the ethical motivation that drives Spinoza's entire philosophical project:

1. **The opening crisis**: The treatise begins with the recognition that ordinary objects of love — wealth, fame, and pleasure — are "vain and futile" (001-001). It is the failure of ordinary love that motivates the search for the true good.

2. **The true good as object of love**: The true and final good is characterized in terms of love — it is "the knowledge of the union existing between the mind and the whole of nature" (002-008), and the love directed toward this eternal object is the emotional correlate of adequate knowledge.

3. **Love as cognitive affect**: Spinoza's treatment of love as an affect "referred to thought" (016-003) integrates emotion into the cognitive project. Love is not opposed to reason but can be either confused (when directed at transient objects) or adequate (when directed at eternal things).

4. **Therapeutic function**: The transition from ordinary love to intellectual love is the emotional dimension of the method's therapeutic work — as the understanding improves, the affects are reoriented toward their proper objects.

## Key Passages

> "The quality of the object which we love." (002-007)

> "When a thing is not loved, no quarrels will arise concerning it — no sadness will be felt if it perishes — no envy if it is possessed by another — no fear, no hatred." (002-007)

> "But love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is unmingled with any sadness." (002-008)

> "Affects referred to thought, such as love, joy, &c." (016-003)

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of love in the *Tractatus* is notable for its integration of affect into epistemology. Love is not a distraction from the philosophical project but an essential component of it — properly directed, it is the emotional correlate of wisdom. The distinction between ordinary love (directed at transient objects) and intellectual love (directed at the eternal and infinite) anticipates the fuller theory of the affects in the *Ethics*, where *amor Dei intellectualis* (the intellectual love of God) becomes the highest form of human blessedness. Even in this early work, the insight is clear: the problem is not love itself but its misdirection toward objects incapable of providing lasting fulfillment.
