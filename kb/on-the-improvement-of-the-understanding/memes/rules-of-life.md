# Rules of Life

The rules of life (*certae regulae vitae*) are three provisional ethical maxims that Spinoza lays down at the beginning of his methodological project. Recognizing that the pursuit of the highest good will take time, he establishes these rules "as provisionally good" so that ordinary life can continue without obstructing the philosophical quest (004-001).

## Definition

The three rules (004-001) are:

**I. Speech** — "To speak in a manner intelligible to the multitude, and to comply with every general custom that does not hinder the attainment of our purpose." Accommodation to common understanding gains "a friendly audience for the reception of the truth."

**II. Pleasure** — "To indulge ourselves with pleasures only in so far as they are necessary for preserving health."

**III. Wealth** — "To endeavor to obtain only sufficient money or other commodities to enable us to preserve our life and health, and to follow such general customs as are consistent with our purpose."

## Provisional Character

The rules are explicitly *provisional* (not final): they are adopted "while we are endeavoring to attain our purpose, and bring the understanding into the right path" (004-001). Once the understanding is perfected, the rules may be revised or discarded. This provisionality is key: Spinoza does not advocate ascetic renunciation but a controlled engagement with ordinary goods.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The rules solve a practical problem: how to pursue the highest good — which requires sustained philosophical reflection — while still participating in social life. Without them, the mind would be "absorbed" by riches, fame, and pleasure (001-002), making inquiry impossible.

The rules also embody Spinoza's instrumental view of ordinary goods: "if they be sought as means, they will be under restraint, and, far from being hindrances, will further not a little the end for which they are sought" (003-002). This transforms the objects of ordinary desire from obstacles into tools.

## Key Passages

> "Yet, as it is necessary that while we are endeavoring to attain our purpose, and bring the understanding into the right path we should carry on our life, we are compelled first of all to lay down certain rules of life as provisionally good." (004-001)

## Notes

The three rules mirror the three ordinary objects of desire (riches, fame, pleasure) analyzed in the opening paragraphs — but reframed from obstacles into regulated instruments. This symmetry is deliberate: Spinoza does not reject ordinary life but reorders it under the governance of reason. The phrase "provisionally good" (*provisorium bonum*) is philosophically significant — it introduces a pragmatic, non-absolute concept of goodness keyed to the stage of one's intellectual development.
