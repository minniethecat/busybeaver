# Foundation

The foundation (*principium*) of Spinoza's method is a true idea that serves as the incorrigible starting-point from which all subsequent knowledge is deduced. Without such a foundation, the chain of reasoning collapses into infinite regress or skepticism (002-001).

## Definition

Spinoza identifies the foundation of knowledge with a **true idea** — an idea that corresponds to its object (*ideatum*) in reality and is known through itself, not through another idea:

- A foundation must be **self-evident**: "thought is said to be true, if it involves subjectively the essence of any principle which has no cause, and is known through itself and in itself" (010-007).
- A foundation cannot be abstract or universal: "the first principle of nature cannot be conceived abstractedly or universally" (010-013).
- The "best basis for drawing a conclusion is a particular affirmative essence" (015-005) — particular, not general.

The foundation is not an arbitrary axiom but the **essence of a real particular thing**, grasped intuitively through the fourth mode of perception.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The foundation plays the architectonic role in Spinoza's method:

1. **Starting-point of deduction**: All certain knowledge proceeds from a true idea as foundation, through a chain of reasoning where each link is validated by the previous one.
2. **Antidote to infinite regress**: Without a foundation, the demand for proof would regress infinitely. The true idea stops the regress because it is "known through itself."
3. **Standard of truth**: The foundation-idea serves as the criterion by which all other ideas are judged: "as the true good became more and more discernible to me" (002-009), the mind moves from uncertainty toward the fixed foundation.

Spinoza's search is framed explicitly as the search for a foundation: "I therefore debated whether it would not be possible to arrive at the new principle" (002-001).

## Key Passages

> "I therefore debated whether it would not be possible to arrive at the new principle, or at any rate at a certainty concerning its existence, without changing the conduct and usual plan of my life." (002-001)

> "Thought is said to be true, if it involves subjectively the essence of any principle which has no cause, and is known through itself and in itself." (010-007)

> "The best basis for drawing a conclusion is a particular affirmative essence. The more special the idea, the more distinct, and therefore the more clear." (015-005)

> "While my mind was employed with these thoughts it turned away from its former objects of desire, and seriously considered the search for a new principle." (002-009)

## Notes

The concept of foundation in the *Tractatus* anticipates Descartes' metaphor of the tree of knowledge (roots = metaphysics, trunk = physics, branches = sciences) but is more radical: Spinoza requires the foundation to be a *particular essence*, not a general principle. This reflects his nominalist tendency — universals are mere abstractions; only particulars are real. The foundation of knowledge must therefore be the essence of a real, particular thing, grasped intuitively.
