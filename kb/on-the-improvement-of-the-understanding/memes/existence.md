# Existence

Existence (*existentia*) is treated by Spinoza as the correlate of essence: together they constitute the full reality of a thing. The treatise develops a nuanced account of how existence relates to essence, how existence is known, and what distinguishes the existence of contingent things from the necessary existence of eternal things.

## Definition

Spinoza distinguishes several dimensions of existence:

- **Existence through essence alone**: Some things — eternal and infinite things — have an essence that necessarily involves existence. "If a thing is in itself... it must be understood through itself alone" (010-002), and its existence is self-grounding.

- **Existence through external causes**: Created or contingent things have an essence that "does not involve existence" (015-007). For such things, "existence depends on external causes" (010-004), and they must be understood through the series of causes that bring them into being.

- **Existence as the object of fiction**: Fiction can concern "only the existence of an object... while the thing so conceived is understood, and its essence is perceived" (009-003). One may know the essence of a thing while being uncertain or mistaken about whether it exists.

- **Existence and impossibility**: A thing is impossible "when its existence would imply a contradiction" (009-004). The boundary between the possible and the impossible is the boundary of what can be coherently conceived as existing.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The distinction between essence and existence is methodologically critical for definition and the theory of ideas:

1. **Existence in the theory of fiction**: Spinoza analyzes fiction by distinguishing three cases: fictions concerning essence only, fictions concerning existence only, and fictions concerning both simultaneously (009-009). His analysis shows that fiction about existence is bounded by the mind's partial knowledge — when the mind lacks "the means and the causes whereby the event took place," it fills the gap with feigned existence.

2. **Existence and truth**: A true idea must correspond to existence. False ideas arise "when we consider at the same time the causes and manner of such action" and find no adequate ground (010-002). If a thing's existence "depends on external causes, then we must follow the same method" — seeking those causes to validate the idea (010-004).

3. **Existence in definition**: The distinction between things whose essence involves existence and things whose essence does not is central to the theory of definition. For created things, definition must state the proximate cause through which the thing attains existence (014-002, 015-007). A perfect definition "must be such that all the properties of the thing... can be deduced from it" — which is possible because the cause of existence explains all that follows from it (015-008).

4. **Series of existence**: Spinoza warns against seeking definitions "from their series or order of existence" (015-008, 015-009). The temporal order in which things come to exist is not the same as the logical order through which they are understood. The understanding of existence must follow the order of causes, not the order of temporal succession.

## Key Passages

> "Fictions are concerned either with... the existence of an object, and the action whereby it is produced." (009-003)

> "A thing is said to be impossible when its existence would imply a contradiction." (009-004)

> "The difference between the essence of one thing and the essence of another is the same as the difference between the reality or existence of one thing and the reality or existence of another." (009-006)

> "If the existence of a thing depends on external causes, then we must follow the same method." (010-004)

> "A definition... may be the cause of its existence or non-existence." (015-007)

> "Definitions must not be sought from their series or order of existence, nor from eternal things." (015-009)

## Notes

The *Tractatus* treatment of existence lays groundwork for the *Ethics* doctrine of substance. In the *Ethics*, Spinoza famously argues that God's essence necessarily involves existence (E1P11) — God is *causa sui*. The *Tractatus* already draws the crucial distinction between what exists through its own essence and what requires external causes, though without the full geometrical apparatus of the later work. The methodological prohibition against seeking definitions "from the series of existence" (015-009) is a rejection of empirical or historical methods of definition: to know what a thing is, one must grasp its proximate cause, not trace its temporal genealogy. This separates Spinoza's rationalism sharply from any form of natural history or genetic epistemology.
