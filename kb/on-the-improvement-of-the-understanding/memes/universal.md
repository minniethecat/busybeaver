# Universal

Universal notions — general ideas abstracted from particulars — are treated with deep suspicion in Spinoza's method. While ordinary cognition habitually "constructs a universal axiom from experience" (005-005), the method insists on the reverse movement: from adequate knowledge of particular things toward genuine universals, never "passing to universals and abstractions" prematurely (015-006).

## Definition

Spinoza characterizes universal notions as ideas "conceived in the abstract, as are all universal notions" (010-013). Unlike adequate ideas of determinate particulars, universal notions are the product of abstraction — they strip away the determinate features that make genuine knowledge possible.

Key characteristics:

- **Products of abstraction**: Universals arise when the mind abstracts common features from particulars, losing the determinate essence of each thing in the process (010-013)
- **Insufficient for deduction**: "The understanding cannot descend from universal axioms by themselves to particulars" (014-003) — universals alone cannot generate knowledge of concrete things
- **Often confused with adequate knowledge**: Ordinary cognition treats universal axioms as foundational, but they are in fact derived — and often poorly derived — from limited experience (005-005)
- **Legitimate only when grounded in particulars**: Spinoza does not reject universals entirely, but insists they must arise from adequate knowledge of determinate things rather than being imposed as a priori categories (015-006)

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The treatment of universals is central to Spinoza's critique of received philosophical method:

1. **Reversal of scholastic method**: Traditional philosophy begins with universal definitions and deduces particulars from them. Spinoza's method reverses this — begin with "primary elements — that is, from the source and origin of nature" (010-012) and only then ascend to universals.

2. **Universals as obstacles**: When the mind treats "universal notions" as adequate representations of things, "the said universal notions are more widely extended in the mind, and more frequently meet the mind, than the particular things themselves" (010-013). This creates an illusion of knowledge that blocks genuine understanding.

3. **The rule against premature universalization**: The rules for good definitions explicitly forbid "passing to universals and abstractions, either for the purpose of drawing any conclusion from them or of explaining any particular thing through them" (015-006).

## Notes

Spinoza's critique of universals places him in tension with much of the rationalist tradition. Where Descartes and Leibniz treat universal ideas (clear and distinct perceptions, innate ideas) as foundations for deduction, Spinoza insists that the foundation must be the determinate particular — ultimately, the single infinite individual that is Nature or God. This nominalist tendency in Spinoza's epistemology has important consequences for his metaphysics, particularly the doctrine that God is not an abstract universal but an absolutely infinite individual whose essence involves existence.
