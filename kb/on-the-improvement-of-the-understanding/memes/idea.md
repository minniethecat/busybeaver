# Idea

An idea (*idea*) is the mind's representation of a thing — what Spinoza calls the **subjective essence** (*essentia objectiva*). The idea of a circle is not itself circular, and the idea of a body is not itself a body; rather, the idea is a distinct real entity that represents the object in the mind (007-001).

## Definition

Spinoza's definition of an idea rests on three core claims:

- **Idea as distinct from its object**: "A true idea is something different from its correlate (*ideatum*); thus a circle is different from the idea of a circle" (007-001). The idea is not a copy but a separate reality.
- **Idea as self-intelligible**: Because an idea is a real thing distinct from its object, "it is capable of being understood through itself" (007-001). One idea can become the object of another idea — what Spinoza calls *reflective knowledge*.
- **The infinite regress of ideas**: This process continues indefinitely — "this second subjective essence will, regarded in itself, be something real, capable of being understood; and so on, indefinitely" (007-001).

The paradigm example is Peter: the real Peter, the true idea of Peter, the idea of the idea of Peter, and so on — each is a distinct real entity (007-002).

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The idea plays a foundational role in Spinoza's method in three ways:

1. **Natural instrument**: "A true idea must necessarily first of all exist in us as a natural instrument" (007-007). Just as the first humans used natural tools to make better tools, the mind uses its innate true idea to form further true ideas.
2. **Standard of truth**: The true idea serves as the standard by which all other perceptions are judged. Method is "the discernment of a true idea, by distinguishing it from other perceptions" (007-005).
3. **Reflective knowledge**: Method itself is "nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea" (007-006). Understanding progresses not by seeking external signs of truth but by reflecting on the true ideas we already possess.

## Key Passages

> "A true idea, (for we possess a true idea) is something different from its correlate (ideatum); thus a circle is different from the idea of a circle. The idea of a circle is not something having a circumference and a center, as a circle has; nor is the idea of a body that body itself." (007-001)

> "The man Peter is something real; the true idea of Peter is the reality of Peter represented subjectively, and is in itself something real, and quite distinct from the actual Peter." (007-002)

> "Method is nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea; and as there can be no idea of an idea — unless an idea exists previously — there can be no method without a pre-existent idea." (007-006)

> "The idea in the world of thought is in the same case as its correlate in the world of reality." (007-009)

## Notes

The concept of *idea* in the *Tractatus* anticipates Spinoza's mature doctrine in the *Ethics* (E2d3: "By idea I understand a conception of the mind which the mind forms because it is a thinking thing"). In both works, the idea is not a passive image but an active *conception*. The *Tractatus* emphasizes the generative power of ideas: starting from one true idea, the mind can generate an indefinite chain of further ideas, each more comprehensive than the last — the intellectual analogue of tool-making (007-008).
