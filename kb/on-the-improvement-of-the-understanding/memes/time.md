# Time

Time (*tempus*) in Spinoza's *Tractatus* is an auxiliary of the imagination — a way the mind represents things when it does not grasp them through their essences. The intellect, by contrast, perceives things "under a certain form of eternity, and in an infinite number" (016-001), without regard to temporal succession.

## Definition

Spinoza distinguishes sharply between time as imagined and eternity as understood:

- **Time belongs to imagination**: Duration is the mind's way of tracking mutable things whose existence is not an eternal truth. "Particular mutable things... have no connection with their essence, or... is not an eternal truth" (015-007). Time is the measure of what comes into being and passes away.
- **Eternity belongs to understanding**: The intellect "perceives things not so much under the condition of duration as under a certain form of eternity" (016-001). Eternal things — the fixed laws of nature, essences, God — are not in time.
- **Time as a means**: "If they be sought as means, they will be under restraint" (003-004) — time is one of the dimensions in which we order our practical life, not a category of ultimate knowledge.

The pursuit of the eternal and infinite good is precisely the pursuit of what is "unmingled with any sadness" — unlike temporal goods, which produce "extreme melancholy" when they pass (002-008, 002-009).

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Time figures in the method in three ways:

1. **Practical ordering**: The provisional rules of life (004-002) acknowledge that we must live in time while seeking what is beyond time. "While we are endeavoring to attain our purpose... we should carry on our life" (004-001).

2. **Sequence of inquiry**: Method proceeds in an order — but this is the logical order of ideas, not chronological time. The mind should "proceed from one real entity to another... according to the series of causes" (015-007), not according to temporal succession.

3. **Overcoming time-bound thinking**: The method's goal is to free the mind from absorption in temporal goods (riches, fame, pleasure) and redirect it toward eternal things. The autobiographical opening of the treatise (002-009) narrates this very transition — from time-bound desire to the love of "a thing eternal and infinite."

## Key Passages

> "But love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unmingled with any sadness." (002-008)

> "One thing was evident, namely, that while my mind was employed with these thoughts it turned away from its former objects of desire." (002-009)

> "However, human weakness cannot attain to this order in its own thoughts, but meanwhile man conceives a human character much more stable than his own." (003-002)

> "We need, therefore, be in no fear of forming hypotheses, so long as we have a clear and distinct perception of what is involved." (009-013)

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of time in the *Tractatus* anticipates the *Ethics*'s distinction between *duration* (existence measured by the imagination) and *eternity* (existence conceived by the intellect). The method is itself a process of temporal progression toward an atemporal goal — the knowledge of God or Nature "under a form of eternity." The tension between living in time and knowing eternally is never fully resolved; it is the existential condition the treatise both diagnoses and seeks to ameliorate.
