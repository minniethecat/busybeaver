# Union of Mind and Body

Spinoza identifies the knowledge of the union existing between the mind and the whole of nature as the "chief good" (*summum bonum*) — the ultimate end toward which the emendation of the understanding is directed.

## Definition

The union of mind and body (or mind and nature) represents the highest state of human knowledge: the mind's reflective grasp of its own place within the causal order of nature. Spinoza writes that "the chief good is... the knowledge of the union existing between the mind and the whole of nature" (013). This is not a mystical union but an epistemological achievement — the mind coming to understand itself as part of nature's causal nexus.

## Key Passages

> "The chief good is that he should arrive, together with other individuals if possible, at the possession of the aforesaid character. What that character is we shall show in due time, namely, that it is the knowledge of the union existing being the mind and the whole of nature." (013)

> "Thus we can see that it is before all things necessary for us to deduce all our ideas from physical things — that is, from real entities, proceeding, as far as may be, according to the series of causes, from one real entity to another real entity." (099)

## Notes

The concept of mind-body union in the *Tractatus* is programmatic — Spinoza promises to "show in due time" what this character consists in, a task taken up more fully in the *Ethics*.
