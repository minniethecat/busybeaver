# Forgetfulness

Forgetfulness (*oblivio*) is, in Spinoza's analysis, not a defect of the understanding but evidence of the difference between understanding and memory. The phenomenon is treated briefly but precisely: "the more intelligible a thing is, the more easily is it remembered, and the less intelligible it is, the more easily do we forget it" (012-001).

## Definition

Spinoza's account of forgetfulness is a corollary of his theory of memory:

- Things are forgotten in proportion to their *unintelligibility* — "a number of unconnected words is much more difficult to remember than the same number in the form of a narration" (012-001).
- Memory can be strengthened without the understanding (through repetition of similar physical impressions), but this kind of memory is prone to confusion and forgetting (012-002).
- Crucially: "in the understanding considered in itself there is neither memory nor forgetfulness" (012-002). The intellect grasps essences; it does not store or lose them.

## Distinction from Memory

Forgetfulness is the privation that reveals the nature of memory. Memory is "the actual sensation of impressions on the brain, accompanied with the thought of a definite duration" (012-003). When the duration is indefinite, recollection is imperfect. Forgetfulness occurs when the brain-impression fades or when conflicting impressions interfere — as when reading many similar comedies causes confusion (012-002).

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The analysis of forgetfulness serves a specific methodological purpose: it helps the reader distinguish the *understanding* (which operates through clear and distinct ideas, immune to forgetfulness) from the *imagination* (which operates through bodily impressions, subject to forgetting and error).

The recognition that words belong to imagination — "we form many conceptions in accordance with confused arrangements of words in the memory" (013-001) — connects forgetfulness to the broader theme of mental hindrances from language. Both memory and words are bodily, not intellectual.

## Key Passages

> "The more intelligible a thing is, the more easily is it remembered, and the less intelligible it is, the more easily do we forget it." (012-001)

> "In the understanding considered in itself there is neither memory nor forgetfulness." (012-002)

> "Memory is nothing else than the actual sensation of impressions on the brain, accompanied with the thought of a definite duration of the sensation." (012-003)

## Notes

Spinoza's brief treatment of forgetfulness in paragraphs [81]–[83] is embedded in the transition from the first part of method (false ideas, doubt) to the second part (clear and distinct ideas). The placement is strategic: having cleared away obstacles to knowledge, Spinoza adds a "few words on memory and forgetfulness" to complete his account of the understanding's faculties and their distinction from imagination.
