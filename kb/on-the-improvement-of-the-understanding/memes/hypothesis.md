# Hypothesis

Hypothesis (*hypothesis*, *suppositio*) is a supposition formed for the purpose of inquiry. Unlike fiction, which is arbitrary and confused, a properly formed hypothesis is made "with a clear and distinct perception of what is involved" (009-013). Unlike error, it does not involve assent — it is held provisionally, pending deductive verification.

## Definition

Spinoza carefully distinguishes hypothesis from neighboring concepts:

- **Hypothesis vs. fiction**: Fictions arise from confusion; hypotheses arise from understanding. "We need, therefore, be in no fear of forming hypotheses, so long as we have a clear and distinct perception of what is involved" (009-013). A fiction is formed when the mind does not know the causes; a hypothesis is formed when it does.
- **Hypothesis vs. error**: Error involves mental assent to what is false; hypothesis suspends assent. The mind "pays attention to a thing hypothetical or false, so as to meditate upon it and understand it, and derive the proper conclusions in due order" (009-012) — and through this process, falsity is exposed.
- **Hypotheses in problems**: Some hypotheses involve apparent impossibilities (e.g., "assume this burning candle is not burning") — but these are not fictions because the mind knows exactly what it is abstracting from and why (009-008).

The omniscient Being "cannot form fictitious hypotheses" (009-005) because complete knowledge precludes the confusion that fiction requires. But even finite minds can form legitimate hypotheses when they distinguish what is known from what is supposed.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Hypothesis plays a constructive role in method:

1. **Heuristic device**: Hypotheses enable the mind to isolate variables and test consequences. When we assume a candle burns in empty space, we learn that "the candle contains in itself no causes for its own destruction" (009-008).

2. **Path to truth**: A true hypothesis, when pursued deductively, "will proceed with an uninterrupted series of apt conclusions" (009-012). The internal consistency of the deductions confirms the hypothesis without need of external verification.

3. **Safeguard against doubt**: "We cannot cast doubt on true ideas by the supposition that there is a deceitful Deity" (011-003). A mere hypothesis cannot overturn a clear and distinct idea — the hierarchy of certainty protects true ideas from hypothetical assault.

## Key Passages

> "Whence it follows that if there be a God, or omniscient Being, such an one cannot form fictitious hypotheses." (009-005)

> "It remains for us to consider hypotheses made in problems, which sometimes involve impossibilities... there is here no fiction, but true and bare assertions." (009-008)

> "While leaving such persons in their error, we will take care to derive from our argument with them a truth serviceable for our purpose, namely, that the mind, in paying attention to a thing hypothetical or false, so as to meditate upon it and understand it, and derive the proper conclusions in due order therefrom, will readily discover its falsity." (009-012)

> "We need, therefore, be in no fear of forming hypotheses, so long as we have a clear and distinct perception of what is involved." (009-013)

## Notes

Spinoza's defense of hypothesis as a legitimate methodical instrument is striking in a rationalist treatise. He is not a naive deductivist who rejects all supposition — he recognizes that finite minds must proceed hypothetically, testing suppositions against the standard of true ideas. The key distinction is epistemic: fiction is formed in confusion, hypothesis in clarity. The same verbal form ("let us assume...") can express either, depending on whether the mind understands the causes involved. This anticipates the *Ethics*'s use of counterfactual reasoning and the *more geometrico* method's deployment of "axioms" and "postulates" as licensed suppositions.
