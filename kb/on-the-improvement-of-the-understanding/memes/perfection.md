# Perfection

Perfection (*perfectio*) is the teleological terminus of Spinoza's entire method. It is the state of "supreme human perfection" (003-005) that the emendation of the understanding is designed to achieve. Spinoza's concept of perfection is distinctive: it is not a static ideal but a dynamic process — the mind's progressive improvement through the acquisition of true ideas.

## Definition

Spinoza's account of perfection is relational and practical, not absolute. "Nothing regarded in its own nature can be called perfect or imperfect" (003-001) — perfection is always relative to an end. Man "conceives a human character much more stable than his own, and sees that there is no reason why he should not himself acquire such a character" (003-002). This conceived ideal becomes the standard of perfection.

Perfection consists in:
- The possession of the "aforesaid character" — knowledge of the union of mind and nature (003-004)
- The mind's capacity to form true and adequate ideas (006-001)
- A state progressively approached: "gradually mounted from stage to stage" (006-007)

## How Method Produces Perfection

The method operates to increase perfection by enabling the mind to "discern the highest degree of perfection to which man is capable of attaining" (006-001) and to pursue it systematically. Each true idea acquired "simultaneously acquires fresh instruments for pursuing its inquiries further" (007-007), creating a self-amplifying ascent toward perfection.

The third mode of perception gives us ideas "without risk of error; yet it is not by itself sufficient to put us in possession of the perfection we aim at" (006-004). Only the fourth mode — intuitive knowledge of essences — achieves the perfection sought.

## Key Passages

> "Man conceives a human character much more stable than his own... Thus he is led to seek for means which will bring him to this pitch of perfection." (003-002)

> "We may attain to the supreme human perfection which we have named." (003-005)

> "We must discern the highest degree of perfection to which man is capable of attaining." (006-001)

> "Gradually mounted from stage to stage, we may in time attain to the summit of wisdom." (006-007)

## Notes

Spinoza's relational theory of perfection anticipates the *Ethics* Preface to Part IV, where "perfect" and "imperfect" are explicitly said to be "modes of thinking" — notions we form by comparing things to a model (*exemplar*) we have conceived. The *Tractatus* already contains this insight: nothing is perfect in itself; perfection is relative to the end we set. The ideal of "supreme human perfection" is not a transcendent Platonic form but a regulative ideal constructed by reason.
