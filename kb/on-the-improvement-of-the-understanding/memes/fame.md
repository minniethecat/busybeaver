# Fame

Fame — public esteem, reputation, honor — is one of the three ordinary objects of desire that Spinoza identifies as obstacles to the true good. "In the case of fame the mind is still more absorbed" (002-003) than with sensual pleasure, because fame compels the mind to orient itself entirely toward the opinions of others rather than toward what is genuinely good.

## Definition

Spinoza treats fame as a particularly insidious object of desire because it "compels the mind to direct its course according to the opinions of others" (002-003). Unlike wealth or pleasure, which can be pursued privately, fame requires constant attention to public perception — and thus represents a more total alienation of the mind from its own proper good.

Key characteristics:

- **Most absorbing of ordinary goods**: "In the case of fame the mind is still more absorbed" (002-003) than with pleasure — fame dominates the mind's attention more completely
- **Other-directed**: Fame "compels the mind to direct its course according to the opinions of others" (002-003), alienating it from its own judgment
- **Self-reinforcing**: The pursuit of fame requires ever-increasing effort to maintain public standing, creating a cycle of dependence
- **Hindrance when pursued as end**: Like wealth and pleasure, fame "is only a hindrance, so long as we seek [it] as [an end] in itself" (002-009)

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Fame plays a key role in the treatise's opening ethical diagnosis:

1. **Motivation for withdrawal**: Spinoza reports that he "resolved at length to seek a good, which, once found, would give me continuous and supreme joy for eternity" — and that fame and riches were among "the things which are acquired through fame and riches" (001-002) that he had to set aside.

2. **The alienation of other-directedness**: Fame is uniquely problematic among the three ordinary objects because it requires the mind to orient itself externally — toward the opinions and judgments of others. This external orientation is the opposite of the inward turn that the method requires.

3. **Fame as motivation for philosophy**: Spinoza notes that some pursue philosophy "for the sake of fame" (017-001), which perverts the very purpose of the enterprise. Philosophy pursued for fame cannot attain genuine wisdom, because the object of pursuit remains external to the mind itself.

## Key Passages

> "I resolved at length to seek a good... and that I should relinquish those things which are acquired through fame and riches." (001-002)

> "In the case of fame the mind is still more absorbed: for fame is ever assumed as a good in itself, and as an end to which everything else must be subordinated." (002-003)

> "The acquisition of wealth, sensual pleasure, or fame, is only a hindrance, so long as we seek them as ends in themselves." (002-009)

## Notes

Spinoza's critique of fame anticipates themes that would later be developed by Rousseau and other critics of *amour-propre* (self-love dependent on others' opinions). Spinoza's distinctive contribution is to analyze fame not merely as a moral failing but as an epistemological obstacle: fame directs the mind outward, toward the unstable opinions of the multitude, when the method requires it to turn inward, toward the stable knowledge of adequate ideas. The pursuit of fame and the pursuit of wisdom are structurally incompatible — a theme that runs throughout the *Tractatus* and resonates with the ancient philosophical tradition Spinoza inherits.
