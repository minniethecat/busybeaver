# Deduction

Deduction (*deductio*) is the logical operation by which the mind moves from known truths to new truths through valid inference. In Spinoza's method, deduction from true ideas is the principal means by which knowledge is extended.

## Definition

Spinoza conceives deduction as the act of deriving particular conclusions from a true and adequate starting-point. The reliability of deduction depends entirely on the truth of its premises and the validity of its chain: "If there yet remains some sceptic, who doubts of our primary truth, and of all deductions we make, taking such truth as our standard, he must either be arguing in bad faith, or we must confess that there are men in complete mental blindness" (047). Deduction is what allows the mind to proceed from the known to the unknown in an orderly fashion.

## Key Passages

> "If there yet remains some sceptic, who doubts of our primary truth, and of all deductions we make, taking such truth as our standard, he must either be arguing in bad faith, or we must confess that there are men in complete mental blindness either innate or due to misconceptions." (047)

> "Thus we can see that it is before all things necessary for us to deduce all our ideas from physical things — that is, from real entities, proceeding, as far as may be, according to the series of causes, from one real entity to another real entity." (099)

## Notes

Deduction in Spinoza's *Tractatus* is part of the "reflective knowledge" that constitutes method — it is the ordered way of thinking that compensates for the rarity of spontaneous intuitive discovery (044).
