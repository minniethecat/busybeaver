# Reflection

Reflection (*reflexio*) is the mind's capacity to turn upon itself and examine its own ideas. It is the meta-cognitive act that makes method possible: "method is nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea" (007-006).

## Definition

Spinoza characterizes reflection as:

- **Self-examination**: The mind steps back from its objects and investigates its own cognitive operations. "Further reflection convinced me that if I could really get to the root of the matter I should be leaving certain evils for a certain good" (002-005).
- **Categorical discovery**: "Reflection shows that all modes of perception or knowledge may be reduced to four" (005-001). Reflection reveals the *structure* of cognition — it is the instrument of philosophical taxonomy.
- **The method itself**: Method is defined genetically as reflective knowledge — "the idea of an idea" (007-006). To have a method is to reflect on how one knows.

Reflection is thus distinct from direct perception: perception is of objects; reflection is of *ideas* of objects.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Reflection is the *enabling condition* of the entire treatise:

1. **Genesis of method**: Without reflection, there is no method — only unexamined habits of thought. The treatise begins with Spinoza's autobiographical reflection on the insufficiency of ordinary objects of desire (002-005).
2. **Classification of knowledge**: Reflection on cognition itself yields the four modes of perception (005-001). This taxonomy is the groundwork for the method's prescriptions.
3. **Distinction of true from false**: To distinguish a true idea from a false one, the mind must reflect on the *idea itself*, not on its object. "The true idea of Peter is the reality of Peter represented subjectively" — grasping this requires reflection.
4. **Normative guidance**: Reflection reveals what the mind *ought* to do: choose the best mode of perception, seek clear and distinct ideas, avoid abstraction.

## Key Passages

> "Further reflection convinced me that if I could really get to the root of the matter I should be leaving certain evils for a certain good." (002-005)

> "Reflection shows that all modes of perception or knowledge may be reduced to four." (005-001)

> "Method is nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea; and as there is no idea of an idea, unless there is first an idea, so there is no method unless there is first an idea." (007-006)

## Notes

The concept of *reflexio* in the *Tractatus* anticipates the *Ethics*'s doctrine of *idea ideae* (E2p21s): the mind knows itself only through ideas of the affections of the body. Reflection is therefore not a Cartesian "transparent self" but a second-order idea — an idea that takes another idea as its object. This prevents infinite regress (there is no "idea of an idea of an idea" required) because the first-order idea is already known; reflection merely makes that knowledge *explicit*.
