# Mind

The mind (*mens*) is, for Spinoza, the thinking subject whose states, affections, and capacities define the entire problematic of the *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione*. It is at once that which is "affected" by external goods (001-001), "absorbed" by the three ordinary objects of desire (002-001), and the faculty capable of being "directed" toward knowledge of its union with the whole of nature (007-006).

## Definition

Spinoza never offers a formal definition of *mens* in the treatise — the mind is treated as the given subject of inquiry, whose nature is progressively clarified through method. However, several essential characteristics emerge:

- The mind is **passively affected**: experience teaches that "all the ordinary objects of desire" are "bad, except in so far as the mind is affected by them" (001-001)
- The mind is **absorbable**: "with these three the mind is so absorbed that it has little power to think of any other good" (002-001)
- The mind is **unitable**: the highest good is "the knowledge of the union existing between the mind and the whole of nature" (003-002)
- The mind is **trainable**: method must show "how the mind should be directed, according to the standard of the given true idea" (007-006)
- The mind is **self-augmenting**: "in proportion as it acquires new ideas, the mind simultaneously acquires fresh instruments for pursuing its inquiries further" (007-007)

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The improvement of the mind is the treatise's explicit goal. The entire argument is structured around the question of how the mind can be freed from absorption in ordinary objects (002-001 through 002-009) and directed toward its true good — the knowledge of nature and of itself.

The mind's role in method unfolds across three stages:

1. **Diagnosis**: The mind's ordinary condition is absorption in sensual pleasure, fame, and riches — goods that "disturb" the mind and prevent it from thinking of any other end (002-001, 002-002, 002-003). The mind is "enthralled" by pleasure to the extent of "quiescence, as though it had attained its highest good" (002-002).

2. **Reorientation**: The mind can recognize that "what is external and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unalloyed by any sorrow" (002-008). While the mind was employed with thoughts of this eternal and infinite good, "it was turning away from those ordinary objects, and was seriously considering the quest of a new principle" (002-009).

3. **Methodical Direction**: Method is defined as "reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea" (007-006), and since "there can be no method without a pre-existent idea," the mind must already possess true ideas as "natural instruments" (007-005, 007-007). The method's task is to "train" the mind so that it may represent nature "by a given standard" (007-005).

The mind's relation to the body is noted briefly: Spinoza states that "the mind is united to the body" and that this union is "the cause of the given sensation" (005-003). But from "knowing the essence of the mind, I know that it is united to the body" (005-004) — essence-knowledge is prior to union-knowledge.

## Key Passages

> "All the ordinary objects of desire... I found were in their own nature indifferent — good or bad, except in so far as the mind is affected by them." (001-001)

> "By sensual pleasure the mind is enthralled to the extent of quiescence, as though it had attained its highest good, so that it is quite incapable of thinking of any other thing." (002-002)

> "What is external and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unalloyed by any sorrow." (002-008)

> "Method is nothing else than reflective knowledge, or the idea of an idea; and... there can be no method without a pre-existent idea." (007-006)

> "In proportion as it acquires new ideas, the mind simultaneously acquires fresh instruments for pursuing its inquiries further." (007-007)

## Notes

Spinoza's *mens* in the *Tractatus* is not yet the full metaphysical doctrine of *Ethics* II (where the mind is "the idea of the body"), but it already anticipates key features: the mind is both passive (affected by external things) and active (capable of forming true ideas and directing itself). The treatise's opening diagnosis — that ordinary objects of desire absorb the mind and prevent it from thinking of the true good — is a psychological and epistemological preamble to the ethical project Spinoza would later systematize. The "union of the mind with the whole of nature" (003-002) is an early formulation of what would become the intellectual love of God (*amor Dei intellectualis*) in the *Ethics*.
