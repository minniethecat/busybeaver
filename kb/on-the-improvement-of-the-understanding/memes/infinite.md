# Infinite

The infinite (*infinitum*) is what has no boundary or external determination. For Spinoza, the infinite is not a mathematical abstraction but the nature of the first principle itself: "a being single and infinite; in other words, it is the sum total of being, beyond which there is no being found" (010-011). The love of the infinite is the highest human good (002-008).

## Definition

Spinoza unfolds the concept of the infinite across several registers:

- **The object of supreme love**: "Love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unmingled with any sadness" (002-008). Unlike finite, perishable goods, the infinite good cannot be lost.
- **A property of the first principle**: The origin of nature is "a being single and infinite" (010-011). It is not one thing among others but the totality of being.
- **A mark of the understanding's power**: "Those ideas which the understanding forms absolutely express infinity" (016-001, property III). The intellect can conceive the infinite positively, without negation.
- **Contrasted with fiction's limits**: "When we know the nature of body we cannot imagine an infinite fly" (009-009) — fiction is bounded by the known; understanding can reach the truly infinite.

Importantly, the infinite is not the indefinite. The indefinite is a failure of determination; the infinite is a positive plenitude of being.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The infinite structures the method in three dimensions:

1. **Teleological**: The method aims at knowledge of the infinite Being — this is its "absolutely perfect" endpoint (007-010). All finite investigations are ordered toward this infinite goal.

2. **Epistemological**: The understanding forms ideas of infinity "absolutely" — not by negating the finite, but by a positive intellectual act (016-001). This capacity distinguishes the intellect from the imagination, which can only represent finite things.

3. **Methodological constraint**: "We may never, while we are concerned with inquiries into actual things, draw any conclusion from abstractions" (014-003). The infinite is not an abstraction extrapolated from finite things; it is the concrete reality from which finite things derive. The method must proceed from the infinite to the finite, not the reverse.

## Key Passages

## Notes

The *Tractatus*'s treatment of the infinite anticipates Spinoza's mature doctrine in the *Ethics*, where God is defined as "a substance consisting of an infinity of attributes" (E1d6). Already in the *Tractatus*, the infinite is not a negation of the finite (as in negative theology) but the positive ground of all being. Words like "infinite" and "incorporeal" are negative in form only because "their contraries are much more easily imagined" (013-004) — the linguistic negativity conceals a conceptual positivity.
