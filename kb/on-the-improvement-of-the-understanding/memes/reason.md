# Reason

Reason is the discursive faculty of the mind that proceeds from given true ideas to derive further truths through valid chains of inference. In Spinoza's method, reason is both the instrument and the object of the emendation of the intellect.

## Definition

Spinoza treats reason (*ratio*) as the capacity for ordered, logical inference from true premises. Unlike the intellect's direct intuition of simple truths, reason proceeds discursively through chains of reasoning. As Spinoza states, method "must necessarily be concerned with reasoning or understanding" (037), distinguishing method from mere causal inquiry. Reason validates itself: "by valid reasoning I have established valid reasoning" (044).

## Key Passages

> "If our reasoning be sound, we must take as a starting-point a true idea. Now, to be certain that our starting-point is really a true idea, we need proof. This first course of reasoning must be supported by a second, the second by a third, and so on to infinity." (043)

> "I reply by warning him not to reject as false any paradoxes he may find here, but to take the trouble to reflect on the chain of reasoning by which they are supported." (046)

## Notes

The concept of reason in the *Tractatus* is less developed than in the *Ethics*, where it becomes a distinct kind of knowledge (the second kind). Here it functions primarily as the discursive complement to intuition.
