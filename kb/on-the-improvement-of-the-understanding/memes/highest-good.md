# Highest Good

The highest good (*summum bonum*) is the ethical and teleological foundation of Spinoza's entire project. The *Tractatus* opens with a personal narrative of the search for the true good, and the method is presented as the means to attain it. "What that character is we shall show in due time, namely, that it is the knowledge of the union existing between the mind and the whole of nature" (003-004).

## Definition

Spinoza's account of the highest good is carefully constructed:

"Human weakness cannot attain to this order in its own thoughts, but meanwhile man conceives a human character much more stable than his own... Thus he is led to seek for means which will bring him to this pitch of perfection, and calls everything which will serve as such means a true good. The chief good is that he should arrive, together with other individuals if possible, at the possession of the aforesaid character" (003-002–003).

The highest good is therefore:
- A state of character — supreme human perfection
- Knowledge-based — the knowledge of the union of mind and nature
- Communal — to be shared with others, not pursued solipsistically

## The Three Ordinary Goods Rejected

Spinoza identifies three "ordinary objects of desire" that men mistake for the highest good: Riches, Fame, and the Pleasures of Sense (002-001). These are rejected because:
- They are perishable and uncertain (002-001)
- Pursuit of them "causes the death not seldom of those who possess them, and always of those who are possessed by them" (002-002)
- They are sought as ends, not as means (002-003)

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The highest good is what gives the method its purpose. "All our actions and thoughts must be directed to this one end" (003-006). The method is not pursued for its own sake but as the means to this ethical goal. The entire structure — distinguishing true ideas, forming clear and distinct ideas through definitions — is instrumental to the attainment of the highest good.

## Notes

Spinoza's concept of the highest good deliberately echoes ancient eudaimonistic ethics (Aristotle, the Stoics) while transforming it. The highest good is not pleasure or virtue alone but *knowledge* — specifically, the knowledge of nature's unity. The communal dimension ("together with other individuals if possible") distinguishes Spinoza's ethics from both Stoic self-sufficiency and Christian salvation. The *Ethics* will later identify the highest good with the "intellectual love of God" (*amor Dei intellectualis*, E5p32c).
