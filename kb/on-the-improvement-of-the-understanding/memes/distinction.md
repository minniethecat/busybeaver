# Distinction

In Spinoza's *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione*, making distinctions (*distinctio*) is a fundamental intellectual operation that underlies the entire method. To improve the understanding is, in large part, to learn to make the right distinctions — between true ideas and false ones, between the intellect and the imagination, between essences and existence, and between what belongs to words and what belongs to things.

## Definition

Spinoza repeatedly invokes the act of distinguishing as essential to philosophical clarity:

- **Methodical distinction**: Spinoza introduces the first part of the method by noting that he has "at length" decided "to keep a distinction so necessary before the reader" (009-001) — separating true ideas from fictitious and false ideas. The entire structure of the *Tractatus* — first distinguishing true from fictitious, then true from false, then addressing doubt, memory, and verbal hindrances — is an exercise in systematic distinction.
- **Distinction vs. confused perception**: "If a man perceives the whole object at once without making distinctions, it follows... that he will have only a very confused idea" (009-014). Making distinctions is the opposite of confused perception; it is the act of separating what is really distinct and connecting what is really related.
- **Imagination vs. intellect**: "Sometimes, while making no distinction between the imagination and the intellect, they think that what they more easily imagine is clearer to them" (013-005). The failure to distinguish between imagination and intellect is a primary source of error — people mistake vivid images for clear ideas.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Distinction serves the method in three fundamental ways:

1. **First part of method — separating kinds of ideas**: The entire first part of the method (chapters on fictitious ideas, false ideas, doubt, memory, and verbal hindrances) is organized around making distinctions. Each chapter clarifies one kind of non-adequate idea and distinguishes it from true ideas, progressively training the reader to recognize what a true idea is and is not.

2. **Foundation for definition**: Good definitions depend on making the right distinctions. To define a thing is to distinguish it from all other things — to mark out its essence as unique. The distinction between the series of causes and the series of existence (see [[series]]) is itself a definitional distinction.

3. **Overcoming verbal confusion**: Spinoza notes that words cause confusion "through the imagination" and that these verbal associations "will be shown through the progress of the intellect to be distinct" (017-024). Making proper intellectual distinctions overcomes the associative confusion that words produce in the imagination.

## Notes

The concept of distinction in the *Tractatus* is closely related to the Cartesian criterion of "clear and distinct ideas," but Spinoza develops it in a more operational direction. For Spinoza, distinction is not merely a property that ideas can have but an *activity* that the intellect performs. The method is the systematic practice of making distinctions — between kinds of ideas, between faculties, between types of causation, between ends and means. This is why the *Tractatus* is structured as a series of successive distinctions rather than as a linear argument: each distinction clears the ground for the next, progressively refining the understanding until it can operate with clear and distinct ideas as a matter of course.
