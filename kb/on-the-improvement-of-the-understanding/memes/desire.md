# Desire

Desire (*cupiditas*) is, for Spinoza, the fundamental human striving toward objects conceived as goods. The *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione* opens with a diagnosis of desire's ordinary objects — riches, fame, and sensual pleasure — as the threefold obstacle that absorbs the mind and prevents it from seeking the true good (002-001). The reorientation of desire from perishable to eternal objects is the ethical precondition for the improvement of the understanding.

## Definition

Spinoza does not offer a standalone definition of desire in the *Tractatus*, but its nature unfolds through his analysis of the "ordinary objects of men's desires" (002-001 through 002-009). Desire is characterized by:

- **Absorption**: The ordinary objects of desire "so absorb the mind that it has little power to reflect on any different good" (002-001)
- **Enthrallment**: Sensual pleasure in particular enthralls the mind "to the extent of quiescence, as if the supreme good were actually attained, so that it is quite incapable of thinking of any other object" (002-002)
- **Escalation**: Wealth and fame, unlike sensual pleasure, produce increasing rather than satiating desire — "the more we acquire, the greater is our delight, and, consequently, the more are we incited to increase both the one and the other" (002-003)
- **Dependence on the object**: "Happiness or unhappiness is made wholly dependent on the quality of the object which we love" (002-007) — when the object is perishable, disturbances of mind inevitably follow
- **Redirectability**: Desire is not inherently bad; it becomes destructive only when directed toward perishable objects sought as ends rather than as means (002-009)

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The analysis of desire serves as the treatise's motivational starting point. Spinoza frames the entire project of intellectual improvement as a reorientation of desire:

1. **Diagnosis of ordinary desire**: The treatise opens by cataloguing how desire for riches, fame, and sensual pleasure absorbs, disturbs, and ultimately destroys those possessed by these objects (002-001 through 002-006). "All the objects pursued by the multitude not only bring no remedy that tends to preserve our being, but even act as hindrances, causing the death not seldom of those who possess them, and always of those who are possessed by them" (002-005).

2. **The contrast with eternal love**: Against ordinary desire, Spinoza sets "love towards a thing eternal and infinite" which "feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unmingled with any sadness, wherefore it is greatly to be desired and sought for with all our strength" (002-008). The double use of "desired" here is deliberate — desire itself is redirected rather than eliminated.

3. **Reorientation through understanding**: As the true good becomes "more and more discernible," the mind's intervals of freedom from ordinary desire "became more frequent and more lasting" (002-009). Spinoza recognizes that ordinary objects can be "sought as means" rather than ends, and when so sought "will be under restraint, and, far from being hindrances, will further not a little the end for which they are sought" (002-009).

4. **The social dimension of desire**: The highest good includes the desire that others share in it — "it is part of my happiness to lend a helping hand, that many others may understand even as I do, so that their understanding and desire may entirely agree with my own" (003-002). This transforms desire from a private passion into a communal striving.

## Key Passages

> "All the ordinary surroundings of life which are esteemed by men (as their actions testify) to be the highest good, may be classed under the three heads — Riches, Fame, and the Pleasures of Sense: with these three the mind is so absorbed that it has little power to reflect on any different good." (002-001)

> "By sensual pleasure the mind is enthralled to the extent of quiescence, as if the supreme good were actually attained, so that it is quite incapable of thinking of any other object; when such pleasure has been gratified it is followed by extreme melancholy." (002-002)

> "All the objects pursued by the multitude not only bring no remedy that tends to preserve our being, but even act as hindrances, causing the death not seldom of those who possess them, and always of those who are possessed by them." (002-005)

> "But love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unmingled with any sadness, wherefore it is greatly to be desired and sought for with all our strength." (002-008)

> "While my mind was employed with these thoughts it turned away from its former objects of desire, and seriously considered the search for a new principle." (002-009)

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of desire in the *Tractatus* is primarily negative and diagnostic — it identifies the problem that the improvement of the understanding must overcome. This contrasts with the *Ethics*, where desire (*cupiditas*) is elevated to the very essence of man (*Ethics* III, Definitions of the Affects I: "Desire is the very essence of man, in so far as his essence is conceived as determined to any action from any given affection of itself"). The *Tractatus* anticipates this by making desire not a faculty to be extirpated but a force to be redirected: ordinary objects become "means" rather than "ends" (002-009), and the desire for the true good includes the desire that others share in it.

The threefold classification of ordinary objects — riches, fame, sensual pleasure — echoes classical and medieval moral psychology (the threefold concupiscence of 1 John 2:16), but Spinoza's analysis is distinctively naturalistic. He traces all disturbances of mind to the perishability of the loved object (002-007), not to any inherent sinfulness of desire itself.
