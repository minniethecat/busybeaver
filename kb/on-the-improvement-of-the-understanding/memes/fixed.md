# Fixed and Eternal Things

The concept of "fixed and eternal things" (*res fixae et aeternae*) is the ontological and methodological cornerstone of Spinoza's theory of knowledge. All adequate knowledge must be anchored in what is fixed and eternal — in contrast to the mutable, particular, and merely temporal.

## Definition

Spinoza draws a fundamental distinction between two orders of reality:

- **Singular, changeable things**: Particular entities that come into being and pass away, perceived through the senses. Knowledge of these alone cannot provide genuine understanding because they are contingent and derivative.
- **Fixed and eternal things**: The immutable realities — causal laws, the essences of things, the eternal order of Nature itself. These are "fixed" in the sense of being unchanging and "eternal" in the sense of being outside temporal succession.

The key passages articulate this definition:

> "The series of fixed and eternal things" (015-007) — these things form a *series*, an ordered chain of causal dependence, which the method must trace.

> "Knowledge of singular changeable things must be sought solely from fixed and eternal things, and from the laws inscribed in those things as their true codes" (015-008). Singular things are known *through* the fixed and eternal, not the reverse.

> "All things come to pass by fixed laws, so that each thing must have its fixed effect" (017-024). This lawfulness extends to both physical and mental domains.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The fixed and eternal things anchor the entire epistemological method:

1. **The foundation of definition**: A proper definition of any uncreated thing must "exclude every condition — that is, everything that limits it" (015-003). This is only possible by appealing to what is fixed and eternal, not to contingent circumstances.

2. **The order of inquiry**: Spinoza insists that the intellect proceeds "according to fixed rules" (008-007). The method itself is grounded in the same fixed order it seeks to discover — inquiry is lawful, not arbitrary.

3. **The soul as law-governed**: Extending the concept to psychology, Spinoza argues that "the soul acts according to fixed laws, and is as it were an immaterial automaton" (012-005). The fixed and eternal is not merely external Nature but also the internal constitutional principles of the mind.

4. **The fixed as the goal of desire**: Early in the treatise, Spinoza describes his search "for a fixed good" (*fixum bonum*, 002-004). The epistemological method is motivated by the ethical desire for something stable — the fixed is not only a category of knowledge but an object of striving.

5. **Experimental method under fixed rules**: Even experimental knowledge must be pursued "under fixed rules and arrangements" (015-010) — the fixed provides the framework within which empirical investigation becomes scientific.

## Key Passages

## Notes

The concept of "fixed and eternal things" is among the most important in the *Tractatus* for understanding Spinoza's departure from Cartesian and scholastic epistemology. Where Descartes sought a *fixed point* in the *cogito* (a subjective certainty), Spinoza seeks fixed *things* — objective, law-governed realities that exist independently of the mind's certitude. The entire method for improving the understanding is, in essence, a method for progressively orienting the mind toward the fixed and eternal rather than the changeable and particular.
