# Body

The body (*corpus*) in Spinoza's *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione* is the physical correlate of the mind — that to which the mind is united, through which sensation arises, and whose nature, when known, constrains the imagination and grounds the distinction between true and fictitious ideas.

## Definition

Spinoza does not provide a formal definition of body in the *Tractatus*, but several essential characteristics can be extracted:

- **The body as union-term**: "When we clearly perceive that we feel a certain body and no other, we thence clearly infer that the mind is united to the body, and that their union is the cause of the given sensation" (005-003).

- **The body as essence-known**: From "knowing the essence of the mind, I know that it is united to the body" (005-004) — knowledge of the mind-body union follows from essence-knowledge, not from sensory inference.

- **The body as distinct from its idea**: "The idea of a circle is not something having a circumference and a center, as a circle has; nor is the idea of a body that body itself" (007-001).

- **The body as constraining fiction**: "When we know the nature of body we cannot imagine an infinite fly" (009-009) — knowledge of the body's nature places limits on what can be fictitiously imagined.

- **The body as indeterminate in imagination**: Spinoza warns readers not to be "astonished that before proving the existence of body, and other necessary things, I speak of imagination of body, and of its composition" (012-004).

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The body plays several distinct roles in the epistemological progression of the *Tractatus*:

1. **As the ground of sensation**: The union of mind and body is the causal basis of all sensation. Spinoza illustrates this through a progression: from feeling a body we infer the union; from knowing the nature of vision we infer that the sun is larger than it appears (005-003). But sensation alone cannot deliver the *nature* of this union — for that, essence-knowledge is required.

2. **As the object of imagination**: The body is the primary object with which the imagination (*imaginatio*) engages. "The imagination is only affected by physical objects" (012-004), and words are "signs of things as existing in the imagination, not as existing in the understanding" (013-002). The imagination's dependence on bodily affection makes it an unreliable source of ideas.

3. **As a limit on fiction**: Knowledge of the body's nature functions as a constraint on the power of feigning. When we know the nature of body, "we cannot imagine an infinite fly" — the known essence precludes arbitrary combinations (009-009). Conversely, those ignorant of bodily nature "can coin fictitious ideas, such as trees speaking, men instantly changed into stones" (009-009).

4. **As a determinant in idea-formation**: In the enumeration of the understanding's properties, Spinoza notes that determinate ideas are formed through the idea of quantity perceived "by means of a cause" — "as when a body is perceived to be formed by the motion of a plane, a plane by the motion of a line" (016-002). The body serves as a paradigm of determinate, causally-structured cognition.

5. **As a methodological concession**: Spinoza explicitly acknowledges that he speaks of the body's imagination and composition before proving its existence (012-004). This is a methodological concession: the treatise's order of exposition does not follow the order of demonstration, because certain concepts (body, imagination) must be deployed before they can be rigorously grounded.

## Key Passages

> "When we clearly perceive that we feel a certain body and no other, we thence clearly infer that the mind is united to the body, and that their union is the cause of the given sensation; but we cannot thence absolutely understand the nature of the sensation and the union." (005-003)

> "From knowing the essence of the mind, I know that it is united to the body." (005-004)

> "The idea of a circle is not something having a circumference and a center, as a circle has; nor is the idea of a body that body itself." (007-001)

> "When we know the nature of body we cannot imagine an infinite fly; or, when we know the nature of the soul, we cannot imagine it as square, though anything may be expressed verbally." (009-009)

> "Let no one then be astonished that before proving the existence of body, and other necessary things, I speak of imagination of body, and of its composition." (012-004)

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of body in the *Tractatus* is notably different from his mature doctrine in the *Ethics*, where the mind is defined as "the idea of the body" (*Ethics* II, Proposition 13). In the *Tractatus*, the body is primarily an epistemological obstacle and constraint — the source of inadequate sensory ideas — rather than the constitutive object of the mind's idea. The doctrine that the mind-body union is known through the mind's essence (005-004) gestures toward the *Ethics* parallelism but does not yet articulate it.

The methodological concession at 012-004 is significant: Spinoza recognizes that the *ordo cognoscendi* (order of knowing) differs from the *ordo essendi* (order of being). The body can be discussed as an object of imagination before its existence is demonstrated, because the treatise follows the mind's path of improvement, not a deductive chain from first principles.
