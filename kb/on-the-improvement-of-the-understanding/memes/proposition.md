# Proposition

A proposition (*propositio*) is a statement affirmed or demonstrated within Spinoza's geometrical method. Modeled on Euclid's *Elements*, propositions are the incremental steps by which knowledge advances from foundations to remote conclusions (005-006).

## Definition

Spinoza uses "proposition" in its strict Euclidean sense:

- A proposition states a truth that **requires demonstration**: it is not self-evident but must be proved from prior propositions or axioms.
- "Mathematicians know by the proof of the nineteenth proposition of the seventh book of Euclid, what numbers are proportionals" (005-006) — the proposition is the *statement*; the proof is the *demonstration* of that statement.
- Propositions form a **chain**: each proposition builds on earlier ones, extending the reach of knowledge incrementally.

The term also appears in a looser sense: "Let us now return to our proposition" (008-007), where Spinoza uses it to mean the thesis or subject under discussion.

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The proposition is the basic unit of demonstrated knowledge:

1. **Structural**: Propositions are the building blocks of the deductive edifice. Each proposition adds one determinate piece of knowledge to the system.
2. **Pedagogical**: By following the chain of propositions, the reader retraces the path of discovery and arrives at understanding — not through hearsay but through reason.
3. **Normative**: The Euclidean model sets a standard: only what can be demonstrated as a proposition counts as genuine knowledge. "Tradesmen" who apply rules "without proof" (005-005) possess mere skill, not understanding.

Propositions are intimately paired with **proofs**: a proposition without proof is mere assertion; a proof without a proposition has nothing to demonstrate.

## Key Passages

> "Mathematicians, however, know by the proof of the nineteenth proposition of the seventh book of Euclid, what numbers are proportionals, namely, from the nature and property of proportion." (005-006)

> "Let us now return to our proposition. Up to the present, we have, first, defined the end to which we desire to direct all our thoughts." (008-007)

> "Others construct a universal axiom from their experience with simple numbers, where the fourth number is self-evident, as in the case of 2, 4, 3, 6." (005-005)

## Notes

Spinoza's commitment to the geometrical method is already visible in the *Tractatus*, though he has not yet adopted the full apparatus of the *Ethics* (definitiones, axiomata, propositiones, demonstrationes, scholia, corollaria). The Euclid example of the fourth proportional (005-005 through 005-006) is his clearest illustration: tradesmen know the rule by rote, empiricists generalize from cases, but only the mathematician who grasps the *proposition* and its *proof* truly understands *why* the rule works.
