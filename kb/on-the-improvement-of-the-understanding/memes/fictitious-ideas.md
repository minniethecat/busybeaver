# Fictitious Ideas

A class of inadequate ideas concerning possible existences, distinguished from false
and doubtful ideas in the first part of Spinoza's method.

## Definition

A fictitious idea (*idea ficta*) is an idea whereby the mind feigns the existence of
a thing that is possible (neither necessary nor impossible). Spinoza defines it as
concerned "with things possible, and not with things necessary or impossible" (009-003).
Fiction occupies an intermediate position: unlike false ideas, it involves no affirmation
of existence; unlike true ideas, it does not correspond to a real object.

The scope of fiction is limited: "fiction cannot be concerned with eternal truths"
(009-005), and "the more the mind's understanding increases, its capacity for
entertaining fictitious ideas becomes less" (009-009).

## Role in Spinoza's Method

The first part of Spinoza's method is "to distinguish and separate the true idea from
other perceptions, and to keep the mind from confusing with true ideas those which are
false, fictitious, and doubtful" (009-001). Fictitious ideas are the first class
examined because understanding their nature reveals the conditions under which fiction
is possible — and thus the conditions under which it is not.

Key methodological findings:
- Fiction concerns only possible things, not necessary or impossible ones (009-003)
- If we had adequate knowledge of a thing's causes, we could not feign otherwise
  (009-004)
- An omniscient being cannot form fictions (009-005)
- Fiction diminishes as understanding grows (009-009)

> If the first idea be not fictitious, and if all the other ideas be deduced therefrom,
> our hurry to form fictitious ideas will gradually subside. (009-014)

## Key Passages

> Every perception has for its object either a thing considered as existing, or solely
> the essence of a thing. Now "fiction" is chiefly occupied with things considered as
> existing. (009-003)

> I call a thing impossible when its existence would imply a contradiction; necessary,
> when its non-existence would imply a contradiction; possible, when neither its
> existence nor non-existence imply a contradiction. (009-004)

> In proportion as the mind's understanding is smaller... so will its power of coining
> fictions be larger, whereas as its understanding increases, its capacity for
> entertaining fictitious ideas becomes less. (009-009)

> False and fictitious ideas have nothing positive about them. (016-004)

## Notes

The TIE's treatment of fictitious ideas (chapter 009, §§52–67) is one of the most
detailed analyses in the treatise. Spinoza's key insight is that the possibility of
fiction is inversely proportional to knowledge: the more one understands, the fewer
things one can feign. This prefigures the *Ethics* doctrine that adequate ideas
leave no room for doubt or fiction.
