# Joy

Joy (*laetitia*) is the positive affective state that arises from the mind's possession of or movement toward a true good. In the *Tractatus*, Spinoza presents joy as the emotional counterpart of intellectual progress — the love of eternal things "feeds the mind wholly with joy" (010).

## Definition

For Spinoza, joy is not a mere feeling but the necessary affective consequence of the mind's engagement with what is truly good. The pursuit of the true good is simultaneously the pursuit of "continuous, supreme, and unending happiness" (001). Joy is unmingled with sadness only when its object is eternal and infinite: "love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unmingled with any sadness" (010).

## Key Passages

> "But love towards a thing eternal and infinite feeds the mind wholly with joy, and is itself unmingled with any sadness, wherefore it is greatly to be desired and sought for with all our strength." (010)

> "I finally resolved to inquire whether there might be some real good having power to communicate itself, which would affect the mind singly, to the exclusion of all else: whether, in fact, there might be anything of which the discovery and attainment would enable me to enjoy continuous, supreme, and unending happiness." (001)

## Related Concepts

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of joy in the *Tractatus* is less systematic than in the *Ethics* (where joy is one of the three primary affects). Here joy functions as a criterion: the quality of our joy reveals the quality of the objects we pursue.
