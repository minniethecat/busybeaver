# Definition

Definition (*definitio*) is the central instrument of the second part of Spinoza's method. Where the first part of method distinguishes true ideas from false, fictitious, and doubtful ones, the second part aims at "the acquisition of clear and distinct ideas" (014-001), and definition is the means by which this is accomplished: "The chief requirement of our method is... that our mind may form thoughts from some given definition" (015-001).

## What Makes a Perfect Definition

Spinoza specifies that "a definition, if it is to be called perfect, must explain the inmost essence of a thing, and must take care not to substitute certain properties for the thing itself" (015-002). A perfect definition captures *what the thing is*, not merely how it appears or what predicates it has.

This is linked to causality: for created things, the definition "must include the proximate cause" (015-003). To define a circle, one must explain it as the figure described by a line with one end fixed and the other in motion — not merely as "a figure in which all lines drawn from the center to the circumference are equal" (which states a property, not the essence).

## Rules of Definition

Spinoza distinguishes two classes of things and their definitional rules:

**For created things** (015-003):
1. The definition must include the proximate cause
2. All properties of the thing must be deducible from the definition alone

**For uncreated things** (015-004):
1. The definition must exclude every trace of cause (the thing is self-caused)
2. The definition must leave no room for the question "does it exist?"
3. The definition must contain no abstractions or universals
4. All properties must be deducible from the definition

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Definition is the bridge between the first and second parts of method. Once the mind possesses a true idea (from the first part), it uses definition to produce clear and distinct ideas of created things. The understanding, when it "attends to a true and legitimate definition," grasps the thing's essence and can deduce its properties (014-003).

## Notes

Spinoza's theory of definition is deeply indebted to Hobbes' genetic definition (*De Corpore* I.6) and to the Aristotelian-Scholastic tradition of real definition. Spinoza's innovation is to tie definition to causality: defining a thing means stating its proximate cause, not merely listing its genus and differentia. This causal conception of definition becomes central to the geometric method of the *Ethics*, where each definition is a genetic account of its object.
