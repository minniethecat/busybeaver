# Memory

Memory (*memoria*) is treated by Spinoza within his discussion of "memory and forgetfulness" as a mental faculty that depends on bodily conditions. It is "nothing else than a certain sensation of impressions on the brain, together with the thought of a determinate duration of the sensation" (012-003). Memory is not part of the understanding proper but belongs to the sphere of imagination.

## Definition

Spinoza gives a physiological-psychological account: "Memory is the sensation of impressions on the brain, together with the thought of a determinate duration" (012-003). Memory is strengthened in two ways: "either by the aid of the understanding, or by the aid of the imagination" (012-002). When strengthened by understanding, a thing is "recognized" and more easily recalled; when by imagination, the association of images reinforces retention.

The key insight is that memory is essentially bodily: it depends on "particular bodily conditions" (013-003) and on the association of impressions (012-002). This distinguishes memory from intellectual knowledge, which grasps essences directly and does not depend on corporeal traces.

## Memory and the Mind

Memory is not a neutral receptacle. Spinoza notes that words — "arrangements of words in the memory" — can become obstacles to clear thinking when they are confused with the things they signify (013-003). This connects memory to the broader theme of "mental hindrances from words."

## Role in Spinoza's Method

Memory is discussed in the context of what can obstruct the method. Since it depends on bodily association rather than rational order, memory alone cannot guarantee the order of ideas that method requires. The understanding, by contrast, "forms thoughts from some given definition" (015-001), proceeding by logical rather than associative connections.

## Key Passages

> "What, then, is memory? It is nothing else than a certain sensation of impressions on the brain, together with the thought of a determinate duration of the sensation." (012-003)

> "The memory is also strengthened without the aid of the understanding — namely, by the force wherewith the imagination or the sense called common is affected by some particular physical object." (012-002)

## Notes

Spinoza's treatment of memory reflects the influence of Descartes' *Passions of the Soul* (art. 42) and anticipates the more developed account in *Ethics* E2p18, where memory is explained as the association of ideas according to the order of bodily affections. The bodily basis of memory is a direct consequence of Spinoza's parallelism: every idea in the mind corresponds to a state of the body.
