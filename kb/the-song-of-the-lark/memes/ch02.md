## II

[002-001] For the next four days it seemed to [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] that his patient might slip

[002-002] through his hands, do what he might. But she did not. On the contrary, after

[002-003] that she recovered very rapidly. As her father remarked, she must have

[002-004] inherited the “constitution” which he was never tired of admiring

[002-005] in her mother.

[002-006] One afternoon, when her new brother was a week old, the doctor found Thea very

[002-007] comfortable and happy in her bed in the parlor. The sunlight was pouring in

[002-008] over her shoulders, the baby was asleep on a pillow in a big rocking-chair

[002-009] beside her. Whenever he stirred, she put out her hand and rocked him. Nothing

[002-010] of him was visible but a flushed, puffy forehead and an uncompromisingly big,

[002-011] bald cranium. The door into her mother’s room stood open, and Mrs.

[002-012] Kronborg was sitting up in bed darning stockings. She was a short, stalwart

[002-013] woman, with a short neck and a determined-looking head. Her skin was very fair,

[002-014] her face calm and unwrinkled, and her yellow hair, braided down her back as she

[002-015] lay in bed, still looked like a girl’s. She was a woman whom Dr. Archie

[002-016] respected; active, practical, unruffled; goodhumored, but determined. Exactly

[002-017] the sort of woman to take care of a flighty preacher. She had brought her

[002-018] husband some property, too,—one fourth of her father’s broad acres

[002-019] in Nebraska,—but this she kept in her own name. She had profound respect

[002-020] for her husband’s erudition and eloquence. She sat under his preaching

[002-021] with deep humility, and was as much taken in by his stiff shirt and white

[002-022] neckties as if she had not ironed them herself by lamplight the night before

[002-023] they appeared correct and spotless in the pulpit. But for all this, she had no

[002-024] confidence in his administration of worldly affairs. She looked to him for

[002-025] morning prayers and grace at table; she expected him to name the babies and to

[002-026] supply whatever parental sentiment there was in the house, to remember

[002-027] birthdays and anniversaries, to point the children to moral and patriotic

[002-028] ideals. It was her work to keep their bodies, their clothes, and their conduct

[002-029] in some sort of order, and this she accomplished with a success that was a

[002-030] source of wonder to her neighbors. As she used to remark, and her husband

[002-031] admiringly to echo, she “had never lost one.” With all his

[002-032] flightiness, Peter Kronborg appreciated the matter-of-fact, punctual way in

[002-033] which his wife got her children into the world and along in it. He believed,

[002-034] and he was right in believing, that the sovereign State of Colorado was much

[002-035] indebted to [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] and women like her.

[002-036] Mrs. Kronborg believed that the size of every family was decided in heaven.

[002-037] More modern views would not have startled her; they would simply have seemed

[002-038] foolish—thin chatter, like the boasts of the men who built the tower of

[002-039] Babel, or like Axel’s plan to breed ostriches in the chicken yard. From

[002-040] what evidence Mrs. Kronborg formed her opinions on this and other matters, it

[002-041] would have been difficult to say, but once formed, they were unchangeable. She

[002-042] would no more have questioned her convictions than she would have questioned

[002-043] revelation. Calm and even tempered, naturally kind, she was capable of strong

[002-044] prejudices, and she never forgave.

[002-045] When the doctor came in to see Thea, Mrs. Kronborg was reflecting that the

[002-046] washing was a week behind, and deciding what she had better do about it. The

[002-047] arrival of a new baby meant a revision of her entire domestic schedule, and as

[002-048] she drove her needle along she had been working out new sleeping arrangements

[002-049] and cleaning days. The doctor had entered the house without knocking, after

[002-050] making noise enough in the hall to prepare his patients. Thea was reading, her

[002-051] book propped up before her in the sunlight.

[002-052] “Mustn’t do that; bad for your eyes,” he said, as Thea shut

[002-053] the book quickly and slipped it under the covers.

[002-054] Mrs. Kronborg called from her bed: “Bring the baby here, doctor, and have

[002-055] that chair. She wanted him in there for company.”

[002-056] Before the doctor picked up the baby, he put a yellow paper bag down on

[002-057] Thea’s coverlid and winked at her. They had a code of winks and grimaces.

[002-058] When he went in to chat with her mother, Thea opened the bag cautiously, trying

[002-059] to keep it from crackling. She drew out a long bunch of white grapes, with a

[002-060] little of the sawdust in which they had been packed still clinging to them.

[002-061] They were called Malaga grapes in Moonstone, and once or twice during the

[002-062] winter the leading grocer got a keg of them. They were used mainly for table

[002-063] decoration, about Christmas-time. Thea had never had more than one grape at a

[002-064] time before. When the doctor came back she was holding the almost transparent

[002-065] fruit up in the sunlight, feeling the pale-green skins softly with the tips of

[002-066] her fingers. She did not thank him; she only snapped her eyes at him in a

[002-067] special way which he understood, and, when he gave her his hand, put it quickly

[002-068] and shyly under her cheek, as if she were trying to do so without knowing

[002-069] it—and without his knowing it.

[002-070] Dr. Archie sat down in the rocking-chair. “And how’s Thea feeling

[002-071] to-day?”

[002-072] He was quite as shy as his patient, especially when a third person overheard

[002-073] his conversation. Big and handsome and superior to his fellow townsmen as Dr.

[002-074] Archie was, he was seldom at his ease, and like Peter Kronborg he often dodged

[002-075] behind a professional manner. There was sometimes a contraction of

[002-076] embarrassment and self consciousness all over his big body, which made him

[002-077] awkward—likely to stumble, to kick up rugs, or to knock over chairs. If

[002-078] any one was very sick, he forgot himself, but he had a clumsy touch in

[002-079] convalescent gossip.

[002-080] Thea curled up on her side and looked at him with pleasure. “All right. I

[002-081] like to be sick. I have more fun then than other times.”

[002-082] “How’s that?”

[002-083] “I don’t have to go to school, and I don’t have to practice.

[002-084] I can read all I want to, and have good things,”—she patted the

[002-085] grapes. “I had lots of fun that time I mashed my finger and you

[002-086] wouldn’t let [[professor-wunsch|Professor Wunsch]] make me practice. Only I had to do left

[002-087] hand, even then. I think that was mean.”

[002-088] The doctor took her hand and examined the forefinger, where the nail had grown

[002-089] back a little crooked. “You mustn’t trim it down close at the

[002-090] corner there, and then it will grow straight. You won’t want it crooked

[002-091] when you’re a big girl and wear rings and have sweethearts.”

[002-092] She made a mocking little face at him and looked at his new scarf-pin.

[002-093] “That’s the prettiest one you ev-ER had. I wish you’d stay a

[002-094] long while and let me look at it. What is it?”

[002-095] Dr. Archie laughed. “It’s an opal. [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]] brought it up for

[002-096] me from Chihuahua in his shoe. I had it set in Denver, and I wore it to-day for

[002-097] your benefit.”

[002-098] Thea had a curious passion for jewelry. She wanted every shining stone she saw,

[002-099] and in summer she was always going off into the sand hills to hunt for crystals

[002-100] and agates and bits of pink chalcedony. She had two cigar boxes full of stones

[002-101] that she had found or traded for, and she imagined that they were of enormous

[002-102] value. She was always planning how she would have them set.

[002-103] “What are you reading?” The doctor reached under the covers and

[002-104] pulled out a book of Byron’s poems. “Do you like this?”

[002-105] She looked confused, turned over a few pages rapidly, and pointed to “My

[002-106] native land, good-night.” “That,” she said sheepishly.

[002-107] “How about ‘Maid of Athens’?”

[002-108] She blushed and looked at him suspiciously. “I like ‘There was a

[002-109] sound of revelry,’” she muttered.

[002-110] The doctor laughed and closed the book. It was clumsily bound in padded leather

[002-111] and had been presented to the Reverend Peter Kronborg by his Sunday-School

[002-112] class as an ornament for his parlor table.

[002-113] “Come into the office some day, and I’ll lend you a nice book. You

[002-114] can skip the parts you don’t understand. You can read it in vacation.

[002-115] Perhaps you’ll be able to understand all of it by then.”

[002-116] Thea frowned and looked fretfully toward the piano. “In vacation I have

[002-117] to practice four hours every day, and then there’ll be Thor to take care

[002-118] of.” She pronounced it “Tor.”

[002-119] “Thor? Oh, you’ve named the baby Thor?” exclaimed the doctor.

[002-120] Thea frowned again, still more fiercely, and said quickly, “That’s

[002-121] a nice name, only maybe it’s a little—old fashioned.” She was

[002-122] very sensitive about being thought a foreigner, and was proud of the fact that,

[002-123] in town, her father always preached in English; very bookish English, at that,

[002-124] one might add.

[002-125] Born in an old Scandinavian colony in Minnesota, Peter Kronborg had been sent

[002-126] to a small divinity school in Indiana by the women of a Swedish evangelical

[002-127] mission, who were convinced of his gifts and who skimped and begged and gave

[002-128] church suppers to get the long, lazy youth through the seminary. He could still

[002-129] speak enough Swedish to exhort and to bury the members of his country church

[002-130] out at Copper Hole, and he wielded in his Moonstone pulpit a somewhat pompous

[002-131] English vocabulary he had learned out of books at college. He always spoke of

[002-132] “the infant Saviour,” “our Heavenly Father,” etc. The

[002-133] poor man had no natural, spontaneous human speech. If he had his sincere

[002-134] moments, they were perforce inarticulate. Probably a good deal of his

[002-135] pretentiousness was due to the fact that he habitually expressed himself in a

[002-136] book learned language, wholly remote from anything personal, native, or homely.

[002-137] Mrs. Kronborg spoke Swedish to her own sisters and to her sister-in-law Tillie,

[002-138] and colloquial English to her neighbors. Thea, who had a rather sensitive ear,

[002-139] until she went to school never spoke at all, except in monosyllables, and her

[002-140] mother was convinced that she was tongue-tied. She was still inept in speech

[002-141] for a child so intelligent. Her ideas were usually clear, but she seldom

[002-142] attempted to explain them, even at school, where she excelled in “written

[002-143] work” and never did more than mutter a reply.

[002-144] “Your music professor stopped me on the street to-day and asked me how

[002-145] you were,” said the doctor, rising. “He’ll be sick himself,

[002-146] trotting around in this slush with no overcoat or overshoes.”

[002-147] “He’s poor,” said Thea simply.

[002-148] The doctor sighed. “I’m afraid he’s worse than that. Is he

[002-149] always all right when you take your lessons? Never acts as if he’d been

[002-150] drinking?”

[002-151] Thea looked angry and spoke excitedly. “He knows a lot. More than

[002-152] anybody. I don’t care if he does drink; he’s old and poor.”

[002-153] Her voice shook a little.

[002-154] Mrs. Kronborg spoke up from the next room. “He’s a good teacher,

[002-155] doctor. It’s good for us he does drink. He’d never be in a little

[002-156] place like this if he didn’t have some weakness. These women that teach

[002-157] music around here don’t know nothing. I wouldn’t have my child

[002-158] wasting time with them. If Professor Wunsch goes away, Thea’ll have

[002-159] nobody to take from. He’s careful with his scholars; he don’t use

[002-160] bad language. Mrs. Kohler is always present when Thea takes her lesson.

[002-161] It’s all right.” Mrs. Kronborg spoke calmly and judicially. One

[002-162] could see that she had thought the matter out before.

[002-163] “I’m glad to hear that, Mrs. Kronborg. I wish we could get the old

[002-164] man off his bottle and keep him tidy. Do you suppose if I gave you an old

[002-165] overcoat you could get him to wear it?” The doctor went to the bedroom

[002-166] door and Mrs. Kronborg looked up from her darning.

[002-167] “Why, yes, I guess he’d be glad of it. He’ll take most

[002-168] anything from me. He won’t buy clothes, but I guess he’d wear

[002-169] ’em if he had ’em. I’ve never had any clothes to give him,

[002-170] having so many to make over for.”

[002-171] “I’ll have Larry bring the coat around to-night. You aren’t

[002-172] cross with me, Thea?” taking her hand.

[002-173] Thea grinned warmly. “Not if you give Professor Wunsch a coat—and

[002-174] things,” she tapped the grapes significantly. The doctor bent over and

[002-175] kissed her.
