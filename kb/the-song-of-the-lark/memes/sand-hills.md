# The Sand Hills

## Overview

The sand hills east of Moonstone are the most persistent natural landmark in Thea Kronborg's childhood landscape. Visible from the town, they gleam white in sunlight and shift through the day's colors—yellow, lavender, rose—becoming a visual refuge that Thea turns to in moments of boredom, frustration, and reverie. When she leaves for Chicago, the sand hills are the last thing she sees; when she arrives there, they are the first thing she misses (025-167).

## Location & Geography

The sand hills lie to the east of Moonstone, Colorado—"clear that the white sand hills to the east of Moonstone gleamed" (001-067). They are within driving distance: Dr. Archie takes Thea driving "out among the sand hills" on Sundays (015-030). The hills are a constant backdrop to Moonstone life, always present at the edge of vision. From the Kohlers' garden, "the sand hills looked dim and sleepy" (010-006); in the evening, "the sand hills were lavender" (030-076).

## Role in the Novel

The sand hills function as Thea's private landscape—a place of retreat in her mind even when she cannot visit them physically. Professor Wunsch tells her that a place of retreat, like "the sand hills," is essential for an artist (025-087). When Thea arrives in Chicago, her thoughts turn immediately to home: "Here were the sand hills," the remembered landscape that anchors her identity (025-167). The hills represent the silent, spacious world of nature that nourishes her artistic temperament long before she understands what she is becoming.

## Key Scenes

- **Childhood Presence**: The white sand hills gleam to the east of Moonstone, a constant visual anchor in Thea's world (001-067).
- **Sunday Escapes**: Dr. Archie takes Thea driving among the sand hills, offering her respite from the constraints of small-town life (015-030).
- **Departure**: Leaving Moonstone, Thea watches "the last of the sand hills" disappear, realizing she is truly departing her childhood home (020-207).
- **Chicago Homesickness**: In Chicago, Thea misses the sand hills deeply: "She had missed them in Chicago" (029-062).
- **Wunsch's Wisdom**: Professor Wunsch identifies the sand hills as Thea's essential "place of retreat"—a psychological necessity for an artist (025-087).
- **Adult Memory**: Years later, Thea recalls "memories of light on the sand hills, of masses of prickly-pear" blossom, the sensory imprint of her Colorado childhood (040-008).
