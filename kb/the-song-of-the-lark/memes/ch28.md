## VIII

[028-001] One warm damp June night the Denver Express was speeding westward across the

[028-002] earthy-smelling plains of Iowa. The lights in the day-coach were turned low and

[028-003] the ventilators were open, admitting showers of soot and dust upon the

[028-004] occupants of the narrow green plush chairs which were tilted at various angles

[028-005] of discomfort. In each of these chairs some uncomfortable human being lay drawn

[028-006] up, or stretched out, or writhing from one position to another. There were

[028-007] tired men in rumpled shirts, their necks bare and their suspenders down; old

[028-008] women with their heads tied up in black handkerchiefs; bedraggled young women

[028-009] who went to sleep while they were nursing their babies and forgot to button up

[028-010] their dresses; dirty boys who added to the general discomfort by taking off

[028-011] their boots. The brakeman, when he came through at midnight, sniffed the heavy

[028-012] air disdainfully and looked up at the ventilators. As he glanced down the

[028-013] double rows of contorted figures, he saw one pair of eyes that were wide open

[028-014] and bright, a yellow head that was not overcome by the stupefying heat and

[028-015] smell in the car. “There’s a girl for you,” he thought as he

[028-016] stopped by Thea’s chair.

[028-017] “Like to have the window up a little?” he asked.

[028-018] Thea smiled up at him, not misunderstanding his friendliness. “The girl

[028-019] behind me is sick; she can’t stand a draft. What time is it,

[028-020] please?”

[028-021] He took out his open-faced watch and held it before her eyes with a knowing

[028-022] look. “In a hurry?” he asked. “I’ll leave the end door

[028-023] open and air you out. Catch a wink; the time’ll go faster.”

[028-024] Thea nodded good-night to him and settled her head back on her pillow, looking

[028-025] up at the oil lamps. She was going back to Moonstone for her summer vacation,

[028-026] and she was sitting up all night in a day-coach because that seemed such an

[028-027] easy way to save money. At her age discomfort was a small matter, when one made

[028-028] five dollars a day by it. She had confidently expected to sleep after the car

[028-029] got quiet, but in the two chairs behind her were a sick girl and her mother,

[028-030] and the girl had been coughing steadily since ten o’clock. They had come

[028-031] from somewhere in Pennsylvania, and this was their second night on the road.

[028-032] The mother said they were going to Colorado “for her daughter’s

[028-033] lungs.” The daughter was a little older than Thea, perhaps nineteen, with

[028-034] patient dark eyes and curly brown hair. She was pretty in spite of being so

[028-035] sooty and travel-stained. She had put on an ugly figured satine kimono over her

[028-036] loosened clothes. Thea, when she boarded the train in [[chicago|Chicago]], happened to stop

[028-037] and plant her heavy telescope on this seat. She had not intended to remain

[028-038] there, but the sick girl had looked up at her with an eager smile and said,

[028-039] “Do sit there, miss. I’d so much rather not have a gentleman in

[028-040] front of me.”

[028-041] After the girl began to cough there were no empty seats left, and if there had

[028-042] been Thea could scarcely have changed without hurting her feelings. The mother

[028-043] turned on her side and went to sleep; she was used to the cough. But the girl

[028-044] lay wide awake, her eyes fixed on the roof of the car, as Thea’s were.

[028-045] The two girls must have seen very different things there.

[028-046] Thea fell to going over her winter in Chicago. It was only under unusual or

[028-047] uncomfortable conditions like these that she could keep her mind fixed upon

[028-048] herself or her own affairs for any length of time. The rapid motion and the

[028-049] vibration of the wheels under her seemed to give her thoughts rapidity and

[028-050] clearness. She had taken twenty very expensive lessons from [[madison-bowers|Madison Bowers]], but

[028-051] she did not yet know what he thought of her or of her ability. He was different

[028-052] from any man with whom she had ever had to do. With her other teachers she had

[028-053] felt a personal relation; but with him she did not. Bowers was a cold, bitter,

[028-054] avaricious man, but he knew a great deal about voices. He worked with a voice

[028-055] as if he were in a laboratory, conducting a series of experiments. He was

[028-056] conscientious and industrious, even capable of a certain cold fury when he was

[028-057] working with an interesting voice, but Harsanyi declared that he had the soul

[028-058] of a shrimp, and could no more make an artist than a throat specialist could.

[028-059] Thea realized that he had taught her a great deal in twenty lessons.

[028-060] Although she cared so much less for Bowers than for Harsanyi, Thea was, on the

[028-061] whole, happier since she had been studying with him than she had been before.

[028-062] She had always told herself that she studied piano to fit herself to be a music

[028-063] teacher. But she never asked herself why she was studying voice. Her voice,

[028-064] more than any other part of her, had to do with that confidence, that sense of

[028-065] wholeness and inner well-being that she had felt at moments ever since she

[028-066] could remember.

[028-067] Of this feeling Thea had never spoken to any human being until that day when

[028-068] she told Harsanyi that “there had always been—something.”

[028-069] Hitherto she had felt but one obligation toward it—secrecy; to protect it

[028-070] even from herself. She had always believed that by doing all that was required

[028-071] of her by her family, her teachers, her pupils, she kept that part of herself

[028-072] from being caught up in the meshes of common things. She took it for granted

[028-073] that some day, when she was older, she would know a great deal more about it.

[028-074] It was as if she had an appointment to meet the rest of herself sometime,

[028-075] somewhere. It was moving to meet her and she was moving to meet it. That

[028-076] meeting awaited her, just as surely as, for the poor girl in the seat behind

[028-077] her, there awaited a hole in the earth, already dug.

[028-078] For Thea, so much had begun with a hole in the earth. Yes, she reflected, this

[028-079] new part of her life had all begun that morning when she sat on the clay bank

[028-080] beside [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]], under the flickering shade of the cottonwood tree. She

[028-081] remembered the way Ray had looked at her that morning. Why had he cared so

[028-082] much? And Wunsch, and [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]], and [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]], why had they? It was

[028-083] something that had to do with her that made them care, but it was not she. It

[028-084] was something they believed in, but it was not she. Perhaps each of them

[028-085] concealed another person in himself, just as she did. Why was it that they

[028-086] seemed to feel and to hunt for a second person in her and not in each other?

[028-087] Thea frowned up at the dull lamp in the roof of the car. What if one’s

[028-088] second self could somehow speak to all these second selves? What if one could

[028-089] bring them out, as whiskey did Spanish Johnny’s? How deep they lay, these

[028-090] second persons, and how little one knew about them, except to guard them

[028-091] fiercely. It was to music, more than to anything else, that these hidden things

[028-092] in people responded. Her mother—even her mother had something of that

[028-093] sort which replied to music.

[028-094] Thea found herself listening for the coughing behind her and not hearing it.

[028-095] She turned cautiously and looked back over the head-rest of her chair. The poor

[028-096] girl had fallen asleep. Thea looked at her intently. Why was she so afraid of

[028-097] men? Why did she shrink into herself and avert her face whenever a man passed

[028-098] her chair? Thea thought she knew; of course, she knew. How horrible to waste

[028-099] away like that, in the time when one ought to be growing fuller and stronger

[028-100] and rounder every day. Suppose there were such a dark hole open for her,

[028-101] between to-night and that place where she was to meet herself? Her eyes

[028-102] narrowed. She put her hand on her breast and felt how warm it was; and within

[028-103] it there was a full, powerful pulsation. She smiled—though she was

[028-104] ashamed of it—with the natural contempt of strength for weakness, with

[028-105] the sense of physical security which makes the savage merciless. Nobody could

[028-106] die while they felt like that inside. The springs there were wound so tight

[028-107] that it would be a long while before there was any slack in them. The life in

[028-108] there was rooted deep. She was going to have a few things before she died. She

[028-109] realized that there were a great many trains dashing east and west on the face

[028-110] of the continent that night, and that they all carried young people who meant

[028-111] to have things. But the difference was that *she was going to get them!*

[028-112] That was all. Let people try to stop her! She glowered at the rows of feckless

[028-113] bodies that lay sprawled in the chairs. Let them try it once! Along with the

[028-114] yearning that came from some deep part of her, that was selfless and exalted,

[028-115] Thea had a hard kind of cockiness, a determination to get ahead. Well, there

[028-116] are passages in life when that fierce, stubborn self-assertion will stand its

[028-117] ground after the nobler feeling is overwhelmed and beaten under.

[028-118] Having told herself once more that she meant to grab a few things, Thea went to

[028-119] sleep.

[028-120] She was wakened in the morning by the sunlight, which beat fiercely through the

[028-121] glass of the car window upon her face. She made herself as clean as she could,

[028-122] and while the people all about her were getting cold food out of their

[028-123] lunch-baskets she escaped into the dining-car. Her thrift did not go to the

[028-124] point of enabling her to carry a lunchbasket. At that early hour there were few

[028-125] people in the dining-car. The linen was white and fresh, the darkies were trim

[028-126] and smiling, and the sunlight gleamed pleasantly upon the silver and the glass

[028-127] water-bottles. On each table there was a slender vase with a single pink rose

[028-128] in it. When Thea sat down she looked into her rose and thought it the most

[028-129] beautiful thing in the world; it was wide open, recklessly offering its yellow

[028-130] heart, and there were drops of water on the petals. All the future was in that

[028-131] rose, all that one would like to be. The flower put her in an absolutely regal

[028-132] mood. She had a whole pot of coffee, and scrambled eggs with chopped ham,

[028-133] utterly disregarding the astonishing price they cost. She had faith enough in

[028-134] what she could do, she told herself, to have eggs if she wanted them. At the

[028-135] table opposite her sat a man and his wife and little boy—Thea classified

[028-136] them as being “from the East.” They spoke in that quick, sure

[028-137] staccato, which Thea, like Ray Kennedy, pretended to scorn and secretly

[028-138] admired. People who could use words in that confident way, and who spoke them

[028-139] elegantly, had a great advantage in life, she reflected. There were so many

[028-140] words which she could not pronounce in speech as she had to do in singing.

[028-141] Language was like clothes; it could be a help to one, or it could give one

[028-142] away. But the most important thing was that one should not pretend to be what

[028-143] one was not.

[028-144] When she paid her check she consulted the waiter. “Waiter, do you suppose

[028-145] I could buy one of those roses? I’m out of the day-coach, and there is a

[028-146] sick girl in there. I’d like to take her a cup of coffee and one of those

[028-147] flowers.”

[028-148] The waiter liked nothing better than advising travelers less sophisticated than

[028-149] himself. He told Thea there were a few roses left in the icebox and he would

[028-150] get one. He took the flower and the coffee into the day-coach. Thea pointed out

[028-151] the girl, but she did not accompany him. She hated thanks and never received

[028-152] them gracefully. She stood outside on the platform to get some fresh air into

[028-153] her lungs. The train was crossing the Platte River now, and the sunlight was so

[028-154] intense that it seemed to quiver in little flames on the glittering sandbars,

[028-155] the scrub willows, and the curling, fretted shallows.

[028-156] Thea felt that she was coming back to her own land. She had often heard Mrs.

[028-157] Kronborg say that she “believed in immigration,” and so did Thea

[028-158] believe in it. This earth seemed to her young and fresh and kindly, a place

[028-159] where refugees from old, sad countries were given another chance. The mere

[028-160] absence of rocks gave the soil a kind of amiability and generosity, and the

[028-161] absence of natural boundaries gave the spirit a wider range. Wire fences might

[028-162] mark the end of a man’s pasture, but they could not shut in his thoughts

[028-163] as mountains and forests can. It was over flat lands like this, stretching out

[028-164] to drink the sun, that the larks sang—and one’s heart sang there,

[028-165] too. Thea was glad that this was her country, even if one did not learn to

[028-166] speak elegantly there. It was, somehow, an honest country, and there was a new

[028-167] song in that blue air which had never been sung in the world before. It was

[028-168] hard to tell about it, for it had nothing to do with words; it was like the

[028-169] light of the desert at noon, or the smell of the sagebrush after rain;

[028-170] intangible but powerful. She had the sense of going back to a friendly soil,

[028-171] whose friendship was somehow going to strengthen her; a naive, generous country

[028-172] that gave one its joyous force, its large-hearted, childlike power to love,

[028-173] just as it gave one its coarse, brilliant flowers.

[028-174] As she drew in that glorious air Thea’s mind went back to Ray Kennedy.

[028-175] He, too, had that feeling of empire; as if all the Southwest really belonged to

[028-176] him because he had knocked about over it so much, and knew it, as he said,

[028-177] “like the blisters on his own hands.” That feeling, she reflected,

[028-178] was the real element of companionship between her and Ray. Now that she was

[028-179] going back to Colorado, she realized this as she had not done before.
