## Overview

Grace Johnson is a minor figure in the Moonstone social landscape — the daughter of the socially prominent Mrs. Livery Johnson and a contemporary of Thea Kronborg.

Her few appearances capture a recognizable dynamic of small-town life: the mixture of envy and resentment that accompanies a peer's departure for wider opportunities. Grace Johnson represents the Moonstone youth who stay behind, whose horizons remain bounded by the town Thea leaves.

## Biography / Character Arc

### The Johnson Family

Grace Johnson is the daughter of Mrs. Livery Johnson — "her husband kept the livery stable" [008-100] and the family name served "to distinguish her from other families" [008-101] of the same name in Moonstone.

Mrs. Johnson is "a prominent Baptist" [008-102] and a figure of considerable social influence in the town, a rival to Mrs. Kronborg whose competitive instinct emerges vividly at a church social. She asks "whether her rival was to be allowed to sing" [008-105] with "an eagerness which told how she had waited for this moment" [008-106].

Grace inherits this social position, moving within Moonstone's limited circles as a girl of good family whose mother believes she has "taken lessons of the best" [015-024] music teacher available.

### The Music Lesson Controversy

Grace Johnson makes an earlier indirect appearance during a dispute over music lessons. When Mrs. Livery Johnson feels that her daughter has been slighted, she "demanded an apology to Grace" [015-023].

The demand reveals the social stakes attached to musical education in Moonstone. Mrs. Johnson's insistence that Grace has studied with the best available teacher is both a claim of status and an implicit challenge to the Kronborgs' musical pretensions.

Grace herself remains silent in this episode — her mother speaks for her — but the demand for an apology in her name establishes her as a figure around whom social competition crystallizes.

### Thea's Departure

Grace Johnson's most telling appearance comes at a moment of imaginative projection. As Thea prepares to leave Moonstone for Chicago, she reflects on the reactions her departure will provoke.

"She wondered whether Grace Johnson would be furious when she" [019-094] learned of Thea's going. The sentence is incomplete in the available record, but its import is clear: Thea anticipates that her departure will stir resentment in those left behind.

This flicker of anticipated hostility is revealing. Grace Johnson, by virtue of her family's social standing, might have expected to be Moonstone's representative to the wider world — the girl whose mother's prominence entitled her to larger prospects.

That it is Thea Kronborg, the daughter of the Methodist minister, who is departing for Chicago represents a disruption of the town's implicit social order. Grace Johnson's imagined fury is the response of someone whose unearned expectations have been overturned.

The earlier demand for an "apology to Grace" [015-023] now reads as prelude: the Johnsons have always felt that Thea's musical ambitions encroached on territory that rightfully belonged to them.

## Relationships

### Mrs. Livery Johnson

Grace Johnson is defined largely through her mother, the formidable Mrs. Livery Johnson.

As the daughter of one of Moonstone's most socially active women — a "prominent Baptist" [008-102] known for her jangling bracelets [008-171] and her "duplicity" [009-043] — Grace inherits both privilege and limitation. The standing that comes from family prominence is paired with the confinement of being known primarily as someone's daughter.

When Mrs. Johnson "rustled away" [015-026] after demanding an apology for Grace, the verb captures the performance of maternal outrage that surrounds the girl, defining her social identity without letting her speak for herself.

### Thea Kronborg

The relationship between Grace Johnson and Thea Kronborg is not depicted directly but is instead imagined by Thea — a projection of rivalry that may say more about Thea's anxieties than about Grace's actual feelings.

Thea's wondering whether Grace "would be furious" [019-094] suggests a peer relationship marked by unspoken competition. This is the kind of tension that arises between young women of similar age but different prospects in a small community where everyone knows everyone else's business.

## Significance

Grace Johnson is one of the novel's most minor characters, but her brief appearance — or rather, Thea's brief thought of her — serves a narrative purpose.

She represents the community Thea is leaving: not the supportive members like Dr. Archie or Spanish Johnny, but those whose response to Thea's success is likely to be resentment rather than pride. In wondering whether Grace Johnson will be furious, Thea acknowledges that her departure is not universally celebrated.

The Johnson family more broadly — Mrs. Livery Johnson's gossip, her rivalry with Mrs. Kronborg, her Baptist propriety — represents a particular strand of Moonstone society: respectable, envious, small. Grace Johnson, as the daughter of this household, is positioned to inherit its attitudes. Her anticipated fury is the voice of a town that cannot quite forgive those who escape it.
