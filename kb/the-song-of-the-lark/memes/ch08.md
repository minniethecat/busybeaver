## VIII

[008-001] Winter was long in coming that year. Throughout October the days were bathed in

[008-002] sunlight and the air was clear as crystal. The town kept its cheerful summer

[008-003] aspect, the desert glistened with light, the sand hills every day went through

[008-004] magical changes of color. The scarlet sage bloomed late in the front yards, the

[008-005] cottonwood leaves were bright gold long before they fell, and it was not until

[008-006] November that the green on the tamarisks began to cloud and fade. There was a

[008-007] flurry of snow about Thanksgiving, and then December came on warm and clear.

[008-008] Thea had three music pupils now, little girls whose mothers declared that

[008-009] [[professor-wunsch|Professor Wunsch]] was “much too severe.” They took their lessons on

[008-010] Saturday, and this, of course, cut down her time for play. She did not really

[008-011] mind this because she was allowed to use the money—her pupils paid her

[008-012] twenty-five cents a lesson—to fit up a little room for herself upstairs

[008-013] in the half-story. It was the end room of the wing, and was not plastered, but

[008-014] was snugly lined with soft pine. The ceiling was so low that a grown person

[008-015] could reach it with the palm of the hand, and it sloped down on either side.

[008-016] There was only one window, but it was a double one and went to the floor. In

[008-017] October, while the days were still warm, Thea and Tillie papered the room,

[008-018] walls and ceiling in the same paper, small red and brown roses on a yellowish

[008-019] ground. Thea bought a brown cotton carpet, and her big brother, Gus, put it

[008-020] down for her one Sunday. She made white cheesecloth curtains and hung them on a

[008-021] tape. Her mother gave her an old walnut dresser with a broken mirror, and she

[008-022] had her own dumpy walnut single bed, and a blue washbowl and pitcher which she

[008-023] had drawn at a church fair lottery. At the head of her bed she had a tall round

[008-024] wooden hat-crate, from the clothing store. This, standing on end and draped

[008-025] with cretonne, made a fairly steady table for her lantern. She was not allowed

[008-026] to take a lamp upstairs, so [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] gave her a railroad lantern by which

[008-027] she could read at night.

[008-028] In winter this loft room of Thea’s was bitterly cold, but against her

[008-029] mother’s advice—and Tillie’s—she always left her window

[008-030] open a little way. [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] declared that she “had no patience with

[008-031] American physiology,” though the lessons about the injurious effects of

[008-032] alcohol and tobacco were well enough for the boys. Thea asked [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] about

[008-033] the window, and he told her that a girl who sang must always have plenty of

[008-034] fresh air, or her voice would get husky, and that the cold would harden her

[008-035] throat. The important thing, he said, was to keep your feet warm. On very cold

[008-036] nights Thea always put a brick in the oven after supper, and when she went

[008-037] upstairs she wrapped it in an old flannel petticoat and put it in her bed. The

[008-038] boys, who would never heat bricks for themselves, sometimes carried off

[008-039] Thea’s, and thought it a good joke to get ahead of her.

[008-040] When Thea first plunged in between her red blankets, the cold sometimes kept

[008-041] her awake for a good while, and she comforted herself by remembering all she

[008-042] could of “Polar Explorations,” a fat, calf-bound volume her father

[008-043] had bought from a book-agent, and by thinking about the members of

[008-044] Greely’s party: how they lay in their frozen sleeping-bags, each man

[008-045] hoarding the warmth of his own body and trying to make it last as long as

[008-046] possible against the on-coming cold that would be everlasting. After half an

[008-047] hour or so, a warm wave crept over her body and round, sturdy legs; she glowed

[008-048] like a little stove with the warmth of her own blood, and the heavy quilts and

[008-049] red blankets grew warm wherever they touched her, though her breath sometimes

[008-050] froze on the coverlid. Before daylight, her internal fires went down a little,

[008-051] and she often wakened to find herself drawn up into a tight ball, somewhat

[008-052] stiff in the legs. But that made it all the easier to get up.

[008-053] The acquisition of this room was the beginning of a new era in Thea’s

[008-054] life. It was one of the most important things that ever happened to her.

[008-055] Hitherto, except in summer, when she could be out of doors, she had lived in

[008-056] constant turmoil; the family, the day school, the Sunday-School. The clamor

[008-057] about her drowned the voice within herself. In the end of the wing, separated

[008-058] from the other upstairs sleeping-rooms by a long, cold, unfinished lumber room,

[008-059] her mind worked better. She thought things out more clearly. Pleasant plans and

[008-060] ideas occurred to her which had never come before. She had certain thoughts

[008-061] which were like companions, ideas which were like older and wiser friends. She

[008-062] left them there in the morning, when she finished dressing in the cold, and at

[008-063] night, when she came up with her lantern and shut the door after a busy day,

[008-064] she found them awaiting her. There was no possible way of heating the room, but

[008-065] that was fortunate, for otherwise it would have been occupied by one of her

[008-066] older brothers.

[008-067] From the time when she moved up into the wing, Thea began to live a double

[008-068] life. During the day, when the hours were full of tasks, she was one of the

[008-069] Kronborg children, but at night she was a different person. On Friday and

[008-070] Saturday nights she always read for a long while after she was in bed. She had

[008-071] no clock, and there was no one to nag her.

[008-072] Ray Kennedy, on his way from the depot to his boardinghouse, often looked up

[008-073] and saw Thea’s light burning when the rest of the house was dark, and

[008-074] felt cheered as by a friendly greeting. He was a faithful soul, and many

[008-075] disappointments had not changed his nature. He was still, at heart, the same

[008-076] boy who, when he was sixteen, had settled down to freeze with his sheep in a

[008-077] Wyoming blizzard, and had been rescued only to play the losing game of fidelity

[008-078] to other charges.

[008-079] Ray had no very clear idea of what might be going on in Thea’s head, but

[008-080] he knew that something was. He used to remark to [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]], “That

[008-081] girl is developing something fine.” Thea was patient with Ray, even in

[008-082] regard to the liberties he took with her name. Outside the family, every one in

[008-083] Moonstone, except Wunsch and Dr. Archie, called her “Thee-a,” but

[008-084] this seemed cold and distant to Ray, so he called her “Thee.” Once,

[008-085] in a moment of exasperation, Thea asked him why he did this, and he explained

[008-086] that he once had a chum, Theodore, whose name was always abbreviated thus, and

[008-087] that since he was killed down on the Santa Fé, it seemed natural to call

[008-088] somebody “Thee.” Thea sighed and submitted. She was always helpless

[008-089] before homely sentiment and usually changed the subject.

[008-090] It was the custom for each of the different Sunday Schools in Moonstone to give

[008-091] a concert on Christmas Eve. But this year all the churches were to unite and

[008-092] give, as was announced from the pulpits, “a semi-sacred concert of picked

[008-093] talent” at the opera house. [[moonstone-orchestra|The Moonstone Orchestra]], under the direction

[008-094] of Professor Wunsch, was to play, and the most talented members of each Sunday

[008-095] School were to take part in the programme. Thea was put down by the committee

[008-096] “for instrumental.” This made her indignant, for the vocal numbers

[008-097] were always more popular. Thea went to the president of the committee and

[008-098] demanded hotly if her rival, [[lily-fisher|Lily Fisher]], were going to sing. The president was

[008-099] a big, florid, powdered woman, a fierce W.C.T.U. worker, one of Thea’s

[008-100] natural enemies. Her name was Johnson; her husband kept the livery stable, and

[008-101] she was called Mrs. Livery Johnson, to distinguish her from other families of

[008-102] the same surname. Mrs. Johnson was a prominent Baptist, and Lily Fisher was the

[008-103] Baptist prodigy. There was a not very Christian rivalry between the Baptist

[008-104] Church and Mr. Kronborg’s church.

[008-105] When Thea asked Mrs. Johnson whether her rival was to be allowed to sing, Mrs.

[008-106] Johnson, with an eagerness which told how she had waited for this moment,

[008-107] replied that “Lily was going to recite to be obliging, and to give other

[008-108] children a chance to sing.” As she delivered this thrust, her eyes

[008-109] glittered more than the Ancient Mariner’s, Thea thought. Mrs. Johnson

[008-110] disapproved of the way in which Thea was being brought up, of a child whose

[008-111] chosen associates were Mexicans and sinners, and who was, as she pointedly put

[008-112] it, “bold with men.” She so enjoyed an opportunity to rebuke Thea,

[008-113] that, tightly corseted as she was, she could scarcely control her breathing,

[008-114] and her lace and her gold watch chain rose and fell “with short, uneasy

[008-115] motion.” Frowning, Thea turned away and walked slowly homeward. She

[008-116] suspected guile. Lily Fisher was the most stuck-up doll in the world, and it

[008-117] was certainly not like her to recite to be obliging. Nobody who could sing ever

[008-118] recited, because the warmest applause always went to the singers.

[008-119] However, when the programme was printed in the Moonstone *Gleam*, there it

[008-120] was: “Instrumental solo, [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]]. Recitation, Lily Fisher.”

[008-121] Because his orchestra was to play for the concert, Mr. Wunsch imagined that he

[008-122] had been put in charge of the music, and he became arrogant. He insisted that

[008-123] Thea should play a “Ballade” by Reinecke. When Thea consulted her

[008-124] mother, Mrs. Kronborg agreed with her that the “Ballade” would

[008-125] “never take” with a Moonstone audience. She advised Thea to play

[008-126] “something with variations,” or, at least, “The Invitation to

[008-127] the Dance.”

[008-128] “It makes no matter what they like,” Wunsch replied to Thea’s

[008-129] entreaties. “It is time already that they learn something.”

[008-130] Thea’s fighting powers had been impaired by an ulcerated tooth and

[008-131] consequent loss of sleep, so she gave in. She finally had the molar pulled,

[008-132] though it was a second tooth and should have been saved. The dentist was a

[008-133] clumsy, ignorant country boy, and Mr. Kronborg would not hear of Dr.

[008-134] Archie’s taking Thea to a dentist in Denver, though Ray Kennedy said he

[008-135] could get a pass for her. What with the pain of the tooth, and family

[008-136] discussions about it, with trying to make Christmas presents and to keep up her

[008-137] school work and practicing, and giving lessons on Saturdays, Thea was fairly

[008-138] worn out.

[008-139] On Christmas Eve she was nervous and excited. It was the first time she had

[008-140] ever played in the opera house, and she had never before had to face so many

[008-141] people. Wunsch would not let her play with her notes, and she was afraid of

[008-142] forgetting. Before the concert began, all the participants had to assemble on

[008-143] the stage and sit there to be looked at. Thea wore her white summer dress and a

[008-144] blue sash, but Lily Fisher had a new pink silk, trimmed with white swansdown.

[008-145] The hall was packed. It seemed as if every one in Moonstone was there, even

[008-146] Mrs. Kohler, in her hood, and old Fritz. The seats were wooden kitchen chairs,

[008-147] numbered, and nailed to long planks which held them together in rows. As the

[008-148] floor was not raised, the chairs were all on the same level. The more

[008-149] interested persons in the audience peered over the heads of the people in front

[008-150] of them to get a good view of the stage. From the platform Thea picked out many

[008-151] friendly faces. There was Dr. Archie, who never went to church entertainments;

[008-152] there was the friendly jeweler who ordered her music for her,—he sold

[008-153] accordions and guitars as well as watches,—and the druggist who often

[008-154] lent her books, and her favorite teacher from the school. There was Ray

[008-155] Kennedy, with a party of freshly barbered railroad men he had brought along

[008-156] with him. There was Mrs. Kronborg with all the children, even Thor, who had

[008-157] been brought out in a new white plush coat. At the back of the hall sat a

[008-158] little group of Mexicans, and among them Thea caught the gleam of Spanish

[008-159] Johnny’s white teeth, and of Mrs. Tellamantez’s lustrous, smoothly

[008-160] coiled black hair.

[008-161] After the orchestra played “Selections from Erminie,” and the

[008-162] Baptist preacher made a long prayer, Tillie Kronborg came on with a highly

[008-163] colored recitation, “The Polish Boy.” When it was over every one

[008-164] breathed more freely. No committee had the courage to leave Tillie off a

[008-165] programme. She was accepted as a trying feature of every entertainment. The

[008-166] Progressive Euchre Club was the only social organization in the town that

[008-167] entirely escaped Tillie. After Tillie sat down, the Ladies’ Quartette

[008-168] sang, “Beloved, it is Night,” and then it was Thea’s turn.

[008-169] The “Ballade” took ten minutes, which was five minutes too long.

[008-170] The audience grew restive and fell to whispering. Thea could hear Mrs. Livery

[008-171] Johnson’s bracelets jangling as she fanned herself, and she could hear

[008-172] her father’s nervous, ministerial cough. Thor behaved better than any one

[008-173] else. When Thea bowed and returned to her seat at the back of the stage there

[008-174] was the usual applause, but it was vigorous only from the back of the house

[008-175] where the Mexicans sat, and from Ray Kennedy’s *claqueurs*. Any one

[008-176] could see that a good-natured audience had been bored.

[008-177] Because Mr. Kronborg’s sister was on the programme, it had also been

[008-178] necessary to ask the Baptist preacher’s wife’s cousin to sing. She

[008-179] was a “deep alto” from McCook, and she sang, “Thy Sentinel Am

[008-180] I.” After her came Lily Fisher. Thea’s rival was also a blonde, but

[008-181] her hair was much heavier than Thea’s, and fell in long round curls over

[008-182] her shoulders. She was the angel-child of the Baptists, and looked exactly like

[008-183] the beautiful children on soap calendars. Her pink-and-white face, her set

[008-184] smile of innocence, were surely born of a color-press. She had long, drooping

[008-185] eyelashes, a little pursed-up mouth, and narrow, pointed teeth, like a

[008-186] squirrel’s.

[008-187] Lily began:—

[008-188] “*Rock of Ages, cleft for me*, carelessly the maiden sang.”

[008-189] Thea drew a long breath. That was the game; it was a recitation and a song in

[008-190] one. Lily trailed the hymn through half a dozen verses with great effect. The

[008-191] Baptist preacher had announced at the beginning of the concert that

[008-192] “owing to the length of the programme, there would be no encores.”

[008-193] But the applause which followed Lily to her seat was such an unmistakable

[008-194] expression of enthusiasm that Thea had to admit Lily was justified in going

[008-195] back. She was attended this time by Mrs. Livery Johnson herself, crimson with

[008-196] triumph and gleaming-eyed, nervously rolling and unrolling a sheet of music.

[008-197] She took off her bracelets and played Lily’s accompaniment. Lily had the

[008-198] effrontery to come out with, “She sang the song of Home, Sweet Home, the

[008-199] song that touched my heart.” But this did not surprise Thea; as Ray said

[008-200] later in the evening, “the cards had been stacked against her from the

[008-201] beginning.” The next issue of the *Gleam* correctly stated that

[008-202] “unquestionably the honors of the evening must be accorded to Miss Lily

[008-203] Fisher.” The Baptists had everything their own way.

[008-204] After the concert Ray Kennedy joined the Kronborgs’ party and walked home

[008-205] with them. Thea was grateful for his silent sympathy, even while it irritated

[008-206] her. She inwardly vowed that she would never take another lesson from old

[008-207] Wunsch. She wished that her father would not keep cheerfully singing,

[008-208] “When Shepherds Watched,” as he marched ahead, carrying Thor. She

[008-209] felt that silence would become the Kronborgs for a while. As a family, they

[008-210] somehow seemed a little ridiculous, trooping along in the starlight. There were

[008-211] so many of them, for one thing. Then Tillie was so absurd. She was giggling and

[008-212] talking to Anna just as if she had not made, as even Mrs. Kronborg admitted, an

[008-213] exhibition of herself.

[008-214] When they got home, Ray took a box from his overcoat pocket and slipped it into

[008-215] Thea’s hand as he said goodnight. They all hurried in to the glowing

[008-216] stove in the parlor. The sleepy children were sent to bed. Mrs. Kronborg and

[008-217] Anna stayed up to fill the stockings.

[008-218] “I guess you’re tired, Thea. You needn’t stay up.” Mrs.

[008-219] Kronborg’s clear and seemingly indifferent eye usually measured Thea

[008-220] pretty accurately.

[008-221] Thea hesitated. She glanced at the presents laid out on the dining-room table,

[008-222] but they looked unattractive. Even the brown plush monkey she had bought for

[008-223] Thor with such enthusiasm seemed to have lost his wise and humorous expression.

[008-224] She murmured, “All right,” to her mother, lit her lantern, and went

[008-225] upstairs.

[008-226] Ray’s box contained a hand-painted white satin fan, with pond

[008-227] lilies—an unfortunate reminder. Thea smiled grimly and tossed it into her

[008-228] upper drawer. She was not to be consoled by toys. She undressed quickly and

[008-229] stood for some time in the cold, frowning in the broken looking glass at her

[008-230] flaxen pig-tails, at her white neck and arms. Her own broad, resolute face set

[008-231] its chin at her, her eyes flashed into her own defiantly. Lily Fisher was

[008-232] pretty, and she was willing to be just as big a fool as people wanted her to

[008-233] be. Very well; Thea Kronborg wasn’t. She would rather be hated than be

[008-234] stupid, any day. She popped into bed and read stubbornly at a queer paper book

[008-235] the drug-store man had given her because he couldn’t sell it. She had

[008-236] trained herself to put her mind on what she was doing, otherwise she would have

[008-237] come to grief with her complicated daily schedule. She read, as intently as if

[008-238] she had not been flushed with anger, the strange “Musical Memories”

[008-239] of the Reverend H. R. Haweis. At last she blew out the lantern and went to

[008-240] sleep. She had many curious dreams that night. In one of them Mrs. Tellamantez

[008-241] held her shell to Thea’s ear, and she heard the roaring, as before, and

[008-242] distant voices calling, “Lily Fisher! Lily Fisher!”
