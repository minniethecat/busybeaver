## XI

[011-001] One Saturday, late in June, Thea arrived early for her lesson. As she perched

[011-002] herself upon the piano stool,—a wobbly, old-fashioned thing that worked

[011-003] on a creaky screw,—she gave Wunsch a side glance, smiling. “You

[011-004] must not be cross to me to-day. This is my birthday.”

[011-005] “So?” he pointed to the keyboard.

[011-006] After the lesson they went out to join Mrs. Kohler, who had asked Thea to come

[011-007] early, so that she could stay and smell the linden bloom. It was one of those

[011-008] still days of intense light, when every particle of mica in the soil flashed

[011-009] like a little mirror, and the glare from the plain below seemed more intense

[011-010] than the rays from above. The sand ridges ran glittering gold out to where the

[011-011] mirage licked them up, shining and steaming like a lake in the tropics. The sky

[011-012] looked like blue lava, forever incapable of clouds,—a turquoise bowl that

[011-013] was the lid of the desert. And yet within Mrs. Kohler’s green patch the

[011-014] water dripped, the beds had all been hosed, and the air was fresh with rapidly

[011-015] evaporating moisture.

[011-016] The two symmetrical linden trees were the proudest things in the garden. Their

[011-017] sweetness embalmed all the air. At every turn of the paths,—whether one

[011-018] went to see the hollyhocks or the bleeding heart, or to look at the purple

[011-019] morning-glories that ran over the bean-poles,—wherever one went, the

[011-020] sweetness of the lindens struck one afresh and one always came back to them.

[011-021] Under the round leaves, where the waxen yellow blossoms hung, bevies of wild

[011-022] bees were buzzing. The tamarisks were still pink, and the flower-beds were

[011-023] doing their best in honor of the linden festival. The white dove-house was

[011-024] shining with a fresh coat of paint, and the pigeons were crooning contentedly,

[011-025] flying down often to drink at the drip from the water tank. Mrs. Kohler, who

[011-026] was transplanting pansies, came up with her trowel and told Thea it was lucky

[011-027] to have your birthday when the lindens were in bloom, and that she must go and

[011-028] look at the sweet peas. Wunsch accompanied her, and as they walked between the

[011-029] flower-beds he took Thea’s hand.

[011-030] “Es flüstern und sprechen die Blumen,”—

[011-031] he muttered. “You know that von Heine? *Im leuchtenden

[011-032] Sommermorgen?*” He looked down at Thea and softly pressed her hand.

[011-033] “No, I don’t know it. What does *flüstern* mean?”

[011-034] “*Flüstern?*—to whisper. You must begin now to know such

[011-035] things. That is necessary. How many birthdays?”

[011-036] “Thirteen. I’m in my ’teens now. But how can I know words

[011-037] like that? I only know what you say at my lessons. They don’t teach

[011-038] German at school. How can I learn?”

[011-039] “It is always possible to learn when one likes,” said Wunsch. His

[011-040] words were peremptory, as usual, but his tone was mild, even confidential.

[011-041] “There is always a way. And if some day you are going to sing, it is

[011-042] necessary to know well the German language.”

[011-043] Thea stooped over to pick a leaf of rosemary. How did Wunsch know that, when

[011-044] the very roses on her wall-paper had never heard it? “But am I going

[011-045] to?” she asked, still stooping.

[011-046] “That is for you to say,” returned Wunsch coldly. “You would

[011-047] better marry some *Jacob* here and keep the house for him, may-be? That is

[011-048] as one desires.”

[011-049] Thea flashed up at him a clear, laughing look. “No, I don’t want to

[011-050] do that. You know,” she brushed his coat sleeve quickly with her yellow

[011-051] head. “Only how can I learn anything here? It’s so far from

[011-052] Denver.”

[011-053] Wunsch’s loose lower lip curled in amusement. Then, as if he suddenly

[011-054] remembered something, he spoke seriously. “Nothing is far and nothing is

[011-055] near, if one desires. The world is little, people are little, human life is

[011-056] little. There is only one big thing—desire. And before it, when it is

[011-057] big, all is little. It brought Columbus across the sea in a little boat, *und

[011-058] so weiter*.” Wunsch made a grimace, took his pupil’s hand and

[011-059] drew her toward the grape arbor. “Hereafter I will more speak to you in

[011-060] German. Now, sit down and I will teach you for your birthday that little song.

[011-061] Ask me the words you do not know already. Now: *Im leuchtenden

[011-062] Sommermorgen*.”

[011-063] Thea memorized quickly because she had the power of listening intently. In a

[011-064] few moments she could repeat the eight lines for him. Wunsch nodded

[011-065] encouragingly and they went out of the arbor into the sunlight again. As they

[011-066] went up and down the gravel paths between the flowerbeds, the white and yellow

[011-067] butterflies kept darting before them, and the pigeons were washing their pink

[011-068] feet at the drip and crooning in their husky bass. Over and over again Wunsch

[011-069] made her say the lines to him. “You see it is nothing. If you learn a

[011-070] great many of the *Lieder*, you will know the German language already.

[011-071] *Weiter, nun*.” He would incline his head gravely and listen.

[011-072] “Im leuchtenden Sommermorgen\

[011-073] Geh’ ich im Garten herum;\

[011-074] Es flüstern und sprechen die Blumen,\

[011-075] Ich aber, ich wandte stumm.\

[011-076] \

[011-077] “Es flüstern und sprechen die Blumen\

[011-078] Und schau’n mitleidig mich an:\

[011-079] ‘Sei unserer Schwester nicht böse,\

[011-080] Du trauriger, blasser Mann!’”

[011-081] (In the soft-shining summer morning\

[011-082] I wandered the garden within.\

[011-083] The flowers they whispered and murmured,\

[011-084] But I, I wandered dumb.\

[011-085] \

[011-086] The flowers they whisper and murmur,\

[011-087] And me with compassion they scan:\

[011-088] “Oh, be not harsh to our sister,\

[011-089] Thou sorrowful, death-pale man!”)

[011-090] Wunsch had noticed before that when his pupil read anything in verse the

[011-091] character of her voice changed altogether; it was no longer the voice which

[011-092] spoke the speech of Moonstone. It was a soft, rich contralto, and she read

[011-093] quietly; the feeling was in the voice itself, not indicated by emphasis or

[011-094] change of pitch. She repeated the little verses musically, like a song, and the

[011-095] entreaty of the flowers was even softer than the rest, as the shy speech of

[011-096] flowers might be, and she ended with the voice suspended, almost with a rising

[011-097] inflection. It was a nature-voice, Wunsch told himself, breathed from the

[011-098] creature and apart from language, like the sound of the wind in the trees, or

[011-099] the murmur of water.

[011-100] “What is it the flowers mean when they ask him not to be harsh to their

[011-101] sister, eh?” he asked, looking down at her curiously and wrinkling his

[011-102] dull red forehead.

[011-103] Thea glanced at him in surprise. “I suppose he thinks they are asking him

[011-104] not to be harsh to his sweetheart—or some girl they remind him of.”

[011-105] “And why *trauriger, blasser Mann?*”

[011-106] They had come back to the grape arbor, and Thea picked out a sunny place on the

[011-107] bench, where a tortoise-shell cat was stretched at full length. She sat down,

[011-108] bending over the cat and teasing his whiskers. “Because he had been awake

[011-109] all night, thinking about her, wasn’t it? Maybe that was why he was up so

[011-110] early.”

[011-111] Wunsch shrugged his shoulders. “If he think about her all night already,

[011-112] why do you say the flowers remind him?”

[011-113] Thea looked up at him in perplexity. A flash of comprehension lit her face and

[011-114] she smiled eagerly. “Oh, I didn’t mean ‘remind’ in that

[011-115] way! I didn’t mean they brought her to his mind! I meant it was only when

[011-116] he came out in the morning, that she seemed to him like that,—like one of

[011-117] the flowers.”

[011-118] “And before he came out, how did she seem?”

[011-119] This time it was Thea who shrugged her shoulders. The warm smile left her face.

[011-120] She lifted her eyebrows in annoyance and looked off at the sand hills.

[011-121] Wunsch persisted. “Why you not answer me?”

[011-122] “Because it would be silly. You are just trying to make me say things. It

[011-123] spoils things to ask questions.”

[011-124] Wunsch bowed mockingly; his smile was disagreeable. Suddenly his face grew

[011-125] grave, grew fierce, indeed. He pulled himself up from his clumsy stoop and

[011-126] folded his arms. “But it is necessary to know if you know some things.

[011-127] Some things cannot be taught. If you not know in the beginning, you not know in

[011-128] the end. For a singer there must be something in the inside from the beginning.

[011-129] I shall not be long in this place, may-be, and I like to know.

[011-130] Yes,”—he ground his heel in the gravel,—“yes, when you

[011-131] are barely six, you must know that already. That is the beginning of all

[011-132] things; *der Geist, die Phantasie*. It must be in the baby, when it makes

[011-133] its first cry, like *der Rhythmus*, or it is not to be. You have some

[011-134] voice already, and if in the beginning, when you are with things-to-play, you

[011-135] know that what you will not tell me, then you can learn to sing, may-be.”

[011-136] Wunsch began to pace the arbor, rubbing his hands together. The dark flush of

[011-137] his face had spread up under the iron-gray bristles on his head. He was talking

[011-138] to himself, not to Thea. Insidious power of the linden bloom! “Oh, much

[011-139] you can learn! *Aber nicht die Americanischen Fräulein*. They have nothing

[011-140] inside them,” striking his chest with both fists. “They are like

[011-141] the ones in the *Märchen*, a grinning face and hollow in the insides.

[011-142] Something they can learn, oh, yes, may-be! But the secret—what make the

[011-143] rose to red, the sky to blue, the man to love—*in der Brust, in der

[011-144] Brust* it is, *und ohne dieses giebt es keine Kunst, giebt es keine

[011-145] Kunst!*” He threw up his square hand and shook it, all the fingers

[011-146] apart and wagging. Purple and breathless he went out of the arbor and into the

[011-147] house, without saying good-bye. These outbursts frightened Wunsch. They were

[011-148] always harbingers of ill.

[011-149] Thea got her music-book and stole quietly out of the garden. She did not go

[011-150] home, but wandered off into the sand dunes, where the prickly pear was in

[011-151] blossom and the green lizards were racing each other in the glittering light.

[011-152] She was shaken by a passionate excitement. She did not altogether understand

[011-153] what Wunsch was talking about; and yet, in a way she knew. She knew, of course,

[011-154] that there was something about her that was different. But it was more like a

[011-155] friendly spirit than like anything that was a part of herself. She thought

[011-156] everything to it, and it answered her; happiness consisted of that backward and

[011-157] forward movement of herself. The something came and went, she never knew how.

[011-158] Sometimes she hunted for it and could not find it; again, she lifted her eyes

[011-159] from a book, or stepped out of doors, or wakened in the morning, and it was

[011-160] there,—under her cheek, it usually seemed to be, or over her

[011-161] breast,—a kind of warm sureness. And when it was there, everything was

[011-162] more interesting and beautiful, even people. When this companion was with her,

[011-163] she could get the most wonderful things out of [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]], or Wunsch, or

[011-164] [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]].

[011-165] On her thirteenth birthday she wandered for a long while about the sand ridges,

[011-166] picking up crystals and looking into the yellow prickly-pear blossoms with

[011-167] their thousand stamens. She looked at the sand hills until she wished she

[011-168] *were* a sand hill. And yet she knew that she was going to leave them all

[011-169] behind some day. They would be changing all day long, yellow and purple and

[011-170] lavender, and she would not be there. From that day on, she felt there was a

[011-171] secret between her and Wunsch. Together they had lifted a lid, pulled out a

[011-172] drawer, and looked at something. They hid it away and never spoke of what they

[011-173] had seen; but neither of them forgot it.
