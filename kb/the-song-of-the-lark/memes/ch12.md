## XII

[012-001] One July night, when the moon was full, [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] was coming up from the

[012-002] depot, restless and discontented, wishing there were something to do. He

[012-003] carried his straw hat in his hand, and kept brushing his hair back from his

[012-004] forehead with a purposeless, unsatisfied gesture. After he passed Uncle Billy

[012-005] Beemer’s cottonwood grove, the sidewalk ran out of the shadow into the

[012-006] white moonlight and crossed the sand gully on high posts, like a bridge. As the

[012-007] doctor approached this trestle, he saw a white figure, and recognized Thea

[012-008] Kronborg. He quickened his pace and she came to meet him.

[012-009] “What are you doing out so late, my girl?” he asked as he took her

[012-010] hand.

[012-011] “Oh, I don’t know. What do people go to bed so early for? I’d

[012-012] like to run along before the houses and screech at them. Isn’t it

[012-013] glorious out here?”

[012-014] The young doctor gave a melancholy laugh and pressed her hand.

[012-015] “Think of it,” Thea snorted impatiently. “Nobody up but us

[012-016] and the rabbits! I’ve started up half a dozen of ’em. Look at that

[012-017] little one down there now,”—she stooped and pointed. In the gully

[012-018] below them there was, indeed, a little rabbit with a white spot of a tail,

[012-019] crouching down on the sand, quite motionless. It seemed to be lapping up the

[012-020] moonlight like cream. On the other side of the walk, down in the ditch, there

[012-021] was a patch of tall, rank sunflowers, their shaggy leaves white with dust. The

[012-022] moon stood over the cottonwood grove. There was no wind, and no sound but the

[012-023] wheezing of an engine down on the tracks.

[012-024] “Well, we may as well watch the rabbits.” Dr. Archie sat down on

[012-025] the sidewalk and let his feet hang over the edge. He pulled out a smooth linen

[012-026] handkerchief that smelled of German cologne water. “Well, how goes it?

[012-027] Working hard? You must know about all Wunsch can teach you by this time.”

[012-028] Thea shook her head. “Oh, no, I don’t, Dr. Archie. He’s hard

[012-029] to get at, but he’s been a real musician in his time. Mother says she

[012-030] believes he’s forgotten more than the music-teachers down in Denver ever

[012-031] knew.”

[012-032] “I’m afraid he won’t be around here much longer,” said

[012-033] Dr. Archie. “He’s been making a tank of himself lately. He’ll

[012-034] be pulling his freight one of these days. That’s the way they do, you

[012-035] know. I’ll be sorry on your account.” He paused and ran his fresh

[012-036] handkerchief over his face. “What the deuce are we all here for anyway,

[012-037] Thea?” he said abruptly.

[012-038] “On earth, you mean?” Thea asked in a low voice.

[012-039] “Well, primarily, yes. But secondarily, why are we in Moonstone? It

[012-040] isn’t as if we’d been born here. You were, but Wunsch wasn’t,

[012-041] and I wasn’t. I suppose I’m here because I married as soon as I got

[012-042] out of medical school and had to get a practice quick. If you hurry things, you

[012-043] always get left in the end. I don’t learn anything here, and as for the

[012-044] people—In my own town in Michigan, now, there were people who liked me on

[012-045] my father’s account, who had even known my grandfather. That meant

[012-046] something. But here it’s all like the sand: blows north one day and south

[012-047] the next. We’re all a lot of gamblers without much nerve, playing for

[012-048] small stakes. The railroad is the one real fact in this country. That has to

[012-049] be; the world has to be got back and forth. But the rest of us are here just

[012-050] because it’s the end of a run and the engine has to have a drink. Some

[012-051] day I’ll get up and find my hair turning gray, and I’ll have

[012-052] nothing to show for it.”

[012-053] Thea slid closer to him and caught his arm. “No, no. I won’t let

[012-054] you get gray. You’ve got to stay young for me. I’m getting young

[012-055] now, too.”

[012-056] Archie laughed. “Getting?”

[012-057] “Yes. People aren’t young when they’re children. Look at

[012-058] Thor, now; he’s just a little old man. But Gus has a sweetheart, and

[012-059] he’s young!”

[012-060] “Something in that!” Dr. Archie patted her head, and then felt the

[012-061] shape of her skull gently, with the tips of his fingers. “When you were

[012-062] little, Thea, I used always to be curious about the shape of your head. You

[012-063] seemed to have more inside it than most youngsters. I haven’t examined it

[012-064] for a long time. Seems to be the usual shape, but uncommonly hard, some how.

[012-065] What are you going to do with yourself, anyway?”

[012-066] “I don’t know.”

[012-067] “Honest, now?” He lifted her chin and looked into her eyes.

[012-068] Thea laughed and edged away from him.

[012-069] “You’ve got something up your sleeve, haven’t you? Anything

[012-070] you like; only don’t marry and settle down here without giving yourself a

[012-071] chance, will you?”

[012-072] “Not much. See, there’s another rabbit!”

[012-073] “That’s all right about the rabbits, but I don’t want you to

[012-074] get tied up. Remember that.”

[012-075] Thea nodded. “Be nice to Wunsch, then. I don’t know what I’d

[012-076] do if he went away.”

[012-077] “You’ve got older friends than Wunsch here, Thea.”

[012-078] “I know.” Thea spoke seriously and looked up at the moon, propping

[012-079] her chin on her hand. “But Wunsch is the only one that can teach me what

[012-080] I want to know. I’ve got to learn to do something well, and that’s

[012-081] the thing I can do best.”

[012-082] “Do you want to be a music-teacher?”

[012-083] “Maybe, but I want to be a good one. I’d like to go to Germany to

[012-084] study, some day. Wunsch says that’s the best place,—the only place

[012-085] you can really learn.” Thea hesitated and then went on nervously,

[012-086] “I’ve got a book that says so, too. It’s called ‘My

[012-087] Musical Memories.’ It made me want to go to Germany even before Wunsch

[012-088] said anything. Of course it’s a secret. You’re the first one

[012-089] I’ve told.”

[012-090] Dr. Archie smiled indulgently. “That’s a long way off. Is that what

[012-091] you’ve got in your hard noddle?” He put his hand on her hair, but

[012-092] this time she shook him off.

[012-093] “No, I don’t think much about it. But you talk about going, and a

[012-094] body has to have something to go *to!*”

[012-095] “That’s so.” Dr. Archie sighed. “You’re lucky if

[012-096] you have. Poor Wunsch, now, he hasn’t. What do such fellows come out here

[012-097] for? He’s been asking me about my mining stock, and about mining towns.

[012-098] What would he do in a mining town? He wouldn’t know a piece of ore if he

[012-099] saw one. He’s got nothing to sell that a mining town wants to buy. Why

[012-100] don’t those old fellows stay at home? We won’t need them for

[012-101] another hundred years. An engine wiper can get a job, but a piano player! Such

[012-102] people can’t make good.”

[012-103] “My grandfather Alstrom was a musician, and he made good.”

[012-104] Dr. Archie chuckled. “Oh, a Swede can make good anywhere, at anything!

[012-105] You’ve got that in your favor, miss. Come, you must be getting

[012-106] home.”

[012-107] Thea rose. “Yes, I used to be ashamed of being a Swede, but I’m not

[012-108] any more. Swedes are kind of common, but I think it’s better to be

[012-109] *something*.”

[012-110] “It surely is! How tall you are getting. You come above my shoulder

[012-111] now.”

[012-112] “I’ll keep on growing, don’t you think? I particularly want

[012-113] to be tall. Yes, I guess I must go home. I wish there’d be a fire.”

[012-114] “A fire?”

[012-115] “Yes, so the fire-bell would ring and the roundhouse whistle would blow,

[012-116] and everybody would come running out. Sometime I’m going to ring the

[012-117] fire-bell myself and stir them all up.”

[012-118] “You’d be arrested.”

[012-119] “Well, that would be better than going to bed.”

[012-120] “I’ll have to lend you some more books.”

[012-121] Thea shook herself impatiently. “I can’t read every night.”

[012-122] Dr. Archie gave one of his low, sympathetic chuckles as he opened the gate for

[012-123] her. “You’re beginning to grow up, that’s what’s the

[012-124] matter with you. I’ll have to keep an eye on you. Now you’ll have

[012-125] to say good-night to the moon.”

[012-126] “No, I won’t. I sleep on the floor now, right in the moonlight. My

[012-127] window comes down to the floor, and I can look at the sky all night.”

[012-128] She shot round the house to the kitchen door, and Dr. Archie watched her

[012-129] disappear with a sigh. He thought of the hard, mean, frizzy little woman who

[012-130] kept his house for him; once the belle of a Michigan town, now dry and withered

[012-131] up at thirty. “If I had a daughter like Thea to watch,” he

[012-132] reflected, “I wouldn’t mind anything. I wonder if all of my

[012-133] life’s going to be a mistake just because I made a big one then? Hardly

[012-134] seems fair.”

[012-135] Howard Archie was “respected” rather than popular in Moonstone.

[012-136] Everyone recognized that he was a good physician, and a progressive Western

[012-137] town likes to be able to point to a handsome, well-set-up, well-dressed man

[012-138] among its citizens. But a great many people thought Archie

[012-139] “distant,” and they were right. He had the uneasy manner of a man

[012-140] who is not among his own kind, and who has not seen enough of the world to feel

[012-141] that all people are in some sense his own kind. He knew that every one was

[012-142] curious about his wife, that she played a sort of character part in Moonstone,

[012-143] and that people made fun of her, not very delicately. Her own

[012-144] friends—most of them women who were distasteful to Archie—liked to

[012-145] ask her to contribute to church charities, just to see how mean she could be.

[012-146] The little, lop-sided cake at the church supper, the cheapest pincushion, the

[012-147] skimpiest apron at the bazaar, were always Mrs. Archie’s contribution.

[012-148] All this hurt the doctor’s pride. But if there was one thing he had

[012-149] learned, it was that there was no changing Belle’s nature. He had married

[012-150] a mean woman; and he must accept the consequences. Even in Colorado he would

[012-151] have had no pretext for divorce, and, to do him justice, he had never thought

[012-152] of such a thing. The tenets of the Presbyterian Church in which he had grown

[012-153] up, though he had long ceased to believe in them, still influenced his conduct

[012-154] and his conception of propriety. To him there was something vulgar about

[012-155] divorce. A divorced man was a disgraced man; at least, he had exhibited his

[012-156] hurt, and made it a matter for common gossip. Respectability was so necessary

[012-157] to Archie that he was willing to pay a high price for it. As long as he could

[012-158] keep up a decent exterior, he could manage to get on; and if he could have

[012-159] concealed his wife’s littleness from all his friends, he would scarcely

[012-160] have complained. He was more afraid of pity than he was of any unhappiness. Had

[012-161] there been another woman for whom he cared greatly, he might have had plenty of

[012-162] courage; but he was not likely to meet such a woman in Moonstone.

[012-163] There was a puzzling timidity in Archie’s make-up. The thing that held

[012-164] his shoulders stiff, that made him resort to a mirthless little laugh when he

[012-165] was talking to dull people, that made him sometimes stumble over rugs and

[012-166] carpets, had its counterpart in his mind. He had not the courage to be an

[012-167] honest thinker. He could comfort himself by evasions and compromises. He

[012-168] consoled himself for his own marriage by telling himself that other

[012-169] people’s were not much better. In his work he saw pretty deeply into

[012-170] marital relations in Moonstone, and he could honestly say that there were not

[012-171] many of his friends whom he envied. Their wives seemed to suit them well

[012-172] enough, but they would never have suited him.

[012-173] Although Dr. Archie could not bring himself to regard marriage merely as a

[012-174] social contract, but looked upon it as somehow made sacred by a church in which

[012-175] he did not believe,—as a physician he knew that a young man whose

[012-176] marriage is merely nominal must yet go on living his life. When he went to

[012-177] Denver or to [[chicago|Chicago]], he drifted about in careless company where gayety and

[012-178] good-humor can be bought, not because he had any taste for such society, but

[012-179] because he honestly believed that anything was better than divorce. He often

[012-180] told himself that “hanging and wiving go by destiny.” If wiving

[012-181] went badly with a man,—and it did oftener than not,—then he must do

[012-182] the best he could to keep up appearances and help the tradition of domestic

[012-183] happiness along. The Moonstone gossips, assembled in Mrs. Smiley’s

[012-184] millinery and notion store, often discussed Dr. Archie’s politeness to

[012-185] his wife, and his pleasant manner of speaking about her. “Nobody has ever

[012-186] got a thing out of him yet,” they agreed. And it was certainly not

[012-187] because no one had ever tried.

[012-188] When he was down in Denver, feeling a little jolly, Archie could forget how

[012-189] unhappy he was at home, and could even make himself believe that he missed his

[012-190] wife. He always bought her presents, and would have liked to send her flowers

[012-191] if she had not repeatedly told him never to send her anything but

[012-192] bulbs,—which did not appeal to him in his expansive moments. At the

[012-193] Denver Athletic Club banquets, or at dinner with his colleagues at the Brown

[012-194] Palace Hotel, he sometimes spoke sentimentally about “little Mrs.

[012-195] Archie,” and he always drank the toast “to our wives, God bless

[012-196] them!” with gusto.

[012-197] The determining factor about Dr. Archie was that he was romantic. He had

[012-198] married Belle White because he was romantic—too romantic to know anything

[012-199] about women, except what he wished them to be, or to repulse a pretty girl who

[012-200] had set her cap for him. At medical school, though he was a rather wild boy in

[012-201] behavior, he had always disliked coarse jokes and vulgar stories. In his old

[012-202] Flint’s Physiology there was still a poem he had pasted there when he was

[012-203] a student; some verses by Dr. Oliver Wendell Holmes about the ideals of the

[012-204] medical profession. After so much and such disillusioning experience with it,

[012-205] he still had a romantic feeling about the human body; a sense that finer things

[012-206] dwelt in it than could be explained by anatomy. He never jested about birth or

[012-207] death or marriage, and did not like to hear other doctors do it. He was a good

[012-208] nurse, and had a reverence for the bodies of women and children. When he was

[012-209] tending them, one saw him at his best. Then his constraint and

[012-210] self-consciousness fell away from him. He was easy, gentle, competent, master

[012-211] of himself and of other people. Then the idealist in him was not afraid of

[012-212] being discovered and ridiculed.

[012-213] In his tastes, too, the doctor was romantic. Though he read Balzac all the year

[012-214] through, he still enjoyed the Waverley Novels as much as when he had first come

[012-215] upon them, in thick leather-bound volumes, in his grandfather’s library.

[012-216] He nearly always read Scott on Christmas and holidays, because it brought back

[012-217] the pleasures of his boyhood so vividly. He liked Scott’s women.

[012-218] Constance de Beverley and the minstrel girl in “The Fair Maid of

[012-219] Perth,” not the Duchesse de Langeais, were his heroines. But better than

[012-220] anything that ever got from the heart of a man into printer’s ink, he

[012-221] loved the poetry of Robert Burns. “Death and Dr. Hornbook” and

[012-222] “The Jolly Beggars,” Burns’s “Reply to his

[012-223] Tailor,” he often read aloud to himself in his office, late at night,

[012-224] after a glass of hot toddy. He used to read “Tam o’Shanter”

[012-225] to [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]], and he got her some of the songs, set to the old airs for

[012-226] which they were written. He loved to hear her sing them. Sometimes when she

[012-227] sang, “Oh, wert thou in the cauld blast,” the doctor and even Mr.

[012-228] Kronborg joined in. Thea never minded if people could not sing; she directed

[012-229] them with her head and somehow carried them along. When her father got off the

[012-230] pitch she let her own voice out and covered him.
