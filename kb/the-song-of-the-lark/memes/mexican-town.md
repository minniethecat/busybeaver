## Overview

Mexican Town is the Mexican settlement on the southern edge of Moonstone, Colorado, separated from the main town by a deep ravine. Though geographically marginal, it is culturally central to Thea Kronborg's childhood — a place of music, warmth, and community that stands in vivid contrast to the austere propriety of Moonstone's Scandinavian and Anglo society. The settlement is home to Spanish Johnny Tellamantez, his wife, and several other Mexican families whose presence enriches the town's cultural life.

## Location & Geography

Mexican Town lies south of Moonstone's center, beyond the deep ravine that winds through the landscape east of Main Street [005-024]. To reach it, one crosses sandy paths past the cottonwood grove and the railroad depot. The settlement is modest — adobe-style dwellings and, later, a dance-hall that resembles an ordinary house "except that it was a little longer" [030-057]. North of Pueblo, Colorado, Mexican settlements were rare at that time; this one came about accidentally when Spanish Johnny, a painter and decorator from Trinidad, followed Ray Kennedy's report of a "boom" in Moonstone [006-123].

## Role in the Novel

Mexican Town is introduced early in Part I as the source of the music that floats across the ravine to the Kohlers' garden, where Thea takes her piano lessons with Wunsch. Mrs. Kohler, who does not attend church, listens to "the songs that floated over from Mexican Town," and Thea often sings for her after lessons [004-198]. At night, "the sound of fiddles and guitars came across the ravine from Mexican Town" while Fritz and Wunsch smoke in the grape arbor [004-216].

The town is the setting for Thea's first exposure to a culture outside Scandinavian Moonstone. She runs away to Mexican Town as a child — a "queer freak" that Dr. Archie later recalls as evidence of her innate cosmopolitanism [049-121]. It is also where Thea attends the Mexican ball, a transformative sensory experience. However, after a painful personal disappointment, "Mexican Town, somehow, was spoiled for her just then" [031-162], marking a moment of lost innocence.

## Key Scenes

- **Music across the ravine** [004-198]: Thea sings for Mrs. Kohler after piano lessons; the sounds of Mexican fiddles and guitars drift across the ravine at night as Wunsch sits in the grape arbor, looking "up through the woolly vine leaves at the glittering machinery of heaven."

- **The walk to Mexican Town** [006-123]: Dr. Archie, carrying his straw hat, walks with Spanish Johnny across the sand toward Mexican Town. The narrative notes how the settlement came into being and describes the humbler citizens who live on the east side of Main Street, near the ravine that "farther south, wound by Mexican Town" [005-024].

- **The picnic departure** [007-030]: Thea rides on the front seat of the wagon with Ray Kennedy and Spanish Johnny as the party leaves Mexican Town for a desert picnic, with Gunner and Axel Kronborg in the back seat beside Mrs. Tellamantez.

- **The Mexican ball** [030-057]: Thea attends a dance at the new adobe hall, where "the scraping of violins being tuned, the tinkle of mandolins, and the growl of a double bass" fill the night air. The event is a sensory awakening for Thea [030-080].

- **Thea's return visit** [029-124]: After years in Chicago, Thea returns and thinks, "Now for Mexican Town and the Kohlers!" — planning to surprise the old woman with a hug.

- **Spoiled innocence** [031-162]: After a painful experience, "Mexican Town, somehow, was spoiled for her just then," and Thea walks aimlessly through the empty main street.
