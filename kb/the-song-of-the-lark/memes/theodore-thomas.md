## Overview

Theodore Thomas (1835–1905) was the founding conductor of the Chicago Symphony Orchestra and one of the most influential figures in American orchestral music. In *The Song of the Lark*, he appears as a real historical personage whose judgment carries weight in the Chicago musical world. He is consulted about Thea Kronborg's potential, and his verification of her gift serves as an external, authoritative endorsement of the talent that Harsanyi has already recognized.

## Biography / Character Arc

### The Conductor in Chicago

Theodore Thomas was "perhaps the only man in" — the unfinished qualification suggests his singular authority in Chicago's musical life [023-227]. By the time of the novel's Chicago chapters (the early 1890s), Thomas had established the Chicago Orchestra (later the Chicago Symphony Orchestra) as a major American musical institution, bringing European standards of orchestral performance to the Midwest.

### Evaluating Thea

Thomas's role in the novel is that of an offstage authority. In Chapter 26, "one afternoon in April, Theodore Thomas, the conductor of the Chicago" [026-001] — the broken sentence hinting at a consultation or encounter. The narrative reveals that Thomas has been consulted about Thea: "I have even consulted Theodore Thomas about" her [027-034], Harsanyi reports, indicating that Thea's talent has been brought to the attention of the highest musical authority in Chicago.

That Thomas was consulted — and that the consultation is cited as evidence of Thea's exceptional promise — confirms her talent from the most objective source available in the novel's musical world. His implicit endorsement validates Harsanyi's own judgment and helps justify the investment of resources and attention that Thea receives.

## Relationships

### Andor Harsanyi

Harsanyi consults Thomas about Thea [027-034], seeking confirmation of his own assessment of her gift. The consultation reflects the professional network of Chicago's musical world, where Harsanyi's reputation as a teacher gives him access to the city's most important conductor.

### Thea Kronborg

Thomas never meets Thea directly in the novel, but his opinion of her potential carries decisive weight. His verification, even at a remove, functions as a kind of professional imprimatur — the judgment of the highest musical authority in Chicago that Thea is worth taking seriously.

## Significance

Theodore Thomas's presence in the novel — brief, offstage, but authoritative — serves a specific narrative function. By invoking a real historical figure of Thomas's stature, Cather grounds Thea's fictional gift in the actual musical world of late-nineteenth-century America. Thomas was not merely a conductor but an institution-builder: he brought symphonic music to American cities that had never heard it, educated audiences, and set standards. That such a figure would take an interest in Thea (or that Harsanyi would seek his opinion) places her within a credible trajectory of musical recognition. Thomas also represents the professional, institutional validation of talent — a counterpoint to the personal, emotional recognition Thea receives from her teachers and patrons.
