## XVI

[016-001] The pleasantest experience Thea had that summer was a trip that she and her

[016-002] mother made to Denver in [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]]’s caboose. [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] had been

[016-003] looking forward to this excursion for a long while, but as Ray never knew at

[016-004] what hour his freight would leave Moonstone, it was difficult to arrange. The

[016-005] call-boy was as likely to summon him to start on his run at twelve

[016-006] o’clock midnight as at twelve o’clock noon. The first week in June

[016-007] started out with all the scheduled trains running on time, and a light freight

[016-008] business. Tuesday evening Ray, after consulting with the dispatcher, stopped at

[016-009] the Kronborgs’ front gate to tell Mrs. Kronborg—who was helping

[016-010] Tillie water the flowers—that if she and Thea could be at the depot at

[016-011] eight o’clock the next morning, he thought he could promise them a

[016-012] pleasant ride and get them into Denver before nine o’clock in the

[016-013] evening. Mrs. Kronborg told him cheerfully, across the fence, that she would

[016-014] “take him up on it,” and Ray hurried back to the yards to scrub out

[016-015] his car.

[016-016] The one complaint Ray’s brakemen had to make of him was that he was too

[016-017] fussy about his caboose. His former brakeman had asked to be transferred

[016-018] because, he said, “Kennedy was as fussy about his car as an old maid

[016-019] about her bird-cage.” Joe Giddy, who was braking with Ray now, called him

[016-020] “the bride,” because he kept the caboose and bunks so clean.

[016-021] It was properly the brakeman’s business to keep the car clean, but when

[016-022] Ray got back to the depot, Giddy was nowhere to be found. Muttering that all

[016-023] his brakemen seemed to consider him “easy,” Ray went down to his

[016-024] car alone. He built a fire in the stove and put water on to heat while he got

[016-025] into his overalls and jumper. Then he set to work with a scrubbing-brush and

[016-026] plenty of soap and “cleaner.” He scrubbed the floor and seats,

[016-027] blacked the stove, put clean sheets on the bunks, and then began to demolish

[016-028] Giddy’s picture gallery. Ray found that his brakemen were likely to have

[016-029] what he termed “a taste for the nude in art,” and Giddy was no

[016-030] exception. Ray took down half a dozen girls in tights and ballet

[016-031] skirts,—premiums for cigarette coupons,—and some racy calendars

[016-032] advertising saloons and sporting clubs, which had cost Giddy both time and

[016-033] trouble; he even removed Giddy’s particular pet, a naked girl lying on a

[016-034] couch with her knee carelessly poised in the air. Underneath the picture was

[016-035] printed the title, “The Odalisque.” Giddy was under the happy

[016-036] delusion that this title meant something wicked,—there was a wicked look

[016-037] about the consonants,—but Ray, of course, had looked it up, and Giddy was

[016-038] indebted to the dictionary for the privilege of keeping his lady. If

[016-039] “odalisque” had been what Ray called an objectionable word, he

[016-040] would have thrown the picture out in the first place. Ray even took down a

[016-041] picture of Mrs. Langtry in evening dress, because it was entitled the

[016-042] “Jersey Lily,” and because there was a small head of Edward VII,

[016-043] then Prince of Wales, in one corner. Albert Edward’s conduct was a

[016-044] popular subject of discussion among railroad men in those days, and as Ray

[016-045] pulled the tacks out of this lithograph he felt more indignant with the English

[016-046] than ever. He deposited all these pictures under the mattress of Giddy’s

[016-047] bunk, and stood admiring his clean car in the lamplight; the walls now

[016-048] exhibited only a wheatfield, advertising agricultural implements, a map of

[016-049] Colorado, and some pictures of race-horses and hunting-dogs. At this moment

[016-050] Giddy, freshly shaved and shampooed, his shirt shining with the highest polish

[016-051] known to Chinese laundrymen, his straw hat tipped over his right eye, thrust

[016-052] his head in at the door.

[016-053] “What in hell—” he brought out furiously. His good humored,

[016-054] sunburned face seemed fairly to swell with amazement and anger.

[016-055] “That’s all right, Giddy,” Ray called in a conciliatory tone.

[016-056] “Nothing injured. I’ll put ’em all up again as I found

[016-057] ’em. Going to take some ladies down in the car to-morrow.”

[016-058] Giddy scowled. He did not dispute the propriety of Ray’s measures, if

[016-059] there were to be ladies on board, but he felt injured. “I suppose

[016-060] you’ll expect me to behave like a Y.M.C.A. secretary,” he growled.

[016-061] “I can’t do my work and serve tea at the same time.”

[016-062] “No need to have a tea-party,” said Ray with determined

[016-063] cheerfulness. “Mrs. Kronborg will bring the lunch, and it will be a

[016-064] darned good one.”

[016-065] Giddy lounged against the car, holding his cigar between two thick fingers.

[016-066] “Then I guess she’ll get it,” he observed knowingly. “I

[016-067] don’t think your musical friend is much on the grub-box. Has to keep her

[016-068] hands white to tickle the ivories.” Giddy had nothing against Thea, but

[016-069] he felt cantankerous and wanted to get a rise out of Kennedy.

[016-070] “Every man to his own job,” Ray replied agreeably, pulling his

[016-071] white shirt on over his head.

[016-072] Giddy emitted smoke disdainfully. “I suppose so. The man that gets her

[016-073] will have to wear an apron and bake the pancakes. Well, some men like to mess

[016-074] about the kitchen.” He paused, but Ray was intent on getting into his

[016-075] clothes as quickly as possible. Giddy thought he could go a little further.

[016-076] “Of course, I don’t dispute your right to haul women in this car if

[016-077] you want to; but personally, so far as I’m concerned, I’d a good

[016-078] deal rather drink a can of tomatoes and do without the women *and* their

[016-079] lunch. I was never much enslaved to hard-boiled eggs, anyhow.”

[016-080] “You’ll eat ’em to-morrow, all the same.” Ray’s

[016-081] tone had a steely glitter as he jumped out of the car, and Giddy stood aside to

[016-082] let him pass. He knew that Kennedy’s next reply would be delivered by

[016-083] hand. He had once seen Ray beat up a nasty fellow for insulting a Mexican woman

[016-084] who helped about the grub-car in the work train, and his fists had worked like

[016-085] two steel hammers. Giddy wasn’t looking for trouble.

[016-086] At eight o’clock the next morning Ray greeted his ladies and helped them

[016-087] into the car. Giddy had put on a clean shirt and yellow pig-skin gloves and was

[016-088] whistling his best. He considered Kennedy a fluke as a ladies’ man, and

[016-089] if there was to be a party, the honors had to be done by some one who

[016-090] wasn’t a blacksmith at small-talk. Giddy had, as Ray sarcastically

[016-091] admitted, “a local reputation as a jollier,” and he was fluent in

[016-092] gallant speeches of a not too-veiled nature. He insisted that Thea should take

[016-093] his seat in the cupola, opposite Ray’s, where she could look out over the

[016-094] country. Thea told him, as she clambered up, that she cared a good deal more

[016-095] about riding in that seat than about going to Denver. Ray was never so

[016-096] companionable and easy as when he sat chatting in the lookout of his little

[016-097] house on wheels. Good stories came to him, and interesting recollections. Thea

[016-098] had a great respect for the reports he had to write out, and for the telegrams

[016-099] that were handed to him at stations; for all the knowledge and experience it

[016-100] must take to run a freight train.

[016-101] Giddy, down in the car, in the pauses of his work, made himself agreeable to

[016-102] Mrs. Kronborg.

[016-103] “It’s a great rest to be where my family can’t get at me, Mr.

[016-104] Giddy,” she told him. “I thought you and Ray might have some

[016-105] housework here for me to look after, but I couldn’t improve any on this

[016-106] car.”

[016-107] “Oh, we like to keep her neat,” returned Giddy glibly, winking up

[016-108] at Ray’s expressive back. “If you want to see a clean ice-box, look

[016-109] at this one. Yes, Kennedy always carries fresh cream to eat on his oatmeal.

[016-110] I’m not particular. The tin cow’s good enough for me.”

[016-111] “Most of you boys smoke so much that all victuals taste alike to

[016-112] you,” said Mrs. Kronborg. “I’ve got no religious scruples

[016-113] against smoking, but I couldn’t take as much interest cooking for a man

[016-114] that used tobacco. I guess it’s all right for bachelors who have to eat

[016-115] round.”

[016-116] Mrs. Kronborg took off her hat and veil and made herself comfortable. She

[016-117] seldom had an opportunity to be idle, and she enjoyed it. She could sit for

[016-118] hours and watch the sage-hens fly up and the jack-rabbits dart away from the

[016-119] track, without being bored. She wore a tan bombazine dress, made very plainly,

[016-120] and carried a roomy, worn, mother-of-the-family handbag.

[016-121] Ray Kennedy always insisted that Mrs. Kronborg was “a fine-looking

[016-122] lady,” but this was not the common opinion in Moonstone. Ray had lived

[016-123] long enough among the Mexicans to dislike fussiness, to feel that there was

[016-124] something more attractive in ease of manner than in absentminded concern about

[016-125] hairpins and dabs of lace. He had learned to think that the way a woman stood,

[016-126] moved, sat in her chair, looked at you, was more important than the absence of

[016-127] wrinkles from her skirt. Ray had, indeed, such unusual perceptions in some

[016-128] directions, that one could not help wondering what he would have been if he had

[016-129] ever, as he said, had “half a chance.”

[016-130] He was right; Mrs. Kronborg was a fine-looking woman. She was short and square,

[016-131] but her head was a real head, not a mere jerky termination of the body. It had

[016-132] some individuality apart from hats and hairpins. Her hair, Moonstone women

[016-133] admitted, would have been very pretty “on anybody else.” Frizzy

[016-134] bangs were worn then, but Mrs. Kronborg always dressed her hair in the same

[016-135] way, parted in the middle, brushed smoothly back from her low, white forehead,

[016-136] pinned loosely on the back of her head in two thick braids. It was growing gray

[016-137] about the temples, but after the manner of yellow hair it seemed only to have

[016-138] grown paler there, and had taken on a color like that of English primroses. Her

[016-139] eyes were clear and untroubled; her face smooth and calm, and, as Ray said,

[016-140] “strong.”

[016-141] Thea and Ray, up in the sunny cupola, were laughing and talking. Ray got great

[016-142] pleasure out of seeing her face there in the little box where he so often

[016-143] imagined it. They were crossing a plateau where great red sandstone boulders

[016-144] lay about, most of them much wider at the top than at the base, so that they

[016-145] looked like great toadstools.

[016-146] “The sand has been blowing against them for a good many hundred

[016-147] years,” Ray explained, directing Thea’s eyes with his gloved hand.

[016-148] “You see the sand blows low, being so heavy, and cuts them out

[016-149] underneath. Wind and sand are pretty high-class architects. That’s the

[016-150] principle of most of the Cliff-Dweller remains down at Canyon de Chelly. The

[016-151] sandstorms had dug out big depressions in the face of a cliff, and the Indians

[016-152] built their houses back in that depression.”

[016-153] “You told me that before, Ray, and of course you know. But the geography

[016-154] says their houses were cut out of the face of the living rock, and I like that

[016-155] better.”

[016-156] Ray sniffed. “What nonsense does get printed! It’s enough to give a

[016-157] man disrespect for learning. How could them Indians cut houses out of the

[016-158] living rock, when they knew nothing about the art of forging metals?” Ray

[016-160] was in one of his favorite fields of speculation, and nothing gave him more

[016-162] tell you, Thee, if those old fellows had learned to work metals once, your

[016-165] to-day, the corners as true as the Denver Capitol. They were clever at most

[016-166] everything but metals; and that one failure kept them from getting across. It

[016-167] was the quicksand that swallowed ’em up, as a race. I guess civilization

[016-173] “expression” is obligatory. He still carried in his trunk, among

[016-185] Ray’s pale-blue eyes and the feeling in his voice more than made up for

[016-187] “Were the Cliff-Dwellers really clever with their hands, Ray, or do you
