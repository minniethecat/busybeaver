## IV

[035-001] Thea noticed that Bowers took rather more pains with her now that Fred

[035-002] Ottenburg often dropped in at eleven-thirty to hear her lesson. After the

[035-003] lesson the young man took Bowers off to lunch with him, and Bowers liked good

[035-004] food when another man paid for it. He encouraged Fred’s visits, and Thea

[035-005] soon saw that Fred knew exactly why.

[035-006] One morning, after her lesson, Ottenburg turned to Bowers. “If

[035-007] you’ll lend me Miss Thea, I think I have an engagement for her. Mrs.

[035-008] Henry Nathanmeyer is going to give three musical evenings in April, first three

[035-009] Saturdays, and she has consulted me about soloists. For the first evening she

[035-010] has a young violinist, and she would be charmed to have Miss Kronborg. She will

[035-011] pay fifty dollars. Not much, but Miss Thea would meet some people there who

[035-012] might be useful. What do you say?”

[035-013] Bowers passed the question on to Thea. “I guess you could use the fifty,

[035-014] couldn’t you, Miss Kronborg? You can easily work up some songs.”

[035-015] Thea was perplexed. “I need the money awfully,” she said frankly;

[035-016] “but I haven’t got the right clothes for that sort of thing. I

[035-017] suppose I’d better try to get some.”

[035-018] Ottenburg spoke up quickly, “Oh, you’d make nothing out of it if

[035-019] you went to buying evening clothes. I’ve thought of that. Mrs.

[035-020] Nathanmeyer has a troop of daughters, a perfect seraglio, all ages and sizes.

[035-021] She’ll be glad to fit you out, if you aren’t sensitive about

[035-022] wearing kosher clothes. Let me take you to see her, and you’ll find that

[035-023] she’ll arrange that easily enough. I told her she must produce something

[035-024] nice, blue or yellow, and properly cut. I brought half a dozen Worth gowns

[035-025] through the customs for her two weeks ago, and she’s not ungrateful. When

[035-026] can we go to see her?”

[035-027] “I haven’t any time free, except at night,” Thea replied in

[035-028] some confusion.

[035-029] “To-morrow evening, then? I shall call for you at eight. Bring all your

[035-030] songs along; she will want us to give her a little rehearsal, perhaps.

[035-031] I’ll play your accompaniments, if you’ve no objection. That will

[035-032] save money for you and for Mrs. Nathanmeyer. She needs it.” Ottenburg

[035-033] chuckled as he took down the number of Thea’s boarding-house.

[035-034] The Nathanmeyers were so rich and great that even Thea had heard of them, and

[035-035] this seemed a very remarkable opportunity. Ottenburg had brought it about by

[035-036] merely lifting a finger, apparently. He was a beer prince sure enough, as

[035-037] Bowers had said.

[035-038] The next evening at a quarter to eight Thea was dressed and waiting in the

[035-039] boarding-house parlor. She was nervous and fidgety and found it difficult to

[035-040] sit still on the hard, convex upholstery of the chairs. She tried them one

[035-041] after another, moving about the dimly lighted, musty room, where the gas always

[035-042] leaked gently and sang in the burners. There was no one in the parlor but the

[035-043] medical student, who was playing one of Sousa’s marches so vigorously

[035-044] that the china ornaments on the top of the piano rattled. In a few moments some

[035-045] of the pension-office girls would come in and begin to two-step. Thea wished

[035-046] that Ottenburg would come and let her escape. She glanced at herself in the

[035-047] long, somber mirror. She was wearing her pale-blue broadcloth church dress,

[035-048] which was not unbecoming but was certainly too heavy to wear to anybody’s

[035-049] house in the evening. Her slippers were run over at the heel and she had not

[035-050] had time to have them mended, and her white gloves were not so clean as they

[035-051] should be. However, she knew that she would forget these annoying things as

[035-052] soon as Ottenburg came.

[035-053] Mary, the Hungarian chambermaid, came to the door, stood between the plush

[035-054] portières, beckoned to Thea, and made an inarticulate sound in her throat. Thea

[035-055] jumped up and ran into the hall, where Ottenburg stood smiling, his caped cloak

[035-056] open, his silk hat in his white-kid hand. The Hungarian girl stood like a

[035-057] monument on her flat heels, staring at the pink carnation in Ottenburg’s

[035-058] coat. Her broad, pockmarked face wore the only expression of which it was

[035-059] capable, a kind of animal wonder. As the young man followed Thea out, he

[035-060] glanced back over his shoulder through the crack of the door; the Hun clapped

[035-061] her hands over her stomach, opened her mouth, and made another raucous sound in

[035-062] her throat.

[035-063] “Isn’t she awful?” Thea exclaimed. “I think she’s

[035-064] half-witted. Can you understand her?”

[035-065] Ottenburg laughed as he helped her into the carriage. “Oh, yes; I can

[035-066] understand her!” He settled himself on the front seat opposite Thea.

[035-067] “Now, I want to tell you about the people we are going to see. We may

[035-068] have a musical public in this country some day, but as yet there are only the

[035-069] Germans and the Jews. All the other people go to hear Jessie Darcey sing,

[035-070] ‘O, Promise Me!’ The Nathanmeyers are the finest kind of Jews. If

[035-071] you do anything for Mrs. Henry Nathanmeyer, you must put yourself into her

[035-072] hands. Whatever she says about music, about clothes, about life, will be

[035-073] correct. And you may feel at ease with her. She expects nothing of people; she

[035-074] has lived in [[chicago|Chicago]] twenty years. If you were to behave like the Magyar who

[035-075] was so interested in my buttonhole, she would not be surprised. If you were to

[035-076] sing like Jessie Darcey, she would not be surprised; but she would manage not

[035-077] to hear you again.”

[035-078] “Would she? Well, that’s the kind of people I want to find.”

[035-079] Thea felt herself growing bolder.

[035-080] “You will be all right with her so long as you do not try to be anything

[035-081] that you are not. Her standards have nothing to do with Chicago. Her

[035-082] perceptions—or her grandmother’s, which is the same

[035-083] thing—were keen when all this was an Indian village. So merely be

[035-084] yourself, and you will like her. She will like you because the Jews always

[035-085] sense talent, and,” he added ironically, “they admire certain

[035-086] qualities of feeling that are found only in the white-skinned races.”

[035-087] Thea looked into the young man’s face as the light of a street lamp

[035-088] flashed into the carriage. His somewhat academic manner amused her.

[035-089] “What makes you take such an interest in singers?” she asked

[035-090] curiously. “You seem to have a perfect passion for hearing music-lessons.

[035-091] I wish I could trade jobs with you!”

[035-092] “I’m not interested in singers.” His tone was offended.

[035-093] “I am interested in talent. There are only two interesting things in the

[035-094] world, anyhow; and talent is one of them.”

[035-095] “What’s the other?” The question came meekly from the figure

[035-096] opposite him. Another arc-light flashed in at the window.

[035-097] Fred saw her face and broke into a laugh. “Why, you’re guying me,

[035-098] you little wretch! You won’t let me behave properly.” He dropped

[035-099] his gloved hand lightly on her knee, took it away and let it hang between his

[035-100] own. “Do you know,” he said confidentially, “I believe

[035-101] I’m more in earnest about all this than you are.”

[035-102] “About all what?”

[035-103] “All you’ve got in your throat there.”

[035-104] “Oh! I’m in earnest all right; only I never was much good at

[035-105] talking. Jessie Darcey is the smooth talker. ‘You notice the effect I get

[035-106] there—’ If she only got ’em, she’d be a wonder, you

[035-107] know!”

[035-108] Mr. and Mrs. Nathanmeyer were alone in their great library. Their three

[035-109] unmarried daughters had departed in successive carriages, one to a dinner, one

[035-110] to a Nietszche club, one to a ball given for the girls employed in the big

[035-111] department stores. When Ottenburg and Thea entered, Henry Nathanmeyer and his

[035-112] wife were sitting at a table at the farther end of the long room, with a

[035-113] reading-lamp and a tray of cigarettes and cordial-glasses between them. The

[035-114] overhead lights were too soft to bring out the colors of the big rugs, and none

[035-115] of the picture lights were on. One could merely see that there were pictures

[035-116] there. Fred whispered that they were Rousseaus and Corots, very fine ones which

[035-117] the old banker had bought long ago for next to nothing. In the hall Ottenburg

[035-118] had stopped Thea before a painting of a woman eating grapes out of a paper bag,

[035-119] and had told her gravely that there was the most beautiful Manet in the world.

[035-120] He made her take off her hat and gloves in the hall, and looked her over a

[035-121] little before he took her in. But once they were in the library he seemed

[035-122] perfectly satisfied with her and led her down the long room to their hostess.

[035-123] Mrs. Nathanmeyer was a heavy, powerful old Jewess, with a great pompadour of

[035-124] white hair, a swarthy complexion, an eagle nose, and sharp, glittering eyes.

[035-125] She wore a black velvet dress with a long train, and a diamond necklace and

[035-126] earrings. She took Thea to the other side of the table and presented her to Mr.

[035-127] Nathanmeyer, who apologized for not rising, pointing to a slippered foot on a

[035-128] cushion; he said that he suffered from gout. He had a very soft voice and spoke

[035-129] with an accent which would have been heavy if it had not been so caressing. He

[035-130] kept Thea standing beside him for some time. He noticed that she stood easily,

[035-131] looked straight down into his face, and was not embarrassed. Even when Mrs.

[035-132] Nathanmeyer told Ottenburg to bring a chair for Thea, the old man did not

[035-133] release her hand, and she did not sit down. He admired her just as she was, as

[035-134] she happened to be standing, and she felt it. He was much handsomer than his

[035-135] wife, Thea thought. His forehead was high, his hair soft and white, his skin

[035-136] pink, a little puffy under his clear blue eyes. She noticed how warm and

[035-137] delicate his hands were, pleasant to touch and beautiful to look at. Ottenburg

[035-138] had told her that Mr. Nathanmeyer had a very fine collection of medals and

[035-139] cameos, and his fingers looked as if they had never touched anything but

[035-140] delicately cut surfaces.

[035-141] He asked Thea where Moonstone was; how many inhabitants it had; what her

[035-142] father’s business was; from what part of Sweden her grandfather came; and

[035-143] whether she spoke Swedish as a child. He was interested to hear that her

[035-144] mother’s mother was still living, and that her grandfather had played the

[035-145] oboe. Thea felt at home standing there beside him; she felt that he was very

[035-146] wise, and that he some way took one’s life up and looked it over kindly,

[035-147] as if it were a story. She was sorry when they left him to go into the

[035-148] music-room.

[035-149] As they reached the door of the music-room, Mrs. Nathanmeyer turned a switch

[035-150] that threw on many lights. The room was even larger than the library, all

[035-151] glittering surfaces, with two Steinway pianos.

[035-152] Mrs. Nathanmeyer rang for her own maid. “Selma will take you upstairs,

[035-153] Miss Kronborg, and you will find some dresses on the bed. Try several of them,

[035-154] and take the one you like best. Selma will help you. She has a great deal of

[035-155] taste. When you are dressed, come down and let us go over some of your songs

[035-156] with Mr. Ottenburg.”

[035-157] After Thea went away with the maid, Ottenburg came up to Mrs. Nathanmeyer and

[035-158] stood beside her, resting his hand on the high back of her chair.

[035-159] “Well, *gnädige Frau*, do you like her?”

[035-160] “I think so. I liked her when she talked to father. She will always get

[035-161] on better with men.”

[035-162] Ottenburg leaned over her chair. “Prophetess! Do you see what I

[035-163] meant?”

[035-164] “About her beauty? She has great possibilities, but you can never tell

[035-165] about those Northern women. They look so strong, but they are easily battered.

[035-166] The face falls so early under those wide cheek-bones. A single idea—hate

[035-167] or greed, or even love—can tear them to shreds. She is nineteen? Well, in

[035-168] ten years she may have quite a regal beauty, or she may have a heavy,

[035-169] discontented face, all dug out in channels. That will depend upon the kind of

[035-170] ideas she lives with.”

[035-171] “Or the kind of people?” Ottenburg suggested.

[035-172] The old Jewess folded her arms over her massive chest, drew back her shoulders,

[035-173] and looked up at the young man. “With that hard glint in her eye? The

[035-174] people won’t matter much, I fancy. They will come and go. She is very

[035-175] much interested in herself—as she should be.”

[035-176] Ottenburg frowned. “Wait until you hear her sing. Her eyes are different

[035-177] then. That gleam that comes in them is curious, isn’t it? As you say,

[035-178] it’s impersonal.”

[035-179] The object of this discussion came in, smiling. She had chosen neither the blue

[035-180] nor the yellow gown, but a pale rose-color, with silver butterflies. Mrs.

[035-181] Nathanmeyer lifted her lorgnette and studied her as she approached. She caught

[035-182] the characteristic things at once: the free, strong walk, the calm carriage of

[035-183] the head, the milky whiteness of the girl’s arms and shoulders.

[035-184] “Yes, that color is good for you,” she said approvingly. “The

[035-185] yellow one probably killed your hair? Yes; this does very well indeed, so we

[035-186] need think no more about it.”

[035-187] Thea glanced questioningly at Ottenburg. He smiled and bowed, seemed perfectly

[035-188] satisfied. He asked her to stand in the elbow of the piano, in front of him,

[035-189] instead of behind him as she had been taught to do.

[035-190] “Yes,” said the hostess with feeling. “That other position is

[035-191] barbarous.”

[035-192] Thea sang an aria from ‘Gioconda,’ some songs by Schumann which she

[035-193] had studied with Harsanyi, and the “*Tak for Dit Råd*,” which

[035-194] Ottenburg liked.

[035-195] “That you must do again,” he declared when they finished this song.

[035-196] “You did it much better the other day. You accented it more, like a dance

[035-197] or a galop. How did you do it?”

[035-198] Thea laughed, glancing sidewise at Mrs. Nathanmeyer. “You want it

[035-199] rough-house, do you? Bowers likes me to sing it more seriously, but it always

[035-200] makes me think about a story my grandmother used to tell.”

[035-201] Fred pointed to the chair behind her. “Won’t you rest a moment and

[035-202] tell us about it? I thought you had some notion about it when you first sang it

[035-203] for me.”

[035-204] Thea sat down. “In Norway my grandmother knew a girl who was awfully in

[035-205] love with a young fellow. She went into service on a big dairy farm to make

[035-206] enough money for her outfit. They were married at Christmastime, and everybody

[035-207] was glad, because they’d been sighing around about each other for so

[035-208] long. That very summer, the day before St. John’s Day, her husband caught

[035-209] her carrying on with another farm-hand. The next night all the farm people had

[035-210] a bonfire and a big dance up on the mountain, and everybody was dancing and

[035-211] singing. I guess they were all a little drunk, for they got to seeing how near

[035-212] they could make the girls dance to the edge of the cliff. Ole—he was the

[035-213] girl’s husband—seemed the jolliest and the drunkest of anybody. He

[035-214] danced his wife nearer and nearer the edge of the rock, and his wife began to

[035-215] scream so that the others stopped dancing and the music stopped; but Ole went

[035-216] right on singing, and he danced her over the edge of the cliff and they fell

[035-217] hundreds of feet and were all smashed to pieces.”

[035-218] Ottenburg turned back to the piano. “That’s the idea! Now, come

[035-219] Miss Thea. Let it go!”

[035-220] Thea took her place. She laughed and drew herself up out of her corsets, threw

[035-221] her shoulders high and let them drop again. She had never sung in a low dress

[035-222] before, and she found it comfortable. Ottenburg jerked his head and they began

[035-223] the song. The accompaniment sounded more than ever like the thumping and

[035-224] scraping of heavy feet.

[035-225] When they stopped, they heard a sympathetic tapping at the end of the room. Old

[035-226] Mr. Nathanmeyer had come to the door and was sitting back in the shadow, just

[035-227] inside the library, applauding with his cane. Thea threw him a bright smile. He

[035-228] continued to sit there, his slippered foot on a low chair, his cane between his

[035-229] fingers, and she glanced at him from time to time. The doorway made a frame for

[035-230] him, and he looked like a man in a picture, with the long, shadowy room behind

[035-231] him.

[035-232] Mrs. Nathanmeyer summoned the maid again. “Selma will pack that gown in a

[035-233] box for you, and you can take it home in Mr. Ottenburg’s carriage.”

[035-234] Thea turned to follow the maid, but hesitated. “Shall I wear

[035-235] gloves?” she asked, turning again to Mrs. Nathanmeyer.

[035-236] “No, I think not. Your arms are good, and you will feel freer without.

[035-237] You will need light slippers, pink—or white, if you have them, will do

[035-238] quite as well.”

[035-239] Thea went upstairs with the maid and Mrs. Nathanmeyer rose, took

[035-240] Ottenburg’s arm, and walked toward her husband. “That’s the

[035-241] first real voice I have heard in Chicago,” she said decidedly. “I

[035-242] don’t count that stupid Priest woman. What do you say, father?”

[035-243] Mr. Nathanmeyer shook his white head and smiled softly, as if he were thinking

[035-244] about something very agreeable. “*Svensk sommar*,” he

[035-245] murmured. “She is like a Swedish summer. I spent nearly a year there when

[035-246] I was a young man,” he explained to Ottenburg.

[035-247] When Ottenburg got Thea and her big box into the carriage, it occurred to him

[035-248] that she must be hungry, after singing so much. When he asked her, she admitted

[035-249] that she was very hungry, indeed.

[035-250] He took out his watch. “Would you mind stopping somewhere with me?

[035-251] It’s only eleven.”

[035-252] “Mind? Of course, I wouldn’t mind. I wasn’t brought up like

[035-253] that. I can take care of myself.”

[035-254] Ottenburg laughed. “And I can take care of myself, so we can do lots of

[035-255] jolly things together.” He opened the carriage door and spoke to the

[035-256] driver. “I’m stuck on the way you sing that Grieg song,” he

[035-257] declared.

[035-258] When Thea got into bed that night she told herself that this was the happiest

[035-259] evening she had had in Chicago. She had enjoyed the Nathanmeyers and their

[035-260] grand house, her new dress, and Ottenburg, her first real carriage ride, and

[035-261] the good supper when she was so hungry. And Ottenburg *was* jolly! He made

[035-262] you want to come back at him. You weren’t always being caught up and

[035-263] mystified. When you started in with him, you went; you cut the breeze, as Ray

[035-264] used to say. He had some go in him.

[035-265] Philip Frederick Ottenburg was the third son of the great brewer. His mother

[035-266] was Katarina Fürst, the daughter and heiress of a brewing business older and

[035-267] richer than Otto Ottenburg’s. As a young woman she had been a conspicuous

[035-268] figure in German-American society in New York, and not untouched by scandal.

[035-269] She was a handsome, headstrong girl, a rebellious and violent force in a

[035-270] provincial society. She was brutally sentimental and heavily romantic. Her free

[035-271] speech, her Continental ideas, and her proclivity for championing new causes,

[035-272] even when she did not know much about them, made her an object of suspicion.

[035-273] She was always going abroad to seek out intellectual affinities, and was one of

[035-274] the group of young women who followed Wagner about in his old age, keeping at a

[035-275] respectful distance, but receiving now and then a gracious acknowledgment that

[035-276] he appreciated their homage. When the composer died, Katarina, then a matron

[035-277] with a family, took to her bed and saw no one for a week.

[035-278] After having been engaged to an American actor, a Welsh socialist agitator, and

[035-279] a German army officer, Fräulein Fürst at last placed herself and her great

[035-280] brewery interests into the trustworthy hands of Otto Ottenburg, who had been

[035-281] her suitor ever since he was a clerk, learning his business in her

[035-282] father’s office.

[035-283] Her first two sons were exactly like their father. Even as children they were

[035-284] industrious, earnest little tradesmen. As Frau Ottenburg said, “she had

[035-285] to wait for her Fred, but she got him at last,” the first man who had

[035-286] altogether pleased her. Frederick entered Harvard when he was eighteen. When

[035-287] his mother went to Boston to visit him, she not only got him everything he

[035-288] wished for, but she made handsome and often embarrassing presents to all his

[035-289] friends. She gave dinners and supper parties for the Glee Club, made the crew

[035-290] break training, and was a generally disturbing influence. In his third year

[035-291] Fred left the university because of a serious escapade which had somewhat

[035-292] hampered his life ever since. He went at once into his father’s business,

[035-293] where, in his own way, he had made himself very useful.

[035-294] [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] was now twenty-eight, and people could only say of him that he

[035-295] had been less hurt by his mother’s indulgence than most boys would have

[035-296] been. He had never wanted anything that he could not have it, and he might have

[035-297] had a great many things that he had never wanted. He was extravagant, but not

[035-298] prodigal. He turned most of the money his mother gave him into the business,

[035-299] and lived on his generous salary.

[035-300] Fred had never been bored for a whole day in his life. When he was in Chicago

[035-301] or St. Louis, he went to ballgames, prize-fights, and horse-races. When he was

[035-302] in Germany, he went to concerts and to the opera. He belonged to a long list of

[035-303] sporting-clubs and hunting-clubs, and was a good boxer. He had so many natural

[035-304] interests that he had no affectations. At Harvard he kept away from the

[035-305] aesthetic circle that had already discovered Francis Thompson. He liked no

[035-306] poetry but German poetry. Physical energy was the thing he was full to the brim

[035-307] of, and music was one of its natural forms of expression. He had a healthy love

[035-308] of sport and art, of eating and drinking. When he was in Germany, he scarcely

[035-309] knew where the soup ended and the symphony began.
