## VI

[026-001] One afternoon in April, Theodore Thomas, the conductor of the [[chicago|Chicago]] Symphony

[026-002] Orchestra, had turned out his desk light and was about to leave his office in

[026-003] the Auditorium Building, when Harsanyi appeared in the doorway. The conductor

[026-004] welcomed him with a hearty hand-grip and threw off the overcoat he had just put

[026-005] on. He pushed Harsanyi into a chair and sat down at his burdened desk, pointing

[026-006] to the piles of papers and railway folders upon it.

[026-007] “Another tour, clear to the coast. This traveling is the part of my work

[026-008] that grinds me, Andor. You know what it means: bad food, dirt, noise,

[026-009] exhaustion for the men and for me. I’m not so young as I once was.

[026-010] It’s time I quit the highway. This is the last tour, I swear!”

[026-011] “Then I’m sorry for the ‘highway.’ I remember when I

[026-012] first heard you in Pittsburg, long ago. It was a life-line you threw me.

[026-013] It’s about one of the people along your highway that I’ve come to

[026-014] see you. Whom do you consider the best teacher for voice in Chicago?”

[026-015] Mr. Thomas frowned and pulled his heavy mustache. “Let me see; I suppose

[026-016] on the whole [[madison-bowers|Madison Bowers]] is the best. He’s intelligent, and he had

[026-017] good training. I don’t like him.”

[026-018] Harsanyi nodded. “I thought there was no one else. I don’t like

[026-019] him, either, so I hesitated. But I suppose he must do, for the present.”

[026-020] “Have you found anything promising? One of your own students?”

[026-021] “Yes, sir. A young Swedish girl from somewhere in Colorado. She is very

[026-022] talented, and she seems to me to have a remarkable voice.”

[026-023] “High voice?”

[026-024] “I think it will be; though her low voice has a beautiful quality, very

[026-025] individual. She has had no instruction in voice at all, and I shrink from

[026-026] handing her over to anybody; her own instinct about it has been so good. It is

[026-027] one of those voices that manages itself easily, without thinning as it goes up;

[026-028] good breathing and perfect relaxation. But she must have a teacher, of course.

[026-029] There is a break in the middle voice, so that the voice does not all work

[026-030] together; an unevenness.”

[026-031] Thomas looked up. “So? Curious; that cleft often happens with the Swedes.

[026-032] Some of their best singers have had it. It always reminds me of the space you

[026-033] so often see between their front teeth. Is she strong physically?”

[026-034] Harsanyi’s eye flashed. He lifted his hand before him and clenched it.

[026-035] “Like a horse, like a tree! Every time I give her a lesson, I lose a

[026-036] pound. She goes after what she wants.”

[026-037] “Intelligent, you say? Musically intelligent?”

[026-038] “Yes; but no cultivation whatever. She came to me like a fine young

[026-039] savage, a book with nothing written in it. That is why I feel the

[026-040] responsibility of directing her.” Harsanyi paused and crushed his soft

[026-041] gray hat over his knee. “She would interest you, Mr. Thomas,” he

[026-042] added slowly. “She has a quality—very individual.”

[026-043] “Yes; the Scandinavians are apt to have that, too. She can’t go to

[026-044] Germany, I suppose?”

[026-045] “Not now, at any rate. She is poor.”

[026-046] Thomas frowned again “I don’t think Bowers a really first-rate man.

[026-047] He’s too petty to be really first-rate; in his nature, I mean. But I dare

[026-048] say he’s the best you can do, if you can’t give her time enough

[026-049] yourself.”

[026-050] Harsanyi waved his hand. “Oh, the time is nothing—she may have all

[026-051] she wants. But I cannot teach her to sing.”

[026-052] “Might not come amiss if you made a musician of her, however,” said

[026-053] Mr. Thomas dryly.

[026-054] “I have done my best. But I can only play with a voice, and this is not a

[026-055] voice to be played with. I think she will be a musician, whatever happens. She

[026-056] is not quick, but she is solid, real; not like these others. My wife says that

[026-057] with that girl one swallow does not make a summer.”

[026-058] Mr. Thomas laughed. “Tell Mrs. Harsanyi that her remark conveys something

[026-059] to me. Don’t let yourself get too much interested. Voices are so often

[026-060] disappointing; especially women’s voices. So much chance about it, so

[026-061] many factors.”

[026-062] “Perhaps that is why they interest one. All the intelligence and talent

[026-063] in the world can’t make a singer. The voice is a wild thing. It

[026-064] can’t be bred in captivity. It is a sport, like the silver fox. It

[026-065] happens.”

[026-066] Mr. Thomas smiled into Harsanyi’s gleaming eye. “Why haven’t

[026-067] you brought her to sing for me?”

[026-068] “I’ve been tempted to, but I knew you were driven to death, with

[026-069] this tour confronting you.”

[026-070] “Oh, I can always find time to listen to a girl who has a voice, if she

[026-071] means business. I’m sorry I’m leaving so soon. I could advise you

[026-072] better if I had heard her. I can sometimes give a singer suggestions.

[026-073] I’ve worked so much with them.”

[026-074] “You’re the only conductor I know who is not snobbish about

[026-075] singers.” Harsanyi spoke warmly.

[026-076] “Dear me, why should I be? They’ve learned from me, and I’ve

[026-077] learned from them.” As they rose, Thomas took the younger man

[026-078] affectionately by the arm. “Tell me about that wife of yours. Is she

[026-079] well, and as lovely as ever? And such fine children! Come to see me oftener,

[026-080] when I get back. I miss it when you don’t.”

[026-081] The two men left the Auditorium Building together. Harsanyi walked home. Even a

[026-082] short talk with Thomas always stimulated him. As he walked he was recalling an

[026-083] evening they once spent together in Cincinnati.

[026-084] Harsanyi was the soloist at one of Thomas’s concerts there, and after the

[026-085] performance the conductor had taken him off to a *Rathskeller* where there

[026-086] was excellent German cooking, and where the proprietor saw to it that Thomas

[026-087] had the best wines procurable. Thomas had been working with the great chorus of

[026-088] the Festival Association and was speaking of it with enthusiasm when Harsanyi

[026-089] asked him how it was that he was able to feel such an interest in choral

[026-090] directing and in voices generally. Thomas seldom spoke of his youth or his

[026-091] early struggles, but that night he turned back the pages and told Harsanyi a

[026-092] long story.

[026-093] He said he had spent the summer of his fifteenth year wandering about alone in

[026-094] the South, giving violin concerts in little towns. He traveled on horseback.

[026-095] When he came into a town, he went about all day tacking up posters announcing

[026-096] his concert in the evening. Before the concert, he stood at the door taking in

[026-097] the admission money until his audience had arrived, and then he went on the

[026-098] platform and played. It was a lazy, hand-to-mouth existence, and Thomas said he

[026-099] must have got to like that easy way of living and the relaxing Southern

[026-100] atmosphere. At any rate, when he got back to New York in the fall, he was

[026-101] rather torpid; perhaps he had been growing too fast. From this adolescent

[026-102] drowsiness the lad was awakened by two voices, by two women who sang in New

[026-103] York in 1851,—Jenny Lind and Henrietta Sontag. They were the first great

[026-104] artists he had ever heard, and he never forgot his debt to them.

[026-105] As he said, “It was not voice and execution alone. There was a greatness

[026-106] about them. They were great women, great artists. They opened a new world to

[026-107] me.” Night after night he went to hear them, striving to reproduce the

[026-108] quality of their tone upon his violin. From that time his idea about strings

[026-109] was completely changed, and on his violin he tried always for the singing,

[026-110] vibrating tone, instead of the loud and somewhat harsh tone then prevalent

[026-111] among even the best German violinists. In later years he often advised

[026-112] violinists to study singing, and singers to study violin. He told Harsanyi that

[026-113] he got his first conception of tone quality from Jenny Lind.

[026-114] “But, of course,” he added, “the great thing I got from Lind

[026-115] and Sontag was the indefinite, not the definite, thing. For an impressionable

[026-116] boy, their inspiration was incalculable. They gave me my first feeling for the

[026-117] Italian style—but I could never say how much they gave me. At that age,

[026-118] such influences are actually creative. I always think of my artistic

[026-119] consciousness as beginning then.”

[026-120] All his life Thomas did his best to repay what he felt he owed to the

[026-121] singer’s art. No man could get such singing from choruses, and no man

[026-122] worked harder to raise the standard of singing in schools and churches and

[026-123] choral societies.
