# German Musical Culture

## Overview

German musical culture is the aesthetic bloodstream of *The Song of the Lark*. From Thea's first piano teacher, the German exile Professor Wunsch, to her eventual mastery of Wagner's operas, Germanic musical traditions provide the framework within which Thea's artistry develops. Cather presents this tradition not as foreign import but as the lingua franca of serious music in America—the standard against which all artistic aspiration must measure itself (004-226).

## The German Teacher

Professor Wunsch embodies the novel's complex relationship with German musical culture. He is a broken man—an alcoholic whose career was destroyed by personal weakness—yet he carries within him the authentic European tradition that Thea needs. Wunsch "had taught in music schools in St. Louis and Kansas City" before drink undid him, and his teaching represents a transmission of Old World expertise to New World talent (004-226).

Wunsch's recognition of Thea's gift is one of the novel's founding moments. His verdict—that she has something exceptional—carries the authority of the German musical establishment. When he tells Thea's family that she should pursue serious training, he speaks with the weight of a tradition that stretches back to Bach and Beethoven.

## Wagner and Transcendence

Wagner's operas represent the apex of Thea's artistic aspiration. The novel traces her gradual initiation into Wagnerian repertoire—from her first encounters with the scores to her eventual triumph as a Wagnerian soprano. Wagner's music-dramas, with their mythic scope and demands for total artistic commitment, provide the perfect vehicle for Thea's mature artistry.

The novel's treatment of Wagner is reverent but not uncritical. Wagner's own life—"every hour of it," as Thea reflects—exemplifies the total dedication that art demands, and his music provides the most demanding and rewarding repertoire for the dramatic soprano voice (059-287). Yet Cather also suggests that Thea's American identity—her roots in the Colorado desert and the immigrant experience—gives her something that European Wagnerians lack: a direct connection to primal sources of creativity that Wagner himself only intuited.

## Key Passages

The "trunkful of music" sent from Denver contains the sheet music that connects Moonstone to Europe—the physical artifacts of the Germanic tradition that Thea studies and eventually masters. This trunk represents the cultural transmission that the novel traces: the movement of musical knowledge from the Old World to the New, from teacher to student, from the past into the future (004-058).
