## V

[042-001] One Sunday afternoon late in July old Henry Biltmer was rheumatically

[042-002] descending into the head of the canyon. The Sunday before had been one of those

[042-003] cloudy days—fortunately rare—when the life goes out of that country

[042-004] and it becomes a gray ghost, an empty, shivering uncertainty. Henry had spent

[042-005] the day in the barn; his canyon was a reality only when it was flooded with the

[042-006] light of its great lamp, when the yellow rocks cast purple shadows, and the

[042-007] resin was fairly cooking in the corkscrew cedars. The yuccas were in blossom

[042-008] now. Out of each clump of sharp bayonet leaves rose a tall stalk hung with

[042-009] greenish-white bells with thick, fleshy petals. The niggerhead cactus was

[042-010] thrusting its crimson blooms up out of every crevice in the rocks.

[042-011] Henry had come out on the pretext of hunting a spade and pick-axe that young

[042-012] Ottenburg had borrowed, but he was keeping his eyes open. He was really very

[042-013] curious about the new occupants of the canyon, and what they found to do there

[042-014] all day long. He let his eye travel along the gulf for a mile or so to the

[042-015] first turning, where the fissure zigzagged out and then receded behind a stone

[042-016] promontory on which stood the yellowish, crumbling ruin of the old watch-tower.

[042-017] From the base of this tower, which now threw its shadow forward, bits of rock

[042-018] kept flying out into the open gulf—skating upon the air until they lost

[042-019] their momentum, then falling like chips until they rang upon the ledges at the

[042-020] bottom of the gorge or splashed into the stream. Biltmer shaded his eyes with

[042-021] his hand. There on the promontory, against the cream-colored cliff, were two

[042-022] figures nimbly moving in the light, both slender and agile, entirely absorbed

[042-023] in their game. They looked like two boys. Both were hatless and both wore white

[042-024] shirts.

[042-025] Henry forgot his pick-axe and followed the trail before the cliff-houses toward

[042-026] the tower. Behind the tower, as he well knew, were heaps of stones, large and

[042-027] small, piled against the face of the cliff. He had always believed that the

[042-028] Indian watchmen piled them there for ammunition. Thea and Fred had come upon

[042-029] these missiles and were throwing them for distance. As Biltmer approached he

[042-030] could hear them laughing, and he caught Thea’s voice, high and excited,

[042-031] with a ring of vexation in it. Fred was teaching her to throw a heavy stone

[042-032] like a discus. When it was Fred’s turn, he sent a triangular-shaped stone

[042-033] out into the air with considerable skill. Thea watched it enviously, standing

[042-034] in a half-defiant posture, her sleeves rolled above her elbows and her face

[042-035] flushed with heat and excitement. After Fred’s third missile had rung

[042-036] upon the rocks below, she snatched up a stone and stepped impatiently out on

[042-037] the ledge in front of him. He caught her by the elbows and pulled her back.

[042-038] “Not so close, you silly! You’ll spin yourself off in a

[042-039] minute.”

[042-040] “You went that close. There’s your heel-mark,” she retorted.

[042-041] “Well, I know how. That makes a difference.” He drew a mark in the

[042-042] dust with his toe. “There, that’s right. Don’t step over

[042-043] that. Pivot yourself on your spine, and make a half turn. When you’ve

[042-044] swung your length, let it go.”

[042-045] Thea settled the flat piece of rock between her wrist and fingers, faced the

[042-046] cliff wall, stretched her arm in position, whirled round on her left foot to

[042-047] the full stretch of her body, and let the missile spin out over the gulf. She

[042-048] hung expectantly in the air, forgetting to draw back her arm, her eyes

[042-049] following the stone as if it carried her fortunes with it. Her comrade watched

[042-050] her; there weren’t many girls who could show a line like that from the

[042-051] toe to the thigh, from the shoulder to the tip of the outstretched hand. The

[042-052] stone spent itself and began to fall. Thea drew back and struck her knee

[042-053] furiously with her palm.

[042-054] “There it goes again! Not nearly so far as yours. What *is* the

[042-055] matter with me? Give me another.” She faced the cliff and whirled again.

[042-056] The stone spun out, not quite so far as before.

[042-057] Ottenburg laughed. “Why do you keep on working *after* you’ve

[042-058] thrown it? You can’t help it along then.”

[042-059] Without replying, Thea stooped and selected another stone, took a deep breath

[042-060] and made another turn. Fred watched the disk, exclaiming, “Good girl! You

[042-061] got past the pine that time. That’s a good throw.”

[042-062] She took out her handkerchief and wiped her glowing face and throat, pausing to

[042-063] feel her right shoulder with her left hand.

[042-064] “Ah—ha, you’ve made yourself sore, haven’t you? What

[042-065] did I tell you? You go at things too hard. I’ll tell you what I’m

[042-066] going to do, Thea,” Fred dusted his hands and began tucking in the blouse

[042-067] of his shirt, “I’m going to make some single-sticks and teach you

[042-068] to fence. You’d be all right there. You’re light and quick and

[042-069] you’ve got lots of drive in you. I’d like to have you come at me

[042-070] with foils; you’d look so fierce,” he chuckled.

[042-071] She turned away from him and stubbornly sent out another stone, hanging in the

[042-072] air after its flight. Her fury amused Fred, who took all games lightly and

[042-073] played them well. She was breathing hard, and little beads of moisture had

[042-074] gathered on her upper lip. He slipped his arm about her. “If you will

[042-075] look as pretty as that—” he bent his head and kissed her. Thea was

[042-076] startled, gave him an angry push, drove at him with her free hand in a manner

[042-077] quite hostile. Fred was on his mettle in an instant. He pinned both her arms

[042-078] down and kissed her resolutely.

[042-079] When he released her, she turned away and spoke over her shoulder. “That

[042-080] was mean of you, but I suppose I deserved what I got.”

[042-081] “I should say you did deserve it,” Fred panted, “turning

[042-082] savage on me like that! I should say you did deserve it!”

[042-083] He saw her shoulders harden. “Well, I just said I deserved it,

[042-084] didn’t I? What more do you want?”

[042-085] “I want you to tell me why you flew at me like that! You weren’t

[042-086] playing; you looked as if you’d like to murder me.”

[042-087] She brushed back her hair impatiently. “I didn’t mean anything,

[042-088] really. You interrupted me when I was watching the stone. I can’t jump

[042-089] from one thing to another. I pushed you without thinking.”

[042-090] Fred thought her back expressed contrition. He went up to her, stood behind her

[042-091] with his chin above her shoulder, and said something in her ear. Thea laughed

[042-092] and turned toward him. They left the stone-pile carelessly, as if they had

[042-093] never been interested in it, rounded the yellow tower, and disappeared into the

[042-094] second turn of the canyon, where the dead city, interrupted by the jutting

[042-095] promontory, began again.

[042-096] Old Biltmer had been somewhat embarrassed by the turn the game had taken. He

[042-097] had not heard their conversation, but the pantomime against the rocks was clear

[042-098] enough. When the two young people disappeared, their host retreated rapidly

[042-099] toward the head of the canyon.

[042-100] “I guess that young lady can take care of herself,” he chuckled.

[042-101] “Young Fred, though, he has quite a way with them.”
