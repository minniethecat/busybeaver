## II

[039-001] Thea’s life at the Ottenburg ranch was simple and full of light, like the

[039-002] days themselves. She awoke every morning when the first fierce shafts of

[039-003] sunlight darted through the curtainless windows of her room at the ranch house.

[039-004] After breakfast she took her lunch-basket and went down to the canyon. Usually

[039-005] she did not return until sunset.

[039-006] [[panther-canyon|Panther Canyon]] was like a thousand others—one of those abrupt fissures

[039-007] with which the earth in the Southwest is riddled; so abrupt that you might walk

[039-008] over the edge of any one of them on a dark night and never know what had

[039-009] happened to you. This canyon headed on the Ottenburg ranch, about a mile from

[039-010] the ranch house, and it was accessible only at its head. The canyon walls, for

[039-011] the first two hundred feet below the surface, were perpendicular cliffs,

[039-012] striped with even-running strata of rock. From there on to the bottom the sides

[039-013] were less abrupt, were shelving, and lightly fringed with *piñons* and

[039-014] dwarf cedars. The effect was that of a gentler canyon within a wilder one. The

[039-015] dead city lay at the point where the perpendicular outer wall ceased and the

[039-016] V-shaped inner gorge began. There a stratum of rock, softer than those above,

[039-017] had been hollowed out by the action of time until it was like a deep groove

[039-018] running along the sides of the canyon. In this hollow (like a great fold in the

[039-019] rock) the Ancient People had built their houses of yellowish stone and mortar.

[039-020] The over-hanging cliff above made a roof two hundred feet thick. The hard

[039-021] stratum below was an everlasting floor. The houses stood along in a row, like

[039-022] the buildings in a city block, or like a barracks.

[039-023] In both walls of the canyon the same streak of soft rock had been washed out,

[039-024] and the long horizontal groove had been built up with houses. The dead city had

[039-025] thus two streets, one set in either cliff, facing each other across the ravine,

[039-026] with a river of blue air between them.

[039-027] The canyon twisted and wound like a snake, and these two streets went on for

[039-028] four miles or more, interrupted by the abrupt turnings of the gorge, but

[039-029] beginning again within each turn. The canyon had a dozen of these false endings

[039-030] near its head. Beyond, the windings were larger and less perceptible, and it

[039-031] went on for a hundred miles, too narrow, precipitous, and terrible for man to

[039-032] follow it. The Cliff Dwellers liked wide canyons, where the great cliffs caught

[039-033] the sun. Panther Canyon had been deserted for hundreds of years when the first

[039-034] Spanish missionaries came into Arizona, but the masonry of the houses was still

[039-035] wonderfully firm; had crumbled only where a landslide or a rolling boulder had

[039-036] torn it.

[039-037] All the houses in the canyon were clean with the cleanness of sun-baked,

[039-038] wind-swept places, and they all smelled of the tough little cedars that twisted

[039-039] themselves into the very doorways. One of these rock-rooms Thea took for her

[039-040] own. Fred had told her how to make it comfortable. The day after she came old

[039-041] Henry brought over on one of the pack-ponies a roll of Navajo blankets that

[039-042] belonged to Fred, and Thea lined her cave with them. The room was not more than

[039-043] eight by ten feet, and she could touch the stone roof with her finger-tips.

[039-044] This was her old idea: a nest in a high cliff, full of sun. All morning long

[039-045] the sun beat upon her cliff, while the ruins on the opposite side of the canyon

[039-046] were in shadow. In the afternoon, when she had the shade of two hundred feet of

[039-047] rock wall, the ruins on the other side of the gulf stood out in the blazing

[039-048] sunlight. Before her door ran the narrow, winding path that had been the street

[039-049] of the Ancient People. The yucca and niggerhead cactus grew everywhere. From

[039-050] her doorstep she looked out on the ocher-colored slope that ran down several

[039-051] hundred feet to the stream, and this hot rock was sparsely grown with dwarf

[039-052] trees. Their colors were so pale that the shadows of the little trees on the

[039-053] rock stood out sharper than the trees themselves. When Thea first came, the

[039-054] chokecherry bushes were in blossom, and the scent of them was almost

[039-055] sickeningly sweet after a shower. At the very bottom of the canyon, along the

[039-056] stream, there was a thread of bright, flickering,

[039-057] golden-green,—cottonwood seedlings. They made a living, chattering screen

[039-058] behind which she took her bath every morning.

[039-059] Thea went down to the stream by the Indian water trail. She had found a

[039-060] bathing-pool with a sand bottom, where the creek was damned by fallen trees.

[039-061] The climb back was long and steep, and when she reached her little house in the

[039-062] cliff she always felt fresh delight in its comfort and inaccessibility. By the

[039-063] time she got there, the woolly red-and-gray blankets were saturated with

[039-064] sunlight, and she sometimes fell asleep as soon as she stretched her body on

[039-065] their warm surfaces. She used to wonder at her own inactivity. She could lie

[039-066] there hour after hour in the sun and listen to the strident whir of the big

[039-067] locusts, and to the light, ironical laughter of the quaking asps. All her life

[039-068] she had been hurrying and sputtering, as if she had been born behind time and

[039-069] had been trying to catch up. Now, she reflected, as she drew herself out long

[039-070] upon the rugs, it was as if she were waiting for something to catch up with

[039-071] her. She had got to a place where she was out of the stream of meaningless

[039-072] activity and undirected effort.

[039-073] Here she could lie for half a day undistracted, holding pleasant and incomplete

[039-074] conceptions in her mind—almost in her hands. They were scarcely clear

[039-075] enough to be called ideas. They had something to do with fragrance and color

[039-076] and sound, but almost nothing to do with words. She was singing very little

[039-077] now, but a song would go through her head all morning, as a spring keeps

[039-078] welling up, and it was like a pleasant sensation indefinitely prolonged. It was

[039-079] much more like a sensation than like an idea, or an act of remembering. [[music|Music]]

[039-080] had never come to her in that sensuous form before. It had always been a thing

[039-081] to be struggled with, had always brought anxiety and exaltation and

[039-082] chagrin—never content and indolence. Thea began to wonder whether people

[039-083] could not utterly lose the power to work, as they can lose their voice or their

[039-084] memory. She had always been a little drudge, hurrying from one task to

[039-085] another—as if it mattered! And now her power to think seemed converted

[039-086] into a power of sustained sensation. She could become a mere receptacle for

[039-087] heat, or become a color, like the bright lizards that darted about on the hot

[039-088] stones outside her door; or she could become a continuous repetition of sound,

[039-089] like the cicadas.
