# Anna Kronborg

## Overview

Anna Kronborg is Thea's sister—the daughter of the Kronborg family who chooses the conventional path of marriage and domesticity that Thea decisively rejects. Anna's life serves as a quiet counterpoint to Thea's artistic trajectory: not a failure but a different choice, made from different needs and different capacities. Cather presents Anna without condescension, honoring the validity of a life Thea could not have lived.

## The Conventional Path

Anna's choice of marriage represents the path expected of women in Moonstone—the path Thea's mother took, and the path most of Thea's female contemporaries would follow. The novel does not present this as tragedy; Anna is content, her life works on its own terms. But her contentment throws Thea's restlessness into sharper relief: the sister who can be satisfied with what Moonstone offers and the sister who cannot.

## Relationship with Thea

The relationship between the two sisters is marked by mutual incomprehension. Anna cannot understand why Thea would sacrifice comfort, security, and social approval for an uncertain artistic career. Thea cannot understand how Anna can be content with a life so circumscribed. Yet beneath this incomprehension runs a current of familial loyalty that neither sister entirely repudiates.
