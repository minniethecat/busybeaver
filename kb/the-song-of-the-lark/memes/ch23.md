## III

[023-001] Andor Harsanyi had never had a pupil in the least like [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]]. He had

[023-002] never had one more intelligent, and he had never had one so ignorant. When Thea

[023-003] sat down to take her first lesson from him, she had never heard a work by

[023-004] Beethoven or a composition by Chopin. She knew their names vaguely. Wunsch had

[023-005] been a musician once, long before he wandered into Moonstone, but when Thea

[023-006] awoke his interest there was not much left of him. From him Thea had learned

[023-007] something about the works of Gluck and Bach, and he used to play her some of

[023-008] the compositions of Schumann. In his trunk he had a mutilated score of the F

[023-009] sharp minor sonata, which he had heard Clara Schumann play at a festival in

[023-010] Leipsic. Though his powers of execution were at such a low ebb, he used to play

[023-011] at this sonata for his pupil and managed to give her some idea of its beauty.

[023-012] When Wunsch was a young man, it was still daring to like Schumann; enthusiasm

[023-013] for his work was considered an expression of youthful waywardness. Perhaps that

[023-014] was why Wunsch remembered him best. Thea studied some of the

[023-015] *Kinderszenen* with him, as well as some little sonatas by Mozart and

[023-016] Clementi. But for the most part Wunsch stuck to Czerny and Hummel.

[023-017] Harsanyi found in Thea a pupil with sure, strong hands, one who read rapidly

[023-018] and intelligently, who had, he felt, a richly gifted nature. But she had been

[023-019] given no direction, and her ardor was unawakened. She had never heard a

[023-020] symphony orchestra. The literature of the piano was an undiscovered world to

[023-021] her. He wondered how she had been able to work so hard when she knew so little

[023-022] of what she was working toward. She had been taught according to the old

[023-023] Stuttgart method; stiff back, stiff elbows, a very formal position of the

[023-024] hands. The best thing about her preparation was that she had developed an

[023-025] unusual power of work. He noticed at once her way of charging at difficulties.

[023-026] She ran to meet them as if they were foes she had long been seeking, seized

[023-027] them as if they were destined for her and she for them. Whatever she did well,

[023-028] she took for granted. Her eagerness aroused all the young Hungarian’s

[023-029] chivalry. Instinctively one went to the rescue of a creature who had so much to

[023-030] overcome and who struggled so hard. He used to tell his wife that Miss

[023-031] Kronborg’s hour took more out of him than half a dozen other lessons. He

[023-032] usually kept her long over time; he changed her lessons about so that he could

[023-033] do so, and often gave her time at the end of the day, when he could talk to her

[023-034] afterward and play for her a little from what he happened to be studying. It

[023-035] was always interesting to play for her. Sometimes she was so silent that he

[023-036] wondered, when she left him, whether she had got anything out of it. But a week

[023-037] later, two weeks later, she would give back his idea again in a way that set

[023-038] him vibrating.

[023-039] All this was very well for Harsanyi; an interesting variation in the routine of

[023-040] teaching. But for Thea Kronborg, that winter was almost beyond enduring. She

[023-041] always remembered it as the happiest and wildest and saddest of her life.

[023-042] Things came too fast for her; she had not had enough preparation. There were

[023-043] times when she came home from her lesson and lay upon her bed hating Wunsch and

[023-044] her family, hating a world that had let her grow up so ignorant; when she

[023-045] wished that she could die then and there, and be born over again to begin anew.

[023-046] She said something of this kind once to her teacher, in the midst of a bitter

[023-047] struggle. Harsanyi turned the light of his wonderful eye upon her—poor

[023-048] fellow, he had but one, though that was set in such a handsome head—and

[023-049] said slowly: “Every artist makes himself born. It is very much harder

[023-050] than the other time, and longer. Your mother did not bring anything into the

[023-051] world to play piano. That you must bring into the world yourself.”

[023-052] This comforted Thea temporarily, for it seemed to give her a chance. But a

[023-053] great deal of the time she was comfortless. Her letters to [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] were

[023-054] brief and businesslike. She was not apt to chatter much, even in the

[023-055] stimulating company of people she liked, and to chatter on paper was simply

[023-056] impossible for her. If she tried to write him anything definite about her work,

[023-057] she immediately scratched it out as being only partially true, or not true at

[023-058] all. Nothing that she could say about her studies seemed unqualifiedly true,

[023-059] once she put it down on paper.

[023-060] Late one afternoon, when she was thoroughly tired and wanted to struggle on

[023-061] into the dusk, Harsanyi, tired too, threw up his hands and laughed at her.

[023-062] “Not to-day, Miss Kronborg. That sonata will keep; it won’t run

[023-063] away. Even if you and I should not waken up to-morrow, it will be there.”

[023-064] Thea turned to him fiercely. “No, it isn’t here unless I have

[023-065] it—not for me,” she cried passionately. “Only what I hold in

[023-066] my two hands is there for me!”

[023-067] Harsanyi made no reply. He took a deep breath and sat down again. “The

[023-068] second movement now, quietly, with the shoulders relaxed.”

[023-069] There were hours, too, of great exaltation; when she was at her best and became

[023-070] a part of what she was doing and ceased to exist in any other sense. There were

[023-071] other times when she was so shattered by ideas that she could do nothing worth

[023-072] while; when they trampled over her like an army and she felt as if she were

[023-073] bleeding to death under them. She sometimes came home from a late lesson so

[023-074] exhausted that she could eat no supper. If she tried to eat, she was ill

[023-075] afterward. She used to throw herself upon the bed and lie there in the dark,

[023-076] not thinking, not feeling, but evaporating. That same night, perhaps, she would

[023-077] waken up rested and calm, and as she went over her work in her mind, the

[023-078] passages seemed to become something of themselves, to take a sort of pattern in

[023-079] the darkness. She had never learned to work away from the piano until she came

[023-080] to Harsanyi, and it helped her more than anything had ever helped her before.

[023-081] She almost never worked now with the sunny, happy contentment that had filled

[023-082] the hours when she worked with Wunsch—“like a fat horse turning a

[023-083] sorgum mill,” she said bitterly to herself. Then, by sticking to it, she

[023-084] could always do what she set out to do. Now, everything that she really wanted

[023-085] was impossible; a *cantabile* like Harsanyi’s, for instance, instead

[023-086] of her own cloudy tone. No use telling her she might have it in ten years. She

[023-087] wanted it now. She wondered how she had ever found other things interesting:

[023-088] books, “Anna Karenina”—all that seemed so unreal and on the

[023-089] outside of things. She was not born a musician, she decided; there was no other

[023-090] way of explaining it.

[023-091] Sometimes she got so nervous at the piano that she left it, and snatching up

[023-092] her hat and cape went out and walked, hurrying through the streets like

[023-093] Christian fleeing from the City of Destruction. And while she walked she cried.

[023-094] There was scarcely a street in the neighborhood that she had not cried up and

[023-095] down before that winter was over. The thing that used to lie under her cheek,

[023-096] that sat so warmly over her heart when she glided away from the sand hills that

[023-097] autumn morning, was far from her. She had come to [[chicago|Chicago]] to be with it, and it

[023-098] had deserted her, leaving in its place a painful longing, an unresigned

[023-099] despair.

[023-100] Harsanyi knew that his interesting pupil—“the savage blonde,”

[023-101] one of his male students called her—was sometimes very unhappy. He saw in

[023-102] her discontent a curious definition of character. He would have said that a

[023-103] girl with so much musical feeling, so intelligent, with good training of eye

[023-104] and hand, would, when thus suddenly introduced to the great literature of the

[023-105] piano, have found boundless happiness. But he soon learned that she was not

[023-106] able to forget her own poverty in the richness of the world he opened to her.

[023-107] Often when he played to her, her face was the picture of restless misery. She

[023-108] would sit crouching forward, her elbows on her knees, her brows drawn together

[023-109] and her gray-green eyes smaller than ever, reduced to mere pin-points of cold,

[023-110] piercing light. Sometimes, while she listened, she would swallow hard, two or

[023-111] three times, and look nervously from left to right, drawing her shoulders

[023-112] together. “Exactly,” he thought, “as if she were being

[023-113] watched, or as if she were naked and heard some one coming.”

[023-114] On the other hand, when she came several times to see Mrs. Harsanyi and the two

[023-115] babies, she was like a little girl, jolly and gay and eager to play with the

[023-116] children, who loved her. The little daughter, Tanya, liked to touch Miss

[023-117] Kronborg’s yellow hair and pat it, saying, “Dolly, dolly,”

[023-118] because it was of a color much oftener seen on dolls than on people. But if

[023-119] Harsanyi opened the piano and sat down to play, Miss Kronborg gradually drew

[023-120] away from the children, retreated to a corner and became sullen or troubled.

[023-121] Mrs. Harsanyi noticed this, also, and thought it very strange behavior.

[023-122] Another thing that puzzled Harsanyi was Thea’s apparent lack of

[023-123] curiosity. Several times he offered to give her tickets to concerts, but she

[023-124] said she was too tired or that it “knocked her out to be up late.”

[023-125] Harsanyi did not know that she was singing in a choir, and had often to sing at

[023-126] funerals, neither did he realize how much her work with him stirred her and

[023-127] exhausted her. Once, just as she was leaving his studio, he called her back and

[023-128] told her he could give her some tickets that had been sent him for Emma Juch

[023-129] that evening. Thea fingered the black wool on the edge of her plush cape and

[023-130] replied, “Oh, thank you, Mr. Harsanyi, but I have to wash my hair

[023-131] to-night.”

[023-132] Mrs. Harsanyi liked Miss Kronborg thoroughly. She saw in her the making of a

[023-133] pupil who would reflect credit upon Harsanyi. She felt that the girl could be

[023-134] made to look strikingly handsome, and that she had the kind of personality

[023-135] which takes hold of audiences. Moreover, Miss Kronborg was not in the least

[023-136] sentimental about her husband. Sometimes from the show pupils one had to endure

[023-137] a good deal. “I like that girl,” she used to say, when Harsanyi

[023-138] told her of one of Thea’s *gaucheries*. “She doesn’t

[023-139] sigh every time the wind blows. With her one swallow doesn’t make a

[023-140] summer.”

[023-141] Thea told them very little about herself. She was not naturally communicative,

[023-142] and she found it hard to feel confidence in new people. She did not know why,

[023-143] but she could not talk to Harsanyi as she could to Dr. Archie, or to Johnny and

[023-144] Mrs. Tellamantez. With Mr. Larsen she felt more at home, and when she was

[023-145] walking she sometimes stopped at his study to eat candy with him or to hear the

[023-146] plot of the novel he happened to be reading.

[023-147] One evening toward the middle of December Thea was to dine with the Harsanyis.

[023-148] She arrived early, to have time to play with the children before they went to

[023-149] bed. Mrs. Harsanyi took her into her own room and helped her take off her

[023-150] country “fascinator” and her clumsy plush cape. Thea had bought

[023-151] this cape at a big department store and had paid $16.50 for it. As she had

[023-152] never paid more than ten dollars for a coat before, that seemed to her a large

[023-153] price. It was very heavy and not very warm, ornamented with a showy pattern in

[023-154] black disks, and trimmed around the collar and the edges with some kind of

[023-155] black wool that “crocked” badly in snow or rain. It was lined with

[023-156] a cotton stuff called “farmer’s satin.” Mrs. Harsanyi was one

[023-157] woman in a thousand. As she lifted this cape from Thea’s shoulders and

[023-158] laid it on her white bed, she wished that her husband did not have to charge

[023-159] pupils like this one for their lessons. Thea wore her Moonstone party dress,

[023-160] white organdie, made with a “V” neck and elbow sleeves, and a blue

[023-161] sash. She looked very pretty in it, and around her throat she had a string of

[023-162] pink coral and tiny white shells that Ray once brought her from Los Angeles.

[023-163] Mrs. Harsanyi noticed that she wore high heavy shoes which needed blacking. The

[023-164] choir in Mr. Larsen’s church stood behind a railing, so Thea did not pay

[023-165] much attention to her shoes.

[023-166] “You have nothing to do to your hair,” Mrs. Harsanyi said kindly,

[023-167] as Thea turned to the mirror. “However it happens to lie, it’s

[023-168] always pretty. I admire it as much as Tanya does.”

[023-169] Thea glanced awkwardly away from her and looked stern, but Mrs. Harsanyi knew

[023-170] that she was pleased. They went into the living-room, behind the studio, where

[023-171] the two children were playing on the big rug before the coal grate. Andor, the

[023-172] boy, was six, a sturdy, handsome child, and the little girl was four. She came

[023-173] tripping to meet Thea, looking like a little doll in her white net

[023-174] dress—her mother made all her clothes. Thea picked her up and hugged her.

[023-175] Mrs. Harsanyi excused herself and went to the dining-room. She kept only one

[023-176] maid and did a good deal of the housework herself, besides cooking her

[023-177] husband’s favorite dishes for him. She was still under thirty, a slender,

[023-178] graceful woman, gracious, intelligent, and capable. She adapted herself to

[023-179] circumstances with a well-bred ease which solved many of her husband’s

[023-180] difficulties, and kept him, as he said, from feeling cheap and down at the

[023-181] heel. No musician ever had a better wife. Unfortunately her beauty was of a

[023-182] very frail and impressionable kind, and she was beginning to lose it. Her face

[023-183] was too thin now, and there were often dark circles under her eyes.

[023-184] Left alone with the children, Thea sat down on Tanya’s little

[023-185] chair—she would rather have sat on the floor, but was afraid of rumpling

[023-186] her dress—and helped them play “cars” with Andor’s iron

[023-187] railway set. She showed him new ways to lay his tracks and how to make

[023-188] switches, set up his Noah’s ark village for stations and packed the

[023-189] animals in the open coal cars to send them to the stockyards. They worked out

[023-190] their shipment so realistically that when Andor put the two little reindeer

[023-191] into the stock car, Tanya snatched them out and began to cry, saying she

[023-192] wasn’t going to have all their animals killed.

[023-193] Harsanyi came in, jaded and tired, and asked Thea to go on with her game, as he

[023-194] was not equal to talking much before dinner. He sat down and made pretense of

[023-195] glancing at the evening paper, but he soon dropped it. After the railroad began

[023-196] to grow tiresome, Thea went with the children to the lounge in the corner, and

[023-197] played for them the game with which she used to amuse Thor for hours together

[023-198] behind the parlor stove at home, making shadow pictures against the wall with

[023-199] her hands. Her fingers were very supple, and she could make a duck and a cow

[023-200] and a sheep and a fox and a rabbit and even an elephant. Harsanyi, from his low

[023-201] chair, watched them, smiling. The boy was on his knees, jumping up and down

[023-202] with the excitement of guessing the beasts, and Tanya sat with her feet tucked

[023-203] under her and clapped her frail little hands. Thea’s profile, in the

[023-204] lamplight, teased his fancy. Where had he seen a head like it before?

[023-205] When dinner was announced, little Andor took Thea’s hand and walked to

[023-206] the dining-room with her. The children always had dinner with their parents and

[023-207] behaved very nicely at table. “Mamma,” said Andor seriously as he

[023-208] climbed into his chair and tucked his napkin into the collar of his blouse,

[023-209] “Miss Kronborg’s hands are every kind of animal there is.”

[023-210] His father laughed. “I wish somebody would say that about my hands,

[023-211] Andor.”

[023-212] When Thea dined at the Harsanyis before, she noticed that there was an intense

[023-213] suspense from the moment they took their places at the table until the master

[023-214] of the house had tasted the soup. He had a theory that if the soup went well,

[023-215] the dinner would go well; but if the soup was poor, all was lost. To-night he

[023-216] tasted his soup and smiled, and Mrs. Harsanyi sat more easily in her chair and

[023-217] turned her attention to Thea. Thea loved their dinner table, because it was

[023-218] lighted by candles in silver candle-sticks, and she had never seen a table so

[023-219] lighted anywhere else. There were always flowers, too. To-night there was a

[023-220] little orange tree, with oranges on it, that one of Harsanyi’s pupils had

[023-221] sent him at Thanksgiving time. After Harsanyi had finished his soup and a glass

[023-222] of red Hungarian wine, he lost his fagged look and became cordial and witty. He

[023-223] persuaded Thea to drink a little wine to-night. The first time she dined with

[023-224] them, when he urged her to taste the glass of sherry beside her plate, she

[023-225] astonished them by telling them that she “never drank.”

[023-226] Harsanyi was then a man of thirty-two. He was to have a very brilliant career,

[023-227] but he did not know it then. Theodore Thomas was perhaps the only man in

[023-228] Chicago who felt that Harsanyi might have a great future. Harsanyi belonged to

[023-229] the softer Slavic type, and was more like a Pole than a Hungarian. He was tall,

[023-230] slender, active, with sloping, graceful shoulders and long arms. His head was

[023-231] very fine, strongly and delicately modelled, and, as Thea put it, “so

[023-232] independent.” A lock of his thick brown hair usually hung over his

[023-233] forehead. His eye was wonderful; full of light and fire when he was interested,

[023-234] soft and thoughtful when he was tired or melancholy. The meaning and power of

[023-235] two very fine eyes must all have gone into this one—the right one,

[023-236] fortunately, the one next his audience when he played. He believed that the

[023-237] glass eye which gave one side of his face such a dull, blind look, had ruined

[023-238] his career, or rather had made a career impossible for him. Harsanyi lost his

[023-239] eye when he was twelve years old, in a Pennsylvania mining town where

[023-240] explosives happened to be kept too near the frame shanties in which the company

[023-241] packed newly arrived Hungarian families.

[023-242] His father was a musician and a good one, but he had cruelly over-worked the

[023-243] boy; keeping him at the piano for six hours a day and making him play in cafes

[023-244] and dance halls for half the night. Andor ran away and crossed the ocean with

[023-245] an uncle, who smuggled him through the port as one of his own many children.

[023-246] The explosion in which Andor was hurt killed a score of people, and he was

[023-247] thought lucky to get off with an eye. He still had a clipping from a Pittsburg

[023-248] paper, giving a list of the dead and injured. He appeared as “Harsanyi,

[023-249] Andor, left eye and slight injuries about the head.” That was his first

[023-250] American “notice”; and he kept it. He held no grudge against the

[023-251] coal company; he understood that the accident was merely one of the things that

[023-252] are bound to happen in the general scramble of American life, where every one

[023-253] comes to grab and takes his chance.

[023-254] While they were eating dessert, Thea asked Harsanyi if she could change her

[023-255] Tuesday lesson from afternoon to morning. “I have to be at a choir

[023-256] rehearsal in the afternoon, to get ready for the Christmas music, and I expect

[023-257] it will last until late.”

[023-258] Harsanyi put down his fork and looked up. “A choir rehearsal? You sing in

[023-259] a church?”

[023-260] “Yes. A little Swedish church, over on the North side.”

[023-261] “Why did you not tell us?”

[023-262] “Oh, I’m only a temporary. The regular soprano is not well.”

[023-263] “How long have you been singing there?”

[023-264] “Ever since I came. I had to get a position of some kind,” Thea

[023-265] explained, flushing, “and the preacher took me on. He runs the choir

[023-266] himself. He knew my father, and I guess he took me to oblige.”

[023-267] Harsanyi tapped the tablecloth with the ends of his fingers. “But why did

[023-268] you never tell us? Why are you so reticent with us?”

[023-269] Thea looked shyly at him from under her brows. “Well, it’s

[023-270] certainly not very interesting. It’s only a little church. I only do it

[023-271] for business reasons.”

[023-272] “What do you mean? Don’t you like to sing? Don’t you sing

[023-273] well?”

[023-274] “I like it well enough, but, of course, I don’t know anything about

[023-275] singing. I guess that’s why I never said anything about it. Anybody

[023-276] that’s got a voice can sing in a little church like that.”

[023-277] Harsanyi laughed softly—a little scornfully, Thea thought. “So you

[023-278] have a voice, have you?”

[023-279] Thea hesitated, looked intently at the candles and then at Harsanyi.

[023-280] “Yes,” she said firmly; “I have got some, anyway.”

[023-281] “Good girl,” said Mrs. Harsanyi, nodding and smiling at Thea.

[023-282] “You must let us hear you sing after dinner.”

[023-283] This remark seemingly closed the subject, and when the coffee was brought they

[023-284] began to talk of other things. Harsanyi asked Thea how she happened to know so

[023-285] much about the way in which freight trains are operated, and she tried to give

[023-286] him some idea of how the people in little desert towns live by the railway and

[023-287] order their lives by the coming and going of the trains. When they left the

[023-288] diningroom the children were sent to bed and Mrs. Harsanyi took Thea into the

[023-289] studio. She and her husband usually sat there in the evening.

[023-290] Although their apartment seemed so elegant to Thea, it was small and cramped.

[023-291] The studio was the only spacious room. The Harsanyis were poor, and it was due

[023-292] to Mrs. Harsanyi’s good management that their lives, even in hard times,

[023-293] moved along with dignity and order. She had long ago found out that bills or

[023-294] debts of any kind frightened her husband and crippled his working power. He

[023-295] said they were like bars on the windows, and shut out the future; they meant

[023-296] that just so many hundred dollars’ worth of his life was debilitated and

[023-297] exhausted before he got to it. So Mrs. Harsanyi saw to it that they never owed

[023-298] anything. Harsanyi was not extravagant, though he was sometimes careless about

[023-299] money. Quiet and order and his wife’s good taste were the things that

[023-300] meant most to him. After these, good food, good cigars, a little good wine. He

[023-301] wore his clothes until they were shabby, until his wife had to ask the tailor

[023-302] to come to the house and measure him for new ones. His neckties she usually

[023-303] made herself, and when she was in shops she always kept her eye open for silks

[023-304] in very dull or pale shades, grays and olives, warm blacks and browns.

[023-305] When they went into the studio Mrs. Harsanyi took up her embroidery and Thea

[023-306] sat down beside her on a low stool, her hands clasped about her knees. While

[023-307] his wife and his pupil talked, Harsanyi sank into a *chaise longue* in

[023-308] which he sometimes snatched a few moments’ rest between his lessons, and

[023-309] smoked. He sat well out of the circle of the lamplight, his feet to the fire.

[023-310] His feet were slender and well shaped, always elegantly shod. Much of the grace

[023-311] of his movements was due to the fact that his feet were almost as sure and

[023-312] flexible as his hands. He listened to the conversation with amusement. He

[023-313] admired his wife’s tact and kindness with crude young people; she taught

[023-314] them so much without seeming to be instructing. When the clock struck nine,

[023-315] Thea said she must be going home.

[023-316] Harsanyi rose and flung away his cigarette. “Not yet. We have just begun

[023-317] the evening. Now you are going to sing for us. I have been waiting for you to

[023-318] recover from dinner. Come, what shall it be?” he crossed to the piano.

[023-319] Thea laughed and shook her head, locking her elbows still tighter about her

[023-320] knees. “Thank you, Mr. Harsanyi, but if you really make me sing,

[023-321] I’ll accompany myself. You couldn’t stand it to play the sort of

[023-322] things I have to sing.”

[023-323] As Harsanyi still pointed to the chair at the piano, she left her stool and

[023-324] went to it, while he returned to his *chaise longue*. Thea looked at the

[023-325] keyboard uneasily for a moment, then she began “Come, ye

[023-326] Disconsolate,” the hymn Wunsch had always liked to hear her sing. Mrs.

[023-327] Harsanyi glanced questioningly at her husband, but he was looking intently at

[023-328] the toes of his boots, shading his forehead with his long white hand. When Thea

[023-329] finished the hymn she did not turn around, but immediately began “The

[023-330] Ninety and Nine.” Mrs. Harsanyi kept trying to catch her husband’s

[023-331] eye; but his chin only sank lower on his collar.

[023-332] “There were ninety and nine that safely lay\

[023-333] In the shelter of the fold,\

[023-334] But one was out on the hills away,\

[023-335] Far off from the gates of gold.”

[023-336] Harsanyi looked at her, then back at the fire.

[023-337] “Rejoice, for the Shepherd has found his sheep.”

[023-338] Thea turned on the chair and grinned. “That’s about enough,

[023-339] isn’t it? That song got me my job. The preacher said it was

[023-340] sympathetic,” she minced the word, remembering Mr. Larsen’s manner.

[023-341] Harsanyi drew himself up in his chair, resting his elbows on the low arms.
