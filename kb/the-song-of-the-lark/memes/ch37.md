## VI

[037-001] “Thea,” said [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] one drizzly afternoon in April, while

[037-002] they sat waiting for their tea at a restaurant in the Pullman Building,

[037-003] overlooking the lake, “what are you going to do this summer?”

[037-004] “I don’t know. Work, I suppose.”

[037-005] “With Bowers, you mean? Even Bowers goes fishing for a month.

[037-006] [[chicago|Chicago]]’s no place to work, in the summer. Haven’t you made any

[037-007] plans?”

[037-008] Thea shrugged her shoulders. “No use having any plans when you

[037-009] haven’t any money. They are unbecoming.”

[037-010] “Aren’t you going home?”

[037-011] She shook her head. “No. It won’t be comfortable there till

[037-012] I’ve got something to show for myself. I’m not getting on at all,

[037-013] you know. This year has been mostly wasted.”

[037-014] “You’re stale; that’s what’s the matter with you. And

[037-015] just now you’re dead tired. You’ll talk more rationally after

[037-016] you’ve had some tea. Rest your throat until it comes.” They were

[037-017] sitting by a window. As Ottenburg looked at her in the gray light, he

[037-018] remembered what Mrs. Nathanmeyer had said about the Swedish face

[037-019] “breaking early.” Thea was as gray as the weather. Her skin looked

[037-020] sick. Her hair, too, though on a damp day it curled charmingly about her face,

[037-021] looked pale.

[037-022] Fred beckoned the waiter and increased his order for food. Thea did not hear

[037-023] him. She was staring out of the window, down at the roof of the Art Institute

[037-024] and the green lions, dripping in the rain. The lake was all rolling mist, with

[037-025] a soft shimmer of robin’s-egg blue in the gray. A lumber boat, with two

[037-026] very tall masts, was emerging gaunt and black out of the fog. When the tea came

[037-027] Thea ate hungrily, and Fred watched her. He thought her eyes became a little

[037-028] less bleak. The kettle sang cheerfully over the spirit lamp, and she seemed to

[037-029] concentrate her attention upon that pleasant sound. She kept looking toward it

[037-030] listlessly and indulgently, in a way that gave him a realization of her

[037-031] loneliness. Fred lit a cigarette and smoked thoughtfully. He and Thea were

[037-032] alone in the quiet, dusky room full of white tables. In those days Chicago

[037-033] people never stopped for tea. “Come,” he said at last, “what

[037-034] would you do this summer, if you could do whatever you wished?”

[037-035] “I’d go a long way from here! West, I think. Maybe I could get some

[037-036] of my spring back. All this cold, cloudy weather,”—she looked out

[037-037] at the lake and shivered,—“I don’t know, it does things to

[037-038] me,” she ended abruptly.

[037-039] Fred nodded. “I know. You’ve been going down ever since you had

[037-040] tonsilitis. I’ve seen it. What you need is to sit in the sun and bake for

[037-041] three months. You’ve got the right idea. I remember once when we were

[037-042] having dinner somewhere you kept asking me about the Cliff-Dweller ruins. Do

[037-043] they still interest you?”

[037-044] “Of course they do. I’ve always wanted to go down there—long

[037-045] before I ever got in for this.”

[037-046] “I don’t think I told you, but my father owns a whole canyon full

[037-047] of Cliff-Dweller ruins. He has a big worthless ranch down in Arizona, near a

[037-048] Navajo reservation, and there’s a canyon on the place they call Panther

[037-049] Canyon, chock full of that sort of thing. I often go down there to hunt. Henry

[037-050] Biltmer and his wife live there and keep a tidy place. He’s an old German

[037-051] who worked in the brewery until he lost his health. Now he runs a few cattle.

[037-052] Henry likes to do me a favor. I’ve done a few for him.” Fred

[037-053] drowned his cigarette in his saucer and studied Thea’s expression, which

[037-054] was wistful and intent, envious and admiring. He continued with satisfaction:

[037-055] “If you went down there and stayed with them for two or three months,

[037-056] they wouldn’t let you pay anything. I might send Henry a new gun, but

[037-057] even I couldn’t offer him money for putting up a friend of mine.

[037-058] I’ll get you transportation. It would make a new girl of you. Let me

[037-059] write to Henry, and you pack your trunk. That’s all that’s

[037-060] necessary. No red tape about it. What do you say, Thea?”

[037-061] She bit her lip, and sighed as if she were waking up.

[037-062] Fred crumpled his napkin impatiently. “Well, isn’t it easy

[037-063] enough?”

[037-064] “That’s the trouble; it’s *too* easy. Doesn’t

[037-065] sound probable. I’m not used to getting things for nothing.”

[037-066] Ottenburg laughed. “Oh, if that’s all, I’ll show you how to

[037-067] begin. You won’t get this for nothing, quite. I’ll ask you to let

[037-068] me stop off and see you on my way to California. Perhaps by that time you will

[037-069] be glad to see me. Better let me break the news to Bowers. I can manage him. He

[037-070] needs a little transportation himself now and then. You must get corduroy

[037-071] riding-things and leather leggings. There are a few snakes about. Why do you

[037-072] keep frowning?”

[037-073] “Well, I don’t exactly see why you take the trouble. What do you

[037-074] get out of it? You haven’t liked me so well the last two or three

[037-075] weeks.”

[037-076] Fred dropped his third cigarette and looked at his watch. “If you

[037-077] don’t see that, it’s because you need a tonic. I’ll show you

[037-078] what I’ll get out of it. Now I’m going to get a cab and take you

[037-079] home. You are too tired to walk a step. You’d better get to bed as soon

[037-080] as you get there. Of course, I don’t like you so well when you’re

[037-081] half anaesthetized all the time. What have you been doing to yourself?”

[037-082] Thea rose. “I don’t know. Being bored eats the heart out of me, I

[037-083] guess.” She walked meekly in front of him to the elevator. Fred noticed

[037-084] for the hundredth time how vehemently her body proclaimed her state of feeling.

[037-085] He remembered how remarkably brilliant and beautiful she had been when she sang

[037-086] at Mrs. Nathanmeyer’s: flushed and gleaming, round and supple, something

[037-087] that couldn’t be dimmed or downed. And now she seemed a moving figure of

[037-088] discouragement. The very waiters glanced at her apprehensively. It was not that

[037-089] she made a fuss, but her back was most extraordinarily vocal. One never needed

[037-090] to see her face to know what she was full of that day. Yet she was certainly

[037-091] not mercurial. Her flesh seemed to take a mood and to “set,” like

[037-092] plaster. As he put her into the cab, Fred reflected once more that he

[037-093] “gave her up.” He would attack her when his lance was brighter.
