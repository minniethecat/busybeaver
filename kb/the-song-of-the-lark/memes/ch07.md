## VII

[007-001] Thea’s twelfth birthday had passed a few weeks before her memorable call

[007-002] upon Mrs. Tellamantez. There was a worthy man in Moonstone who was already

[007-003] planning to marry Thea as soon as she should be old enough. His name was Ray

[007-004] Kennedy, his age was thirty, and he was conductor on a freight train, his run

[007-005] being from Moonstone to Denver. Ray was a big fellow, with a square, open

[007-006] American face, a rock chin, and features that one would never happen to

[007-007] remember. He was an aggressive idealist, a freethinker, and, like most railroad

[007-008] men, deeply sentimental. Thea liked him for reasons that had to do with the

[007-009] adventurous life he had led in Mexico and the Southwest, rather than for

[007-010] anything very personal. She liked him, too, because he was the only one of her

[007-011] friends who ever took her to the sand hills. The sand hills were a constant

[007-012] tantalization; she loved them better than anything near Moonstone, and yet she

[007-013] could so seldom get to them. The first dunes were accessible enough; they were

[007-014] only a few miles beyond the Kohlers’, and she could run out there any day

[007-015] when she could do her practicing in the morning and get Thor off her hands for

[007-016] an afternoon. But the real hills—the Turquoise Hills, the Mexicans called

[007-017] them—were ten good miles away, and one reached them by a heavy, sandy

[007-018] road. [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] sometimes took Thea on his long drives, but as nobody lived in

[007-019] the sand hills, he never had calls to make in that direction. [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] was

[007-020] her only hope of getting there.

[007-021] This summer Thea had not been to the hills once, though Ray had planned several

[007-022] Sunday expeditions. Once Thor was sick, and once the organist in her

[007-023] father’s church was away and Thea had to play the organ for the three

[007-024] Sunday services. But on the first Sunday in September, Ray drove up to the

[007-025] Kronborgs’ front gate at nine o’clock in the morning and the party

[007-026] actually set off. Gunner and Axel went with Thea, and Ray had asked Spanish

[007-027] Johnny to come and to bring Mrs. Tellamantez and his mandolin. Ray was

[007-028] artlessly fond of music, especially of Mexican music. He and Mrs. Tellamantez

[007-029] had got up the lunch between them, and they were to make coffee in the desert.

[007-030] When they left Mexican Town, Thea was on the front seat with Ray and Johnny,

[007-031] and Gunner and Axel sat behind with Mrs. Tellamantez. They objected to this, of

[007-032] course, but there were some things about which Thea would have her own way.

[007-033] “As stubborn as a Finn,” [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] sometimes said of her,

[007-034] quoting an old Swedish saying. When they passed the Kohlers’, old Fritz

[007-035] and Wunsch were cutting grapes at the arbor. Thea gave them a businesslike nod.

[007-036] Wunsch came to the gate and looked after them. He divined Ray Kennedy’s

[007-037] hopes, and he distrusted every expedition that led away from the piano.

[007-038] Unconsciously he made Thea pay for frivolousness of this sort.

[007-039] As Ray Kennedy’s party followed the faint road across the sagebrush, they

[007-040] heard behind them the sound of church bells, which gave them a sense of escape

[007-041] and boundless freedom. Every rabbit that shot across the path, every sage hen

[007-042] that flew up by the trail, was like a runaway thought, a message that one sent

[007-043] into the desert. As they went farther, the illusion of the mirage became more

[007-044] instead of less convincing; a shallow silver lake that spread for many miles, a

[007-045] little misty in the sunlight. Here and there one saw reflected the image of a

[007-046] heifer, turned loose to live upon the sparse sand-grass. They were magnified to

[007-047] a preposterous height and looked like mammoths, prehistoric beasts standing

[007-048] solitary in the waters that for many thousands of years actually washed over

[007-049] that desert;—the mirage itself may be the ghost of that long-vanished

[007-050] sea. Beyond the phantom lake lay the line of many-colored hills; rich,

[007-051] sun-baked yellow, glowing turquoise, lavender, purple; all the open, pastel

[007-052] colors of the desert.

[007-053] After the first five miles the road grew heavier. The horses had to slow down

[007-054] to a walk and the wheels sank deep into the sand, which now lay in long ridges,

[007-055] like waves, where the last high wind had drifted it. Two hours brought the

[007-056] party to Pedro’s Cup, named for a Mexican desperado who had once held the

[007-057] sheriff at bay there. The Cup was a great amphitheater, cut out in the hills,

[007-058] its floor smooth and packed hard, dotted with sagebrush and greasewood.

[007-059] On either side of the Cup the yellow hills ran north and south, with winding

[007-060] ravines between them, full of soft sand which drained down from the crumbling

[007-061] banks. On the surface of this fluid sand, one could find bits of brilliant

[007-062] stone, crystals and agates and onyx, and petrified wood as red as blood. Dried

[007-063] toads and lizards were to be found there, too. Birds, decomposing more rapidly,

[007-064] left only feathered skeletons.

[007-065] After a little reconnoitering, Mrs. Tellamantez declared that it was time for

[007-066] lunch, and Ray took his hatchet and began to cut greasewood, which burns

[007-067] fiercely in its green state. The little boys dragged the bushes to the spot

[007-068] that Mrs. Tellamantez had chosen for her fire. Mexican women like to cook out

[007-069] of doors.

[007-070] After lunch Thea sent Gunner and Axel to hunt for agates. “If you see a

[007-071] rattlesnake, run. Don’t try to kill it,” she enjoined.

[007-072] Gunner hesitated. “If Ray would let me take the hatchet, I could kill one

[007-073] all right.”

[007-074] Mrs. Tellamantez smiled and said something to Johnny in Spanish.

[007-075] “Yes,” her husband replied, translating, “they say in Mexico,

[007-076] kill a snake but never hurt his feelings. Down in the hot country,

[007-077] *muchacha*,” turning to Thea, “people keep a pet snake in the

[007-078] house to kill rats and mice. They call him the house snake. They keep a little

[007-079] mat for him by the fire, and at night he curl up there and sit with the family,

[007-080] just as friendly!”

[007-081] Gunner sniffed with disgust. “Well, I think that’s a dirty Mexican

[007-082] way to keep house; so there!”

[007-083] Johnny shrugged his shoulders. “Perhaps,” he muttered. A Mexican

[007-084] learns to dive below insults or soar above them, after he crosses the border.

[007-085] By this time the south wall of the amphitheater cast a narrow shelf of shadow,

[007-086] and the party withdrew to this refuge. Ray and Johnny began to talk about the

[007-087] Grand Canyon and Death Valley, two places much shrouded in mystery in those

[007-088] days, and Thea listened intently. Mrs. Tellamantez took out her drawn-work and

[007-089] pinned it to her knee. Ray could talk well about the large part of the

[007-090] continent over which he had been knocked about, and Johnny was appreciative.

[007-091] “You been all over, pretty near. Like a Spanish boy,” he commented

[007-092] respectfully.

[007-093] Ray, who had taken off his coat, whetted his pocketknife thoughtfully on the

[007-094] sole of his shoe. “I began to browse around early. I had a mind to see

[007-095] something of this world, and I ran away from home before I was twelve. Rustled

[007-096] for myself ever since.”

[007-097] “Ran away?” Johnny looked hopeful. “What for?”

[007-098] “Couldn’t make it go with my old man, and didn’t take to

[007-099] farming. There were plenty of boys at home. I wasn’t missed.”

[007-100] Thea wriggled down in the hot sand and rested her chin on her arm. “Tell

[007-101] Johnny about the melons, Ray, please do!”

[007-102] Ray’s solid, sunburned cheeks grew a shade redder, and he looked

[007-103] reproachfully at Thea. “You’re stuck on that story, kid. You like

[007-104] to get the laugh on me, don’t you? That was the finishing split I had

[007-105] with my old man, John. He had a claim along the creek, not far from Denver, and

[007-106] raised a little garden stuff for market. One day he had a load of melons and he

[007-107] decided to take ’em to town and sell ’em along the street, and he

[007-108] made me go along and drive for him. Denver wasn’t the queen city it is

[007-109] now, by any means, but it seemed a terrible big place to me; and when we got

[007-110] there, if he didn’t make me drive right up Capitol Hill! Pap got out and

[007-111] stopped at folkses houses to ask if they didn’t want to buy any melons,

[007-112] and I was to drive along slow. The farther I went the madder I got, but I was

[007-113] trying to look unconscious, when the end-gate came loose and one of the melons

[007-114] fell out and squashed. Just then a swell girl, all dressed up, comes out of one

[007-115] of the big houses and calls out, ‘Hello, boy, you’re losing your

[007-116] melons!’ Some dudes on the other side of the street took their hats off

[007-117] to her and began to laugh. I couldn’t stand it any longer. I grabbed the

[007-118] whip and lit into that team, and they tore up the hill like jack-rabbits, them

[007-119] damned melons bouncing out the back every jump, the old man cussin’

[007-120] an’ yellin’ behind and everybody laughin’. I never looked

[007-121] behind, but the whole of Capitol Hill must have been a mess with them squashed

[007-122] melons. I didn’t stop the team till I got out of sight of town. Then I

[007-123] pulled up an’ left ’em with a rancher I was acquainted with, and I

[007-124] never went home to get the lickin’ that was waitin’ for me. I

[007-125] expect it’s waitin’ for me yet.”

[007-126] Thea rolled over in the sand. “Oh, I wish I could have seen those melons

[007-127] fly, Ray! I’ll never see anything as funny as that. Now, tell Johnny

[007-128] about your first job.”

[007-129] Ray had a collection of good stories. He was observant, truthful, and

[007-130] kindly—perhaps the chief requisites in a good story-teller. Occasionally

[007-131] he used newspaper phrases, conscientiously learned in his efforts at

[007-132] self-instruction, but when he talked naturally he was always worth listening

[007-133] to. Never having had any schooling to speak of, he had, almost from the time he

[007-134] first ran away, tried to make good his loss. As a sheep-herder he had worried

[007-135] an old grammar to tatters, and read instructive books with the help of a pocket

[007-136] dictionary. By the light of many camp-fires he had pondered upon

[007-137] Prescott’s histories, and the works of Washington Irving, which he bought

[007-138] at a high price from a book-agent. Mathematics and physics were easy for him,

[007-139] but general culture came hard, and he was determined to get it. Ray was a

[007-140] freethinker, and inconsistently believed himself damned for being one. When he

[007-141] was braking, down on the Santa Fé, at the end of his run he used to climb into

[007-142] the upper bunk of the caboose, while a noisy gang played poker about the stove

[007-143] below him, and by the roof-lamp read Robert Ingersoll’s speeches and

[007-144] “The Age of Reason.”

[007-145] Ray was a loyal-hearted fellow, and it had cost him a great deal to give up his

[007-146] God. He was one of the stepchildren of Fortune, and he had very little to show

[007-147] for all his hard work; the other fellow always got the best of it. He had come

[007-148] in too late, or too early, on several schemes that had made money. He brought

[007-149] with him from all his wanderings a good deal of information (more or less

[007-150] correct in itself, but unrelated, and therefore misleading), a high standard of

[007-151] personal honor, a sentimental veneration for all women, bad as well as good,

[007-152] and a bitter hatred of Englishmen. Thea often thought that the nicest thing

[007-153] about Ray was his love for Mexico and the Mexicans, who had been kind to him

[007-154] when he drifted, a homeless boy, over the border. In Mexico, Ray was Señor

[007-155] Ken-áy-dy, and when he answered to that name he was somehow a different fellow.

[007-156] He spoke Spanish fluently, and the sunny warmth of that tongue kept him from

[007-157] being quite as hard as his chin, or as narrow as his popular science.

[007-158] While Ray was smoking his cigar, he and Johnny fell to talking about the great

[007-159] fortunes that had been made in the Southwest, and about fellows they knew who

[007-160] had “struck it rich.”

[007-161] “I guess you been in on some big deals down there?” Johnny asked

[007-162] trustfully.

[007-163] Ray smiled and shook his head. “I’ve been out on some, John.

[007-164] I’ve never been exactly in on any. So far, I’ve either held on too

[007-165] long or let go too soon. But mine’s coming to me, all right.” Ray

[007-166] looked reflective. He leaned back in the shadow and dug out a rest for his

[007-167] elbow in the sand. “The narrowest escape I ever had, was in the Bridal

[007-168] Chamber. If I hadn’t let go there, it would have made me rich. That was a

[007-169] close call.”

[007-170] Johnny looked delighted. “You don’ say! She was silver mine, I

[007-171] guess?”

[007-172] “I guess she was! Down at Lake Valley. I put up a few hundred for the

[007-173] prospector, and he gave me a bunch of stock. Before we’d got anything out

[007-174] of it, my brother-in-law died of the fever in Cuba. My sister was beside

[007-175] herself to get his body back to Colorado to bury him. Seemed foolish to me, but

[007-176] she’s the only sister I got. It’s expensive for dead folks to

[007-177] travel, and I had to sell my stock in the mine to raise the money to get Elmer

[007-178] on the move. Two months afterward, the boys struck that big pocket in the rock,

[007-179] full of virgin silver. They named her the Bridal Chamber. It wasn’t ore,

[007-180] you remember. It was pure, soft metal you could have melted right down into

[007-181] dollars. The boys cut it out with chisels. If old Elmer hadn’t played

[007-182] that trick on me, I’d have been in for about fifty thousand. That was a

[007-183] close call, Spanish.”

[007-184] “I recollec’. When the pocket gone, the town go bust.”

[007-185] “You bet. Higher’n a kite. There was no vein, just a pocket in the

[007-186] rock that had sometime or another got filled up with molten silver. You’d

[007-187] think there would be more somewhere about, but *nada*. There’s fools

[007-188] digging holes in that mountain yet.”

[007-189] When Ray had finished his cigar, Johnny took his mandolin and began

[007-190] Kennedy’s favorite, “Ultimo Amor.” It was now three

[007-191] o’clock in the afternoon, the hottest hour in the day. The narrow shelf

[007-192] of shadow had widened until the floor of the amphitheater was marked off in two

[007-193] halves, one glittering yellow, and one purple. The little boys had come back

[007-194] and were making a robbers’ cave to enact the bold deeds of Pedro the

[007-195] bandit. Johnny, stretched gracefully on the sand, passed from “Ultimo

[007-196] Amor” to “Fluvia de Oro,” and then to “Noches de

[007-197] Algeria,” playing languidly.

[007-198] Every one was busy with his own thoughts. Mrs. Tellamantez was thinking of the

[007-199] square in the little town in which she was born; of the white church steps,

[007-200] with people genuflecting as they passed, and the round-topped acacia trees, and

[007-201] the band playing in the plaza. Ray Kennedy was thinking of the future, dreaming

[007-202] the large Western dream of easy money, of a fortune kicked up somewhere in the

[007-203] hills,—an oil well, a gold mine, a ledge of copper. He always told

[007-204] himself, when he accepted a cigar from a newly married railroad man, that he

[007-205] knew enough not to marry until he had found his ideal, and could keep her like

[007-206] a queen. He believed that in the yellow head over there in the sand he had

[007-207] found his ideal, and that by the time she was old enough to marry, he would be

[007-208] able to keep her like a queen. He would kick it up from somewhere, when he got

[007-209] loose from the railroad.

[007-210] Thea, stirred by tales of adventure, of the Grand Canyon and Death Valley, was

[007-211] recalling a great adventure of her own. Early in the summer her father had been

[007-212] invited to conduct a reunion of old frontiersmen, up in Wyoming, near Laramie,

[007-213] and he took Thea along with him to play the organ and sing patriotic songs.

[007-214] There they stayed at the house of an old ranchman who told them about a ridge

[007-215] up in the hills called Laramie Plain, where the wagon-trails of the

[007-216] Forty-niners and the Mormons were still visible. The old man even volunteered

[007-217] to take Mr. Kronborg up into the hills to see this place, though it was a very

[007-218] long drive to make in one day. Thea had begged frantically to go along, and the

[007-219] old rancher, flattered by her rapt attention to his stories, had interceded for

[007-220] her.

[007-221] They set out from Laramie before daylight, behind a strong team of mules. All

[007-222] the way there was much talk of the Forty-niners. The old rancher had been a

[007-223] teamster in a freight train that used to crawl back and forth across the plains

[007-224] between Omaha and Cherry Creek, as Denver was then called, and he had met many

[007-225] a wagon train bound for California. He told of Indians and buffalo, thirst and

[007-226] slaughter, wanderings in snowstorms, and lonely graves in the desert.

[007-227] The road they followed was a wild and beautiful one. It led up and up, by

[007-228] granite rocks and stunted pines, around deep ravines and echoing gorges. The

[007-229] top of the ridge, when they reached it, was a great flat plain, strewn with

[007-230] white boulders, with the wind howling over it. There was not one trail, as Thea

[007-231] had expected; there were a score; deep furrows, cut in the earth by heavy wagon

[007-232] wheels, and now grown over with dry, whitish grass. The furrows ran side by

[007-233] side; when one trail had been worn too deep, the next party had abandoned it

[007-234] and made a new trail to the right or left. They were, indeed, only old wagon

[007-235] ruts, running east and west, and grown over with grass. But as Thea ran about

[007-236] among the white stones, her skirts blowing this way and that, the wind brought

[007-237] to her eyes tears that might have come anyway. The old rancher picked up an

[007-238] iron ox-shoe from one of the furrows and gave it to her for a keepsake. To the

[007-239] west one could see range after range of blue mountains, and at last the snowy

[007-240] range, with its white, windy peaks, the clouds caught here and there on their

[007-241] spurs. Again and again Thea had to hide her face from the cold for a moment.

[007-242] The wind never slept on this plain, the old man said. Every little while eagles

[007-243] flew over.

[007-244] Coming up from Laramie, the old man had told them that he was in Brownsville,

[007-245] Nebraska, when the first telegraph wires were put across the Missouri River,

[007-246] and that the first message that ever crossed the river was “Westward the

[007-247] course of Empire takes its way.” He had been in the room when the

[007-248] instrument began to click, and all the men there had, without thinking what

[007-249] they were doing, taken off their hats, waiting bareheaded to hear the message

[007-250] translated. Thea remembered that message when she sighted down the wagon tracks

[007-251] toward the blue mountains. She told herself she would never, never forget it.

[007-252] The spirit of human courage seemed to live up there with the eagles. For long

[007-253] after, when she was moved by a Fourth-of-July oration, or a band, or a circus

[007-254] parade, she was apt to remember that windy ridge.

[007-255] To-day she went to sleep while she was thinking about it. When Ray wakened her,

[007-256] the horses were hitched to the wagon and Gunner and Axel were begging for a

[007-257] place on the front seat. The air had cooled, the sun was setting, and the

[007-258] desert was on fire. Thea contentedly took the back seat with Mrs. Tellamantez.

[007-259] As they drove homeward the stars began to come out, pale yellow in a yellow

[007-260] sky, and Ray and Johnny began to sing one of those railroad ditties that are

[007-261] usually born on the Southern Pacific and run the length of the Santa Fé and the

[007-262] “Q” system before they die to give place to a new one. This was a

[007-263] song about a Greaser dance, the refrain being something like this:—

[007-264] “Pedró, Pedró, swing high, swing low,\

[007-265] And it’s allamand left again;\

[007-266] For there’s boys that’s bold and there’s some that’s

[007-267] cold,\

[007-268] But the góld boys come from Spain,\

[007-269] Oh, the góld boys come from Spain!”
