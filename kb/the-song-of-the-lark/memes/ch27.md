## VII

[027-001] All through the lesson Thea had felt that Harsanyi was restless and abstracted.

[027-002] Before the hour was over, he pushed back his chair and said resolutely,

[027-003] “I am not in the mood, Miss Kronborg. I have something on my mind, and I

[027-004] must talk to you. When do you intend to go home?”

[027-005] Thea turned to him in surprise. “The first of June, about. Mr. Larsen

[027-006] will not need me after that, and I have not much money ahead. I shall work hard

[027-007] this summer, though.”

[027-008] “And to-day is the first of May; May-day.” Harsanyi leaned forward,

[027-009] his elbows on his knees, his hands locked between them. “Yes, I must talk

[027-010] to you about something. I have asked [[madison-bowers|Madison Bowers]] to let me bring you to him

[027-011] on Thursday, at your usual lesson-time. He is the best vocal teacher in

[027-012] [[chicago|Chicago]], and it is time you began to work seriously with your voice.”

[027-013] Thea’s brow wrinkled. “You mean take lessons of Bowers?”

[027-014] Harsanyi nodded, without lifting his head.

[027-015] “But I can’t, Mr. Harsanyi. I haven’t got the time, and,

[027-016] besides—” she blushed and drew her shoulders up

[027-017] stiffly—“besides, I can’t afford to pay two teachers.”

[027-018] Thea felt that she had blurted this out in the worst possible way, and she

[027-019] turned back to the keyboard to hide her chagrin.

[027-020] “I know that. I don’t mean that you shall pay two teachers. After

[027-021] you go to Bowers you will not need me. I need scarcely tell you that I

[027-022] shan’t be happy at losing you.”

[027-023] Thea turned to him, hurt and angry. “But I don’t want to go to

[027-024] Bowers. I don’t want to leave you. What’s the matter? Don’t I

[027-025] work hard enough? I’m sure you teach people that don’t try half as

[027-026] hard.”

[027-027] Harsanyi rose to his feet. “Don’t misunderstand me, Miss Kronborg.

[027-028] You interest me more than any pupil I have. I have been thinking for months

[027-029] about what you ought to do, since that night when you first sang for me.”

[027-030] He walked over to the window, turned, and came toward her again. “I

[027-031] believe that your voice is worth all that you can put into it. I have not come

[027-032] to this decision rashly. I have studied you, and I have become more and more

[027-033] convinced, against my own desires. I cannot make a singer of you, so it was my

[027-034] business to find a man who could. I have even consulted Theodore Thomas about

[027-035] it.”

[027-036] “But suppose I don’t want to be a singer? I want to study with you.

[027-037] What’s the matter? Do you really think I’ve no talent? Can’t

[027-038] I be a pianist?”

[027-039] Harsanyi paced up and down the long rug in front of her. “My girl, you

[027-040] are very talented. You could be a pianist, a good one. But the early training

[027-041] of a pianist, such a pianist as you would want to be, must be something

[027-042] tremendous. He must have had no other life than music. At your age he must be

[027-043] the master of his instrument. Nothing can ever take the place of that first

[027-044] training. You know very well that your technique is good, but it is not

[027-045] remarkable. It will never overtake your intelligence. You have a fine power of

[027-046] work, but you are not by nature a student. You are not by nature, I think, a

[027-047] pianist. You would never find yourself. In the effort to do so, I’m

[027-048] afraid your playing would become warped, eccentric.” He threw back his

[027-049] head and looked at his pupil intently with that one eye which sometimes seemed

[027-050] to see deeper than any two eyes, as if its singleness gave it privileges.

[027-051] “Oh, I have watched you very carefully, Miss Kronborg. Because you had

[027-052] had so little and had yet done so much for yourself, I had a great wish to help

[027-053] you. I believe that the strongest need of your nature is to find yourself, to

[027-054] emerge AS yourself. Until I heard you sing I wondered how you were to do this,

[027-055] but it has grown clearer to me every day.”

[027-056] Thea looked away toward the window with hard, narrow eyes. “You mean I

[027-057] can be a singer because I haven’t brains enough to be a pianist.”

[027-058] “You have brains enough and talent enough. But to do what you will want

[027-059] to do, it takes more than these—it takes vocation. Now, I think you have

[027-060] vocation, but for the voice, not for the piano. If you knew,”—he

[027-061] stopped and sighed,—“if you knew how fortunate I sometimes think

[027-062] you. With the voice the way is so much shorter, the rewards are more easily

[027-063] won. In your voice I think Nature herself did for you what it would take you

[027-064] many years to do at the piano. Perhaps you were not born in the wrong place

[027-065] after all. Let us talk frankly now. We have never done so before, and I have

[027-066] respected your reticence. What you want more than anything else in the world is

[027-067] to be an artist; is that true?”

[027-068] She turned her face away from him and looked down at the keyboard. Her answer

[027-069] came in a thickened voice. “Yes, I suppose so.”

[027-070] “When did you first feel that you wanted to be an artist?”

[027-071] “I don’t know. There was always—something.”

[027-072] “Did you never think that you were going to sing?”

[027-073] “Yes.”

[027-074] “How long ago was that?”

[027-075] “Always, until I came to you. It was you who made me want to play

[027-076] piano.” Her voice trembled. “Before, I tried to think I did, but I

[027-077] was pretending.”

[027-078] Harsanyi reached out and caught the hand that was hanging at her side. He

[027-079] pressed it as if to give her something. “Can’t you see, my dear

[027-080] girl, that was only because I happened to be the first artist you have ever

[027-081] known? If I had been a trombone player, it would have been the same; you would

[027-082] have wanted to play trombone. But all the while you have been working with such

[027-083] good-will, something has been struggling against me. See, here we were, you and

[027-084] I and this instrument,”—he tapped the piano,—“three

[027-085] good friends, working so hard. But all the while there was something fighting

[027-086] us: your gift, and the woman you were meant to be. When you find your way to

[027-087] that gift and to that woman, you will be at peace. In the beginning it was an

[027-088] artist that you wanted to be; well, you may be an artist, always.”

[027-089] Thea drew a long breath. Her hands fell in her lap. “So I’m just

[027-090] where I began. No teacher, nothing done. No money.”

[027-091] Harsanyi turned away. “Feel no apprehension about the money, Miss

[027-092] Kronborg. Come back in the fall and we shall manage that. I shall even go to

[027-093] Mr. Thomas if necessary. This year will not be lost. If you but knew what an

[027-094] advantage this winter’s study, all your study of the piano, will give you

[027-095] over most singers. Perhaps things have come out better for you than if we had

[027-096] planned them knowingly.”

[027-097] “You mean they have *if* I can sing.”

[027-098] Thea spoke with a heavy irony, so heavy, indeed, that it was coarse. It grated

[027-099] upon Harsanyi because he felt that it was not sincere, an awkward affectation.

[027-100] He wheeled toward her. “Miss Kronborg, answer me this. *You know that

[027-101] you can sing*, do you not? You have always known it. While we worked here

[027-102] together you sometimes said to yourself, ‘I have something you know

[027-103] nothing about; I could surprise you.’ Is that also true?”

[027-104] Thea nodded and hung her head.

[027-105] “Why were you not frank with me? Did I not deserve it?”

[027-106] She shuddered. Her bent shoulders trembled. “I don’t know,”

[027-107] she muttered. “I didn’t mean to be like that. I couldn’t. I

[027-108] can’t. It’s different.”

[027-109] “You mean it is very personal?” he asked kindly.

[027-110] She nodded. “Not at church or funerals, or with people like Mr. Larsen.

[027-111] But with you it was—personal. I’m not like you and Mrs. Harsanyi. I

[027-112] come of rough people. I’m rough. But I’m independent, too. It

[027-113] was—all I had. There is no use my talking, Mr. Harsanyi. I can’t

[027-114] tell you.”

[027-115] “You needn’t tell me. I know. Every artist knows.” Harsanyi

[027-116] stood looking at his pupil’s back, bent as if she were pushing something,

[027-117] at her lowered head. “You can sing for those people because with them you

[027-118] do not commit yourself. But the reality, one cannot uncover *that* until

[027-119] one is sure. One can fail one’s self, but one must not live to see that

[027-120] fail; better never reveal it. Let me help you to make yourself sure of it. That

[027-121] I can do better than Bowers.”

[027-122] Thea lifted her face and threw out her hands.

[027-123] Harsanyi shook his head and smiled. “Oh, promise nothing! You will have

[027-124] much to do. There will not be voice only, but French, German, Italian. You will

[027-125] have work enough. But sometimes you will need to be understood; what you never

[027-126] show to any one will need companionship. And then you must come to me.”

[027-127] He peered into her face with that searching, intimate glance. “You know

[027-128] what I mean, the thing in you that has no business with what is little, that

[027-129] will have to do only with beauty and power.”

[027-130] Thea threw out her hands fiercely, as if to push him away. She made a sound in

[027-131] her throat, but it was not articulate. Harsanyi took one of her hands and

[027-132] kissed it lightly upon the back. His salute was one of greeting, not of

[027-133] farewell, and it was for some one he had never seen.

[027-134] When Mrs. Harsanyi came in at six o’clock, she found her husband sitting

[027-135] listlessly by the window. “Tired?” she asked.

[027-136] “A little. I’ve just got through a difficulty. I’ve sent Miss

[027-137] Kronborg away; turned her over to Bowers, for voice.”

[027-138] “Sent Miss Kronborg away? Andor, what is the matter with you?”

[027-139] “It’s nothing rash. I’ve known for a long while I ought to do

[027-140] it. She is made for a singer, not a pianist.”

[027-141] Mrs. Harsanyi sat down on the piano chair. She spoke a little bitterly:

[027-142] “How can you be sure of that? She was, at least, the best you had. I

[027-143] thought you meant to have her play at your students’ recital next fall. I

[027-144] am sure she would have made an impression. I could have dressed her so that she

[027-145] would have been very striking. She had so much individuality.”

[027-146] Harsanyi bent forward, looking at the floor. “Yes, I know. I shall miss

[027-147] her, of course.”

[027-148] Mrs. Harsanyi looked at her husband’s fine head against the gray window.

[027-149] She had never felt deeper tenderness for him than she did at that moment. Her

[027-150] heart ached for him. “You will never get on, Andor,” she said

[027-151] mournfully.

[027-152] Harsanyi sat motionless. “No, I shall never get on,” he repeated

[027-153] quietly. Suddenly he sprang up with that light movement she knew so well, and

[027-154] stood in the window, with folded arms. “But some day I shall be able to

[027-155] look her in the face and laugh because I did what I could for her. I believe in

[027-156] her. She will do nothing common. She is uncommon, in a common, common world.

[027-157] That is what I get out of it. It means more to me than if she played at my

[027-158] concert and brought me a dozen pupils. All this drudgery will kill me if once

[027-159] in a while I cannot hope something, for somebody! If I cannot sometimes see a

[027-160] bird fly and wave my hand to it.”

[027-161] His tone was angry and injured. Mrs. Harsanyi understood that this was one of

[027-162] the times when his wife was a part of the drudgery, of the “common,

[027-163] common world.”

[027-164] He had let something he cared for go, and he felt bitterly about whatever was

[027-165] left. The mood would pass, and he would be sorry. She knew him. It wounded her,

[027-166] of course, but that hurt was not new. It was as old as her love for him. She

[027-167] went out and left him alone.
