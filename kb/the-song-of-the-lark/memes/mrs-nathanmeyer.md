## Overview

Mrs. Nathanmeyer is a wealthy Jewish patroness of music in Chicago who, together with her husband Henry Nathanmeyer, hosts Thea Kronborg's pivotal debut concert. Described as "a heavy, powerful old Jewess, with a great pompadour of" [035-123] hair, she presides over a mansion with a great library and a music-room that becomes the setting for Thea's first real public triumph. Mrs. Nathanmeyer represents the alliance of wealth, culture, and social influence that opens doors for the young singer at a critical moment in her career.

## Biography / Character Arc

### The Nathanmeyer Mansion

The Nathanmeyers occupy a position of considerable wealth and cultural authority in Chicago. Their home features a "great library" [035-108] where they receive guests alone — an intimate setting for what is effectively a private concert before Chicago's elite. The house also contains a dedicated music-room, revealed when "Mrs. Nathanmeyer turned a switch" [035-149] to illuminate it — a gesture that is both practical and theatrical, the unveiling of a stage.

### The Debut Concert

The evening of Thea's debut at the Nathanmeyer home is carefully orchestrated. Mrs. Nathanmeyer manages the practical details herself, summoning her maid Selma for the guests' needs [035-152] and arranging for Thea's gown to be packed [035-232]. Her attention to these material details — the dress, the gloves, the timing — reveals a patroness who understands that artistic success depends as much on presentation as on talent.

Before Thea's performance, Ottenburg speaks privately with Mrs. Nathanmeyer [035-157], a moment that suggests the complex social negotiations underlying the evening. The patroness is not merely a neutral host; she is an active participant in the machinery of advancement, someone whose approval must be cultivated.

### After the Concert

The concert proves a success — a turning point in Thea's career. Mrs. Nathanmeyer's endorsement carries weight in Chicago society. Thea "stayed on there because her room" [036-004] at the Nathanmeyer residence remained available to her, the patroness extending hospitality beyond the single evening. The experience at Mrs. Nathanmeyer's becomes a reference point for Thea: weeks later, she "remembered what Mrs. Nathanmeyer had said about the Swedish face" [037-018], an observation about physiognomy and national character that lingers in her mind.

The effect of the Nathanmeyer salon on Thea is lasting. At a later musical evening at the same house, Thea appears "flushed and gleaming, round and supple" [037-086] — transformed, more confident, more fully herself as a performer. Mrs. Nathanmeyer's patronage has not merely provided a venue; it has helped create the conditions for Thea's artistic self-possession.

## Relationships

### Henry Nathanmeyer

Mrs. Nathanmeyer's husband is her partner in patronage. "Mr. and Mrs. Nathanmeyer were alone in their great library" [035-108] when they receive Thea — the two of them a unit, their wealth and cultural authority shared. Henry Nathanmeyer is a wealthy Chicago businessman; together they form one of the city's significant musical households.

### Fred Ottenburg

Ottenburg is the connector who brings Thea to the Nathanmeyers' attention. His conversation with Mrs. Nathanmeyer before the concert [035-157] reveals a relationship of mutual social calculation — he needs her influence, she enjoys the role of discoverer. "She needs it," Ottenburg tells Mrs. Nathanmeyer of Thea [035-032], acknowledging that the patroness's help is not charity but a transaction in the economy of artistic advancement.

### Thea Kronborg

Mrs. Nathanmeyer is the first major society patroness Thea encounters, and the relationship establishes a pattern that will recur throughout Thea's career: wealthy, culturally sophisticated women who take an interest in the young singer's talent and use their social position to advance it. Mrs. Nathanmeyer's practical kindness — arranging the gown, providing a room, hosting the musical evenings — is the material form of this patronage.

## Significance

Mrs. Nathanmeyer belongs to a recognizable type in the novel: the society patroness whose wealth and influence create the conditions for artistic production. Unlike the Kohlers, whose support of music is domestic and modest, the Nathanmeyers operate at the level of high society — their mansion, their library, their music-room all announce a world in which art is both cultivated and displayed.

Her Jewish identity is noted by Cather without elaboration — "a heavy, powerful old Jewess" — and in the Chicago of the novel, Jewish patronage of the arts was a well-established pattern. The Nathanmeyers represent a particular strand of American cultural philanthropy: private wealth deployed in domestic settings to support emerging artists, outside the institutional frameworks of concert halls and conservatories.

Mrs. Nathanmeyer's practical competence — summoning maids, arranging gowns, packing gloves — is as important to her function as her wealth. She knows that art requires infrastructure, and she provides it without fanfare. In this she resembles Mrs. Kohler, though the scale and sophistication of their respective operations differ enormously.
