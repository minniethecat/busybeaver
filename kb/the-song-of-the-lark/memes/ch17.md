## XVII

[017-001] The summer flew by. Thea was glad when [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] had a Sunday in town and

[017-002] could take her driving. Out among the sand hills she could forget the

[017-003] “new room” which was the scene of wearing and fruitless labor. Dr.

[017-004] Archie was away from home a good deal that year. He had put all his money into

[017-005] mines above Colorado Springs, and he hoped for great returns from them.

[017-006] In the fall of that year, Mr. Kronborg decided that Thea ought to show more

[017-007] interest in church work. He put it to her frankly, one night at supper, before

[017-008] the whole family. “How can I insist on the other girls in the

[017-009] congregation being active in the work, when one of my own daughters manifests

[017-010] so little interest?”

[017-011] “But I sing every Sunday morning, and I have to give up one night a week

[017-012] to choir practice,” Thea declared rebelliously, pushing back her plate

[017-013] with an angry determination to eat nothing more.

[017-014] “One night a week is not enough for the pastor’s daughter,”

[017-015] her father replied. “You won’t do anything in the sewing society,

[017-016] and you won’t take part in the Christian Endeavor or the Band of Hope.

[017-017] Very well, you must make it up in other ways. I want some one to play the organ

[017-018] and lead the singing at prayer-meeting this winter. Deacon Potter told me some

[017-019] time ago that he thought there would be more interest in our prayer-meetings if

[017-020] we had the organ. Miss Meyers don’t feel that she can play on Wednesday

[017-021] nights. And there ought to be somebody to start the hymns. Mrs. Potter is

[017-022] getting old, and she always starts them too high. It won’t take much of

[017-023] your time, and it will keep people from talking.”

[017-024] This argument conquered Thea, though she left the table sullenly. The fear of

[017-025] the tongue, that terror of little towns, is usually felt more keenly by the

[017-026] minister’s family than by other households. Whenever the Kronborgs wanted

[017-027] to do anything, even to buy a new carpet, they had to take counsel together as

[017-028] to whether people would talk. [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] had her own conviction that people

[017-029] talked when they felt like it, and said what they chose, no matter how the

[017-030] minister’s family conducted themselves. But she did not impart these

[017-031] dangerous ideas to her children. Thea was still under the belief that public

[017-032] opinion could be placated; that if you clucked often enough, the hens would

[017-033] mistake you for one of themselves.

[017-034] Mrs. Kronborg did not have any particular zest for prayer-meetings, and she

[017-035] stayed at home whenever she had a valid excuse. Thor was too old to furnish

[017-036] such an excuse now, so every Wednesday night, unless one of the children was

[017-037] sick, she trudged off with Thea, behind Mr. Kronborg. At first Thea was

[017-038] terribly bored. But she got used to prayer-meeting, got even to feel a mournful

[017-039] interest in it.

[017-040] The exercises were always pretty much the same. After the first hymn her father

[017-041] read a passage from the Bible, usually a Psalm. Then there was another hymn,

[017-042] and then her father commented upon the passage he had read and, as he said,

[017-043] “applied the Word to our necessities.” After a third hymn, the

[017-044] meeting was declared open, and the old men and women took turns at praying and

[017-045] talking. Mrs. Kronborg never spoke in meeting. She told people firmly that she

[017-046] had been brought up to keep silent and let the men talk, but she gave

[017-047] respectful attention to the others, sitting with her hands folded in her lap.

[017-048] The prayer-meeting audience was always small. The young and energetic members

[017-049] of the congregation came only once or twice a year, “to keep people from

[017-050] talking.” The usual Wednesday night gathering was made up of old women,

[017-051] with perhaps six or eight old men, and a few sickly girls who had not much

[017-052] interest in life; two of them, indeed, were already preparing to die. Thea

[017-053] accepted the mournfulness of the prayer-meetings as a kind of spiritual

[017-054] discipline, like funerals. She always read late after she went home and felt a

[017-055] stronger wish than usual to live and to be happy.

[017-056] The meetings were conducted in the Sunday-School room, where there were wooden

[017-057] chairs instead of pews; an old map of Palestine hung on the wall, and the

[017-058] bracket lamps gave out only a dim light. The old women sat motionless as

[017-059] Indians in their shawls and bonnets; some of them wore long black mourning

[017-060] veils. The old men drooped in their chairs. Every back, every face, every head

[017-061] said “resignation.” Often there were long silences, when you could

[017-062] hear nothing but the crackling of the soft coal in the stove and the muffled

[017-063] cough of one of the sick girls.

[017-064] There was one nice old lady,—tall, erect, self-respecting, with a

[017-065] delicate white face and a soft voice. She never whined, and what she said was

[017-066] always cheerful, though she spoke so nervously that Thea knew she dreaded

[017-067] getting up, and that she made a real sacrifice to, as she said, “testify

[017-068] to the goodness of her Saviour.” She was the mother of the girl who

[017-069] coughed, and Thea used to wonder how she explained things to herself. There

[017-070] was, indeed, only one woman who talked because she was, as Mr. Kronborg said,

[017-071] “tonguey.” The others were somehow impressive. They told about the

[017-072] sweet thoughts that came to them while they were at their work; how, amid their

[017-073] household tasks, they were suddenly lifted by the sense of a divine Presence.

[017-074] Sometimes they told of their first conversion, of how in their youth that

[017-075] higher Power had made itself known to them. Old Mr. Carsen, the carpenter, who

[017-076] gave his services as janitor to the church, used often to tell how, when he was

[017-077] a young man and a scoffer, bent on the destruction of both body and soul, his

[017-078] Saviour had come to him in the Michigan woods and had stood, it seemed to him,

[017-079] beside the tree he was felling; and how he dropped his axe and knelt in prayer

[017-080] “to Him who died for us upon the tree.” Thea always wanted to ask

[017-081] him more about it; about his mysterious wickedness, and about the vision.

[017-082] Sometimes the old people would ask for prayers for their absent children.

[017-083] Sometimes they asked their brothers and sisters in Christ to pray that they

[017-084] might be stronger against temptations. One of the sick girls used to ask them

[017-085] to pray that she might have more faith in the times of depression that came to

[017-086] her, “when all the way before seemed dark.” She repeated that husky

[017-087] phrase so often, that Thea always remembered it.

[017-088] One old woman, who never missed a Wednesday night, and who nearly always took

[017-089] part in the meeting, came all the way up from the depot settlement. She always

[017-090] wore a black crocheted “fascinator” over her thin white hair, and

[017-091] she made long, tremulous prayers, full of railroad terminology. She had six

[017-092] sons in the service of different railroads, and she always prayed “for

[017-093] the boys on the road, who know not at what moment they may be cut off. When, in

[017-094] Thy divine wisdom, their hour is upon them, may they, O our Heavenly Father,

[017-095] see only white lights along the road to Eternity.” She used to speak,

[017-096] too, of “the engines that race with death”; and though she looked

[017-097] so old and little when she was on her knees, and her voice was so shaky, her

[017-098] prayers had a thrill of speed and danger in them; they made one think of the

[017-099] deep black canyons, the slender trestles, the pounding trains. Thea liked to

[017-100] look at her sunken eyes that seemed full of wisdom, at her black thread gloves,

[017-101] much too long in the fingers and so meekly folded one over the other. Her face

[017-102] was brown, and worn away as rocks are worn by water. There are many ways of

[017-103] describing that color of age, but in reality it is not like parchment, or like

[017-104] any of the things it is said to be like. That brownness and that texture of

[017-105] skin are found only in the faces of old human creatures, who have worked hard

[017-106] and who have always been poor.

[017-107] One bitterly cold night in December the prayer-meeting seemed to Thea longer

[017-108] than usual. The prayers and the talks went on and on. It was as if the old

[017-109] people were afraid to go out into the cold, or were stupefied by the hot air of

[017-110] the room. She had left a book at home that she was impatient to get back to. At

[017-111] last the Doxology was sung, but the old people lingered about the stove to

[017-112] greet each other, and Thea took her mother’s arm and hurried out to the

[017-113] frozen sidewalk, before her father could get away. The wind was whistling up

[017-114] the street and whipping the naked cottonwood trees against the telegraph poles

[017-115] and the sides of the houses. Thin snow clouds were flying overhead, so that the

[017-116] sky looked gray, with a dull phosphorescence. The icy streets and the shingle

[017-117] roofs of the houses were gray, too. All along the street, shutters banged or

[017-118] windows rattled, or gates wobbled, held by their latch but shaking on loose

[017-119] hinges. There was not a cat or a dog in Moonstone that night that was not given

[017-120] a warm shelter; the cats under the kitchen stove, the dogs in barns or

[017-121] coal-sheds. When Thea and her mother reached home, their mufflers were covered

[017-122] with ice, where their breath had frozen. They hurried into the house and made a

[017-123] dash for the parlor and the hard-coal burner, behind which Gunner was sitting

[017-124] on a stool, reading his Jules Verne book. The door stood open into the

[017-125] dining-room, which was heated from the parlor. Mr. Kronborg always had a lunch

[017-126] when he came home from prayer-meeting, and his pumpkin pie and milk were set

[017-127] out on the dining-table. Mrs. Kronborg said she thought she felt hungry, too,

[017-128] and asked Thea if she didn’t want something to eat.

[017-129] “No, I’m not hungry, mother. I guess I’ll go upstairs.”

[017-130] “I expect you’ve got some book up there,” said Mrs. Kronborg,

[017-131] bringing out another pie. “You’d better bring it down here and

[017-132] read. Nobody’ll disturb you, and it’s terrible cold up in that

[017-133] loft.”

[017-134] Thea was always assured that no one would disturb her if she read downstairs,

[017-135] but the boys talked when they came in, and her father fairly delivered

[017-136] discourses after he had been renewed by half a pie and a pitcher of milk.

[017-137] “I don’t mind the cold. I’ll take a hot brick up for my feet.

[017-138] I put one in the stove before I left, if one of the boys hasn’t stolen

[017-139] it. Good-night, mother.” Thea got her brick and lantern, and dashed

[017-140] upstairs through the windy loft. She undressed at top speed and got into bed

[017-141] with her brick. She put a pair of white knitted gloves on her hands, and pinned

[017-142] over her head a piece of soft flannel that had been one of Thor’s long

[017-143] petticoats when he was a baby. Thus equipped, she was ready for business. She

[017-144] took from her table a thick paper-backed volume, one of the “line”

[017-145] of paper novels the druggist kept to sell to traveling men. She had bought it,

[017-146] only yesterday, because the first sentence interested her very much, and

[017-147] because she saw, as she glanced over the pages, the magical names of two

[017-148] Russian cities. The book was a poor translation of “Anna Karenina.”

[017-149] Thea opened it at a mark, and fixed her eyes intently upon the small print. The

[017-150] hymns, the sick girl, the resigned black figures were forgotten. It was the

[017-151] night of the ball in Moscow.

[017-152] Thea would have been astonished if she could have known how, years afterward,

[017-153] when she had need of them, those old faces were to come back to her, long after

[017-154] they were hidden away under the earth; that they would seem to her then as full

[017-155] of meaning, as mysteriously marked by Destiny, as the people who danced the

[017-156] mazurka under the elegant Korsunsky.
