## IV

[049-001] At nine o’clock that evening our three friends were seated in the balcony

[049-002] of a French restaurant, much gayer and more intimate than any that exists in

[049-003] New York to-day. This old restaurant was built by a lover of pleasure, who knew

[049-004] that to dine gayly human beings must have the reassurance of certain

[049-005] limitations of space and of a certain definite style; that the walls must be

[049-006] near enough to suggest shelter, the ceiling high enough to give the chandeliers

[049-007] a setting. The place was crowded with the kind of people who dine late and

[049-008] well, and [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]], as he watched the animated groups in the long room below

[049-009] the balcony, found this much the most festive scene he had ever looked out

[049-010] upon. He said to himself, in a jovial mood somewhat sustained by the cheer of

[049-011] the board, that this evening alone was worth his long journey. He followed

[049-012] attentively the orchestra, ensconced at the farther end of the balcony, and

[049-013] told Thea it made him feel “quite musical” to recognize “The

[049-014] Invitation to the Dance” or “The Blue Danube,” and that he

[049-015] could remember just what kind of day it was when he heard her practicing them

[049-016] at home, and lingered at the gate to listen.

[049-017] For the first few moments, when he was introduced to young Ottenburg in the

[049-018] parlor of the Everett House, the doctor had been awkward and unbending. But

[049-019] Fred, as his father had often observed, “was not a good mixer for

[049-020] nothing.” He had brought Dr. Archie around during the short cab ride, and

[049-021] in an hour they had become old friends.

[049-022] From the moment when the doctor lifted his glass and, looking consciously at

[049-023] Thea, said, “To your success,” Fred liked him. He felt his quality;

[049-024] understood his courage in some directions and what Thea called his timidity in

[049-025] others, his unspent and miraculously preserved youthfulness. Men could never

[049-026] impose upon the doctor, he guessed, but women always could. Fred liked, too,

[049-027] the doctor’s manner with Thea, his bashful admiration and the little

[049-028] hesitancy by which he betrayed his consciousness of the change in her. It was

[049-029] just this change that, at present, interested Fred more than anything else.

[049-030] That, he felt, was his “created value,” and it was his best chance

[049-031] for any peace of mind. If that were not real, obvious to an old friend like

[049-032] Archie, then he cut a very poor figure, indeed.

[049-033] Fred got a good deal, too, out of their talk about Moonstone. From her

[049-034] questions and the doctor’s answers he was able to form some conception of

[049-035] the little world that was almost the measure of Thea’s experience, the

[049-036] one bit of the human drama that she had followed with sympathy and

[049-037] understanding. As the two ran over the list of their friends, the mere sound of

[049-038] a name seemed to recall volumes to each of them, to indicate mines of knowledge

[049-039] and observation they had in common. At some names they laughed delightedly, at

[049-040] some indulgently and even tenderly.

[049-041] “You two young people must come out to Moonstone when Thea gets

[049-042] back,” the doctor said hospitably.

[049-043] “Oh, we shall!” Fred caught it up. “I’m keen to know

[049-044] all these people. It is very tantalizing to hear only their names.”

[049-045] “Would they interest an outsider very much, do you think, Dr.

[049-046] Archie?” Thea leaned toward him. “Isn’t it only because

[049-047] we’ve known them since I was little?”

[049-048] The doctor glanced at her deferentially. Fred had noticed that he seemed a

[049-049] little afraid to look at her squarely—perhaps a trifle embarrassed by a

[049-050] mode of dress to which he was unaccustomed. “Well, you are practically an

[049-051] outsider yourself, Thea, now,” he observed smiling. “Oh, I

[049-052] know,” he went on quickly in response to her gesture of

[049-053] protest,—“I know you don’t change toward your old friends,

[049-054] but you can see us all from a distance now. It’s all to your advantage

[049-055] that you can still take your old interest, isn’t it, Mr.

[049-056] Ottenburg?”

[049-057] “That’s exactly one of her advantages, Dr. Archie. Nobody can ever

[049-058] take that away from her, and none of us who came later can ever hope to rival

[049-059] Moonstone in the impression we make. Her scale of values will always be the

[049-060] Moonstone scale. And, with an artist, that *is* an advantage.” Fred

[049-061] nodded.

[049-062] Dr. Archie looked at him seriously. “You mean it keeps them from getting

[049-063] affected?”

[049-064] “Yes; keeps them from getting off the track generally.”

[049-065] While the waiter filled the glasses, Fred pointed out to Thea a big black

[049-066] French barytone who was eating anchovies by their tails at one of the tables

[049-067] below, and the doctor looked about and studied his fellow diners.

[049-068] “Do you know, Mr. Ottenburg,” he said deeply, “these people

[049-069] all look happier to me than our Western people do. Is it simply good manners on

[049-070] their part, or do they get more out of life?”

[049-071] Fred laughed to Thea above the glass he had just lifted. “Some of them

[049-072] are getting a good deal out of it now, doctor. This is the hour when bench-joy

[049-073] brightens.”

[049-074] Thea chuckled and darted him a quick glance. “Benchjoy! Where did you get

[049-075] that slang?”

[049-076] “That happens to be very old slang, my dear. Older than Moonstone or the

[049-077] sovereign State of Colorado. Our old friend Mr. Nathanmeyer could tell us why

[049-078] it happens to hit you.” He leaned forward and touched Thea’s wrist,

[049-079] “See that fur coat just coming in, Thea. It’s D’Albert.

[049-080] He’s just back from his Western tour. Fine head, hasn’t he?”

[049-081] “To go back,” said Dr. Archie; “I insist that people do look

[049-082] happier here. I’ve noticed it even on the street, and especially in the

[049-083] hotels.”

[049-084] Fred turned to him cheerfully. “New York people live a good deal in the

[049-085] fourth dimension, Dr. Archie. It’s that you notice in their faces.”

[049-086] The doctor was interested. “The fourth dimension,” he repeated

[049-087] slowly; “and is that slang, too?”

[049-088] “No,”—Fred shook his head,—“that’s merely a

[049-089] figure. I mean that life is not quite so personal here as it is in your part of

[049-090] the world. People are more taken up by hobbies, interests that are less subject

[049-091] to reverses than their personal affairs. If you’re interested in

[049-092] Thea’s voice, for instance, or in voices in general, that interest is

[049-093] just the same, even if your mining stocks go down.”

[049-094] The doctor looked at him narrowly. “You think that’s about the

[049-095] principal difference between country people and city people, don’t

[049-096] you?”

[049-097] Fred was a little disconcerted at being followed up so resolutely, and he

[049-098] attempted to dismiss it with a pleasantry. “I’ve never thought much

[049-099] about it, doctor. But I should say, on the spur of the moment, that that is one

[049-100] of the principal differences between people anywhere. It’s the

[049-101] consolation of fellows like me who don’t accomplish much. The fourth

[049-102] dimension is not good for business, but we think we have a better time.”

[049-103] Dr. Archie leaned back in his chair. His heavy shoulders were contemplative.

[049-104] “And she,” he said slowly; “should you say that she is one of

[049-105] the kind you refer to?” He inclined his head toward the shimmer of the

[049-106] pale-green dress beside him. Thea was leaning, just then, over the balcony

[049-107] rail, her head in the light from the chandeliers below.

[049-108] “Never, never!” Fred protested. “She’s as hard-headed

[049-109] as the worst of you—with a difference.”

[049-110] The doctor sighed. “Yes, with a difference; something that makes a good

[049-111] many revolutions to the second. When she was little I used to feel her head to

[049-112] try to locate it.”

[049-113] Fred laughed. “Did you, though? So you were on the track of it? Oh,

[049-114] it’s there! We can’t get round it, miss,” as Thea looked back

[049-115] inquiringly. “Dr. Archie, there’s a fellow townsman of yours I feel

[049-116] a real kinship for.” He pressed a cigar upon Dr. Archie and struck a

[049-117] match for him. “Tell me about [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]].”

[049-118] The doctor smiled benignantly through the first waves of smoke. “Well,

[049-119] Johnny’s an old patient of mine, and he’s an old admirer of

[049-120] Thea’s. She was born a cosmopolitan, and I expect she learned a good deal

[049-121] from Johnny when she used to run away and go to Mexican Town. We thought it a

[049-122] queer freak then.”

[049-123] The doctor launched into a long story, in which he was often eagerly

[049-124] interrupted or joyously confirmed by Thea, who was drinking her coffee and

[049-125] forcing open the petals of the roses with an ardent and rather rude hand. Fred

[049-126] settled down into enjoying his comprehension of his guests. Thea, watching Dr.

[049-127] Archie and interested in his presentation, was unconsciously impersonating her

[049-128] suave, gold-tinted friend. It was delightful to see her so radiant and

[049-129] responsive again. She had kept her promise about looking her best; when one

[049-130] could so easily get together the colors of an apple branch in early spring,

[049-131] that was not hard to do. Even Dr. Archie felt, each time he looked at her, a

[049-132] fresh consciousness. He recognized the fine texture of her mother’s skin,

[049-133] with the difference that, when she reached across the table to give him a bunch

[049-134] of grapes, her arm was not only white, but somehow a little dazzling. She

[049-135] seemed to him taller, and freer in all her movements. She had now a way of

[049-136] taking a deep breath when she was interested, that made her seem very strong,

[049-137] somehow, and brought her at one quite overpoweringly. If he seemed shy, it was

[049-138] not that he was intimidated by her worldly clothes, but that her greater

[049-139] positiveness, her whole augmented self, made him feel that his accustomed

[049-140] manner toward her was inadequate.

[049-141] Fred, on his part, was reflecting that the awkward position in which he had

[049-142] placed her would not confine or chafe her long. She looked about at other

[049-143] people, at other women, curiously. She was not quite sure of herself, but she

[049-144] was not in the least afraid or apologetic. She seemed to sit there on the edge,

[049-145] emerging from one world into another, taking her bearings, getting an idea of

[049-146] the concerted movement about her, but with absolute self-confidence. So far

[049-147] from shrinking, she expanded. The mere kindly effort to please Dr. Archie was

[049-148] enough to bring her out.

[049-149] There was much talk of aurae at that time, and Fred mused that every beautiful,

[049-150] every compellingly beautiful woman, had an aura, whether other people did or

[049-151] no. There was, certainly, about the woman he had brought up from Mexico, such

[049-152] an emanation. She existed in more space than she occupied by measurement. The

[049-153] enveloping air about her head and shoulders was subsidized—was more

[049-154] moving than she herself, for in it lived the awakenings, all the first

[049-155] sweetness that life kills in people. One felt in her such a wealth of

[049-156] *Jugendzeit*, all those flowers of the mind and the blood that bloom and

[049-157] perish by the myriad in the few exhaustless years when the imagination first

[049-158] kindles. It was in watching her as she emerged like this, in being near and not

[049-159] too near, that one got, for a moment, so much that one had lost; among other

[049-160] legendary things the legendary theme of the absolutely magical power of a

[049-161] beautiful woman.

[049-162] After they had left Thea at her hotel, Dr. Archie admitted to Fred, as they

[049-163] walked up Broadway through the rapidly chilling air, that once before he had

[049-164] seen their young friend flash up into a more potent self, but in a darker mood.

[049-165] It was in his office one night, when she was at home the summer before last.

[049-166] “And then I got the idea,” he added simply, “that she would

[049-167] not live like other people: that, for better or worse, she had uncommon

[049-168] gifts.”

[049-169] “Oh, we’ll see that it’s for better, you and I,” Fred

[049-170] reassured him. “Won’t you come up to my hotel with me? I think we

[049-171] ought to have a long talk.”

[049-172] “Yes, indeed,” said Dr. Archie gratefully; “I think we

[049-173] ought.”
