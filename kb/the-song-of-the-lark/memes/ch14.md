## XIV

[014-001] “Mother,” said Peter Kronborg to his wife one morning about two

[014-002] weeks after Wunsch’s departure, “how would you like to drive out to

[014-003] Copper Hole with me to-day?”

[014-004] [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] said she thought she would enjoy the drive. She put on her gray

[014-005] cashmere dress and gold watch and chain, as befitted a minister’s wife,

[014-006] and while her husband was dressing she packed a black oilcloth satchel with

[014-007] such clothing as she and Thor would need overnight.

[014-008] Copper Hole was a settlement fifteen miles northwest of Moonstone where Mr.

[014-009] Kronborg preached every Friday evening. There was a big spring there and a

[014-010] creek and a few irrigating ditches. It was a community of discouraged

[014-011] agriculturists who had disastrously experimented with dry farming. Mr. Kronborg

[014-012] always drove out one day and back the next, spending the night with one of his

[014-013] parishioners. Often, when the weather was fine, his wife accompanied him.

[014-014] To-day they set out from home after the midday meal, leaving Tillie in charge

[014-015] of the house. Mrs. Kronborg’s maternal feeling was always garnered up in

[014-016] the baby, whoever the baby happened to be. If she had the baby with her, the

[014-017] others could look out for themselves. Thor, of course, was not, accurately

[014-018] speaking, a baby any longer. In the matter of nourishment he was quite

[014-019] independent of his mother, though this independence had not been won without a

[014-020] struggle. Thor was conservative in all things, and the whole family had

[014-021] anguished with him when he was being weaned. Being the youngest, he was still

[014-022] the baby for Mrs. Kronborg, though he was nearly four years old and sat up

[014-023] boldly on her lap this afternoon, holding on to the ends of the lines and

[014-024] shouting “’mup, ’mup, horsey.” His father watched him

[014-025] affectionately and hummed hymn tunes in the jovial way that was sometimes such

[014-026] a trial to Thea.

[014-027] Mrs. Kronborg was enjoying the sunshine and the brilliant sky and all the

[014-028] faintly marked features of the dazzling, monotonous landscape. She had a rather

[014-029] unusual capacity for getting the flavor of places and of people. Although she

[014-030] was so enmeshed in family cares most of the time, she could emerge serene when

[014-031] she was away from them. For a mother of seven, she had a singularly

[014-032] unprejudiced point of view. She was, moreover, a fatalist, and as she did not

[014-033] attempt to direct things beyond her control, she found a good deal of time to

[014-034] enjoy the ways of man and nature.

[014-035] When they were well upon their road, out where the first lean pasture lands

[014-036] began and the sand grass made a faint showing between the sagebrushes, Mr.

[014-037] Kronborg dropped his tune and turned to his wife. “Mother, I’ve

[014-038] been thinking about something.”

[014-039] “I guessed you had. What is it?” She shifted Thor to her left knee,

[014-040] where he would be more out of the way.

[014-041] “Well, it’s about Thea. Mr. Follansbee came to my study at the

[014-042] church the other day and said they would like to have their two girls take

[014-043] lessons of Thea. Then I sounded Miss Meyers” (Miss Meyers was the

[014-044] organist in Mr. Kronborg’s church) “and she said there was a good

[014-045] deal of talk about whether Thea wouldn’t take over Wunsch’s pupils.

[014-046] She said if Thea stopped school she wouldn’t wonder if she could get

[014-047] pretty much all Wunsch’s class. People think Thea knows about all Wunsch

[014-048] could teach.”

[014-049] Mrs. Kronborg looked thoughtful. “Do you think we ought to take her out

[014-050] of school so young?”

[014-051] “She is young, but next year would be her last year anyway. She’s

[014-052] far along for her age. And she can’t learn much under the principal

[014-053] we’ve got now, can she?”

[014-054] “No, I’m afraid she can’t,” his wife admitted.

[014-055] “She frets a good deal and says that man always has to look in the back

[014-056] of the book for the answers. She hates all that diagramming they have to do,

[014-057] and I think myself it’s a waste of time.”

[014-058] Mr. Kronborg settled himself back into the seat and slowed the mare to a walk.

[014-059] “You see, it occurs to me that we might raise Thea’s prices, so it

[014-060] would be worth her while. Seventy-five cents for hour lessons, fifty cents for

[014-061] half-hour lessons. If she got, say two thirds of Wunsch’s class, that

[014-062] would bring her in upwards of ten dollars a week. Better pay than teaching a

[014-063] country school, and there would be more work in vacation than in winter. Steady

[014-064] work twelve months in the year; that’s an advantage. And she’d be

[014-065] living at home, with no expenses.”

[014-066] “There’d be talk if you raised her prices,” said Mrs.

[014-067] Kronborg dubiously.

[014-068] “At first there would. But Thea is so much the best musician in town that

[014-069] they’d all come into line after a while. A good many people in Moonstone

[014-070] have been making money lately, and have bought new pianos. There were ten new

[014-071] pianos shipped in here from Denver in the last year. People ain’t going

[014-072] to let them stand idle; too much money invested. I believe Thea can have as

[014-073] many scholars as she can handle, if we set her up a little.”

[014-074] “How set her up, do you mean?” Mrs. Kronborg felt a certain

[014-075] reluctance about accepting this plan, though she had not yet had time to think

[014-076] out her reasons.

[014-077] “Well, I’ve been thinking for some time we could make good use of

[014-078] another room. We couldn’t give up the parlor to her all the time. If we

[014-079] built another room on the ell and put the piano in there, she could give

[014-080] lessons all day long and it wouldn’t bother us. We could build a

[014-081] clothes-press in it, and put in a bed-lounge and a dresser and let Anna have it

[014-082] for her sleeping-room. She needs a place of her own, now that she’s

[014-083] beginning to be dressy.”

[014-084] “Seems like Thea ought to have the choice of the room, herself,”

[014-085] said Mrs. Kronborg.

[014-086] “But, my dear, she don’t want it. Won’t have it. I sounded

[014-087] her coming home from church on Sunday; asked her if she would like to sleep in

[014-088] a new room, if we built on. She fired up like a little wild-cat and said

[014-089] she’d made her own room all herself, and she didn’t think anybody

[014-090] ought to take it away from her.”

[014-091] “She don’t mean to be impertinent, father. She’s made decided

[014-092] that way, like my father.” Mrs. Kronborg spoke warmly. “I never

[014-093] have any trouble with the child. I remember my father’s ways and go at

[014-094] her carefully. Thea’s all right.”

[014-095] Mr. Kronborg laughed indulgently and pinched Thor’s full cheek.

[014-096] “Oh, I didn’t mean anything against your girl, mother! She’s

[014-097] all right, but she’s a little wild-cat, just the same. I think Ray

[014-098] Kennedy’s planning to spoil a born old maid.”

[014-099] “Huh! She’ll get something a good sight better than [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]],

[014-100] you see! Thea’s an awful smart girl. I’ve seen a good many girls

[014-101] take music lessons in my time, but I ain’t seen one that took to it so.

[014-102] Wunsch said so, too. She’s got the making of something in her.”

[014-103] “I don’t deny that, and the sooner she gets at it in a businesslike

[014-104] way, the better. She’s the kind that takes responsibility, and

[014-105] it’ll be good for her.”

[014-106] Mrs. Kronborg was thoughtful. “In some ways it will, maybe. But

[014-107] there’s a good deal of strain about teaching youngsters, and she’s

[014-108] always worked so hard with the scholars she has. I’ve often listened to

[014-109] her pounding it into ’em. I don’t want to work her too hard.

[014-110] She’s so serious that she’s never had what you might call any real

[014-111] childhood. Seems like she ought to have the next few years sort of free and

[014-112] easy. She’ll be tied down with responsibilities soon enough.”

[014-113] Mr. Kronborg patted his wife’s arm. “Don’t you believe it,

[014-114] mother. Thea is not the marrying kind. I’ve watched ’em. Anna will

[014-115] marry before long and make a good wife, but I don’t see Thea bringing up

[014-116] a family. She’s got a good deal of her mother in her, but she

[014-117] hasn’t got all. She’s too peppery and too fond of having her own

[014-118] way. Then she’s always got to be ahead in everything. That kind make good

[014-119] church-workers and missionaries and school teachers, but they don’t make

[014-120] good wives. They fret all their energy away, like colts, and get cut on the

[014-121] wire.”

[014-122] Mrs. Kronborg laughed. “Give me the graham crackers I put in your pocket

[014-123] for Thor. He’s hungry. You’re a funny man, Peter. A body

[014-124] wouldn’t think, to hear you, you was talking about your own daughters. I

[014-125] guess you see through ’em. Still, even if Thea ain’t apt to have

[014-126] children of her own, I don’t know as that’s a good reason why she

[014-127] should wear herself out on other people’s.”

[014-128] “That’s just the point, mother. A girl with all that energy has got

[014-129] to do something, same as a boy, to keep her out of mischief. If you don’t

[014-130] want her to marry Ray, let her do something to make herself independent.”

[014-131] “Well, I’m not against it. It might be the best thing for her. I

[014-132] wish I felt sure she wouldn’t worry. She takes things hard. She nearly

[014-133] cried herself sick about Wunsch’s going away. She’s the smartest

[014-134] child of ’em all, Peter, by a long ways.”

[014-135] Peter Kronborg smiled. “There you go, Anna. That’s you all over

[014-136] again. Now, I have no favorites; they all have their good points. But

[014-137] you,” with a twinkle, “always did go in for brains.”

[014-138] Mrs. Kronborg chuckled as she wiped the cracker crumbs from Thor’s chin

[014-139] and fists. “Well, you’re mighty conceited, Peter! But I don’t

[014-140] know as I ever regretted it. I prefer having a family of my own to fussing with

[014-141] other folks’ children, that’s the truth.”

[014-142] Before the Kronborgs reached Copper Hole, Thea’s destiny was pretty well

[014-143] mapped out for her. Mr. Kronborg was always delighted to have an excuse for

[014-144] enlarging the house.

[014-145] Mrs. Kronborg was quite right in her conjecture that there would be unfriendly

[014-146] comment in Moonstone when Thea raised her prices for music-lessons. People said

[014-147] she was getting too conceited for anything. Mrs. Livery Johnson put on a new

[014-148] bonnet and paid up all her back calls to have the pleasure of announcing in

[014-149] each parlor she entered that her daughters, at least, would “never pay

[014-150] professional prices to [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]].”

[014-151] Thea raised no objection to quitting school. She was now in the “high

[014-152] room,” as it was called, in next to the highest class, and was studying

[014-153] geometry and beginning Caesar. She no longer recited her lessons to the teacher

[014-154] she liked, but to the Principal, a man who belonged, like Mrs. Livery Johnson,

[014-155] to the camp of Thea’s natural enemies. He taught school because he was

[014-156] too lazy to work among grown-up people, and he made an easy job of it. He got

[014-157] out of real work by inventing useless activities for his pupils, such as the

[014-158] “tree-diagramming system.” Thea had spent hours making trees out of

[014-159] “Thanatopsis,” Hamlet’s soliloquy, Cato on

[014-160] “Immortality.” She agonized under this waste of time, and was only

[014-161] too glad to accept her father’s offer of liberty.

[014-162] So Thea left school the first of November. By the first of January she had

[014-163] eight one-hour pupils and ten half-hour pupils, and there would be more in the

[014-164] summer. She spent her earnings generously. She bought a new Brussels carpet for

[014-165] the parlor, and a rifle for Gunner and Axel, and an imitation tiger-skin coat

[014-166] and cap for Thor. She enjoyed being able to add to the family possessions, and

[014-167] thought Thor looked quite as handsome in his spots as the rich children she had

[014-168] seen in Denver. Thor was most complacent in his conspicuous apparel. He could

[014-169] walk anywhere by this time—though he always preferred to sit, or to be

[014-170] pulled in his cart. He was a blissfully lazy child, and had a number of long,

[014-171] dull plays, such as making nests for his china duck and waiting for her to lay

[014-172] him an egg. Thea thought him very intelligent, and she was proud that he was so

[014-173] big and burly. She found him restful, loved to hear him call her

[014-174] “sitter,” and really liked his companionship, especially when she

[014-175] was tired. On Saturday, for instance, when she taught from nine in the morning

[014-176] until five in the afternoon, she liked to get off in a corner with Thor after

[014-177] supper, away from all the bathing and dressing and joking and talking that went

[014-178] on in the house, and ask him about his duck, or hear him tell one of his

[014-179] rambling stories.
