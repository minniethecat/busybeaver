## Overview

Andor Harsanyi is Thea Kronborg's piano teacher in Chicago, introduced in Part 2 of the novel. A Hungarian-born musician of exacting standards, Harsanyi is widely regarded as the finest piano instructor in the city [021-060]. He takes Thea as a pupil on the recommendation of her earlier teacher, Professor Wunsch, and quickly discerns that she possesses a rare and powerful musical gift that transcends mere technical proficiency. Though their teacher–student relationship is relatively brief in narrative terms, Harsanyi's role is pivotal: he is the first professional musician to fully recognize the scope of Thea's talent and to treat her as a serious artist rather than a promising provincial girl.

## Biography / Character Arc

### Early Life and Background

Harsanyi is Hungarian by birth and trained in the European classical tradition. By the time Thea arrives in Chicago, he has established himself as the city's preeminent piano pedagogue, with a studio attached to his home. He is married to Mrs. Harsanyi, a warm and socially perceptive woman who takes a personal interest in his students' welfare [023-114].

### Teaching Thea Kronborg

When Thea first comes to study with him around age seventeen, Harsanyi has "never had a pupil in the..." [023-001] — a phrase suggesting either her exceptional nature or his own high selectivity. Thea arrives with technical gaps from her years with the alcoholic Professor Wunsch, but Harsanyi immediately recognizes the quality of her musical instinct.

He finds in her "a pupil with su..." [023-017] extraordinary natural gifts. His pedagogical approach is described as patient yet demanding: "Harsanyi turned the light of his wonderful understanding upon her" [023-047], illuminating both her strengths and the technical work still required. He is captivated by the challenge she presents — "an interesting variation in his routine" [023-039] — and becomes deeply invested in her development.

### Recognizing Thea's True Gift

As their work progresses, Harsanyi makes a crucial observation: Thea's musical power does not lie primarily in the piano. Her singing voice, and the emotional depth with which she approaches music, suggest that her true instrument is vocal rather than keyboard. "Harsanyi knew that his interesting pupil had it in her to be something extraordinary" [023-100]. This recognition — that he is training a future singer, not a future pianist — marks the beginning of a transition that will define Thea's artistic path.

### Domestic Life

The Harsanyi household forms a significant backdrop to Thea's Chicago period. Mrs. Harsanyi takes an active interest in the young student, inviting her to dine with the family [023-147], tending to her when she is unwell [023-149], and offering gentle guidance on matters of dress and grooming [023-166]. These scenes establish the Harsanyis as a cultured, kindly couple who provide Thea with a model of artistic domesticity quite different from the rough-and-tumble Kronborg household in Moonstone.

## Relationships

### Thea Kronborg
Harsanyi is Thea's teacher and, briefly, her most important artistic mentor in Chicago. Their relationship is professional but warm; Thea respects him deeply but cannot speak to him with the same openness she feels toward Dr. Archie [023-143].

### Mrs. Harsanyi
Mrs. Harsanyi serves as a maternal figure to Thea during her Chicago years. She "liked Miss Kronborg thoroughly" [023-132] and saw her as a pupil who "would reflect credit upon Harsanyi" [023-133].

### Professor Wunsch
Wunsch, Thea's earlier teacher in Moonstone, recommended Harsanyi to the Kronborgs. The contrast between the dissipated, alcoholic Wunsch and the disciplined, professional Harsanyi underscores Thea's progression from provincial to serious artistic training.

## Significance

Harsanyi represents the gatekeeper of the European classical tradition in Thea's journey. He is the first credentialed, professional musician to validate her talent, and his endorsement carries weight in the Chicago musical community. His recognition that she is a singer rather than a pianist — and his willingness to redirect her accordingly — demonstrates a rare combination of pedagogical honesty and artistic humility.

In the novel's thematic architecture, Harsanyi belongs to the pattern of mentors who recognize Thea's specialness and help clear her path: Professor Wunsch (first awakening), Harsanyi (technical foundation and redirection), Madison Bowers (vocal training), and finally Fred Ottenburg (patronage and emotional awakening). Harsanyi occupies a crucial middle position in this chain, bridging provincial Moonstone and the professional musical world.
