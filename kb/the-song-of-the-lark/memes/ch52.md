## II

[052-001] When Ottenburg and his host reached the house on Colfax Avenue, they went

[052-002] directly to the library, a long double room on the second floor which Archie

[052-003] had arranged exactly to his own taste. It was full of books and mounted

[052-004] specimens of wild game, with a big writing-table at either end, stiff,

[052-005] old-fashioned engravings, heavy hangings and deep upholstery.

[052-006] When one of the Japanese boys brought the cocktails, Fred turned from the fine

[052-007] specimen of peccoray he had been examining and said, “A man is an owl to

[052-008] live in such a place alone, Archie. Why don’t you marry? As for me, just

[052-009] because I can’t marry, I find the world full of charming, unattached

[052-010] women, any one of whom I could fit up a house for with alacrity.”

[052-011] “You’re more knowing than I.” Archie spoke politely.

[052-012] “I’m not very wide awake about women. I’d be likely to pick

[052-013] out one of the uncomfortable ones—and there are a few of them, you

[052-014] know.” He drank his cocktail and rubbed his hands together in a friendly

[052-015] way. “My friends here have charming wives, and they don’t give me a

[052-016] chance to get lonely. They are very kind to me, and I have a great many

[052-017] pleasant friendships.”

[052-018] Fred put down his glass. “Yes, I’ve always noticed that women have

[052-019] confidence in you. You have the doctor’s way of getting next. And you

[052-020] enjoy that kind of thing?”

[052-021] “The friendship of attractive women? Oh, dear, yes! I depend upon it a

[052-022] great deal.”

[052-023] The butler announced dinner, and the two men went downstairs to the

[052-024] dining-room. [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]]’s dinners were always good and well served, and

[052-025] his wines were excellent.

[052-026] “I saw the Fuel and Iron people to-day,” Ottenburg said, looking up

[052-027] from his soup. “Their heart is in the right place. I can’t see why

[052-028] in the mischief you ever got mixed up with that reform gang, Archie.

[052-029] You’ve got nothing to reform out here. The situation has always been as

[052-030] simple as two and two in Colorado; mostly a matter of a friendly

[052-031] understanding.”

[052-032] “Well,”—Archie spoke tolerantly,—“some of the

[052-033] young fellows seemed to have red-hot convictions, and I thought it was better

[052-034] to let them try their ideas out.”

[052-035] Ottenburg shrugged his shoulders. “A few dull young men who haven’t

[052-036] ability enough to play the old game the old way, so they want to put on a new

[052-037] game which doesn’t take so much brains and gives away more advertising

[052-038] that’s what your anti-saloon league and vice commission amounts to. They

[052-039] provide notoriety for the fellows who can’t distinguish themselves at

[052-040] running a business or practicing law or developing an industry. Here you have a

[052-041] mediocre lawyer with no brains and no practice, trying to get a look-in on

[052-042] something. He comes up with the novel proposition that the prostitute has a

[052-043] hard time of it, puts his picture in the paper, and the first thing you know,

[052-044] he’s a celebrity. He gets the rake-off and she’s just where she was

[052-045] before. How could you fall for a mouse-trap like Pink Alden, Archie?”

[052-046] Dr. Archie laughed as he began to carve. “Pink seems to get under your

[052-047] skin. He’s not worth talking about. He’s gone his limit. People

[052-048] won’t read about his blameless life any more. I knew those interviews he

[052-049] gave out would cook him. They were a last resort. I could have stopped him, but

[052-050] by that time I’d come to the conclusion that I’d let the reformers

[052-051] down. I’m not against a general shaking-up, but the trouble with

[052-052] Pinky’s crowd is they never get beyond a general writing-up. We gave them

[052-053] a chance to do something, and they just kept on writing about each other and

[052-054] what temptations they had overcome.”

[052-055] While Archie and his friend were busy with Colorado politics, the impeccable

[052-056] Japanese attended swiftly and intelligently to his duties, and the dinner, as

[052-057] Ottenburg at last remarked, was worthy of more profitable conversation.

[052-058] “So it is,” the doctor admitted. “Well, we’ll go

[052-059] upstairs for our coffee and cut this out. Bring up some cognac and arak,

[052-060] Tai,” he added as he rose from the table.

[052-061] They stopped to examine a moose’s head on the stairway, and when they

[052-062] reached the library the pine logs in the fireplace had been lighted, and the

[052-063] coffee was bubbling before the hearth. Tai placed two chairs before the fire

[052-064] and brought a tray of cigarettes.

[052-065] “Bring the cigars in my lower desk drawer, boy,” the doctor

[052-066] directed. “Too much light in here, isn’t there, Fred? Light the

[052-067] lamp there on my desk, Tai.” He turned off the electric glare and settled

[052-068] himself deep into the chair opposite Ottenburg’s.

[052-069] “To go back to our conversation, doctor,” Fred began while he

[052-070] waited for the first steam to blow off his coffee; “why don’t you

[052-071] make up your mind to go to Washington? There’d be no fight made against

[052-072] you. I needn’t say the United Breweries would back you. There’d be

[052-073] some *kudos* coming to us, too; backing a reform candidate.”

[052-074] Dr. Archie measured his length in his chair and thrust his large boots toward

[052-075] the crackling pitch-pine. He drank his coffee and lit a big black cigar while

[052-076] his guest looked over the assortment of cigarettes on the tray. “You say

[052-077] why don’t I,” the doctor spoke with the deliberation of a man in

[052-078] the position of having several courses to choose from, “but, on the other

[052-079] hand, why should I?” He puffed away and seemed, through his half-closed

[052-080] eyes, to look down several long roads with the intention of luxuriously

[052-081] rejecting all of them and remaining where he was. “I’m sick of

[052-082] politics. I’m disillusioned about serving my crowd, and I don’t

[052-083] particularly want to serve yours. Nothing in it that I particularly want; and a

[052-084] man’s not effective in politics unless he wants something for himself,

[052-085] and wants it hard. I can reach my ends by straighter roads. There are plenty of

[052-086] things to keep me busy. We haven’t begun to develop our resources in this

[052-087] State; we haven’t had a look in on them yet. That’s the only thing

[052-088] that isn’t fake—making men and machines go, and actually turning

[052-089] out a product.”

[052-090] The doctor poured himself some white cordial and looked over the little glass

[052-091] into the fire with an expression which led Ottenburg to believe that he was

[052-092] getting at something in his own mind. Fred lit a cigarette and let his friend

[052-093] grope for his idea.

[052-094] “My boys, here,” Archie went on, “have got me rather

[052-095] interested in Japan. Think I’ll go out there in the spring, and come back

[052-096] the other way, through Siberia. I’ve always wanted to go to

[052-097] Russia.” His eyes still hunted for something in his big fireplace. With a

[052-098] slow turn of his head he brought them back to his guest and fixed them upon

[052-099] him. “Just now, I’m thinking of running on to New York for a few

[052-100] weeks,” he ended abruptly.

[052-101] Ottenburg lifted his chin. “Ah!” he exclaimed, as if he began to

[052-102] see Archie’s drift. “Shall you see Thea?”

[052-103] “Yes.” The doctor replenished his cordial glass. “In fact, I

[052-104] suspect I am going exactly *to* see her. I’m getting stale on things

[052-105] here, Fred. Best people in the world and always doing things for me. I’m

[052-106] fond of them, too, but I’ve been with them too much. I’m getting

[052-107] ill-tempered, and the first thing I know I’ll be hurting people’s

[052-108] feelings. I snapped Mrs. Dandridge up over the telephone this afternoon when

[052-109] she asked me to go out to Colorado Springs on Sunday to meet some English

[052-110] people who are staying at the Antlers. Very nice of her to want me, and I was

[052-111] as sour as if she’d been trying to work me for something. I’ve got

[052-112] to get out for a while, to save my reputation.”

[052-113] To this explanation Ottenburg had not paid much attention. He seemed to be

[052-114] looking at a fixed point: the yellow glass eyes of a fine wildcat over one of

[052-115] the bookcases. “You’ve never heard her at all, have you?” he

[052-116] asked reflectively. “Curious, when this is her second season in New

[052-117] York.”

[052-118] “I was going on last March. Had everything arranged. And then old Cap

[052-119] Harris thought he could drive his car and me through a lamp-post and I was laid

[052-120] up with a compound fracture for two months. So I didn’t get to see

[052-121] Thea.”

[052-122] Ottenburg studied the red end of his cigarette attentively. “She might

[052-123] have come out to see you. I remember you covered the distance like a streak

[052-124] when she wanted you.”

[052-125] Archie moved uneasily. “Oh, she couldn’t do that. She had to get

[052-126] back to Vienna to work on some new parts for this year. She sailed two days

[052-127] after the New York season closed.”

[052-128] “Well, then she couldn’t, of course.” Fred smoked his

[052-129] cigarette close and tossed the end into the fire. “I’m tremendously

[052-130] glad you’re going now. If you’re stale, she’ll jack you up.

[052-131] That’s one of her specialties. She got a rise out of me last December

[052-132] that lasted me all winter.”

[052-133] “Of course,” the doctor apologized, “you know so much more

[052-134] about such things. I’m afraid it will be rather wasted on me. I’m

[052-135] no judge of music.”

[052-136] “Never mind that.” The younger man pulled himself up in his chair.

[052-137] “She gets it across to people who aren’t judges. That’s just

[052-138] what she does.” He relapsed into his former lassitude. “If you were

[052-139] stone deaf, it wouldn’t all be wasted. It’s a great deal to watch

[052-140] her. Incidentally, you know, she is very beautiful. Photographs give you no

[052-141] idea.”

[052-142] Dr. Archie clasped his large hands under his chin. “Oh, I’m

[052-143] counting on that. I don’t suppose her voice will sound natural to me.

[052-144] Probably I wouldn’t know it.”

[052-145] Ottenburg smiled. “You’ll know it, if you ever knew it. It’s

[052-146] the same voice, only more so. You’ll know it.”

[052-147] “Did you, in Germany that time, when you wrote me? Seven years ago, now.

[052-148] That must have been at the very beginning.”

[052-149] “Yes, somewhere near the beginning. She sang one of the Rhine

[052-150] daughters.” Fred paused and drew himself up again. “Sure, I knew it

[052-151] from the first note. I’d heard a good many young voices come up out of

[052-152] the Rhine, but, by gracious, I hadn’t heard one like that!” He

[052-153] fumbled for another cigarette. “Mahler was conducting that night. I met

[052-154] him as he was leaving the house and had a word with him. ‘Interesting

[052-155] voice you tried out this evening,’ I said. He stopped and smiled.

[052-156] ‘Miss Kronborg, you mean? Yes, very. She seems to sing for the idea.

[052-157] Unusual in a young singer.’ I’d never heard him admit before that a

[052-158] singer could have an idea. She not only had it, but she got it across. The

[052-159] Rhine music, that I’d known since I was a boy, was fresh to me, vocalized

[052-160] for the first time. You realized that she was beginning that long story,

[052-161] adequately, with the end in view. Every phrase she sang was basic. She simply

[052-162] *was* the idea of the Rhine music.” Ottenburg rose and stood with

[052-163] his back to the fire. “And at the end, where you don’t see the

[052-164] maidens at all, the same thing again: two pretty voices *and* the Rhine

[052-165] voice.” Fred snapped his fingers and dropped his hand.

[052-166] The doctor looked up at him enviously. “You see, all that would be lost

[052-167] on me,” he said modestly. “I don’t know the dream nor the

[052-168] interpretation thereof. I’m out of it. It’s too bad that so few of

[052-169] her old friends can appreciate her.”

[052-170] “Take a try at it,” Fred encouraged him. “You’ll get in

[052-171] deeper than you can explain to yourself. People with no personal interest do

[052-172] that.”

[052-173] “I suppose,” said Archie diffidently, “that college German,

[052-174] gone to seed, wouldn’t help me out much. I used to be able to make my

[052-175] German patients understand me.”

[052-176] “Sure it would!” cried Ottenburg heartily. “Don’t be

[052-177] above knowing your libretto. That’s all very well for musicians, but

[052-178] common mortals like you and me have got to know what she’s singing about.

[052-179] Get out your dictionary and go at it as you would at any other proposition. Her

[052-180] diction is beautiful, and if you know the text you’ll get a great deal.

[052-181] So long as you’re going to hear her, get all that’s coming to you.

[052-182] You bet in Germany people know their librettos by heart! You Americans are so

[052-183] afraid of stooping to learn anything.”

[052-184] “I *am* a little ashamed,” Archie admitted. “I guess

[052-185] that’s the way we mask our general ignorance. However, I’ll stoop

[052-186] this time; I’m more ashamed not to be able to follow her. The papers

[052-187] always say she’s such a fine actress.” He took up the tongs and

[052-188] began to rearrange the logs that had burned through and fallen apart. “I

[052-189] suppose she has changed a great deal?” he asked absently.

[052-190] “We’ve all changed, my dear Archie,—she more than most of us.

[052-191] Yes, and no. She’s all there, only there’s a great deal more of

[052-192] her. I’ve had only a few words with her in several years. It’s

[052-193] better not, when I’m tied up this way. The laws are barbarous,

[052-194] Archie.”

[052-195] “Your wife is—still the same?” the doctor asked

[052-196] sympathetically.

[052-197] “Absolutely. Hasn’t been out of a sanitarium for seven years now.

[052-198] No prospect of her ever being out, and as long as she’s there I’m

[052-199] tied hand and foot. What does society get out of such a state of things,

[052-200] I’d like to know, except a tangle of irregularities? If you want to

[052-201] reform, there’s an opening for you!”

[052-202] “It’s bad, oh, very bad; I agree with you!” Dr. Archie shook

[052-203] his head. “But there would be complications under another system, too.

[052-204] The whole question of a young man’s marrying has looked pretty grave to

[052-205] me for a long while. How have they the courage to keep on doing it? It

[052-206] depresses me now to buy wedding presents.” For some time the doctor

[052-207] watched his guest, who was sunk in bitter reflections. “Such things used

[052-208] to go better than they do now, I believe. Seems to me all the married people I

[052-209] knew when I was a boy were happy enough.” He paused again and bit the end

[052-210] off a fresh cigar. “You never saw Thea’s mother, did you,

[052-211] Ottenburg? That’s a pity. [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] was a fine woman. I’ve

[052-212] always been afraid Thea made a mistake, not coming home when Mrs. Kronborg was

[052-213] ill, no matter what it cost her.”

[052-215] positively couldn’t. I felt you never understood that, but I was in

[052-216] [[dresden|Dresden]] at the time, and though I wasn’t seeing much of her, I could size

[052-217] up the situation for myself. It was by just a lucky chance that she got to sing

[052-218] *Elizabeth* that time at the Dresden Opera, a complication of

[052-219] circumstances. If she’d run away, for any reason, she might have waited

[052-220] years for such a chance to come again. She gave a wonderful performance and

[052-221] made a great impression. They offered her certain terms; she had to take them

[052-222] and follow it up then and there. In that game you can’t lose a single

[052-226] You hear from her regularly?”

[052-227] “More or less regularly. She was never much of a letter-writer. She tells

[052-228] me about her engagements and contracts, but I know so little about that

[052-229] business that it doesn’t mean much to me beyond the figures, which seem

[052-230] very impressive. We’ve had a good deal of business correspondence, about

[052-231] putting up a stone to her father and mother, and, lately, about her youngest

[052-234] Ottenburg, who had picked up his overcoat, dropped it. “Drives your

[052-235] car?” he asked incredulously.

[052-236] “Yes. Thea and I have had a good deal of bother about Thor. We tried a

[052-240] waiting for the automobile to be invented. He’s just as much a part of a

[052-244] Fred plunged into his coat. “Well, it’s a queer world, Archie. But

[052-245] you’ll think better of it, if you go to New York. Wish I were going with

[052-246] you. I’ll drop in on you in the morning at about eleven. I want a word

[052-248] Dr. Archie saw his guest to the motor which was waiting below, and then went

[052-249] back to his library, where he replenished the fire and sat down for a long

[052-250] smoke. A man of Archie’s modest and rather credulous nature develops

[052-251] late, and makes his largest gain between forty and fifty. At thirty, indeed, as

[052-252] we have seen, Archie was a soft-hearted boy under a manly exterior, still

[052-253] whistling to keep up his courage. Prosperity and large

[052-254] responsibilities—above all, getting free of poor Mrs. Archie—had

[052-255] brought out a good deal more than he knew was in him. He was thinking tonight

[052-256] as he sat before the fire, in the comfort he liked so well, that but for lucky

[052-257] chances, and lucky holes in the ground, he would still be a country

[052-258] practitioner, reading his old books by his office lamp. And yet, he was not so

[052-259] fresh and energetic as he ought to be. He was tired of business and of

[052-260] politics. Worse than that, he was tired of the men with whom he had to do and

[052-261] of the women who, as he said, had been kind to him. He felt as if he were still

[052-262] hunting for something, like old Jasper Flight. He knew that this was an

[052-263] unbecoming and ungrateful state of mind, and he reproached himself for it. But

[052-264] he could not help wondering why it was that life, even when it gave so much,

[052-265] after all gave so little. What was it that he had expected and missed? Why was

[052-266] he, more than he was anything else, disappointed?

[052-267] He fell to looking back over his life and asking himself which years of it he

[052-268] would like to live over again,—just as they had been,—and they were

[052-269] not many. His college years he would live again, gladly. After them there was

[052-270] nothing he would care to repeat until he came to [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]]. There had been

[052-271] something stirring about those years in Moonstone, when he was a restless young

[052-272] man on the verge of breaking into larger enterprises, and when she was a

[052-273] restless child on the verge of growing up into something unknown. He realized

[052-274] now that she had counted for a great deal more to him than he knew at the time.

[052-275] It was a continuous sort of relationship. He was always on the lookout for her

[052-276] as he went about the town, always vaguely expecting her as he sat in his office

[052-277] at night. He had never asked himself then if it was strange that he should find

[052-278] a child of twelve the most interesting and companionable person in Moonstone.

[052-279] It had seemed a pleasant, natural kind of solicitude. He explained it then by

[052-280] the fact that he had no children of his own. But now, as he looked back at

[052-281] those years, the other interests were faded and inanimate. The thought of them

[052-282] was heavy. But wherever his life had touched Thea Kronborg’s, there was

[052-283] still a little warmth left, a little sparkle. Their friendship seemed to run

[052-284] over those discontented years like a leafy pattern, still bright and fresh when

[052-285] the other patterns had faded into the dull background. Their walks and drives

[052-286] and confidences, the night they watched the rabbit in the moonlight,—why

[052-287] were these things stirring to remember? Whenever he thought of them, they were

[052-288] distinctly different from the other memories of his life; always seemed

[052-289] humorous, gay, with a little thrill of anticipation and mystery about them.

[052-290] They came nearer to being tender secrets than any others he possessed. Nearer

[052-291] than anything else they corresponded to what he had hoped to find in the world,

[052-292] and had not found. It came over him now that the unexpected favors of fortune,

[052-293] no matter how dazzling, do not mean very much to us. They may excite or divert

[052-294] us for a time, but when we look back, the only things we cherish are those

[052-295] which in some way met our original want; the desire which formed in us in early

[052-296] youth, undirected, and of its own accord.
