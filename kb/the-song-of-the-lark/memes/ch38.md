## I

[038-001] The San Francisco Mountain lies in Northern Arizona, above Flagstaff, and its

[038-002] blue slopes and snowy summit entice the eye for a hundred miles across the

[038-003] desert. About its base lie the pine forests of the Navajos, where the great

[038-004] red-trunked trees live out their peaceful centuries in that sparkling air. The

[038-005] *piñons* and scrub begin only where the forest ends, where the country

[038-006] breaks into open, stony clearings and the surface of the earth cracks into deep

[038-007] canyons. The great pines stand at a considerable distance from each other. Each

[038-008] tree grows alone, murmurs alone, thinks alone. They do not intrude upon each

[038-009] other. The Navajos are not much in the habit of giving or of asking help. Their

[038-010] language is not a communicative one, and they never attempt an interchange of

[038-011] personality in speech. Over their forests there is the same inexorable reserve.

[038-012] Each tree has its exalted power to bear.

[038-013] That was the first thing [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] felt about the forest, as she drove

[038-014] through it one May morning in Henry Biltmer’s democrat wagon—and it

[038-015] was the first great forest she had ever seen. She had got off the train at

[038-016] Flagstaff that morning, rolled off into the high, chill air when all the pines

[038-017] on the mountain were fired by sunrise, so that she seemed to fall from sleep

[038-018] directly into the forest.

[038-019] Old Biltmer followed a faint wagon trail which ran southeast, and which, as

[038-020] they traveled, continually dipped lower, falling away from the high plateau on

[038-021] the slope of which Flagstaff sits. The white peak of the mountain, the snow

[038-022] gorges above the timber, now disappeared from time to time as the road dropped

[038-023] and dropped, and the forest closed behind the wagon. More than the mountain

[038-024] disappeared as the forest closed thus. Thea seemed to be taking very little

[038-025] through the wood with her. The personality of which she was so tired seemed to

[038-026] let go of her. The high, sparkling air drank it up like blotting-paper. It was

[038-027] lost in the thrilling blue of the new sky and the song of the thin wind in the

[038-028] *piñons*. The old, fretted lines which marked one off, which defined

[038-029] her,—made her Thea Kronborg, Bowers’s accompanist, a soprano with a

[038-030] faulty middle voice,—were all erased.

[038-031] So far she had failed. Her two years in [[chicago|Chicago]] had not resulted in anything.

[038-032] She had failed with Harsanyi, and she had made no great progress with her

[038-033] voice. She had come to believe that whatever Bowers had taught her was of

[038-034] secondary importance, and that in the essential things she had made no advance.

[038-035] Her student life closed behind her, like the forest, and she doubted whether

[038-036] she could go back to it if she tried. Probably she would teach music in little

[038-037] country towns all her life. Failure was not so tragic as she would have

[038-038] supposed; she was tired enough not to care.

[038-039] She was getting back to the earliest sources of gladness that she could

[038-040] remember. She had loved the sun, and the brilliant solitudes of sand and sun,

[038-041] long before these other things had come along to fasten themselves upon her and

[038-042] torment her. That night, when she clambered into her big German feather bed,

[038-043] she felt completely released from the enslaving desire to get on in the world.

[038-044] Darkness had once again the sweet wonder that it had in childhood.
