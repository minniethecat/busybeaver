## IV

[054-001] One bright morning late in February [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] was breakfasting comfortably at

[054-002] the Waldorf. He had got into Jersey City on an early train, and a red, windy

[054-003] sunrise over the North River had given him a good appetite. He consulted the

[054-004] morning paper while he drank his coffee and saw that “Lohengrin”

[054-005] was to be sung at the opera that evening. In the list of the artists who would

[054-006] appear was the name “Kronborg.” Such abruptness rather startled

[054-007] him. “Kronborg”: it was impressive and yet, somehow, disrespectful;

[054-008] somewhat rude and brazen, on the back page of the morning paper. After

[054-009] breakfast he went to the hotel ticket office and asked the girl if she could

[054-010] give him something for “Lohengrin,” “near the front.”

[054-011] His manner was a trifle awkward and he wondered whether the girl noticed it.

[054-012] Even if she did, of course, she could scarcely suspect. Before the ticket stand

[054-013] he saw a bunch of blue posters announcing the opera casts for the week. There

[054-014] was “Lohengrin,” and under it he saw:—

[054-015] *Elsa von Brabant* . . . . [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]].

[054-016] That looked better. The girl gave him a ticket for a seat which she said was

[054-017] excellent. He paid for it and went out to the cabstand. He mentioned to the

[054-018] driver a number on Riverside Drive and got into a taxi. It would not, of

[054-019] course, be the right thing to call upon Thea when she was going to sing in the

[054-020] evening. He knew that much, thank goodness! [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] had hinted to him

[054-021] that, more than almost anything else, that would put one in wrong.

[054-022] When he reached the number to which he directed his letters, he dismissed the

[054-023] cab and got out for a walk. The house in which Thea lived was as impersonal as

[054-024] the Waldorf, and quite as large. It was above 116th Street, where the Drive

[054-025] narrows, and in front of it the shelving bank dropped to the North River. As

[054-026] Archie strolled about the paths which traversed this slope, below the street

[054-027] level, the fourteen stories of the apartment hotel rose above him like a

[054-028] perpendicular cliff. He had no idea on which floor Thea lived, but he

[054-029] reflected, as his eye ran over the many windows, that the outlook would be fine

[054-030] from any floor. The forbidding hugeness of the house made him feel as if he had

[054-031] expected to meet Thea in a crowd and had missed her. He did not really believe

[054-032] that she was hidden away behind any of those glittering windows, or that he was

[054-033] to hear her this evening. His walk was curiously uninspiring and unsuggestive.

[054-034] Presently remembering that Ottenburg had encouraged him to study his lesson, he

[054-035] went down to the opera house and bought a libretto. He had even brought his old

[054-036] “Adler’s German and English” in his trunk, and after luncheon

[054-037] he settled down in his gilded suite at the Waldorf with a big cigar and the

[054-038] text of “Lohengrin.”

[054-039] The opera was announced for seven-forty-five, but at half-past seven Archie

[054-040] took his seat in the right front of the orchestra circle. He had never been

[054-041] inside the [[metropolitan-opera-house|Metropolitan Opera House]] before, and the height of the audience

[054-042] room, the rich color, and the sweep of the balconies were not without their

[054-043] effect upon him. He watched the house fill with a growing feeling of

[054-044] expectation. When the steel curtain rose and the men of the orchestra took

[054-045] their places, he felt distinctly nervous. The burst of applause which greeted

[054-046] the conductor keyed him still higher. He found that he had taken off his gloves

[054-047] and twisted them to a string. When the lights went down and the violins began

[054-048] the overture, the place looked larger than ever; a great pit, shadowy and

[054-049] solemn. The whole atmosphere, he reflected, was somehow more serious than he

[054-050] had anticipated.

[054-051] After the curtains were drawn back upon the scene beside the Scheldt, he got

[054-052] readily into the swing of the story. He was so much interested in the bass who

[054-053] sang *King Henry* that he had almost forgotten for what he was waiting so

[054-054] nervously, when the *Herald* began in stentorian tones to summon *Elsa

[054-055] Von Brabant*. Then he began to realize that he was rather frightened. There

[054-056] was a flutter of white at the back of the stage, and women began to come in:

[054-057] two, four, six, eight, but not the right one. It flashed across him that this

[054-058] was something like buck-fever, the paralyzing moment that comes upon a man when

[054-059] his first elk looks at him through the bushes, under its great antlers; the

[054-060] moment when a man’s mind is so full of shooting that he forgets the gun

[054-061] in his hand until the buck nods adieu to him from a distant hill.

[054-062] All at once, before the buck had left him, she was there. Yes, unquestionably

[054-063] it was she. Her eyes were downcast, but the head, the cheeks, the

[054-064] chin—there could be no mistake; she advanced slowly, as if she were

[054-065] walking in her sleep. Some one spoke to her; she only inclined her head. He

[054-066] spoke again, and she bowed her head still lower. Archie had forgotten his

[054-067] libretto, and he had not counted upon these long pauses. He had expected her to

[054-068] appear and sing and reassure him. They seemed to be waiting for her. Did she

[054-069] ever forget? Why in thunder didn’t she—She made a sound, a faint

[054-070] one. The people on the stage whispered together and seemed confounded. His

[054-071] nervousness was absurd. She must have done this often before; she knew her

[054-072] bearings. She made another sound, but he could make nothing of it. Then the

[054-073] King sang to her, and Archie began to remember where they were in the story.

[054-074] She came to the front of the stage, lifted her eyes for the first time, clasped

[054-075] her hands and began, “*Einsam in trüben Tagen.*”

[054-076] Yes, it was exactly like buck-fever. Her face was there, toward the house now,

[054-077] before his eyes, and he positively could not see it. She was singing, at last,

[054-078] and he positively could not hear her. He was conscious of nothing but an

[054-079] uncomfortable dread and a sense of crushing disappointment. He had, after all,

[054-080] missed her. Whatever was there, she was not there—for him.

[054-081] The King interrupted her. She began again, “*In lichter Waffen

[054-082] Scheine.*” Archie did not know when his buckfever passed, but

[054-083] presently he found that he was sitting quietly in a darkened house, not

[054-084] listening to but dreaming upon a river of silver sound. He felt apart from the

[054-085] others, drifting alone on the melody, as if he had been alone with it for a

[054-086] long while and had known it all before. His power of attention was not great

[054-087] just then, but in so far as it went he seemed to be looking through an exalted

[054-088] calmness at a beautiful woman from far away, from another sort of life and

[054-089] feeling and understanding than his own, who had in her face something he had

[054-090] known long ago, much brightened and beautified. As a lad he used to believe

[054-091] that the faces of people who died were like that in the next world; the same

[054-092] faces, but shining with the light of a new understanding. No, Ottenburg had not

[054-093] prepared him!

[054-094] What he felt was admiration and estrangement. The homely reunion, that he had

[054-095] somehow expected, now seemed foolish. Instead of feeling proud that he knew her

[054-096] better than all these people about him, he felt chagrined at his own

[054-097] ingenuousness. For he did not know her better. This woman he had never known;

[054-098] she had somehow devoured his little friend, as the wolf ate up Red Ridinghood.

[054-099] Beautiful, radiant, tender as she was, she chilled his old affection; that sort

[054-100] of feeling was not appropriate. She seemed much, much farther away from him

[054-101] than she had seemed all those years when she was in Germany. The ocean he could

[054-102] cross, but there was something here he could not cross. There was a moment,

[054-103] when she turned to the King and smiled that rare, sunrise smile of her

[054-104] childhood, when he thought she was coming back to him. After the

[054-105] *Herald’s* second call for her champion, when she knelt in her

[054-106] impassioned prayer, there was again something familiar, a kind of wild wonder

[054-107] that she had had the power to call up long ago. But she merely reminded him of

[054-108] Thea; this was not the girl herself.

[054-109] After the tenor came on, the doctor ceased trying to make the woman before him

[054-110] fit into any of his cherished recollections. He took her, in so far as he

[054-111] could, for what she was then and there. When the knight raised the kneeling

[054-112] girl and put his mailed hand on her hair, when she lifted to him a face full of

[054-113] worship and passionate humility, Archie gave up his last reservation. He knew

[054-114] no more about her than did the hundreds around him, who sat in the shadow and

[054-115] looked on, as he looked, some with more understanding, some with less. He knew

[054-116] as much about *Ortrude* or *Lohengrin* as he knew about

[054-117] *Elsa*—more, because she went further than they, she sustained the

[054-118] legendary beauty of her conception more consistently. Even he could see that.

[054-119] Attitudes, movements, her face, her white arms and fingers, everything was

[054-120] suffused with a rosy tenderness, a warm humility, a gracious and yet—to

[054-121] him—wholly estranging beauty.

[054-122] During the balcony singing in the second act the doctor’s thoughts were

[054-123] as far away from Moonstone as the singer’s doubtless were. He had begun,

[054-124] indeed, to feel the exhilaration of getting free from personalities, of being

[054-125] released from his own past as well as from Thea Kronborg’s. It was very

[054-126] much, he told himself, like a military funeral, exalting and impersonal.

[054-127] Something old died in one, and out of it something new was born. During the

[054-128] duet with *Ortrude*, and the splendors of the wedding processional, this

[054-129] new feeling grew and grew. At the end of the act there were many curtain calls

[054-130] and *Elsa* acknowledged them, brilliant, gracious, spirited, with her

[054-131] far-breaking smile; but on the whole she was harder and more self-contained

[054-132] before the curtain than she was in the scene behind it. Archie did his part in

[054-133] the applause that greeted her, but it was the new and wonderful he applauded,

[054-134] not the old and dear. His personal, proprietary pride in her was frozen out.

[054-135] He walked about the house during the *entr’acte*, and here and there

[054-136] among the people in the foyer he caught the name “Kronborg.” On the

[054-137] staircase, in front of the coffeeroom, a long-haired youth with a fat face was

[054-138] discoursing to a group of old women about “die Kronborg.” Dr.

[054-139] Archie gathered that he had crossed on the boat with her.

[054-140] After the performance was over, Archie took a taxi and started for Riverside

[054-141] Drive. He meant to see it through to-night. When he entered the reception hall

[054-142] of the hotel before which he had strolled that morning, the hall porter

[054-143] challenged him. He said he was waiting for Miss Kronborg. The porter looked at

[054-144] him suspiciously and asked whether he had an appointment. He answered brazenly

[054-145] that he had. He was not used to being questioned by hall boys. Archie sat first

[054-146] in one tapestry chair and then in another, keeping a sharp eye on the people

[054-147] who came in and went up in the elevators. He walked about and looked at his

[054-148] watch. An hour dragged by. No one had come in from the street now for about

[054-149] twenty minutes, when two women entered, carrying a great many flowers and

[054-150] followed by a tall young man in chauffeur’s uniform. Archie advanced

[054-151] toward the taller of the two women, who was veiled and carried her head very

[054-152] firmly. He confronted her just as she reached the elevator. Although he did not

[054-153] stand directly in her way, something in his attitude compelled her to stop. She

[054-154] gave him a piercing, defiant glance through the white scarf that covered her

[054-155] face. Then she lifted her hand and brushed the scarf back from her head. There

[054-156] was still black on her brows and lashes. She was very pale and her face was

[054-157] drawn and deeply lined. She looked, the doctor told himself with a sinking

[054-158] heart, forty years old. Her suspicious, mystified stare cleared slowly.

[054-159] “Pardon me,” the doctor murmured, not knowing just how to address

[054-160] her here before the porters, “I came up from the opera. I merely wanted

[054-161] to say good-night to you.”

[054-162] Without speaking, still looking incredulous, she pushed him into the elevator.

[054-163] She kept her hand on his arm while the cage shot up, and she looked away from

[054-164] him, frowning, as if she were trying to remember or realize something. When the

[054-165] cage stopped, she pushed him out of the elevator through another door, which a

[054-166] maid opened, into a square hall. There she sank down on a chair and looked up

[054-167] at him.

[054-168] “Why didn’t you let me know?” she asked in a hoarse voice.

[054-169] Archie heard himself laughing the old, embarrassed laugh that seldom happened

[054-170] to him now. “Oh, I wanted to take my chance with you, like anybody else.

[054-171] It’s been so long, now!”

[054-172] She took his hand through her thick glove and her head dropped forward.

[054-173] “Yes, it has been long,” she said in the same husky voice,

[054-174] “and so much has happened.”

[054-175] “And you are so tired, and I am a clumsy old fellow to break in on you

[054-176] to-night,” the doctor added sympathetically. “Forgive me, this

[054-177] time.” He bent over and put his hand soothingly on her shoulder. He felt

[054-178] a strong shudder run through her from head to foot.

[054-179] Still bundled in her fur coat as she was, she threw both arms about him and

[054-180] hugged him. “Oh, Dr. Archie, *Dr. Archie*,”—she shook

[054-181] him,—“don’t let me go. Hold on, now you’re here,”

[054-182] she laughed, breaking away from him at the same moment and sliding out of her

[054-183] fur coat. She left it for the maid to pick up and pushed the doctor into the

[054-184] sitting-room, where she turned on the lights. “Let me *look* at you.

[054-185] Yes; hands, feet, head, shoulders—just the same. You’ve grown no

[054-186] older. You can’t say as much for me, can you?”

[054-187] She was standing in the middle of the room, in a white silk shirtwaist and a

[054-188] short black velvet skirt, which somehow suggested that they had ‘cut off

[054-189] her petticoats all round about.’ She looked distinctly clipped and

[054-190] plucked. Her hair was parted in the middle and done very close to her head, as

[054-191] she had worn it under the wig. She looked like a fugitive, who had escaped from

[054-192] something in clothes caught up at hazard. It flashed across Dr. Archie that she

[054-193] was running away from the other woman down at the opera house, who had used her

[054-194] hardly.

[054-195] He took a step toward her. “I can’t tell a thing in the world about

[054-196] you, Thea—if I may still call you that.”

[054-197] She took hold of the collar of his overcoat. “Yes, call me that. Do: I

[054-198] like to hear it. You frighten me a little, but I expect I frighten you more.

[054-199] I’m always a scarecrow after I sing a long part like that—so high,

[054-200] too.” She absently pulled out the handkerchief that protruded from his

[054-201] breast pocket and began to wipe the black paint off her eyebrows and lashes.

[054-202] “I can’t take you in much to-night, but I must see you for a little

[054-203] while.” She pushed him to a chair. “I shall be more recognizable

[054-204] to-morrow. You mustn’t think of me as you see me to-night. Come at four

[054-205] to-morrow afternoon and have tea with me. Can you? That’s good.”

[054-206] She sat down in a low chair beside him and leaned forward, drawing her

[054-207] shoulders together. She seemed to him inappropriately young and inappropriately

[054-208] old, shorn of her long tresses at one end and of her long robes at the other.

[054-209] “How do you happen to be here?” she asked abruptly. “How can

[054-210] you leave a silver mine? I couldn’t! Sure nobody’ll cheat you? But

[054-211] you can explain everything tomorrow.” She paused. “You remember how

[054-212] you sewed me up in a poultice, once? I wish you could to-night. I need a

[054-213] poultice, from top to toe. Something very disagreeable happened down there. You

[054-214] said you were out front? Oh, don’t say anything about it. I always know

[054-215] exactly how it goes, unfortunately. I was rotten in the balcony. I never get

[054-216] that. You didn’t notice it? Probably not, but I did.”

[054-217] Here the maid appeared at the door and her mistress rose. “My supper?

[054-218] Very well, I’ll come. I’d ask you to stay, doctor, but there

[054-219] wouldn’t be enough for two. They seldom send up enough for

[054-220] one,”—she spoke bitterly. “I haven’t got a sense of you

[054-221] yet,”—turning directly to Archie again. “You haven’t

[054-222] been here. You’ve only announced yourself, and told me you are coming

[054-223] to-morrow. You haven’t seen me, either. This is not I. But I’ll be

[054-224] here waiting for you to-morrow, my whole works! Goodnight, till then.”

[054-225] She patted him absently on the sleeve and gave him a little shove toward the

[054-226] door.
