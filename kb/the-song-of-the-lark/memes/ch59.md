## IX

[059-001] When Archie and Ottenburg dined with Thea on Saturday evening, they were served

[059-002] downstairs in the hotel dining-room, but they were to have their coffee in her

[059-003] own apartment. As they were going up in the elevator after dinner, Fred turned

[059-004] suddenly to Thea. “And why, please, did you break Landry’s amber

[059-005] elephant?”

[059-006] She looked guilty and began to laugh. “Hasn’t he got over that yet?

[059-007] I didn’t really mean to break it. I was perhaps careless. His things are

[059-008] so over-petted that I was tempted to be careless with a lot of them.”

[059-009] “How can you be so heartless, when they’re all he has in the

[059-010] world?”

[059-011] “He has me. I’m a great deal of diversion for him; all he needs.

[059-012] There,” she said as she opened the door into her own hall, “I

[059-013] shouldn’t have said that before the elevator boy.”

[059-014] “Even an elevator boy couldn’t make a scandal about Oliver.

[059-015] He’s such a catnip man.”

[059-016] [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] laughed, but Thea, who seemed suddenly to have thought of something

[059-017] annoying, repeated blankly, “Catnip man?”

[059-018] “Yes, he lives on catnip, and rum tea. But he’s not the only one.

[059-019] You are like an eccentric old woman I know in Boston, who goes about in the

[059-020] spring feeding catnip to street cats. You dispense it to a lot of fellows. Your

[059-021] pull seems to be more with men than with women, you know; with seasoned men,

[059-022] about my age, or older. Even on Friday afternoon I kept running into them, old

[059-023] boys I hadn’t seen for years, thin at the part and thick at the girth,

[059-024] until I stood still in the draft and held my hair on. They’re always

[059-025] there; I hear them talking about you in the smoking room. Probably we

[059-026] don’t get to the point of apprehending anything good until we’re

[059-027] about forty. Then, in the light of what is going, and of what, God help us! is

[059-028] coming, we arrive at understanding.”

[059-029] “I don’t see why people go to the opera, anyway,—serious

[059-030] people.” She spoke discontentedly. “I suppose they get something,

[059-031] or think they do. Here’s the coffee. There, please,” she directed

[059-032] the waiter. Going to the table she began to pour the coffee, standing. She wore

[059-033] a white dress trimmed with crystals which had rattled a good deal during

[059-034] dinner, as all her movements had been impatient and nervous, and she had

[059-035] twisted the dark velvet rose at her girdle until it looked rumpled and weary.

[059-036] She poured the coffee as if it were a ceremony in which she did not believe.

[059-037] “Can you make anything of Fred’s nonsense, Dr. Archie?” she

[059-038] asked, as he came to take his cup.

[059-039] Fred approached her. “My nonsense is all right. The same brand has gone

[059-040] with you before. It’s you who won’t be jollied. What’s the

[059-041] matter? You have something on your mind.”

[059-042] “I’ve a good deal. Too much to be an agreeable hostess.” She

[059-043] turned quickly away from the coffee and sat down on the piano bench, facing the

[059-044] two men. “For one thing, there’s a change in the cast for Friday

[059-045] afternoon. They’re going to let me sing *Sieglinde*.” Her

[059-046] frown did not conceal the pleasure with which she made this announcement.

[059-047] “Are you going to keep us dangling about here forever, Thea? Archie and I

[059-048] are supposed to have other things to do.” Fred looked at her with an

[059-049] excitement quite as apparent as her own.

[059-050] “Here I’ve been ready to sing *Sieglinde* for two years, kept

[059-051] in torment, and now it comes off within two weeks, just when I want to be

[059-052] seeing something of Dr. Archie. I don’t know what their plans are down

[059-053] there. After Friday they may let me cool for several weeks, and they may rush

[059-054] me. I suppose it depends somewhat on how things go Friday afternoon.”

[059-055] “Oh, they’ll go fast enough! That’s better suited to your

[059-056] voice than anything you’ve sung here. That gives you every opportunity

[059-057] I’ve waited for.” Ottenburg crossed the room and standing beside

[059-058] her began to play “*Du bist der Lenz*.”

[059-059] With a violent movement Thea caught his wrists and pushed his hands away from

[059-060] the keys.

[059-061] “Fred, can’t you be serious? A thousand things may happen between

[059-062] this and Friday to put me out. Something will happen. If that part were sung

[059-063] well, as well as it ought to be, it would be one of the most beautiful things

[059-064] in the world. That’s why it never is sung right, and never will

[059-065] be.” She clenched her hands and opened them despairingly, looking out of

[059-066] the open window. “It’s inaccessibly beautiful!” she brought

[059-067] out sharply.

[059-068] Fred and Dr. Archie watched her. In a moment she turned back to them.

[059-069] “It’s impossible to sing a part like that well for the first time,

[059-070] except for the sort who will never sing it any better. Everything hangs on that

[059-071] first night, and that’s bound to be bad. There you are,” she

[059-072] shrugged impatiently. “For one thing, they change the cast at the

[059-073] eleventh hour and then rehearse the life out of me.”

[059-074] Ottenburg put down his cup with exaggerated care. “Still, you really want

[059-075] to do it, you know.”

[059-076] “Want to?” she repeated indignantly; “of course I want to! If

[059-077] this were only next Thursday night—But between now and Friday I’ll

[059-078] do nothing but fret away my strength. Oh, I’m not saying I don’t

[059-079] need the rehearsals! But I don’t need them strung out through a week.

[059-080] That system’s well enough for phlegmatic singers; it only drains me.

[059-081] Every single feature of operatic routine is detrimental to me. I usually go on

[059-082] like a horse that’s been fixed to lose a race. I have to work hard to do

[059-083] my worst, let alone my best. I wish you could hear me sing well, once,”

[059-084] she turned to Fred defiantly; “I have, a few times in my life, when there

[059-085] was nothing to gain by it.”

[059-086] Fred approached her again and held out his hand. “I recall my

[059-087] instructions, and now I’ll leave you to fight it out with Archie. He

[059-088] can’t possibly represent managerial stupidity to you as I seem to have a

[059-089] gift for doing.”

[059-090] As he smiled down at her, his good humor, his good wishes, his understanding,

[059-091] embarrassed her and recalled her to herself. She kept her seat, still holding

[059-092] his hand. “All the same, Fred, isn’t it too bad, that there are so

[059-093] many things—” She broke off with a shake of the head.

[059-094] “My dear girl, if I could bridge over the agony between now and Friday

[059-095] for you—But you know the rules of the game; why torment yourself? You saw

[059-096] the other night that you had the part under your thumb. Now walk, sleep, play

[059-097] with Archie, keep your tiger hungry, and she’ll spring all right on

[059-098] Friday. I’ll be there to see her, and there’ll be more than I, I

[059-099] suspect. Harsanyi’s on the Wilhelm der Grosse; gets in on

[059-100] Thursday.”

[059-101] “Harsanyi?” Thea’s eye lighted. “I haven’t seen

[059-102] him for years. We always miss each other.” She paused, hesitating.

[059-103] “Yes, I should like that. But he’ll be busy, maybe?”

[059-104] “He gives his first concert at Carnegie Hall, week after next. Better

[059-105] send him a box if you can.”

[059-106] “Yes, I’ll manage it.” Thea took his hand again. “Oh, I

[059-107] should like that, Fred!” she added impulsively. “Even if I were put

[059-108] out, he’d get the idea,”—she threw back her

[059-109] head,—“for there is an idea!”

[059-110] “Which won’t penetrate here,” he tapped his brow and began to

[059-111] laugh. “You are an ungrateful huzzy, *comme les autres!*”

[059-112] Thea detained him as he turned away. She pulled a flower out of a bouquet on

[059-113] the piano and absently drew the stem through the lapel of his coat. “I

[059-114] shall be walking in the Park to-morrow afternoon, on the reservoir path,

[059-115] between four and five, if you care to join me. You know that after Harsanyi

[059-116] I’d rather please you than anyone else. You know a lot, but he knows even

[059-117] more than you.”

[059-118] “Thank you. Don’t try to analyze it. *Schlafen sie

[059-119] wohl!*” he kissed her fingers and waved from the door, closing it

[059-120] behind him.

[059-121] “He’s the right sort, Thea.” Dr. Archie looked warmly after

[059-122] his disappearing friend. “I’ve always hoped you’d make it up

[059-123] with Fred.”

[059-124] “Well, haven’t I? Oh, marry him, you mean! Perhaps it may come

[059-125] about, some day. Just at present he’s not in the marriage market any more

[059-126] than I am, is he?”

[059-127] “No, I suppose not. It’s a damned shame that a man like Ottenburg

[059-128] should be tied up as he is, wasting all the best years of his life. A woman

[059-129] with general paresis ought to be legally dead.”

[059-130] “Don’t let us talk about Fred’s wife, please. He had no

[059-131] business to get into such a mess, and he had no business to stay in it.

[059-132] He’s always been a softy where women were concerned.”

[059-133] “Most of us are, I’m afraid,” Dr. Archie admitted meekly.

[059-134] “Too much light in here, isn’t there? Tires one’s eyes. The

[059-135] stage lights are hard on mine.” Thea began turning them out.

[059-136] “We’ll leave the little one, over the piano.” She sank down

[059-137] by Archie on the deep sofa. “We two have so much to talk about that we

[059-138] keep away from it altogether; have you noticed? We don’t even nibble the

[059-139] edges. I wish we had Landry here to-night to play for us. He’s very

[059-140] comforting.”

[059-141] “I’m afraid you don’t have enough personal life, outside your

[059-142] work, Thea.” The doctor looked at her anxiously.

[059-143] She smiled at him with her eyes half closed. “My dear doctor, I

[059-144] don’t have any. Your work becomes your personal life. You are not much

[059-145] good until it does. It’s like being woven into a big web. You can’t

[059-146] pull away, because all your little tendrils are woven into the picture. It

[059-147] takes you up, and uses you, and spins you out; and that is your life. Not much

[059-148] else can happen to you.”

[059-149] “Didn’t you think of marrying, several years ago?”

[059-150] “You mean Nordquist? Yes; but I changed my mind. We had been singing a

[059-151] good deal together. He’s a splendid creature.”

[059-152] “Were you much in love with him, Thea?” the doctor asked hopefully.

[059-153] She smiled again. “I don’t think I know just what that expression

[059-154] means. I’ve never been able to find out. I think I was in love with you

[059-155] when I was little, but not with any one since then. There are a great many ways

[059-156] of caring for people. It’s not, after all, a simple state, like measles

[059-157] or tonsilitis. Nordquist is a taking sort of man. He and I were out in a

[059-158] rowboat once in a terrible storm. The lake was fed by glaciers,—ice

[059-159] water,—and we couldn’t have swum a stroke if the boat had filled.

[059-160] If we hadn’t both been strong and kept our heads, we’d have gone

[059-161] down. We pulled for every ounce there was in us, and we just got off with our

[059-162] lives. We were always being thrown together like that, under some kind of

[059-163] pressure. Yes, for a while I thought he would make everything right.” She

[059-164] paused and sank back, resting her head on a cushion, pressing her eyelids down

[059-165] with her fingers. “You see,” she went on abruptly, “he had a

[059-166] wife and two children. He hadn’t lived with her for several years, but

[059-167] when she heard that he wanted to marry again, she began to make trouble. He

[059-168] earned a good deal of money, but he was careless and always wretchedly in debt.

[059-169] He came to me one day and told me he thought his wife would settle for a

[059-170] hundred thousand marks and consent to a divorce. I got very angry and sent him

[059-171] away. Next day he came back and said he thought she’d take fifty

[059-172] thousand.”

[059-173] Dr. Archie drew away from her, to the end of the sofa.

[059-174] “Good God, Thea,”—He ran his handkerchief over his forehead.

[059-175] “What sort of people—” He stopped and shook his head.

[059-176] Thea rose and stood beside him, her hand on his shoulder. “That’s

[059-177] exactly how it struck me,” she said quietly. “Oh, we have things in

[059-178] common, things that go away back, under everything. You understand, of course.

[059-179] Nordquist didn’t. He thought I wasn’t willing to part with the

[059-180] money. I couldn’t let myself buy him from Fru Nordquist, and he

[059-181] couldn’t see why. He had always thought I was close about money, so he

[059-182] attributed it to that. I am careful,”—she ran her arm through

[059-183] Archie’s and when he rose began to walk about the room with him. “I

[059-184] can’t be careless with money. I began the world on six hundred dollars,

[059-185] and it was the price of a man’s life. [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] had worked hard and

[059-186] been sober and denied himself, and when he died he had six hundred dollars to

[059-187] show for it. I always measure things by that six hundred dollars, just as I

[059-188] measure high buildings by the Moonstone standpipe. There are standards we

[059-189] can’t get away from.”

[059-190] Dr. Archie took her hand. “I don’t believe we should be any happier

[059-191] if we did get away from them. I think it gives you some of your poise, having

[059-192] that anchor. You look,” glancing down at her head and shoulders,

[059-193] “sometimes so like your mother.”

[059-194] “Thank you. You couldn’t say anything nicer to me than that. On

[059-195] Friday afternoon, didn’t you think?”

[059-196] “Yes, but at other times, too. I love to see it. Do you know what I

[059-197] thought about that first night when I heard you sing? I kept remembering the

[059-198] night I took care of you when you had pneumonia, when you were ten years old.

[059-199] You were a terribly sick child, and I was a country doctor without much

[059-200] experience. There were no oxygen tanks about then. You pretty nearly slipped

[059-201] away from me. If you had—”

[059-202] Thea dropped her head on his shoulder. “I’d have saved myself and

[059-203] you a lot of trouble, wouldn’t I? Dear Dr. Archie!” she murmured.

[059-204] “As for me, life would have been a pretty bleak stretch, with you left

[059-205] out.” The doctor took one of the crystal pendants that hung from her

[059-206] shoulder and looked into it thoughtfully. “I guess I’m a romantic

[059-207] old fellow, underneath. And you’ve always been my romance. Those years

[059-208] when you were growing up were my happiest. When I dream about you, I always see

[059-209] you as a little girl.”

[059-210] They paused by the open window. “Do you? Nearly all my dreams, except

[059-211] those about breaking down on the stage or missing trains, are about Moonstone.

[059-212] You tell me the old house has been pulled down, but it stands in my mind, every

[059-213] stick and timber. In my sleep I go all about it, and look in the right drawers

[059-214] and cupboards for everything. I often dream that I’m hunting for my

[059-215] rubbers in that pile of overshoes that was always under the hatrack in the

[059-216] hall. I pick up every overshoe and know whose it is, but I can’t find my

[059-217] own. Then the school bell begins to ring and I begin to cry. That’s the

[059-218] house I rest in when I’m tired. All the old furniture and the worn spots

[059-219] in the carpet—it rests my mind to go over them.”

[059-220] They were looking out of the window. Thea kept his arm. Down on the river four

[059-221] battleships were anchored in line, brilliantly lighted, and launches were

[059-222] coming and going, bringing the men ashore. A searchlight from one of the

[059-223] ironclads was playing on the great headland up the river, where it makes its

[059-224] first resolute turn. Overhead the night-blue sky was intense and clear.

[059-225] “There’s so much that I want to tell you,” she said at last,

[059-226] “and it’s hard to explain. My life is full of jealousies and

[059-227] disappointments, you know. You get to hating people who do contemptible work

[059-228] and who get on just as well as you do. There are many disappointments in my

[059-229] profession, and bitter, bitter contempts!” Her face hardened, and looked

[059-230] much older. “If you love the good thing vitally, enough to give up for it

[059-231] all that one must give up for it, then you must hate the cheap thing just as

[059-232] hard. I tell you, there is such a thing as creative hate! A contempt that

[059-233] drives you through fire, makes you risk everything and lose everything, makes

[059-234] you a long sight better than you ever knew you could be.” As she glanced

[059-235] at Dr. Archie’s face, Thea stopped short and turned her own face away.

[059-236] Her eyes followed the path of the searchlight up the river and rested upon the

[059-237] illumined headland.

[059-238] “You see,” she went on more calmly, “voices are accidental

[059-239] things. You find plenty of good voices in common women, with common minds and

[059-240] common hearts. Look at that woman who sang *Ortrude* with me last week.

[059-241] She’s new here and the people are wild about her. ‘Such a beautiful

[059-242] volume of tone!’ they say. I give you my word she’s as stupid as an

[059-243] owl and as coarse as a pig, and any one who knows anything about singing would

[059-244] see that in an instant. Yet she’s quite as popular as Necker, who’s

[059-245] a great artist. How can I get much satisfaction out of the enthusiasm of a

[059-246] house that likes her atrociously bad performance at the same time that it

[059-247] pretends to like mine? If they like her, then they ought to hiss me off the

[059-248] stage. We stand for things that are irreconcilable, absolutely. You can’t

[059-249] try to do things right and not despise the people who do them wrong. How can I

[059-250] be indifferent? If that doesn’t matter, then nothing matters. Well,

[059-251] sometimes I’ve come home as I did the other night when you first saw me,

[059-252] so full of bitterness that it was as if my mind were full of daggers. And

[059-253] I’ve gone to sleep and wakened up in the Kohlers’ garden, with the

[059-254] pigeons and the white rabbits, so happy! And that saves me.” She sat down

[059-255] on the piano bench. Archie thought she had forgotten all about him, until she

[059-256] called his name. Her voice was soft now, and wonderfully sweet. It seemed to

[059-257] come from somewhere deep within her, there were such strong vibrations in it.

[059-258] “You see, Dr. Archie, what one really strives for in art is not the sort

[059-259] of thing you are likely to find when you drop in for a performance at the

[059-260] opera. What one strives for is so far away, so deep, so

[059-261] beautiful”—she lifted her shoulders with a long breath, folded her

[059-262] hands in her lap and sat looking at him with a resignation that made her face

[059-263] noble,—“that there’s nothing one can say about it, Dr.

[059-264] Archie.”

[059-265] Without knowing very well what it was all about, Archie was passionately

[059-266] stirred for her. “I’ve always believed in you, Thea; always

[059-267] believed,” he muttered.

[059-268] She smiled and closed her eyes. “They save me: the old things, things

[059-269] like the Kohlers’ garden. They are in everything I do.”

[059-270] “In what you sing, you mean?”

[059-271] “Yes. Not in any direct way,”—she spoke

[059-272] hurriedly,—“the light, the color, the feeling. Most of all the

[059-273] feeling. It comes in when I’m working on a part, like the smell of a

[059-274] garden coming in at the window. I try all the new things, and then go back to

[059-275] the old. Perhaps my feelings were stronger then. A child’s attitude

[059-276] toward everything is an artist’s attitude. I am more or less of an artist

[059-277] now, but then I was nothing else. When I went with you to [[chicago|Chicago]] that first

[059-278] time, I carried with me the essentials, the foundation of all I do now. The

[059-279] point to which I could go was scratched in me then. I haven’t reached it

[059-280] yet, by a long way.”

[059-281] Archie had a swift flash of memory. Pictures passed before him. “You

[059-282] mean,” he asked wonderingly, “that you knew then that you were so

[059-283] gifted?”

[059-284] Thea looked up at him and smiled. “Oh, I didn’t know anything! Not

[059-285] enough to ask you for my trunk when I needed it. But you see, when I set out

[059-286] from Moonstone with you, I had had a rich, romantic past. I had lived a long,

[059-287] eventful life, and an artist’s life, every hour of it. Wagner says, in

[059-288] his most beautiful opera, that art is only a way of remembering youth. And the

[059-289] older we grow the more precious it seems to us, and the more richly we can

[059-290] present that memory. When we’ve got it all out,—the last, the

[059-291] finest thrill of it, the brightest hope of it,”—she lifted her hand

[059-292] above her head and dropped it,—“then we stop. We do nothing but

[059-293] repeat after that. The stream has reached the level of its source. That’s

[059-294] our measure.”

[059-295] There was a long, warm silence. Thea was looking hard at the floor, as if she

[059-296] were seeing down through years and years, and her old friend stood watching her

[059-297] bent head. His look was one with which he used to watch her long ago, and

[059-298] which, even in thinking about her, had become a habit of his face. It was full

[059-299] of solicitude, and a kind of secret gratitude, as if to thank her for some

[059-300] inexpressible pleasure of the heart. Thea turned presently toward the piano and

[059-301] began softly to waken an old air:—

[059-302] “Ca’ the yowes to the knowes,\

[059-303] Ca’ them where the heather grows,\

[059-304] Ca’ them where the burnie rowes,\

[059-305] My bonnie dear-ie.”

[059-306] Archie sat down and shaded his eyes with his hand. She turned her head and

[059-307] spoke to him over her shoulder. “Come on, you know the words better than

[059-308] I. That’s right.”

[059-309] “We’ll gae down by Clouden’s side,\

[059-310] Through the hazels spreading wide,\

[059-311] O’er the waves that sweetly glide,\

[059-312] To the moon sae clearly.\

[059-313] Ghaist nor bogle shalt thou fear,\

[059-314] Thou’rt to love and Heav’n sae dear,\

[059-315] Nocht of ill may come thee near,\

[059-316] My bonnie dear-ie!”

[059-317] “We can get on without Landry. Let’s try it again, I have all the

[059-318] words now. Then we’ll have ‘Sweet Afton.’ Come:

[059-319] ‘*Ca’ the yowes to the knowes*’—”
