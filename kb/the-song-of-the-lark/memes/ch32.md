## I

[032-001] So many grinning, stupid faces! Thea was sitting by the window in

[032-002] Bowers’s studio, waiting for him to come back from lunch. On her knee was

[032-003] the latest number of an illustrated musical journal in which musicians great

[032-004] and little stridently advertised their wares. Every afternoon she played

[032-005] accompaniments for people who looked and smiled like these. She was getting

[032-006] tired of the human countenance.

[032-007] Thea had been in [[chicago|Chicago]] for two months. She had a small church position which

[032-008] partly paid her living expenses, and she paid for her singing lessons by

[032-009] playing Bowers’s accompaniments every afternoon from two until six. She

[032-010] had been compelled to leave her old friends Mrs. Lorch and Mrs. Andersen,

[032-011] because the long ride from North Chicago to Bowers’s studio on Michigan

[032-012] Avenue took too much time—an hour in the morning, and at night, when the

[032-013] cars were crowded, an hour and a half. For the first month she had clung to her

[032-014] old room, but the bad air in the cars, at the end of a long day’s work,

[032-015] fatigued her greatly and was bad for her voice. Since she left Mrs. Lorch, she

[032-016] had been staying at a students’ club to which she was introduced by Miss

[032-017] Adler, Bowers’s morning accompanist, an intelligent Jewish girl from

[032-018] Evanston.

[032-019] Thea took her lesson from Bowers every day from eleven-thirty until twelve.

[032-020] Then she went out to lunch with an Italian grammar under her arm, and came back

[032-021] to the studio to begin her work at two. In the afternoon Bowers coached

[032-022] professionals and taught his advanced pupils. It was his theory that Thea ought

[032-023] to be able to learn a great deal by keeping her ears open while she played for

[032-024] him.

[032-025] The concert-going public of Chicago still remembers the long, sallow,

[032-026] discontented face of [[madison-bowers|Madison Bowers]]. He seldom missed an evening concert, and

[032-027] was usually to be seen lounging somewhere at the back of the concert hall,

[032-028] reading a newspaper or review, and conspicuously ignoring the efforts of the

[032-029] performers. At the end of a number he looked up from his paper long enough to

[032-030] sweep the applauding audience with a contemptuous eye. His face was

[032-031] intelligent, with a narrow lower jaw, a thin nose, faded gray eyes, and a

[032-032] close-cut brown mustache. His hair was iron-gray, thin and dead-looking. He

[032-033] went to concerts chiefly to satisfy himself as to how badly things were done

[032-034] and how gullible the public was. He hated the whole race of artists; the work

[032-035] they did, the wages they got, and the way they spent their money. His father,

[032-036] old Hiram Bowers, was still alive and at work, a genial old choirmaster in

[032-037] Boston, full of enthusiasm at seventy. But Madison was of the colder stuff of

[032-038] his grandfathers, a long line of New Hampshire farmers; hard workers, close

[032-039] traders, with good minds, mean natures, and flinty eyes. As a boy Madison had a

[032-040] fine barytone voice, and his father made great sacrifices for him, sending him

[032-041] to Germany at an early age and keeping him abroad at his studies for years.

[032-042] Madison worked under the best teachers, and afterward sang in England in

[032-043] oratorio. His cold nature and academic methods were against him. His audiences

[032-044] were always aware of the contempt he felt for them. A dozen poorer singers

[032-045] succeeded, but Bowers did not.

[032-046] Bowers had all the qualities which go to make a good teacher—except

[032-047] generosity and warmth. His intelligence was of a high order, his taste never at

[032-048] fault. He seldom worked with a voice without improving it, and in teaching the

[032-049] delivery of oratorio he was without a rival. Singers came from far and near to

[032-050] study Bach and Handel with him. Even the fashionable sopranos and contraltos of

[032-051] Chicago, St. Paul, and St. Louis (they were usually ladies with very rich

[032-052] husbands, and Bowers called them the “pampered jades of Asia”)

[032-053] humbly endured his sardonic humor for the sake of what he could do for them. He

[032-054] was not at all above helping a very lame singer across, if her husband’s

[032-055] check-book warranted it. He had a whole bag of tricks for stupid people,

[032-056] “life-preservers,” he called them. “Cheap repairs for a cheap

[032-057] ’un,” he used to say, but the husbands never found the repairs very

[032-058] cheap. Those were the days when lumbermen’s daughters and brewers’

[032-059] wives contended in song; studied in Germany and then floated from

[032-060] *Sängerfest* to *Sängerfest*. Choral societies flourished in all the

[032-061] rich lake cities and river cities. The soloists came to Chicago to coach with

[032-062] Bowers, and he often took long journeys to hear and instruct a chorus. He was

[032-063] intensely avaricious, and from these semi-professionals he reaped a golden

[032-064] harvest. They fed his pockets and they fed his ever-hungry contempt, his scorn

[032-065] of himself and his accomplices. The more money he made, the more parsimonious

[032-066] he became. His wife was so shabby that she never went anywhere with him, which

[032-067] suited him exactly. Because his clients were luxurious and extravagant, he took

[032-068] a revengeful pleasure in having his shoes halfsoled a second time, and in

[032-069] getting the last wear out of a broken collar. He had first been interested in

[032-070] [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] because of her bluntness, her country roughness, and her manifest

[032-071] carefulness about money. The mention of Harsanyi’s name always made him

[032-072] pull a wry face. For the first time Thea had a friend who, in his own cool and

[032-073] guarded way, liked her for whatever was least admirable in her.

[032-074] Thea was still looking at the musical paper, her grammar unopened on the

[032-075] window-sill, when Bowers sauntered in a little before two o’clock. He was

[032-076] smoking a cheap cigarette and wore the same soft felt hat he had worn all last

[032-077] winter. He never carried a cane or wore gloves.

[032-078] Thea followed him from the reception-room into the studio. “I may cut my

[032-079] lesson out to-morrow, Mr. Bowers. I have to hunt a new boarding-place.”

[032-080] Bowers looked up languidly from his desk where he had begun to go over a pile

[032-081] of letters. “What’s the matter with the Studio Club? Been fighting

[032-082] with them again?”

[032-083] “The Club’s all right for people who like to live that way. I

[032-084] don’t.”

[032-085] Bowers lifted his eyebrows. “Why so tempery?” he asked as he drew a

[032-086] check from an envelope postmarked “Minneapolis.”

[032-087] “I can’t work with a lot of girls around. They’re too

[032-088] familiar. I never could get along with girls of my own age. It’s all too

[032-089] chummy. Gets on my nerves. I didn’t come here to play kindergarten

[032-090] games.” Thea began energetically to arrange the scattered music on the

[032-091] piano.

[032-092] Bowers grimaced good-humoredly at her over the three checks he was pinning

[032-093] together. He liked to play at a rough game of banter with her. He flattered

[032-094] himself that he had made her harsher than she was when she first came to him;

[032-095] that he had got off a little of the sugar-coating Harsanyi always put on his

[032-096] pupils.

[032-097] “The art of making yourself agreeable never comes amiss, Miss Kronborg. I

[032-098] should say you rather need a little practice along that line. When you come to

[032-099] marketing your wares in the world, a little smoothness goes farther than a

[032-100] great deal of talent sometimes. If you happen to be cursed with a real talent,

[032-101] then you’ve got to be very smooth indeed, or you’ll never get your

[032-102] money back.” Bowers snapped the elastic band around his bank-book.

[032-103] Thea gave him a sharp, recognizing glance. “Well, that’s the money

[032-104] I’ll have to go without,” she replied.

[032-105] “Just what do you mean?”

[032-106] “I mean the money people have to grin for. I used to know a railroad man

[032-107] who said there was money in every profession that you couldn’t take.

[032-108] He’d tried a good many jobs,” Thea added musingly; “perhaps

[032-109] he was too particular about the kind he could take, for he never picked up

[032-110] much. He was proud, but I liked him for that.”

[032-111] Bowers rose and closed his desk. “Mrs. Priest is late again. By the way,

[032-112] Miss Kronborg, remember not to frown when you are playing for Mrs. Priest. You

[032-113] did not remember yesterday.”

[032-114] “You mean when she hits a tone with her breath like that? Why do you let

[032-115] her? You wouldn’t let me.”

[032-116] “I certainly would not. But that is a mannerism of Mrs. Priest’s.

[032-117] The public like it, and they pay a great deal of money for the pleasure of

[032-118] hearing her do it. There she is. Remember!”

[032-119] Bowers opened the door of the reception-room and a tall, imposing woman rustled

[032-120] in, bringing with her a glow of animation which pervaded the room as if half a

[032-121] dozen persons, all talking gayly, had come in instead of one. She was large,

[032-122] handsome, expansive, uncontrolled; one felt this the moment she crossed the

[032-123] threshold. She shone with care and cleanliness, mature vigor, unchallenged

[032-124] authority, gracious good-humor, and absolute confidence in her person, her

[032-125] powers, her position, and her way of life; a glowing, overwhelming

[032-126] self-satisfaction, only to be found where human society is young and strong and

[032-127] without yesterdays. Her face had a kind of heavy, thoughtless beauty, like a

[032-128] pink peony just at the point of beginning to fade. Her brown hair was waved in

[032-129] front and done up behind in a great twist, held by a tortoiseshell comb with

[032-130] gold filigree. She wore a beautiful little green hat with three long green

[032-131] feathers sticking straight up in front, a little cape made of velvet and fur

[032-132] with a yellow satin rose on it. Her gloves, her shoes, her veil, somehow made

[032-133] themselves felt. She gave the impression of wearing a cargo of splendid

[032-134] merchandise.

[032-135] Mrs. Priest nodded graciously to Thea, coquettishly to Bowers, and asked him to

[032-136] untie her veil for her. She threw her splendid wrap on a chair, the yellow

[032-137] lining out. Thea was already at the piano. Mrs. Priest stood behind her.

[032-138] “‘Rejoice Greatly’ first, please. And please don’t

[032-139] hurry it in there,” she put her arm over Thea’s shoulder, and

[032-140] indicated the passage by a sweep of her white glove. She threw out her chest,

[032-141] clasped her hands over her abdomen, lifted her chin, worked the muscles of her

[032-142] cheeks back and forth for a moment, and then began with conviction,

[032-143] “Re-jo-oice! Re-jo-oice!”

[032-144] Bowers paced the room with his catlike tread. When he checked Mrs.

[032-145] Priest’s vehemence at all, he handled her roughly; poked and hammered her

[032-146] massive person with cold satisfaction, almost as if he were taking out a grudge

[032-147] on this splendid creation. Such treatment the imposing lady did not at all

[032-148] resent. She tried harder and harder, her eyes growing all the while more

[032-149] lustrous and her lips redder. Thea played on as she was told, ignoring the

[032-150] singer’s struggles.

[032-151] When she first heard Mrs. Priest sing in church, Thea admired her. Since she

[032-152] had found out how dull the goodnatured soprano really was, she felt a deep

[032-153] contempt for her. She felt that Mrs. Priest ought to be reproved and even

[032-154] punished for her shortcomings; that she ought to be exposed,—at least to

[032-155] herself,—and not be permitted to live and shine in happy ignorance of

[032-156] what a poor thing it was she brought across so radiantly. Thea’s cold

[032-157] looks of reproof were lost upon Mrs. Priest; although the lady did murmur one

[032-158] day when she took Bowers home in her carriage, “How handsome your

[032-159] afternoon girl would be if she did not have that unfortunate squint; it gives

[032-160] her that vacant Swede look, like an animal.” That amused Bowers. He liked

[032-161] to watch the germination and growth of antipathies.

[032-162] One of the first disappointments Thea had to face when she returned to Chicago

[032-163] that fall, was the news that the Harsanyis were not coming back. They had spent

[032-164] the summer in a camp in the Adirondacks and were moving to New York. An old

[032-165] teacher and friend of Harsanyi’s, one of the best-known piano teachers in

[032-166] New York, was about to retire because of failing health and had arranged to

[032-167] turn his pupils over to Harsanyi. Andor was to give two recitals in New York in

[032-168] November, to devote himself to his new students until spring, and then to go on

[032-169] a short concert tour. The Harsanyis had taken a furnished apartment in New

[032-170] York, as they would not attempt to settle a place of their own until

[032-171] Andor’s recitals were over. The first of December, however, Thea received

[032-172] a note from Mrs. Harsanyi, asking her to call at the old studio, where she was

[032-173] packing their goods for shipment.

[032-174] The morning after this invitation reached her, Thea climbed the stairs and

[032-175] knocked at the familiar door. Mrs. Harsanyi herself opened it, and embraced her

[032-176] visitor warmly. Taking Thea into the studio, which was littered with excelsior

[032-177] and packing-cases, she stood holding her hand and looking at her in the strong

[032-178] light from the big window before she allowed her to sit down. Her quick eye saw

[032-179] many changes. The girl was taller, her figure had become definite, her carriage

[032-180] positive. She had got used to living in the body of a young woman, and she no

[032-181] longer tried to ignore it and behave as if she were a little girl. With that

[032-182] increased independence of body there had come a change in her face; an

[032-183] indifference, something hard and skeptical. Her clothes, too, were different,

[032-184] like the attire of a shopgirl who tries to follow the fashions; a purple suit,

[032-185] a piece of cheap fur, a three-cornered purple hat with a pompon sticking up in

[032-186] front. The queer country clothes she used to wear suited her much better, Mrs.

[032-187] Harsanyi thought. But such trifles, after all, were accidental and remediable.

[032-188] She put her hand on the girl’s strong shoulder.

[032-189] “How much the summer has done for you! Yes, you are a young lady at last.

[032-190] Andor will be so glad to hear about you.”

[032-191] Thea looked about at the disorder of the familiar room. The pictures were piled

[032-192] in a corner, the piano and the *chaise longue* were gone. “I suppose

[032-193] I ought to be glad you have gone away,” she said, “but I’m

[032-194] not. It’s a fine thing for Mr. Harsanyi, I suppose.”

[032-195] Mrs. Harsanyi gave her a quick glance that said more than words. “If you

[032-196] knew how long I have wanted to get him away from here, Miss Kronborg! He is

[032-197] never tired, never discouraged, now.”

[032-198] Thea sighed. “I’m glad for that, then.” Her eyes traveled

[032-199] over the faint discolorations on the walls where the pictures had hung.

[032-200] “I may run away myself. I don’t know whether I can stand it here

[032-201] without you.”

[032-202] “We hope that you can come to New York to study before very long. We have

[032-203] thought of that. And you must tell me how you are getting on with Bowers. Andor

[032-204] will want to know all about it.”

[032-205] “I guess I get on more or less. But I don’t like my work very well.

[032-206] It never seems serious as my work with Mr. Harsanyi did. I play Bowers’s

[032-207] accompaniments in the afternoons, you know. I thought I would learn a good deal

[032-208] from the people who work with him, but I don’t think I get much.”

[032-209] Mrs. Harsanyi looked at her inquiringly. Thea took out a carefully folded

[032-210] handkerchief from the bosom of her dress and began to draw the corners apart.

[032-211] “Singing doesn’t seem to be a very brainy profession, Mrs.

[032-212] Harsanyi,” she said slowly. “The people I see now are not a bit

[032-213] like the ones I used to meet here. Mr. Harsanyi’s pupils, even the dumb

[032-214] ones, had more—well, more of everything, it seems to me. The people I

[032-215] have to play accompaniments for are discouraging. The professionals, like

[032-216] Katharine Priest and Miles Murdstone, are worst of all. If I have to play

[032-217] ‘The Messiah’ much longer for Mrs. Priest, I’ll go out of my

[032-218] mind!” Thea brought her foot down sharply on the bare floor.

[032-219] Mrs. Harsanyi looked down at the foot in perplexity. “You mustn’t

[032-220] wear such high heels, my dear. They will spoil your walk and make you mince

[032-221] along. Can’t you at least learn to avoid what you dislike in these

[032-222] singers? I was never able to care for Mrs. Priest’s singing.”

[032-223] Thea was sitting with her chin lowered. Without moving her head she looked up

[032-224] at Mrs. Harsanyi and smiled; a smile much too cold and desperate to be seen on

[032-225] a young face, Mrs. Harsanyi felt. “Mrs. Harsanyi, it seems to me that

[032-226] what I learn is just *to dislike*. I dislike so much and so hard that it

[032-227] tires me out. I’ve got no heart for anything.” She threw up her

[032-228] head suddenly and sat in defiance, her hand clenched on the arm of the chair.

[032-229] “Mr. Harsanyi couldn’t stand these people an hour, I know he

[032-230] couldn’t. He’d put them right out of the window there, frizzes and

[032-231] feathers and all. Now, take that new soprano they’re all making such a

[032-232] fuss about, Jessie Darcey. She’s going on tour with a symphony orchestra

[032-233] and she’s working up her repertory with Bowers. She’s singing some

[032-234] Schumann songs Mr. Harsanyi used to go over with me. Well, I don’t know

[032-235] what he *would* do if he heard her.”

[032-236] “But if your own work goes well, and you know these people are wrong, why

[032-237] do you let them discourage you?”

[032-238] Thea shook her head. “That’s just what I don’t understand

[032-239] myself. Only, after I’ve heard them all afternoon, I come out frozen up.

[032-240] Somehow it takes the shine off of everything. People want Jessie Darcey and the

[032-241] kind of thing she does; so what’s the use?”

[032-242] Mrs. Harsanyi smiled. “That stile you must simply vault over. You must

[032-243] not begin to fret about the successes of cheap people. After all, what have

[032-244] they to do with you?”

[032-245] “Well, if I had somebody like Mr. Harsanyi, perhaps I wouldn’t fret

[032-246] about them. He was the teacher for me. Please tell him so.”

[032-247] Thea rose and Mrs. Harsanyi took her hand again. “I am sorry you have to

[032-248] go through this time of discouragement. I wish Andor could talk to you, he

[032-249] would understand it so well. But I feel like urging you to keep clear of Mrs.

[032-250] Priest and Jessie Darcey and all their works.”

[032-251] Thea laughed discordantly. “No use urging me. I don’t get on with

[032-252] them *at all*. My spine gets like a steel rail when they come near me. I

[032-253] liked them at first, you know. Their clothes and their manners were so fine,

[032-254] and Mrs. Priest *is* handsome. But now I keep wanting to tell them how

[032-255] stupid they are. Seems like they ought to be informed, don’t you think

[032-256] so?” There was a flash of the shrewd grin that Mrs. Harsanyi remembered.

[032-257] Thea pressed her hand. “I must go now. I had to give my lesson hour this

[032-258] morning to a Duluth woman who has come on to coach, and I must go and play

[032-259] ‘On Mighty Pens’ for her. Please tell Mr. Harsanyi that I think

[032-260] oratorio is a great chance for bluffers.”

[032-261] Mrs. Harsanyi detained her. “But he will want to know much more than that

[032-262] about you. You are free at seven? Come back this evening, then, and we will go

[032-263] to dinner somewhere, to some cheerful place. I think you need a party.”

[032-264] Thea brightened. “Oh, I do! I’ll love to come; that will be like

[032-265] old times. You see,” she lingered a moment, softening, “I

[032-266] wouldn’t mind if there were only *one* of them I could really

[032-267] admire.”

[032-268] “How about Bowers?” Mrs. Harsanyi asked as they were approaching

[032-269] the stairway.

[032-270] “Well, there’s nothing he loves like a good fakir, and nothing he

[032-271] hates like a good artist. I always remember something Mr. Harsanyi said about

[032-272] him. He said Bowers was the cold muffin that had been left on the plate.”

[032-273] Mrs. Harsanyi stopped short at the head of the stairs and said decidedly:

[032-274] “I think Andor made a mistake. I can’t believe that is the right

[032-275] atmosphere for you. It would hurt you more than most people. It’s all

[032-276] wrong.”

[032-277] “Something’s wrong,” Thea called back as she clattered down

[032-278] the stairs in her high heels.
