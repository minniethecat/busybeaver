## II

[047-001] [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]], smartly dressed for the afternoon, with a long black coat and

[047-002] gaiters was sitting in the dusty parlor of the Everett House. His manner was

[047-003] not in accord with his personal freshness, the good lines of his clothes, and

[047-004] the shining smoothness of his hair. His attitude was one of deep dejection, and

[047-005] his face, though it had the cool, unimpeachable fairness possible only to a

[047-006] very blond young man, was by no means happy. A page shuffled into the room and

[047-007] looked about. When he made out the dark figure in a shadowy corner, tracing

[047-008] over the carpet pattern with a cane, he droned, “The lady says you can

[047-009] come up, sir.”

[047-010] Fred picked up his hat and gloves and followed the creature, who seemed an aged

[047-011] boy in uniform, through dark corridors that smelled of old carpets. The page

[047-012] knocked at the door of Thea’s sitting-room, and then wandered away. Thea

[047-013] came to the door with a telegram in her hand. She asked Ottenburg to come in

[047-014] and pointed to one of the clumsy, sullen-looking chairs that were as thick as

[047-015] they were high. The room was brown with time, dark in spite of two windows that

[047-016] opened on Union Square, with dull curtains and carpet, and heavy,

[047-017] respectable-looking furniture in somber colors. The place was saved from utter

[047-018] dismalness by a coal fire under the black marble mantelpiece,—brilliantly

[047-019] reflected in a long mirror that hung between the two windows. This was the

[047-020] first time Fred had seen the room, and he took it in quickly, as he put down

[047-021] his hat and gloves.

[047-022] Thea seated herself at the walnut writing-desk, still holding the slip of

[047-023] yellow paper. “[[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] is coming,” she said. “He will be

[047-024] here Friday morning.”

[047-025] “Well, that’s good, at any rate,” her visitor replied with a

[047-026] determined effort at cheerfulness. Then, turning to the fire, he added blankly,

[047-027] “If you want him.”

[047-028] “Of course I want him. I would never have asked such a thing of him if I

[047-029] hadn’t wanted him a great deal. It’s a very expensive trip.”

[047-030] Thea spoke severely. Then she went on, in a milder tone. “He

[047-031] doesn’t say anything about the money, but I think his coming means that

[047-032] he can let me have it.”

[047-033] Fred was standing before the mantel, rubbing his hands together nervously.

[047-034] “Probably. You are still determined to call on him?” He sat down

[047-035] tentatively in the chair Thea had indicated. “I don’t see why you

[047-036] won’t borrow from me, and let him sign with you, for instance. That would

[047-037] constitute a perfectly regular business transaction. I could bring suit against

[047-038] either of you for my money.”

[047-039] Thea turned toward him from the desk. “We won’t take that up again,

[047-040] Fred. I should have a different feeling about it if I went on your money. In a

[047-041] way I shall feel freer on Dr. Archie’s, and in another way I shall feel

[047-042] more bound. I shall try even harder.” She paused. “He is almost

[047-043] like my father,” she added irrelevantly.

[047-044] “Still, he isn’t, you know,” Fred persisted. “It

[047-045] wouldn’t be anything new. I’ve loaned money to students before, and

[047-046] got it back, too.”

[047-047] “Yes; I know you’re generous,” Thea hurried over it,

[047-048] “but this will be the best way. He will be here on Friday did I tell

[047-049] you?”

[047-050] “I think you mentioned it. That’s rather soon. May I smoke?”

[047-051] he took out a small cigarette case. “I suppose you’ll be off next

[047-052] week?” he asked as he struck a match.

[047-053] “Just as soon as I can,” she replied with a restless movement of

[047-054] her arms, as if her dark-blue dress were too tight for her. “It seems as

[047-055] if I’d been here forever.”

[047-056] “And yet,” the young man mused, “we got in only four days

[047-057] ago. Facts really don’t count for much, do they? It’s all in the

[047-058] way people feel: even in little things.”

[047-059] Thea winced, but she did not answer him. She put the telegram back in its

[047-060] envelope and placed it carefully in one of the pigeonholes of the desk.

[047-061] “I suppose,” Fred brought out with effort, “that your friend

[047-062] is in your confidence?”

[047-063] “He always has been. I shall have to tell him about myself. I wish I

[047-064] could without dragging you in.”

[047-065] Fred shook himself. “Don’t bother about where you drag me,

[047-066] please,” he put in, flushing. “I don’t give—” he

[047-067] subsided suddenly.

[047-068] “I’m afraid,” Thea went on gravely, “that he

[047-069] won’t understand. He’ll be hard on you.”

[047-070] Fred studied the white ash of his cigarette before he flicked it off.

[047-071] “You mean he’ll see me as even worse than I am. Yes, I suppose I

[047-072] shall look very low to him: a fifthrate scoundrel. But that only matters in so

[047-073] far as it hurts his feelings.”

[047-074] Thea sighed. “We’ll both look pretty low. And after all, we must

[047-075] really be just about as we shall look to him.”

[047-076] Ottenburg started up and threw his cigarette into the grate. “That I

[047-077] deny. Have you ever been really frank with this preceptor of your childhood,

[047-078] even when you *were* a child? Think a minute, have you? Of course not!

[047-079] From your cradle, as I once told you, you’ve been ‘doing it’

[047-080] on the side, living your own life, admitting to yourself things that would

[047-081] horrify him. You’ve always deceived him to the extent of letting him

[047-082] think you different from what you are. He couldn’t understand then, he

[047-083] can’t understand now. So why not spare yourself and him?”

[047-084] She shook her head. “Of course, I’ve had my own thoughts. Maybe he

[047-085] has had his, too. But I’ve never done anything before that he would much

[047-086] mind. I must put myself right with him,—as right as I can,—to begin

[047-087] over. He’ll make allowances for me. He always has. But I’m afraid

[047-088] he won’t for you.”

[047-089] “Leave that to him and me. I take it you want me to see him?” Fred

[047-090] sat down again and began absently to trace the carpet pattern with his cane.

[047-091] “At the worst,” he spoke wanderingly, “I thought you’d

[047-092] perhaps let me go in on the business end of it and invest along with you.

[047-093] You’d put in your talent and ambition and hard work, and I’d put in

[047-094] the money and—well, nobody’s good wishes are to be scorned, not

[047-095] even mine. Then, when the thing panned out big, we could share together. Your

[047-096] doctor friend hasn’t cared half so much about your future as I

[047-097] have.”

[047-098] “He’s cared a good deal. He doesn’t know as much about such

[047-099] things as you do. Of course you’ve been a great deal more help to me than

[047-100] any one else ever has,” Thea said quietly. The black clock on the mantel

[047-101] began to strike. She listened to the five strokes and then said,

[047-102] “I’d have liked your helping me eight months ago. But now,

[047-103] you’d simply be keeping me.”

[047-104] “You weren’t ready for it eight months ago.” Fred leaned back

[047-105] at last in his chair. “You simply weren’t ready for it. You were

[047-106] too tired. You were too timid. Your whole tone was too low. You couldn’t

[047-107] rise from a chair like that,”—she had started up apprehensively and

[047-108] gone toward the window.—“You were fumbling and awkward. Since then

[047-109] you’ve come into your personality. You were always locking horns with it

[047-110] before. You were a sullen little drudge eight months ago, afraid of being

[047-111] caught at either looking or moving like yourself. Nobody could tell anything

[047-112] about you. A voice is not an instrument that’s found ready-made. A voice

[047-113] is personality. It can be as big as a circus and as common as

[047-114] dirt.—There’s good money in that kind, too, but I don’t

[047-115] happen to be interested in them.—Nobody could tell much about what you

[047-116] might be able to do, last winter. I divined more than anybody else.”

[047-117] “Yes, I know you did.” Thea walked over to the oldfashioned mantel

[047-118] and held her hands down to the glow of the fire. “I owe so much to you,

[047-119] and that’s what makes things hard. That’s why I have to get away

[047-120] from you altogether. I depend on you for so many things. Oh, I did even last

[047-121] winter, in [[chicago|Chicago]]!” She knelt down by the grate and held her hands

[047-122] closer to the coals. “And one thing leads to another.”

[047-123] Ottenburg watched her as she bent toward the fire. His glance brightened a

[047-124] little. “Anyhow, you couldn’t look as you do now, before you knew

[047-125] me. You *were* clumsy. And whatever you do now, you do splendidly. And you

[047-126] can’t cry enough to spoil your face for more than ten minutes. It comes

[047-127] right back, in spite of you. It’s only since you’ve known me that

[047-128] you’ve let yourself be beautiful.”

[047-129] Without rising she turned her face away. Fred went on impetuously. “Oh,

[047-130] you can turn it away from me, Thea; you can take it away from me! All the

[047-131] same—” his spurt died and he fell back. “How can you turn on

[047-132] me so, after all!” he sighed.

[047-133] “I haven’t. But when you arranged with yourself to take me in like

[047-134] that, you couldn’t have been thinking very kindly of me. I can’t

[047-135] understand how you carried it through, when I was so easy, and all the

[047-136] circumstances were so easy.”

[047-137] Her crouching position by the fire became threatening. Fred got up, and Thea

[047-138] also rose.

[047-139] “No,” he said, “I can’t make you see that now. Some

[047-140] time later, perhaps, you will understand better. For one thing, I honestly

[047-141] could not imagine that words, names, meant so much to you.” Fred was

[047-142] talking with the desperation of a man who has put himself in the wrong and who

[047-143] yet feels that there was an idea of truth in his conduct. “Suppose that

[047-144] you had married your brakeman and lived with him year after year, caring for

[047-145] him even less than you do for your doctor, or for Harsanyi. I suppose you would

[047-146] have felt quite all right about it, because that relation has a name in good

[047-147] standing. To me, that seems—sickening!” He took a rapid turn about

[047-148] the room and then as Thea remained standing, he rolled one of the elephantine

[047-149] chairs up to the hearth for her.

[047-150] “Sit down and listen to me for a moment, Thea.” He began pacing

[047-151] from the hearthrug to the window and back again, while she sat down

[047-152] compliantly. “Don’t you know most of the people in the world are

[047-153] not individuals at all? They never have an individual idea or experience. A lot

[047-154] of girls go to boarding-school together, come out the same season, dance at the

[047-155] same parties, are married off in groups, have their babies at about the same

[047-156] time, send their children to school together, and so the human crop renews

[047-157] itself. Such women know as much about the reality of the forms they go through

[047-158] as they know about the wars they learn the dates of. They get their most

[047-159] personal experiences out of novels and plays. Everything is second-hand with

[047-160] them. Why, you *couldn’t* live like that.”

[047-161] Thea sat looking toward the mantel, her eyes half closed, her chin level, her

[047-162] head set as if she were enduring something. Her hands, very white, lay passive

[047-163] on her dark gown. From the window corner Fred looked at them and at her. He

[047-164] shook his head and flashed an angry, tormented look out into the blue twilight

[047-165] over the Square, through which muffled cries and calls and the clang of car

[047-166] bells came up from the street. He turned again and began to pace the floor, his

[047-167] hands in his pockets.

[047-168] “Say what you will, [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]], you are not that sort of person. You

[047-169] will never sit alone with a pacifier and a novel. You won’t subsist on

[047-170] what the old ladies have put into the bottle for you. You will always break

[047-171] through into the realities. That was the first thing Harsanyi found out about

[047-172] you; that you couldn’t be kept on the outside. If you’d lived in

[047-173] Moonstone all your life and got on with the discreet brakeman, you’d have

[047-174] had just the same nature. Your children would have been the realities then,

[047-175] probably. If they’d been commonplace, you’d have killed them with

[047-176] driving. You’d have managed some way to live twenty times as much as the

[047-177] people around you.”

[047-178] Fred paused. He sought along the shadowy ceiling and heavy mouldings for words.

[047-179] When he began again, his voice was lower, and at first he spoke with less

[047-180] conviction, though again it grew on him. “Now I knew all this—oh,

[047-181] knew it better than I can ever make you understand! You’ve been running a

[047-182] handicap. You had no time to lose. I wanted you to have what you need and to

[047-183] get on fast—get through with me, if need be; I counted on that.

[047-184] You’ve no time to sit round and analyze your conduct or your feelings.

[047-185] Other women give their whole lives to it. They’ve nothing else to do.

[047-186] Helping a man to get his divorce is a career for them; just the sort of

[047-187] intellectual exercise they like.”

[047-188] Fred dived fiercely into his pockets as if he would rip them out and scatter

[047-189] their contents to the winds. Stopping before her, he took a deep breath and

[047-190] went on again, this time slowly. “All that sort of thing is foreign to

[047-191] you. You’d be nowhere at it. You haven’t that kind of mind. The

[047-192] grammatical niceties of conduct are dark to you. You’re simple—and

[047-193] poetic.” Fred’s voice seemed to be wandering about in the

[047-194] thickening dusk. “You won’t play much. You won’t, perhaps,

[047-195] love many times.” He paused. “And you did love me, you know. Your

[047-196] railroad friend would have understood me. I *could* have thrown you back.

[047-197] The reverse was there,—it stared me in the face,—but I

[047-198] couldn’t pull it. I let you drive ahead.” He threw out his hands.

[047-199] What Thea noticed, oddly enough, was the flash of the firelight on his cuff

[047-200] link. He turned again. “And you’ll always drive ahead,” he

[047-201] muttered. “It’s your way.”

[047-202] There was a long silence. Fred had dropped into a chair. He seemed, after such

[047-203] an explosion, not to have a word left in him. Thea put her hand to the back of

[047-204] her neck and pressed it, as if the muscles there were aching.

[047-205] “Well,” she said at last, “I at least overlook more in you

[047-206] than I do in myself. I am always excusing you to myself. I don’t do much

[047-207] else.”

[047-208] “Then why, in Heaven’s name, won’t you let me be your friend?

[047-209] You make a scoundrel of me, borrowing money from another man to get out of my

[047-210] clutches.”

[047-211] “If I borrow from him, it’s to study. Anything I took from you

[047-212] would be different. As I said before, you’d be keeping me.”

[047-213] “Keeping! I like your language. It’s pure Moonstone,

[047-214] Thea,—like your point of view. I wonder how long you’ll be a

[047-215] Methodist.” He turned away bitterly.

[047-216] “Well, I’ve never said I wasn’t Moonstone, have I? I am, and

[047-217] that’s why I want Dr. Archie. I can’t see anything so funny about

[047-218] Moonstone, you know.” She pushed her chair back a little from the hearth

[047-219] and clasped her hands over her knee, still looking thoughtfully into the red

[047-220] coals. “We always come back to the same thing, Fred. The name, as you

[047-221] call it, makes a difference to me how I feel about myself. You would have acted

[047-222] very differently with a girl of your own kind, and that’s why I

[047-223] can’t take anything from you now. You’ve made everything

[047-224] impossible. Being married is one thing and not being married is the other

[047-225] thing, and that’s all there is to it. I can’t see how you reasoned

[047-226] with yourself, if you took the trouble to reason. You say I was too much alone,

[047-227] and yet what you did was to cut me off more than I ever had been. Now I’m

[047-228] going to try to make good to my friends out there. That’s all there is

[047-229] left for me.”

[047-230] “Make good to your friends!” Fred burst out. “What one of

[047-231] them cares as I care, or believes as I believe? I’ve told you I’ll

[047-232] never ask a gracious word from you until I can ask it with all the churches in

[047-233] Christendom at my back.”

[047-234] Thea looked up, and when she saw Fred’s face, she thought sadly that he,

[047-235] too, looked as if things were spoiled for him. “If you know me as well as

[047-236] you say you do, Fred,” she said slowly, “then you are not being

[047-237] honest with yourself. You know that I can’t do things halfway. If you

[047-238] kept me at all—you’d keep me.” She dropped her head wearily

[047-239] on her hand and sat with her forehead resting on her fingers.

[047-240] Fred leaned over her and said just above his breath, “Then, when I get

[047-241] that divorce, you’ll take it up with me again? You’ll at least let

[047-242] me know, warn me, before there is a serious question of anybody else?”

[047-243] Without lifting her head, Thea answered him. “Oh, I don’t think

[047-244] there will ever be a question of anybody else. Not if I can help it. I suppose

[047-245] I’ve given you every reason to think there will be,—at once, on

[047-246] shipboard, any time.”

[047-247] Ottenburg drew himself up like a shot. “Stop it, Thea!” he said

[047-248] sharply. “That’s one thing you’ve never done. That’s

[047-249] like any common woman.” He saw her shoulders lift a little and grow calm.

[047-250] Then he went to the other side of the room and took up his hat and gloves from

[047-251] the sofa. He came back cheerfully. “I didn’t drop in to bully you

[047-252] this afternoon. I came to coax you to go out for tea with me somewhere.”

[047-253] He waited, but she did not look up or lift her head, still sunk on her hand.

[047-254] Her handkerchief had fallen. Fred picked it up and put it on her knee, pressing

[047-255] her fingers over it. “Good-night, dear and wonderful,” he

[047-256] whispered,—“wonderful and dear! How can you ever get away from me

[047-257] when I will always follow you, through every wall, through every door, wherever

[047-258] you go.” He looked down at her bent head, and the curve of her neck that

[047-259] was so sad. He stooped, and with his lips just touched her hair where the

[047-260] firelight made it ruddiest. “I didn’t know I had it in me, Thea. I

[047-261] thought it was all a fairy tale. I don’t know myself any more.” He

[047-262] closed his eyes and breathed deeply. “The salt’s all gone out of

[047-263] your hair. It’s full of sun and wind again. I believe it has

[047-264] memories.” Again she heard him take a deep breath. “I could do

[047-265] without you for a lifetime, if that would give you to yourself. A woman like

[047-266] you doesn’t find herself, alone.”

[047-267] She thrust her free hand up to him. He kissed it softly, as if she were asleep

[047-268] and he were afraid of waking her.

[047-269] From the door he turned back irrelevantly. “As to your old friend, Thea,

[047-270] if he’s to be here on Friday, why,”—he snatched out his watch

[047-271] and held it down to catch the light from the grate,—“he’s on

[047-272] the train now! That ought to cheer you. Good-night.” She heard the door

[047-273] close.
