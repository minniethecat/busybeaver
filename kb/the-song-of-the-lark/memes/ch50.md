## V

[050-001] Thea was to sail on Tuesday, at noon, and on Saturday [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] arranged

[050-002] for her passage, while she and [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] went shopping. With rugs and

[050-003] sea-clothes she was already provided; Fred had got everything of that sort she

[050-004] needed for the voyage up from Vera Cruz. On Sunday afternoon Thea went to see

[050-005] the Harsanyis. When she returned to her hotel, she found a note from Ottenburg,

[050-006] saying that he had called and would come again to-morrow.

[050-007] On Monday morning, while she was at breakfast, Fred came in. She knew by his

[050-008] hurried, distracted air as he entered the dining-room that something had gone

[050-009] wrong. He had just got a telegram from home. His mother had been thrown from

[050-010] her carriage and hurt; a concussion of some sort, and she was unconscious. He

[050-011] was leaving for St. Louis that night on the eleven o’clock train. He had

[050-012] a great deal to attend to during the day. He would come that evening, if he

[050-013] might, and stay with her until train time, while she was doing her packing.

[050-014] Scarcely waiting for her consent, he hurried away.

[050-015] All day Thea was somewhat cast down. She was sorry for Fred, and she missed the

[050-016] feeling that she was the one person in his mind. He had scarcely looked at her

[050-017] when they exchanged words at the breakfast-table. She felt as if she were set

[050-018] aside, and she did not seem so important even to herself as she had yesterday.

[050-019] Certainly, she reflected, it was high time that she began to take care of

[050-020] herself again. Dr. Archie came for dinner, but she sent him away early, telling

[050-021] him that she would be ready to go to the boat with him at half-past ten the

[050-022] next morning. When she went upstairs, she looked gloomily at the open trunk in

[050-023] her sitting-room, and at the trays piled on the sofa. She stood at the window

[050-024] and watched a quiet snowstorm spending itself over the city. More than anything

[050-025] else, falling snow always made her think of Moonstone; of the Kohlers’

[050-026] garden, of Thor’s sled, of dressing by lamplight and starting off to

[050-027] school before the paths were broken.

[050-028] When Fred came, he looked tired, and he took her hand almost without seeing

[050-029] her.

[050-030] “I’m so sorry, Fred. Have you had any more word?”

[050-031] “She was still unconscious at four this afternoon. It doesn’t look

[050-032] very encouraging.” He approached the fire and warmed his hands. He seemed

[050-033] to have contracted, and he had not at all his habitual ease of manner.

[050-034] “Poor mother!” he exclaimed; “nothing like this should have

[050-035] happened to her. She has so much pride of person. She’s not at all an old

[050-036] woman, you know. She’s never got beyond vigorous and rather dashing

[050-037] middle age.” He turned abruptly to Thea and for the first time really

[050-038] looked at her. “How badly things come out! She’d have liked you for

[050-039] a daughter-in-law. Oh, you’d have fought like the devil, but you’d

[050-040] have respected each other.” He sank into a chair and thrust his feet out

[050-041] to the fire. “Still,” he went on thoughtfully, seeming to address

[050-042] the ceiling, “it might have been bad for you. Our big German houses, our

[050-043] good German cooking—you might have got lost in the upholstery. That

[050-044] substantial comfort might take the temper out of you, dull your edge.

[050-045] Yes,” he sighed, “I guess you were meant for the jolt of the

[050-046] breakers.”

[050-047] “I guess I’ll get plenty of jolt,” Thea murmured, turning to

[050-048] her trunk.

[050-049] “I’m rather glad I’m not staying over until to-morrow,”

[050-050] Fred reflected. “I think it’s easier for me to glide out like this.

[050-051] I feel now as if everything were rather casual, anyhow. A thing like that dulls

[050-052] one’s feelings.”

[050-053] Thea, standing by her trunk, made no reply. Presently he shook himself and

[050-054] rose. “Want me to put those trays in for you?”

[050-055] “No, thank you. I’m not ready for them yet.”

[050-056] Fred strolled over to the sofa, lifted a scarf from one of the trays and stood

[050-057] abstractedly drawing it through his fingers. “You’ve been so kind

[050-058] these last few days, Thea, that I began to hope you might soften a little; that

[050-059] you might ask me to come over and see you this summer.”

[050-060] “If you thought that, you were mistaken,” she said slowly.

[050-061] “I’ve hardened, if anything. But I shan’t carry any grudge

[050-062] away with me, if you mean that.”

[050-063] He dropped the scarf. “And there’s nothing—nothing at all

[050-064] you’ll let me do?”

[050-065] “Yes, there is one thing, and it’s a good deal to ask. If I get

[050-066] knocked out, or never get on, I’d like you to see that Dr. Archie gets

[050-067] his money back. I’m taking three thousand dollars of his.”

[050-068] “Why, of course I shall. You may dismiss that from your mind. How fussy

[050-069] you are about money, Thea. You make such a point of it.” He turned

[050-070] sharply and walked to the windows.

[050-071] Thea sat down in the chair he had quitted. “It’s only poor people

[050-072] who feel that way about money, and who are really honest,” she said

[050-073] gravely. “Sometimes I think that to be really honest, you must have been

[050-074] so poor that you’ve been tempted to steal.”

[050-075] “To what?”

[050-076] “To steal. I used to be, when I first went to [[chicago|Chicago]] and saw all the

[050-077] things in the big stores there. Never anything big, but little things, the kind

[050-078] I’d never seen before and could never afford. I did take something once,

[050-079] before I knew it.”

[050-080] Fred came toward her. For the first time she had his whole attention, in the

[050-081] degree to which she was accustomed to having it. “Did you? What was

[050-082] it?” he asked with interest.

[050-083] “A sachet. A little blue silk bag of orris-root powder. There was a whole

[050-084] counterful of them, marked down to fifty cents. I’d never seen any

[050-085] before, and they seemed irresistible. I took one up and wandered about the

[050-086] store with it. Nobody seemed to notice, so I carried it off.”

[050-087] Fred laughed. “Crazy child! Why, your things always smell of orris; is it

[050-088] a penance?”

[050-089] “No, I love it. But I saw that the firm didn’t lose anything by me.

[050-090] I went back and bought it there whenever I had a quarter to spend. I got a lot

[050-091] to take to Arizona. I made it up to them.”

[050-092] “I’ll bet you did!” Fred took her hand. “Why

[050-093] didn’t I find you that first winter? I’d have loved you just as you

[050-094] came!”

[050-095] Thea shook her head. “No, you wouldn’t, but you might have found me

[050-096] amusing. The Harsanyis said yesterday afternoon that I wore such a funny cape

[050-097] and that my shoes always squeaked. They think I’ve improved. I told them

[050-098] it was your doing if I had, and then they looked scared.”

[050-099] “Did you sing for Harsanyi?”

[050-100] “Yes. He thinks I’ve improved there, too. He said nice things to

[050-101] me. Oh, he was very nice! He agrees with you about my going to Lehmann, if

[050-102] she’ll take me. He came out to the elevator with me, after we had said

[050-103] good-bye. He said something nice out there, too, but he seemed sad.”

[050-104] “What was it that he said?”

[050-105] “He said, ‘When people, serious people, believe in you, they give

[050-106] you some of their best, so—take care of it, Miss Kronborg.’ Then he

[050-107] waved his hands and went back.”

[050-108] “If you sang, I wish you had taken me along. Did you sing well?”

[050-109] Fred turned from her and went back to the window. “I wonder when I shall

[050-110] hear you sing again.” He picked up a bunch of violets and smelled them.

[050-111] “You know, your leaving me like this—well, it’s almost

[050-112] inhuman to be able to do it so kindly and unconditionally.”

[050-113] “I suppose it is. It was almost inhuman to be able to leave home,

[050-114] too,—the last time, when I knew it was for good. But all the same, I

[050-115] cared a great deal more than anybody else did. I lived through it. I have no

[050-116] choice now. No matter how much it breaks me up, I have to go. Do I seem to

[050-117] enjoy it?”

[050-118] Fred bent over her trunk and picked up something which proved to be a score,

[050-119] clumsily bound. “What’s this? Did you ever try to sing this?”

[050-120] He opened it and on the engraved title-page read Wunsch’s inscription,

[050-121] “*Einst, O Wunder!*” He looked up sharply at Thea.

[050-122] “Wunsch gave me that when he went away. I’ve told you about him, my

[050-123] old teacher in Moonstone. He loved that opera.”

[050-124] Fred went toward the fireplace, the book under his arm, singing softly:—

[050-125] “Einst, O Wunder, entblüht auf meinem Grabe,\

[050-126] Eine Blume der Asche meines Herzens;”

[050-127] “You have no idea at all where he is, Thea?” He leaned against the

[050-128] mantel and looked down at her.

[050-129] “No, I wish I had. He may be dead by this time. That was five years ago,

[050-130] and he used himself hard. Mrs. Kohler was always afraid he would die off alone

[050-131] somewhere and be stuck under the prairie. When we last heard of him, he was in

[050-132] Kansas.”

[050-133] “If he were to be found, I’d like to do something for him. I seem

[050-134] to get a good deal of him from this.” He opened the book again, where he

[050-135] kept the place with his finger, and scrutinized the purple ink. “How like

[050-136] a German! Had he ever sung the song for you?”

[050-137] “No. I didn’t know where the words were from until once, when

[050-138] Harsanyi sang it for me, I recognized them.”

[050-139] Fred closed the book. “Let me see, what was your noble brakeman’s

[050-140] name?”

[050-141] Thea looked up with surprise. “Ray, [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]].”

[050-142] “Ray Kennedy!” he laughed. “It couldn’t well have been

[050-143] better! Wunsch and Dr. Archie, and Ray, and I,”—he told them off on

[050-144] his fingers,—“your whistling-posts! You haven’t done so

[050-145] badly. We’ve backed you as we could, some in our weakness and some in our

[050-146] might. In your dark hours—and you’ll have them—you may like

[050-147] to remember us.” He smiled whimsically and dropped the score into the

[050-148] trunk. “You are taking that with you?”

[050-149] “Surely I am. I haven’t so many keepsakes that I can afford to

[050-150] leave that. I haven’t got many that I value so highly.”

[050-151] “That you value so highly?” Fred echoed her gravity playfully.

[050-152] “You are delicious when you fall into your vernacular.” He laughed

[050-153] half to himself.

[050-154] “What’s the matter with that? Isn’t it perfectly good

[050-155] English?”

[050-156] “Perfectly good Moonstone, my dear. Like the readymade clothes that hang

[050-157] in the windows, made to fit everybody and fit nobody, a phrase that can be used

[050-158] on all occasions. Oh,”—he started across the room

[050-159] again,—“that’s one of the fine things about your going!

[050-160] You’ll be with the right sort of people and you’ll learn a good,

[050-161] live, warm German, that will be like yourself. You’ll get a new speech

[050-162] full of shades and color like your voice; alive, like your mind. It will be

[050-163] almost like being born again, Thea.”

[050-164] She was not offended. Fred had said such things to her before, and she wanted

[050-165] to learn. In the natural course of things she would never have loved a man from

[050-166] whom she could not learn a great deal.

[050-167] “Harsanyi said once,” she remarked thoughtfully, “that if one

[050-168] became an artist one had to be born again, and that one owed nothing to

[050-169] anybody.”

[050-170] “Exactly. And when I see you again I shall not see you, but your

[050-171] daughter. May I?” He held up his cigarette case questioningly and then

[050-172] began to smoke, taking up again the song which ran in his head:—

[050-173] “Deutlich schimmert auf jedem, Purpurblättchen, Adelaide!”

[050-174] “I have half an hour with you yet, and then, exit Fred.” He walked

[050-175] about the room, smoking and singing the words under his breath.

[050-176] “You’ll like the voyage,” he said abruptly. “That first

[050-177] approach to a foreign shore, stealing up on it and finding

[050-178] it—there’s nothing like it. It wakes up everything that’s

[050-179] asleep in you. You won’t mind my writing to some people in Berlin?

[050-180] They’ll be nice to you.”

[050-181] “I wish you would.” Thea gave a deep sigh. “I wish one could

[050-182] look ahead and see what is coming to one.”

[050-183] “Oh, no!” Fred was smoking nervously; “that would never do.

[050-184] It’s the uncertainty that makes one try. You’ve never had any sort

[050-185] of chance, and now I fancy you’ll make it up to yourself. You’ll

[050-186] find the way to let yourself out in one long flight.”

[050-187] Thea put her hand on her heart. “And then drop like the rocks we used to

[050-188] throw—anywhere.” She left the chair and went over to the sofa,

[050-189] hunting for something in the trunk trays. When she came back she found Fred

[050-190] sitting in her place. “Here are some handkerchiefs of yours. I’ve

[050-191] kept one or two. They’re larger than mine and useful if one has a

[050-192] headache.”

[050-193] “Thank you. How nicely they smell of your things!” He looked at the

[050-194] white squares for a moment and then put them in his pocket. He kept the low

[050-195] chair, and as she stood beside him he took her hands and sat looking intently

[050-196] at them, as if he were examining them for some special purpose, tracing the

[050-197] long round fingers with the tips of his own. “Ordinarily, you know, there

[050-198] are reefs that a man catches to and keeps his nose above water. But this is a

[050-199] case by itself. There seems to be no limit as to how much I can be in love with

[050-200] you. I keep going.” He did not lift his eyes from her fingers, which he

[050-201] continued to study with the same fervor. “Every kind of stringed

[050-202] instrument there is plays in your hands, Thea,” he whispered, pressing

[050-203] them to his face.

[050-204] She dropped beside him and slipped into his arms, shutting her eyes and lifting

[050-205] her cheek to his. “Tell me one thing,” Fred whispered. “You

[050-206] said that night on the boat, when I first told you, that if you could you would

[050-207] crush it all up in your hands and throw it into the sea. Would you, all those

[050-208] weeks?”

[050-209] She shook her head.

[050-210] “Answer me, would you?”

[050-211] “No, I was angry then. I’m not now. I’d never give them up.

[050-212] Don’t make me pay too much.” In that embrace they lived over again

[050-213] all the others. When Thea drew away from him, she dropped her face in her

[050-214] hands. “You are good to me,” she breathed, “you are!”

[050-215] Rising to his feet, he put his hands under her elbows and lifted her gently. He

[050-216] drew her toward the door with him. “Get all you can. Be generous with

[050-217] yourself. Don’t stop short of splendid things. I want them for you more

[050-218] than I want anything else, more than I want one splendid thing for myself. I

[050-219] can’t help feeling that you’ll gain, somehow, by my losing so much.

[050-220] That you’ll gain the very thing I lose. Take care of her, as Harsanyi

[050-221] said. She’s wonderful!” He kissed her and went out of the door

[050-222] without looking back, just as if he were coming again to-morrow.

[050-223] Thea went quickly into her bedroom. She brought out an armful of muslin things,

[050-224] knelt down, and began to lay them in the trays. Suddenly she stopped, dropped

[050-225] forward and leaned against the open trunk, her head on her arms. The tears fell

[050-226] down on the dark old carpet. It came over her how many people must have said

[050-227] good-bye and been unhappy in that room. Other people, before her time, had

[050-228] hired this room to cry in. Strange rooms and strange streets and faces, how

[050-229] sick at heart they made one! Why was she going so far, when what she wanted was

[050-230] some familiar place to hide in?—the rock house, her little room in

[050-231] Moonstone, her own bed. Oh, how good it would be to lie down in that little

[050-232] bed, to cut the nerve that kept one struggling, that pulled one on and on, to

[050-233] sink into peace there, with all the family safe and happy downstairs. After

[050-234] all, she was a Moonstone girl, one of the preacher’s children. Everything

[050-235] else was in Fred’s imagination. Why was she called upon to take such

[050-236] chances? Any safe, humdrum work that did not compromise her would be better.

[050-237] But if she failed now, she would lose her soul. There was nowhere to fall,

[050-238] after one took that step, except into abysses of wretchedness. She knew what

[050-239] abysses, for she could still hear the old man playing in the snowstorm,

[050-240] “*Ach, ich habe sie verloren!*” That melody was released in

[050-241] her like a passion of longing. Every nerve in her body thrilled to it. It

[050-242] brought her to her feet, carried her somehow to bed and into troubled sleep.

[050-243] That night she taught in Moonstone again: she beat her pupils in hideous rages,

[050-244] she kept on beating them. She sang at funerals, and struggled at the piano with

[050-245] Harsanyi. In one dream she was looking into a hand-glass and thinking that she

[050-246] was getting better-looking, when the glass began to grow smaller and smaller

[050-247] and her own reflection to shrink, until she realized that she was looking into

[050-248] Ray Kennedy’s eyes, seeing her face in that look of his which she could

[050-249] never forget. All at once the eyes were Fred Ottenburg’s, and not

[050-250] Ray’s. All night she heard the shrieking of trains, whistling in and out

[050-251] of Moonstone, as she used to hear them in her sleep when they blew shrill in

[050-252] the winter air. But to-night they were terrifying,—the spectral, fated

[050-253] trains that “raced with death,” about which the old woman from the

[050-254] depot used to pray.

[050-255] In the morning she wakened breathless after a struggle with Mrs. Livery

[050-256] Johnson’s daughter. She started up with a bound, threw the blankets back

[050-257] and sat on the edge of the bed, her night-dress open, her long braids hanging

[050-258] over her bosom, blinking at the daylight. After all, it was not too late. She

[050-259] was only twenty years old, and the boat sailed at noon. There was still time!
