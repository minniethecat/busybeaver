## III

[048-001] On Friday afternoon [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] was walking excitedly up and down her

[048-002] sitting-room, which at that hour was flooded by thin, clear sunshine. Both

[048-003] windows were open, and the fire in the grate was low, for the day was one of

[048-004] those false springs that sometimes blow into New York from the sea in the

[048-005] middle of winter, soft, warm, with a persuasive salty moisture in the air and a

[048-006] relaxing thaw under foot. Thea was flushed and animated, and she seemed as

[048-007] restless as the sooty sparrows that chirped and cheeped distractingly about the

[048-008] windows. She kept looking at the black clock, and then down into the Square.

[048-009] The room was full of flowers, and she stopped now and then to arrange them or

[048-010] to move them into the sunlight. After the bellboy came to announce a visitor,

[048-011] she took some Roman hyacinths from a glass and stuck them in the front of her

[048-012] dark-blue dress.

[048-013] When at last [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] appeared in the doorway, she met him with an

[048-014] exclamation of pleasure. “I am glad you’ve come, Fred. I was afraid

[048-015] you might not get my note, and I wanted to see you before you see [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]].

[048-016] He’s so nice!” She brought her hands together to emphasize her

[048-017] statement.

[048-018] “Is he? I’m glad. You see I’m quite out of breath. I

[048-019] didn’t wait for the elevator, but ran upstairs. I was so pleased at being

[048-020] sent for.” He dropped his hat and overcoat. “Yes, I should say he

[048-021] is nice! I don’t seem to recognize all of these,” waving his

[048-022] handkerchief about at the flowers.

[048-023] “Yes, he brought them himself, in a big box. He brought lots with him

[048-024] besides flowers. Oh, lots of things! The old Moonstone

[048-025] feeling,”—Thea moved her hand back and forth in the air, fluttering

[048-026] her fingers,—“the feeling of starting out, early in the morning, to

[048-027] take my lesson.”

[048-028] “And you’ve had everything out with him?”

[048-029] “No, I haven’t.”

[048-030] “Haven’t?” He looked up in consternation.

[048-031] “No, I haven’t!” Thea spoke excitedly, moving about over the

[048-032] sunny patches on the grimy carpet. “I’ve lied to him, just as you

[048-033] said I had always lied to him, and that’s why I’m so happy.

[048-034] I’ve let him think what he likes to think. Oh, I couldn’t do

[048-035] anything else, Fred,”—she shook her head emphatically. “If

[048-036] you’d seen him when he came in, so pleased and excited! You see this is a

[048-037] great adventure for him. From the moment I began to talk to him, he entreated

[048-038] me not to say too much, not to spoil his notion of me. Not in so many words, of

[048-039] course. But if you’d seen his eyes, his face, his kind hands! Oh, no! I

[048-040] couldn’t.” She took a deep breath, as if with a renewed sense of

[048-041] her narrow escape.

[048-042] “Then, what did you tell him?” Fred demanded.

[048-043] Thea sat down on the edge of the sofa and began shutting and opening her hands

[048-044] nervously. “Well, I told him enough, and not too much. I told him all

[048-045] about how good you were to me last winter, getting me engagements and things,

[048-046] and how you had helped me with my work more than anybody. Then I told him about

[048-047] how you sent me down to the ranch when I had no money or anything.” She

[048-048] paused and wrinkled her forehead. “And I told him that I wanted to marry

[048-049] you and ran away to Mexico with you, and that I was awfully happy until you

[048-050] told me that you couldn’t marry me because—well, I told him

[048-051] why.” Thea dropped her eyes and moved the toe of her shoe about

[048-052] restlessly on the carpet.

[048-053] “And he took it from you, like that?” Fred asked, almost with awe.

[048-054] “Yes, just like that, and asked no questions. He was hurt; he had some

[048-055] wretched moments. I could see him squirming and squirming and trying to get

[048-056] past it. He kept shutting his eyes and rubbing his forehead. But when I told

[048-057] him that I absolutely knew you wanted to marry me, that you would whenever you

[048-058] could, that seemed to help him a good deal.”

[048-059] “And that satisfied him?” Fred asked wonderingly. He could not

[048-060] quite imagine what kind of person Dr. Archie might be.

[048-061] “He took me by the shoulders once and asked, oh, in such a frightened

[048-062] way, ‘Thea, was he *good* to you, this young man?’ When I told

[048-063] him you were, he looked at me again: ‘And you care for him a great deal,

[048-064] you believe in him?’ Then he seemed satisfied.” Thea paused.

[048-065] “You see, he’s just tremendously good, and tremendously afraid of

[048-066] things—of some things. Otherwise he would have got rid of Mrs.

[048-067] Archie.” She looked up suddenly: “You were right, though; one

[048-068] can’t tell people about things they don’t know already.”

[048-069] Fred stood in the window, his back to the sunlight, fingering the jonquils.

[048-070] “Yes, you can, my dear. But you must tell it in such a way that they

[048-071] don’t know you’re telling it, and that they don’t know

[048-072] they’re hearing it.”

[048-073] Thea smiled past him, out into the air. “I see. It’s a secret. Like

[048-074] the sound in the shell.”

[048-075] “What’s that?” Fred was watching her and thinking how moving

[048-076] that faraway expression, in her, happened to be. “What did you

[048-077] say?”

[048-078] She came back. “Oh, something old and Moonstony! I have almost forgotten

[048-079] it myself. But I feel better than I thought I ever could again. I can’t

[048-080] wait to be off. Oh, Fred,” she sprang up, “I want to get at

[048-081] it!”

[048-082] As she broke out with this, she threw up her head and lifted herself a little

[048-083] on her toes. Fred colored and looked at her fearfully, hesitatingly. Her eyes,

[048-084] which looked out through the window, were bright—they had no memories.

[048-085] No, she did not remember. That momentary elevation had no associations for her.

[048-086] It was unconscious.

[048-087] He looked her up and down and laughed and shook his head. “You are just

[048-088] all I want you to be—and that is,—not for me! Don’t worry,

[048-089] you’ll get at it. You are at it. My God! have you ever, for one moment,

[048-090] been at anything else?”

[048-091] Thea did not answer him, and clearly she had not heard him. She was watching

[048-092] something out in the thin light of the false spring and its treacherously soft

[048-093] air.

[048-094] Fred waited a moment. “Are you going to dine with your friend

[048-095] to-night?”

[048-096] “Yes. He has never been in New York before. He wants to go about. Where

[048-097] shall I tell him to go?”

[048-098] “Wouldn’t it be a better plan, since you wish me to meet him, for

[048-099] you both to dine with me? It would seem only natural and friendly. You’ll

[048-100] have to live up a little to his notion of us.” Thea seemed to consider

[048-101] the suggestion favorably. “If you wish him to be easy in his mind,”

[048-102] Fred went on, “that would help. I think, myself, that we are rather nice

[048-103] together. Put on one of the new dresses you got down there, and let him see how

[048-104] lovely you can be. You owe him some pleasure, after all the trouble he has

[048-105] taken.”

[048-106] Thea laughed, and seemed to find the idea exciting and pleasant. “Oh,

[048-107] very well! I’ll do my best. Only don’t wear a dress coat, please.

[048-108] He hasn’t one, and he’s nervous about it.”

[048-109] Fred looked at his watch. “Your monument up there is fast. I’ll be

[048-110] here with a cab at eight. I’m anxious to meet him. You’ve given me

[048-111] the strangest idea of his callow innocence and aged indifference.”

[048-112] She shook her head. “No, he’s none of that. He’s very good,

[048-113] and he won’t admit things. I love him for it. Now, as I look back on it,

[048-114] I see that I’ve always, even when I was little, shielded him.”

[048-115] As she laughed, Fred caught the bright spark in her eye that he knew so well,

[048-116] and held it for a happy instant. Then he blew her a kiss with his finger-tips

[048-117] and fled.
