## Overview

Captain Harris is an old pioneer and resident of Moonstone — a figure of imposing physical presence and local legend. He appears briefly in the novel's later chapters, "tall and imposing, wearing a Stetson" [046-075] hat, his name attached to persistent rumors of a "silver hoard which old Captain Harris had always accused" [051-145] the landscape of concealing. Captain Harris belongs to the vanishing generation of Western pioneers — men who came to the frontier when it was still wild and who carry its legends into the more settled present.

## Biography / Character Arc

### The Old Pioneer

Captain Harris is introduced late in the novel, a figure from an earlier era of Colorado life. His title — "Captain" — suggests military service, perhaps in the Civil War or the Indian campaigns, though Cather does not specify. His "Stetson hat" [046-075] marks him visually as a Western type: the frontiersman whose costume signals a life lived outdoors, in command, on the edge of settlement.

When he "issued from one of them, tall" [046-075] — the "them" referring to hotel rooms or doors — his entrance has a theatrical quality. Captain Harris commands attention by his mere physical presence, a quality that the frontier valued highly: a man's stature was his first credential.

### The Silver Hoard Legend

Captain Harris's most distinctive contribution to the novel is the legend attached to his name. The landscape around Moonstone is said to conceal a "silver hoard which old Captain Harris had always accused it of concealing" [051-145]. This rumor — of hidden wealth, of treasure buried in the earth — is a classic Western motif, and Captain Harris is its custodian. Whether the hoard is real or imaginary is less important than the persistence of the belief; Captain Harris's name gives the legend authority, his long residence in the region lending credence to the possibility that he knows something others do not.

The silver hoard rumor also connects Captain Harris to the novel's deeper themes of hidden value and concealed beauty. Just as Thea's talent lies dormant in Moonstone waiting to be discovered, just as the ancient cliff-dwellers' pottery lies buried in Panther Canyon, so the silver hoard lies hidden in the landscape — a secret the earth keeps, which only old-timers like Captain Harris remember to look for.

## Relationships

### Moonstone Community

Captain Harris is a figure of local lore rather than intimate relationships. The novel does not depict him in domestic or social contexts; he exists as a presence — tall, Stetson-hatted, associated with treasure — rather than as a character whose inner life or personal connections are explored. His relationship to Moonstone is that of the pioneer to the place he helped settle: proprietary, knowing, invested with the authority of long memory.

## Significance

Captain Harris is a type figure: the old Western pioneer who survives into a more settled era, carrying with him the legends and the physical presence of the frontier past. His Stetson hat and his tall frame mark him as belonging to a generation that is passing; his silver hoard rumor marks him as a keeper of local mythology.

Cather uses such figures sparingly but effectively. Captain Harris never becomes a developed character — he appears in only two brief passages — but his presence adds texture to the novel's portrait of Moonstone, suggesting the layers of history and legend that accumulate in even the smallest communities. The old captain with his Stetson and his rumor of hidden silver is part of the town's imaginative furniture, one of the stories Moonstone tells about itself.
