## IV

[004-001] “And it was Summer, beautiful Summer!” Those were the closing words

[004-002] of Thea’s favorite fairy tale, and she thought of them as she ran out

[004-003] into the world one Saturday morning in May, her music book under her arm. She

[004-004] was going to the Kohlers’ to take her lesson, but she was in no hurry.

[004-005] It was in the summer that one really lived. Then all the little overcrowded

[004-006] houses were opened wide, and the wind blew through them with sweet, earthy

[004-007] smells of garden-planting. The town looked as if it had just been washed.

[004-008] People were out painting their fences. The cottonwood trees were a-flicker with

[004-009] sticky, yellow little leaves, and the feathery tamarisks were in pink bud. With

[004-010] the warm weather came freedom for everybody. People were dug up, as it were.

[004-011] The very old people, whom one had not seen all winter, came out and sunned

[004-012] themselves in the yard. The double windows were taken off the houses, the

[004-013] tormenting flannels in which children had been encased all winter were put away

[004-014] in boxes, and the youngsters felt a pleasure in the cool cotton things next

[004-015] their skin.

[004-016] Thea had to walk more than a mile to reach the Kohlers’ house, a very

[004-017] pleasant mile out of town toward the glittering sand hills,—yellow this

[004-018] morning, with lines of deep violet where the clefts and valleys were. She

[004-019] followed the sidewalk to the depot at the south end of the town; then took the

[004-020] road east to the little group of adobe houses where the Mexicans lived, then

[004-021] dropped into a deep ravine; a dry sand creek, across which the railroad track

[004-022] ran on a trestle. Beyond that gulch, on a little rise of ground that faced the

[004-023] open sandy plain, was the Kohlers’ house, where [[professor-wunsch|Professor Wunsch]] lived.

[004-024] Fritz Kohler was the town tailor, one of the first settlers. He had moved

[004-025] there, built a little house and made a garden, when Moonstone was first marked

[004-026] down on the map. He had three sons, but they now worked on the railroad and

[004-027] were stationed in distant cities. One of them had gone to work for the Santa

[004-028] Fé, and lived in New Mexico.

[004-029] Mrs. Kohler seldom crossed the ravine and went into the town except at

[004-030] Christmas-time, when she had to buy presents and Christmas cards to send to her

[004-031] old friends in Freeport, Illinois. As she did not go to church, she did not

[004-032] possess such a thing as a hat. Year after year she wore the same red hood in

[004-033] winter and a black sunbonnet in summer. She made her own dresses; the skirts

[004-034] came barely to her shoe-tops, and were gathered as full as they could possibly

[004-035] be to the waistband. She preferred men’s shoes, and usually wore the

[004-036] cast-offs of one of her sons. She had never learned much English, and her

[004-037] plants and shrubs were her companions. She lived for her men and her garden.

[004-038] Beside that sand gulch, she had tried to reproduce a bit of her own village in

[004-039] the Rhine Valley. She hid herself behind the growth she had fostered, lived

[004-040] under the shade of what she had planted and watered and pruned. In the blaze of

[004-041] the open plain she was stupid and blind like an owl. Shade, shade; that was

[004-042] what she was always planning and making. Behind the high tamarisk hedge, her

[004-043] garden was a jungle of verdure in summer. Above the cherry trees and peach

[004-044] trees and golden plums stood the windmill, with its tank on stilts, which kept

[004-045] all this verdure alive. Outside, the sage-brush grew up to the very edge of the

[004-046] garden, and the sand was always drifting up to the tamarisks.

[004-047] Every one in Moonstone was astonished when the Kohlers took the wandering

[004-048] music-teacher to live with them. In seventeen years old Fritz had never had a

[004-049] crony, except the harness-maker and [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]]. This Wunsch came from God

[004-050] knew where,—followed Spanish Johnny into town when that wanderer came

[004-051] back from one of his tramps. Wunsch played in the dance orchestra, tuned

[004-052] pianos, and gave lessons. When Mrs. Kohler rescued him, he was sleeping in a

[004-053] dirty, unfurnished room over one of the saloons, and he had only two shirts in

[004-054] the world. Once he was under her roof, the old woman went at him as she did at

[004-055] her garden. She sewed and washed and mended for him, and made him so clean and

[004-056] respectable that he was able to get a large class of pupils and to rent a

[004-057] piano. As soon as he had money ahead, he sent to the Narrow Gauge

[004-058] lodging-house, in Denver, for a trunkful of music which had been held there for

[004-059] unpaid board. With tears in his eyes the old man—he was not over fifty,

[004-060] but sadly battered—told Mrs. Kohler that he asked nothing better of God

[004-061] than to end his days with her, and to be buried in the garden, under her linden

[004-062] trees. They were not American basswood, but the European linden, which has

[004-063] honey-colored blooms in summer, with a fragrance that surpasses all trees and

[004-064] flowers and drives young people wild with joy.

[004-065] Thea was reflecting as she walked along that had it not been for Professor

[004-066] Wunsch she might have lived on for years in Moonstone without ever knowing the

[004-067] Kohlers, without ever seeing their garden or the inside of their house. Besides

[004-068] the cuckoo clock,—which was wonderful enough, and which Mrs. Kohler said

[004-069] she kept for “company when she was lonesome,”—the Kohlers had

[004-070] in their house the most wonderful thing Thea had ever seen—but of that

[004-071] later.

[004-072] Professor Wunsch went to the houses of his other pupils to give them their

[004-073] lessons, but one morning he told [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] that Thea had talent, and that

[004-074] if she came to him he could teach her in his slippers, and that would be

[004-075] better. Mrs. Kronborg was a strange woman. That word “talent,”

[004-076] which no one else in Moonstone, not even [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]], would have understood, she

[004-077] comprehended perfectly. To any other woman there, it would have meant that a

[004-078] child must have her hair curled every day and must play in public. Mrs.

[004-079] Kronborg knew it meant that Thea must practice four hours a day. A child with

[004-080] talent must be kept at the piano, just as a child with measles must be kept

[004-081] under the blankets. Mrs. Kronborg and her three sisters had all studied piano,

[004-082] and all sang well, but none of them had talent. Their father had played the

[004-083] oboe in an orchestra in Sweden, before he came to America to better his

[004-084] fortunes. He had even known Jenny Lind. A child with talent had to be kept at

[004-085] the piano; so twice a week in summer and once a week in winter Thea went over

[004-086] the gulch to the Kohlers’, though the Ladies’ Aid Society thought

[004-087] it was not proper for their preacher’s daughter to go “where there

[004-088] was so much drinking.” Not that the Kohler sons ever so much as looked at

[004-089] a glass of beer. They were ashamed of their old folks and got out into the

[004-090] world as fast as possible; had their clothes made by a Denver tailor and their

[004-091] necks shaved up under their hair and forgot the past. Old Fritz and Wunsch,

[004-092] however, indulged in a friendly bottle pretty often. The two men were like

[004-093] comrades; perhaps the bond between them was the glass wherein lost hopes are

[004-094] found; perhaps it was common memories of another country; perhaps it was the

[004-095] grapevine in the garden—knotty, fibrous shrub, full of homesickness and

[004-096] sentiment, which the Germans have carried around the world with them.

[004-097] As Thea approached the house she peeped between the pink sprays of the tamarisk

[004-098] hedge and saw the Professor and Mrs. Kohler in the garden, spading and raking.

[004-099] The garden looked like a relief-map now, and gave no indication of what it

[004-100] would be in August; such a jungle! Pole beans and potatoes and corn and leeks

[004-101] and kale and red cabbage—there would even be vegetables for which there

[004-102] is no American name. Mrs. Kohler was always getting by mail packages of seeds

[004-103] from Freeport and from the old country. Then the flowers! There were big

[004-104] sunflowers for the canary bird, tiger lilies and phlox and zinnias and

[004-105] lady’s-slippers and portulaca and hollyhocks,—giant hollyhocks.

[004-106] Beside the fruit trees there was a great umbrella-shaped catalpa, and a

[004-107] balm-of-Gilead, two lindens, and even a ginka,—a rigid, pointed tree with

[004-108] leaves shaped like butterflies, which shivered, but never bent to the wind.

[004-109] This morning Thea saw to her delight that the two oleander trees, one white and

[004-110] one red, had been brought up from their winter quarters in the cellar. There is

[004-111] hardly a German family in the most arid parts of Utah, New Mexico, Arizona, but

[004-112] has its oleander trees. However loutish the American-born sons of the family

[004-113] may be, there was never one who refused to give his muscle to the back-breaking

[004-114] task of getting those tubbed trees down into the cellar in the fall and up into

[004-115] the sunlight in the spring. They may strive to avert the day, but they grapple

[004-116] with the tub at last.

[004-117] When Thea entered the gate, her professor leaned his spade against the white

[004-118] post that supported the turreted dove-house, and wiped his face with his

[004-119] shirt-sleeve; someway he never managed to have a handkerchief about him. Wunsch

[004-120] was short and stocky, with something rough and bear-like about his shoulders.

[004-121] His face was a dark, bricky red, deeply creased rather than wrinkled, and the

[004-122] skin was like loose leather over his neck band—he wore a brass collar

[004-123] button but no collar. His hair was cropped close; iron-gray bristles on a

[004-124] bullet-like head. His eyes were always suffused and bloodshot. He had a coarse,

[004-125] scornful mouth, and irregular, yellow teeth, much worn at the edges. His hands

[004-126] were square and red, seldom clean, but always alive, impatient, even

[004-127] sympathetic.

[004-128] “*Morgen*,” he greeted his pupil in a businesslike way, put on

[004-129] a black alpaca coat, and conducted her at once to the piano in Mrs.

[004-130] Kohler’s sitting-room. He twirled the stool to the proper height, pointed

[004-131] to it, and sat down in a wooden chair beside Thea.

[004-132] “The scale of B flat major,” he directed, and then fell into an

[004-133] attitude of deep attention. Without a word his pupil set to work.

[004-134] To Mrs. Kohler, in the garden, came the cheerful sound of effort, of vigorous

[004-135] striving. Unconsciously she wielded her rake more lightly. Occasionally she

[004-136] heard the teacher’s voice. “Scale of E minor.... *Weiter,

[004-137] weiter!... Immer* I hear the thumb, like a lame foot. *Weiter...

[004-138] weiter*, once... *Schön!* The chords, quick!”

[004-139] The pupil did not open her mouth until they began the second movement of the

[004-140] Clementi sonata, when she remonstrated in low tones about the way he had marked

[004-141] the fingering of a passage.

[004-142] “It makes no matter what you think,” replied her teacher coldly.

[004-143] “There is only one right way. The thumb there. *Ein, zwei, drei,

[004-144] vier*,” etc. Then for an hour there was no further interruption.

[004-145] At the end of the lesson Thea turned on her stool and leaned her arm on the

[004-146] keyboard. They usually had a little talk after the lesson.

[004-147] Herr Wunsch grinned. “How soon is it you are free from school? Then we

[004-148] make ahead faster, eh?”

[004-149] “First week in June. Then will you give me the ‘Invitation to the

[004-150] Dance’?”

[004-151] He shrugged his shoulders. “It makes no matter. If you want him, you play

[004-152] him out of lesson hours.”

[004-153] “All right.” Thea fumbled in her pocket and brought out a crumpled

[004-154] slip of paper. “What does this mean, please? I guess it’s

[004-155] Latin.”

[004-156] Wunsch blinked at the line penciled on the paper. “Wherefrom you get

[004-157] this?” he asked gruffly.

[004-158] “Out of a book Dr. Archie gave me to read. It’s all English but

[004-159] that. Did you ever see it before?” she asked, watching his face.

[004-160] “Yes. A long time ago,” he muttered, scowling.

[004-161] “Ovidius!” He took a stub of lead pencil from his vest pocket,

[004-162] steadied his hand by a visible effort, and under the words:

[004-163] “Lente currite, lente currite, noctis equi,”

[004-164] he wrote in a clear, elegant Gothic hand,—

[004-165] “Go slowly, go slowly, ye steeds of the night.”

[004-166] He put the pencil back in his pocket and continued to stare at the Latin. It

[004-167] recalled the poem, which he had read as a student, and thought very fine. There

[004-168] were treasures of memory which no lodging-house keeper could attach. One

[004-169] carried things about in one’s head, long after one’s linen could be

[004-170] smuggled out in a tuning-bag. He handed the paper back to Thea. “There is

[004-171] the English, quite elegant,” he said, rising.

[004-172] Mrs. Kohler stuck her head in at the door, and Thea slid off the stool.

[004-173] “Come in, Mrs. Kohler,” she called, “and show me the

[004-174] piece-picture.”

[004-175] The old woman laughed, pulled off her big gardening gloves, and pushed Thea to

[004-176] the lounge before the object of her delight. The “piece-picture,”

[004-177] which hung on the wall and nearly covered one whole end of the room, was the

[004-178] handiwork of Fritz Kohler. He had learned his trade under an old-fashioned

[004-179] tailor in Magdeburg who required from each of his apprentices a thesis: that

[004-180] is, before they left his shop, each apprentice had to copy in cloth some well

[004-181] known German painting, stitching bits of colored stuff together on a linen

[004-182] background; a kind of mosaic. The pupil was allowed to select his subject, and

[004-183] Fritz Kohler had chosen a popular painting of Napoleon’s retreat from

[004-184] Moscow. The gloomy Emperor and his staff were represented as crossing a stone

[004-185] bridge, and behind them was the blazing city, the walls and fortresses done in

[004-186] gray cloth with orange tongues of flame darting about the domes and minarets.

[004-187] Napoleon rode his white horse; Murat, in Oriental dress, a bay charger. Thea

[004-188] was never tired of examining this work, of hearing how long it had taken Fritz

[004-189] to make it, how much it had been admired, and what narrow escapes it had had

[004-190] from moths and fire. Silk, Mrs. Kohler explained, would have been much easier

[004-191] to manage than woolen cloth, in which it was often hard to get the right

[004-192] shades. The reins of the horses, the wheels of the spurs, the brooding eyebrows

[004-193] of the Emperor, Murat’s fierce mustaches, the great shakos of the Guard,

[004-194] were all worked out with the minutest fidelity. Thea’s admiration for

[004-195] this picture had endeared her to Mrs. Kohler. It was now many years since she

[004-196] used to point out its wonders to her own little boys. As Mrs. Kohler did not go

[004-197] to church, she never heard any singing, except the songs that floated over from

[004-198] Mexican Town, and Thea often sang for her after the lesson was over. This

[004-199] morning Wunsch pointed to the piano.

[004-200] “On Sunday, when I go by the church, I hear you sing something.”

[004-201] Thea obediently sat down on the stool again and began, “*Come, ye

[004-202] Disconsolate*.” Wunsch listened thoughtfully, his hands on his knees.

[004-203] Such a beautiful child’s voice! Old Mrs. Kohler’s face relaxed in a

[004-204] smile of happiness; she half closed her eyes. A big fly was darting in and out

[004-205] of the window; the sunlight made a golden pool on the rag carpet and bathed the

[004-206] faded cretonne pillows on the lounge, under the piece-picture. “*Earth

[004-207] has no sorrow that Heaven cannot heal*,” the song died away.

[004-208] “That is a good thing to remember,” Wunsch shook himself.

[004-209] “You believe that?” looking quizzically at Thea.

[004-210] She became confused and pecked nervously at a black key with her middle finger.

[004-211] “I don’t know. I guess so,” she murmured.

[004-212] Her teacher rose abruptly. “Remember, for next time, thirds. You ought to

[004-213] get up earlier.”

[004-214] That night the air was so warm that Fritz and Herr Wunsch had their

[004-215] after-supper pipe in the grape arbor, smoking in silence while the sound of

[004-216] fiddles and guitars came across the ravine from Mexican Town. Long after Fritz

[004-217] and his old Paulina had gone to bed, Wunsch sat motionless in the arbor,

[004-218] looking up through the woolly vine leaves at the glittering machinery of

[004-219] heaven.

[004-220] “Lente currite, noctis equi.”

[004-221] That line awoke many memories. He was thinking of youth; of his own, so long

[004-222] gone by, and of his pupil’s, just beginning. He would even have cherished

[004-223] hopes for her, except that he had become superstitious. He believed that

[004-224] whatever he hoped for was destined not to be; that his affection brought

[004-225] ill-fortune, especially to the young; that if he held anything in his thoughts,

[004-226] he harmed it. He had taught in music schools in St. Louis and Kansas City,

[004-227] where the shallowness and complacency of the young misses had maddened him. He

[004-228] had encountered bad manners and bad faith, had been the victim of sharpers of

[004-229] all kinds, was dogged by bad luck. He had played in orchestras that were never

[004-230] paid and wandering opera troupes which disbanded penniless. And there was

[004-231] always the old enemy, more relentless than the others. It was long since he had

[004-232] wished anything or desired anything beyond the necessities of the body. Now

[004-233] that he was tempted to hope for another, he felt alarmed and shook his head.

[004-234] It was his pupil’s power of application, her rugged will, that interested

[004-235] him. He had lived for so long among people whose sole ambition was to get

[004-236] something for nothing that he had learned not to look for seriousness in

[004-237] anything. Now that he by chance encountered it, it recalled standards,

[004-238] ambitions, a society long forgot. What was it she reminded him of? A yellow

[004-239] flower, full of sunlight, perhaps. No; a thin glass full of sweet-smelling,

[004-240] sparkling Moselle wine. He seemed to see such a glass before him in the arbor,

[004-241] to watch the bubbles rising and breaking, like the silent discharge of energy

[004-242] in the nerves and brain, the rapid florescence in young blood—Wunsch felt

[004-243] ashamed and dragged his slippers along the path to the kitchen, his eyes on the

[004-244] ground.
