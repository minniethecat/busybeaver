## III

[053-001] For the first four years after Thea went to Germany things went on as usual

[053-002] with the Kronborg family. [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]]’s land in Nebraska increased in

[053-003] value and brought her in a good rental. The family drifted into an easier way

[053-004] of living, half without realizing it, as families will. Then Mr. Kronborg, who

[053-005] had never been ill, died suddenly of cancer of the liver, and after his death

[053-006] Mrs. Kronborg went, as her neighbors said, into a decline. Hearing discouraging

[053-007] reports of her from the physician who had taken over his practice, [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]]

[053-008] went up from Denver to see her. He found her in bed, in the room where he had

[053-009] more than once attended her, a handsome woman of sixty with a body still firm

[053-010] and white, her hair, faded now to a very pale primrose, in two thick braids

[053-011] down her back, her eyes clear and calm. When the doctor arrived, she was

[053-012] sitting up in her bed, knitting. He felt at once how glad she was to see him,

[053-013] but he soon gathered that she had made no determination to get well. She told

[053-014] him, indeed, that she could not very well get along without Mr. Kronborg. The

[053-015] doctor looked at her with astonishment. Was it possible that she could miss the

[053-016] foolish old man so much? He reminded her of her children.

[053-017] “Yes,” she replied; “the children are all very well, but they

[053-018] are not father. We were married young.”

[053-019] The doctor watched her wonderingly as she went on knitting, thinking how much

[053-020] she looked like Thea. The difference was one of degree rather than of kind. The

[053-021] daughter had a compelling enthusiasm, the mother had none. But their framework,

[053-022] their foundation, was very much the same.

[053-023] In a moment Mrs. Kronborg spoke again. “Have you heard anything from Thea

[053-024] lately?”

[053-025] During his talk with her, the doctor gathered that what Mrs. Kronborg really

[053-026] wanted was to see her daughter Thea. Lying there day after day, she wanted it

[053-027] calmly and continuously. He told her that, since she felt so, he thought they

[053-028] might ask Thea to come home.

[053-029] “I’ve thought a good deal about it,” said Mrs. Kronborg

[053-030] slowly. “I hate to interrupt her, now that she’s begun to get

[053-031] advancement. I expect she’s seen some pretty hard times, though she was

[053-032] never one to complain. Perhaps she’d feel that she would like to come. It

[053-033] would be hard, losing both of us while she’s off there.”

[053-034] When Dr. Archie got back to Denver he wrote a long letter to Thea, explaining

[053-035] her mother’s condition and how much she wished to see her, and asking

[053-036] Thea to come, if only for a few weeks. Thea had repaid the money she had

[053-037] borrowed from him, and he assured her that if she happened to be short of funds

[053-038] for the journey, she had only to cable him.

[053-039] A month later he got a frantic sort of reply from Thea. Complications in the

[053-040] opera at [[dresden|Dresden]] had given her an unhoped-for opportunity to go on in a big

[053-041] part. Before this letter reached the doctor, she would have made her debut as

[053-042] *Elizabeth*, in “Tannhäuser.” She wanted to go to her mother

[053-043] more than she wanted anything else in the world, but, unless she

[053-044] failed,—which she would not,—she absolutely could not leave Dresden

[053-045] for six months. It was not that she chose to stay; she had to stay—or

[053-046] lose everything. The next few months would put her five years ahead, or would

[053-047] put her back so far that it would be of no use to struggle further. As soon as

[053-048] she was free, she would go to Moonstone and take her mother back to Germany

[053-049] with her. Her mother, she was sure, could live for years yet, and she would

[053-050] like German people and German ways, and could be hearing music all the time.

[053-051] Thea said she was writing her mother and begging her to help her one last time;

[053-052] to get strength and to wait for her six months, and then she (Thea) would do

[053-053] everything. Her mother would never have to make an effort again.

[053-054] Dr. Archie went up to Moonstone at once. He had great confidence in Mrs.

[053-055] Kronborg’s power of will, and if Thea’s appeal took hold of her

[053-056] enough, he believed she might get better. But when he was shown into the

[053-057] familiar room off the parlor, his heart sank. Mrs. Kronborg was lying serene

[053-058] and fateful on her pillows. On the dresser at the foot of her bed there was a

[053-059] large photograph of Thea in the character in which she was to make her debut.

[053-060] Mrs. Kronborg pointed to it.

[053-061] “Isn’t she lovely, doctor? It’s nice that she hasn’t

[053-062] changed much. I’ve seen her look like that many a time.”

[053-063] They talked for a while about Thea’s good fortune. Mrs. Kronborg had had

[053-064] a cablegram saying, “First performance well received. Great

[053-065] relief.” In her letter Thea said; “If you’ll only get better,

[053-066] dear mother, there’s nothing I can’t do. I will make a really great

[053-067] success, if you’ll try with me. You shall have everything you want, and

[053-068] we will always be together. I have a little house all picked out where we are

[053-069] to live.”

[053-070] “Bringing up a family is not all it’s cracked up to be,” said

[053-071] Mrs. Kronborg with a flicker of irony, as she tucked the letter back under her

[053-072] pillow. “The children you don’t especially need, you have always

[053-073] with you, like the poor. But the bright ones get away from you. They have their

[053-074] own way to make in the world. Seems like the brighter they are, the farther

[053-075] they go. I used to feel sorry that you had no family, doctor, but maybe

[053-076] you’re as well off.”

[053-077] “Thea’s plan seems sound to me, Mrs. Kronborg. There’s no

[053-078] reason I can see why you shouldn’t pull up and live for years yet, under

[053-079] proper care. You’d have the best doctors in the world over there, and it

[053-080] would be wonderful to live with anybody who looks like that.” He nodded

[053-081] at the photograph of the young woman who must have been singing “*Dich,

[053-082] theure Halle, grüss’ ich wieder*,” her eyes looking up, her

[053-083] beautiful hands outspread with pleasure.

[053-084] Mrs. Kronborg laughed quite cheerfully. “Yes, wouldn’t it? If

[053-085] father were here, I might rouse myself. But sometimes it’s hard to come

[053-086] back. Or if she were in trouble, maybe I could rouse myself.”

[053-087] “But, dear Mrs. Kronborg, she is in trouble,” her old friend

[053-088] expostulated. “As she says, she’s never needed you as she needs you

[053-089] now. I make my guess that she’s never begged anybody to help her

[053-090] before.”

[053-091] Mrs. Kronborg smiled. “Yes, it’s pretty of her. But that will pass.

[053-092] When these things happen far away they don’t make such a mark; especially

[053-093] if your hands are full and you’ve duties of your own to think about. My

[053-094] own father died in Nebraska when Gunner was born,—we were living in Iowa

[053-095] then,—and I was sorry, but the baby made it up to me. I was

[053-096] father’s favorite, too. That’s the way it goes, you see.”

[053-097] The doctor took out Thea’s letter to him, and read it over to Mrs.

[053-098] Kronborg. She seemed to listen, and not to listen.

[053-099] When he finished, she said thoughtfully: “I’d counted on hearing

[053-100] her sing again. But I always took my pleasures as they come. I always enjoyed

[053-101] her singing when she was here about the house. While she was practicing I often

[053-102] used to leave my work and sit down in a rocker and give myself up to it, the

[053-103] same as if I’d been at an entertainment. I was never one of these

[053-104] housekeepers that let their work drive them to death. And when she had the

[053-105] Mexicans over here, I always took it in. First and last,”—she

[053-106] glanced judicially at the photograph,—“I guess I got about as much

[053-107] out of Thea’s voice as anybody will ever get.”

[053-108] “I guess you did!” the doctor assented heartily; “and I got a

[053-109] good deal myself. You remember how she used to sing those Scotch songs for me,

[053-110] and lead us with her head, her hair bobbing?”

[053-111] “‘Flow Gently, Sweet Afton,’—I can hear it now,”

[053-112] said Mrs. Kronborg; “and poor father never knew when he sang sharp! He

[053-113] used to say, ‘Mother, how do you always know when they make mistakes

[053-114] practicing?’” Mrs. Kronborg chuckled.

[053-115] Dr. Archie took her hand, still firm like the hand of a young woman. “It

[053-116] was lucky for her that you did know. I always thought she got more from you

[053-117] than from any of her teachers.”

[053-118] “Except Wunsch; he was a real musician,” said Mrs. Kronborg

[053-119] respectfully. “I gave her what chance I could, in a crowded house. I kept

[053-120] the other children out of the parlor for her. That was about all I could do. If

[053-121] she wasn’t disturbed, she needed no watching. She went after it like a

[053-122] terrier after rats from the first, poor child. She was downright afraid of it.

[053-123] That’s why I always encouraged her taking Thor off to outlandish places.

[053-124] When she was out of the house, then she was rid of it.”

[053-125] After they had recalled many pleasant memories together, Mrs. Kronborg said

[053-126] suddenly: “I always understood about her going off without coming to see

[053-127] us that time. Oh, I know! You had to keep your own counsel. You were a good

[053-128] friend to her. I’ve never forgot that.” She patted the

[053-129] doctor’s sleeve and went on absently. “There was something she

[053-130] didn’t want to tell me, and that’s why she didn’t come.

[053-131] Something happened when she was with those people in Mexico. I worried for a

[053-132] good while, but I guess she’s come out of it all right. She’d had a

[053-133] pretty hard time, scratching along alone like that when she was so young, and

[053-134] my farms in Nebraska were down so low that I couldn’t help her none.

[053-135] That’s no way to send a girl out. But I guess, whatever there was, she

[053-136] wouldn’t be afraid to tell me now.” Mrs. Kronborg looked up at the

[053-137] photograph with a smile. “She doesn’t look like she was beholding

[053-138] to anybody, does she?”

[053-139] “She isn’t, Mrs. Kronborg. She never has been. That was why she

[053-140] borrowed the money from me.”

[053-141] “Oh, I knew she’d never have sent for you if she’d done

[053-142] anything to shame us. She was always proud.” Mrs. Kronborg paused and

[053-143] turned a little on her side. “It’s been quite a satisfaction to you

[053-144] and me, doctor, having her voice turn out so fine. The things you hope for

[053-145] don’t always turn out like that, by a long sight. As long as old Mrs.

[053-146] Kohler lived, she used always to translate what it said about Thea in the

[053-147] German papers she sent. I could make some of it out myself,—it’s

[053-148] not very different from Swedish,—but it pleased the old lady. She left

[053-149] Thea her piece-picture of the burning of Moscow. I’ve got it put away in

[053-150] moth-balls for her, along with the oboe her grandfather brought from Sweden. I

[053-151] want her to take father’s oboe back there some day.” Mrs. Kronborg

[053-152] paused a moment and compressed her lips. “But I guess she’ll take a

[053-153] finer instrument than that with her, back to Sweden!” she added.

[053-154] Her tone fairly startled the doctor, it was so vibrating with a fierce, defiant

[053-155] kind of pride he had heard often in Thea’s voice. He looked down

[053-156] wonderingly at his old friend and patient. After all, one never knew people to

[053-157] the core. Did she, within her, hide some of that still passion of which her

[053-158] daughter was all-compact?

[053-159] “That last summer at home wasn’t very nice for her,” Mrs.

[053-160] Kronborg began as placidly as if the fire had never leaped up in her.

[053-161] “The other children were acting-up because they thought I might make a

[053-162] fuss over her and give her the big-head. We gave her the dare, somehow, the lot

[053-163] of us, because we couldn’t understand her changing teachers and all that.

[053-164] That’s the trouble about giving the dare to them quiet, unboastful

[053-165] children; you never know how far it’ll take ’em. Well, we ought not

[053-166] to complain, doctor; she’s given us a good deal to think about.”

[053-167] The next time Dr. Archie came to Moonstone, he came to be a pall-bearer at Mrs.

[053-168] Kronborg’s funeral. When he last looked at her, she was so serene and

[053-169] queenly that he went back to Denver feeling almost as if he had helped to bury

[053-170] [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] herself. The handsome head in the coffin seemed to him much more

[053-171] really Thea than did the radiant young woman in the picture, looking about at

[053-172] the Gothic vaultings and greeting the Hall of Song.
