## Overview

The Andersen family enters *The Song of the Lark* through Irene Andersen, the widowed daughter of Mrs. Lorch who lives at the Chicago boarding house where Thea Kronborg stays during her studies with Andor Harsanyi. Irene had married Oscar Andersen, the youngest son of a wealthy Swedish lumber family in St. Paul, but was defrauded by his brothers after his death and returned to live with her mother in reduced circumstances [022-064]. The family name recurs throughout Part 2 as Thea navigates her new Chicago life under the well-meaning but oppressive attentions of the sentimental Mrs. Andersen.

## Biography / Character Arc

### Oscar Andersen

Oscar Andersen was a "strong, full-blooded fellow," "lusty" and "gay-looking," who married Irene and lived in St. Paul as part of the prosperous Andersen lumber business [022-064]. He was killed by the explosion of a steam boiler in the mills, and his brothers used his careless business practices to claim he held little stock, effectively defrauding his widow [022-071]. He was buried in the Andersen lot in St. Paul, "with a locked iron fence around it," a symbol of the family's exclusion of his wife even in death [022-081].

### Irene Andersen (née Lorch)

Irene Andersen — "Irene, her mother called her" — is approximately forty years old, "angular, big-boned, with large, thin features, light-blue eyes, and dry, yellow hair" [022-059]. She is "pale, anaemic, and sentimental" [022-062], her nature bearing "an incurable hurt" from being cast out by the Andersen family after her husband's death [022-076]. Rather than fight the family that had "always snubbed and wounded her," she felt "the humiliation of being thrust out more than she felt her impoverishment" [022-072], and returned to Chicago to live with her mother on five hundred dollars a year [022-074].

At the boarding house, Mrs. Andersen spends most of her time doing fancywork or writing letters to "sympathizing German friends in St. Paul, surrounded by keepsakes and photographs of the burly Oscar Andersen" [022-089]. She admires Thea greatly, considering it "a distinction to be even a 'temporary soprano' in the Swedish Church" and "distinguished to be a pupil of Harsanyi's" [022-098]. She "fluttered about the upper floor when Thea was practicing" and "tried to make a heroine of her, just as Tillie Kronborg had always done" [022-102].

### The Extended Andersen Family

The Andersen brothers in St. Paul were rich, arrogant lumber merchants who "strongly disapproved" of Oscar's marriage [022-068]. After his death, they agreed among themselves that they were "entirely justified in defrauding his widow, who, they said, 'would only marry again and give some fellow a good thing of it'" [022-071]. The locked iron fence around Oscar's grave, to which Irene had to ask his eldest brother for the key, epitomizes the family's cold exclusion [022-082].

## Relationships

### Mrs. Lorch
Irene Andersen is the daughter of Mrs. Lorch, the kindly, fat, jolly landlady who runs the boarding house where Thea stays [022-051]. The two widows live together and jointly look after their boarders, often discussing Thea's prospects together [025-030].

### Thea Kronborg
Mrs. Andersen takes a keen, if cloying, interest in Thea. She accompanies her to a German dressmaker [022-112], brings her word of performance opportunities [025-036], and praises her at every turn. Thea, for her part, finds Mrs. Andersen "a depressing person" [022-094] and is annoyed by her fawning attention — when she hears "Mrs. Andersen tip-toeing past her door, she used to shrug her shoulders and wonder whether she was always to have a Tillie diving furtively about her in some disguise or other" [022-104].

### Tillie Kronborg
Mrs. Andersen's doting on Thea repeatedly reminds Thea of her Aunt Tillie Kronborg, the family member back in Moonstone who similarly idolized her. Mrs. Andersen's behavior at the dressmaker's "recalled Tillie even more painfully" [022-107], and Thea perceives the pattern of small-town worship following her to Chicago in a new, equally suffocating form.

## Significance

The Andersen family serves several narrative functions in the novel. Oscar Andersen's death and Irene's subsequent mistreatment by his family illustrate a recurring theme in Cather's work: the cruelty of respectable, prosperous families toward those they deem outsiders. Irene Andersen herself represents a cautionary tale — a woman whose sentimental nature has been permanently damaged by grief and humiliation, and who now lives vicariously through others' achievements. Her relationship with Thea also signals that Thea has not escaped the claustrophobic attentions of small-town admirers, even in Chicago; the pattern established by Tillie Kronborg in Moonstone reemerges in Mrs. Andersen's fussing, suggesting that Thea must repeatedly negotiate between gratitude for support and the need for artistic solitude.
