## I

[046-001] Dr. Howard Archie had come down to Denver for a meeting of the stockholders in

[046-002] the San Felipe silver mine. It was not absolutely necessary for him to come,

[046-003] but he had no very pressing cases at home. Winter was closing down in

[046-004] Moonstone, and he dreaded the dullness of it. On the 10th day of January,

[046-005] therefore, he was registered at the Brown Palace Hotel. On the morning of the

[046-006] 11th he came down to breakfast to find the streets white and the air thick with

[046-007] snow. A wild northwester was blowing down from the mountains, one of those

[046-008] beautiful storms that wrap Denver in dry, furry snow, and make the city a

[046-009] loadstone to thousands of men in the mountains and on the plains. The brakemen

[046-010] out on their box-cars, the miners up in their diggings, the lonely homesteaders

[046-011] in the sand hills of Yucca and Kit Carson Counties, begin to think of Denver,

[046-012] muffled in snow, full of food and drink and good cheer, and to yearn for her

[046-013] with that admiration which makes her, more than other American cities, an

[046-014] object of sentiment.

[046-015] Howard Archie was glad he had got in before the storm came. He felt as cheerful

[046-016] as if he had received a legacy that morning, and he greeted the clerk with even

[046-017] greater friendliness than usual when he stopped at the desk for his mail. In

[046-018] the dining-room he found several old friends seated here and there before

[046-019] substantial breakfasts: cattlemen and mining engineers from odd corners of the

[046-020] State, all looking fresh and well pleased with themselves. He had a word with

[046-021] one and another before he sat down at the little table by a window, where the

[046-022] Austrian head waiter stood attentively behind a chair. After his breakfast was

[046-023] put before him, the doctor began to run over his letters. There was one

[046-024] directed in [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]]’s handwriting, forwarded from Moonstone. He saw

[046-025] with astonishment, as he put another lump of sugar into his cup, that this

[046-026] letter bore a New York postmark. He had known that Thea was in Mexico,

[046-027] traveling with some [[chicago|Chicago]] people, but New York, to a Denver man, seems much

[046-028] farther away than Mexico City. He put the letter behind his plate, upright

[046-029] against the stem of his water goblet, and looked at it thoughtfully while he

[046-030] drank his second cup of coffee. He had been a little anxious about Thea; she

[046-031] had not written to him for a long while.

[046-032] As he never got good coffee at home, the doctor always drank three cups for

[046-033] breakfast when he was in Denver. Oscar knew just when to bring him a second

[046-034] pot, fresh and smoking. “And more cream, Oscar, please. You know I like

[046-035] lots of cream,” the doctor murmured, as he opened the square envelope,

[046-036] marked in the upper right-hand corner, “Everett House, Union

[046-037] Square.” The text of the letter was as follows:—

[046-038] DEAR DOCTOR ARCHIE:—\

[046-039] I have not written to you for a long time, but it has not been

[046-040] unintentional. I could not write you frankly, and so I would not write at all.

[046-041] I can be frank with you now, but not by letter. It is a great deal to ask, but

[046-042] I wonder if you could come to New York to help me out? I have got into

[046-043] difficulties, and I need your advice. I need your friendship. I am afraid I

[046-044] must even ask you to lend me money, if you can without serious inconvenience. I

[046-045] have to go to Germany to study, and it can’t be put off any longer. My

[046-046] voice is ready. Needless to say, I don’t want any word of this to reach

[046-047] my family. They are the last people I would turn to, though I love my mother

[046-048] dearly. If you can come, please telegraph me at this hotel. Don’t despair

[046-049] of me. I’ll make it up to you yet.

[046-050] Your old friend,\

[046-051] THEA KRONBORG.

[046-052] This in a bold, jagged handwriting with a Gothic turn to the

[046-053] letters,—something between a highly sophisticated hand and a very

[046-054] unsophisticated one,—not in the least smooth or flowing.

[046-055] The doctor bit off the end of a cigar nervously and read the letter through

[046-056] again, fumbling distractedly in his pockets for matches, while the waiter kept

[046-057] trying to call his attention to the box he had just placed before him. At last

[046-058] Oscar came out, as if the idea had just struck him, “Matches, sir?”

[046-059] “Yes, thank you.” The doctor slipped a coin into his palm and rose,

[046-060] crumpling Thea’s letter in his hand and thrusting the others into his

[046-061] pocket unopened. He went back to the desk in the lobby and beckoned to the

[046-062] clerk, upon whose kindness he threw himself apologetically.

[046-063] “Harry, I’ve got to pull out unexpectedly. Call up the Burlington,

[046-064] will you, and ask them to route me to New York the quickest way, and to let us

[046-065] know. Ask for the hour I’ll get in. I have to wire.”

[046-066] “Certainly, [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]]. Have it for you in a minute.” The young

[046-067] man’s pallid, clean-scraped face was all sympathetic interest as he

[046-068] reached for the telephone. Dr. Archie put out his hand and stopped him.

[046-069] “Wait a minute. Tell me, first, is Captain Harris down yet?”

[046-070] “No, sir. The Captain hasn’t come down yet this morning.”

[046-071] “I’ll wait here for him. If I don’t happen to catch him, nail

[046-072] him and get me. Thank you, Harry.”

[046-073] The doctor spoke gratefully and turned away. He began to pace the lobby, his

[046-074] hands behind him, watching the bronze elevator doors like a hawk. At last

[046-075] Captain Harris issued from one of them, tall and imposing, wearing a Stetson

[046-076] and fierce mustaches, a fur coat on his arm, a solitaire glittering upon his

[046-077] little finger and another in his black satin ascot. He was one of the grand old

[046-078] bluffers of those good old days. As gullible as a schoolboy, he had managed,

[046-079] with his sharp eye and knowing air and twisted blond mustaches, to pass himself

[046-080] off for an astute financier, and the Denver papers respectfully referred to him

[046-081] as the Rothschild of Cripple Creek.

[046-082] Dr. Archie stopped the Captain on his way to breakfast. “Must see you a

[046-083] minute, Captain. Can’t wait. Want to sell you some shares in the San

[046-084] Felipe. Got to raise money.”

[046-085] The Captain grandly bestowed his hat upon an eager porter who had already

[046-086] lifted his fur coat tenderly from his arm and stood nursing it. In removing his

[046-087] hat, the Captain exposed a bald, flushed dome, thatched about the ears with

[046-088] yellowish gray hair. “Bad time to sell, doctor. You want to hold on to

[046-089] San Felipe, and buy more. What have you got to raise?”

[046-090] “Oh, not a great sum. Five or six thousand. I’ve been buying up

[046-091] close and have run short.”

[046-092] “I see, I see. Well, doctor, you’ll have to let me get through that

[046-093] door. I was out last night, and I’m going to get my bacon, if you lose

[046-094] your mine.” He clapped Archie on the shoulder and pushed him along in

[046-095] front of him. “Come ahead with me, and we’ll talk business.”

[046-096] Dr. Archie attended the Captain and waited while he gave his order, taking the

[046-097] seat the old promoter indicated.

[046-098] “Now, sir,” the Captain turned to him, “you don’t want

[046-099] to sell anything. You must be under the impression that I’m one of these

[046-100] damned New England sharks that get their pound of flesh off the widow and

[046-101] orphan. If you’re a little short, sign a note and I’ll write a

[046-102] check. That’s the way gentlemen do business. If you want to put up some

[046-103] San Felipe as collateral, let her go, but I shan’t touch a share of it.

[046-104] Pens and ink, please, Oscar,”—he lifted a large forefinger to the

[046-105] Austrian.

[046-106] The Captain took out his checkbook and a book of blank notes, and adjusted his

[046-107] nose-nippers. He wrote a few words in one book and Archie wrote a few in the

[046-108] other. Then they each tore across perforations and exchanged slips of paper.

[046-109] “That’s the way. Saves office rent,” the Captain commented

[046-110] with satisfaction, returning the books to his pocket. “And now, Archie,

[046-111] where are you off to?”

[046-112] “Got to go East to-night. A deal waiting for me in New York.” Dr.

[046-113] Archie rose.

[046-114] The Captain’s face brightened as he saw Oscar approaching with a tray,

[046-115] and he began tucking the corner of his napkin inside his collar, over his

[046-116] ascot. “Don’t let them unload anything on you back there,

[046-117] doctor,” he said genially, “and don’t let them relieve you of

[046-118] anything, either. Don’t let them get any Cripple stuff off you. We can

[046-119] manage our own silver out here, and we’re going to take it out by the

[046-120] ton, sir!”

[046-121] The doctor left the dining-room, and after another consultation with the clerk,

[046-122] he wrote his first telegram to Thea:—

[046-123] Miss Thea Kronborg,\

[046-124] Everett House, New York.\

[046-125] \

[046-126] Will call at your hotel eleven o’clock Friday morning. Glad to come.

[046-127] Thank you.

[046-128] ARCHIE

[046-129] He stood and heard the message actually clicked off on the wire, with the

[046-130] feeling that she was hearing the click at the other end. Then he sat down in

[046-131] the lobby and wrote a note to his wife and one to the other doctor in

[046-132] Moonstone. When he at last issued out into the storm, it was with a feeling of

[046-133] elation rather than of anxiety. Whatever was wrong, he could make it right. Her

[046-134] letter had practically said so.

[046-135] He tramped about the snowy streets, from the bank to the Union Station, where

[046-136] he shoved his money under the grating of the ticket window as if he could not

[046-137] get rid of it fast enough. He had never been in New York, never been farther

[046-138] east than Buffalo. “That’s rather a shame,” he reflected

[046-139] boyishly as he put the long tickets in his pocket, “for a man nearly

[046-140] forty years old.” However, he thought as he walked up toward the club, he

[046-141] was on the whole glad that his first trip had a human interest, that he was

[046-142] going for something, and because he was wanted. He loved holidays. He felt as

[046-143] if he were going to Germany himself. “Queer,”—he went over it

[046-144] with the snow blowing in his face,—“but that sort of thing is more

[046-145] interesting than mines and making your daily bread. It’s worth paying out

[046-146] to be in on it,—for a fellow like me. And when it’s Thea—Oh,

[046-147] I back her!” he laughed aloud as he burst in at the door of the Athletic

[046-148] Club, powdered with snow.

[046-149] Archie sat down before the New York papers and ran over the advertisements of

[046-150] hotels, but he was too restless to read. Probably he had better get a new

[046-151] overcoat, and he was not sure about the shape of his collars. “I

[046-152] don’t want to look different to her from everybody else there,” he

[046-153] mused. “I guess I’ll go down and have Van look me over. He’ll

[046-154] put me right.”

[046-155] So he plunged out into the snow again and started for his tailor’s. When

[046-156] he passed a florist’s shop he stopped and looked in at the window,

[046-157] smiling; how naturally pleasant things recalled one another. At the

[046-158] tailor’s he kept whistling, “Flow gently, Sweet Afton,” while

[046-159] Van Dusen advised him, until that resourceful tailor and haberdasher exclaimed,

[046-160] “You must have a date back there, doctor; you behave like a

[046-161] bridegroom,” and made him remember that he wasn’t one.

[046-162] Before he let him go, Van put his finger on the Masonic pin in his

[046-163] client’s lapel. “Mustn’t wear that, doctor. Very bad form

[046-164] back there.”
