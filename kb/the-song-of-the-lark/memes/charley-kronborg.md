## Biography / Character Arc

### Youth and Early Work

Charley Kronborg is one of Thea's older brothers, eighteen months younger than Gus and already working by the time the novel opens. He works in a feed store as a young man [003-037], part of the Kronborg family's pattern of modest but respectable employment in Moonstone. His father, the Reverend Kronborg, claims that Charley is "a natural electrician," noting that Charley "had put in a doorbell himself" [009-005]. This practical, mechanical bent defines Charley throughout the novel.

### Sibling Dynamics

In the crowded Kronborg household, Charley and his brother Gus are "likely to want the same thing" and their desires are "always conflicting with one another" [009-010]. This detail suggests the ordinary frictions of a large family with limited resources. Charley is among the children who "breakfasted in the kitchen" during the cold Colorado winters [003-035], sharing the intimate chaos of family life that forms the backdrop to Thea's emergence as an artist.

### Thea's Departure and Return

When Thea leaves for Chicago, it is Charley who performs the practical service of taking "Thea's trunk and telescope to the depot in the express wagon" [020-114] — a small but significant gesture that shows the family's support for Thea's venture, even if Charley himself may not fully understand her ambitions.

When Thea returns home from Chicago, Charley has grown into a young man. He warns his mother not to "make a fuss over Thea just because she's been to Chicago" [029-005], a remark that Mrs. Kronborg silences with a level gaze [029-008]. This exchange captures the tension between Thea's expanding world and the small-town values that Charley embodies: for him, Chicago confers no special status, and he is determined to treat his sister as an equal, not as a returning celebrity.

### Skepticism at the Dinner Table

Charley's most vivid scene occurs at the family dinner table upon Thea's return. When the family discusses Thea's Chicago experiences, Charley remarks with heavy irony: "High society, that" [031-013]. He follows with "Everybody according to their own taste" [031-026], prompting his mother to cut him off: "That's all right, Charley. Drop it there" [031-032]. Charley and Gus soon leave the table — "they had engagements" [031-037] — and depart as soon as possible.

Later, Thea reflects on her siblings with the perspective that distance has given her, "and she saw that Anna and Gus and Charley were among the people who had always been like this" — ordinary, conventional, incapable of understanding her inner life [031-117]. Charley has by this point "been promoted from the grocery department" [031-120], a modest advancement that represents the ceiling of ambition in Moonstone.

## Relationships

### Thea Kronborg
Charley is skeptical of Thea's Chicago ambitions and resists the idea that she deserves special treatment upon returning home. His needling at the dinner table is good-natured but reveals the gulf between his practical, small-town sensibilities and Thea's artistic nature. Thea, in turn, sees Charley clearly as one of the people from whom she has always felt separate [031-117].

### Gus Kronborg
Charley's elder brother and closest peer. The two are often mentioned together: they work, eat, and leave family gatherings as a pair. Their "conflicting" desires suggest a competitive but intimate fraternal bond [009-010].

### Mrs. Kronborg
Charley's mother maintains order at the family table, checking his sarcastic remarks with quiet authority. Her "leveled eyes" at Charley [029-008] demonstrate the matriarchal discipline that holds the large Kronborg family together.

### Reverend Kronborg
Charley's father clearly takes pride in his son's practical skills, praising him as "a natural electrician" [009-005]. This paternal approval of Charley's mechanical aptitude contrasts with the reverend's more uncertain response to Thea's musical gifts.

## Significance

Charley Kronborg represents the small-town pragmatism that Thea must transcend to become an artist. He is not a villain — his remarks about Thea's Chicago airs are not cruel, merely the deflating humor of a brother who refuses to be impressed. But in his refusal to grant Thea any special status, Charley inadvertently articulates the very values that would have kept her tethered to Moonstone: the belief that everyone should know their place, that ambition beyond the town's horizons is a form of pretension. His promotion "from the grocery department" [031-120] is the kind of achievement that Moonstone can recognize and reward; Thea's artistic development, by contrast, is invisible to him. The dinner-table scene, in which Charley is silenced by his mother and then escapes with Gus, is a miniature of the novel's central drama: the artist's struggle to be seen and understood by those who love her but cannot fathom what she is becoming.
