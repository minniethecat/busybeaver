## X

[060-001] Ottenburg dismissed his taxicab at the 91st Street entrance of the Park and

[060-002] floundered across the drive through a wild spring snowstorm. When he reached

[060-003] the reservoir path he saw Thea ahead of him, walking rapidly against the wind.

[060-004] Except for that one figure, the path was deserted. A flock of gulls were

[060-005] hovering over the reservoir, seeming bewildered by the driving currents of snow

[060-006] that whirled above the black water and then disappeared within it. When he had

[060-007] almost overtaken Thea, Fred called to her, and she turned and waited for him

[060-008] with her back to the wind. Her hair and furs were powdered with snowflakes, and

[060-009] she looked like some rich-pelted animal, with warm blood, that had run in out

[060-010] of the woods. Fred laughed as he took her hand.

[060-011] “No use asking how you do. You surely needn’t feel much anxiety

[060-012] about Friday, when you can look like this.”

[060-013] She moved close to the iron fence to make room for him beside her, and faced

[060-014] the wind again. “Oh, I’m *well* enough, in so far as that

[060-015] goes. But I’m not lucky about stage appearances. I’m easily upset,

[060-016] and the most perverse things happen.”

[060-017] “What’s the matter? Do you still get nervous?”

[060-018] “Of course I do. I don’t mind nerves so much as getting

[060-019] numbed,” Thea muttered, sheltering her face for a moment with her muff.

[060-020] “I’m under a spell, you know, hoodooed. It’s the thing I

[060-021] *want* to do that I can never do. Any other effects I can get easily

[060-022] enough.”

[060-023] “Yes, you get effects, and not only with your voice. That’s where

[060-024] you have it over all the rest of them; you’re as much at home on the

[060-025] stage as you were down in [[panther-canyon|Panther Canyon]]—as if you’d just been let

[060-026] out of a cage. Didn’t you get some of your ideas down there?”

[060-027] Thea nodded. “Oh, yes! For heroic parts, at least. Out of the rocks, out

[060-028] of the dead people. You mean the idea of standing up under things, don’t

[060-029] you, meeting catastrophe? No fussiness. Seems to me they must have been a

[060-030] reserved, somber people, with only a muscular language, all their movements for

[060-031] a purpose; simple, strong, as if they were dealing with fate

[060-032] bare-handed.” She put her gloved fingers on Fred’s arm. “I

[060-033] don’t know how I can ever thank you enough. I don’t know if

[060-034] I’d ever have got anywhere without Panther Canyon. How did you know that

[060-035] was the one thing to do for me? It’s the sort of thing nobody ever helps

[060-036] one to, in this world. One can learn how to sing, but no singing teacher can

[060-037] give anybody what I got down there. How did you know?”

[060-038] “I didn’t know. Anything else would have done as well. It was your

[060-039] creative hour. I knew you were getting a lot, but I didn’t realize how

[060-040] much.”

[060-041] Thea walked on in silence. She seemed to be thinking.

[060-042] “Do you know what they really taught me?” she came out suddenly.

[060-043] “They taught me the inevitable hardness of human life. No artist gets far

[060-044] who doesn’t know that. And you can’t know it with your mind. You

[060-045] have to realize it in your body, somehow; deep. It’s an animal sort of

[060-046] feeling. I sometimes think it’s the strongest of all. Do you know what

[060-047] I’m driving at?”

[060-048] “I think so. Even your audiences feel it, vaguely: that you’ve

[060-049] sometime or other faced things that make you different.”

[060-050] Thea turned her back to the wind, wiping away the snow that clung to her brows

[060-051] and lashes. “Ugh!” she exclaimed; “no matter how long a

[060-052] breath you have, the storm has a longer. I haven’t signed for next

[060-053] season, yet, Fred. I’m holding out for a big contract: forty

[060-054] performances. Necker won’t be able to do much next winter. It’s

[060-055] going to be one of those between seasons; the old singers are too old, and the

[060-056] new ones are too new. They might as well risk me as anybody. So I want good

[060-057] terms. The next five or six years are going to be my best.”

[060-058] “You’ll get what you demand, if you are uncompromising. I’m

[060-059] safe in congratulating you now.”

[060-060] Thea laughed. “It’s a little early. I may not get it at all. They

[060-061] don’t seem to be breaking their necks to meet me. I can go back to

[060-062] [[dresden|Dresden]].”

[060-063] As they turned the curve and walked westward they got the wind from the side,

[060-064] and talking was easier.

[060-065] Fred lowered his collar and shook the snow from his shoulders. “Oh, I

[060-066] don’t mean on the contract particularly. I congratulate you on what you

[060-067] can do, Thea, and on all that lies behind what you do. On the life that’s

[060-068] led up to it, and on being able to care so much. That, after all, is the

[060-069] unusual thing.”

[060-070] She looked at him sharply, with a certain apprehension. “Care? Why

[060-071] shouldn’t I care? If I didn’t, I’d be in a bad way. What else

[060-072] have I got?” She stopped with a challenging interrogation, but Ottenburg

[060-073] did not reply. “You mean,” she persisted, “that you

[060-074] don’t care as much as you used to?”

[060-075] “I care about your success, of course.” Fred fell into a slower

[060-076] pace. Thea felt at once that he was talking seriously and had dropped the tone

[060-077] of half-ironical exaggeration he had used with her of late years. “And

[060-078] I’m grateful to you for what you demand from yourself, when you might get

[060-079] off so easily. You demand more and more all the time, and you’ll do more

[060-080] and more. One is grateful to anybody for that; it makes life in general a

[060-081] little less sordid. But as a matter of fact, I’m not much interested in

[060-082] how anybody sings anything.”

[060-083] “That’s too bad of you, when I’m just beginning to see what

[060-084] is worth doing, and how I want to do it!” Thea spoke in an injured tone.

[060-085] “That’s what I congratulate you on. That’s the great

[060-086] difference between your kind and the rest of us. It’s how long

[060-087] you’re able to keep it up that tells the story. When you needed

[060-088] enthusiasm from the outside, I was able to give it to you. Now you must let me

[060-089] withdraw.”

[060-090] “I’m not tying you, am I?” she flashed out. “But

[060-091] withdraw to what? What do you want?”

[060-092] Fred shrugged. “I might ask you, What have I got? I want things that

[060-093] wouldn’t interest you; that you probably wouldn’t understand. For

[060-094] one thing, I want a son to bring up.”

[060-095] “I can understand that. It seems to me reasonable. Have you also found

[060-096] somebody you want to marry?”

[060-097] “Not particularly.” They turned another curve, which brought the

[060-098] wind to their backs, and they walked on in comparative calm, with the snow

[060-099] blowing past them. “It’s not your fault, Thea, but I’ve had

[060-100] you too much in my mind. I’ve not given myself a fair chance in other

[060-101] directions. I was in Rome when you and Nordquist were there. If that had kept

[060-102] up, it might have cured me.”

[060-103] “It might have cured a good many things,” remarked Thea grimly.

[060-104] Fred nodded sympathetically and went on. “In my library in St. Louis,

[060-105] over the fireplace, I have a property spear I had copied from one in

[060-106] Venice,—oh, years ago, after you first went abroad, while you were

[060-107] studying. You’ll probably be singing *Brünnhilde* pretty soon now,

[060-108] and I’ll send it on to you, if I may. You can take it and its history for

[060-109] what they’re worth. But I’m nearly forty years old, and I’ve

[060-110] served my turn. You’ve done what I hoped for you, what I was honestly

[060-111] willing to lose you for—then. I’m older now, and I think I was an

[060-112] ass. I wouldn’t do it again if I had the chance, not much! But I’m

[060-113] not sorry. It takes a great many people to make

[060-114] one—*Brünnhilde*.”

[060-115] Thea stopped by the fence and looked over into the black choppiness on which

[060-116] the snowflakes fell and disappeared with magical rapidity. Her face was both

[060-117] angry and troubled. “So you really feel I’ve been ungrateful. I

[060-118] thought you sent me out to get something. I didn’t know you wanted me to

[060-119] bring in something easy. I thought you wanted something—” She took

[060-120] a deep breath and shrugged her shoulders. “But there! nobody on

[060-121] God’s earth wants it, *really!* If one other person wanted

[060-122] it,”—she thrust her hand out before him and clenched

[060-123] it,—“my God, what I could do!”

[060-124] Fred laughed dismally. “Even in my ashes I feel myself pushing you! How

[060-125] can anybody help it? My dear girl, can’t you see that anybody else who

[060-126] wanted it as you do would be your rival, your deadliest danger? Can’t you

[060-127] see that it’s your great good fortune that other people can’t care

[060-128] about it so much?”

[060-129] But Thea seemed not to take in his protest at all. She went on vindicating

[060-130] herself. “It’s taken me a long while to do anything, of course, and

[060-131] I’ve only begun to see daylight. But anything good is—expensive. It

[060-132] hasn’t seemed long. I’ve always felt responsible to you.”

[060-133] Fred looked at her face intently, through the veil of snowflakes, and shook his

[060-134] head. “To me? You are a truthful woman, and you don’t mean to lie

[060-135] to me. But after the one responsibility you do feel, I doubt if you’ve

[060-136] enough left to feel responsible to God! Still, if you’ve ever in an idle

[060-137] hour fooled yourself with thinking I had anything to do with it, Heaven knows

[060-138] I’m grateful.”

[060-139] “Even if I’d married Nordquist,” Thea went on, turning down

[060-140] the path again, “there would have been something left out. There always

[060-141] is. In a way, I’ve always been married to you. I’m not very

[060-142] flexible; never was and never shall be. You caught me young. I could never have

[060-143] that over again. One can’t, after one begins to know anything. But I look

[060-144] back on it. My life hasn’t been a gay one, any more than yours. If I shut

[060-145] things out from you, you shut them out from me. We’ve been a help and a

[060-146] hindrance to each other. I guess it’s always that way, the good and the

[060-147] bad all mixed up. There’s only one thing that’s all

[060-148] beautiful—and always beautiful! That’s why my interest keeps

[060-149] up.”

[060-150] “Yes, I know.” Fred looked sidewise at the outline of her head

[060-151] against the thickening atmosphere. “And you give one the impression that

[060-152] that is enough. I’ve gradually, gradually given you up.”

[060-153] “See, the lights are coming out.” Thea pointed to where they

[060-154] flickered, flashes of violet through the gray tree-tops. Lower down the globes

[060-155] along the drives were becoming a pale lemon color. “Yes, I don’t

[060-156] see why anybody wants to marry an artist, anyhow. I remember [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] used

[060-157] to say he didn’t see how any woman could marry a gambler, for she would

[060-158] only be marrying what the game left.” She shook her shoulders

[060-159] impatiently. “Who marries who is a small matter, after all. But I hope I

[060-160] can bring back your interest in my work. You’ve cared longer and more

[060-161] than anybody else, and I’d like to have somebody human to make a report

[060-162] to once in a while. You can send me your spear. I’ll do my best. If

[060-163] you’re not interested, I’ll do my best anyhow. I’ve only a

[060-164] few friends, but I can lose every one of them, if it has to be. I learned how

[060-165] to lose when my mother died.—We must hurry now. My taxi must be

[060-166] waiting.”

[060-167] The blue light about them was growing deeper and darker, and the falling snow

[060-168] and the faint trees had become violet. To the south, over Broadway, there was

[060-169] an orange reflection in the clouds. Motors and carriage lights flashed by on

[060-170] the drive below the reservoir path, and the air was strident with horns and

[060-171] shrieks from the whistles of the mounted policemen.

[060-172] Fred gave Thea his arm as they descended from the embankment. “I guess

[060-173] you’ll never manage to lose me or Archie, Thea. You do pick up queer

[060-174] ones. But loving you is a heroic discipline. It wears a man out. Tell me one

[060-175] thing: could I have kept you, once, if I’d put on every screw?”

[060-176] Thea hurried him along, talking rapidly, as if to get it over. “You might

[060-177] have kept me in misery for a while, perhaps. I don’t know. I have to

[060-178] think well of myself, to work. You could have made it hard. I’m not

[060-179] ungrateful. I was a difficult proposition to deal with. I understand now, of

[060-180] course. Since you didn’t tell me the truth in the beginning, you

[060-181] couldn’t very well turn back after I’d set my head. At least, if

[060-182] you’d been the sort who could, you wouldn’t have had to,—for

[060-183] I’d not have cared a button for that sort, even then.” She stopped

[060-184] beside a car that waited at the curb and gave him her hand. “There. We

[060-185] part friends?”

[060-186] Fred looked at her. “You know. Ten years.”

[060-187] “I’m not ungrateful,” Thea repeated as she got into her cab.

[060-188] “Yes,” she reflected, as the taxi cut into the Park carriage road,

[060-189] “we don’t get fairy tales in this world, and he has, after all,

[060-190] cared more and longer than anybody else.” It was dark outside now, and

[060-191] the light from the lamps along the drive flashed into the cab. The snowflakes

[060-192] hovered like swarms of white bees about the globes.

[060-193] Thea sat motionless in one corner staring out of the window at the cab lights

[060-194] that wove in and out among the trees, all seeming to be bent upon joyous

[060-195] courses. Taxicabs were still new in New York, and the theme of popular

[060-196] minstrelsy. Landry had sung her a ditty he heard in some theater on Third

[060-197] Avenue, about:

[060-198] “But there passed him a bright-eyed taxi\

[060-199] With the girl of his heart inside.”

[060-200] Almost inaudibly Thea began to hum the air, though she was thinking of

[060-201] something serious, something that had touched her deeply. At the beginning of

[060-202] the season, when she was not singing often, she had gone one afternoon to hear

[060-203] Paderewski’s recital. In front of her sat an old German couple, evidently

[060-204] poor people who had made sacrifices to pay for their excellent seats. Their

[060-205] intelligent enjoyment of the music, and their friendliness with each other, had

[060-206] interested her more than anything on the programme. When the pianist began a

[060-207] lovely melody in the first movement of the Beethoven D minor sonata, the old

[060-208] lady put out her plump hand and touched her husband’s sleeve and they

[060-209] looked at each other in recognition. They both wore glasses, but such a look!

[060-210] Like forget-menots, and so full of happy recollections. Thea wanted to put her

[060-211] arms around them and ask them how they had been able to keep a feeling like

[060-212] that, like a nosegay in a glass of water.
