## XIII

[013-001] At the beginning of June, when school closed, Thea had told Wunsch that she

[013-002] didn’t know how much practicing she could get in this summer because Thor

[013-003] had his worst teeth still to cut.

[013-004] “My God! all last summer he was doing that!” Wunsch exclaimed

[013-005] furiously.

[013-006] “I know, but it takes them two years, and Thor is slow,” Thea

[013-007] answered reprovingly.

[013-008] The summer went well beyond her hopes, however. She told herself that it was

[013-009] the best summer of her life, so far. Nobody was sick at home, and her lessons

[013-010] were uninterrupted. Now that she had four pupils of her own and made a dollar a

[013-011] week, her practicing was regarded more seriously by the household. Her mother

[013-012] had always arranged things so that she could have the parlor four hours a day

[013-013] in summer. Thor proved a friendly ally. He behaved handsomely about his molars,

[013-014] and never objected to being pulled off into remote places in his cart. When

[013-015] Thea dragged him over the hill and made a camp under the shade of a bush or a

[013-016] bank, he would waddle about and play with his blocks, or bury his monkey in the

[013-017] sand and dig him up again. Sometimes he got into the cactus and set up a howl,

[013-018] but usually he let his sister read peacefully, while he coated his hands and

[013-019] face, first with an all-day sucker and then with gravel.

[013-020] Life was pleasant and uneventful until the first of September, when Wunsch

[013-021] began to drink so hard that he was unable to appear when Thea went to take her

[013-022] mid-week lesson, and Mrs. Kohler had to send her home after a tearful apology.

[013-023] On Saturday morning she set out for the Kohlers’ again, but on her way,

[013-024] when she was crossing the ravine, she noticed a woman sitting at the bottom of

[013-025] the gulch, under the railroad trestle. She turned from her path and saw that it

[013-026] was Mrs. Tellamantez, and she seemed to be doing drawn-work. Then Thea noticed

[013-027] that there was something beside her, covered up with a purple and yellow

[013-028] Mexican blanket. She ran up the gulch and called to Mrs. Tellamantez. The

[013-029] Mexican woman held up a warning finger. Thea glanced at the blanket and

[013-030] recognized a square red hand which protruded. The middle finger twitched

[013-031] slightly.

[013-032] “Is he hurt?” she gasped.

[013-033] Mrs. Tellamantez shook her head. “No; very sick. He knows nothing,”

[013-034] she said quietly, folding her hands over her drawn-work.

[013-035] Thea learned that Wunsch had been out all night, that this morning Mrs. Kohler

[013-036] had gone to look for him and found him under the trestle covered with dirt and

[013-037] cinders. Probably he had been trying to get home and had lost his way. Mrs.

[013-038] Tellamantez was watching beside the unconscious man while Mrs. Kohler and

[013-039] Johnny went to get help.

[013-040] “You better go home now, I think,” said Mrs. Tellamantez, in

[013-041] closing her narration.

[013-042] Thea hung her head and looked wistfully toward the blanket.

[013-043] “Couldn’t I just stay till they come?” she asked.

[013-044] “I’d like to know if he’s very bad.”

[013-045] “Bad enough,” sighed Mrs. Tellamantez, taking up her work again.

[013-046] Thea sat down under the narrow shade of one of the trestle posts and listened

[013-047] to the locusts rasping in the hot sand while she watched Mrs. Tellamantez

[013-048] evenly draw her threads. The blanket looked as if it were over a heap of

[013-049] bricks.

[013-050] “I don’t see him breathing any,” she said anxiously.

[013-051] “Yes, he breathes,” said Mrs. Tellamantez, not lifting her eyes.

[013-052] It seemed to Thea that they waited for hours. At last they heard voices, and a

[013-053] party of men came down the hill and up the gulch. [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] and Fritz Kohler

[013-054] came first; behind were Johnny and Ray, and several men from the roundhouse.

[013-055] Ray had the canvas litter that was kept at the depot for accidents on the road.

[013-056] Behind them trailed half a dozen boys who had been hanging round the depot.

[013-057] When Ray saw Thea, he dropped his canvas roll and hurried forward.

[013-058] “Better run along home, Thee. This is ugly business.” Ray was

[013-059] indignant that anybody who gave Thea music lessons should behave in such a

[013-060] manner.

[013-061] Thea resented both his proprietary tone and his superior virtue. “I

[013-062] won’t. I want to know how bad he is. I’m not a baby!” she

[013-063] exclaimed indignantly, stamping her foot into the sand.

[013-064] Dr. Archie, who had been kneeling by the blanket, got up and came toward Thea,

[013-065] dusting his knees. He smiled and nodded confidentially. “He’ll be

[013-066] all right when we get him home. But he wouldn’t want you to see him like

[013-067] this, poor old chap! Understand? Now, skip!”

[013-068] Thea ran down the gulch and looked back only once, to see them lifting the

[013-069] canvas litter with Wunsch upon it, still covered with the blanket.

[013-070] The men carried Wunsch up the hill and down the road to the Kohlers’.

[013-071] Mrs. Kohler had gone home and made up a bed in the sitting-room, as she knew

[013-072] the litter could not be got round the turn in the narrow stairway. Wunsch was

[013-073] like a dead man. He lay unconscious all day. [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] stayed with him till

[013-074] two o’clock in the afternoon, when he had to go out on his run. It was

[013-075] the first time he had ever been inside the Kohlers’ house, and he was so

[013-076] much impressed by Napoleon that the piece-picture formed a new bond between him

[013-077] and Thea.

[013-078] Dr. Archie went back at six o’clock, and found Mrs. Kohler and Spanish

[013-079] Johnny with Wunsch, who was in a high fever, muttering and groaning.

[013-080] “There ought to be some one here to look after him to-night, Mrs.

[013-081] Kohler,” he said. “I’m on a confinement case, and I

[013-082] can’t be here, but there ought to be somebody. He may get violent.”

[013-083] Mrs. Kohler insisted that she could always do anything with Wunsch, but the

[013-084] doctor shook his head and [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]] grinned. He said he would stay. The

[013-085] doctor laughed at him. “Ten fellows like you couldn’t hold him,

[013-086] Spanish, if he got obstreperous; an Irishman would have his hands full. Guess

[013-087] I’d better put the soft pedal on him.” He pulled out his

[013-088] hypodermic.

[013-089] Spanish Johnny stayed, however, and the Kohlers went to bed. At about two

[013-090] o’clock in the morning Wunsch rose from his ignominious cot. Johnny, who

[013-091] was dozing on the lounge, awoke to find the German standing in the middle of

[013-092] the room in his undershirt and drawers, his arms bare, his heavy body seeming

[013-093] twice its natural girth. His face was snarling and savage, and his eyes were

[013-094] crazy. He had risen to avenge himself, to wipe out his shame, to destroy his

[013-095] enemy. One look was enough for Johnny. Wunsch raised a chair threateningly, and

[013-096] Johnny, with the lightness of a *picador*, darted under the missile and

[013-097] out of the open window. He shot across the gully to get help, meanwhile leaving

[013-098] the Kohlers to their fate.

[013-099] Fritz, upstairs, heard the chair crash upon the stove. Then he heard doors

[013-100] opening and shutting, and some one stumbling about in the shrubbery of the

[013-101] garden. He and Paulina sat up in bed and held a consultation. Fritz slipped

[013-102] from under the covers, and going cautiously over to the window, poked out his

[013-103] head. Then he rushed to the door and bolted it.

[013-104] “*Mein Gott*, Paulina,” he gasped, “he has the axe, he

[013-105] will kill us!”

[013-106] “The dresser,” cried Mrs. Kohler; “push the dresser before

[013-107] the door. *Ach*, if you had your rabbit gun, now!”

[013-108] “It is in the barn,” said Fritz sadly. “It would do no good;

[013-109] he would not be afraid of anything now. Stay you in the bed, Paulina.”

[013-110] The dresser had lost its casters years ago, but he managed to drag it in front

[013-111] of the door. “He is in the garden. He makes nothing. He will get sick

[013-112] again, may-be.”

[013-113] Fritz went back to bed and his wife pulled the quilt over him and made him lie

[013-114] down. They heard stumbling in the garden again, then a smash of glass.

[013-115] “*Ach, das Mistbeet!*” gasped Paulina, hearing her hotbed

[013-116] shivered. “The poor soul, Fritz, he will cut himself. *Ach!* what is

[013-117] that?” They both sat up in bed. “*Wieder! Ach*, What is he

[013-118] doing?”

[013-119] The noise came steadily, a sound of chopping. Paulina tore off her night-cap.

[013-120] “*Die Bäume, die Bäume!* He is cutting our trees, Fritz!”

[013-121] Before her husband could prevent her, she had sprung from the bed and rushed to

[013-122] the window. “*Der Taubenschlag! Gerechter Himmel*, he is chopping

[013-123] the dove-house down!”

[013-124] Fritz reached her side before she had got her breath again, and poked his head

[013-125] out beside hers. There, in the faint starlight, they saw a bulky man, barefoot,

[013-126] half dressed, chopping away at the white post that formed the pedestal of the

[013-127] dove-house. The startled pigeons were croaking and flying about his head, even

[013-128] beating their wings in his face, so that he struck at them furiously with the

[013-129] axe. In a few seconds there was a crash, and Wunsch had actually felled the

[013-130] dove-house.

[013-131] “Oh, if only it is not the trees next!” prayed Paulina. “The

[013-132] dove-house you can make new again, but not *die Bäume*.”

[013-133] They watched breathlessly. In the garden below Wunsch stood in the attitude of

[013-134] a woodman, contemplating the fallen cote. Suddenly he threw the axe over his

[013-135] shoulder and went out of the front gate toward the town.

[013-136] “The poor soul, he will meet his death!” Mrs. Kohler wailed. She

[013-137] ran back to her feather bed and hid her face in the pillow.

[013-138] Fritz kept watch at the window. “No, no, Paulina,” he called

[013-139] presently; “I see lanterns coming. Johnny must have gone for somebody.

[013-140] Yes, four lanterns, coming along the gulch. They stop; they must have seen him

[013-141] already. Now they are under the hill and I cannot see them, but I think they

[013-142] have him. They will bring him back. I must dress and go down.” He caught

[013-143] his trousers and began pulling them on by the window. “Yes, here they

[013-144] come, half a dozen men. And they have tied him with a rope, Paulina!”

[013-145] “*Ach*, the poor man! To be led like a cow,” groaned Mrs.

[013-146] Kohler. “Oh, it is good that he has no wife!” She was reproaching

[013-147] herself for nagging Fritz when he drank himself into foolish pleasantry or mild

[013-148] sulks, and felt that she had never before appreciated her blessings.

[013-149] Wunsch was in bed for ten days, during which time he was gossiped about and

[013-150] even preached about in Moonstone. The Baptist preacher took a shot at the

[013-151] fallen man from his pulpit, Mrs. Livery Johnson nodding approvingly from her

[013-152] pew. The mothers of Wunsch’s pupils sent him notes informing him that

[013-153] their daughters would discontinue their music-lessons. The old maid who had

[013-154] rented him her piano sent the town dray for her contaminated instrument, and

[013-155] ever afterward declared that Wunsch had ruined its tone and scarred its glossy

[013-156] finish. The Kohlers were unremitting in their kindness to their friend. Mrs.

[013-157] Kohler made him soups and broths without stint, and Fritz repaired the

[013-158] dove-house and mounted it on a new post, lest it might be a sad reminder.

[013-159] As soon as Wunsch was strong enough to sit about in his slippers and wadded

[013-160] jacket, he told Fritz to bring him some stout thread from the shop. When Fritz

[013-161] asked what he was going to sew, he produced the tattered score of

[013-162] “Orpheus” and said he would like to fix it up for a little present.

[013-163] Fritz carried it over to the shop and stitched it into pasteboards, covered

[013-164] with dark suiting-cloth. Over the stitches he glued a strip of thin red leather

[013-165] which he got from his friend, the harness-maker. After Paulina had cleaned the

[013-166] pages with fresh bread, Wunsch was amazed to see what a fine book he had. It

[013-167] opened stiffly, but that was no matter.

[013-168] Sitting in the arbor one morning, under the ripe grapes and the brown, curling

[013-169] leaves, with a pen and ink on the bench beside him and the Gluck score on his

[013-170] knee, Wunsch pondered for a long while. Several times he dipped the pen in the

[013-171] ink, and then put it back again in the cigar box in which Mrs. Kohler kept her

[013-172] writing utensils. His thoughts wandered over a wide territory; over many

[013-173] countries and many years. There was no order or logical sequence in his ideas.

[013-174] Pictures came and went without reason. Faces, mountains, rivers, autumn days in

[013-175] other vineyards far away. He thought of a *Fuszreise* he had made through

[013-176] the Hartz Mountains in his student days; of the innkeeper’s pretty

[013-177] daughter who had lighted his pipe for him in the garden one summer evening, of

[013-178] the woods above Wiesbaden, haymakers on an island in the river. The roundhouse

[013-179] whistle woke him from his reveries. Ah, yes, he was in [[moonstone|Moonstone, Colorado]]. He

[013-180] frowned for a moment and looked at the book on his knee. He had thought of a

[013-181] great many appropriate things to write in it, but suddenly he rejected all of

[013-182] them, opened the book, and at the top of the much-engraved title-page he wrote

[013-183] rapidly in purple ink:—

[013-184] Einst, O Wunder!—

[013-185] A. Wunsch.

[013-186] Moonstone, Colo.\

[013-187] September 30, 18—

[013-188] Nobody in Moonstone ever found what Wunsch’s first name was. That

[013-189] “A” may have stood for Adam, or August, or even Amadeus; he got

[013-190] very angry if any one asked him.

[013-191] He remained A. Wunsch to the end of his chapter there. When he presented this

[013-192] score to Thea, he told her that in ten years she would either know what the

[013-193] inscription meant, or she would not have the least idea, in which case it would

[013-194] not matter.

[013-195] When Wunsch began to pack his trunk, both the Kohlers were very unhappy. He

[013-196] said he was coming back some day, but that for the present, since he had lost

[013-197] all his pupils, it would be better for him to try some “new town.”

[013-198] Mrs. Kohler darned and mended all his clothes, and gave him two new shirts she

[013-199] had made for Fritz. Fritz made him a new pair of trousers and would have made

[013-200] him an overcoat but for the fact that overcoats were so easy to pawn.

[013-201] Wunsch would not go across the ravine to the town until he went to take the

[013-202] morning train for Denver. He said that after he got to Denver he would

[013-203] “look around.” He left Moonstone one bright October morning,

[013-204] without telling any one good-bye. He bought his ticket and went directly into

[013-205] the smoking-car. When the train was beginning to pull out, he heard his name

[013-206] called frantically, and looking out of the window he saw [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] standing

[013-207] on the siding, bareheaded and panting. Some boys had brought word to school

[013-208] that they saw Wunsch’s trunk going over to the station, and Thea had run

[013-209] away from school. She was at the end of the station platform, her hair in two

[013-210] braids, her blue gingham dress wet to the knees because she had run across lots

[013-211] through the weeds. It had rained during the night, and the tall sunflowers

[013-212] behind her were fresh and shining.

[013-213] “Good-bye, Herr Wunsch, good-bye!” she called waving to him.

[013-214] He thrust his head out at the car window and called back, “*Leben sie

[013-215] wohl, leben sie wohl, mein Kind!*” He watched her until the train

[013-216] swept around the curve beyond the roundhouse, and then sank back into his seat,

[013-217] muttering, “She had been running. Ah, she will run a long way; they

[013-218] cannot stop her!”

[013-219] What was it about the child that one believed in? Was it her dogged industry,

[013-220] so unusual in this free-and-easy country? Was it her imagination? More likely

[013-221] it was because she had both imagination and a stubborn will, curiously

[013-222] balancing and interpenetrating each other. There was something unconscious and

[013-223] unawakened about her, that tempted curiosity. She had a kind of seriousness

[013-224] that he had not met with in a pupil before. She hated difficult things, and yet

[013-225] she could never pass one by. They seemed to challenge her; she had no peace

[013-226] until she mastered them. She had the power to make a great effort, to lift a

[013-227] weight heavier than herself. Wunsch hoped he would always remember her as she

[013-228] stood by the track, looking up at him; her broad eager face, so fair in color,

[013-229] with its high cheek-bones, yellow eyebrows and greenishhazel eyes. It was a

[013-230] face full of light and energy, of the unquestioning hopefulness of first youth.

[013-231] Yes, she was like a flower full of sun, but not the soft German flowers of his

[013-232] childhood. He had it now, the comparison he had absently reached for before:

[013-233] she was like the yellow prickly pear blossoms that open there in the desert;

[013-234] thornier and sturdier than the maiden flowers he remembered; not so sweet, but

[013-235] wonderful.

[013-236] That night Mrs. Kohler brushed away many a tear as she got supper and set the

[013-237] table for two. When they sat down, Fritz was more silent than usual. People who

[013-238] have lived long together need a third at table: they know each other’s

[013-239] thoughts so well that they have nothing left to say. Mrs. Kohler stirred and

[013-240] stirred her coffee and clattered the spoon, but she had no heart for her

[013-241] supper. She felt, for the first time in years, that she was tired of her own

[013-242] cooking. She looked across the glass lamp at her husband and asked him if the

[013-243] butcher liked his new overcoat, and whether he had got the shoulders right in a

[013-244] ready-made suit he was patching over for Ray Kennedy. After supper Fritz

[013-245] offered to wipe the dishes for her, but she told him to go about his business,

[013-246] and not to act as if she were sick or getting helpless.

[013-247] When her work in the kitchen was all done, she went out to cover the oleanders

[013-248] against frost, and to take a last look at her chickens. As she came back from

[013-249] the hen-house she stopped by one of the linden trees and stood resting her hand

[013-250] on the trunk. He would never come back, the poor man; she knew that. He would

[013-251] drift on from new town to new town, from catastrophe to catastrophe. He would

[013-252] hardly find a good home for himself again. He would die at last in some rough

[013-253] place, and be buried in the desert or on the wild prairie, far enough from any

[013-254] linden tree!

[013-255] Fritz, smoking his pipe on the kitchen doorstep, watched his Paulina and

[013-256] guessed her thoughts. He, too, was sorry to lose his friend. But Fritz was

[013-257] getting old; he had lived a long while and had learned to lose without

[013-258] struggle.
