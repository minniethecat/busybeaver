## VIII

[058-001] The “Ring of the Niebelungs” was to be given at the Metropolitan on

[058-002] four successive Friday afternoons. After the first of these performances, Fred

[058-003] Ottenburg went home with Landry for tea. Landry was one of the few public

[058-004] entertainers who own real estate in New York. He lived in a little three-story

[058-005] brick house on Jane Street, in Greenwich Village, which had been left to him by

[058-006] the same aunt who paid for his musical education.

[058-007] Landry was born, and spent the first fifteen years of his life, on a rocky

[058-008] Connecticut farm not far from Cos Cob. His father was an ignorant, violent man,

[058-009] a bungling farmer and a brutal husband. The farmhouse, dilapidated and damp,

[058-010] stood in a hollow beside a marshy pond. Oliver had worked hard while he lived

[058-011] at home, although he was never clean or warm in winter and had wretched food

[058-012] all the year round. His spare, dry figure, his prominent larynx, and the

[058-013] peculiar red of his face and hands belonged to the choreboy he had never

[058-014] outgrown. It was as if the farm, knowing he would escape from it as early as he

[058-015] could, had ground its mark on him deep. When he was fifteen Oliver ran away and

[058-016] went to live with his Catholic aunt, on Jane Street, whom his mother was never

[058-017] allowed to visit. The priest of St. Joseph’s Parish discovered that he

[058-018] had a voice.

[058-019] Landry had an affection for the house on Jane Street, where he had first

[058-020] learned what cleanliness and order and courtesy were. When his aunt died he had

[058-021] the place done over, got an Irish housekeeper, and lived there with a great

[058-022] many beautiful things he had collected. His living expenses were never large,

[058-023] but he could not restrain himself from buying graceful and useless objects. He

[058-024] was a collector for much the same reason that he was a Catholic, and he was a

[058-025] Catholic chiefly because his father used to sit in the kitchen and read aloud

[058-026] to his hired men disgusting “exposures” of the Roman Church,

[058-027] enjoying equally the hideous stories and the outrage to his wife’s

[058-028] feelings.

[058-029] At first Landry bought books; then rugs, drawings, china. He had a beautiful

[058-030] collection of old French and Spanish fans. He kept them in an escritoire he had

[058-031] brought from Spain, but there were always a few of them lying about in his

[058-032] sitting-room.

[058-033] While Landry and his guest were waiting for the tea to be brought, Ottenburg

[058-034] took up one of these fans from the low marble mantel-shelf and opened it in the

[058-035] firelight. One side was painted with a pearly sky and floating clouds. On the

[058-036] other was a formal garden where an elegant shepherdess with a mask and crook

[058-037] was fleeing on high heels from a satin-coated shepherd.

[058-038] “You ought not to keep these things about, like this, Oliver. The dust

[058-039] from your grate must get at them.”

[058-040] “It does, but I get them to enjoy them, not to have them. They’re

[058-041] pleasant to glance at and to play with at odd times like this, when one is

[058-042] waiting for tea or something.”

[058-043] Fred smiled. The idea of Landry stretched out before his fire playing with his

[058-044] fans, amused him. Mrs. McGinnis brought the tea and put it before the hearth:

[058-045] old teacups that were velvety to the touch and a pot-bellied silver cream

[058-046] pitcher of an Early Georgian pattern, which was always brought, though Landry

[058-047] took rum.

[058-048] Fred drank his tea walking about, examining Landry’s sumptuous

[058-049] writing-table in the alcove and the Boucher drawing in red chalk over the

[058-050] mantel. “I don’t see how you can stand this place without a

[058-051] heroine. It would give me a raging thirst for gallantries.”

[058-052] Landry was helping himself to a second cup of tea. “Works quite the other

[058-053] way with me. It consoles me for the lack of her. It’s just feminine

[058-054] enough to be pleasant to return to. Not any more tea? Then sit down and play

[058-055] for me. I’m always playing for other people, and I never have a chance to

[058-056] sit here quietly and listen.”

[058-057] Ottenburg opened the piano and began softly to boom forth the shadowy

[058-058] introduction to the opera they had just heard. “Will that do?” he

[058-059] asked jokingly. “I can’t seem to get it out of my head.”

[058-060] “Oh, excellently! Thea told me it was quite wonderful, the way you can do

[058-061] Wagner scores on the piano. So few people can give one any idea of the music.

[058-062] Go ahead, as long as you like. I can smoke, too.” Landry flattened

[058-063] himself out on his cushions and abandoned himself to ease with the circumstance

[058-064] of one who has never grown quite accustomed to ease.

[058-065] Ottenburg played on, as he happened to remember. He understood now why Thea

[058-066] wished him to hear her in “Rheingold.” It had been clear to him as

[058-067] soon as *Fricka* rose from sleep and looked out over the young world,

[058-068] stretching one white arm toward the new Götterburg shining on the heights.

[058-069] “*Wotan! Gemahl! erwache!*” She was pure Scandinavian, this

[058-070] *Fricka:* “Swedish summer”! he remembered old Mr.

[058-071] Nathanmeyer’s phrase. She had wished him to see her because she had a

[058-072] distinct kind of loveliness for this part, a shining beauty like the light of

[058-073] sunset on distant sails. She seemed to take on the look of immortal loveliness,

[058-074] the youth of the golden apples, the shining body and the shining mind.

[058-075] *Fricka* had been a jealous spouse to him for so long that he had forgot

[058-076] she meant wisdom before she meant domestic order, and that, in any event, she

[058-077] was always a goddess. The *Fricka* of that afternoon was so clear and

[058-078] sunny, so nobly conceived, that she made a whole atmosphere about herself and

[058-079] quite redeemed from shabbiness the helplessness and unscrupulousness of the

[058-080] gods. Her reproaches to *Wotan* were the pleadings of a tempered mind, a

[058-081] consistent sense of beauty. In the long silences of her part, her shining

[058-082] presence was a visible complement to the discussion of the orchestra. As the

[058-083] themes which were to help in weaving the drama to its end first came vaguely

[058-084] upon the ear, one saw their import and tendency in the face of this

[058-085] clearest-visioned of the gods.

[058-086] In the scene between *Fricka* and *Wotan*, Ottenburg stopped.

[058-087] “I can’t seem to get the voices, in there.”

[058-088] Landry chuckled. “Don’t try. I know it well enough. I expect

[058-089] I’ve been over that with her a thousand times. I was playing for her

[058-090] almost every day when she was first working on it. When she begins with a part

[058-091] she’s hard to work with: so slow you’d think she was stupid if you

[058-092] didn’t know her. Of course she blames it all on her accompanist. It goes

[058-093] on like that for weeks sometimes. This did. She kept shaking her head and

[058-094] staring and looking gloomy. All at once, she got her line—it usually

[058-095] comes suddenly, after stretches of not getting anywhere at all—and after

[058-096] that it kept changing and clearing. As she worked her voice into it, it got

[058-097] more and more of that ‘gold’ quality that makes her *Fricka*

[058-098] so different.”

[058-099] Fred began *Fricka’s* first aria again. “It’s certainly

[058-100] different. Curious how she does it. Such a beautiful idea, out of a part

[058-101] that’s always been so ungrateful. She’s a lovely thing, but she was

[058-102] never so beautiful as that, really. Nobody is.” He repeated the loveliest

[058-103] phrase. “How does she manage it, Landry? You’ve worked with

[058-104] her.”

[058-105] Landry drew cherishingly on the last cigarette he meant to permit himself

[058-106] before singing. “Oh, it’s a question of a big personality—and

[058-107] all that goes with it. Brains, of course. Imagination, of course. But the

[058-108] important thing is that she was born full of color, with a rich personality.

[058-109] That’s a gift of the gods, like a fine nose. You have it, or you

[058-110] haven’t. Against it, intelligence and musicianship and habits of industry

[058-111] don’t count at all. Singers are a conventional race. When Thea was

[058-112] studying in Berlin the other girls were mortally afraid of her. She has a

[058-113] pretty rough hand with women, dull ones, and she could be rude, too! The girls

[058-114] used to call her *die Wölfin*.”

[058-115] Fred thrust his hands into his pockets and leaned back against the piano.

[058-116] “Of course, even a stupid woman could get effects with such machinery:

[058-117] such a voice and body and face. But they couldn’t possibly belong to a

[058-118] stupid woman, could they?”

[058-119] Landry shook his head. “It’s personality; that’s as near as

[058-120] you can come to it. That’s what constitutes real equipment. What she does

[058-121] is interesting because she does it. Even the things she discards are

[058-122] suggestive. I regret some of them. Her conceptions are colored in so many

[058-123] different ways. You’ve heard her *Elizabeth?* Wonderful, isn’t

[058-124] it? She was working on that part years ago when her mother was ill. I could see

[058-125] her anxiety and grief getting more and more into the part. The last act is

[058-126] heart-breaking. It’s as homely as a country prayer meeting: might be any

[058-127] lonely woman getting ready to die. It’s full of the thing every plain

[058-128] creature finds out for himself, but that never gets written down. It’s

[058-129] unconscious memory, maybe; inherited memory, like folk-music. I call it

[058-130] personality.”

[058-131] Fred laughed, and turning to the piano began coaxing the *Fricka* music

[058-132] again. “Call it anything you like, my boy. I have a name for it myself,

[058-133] but I shan’t tell you.” He looked over his shoulder at Landry,

[058-134] stretched out by the fire. “You have a great time watching her,

[058-135] don’t you?”

[058-136] “Oh, yes!” replied Landry simply. “I’m not interested

[058-137] in much that goes on in New York. Now, if you’ll excuse me, I’ll

[058-138] have to dress.” He rose with a reluctant sigh. “Can I get you

[058-139] anything? Some whiskey?”

[058-140] “Thank you, no. I’ll amuse myself here. I don’t often get a

[058-141] chance at a good piano when I’m away from home. You haven’t had

[058-142] this one long, have you? Action’s a bit stiff. I say,” he stopped

[058-143] Landry in the doorway, “has Thea ever been down here?”

[058-144] Landry turned back. “Yes. She came several times when I had erysipelas. I

[058-145] was a nice mess, with two nurses. She brought down some inside window-boxes,

[058-146] planted with crocuses and things. Very cheering, only I couldn’t see them

[058-147] or her.”

[058-148] “Didn’t she like your place?”

[058-149] “She thought she did, but I fancy it was a good deal cluttered up for her

[058-150] taste. I could hear her pacing about like something in a cage. She pushed the

[058-151] piano back against the wall and the chairs into corners, and she broke my amber

[058-152] elephant.” Landry took a yellow object some four inches high from one of

[058-153] his low bookcases. “You can see where his leg is glued on,—a

[058-154] souvenir. Yes, he’s lemon amber, very fine.”

[058-155] Landry disappeared behind the curtains and in a moment Fred heard the wheeze of

[058-156] an atomizer. He put the amber elephant on the piano beside him and seemed to

[058-157] get a great deal of amusement out of the beast.
