## VI

[056-001] It was late on the morning after the night she sang *Elsa*, when Thea

[056-002] Kronborg stirred uneasily in her bed. The room was darkened by two sets of

[056-003] window shades, and the day outside was thick and cloudy. She turned and tried

[056-004] to recapture unconsciousness, knowing that she would not be able to do so. She

[056-005] dreaded waking stale and disappointed after a great effort. The first thing

[056-006] that came was always the sense of the futility of such endeavor, and of the

[056-007] absurdity of trying too hard. Up to a certain point, say eighty degrees,

[056-008] artistic endeavor could be fat and comfortable, methodical and prudent. But if

[056-009] you went further than that, if you drew yourself up toward ninety degrees, you

[056-010] parted with your defenses and left yourself exposed to mischance. The legend

[056-011] was that in those upper reaches you might be divine; but you were much likelier

[056-012] to be ridiculous. Your public wanted just about eighty degrees; if you gave it

[056-013] more it blew its nose and put a crimp in you. In the morning, especially, it

[056-014] seemed to her very probable that whatever struggled above the good average was

[056-015] not quite sound. Certainly very little of that superfluous ardor, which cost so

[056-016] dear, ever got across the footlights. These misgivings waited to pounce upon

[056-017] her when she wakened. They hovered about her bed like vultures.

[056-018] She reached under her pillow for her handkerchief, without opening her eyes.

[056-019] She had a shadowy memory that there was to be something unusual, that this day

[056-020] held more disquieting possibilities than days commonly held. There was

[056-021] something she dreaded; what was it? Oh, yes, [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] was to come at four.

[056-022] A reality like Dr. Archie, poking up out of the past, reminded one of

[056-023] disappointments and losses, of a freedom that was no more: reminded her of

[056-024] blue, golden mornings long ago, when she used to waken with a burst of joy at

[056-025] recovering her precious self and her precious world; when she never lay on her

[056-026] pillows at eleven o’clock like something the waves had washed up. After

[056-027] all, why had he come? It had been so long, and so much had happened. The things

[056-028] she had lost, he would miss readily enough. What she had gained, he would

[056-029] scarcely perceive. He, and all that he recalled, lived for her as memories. In

[056-030] sleep, and in hours of illness or exhaustion, she went back to them and held

[056-031] them to her heart. But they were better as memories. They had nothing to do

[056-032] with the struggle that made up her actual life. She felt drearily that she was

[056-033] not flexible enough to be the person her old friend expected her to be, the

[056-034] person she herself wished to be with him.

[056-035] Thea reached for the bell and rang twice,—a signal to her maid to order

[056-036] her breakfast. She rose and ran up the window shades and turned on the water in

[056-037] her bathroom, glancing into the mirror apprehensively as she passed it. Her

[056-038] bath usually cheered her, even on low mornings like this. Her white bathroom,

[056-039] almost as large as her sleeping-room, she regarded as a refuge. When she turned

[056-040] the key behind her, she left care and vexation on the other side of the door.

[056-041] Neither her maid nor the management nor her letters nor her accompanist could

[056-042] get at her now.

[056-043] When she pinned her braids about her head, dropped her nightgown and stepped

[056-044] out to begin her Swedish movements, she was a natural creature again, and it

[056-045] was so that she liked herself best. She slid into the tub with anticipation and

[056-046] splashed and tumbled about a good deal. Whatever else she hurried, she never

[056-047] hurried her bath. She used her brushes and sponges and soaps like toys, fairly

[056-048] playing in the water. Her own body was always a cheering sight to her. When she

[056-049] was careworn, when her mind felt old and tired, the freshness of her physical

[056-050] self, her long, firm lines, the smoothness of her skin, reassured her. This

[056-051] morning, because of awakened memories, she looked at herself more carefully

[056-052] than usual, and was not discouraged. While she was in the tub she began to

[056-053] whistle softly the tenor aria, “*Ah! Fuyez, douce image*,”

[056-054] somehow appropriate to the bath. After a noisy moment under the cold shower,

[056-055] she stepped out on the rug flushed and glowing, threw her arms above her head,

[056-056] and rose on her toes, keeping the elevation as long as she could. When she

[056-057] dropped back on her heels and began to rub herself with the towels, she took up

[056-058] the aria again, and felt quite in the humor for seeing Dr. Archie. After she

[056-059] had returned to her bed, the maid brought her letters and the morning papers

[056-060] with her breakfast.

[056-061] “Telephone Mr. Landry and ask him if he can come at half-past three,

[056-062] Theresa, and order tea to be brought up at five.”

[056-063] When Howard Archie was admitted to Thea’s apartment that afternoon, he

[056-064] was shown into the music-room back of the little reception room. Thea was

[056-065] sitting in a davenport behind the piano, talking to a young man whom she later

[056-066] introduced as her friend Mr. Landry. As she rose, and came to meet him, Archie

[056-067] felt a deep relief, a sudden thankfulness. She no longer looked clipped and

[056-068] plucked, or dazed and fleeing.

[056-069] Dr. Archie neglected to take account of the young man to whom he was presented.

[056-070] He kept Thea’s hands and held her where he met her, taking in the light,

[056-071] lively sweep of her hair, her clear green eyes and her throat that came up

[056-072] strong and dazzlingly white from her green velvet gown. The chin was as lovely

[056-073] as ever, the cheeks as smooth. All the lines of last night had disappeared.

[056-074] Only at the outer corners of her eyes, between the eye and the temple, were the

[056-075] faintest indications of a future attack—mere kitten scratches that

[056-076] playfully hinted where one day the cat would claw her. He studied her without

[056-077] any embarrassment. Last night everything had been awkward; but now, as he held

[056-078] her hands, a kind of harmony came between them, a reëstablishment of

[056-079] confidence.

[056-080] “After all, Thea,—in spite of all, I still know you,” he

[056-081] murmured.

[056-082] She took his arm and led him up to the young man who was standing beside the

[056-083] piano. “Mr. Landry knows all about you, Dr. Archie. He has known about

[056-084] you for many years.” While the two men shook hands she stood between

[056-085] them, drawing them together by her presence and her glances. “When I

[056-086] first went to Germany, Landry was studying there. He used to be good enough to

[056-087] work with me when I could not afford to have an accompanist for more than two

[056-088] hours a day. We got into the way of working together. He is a singer, too, and

[056-089] has his own career to look after, but he still manages to give me some time. I

[056-090] want you to be friends.” She smiled from one to the other.

[056-091] The rooms, Archie noticed, full of last night’s flowers, were furnished

[056-092] in light colors, the hotel bleakness of them a little softened by a magnificent

[056-093] Steinway piano, white bookshelves full of books and scores, some drawings of

[056-094] ballet dancers, and the very deep sofa behind the piano.

[056-095] “Of course,” Archie asked apologetically, “you have seen the

[056-096] papers?”

[056-097] “Very cordial, aren’t they? They evidently did not expect as much

[056-098] as I did. *Elsa* is not really in my voice. I can sing the music, but I

[056-099] have to go after it.”

[056-100] “That is exactly,” the doctor came out boldly, “what Fred

[056-101] Ottenburg said this morning.”

[056-102] They had remained standing, the three of them, by the piano, where the gray

[056-103] afternoon light was strongest. Thea turned to the doctor with interest.

[056-104] “Is Fred in town? They were from him, then—some flowers that came

[056-105] last night without a card.” She indicated the white lilacs on the window

[056-106] sill. “Yes, he would know, certainly,” she said thoughtfully.

[056-107] “Why don’t we sit down? There will be some tea for you in a minute,

[056-108] Landry. He’s very dependent upon it,” disapprovingly to Archie.

[056-109] “Now tell me, Doctor, did you really have a good time last night, or were

[056-110] you uncomfortable? Did you feel as if I were trying to hold my hat on by my

[056-111] eyebrows?”

[056-112] He smiled. “I had all kinds of a time. But I had no feeling of that sort.

[056-113] I couldn’t be quite sure that it was you at all. That was why I came up

[056-114] here last night. I felt as if I’d lost you.”

[056-115] She leaned toward him and brushed his sleeve reassuringly. “Then I

[056-116] didn’t give you an impression of painful struggle? Landry was singing at

[056-117] Weber and Fields’ last night. He didn’t get in until the

[056-118] performance was half over. But I see the *Tribune* man felt that I was

[056-119] working pretty hard. Did you see that notice, Oliver?”

[056-120] Dr. Archie looked closely at the red-headed young man for the first time, and

[056-121] met his lively brown eyes, full of a droll, confiding sort of humor. Mr. Landry

[056-122] was not prepossessing. He was undersized and clumsily made, with a red, shiny

[056-123] face and a sharp little nose that looked as if it had been whittled out of wood

[056-124] and was always in the air, on the scent of something. Yet it was this queer

[056-125] little beak, with his eyes, that made his countenance anything of a face at

[056-126] all. From a distance he looked like the groceryman’s delivery boy in a

[056-127] small town. His dress seemed an acknowledgment of his grotesqueness: a short

[056-128] coat, like a little boys’ roundabout, and a vest fantastically sprigged

[056-129] and dotted, over a lavender shirt.

[056-130] At the sound of a muffled buzz, Mr. Landry sprang up.

[056-131] “May I answer the telephone for you?” He went to the writing-table

[056-132] and took up the receiver. “Mr. Ottenburg is downstairs,” he said,

[056-133] turning to Thea and holding the mouthpiece against his coat.

[056-134] “Tell him to come up,” she replied without hesitation. “How

[056-135] long are you going to be in town, Dr. Archie?”

[056-136] “Oh, several weeks, if you’ll let me stay. I won’t hang

[056-137] around and be a burden to you, but I want to try to get educated up to you,

[056-138] though I expect it’s late to begin.”

[056-139] Thea rose and touched him lightly on the shoulder. “Well, you’ll

[056-140] never be any younger, will you?”

[056-141] “I’m not so sure about that,” the doctor replied gallantly.

[056-142] The maid appeared at the door and announced Mr. Frederick Ottenburg. Fred came

[056-143] in, very much got up, the doctor reflected, as he watched him bending over

[056-144] Thea’s hand. He was still pale and looked somewhat chastened, and the

[056-145] lock of hair that hung down over his forehead was distinctly moist. But his

[056-146] black afternoon coat, his gray tie and gaiters were of a correctness that Dr.

[056-147] Archie could never attain for all the efforts of his faithful slave, Van

[056-148] Deusen, the Denver haberdasher. To be properly up to those tricks, the doctor

[056-149] supposed, you had to learn them young. If he were to buy a silk hat that was

[056-150] the twin of Ottenburg’s, it would be shaggy in a week, and he could never

[056-151] carry it as Fred held his.

[056-152] Ottenburg had greeted Thea in German, and as she replied in the same language,

[056-153] Archie joined Mr. Landry at the window. “You know Mr. Ottenburg, he tells

[056-154] me?”

[056-155] Mr. Landry’s eyes twinkled. “Yes, I regularly follow him about,

[056-156] when he’s in town. I would, even if he didn’t send me such

[056-157] wonderful Christmas presents: Russian vodka by the half-dozen!”

[056-158] Thea called to them, “Come, Mr. Ottenburg is calling on all of us.

[056-159] Here’s the tea.”

[056-160] The maid opened the door and two waiters from downstairs appeared with covered

[056-161] trays. The tea-table was in the parlor. Thea drew Ottenburg with her and went

[056-162] to inspect it. “Where’s the rum? Oh, yes, in that thing! Everything

[056-163] seems to be here, but send up some currant preserves and cream cheese for Mr.

[056-164] Ottenburg. And in about fifteen minutes, bring some fresh toast. That’s

[056-165] all, thank you.”

[056-166] For the next few minutes there was a clatter of teacups and responses about

[056-167] sugar. “Landry always takes rum. I’m glad the rest of you

[056-168] don’t. I’m sure it’s bad.” Thea poured the tea standing

[056-169] and got through with it as quickly as possible, as if it were a refreshment

[056-170] snatched between trains. The tea-table and the little room in which it stood

[056-171] seemed to be out of scale with her long step, her long reach, and the energy of

[056-172] her movements. Dr. Archie, standing near her, was pleasantly aware of the

[056-173] animation of her figure. Under the clinging velvet, her body seemed independent

[056-174] and unsubdued.

[056-175] They drifted, with their plates and cups, back to the music-room. When Thea

[056-176] followed them, Ottenburg put down his tea suddenly. “Aren’t you

[056-177] taking anything? Please let me.” He started back to the table.

[056-178] “No, thank you, nothing. I’m going to run over that aria for you

[056-179] presently, to convince you that I can do it. How did the duet go, with

[056-180] Schlag?”

[056-181] She was standing in the doorway and Fred came up to her: “That

[056-182] you’ll never do any better. You’ve worked your voice into it

[056-183] perfectly. Every *nunance*—wonderful!”

[056-184] “Think so?” She gave him a sidelong glance and spoke with a certain

[056-185] gruff shyness which did not deceive anybody, and was not meant to deceive. The

[056-186] tone was equivalent to “Keep it up. I like it, but I’m awkward with

[056-187] it.”

[056-188] Fred held her by the door and did keep it up, furiously, for full five minutes.

[056-189] She took it with some confusion, seeming all the while to be hesitating, to be

[056-190] arrested in her course and trying to pass him. But she did not really try to

[056-191] pass, and her color deepened. Fred spoke in German, and Archie caught from her

[056-192] an occasional *Ja? So?* muttered rather than spoken.

[056-193] When they rejoined Landry and Dr. Archie, Fred took up his tea again. “I

[056-194] see you’re singing *Venus* Saturday night. Will they never let you

[056-195] have a chance at *Elizabeth?*”

[056-196] She shrugged her shoulders. “Not here. There are so many singers here,

[056-197] and they try us out in such a stingy way. Think of it, last year I came over in

[056-198] October, and it was the first of December before I went on at all! I’m

[056-199] often sorry I left [[dresden|Dresden]].”

[056-200] “Still,” Fred argued, “Dresden is limited.”

[056-201] “Just so, and I’ve begun to sigh for those very limitations. In New

[056-202] York everything is impersonal. Your audience never knows its own mind, and its

[056-203] mind is never twice the same. I’d rather sing where the people are

[056-204] pig-headed and throw carrots at you if you don’t do it the way they like

[056-205] it. The house here is splendid, and the night audiences are exciting. I hate

[056-206] the matinees; like singing at a *Kaffeklatsch*.” She rose and turned

[056-207] on the lights.

[056-208] “Ah!” Fred exclaimed, “why do you do that? That is a signal

[056-209] that tea is over.” He got up and drew out his gloves.

[056-210] “Not at all. Shall you be here Saturday night?” She sat down on the

[056-211] piano bench and leaned her elbow back on the keyboard. “Necker sings

[056-212] *Elizabeth*. Make Dr. Archie go. Everything she sings is worth

[056-213] hearing.”

[056-214] “But she’s failing so. The last time I heard her she had no voice

[056-215] at all. She *is* a poor vocalist!”

[056-216] Thea cut him off. “She’s a great artist, whether she’s in

[056-217] voice or not, and she’s the only one here. If you want a big voice, you

[056-218] can take my *Ortrude* of last night; that’s big enough, and vulgar

[056-219] enough.”

[056-220] Fred laughed and turned away, this time with decision. “I don’t

[056-221] want her!” he protested energetically. “I only wanted to get a rise

[056-222] out of you. I like Necker’s *Elizabeth* well enough. I like your

[056-223] *Venus* well enough, too.”

[056-224] “It’s a beautiful part, and it’s often dreadfully sung.

[056-225] It’s very hard to sing, of course.”

[056-226] Ottenburg bent over the hand she held out to him. “For an uninvited

[056-227] guest, I’ve fared very well. You were nice to let me come up. I’d

[056-228] have been terribly cut up if you’d sent me away. May I?” He kissed

[056-229] her hand lightly and backed toward the door, still smiling, and promising to

[056-230] keep an eye on Archie. “He can’t be trusted at all, Thea. One of

[056-231] the waiters at Martin’s worked a Tourainian hare off on him at luncheon

[056-232] yesterday, for seven twenty-five.”

[056-233] Thea broke into a laugh, the deep one he recognized. “Did he have a

[056-234] ribbon on, this hare? Did they bring him in a gilt cage?”

[056-235] “No,”—Archie spoke up for himself,—“they brought

[056-236] him in a brown sauce, which was very good. He didn’t taste very different

[056-237] from any rabbit.”

[056-238] “Probably came from a push-cart on the East Side.” Thea looked at

[056-239] her old friend commiseratingly. “Yes, *do* keep an eye on him, Fred.

[056-240] I had no idea,” shaking her head. “Yes, I’ll be obliged to

[056-241] you.”

[056-242] “Count on me!” Their eyes met in a gay smile, and Fred bowed

[056-243] himself out.
