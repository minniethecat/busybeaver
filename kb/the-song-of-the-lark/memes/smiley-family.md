## Overview

The Smiley family are proprietors of Mrs. Smiley's millinery and notion store in Moonstone, Colorado. The store serves as a gathering place for the town's women — a site of commerce, gossip, and community connection. Mrs. Smiley and her daughter Jenny Smiley are the named members of the family, and their establishment is one of the fixtures of Moonstone's modest commercial life, appearing at intervals throughout the novel as a backdrop to the town's social world.

## Biography / Character Arc

### Mrs. Smiley's Millinery Store

Mrs. Smiley's millinery and notion store is a Moonstone institution [005-096]. It is where the town's women gather — to shop for hats and ribbons, certainly, but also to talk. The store functions as an informal social club, a site where "Moonstone gossips assembled" [012-183] to exchange news and opinions.

The store's centrality to the town's female social life persists throughout the novel. In Chapter 51, decades after Thea has left Moonstone, it is noted that Mrs. Kronborg's "old cronies at Mrs. Smiley's millinery store said" [051-135] — the store remaining, across the years, the place where Moonstone's women gather and talk.

### Jenny Smiley

Jenny Smiley is Mrs. Smiley's daughter and a contemporary of Thea Kronborg. She is known as a reliable copier of information: "Jenny Smiley'll" copy things down [003-095], suggesting she is a diligent, perhaps slightly officious young woman. Thea remarks irritably that "Jenny Smiley is the only one" [015-038] — the context suggesting Jenny is held up as an example of conventional propriety against which Thea chafes. Like many of Thea's Moonstone contemporaries, Jenny represents the conventional life that Thea is destined to leave behind.

## Relationships

### The Women of Moonstone

The Smileys' store is the de facto gathering place for Moonstone's women — Mrs. Kronborg's "old cronies" [051-135], the gossips [012-183], and the general female population of the town. Mrs. Smiley, as proprietress, occupies a central position in this social network, facilitating the exchanges of news and opinion that constitute the town's informal public sphere.

### Thea Kronborg

Thea's primary connection to the Smileys is through Moonstone's social texture. Jenny Smiley is a contemporary whose conventionality contrasts with Thea's unconventionality. Thea's irritation at the mention of Jenny [015-038] registers her impatience with the standards by which Moonstone judges its young women.

## Significance

The Smiley family and their millinery store represent the commercial and social infrastructure of small-town life that Cather depicts with ethnographic precision. The store is not merely a business; it is a social institution — a place where information circulates, reputations are made and unmade, and the female half of the community maintains its connections. The Smileys' persistence across the novel's long time span (from Thea's childhood to her mother's old age) marks the continuity of Moonstone life, an enduring backdrop against which Thea's dramatic departure and ascent are measured.
