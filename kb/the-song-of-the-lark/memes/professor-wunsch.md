# Professor Wunsch

## Overview

Professor Wunsch is a figure of tragic grandeur in *The Song of the Lark*—a German musician whose career was destroyed by alcoholism, who has washed up in the small Colorado town of Moonstone, and who, despite his personal ruin, possesses the discernment to recognize Thea Kronborg's extraordinary musical gift. He is the novel's first portrait of the artist broken by life, serving as both inspiration and warning for the young Thea (002-144).

## Biography

Wunsch's past is sketched in fragments. He "had taught in music schools in St. Louis and Kansas City" before drink undid his professional prospects (004-226). Now he teaches piano to the children of Moonstone, including Thea, in whom he sees something he has not encountered before—a talent that transcends the ordinary capacities of even gifted students.

His recognition of Thea is conveyed in one of the novel's most significant early scenes: he tells the Kronborg family that their daughter possesses exceptional musical ability and must be given the opportunity for serious training. This verdict, coming from a man who carries the authority of European musical tradition, changes the course of Thea's life.

## The Broken Artist

Wunsch embodies the novel's understanding of the artist's vulnerability. His own gift was real but insufficiently protected—by character, by circumstance, by the lack of the kind of support system that later sustains Thea. His alcoholism is presented not as moral failure but as a kind of affliction, the dark side of the sensitivity that makes artistic perception possible.

Yet Wunsch's fall does not invalidate his teaching. The novel insists that even a broken artist can transmit something essential—that the tradition is passed on not only by the successful but also by the wounded, whose recognition of talent in the young may be all the more acute for their own failure to fulfill it.

## Legacy

Wunsch's legacy is Thea herself. The techniques he taught her, the standards he instilled, the recognition he offered—these become part of the foundation on which her later development rests. Though he disappears from the narrative after the early sections, his influence persists as a kind of benediction: the first voice to say, with authority, that this child is meant for something greater.
