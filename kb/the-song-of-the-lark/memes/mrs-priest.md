## Overview

Mrs. Katharine Priest is a singer in Chicago and a student of Madison Bowers, who becomes a professional rival to Thea Kronborg during Part 3 of *The Song of the Lark*. She is characterized by her chronic lateness, her showy but mannered vocal style, and the mixture of admiration and contempt she evokes in Thea. Mrs. Priest represents a certain type of accomplished but undisciplined performer — talented enough to command attention, but lacking the artistic integrity that Thea is beginning to recognize as essential.

## Biography / Character Arc

### A Bowers Student

Mrs. Priest studies voice with Madison Bowers, the same Chicago teacher under whom Thea works as an accompanist. Her lessons are marked by her consistent tardiness: "Mrs. Priest is late again," Bowers remarks dryly as he closes his desk [032-111]. When Thea plays for Mrs. Priest, Bowers instructs her: "remember not to frown when you are playing for Mrs. Priest. You are accompanying, not criticizing" [032-112], acknowledging that Thea's musical standards already exceed what Mrs. Priest can deliver.

### Vocal Style and Mannerisms

Mrs. Priest possesses a "fine, big, showy voice and an extensive repertoire" [033-031], but her singing is undercut by affectation. Bowers notes "that is a mannerism of Mrs. Priest's" [032-116], indicating that her vocal habits are recognized as flaws even by her teacher. During the lesson, "Bowers made no comment on Mrs. Priest's vehemence at all, he handled her roughly; poked and hammered her shoulders" [032-145], treating her as a difficult instrument rather than an artist.

### Thea's Evolving Judgment

When Thea first heard Mrs. Priest sing in church, she admired her [032-151]. But as Thea's own standards develop, her admiration turns to contempt: "She felt that Mrs. Priest ought to be reproved and even punished for her shortcomings" [032-153]. However, "looks of reproof were lost upon Mrs. Priest; although the lady did murmur one day when Thea pushed her — 'You are very exacting, Miss Kronborg'" [032-157]. This exchange captures the tension between Thea's growing artistic rigor and the complacency of the established singers around her.

### Place in the Chicago Musical Scene

Mrs. Priest occupies a secure position in the Chicago musical world. She is slated to sing in productions alongside other local singers, including "that new woman from Detroit. And Mrs. Priest will be in this company" [034-031]. Fred Ottenburg dismisses her entirely — "don't count that stupid Priest woman" [035-242] — reflecting how the more discerning musical patrons view her as a competent but uninspired performer.

## Relationships

### Madison Bowers
Mrs. Priest's voice teacher, who tolerates her mannerisms and lateness with professional resignation. Bowers treats her roughly during lessons, suggesting a transactional relationship rather than genuine artistic mentorship [032-145].

### Thea Kronborg
Thea serves as Mrs. Priest's accompanist at Bowers's studio, a subordinate position that gives Thea an intimate view of Mrs. Priest's vocal flaws. Thea develops contempt for her, feeling that Mrs. Priest "ought to be reproved" [032-153]. Mrs. Priest, for her part, finds Thea "very exacting" [032-157] — a criticism that Thea likely wears as a badge of honor.

### Jessie Darcey
Mrs. Priest is often mentioned alongside Jessie Darcey, another Bowers student. The two are associated as examples of the same musical type — accomplished sopranos who lack the deeper artistic commitment that Thea embodies. Bowers groups them together when complaining about his students: "Katharine Priest and Miles Murdstone, are worst of all" [032-216].

## Significance

Mrs. Priest functions in the novel as a foil to Thea Kronborg. Where Thea is conscientious, self-critical, and driven by an inner artistic standard, Mrs. Priest is late, mannered, and satisfied with surface-level accomplishment. Her "fine, big, showy voice" [033-031] represents the kind of conventional talent that can succeed commercially without the transformative artistic vision Thea is developing. For Thea, encountering Mrs. Priest is an education in what she does not want to become — a lesson as important as any technical instruction she receives. The contempt Thea feels for Mrs. Priest is also a marker of Thea's own artistic coming-of-age: the ability to distinguish between mere facility and genuine artistry.
