## VI

[043-001] Day was breaking over [[panther-canyon|Panther Canyon]]. The gulf was cold and full of heavy,

[043-002] purplish twilight. The wood smoke which drifted from one of the cliff-houses

[043-003] hung in a blue scarf across the chasm, until the draft caught it and whirled it

[043-004] away. Thea was crouching in the doorway of her rock house, while Ottenburg

[043-005] looked after the crackling fire in the next cave. He was waiting for it to burn

[043-006] down to coals before he put the coffee on to boil.

[043-007] They had left the ranch house that morning a little after three o’clock,

[043-008] having packed their camp equipment the day before, and had crossed the open

[043-009] pasture land with their lantern while the stars were still bright. During the

[043-010] descent into the canyon by lantern-light, they were chilled through their coats

[043-011] and sweaters. The lantern crept slowly along the rock trail, where the heavy

[043-012] air seemed to offer resistance. The voice of the stream at the bottom of the

[043-013] gorge was hollow and threatening, much louder and deeper than it ever was by

[043-014] day—another voice altogether. The sullenness of the place seemed to say

[043-015] that the world could get on very well without people, red or white; that under

[043-016] the human world there was a geological world, conducting its silent, immense

[043-017] operations which were indifferent to man. Thea had often seen the desert

[043-018] sunrise,—a lighthearted affair, where the sun springs out of bed and the

[043-019] world is golden in an instant. But this canyon seemed to waken like an old man,

[043-020] with rheum and stiffness of the joints, with heaviness, and a dull, malignant

[043-021] mind. She crouched against the wall while the stars faded, and thought what

[043-022] courage the early races must have had to endure so much for the little they got

[043-023] out of life.

[043-024] At last a kind of hopefulness broke in the air. In a moment the pine trees up

[043-025] on the edge of the rim were flashing with coppery fire. The thin red clouds

[043-026] which hung above their pointed tops began to boil and move rapidly, weaving in

[043-027] and out like smoke. The swallows darted out of their rock houses as at a

[043-028] signal, and flew upward, toward the rim. Little brown birds began to chirp in

[043-029] the bushes along the watercourse down at the bottom of the ravine, where

[043-030] everything was still dusky and pale. At first the golden light seemed to hang

[043-031] like a wave upon the rim of the canyon; the trees and bushes up there, which

[043-032] one scarcely noticed at noon, stood out magnified by the slanting rays. Long,

[043-033] thin streaks of light began to reach quiveringly down into the canyon. The red

[043-034] sun rose rapidly above the tops of the blazing pines, and its glow burst into

[043-035] the gulf, about the very doorstep on which Thea sat. It bored into the wet,

[043-036] dark underbrush. The dripping cherry bushes, the pale aspens, and the frosty

[043-037] *piñons* were glittering and trembling, swimming in the liquid gold. All

[043-038] the pale, dusty little herbs of the bean family, never seen by any one but a

[043-039] botanist, became for a moment individual and important, their silky leaves

[043-040] quite beautiful with dew and light. The arch of sky overhead, heavy as lead a

[043-041] little while before, lifted, became more and more transparent, and one could

[043-042] look up into depths of pearly blue.

[043-043] The savor of coffee and bacon mingled with the smell of wet cedars drying, and

[043-044] Fred called to Thea that he was ready for her. They sat down in the doorway of

[043-045] his kitchen, with the warmth of the live coals behind them and the sunlight on

[043-046] their faces, and began their breakfast, Mrs. Biltmer’s thick coffee cups

[043-047] and the cream bottle between them, the coffee-pot and frying-pan conveniently

[043-048] keeping hot among the embers.

[043-049] “I thought you were going back on the whole proposition, Thea, when you

[043-050] were crawling along with that lantern. I couldn’t get a word out of

[043-051] you.”

[043-052] “I know. I was cold and hungry, and I didn’t believe there was

[043-053] going to be any morning, anyway. Didn’t you feel queer, at all?”

[043-054] Fred squinted above his smoking cup. “Well, I am never strong for getting

[043-055] up before the sun. The world looks unfurnished. When I first lit the fire and

[043-056] had a square look at you, I thought I’d got the wrong girl. Pale,

[043-057] grim—you were a sight!”

[043-058] Thea leaned back into the shadow of the rock room and warmed her hands over the

[043-059] coals. “It was dismal enough. How warm these walls are, all the way

[043-060] round; and your breakfast is so good. I’m all right now, Fred.”

[043-061] “Yes, you’re all right now.” Fred lit a cigarette and looked

[043-062] at her critically as her head emerged into the sun again. “You get up

[043-063] every morning just a little bit handsomer than you were the day before.

[043-064] I’d love you just as much if you were not turning into one of the

[043-065] loveliest women I’ve ever seen; but you are, and that’s a fact to

[043-066] be reckoned with.” He watched her across the thin line of smoke he blew

[043-067] from his lips. “What are you going to do with all that beauty and all

[043-068] that talent, Miss Kronborg?”

[043-069] She turned away to the fire again. “I don’t know what you’re

[043-070] talking about,” she muttered with an awkwardness which did not conceal

[043-071] her pleasure.

[043-072] Ottenburg laughed softly. “Oh, yes, you do! Nobody better! You’re a

[043-073] close one, but you give yourself away sometimes, like everybody else. Do you

[043-074] know, I’ve decided that you never do a single thing without an ulterior

[043-075] motive.” He threw away his cigarette, took out his tobacco-pouch and

[043-076] began to fill his pipe. “You ride and fence and walk and climb, but I

[043-077] know that all the while you’re getting somewhere in your mind. All these

[043-078] things are instruments; and I, too, am an instrument.” He looked up in

[043-079] time to intercept a quick, startled glance from Thea. “Oh, I don’t

[043-080] mind,” he chuckled; “not a bit. Every woman, every interesting

[043-081] woman, has ulterior motives, many of ’em less creditable than yours.

[043-082] It’s your constancy that amuses me. You must have been doing it ever

[043-083] since you were two feet high.”

[043-084] Thea looked slowly up at her companion’s good-humored face. His eyes,

[043-085] sometimes too restless and sympathetic in town, had grown steadier and clearer

[043-086] in the open air. His short curly beard and yellow hair had reddened in the sun

[043-087] and wind. The pleasant vigor of his person was always delightful to her,

[043-088] something to signal to and laugh with in a world of negative people. With Fred

[043-089] she was never becalmed. There was always life in the air, always something

[043-090] coming and going, a rhythm of feeling and action,—stronger than the

[043-091] natural accord of youth. As she looked at him, leaning against the sunny wall,

[043-092] she felt a desire to be frank with him. She was not willfully holding anything

[043-093] back. But, on the other hand, she could not force things that held themselves

[043-094] back. “Yes, it was like that when I was little,” she said at last.

[043-095] “I had to be close, as you call it, or go under. But I didn’t know

[043-096] I had been like that since you came. I’ve had nothing to be close about.

[043-097] I haven’t thought about anything but having a good time with you.

[043-098] I’ve just drifted.”

[043-099] Fred blew a trail of smoke out into the breeze and looked knowing. “Yes,

[043-100] you drift like a rifle ball, my dear. It’s your—your direction that

[043-101] I like best of all. Most fellows wouldn’t, you know. I’m

[043-102] unusual.”

[043-103] They both laughed, but Thea frowned questioningly. “Why wouldn’t

[043-104] most fellows? Other fellows have liked me.”

[043-105] “Yes, serious fellows. You told me yourself they were all old, or solemn.

[043-106] But jolly fellows want to be the whole target. They would say you were all

[043-107] brain and muscle; that you have no feeling.”

[043-108] She glanced at him sidewise. “Oh, they would, would they?”

[043-109] “Of course they would,” Fred continued blandly. “Jolly

[043-110] fellows have no imagination. They want to be the animating force. When they are

[043-111] not around, they want a girl to be—extinct,” he waved his hand.

[043-112] “Old fellows like Mr. Nathanmeyer understand your kind; but among the

[043-113] young ones, you are rather lucky to have found me. Even I wasn’t always

[043-114] so wise. I’ve had my time of thinking it would not bore me to be the

[043-115] Apollo of a homey flat, and I’ve paid out a trifle to learn better. All

[043-116] those things get very tedious unless they are hooked up with an idea of some

[043-117] sort. It’s because we *don’t* come out here only to look at

[043-118] each other and drink coffee that it’s so pleasant to—look at each

[043-119] other.” Fred drew on his pipe for a while, studying Thea’s

[043-120] abstraction. She was staring up at the far wall of the canyon with a troubled

[043-121] expression that drew her eyes narrow and her mouth hard. Her hands lay in her

[043-122] lap, one over the other, the fingers interlacing. “Suppose,” Fred

[043-123] came out at length,—“suppose I were to offer you what most of the

[043-124] young men I know would offer a girl they’d been sitting up nights about:

[043-125] a comfortable flat in [[chicago|Chicago]], a summer camp up in the woods, musical evenings,

[043-126] and a family to bring up. Would it look attractive to you?”

[043-127] Thea sat up straight and stared at him in alarm, glared into his eyes.

[043-128] “Perfectly hideous!” she exclaimed.

[043-129] Fred dropped back against the old stonework and laughed deep in his chest.

[043-130] “Well, don’t be frightened. I won’t offer them. You’re

[043-131] not a nest-building bird. You know I always liked your song, ‘Me for the

[043-132] jolt of the breakers!’ I understand.”

[043-133] She rose impatiently and walked to the edge of the cliff. “It’s not

[043-134] that so much. It’s waking up every morning with the feeling that your

[043-135] life is your own, and your strength is your own, and your talent is your own;

[043-136] that you’re all there, and there’s no sag in you.” She stood

[043-137] for a moment as if she were tortured by uncertainty, then turned suddenly back

[043-138] to him. “Don’t talk about these things any more now,” she

[043-139] entreated. “It isn’t that I want to keep anything from you. The

[043-140] trouble is that I’ve got nothing to keep—except (you know as well

[043-141] as I) that feeling. I told you about it in Chicago once. But it always makes me

[043-142] unhappy to talk about it. It will spoil the day. Will you go for a climb with

[043-143] me?” She held out her hands with a smile so eager that it made Ottenburg

[043-144] feel how much she needed to get away from herself.

[043-145] He sprang up and caught the hands she put out so cordially, and stood swinging

[043-146] them back and forth. “I won’t tease you. A word’s enough to

[043-147] me. But I love it, all the same. Understand?” He pressed her hands and

[043-148] dropped them. “Now, where are you going to drag me?”

[043-149] “I want you to drag me. Over there, to the other houses. They are more

[043-150] interesting than these.” She pointed across the gorge to the row of white

[043-151] houses in the other cliff. “The trail is broken away, but I got up there

[043-152] once. It’s possible. You have to go to the bottom of the canyon, cross

[043-153] the creek, and then go up hand-over-hand.”

[043-154] Ottenburg, lounging against the sunny wall, his hands in the pockets of his

[043-155] jacket, looked across at the distant dwellings. “It’s an awful

[043-156] climb,” he sighed, “when I could be perfectly happy here with my

[043-157] pipe. However—” He took up his stick and hat and followed Thea down

[043-158] the water trail. “Do you climb this path every day? You surely earn your

[043-159] bath. I went down and had a look at your pool the other afternoon. Neat place,

[043-160] with all those little cottonwoods. Must be very becoming.”

[043-161] “Think so?” Thea said over her shoulder, as she swung round a turn.

[043-162] “Yes, and so do you, evidently. I’m becoming expert at reading your

[043-163] meaning in your back. I’m behind you so much on these single-foot trails.

[043-164] You don’t wear stays, do you?”

[043-165] “Not here.”

[043-166] “I wouldn’t, anywhere, if I were you. They will make you less

[043-167] elastic. The side muscles get flabby. If you go in for opera, there’s a

[043-168] fortune in a flexible body. Most of the German singers are clumsy, even when

[043-169] they’re well set up.”

[043-170] Thea switched a *piñon* branch back at him. “Oh, I’ll never

[043-171] get fat! That I can promise you.”

[043-172] Fred smiled, looking after her. “Keep that promise, no matter how many

[043-173] others you break,” he drawled.

[043-174] The upward climb, after they had crossed the stream, was at first a breathless

[043-175] scramble through underbrush. When they reached the big boulders, Ottenburg went

[043-176] first because he had the longer leg-reach, and gave Thea a hand when the step

[043-177] was quite beyond her, swinging her up until she could get a foothold. At last

[043-178] they reached a little platform among the rocks, with only a hundred feet of

[043-179] jagged, sloping wall between them and the cliff-houses.

[043-180] Ottenburg lay down under a pine tree and declared that he was going to have a

[043-181] pipe before he went any farther. “It’s a good thing to know when to

[043-182] stop, Thea,” he said meaningly.

[043-183] “I’m not going to stop now until I get there,” Thea insisted.

[043-184] “I’ll go on alone.”

[043-185] Fred settled his shoulder against the tree-trunk. “Go on if you like, but

[043-186] I’m here to enjoy myself. If you meet a rattler on the way, have it out

[043-187] with him.”

[043-188] She hesitated, fanning herself with her felt hat. “I never have met

[043-189] one.”

[043-190] “There’s reasoning for you,” Fred murmured languidly.

[043-191] Thea turned away resolutely and began to go up the wall, using an irregular

[043-192] cleft in the rock for a path. The cliff, which looked almost perpendicular from

[043-193] the bottom, was really made up of ledges and boulders, and behind these she

[043-194] soon disappeared. For a long while Fred smoked with half-closed eyes, smiling

[043-195] to himself now and again. Occasionally he lifted an eyebrow as he heard the

[043-196] rattle of small stones among the rocks above. “In a temper,” he

[043-197] concluded; “do her good.” Then he subsided into warm drowsiness and

[043-198] listened to the locusts in the yuccas, and the tap-tap of the old woodpecker

[043-199] that was never weary of assaulting the big pine.

[043-200] Fred had finished his pipe and was wondering whether he wanted another, when he

[043-201] heard a call from the cliff far above him. Looking up, he saw Thea standing on

[043-202] the edge of a projecting crag. She waved to him and threw her arm over her

[043-203] head, as if she were snapping her fingers in the air.

[043-204] As he saw her there between the sky and the gulf, with that great wash of air

[043-205] and the morning light about her, Fred recalled the brilliant figure at Mrs.

[043-206] Nathanmeyer’s. Thea was one of those people who emerge, unexpectedly,

[043-207] larger than we are accustomed to see them. Even at this distance one got the

[043-208] impression of muscular energy and audacity,—a kind of brilliancy of

[043-209] motion,—of a personality that carried across big spaces and expanded

[043-210] among big things. Lying still, with his hands under his head, Ottenburg

[043-211] rhetorically addressed the figure in the air. “You are the sort that used

[043-212] to run wild in Germany, dressed in their hair and a piece of skin. Soldiers

[043-213] caught ’em in nets. Old Nathanmeyer,” he mused, “would like a

[043-214] peep at her now. Knowing old fellow. Always buying those Zorn etchings of

[043-215] peasant girls bathing. No sag in them either. Must be the cold climate.”

[043-216] He sat up. “She’ll begin to pitch rocks on me if I don’t

[043-217] move.” In response to another impatient gesture from the crag, he rose

[043-218] and began swinging slowly up the trail.

[043-219] It was the afternoon of that long day. Thea was lying on a blanket in the door

[043-220] of her rock house. She and Ottenburg had come back from their climb and had

[043-221] lunch, and he had gone off for a nap in one of the cliff-houses farther down

[043-222] the path. He was sleeping peacefully, his coat under his head and his face

[043-223] turned toward the wall.

[043-224] Thea, too, was drowsy, and lay looking through halfclosed eyes up at the

[043-225] blazing blue arch over the rim of the canyon. She was thinking of nothing at

[043-226] all. Her mind, like her body, was full of warmth, lassitude, physical content.

[043-227] Suddenly an eagle, tawny and of great size, sailed over the cleft in which she

[043-228] lay, across the arch of sky. He dropped for a moment into the gulf between the

[043-229] walls, then wheeled, and mounted until his plumage was so steeped in light that

[043-230] he looked like a golden bird. He swept on, following the course of the canyon a

[043-231] little way and then disappearing beyond the rim. Thea sprang to her feet as if

[043-232] she had been thrown up from the rock by volcanic action. She stood rigid on the

[043-233] edge of the stone shelf, straining her eyes after that strong, tawny flight. O

[043-234] eagle of eagles! Endeavor, achievement, desire, glorious striving of human art!

[043-235] From a cleft in the heart of the world she saluted it...It had come all the

[043-236] way; when men lived in caves, it was there. A vanished race; but along the

[043-237] trails, in the stream, under the spreading cactus, there still glittered in the

[043-238] sun the bits of their frail clay vessels, fragments of their desire.
