## I

[001-001] Dr. Howard Archie had just come up from a game of pool with the Jewish clothier

[001-002] and two traveling men who happened to be staying overnight in Moonstone. His

[001-003] offices were in the Duke Block, over the drug store. Larry, the doctor’s

[001-004] man, had lit the overhead light in the waiting-room and the double

[001-005] student’s lamp on the desk in the study. The isinglass sides of the

[001-006] hard-coal burner were aglow, and the air in the study was so hot that as he

[001-007] came in the doctor opened the door into his little operating-room, where there

[001-008] was no stove. The waiting room was carpeted and stiffly furnished, something

[001-009] like a country parlor. The study had worn, unpainted floors, but there was a

[001-010] look of winter comfort about it. The doctor’s flat-top desk was large and

[001-011] well made; the papers were in orderly piles, under glass weights. Behind the

[001-012] stove a wide bookcase, with double glass doors, reached from the floor to the

[001-013] ceiling. It was filled with medical books of every thickness and color. On the

[001-014] top shelf stood a long row of thirty or forty volumes, bound all alike in dark

[001-015] mottled board covers, with imitation leather backs.

[001-016] As the doctor in New England villages is proverbially old, so the doctor in

[001-017] small Colorado towns twenty-five years ago was generally young. [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] was

[001-018] barely thirty. He was tall, with massive shoulders which he held stiffly, and a

[001-019] large, well-shaped head. He was a distinguished-looking man, for that part of

[001-020] the world, at least.

[001-021] There was something individual in the way in which his reddish-brown hair,

[001-022] parted cleanly at the side, bushed over his high forehead. His nose was

[001-023] straight and thick, and his eyes were intelligent. He wore a curly, reddish

[001-024] mustache and an imperial, cut trimly, which made him look a little like the

[001-025] pictures of Napoleon III. His hands were large and well kept, but ruggedly

[001-026] formed, and the backs were shaded with crinkly reddish hair. He wore a blue

[001-027] suit of woolly, wide-waled serge; the traveling men had known at a glance that

[001-028] it was made by a Denver tailor. The doctor was always well dressed.

[001-029] Dr. Archie turned up the student’s lamp and sat down in the swivel chair

[001-030] before his desk. He sat uneasily, beating a tattoo on his knees with his

[001-031] fingers, and looked about him as if he were bored. He glanced at his watch,

[001-032] then absently took from his pocket a bunch of small keys, selected one and

[001-033] looked at it. A contemptuous smile, barely perceptible, played on his lips, but

[001-034] his eyes remained meditative. Behind the door that led into the hall, under his

[001-035] buffalo-skin driving-coat, was a locked cupboard. This the doctor opened

[001-036] mechanically, kicking aside a pile of muddy overshoes. Inside, on the shelves,

[001-037] were whiskey glasses and decanters, lemons, sugar, and bitters. Hearing a step

[001-038] in the empty, echoing hall without, the doctor closed the cupboard again,

[001-039] snapping the Yale lock. The door of the waiting-room opened, a man entered and

[001-040] came on into the consulting-room.

[001-041] “Good-evening, Mr. Kronborg,” said the doctor carelessly.

[001-042] “Sit down.”

[001-043] His visitor was a tall, loosely built man, with a thin brown beard, streaked

[001-044] with gray. He wore a frock coat, a broad-brimmed black hat, a white lawn

[001-045] necktie, and steel rimmed spectacles. Altogether there was a pretentious and

[001-046] important air about him, as he lifted the skirts of his coat and sat down.

[001-047] “Good-evening, doctor. Can you step around to the house with me? I think

[001-048] [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] will need you this evening.” This was said with profound

[001-049] gravity and, curiously enough, with a slight embarrassment.

[001-050] “Any hurry?” the doctor asked over his shoulder as he went into his

[001-051] operating-room.

[001-052] Mr. Kronborg coughed behind his hand, and contracted his brows. His face

[001-053] threatened at every moment to break into a smile of foolish excitement. He

[001-054] controlled it only by calling upon his habitual pulpit manner. “Well, I

[001-055] think it would be as well to go immediately. Mrs. Kronborg will be more

[001-056] comfortable if you are there. She has been suffering for some time.”

[001-057] The doctor came back and threw a black bag upon his desk. He wrote some

[001-058] instructions for his man on a prescription pad and then drew on his overcoat.

[001-059] “All ready,” he announced, putting out his lamp. Mr. Kronborg rose

[001-060] and they tramped through the empty hall and down the stairway to the street.

[001-061] The drug store below was dark, and the saloon next door was just closing. Every

[001-062] other light on Main Street was out.

[001-063] On either side of the road and at the outer edge of the board sidewalk, the

[001-064] snow had been shoveled into breastworks. The town looked small and black,

[001-065] flattened down in the snow, muffled and all but extinguished. Overhead the

[001-066] stars shone gloriously. It was impossible not to notice them. The air was so

[001-067] clear that the white sand hills to the east of Moonstone gleamed softly.

[001-068] Following the Reverend Mr. Kronborg along the narrow walk, past the little

[001-069] dark, sleeping houses, the doctor looked up at the flashing night and whistled

[001-070] softly. It did seem that people were stupider than they need be; as if on a

[001-071] night like this there ought to be something better to do than to sleep nine

[001-072] hours, or to assist Mrs. Kronborg in functions which she could have performed

[001-073] so admirably unaided. He wished he had gone down to Denver to hear Fay

[001-074] Templeton sing “See-Saw.” Then he remembered that he had a personal

[001-075] interest in this family, after all. They turned into another street and saw

[001-076] before them lighted windows; a low story-and-a-half house, with a wing built on

[001-077] at the right and a kitchen addition at the back, everything a little on the

[001-078] slant—roofs, windows, and doors. As they approached the gate, Peter

[001-079] Kronborg’s pace grew brisker. His nervous, ministerial cough annoyed the

[001-080] doctor. “Exactly as if he were going to give out a text,” he

[001-081] thought. He drew off his glove and felt in his vest pocket. “Have a

[001-082] troche, Kronborg,” he said, producing some. “Sent me for samples.

[001-083] Very good for a rough throat.”

[001-084] “Ah, thank you, thank you. I was in something of a hurry. I neglected to

[001-085] put on my overshoes. Here we are, doctor.” Kronborg opened his front

[001-086] door—seemed delighted to be at home again.

[001-087] The front hall was dark and cold; the hatrack was hung with an astonishing

[001-088] number of children’s hats and caps and cloaks. They were even piled on

[001-089] the table beneath the hatrack. Under the table was a heap of rubbers and

[001-090] overshoes. While the doctor hung up his coat and hat, Peter Kronborg opened the

[001-091] door into the living-room. A glare of light greeted them, and a rush of hot,

[001-092] stale air, smelling of warming flannels.

[001-093] At three o’clock in the morning Dr. Archie was in the parlor putting on

[001-094] his cuffs and coat—there was no spare bedroom in that house. Peter

[001-095] Kronborg’s seventh child, a boy, was being soothed and cosseted by his

[001-096] aunt, Mrs. Kronborg was asleep, and the doctor was going home. But he wanted

[001-097] first to speak to Kronborg, who, coatless and fluttery, was pouring coal into

[001-098] the kitchen stove. As the doctor crossed the dining-room he paused and

[001-099] listened. From one of the wing rooms, off to the left, he heard rapid,

[001-100] distressed breathing. He went to the kitchen door.

[001-101] “One of the children sick in there?” he asked, nodding toward the

[001-102] partition.

[001-103] Kronborg hung up the stove-lifter and dusted his fingers. “It must be

[001-104] Thea. I meant to ask you to look at her. She has a croupy cold. But in my

[001-105] excitement—Mrs. Kronborg is doing finely, eh, doctor? Not many of your

[001-106] patients with such a constitution, I expect.”

[001-107] “Oh, yes. She’s a fine mother.” The doctor took up the lamp

[001-108] from the kitchen table and unceremoniously went into the wing room. Two chubby

[001-109] little boys were asleep in a double bed, with the coverlids over their noses

[001-110] and their feet drawn up. In a single bed, next to theirs, lay a little girl of

[001-111] eleven, wide awake, two yellow braids sticking up on the pillow behind her. Her

[001-112] face was scarlet and her eyes were blazing.

[001-113] The doctor shut the door behind him. “Feel pretty sick, Thea?” he

[001-114] asked as he took out his thermometer. “Why didn’t you call

[001-115] somebody?”

[001-116] She looked at him with greedy affection. “I thought you were here,”

[001-117] she spoke between quick breaths. “There is a new baby, isn’t there?

[001-118] Which?”

[001-119] “Which?” repeated the doctor.

[001-120] “Brother or sister?”

[001-121] He smiled and sat down on the edge of the bed. “Brother,” he said,

[001-122] taking her hand. “Open.”

[001-123] “Good. Brothers are better,” she murmured as he put the glass tube

[001-124] under her tongue.

[001-125] “Now, be still, I want to count.” Dr. Archie reached for her hand

[001-126] and took out his watch. When he put her hand back under the quilt he went over

[001-127] to one of the windows—they were both tight shut—and lifted it a

[001-128] little way. He reached up and ran his hand along the cold, unpapered wall.

[001-129] “Keep under the covers; I’ll come back to you in a moment,”

[001-130] he said, bending over the glass lamp with his thermometer. He winked at her

[001-131] from the door before he shut it.

[001-132] Peter Kronborg was sitting in his wife’s room, holding the bundle which

[001-133] contained his son. His air of cheerful importance, his beard and glasses, even

[001-134] his shirt-sleeves, annoyed the doctor. He beckoned Kronborg into the

[001-135] living-room and said sternly:—

[001-136] “You’ve got a very sick child in there. Why didn’t you call

[001-137] me before? It’s pneumonia, and she must have been sick for several days.

[001-138] Put the baby down somewhere, please, and help me make up the bed-lounge here in

[001-139] the parlor. She’s got to be in a warm room, and she’s got to be

[001-140] quiet. You must keep the other children out. Here, this thing opens up, I

[001-141] see,” swinging back the top of the carpet lounge. “We can lift her

[001-142] mattress and carry her in just as she is. I don’t want to disturb her

[001-143] more than is necessary.”

[001-144] Kronborg was all concern immediately. The two men took up the mattress and

[001-145] carried the sick child into the parlor. “I’ll have to go down to my

[001-146] office to get some medicine, Kronborg. The drug store won’t be open. Keep

[001-147] the covers on her. I won’t be gone long. Shake down the stove and put on

[001-148] a little coal, but not too much; so it’ll catch quickly, I mean. Find an

[001-149] old sheet for me, and put it there to warm.”

[001-150] The doctor caught his coat and hurried out into the dark street. Nobody was

[001-151] stirring yet, and the cold was bitter. He was tired and hungry and in no mild

[001-152] humor. “The idea!” he muttered; “to be such an ass at his

[001-153] age, about the seventh! And to feel no responsibility about the little girl.

[001-154] Silly old goat! The baby would have got into the world somehow; they always do.

[001-155] But a nice little girl like that—she’s worth the whole litter.

[001-156] Where she ever got it from—” He turned into the Duke Block and ran

[001-157] up the stairs to his office.

[001-158] [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]], meanwhile, was wondering why she happened to be in the parlor,

[001-159] where nobody but company—usually visiting preachers—ever slept. She

[001-160] had moments of stupor when she did not see anything, and moments of excitement

[001-161] when she felt that something unusual and pleasant was about to happen, when she

[001-162] saw everything clearly in the red light from the isinglass sides of the

[001-163] hard-coal burner—the nickel trimmings on the stove itself, the pictures

[001-164] on the wall, which she thought very beautiful, the flowers on the Brussels

[001-165] carpet, Czerny’s “Daily Studies” which stood open on the

[001-166] upright piano. She forgot, for the time being, all about the new baby.

[001-167] When she heard the front door open, it occurred to her that the pleasant thing

[001-168] which was going to happen was Dr. Archie himself. He came in and warmed his

[001-169] hands at the stove. As he turned to her, she threw herself wearily toward him,

[001-170] half out of her bed. She would have tumbled to the floor had he not caught her.

[001-171] He gave her some medicine and went to the kitchen for something he needed. She

[001-172] drowsed and lost the sense of his being there. When she opened her eyes again,

[001-173] he was kneeling before the stove, spreading something dark and sticky on a

[001-174] white cloth, with a big spoon; batter, perhaps. Presently she felt him taking

[001-175] off her nightgown. He wrapped the hot plaster about her chest. There seemed to

[001-176] be straps which he pinned over her shoulders. Then he took out a thread and

[001-177] needle and began to sew her up in it. That, she felt, was too strange; she must

[001-178] be dreaming anyhow, so she succumbed to her drowsiness.

[001-179] Thea had been moaning with every breath since the doctor came back, but she did

[001-180] not know it. She did not realize that she was suffering pain. When she was

[001-181] conscious at all, she seemed to be separated from her body; to be perched on

[001-182] top of the piano, or on the hanging lamp, watching the doctor sew her up. It

[001-183] was perplexing and unsatisfactory, like dreaming. She wished she could waken up

[001-184] and see what was going on.

[001-185] The doctor thanked God that he had persuaded Peter Kronborg to keep out of the

[001-186] way. He could do better by the child if he had her to himself. He had no

[001-187] children of his own. His marriage was a very unhappy one. As he lifted and

[001-188] undressed Thea, he thought to himself what a beautiful thing a little

[001-189] girl’s body was,—like a flower. It was so neatly and delicately

[001-190] fashioned, so soft, and so milky white. Thea must have got her hair and her

[001-191] silky skin from her mother. She was a little Swede, through and through. Dr.

[001-192] Archie could not help thinking how he would cherish a little creature like this

[001-193] if she were his. Her hands, so little and hot, so clever, too,—he glanced

[001-194] at the open exercise book on the piano. When he had stitched up the flaxseed

[001-195] jacket, he wiped it neatly about the edges, where the paste had worked out on

[001-196] the skin. He put on her the clean nightgown he had warmed before the fire, and

[001-197] tucked the blankets about her. As he pushed back the hair that had fuzzed down

[001-198] over her eyebrows, he felt her head thoughtfully with the tips of his fingers.

[001-199] No, he couldn’t say that it was different from any other child’s

[001-200] head, though he believed that there was something very different about her. He

[001-201] looked intently at her wide, flushed face, freckled nose, fierce little mouth,

[001-202] and her delicate, tender chin—the one soft touch in her hard little

[001-203] Scandinavian face, as if some fairy godmother had caressed her there and left a

[001-204] cryptic promise. Her brows were usually drawn together defiantly, but never

[001-205] when she was with Dr. Archie. Her affection for him was prettier than most of

[001-206] the things that went to make up the doctor’s life in Moonstone.

[001-207] The windows grew gray. He heard a tramping on the attic floor, on the back

[001-208] stairs, then cries: “Give me my shirt!” “Where’s my

[001-209] other stocking?”

[001-210] “I’ll have to stay till they get off to school,” he

[001-211] reflected, “or they’ll be in here tormenting her, the whole lot of

[001-212] them.”
