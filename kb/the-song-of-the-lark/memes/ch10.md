## X

[010-001] Wunsch and old Fritz and [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]] celebrated Christmas together, so

[010-002] riotously that Wunsch was unable to give Thea her lesson the next day. In the

[010-003] middle of the vacation week Thea went to the Kohlers’ through a soft,

[010-004] beautiful snowstorm. The air was a tender blue-gray, like the color on the

[010-005] doves that flew in and out of the white dove-house on the post in the

[010-006] Kohlers’ garden. The sand hills looked dim and sleepy. The tamarisk hedge

[010-007] was full of snow, like a foam of blossoms drifted over it. When Thea opened the

[010-008] gate, old Mrs. Kohler was just coming in from the chicken yard, with five fresh

[010-009] eggs in her apron and a pair of old top-boots on her feet. She called Thea to

[010-010] come and look at a bantam egg, which she held up proudly. Her bantam hens were

[010-011] remiss in zeal, and she was always delighted when they accomplished anything.

[010-012] She took Thea into the sitting-room, very warm and smelling of food, and

[010-013] brought her a plateful of little Christmas cakes, made according to old and

[010-014] hallowed formulae, and put them before her while she warmed her feet. Then she

[010-015] went to the door of the kitchen stairs and called: “Herr Wunsch, Herr

[010-016] Wunsch!”

[010-017] Wunsch came down wearing an old wadded jacket, with a velvet collar. The brown

[010-018] silk was so worn that the wadding stuck out almost everywhere. He avoided

[010-019] Thea’s eyes when he came in, nodded without speaking, and pointed

[010-020] directly to the piano stool. He was not so insistent upon the scales as usual,

[010-021] and throughout the little sonata of Mozart’s she was studying, he

[010-022] remained languid and absent-minded. His eyes looked very heavy, and he kept

[010-023] wiping them with one of the new silk handkerchiefs Mrs. Kohler had given him

[010-024] for Christmas. When the lesson was over he did not seem inclined to talk. Thea,

[010-025] loitering on the stool, reached for a tattered book she had taken off the

[010-026] music-rest when she sat down. It was a very old Leipsic edition of the piano

[010-027] score of Gluck’s “Orpheus.” She turned over the pages

[010-028] curiously.

[010-029] “Is it nice?” she asked.

[010-030] “It is the most beautiful opera ever made,” Wunsch declared

[010-031] solemnly. “You know the story, eh? How, when she die, Orpheus went down

[010-032] below for his wife?”

[010-033] “Oh, yes, I know. I didn’t know there was an opera about it,

[010-034] though. Do people sing this now?”

[010-035] “*Aber ja!* What else? You like to try? See.” He drew her from

[010-036] the stool and sat down at the piano. Turning over the leaves to the third act,

[010-037] he handed the score to Thea. “Listen, I play it through and you get the

[010-038] *rhythmus. Eins, zwei, drei, vier*.” He played through

[010-039] Orpheus’ lament, then pushed back his cuffs with awakening interest and

[010-040] nodded at Thea. “Now, *vom blatt, mit mir*.”

[010-041] “Ach, ich habe sie verloren,\

[010-042] All’ mein Glück ist nun dahin.”

[010-043] Wunsch sang the aria with much feeling. It was evidently one that was very dear

[010-044] to him.

[010-045] “*Noch einmal*, alone, yourself.” He played the introductory

[010-046] measures, then nodded at her vehemently, and she began:—

[010-047] “Ach, ich habe sie verloren.”

[010-048] When she finished, Wunsch nodded again. “*Schön*,” he muttered

[010-049] as he finished the accompaniment softly. He dropped his hands on his knees and

[010-050] looked up at Thea. “That is very fine, eh? There is no such beautiful

[010-051] melody in the world. You can take the book for one week and learn something, to

[010-052] pass the time. It is good to know—always. *Euridice,

[010-053] Eu—ri—di—ce, weh dass ich auf Erden bin!*” he sang

[010-054] softly, playing the melody with his right hand.

[010-055] Thea, who was turning over the pages of the third act, stopped and scowled at a

[010-056] passage. The old German’s blurred eyes watched her curiously.

[010-057] “For what do you look so, *immer?*” puckering up his own face.

[010-058] “You see something a little difficult, may-be, and you make such a face

[010-059] like it was an enemy.”

[010-060] Thea laughed, disconcerted. “Well, difficult things are enemies,

[010-061] aren’t they? When you have to get them?”

[010-062] Wunsch lowered his head and threw it up as if he were butting something.

[010-063] “Not at all! By no means.” He took the book from her and looked at

[010-064] it. “Yes, that is not so easy, there. This is an old book. They do not

[010-065] print it so now any more, I think. They leave it out, may-be. Only one woman

[010-066] could sing that good.”

[010-067] Thea looked at him in perplexity.

[010-068] Wunsch went on. “It is written for alto, you see. A woman sings the part,

[010-069] and there was only one to sing that good in there. You understand? Only

[010-070] one!” He glanced at her quickly and lifted his red forefinger upright

[010-071] before her eyes.

[010-072] Thea looked at the finger as if she were hypnotized. “Only one?”

[010-073] she asked breathlessly; her hands, hanging at her sides, were opening and

[010-074] shutting rapidly.

[010-075] Wunsch nodded and still held up that compelling finger. When he dropped his

[010-076] hands, there was a look of satisfaction in his face.

[010-077] “Was she very great?”

[010-078] Wunsch nodded.

[010-079] “Was she beautiful?”

[010-080] “*Aber gar nicht!* Not at all. She was ugly; big mouth, big teeth,

[010-081] no figure, nothing at all,” indicating a luxuriant bosom by sweeping his

[010-082] hands over his chest. “A pole, a post! But for the

[010-083] voice—*ach!* She have something in there, behind the eyes,”

[010-084] tapping his temples.

[010-085] Thea followed all his gesticulations intently. “Was she German?”

[010-086] “No, *Spanisch*.” He looked down and frowned for a moment.

[010-087] “*Ach*, I tell you, she look like the Frau Tellamantez, some-thing.

[010-088] Long face, long chin, and ugly al-so.”

[010-089] “Did she die a long while ago?”

[010-090] “Die? I think not. I never hear, anyhow. I guess she is alive somewhere

[010-091] in the world; Paris, may-be. But old, of course. I hear her when I was a youth.

[010-092] She is too old to sing now any more.”

[010-093] “Was she the greatest singer you ever heard?”

[010-094] Wunsch nodded gravely. “Quite so. She was the most—” he

[010-095] hunted for an English word, lifted his hand over his head and snapped his

[010-096] fingers noiselessly in the air, enunciating fiercely,

[010-097] “*künst-ler-isch!*” The word seemed to glitter in his uplifted

[010-098] hand, his voice was so full of emotion.

[010-099] Wunsch rose from the stool and began to button his wadded jacket, preparing to

[010-100] return to his half-heated room in the loft. Thea regretfully put on her cloak

[010-101] and hood and set out for home.

[010-102] When Wunsch looked for his score late that afternoon, he found that Thea had

[010-103] not forgotten to take it with her. He smiled his loose, sarcastic smile, and

[010-104] thoughtfully rubbed his stubbly chin with his red fingers. When Fritz came home

[010-105] in the early blue twilight the snow was flying faster, Mrs. Kohler was cooking

[010-106] *Hasenpfeffer* in the kitchen, and the professor was seated at the piano,

[010-107] playing the Gluck, which he knew by heart. Old Fritz took off his shoes quietly

[010-108] behind the stove and lay down on the lounge before his masterpiece, where the

[010-109] firelight was playing over the walls of Moscow. He listened, while the room

[010-110] grew darker and the windows duller. Wunsch always came back to the same

[010-111] thing:—

[010-112] “Ach, ich habe sie verloren,\

[010-113] ...\

[010-114] Euridice, Euridice!”

[010-115] From time to time Fritz sighed softly. He, too, had lost a Euridice.
