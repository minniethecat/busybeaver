## XI

[061-001] [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] saw nothing of Thea during the following week. After several

[061-002] fruitless efforts, he succeeded in getting a word with her over the telephone,

[061-003] but she sounded so distracted and driven that he was glad to say good-night and

[061-004] hang up the instrument. There were, she told him, rehearsals not only for

[061-005] “Walküre,” but also for “Götterdämmerung,” in which she

[061-006] was to sing *Waltraute* two weeks later.

[061-007] On Thursday afternoon Thea got home late, after an exhausting rehearsal. She

[061-008] was in no happy frame of mind. Madame Necker, who had been very gracious to her

[061-009] that night when she went on to complete Gloeckler’s performance of

[061-010] *Sieglinde*, had, since Thea was cast to sing the part instead of

[061-011] Gloeckler in the production of the “Ring,” been chilly and

[061-012] disapproving, distinctly hostile. Thea had always felt that she and Necker

[061-013] stood for the same sort of endeavor, and that Necker recognized it and had a

[061-014] cordial feeling for her. In Germany she had several times sung *Brangaena*

[061-015] to Necker’s *Isolde*, and the older artist had let her know that she

[061-016] thought she sang it beautifully. It was a bitter disappointment to find that

[061-017] the approval of so honest an artist as Necker could not stand the test of any

[061-018] significant recognition by the management. Madame Necker was forty, and her

[061-019] voice was failing just when her powers were at their height. Every fresh young

[061-020] voice was an enemy, and this one was accompanied by gifts which she could not

[061-021] fail to recognize.

[061-022] Thea had her dinner sent up to her apartment, and it was a very poor one. She

[061-023] tasted the soup and then indignantly put on her wraps to go out and hunt a

[061-024] dinner. As she was going to the elevator, she had to admit that she was

[061-025] behaving foolishly. She took off her hat and coat and ordered another dinner.

[061-026] When it arrived, it was no better than the first. There was even a burnt match

[061-027] under the milk toast. She had a sore throat, which made swallowing painful and

[061-028] boded ill for the morrow. Although she had been speaking in whispers all day to

[061-029] save her throat, she now perversely summoned the housekeeper and demanded an

[061-030] account of some laundry that had been lost. The housekeeper was indifferent and

[061-031] impertinent, and Thea got angry and scolded violently. She knew it was very bad

[061-032] for her to get into a rage just before bedtime, and after the housekeeper left

[061-033] she realized that for ten dollars’ worth of underclothing she had been

[061-034] unfitting herself for a performance which might eventually mean many thousands.

[061-035] The best thing now was to stop reproaching herself for her lack of sense, but

[061-036] she was too tired to control her thoughts.

[061-037] While she was undressing—Therese was brushing out her *Sieglinde*

[061-038] wig in the trunk-room—she went on chiding herself bitterly. “And

[061-039] how am I ever going to get to sleep in this state?” she kept asking

[061-040] herself. “If I don’t sleep, I’ll be perfectly worthless

[061-041] to-morrow. I’ll go down there to-morrow and make a fool of myself. If

[061-042] I’d let that laundry alone with whatever nigger has stolen

[061-043] it—*why* did I undertake to reform the management of this hotel

[061-044] to-night? After to-morrow I could pack up and leave the place. There’s

[061-045] the Phillamon—I liked the rooms there better, anyhow—and the

[061-046] Umberto—” She began going over the advantages and disadvantages of

[061-047] different apartment hotels. Suddenly she checked herself. “What *am*

[061-048] I doing this for? I can’t move into another hotel to-night. I’ll

[061-049] keep this up till morning. I shan’t sleep a wink.”

[061-050] Should she take a hot bath, or shouldn’t she? Sometimes it relaxed her,

[061-051] and sometimes it roused her and fairly put her beside herself. Between the

[061-052] conviction that she must sleep and the fear that she couldn’t, she hung

[061-053] paralyzed. When she looked at her bed, she shrank from it in every nerve. She

[061-054] was much more afraid of it than she had ever been of the stage of any opera

[061-055] house. It yawned before her like the sunken road at Waterloo.

[061-056] She rushed into her bathroom and locked the door. She would risk the bath, and

[061-057] defer the encounter with the bed a little longer. She lay in the bath half an

[061-058] hour. The warmth of the water penetrated to her bones, induced pleasant

[061-059] reflections and a feeling of well-being. It was very nice to have Dr. Archie in

[061-060] New York, after all, and to see him get so much satisfaction out of the little

[061-061] companionship she was able to give him. She liked people who got on, and who

[061-062] became more interesting as they grew older. There was Fred; he was much more

[061-063] interesting now than he had been at thirty. He was intelligent about music, and

[061-064] he must be very intelligent in his business, or he would not be at the head of

[061-065] the Brewers’ Trust. She respected that kind of intelligence and success.

[061-066] Any success was good. She herself had made a good start, at any rate, and now,

[061-067] if she could get to sleep—Yes, they were all more interesting than they

[061-068] used to be. Look at Harsanyi, who had been so long retarded; what a place he

[061-069] had made for himself in Vienna. If she could get to sleep, she would show him

[061-070] something to-morrow that he would understand.

[061-071] She got quickly into bed and moved about freely between the sheets. Yes, she

[061-072] was warm all over. A cold, dry breeze was coming in from the river, thank

[061-073] goodness! She tried to think about her little rock house and the Arizona sun

[061-074] and the blue sky. But that led to memories which were still too disturbing. She

[061-075] turned on her side, closed her eyes, and tried an old device.

[061-076] She entered her father’s front door, hung her hat and coat on the rack,

[061-077] and stopped in the parlor to warm her hands at the stove. Then she went out

[061-078] through the diningroom, where the boys were getting their lessons at the long

[061-079] table; through the sitting-room, where Thor was asleep in his cot bed, his

[061-080] dress and stocking hanging on a chair. In the kitchen she stopped for her

[061-081] lantern and her hot brick. She hurried up the back stairs and through the windy

[061-082] loft to her own glacial room. The illusion was marred only by the consciousness

[061-083] that she ought to brush her teeth before she went to bed, and that she never

[061-084] used to do it. Why—? The water was frozen solid in the pitcher, so she

[061-085] got over that. Once between the red blankets there was a short, fierce battle

[061-086] with the cold; then, warmer—warmer. She could hear her father shaking

[061-087] down the hard-coal burner for the night, and the wind rushing and banging down

[061-088] the village street. The boughs of the cottonwood, hard as bone, rattled against

[061-089] her gable. The bed grew softer and warmer. Everybody was warm and well

[061-090] downstairs. The sprawling old house had gathered them all in, like a hen, and

[061-091] had settled down over its brood. They were all warm in her father’s

[061-092] house. Softer and softer. She was asleep. She slept ten hours without turning

[061-093] over. From sleep like that, one awakes in shining armor.

[061-094] On Friday afternoon there was an inspiring audience; there was not an empty

[061-095] chair in the house. Ottenburg and Dr. Archie had seats in the orchestra circle,

[061-096] got from a ticket broker. Landry had not been able to get a seat, so he roamed

[061-097] about in the back of the house, where he usually stood when he dropped in after

[061-098] his own turn in vaudeville was over. He was there so often and at such

[061-099] irregular hours that the ushers thought he was a singer’s husband, or had

[061-100] something to do with the electrical plant.

[061-101] Harsanyi and his wife were in a box, near the stage, in the second circle. Mrs.

[061-102] Harsanyi’s hair was noticeably gray, but her face was fuller and

[061-103] handsomer than in those early years of struggle, and she was beautifully

[061-104] dressed. Harsanyi himself had changed very little. He had put on his best

[061-105] afternoon coat in honor of his pupil, and wore a pearl in his black ascot. His

[061-106] hair was longer and more bushy than he used to wear it, and there was now one

[061-107] gray lock on the right side. He had always been an elegant figure, even when he

[061-108] went about in shabby clothes and was crushed with work. Before the curtain rose

[061-109] he was restless and nervous, and kept looking at his watch and wishing he had

[061-110] got a few more letters off before he left his hotel. He had not been in New

[061-111] York since the advent of the taxicab, and had allowed himself too much time.

[061-112] His wife knew that he was afraid of being disappointed this afternoon. He did

[061-113] not often go to the opera because the stupid things that singers did vexed him

[061-114] so, and it always put him in a rage if the conductor held the tempo or in any

[061-115] way accommodated the score to the singer.

[061-116] When the lights went out and the violins began to quaver their long D against

[061-117] the rude figure of the basses, Mrs. Harsanyi saw her husband’s fingers

[061-118] fluttering on his knee in a rapid tattoo. At the moment when *Sieglinde*

[061-119] entered from the side door, she leaned toward him and whispered in his ear,

[061-120] “Oh, the lovely creature!” But he made no response, either by voice

[061-121] or gesture. Throughout the first scene he sat sunk in his chair, his head

[061-122] forward and his one yellow eye rolling restlessly and shining like a

[061-123] tiger’s in the dark. His eye followed *Sieglinde* about the stage

[061-124] like a satellite, and as she sat at the table listening to

[061-125] *Siegmund’s* long narrative, it never left her. When she prepared

[061-126] the sleeping draught and disappeared after *Hunding*, Harsanyi bowed his

[061-127] head still lower and put his hand over his eye to rest it. The tenor,—a

[061-128] young man who sang with great vigor, went on:—

[061-129] “Wälse! Wälse!\

[061-130] Wo ist dein Schwert?”

[061-131] Harsanyi smiled, but he did not look forth again until *Sieglinde*

[061-132] reappeared. She went through the story of her shameful bridal feast and into

[061-133] the Walhall’ music, which she always sang so nobly, and the entrance of

[061-134] the one-eyed stranger:—

[061-135] “Mir allein\

[061-136] Weckte das Auge.”

[061-137] Mrs. Harsanyi glanced at her husband, wondering whether the singer on the stage

[061-138] could not feel his commanding glance. On came the *crescendo:*—

[061-139] “Was je ich verlor,\

[061-140] Was je ich beweint\

[061-141] War’ mir gewonnen.”

[061-142] (All that I have lost,\

[061-143] All that I have mourned,\

[061-144] Would I then have won.)

[061-145] Harsanyi touched his wife’s arm softly.

[061-146] Seated in the moonlight, the *Volsung* pair began their loving inspection

[061-147] of each other’s beauties, and the music born of murmuring sound passed

[061-148] into her face, as the old poet said,—and into her body as well. Into one

[061-149] lovely attitude after another the music swept her, love impelled her. And the

[061-150] voice gave out all that was best in it. Like the spring, indeed, it blossomed

[061-151] into memories and prophecies, it recounted and it foretold, as she sang the

[061-152] story of her friendless life, and of how the thing which was truly herself,

[061-153] “bright as the day, rose to the surface” when in the hostile world

[061-154] she for the first time beheld her Friend. Fervently she rose into the hardier

[061-155] feeling of action and daring, the pride in hero-strength and hero-blood, until

[061-156] in a splendid burst, tall and shining like a Victory, she christened

[061-157] him:—

[061-158] “Siegmund—\

[061-159] So nenn ich dich!”

[061-160] Her impatience for the sword swelled with her anticipation of his act, and

[061-161] throwing her arms above her head, she fairly tore a sword out of the empty air

[061-162] for him, before *Nothung* had left the tree. *In höchster

[061-163] Trunkenheit*, indeed, she burst out with the flaming cry of their kinship:

[061-164] “If you are *Siegmund*, I am *Sieglinde!*” Laughing,

[061-165] singing, bounding, exulting,—with their passion and their

[061-166] sword,—the *Volsungs* ran out into the spring night.

[061-167] As the curtain fell, Harsanyi turned to his wife. “At last,” he

[061-168] sighed, “somebody with *Enough!* Enough voice and talent and beauty,

[061-169] enough physical power. And such a noble, noble style!”

[061-170] “I can scarcely believe it, Andor. I can see her now, that clumsy girl,

[061-171] hunched up over your piano. I can see her shoulders. She always seemed to labor

[061-172] so with her back. And I shall never forget that night when you found her

[061-173] voice.”

[061-174] The audience kept up its clamor until, after many reappearances with the tenor,

[061-175] Kronborg came before the curtain alone. The house met her with a roar, a

[061-176] greeting that was almost savage in its fierceness. The singer’s eyes,

[061-177] sweeping the house, rested for a moment on Harsanyi, and she waved her long

[061-178] sleeve toward his box.

[061-179] “She *ought* to be pleased that you are here,” said Mrs.

[061-180] Harsanyi. “I wonder if she knows how much she owes to you.”

[061-181] “She owes me nothing,” replied her husband quickly. “She paid

[061-182] her way. She always gave something back, even then.”

[061-183] “I remember you said once that she would do nothing common,” said

[061-184] Mrs. Harsanyi thoughtfully.

[061-185] “Just so. She might fail, die, get lost in the pack. But if she achieved,

[061-186] it would be nothing common. There are people whom one can trust for that. There

[061-187] is one way in which they will never fail.” Harsanyi retired into his own

[061-188] reflections.

[061-189] After the second act [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] brought Archie to the Harsanyis’ box

[061-190] and introduced him as an old friend of Miss Kronborg. The head of a musical

[061-191] publishing house joined them, bringing with him a journalist and the president

[061-192] of a German singing society. The conversation was chiefly about the new

[061-193] *Sieglinde*. Mrs. Harsanyi was gracious and enthusiastic, her husband

[061-194] nervous and uncommunicative. He smiled mechanically, and politely answered

[061-195] questions addressed to him. “Yes, quite so.” “Oh,

[061-196] certainly.” Every one, of course, said very usual things with great

[061-197] conviction. Mrs. Harsanyi was used to hearing and uttering the commonplaces

[061-198] which such occasions demanded. When her husband withdrew into the shadow, she

[061-199] covered his retreat by her sympathy and cordiality. In reply to a direct

[061-200] question from Ottenburg, Harsanyi said, flinching, “*Isolde?* Yes,

[061-201] why not? She will sing all the great roles, I should think.”

[061-202] The chorus director said something about “dramatic temperament.”

[061-203] The journalist insisted that it was “explosive force,”

[061-204] “projecting power.”

[061-205] Ottenburg turned to Harsanyi. “What is it, Mr. Harsanyi? Miss Kronborg

[061-206] says if there is anything in her, you are the man who can say what it

[061-207] is.”

[061-208] The journalist scented copy and was eager. “Yes, Harsanyi. You know all

[061-209] about her. What’s her secret?”

[061-210] Harsanyi rumpled his hair irritably and shrugged his shoulders. “Her

[061-211] secret? It is every artist’s secret,”—he waved his

[061-212] hand,—“passion. That is all. It is an open secret, and perfectly

[061-213] safe. Like heroism, it is inimitable in cheap materials.”

[061-214] The lights went out. Fred and Archie left the box as the second act came on.

[061-215] Artistic growth is, more than it is anything else, a refining of the sense of

[061-216] truthfulness. The stupid believe that to be truthful is easy; only the artist,

[061-217] the great artist, knows how difficult it is. That afternoon nothing new came to

[061-218] [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]], no enlightenment, no inspiration. She merely came into full

[061-219] possession of things she had been refining and perfecting for so long. Her

[061-220] inhibitions chanced to be fewer than usual, and, within herself, she entered

[061-221] into the inheritance that she herself had laid up, into the fullness of the

[061-222] faith she had kept before she knew its name or its meaning.

[061-223] Often when she sang, the best she had was unavailable; she could not break

[061-224] through to it, and every sort of distraction and mischance came between it and

[061-225] her. But this afternoon the closed roads opened, the gates dropped. What she

[061-226] had so often tried to reach, lay under her hand. She had only to touch an idea

[061-227] to make it live.

[061-228] While she was on the stage she was conscious that every movement was the right

[061-229] movement, that her body was absolutely the instrument of her idea. Not for

[061-230] nothing had she kept it so severely, kept it filled with such energy and fire.

[061-231] All that deep-rooted vitality flowered in her voice, her face, in her very

[061-232] finger-tips. She felt like a tree bursting into bloom. And her voice was as

[061-233] flexible as her body; equal to any demand, capable of every *nuance*. With

[061-234] the sense of its perfect companionship, its entire trustworthiness, she had

[061-235] been able to throw herself into the dramatic exigencies of the part, everything

[061-236] in her at its best and everything working together.

[061-237] The third act came on, and the afternoon slipped by. Thea Kronborg’s

[061-238] friends, old and new, seated about the house on different floors and levels,

[061-239] enjoyed her triumph according to their natures. There was one there, whom

[061-240] nobody knew, who perhaps got greater pleasure out of that afternoon than

[061-241] Harsanyi himself. Up in the top gallery a gray-haired little Mexican, withered

[061-242] and bright as a string of peppers beside a’dobe door, kept praying and

[061-243] cursing under his breath, beating on the brass railing and shouting

[061-244] “Bravó! Bravó!” until he was repressed by his neighbors.

[061-245] He happened to be there because a Mexican band was to be a feature of Barnum

[061-246] and Bailey’s circus that year. One of the managers of the show had

[061-247] traveled about the Southwest, signing up a lot of Mexican musicians at low

[061-248] wages, and had brought them to New York. Among them was [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]]. After

[061-249] Mrs. Tellamantez died, Johnny abandoned his trade and went out with his

[061-250] mandolin to pick up a living for one. His irregularities had become his regular

[061-251] mode of life.

[061-252] When Thea Kronborg came out of the stage entrance on Fortieth Street, the sky

[061-253] was still flaming with the last rays of the sun that was sinking off behind the

[061-254] North River. A little crowd of people was lingering about the

[061-255] door—musicians from the orchestra who were waiting for their comrades,

[061-256] curious young men, and some poorly dressed girls who were hoping to get a

[061-257] glimpse of the singer. She bowed graciously to the group, through her veil, but

[061-258] she did not look to the right or left as she crossed the sidewalk to her cab.

[061-259] Had she lifted her eyes an instant and glanced out through her white scarf, she

[061-260] must have seen the only man in the crowd who had removed his hat when she

[061-261] emerged, and who stood with it crushed up in his hand. And she would have known

[061-262] him, changed as he was. His lustrous black hair was full of gray, and his face

[061-263] was a good deal worn by the *extasi*, so that it seemed to have shrunk

[061-264] away from his shining eyes and teeth and left them too prominent. But she would

[061-265] have known him. She passed so near that he could have touched her, and he did

[061-266] not put on his hat until her taxi had snorted away. Then he walked down

[061-267] Broadway with his hands in his overcoat pockets, wearing a smile which embraced

[061-268] all the stream of life that passed him and the lighted towers that rose into

[061-269] the limpid blue of the evening sky. If the singer, going home exhausted in her

[061-270] cab, was wondering what was the good of it all, that smile, could she have seen

[061-271] it, would have answered her. It is the only commensurate answer.

[061-272] Here we must leave Thea Kronborg. From this time on the story of her life is

[061-273] the story of her achievement. The growth of an artist is an intellectual and

[061-274] spiritual development which can scarcely be followed in a personal narrative.

[061-275] This story attempts to deal only with the simple and concrete beginnings which

[061-276] color and accent an artist’s work, and to give some account of how a

[061-277] Moonstone girl found her way out of a vague, easy-going world into a life of

[061-278] disciplined endeavor. Any account of the loyalty of young hearts to some

[061-279] exalted ideal, and the passion with which they strive, will always, in some of

[061-280] us, rekindle generous emotions.
