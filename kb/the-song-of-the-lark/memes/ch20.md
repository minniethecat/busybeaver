## XX

[020-001] On Monday morning, the day after [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]]’s funeral, [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] called

[020-002] at Mr. Kronborg’s study, a little room behind the church. Mr. Kronborg

[020-003] did not write out his sermons, but spoke from notes jotted upon small pieces of

[020-004] cardboard in a kind of shorthand of his own. As sermons go, they were not worse

[020-005] than most. His conventional rhetoric pleased the majority of his congregation,

[020-006] and Mr. Kronborg was generally regarded as a model preacher. He did not smoke,

[020-007] he never touched spirits. His indulgence in the pleasures of the table was an

[020-008] endearing bond between him and the women of his congregation. He ate

[020-009] enormously, with a zest which seemed incongruous with his spare frame.

[020-010] This morning the doctor found him opening his mail and reading a pile of

[020-011] advertising circulars with deep attention.

[020-012] “Good-morning, Mr. Kronborg,” said Dr. Archie, sitting down.

[020-013] “I came to see you on business. Poor Kennedy asked me to look after his

[020-014] affairs for him. Like most railroad men he spent his wages, except for a few

[020-015] investments in mines which don’t look to me very promising. But his life

[020-016] was insured for six hundred dollars in Thea’s favor.”

[020-017] Mr. Kronborg wound his feet about the standard of his desk-chair. “I

[020-018] assure you, doctor, this is a complete surprise to me.”

[020-019] “Well, it’s not very surprising to me,” Dr. Archie went on.

[020-020] “He talked to me about it the day he was hurt. He said he wanted the

[020-021] money to be used in a particular way, and in no other.” Dr. Archie paused

[020-022] meaningly.

[020-023] Mr. Kronborg fidgeted. “I am sure Thea would observe his wishes in every

[020-024] respect.”

[020-025] “No doubt; but he wanted me to see that you agreed to his plan. It seems

[020-026] that for some time Thea has wanted to go away to study music. It was

[020-027] Kennedy’s wish that she should take this money and go to [[chicago|Chicago]] this

[020-028] winter. He felt that it would be an advantage to her in a business way: that

[020-029] even if she came back here to teach, it would give her more authority and make

[020-030] her position here more comfortable.”

[020-031] Mr. Kronborg looked a little startled. “She is very young,” he

[020-032] hesitated; “she is barely seventeen. Chicago is a long way from home. We

[020-033] would have to consider. I think, Dr. Archie, we had better consult Mrs.

[020-034] Kronborg.”

[020-035] “I think I can bring [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] around, if I have your consent.

[020-036] I’ve always found her pretty level-headed. I have several old classmates

[020-037] practicing in Chicago. One is a throat specialist. He has a good deal to do

[020-038] with singers. He probably knows the best piano teachers and could recommend a

[020-039] boarding-house where music students stay. I think Thea needs to get among a lot

[020-040] of young people who are clever like herself. Here she has no companions but old

[020-041] fellows like me. It’s not a natural life for a young girl. She’ll

[020-042] either get warped, or wither up before her time. If it will make you and Mrs.

[020-043] Kronborg feel any easier, I’ll be glad to take Thea to Chicago and see

[020-044] that she gets started right. This throat man I speak of is a big fellow in his

[020-045] line, and if I can get him interested, he may be able to put her in the way of

[020-046] a good many things. At any rate, he’ll know the right teachers. Of

[020-047] course, six hundred dollars won’t take her very far, but even half the

[020-048] winter there would be a great advantage. I think Kennedy sized the situation up

[020-049] exactly.”

[020-050] “Perhaps; I don’t doubt it. You are very kind, Dr. Archie.”

[020-051] Mr. Kronborg was ornamenting his desk-blotter with hieroglyphics. “I

[020-052] should think Denver might be better. There we could watch over her. She is very

[020-053] young.”

[020-054] Dr. Archie rose. “Kennedy didn’t mention Denver. He said Chicago,

[020-055] repeatedly. Under the circumstances, it seems to me we ought to try to carry

[020-056] out his wishes exactly, if Thea is willing.”

[020-057] “Certainly, certainly. Thea is conscientious. She would not waste her

[020-058] opportunities.” Mr. Kronborg paused. “If Thea were your own

[020-059] daughter, doctor, would you consent to such a plan, at her present age?”

[020-060] “I most certainly should. In fact, if she were my daughter, I’d

[020-061] have sent her away before this. She’s a most unusual child, and

[020-062] she’s only wasting herself here. At her age she ought to be learning, not

[020-063] teaching. She’ll never learn so quickly and easily as she will right

[020-064] now.”

[020-065] “Well, doctor, you had better talk it over with Mrs. Kronborg. I make it

[020-066] a point to defer to her wishes in such matters. She understands all her

[020-067] children perfectly. I may say that she has all a mother’s insight, and

[020-068] more.”

[020-069] Dr. Archie smiled. “Yes, and then some. I feel quite confident about Mrs.

[020-070] Kronborg. We usually agree. Good-morning.”

[020-071] Dr. Archie stepped out into the hot sunshine and walked rapidly toward his

[020-072] office, with a determined look on his face. He found his waiting-room full of

[020-073] patients, and it was one o’clock before he had dismissed the last one.

[020-074] Then he shut his door and took a drink before going over to the hotel for his

[020-075] lunch. He smiled as he locked his cupboard. “I feel almost as gay as if I

[020-076] were going to get away for a winter myself,” he thought.

[020-077] Afterward Thea could never remember much about that summer, or how she lived

[020-078] through her impatience. She was to set off with Dr. Archie on the fifteenth of

[020-079] October, and she gave lessons until the first of September. Then she began to

[020-080] get her clothes ready, and spent whole afternoons in the village

[020-081] dressmaker’s stuffy, littered little sewing-room. Thea and her mother

[020-082] made a trip to Denver to buy the materials for her dresses. Ready-made clothes

[020-083] for girls were not to be had in those days. Miss Spencer, the dressmaker,

[020-084] declared that she could do handsomely by Thea if they would only let her carry

[020-085] out her own ideas. But Mrs. Kronborg and Thea felt that Miss Spencer’s

[020-086] most daring productions might seem out of place in Chicago, so they restrained

[020-087] her with a firm hand. Tillie, who always helped Mrs. Kronborg with the family

[020-088] sewing, was for letting Miss Spencer challenge Chicago on Thea’s person.

[020-089] Since Ray Kennedy’s death, Thea had become more than ever one of

[020-090] Tillie’s heroines. Tillie swore each of her friends to secrecy, and,

[020-091] coming home from church or leaning over the fence, told them the most touching

[020-092] stories about Ray’s devotion, and how Thea would “never get over

[020-093] it.”

[020-094] Tillie’s confidences stimulated the general discussion of Thea’s

[020-095] venture. This discussion went on, upon front porches and in back yards, pretty

[020-096] much all summer. Some people approved of Thea’s going to Chicago, but

[020-097] most people did not. There were others who changed their minds about it every

[020-098] day.

[020-099] Tillie said she wanted Thea to have a ball dress “above all

[020-100] things.” She bought a fashion book especially devoted to evening clothes

[020-101] and looked hungrily over the colored plates, picking out costumes that would be

[020-102] becoming to “a blonde.” She wanted Thea to have all the gay clothes

[020-103] she herself had always longed for; clothes she often told herself she needed

[020-104] “to recite in.”

[020-105] “Tillie,” Thea used to cry impatiently, “can’t you see

[020-106] that if Miss Spencer tried to make one of those things, she’d make me

[020-107] look like a circus girl? Anyhow, I don’t know anybody in Chicago. I

[020-108] won’t be going to parties.”

[020-109] Tillie always replied with a knowing toss of her head, “You see!

[020-110] You’ll be in society before you know it. There ain’t many girls as

[020-111] accomplished as you.”

[020-112] On the morning of the fifteenth of October the Kronborg family, all of them but

[020-113] Gus, who couldn’t leave the store, started for the station an hour before

[020-114] train time. Charley had taken Thea’s trunk and telescope to the depot in

[020-115] his delivery wagon early that morning. Thea wore her new blue serge

[020-116] traveling-dress, chosen for its serviceable qualities. She had done her hair up

[020-117] carefully, and had put a pale-blue ribbon around her throat, under a little

[020-118] lace collar that Mrs. Kohler had crocheted for her. As they went out of the

[020-119] gate, Mrs. Kronborg looked her over thoughtfully. Yes, that blue ribbon went

[020-120] very well with the dress, and with Thea’s eyes. Thea had a rather unusual

[020-121] touch about such things, she reflected comfortably. Tillie always said that

[020-122] Thea was “so indifferent to dress,” but her mother noticed that she

[020-123] usually put her clothes on well. She felt the more at ease about letting Thea

[020-124] go away from home, because she had good sense about her clothes and never tried

[020-125] to dress up too much. Her coloring was so individual, she was so unusually

[020-126] fair, that in the wrong clothes she might easily have been

[020-127] “conspicuous.”

[020-128] It was a fine morning, and the family set out from the house in good spirits.

[020-129] Thea was quiet and calm. She had forgotten nothing, and she clung tightly to

[020-130] her handbag, which held her trunk-key and all of her money that was not in an

[020-131] envelope pinned to her chemise. Thea walked behind the others, holding Thor by

[020-132] the hand, and this time she did not feel that the procession was too long. Thor

[020-133] was uncommunicative that morning, and would only talk about how he would rather

[020-134] get a sand bur in his toe every day than wear shoes and stockings. As they

[020-135] passed the cottonwood grove where Thea often used to bring him in his cart, she

[020-136] asked him who would take him for nice long walks after sister went away.

[020-137] “Oh, I can walk in our yard,” he replied unappreciatively. “I

[020-138] guess I can make a pond for my duck.”

[020-139] Thea leaned down and looked into his face. “But you won’t forget

[020-140] about sister, will you?” Thor shook his head. “And won’t you

[020-141] be glad when sister comes back and can take you over to Mrs. Kohler’s to

[020-142] see the pigeons?”

[020-143] “Yes, I’ll be glad. But I’m going to have a pigeon my own

[020-144] self.”

[020-145] “But you haven’t got any little house for one. Maybe Axel would

[020-146] make you a little house.”

[020-147] “Oh, her can live in the barn, her can,” Thor drawled

[020-148] indifferently.

[020-149] Thea laughed and squeezed his hand. She always liked his sturdy

[020-150] matter-of-factness. Boys ought to be like that, she thought.

[020-151] When they reached the depot, Mr. Kronborg paced the platform somewhat

[020-152] ceremoniously with his daughter. Any member of his flock would have gathered

[020-153] that he was giving her good counsel about meeting the temptations of the world.

[020-154] He did, indeed, begin to admonish her not to forget that talents come from our

[020-155] Heavenly Father and are to be used for his glory, but he cut his remarks short

[020-156] and looked at his watch. He believed that Thea was a religious girl, but when

[020-157] she looked at him with that intent, that passionately inquiring gaze which used

[020-158] to move even Wunsch, Mr. Kronborg suddenly felt his eloquence fail. Thea was

[020-159] like her mother, he reflected; you couldn’t put much sentiment across

[020-160] with her. As a usual thing, he liked girls to be a little more responsive. He

[020-161] liked them to blush at his compliments; as Mrs. Kronborg candidly said,

[020-162] “Father could be very soft with the girls.” But this morning he was

[020-163] thinking that hard-headedness was a reassuring quality in a daughter who was

[020-164] going to Chicago alone.

[020-165] Mr. Kronborg believed that big cities were places where people went to lose

[020-166] their identity and to be wicked. He himself, when he was a student at the

[020-167] Seminary—he coughed and opened his watch again. He knew, of course, that

[020-168] a great deal of business went on in Chicago, that there was an active Board of

[020-169] Trade, and that hogs and cattle were slaughtered there. But when, as a young

[020-170] man, he had stopped over in Chicago, he had not interested himself in the

[020-171] commercial activities of the city. He remembered it as a place full of cheap

[020-172] shows and dance halls and boys from the country who were behaving disgustingly.

[020-173] Dr. Archie drove up to the station about ten minutes before the train was due.

[020-174] His man tied the ponies and stood holding the doctor’s alligator-skin

[020-175] bag—very elegant, Thea thought it. Mrs. Kronborg did not burden the

[020-176] doctor with warnings and cautions. She said again that she hoped he could get

[020-177] Thea a comfortable place to stay, where they had good beds, and she hoped the

[020-178] landlady would be a woman who’d had children of her own. “I

[020-179] don’t go much on old maids looking after girls,” she remarked as

[020-180] she took a pin out of her own hat and thrust it into Thea’s blue turban.

[020-181] “You’ll be sure to lose your hatpins on the train, Thea. It’s

[020-182] better to have an extra one in case.” She tucked in a little curl that

[020-183] had escaped from Thea’s careful twist. “Don’t forget to brush

[020-184] your dress often, and pin it up to the curtains of your berth to-night, so it

[020-185] won’t wrinkle. If you get it wet, have a tailor press it before it

[020-186] draws.”

[020-187] She turned Thea about by the shoulders and looked her over a last time. Yes,

[020-188] she looked very well. She wasn’t pretty, exactly,—her face was too

[020-189] broad and her nose was too big. But she had that lovely skin, and she looked

[020-190] fresh and sweet. She had always been a sweet-smelling child. Her mother had

[020-191] always liked to kiss her, when she happened to think of it.

[020-192] The train whistled in, and Mr. Kronborg carried the canvas

[020-193] “telescope” into the car. Thea kissed them all good-bye. Tillie

[020-194] cried, but she was the only one who did. They all shouted things up at the

[020-195] closed window of the Pullman car, from which Thea looked down at them as from a

[020-196] frame, her face glowing with excitement, her turban a little tilted in spite of

[020-197] three hatpins. She had already taken off her new gloves to save them. Mrs.

[020-198] Kronborg reflected that she would never see just that same picture again, and

[020-199] as Thea’s car slid off along the rails, she wiped a tear from her eye.

[020-200] “She won’t come back a little girl,” Mrs. Kronborg said to

[020-201] her husband as they turned to go home. “Anyhow, she’s been a sweet

[020-202] one.”

[020-203] While the Kronborg family were trooping slowly homeward, Thea was sitting in

[020-204] the Pullman, her telescope in the seat beside her, her handbag tightly gripped

[020-205] in her fingers. Dr. Archie had gone into the smoker. He thought she might be a

[020-206] little tearful, and that it would be kinder to leave her alone for a while. Her

[020-207] eyes did fill once, when she saw the last of the sand hills and realized that

[020-208] she was going to leave them behind for a long while. They always made her think

[020-209] of Ray, too. She had had such good times with him out there.

[020-210] But, of course, it was herself and her own adventure that mattered to her. If

[020-211] youth did not matter so much to itself, it would never have the heart to go on.

[020-212] Thea was surprised that she did not feel a deeper sense of loss at leaving her

[020-213] old life behind her. It seemed, on the contrary, as she looked out at the

[020-214] yellow desert speeding by, that she had left very little. Everything that was

[020-215] essential seemed to be right there in the car with her. She lacked nothing. She

[020-216] even felt more compact and confident than usual. She was all there, and

[020-217] something else was there, too,—in her heart, was it, or under her cheek?

[020-218] Anyhow, it was about her somewhere, that warm sureness, that sturdy little

[020-219] companion with whom she shared a secret.

[020-220] When Dr. Archie came in from the smoker, she was sitting still, looking

[020-221] intently out of the window and smiling, her lips a little parted, her hair in a

[020-222] blaze of sunshine. The doctor thought she was the prettiest thing he had ever

[020-223] seen, and very funny, with her telescope and big handbag. She made him feel

[020-224] jolly, and a little mournful, too. He knew that the splendid things of life are

[020-225] few, after all, and so very easy to miss.
