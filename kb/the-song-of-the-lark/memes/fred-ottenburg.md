# Fred Ottenburg

## Overview

Fred Ottenburg is the most complex of Thea Kronborg's relationships—patron, lover, friend, and the person who most decisively expands her artistic horizons by introducing her to Wagner's operas. Heir to a beer-brewing fortune, Ottenburg is a man of genuine cultivation who uses his wealth to support the arts and to pursue his own aesthetic pleasures. His relationship with Thea is the novel's most sustained exploration of the intersection between love, money, and art (025-180).

## Patronage and Romance

Ottenburg's role in Thea's life begins as patron and friend. He recognizes her exceptional talent and provides the kind of support—financial, social, and intellectual—that enables her to access the highest levels of musical culture. His introduction of Thea to Wagner's operas is a pivotal moment in her artistic development, opening a new dimension of musical ambition and possibility.

The romantic dimension of their relationship complicates the patronage dynamic. Ottenburg is married—a fact he conceals from Thea—and the revelation of his marital status creates the novel's central emotional crisis. Cather handles this crisis with characteristic restraint, neither condemning Ottenburg nor excusing him, but tracing its consequences for Thea's development with psychological precision.

## The Connoisseur

Ottenburg represents a type Cather treats with ambivalence: the wealthy connoisseur whose relationship to art is mediated by money and taste rather than by the creative drive itself. He is genuinely knowledgeable, genuinely appreciative—but he is not an artist. His relationship to Thea is colored by this fact: he recognizes in her something he cannot himself create, and his devotion to her is, in part, devotion to the art she embodies.

## Significance

Ottenburg's most important function in the novel is as catalyst. His introduction of Wagner into Thea's imaginative world sets her on the path toward her mature artistic identity. His love for her, whatever its complications, provides emotional depth and texture to her journey. And his limitations—the fact that he can support art but not create it, love the artist but not fully understand her—clarify by contrast what Thea herself must become.
