## Overview

Henry Nathanmeyer is a wealthy Chicago businessman and music patron introduced in Part 3 of the novel. With his wife, Mrs. Nathanmeyer, he presides over one of Chicago's most prominent households, hosting private musicales that offer Thea Kronborg her first exposure to elite patronage. The Nathanmeyers represent the intersection of wealth, social power, and artistic cultivation in late-nineteenth-century Chicago — they are "so rich and great" that their endorsement carries decisive weight in the musical community [035-034].

## Biography / Character Arc

### Position in Chicago Society

Nathanmeyer is a figure of established wealth and influence. He is described as an elderly man with a "white head" who receives guests in the "great library" of his Chicago mansion [035-108]. His household includes a troop of daughters [035-020] and is staffed with servants, including a maid named Selma [035-152]. The Nathanmeyers are characterized as "the finest kind of Jews" — a phrase that in the novel's idiom signals not only their ethnic identity but their cultural cultivation and generosity as patrons [035-070].

### The Musicales

Nathanmeyer agrees to give three musicales at his home, a commitment brokered by Fred Ottenburg to provide Thea with a platform before a discerning Chicago audience [035-008]. On the evening of Thea's performance, Nathanmeyer is found with his wife in the library, unable to rise easily from his chair — he "apologized for not rising" when Ottenburg and Thea enter [035-127]. He directs Ottenburg to bring a chair [035-132] and later comes to the music-room door to listen [035-226]. These details sketch a man of advanced age whose physical frailty does not diminish his authority as a host and patron.

### The Collection

The Nathanmeyer home contains a notable art collection; Ottenburg had told Thea that Mr. Nathanmeyer had a "very fine collection of" objects — though the novel leaves the nature of the collection unspecified, its presence signals the household's cultural aspirations [035-138].

## Relationships

### Mrs. Nathanmeyer
Mrs. Nathanmeyer is the dominant social presence in the household. A "heavy, powerful old Jewess" [035-123], she manages the musicales, directs the servants, and uses her lorgnette to survey the proceedings [035-181]. Her relationship with her husband appears companionable and mutually reinforcing — they are alone together in the library before guests arrive [035-108], and he defers to her management of social events.

### Fred Ottenburg
Ottenburg serves as the intermediary between the Nathanmeyers and Thea. He has the social standing to approach Nathanmeyer with the proposal of hosting musicales and the charm to manage the delicate negotiation of patronage. His aside that "you must put yourself into her hands" regarding Mrs. Nathanmeyer [035-071] reveals his understanding of how the household's power dynamics operate.

### Thea Kronborg
Nathanmeyer's relationship with Thea is mediated entirely through his role as host and patron. He is kind but distant, a benevolent figure whose wealth and position make her debut possible without requiring any personal connection between them.

## Significance

Henry Nathanmeyer embodies the role of the wealthy patron in the artistic ecosystem Willa Cather depicts. Unlike the hands-on mentorship of figures like Harsanyi or Madison Bowers, Nathanmeyer's patronage operates through the medium of wealth, social position, and the cultural capital of his home. He represents the institutional infrastructure — money, venues, audiences — that enables artistic careers to advance from training to public performance.

In the novel's social taxonomy, the Nathanmeyers occupy a distinct niche: they are neither artists like Thea nor impresarios like Ottenburg, but rather the cultivated wealthy who provide the setting and the audience. Their Jewishness is noted repeatedly in the text [035-070, 035-123], marking them as part of a specific Chicago cultural milieu that valued European high culture and played a significant role in the city's musical life.
