## EPILOGUE

[062-001] Moonstone again, in the year 1909. The Methodists are giving an ice-cream

[062-002] sociable in the grove about the new court-house. It is a warm summer night of

[062-003] full moon. The paper lanterns which hang among the trees are foolish toys, only

[062-004] dimming, in little lurid circles, the great softness of the lunar light that

[062-005] floods the blue heavens and the high plateau. To the east the sand hills shine

[062-006] white as of old, but the empire of the sand is gradually diminishing. The grass

[062-007] grows thicker over the dunes than it used to, and the streets of the town are

[062-008] harder and firmer than they were twenty-five years ago. The old inhabitants

[062-009] will tell you that sandstorms are infrequent now, that the wind blows less

[062-010] persistently in the spring and plays a milder tune. Cultivation has modified

[062-011] the soil and the climate, as it modifies human life.

[062-012] The people seated about under the cottonwoods are much smarter than the

[062-013] Methodists we used to know. The interior of the new Methodist Church looks like

[062-014] a theater, with a sloping floor, and as the congregation proudly say,

[062-015] “opera chairs.” The matrons who attend to serving the refreshments

[062-016] to-night look younger for their years than did the women of Mrs.

[062-017] Kronborg’s time, and the children all look like city children. The little

[062-018] boys wear “Buster Browns” and the little girls Russian blouses. The

[062-019] country child, in made-overs and cut-downs, seems to have vanished from the

[062-020] face of the earth.

[062-021] At one of the tables, with her Dutch-cut twin boys, sits a fair-haired, dimpled

[062-022] matron who was once [[lily-fisher|Lily Fisher]]. Her husband is president of the new bank, and

[062-023] she “goes East for her summers,” a practice which causes envy and

[062-024] discontent among her neighbors. The twins are well-behaved children, biddable,

[062-025] meek, neat about their clothes, and always mindful of the proprieties they have

[062-026] learned at summer hotels. While they are eating their icecream and trying not

[062-027] to twist the spoon in their mouths, a little shriek of laughter breaks from an

[062-028] adjacent table. The twins look up. There sits a spry little old spinster whom

[062-029] they know well. She has a long chin, a long nose, and she is dressed like a

[062-030] young girl, with a pink sash and a lace garden hat with pink rosebuds. She is

[062-031] surrounded by a crowd of boys,—loose and lanky, short and

[062-032] thick,—who are joking with her roughly, but not unkindly.

[062-033] “Mamma,” one of the twins comes out in a shrill treble, “why

[062-034] is Tillie Kronborg always talking about a thousand dollars?”

[062-035] The boys, hearing this question, break into a roar of laughter, the women

[062-036] titter behind their paper napkins, and even from Tillie there is a little

[062-037] shriek of appreciation. The observing child’s remark had made every one

[062-038] suddenly realize that Tillie never stopped talking about that particular sum of

[062-039] money. In the spring, when she went to buy early strawberries, and was told

[062-040] that they were thirty cents a box, she was sure to remind the grocer that

[062-041] though her name was Kronborg she didn’t get a thousand dollars a night.

[062-042] In the autumn, when she went to buy her coal for the winter, she expressed

[062-043] amazement at the price quoted her, and told the dealer he must have got her

[062-044] mixed up with her niece to think she could pay such a sum. When she was making

[062-045] her Christmas presents, she never failed to ask the women who came into her

[062-046] shop what you *could* make for anybody who got a thousand dollars a night.

[062-047] When the Denver papers announced that [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] had married Frederick

[062-048] Ottenburg, the head of the Brewers’ Trust, Moonstone people expected that

[062-049] Tillie’s vain-gloriousness would take another form. But Tillie had hoped

[062-050] that Thea would marry a title, and she did not boast much about

[062-051] Ottenburg,—at least not until after her memorable trip to Kansas City to

[062-052] hear Thea sing.

[062-053] Tillie is the last Kronborg left in Moonstone. She lives alone in a little

[062-054] house with a green yard, and keeps a fancywork and millinery store. Her

[062-055] business methods are informal, and she would never come out even at the end of

[062-056] the year, if she did not receive a draft for a good round sum from her niece at

[062-057] Christmas time. The arrival of this draft always renews the discussion as to

[062-058] what Thea would do for her aunt if she really did the right thing. Most of the

[062-059] Moonstone people think Thea ought to take Tillie to New York and keep her as a

[062-060] companion. While they are feeling sorry for Tillie because she does not live at

[062-061] the Plaza, Tillie is trying not to hurt their feelings by showing too plainly

[062-062] how much she realizes the superiority of her position. She tries to be modest

[062-063] when she complains to the postmaster that her New York paper is more than three

[062-064] days late. It means enough, surely, on the face of it, that she is the only

[062-065] person in Moonstone who takes a New York paper or who has any reason for taking

[062-066] one. A foolish young girl, Tillie lived in the splendid sorrows of

[062-067] “Wanda” and “Strathmore”; a foolish old girl, she lives

[062-068] in her niece’s triumphs. As she often says, she just missed going on the

[062-069] stage herself.

[062-070] That night after the sociable, as Tillie tripped home with a crowd of noisy

[062-071] boys and girls, she was perhaps a shade troubled. The twin’s question

[062-072] rather lingered in her ears. Did she, perhaps, insist too much on that thousand

[062-073] dollars? Surely, people didn’t for a minute think it was the money she

[062-074] cared about? As for that, Tillie tossed her head, she didn’t care a rap.

[062-075] They must understand that this money was different.

[062-076] When the laughing little group that brought her home had gone weaving down the

[062-077] sidewalk through the leafy shadows and had disappeared, Tillie brought out a

[062-078] rocking chair and sat down on her porch. On glorious, soft summer nights like

[062-079] this, when the moon is opulent and full, the day submerged and forgotten, she

[062-080] loves to sit there behind her rose-vine and let her fancy wander where it will.

[062-081] If you chanced to be passing down that Moonstone street and saw that alert

[062-082] white figure rocking there behind the screen of roses and lingering late into

[062-083] the night, you might feel sorry for her, and how mistaken you would be! Tillie

[062-084] lives in a little magic world, full of secret satisfactions. Thea Kronborg has

[062-085] given much noble pleasure to a world that needs all it can get, but to no

[062-086] individual has she given more than to her queer old aunt in Moonstone. The

[062-087] legend of Kronborg, the artist, fills Tillie’s life; she feels rich and

[062-088] exalted in it. What delightful things happen in her mind as she sits there

[062-089] rocking! She goes back to those early days of sand and sun, when Thea was a

[062-090] child and Tillie was herself, so it seems to her, “young.” When she

[062-091] used to hurry to church to hear Mr. Kronborg’s wonderful sermons, and

[062-092] when Thea used to stand up by the organ of a bright Sunday morning and sing

[062-093] “Come, Ye Disconsolate.” Or she thinks about that wonderful time

[062-094] when the Metropolitan Opera Company sang a week’s engagement in Kansas

[062-095] City, and Thea sent for her and had her stay with her at the Coates House and

[062-096] go to every performance at Convention Hall. Thea let Tillie go through her

[062-097] costume trunks and try on her wigs and jewels. And the kindness of Mr.

[062-098] Ottenburg! When Thea dined in her own room, he went down to dinner with Tillie,

[062-099] and never looked bored or absent-minded when she chattered. He took her to the

[062-100] hall the first time Thea sang there, and sat in the box with her and helped her

[062-101] through “Lohengrin.” After the first act, when Tillie turned

[062-102] tearful eyes to him and burst out, “I don’t care, she always seemed

[062-103] grand like that, even when she was a girl. I expect I’m crazy, but she

[062-104] just seems to me full of all them old times!”—Ottenburg was so

[062-105] sympathetic and patted her hand and said, “But that’s just what she

[062-106] is, full of the old times, and you are a wise woman to see it.” Yes, he

[062-107] said that to her. Tillie often wondered how she had been able to bear it when

[062-108] Thea came down the stairs in the wedding robe embroidered in silver, with a

[062-109] train so long it took six women to carry it.

[062-110] Tillie had lived fifty-odd years for that week, but she got it, and no miracle

[062-111] was ever more miraculous than that. When she used to be working in the fields

[062-112] on her father’s Minnesota farm, she couldn’t help believing that

[062-113] she would some day have to do with the “wonderful,” though her

[062-114] chances for it had then looked so slender.

[062-115] The morning after the sociable, Tillie, curled up in bed, was roused by the

[062-116] rattle of the milk cart down the street. Then a neighbor boy came down the

[062-117] sidewalk outside her window, singing “Casey Jones” as if he

[062-118] hadn’t a care in the world. By this time Tillie was wide awake. The

[062-119] twin’s question, and the subsequent laughter, came back with a faint

[062-120] twinge. Tillie knew she was short-sighted about facts, but this time—Why,

[062-121] there were her scrapbooks, full of newspaper and magazine articles about Thea,

[062-122] and half-tone cuts, snap-shots of her on land and sea, and photographs of her

[062-123] in all her parts. There, in her parlor, was the phonograph that had come from

[062-124] Mr. Ottenburg last June, on Thea’s birthday; she had only to go in there

[062-125] and turn it on, and let Thea speak for herself. Tillie finished brushing her

[062-126] white hair and laughed as she gave it a smart turn and brought it into her

[062-127] usual French twist. If Moonstone doubted, she had evidence enough: in black and

[062-128] white, in figures and photographs, evidence in hair lines on metal disks. For

[062-129] one who had so often seen two and two as making six, who had so often stretched

[062-130] a point, added a touch, in the good game of trying to make the world brighter

[062-131] than it is, there was positive bliss in having such deep foundations of

[062-132] support. She need never tremble in secret lest she might sometime stretch a

[062-133] point in Thea’s favor.—Oh, the comfort, to a soul too zealous, of

[062-134] having at last a rose so red it could not be further painted, a lily so truly

[062-135] auriferous that no amount of gilding could exceed the fact!

[062-136] Tillie hurried from her bedroom, threw open the doors and windows, and let the

[062-137] morning breeze blow through her little house.

[062-138] In two minutes a cob fire was roaring in her kitchen stove, in five she had set

[062-139] the table. At her household work Tillie was always bursting out with shrill

[062-140] snatches of song, and as suddenly stopping, right in the middle of a phrase, as

[062-141] if she had been struck dumb. She emerged upon the back porch with one of these

[062-142] bursts, and bent down to get her butter and cream out of the ice-box. The cat

[062-143] was purring on the bench and the morning-glories were thrusting their purple

[062-144] trumpets in through the lattice-work in a friendly way. They reminded Tillie

[062-145] that while she was waiting for the coffee to boil she could get some flowers

[062-146] for her breakfast table. She looked out uncertainly at a bush of sweet-briar

[062-147] that grew at the edge of her yard, off across the long grass and the tomato

[062-148] vines. The front porch, to be sure, was dripping with crimson ramblers that

[062-149] ought to be cut for the good of the vines; but never the rose in the hand for

[062-150] Tillie! She caught up the kitchen shears and off she dashed through grass and

[062-151] drenching dew. Snip, snip; the short-stemmed sweet-briars, salmon-pink and

[062-152] golden-hearted, with their unique and inimitable woody perfume, fell into her

[062-153] apron.

[062-154] After she put the eggs and toast on the table, Tillie took last Sunday’s

[062-155] New York paper from the rack beside the cupboard and sat down, with it for

[062-156] company. In the Sunday paper there was always a page about singers, even in

[062-157] summer, and that week the musical page began with a sympathetic account of

[062-158] Madame Kronborg’s first performance of *Isolde* in London. At the

[062-159] end of the notice, there was a short paragraph about her having sung for the

[062-160] King at Buckingham Palace and having been presented with a jewel by His

[062-161] Majesty.

[062-162] Singing for the King; but Goodness! she was always doing things like that!

[062-163] Tillie tossed her head. All through breakfast she kept sticking her sharp nose

[062-164] down into the glass of sweet-briar, with the old incredible lightness of heart,

[062-165] like a child’s balloon tugging at its string. She had always insisted,

[062-166] against all evidence, that life was full of fairy tales, and it was! She had

[062-167] been feeling a little down, perhaps, and Thea had answered her, from so far.

[062-168] From a common person, now, if you were troubled, you might get a letter. But

[062-169] Thea almost never wrote letters. She answered every one, friends and foes

[062-170] alike, in one way, her own way, her only way. Once more Tillie has to remind

[062-171] herself that it is all true, and is not something she has “made

[062-172] up.” Like all romancers, she is a little terrified at seeing one of her

[062-173] wildest conceits admitted by the hardheaded world. If our dream comes true, we

[062-174] are almost afraid to believe it; for that is the best of all good fortune, and

[062-175] nothing better can happen to any of us.

[062-176] When the people on Sylvester Street tire of Tillie’s stories, she goes

[062-177] over to the east part of town, where her legends are always welcome. The

[062-178] humbler people of Moonstone still live there. The same little houses sit under

[062-179] the cottonwoods; the men smoke their pipes in the front doorways, and the women

[062-180] do their washing in the back yard. The older women remember Thea, and how she

[062-181] used to come kicking her express wagon along the sidewalk, steering by the

[062-182] tongue and holding Thor in her lap. Not much happens in that part of town, and

[062-183] the people have long memories. A boy grew up on one of those streets who went

[062-184] to Omaha and built up a great business, and is now very rich. Moonstone people

[062-185] always speak of him and Thea together, as examples of Moonstone enterprise.

[062-186] They do, however, talk oftener of Thea. A voice has even a wider appeal than a

[062-187] fortune. It is the one gift that all creatures would possess if they could.

[062-188] Dreary Maggie Evans, dead nearly twenty years, is still remembered because Thea

[062-189] sang at her funeral “after she had studied in [[chicago|Chicago]].”

[062-190] However much they may smile at her, the old inhabitants would miss Tillie. Her

[062-191] stories give them something to talk about and to conjecture about, cut off as

[062-192] they are from the restless currents of the world. The many naked little

[062-193] sandbars which lie between Venice and the mainland, in the seemingly stagnant

[062-194] water of the lagoons, are made habitable and wholesome only because, every

[062-195] night, a foot and a half of tide creeps in from the sea and winds its fresh

[062-196] brine up through all that network of shining waterways. So, into all the little

[062-197] settlements of quiet people, tidings of what their boys and girls are doing in

[062-198] the world bring real refreshment; bring to the old, memories, and to the young,

[062-199] dreams.

[062-200] THE END
