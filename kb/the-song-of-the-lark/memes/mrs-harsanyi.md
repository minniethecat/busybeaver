## Overview

Mrs. Harsanyi is the wife of Andor Harsanyi, Thea Kronborg's first significant piano teacher in Chicago. Though she appears only in Chapter 23, during the dinner scene at the Harsanyis' home, she emerges as a warm, discerning presence who recognizes Thea's extraordinary qualities and helps smooth the social awkwardness of the young student from Moonstone. Her kindness provides a crucial counterbalance to the intense artistic pressure of Harsanyi's studio, helping Thea feel at home in a world far removed from her Colorado origins.

## Biography / Character Arc

### The Harsanyi Household

Mrs. Harsanyi and her husband live in Chicago, where Andor Harsanyi maintains his reputation as one of the city's finest piano teachers. She takes an active interest in her husband's pupils, and Thea Kronborg becomes a particular object of her attention. Thea comes to their house several times, once with Dr. Archie [023-114]. Mrs. Harsanyi's opinion of Thea is unequivocal: she "liked Miss Kronborg thoroughly" and felt that the girl could "reflect credit upon Harsanyi" [023-132].

### The Dinner Scene

The most detailed portrait of Mrs. Harsanyi emerges during the dinner at the Harsanyis' home. When Thea arrives, Mrs. Harsanyi takes her into her own room and helps her prepare for the evening with thoughtful attention to Thea's discomfort [023-149]. She notices that Thea wears high heels [023-163] and kindly comments on what "ought to be done" to Thea's hair [023-166]. When Thea looks "stern" under this scrutiny, Mrs. Harsanyi "knew" the look for what it was — the self-consciousness of a young woman unused to such attentions [023-169].

Throughout the dinner, Mrs. Harsanyi's steady presence balances the room. After she excuses herself to attend to household matters [023-175], her absence is noted. When Harsanyi returns from the studio "jaded and tired," Mrs. Harsanyi "sat more easily in her chair" as the dinner progressed [023-216], her quiet satisfaction in the evening's success evident.

### Observation of Thea

Mrs. Harsanyi is a careful observer. She notices Thea's "apparent lack of" something that puzzles Harsanyi [023-122] — suggesting a shared marital conversation about this unusual pupil. She "noticed this, also" when Thea's behavior shifts [023-121], indicating attentive, ongoing observation of the young woman who has become important to her husband's career.

## Relationships

### Andor Harsanyi

Mrs. Harsanyi is devoted to her husband's career and reputation. She evaluates his pupils through the lens of how they will "reflect credit upon Harsanyi" [023-133], marking her as both a supportive wife and a shrewd judge of talent. She and Harsanyi discuss Thea together, sharing observations about this remarkable pupil.

### Thea Kronborg

Mrs. Harsanyi's relationship with Thea is maternal and encouraging. She takes Thea into her own room to help her prepare for dinner [023-149], offers kind advice about her appearance [023-166], and expresses warm approval of her. Her treatment of Thea — respectful, generous, free of the cloying sentimentality of Mrs. Andersen or Tillie Kronborg — marks her as one of the healthiest female influences in Thea's Chicago years.

### Dr. Archie

Mrs. Harsanyi receives Dr. Archie when he visits with Thea [023-114], acting as hostess and facilitating the connection between Thea's old life in Moonstone and her new life in Chicago.

## Significance

Mrs. Harsanyi represents the quiet, enabling role that women patrons and supporters have historically played in artistic careers. She is not herself an artist, but her domestic generosity — the well-appointed dinners, the private room where she helps Thea with her hair, the warm approval she radiates — creates the social conditions in which artistic development can flourish. Unlike the oppressive, needy admiration of Tillie Kronborg or Mrs. Andersen, Mrs. Harsanyi's support is calm, adult, and freeing. She also serves as a narrative mirror: her observations of Thea, reported alongside Harsanyi's, give the reader a second, complementary perspective on the young singer's complex character.
