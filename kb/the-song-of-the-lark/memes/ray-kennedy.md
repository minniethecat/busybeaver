# Ray Kennedy

## Overview

Ray Kennedy is a figure of quiet tragedy in *The Song of the Lark*—a railroad man whose love for Thea Kronborg is never reciprocated in the way he hopes, but whose death in a railway accident provides the life insurance payout that funds her Chicago musical education. He is the novel's most poignant example of the way apparently peripheral figures can decisively shape an artist's destiny.

## The Railroad Man

Kennedy works on the trains that pass through Moonstone—the same trains that represent escape and possibility for Thea. His world is the railroad: its rhythms, its dangers, its particular masculine culture. He is drawn to Thea with a devotion that is touching in its simplicity: he sees in her something finer than himself, something worth protecting and supporting.

Kennedy's feelings for Thea are never presented as ridiculous or pathetic. Cather treats his devotion with respect, acknowledging the genuine emotion behind it even as she makes clear that Thea cannot return it in the way he desires. He is one of several characters in the novel whose love for Thea takes the form of service rather than possession.

## The Fatal Gift

Kennedy's death is the novel's most decisive plot turn. The life insurance policy he leaves to Thea—a gesture of devotion she did not ask for and could not refuse—provides the material means for her to leave Moonstone and pursue serious musical training in Chicago. The gift is freighted with irony: Kennedy, who loved Thea for the very qualities that would take her away from him, provides, in death, the means for that departure.

The novel does not dwell on the morbid aspects of this transaction. Kennedy's gift is presented as one of those accidents of fate that shape a life—tragic in itself, yet generative of something that transcends the tragedy. Without Ray Kennedy's death, Cather implies, there might have been no Thea Kronborg, Wagnerian soprano.

## Significance

Ray Kennedy embodies the novel's understanding that artistic destiny is shaped not only by the artist's own gifts and efforts but by the sacrifices—sometimes unwitting, sometimes fatal—of those who love and support her. He is the most extreme example of a pattern that runs throughout the novel: the people who give Thea what she needs often receive little in return.
