## XIX

[019-001] It is well for its peace of mind that the traveling public takes railroads so

[019-002] much for granted. The only men who are incurably nervous about railway travel

[019-003] are the railroad operatives. A railroad man never forgets that the next run may

[019-004] be his turn.

[019-005] On a single-track road, like that upon which [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] worked, the freight

[019-006] trains make their way as best they can between passenger trains. Even when

[019-007] there is such a thing as a freight time-schedule, it is merely a form. Along

[019-008] the one track dozens of fast and slow trains dash in both directions, kept from

[019-009] collision only by the brains in the dispatcher’s office. If one passenger

[019-010] train is late, the whole schedule must be revised in an instant; the trains

[019-011] following must be warned, and those moving toward the belated train must be

[019-012] assigned new meeting-places.

[019-013] Between the shifts and modifications of the passenger schedule, the freight

[019-014] trains play a game of their own. They have no right to the track at any given

[019-015] time, but are supposed to be on it when it is free, and to make the best time

[019-016] they can between passenger trains. A freight train, on a single-track road,

[019-017] gets anywhere at all only by stealing bases.

[019-018] Ray Kennedy had stuck to the freight service, although he had had opportunities

[019-019] to go into the passenger service at higher pay. He always regarded railroading

[019-020] as a temporary makeshift, until he “got into something,” and he

[019-021] disliked the passenger service. No brass buttons for him, he said; too much

[019-022] like a livery. While he was railroading he would wear a jumper, thank you!

[019-023] The wreck that “caught” Ray was a very commonplace one; nothing

[019-024] thrilling about it, and it got only six lines in the Denver papers. It happened

[019-025] about daybreak one morning, only thirty-two miles from home.

[019-026] At four o’clock in the morning Ray’s train had stopped to take

[019-027] water at Saxony, having just rounded the long curve which lies south of that

[019-028] station. It was Joe Giddy’s business to walk back along the curve about

[019-029] three hundred yards and put out torpedoes to warn any train which might be

[019-030] coming up from behind—a freight crew is not notified of trains following,

[019-031] and the brakeman is supposed to protect his train. Ray was so fussy about the

[019-032] punctilious observance of orders that almost any brakeman would take a chance

[019-033] once in a while, from natural perversity.

[019-034] When the train stopped for water that morning, Ray was at the desk in his

[019-035] caboose, making out his report. Giddy took his torpedoes, swung off the rear

[019-036] platform, and glanced back at the curve. He decided that he would not go back

[019-037] to flag this time. If anything was coming up behind, he could hear it in plenty

[019-038] of time. So he ran forward to look after a hot journal that had been bothering

[019-039] him. In a general way, Giddy’s reasoning was sound. If a freight train,

[019-040] or even a passenger train, had been coming up behind them, he could have heard

[019-041] it in time. But as it happened, a light engine, which made no noise at all, was

[019-042] coming,—ordered out to help with the freight that was piling up at the

[019-043] other end of the division. This engine got no warning, came round the curve,

[019-044] struck the caboose, went straight through it, and crashed into the heavy lumber

[019-045] car ahead.

[019-046] The Kronborgs were just sitting down to breakfast, when the night telegraph

[019-047] operator dashed into the yard at a run and hammered on the front door. Gunner

[019-048] answered the knock, and the telegraph operator told him he wanted to see his

[019-049] father a minute, quick. Mr. Kronborg appeared at the door, napkin in hand. The

[019-050] operator was pale and panting.

[019-051] “Fourteen was wrecked down at Saxony this morning,” he shouted,

[019-052] “and Kennedy’s all broke up. We’re sending an engine down

[019-053] with the doctor, and the operator at Saxony says Kennedy wants you to come

[019-054] along with us and bring your girl.” He stopped for breath.

[019-055] Mr. Kronborg took off his glasses and began rubbing them with his napkin.

[019-056] “Bring—I don’t understand,” he muttered. “How did

[019-057] this happen?”

[019-058] “No time for that, sir. Getting the engine out now. Your girl, Thea.

[019-059] You’ll surely do that for the poor chap. Everybody knows he thinks the

[019-060] world of her.” Seeing that Mr. Kronborg showed no indication of having

[019-061] made up his mind, the operator turned to Gunner. “Call your sister, kid.

[019-062] I’m going to ask the girl herself,” he blurted out.

[019-063] “Yes, yes, certainly. Daughter,” Mr. Kronborg called. He had

[019-064] somewhat recovered himself and reached to the hall hatrack for his hat.

[019-065] Just as Thea came out on the front porch, before the operator had had time to

[019-066] explain to her, [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]]’s ponies came up to the gate at a brisk trot.

[019-067] Archie jumped out the moment his driver stopped the team and came up to the

[019-068] bewildered girl without so much as saying good-morning to any one. He took her

[019-069] hand with the sympathetic, reassuring graveness which had helped her at more

[019-070] than one hard time in her life. “Get your hat, my girl. Kennedy’s

[019-071] hurt down the road, and he wants you to run down with me. They’ll have a

[019-072] car for us. Get into my buggy, Mr. Kronborg. I’ll drive you down, and

[019-073] Larry can come for the team.”

[019-074] The driver jumped out of the buggy and Mr. Kronborg and the doctor got in.

[019-075] Thea, still bewildered, sat on her father’s knee. Dr. Archie gave his

[019-076] ponies a smart cut with the whip.

[019-077] When they reached the depot, the engine, with one car attached, was standing on

[019-078] the main track. The engineer had got his steam up, and was leaning out of the

[019-079] cab impatiently. In a moment they were off. The run to Saxony took forty

[019-080] minutes. Thea sat still in her seat while Dr. Archie and her father talked

[019-081] about the wreck. She took no part in the conversation and asked no questions,

[019-082] but occasionally she looked at Dr. Archie with a frightened, inquiring glance,

[019-083] which he answered by an encouraging nod. Neither he nor her father said

[019-084] anything about how badly Ray was hurt. When the engine stopped near Saxony, the

[019-085] main track was already cleared. As they got out of the car, Dr. Archie pointed

[019-086] to a pile of ties.

[019-087] “Thea, you’d better sit down here and watch the wreck crew while

[019-088] your father and I go up and look Kennedy over. I’ll come back for you

[019-089] when I get him fixed up.”

[019-090] The two men went off up the sand gulch, and Thea sat down and looked at the

[019-091] pile of splintered wood and twisted iron that had lately been Ray’s

[019-092] caboose. She was frightened and absent-minded. She felt that she ought to be

[019-093] thinking about Ray, but her mind kept racing off to all sorts of trivial and

[019-094] irrelevant things. She wondered whether Grace Johnson would be furious when she

[019-095] came to take her music lesson and found nobody there to give it to her; whether

[019-096] she had forgotten to close the piano last night and whether Thor would get into

[019-097] the new room and mess the keys all up with his sticky fingers; whether Tillie

[019-098] would go upstairs and make her bed for her. Her mind worked fast, but she could

[019-099] fix it upon nothing. The grasshoppers, the lizards, distracted her attention

[019-100] and seemed more real to her than poor Ray.

[019-101] On their way to the sand bank where Ray had been carried, Dr. Archie and Mr.

[019-102] Kronborg met the Saxony doctor. He shook hands with them.

[019-103] “Nothing you can do, doctor. I couldn’t count the fractures. His

[019-104] back’s broken, too. He wouldn’t be alive now if he weren’t so

[019-105] confoundedly strong, poor chap. No use bothering him. I’ve given him

[019-106] morphia, one and a half, in eighths.”

[019-107] Dr. Archie hurried on. Ray was lying on a flat canvas litter, under the shelter

[019-108] of a shelving bank, lightly shaded by a slender cottonwood tree. When the

[019-109] doctor and the preacher approached, he looked at them intently.

[019-110] “Didn’t—” he closed his eyes to hide his bitter

[019-111] disappointment.

[019-112] Dr. Archie knew what was the matter. “Thea’s back there, Ray.

[019-113] I’ll bring her as soon as I’ve had a look at you.”

[019-114] Ray looked up. “You might clean me up a trifle, doc. Won’t need you

[019-115] for anything else, thank you all the same.”

[019-116] However little there was left of him, that little was certainly Ray Kennedy.

[019-117] His personality was as positive as ever, and the blood and dirt on his face

[019-118] seemed merely accidental, to have nothing to do with the man himself. Dr.

[019-119] Archie told Mr. Kronborg to bring a pail of water, and he began to sponge

[019-120] Ray’s face and neck. Mr. Kronborg stood by, nervously rubbing his hands

[019-121] together and trying to think of something to say. Serious situations always

[019-122] embarrassed him and made him formal, even when he felt real sympathy.

[019-123] “In times like this, Ray,” he brought out at last, crumpling up his

[019-124] handkerchief in his long fingers,—“in times like this, we

[019-125] don’t want to forget the Friend that sticketh closer than a

[019-126] brother.”

[019-127] Ray looked up at him; a lonely, disconsolate smile played over his mouth and

[019-128] his square cheeks. “Never mind about all that, *padre*,” he

[019-129] said quietly. “Christ and me fell out long ago.”

[019-130] There was a moment of silence. Then Ray took pity on Mr. Kronborg’s

[019-131] embarrassment. “You go back for the little girl, *padre*. I want a

[019-132] word with the doc in private.”

[019-133] Ray talked to Dr. Archie for a few moments, then stopped suddenly, with a broad

[019-134] smile. Over the doctor’s shoulder he saw Thea coming up the gulch, in her

[019-135] pink chambray dress, carrying her sun-hat by the strings. Such a yellow head!

[019-136] He often told himself that he “was perfectly foolish about her

[019-137] hair.” The sight of her, coming, went through him softly, like the

[019-138] morphia. “There she is,” he whispered. “Get the old preacher

[019-139] out of the way, doc. I want to have a little talk with her.”

[019-140] Dr. Archie looked up. Thea was hurrying and yet hanging back. She was more

[019-141] frightened than he had thought she would be. She had gone with him to see very

[019-142] sick people and had always been steady and calm. As she came up, she looked at

[019-143] the ground, and he could see that she had been crying.

[019-144] Ray Kennedy made an unsuccessful effort to put out his hand. “Hello,

[019-145] little kid, nothing to be afraid of. Darned if I don’t believe

[019-146] they’ve gone and scared you! Nothing to cry about. I’m the same old

[019-147] goods, only a little dented. Sit down on my coat there, and keep me company.

[019-148] I’ve got to lay still a bit.”

[019-149] Dr. Archie and Mr. Kronborg disappeared. Thea cast a timid glance after them,

[019-150] but she sat down resolutely and took Ray’s hand.

[019-151] “You ain’t scared now, are you?” he asked affectionately.

[019-152] “You were a regular brick to come, Thee. Did you get any

[019-153] breakfast?”

[019-154] “No, Ray, I’m not scared. Only I’m dreadful sorry

[019-155] you’re hurt, and I can’t help crying.”

[019-156] His broad, earnest face, languid from the opium and smiling with such simple

[019-157] happiness, reassured her. She drew nearer to him and lifted his hand to her

[019-158] knee. He looked at her with his clear, shallow blue eyes. How he loved

[019-159] everything about that face and head! How many nights in his cupola, looking up

[019-160] the track, he had seen that face in the darkness; through the sleet and snow,

[019-161] or in the soft blue air when the moonlight slept on the desert.

[019-162] “You needn’t bother to talk, Thee. The doctor’s medicine

[019-163] makes me sort of dopey. But it’s nice to have company. Kind of cozy,

[019-164] don’t you think? Pull my coat under you more. It’s a darned shame I

[019-165] can’t wait on you.”

[019-166] “No, no, Ray. I’m all right. Yes, I like it here. And I guess you

[019-167] ought not to talk much, ought you? If you can sleep, I’ll stay right

[019-168] here, and be awful quiet. I feel just as much at home with you as ever,

[019-169] now.”

[019-170] That simple, humble, faithful something in Ray’s eyes went straight to

[019-171] Thea’s heart. She did feel comfortable with him, and happy to give him so

[019-172] much happiness. It was the first time she had ever been conscious of that power

[019-173] to bestow intense happiness by simply being near any one. She always remembered

[019-174] this day as the beginning of that knowledge. She bent over him and put her lips

[019-175] softly to his cheek.

[019-176] Ray’s eyes filled with light. “Oh, do that again, kid!” he

[019-177] said impulsively. Thea kissed him on the forehead, blushing faintly. Ray held

[019-178] her hand fast and closed his eyes with a deep sigh of happiness. The morphia

[019-179] and the sense of her nearness filled him with content. The gold mine, the oil

[019-180] well, the copper ledge—all pipe dreams, he mused, and this was a dream,

[019-181] too. He might have known it before. It had always been like that; the things he

[019-182] admired had always been away out of his reach: a college education, a

[019-183] gentleman’s manner, an Englishman’s accent—things over his

[019-184] head. And Thea was farther out of his reach than all the rest put together. He

[019-185] had been a fool to imagine it, but he was glad he had been a fool. She had

[019-186] given him one grand dream. Every mile of his run, from Moonstone to Denver, was

[019-187] painted with the colors of that hope. Every cactus knew about it. But now that

[019-188] it was not to be, he knew the truth. Thea was never meant for any rough fellow

[019-189] like him—hadn’t he really known that all along, he asked himself?

[019-190] She wasn’t meant for common men. She was like wedding cake, a thing to

[019-191] dream on. He raised his eyelids a little. She was stroking his hand and looking

[019-192] off into the distance. He felt in her face that look of unconscious power that

[019-193] Wunsch had seen there. Yes, she was bound for the big terminals of the world;

[019-194] no way stations for her. His lids drooped. In the dark he could see her as she

[019-195] would be after a while; in a box at the Tabor Grand in Denver, with diamonds on

[019-196] her neck and a tiara in her yellow hair, with all the people looking at her

[019-197] through their opera-glasses, and a United States Senator, maybe, talking to

[019-198] her. “Then you’ll remember me!” He opened his eyes, and they

[019-199] were full of tears.

[019-200] Thea leaned closer. “What did you say, Ray? I couldn’t hear.”

[019-201] “Then you’ll remember me,” he whispered.

[019-202] The spark in his eye, which is one’s very self, caught the spark in hers

[019-203] that was herself, and for a moment they looked into each other’s natures.

[019-204] Thea realized how good and how great-hearted he was, and he realized about her

[019-205] many things. When that elusive spark of personality retreated in each of them,

[019-206] Thea still saw in his wet eyes her own face, very small, but much prettier than

[019-207] the cracked glass at home had ever shown it. It was the first time she had seen

[019-208] her face in that kindest mirror a woman can ever find.

[019-209] Ray had felt things in that moment when he seemed to be looking into the very

[019-210] soul of [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]]. Yes, the gold mine, the oil well, the copper ledge,

[019-211] they’d all got away from him, as things will; but he’d backed a

[019-212] winner once in his life! With all his might he gave his faith to the broad

[019-213] little hand he held. He wished he could leave her the rugged strength of his

[019-214] body to help her through with it all. He would have liked to tell her a little

[019-215] about his old dream,—there seemed long years between him and it

[019-216] already,—but to tell her now would somehow be unfair; wouldn’t be

[019-217] quite the straightest thing in the world. Probably she knew, anyway. He looked

[019-218] up quickly. “You know, don’t you, Thee, that I think you are just

[019-219] the finest thing I’ve struck in this world?”

[019-220] The tears ran down Thea’s cheeks. “You’re too good to me,

[019-221] Ray. You’re a lot too good to me,” she faltered.

[019-222] “Why, kid,” he murmured, “everybody in this world’s

[019-223] going to be good to you!”

[019-224] Dr. Archie came to the gulch and stood over his patient. “How’s it

[019-225] going?”

[019-226] “Can’t you give me another punch with your pacifier, doc? The

[019-227] little girl had better run along now.” Ray released Thea’s hand.

[019-228] “See you later, Thee.”

[019-229] She got up and moved away aimlessly, carrying her hat by the strings. Ray

[019-230] looked after her with the exaltation born of bodily pain and said between his

[019-231] teeth, “Always look after that girl, doc. She’s a queen!”

[019-232] Thea and her father went back to Moonstone on the one-o’clock passenger.

[019-233] Dr. Archie stayed with Ray Kennedy until he died, late in the afternoon.
