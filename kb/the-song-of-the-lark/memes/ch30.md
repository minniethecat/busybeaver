## X

[030-001] [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]] had no shop of his own, but he kept a table and an order-book in

[030-002] one corner of the drug store where paints and wall-paper were sold, and he was

[030-003] sometimes to be found there for an hour or so about noon. Thea had gone into

[030-004] the drug store to have a friendly chat with the proprietor, who used to lend

[030-005] her books from his shelves. She found Johnny there, trimming rolls of

[030-006] wall-paper for the parlor of Banker Smith’s new house. She sat down on

[030-007] the top of his table and watched him.

[030-008] “Johnny,” she said suddenly, “I want you to write down the

[030-009] words of that Mexican serenade you used to sing; you know, ‘*Rosa de

[030-010] Noche*.’ It’s an unusual song. I’m going to study it. I

[030-011] know enough Spanish for that.”

[030-012] Johnny looked up from his roller with his bright, affable smile.

[030-013] “*Si*, but it is low for you, I think; *voz contralto*. It is

[030-014] low for me.”

[030-015] “Nonsense. I can do more with my low voice than I used to. I’ll

[030-016] show you. Sit down and write it out for me, please.” Thea beckoned him

[030-017] with the short yellow pencil tied to his order-book.

[030-018] Johnny ran his fingers through his curly black hair. “If you wish. I do

[030-019] not know if that *serenata* all right for young ladies. Down there it is

[030-020] more for married ladies. They sing it for husbands—or somebody else,

[030-021] may-bee.” Johnny’s eyes twinkled and he apologized gracefully with

[030-022] his shoulders. He sat down at the table, and while Thea looked over his arm,

[030-023] began to write the song down in a long, slanting script, with highly ornamental

[030-024] capitals. Presently he looked up. “This-a song not exactly

[030-025] Mexican,” he said thoughtfully. “It come from farther down; Brazil,

[030-026] Venezuela, may-bee. I learn it from some fellow down there, and he learn it

[030-027] from another fellow. It is-a most like Mexican, but not quite.” Thea did

[030-028] not release him, but pointed to the paper. There were three verses of the song

[030-029] in all, and when Johnny had written them down, he sat looking at them

[030-030] meditatively, his head on one side. “I don’ think for a high voice,

[030-031] *señorita*,” he objected with polite persistence. “How you

[030-032] accompany with piano?”

[030-033] “Oh, that will be easy enough.”

[030-034] “For you, may-bee!” Johnny smiled and drummed on the table with the

[030-035] tips of his agile brown fingers. “You know something? Listen, I tell

[030-036] you.” He rose and sat down on the table beside her, putting his foot on

[030-037] the chair. He loved to talk at the hour of noon. “When you was a little

[030-038] girl, no bigger than that, you come to my house one day ’bout noon, like

[030-039] this, and I was in the door, playing guitar. You was barehead, barefoot; you

[030-040] run away from home. You stand there and make a frown at me an’ listen. By

[030-041] ’n by you say for me to sing. I sing some lil’ ting, and then I say

[030-042] for you to sing with me. You don’ know no words, of course, but you take

[030-043] the air and you sing it justa beauti-ful! I never see a child do that, outside

[030-044] Mexico. You was, oh, I do’ know—seven year, may-bee. By ’n by

[030-045] the preacher come look for you and begin for scold. I say, ‘Don’

[030-046] scold, Meester Kronborg. She come for hear guitar. She gotta some music in her,

[030-047] that child. Where she get?’ Then he tell me ’bout your

[030-048] gran’papa play oboe in the old country. I never forgetta that

[030-049] time.” Johnny chuckled softly.

[030-050] Thea nodded. “I remember that day, too. I liked your music better than

[030-051] the church music. When are you going to have a dance over there, Johnny?”

[030-052] Johnny tilted his head. “Well, Saturday night the Spanish boys have a

[030-053] lil’ party, some *danza*. You know Miguel Ramas? He have some young

[030-054] cousins, two boys, very nice-a, come from Torreon. They going to Salt Lake for

[030-055] some job-a, and stay off with him two-three days, and he mus’ have a

[030-056] party. You like to come?”

[030-057] That was how Thea came to go to the Mexican ball. Mexican Town had been

[030-058] increased by half a dozen new families during the last few years, and the

[030-059] Mexicans had put up an adobe dance-hall, that looked exactly like one of their

[030-060] own dwellings, except that it was a little longer, and was so unpretentious

[030-061] that nobody in Moonstone knew of its existence. The “Spanish boys”

[030-062] are reticent about their own affairs. [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] used to know about all their

[030-063] little doings, but since his death there was no one whom the Mexicans

[030-064] considered *simpatico*.

[030-065] On Saturday evening after supper Thea told her mother that she was going over

[030-066] to Mrs. Tellamantez’s to watch the Mexicans dance for a while, and that

[030-067] Johnny would bring her home.

[030-068] [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] smiled. She noticed that Thea had put on a white dress and had

[030-069] done her hair up with unusual care, and that she carried her best blue scarf.

[030-070] “Maybe you’ll take a turn yourself, eh? I wouldn’t mind

[030-071] watching them Mexicans. They’re lovely dancers.”

[030-072] Thea made a feeble suggestion that her mother might go with her, but Mrs.

[030-073] Kronborg was too wise for that. She knew that Thea would have a better time if

[030-074] she went alone, and she watched her daughter go out of the gate and down the

[030-075] sidewalk that led to the depot.

[030-076] Thea walked slowly. It was a soft, rosy evening. The sand hills were lavender.

[030-077] The sun had gone down a glowing copper disk, and the fleecy clouds in the east

[030-078] were a burning rose-color, flecked with gold. Thea passed the cottonwood grove

[030-079] and then the depot, where she left the sidewalk and took the sandy path toward

[030-080] Mexican Town. She could hear the scraping of violins being tuned, the tinkle of

[030-081] mandolins, and the growl of a double bass. Where had they got a double bass?

[030-082] She did not know there was one in Moonstone. She found later that it was the

[030-083] property of one of Ramas’s young cousins, who was taking it to Utah with

[030-084] him to cheer him at his “job-a.”

[030-085] The Mexicans never wait until it is dark to begin to dance, and Thea had no

[030-086] difficulty in finding the new hall, because every other house in the town was

[030-087] deserted. Even the babies had gone to the ball; a neighbor was always willing

[030-088] to hold the baby while the mother danced. Mrs. Tellamantez came out to meet

[030-089] Thea and led her in. Johnny bowed to her from the platform at the end of the

[030-090] room, where he was playing the mandolin along with two fiddles and the bass.

[030-091] The hall was a long low room, with whitewashed walls, a fairly tight plank

[030-092] floor, wooden benches along the sides, and a few bracket lamps screwed to the

[030-093] frame timbers. There must have been fifty people there, counting the children.

[030-094] The Mexican dances were very much family affairs. The fathers always danced

[030-095] again and again with their little daughters, as well as with their wives. One

[030-096] of the girls came up to greet Thea, her dark cheeks glowing with pleasure and

[030-097] cordiality, and introduced her brother, with whom she had just been dancing.

[030-098] “You better take him every time he asks you,” she whispered.

[030-099] “He’s the best dancer here, except Johnny.”

[030-100] Thea soon decided that the poorest dancer was herself. Even Mrs. Tellamantez,

[030-101] who always held her shoulders so stiffly, danced better than she did. The

[030-102] musicians did not remain long at their post. When one of them felt like

[030-103] dancing, he called some other boy to take his instrument, put on his coat, and

[030-104] went down on the floor. Johnny, who wore a blousy white silk shirt, did not

[030-105] even put on his coat.

[030-106] The dances the railroad men gave in Firemen’s Hall were the only dances

[030-107] Thea had ever been allowed to go to, and they were very different from this.

[030-108] The boys played rough jokes and thought it smart to be clumsy and to run into

[030-109] each other on the floor. For the square dances there was always the bawling

[030-110] voice of the caller, who was also the county auctioneer.

[030-111] This Mexican dance was soft and quiet. There was no calling, the conversation

[030-112] was very low, the rhythm of the music was smooth and engaging, the men were

[030-113] graceful and courteous. Some of them Thea had never before seen out of their

[030-114] working clothes, smeared with grease from the round-house or clay from the

[030-115] brickyard. Sometimes, when the music happened to be a popular Mexican waltz

[030-116] song, the dancers sang it softly as they moved. There were three little girls

[030-117] under twelve, in their first communion dresses, and one of them had an orange

[030-118] marigold in her black hair, just over her ear. They danced with the men and

[030-119] with each other. There was an atmosphere of ease and friendly pleasure in the

[030-120] low, dimly lit room, and Thea could not help wondering whether the Mexicans had

[030-121] no jealousies or neighborly grudges as the people in Moonstone had. There was

[030-122] no constraint of any kind there to-night, but a kind of natural harmony about

[030-123] their movements, their greetings, their low conversation, their smiles.

[030-124] Ramas brought up his two young cousins, Silvo and Felipe, and presented them.

[030-125] They were handsome, smiling youths, of eighteen and twenty, with pale-gold

[030-126] skins, smooth cheeks, aquiline features, and wavy black hair, like

[030-127] Johnny’s. They were dressed alike, in black velvet jackets and soft silk

[030-128] shirts, with opal shirt-buttons and flowing black ties looped through gold

[030-129] rings. They had charming manners, and low, guitar-like voices. They knew almost

[030-130] no English, but a Mexican boy can pay a great many compliments with a very

[030-131] limited vocabulary. The Ramas boys thought Thea dazzlingly beautiful. They had

[030-132] never seen a Scandinavian girl before, and her hair and fair skin bewitched

[030-133] them. “*Blanco y oro, semjante la Pascua!*” (White and gold,

[030-134] like Easter!) they exclaimed to each other. Silvo, the younger, declared that

[030-135] he could never go on to Utah; that he and his double bass had reached their

[030-136] ultimate destination. The elder was more crafty; he asked Miguel Ramas whether

[030-137] there would be “plenty more girls like that *a* Salt Lake,

[030-138] maybee?”

[030-139] Silvo, overhearing, gave his brother a contemptuous glance. “Plenty more

[030-140] *a Paraíso* may-bee!” he retorted. When they were not dancing with

[030-141] her, their eyes followed her, over the coiffures of their other partners. That

[030-142] was not difficult; one blonde head moving among so many dark ones.

[030-143] Thea had not meant to dance much, but the Ramas boys danced so well and were so

[030-144] handsome and adoring that she yielded to their entreaties. When she sat out a

[030-145] dance with them, they talked to her about their family at home, and told her

[030-146] how their mother had once punned upon their name. *Rama*, in Spanish,

[030-147] meant a branch, they explained. Once when they were little lads their mother

[030-148] took them along when she went to help the women decorate the church for Easter.

[030-149] Some one asked her whether she had brought any flowers, and she replied that

[030-150] she had brought her “ramas.” This was evidently a cherished family

[030-151] story.

[030-152] When it was nearly midnight, Johnny announced that every one was going to his

[030-153] house to have “some lil’ icecream and some lil’

[030-154] *musica*.” He began to put out the lights and Mrs. Tellamantez led

[030-155] the way across the square to her *casa*. The Ramas brothers escorted Thea,

[030-156] and as they stepped out of the door, Silvo exclaimed, “*Hace

[030-157] frio!*” and threw his velvet coat about her shoulders.

[030-158] Most of the company followed Mrs. Tellamantez, and they sat about on the gravel

[030-159] in her little yard while she and Johnny and Mrs. Miguel Ramas served the

[030-160] ice-cream. Thea sat on Felipe’s coat, since Silvo’s was already

[030-161] about her shoulders. The youths lay down on the shining gravel beside her, one

[030-162] on her right and one on her left. Johnny already called them “*los

[030-163] acolitos*,” the altar-boys. The talk all about them was low, and

[030-164] indolent. One of the girls was playing on Johnny’s guitar, another was

[030-165] picking lightly at a mandolin. The moonlight was so bright that one could see

[030-166] every glance and smile, and the flash of their teeth. The moonflowers over Mrs.

[030-167] Tellamantez’s door were wide open and of an unearthly white. The moon

[030-168] itself looked like a great pale flower in the sky.

[030-169] After all the ice-cream was gone, Johnny approached Thea, his guitar under his

[030-170] arm, and the elder Ramas boy politely gave up his place. Johnny sat down, took

[030-171] a long breath, struck a fierce chord, and then hushed it with his other hand.

[030-172] “Now we have some lil’ *serenata*, eh? You wan’ a

[030-173] try?”

[030-174] When Thea began to sing, instant silence fell upon the company. She felt all

[030-175] those dark eyes fix themselves upon her intently. She could see them shine. The

[030-176] faces came out of the shadow like the white flowers over the door. Felipe

[030-177] leaned his head upon his hand. Silvo dropped on his back and lay looking at the

[030-178] moon, under the impression that he was still looking at Thea. When she finished

[030-179] the first verse, Thea whispered to Johnny, “Again, I can do it better

[030-180] than that.”

[030-181] She had sung for churches and funerals and teachers, but she had never before

[030-182] sung for a really musical people, and this was the first time she had ever felt

[030-183] the response that such a people can give. They turned themselves and all they

[030-184] had over to her. For the moment they cared about nothing in the world but what

[030-185] she was doing. Their faces confronted her, open, eager, unprotected. She felt

[030-186] as if all these warm-blooded people debouched into her. Mrs.

[030-187] Tellamantez’s fateful resignation, Johnny’s madness, the adoration

[030-188] of the boy who lay still in the sand; in an instant these things seemed to be

[030-189] within her instead of without, as if they had come from her in the first place.

[030-190] When she finished, her listeners broke into excited murmur. The men began

[030-191] hunting feverishly for cigarettes. Famos Serranos the barytone bricklayer,

[030-192] touched Johnny’s arm, gave him a questioning look, then heaved a deep

[030-193] sigh. Johnny dropped on his elbow, wiping his face and neck and hands with his

[030-194] handkerchief. “*Señorita*,” he panted, “if you sing like

[030-195] that once in the City of Mexico, they just-a go crazy. In the City of Mexico

[030-196] they ain’t-a sit like stumps when they hear that, not-a much! When they

[030-197] like, they just-a give you the town.”

[030-198] Thea laughed. She, too, was excited. “Think so, Johnny? Come, sing

[030-199] something with me. *El Parreño;* I haven’t sung that for a long

[030-200] time.”

[030-201] Johnny laughed and hugged his guitar. “You not-a forget him?” He

[030-202] began teasing his strings. “Come!” He threw back his head,

[030-203] “*Anoche-e-e*—”

[030-204] “Anoche me confesse\

[030-205] Con un padre carmelite,\

[030-206] Y me dio penitencia\

[030-207] Que besaras tu boquita.”

[030-208] (Last night I made confession\

[030-209] With a Carmelite father,\

[030-210] And he gave me absolution\

[030-211] For the kisses you imprinted.)

[030-212] Johnny had almost every fault that a tenor can have. His voice was thin,

[030-213] unsteady, husky in the middle tones. But it was distinctly a voice, and

[030-214] sometimes he managed to get something very sweet out of it. Certainly it made

[030-215] him happy to sing. Thea kept glancing down at him as he lay there on his elbow.

[030-216] His eyes seemed twice as large as usual and had lights in them like those the

[030-217] moonlight makes on black, running water. Thea remembered the old stories about

[030-218] his “spells.” She had never seen him when his madness was on him,

[030-219] but she felt something tonight at her elbow that gave her an idea of what it

[030-220] might be like. For the first time she fully understood the cryptic explanation

[030-221] that Mrs. Tellamantez had made to [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]], long ago. There were the same

[030-222] shells along the walk; she believed she could pick out the very one. There was

[030-223] the same moon up yonder, and panting at her elbow was the same

[030-224] Johnny—fooled by the same old things!

[030-225] When they had finished, Famos, the barytone, murmured something to Johnny; who

[030-226] replied, “Sure we can sing ‘Trovatore.’ We have no alto, but

[030-227] all the girls can sing alto and make some noise.”

[030-228] The women laughed. Mexican women of the poorer class do not sing like the men.

[030-229] Perhaps they are too indolent. In the evening, when the men are singing their

[030-230] throats dry on the doorstep, or around the camp-fire beside the work-train, the

[030-231] women usually sit and comb their hair.

[030-232] While Johnny was gesticulating and telling everybody what to sing and how to

[030-233] sing it, Thea put out her foot and touched the corpse of Silvo with the toe of

[030-234] her slipper. “Aren’t you going to sing, Silvo?” she asked

[030-235] teasingly.

[030-236] The boy turned on his side and raised himself on his elbow for a moment.

[030-237] “Not this night, *señorita*,” he pleaded softly, “not

[030-238] this night!” He dropped back again, and lay with his cheek on his right

[030-239] arm, the hand lying passive on the sand above his head.

[030-240] “How does he flatten himself into the ground like that?” Thea asked

[030-241] herself. “I wish I knew. It’s very effective, somehow.”

[030-242] Across the gulch the Kohlers’ little house slept among its trees, a dark

[030-243] spot on the white face of the desert. The windows of their upstairs bedroom

[030-244] were open, and Paulina had listened to the dance music for a long while before

[030-245] she drowsed off. She was a light sleeper, and when she woke again, after

[030-246] midnight, Johnny’s concert was at its height. She lay still until she

[030-247] could bear it no longer. Then she wakened Fritz and they went over to the

[030-248] window and leaned out. They could hear clearly there.

[030-249] “*Die Thea*,” whispered Mrs. Kohler; “it must be.

[030-250] *Ach, wunderschön!*”

[030-251] Fritz was not so wide awake as his wife. He grunted and scratched on the floor

[030-252] with his bare foot. They were listening to a Mexican part-song; the tenor, then

[030-253] the soprano, then both together; the barytone joins them, rages, is

[030-254] extinguished; the tenor expires in sobs, and the soprano finishes alone. When

[030-255] the soprano’s last note died away, Fritz nodded to his wife.

[030-256] “*Ja*,” he said; “*schön*.”

[030-257] There was silence for a few moments. Then the guitar sounded fiercely, and

[030-258] several male voices began the sextette from “Lucia.” Johnny’s

[030-259] reedy tenor they knew well, and the bricklayer’s big, opaque barytone;

[030-260] the others might be anybody over there—just Mexican voices. Then at the

[030-261] appointed, at the acute, moment, the soprano voice, like a fountain jet, shot

[030-262] up into the light. “*Horch! Horch!*” the old people whispered,

[030-263] both at once. How it leaped from among those dusky male voices! How it played

[030-264] in and about and around and over them, like a goldfish darting among creek

[030-265] minnows, like a yellow butterfly soaring above a swarm of dark ones.

[030-266] “Ah,” said Mrs. Kohler softly, “the dear man; if he could

[030-267] hear her now!”
