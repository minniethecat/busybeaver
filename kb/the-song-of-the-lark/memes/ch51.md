## I

[051-001] It is a glorious winter day. Denver, standing on her high plateau under a

[051-002] thrilling green-blue sky, is masked in snow and glittering with sunlight. The

[051-003] Capitol building is actually in armor, and throws off the shafts of the sun

[051-004] until the beholder is dazzled and the outlines of the building are lost in a

[051-005] blaze of reflected light. The stone terrace is a white field over which fiery

[051-006] reflections dance, and the trees and bushes are faithfully repeated in

[051-007] snow—on every black twig a soft, blurred line of white. From the terrace

[051-008] one looks directly over to where the mountains break in their sharp, familiar

[051-009] lines against the sky. Snow fills the gorges, hangs in scarfs on the great

[051-010] slopes, and on the peaks the fiery sunshine is gathered up as by a

[051-011] burning-glass.

[051-012] Howard Archie is standing at the window of his private room in the offices of

[051-013] the San Felipe Mining Company, on the sixth floor of the Raton Building,

[051-014] looking off at the mountain glories of his State while he gives dictation to

[051-015] his secretary. He is ten years older than when we saw him last, and

[051-016] emphatically ten years more prosperous. A decade of coming into things has not

[051-017] so much aged him as it has fortified, smoothed, and assured him. His sandy hair

[051-018] and imperial conceal whatever gray they harbor. He has not grown heavier, but

[051-019] more flexible, and his massive shoulders carry fifty years and the control of

[051-020] his great mining interests more lightly than they carried forty years and a

[051-021] country practice. In short, he is one of the friends to whom we feel grateful

[051-022] for having got on in the world, for helping to keep up the general temperature

[051-023] and our own confidence in life. He is an acquaintance that one would hurry to

[051-024] overtake and greet among a hundred. In his warm handshake and generous smile

[051-025] there is the stimulating cordiality of good fellows come into good fortune and

[051-026] eager to pass it on; something that makes one think better of the lottery of

[051-027] life and resolve to try again.

[051-028] When Archie had finished his morning mail, he turned away from the window and

[051-029] faced his secretary. “Did anything come up yesterday afternoon while I

[051-030] was away, T. B.?”

[051-031] Thomas Burk turned over the leaf of his calendar. “Governor Alden sent

[051-032] down to say that he wanted to see you before he sends his letter to the Board

[051-033] of Pardons. Asked if you could go over to the State House this morning.”

[051-034] Archie shrugged his shoulders. “I’ll think about it.”

[051-035] The young man grinned.

[051-036] “Anything else?” his chief continued.

[051-037] T. B. swung round in his chair with a look of interest on his shrewd,

[051-038] clean-shaven face. “Old Jasper Flight was in, [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]]. I never

[051-039] expected to see him alive again. Seems he’s tucked away for the winter

[051-040] with a sister who’s a housekeeper at the Oxford. He’s all crippled

[051-041] up with rheumatism, but as fierce after it as ever. Wants to know if you or the

[051-042] company won’t grub-stake him again. Says he’s sure of it this time;

[051-043] had located something when the snow shut down on him in December. He wants to

[051-044] crawl out at the first break in the weather, with that same old burro with the

[051-045] split ear. He got somebody to winter the beast for him. He’s

[051-046] superstitious about that burro, too; thinks it’s divinely guided. You

[051-047] ought to hear the line of talk he put up here yesterday; said when he rode in

[051-048] his carriage, that burro was a-going to ride along with him.”

[051-049] Archie laughed. “Did he leave you his address?”

[051-050] “He didn’t neglect anything,” replied the clerk cynically.

[051-051] “Well, send him a line and tell him to come in again. I like to hear him.

[051-052] Of all the crazy prospectors I’ve ever known, he’s the most

[051-053] interesting, because he’s really crazy. It’s a religious conviction

[051-054] with him, and with most of ’em it’s a gambling fever or pure

[051-055] vagrancy. But Jasper Flight believes that the Almighty keeps the secret of the

[051-056] silver deposits in these hills, and gives it away to the deserving. He’s

[051-057] a downright noble figure. Of course I’ll stake him! As long as he can

[051-058] crawl out in the spring. He and that burro are a sight together. The beast is

[051-059] nearly as white as Jasper; must be twenty years old.”

[051-060] “If you stake him this time, you won’t have to again,” said

[051-061] T. B. knowingly. “He’ll croak up there, mark my word. Says he never

[051-062] ties the burro at night now, for fear he might be called sudden, and the beast

[051-063] would starve. I guess that animal could eat a lariat rope, all right, and enjoy

[051-064] it.”

[051-065] “I guess if we knew the things those two have eaten, and haven’t

[051-066] eaten, in their time, T. B., it would make us vegetarians.” The doctor

[051-067] sat down and looked thoughtful. “That’s the way for the old man to

[051-068] go. It would be pretty hard luck if he had to die in a hospital. I wish he

[051-069] could turn up something before he cashes in. But his kind seldom do;

[051-070] they’re bewitched. Still, there was Stratton. I’ve been meeting

[051-071] Jasper Flight, and his side meat and tin pans, up in the mountains for years,

[051-072] and I’d miss him. I always halfway believe the fairy tales he spins me.

[051-073] Old Jasper Flight,” Archie murmured, as if he liked the name or the

[051-074] picture it called up.

[051-075] A clerk came in from the outer office and handed Archie a card. He sprang up

[051-076] and exclaimed, “Mr. Ottenburg? Bring him in.”

[051-077] [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] entered, clad in a long, fur-lined coat, holding a checked-cloth

[051-078] hat in his hand, his cheeks and eyes bright with the outdoor cold. The two men

[051-079] met before Archie’s desk and their handclasp was longer than friendship

[051-080] prompts except in regions where the blood warms and quickens to meet the dry

[051-081] cold. Under the general keyingup of the altitude, manners take on a heartiness,

[051-082] a vivacity, that is one expression of the half-unconscious excitement which

[051-083] Colorado people miss when they drop into lower strata of air. The heart, we are

[051-084] told, wears out early in that high atmosphere, but while it pumps it sends out

[051-085] no sluggish stream. Our two friends stood gripping each other by the hand and

[051-086] smiling.

[051-087] “When did you get in, Fred? And what have you come for?” Archie

[051-088] gave him a quizzical glance.

[051-089] “I’ve come to find out what you think you’re doing out

[051-090] here,” the younger man declared emphatically. “I want to get next,

[051-091] I do. When can you see me?”

[051-092] “Anything on to-night? Then suppose you dine with me. Where can I pick

[051-093] you up at five-thirty?”

[051-094] “Bixby’s office, general freight agent of the Burlington.”

[051-095] Ottenburg began to button his overcoat and drew on his gloves.

[051-096] “I’ve got to have one shot at you before I go, Archie. Didn’t

[051-097] I tell you Pinky Alden was a cheap squirt?”

[051-098] Alden’s backer laughed and shook his head. “Oh, he’s worse

[051-099] than that, Fred. It isn’t polite to mention what he is, outside of the

[051-100] Arabian Nights. I guessed you’d come to rub it into me.”

[051-101] Ottenburg paused, his hand on the doorknob, his high color challenging the

[051-102] doctor’s calm. “I’m disgusted with you, Archie, for training

[051-103] with such a pup. A man of your experience!”

[051-104] “Well, he’s been an experience,” Archie muttered.

[051-105] “I’m not coy about admitting it, am I?”

[051-106] Ottenburg flung open the door. “Small credit to you. Even the women are

[051-107] out for capital and corruption, I hear. Your Governor’s done more for the

[051-108] United Breweries in six months than I’ve been able to do in six years.

[051-109] He’s the lily-livered sort we’re looking for. Good-morning.”

[051-110] That afternoon at five o’clock Dr. Archie emerged from the State House

[051-111] after his talk with Governor Alden, and crossed the terrace under a saffron

[051-112] sky. The snow, beaten hard, was blue in the dusk; a day of blinding sunlight

[051-113] had not even started a thaw. The lights of the city twinkled pale below him in

[051-114] the quivering violet air, and the dome of the State House behind him was still

[051-115] red with the light from the west. Before he got into his car, the doctor paused

[051-116] to look about him at the scene of which he never tired. Archie lived in his own

[051-117] house on Colfax Avenue, where he had roomy grounds and a rose garden and a

[051-118] conservatory. His housekeeping was done by three Japanese boys, devoted and

[051-119] resourceful, who were able to manage Archie’s dinner parties, to see that

[051-120] he kept his engagements, and to make visitors who stayed at the house so

[051-121] comfortable that they were always loath to go away.

[051-122] Archie had never known what comfort was until he became a widower, though with

[051-123] characteristic delicacy, or dishonesty, he insisted upon accrediting his peace

[051-124] of mind to the San Felipe, to Time, to anything but his release from Mrs.

[051-125] Archie.

[051-126] Mrs. Archie died just before her husband left Moonstone and came to Denver to

[051-127] live, six years ago. The poor woman’s fight against dust was her undoing

[051-128] at last. One summer day when she was rubbing the parlor upholstery with

[051-129] gasoline,—the doctor had often forbidden her to use it on any account, so

[051-130] that was one of the pleasures she seized upon in his absence,—an

[051-131] explosion occurred. Nobody ever knew exactly how it happened, for Mrs. Archie

[051-132] was dead when the neighbors rushed in to save her from the burning house. She

[051-133] must have inhaled the burning gas and died instantly.

[051-134] Moonstone severity relented toward her somewhat after her death. But even while

[051-135] her old cronies at Mrs. Smiley’s millinery store said that it was a

[051-136] terrible thing, they added that nothing but a powerful explosive *could*

[051-137] have killed Mrs. Archie, and that it was only right the doctor should have a

[051-138] chance.

[051-139] Archie’s past was literally destroyed when his wife died. The house

[051-140] burned to the ground, and all those material reminders which have such power

[051-141] over people disappeared in an hour. His mining interests now took him to Denver

[051-142] so often that it seemed better to make his headquarters there. He gave up his

[051-143] practice and left Moonstone for good. Six months afterward, while Dr. Archie

[051-144] was living at the Brown Palace Hotel, the San Felipe mine began to give up that

[051-145] silver hoard which old Captain Harris had always accused it of concealing, and

[051-146] San Felipe headed the list of mining quotations in every daily paper, East and

[051-147] West. In a few years Dr. Archie was a very rich man. His mine was such an

[051-148] important item in the mineral output of the State, and Archie had a hand in so

[051-149] many of the new industries of Colorado and New Mexico, that his political

[051-150] influence was considerable. He had thrown it all, two years ago, to the new

[051-151] reform party, and had brought about the election of a governor of whose conduct

[051-152] he was now heartily ashamed. His friends believed that Archie himself had

[051-153] ambitious political plans.
