## V

[025-001] By the first of February Thea had been in [[chicago|Chicago]] almost four months, and she

[025-002] did not know much more about the city than if she had never quitted Moonstone.

[025-003] She was, as Harsanyi said, incurious. Her work took most of her time, and she

[025-004] found that she had to sleep a good deal. It had never before been so hard to

[025-005] get up in the morning. She had the bother of caring for her room, and she had

[025-006] to build her fire and bring up her coal. Her routine was frequently interrupted

[025-007] by a message from Mr. Larsen summoning her to sing at a funeral. Every funeral

[025-008] took half a day, and the time had to be made up. When Mrs. Harsanyi asked her

[025-009] if it did not depress her to sing at funerals, she replied that she “had

[025-010] been brought up to go to funerals and didn’t mind.”

[025-011] Thea never went into shops unless she had to, and she felt no interest in them.

[025-012] Indeed, she shunned them, as places where one was sure to be parted from

[025-013] one’s money in some way. She was nervous about counting her change, and

[025-014] she could not accustom herself to having her purchases sent to her address. She

[025-015] felt much safer with her bundles under her arm.

[025-016] During this first winter Thea got no city consciousness. Chicago was simply a

[025-017] wilderness through which one had to find one’s way. She felt no interest

[025-018] in the general briskness and zest of the crowds. The crash and scramble of that

[025-019] big, rich, appetent Western city she did not take in at all, except to notice

[025-020] that the noise of the drays and street-cars tired her. The brilliant window

[025-021] displays, the splendid furs and stuffs, the gorgeous flower-shops, the gay

[025-022] candy-shops, she scarcely noticed. At Christmas-time she did feel some

[025-023] curiosity about the toy-stores, and she wished she held Thor’s little

[025-024] mittened fist in her hand as she stood before the windows. The jewelers’

[025-025] windows, too, had a strong attraction for her—she had always liked bright

[025-026] stones. When she went into the city she used to brave the biting lake winds and

[025-027] stand gazing in at the displays of diamonds and pearls and emeralds; the tiaras

[025-028] and necklaces and earrings, on white velvet. These seemed very well worth while

[025-029] to her, things worth coveting.

[025-030] Mrs. Lorch and Mrs. Andersen often told each other it was strange that Miss

[025-031] Kronborg had so little initiative about “visiting points of

[025-032] interest.” When Thea came to live with them she had expressed a wish to

[025-033] see two places: Montgomery Ward and Company’s big mail-order store, and

[025-034] the packing-houses, to which all the hogs and cattle that went through

[025-035] Moonstone were bound. One of Mrs. Lorch’s lodgers worked in a

[025-036] packing-house, and Mrs. Andersen brought Thea word that she had spoken to Mr.

[025-037] Eckman and he would gladly take her to Packingtown. Eckman was a toughish young

[025-038] Swede, and he thought it would be something of a lark to take a pretty girl

[025-039] through the slaughter-houses. But he was disappointed. Thea neither grew faint

[025-040] nor clung to the arm he kept offering her. She asked innumerable questions and

[025-041] was impatient because he knew so little of what was going on outside of his own

[025-042] department. When they got off the street-car and walked back to Mrs.

[025-043] Lorch’s house in the dusk, Eckman put her hand in his overcoat

[025-044] pocket—she had no muff—and kept squeezing it ardently until she

[025-045] said, “Don’t do that; my ring cuts me.” That night he told

[025-046] his roommate that he “could have kissed her as easy as rolling off a log,

[025-047] but she wasn’t worth the trouble.” As for Thea, she had enjoyed the

[025-048] afternoon very much, and wrote her father a brief but clear account of what she

[025-049] had seen.

[025-050] One night at supper Mrs. Andersen was talking about the exhibit of

[025-051] students’ work she had seen at the Art Institute that afternoon. Several

[025-052] of her friends had sketches in the exhibit. Thea, who always felt that she was

[025-053] behindhand in courtesy to Mrs. Andersen, thought that here was an opportunity

[025-054] to show interest without committing herself to anything. “Where is that,

[025-055] the Institute?” she asked absently.

[025-056] Mrs. Andersen clasped her napkin in both hands. “The Art Institute? Our

[025-057] beautiful Art Institute on Michigan Avenue? Do you mean to say you have never

[025-058] visited it?”

[025-059] “Oh, is it the place with the big lions out in front? I remember; I saw

[025-060] it when I went to Montgomery Ward’s. Yes, I thought the lions were

[025-061] beautiful.”

[025-062] “But the pictures! Didn’t you visit the galleries?”

[025-063] “No. The sign outside said it was a pay-day. I’ve always meant to

[025-064] go back, but I haven’t happened to be down that way since.”

[025-065] Mrs. Lorch and Mrs. Andersen looked at each other. The old mother spoke, fixing

[025-066] her shining little eyes upon Thea across the table. “Ah, but Miss

[025-067] Kronborg, there are old masters! Oh, many of them, such as you could not see

[025-068] anywhere out of Europe.”

[025-069] “And Corots,” breathed Mrs. Andersen, tilting her head feelingly.

[025-070] “Such examples of the Barbizon school!” This was meaningless to

[025-071] Thea, who did not read the art columns of the Sunday *Inter-Ocean* as

[025-072] Mrs. Andersen did.

[025-073] “Oh, I’m going there some day,” she reassured them. “I

[025-074] like to look at oil paintings.”

[025-075] One bleak day in February, when the wind was blowing clouds of dirt like a

[025-076] Moonstone sandstorm, dirt that filled your eyes and ears and mouth, Thea fought

[025-077] her way across the unprotected space in front of the Art Institute and into the

[025-078] doors of the building. She did not come out again until the closing hour. In

[025-079] the street-car, on the long cold ride home, while she sat staring at the

[025-080] waistcoat buttons of a fat strap-hanger, she had a serious reckoning with

[025-081] herself. She seldom thought about her way of life, about what she ought or

[025-082] ought not to do; usually there was but one obvious and important thing to be

[025-083] done. But that afternoon she remonstrated with herself severely. She told

[025-084] herself that she was missing a great deal; that she ought to be more willing to

[025-085] take advice and to go to see things. She was sorry that she had let months pass

[025-086] without going to the Art Institute. After this she would go once a week.

[025-087] The Institute proved, indeed, a place of retreat, as the sand hills or the

[025-088] Kohlers’ garden used to be; a place where she could forget Mrs.

[025-089] Andersen’s tiresome overtures of friendship, the stout contralto in the

[025-090] choir whom she so unreasonably hated, and even, for a little while, the torment

[025-091] of her work. That building was a place in which she could relax and play, and

[025-092] she could hardly ever play now. On the whole, she spent more time with the

[025-093] casts than with the pictures. They were at once more simple and more

[025-094] perplexing; and some way they seemed more important, harder to overlook. It

[025-095] never occurred to her to buy a catalogue, so she called most of the casts by

[025-096] names she made up for them. Some of them she knew; the Dying Gladiator she had

[025-097] read about in “Childe Harold” almost as long ago as she could

[025-098] remember; he was strongly associated with [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] and childish illnesses.

[025-099] The Venus di Milo puzzled her; she could not see why people thought her so

[025-100] beautiful. She told herself over and over that she did not think the Apollo

[025-101] Belvedere “at all handsome.” Better than anything else she liked a

[025-102] great equestrian statue of an evil, cruel-looking general with an

[025-103] unpronounceable name. She used to walk round and round this terrible man and

[025-104] his terrible horse, frowning at him, brooding upon him, as if she had to make

[025-105] some momentous decision about him.

[025-106] The casts, when she lingered long among them, always made her gloomy. It was

[025-107] with a lightening of the heart, a feeling of throwing off the old miseries and

[025-108] old sorrows of the world, that she ran up the wide staircase to the pictures.

[025-109] There she liked best the ones that told stories. There was a painting by Gérôme

[025-110] called “The Pasha’s Grief” which always made her wish for

[025-111] Gunner and Axel. The Pasha was seated on a rug, beside a green candle almost as

[025-112] big as a telegraph pole, and before him was stretched his dead tiger, a

[025-113] splendid beast, and there were pink roses scattered about him. She loved, too,

[025-114] a picture of some boys bringing in a newborn calf on a litter, the cow walking

[025-115] beside it and licking it. The Corot which hung next to this painting she did

[025-116] not like or dislike; she never saw it.

[025-117] But in that same room there was a picture—oh, that was the thing she ran

[025-118] upstairs so fast to see! That was her picture. She imagined that nobody cared

[025-119] for it but herself, and that it waited for her. That was a picture indeed. She

[025-120] liked even the name of it, “The Song of the Lark.” The flat

[025-121] country, the early morning light, the wet fields, the look in the girl’s

[025-122] heavy face—well, they were all hers, anyhow, whatever was there. She told

[025-123] herself that that picture was “right.” Just what she meant by this,

[025-124] it would take a clever person to explain. But to her the word covered the

[025-125] almost boundless satisfaction she felt when she looked at the picture.

[025-126] Before Thea had any idea how fast the weeks were flying, before Mr.

[025-127] Larsen’s “permanent” soprano had returned to her duties,

[025-128] spring came; windy, dusty, strident, shrill; a season almost more violent in

[025-129] Chicago than the winter from which it releases one, or the heat to which it

[025-130] eventually delivers one. One sunny morning the apple trees in Mrs.

[025-131] Lorch’s back yard burst into bloom, and for the first time in months Thea

[025-132] dressed without building a fire. The morning shone like a holiday, and for her

[025-133] it was to be a holiday. There was in the air that sudden, treacherous softness

[025-134] which makes the Poles who work in the packing-houses get drunk. At such times

[025-135] beauty is necessary, and in Packingtown there is no place to get it except at

[025-136] the saloons, where one can buy for a few hours the illusion of comfort, hope,

[025-137] love,—whatever one most longs for.

[025-138] Harsanyi had given Thea a ticket for the symphony concert that afternoon, and

[025-139] when she looked out at the white apple trees her doubts as to whether she ought

[025-140] to go vanished at once. She would make her work light that morning, she told

[025-141] herself. She would go to the concert full of energy. When she set off, after

[025-142] dinner, Mrs. Lorch, who knew Chicago weather, prevailed upon her to take her

[025-143] cape. The old lady said that such sudden mildness, so early in April, presaged

[025-144] a sharp return of winter, and she was anxious about her apple trees.

[025-145] The concert began at two-thirty, and Thea was in her seat in the Auditorium at

[025-146] ten minutes after two—a fine seat in the first row of the balcony, on the

[025-147] side, where she could see the house as well as the orchestra. She had been to

[025-148] so few concerts that the great house, the crowd of people, and the lights, all

[025-149] had a stimulating effect. She was surprised to see so many men in the audience,

[025-150] and wondered how they could leave their business in the afternoon. During the

[025-151] first number Thea was so much interested in the orchestra itself, in the men,

[025-152] the instruments, the volume of sound, that she paid little attention to what

[025-153] they were playing. Her excitement impaired her power of listening. She kept

[025-154] saying to herself, “Now I must stop this foolishness and listen; I may

[025-155] never hear this again”; but her mind was like a glass that is hard to

[025-156] focus. She was not ready to listen until the second number, Dvorak’s

[025-157] Symphony in E minor, called on the programme, “From the New World.”

[025-158] The first theme had scarcely been given out when her mind became clear; instant

[025-159] composure fell upon her, and with it came the power of concentration. This was

[025-160] music she could understand, music from the New World indeed! Strange how, as

[025-161] the first movement went on, it brought back to her that high tableland above

[025-162] Laramie; the grass-grown wagon trails, the far-away peaks of the snowy range,

[025-163] the wind and the eagles, that old man and the first telegraph message.

[025-164] When the first movement ended, Thea’s hands and feet were cold as ice.

[025-165] She was too much excited to know anything except that she wanted something

[025-166] desperately, and when the English horns gave out the theme of the Largo, she

[025-167] knew that what she wanted was exactly that. Here were the sand hills, the

[025-168] grasshoppers and locusts, all the things that wakened and chirped in the early

[025-169] morning; the reaching and reaching of high plains, the immeasurable yearning of

[025-170] all flat lands. There was home in it, too; first memories, first mornings long

[025-171] ago; the amazement of a new soul in a new world; a soul new and yet old, that

[025-172] had dreamed something despairing, something glorious, in the dark before it was

[025-173] born; a soul obsessed by what it did not know, under the cloud of a past it

[025-174] could not recall.

[025-175] If Thea had had much experience in concert-going, and had known her own

[025-176] capacity, she would have left the hall when the symphony was over. But she sat

[025-177] still, scarcely knowing where she was, because her mind had been far away and

[025-178] had not yet come back to her. She was startled when the orchestra began to play

[025-179] again—the entry of the gods into Walhalla. She heard it as people hear

[025-180] things in their sleep. She knew scarcely anything about the Wagner operas. She

[025-181] had a vague idea that “Rhinegold” was about the strife between gods

[025-182] and men; she had read something about it in Mr. Haweis’s book long ago.

[025-183] Too tired to follow the orchestra with much understanding, she crouched down in

[025-184] her seat and closed her eyes. The cold, stately measures of the Walhalla music

[025-185] rang out, far away; the rainbow bridge throbbed out into the air, under it the

[025-186] wailing of the Rhine daughters and the singing of the Rhine. But Thea was sunk

[025-187] in twilight; it was all going on in another world. So it happened that with a

[025-188] dull, almost listless ear she heard for the first time that troubled music,

[025-189] ever-darkening, ever-brightening, which was to flow through so many years of

[025-190] her life.

[025-191] When Thea emerged from the concert hall, Mrs. Lorch’s predictions had

[025-192] been fulfilled. A furious gale was beating over the city from Lake Michigan.

[025-193] The streets were full of cold, hurrying, angry people, running for street-cars

[025-194] and barking at each other. The sun was setting in a clear, windy sky, that

[025-195] flamed with red as if there were a great fire somewhere on the edge of the

[025-196] city. For almost the first time Thea was conscious of the city itself, of the

[025-197] congestion of life all about her, of the brutality and power of those streams

[025-198] that flowed in the streets, threatening to drive one under. People jostled her,

[025-199] ran into her, poked her aside with their elbows, uttering angry exclamations.

[025-200] She got on the wrong car and was roughly ejected by the conductor at a windy

[025-201] corner, in front of a saloon. She stood there dazed and shivering. The cars

[025-202] passed, screaming as they rounded curves, but either they were full to the

[025-203] doors, or were bound for places where she did not want to go. Her hands were so

[025-204] cold that she took off her tight kid gloves. The street lights began to gleam

[025-205] in the dusk. A young man came out of the saloon and stood eyeing her

[025-206] questioningly while he lit a cigarette. “Looking for a friend

[025-207] to-night?” he asked. Thea drew up the collar of her cape and walked on a

[025-208] few paces. The young man shrugged his shoulders and drifted away.

[025-209] Thea came back to the corner and stood there irresolutely. An old man

[025-210] approached her. He, too, seemed to be waiting for a car. He wore an overcoat

[025-211] with a black fur collar, his gray mustache was waxed into little points, and

[025-212] his eyes were watery. He kept thrusting his face up near hers. Her hat blew off

[025-213] and he ran after it—a stiff, pitiful skip he had—and brought it

[025-214] back to her. Then, while she was pinning her hat on, her cape blew up, and he

[025-215] held it down for her, looking at her intently. His face worked as if he were

[025-216] going to cry or were frightened. He leaned over and whispered something to her.

[025-217] It struck her as curious that he was really quite timid, like an old beggar.

[025-218] “Oh, let me *Alone!*” she cried miserably between her teeth.

[025-219] He vanished, disappeared like the Devil in a play. But in the mean time

[025-220] something had got away from her; she could not remember how the violins came in

[025-221] after the horns, just there. When her cape blew up, perhaps—Why did these

[025-222] men torment her? A cloud of dust blew in her face and blinded her. There was

[025-223] some power abroad in the world bent upon taking away from her that feeling with

[025-224] which she had come out of the concert hall. Everything seemed to sweep down on

[025-225] her to tear it out from under her cape. If one had that, the world became

[025-226] one’s enemy; people, buildings, wagons, cars, rushed at one to crush it

[025-227] under, to make one let go of it. Thea glared round her at the crowds, the ugly,

[025-228] sprawling streets, the long lines of lights, and she was not crying now. Her

[025-229] eyes were brighter than even Harsanyi had ever seen them. All these things and

[025-230] people were no longer remote and negligible; they had to be met, they were

[025-231] lined up against her, they were there to take something from her. Very well;

[025-232] they should never have it. They might trample her to death, but they should

[025-233] never have it. As long as she lived that ecstasy was going to be hers. She

[025-234] would live for it, work for it, die for it; but she was going to have it, time

[025-235] after time, height after height. She could hear the crash of the orchestra

[025-236] again, and she rose on the brasses. She would have it, what the trumpets were

[025-237] singing! She would have it, have it,—it! Under the old cape she pressed

[025-238] her hands upon her heaving bosom, that was a little girl’s no longer.
