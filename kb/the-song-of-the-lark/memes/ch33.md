## II

[033-001] During that winter Thea lived in so many places that sometimes at night when

[033-002] she left Bowers’s studio and emerged into the street she had to stop and

[033-003] think for a moment to remember where she was living now and what was the best

[033-004] way to get there.

[033-005] When she moved into a new place her eyes challenged the beds, the carpets, the

[033-006] food, the mistress of the house. The boarding-houses were wretchedly conducted

[033-007] and Thea’s complaints sometimes took an insulting form. She quarreled

[033-008] with one landlady after another and moved on. When she moved into a new room,

[033-009] she was almost sure to hate it on sight and to begin planning to hunt another

[033-010] place before she unpacked her trunk. She was moody and contemptuous toward her

[033-011] fellow boarders, except toward the young men, whom she treated with a careless

[033-012] familiarity which they usually misunderstood. They liked her, however, and when

[033-013] she left the house after a storm, they helped her to move her things and came

[033-014] to see her after she got settled in a new place. But she moved so often that

[033-015] they soon ceased to follow her. They could see no reason for keeping up with a

[033-016] girl who, under her jocularity, was cold, self-centered, and unimpressionable.

[033-017] They soon felt that she did not admire them.

[033-018] Thea used to waken up in the night and wonder why she was so unhappy. She would

[033-019] have been amazed if she had known how much the people whom she met in

[033-020] Bowers’s studio had to do with her low spirits. She had never been

[033-021] conscious of those instinctive standards which are called ideals, and she did

[033-022] not know that she was suffering for them. She often found herself sneering when

[033-023] she was on a street-car, or when she was brushing out her hair before her

[033-024] mirror, as some inane remark or too familiar mannerism flitted across her mind.

[033-025] She felt no creature kindness, no tolerant good-will for Mrs. Priest or Jessie

[033-026] Darcey. After one of Jessie Darcey’s concerts the glowing press notices,

[033-027] and the admiring comments that floated about Bowers’s studio, caused Thea

[033-028] bitter unhappiness. It was not the torment of personal jealousy. She had never

[033-029] thought of herself as even a possible rival of Miss Darcey. She was a poor

[033-030] music student, and Jessie Darcey was a popular and petted professional. Mrs.

[033-031] Priest, whatever one held against her, had a fine, big, showy voice and an

[033-032] impressive presence. She read indifferently, was inaccurate, and was always

[033-033] putting other people wrong, but she at least had the material out of which

[033-034] singers can be made. But people seemed to like Jessie Darcey exactly because

[033-035] she could not sing; because, as they put it, she was “so natural and

[033-036] unprofessional.” Her singing was pronounced “artless,” her

[033-037] voice “birdlike.” Miss Darcey was thin and awkward in person, with

[033-038] a sharp, sallow face. Thea noticed that her plainness was accounted to her

[033-039] credit, and that people spoke of it affectionately. Miss Darcey was singing

[033-040] everywhere just then; one could not help hearing about her. She was backed by

[033-041] some of the packing-house people and by the [[chicago|Chicago]] Northwestern Railroad. Only

[033-042] one critic raised his voice against her. Thea went to several of Jessie

[033-043] Darcey’s concerts. It was the first time she had had an opportunity to

[033-044] observe the whims of the public which singers live by interesting. She saw that

[033-045] people liked in Miss Darcey every quality a singer ought not to have, and

[033-046] especially the nervous complacency that stamped her as a commonplace young

[033-047] woman. They seemed to have a warmer feeling for Jessie than for Mrs. Priest, an

[033-048] affectionate and cherishing regard. Chicago was not so very different from

[033-049] Moonstone, after all, and Jessie Darcey was only [[lily-fisher|Lily Fisher]] under another

[033-050] name.

[033-051] Thea particularly hated to accompany for Miss Darcey because she sang off pitch

[033-052] and didn’t mind it in the least. It was excruciating to sit there day

[033-053] after day and hear her; there was something shameless and indecent about not

[033-054] singing true.

[033-055] One morning Miss Darcey came by appointment to go over the programme for her

[033-056] Peoria concert. She was such a frail-looking girl that Thea ought to have felt

[033-057] sorry for her. True, she had an arch, sprightly little manner, and a flash of

[033-058] salmon-pink on either brown cheek. But a narrow upper jaw gave her face a

[033-059] pinched look, and her eyelids were heavy and relaxed. By the morning light, the

[033-060] purplish brown circles under her eyes were pathetic enough, and foretold no

[033-061] long or brilliant future. A singer with a poor digestion and low vitality; she

[033-062] needed no seer to cast her horoscope. If Thea had ever taken the pains to study

[033-063] her, she would have seen that, under all her smiles and archness, poor Miss

[033-064] Darcey was really frightened to death. She could not understand her success any

[033-065] more than Thea could; she kept catching her breath and lifting her eyebrows and

[033-066] trying to believe that it was true. Her loquacity was not natural, she forced

[033-067] herself to it, and when she confided to you how many defects she could overcome

[033-068] by her unusual command of head resonance, she was not so much trying to

[033-069] persuade you as to persuade herself.

[033-070] When she took a note that was high for her, Miss Darcey always put her right

[033-071] hand out into the air, as if she were indicating height, or giving an exact

[033-072] measurement. Some early teacher had told her that she could “place”

[033-073] a tone more surely by the help of such a gesture, and she firmly believed that

[033-074] it was of great assistance to her. (Even when she was singing in public, she

[033-075] kept her right hand down with difficulty, nervously clasping her white kid

[033-076] fingers together when she took a high note. Thea could always see her elbows

[033-077] stiffen.) She unvaryingly executed this gesture with a smile of gracious

[033-078] confidence, as if she were actually putting her finger on the tone:

[033-079] “There it is, friends!”

[033-080] This morning, in Gounod’s “Ave Maria,” as Miss Darcey

[033-081] approached her B natural,—

[033-082] Dans—nos a-lár———mes!

[033-083] Out went the hand, with the sure airy gesture, though it was little above A she

[033-084] got with her voice, whatever she touched with her finger. Often Bowers let such

[033-085] things pass—with the right people—but this morning he snapped his

[033-086] jaws together and muttered, “God!” Miss Darcey tried again, with

[033-087] the same gesture as of putting the crowning touch, tilting her head and smiling

[033-088] radiantly at Bowers, as if to say, “It is for you I do all this!”

[033-089] Dans—nos a—lár———mes!

[033-090] This time she made B flat, and went on in the happy belief that she had done

[033-091] well enough, when she suddenly found that her accompanist was not going on with

[033-092] her, and this put her out completely.

[033-093] She turned to Thea, whose hands had fallen in her lap. “Oh why did you

[033-094] stop just there! It *is* too trying! Now we’d better go back to that

[033-095] other *crescendo* and try it from there.”

[033-096] “I beg your pardon,” Thea muttered. “I thought you wanted to

[033-097] get that B natural.” She began again, as Miss Darcey indicated.

[033-098] After the singer was gone, Bowers walked up to Thea and asked languidly,

[033-099] “Why do you hate Jessie so? Her little variations from pitch are between

[033-100] her and her public; they don’t hurt you. Has she ever done anything to

[033-101] you except be very agreeable?”

[033-102] “Yes, she has done things to me,” Thea retorted hotly.

[033-103] Bowers looked interested. “What, for example?”

[033-104] “I can’t explain, but I’ve got it in for her.”

[033-105] Bowers laughed. “No doubt about that. I’ll have to suggest that you

[033-106] conceal it a little more effectually. That is—necessary, Miss

[033-107] Kronborg,” he added, looking back over the shoulder of the overcoat he

[033-108] was putting on.

[033-109] He went out to lunch and Thea thought the subject closed. But late in the

[033-110] afternoon, when he was taking his dyspepsia tablet and a glass of water between

[033-111] lessons, he looked up and said in a voice ironically coaxing:—

[033-112] “Miss Kronborg, I wish you would tell me why you hate Jessie.”

[033-113] Taken by surprise Thea put down the score she was reading and answered before

[033-114] she knew what she was saying, “I hate her for the sake of what I used to

[033-115] think a singer might be.”

[033-116] Bowers balanced the tablet on the end of his long forefinger and whistled

[033-117] softly. “And how did you form your conception of what a singer ought to

[033-118] be?” he asked.

[033-119] “I don’t know.” Thea flushed and spoke under her breath;

[033-120] “but I suppose I got most of it from Harsanyi.”

[033-121] Bowers made no comment upon this reply, but opened the door for the next pupil,

[033-122] who was waiting in the reception-room.

[033-123] It was dark when Thea left the studio that night. She knew she had offended

[033-124] Bowers. Somehow she had hurt herself, too. She felt unequal to the

[033-125] boarding-house table, the sneaking divinity student who sat next her and had

[033-126] tried to kiss her on the stairs last night. She went over to the waterside of

[033-127] Michigan Avenue and walked along beside the lake. It was a clear, frosty winter

[033-128] night. The great empty space over the water was restful and spoke of freedom.

[033-129] If she had any money at all, she would go away. The stars glittered over the

[033-130] wide black water. She looked up at them wearily and shook her head. She

[033-131] believed that what she felt was despair, but it was only one of the forms of

[033-132] hope. She felt, indeed, as if she were bidding the stars good-bye; but she was

[033-133] renewing a promise. Though their challenge is universal and eternal, the stars

[033-134] get no answer but that,—the brief light flashed back to them from the

[033-135] eyes of the young who unaccountably aspire.

[033-136] The rich, noisy, city, fat with food and drink, is a spent thing; its chief

[033-137] concern is its digestion and its little game of hide-and-seek with the

[033-138] undertaker. Money and office and success are the consolations of impotence.

[033-139] Fortune turns kind to such solid people and lets them suck their bone in peace.

[033-140] She flecks her whip upon flesh that is more alive, upon that stream of hungry

[033-141] boys and girls who tramp the streets of every city, recognizable by their pride

[033-142] and discontent, who are the Future, and who possess the treasure of creative

[033-143] power.
