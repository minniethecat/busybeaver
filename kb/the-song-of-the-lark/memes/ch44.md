## VII

[044-001] From the day of Fred’s arrival, he and Thea were unceasingly active. They

[044-002] took long rides into the Navajo pine forests, bought turquoises and silver

[044-003] bracelets from the wandering Indian herdsmen, and rode twenty miles to

[044-004] Flagstaff upon the slightest pretext. Thea had never felt this pleasant

[044-005] excitement about any man before, and she found herself trying very hard to

[044-006] please young Ottenburg. She was never tired, never dull. There was a zest about

[044-007] waking up in the morning and dressing, about walking, riding, even about sleep.

[044-008] One morning when Thea came out from her room at seven o’clock, she found

[044-009] Henry and Fred on the porch, looking up at the sky. The day was already hot and

[044-010] there was no breeze. The sun was shining, but heavy brown clouds were hanging

[044-011] in the west, like the smoke of a forest fire. She and Fred had meant to ride to

[044-012] Flagstaff that morning, but Biltmer advised against it, foretelling a storm.

[044-013] After breakfast they lingered about the house, waiting for the weather to make

[044-014] up its mind. Fred had brought his guitar, and as they had the dining-room to

[044-015] themselves, he made Thea go over some songs with him. They got interested and

[044-016] kept it up until Mrs. Biltmer came to set the table for dinner. Ottenburg knew

[044-017] some of the Mexican things [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]] used to sing. Thea had never before

[044-018] happened to tell him about Spanish Johnny, and he seemed more interested in

[044-019] Johnny than in [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] or Wunsch.

[044-020] After dinner they were too restless to endure the ranch house any longer, and

[044-021] ran away to the canyon to practice with single-sticks. Fred carried a slicker

[044-022] and a sweater, and he made Thea wear one of the rubber hats that hung in

[044-023] Biltmer’s gun-room. As they crossed the pasture land the clumsy slicker

[044-024] kept catching in the lacings of his leggings.

[044-025] “Why don’t you drop that thing?” Thea asked. “I

[044-026] won’t mind a shower. I’ve been wet before.”

[044-027] “No use taking chances.”

[044-028] From the canyon they were unable to watch the sky, since only a strip of the

[044-029] zenith was visible. The flat ledge about the watch-tower was the only level

[044-030] spot large enough for single-stick exercise, and they were still practicing

[044-031] there when, at about four o’clock, a tremendous roll of thunder echoed

[044-032] between the cliffs and the atmosphere suddenly became thick.

[044-033] Fred thrust the sticks in a cleft in the rock. “We’re in for it,

[044-034] Thea. Better make for your cave where there are blankets.” He caught her

[044-035] elbow and hurried her along the path before the cliff-houses. They made the

[044-036] half-mile at a quick trot, and as they ran the rocks and the sky and the air

[044-037] between the cliffs turned a turbid green, like the color in a moss agate. When

[044-038] they reached the blanketed rock room, they looked at each other and laughed.

[044-039] Their faces had taken on a greenish pallor. Thea’s hair, even, was green.

[044-040] “Dark as pitch in here,” Fred exclaimed as they hurried over the

[044-041] old rock doorstep. “But it’s warm. The rocks hold the heat.

[044-042] It’s going to be terribly cold outside, all right.” He was

[044-043] interrupted by a deafening peal of thunder. “Lord, what an echo! Lucky

[044-044] you don’t mind. It’s worth watching out there. We needn’t

[044-045] come in yet.”

[044-046] The green light grew murkier and murkier. The smaller vegetation was blotted

[044-047] out. The yuccas, the cedars, and *piñons* stood dark and rigid, like

[044-048] bronze. The swallows flew up with sharp, terrified twitterings. Even the

[044-049] quaking asps were still. While Fred and Thea watched from the doorway, the

[044-050] light changed to purple. Clouds of dark vapor, like chlorine gas, began to

[044-051] float down from the head of the canyon and hung between them and the

[044-052] cliff-houses in the opposite wall. Before they knew it, the wall itself had

[044-053] disappeared. The air was positively venomous-looking, and grew colder every

[044-054] minute. The thunder seemed to crash against one cliff, then against the other,

[044-055] and to go shrieking off into the inner canyon.

[044-056] The moment the rain broke, it beat the vapors down. In the gulf before them the

[044-057] water fell in spouts, and dashed from the high cliffs overhead. It tore aspens

[044-058] and chokecherry bushes out of the ground and left the yuccas hanging by their

[044-059] tough roots. Only the little cedars stood black and unmoved in the torrents

[044-060] that fell from so far above. The rock chamber was full of fine spray from the

[044-061] streams of water that shot over the doorway. Thea crept to the back wall and

[044-062] rolled herself in a blanket, and Fred threw the heavier blankets over her. The

[044-063] wool of the Navajo sheep was soon kindled by the warmth of her body, and was

[044-064] impenetrable to dampness. Her hair, where it hung below the rubber hat,

[044-065] gathered the moisture like a sponge. Fred put on the slicker, tied the sweater

[044-066] about his neck, and settled himself cross-legged beside her. The chamber was so

[044-067] dark that, although he could see the outline of her head and shoulders, he

[044-068] could not see her face. He struck a wax match to light his pipe. As he

[044-069] sheltered it between his hands, it sizzled and sputtered, throwing a yellow

[044-070] flicker over Thea and her blankets.

[044-071] “You look like a gypsy,” he said as he dropped the match.

[044-072] “Any one you’d rather be shut up with than me? No? Sure about

[044-073] that?”

[044-074] “I think I am. Aren’t you cold?”

[044-075] “Not especially.” Fred smoked in silence, listening to the roar of

[044-076] the water outside. “We may not get away from here right away,” he

[044-077] remarked.

[044-078] “I shan’t mind. Shall you?”

[044-079] He laughed grimly and pulled on his pipe. “Do you know where you’re

[044-080] at, Miss [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]]?” he said at last. “You’ve got me

[044-081] going pretty hard, I suppose you know. I’ve had a lot of sweethearts, but

[044-082] I’ve never been so much—engrossed before. What are you going to do

[044-083] about it?” He heard nothing from the blankets. “Are you going to

[044-084] play fair, or is it about my cue to cut away?”

[044-085] “I’ll play fair. I don’t see why you want to go.”

[044-086] “What do you want me around for?—to play with?”

[044-087] Thea struggled up among the blankets. “I want you for everything. I

[044-088] don’t know whether I’m what people call in love with you or not. In

[044-089] Moonstone that meant sitting in a hammock with somebody. I don’t want to

[044-090] sit in a hammock with you, but I want to do almost everything else. Oh,

[044-091] hundreds of things!”

[044-092] “If I run away, will you go with me?”

[044-093] “I don’t know. I’ll have to think about that. Maybe I

[044-094] would.” She freed herself from her wrappings and stood up.

[044-095] “It’s not raining so hard now. Hadn’t we better start this

[044-096] minute? It will be night before we get to Biltmer’s.”

[044-097] Fred struck another match. “It’s seven. I don’t know how much

[044-098] of the path may be washed away. I don’t even know whether I ought to let

[044-099] you try it without a lantern.”

[044-100] Thea went to the doorway and looked out. “There’s nothing else to

[044-101] do. The sweater and the slicker will keep me dry, and this will be my chance to

[044-102] find out whether these shoes are really water-tight. They cost a week’s

[044-103] salary.” She retreated to the back of the cave. “It’s getting

[044-104] blacker every minute.”

[044-105] Ottenburg took a brandy flask from his coat pocket. “Better have some of

[044-106] this before we start. Can you take it without water?”

[044-107] Thea lifted it obediently to her lips. She put on the sweater and Fred helped

[044-108] her to get the clumsy slicker on over it. He buttoned it and fastened the high

[044-109] collar. She could feel that his hands were hurried and clumsy. The coat was too

[044-110] big, and he took off his necktie and belted it in at the waist. While she

[044-111] tucked her hair more securely under the rubber hat he stood in front of her,

[044-112] between her and the gray doorway, without moving.

[044-113] “Are you ready to go?” she asked carelessly.

[044-114] “If you are,” he spoke quietly, without moving, except to bend his

[044-115] head forward a little.

[044-116] Thea laughed and put her hands on his shoulders. “You know how to handle

[044-117] me, don’t you?” she whispered. For the first time, she kissed him

[044-118] without constraint or embarrassment.

[044-119] “Thea, Thea, Thea!” Fred whispered her name three times, shaking

[044-120] her a little as if to waken her. It was too dark to see, but he could feel that

[044-121] she was smiling.

[044-122] When she kissed him she had not hidden her face on his shoulder,—she had

[044-123] risen a little on her toes, and stood straight and free. In that moment when he

[044-124] came close to her actual personality, he felt in her the same expansion that he

[044-125] had noticed at Mrs. Nathanmeyer’s. She became freer and stronger under

[044-126] impulses. When she rose to meet him like that, he felt her flash into

[044-127] everything that she had ever suggested to him, as if she filled out her own

[044-128] shadow.

[044-129] She pushed him away and shot past him out into the rain. “Now for it,

[044-130] Fred,” she called back exultantly. The rain was pouring steadily down

[044-131] through the dying gray twilight, and muddy streams were spouting and foaming

[044-132] over the cliff.

[044-133] Fred caught her and held her back. “Keep behind me, Thea. I don’t

[044-134] know about the path. It may be gone altogether. Can’t tell what there is

[044-135] under this water.”

[044-136] But the path was older than the white man’s Arizona. The rush of water

[044-137] had washed away the dust and stones that lay on the surface, but the rock

[044-138] skeleton of the Indian trail was there, ready for the foot. Where the streams

[044-139] poured down through gullies, there was always a cedar or a *piñon* to

[044-140] cling to. By wading and slipping and climbing, they got along. As they neared

[044-141] the head of the canyon, where the path lifted and rose in steep loops to the

[044-142] surface of the plateau, the climb was more difficult. The earth above had

[044-143] broken away and washed down over the trail, bringing rocks and bushes and even

[044-144] young trees with it. The last ghost of daylight was dying and there was no time

[044-145] to lose. The canyon behind them was already black.

[044-146] “We’ve got to go right through the top of this pine tree, Thea. No

[044-147] time to hunt a way around. Give me your hand.” After they had crashed

[044-148] through the mass of branches, Fred stopped abruptly. “Gosh, what a hole!

[044-149] Can you jump it? Wait a minute.”

[044-150] He cleared the washout, slipped on the wet rock at the farther side, and caught

[044-151] himself just in time to escape a tumble. “If I could only find something

[044-152] to hold to, I could give you a hand. It’s so cursed dark, and there are

[044-153] no trees here where they’re needed. Here’s something; it’s a

[044-154] root. It will hold all right.” He braced himself on the rock, gripped the

[044-155] crooked root with one hand and swung himself across toward Thea, holding out

[044-156] his arm. “Good jump! I must say you don’t lose your nerve in a

[044-157] tight place. Can you keep at it a little longer? We’re almost out. Have

[044-158] to make that next ledge. Put your foot on my knee and catch something to pull

[044-159] by.”

[044-160] Thea went up over his shoulder. “It’s hard ground up here,”

[044-161] she panted. “Did I wrench your arm when I slipped then? It was a cactus I

[044-162] grabbed, and it startled me.”

[044-163] “Now, one more pull and we’re on the level.”

[044-164] They emerged gasping upon the black plateau. In the last five minutes the

[044-165] darkness had solidified and it seemed as if the skies were pouring black water.

[044-166] They could not see where the sky ended or the plain began. The light at the

[044-167] ranch house burned a steady spark through the rain. Fred drew Thea’s arm

[044-168] through his and they struck off toward the light. They could not see each

[044-169] other, and the rain at their backs seemed to drive them along. They kept

[044-170] laughing as they stumbled over tufts of grass or stepped into slippery pools.

[044-171] They were delighted with each other and with the adventure which lay behind

[044-172] them.

[044-173] “I can’t even see the whites of your eyes, Thea. But I’d know

[044-174] who was here stepping out with me, anywhere. Part coyote you are, by the feel

[044-175] of you. When you make up your mind to jump, you jump! My gracious, what’s

[044-176] the matter with your hand?”

[044-177] “Cactus spines. Didn’t I tell you when I grabbed the cactus? I

[044-178] thought it was a root. Are we going straight?”

[044-179] “I don’t know. Somewhere near it, I think. I’m very

[044-180] comfortable, aren’t you? You’re warm, except your cheeks. How funny

[044-181] they are when they’re wet. Still, you always feel like you. I like this.

[044-182] I could walk to Flagstaff. It’s fun, not being able to see anything. I

[044-183] feel surer of you when I can’t see you. Will you run away with me?”

[044-184] Thea laughed. “I won’t run far to-night. I’ll think about it.

[044-185] Look, Fred, there’s somebody coming.”

[044-186] “Henry, with his lantern. Good enough! Halloo!

[044-187] Hallo—o—o!” Fred shouted.

[044-188] The moving light bobbed toward them. In half an hour Thea was in her big

[044-189] feather bed, drinking hot lentil soup, and almost before the soup was swallowed

[044-190] she was asleep.
