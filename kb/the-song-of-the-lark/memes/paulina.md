## Overview

Paulina is the long-time partner (likely common-law wife) of Professor Fritz Wunsch, the itinerant German music teacher who gives Thea Kronborg her first piano lessons in Moonstone. Though she appears primarily in Chapters 4 and 13, she is an essential figure in Wunsch's domestic world — a gardener, a cook, and a steady presence who anchors the volatile old musician's life in the routines of household and garden.

## Biography / Character Arc

### Life with Wunsch

Paulina lives with Wunsch in his lodgings in Moonstone, where she tends a garden with a cherished hotbed (*Mistbeet*) [013-115]. In the evenings, she goes to bed before Wunsch, leaving him to sit alone [004-217]. Her life is one of quiet domestic labor: cooking, cleaning, and maintaining the modest home of a man who, despite his gifts as a teacher, lives on the margins of the town's respectability.

### The Axe Incident

Paulina's most vivid scene occurs during the night of Spanish Johnny's rampage, when the troubled musician takes an axe to the trees. Wunsch and Paulina sit up together in bed, listening in alarm to the chopping outside [013-101]. As the sounds continue, Wunsch exclaims, "Mein Gott, Paulina, he has the axe" [013-104], and urges her to stay in bed [013-109]. Paulina's first cry is not for her own safety but for her garden: "Ach, das Mistbeet!" [013-115] — her treasured hotbed. She tears off her night-cap in panic [013-119] and prays desperately that the trees will be spared [013-131]. When Wunsch reports that the men have tied Spanish Johnny with a rope, she is relieved [013-144]. After the episode, Paulina cleans up the household [013-165], restoring order to their domestic world.

### Later Years

Paulina's presence lingers in the novel's memory. In Chapter 30, when Thea is in Chicago recalling her Moonstone past, it is noted that Paulina had listened to distant dance music with open windows [030-244], a small image of openness and receptivity in an otherwise confined life. By the novel's end, Wunsch too has passed, and Paulina's fate is unremarked — she is one of the many Moonstone figures whose lives recede as Thea's career ascends.

## Relationships

### Fritz Wunsch

Paulina is Wunsch's domestic partner, sharing his bed, his poverty, and his exile. She endures his moods and fears his impulses — her terror during the axe incident suggests a history of living with a man capable of violence. Yet she stays, tends the garden, and maintains the household that gives his life its only stability.

### Thea Kronborg

Paulina has no direct interaction with Thea in the text, but she is part of the Wunsch household that forms the backdrop of Thea's earliest musical education. The domestic world Paulina maintains — the garden, the arbor where Wunsch sits, the kitchen door — is the setting for Thea's first encounter with European musical culture.

### Spanish Johnny

Though they never speak directly, Paulina is one of Spanish Johnny's victims during his axe rampage. Her terror is the novel's most immediate register of the fear that the troubled musician could inspire in his neighbors.

## Significance

Paulina represents the invisible domestic labor that sustains artistic life. While Wunsch teaches — imparting German musical tradition to the young Thea — Paulina gardens, cleans, cooks, and endures, making his teaching possible without any recognition. Her German exclamations during the axe incident ("Mein Gott," "Ach, das Mistbeet") mark her as an immigrant voice, one of the novel's many European-born characters who bring continental culture to the American frontier. Her attachment to the hotbed — a protected space where tender plants are nursed — is a quiet metaphor for the nurturing role she plays in Wunsch's life.
