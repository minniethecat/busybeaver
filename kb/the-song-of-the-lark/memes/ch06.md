## VI

[006-001] Seen from a balloon, Moonstone would have looked like a Noah’s ark town

[006-002] set out in the sand and lightly shaded by gray-green tamarisks and cottonwoods.

[006-003] A few people were trying to make soft maples grow in their turfed lawns, but

[006-004] the fashion of planting incongruous trees from the North Atlantic States had

[006-005] not become general then, and the frail, brightly painted desert town was shaded

[006-006] by the light-reflecting, wind-loving trees of the desert, whose roots are

[006-007] always seeking water and whose leaves are always talking about it, making the

[006-008] sound of rain. The long porous roots of the cottonwood are irrepressible. They

[006-009] break into the wells as rats do into granaries, and thieve the water.

[006-010] The long street which connected Moonstone with the depot settlement traversed

[006-011] in its course a considerable stretch of rough open country, staked out in lots

[006-012] but not built up at all, a weedy hiatus between the town and the railroad. When

[006-013] you set out along this street to go to the station, you noticed that the houses

[006-014] became smaller and farther apart, until they ceased altogether, and the board

[006-015] sidewalk continued its uneven course through sunflower patches, until you

[006-016] reached the solitary, new brick Catholic Church. The church stood there because

[006-017] the land was given to the parish by the man who owned the adjoining waste lots,

[006-018] in the hope of making them more salable—“Farrier’s

[006-019] Addition,” this patch of prairie was called in the clerk’s office.

[006-020] An eighth of a mile beyond the church was a washout, a deep sand-gully, where

[006-021] the board sidewalk became a bridge for perhaps fifty feet. Just beyond the

[006-022] gully was old Uncle Billy Beemer’s grove,—twelve town lots set out

[006-023] in fine, well-grown cottonwood trees, delightful to look upon, or to listen to,

[006-024] as they swayed and rippled in the wind. Uncle Billy had been one of the most

[006-025] worthless old drunkards who ever sat on a store box and told filthy stories.

[006-026] One night he played hide-and-seek with a switch engine and got his sodden

[006-027] brains knocked out. But his grove, the one creditable thing he had ever done in

[006-028] his life, rustled on. Beyond this grove the houses of the depot settlement

[006-029] began, and the naked board walk, that had run in out of the sunflowers, again

[006-030] became a link between human dwellings.

[006-031] One afternoon, late in the summer, Dr. Howard Archie was fighting his way back

[006-032] to town along this walk through a blinding sandstorm, a silk handkerchief tied

[006-033] over his mouth. He had been to see a sick woman down in the depot settlement,

[006-034] and he was walking because his ponies had been out for a hard drive that

[006-035] morning.

[006-036] As he passed the Catholic Church he came upon Thea and Thor. Thea was sitting

[006-037] in a child’s express wagon, her feet out behind, kicking the wagon along

[006-038] and steering by the tongue. Thor was on her lap and she held him with one arm.

[006-039] He had grown to be a big cub of a baby, with a constitutional grievance, and he

[006-040] had to be continually amused. Thea took him philosophically, and tugged and

[006-041] pulled him about, getting as much fun as she could under her encumbrance. Her

[006-042] hair was blowing about her face, and her eyes were squinting so intently at the

[006-043] uneven board sidewalk in front of her that she did not see the doctor until he

[006-044] spoke to her.

[006-045] “Look out, Thea. You’ll steer that youngster into the ditch.”

[006-046] The wagon stopped. Thea released the tongue, wiped her hot, sandy face, and

[006-047] pushed back her hair. “Oh, no, I won’t! I never ran off but once,

[006-048] and then he didn’t get anything but a bump. He likes this better than a

[006-049] baby buggy, and so do I.”

[006-050] “Are you going to kick that cart all the way home?”

[006-051] “Of course. We take long trips; wherever there is a sidewalk. It’s

[006-052] no good on the road.”

[006-053] “Looks to me like working pretty hard for your fun. Are you going to be

[006-054] busy to-night? Want to make a call with me? [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]]’s come home

[006-055] again, all used up. His wife sent me word this morning, and I said I’d go

[006-056] over to see him to-night. He’s an old chum of yours, isn’t

[006-057] he?”

[006-058] “Oh, I’m glad. She’s been crying her eyes out. When did he

[006-059] come?”

[006-060] “Last night, on Number Six. Paid his fare, they tell me. Too sick to beat

[006-061] it. There’ll come a time when that boy won’t get back, I’m

[006-062] afraid. Come around to my office about eight o’clock,—and you

[006-063] needn’t bring that!”

[006-064] Thor seemed to understand that he had been insulted, for he scowled and began

[006-065] to kick the side of the wagon, shouting, “Go-go, go-go!” Thea

[006-066] leaned forward and grabbed the wagon tongue. [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] stepped in front of her

[006-067] and blocked the way. “Why don’t you make him wait? What do you let

[006-068] him boss you like that for?”

[006-069] “If he gets mad he throws himself, and then I can’t do anything

[006-070] with him. When he’s mad he’s lots stronger than me, aren’t

[006-071] you, Thor?” Thea spoke with pride, and the idol was appeased. He grunted

[006-072] approvingly as his sister began to kick rapidly behind her, and the wagon

[006-073] rattled off and soon disappeared in the flying currents of sand.

[006-074] That evening Dr. Archie was seated in his office, his desk chair tilted back,

[006-075] reading by the light of a hot coal-oil lamp. All the windows were open, but the

[006-076] night was breathless after the sandstorm, and his hair was moist where it hung

[006-077] over his forehead. He was deeply engrossed in his book and sometimes smiled

[006-078] thoughtfully as he read. When [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] entered quietly and slipped into a

[006-079] seat, he nodded, finished his paragraph, inserted a bookmark, and rose to put

[006-080] the book back into the case. It was one out of the long row of uniform volumes

[006-081] on the top shelf.

[006-082] “Nearly every time I come in, when you’re alone, you’re

[006-083] reading one of those books,” Thea remarked thoughtfully. “They must

[006-084] be very nice.”

[006-085] The doctor dropped back into his swivel chair, the mottled volume still in his

[006-086] hand. “They aren’t exactly books, Thea,” he said seriously.

[006-087] “They’re a city.”

[006-088] “A history, you mean?”

[006-089] “Yes, and no. They’re a history of a live city, not a dead one. A

[006-090] Frenchman undertook to write about a whole cityful of people, all the kinds he

[006-091] knew. And he got them nearly all in, I guess. Yes, it’s very interesting.

[006-092] You’ll like to read it some day, when you’re grown up.”

[006-093] Thea leaned forward and made out the title on the back, “A Distinguished

[006-094] Provincial in Paris.”

[006-095] “It doesn’t sound very interesting.”

[006-096] “Perhaps not, but it is.” The doctor scrutinized her broad face,

[006-097] low enough to be in the direct light from under the green lamp shade.

[006-098] “Yes,” he went on with some satisfaction, “I think

[006-099] you’ll like them some day. You’re always curious about people, and

[006-100] I expect this man knew more about people than anybody that ever lived.”

[006-101] “City people or country people?”

[006-102] “Both. People are pretty much the same everywhere.”

[006-103] “Oh, no, they’re not. The people who go through in the dining-car

[006-104] aren’t like us.”

[006-105] “What makes you think they aren’t, my girl? Their clothes?”

[006-106] Thea shook her head. “No, it’s something else. I don’t

[006-107] know.” Her eyes shifted under the doctor’s searching gaze and she

[006-108] glanced up at the row of books. “How soon will I be old enough to read

[006-109] them?”

[006-110] “Soon enough, soon enough, little girl.” The doctor patted her hand

[006-111] and looked at her index finger. “The nail’s coming all right,

[006-112] isn’t it? But I think that man makes you practice too much. You have it

[006-113] on your mind all the time.” He had noticed that when she talked to him

[006-114] she was always opening and shutting her hands. “It makes you

[006-115] nervous.”

[006-116] “No, he don’t,” Thea replied stubbornly, watching Dr. Archie

[006-117] return the book to its niche.

[006-118] He took up a black leather case, put on his hat, and they went down the dark

[006-119] stairs into the street. The summer moon hung full in the sky. For the time

[006-120] being, it was the great fact in the world. Beyond the edge of the town the

[006-121] plain was so white that every clump of sage stood out distinct from the sand,

[006-122] and the dunes looked like a shining lake. The doctor took off his straw hat and

[006-123] carried it in his hand as they walked toward Mexican Town, across the sand.

[006-124] North of Pueblo, Mexican settlements were rare in Colorado then. This one had

[006-125] come about accidentally. Spanish Johnny was the first Mexican who came to

[006-126] Moonstone. He was a painter and decorator, and had been working in Trinidad,

[006-127] when [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]] told him there was a “boom” on in Moonstone, and a

[006-128] good many new buildings were going up. A year after Johnny settled in

[006-129] Moonstone, his cousin, Famos Serreños, came to work in the brickyard; then

[006-130] Serreños’ cousins came to help him. During the strike, the master

[006-131] mechanic put a gang of Mexicans to work in the roundhouse. The Mexicans had

[006-132] arrived so quietly, with their blankets and musical instruments, that before

[006-133] Moonstone was awake to the fact, there was a Mexican quarter; a dozen families

[006-134] or more.

[006-135] As Thea and the doctor approached the ’dobe houses, they heard a guitar,

[006-136] and a rich barytone voice—that of Famos Serreños—singing “La

[006-137] Golandrina.” All the Mexican houses had neat little yards, with tamarisk

[006-138] hedges and flowers, and walks bordered with shells or whitewashed stones.

[006-139] Johnny’s house was dark. His wife, Mrs. Tellamantez, was sitting on the

[006-140] doorstep, combing her long, blue-black hair. (Mexican women are like the

[006-141] Spartans; when they are in trouble, in love, under stress of any kind, they

[006-142] comb and comb their hair.) She rose without embarrassment or apology, comb in

[006-143] hand, and greeted the doctor.

[006-144] “Good-evening; will you go in?” she asked in a low, musical voice.

[006-145] “He is in the back room. I will make a light.” She followed them

[006-146] indoors, lit a candle and handed it to the doctor, pointing toward the bedroom.

[006-147] Then she went back and sat down on her doorstep.

[006-148] Dr. Archie and Thea went into the bedroom, which was dark and quiet. There was

[006-149] a bed in the corner, and a man was lying on the clean sheets. On the table

[006-150] beside him was a glass pitcher, half-full of water. Spanish Johnny looked

[006-151] younger than his wife, and when he was in health he was very handsome: slender,

[006-152] gold-colored, with wavy black hair, a round, smooth throat, white teeth, and

[006-153] burning black eyes. His profile was strong and severe, like an Indian’s.

[006-154] What was termed his “wildness” showed itself only in his feverish

[006-155] eyes and in the color that burned on his tawny cheeks. That night he was a

[006-156] coppery green, and his eyes were like black holes. He opened them when the

[006-157] doctor held the candle before his face.

[006-158] “*Mi testa!*” he muttered, “*mi testa*,”

[006-159] doctor. “*La fiebre!*” Seeing the doctor’s companion at

[006-160] the foot of the bed, he attempted a smile. “*Muchacha!*” he

[006-161] exclaimed deprecatingly.

[006-162] Dr. Archie stuck a thermometer into his mouth. “Now, Thea, you can run

[006-163] outside and wait for me.”

[006-164] Thea slipped noiselessly through the dark house and joined Mrs. Tellamantez.

[006-165] The somber Mexican woman did not seem inclined to talk, but her nod was

[006-166] friendly. Thea sat down on the warm sand, her back to the moon, facing Mrs.

[006-167] Tellamantez on her doorstep, and began to count the moon flowers on the vine

[006-168] that ran over the house. Mrs. Tellamantez was always considered a very homely

[006-169] woman. Her face was of a strongly marked type not sympathetic to Americans.

[006-170] Such long, oval faces, with a full chin, a large, mobile mouth, a high nose,

[006-171] are not uncommon in Spain. Mrs. Tellamantez could not write her name, and could

[006-172] read but little. Her strong nature lived upon itself. She was chiefly known in

[006-173] Moonstone for her forbearance with her incorrigible husband.

[006-174] Nobody knew exactly what was the matter with Johnny, and everybody liked him.

[006-175] His popularity would have been unusual for a white man, for a Mexican it was

[006-176] unprecedented. His talents were his undoing. He had a high, uncertain tenor

[006-177] voice, and he played the mandolin with exceptional skill. Periodically he went

[006-178] crazy. There was no other way to explain his behavior. He was a clever workman,

[006-179] and, when he worked, as regular and faithful as a burro. Then some night he

[006-180] would fall in with a crowd at the saloon and begin to sing. He would go on

[006-181] until he had no voice left, until he wheezed and rasped. Then he would play his

[006-182] mandolin furiously, and drink until his eyes sank back into his head. At last,

[006-183] when he was put out of the saloon at closing time, and could get nobody to

[006-184] listen to him, he would run away—along the railroad track, straight

[006-185] across the desert. He always managed to get aboard a freight somewhere. Once

[006-186] beyond Denver, he played his way southward from saloon to saloon until he got

[006-187] across the border. He never wrote to his wife; but she would soon begin to get

[006-188] newspapers from La Junta, Albuquerque, Chihuahua, with marked paragraphs

[006-189] announcing that Juan Tellamantez and his wonderful mandolin could be heard at

[006-190] the Jack Rabbit Grill, or the Pearl of Cadiz Saloon. Mrs. Tellamantez waited

[006-191] and wept and combed her hair. When he was completely wrung out and burned

[006-192] up,—all but destroyed,—her Juan always came back to her to be taken

[006-193] care of,—once with an ugly knife wound in the neck, once with a finger

[006-194] missing from his right hand,—but he played just as well with three

[006-195] fingers as he had with four.

[006-196] Public sentiment was lenient toward Johnny, but everybody was disgusted with

[006-197] Mrs. Tellamantez for putting up with him. She ought to discipline him, people

[006-198] said; she ought to leave him; she had no self-respect. In short, Mrs.

[006-199] Tellamantez got all the blame. Even Thea thought she was much too humble.

[006-200] To-night, as she sat with her back to the moon, looking at the moon flowers and

[006-201] Mrs. Tellamantez’s somber face, she was thinking that there is nothing so

[006-202] sad in the world as that kind of patience and resignation. It was much worse

[006-203] than Johnny’s craziness. She even wondered whether it did not help to

[006-204] make Johnny crazy. People had no right to be so passive and resigned. She would

[006-205] like to roll over and over in the sand and screech at Mrs. Tellamantez. She was

[006-206] glad when the doctor came out.

[006-207] The Mexican woman rose and stood respectful and expectant. The doctor held his

[006-208] hat in his hand and looked kindly at her.

[006-209] “Same old thing, Mrs. Tellamantez. He’s no worse than he’s

[006-210] been before. I’ve left some medicine. Don’t give him anything but

[006-211] toast water until I see him again. You’re a good nurse; you’ll get

[006-212] him out.” Dr. Archie smiled encouragingly. He glanced about the little

[006-213] garden and wrinkled his brows. “I can’t see what makes him behave

[006-214] so. He’s killing himself, and he’s not a rowdy sort of fellow.

[006-215] Can’t you tie him up someway? Can’t you tell when these fits are

[006-216] coming on?”

[006-217] Mrs. Tellamantez put her hand to her forehead. “The saloon, doctor, the

[006-218] excitement; that is what makes him. People listen to him, and it excites

[006-219] him.”

[006-220] The doctor shook his head. “Maybe. He’s too much for my

[006-221] calculations. I don’t see what he gets out of it.”

[006-222] “He is always fooled,”—the Mexican woman spoke rapidly and

[006-223] tremulously, her long under lip quivering.

[006-224] “He is good at heart, but he has no head. He fools himself. You do not

[006-225] understand in this country, you are progressive. But he has no judgment, and he

[006-226] is fooled.” She stooped quickly, took up one of the white conch-shells

[006-227] that bordered the walk, and, with an apologetic inclination of her head, held

[006-228] it to Dr. Archie’s ear. “Listen, doctor. You hear something in

[006-229] there? You hear the sea; and yet the sea is very far from here. You have

[006-230] judgment, and you know that. But he is fooled. To him, it is the sea itself. A

[006-231] little thing is big to him.” She bent and placed the shell in the white

[006-232] row, with its fellows. Thea took it up softly and pressed it to her own ear.

[006-233] The sound in it startled her; it was like something calling one. So that was

[006-234] why Johnny ran away. There was something awe-inspiring about Mrs. Tellamantez

[006-235] and her shell.

[006-236] Thea caught Dr. Archie’s hand and squeezed it hard as she skipped along

[006-237] beside him back toward Moonstone. She went home, and the doctor went back to

[006-238] his lamp and his book. He never left his office until after midnight. If he did

[006-239] not play whist or pool in the evening, he read. It had become a habit with him

[006-240] to lose himself.
