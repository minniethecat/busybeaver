## II

[022-001] So Thea did not go to a boarding-house after all. When [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] left [[chicago|Chicago]]

[022-002] she was comfortably settled with Mrs. Lorch, and her happy reunion with her

[022-003] trunk somewhat consoled her for his departure.

[022-004] Mrs. Lorch and her daughter lived half a mile from the Swedish Reform Church,

[022-005] in an old square frame house, with a porch supported by frail pillars, set in a

[022-006] damp yard full of big lilac bushes. The house, which had been left over from

[022-007] country times, needed paint badly, and looked gloomy and despondent among its

[022-008] smart Queen Anne neighbors. There was a big back yard with two rows of apple

[022-009] trees and a grape arbor, and a warped walk, two planks wide, which led to the

[022-010] coal bins at the back of the lot. Thea’s room was on the second floor,

[022-011] overlooking this back yard, and she understood that in the winter she must

[022-012] carry up her own coal and kindling from the bin. There was no furnace in the

[022-013] house, no running water except in the kitchen, and that was why the room rent

[022-014] was small. All the rooms were heated by stoves, and the lodgers pumped the

[022-015] water they needed from the cistern under the porch, or from the well at the

[022-016] entrance of the grape arbor. Old Mrs. Lorch could never bring herself to have

[022-017] costly improvements made in her house; indeed she had very little money. She

[022-018] preferred to keep the house just as her husband built it, and she thought her

[022-019] way of living good enough for plain people.

[022-020] Thea’s room was large enough to admit a rented upright piano without

[022-021] crowding. It was, the widowed daughter said, “a double room that had

[022-022] always before been occupied by two gentlemen”; the piano now took the

[022-023] place of a second occupant. There was an ingrain carpet on the floor, green ivy

[022-024] leaves on a red ground, and clumsy, old-fashioned walnut furniture. The bed was

[022-025] very wide, and the mattress thin and hard. Over the fat pillows were

[022-026] “shams” embroidered in Turkey red, each with a flowering

[022-027] scroll—one with “Gute’ Nacht,” the other with

[022-028] “Guten Morgen.” The dresser was so big that Thea wondered how it

[022-029] had ever been got into the house and up the narrow stairs. Besides an old

[022-030] horsehair armchair, there were two low plush “spring-rockers,”

[022-031] against the massive pedestals of which one was always stumbling in the dark.

[022-032] Thea sat in the dark a good deal those first weeks, and sometimes a painful

[022-033] bump against one of those brutally immovable pedestals roused her temper and

[022-034] pulled her out of a heavy hour. The wall-paper was brownish yellow, with blue

[022-035] flowers. When it was put on, the carpet, certainly, had not been consulted.

[022-036] There was only one picture on the wall when Thea moved in: a large colored

[022-037] print of a brightly lighted church in a snow-storm, on Christmas Eve, with

[022-038] greens hanging about the stone doorway and arched windows. There was something

[022-039] warm and home, like about this picture, and Thea grew fond of it. One day, on

[022-040] her way into town to take her lesson, she stopped at a bookstore and bought a

[022-041] photograph of the Naples bust of Julius Caesar. This she had framed, and hung

[022-042] it on the big bare wall behind her stove. It was a curious choice, but she was

[022-043] at the age when people do inexplicable things. She had been interested in

[022-044] Caesar’s “Commentaries” when she left school to begin

[022-045] teaching, and she loved to read about great generals; but these facts would

[022-046] scarcely explain her wanting that grim bald head to share her daily existence.

[022-047] It seemed a strange freak, when she bought so few things, and when she had, as

[022-048] Mrs. Andersen said to Mrs. Lorch, “no pictures of the composers at

[022-049] all.”

[022-050] Both the widows were kind to her, but Thea liked the mother better. Old Mrs.

[022-051] Lorch was fat and jolly, with a red face, always shining as if she had just

[022-052] come from the stove, bright little eyes, and hair of several colors. Her own

[022-053] hair was one cast of iron-gray, her switch another, and her false front still

[022-054] another. Her clothes always smelled of savory cooking, except when she was

[022-055] dressed for church or *Kaffeeklatsch*, and then she smelled of bay rum or

[022-056] of the lemon-verbena sprig which she tucked inside her puffy black kid glove.

[022-057] Her cooking justified all that Mr. Larsen had said of it, and Thea had never

[022-058] been so well nourished before.

[022-059] The daughter, Mrs. Andersen,—Irene, her mother called her,—was a

[022-060] different sort of woman altogether. She was perhaps forty years old, angular,

[022-061] big-boned, with large, thin features, light-blue eyes, and dry, yellow hair,

[022-062] the bang tightly frizzed. She was pale, anaemic, and sentimental. She had

[022-063] married the youngest son of a rich, arrogant Swedish family who were lumber

[022-064] merchants in St. Paul. There she dwelt during her married life. Oscar Andersen

[022-065] was a strong, full-blooded fellow who had counted on a long life and had been

[022-066] rather careless about his business affairs. He was killed by the explosion of a

[022-067] steam boiler in the mills, and his brothers managed to prove that he had very

[022-068] little stock in the big business. They had strongly disapproved of his marriage

[022-069] and they agreed among themselves that they were entirely justified in

[022-070] defrauding his widow, who, they said, “would only marry again and give

[022-071] some fellow a good thing of it.” Mrs. Andersen would not go to law with

[022-072] the family that had always snubbed and wounded her—she felt the

[022-073] humiliation of being thrust out more than she felt her impoverishment; so she

[022-074] went back to Chicago to live with her widowed mother on an income of five

[022-075] hundred a year. This experience had given her sentimental nature an incurable

[022-076] hurt. Something withered away in her. Her head had a downward droop; her step

[022-077] was soft and apologetic, even in her mother’s house, and her smile had

[022-078] the sickly, uncertain flicker that so often comes from a secret humiliation.

[022-079] She was affable and yet shrinking, like one who has come down in the world, who

[022-080] has known better clothes, better carpets, better people, brighter hopes. Her

[022-081] husband was buried in the Andersen lot in St. Paul, with a locked iron fence

[022-082] around it. She had to go to his eldest brother for the key when she went to say

[022-083] good-bye to his grave. She clung to the Swedish Church because it had been her

[022-084] husband’s church.

[022-085] As her mother had no room for her household belongings, Mrs. Andersen had

[022-086] brought home with her only her bedroom set, which now furnished her own room at

[022-087] Mrs. Lorch’s. There she spent most of her time, doing fancywork or

[022-088] writing letters to sympathizing German friends in St. Paul, surrounded by

[022-089] keepsakes and photographs of the burly Oscar Andersen. Thea, when she was

[022-090] admitted to this room, and shown these photographs, found herself wondering,

[022-091] like the Andersen family, why such a lusty, gay-looking fellow ever thought he

[022-092] wanted this pallid, long-cheeked woman, whose manner was always that of

[022-093] withdrawing, and who must have been rather thin-blooded even as a girl.

[022-094] Mrs. Andersen was certainly a depressing person. It sometimes annoyed Thea very

[022-095] much to hear her insinuating knock on the door, her flurried explanation of why

[022-096] she had come, as she backed toward the stairs. Mrs. Andersen admired Thea

[022-097] greatly. She thought it a distinction to be even a “temporary

[022-098] soprano”—Thea called herself so quite seriously—in the

[022-099] Swedish Church. She also thought it distinguished to be a pupil of

[022-100] Harsanyi’s. She considered Thea very handsome, very Swedish, very

[022-101] talented. She fluttered about the upper floor when Thea was practicing. In

[022-102] short, she tried to make a heroine of her, just as Tillie Kronborg had always

[022-103] done, and Thea was conscious of something of the sort. When she was working and

[022-104] heard Mrs. Andersen tip-toeing past her door, she used to shrug her shoulders

[022-105] and wonder whether she was always to have a Tillie diving furtively about her

[022-106] in some disguise or other.

[022-107] At the dressmaker’s Mrs. Andersen recalled Tillie even more painfully.

[022-108] After her first Sunday in Mr. Larsen’s choir, Thea saw that she must have

[022-109] a proper dress for morning service. Her Moonstone party dress might do to wear

[022-110] in the evening, but she must have one frock that could stand the light of day.

[022-111] She, of course, knew nothing about Chicago dressmakers, so she let Mrs.

[022-112] Andersen take her to a German woman whom she recommended warmly. The German

[022-113] dressmaker was excitable and dramatic. Concert dresses, she said, were her

[022-114] specialty. In her fitting-room there were photographs of singers in the dresses

[022-115] she had made them for this or that *Sängerfest*. She and Mrs. Andersen

[022-116] together achieved a costume which would have warmed Tillie Kronborg’s

[022-117] heart. It was clearly intended for a woman of forty, with violent tastes. There

[022-118] seemed to be a piece of every known fabric in it somewhere. When it came home,

[022-119] and was spread out on her huge bed, Thea looked it over and told herself

[022-120] candidly that it was “a horror.” However, her money was gone, and

[022-121] there was nothing to do but make the best of the dress. She never wore it

[022-122] except, as she said, “to sing in,” as if it were an unbecoming

[022-123] uniform. When Mrs. Lorch and Irene told her that she “looked like a

[022-124] little bird-of-Paradise in it,” Thea shut her teeth and repeated to

[022-125] herself words she had learned from Joe Giddy and [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]].

[022-126] In these two good women Thea found faithful friends, and in their house she

[022-127] found the quiet and peace which helped her to support the great experiences of

[022-128] that winter.
