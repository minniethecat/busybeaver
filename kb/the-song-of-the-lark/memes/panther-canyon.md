# Panther Canyon

## Overview

Panther Canyon is the novel's most symbolically charged location—an ancient cliff-dweller site in Arizona where Thea Kronborg spends a summer that transforms her understanding of art and her identity as an artist. Here, amid the ruins of a prehistoric civilization, she discovers pottery shards and stone tools made by women centuries before—women who, she realizes, were artists in the same fundamental sense she aspires to be (016-207).

## The Ancient Ruins

The cliff-dweller ruins of Panther Canyon testify to a civilization that flourished and vanished long before European contact. The pottery shards Thea discovers, shaped with an instinct for beauty and proportion, connect her to a tradition of human creativity that predates and transcends the European classical tradition she studies. These ancient artists, living at the edge of survival, still devoted precious energy to making their vessels beautiful—proof that the aesthetic impulse is fundamental.

## Transformative Power

Thea's summer in Panther Canyon is the novel's spiritual center. Removed from the pressures of Chicago's competitive music world, living in direct contact with an ancient landscape and its artifacts, she experiences a kind of rebirth. The canyon strips away everything false or merely conventional, leaving only what is essential. When Thea returns from Panther Canyon, she is ready to become the artist she was meant to be.
