## XV

[015-001] By the time Thea’s fifteenth birthday came round, she was established as

[015-002] a music teacher in Moonstone. The new room had been added to the house early in

[015-003] the spring, and Thea had been giving her lessons there since the middle of May.

[015-004] She liked the personal independence which was accorded her as a wage-earner.

[015-005] The family questioned her comings and goings very little. She could go

[015-006] buggy-riding with [[ray-kennedy|Ray Kennedy]], for instance, without taking Gunner or Axel. She

[015-007] could go to [[spanish-johnny|Spanish Johnny]]’s and sing part songs with the Mexicans, and

[015-008] nobody objected.

[015-009] Thea was still under the first excitement of teaching, and was terribly in

[015-010] earnest about it. If a pupil did not get on well, she fumed and fretted. She

[015-011] counted until she was hoarse. She listened to scales in her sleep. Wunsch had

[015-012] taught only one pupil seriously, but Thea taught twenty. The duller they were,

[015-013] the more furiously she poked and prodded them. With the little girls she was

[015-014] nearly always patient, but with pupils older than herself, she sometimes lost

[015-015] her temper. One of her mistakes was to let herself in for a calling-down from

[015-016] Mrs. Livery Johnson. That lady appeared at the Kronborgs’ one morning and

[015-017] announced that she would allow no girl to stamp her foot at her daughter Grace.

[015-018] She added that Thea’s bad manners with the older girls were being talked

[015-019] about all over town, and that if her temper did not speedily improve she would

[015-020] lose all her advanced pupils. Thea was frightened. She felt she could never

[015-021] bear the disgrace, if such a thing happened. Besides, what would her father

[015-022] say, after he had gone to the expense of building an addition to the house?

[015-023] Mrs. Johnson demanded an apology to Grace. Thea said she was willing to make

[015-024] it. Mrs. Johnson said that hereafter, since she had taken lessons of the best

[015-025] piano teacher in Grinnell, Iowa, she herself would decide what pieces Grace

[015-026] should study. Thea readily consented to that, and Mrs. Johnson rustled away to

[015-027] tell a neighbor woman that [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] could be meek enough when you went at

[015-028] her right.

[015-029] Thea was telling Ray about this unpleasant encounter as they were driving out

[015-030] to the sand hills the next Sunday.

[015-031] “She was stuffing you, all right, Thee,” Ray reassured her.

[015-032] “There’s no general dissatisfaction among your scholars. She just

[015-033] wanted to get in a knock. I talked to the piano tuner the last time he was

[015-034] here, and he said all the people he tuned for expressed themselves very

[015-035] favorably about your teaching. I wish you didn’t take so much pains with

[015-036] them, myself.”

[015-037] “But I have to, Ray. They’re all so dumb. They’ve got no

[015-038] ambition,” Thea exclaimed irritably. “Jenny Smiley is the only one

[015-039] who isn’t stupid. She can read pretty well, and she has such good hands.

[015-040] But she don’t care a rap about it. She has no pride.”

[015-041] Ray’s face was full of complacent satisfaction as he glanced sidewise at

[015-042] Thea, but she was looking off intently into the mirage, at one of those mammoth

[015-043] cattle that are nearly always reflected there. “Do you find it easier to

[015-044] teach in your new room?” he asked.

[015-045] “Yes; I’m not interrupted so much. Of course, if I ever happen to

[015-046] want to practice at night, that’s always the night Anna chooses to go to

[015-047] bed early.”

[015-048] “It’s a darned shame, Thee, you didn’t cop that room for

[015-049] yourself. I’m sore at the *padre* about that. He ought to give you

[015-050] that room. You could fix it up so pretty.”

[015-051] “I didn’t want it, honest I didn’t. Father would have let me

[015-052] have it. I like my own room better. Somehow I can think better in a little

[015-053] room. Besides, up there I am away from everybody, and I can read as late as I

[015-054] please and nobody nags me.”

[015-055] “A growing girl needs lots of sleep,” Ray providently remarked.

[015-056] Thea moved restlessly on the buggy cushions. “They need other things

[015-057] more,” she muttered. “Oh, I forgot. I brought something to show

[015-058] you. Look here, it came on my birthday. Wasn’t it nice of him to

[015-059] remember?” She took from her pocket a postcard, bent in the middle and

[015-060] folded, and handed it to Ray. On it was a white dove, perched on a wreath of

[015-061] very blue forget-me-nots, and “Birthday Greetings” in gold letters.

[015-062] Under this was written, “From A. Wunsch.”

[015-063] Ray turned the card over, examined the postmark, and then began to laugh.

[015-064] “Concord, Kansas. He has my sympathy!”

[015-065] “Why, is that a poor town?”

[015-066] “It’s the jumping-off place, no town at all. Some houses dumped

[015-067] down in the middle of a cornfield. You get lost in the corn. Not even a saloon

[015-068] to keep things going; sell whiskey without a license at the butcher shop, beer

[015-069] on ice with the liver and beefsteak. I wouldn’t stay there over Sunday

[015-070] for a ten-dollar bill.”

[015-071] “Oh, dear! What do you suppose he’s doing there? Maybe he just

[015-072] stopped off there a few days to tune pianos,” Thea suggested hopefully.

[015-073] Ray gave her back the card. “He’s headed in the wrong direction.

[015-074] What does he want to get back into a grass country for? Now, there are lots of

[015-075] good live towns down on the Santa Fé, and everybody down there is musical. He

[015-076] could always get a job playing in saloons if he was dead broke. I’ve

[015-077] figured out that I’ve got no years of my life to waste in a Methodist

[015-078] country where they raise pork.”

[015-079] “We must stop on our way back and show this card to Mrs. Kohler. She

[015-080] misses him so.”

[015-081] “By the way, Thee, I hear the old woman goes to church every Sunday to

[015-082] hear you sing. Fritz tells me he has to wait till two o’clock for his

[015-083] Sunday dinner these days. The church people ought to give you credit for that,

[015-084] when they go for you.”

[015-085] Thea shook her head and spoke in a tone of resignation. “They’ll

[015-086] always go for me, just as they did for Wunsch. It wasn’t because he drank

[015-087] they went for him; not really. It was something else.”

[015-088] “You want to salt your money down, Thee, and go to [[chicago|Chicago]] and take some

[015-089] lessons. Then you come back, and wear a long feather and high heels and put on

[015-090] a few airs, and that’ll fix ’em. That’s what they

[015-091] like.”

[015-092] “I’ll never have money enough to go to Chicago. Mother meant to

[015-093] lend me some, I think, but now they’ve got hard times back in Nebraska,

[015-094] and her farm don’t bring her in anything. Takes all the tenant can raise

[015-095] to pay the taxes. Don’t let’s talk about that. You promised to tell

[015-096] me about the play you went to see in Denver.”

[015-097] Any one would have liked to hear Ray’s simple and clear account of the

[015-098] performance he had seen at the Tabor Grand Opera House—Maggie Mitchell in

[015-099] *Little Barefoot*—and any one would have liked to watch his kind

[015-100] face. Ray looked his best out of doors, when his thick red hands were covered

[015-101] by gloves, and the dull red of his sunburned face somehow seemed right in the

[015-102] light and wind. He looked better, too, with his hat on; his hair was thin and

[015-103] dry, with no particular color or character, “regular Willy-boy

[015-104] hair,” as he himself described it. His eyes were pale beside the reddish

[015-105] bronze of his skin. They had the faded look often seen in the eyes of men who

[015-106] have lived much in the sun and wind and who have been accustomed to train their

[015-107] vision upon distant objects.

[015-108] Ray realized that Thea’s life was dull and exacting, and that she missed

[015-109] Wunsch. He knew she worked hard, that she put up with a great many little

[015-110] annoyances, and that her duties as a teacher separated her more than ever from

[015-111] the boys and girls of her own age. He did everything he could to provide

[015-112] recreation for her. He brought her candy and magazines and pineapples—of

[015-113] which she was very fond—from Denver, and kept his eyes and ears open for

[015-114] anything that might interest her. He was, of course, living for Thea. He had

[015-115] thought it all out carefully and had made up his mind just when he would speak

[015-116] to her. When she was seventeen, then he would tell her his plan and ask her to

[015-117] marry him. He would be willing to wait two, or even three years, until she was

[015-118] twenty, if she thought best. By that time he would surely have got in on

[015-119] something: copper, oil, gold, silver, sheep,—something.

[015-120] Meanwhile, it was pleasure enough to feel that she depended on him more and

[015-121] more, that she leaned upon his steady kindness. He never broke faith with

[015-122] himself about her; he never hinted to her of his hopes for the future, never

[015-123] suggested that she might be more intimately confidential with him, or talked to

[015-124] her of the thing he thought about so constantly. He had the chivalry which is

[015-125] perhaps the proudest possession of his race. He had never embarrassed her by so

[015-126] much as a glance. Sometimes, when they drove out to the sand hills, he let his

[015-127] left arm lie along the back of the buggy seat, but it never came any nearer to

[015-128] Thea than that, never touched her. He often turned to her a face full of pride,

[015-129] and frank admiration, but his glance was never so intimate or so penetrating as

[015-130] [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]]’s. His blue eyes were clear and shallow, friendly,

[015-131] uninquiring. He rested Thea because he was so different; because, though he

[015-132] often told her interesting things, he never set lively fancies going in her

[015-133] head; because he never misunderstood her, and because he never, by any chance,

[015-134] for a single instant, understood her! Yes, with Ray she was safe; by him she

[015-135] would never be discovered!
