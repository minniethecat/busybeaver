# Women and Ambition

## Overview

Published in 1915, *The Song of the Lark* offers one of early twentieth-century American literature's most sustained portraits of female ambition. Thea Kronborg's drive to become a great artist places her in tension with the era's expectations for women—expectations embodied by her mother's domestic life, her sister's conventional marriage, and the limited professional horizons available to women in Moonstone and even in Chicago. The novel treats Thea's ambition not as a deviation from femininity but as a fuller expression of it.

## Ambition vs. Domesticity

The novel sets Thea's artistic ambition against the model of female domesticity represented by her mother and her married sister Anna. Mrs. Kronborg's life is one of service—to her husband's congregation, to her many children, to the endless demands of household management. This life is presented with respect but also as a closed horizon: it is complete, settled, and leaves no room for the kind of self-making Thea requires.

Anna Kronborg's marriage represents the conventional path Thea rejects. The novel is notably unsentimental about Anna's choice, neither condemning it nor presenting it as a failure. But Thea's ambition is of a different order, demanding a scope and intensity that domesticity cannot accommodate.

## The Female Artist in Society

Thea's career as an opera singer places her in a paradoxical social position. As a performer, she commands the stage and the audience's adoration; as a woman, she is subject to the same scrutiny and limitation as any other. The novel makes a radical argument for its era: that the highest form of female achievement is not wifehood or motherhood but artistic creation. Thea's eventual triumph is presented not as a consolation for the domestic life she never had but as a superior fulfillment.

## Key Passages

Throughout the novel, Thea's ambition is described in terms that would conventionally be applied to male protagonists: she is driven, hungry, relentless. Cather refuses to soften these qualities or to present them as unwomanly. Instead, the novel suggests that true artistry transcends gender categories—that the creative drive is neither masculine nor feminine but human.
