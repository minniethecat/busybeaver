## Overview

Mrs. Lorch is the landlady of the boarding house in Chicago where Thea Kronborg settles upon first leaving Moonstone to study music. A warm, maternal figure, she provides Thea with a stable home environment during the critical early years of her artistic development. Along with her widowed daughter Irene Andersen, Mrs. Lorch becomes one of Thea's first Chicago connections outside the conservatory, and her house — with its grape arbor and flowering back yard — becomes a sanctuary for the young singer [022-016].

## Biography / Character Arc

### The Boarding House

Mrs. Lorch and her daughter lived half a mile from the Swedish Reform Church in Chicago, and though they had "never taken boarders" before, they took in Thea at the recommendation of Mr. Larsen [021-177]. Mrs. Lorch is described as "fat and jolly, with a red face, always shining as if she had just come from the stove, bright little eyes, and hair of several colors" [022-051]. Her clothes "always smelled of savory cooking, except when she was dressed for church or Kaffeeklatsch, and then she smelled of bay rum or of the lemon-verbena sprig which she tucked inside her puffy black kid glove" [022-056].

The boarding house itself is modest but comfortable, with a grape arbor at the entrance — "Old Mrs. Lorch could never bring herself to have it pruned" [022-016]. The house becomes Thea's home base for her first years in Chicago.

### Care for Thea

Mrs. Lorch takes genuine maternal interest in Thea. She and Mrs. Andersen "often told each other it was strange that Miss Kronborg did not make more acquaintances" among the Swedish families of Chicago [025-030]. When spring arrives, "Mrs. Lorch's back yard burst into bloom, and for the first time in months Thea wanted to go out into the world" [025-131]. Mrs. Lorch, knowing Chicago weather, also prevails upon Thea to dress warmly for outings [025-142]. Her predictions about bad weather are borne out when Thea emerges from the concert hall: "Mrs. Lorch's predictions had been fulfilled" [025-191].

### Thea's Departure

When Thea eventually moves on from the boarding house, the separation is noted with poignancy. Thea "had been compelled to leave her old friends Mrs. Lorch and Mrs. Andersen" [032-010], suggesting that this was a difficult but necessary step in her artistic growth. The narrative later reflects that since Thea "left Mrs. Lorch, this was the first place where she had got away from" the fatiguing social demands that had burdened her [036-007].

## Relationships

### Irene Andersen
Mrs. Lorch's daughter, widowed and returned to live with her mother on a reduced income. The two women share the management of the boarding house and often consult each other about their boarders' affairs.

### Thea Kronborg
Mrs. Lorch provides Thea with a warm, stable home — the first time Thea has lived away from her family in Moonstone. Thea is "comfortably settled with Mrs. Lorch" [022-002], and Mrs. Lorch's cooking justifies all that Mr. Larsen had promised [022-057]. One of Mrs. Lorch's lodgers works in a packing-house and brings Thea word of performance opportunities [025-036], showing how the boarding house functions as a small community supporting Thea's ambitions.

### Mr. Larsen
Mr. Larsen (Reverend Lars Larsen), the minister at the Swedish Reform Church, recommended Thea to Mrs. Lorch's boarding house [021-177], praising her cooking. This connection links Thea's Moonstone world with her new Chicago life.

## Significance

Mrs. Lorch represents the nurturing, domestic stability that Thea requires during her formative Chicago years. Unlike the Kronborg household in Moonstone — crowded with siblings and marked by Mrs. Kronborg's more reserved nature — Mrs. Lorch's home offers Thea the space to practice and grow while still providing maternal warmth. The grape arbor and the blooming back yard suggest a small pocket of nature in the city, a temporary Arcadia for the young artist. Mrs. Lorch's eventual absence from Thea's life (after Thea moves on) is part of the novel's larger pattern: the protagonist must leave behind one home after another — Moonstone, Mrs. Lorch's, Panther Canyon — each departure marking a new stage of artistic independence.
