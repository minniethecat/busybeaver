## VII

[057-001] On Saturday night [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] went with [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] to hear

[057-002] “Tannhäuser.” Thea had a rehearsal on Sunday afternoon, but as she

[057-003] was not on the bill again until Wednesday, she promised to dine with Archie and

[057-004] Ottenburg on Monday, if they could make the dinner early.

[057-005] At a little after eight on Monday evening, the three friends returned to

[057-006] Thea’s apartment and seated themselves for an hour of quiet talk.

[057-007] “I’m sorry we couldn’t have had Landry with us

[057-008] tonight,” Thea said, “but he’s on at Weber and Fields’

[057-009] every night now. You ought to hear him, Dr. Archie. He often sings the old

[057-010] Scotch airs you used to love.”

[057-011] “Why not go down this evening?” Fred suggested hopefully, glancing

[057-012] at his watch. “That is, if you’d like to go. I can telephone and

[057-013] find what time he comes on.”

[057-014] Thea hesitated. “No, I think not. I took a long walk this afternoon and

[057-015] I’m rather tired. I think I can get to sleep early and be so much ahead.

[057-016] I don’t mean at once, however,” seeing Dr. Archie’s

[057-017] disappointed look. “I always like to hear Landry,” she added.

[057-018] “He never had much voice, and it’s worn, but there’s a

[057-019] sweetness about it, and he sings with such taste.”

[057-020] “Yes, doesn’t he? May I?” Fred took out his cigarette case.

[057-021] “It really doesn’t bother your throat?”

[057-022] “A little doesn’t. But cigar smoke does. Poor Dr. Archie! Can you

[057-023] do with one of those?”

[057-024] “I’m learning to like them,” the doctor declared, taking one

[057-025] from the case Fred proffered him.

[057-026] “Landry’s the only fellow I know in this country who can do that

[057-027] sort of thing,” Fred went on. “Like the best English ballad

[057-028] singers. He can sing even popular stuff by higher lights, as it were.”

[057-029] Thea nodded. “Yes; sometimes I make him sing his most foolish things for

[057-030] me. It’s restful, as he does it. That’s when I’m homesick,

[057-031] Dr. Archie.”

[057-032] “You knew him in Germany, Thea?” Dr. Archie had quietly abandoned

[057-033] his cigarette as a comfortless article. “When you first went over?”

[057-034] “Yes. He was a good friend to a green girl. He helped me with my German

[057-035] and my music and my general discouragement. Seemed to care more about my

[057-036] getting on than about himself. He had no money, either. An old aunt had loaned

[057-037] him a little to study on.—Will you answer that, Fred?”

[057-038] Fred caught up the telephone and stopped the buzz while Thea went on talking to

[057-039] Dr. Archie about Landry. Telling some one to hold the wire, he presently put

[057-040] down the instrument and approached Thea with a startled expression on his face.

[057-041] “It’s the management,” he said quietly. “Gloeckler has

[057-042] broken down: fainting fits. Madame Rheinecker is in Atlantic City and Schramm

[057-043] is singing in Philadelphia tonight. They want to know whether you can come down

[057-044] and finish *Sieglinde*.”

[057-045] “What time is it?”

[057-046] “Eight fifty-five. The first act is just over. They can hold the curtain

[057-047] twenty-five minutes.”

[057-048] Thea did not move. “Twenty-five and thirty-five makes sixty,” she

[057-049] muttered. “Tell them I’ll come if they hold the curtain till I am

[057-050] in the dressing-room. Say I’ll have to wear her costumes, and the dresser

[057-051] must have everything ready. Then call a taxi, please.”

[057-052] Thea had not changed her position since he first interrupted her, but she had

[057-053] grown pale and was opening and shutting her hands rapidly. She looked, Fred

[057-054] thought, terrified. He half turned toward the telephone, but hung on one foot.

[057-055] “Have you ever sung the part?” he asked.

[057-056] “No, but I’ve rehearsed it. That’s all right. Get the

[057-057] cab.” Still she made no move. She merely turned perfectly blank eyes to

[057-058] Dr. Archie and said absently, “It’s curious, but just at this

[057-059] minute I can’t remember a bar of ‘Walküre’ after the first

[057-060] act. And I let my maid go out.” She sprang up and beckoned Archie without

[057-061] so much, he felt sure, as knowing who he was. “Come with me.” She

[057-062] went quickly into her sleeping-chamber and threw open a door into a trunk-room.

[057-063] “See that white trunk? It’s not locked. It’s full of wigs, in

[057-064] boxes. Look until you find one marked ‘Ring 2.’ Bring it

[057-065] quick!” While she directed him, she threw open a square trunk and began

[057-066] tossing out shoes of every shape and color.

[057-067] Ottenburg appeared at the door. “Can I help you?”

[057-068] She threw him some white sandals with long laces and silk stockings pinned to

[057-069] them. “Put those in something, and then go to the piano and give me a few

[057-070] measures in there—you know.” She was behaving somewhat like a

[057-071] cyclone now, and while she wrenched open drawers and closet doors, Ottenburg

[057-072] got to the piano as quickly as possible and began to herald the reappearance of

[057-073] the Volsung pair, trusting to memory.

[057-074] In a few moments Thea came out enveloped in her long fur coat with a scarf over

[057-075] her head and knitted woolen gloves on her hands. Her glassy eye took in the

[057-076] fact that Fred was playing from memory, and even in her distracted state, a

[057-077] faint smile flickered over her colorless lips. She stretched out a woolly hand,

[057-078] “The score, please. Behind you, there.”

[057-079] Dr. Archie followed with a canvas box and a satchel. As they went through the

[057-080] hall, the men caught up their hats and coats. They left the music-room, Fred

[057-081] noticed, just seven minutes after he got the telephone message. In the elevator

[057-082] Thea said in that husky whisper which had so perplexed Dr. Archie when he first

[057-083] heard it, “Tell the driver he must do it in twenty minutes, less if he

[057-084] can. He must leave the light on in the cab. I can do a good deal in twenty

[057-085] minutes. If only you hadn’t made me eat—Damn that duck!” she

[057-086] broke out bitterly; “why did you?”

[057-087] “Wish I had it back! But it won’t bother you, to-night. You need

[057-088] strength,” he pleaded consolingly.

[057-089] But she only muttered angrily under her breath, “Idiot, idiot!”

[057-090] Ottenburg shot ahead and instructed the driver, while the doctor put Thea into

[057-091] the cab and shut the door. She did not speak to either of them again. As the

[057-092] driver scrambled into his seat she opened the score and fixed her eyes upon it.

[057-093] Her face, in the white light, looked as bleak as a stone quarry.

[057-094] As her cab slid away, Ottenburg shoved Archie into a second taxi that waited by

[057-095] the curb. “We’d better trail her,” he explained. “There

[057-096] might be a hold-up of some kind.” As the cab whizzed off he broke into an

[057-097] eruption of profanity.

[057-098] “What’s the matter, Fred?” the doctor asked. He was a good

[057-099] deal dazed by the rapid evolutions of the last ten minutes.

[057-100] “Matter enough!” Fred growled, buttoning his overcoat with a

[057-101] shiver. “What a way to sing a part for the first time! That duck really

[057-102] is on my conscience. It will be a wonder if she can do anything but quack!

[057-103] Scrambling on in the middle of a performance like this, with no rehearsal! The

[057-104] stuff she has to sing in there is a fright—rhythm, pitch,—and

[057-105] terribly difficult intervals.”

[057-106] “She looked frightened,” Dr. Archie said thoughtfully, “but I

[057-107] thought she looked—determined.”

[057-108] Fred sniffed. “Oh, determined! That’s the kind of rough deal that

[057-109] makes savages of singers. Here’s a part she’s worked on and got

[057-110] ready for for years, and now they give her a chance to go on and butcher it.

[057-111] Goodness knows when she’s looked at the score last, or whether she can

[057-112] use the business she’s studied with this cast. Necker’s singing

[057-113] *Brünnhilde;* she may help her, if it’s not one of her sore

[057-114] nights.”

[057-115] “Is she sore at Thea?” Dr. Archie asked wonderingly.

[057-116] “My dear man, Necker’s sore at everything. She’s breaking up;

[057-117] too early; just when she ought to be at her best. There’s one story that

[057-118] she is struggling under some serious malady, another that she learned a bad

[057-119] method at the Prague Conservatory and has ruined her organ. She’s the

[057-120] sorest thing in the world. If she weathers this winter through, it’ll be

[057-121] her last. She’s paying for it with the last rags of her voice. And

[057-122] then—” Fred whistled softly.

[057-123] “Well, what then?”

[057-124] “Then our girl may come in for some of it. It’s dog eat dog, in

[057-125] this game as in every other.”

[057-126] The cab stopped and Fred and Dr. Archie hurried to the box office. The

[057-127] Monday-night house was sold out. They bought standing room and entered the

[057-128] auditorium just as the press representative of the house was thanking the

[057-129] audience for their patience and telling them that although Madame Gloeckler was

[057-130] too ill to sing, Miss Kronborg had kindly consented to finish her part. This

[057-131] announcement was met with vehement applause from the upper circles of the

[057-132] house.

[057-133] “She has her—constituents,” Dr. Archie murmured.

[057-134] “Yes, up there, where they’re young and hungry. These people down

[057-135] here have dined too well. They won’t mind, however. They like fires and

[057-136] accidents and *divertissements*. Two *Sieglindes* are more unusual

[057-137] than one, so they’ll be satisfied.”

[057-138] After the final disappearance of the mother of Siegfried, Ottenburg and the

[057-139] doctor slipped out through the crowd and left the house. Near the stage

[057-140] entrance Fred found the driver who had brought Thea down. He dismissed him and

[057-141] got a larger car. He and Archie waited on the sidewalk, and when Kronborg came

[057-142] out alone they gathered her into the cab and sprang in after her.

[057-143] Thea sank back into a corner of the back seat and yawned. “Well, I got

[057-144] through, eh?” Her tone was reassuring. “On the whole, I think

[057-145] I’ve given you gentlemen a pretty lively evening, for one who has no

[057-146] social accomplishments.”

[057-147] “Rather! There was something like a popular uprising at the end of the

[057-148] second act. Archie and I couldn’t keep it up as long as the rest of them

[057-149] did. A howl like that ought to show the management which way the wind is

[057-150] blowing. You probably know you were magnificent.”

[057-151] “I thought it went pretty well,” she spoke impartially. “I

[057-152] was rather smart to catch his tempo there, at the beginning of the first

[057-153] recitative, when he came in too soon, don’t you think? It’s tricky

[057-154] in there, without a rehearsal. Oh, I was all right! He took that syncopation

[057-155] too fast in the beginning. Some singers take it fast there—think it

[057-156] sounds more impassioned. That’s one way!” She sniffed, and Fred

[057-157] shot a mirthful glance at Archie. Her boastfulness would have been childish in

[057-158] a schoolboy. In the light of what she had done, of the strain they had lived

[057-159] through during the last two hours, it made one laugh,—almost cry. She

[057-160] went on, robustly: “And I didn’t feel my dinner, really, Fred. I am

[057-161] hungry again, I’m ashamed to say,—and I forgot to order anything at

[057-162] my hotel.”

[057-163] Fred put his hand on the door. “Where to? You must have food.”

[057-164] “Do you know any quiet place, where I won’t be stared at?

[057-165] I’ve still got make-up on.”

[057-166] “I do. Nice English chop-house on Forty-fourth Street. Nobody there at

[057-167] night but theater people after the show, and a few bachelors.” He opened

[057-168] the door and spoke to the driver.

[057-169] As the car turned, Thea reached across to the front seat and drew Dr.

[057-170] Archie’s handkerchief out of his breast pocket.

[057-171] “This comes to me naturally,” she said, rubbing her cheeks and

[057-172] eyebrows. “When I was little I always loved your handkerchiefs because

[057-173] they were silk and smelled of Cologne water. I think they must have been the

[057-174] only really clean handkerchiefs in Moonstone. You were always wiping my face

[057-175] with them, when you met me out in the dust, I remember. Did I never have

[057-176] any?”

[057-177] “I think you’d nearly always used yours up on your baby

[057-178] brother.”

[057-179] Thea sighed. “Yes, Thor had such a way of getting messy. You say

[057-180] he’s a good chauffeur?” She closed her eyes for a moment as if they

[057-181] were tired. Suddenly she looked up. “Isn’t it funny, how we travel

[057-182] in circles? Here you are, still getting me clean, and Fred is still feeding me.

[057-183] I would have died of starvation at that boarding-house on Indiana Avenue if he

[057-184] hadn’t taken me out to the Buckingham and filled me up once in a while.

[057-185] What a cavern I was to fill, too. The waiters used to look astonished.

[057-186] I’m still singing on that food.”

[057-187] Fred alighted and gave Thea his arm as they crossed the icy sidewalk. They were

[057-188] taken upstairs in an antiquated lift and found the cheerful chop-room half full

[057-189] of supper parties. An English company playing at the Empire had just come in.

[057-190] The waiters, in red waistcoats, were hurrying about. Fred got a table at the

[057-191] back of the room, in a corner, and urged his waiter to get the oysters on at

[057-192] once.

[057-193] “Takes a few minutes to open them, sir,” the man expostulated.

[057-194] “Yes, but make it as few as possible, and bring the lady’s first.

[057-195] Then grilled chops with kidneys, and salad.”

[057-196] Thea began eating celery stalks at once, from the base to the foliage.

[057-197] “Necker said something nice to me tonight. You might have thought the

[057-198] management would say something, but not they.” She looked at Fred from

[057-199] under her blackened lashes. “It *was* a stunt, to jump in and sing

[057-200] that second act without rehearsal. It doesn’t sing itself.”

[057-201] Ottenburg was watching her brilliant eyes and her face. She was much handsomer

[057-202] than she had been early in the evening. Excitement of this sort enriched her.

[057-203] It was only under such excitement, he reflected, that she was entirely

[057-204] illuminated, or wholly present. At other times there was something a little

[057-205] cold and empty, like a big room with no people in it. Even in her most genial

[057-206] moods there was a shadow of restlessness, as if she were waiting for something

[057-207] and were exercising the virtue of patience. During dinner she had been as kind

[057-208] as she knew how to be, to him and to Archie, and had given them as much of

[057-209] herself as she could. But, clearly, she knew only one way of being really kind,

[057-210] from the core of her heart out; and there was but one way in which she could

[057-211] give herself to people largely and gladly, spontaneously. Even as a girl she

[057-212] had been at her best in vigorous effort, he remembered; physical effort, when

[057-213] there was no other kind at hand. She could be expansive only in explosions. Old

[057-214] Nathanmeyer had seen it. In the very first song Fred had ever heard her sing,

[057-215] she had unconsciously declared it.

[057-216] [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] turned suddenly from her talk with Archie and peered suspiciously

[057-217] into the corner where Ottenburg sat with folded arms, observing her.

[057-218] “What’s the matter with you, Fred? I’m afraid of you when

[057-219] you’re quiet,—fortunately you almost never are. What are you

[057-220] thinking about?”

[057-221] “I was wondering how you got right with the orchestra so quickly, there

[057-222] at first. I had a flash of terror,” he replied easily.

[057-223] She bolted her last oyster and ducked her head. “So had I! I don’t

[057-224] know how I did catch it. Desperation, I suppose; same way the Indian babies

[057-225] swim when they’re thrown into the river. I *had* to. Now it’s

[057-226] over, I’m glad I had to. I learned a whole lot to-night.”

[057-227] Archie, who usually felt that it behooved him to be silent during such

[057-228] discussions, was encouraged by her geniality to venture, “I don’t

[057-229] see how you can learn anything in such a turmoil; or how you can keep your mind

[057-230] on it, for that matter.”

[057-231] Thea glanced about the room and suddenly put her hand up to her hair.

[057-232] “Mercy, I’ve no hat on! Why didn’t you tell me? And I seem to

[057-233] be wearing a rumpled dinner dress, with all this paint on my face! I must look

[057-234] like something you picked up on Second Avenue. I hope there are no Colorado

[057-235] reformers about, Dr. Archie. What a dreadful old pair these people must be

[057-236] thinking you! Well, I had to eat.” She sniffed the savor of the grill as

[057-237] the waiter uncovered it. “Yes, draught beer, please. No, thank you, Fred,

[057-238] *no* champagne.—To go back to your question, Dr. Archie, you can

[057-239] believe I keep my mind on it. That’s the whole trick, in so far as stage

[057-240] experience goes; keeping right there every second. If I think of anything else

[057-241] for a flash, I’m gone, done for. But at the same time, one can take

[057-242] things in—with another part of your brain, maybe. It’s different

[057-243] from what you get in study, more practical and conclusive. There are some

[057-244] things you learn best in calm, and some in storm. You learn the delivery of a

[057-245] part only before an audience.”

[057-246] “Heaven help us,” gasped Ottenburg. “Weren’t you

[057-247] hungry, though! It’s beautiful to see you eat.”

[057-248] “Glad you like it. Of course I’m hungry. Are you staying over for

[057-249] ‘Rheingold’ Friday afternoon?”

[057-250] “My dear Thea,”—Fred lit a cigarette,—“I’m

[057-251] a serious business man now. I have to sell beer. I’m due in [[chicago|Chicago]] on

[057-252] Wednesday. I’d come back to hear you, but *Fricka* is not an

[057-253] alluring part.”

[057-254] “Then you’ve never heard it well done.” She spoke up hotly.

[057-255] “Fat German woman scolding her husband, eh? That’s not my idea.

[057-256] Wait till you hear my *Fricka*. It’s a beautiful part.” Thea

[057-257] leaned forward on the table and touched Archie’s arm. “You

[057-258] remember, Dr. Archie, how my mother always wore her hair, parted in the middle

[057-259] and done low on her neck behind, so you got the shape of her head and such a

[057-260] calm, white forehead? I wear mine like that for *Fricka*. A little more

[057-261] coronet effect, built up a little higher at the sides, but the idea’s the

[057-262] same. I think you’ll notice it.” She turned to Ottenburg

[057-263] reproachfully: “It’s noble music, Fred, from the first measure.

[057-264] There’s nothing lovelier than the *wonniger Hausrath*. It’s

[057-265] all such comprehensive sort of music—fateful. Of course, *Fricka

[057-266] knows*,” Thea ended quietly.

[057-267] Fred sighed. “There, you’ve spoiled my itinerary. Now I’ll

[057-268] have to come back, of course. Archie, you’d better get busy about seats

[057-269] to-morrow.”

[057-270] “I can get you box seats, somewhere. I know nobody here, and I never ask

[057-271] for any.” Thea began hunting among her wraps. “Oh, how funny!

[057-272] I’ve only these short woolen gloves, and no sleeves. Put on my coat

[057-273] first. Those English people can’t make out where you got your lady,

[057-274] she’s so made up of contradictions.” She rose laughing and plunged

[057-275] her arms into the coat Dr. Archie held for her. As she settled herself into it

[057-276] and buttoned it under her chin, she gave him an old signal with her eyelid.

[057-277] “I’d like to sing another part to-night. This is the sort of

[057-278] evening I fancy, when there’s something to do. Let me see: I have to sing

[057-279] in ‘Trovatore’ Wednesday night, and there are rehearsals for the

[057-280] ‘Ring’ every day this week. Consider me dead until Saturday, Dr.

[057-281] Archie. I invite you both to dine with me on Saturday night, the day after

[057-282] ‘Rheingold.’ And Fred must leave early, for I want to talk to you

[057-283] alone. You’ve been here nearly a week, and I haven’t had a serious

[057-284] word with you. *Tak for mad*, Fred, as the Norwegians say.”
