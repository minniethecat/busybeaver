## Overview

Necker — referred to both as "Necker" and "Madame Necker" — is a fellow opera singer who appears in the closing chapters of *The Song of the Lark*, set during Thea Kronborg's rise in the German opera world. She is an established artist who sings major roles including Elizabeth in *Tannhäuser* and Isolde in *Tristan und Isolde*. Her temperament is famously difficult — she is "sore at everything" [057-116] — but she ultimately extends a generous, professional recognition to Thea, acknowledging her as a genuine artistic peer. Necker represents the older generation of singers whose careers are waning even as Thea's star ascends.

## Biography / Character Arc

### An Established Career

By the time Thea encounters her, Necker is an established figure in the German opera company. She is associated with major Wagnerian roles: Elizabeth in *Tannhäuser* [056-222] and Isolde in *Tristan und Isolde* [061-015]. She is "quite as popular as" another well-known singer [059-244], and her performances are significant enough that her availability affects the company's planning — "Necker won't be able to do much next" season [060-054], a fact that opens opportunities for younger singers, including Thea.

### The Difficult Temperament

Necker's personality is a source of tension within the company. She is described as "sore at everything" [057-116] and prone to discontent. Her difficult nature is well-known — a fellow musician dismisses her complaints with "My dear man, Necker's sore at everything" [057-116], suggesting a pattern of habitual dissatisfaction that the company has learned to manage rather than remedy.

### Generosity Toward Thea

Despite her temperament, Necker shows genuine professional generosity toward Thea. She "said something nice to" Thea one evening [057-197], a small gesture that carries weight coming from an established artist. More significantly, Thea feels "that she and Necker" share "some sort of endeavor, and that Necker recognized it and had a" sense of artistic kinship with her [061-012]. This recognition — from a difficult, respected veteran — validates Thea's sense of herself as a serious artist.

In the novel's closing pages, Necker is "very gracious" to Thea [061-008], a softening that suggests professional respect can transcend personal temperament. The older artist, whose career is entering its final phase — "Madame Necker was forty, and her" voice showing the effects of time [061-018] — sees in Thea the future of the art they both serve.

### The Passing of the Torch

The novel frames Necker's story as one of generational transition. She is forty [061-018], still performing but aware of her limitations. Her role as Isolde is one that Thea will presumably inherit. The "management" faces the question of whether "so honest an artist as Necker could not stand the test of a" new production [061-017], and the answer, implied by the novel's end, is that the future belongs to Thea. Necker's gracious acknowledgment of this fact is one of the quiet generosities that make Thea's ascent possible.

## Relationships

### Thea Kronborg
The relationship between Necker and Thea evolves from professional distance to mutual recognition. Necker's initial demeanor may be prickly, but she comes to see Thea as a genuine colleague. Thea, for her part, "had always felt that she and Necker" shared an artistic bond [061-012]. Necker's graciousness toward Thea [061-008] and her generous words [057-197] are markers of professional respect that Thea, having fought so hard for recognition, deeply values.

### The Opera Company
Necker is embedded in the institutional life of the German opera company. Her roles, her temperament, her aging voice — all are part of the company's calculus as it plans seasons and casts productions. Her difficult personality is a known quantity, managed with a mixture of exasperation ("Necker's sore at everything") and professional respect.

## Significance

Necker serves several narrative functions in the novel's closing chapters. First, she represents the established order that Thea must enter and eventually supersede — the older generation of singers whose careers define the professional landscape. Second, her difficult temperament and ultimate graciousness illustrate a truth about artistic communities: that personal prickliness and professional integrity can coexist. Third, her recognition of Thea — "Necker recognized it" [061-013] — provides external validation of Thea's achievement from within the opera world itself, complementing the perspectives of outsiders like Dr. Archie and Fred Ottenburg. Finally, at forty [061-018], she embodies the transience of even the most successful careers, a quiet reminder that Thea's own triumphs will someday give way to a new generation.
