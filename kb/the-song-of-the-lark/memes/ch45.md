## VIII

[045-001] On the first day of September [[fred-ottenburg|Fred Ottenburg]] and [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] left Flagstaff

[045-002] by the east-bound express. As the bright morning advanced, they sat alone on

[045-003] the rear platform of the observation car, watching the yellow miles unfold and

[045-004] disappear. With complete content they saw the brilliant, empty country flash

[045-005] by. They were tired of the desert and the dead races, of a world without change

[045-006] or ideas. Fred said he was glad to sit back and let the Santa Fé do the work

[045-007] for a while.

[045-008] “And where are we going, anyhow?” he added.

[045-009] “To [[chicago|Chicago]], I suppose. Where else would we be going?” Thea hunted

[045-010] for a handkerchief in her handbag.

[045-011] “I wasn’t sure, so I had the trunks checked to Albuquerque. We can

[045-012] recheck there to Chicago, if you like. Why Chicago? You’ll never go back

[045-013] to Bowers. Why wouldn’t this be a good time to make a run for it? We

[045-014] could take the southern branch at Albuquerque, down to El Paso, and then over

[045-015] into Mexico. We are exceptionally free. Nobody waiting for us anywhere.”

[045-016] Thea sighted along the steel rails that quivered in the light behind them.

[045-017] “I don’t see why I couldn’t marry you in Chicago, as well as

[045-018] any place,” she brought out with some embarrassment.

[045-019] Fred took the handbag out of her nervous clasp and swung it about on his

[045-020] finger. “You’ve no particular love for that spot, have you?

[045-021] Besides, as I’ve told you, my family would make a row. They are an

[045-022] excitable lot. They discuss and argue everlastingly. The only way I can ever

[045-023] put anything through is to go ahead, and convince them afterward.”

[045-024] “Yes; I understand. I don’t mind that. I don’t want to marry

[045-025] your family. I’m sure you wouldn’t want to marry mine. But I

[045-026] don’t see why we have to go so far.”

[045-027] “When we get to Winslow, you look about the freight yards and

[045-028] you’ll probably see several yellow cars with my name on them.

[045-029] That’s why, my dear. When your visiting-card is on every beer bottle, you

[045-030] can’t do things quietly. Things get into the papers.” As he watched

[045-031] her troubled expression, he grew anxious. He leaned forward on his camp-chair,

[045-032] and kept twirling the handbag between his knees. “Here’s a

[045-033] suggestion, Thea,” he said presently. “Dismiss it if you

[045-034] don’t like it: suppose we go down to Mexico on the chance. You’ve

[045-035] never seen anything like Mexico City; it will be a lark for you, anyhow. If you

[045-036] change your mind, and don’t want to marry me, you can go back to Chicago,

[045-037] and I’ll take a steamer from Vera Cruz and go up to New York. When I get

[045-038] to Chicago, you’ll be at work, and nobody will ever be the wiser. No

[045-039] reason why we shouldn’t both travel in Mexico, is there? You’ll be

[045-040] traveling alone. I’ll merely tell you the right places to stop, and come

[045-041] to take you driving. I won’t put any pressure on you. Have I ever?”

[045-042] He swung the bag toward her and looked up under her hat.

[045-043] “No, you haven’t,” she murmured. She was thinking that her

[045-044] own position might be less difficult if he had used what he called pressure. He

[045-045] clearly wished her to take the responsibility.

[045-046] “You have your own future in the back of your mind all the time,”

[045-047] Fred began, “and I have it in mine. I’m not going to try to carry

[045-048] you off, as I might another girl. If you wanted to quit me, I couldn’t

[045-049] hold you, no matter how many times you had married me. I don’t want to

[045-050] overpersuade you. But I’d like mighty well to get you down to that jolly

[045-051] old city, where everything would please you, and give myself a chance. Then, if

[045-052] you thought you could have a better time with me than without me, I’d try

[045-053] to grab you before you changed your mind. You are not a sentimental

[045-054] person.”

[045-055] Thea drew her veil down over her face. “I think I am, a little; about

[045-056] you,” she said quietly. Fred’s irony somehow hurt her.

[045-057] “What’s at the bottom of your mind, Thea?” he asked

[045-058] hurriedly. “I can’t tell. Why do you consider it at all, if

[045-059] you’re not sure? Why are you here with me now?”

[045-060] Her face was half-averted. He was thinking that it looked older and more

[045-061] firm—almost hard—under a veil.

[045-062] “Isn’t it possible to do things without having any very clear

[045-063] reason?” she asked slowly. “I have no plan in the back of my mind.

[045-064] Now that I’m with you, I want to be with you; that’s all. I

[045-065] can’t settle down to being alone again. I am here to-day because I want

[045-066] to be with you to-day.” She paused. “One thing, though; if I gave

[045-067] you my word, I’d keep it. And you could hold me, though you don’t

[045-068] seem to think so. Maybe I’m not sentimental, but I’m not very

[045-069] light, either. If I went off with you like this, it wouldn’t be to amuse

[045-070] myself.”

[045-071] Ottenburg’s eyes fell. His lips worked nervously for a moment. “Do

[045-072] you mean that you really care for me, Thea Kronborg?” he asked

[045-073] unsteadily.

[045-074] “I guess so. It’s like anything else. It takes hold of you and

[045-075] you’ve got to go through with it, even if you’re afraid. I was

[045-076] afraid to leave Moonstone, and afraid to leave Harsanyi. But I had to go

[045-077] through with it.”

[045-078] “And are you afraid now?” Fred asked slowly.

[045-079] “Yes; more than I’ve ever been. But I don’t think I could go

[045-080] back. The past closes up behind one, somehow. One would rather have a new kind

[045-081] of misery. The old kind seems like death or unconsciousness. You can’t

[045-082] force your life back into that mould again. No, one can’t go back.”

[045-083] She rose and stood by the back grating of the platform, her hand on the brass

[045-084] rail.

[045-085] Fred went to her side. She pushed up her veil and turned her most glowing face

[045-086] to him. Her eyes were wet and there were tears on her lashes, but she was

[045-087] smiling the rare, whole-hearted smile he had seen once or twice before. He

[045-088] looked at her shining eyes, her parted lips, her chin a little lifted. It was

[045-089] as if they were colored by a sunrise he could not see. He put his hand over

[045-090] hers and clasped it with a strength she felt. Her eyelashes trembled, her mouth

[045-091] softened, but her eyes were still brilliant.

[045-092] “Will you always be like you were down there, if I go with you?”

[045-093] she asked under her breath.

[045-094] His fingers tightened on hers. “By God, I will!” he muttered.

[045-095] “That’s the only promise I’ll ask you for. Now go away for a

[045-096] while and let me think about it. Come back at lunchtime and I’ll tell

[045-097] you. Will that do?”

[045-098] “Anything will do, Thea, if you’ll only let me keep an eye on you.

[045-099] The rest of the world doesn’t interest me much. You’ve got me in

[045-100] deep.”

[045-101] Fred dropped her hand and turned away. As he glanced back from the front end of

[045-102] the observation car, he saw that she was still standing there, and any one

[045-103] would have known that she was brooding over something. The earnestness of her

[045-104] head and shoulders had a certain nobility. He stood looking at her for a

[045-105] moment.

[045-106] When he reached the forward smoking-car, Fred took a seat at the end, where he

[045-107] could shut the other passengers from his sight. He put on his traveling-cap and

[045-108] sat down wearily, keeping his head near the window. “In any case, I shall

[045-109] help her more than I shall hurt her,” he kept saying to himself. He

[045-110] admitted that this was not the only motive which impelled him, but it was one

[045-111] of them. “I’ll make it my business in life to get her on.

[045-112] There’s nothing else I care about so much as seeing her have her chance.

[045-113] She hasn’t touched her real force yet. She isn’t even aware of it.

[045-114] Lord, don’t I know something about them? There isn’t one of them

[045-115] that has such a depth to draw from. She’ll be one of the great artists of

[045-116] our time. Playing accompaniments for that cheese-faced sneak! I’ll get

[045-117] her off to Germany this winter, or take her. She hasn’t got any time to

[045-118] waste now. I’ll make it up to her, all right.”

[045-119] Ottenburg certainly meant to make it up to her, in so far as he could. His

[045-120] feeling was as generous as strong human feelings are likely to be. The only

[045-121] trouble was, that he was married already, and had been since he was twenty.

[045-122] His older friends in Chicago, people who had been friends of his family, knew

[045-123] of the unfortunate state of his personal affairs; but they were people whom in

[045-124] the natural course of things Thea Kronborg would scarcely meet. Mrs. Frederick

[045-125] Ottenburg lived in California, at Santa Barbara, where her health was supposed

[045-126] to be better than elsewhere, and her husband lived in Chicago. He visited his

[045-127] wife every winter to reinforce her position, and his devoted mother, although

[045-128] her hatred for her daughter-inlaw was scarcely approachable in words, went to

[045-129] Santa Barbara every year to make things look better and to relieve her son.

[045-130] When Frederick Ottenburg was beginning his junior year at Harvard, he got a

[045-131] letter from Dick Brisbane, a Kansas City boy he knew, telling him that his

[045-132] *fiancée*, Miss Edith Beers, was going to New York to buy her trousseau.

[045-133] She would be at the Holland House, with her aunt and a girl from Kansas City

[045-134] who was to be a bridesmaid, for two weeks or more. If Ottenburg happened to be

[045-135] going down to New York, would he call upon Miss Beers and “show her a

[045-136] good time”?

[045-137] Fred did happen to be going to New York. He was going down from New Haven,

[045-138] after the Thanksgiving game. He called on Miss Beers and found her, as he that

[045-139] night telegraphed Brisbane, a “ripping beauty, no mistake.” He took

[045-140] her and her aunt and her uninteresting friend to the theater and to the opera,

[045-141] and he asked them to lunch with him at the Waldorf. He took no little pains in

[045-142] arranging the luncheon with the head waiter. Miss Beers was the sort of girl

[045-143] with whom a young man liked to seem experienced. She was dark and slender and

[045-144] fiery. She was witty and slangy; said daring things and carried them off with

[045-145] *nonchalance*. Her childish extravagance and contempt for all the serious

[045-146] facts of life could be charged to her father’s generosity and his long

[045-147] packing-house purse. Freaks that would have been vulgar and ostentatious in a

[045-148] more simpleminded girl, in Miss Beers seemed whimsical and picturesque. She

[045-149] darted about in magnificent furs and pumps and close-clinging gowns, though

[045-150] that was the day of full skirts. Her hats were large and floppy. When she

[045-151] wriggled out of her moleskin coat at luncheon, she looked like a slim black

[045-152] weasel. Her satin dress was a mere sheath, so conspicuous by its severity and

[045-153] scantness that every one in the dining-room stared. She ate nothing but

[045-154] alligator-pear salad and hothouse grapes, drank a little champagne, and took

[045-155] cognac in her coffee. She ridiculed, in the raciest slang, the singers they had

[045-156] heard at the opera the night before, and when her aunt pretended to reprove

[045-157] her, she murmured indifferently, “What’s the matter with you, old

[045-158] sport?” She rattled on with a subdued loquaciousness, always keeping her

[045-159] voice low and monotonous, always looking out of the corner of her eye and

[045-160] speaking, as it were, in asides, out of the corner of her mouth. She was

[045-161] scornful of everything,—which became her eyebrows. Her face was mobile

[045-162] and discontented, her eyes quick and black. There was a sort of smouldering

[045-163] fire about her, young Ottenburg thought. She entertained him prodigiously.

[045-164] After luncheon Miss Beers said she was going uptown to be fitted, and that she

[045-165] would go alone because her aunt made her nervous. When Fred held her coat for

[045-166] her, she murmured, “Thank you, Alphonse,” as if she were addressing

[045-167] the waiter. As she stepped into a hansom, with a long stretch of thin silk

[045-168] stocking, she said negligently, over her fur collar, “Better let me take

[045-169] you along and drop you somewhere.” He sprang in after her, and she told

[045-170] the driver to go to the Park.

[045-171] It was a bright winter day, and bitterly cold. Miss Beers asked Fred to tell

[045-172] her about the game at New Haven, and when he did so paid no attention to what

[045-173] he said. She sank back into the hansom and held her muff before her face,

[045-174] lowering it occasionally to utter laconic remarks about the people in the

[045-175] carriages they passed, interrupting Fred’s narrative in a disconcerting

[045-176] manner. As they entered the Park he happened to glance under her wide black hat

[045-177] at her black eyes and hair—the muff hid everything else—and

[045-178] discovered that she was crying. To his solicitous inquiry she replied that it

[045-179] “was enough to make you damp, to go and try on dresses to marry a man you

[045-180] weren’t keen about.”

[045-181] Further explanations followed. She had thought she was “perfectly

[045-182] cracked” about Brisbane, until she met Fred at the Holland House three

[045-183] days ago. Then she knew she would scratch Brisbane’s eyes out if she

[045-184] married him. What was she going to do?

[045-185] Fred told the driver to keep going. What did she want to do? Well, she

[045-186] didn’t know. One had to marry somebody, after all the machinery had been

[045-187] put in motion. Perhaps she might as well scratch Brisbane as anybody else; for

[045-188] scratch she would, if she didn’t get what she wanted.

[045-189] Of course, Fred agreed, one had to marry somebody. And certainly this girl beat

[045-190] anything he had ever been up against before. Again he told the driver to go

[045-191] ahead. Did she mean that she would think of marrying him, by any chance? Of

[045-192] course she did, Alphonse. Hadn’t he seen that all over her face three

[045-193] days ago? If he hadn’t, he was a snowball.

[045-194] By this time Fred was beginning to feel sorry for the driver. Miss Beers,

[045-195] however, was compassionless. After a few more turns, Fred suggested tea at the

[045-196] Casino. He was very cold himself, and remembering the shining silk hose and

[045-197] pumps, he wondered that the girl was not frozen. As they got out of the hansom,

[045-198] he slipped the driver a bill and told him to have something hot while he

[045-199] waited.

[045-200] At the tea-table, in a snug glass enclosure, with the steam sputtering in the

[045-201] pipes beside them and a brilliant winter sunset without, they developed their

[045-202] plan. Miss Beers had with her plenty of money, destined for tradesmen, which

[045-203] she was quite willing to divert into other channels—the first excitement

[045-204] of buying a trousseau had worn off, anyway. It was very much like any other

[045-205] shopping. Fred had his allowance and a few hundred he had won on the game. She

[045-206] would meet him to-morrow morning at the Jersey ferry. They could take one of

[045-207] the west-bound Pennsylvania trains and go—anywhere, some place where the

[045-208] laws weren’t too fussy.—Fred had not even thought about the

[045-209] laws!—It would be all right with her father; he knew Fred’s family.

[045-210] Now that they were engaged, she thought she would like to drive a little more.

[045-211] They were jerked about in the cab for another hour through the deserted Park.

[045-212] Miss Beers, having removed her hat, reclined upon Fred’s shoulder.

[045-213] The next morning they left Jersey City by the latest fast train out. They had

[045-214] some misadventures, crossed several States before they found a justice obliging

[045-215] enough to marry two persons whose names automatically instigated inquiry. The

[045-216] bride’s family were rather pleased with her originality; besides, any one

[045-217] of the Ottenburg boys was clearly a better match than young Brisbane. With Otto

[045-218] Ottenburg, however, the affair went down hard, and to his wife, the once proud

[045-219] Katarina Fürst, such a disappointment was almost unbearable. Her sons had

[045-220] always been clay in her hands, and now the *geliebter Sohn* had escaped

[045-221] her.

[045-222] Beers, the packer, gave his daughter a house in St. Louis, and Fred went into

[045-223] his father’s business. At the end of a year, he was mutely appealing to

[045-224] his mother for sympathy. At the end of two, he was drinking and in open

[045-225] rebellion. He had learned to detest his wife. Her wastefulness and cruelty

[045-226] revolted him. The ignorance and the fatuous conceit which lay behind her

[045-227] grimacing mask of slang and ridicule humiliated him so deeply that he became

[045-228] absolutely reckless. Her grace was only an uneasy wriggle, her audacity was the

[045-229] result of insolence and envy, and her wit was restless spite. As her personal

[045-230] mannerisms grew more and more odious to him, he began to dull his perceptions

[045-231] with champagne. He had it for tea, he drank it with dinner, and during the

[045-232] evening he took enough to insure that he would be well insulated when he got

[045-233] home. This behavior spread alarm among his friends. It was scandalous, and it

[045-234] did not occur among brewers. He was violating the *noblesse oblige* of his

[045-235] guild. His father and his father’s partners looked alarmed.

[045-236] When Fred’s mother went to him and with clasped hands entreated an

[045-237] explanation, he told her that the only trouble was that he couldn’t hold

[045-238] enough wine to make life endurable, so he was going to get out from under and

[045-239] enlist in the navy. He didn’t want anything but the shirt on his back and

[045-240] clean salt air. His mother could look out; he was going to make a scandal.

[045-241] Mrs. Otto Ottenburg went to Kansas City to see Mr. Beers, and had the

[045-242] satisfaction of telling him that he had brought up his daughter like a savage,

[045-243] *eine Ungebildete*. All the Ottenburgs and all the Beers, and many of

[045-244] their friends, were drawn into the quarrel. It was to public opinion, however

[045-245] and not to his mother’s activities, that Fred owed his partial escape

[045-246] from bondage. The cosmopolitan brewing world of St. Louis had conservative

[045-247] standards. The Ottenburgs’ friends were not predisposed in favor of the

[045-248] plunging Kansas City set, and they disliked young Fred’s wife from the

[045-249] day that she was brought among them. They found her ignorant and ill-bred and

[045-250] insufferably impertinent. When they became aware of how matters were going

[045-251] between her and Fred, they omitted no opportunity to snub her. Young Fred had

[045-252] always been popular, and St. Louis people took up his cause with warmth. Even

[045-253] the younger men, among whom Mrs. Fred tried to draft a following, at first

[045-254] avoided and then ignored her. Her defeat was so conspicuous, her life became

[045-255] such a desert, that she at last consented to accept the house in Santa Barbara

[045-256] which Mrs. Otto Ottenburg had long owned and cherished. This villa, with its

[045-257] luxuriant gardens, was the price of Fred’s furlough. His mother was only

[045-258] too glad to offer it in his behalf. As soon as his wife was established in

[045-259] California, Fred was transferred from St. Louis to Chicago.

[045-260] A divorce was the one thing Edith would never, never, give him. She told him

[045-261] so, and she told his family so, and her father stood behind her. She would

[045-262] enter into no arrangement that might eventually lead to divorce. She had

[045-263] insulted her husband before guests and servants, had scratched his face, thrown

[045-264] hand-mirrors and hairbrushes and nail-scissors at him often enough, but she

[045-265] knew that Fred was hardly the fellow who would go into court and offer that

[045-266] sort of evidence. In her behavior with other men she was discreet.

[045-267] After Fred went to Chicago, his mother visited him often, and dropped a word to

[045-268] her old friends there, who were already kindly disposed toward the young man.

[045-269] They gossiped as little as was compatible with the interest they felt,

[045-270] undertook to make life agreeable for Fred, and told his story only where they

[045-271] felt it would do good: to girls who seemed to find the young brewer attractive.

[045-272] So far, he had behaved well, and had kept out of entanglements.

[045-273] Since he was transferred to Chicago, Fred had been abroad several times, and

[045-274] had fallen more and more into the way of going about among young

[045-275] artists,—people with whom personal relations were incidental. With women,

[045-276] and even girls, who had careers to follow, a young man might have pleasant

[045-277] friendships without being regarded as a prospective suitor or lover. Among

[045-278] artists his position was not irregular, because with them his marriageableness

[045-279] was not an issue. His tastes, his enthusiasm, and his agreeable personality

[045-280] made him welcome.

[045-281] With Thea Kronborg he had allowed himself more liberty than he usually did in

[045-282] his friendships or gallantries with young artists, because she seemed to him

[045-283] distinctly not the marrying kind. She impressed him as equipped to be an

[045-284] artist, and to be nothing else; already directed, concentrated, formed as to

[045-285] mental habit. He was generous and sympathetic, and she was lonely and needed

[045-293] is, she had no intellectual humor; no power to enjoy the absurdities of people,

[045-294] no relish of their pretentiousness and inconsistencies—which only

[045-300] spontaneous. Under the least glow of excitement, as at Mrs.

[045-301] Nathanmeyer’s, he had seen the apprehensive, frowning drudge of

[045-302] Bowers’s studio flash into a resourceful and consciously beautiful woman.

[045-303] His interest in Thea was serious, almost from the first, and so sincere that he

[045-304] felt no distrust of himself. He believed that he knew a great deal more about

[045-305] her possibilities than Bowers knew, and he liked to think that he had given her

[045-308] ever since. He had not anticipated that she would grow more fond of him than

[045-310] and, as he said, she must have been “at it from her cradle.” He had

[045-311] imagined, perhaps, but never really believed, that he would find her waiting

[045-315] flirtatious woman he could have handled easily enough. But a personality like

[045-316] that, unconsciously revealing itself for the first time under the exaltation of

[045-318] himself, in reckless moments back there in the canyon, “You can’t

[045-320] Besides, was he really going to do her any harm? The Lord knew he would marry

[045-321] her if he could! Marriage would be an incident, not an end with her; he was

[045-322] sure of that. If it were not he, it would be some one else; some one who would

[045-323] be a weight about her neck, probably; who would hold her back and beat her down

[045-324] and divert her from the first plunge for which he felt she was gathering all

[045-325] her energies. He meant to help her, and he could not think of another man who

[045-326] would. He went over his unmarried friends, East and West, and he could not

[045-327] think of one who would know what she was driving at—or care. The clever

[045-328] ones were selfish, the kindly ones were stupid.

[045-330] better be me than any of the others—of the sort she’d find. Get her

[045-331] tied up with some conceited ass who’d try to make her over, train her

[045-332] like a puppy! Give one of ’em a big nature like that, and he’d be

[045-333] horrified. He wouldn’t show his face in the clubs until he’d gone

[045-334] after her and combed her down to conform to some fool idea in his own

[045-335] head—put there by some other woman, too, his first sweetheart or his

[045-336] grandmother or a maiden aunt. At least, I understand her. I know what she needs

[045-337] and where she’s bound, and I mean to see that she has a fighting

[045-339] His own conduct looked crooked, he admitted; but he asked himself whether,

[045-340] between men and women, all ways were not more or less crooked. He believed

[045-341] those which are called straight were the most dangerous of all. They seemed to

[045-342] him, for the most part, to lie between windowless stone walls, and their

[045-343] rectitude had been achieved at the expense of light and air. In their

[045-344] unquestioned regularity lurked every sort of human cruelty and meanness, and

[045-345] every kind of humiliation and suffering. He would rather have any woman he

[045-346] cared for wounded than crushed. He would deceive her not once, he told himself

[045-347] fiercely, but a hundred times, to keep her free.
