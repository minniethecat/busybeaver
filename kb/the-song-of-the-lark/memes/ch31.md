## XI

[031-001] [[mrs-kronborg|Mrs. Kronborg]] had said that Thea was not to be disturbed on Sunday morning, and

[031-002] she slept until noon. When she came downstairs the family were just sitting

[031-003] down to dinner, Mr. Kronborg at one end of the long table, Mrs. Kronborg at the

[031-004] other. Anna, stiff and ceremonious, in her summer silk, sat at her

[031-005] father’s right, and the boys were strung along on either side of the

[031-006] table. There was a place left for Thea between her mother and Thor. During the

[031-007] silence which preceded the blessing, Thea felt something uncomfortable in the

[031-008] air. Anna and her older brothers had lowered their eyes when she came in. Mrs.

[031-009] Kronborg nodded cheerfully, and after the blessing, as she began to pour the

[031-010] coffee, turned to her.

[031-011] “I expect you had a good time at that dance, Thea. I hope you got your

[031-012] sleep out.”

[031-013] “High society, that,” remarked Charley, giving the mashed potatoes

[031-014] a vicious swat. Anna’s mouth and eyebrows became half-moons.

[031-015] Thea looked across the table at the uncompromising countenances of her older

[031-016] brothers. “Why, what’s the matter with the Mexicans?” she

[031-017] asked, flushing. “They don’t trouble anybody, and they are kind to

[031-018] their families and have good manners.”

[031-019] “Nice clean people; got some style about them. Do you really like that

[031-020] kind, Thea, or do you just pretend to? That’s what I’d like to

[031-021] know.” Gus looked at her with pained inquiry. But he at least looked at

[031-022] her.

[031-023] “They’re just as clean as white people, and they have a perfect

[031-024] right to their own ways. Of course I like ’em. I don’t pretend

[031-025] things.”

[031-026] “Everybody according to their own taste,” remarked Charley

[031-027] bitterly. “Quit crumbing your bread up, Thor. Ain’t you learned how

[031-028] to eat yet?”

[031-029] “Children, children!” said Mr. Kronborg nervously, looking up from

[031-030] the chicken he was dismembering. He glanced at his wife, whom he expected to

[031-031] maintain harmony in the family.

[031-032] “That’s all right, Charley. Drop it there,” said Mrs.

[031-033] Kronborg. “No use spoiling your Sunday dinner with race prejudices. The

[031-034] Mexicans suit me and Thea very well. They are a useful people. Now you can just

[031-035] talk about something else.”

[031-036] Conversation, however, did not flourish at that dinner. Everybody ate as fast

[031-037] as possible. Charley and Gus said they had engagements and left the table as

[031-038] soon as they finished their apple pie. Anna sat primly and ate with great

[031-039] elegance. When she spoke at all she spoke to her father, about church matters,

[031-040] and always in a commiserating tone, as if he had met with some misfortune. Mr.

[031-041] Kronborg, quite innocent of her intentions, replied kindly and absent-mindedly.

[031-042] After the dessert he went to take his usual Sunday afternoon nap, and Mrs.

[031-043] Kronborg carried some dinner to a sick neighbor. Thea and Anna began to clear

[031-044] the table.

[031-045] “I should think you would show more consideration for father’s

[031-046] position, Thea,” Anna began as soon as she and her sister were alone.

[031-047] Thea gave her a sidelong glance. “Why, what have I done to father?”

[031-048] “Everybody at Sunday-School was talking about you going over there and

[031-049] singing with the Mexicans all night, when you won’t sing for the church.

[031-050] Somebody heard you, and told it all over town. Of course, we all get the blame

[031-051] for it.”

[031-052] “Anything disgraceful about singing?” Thea asked with a provoking

[031-053] yawn.

[031-054] “I must say you choose your company! You always had that streak in you,

[031-055] Thea. We all hoped that going away would improve you. Of course, it reflects on

[031-056] father when you are scarcely polite to the nice people here and make up to the

[031-057] rowdies.”

[031-058] “Oh, it’s my singing with the Mexicans you object to?” Thea

[031-059] put down a tray full of dishes. “Well, I like to sing over there, and I

[031-060] don’t like to over here. I’ll sing for them any time they ask me

[031-061] to. They know something about what I’m doing. They’re a talented

[031-062] people.”

[031-063] “Talented!” Anna made the word sound like escaping steam. “I

[031-064] suppose you think it’s smart to come home and throw that at your

[031-065] family!”

[031-066] Thea picked up the tray. By this time she was as white as the Sunday

[031-067] tablecloth. “Well,” she replied in a cold, even tone,

[031-068] “I’ll have to throw it at them sooner or later. It’s just a

[031-069] question of when, and it might as well be now as any time.” She carried

[031-070] the tray blindly into the kitchen.

[031-071] Tillie, who was always listening and looking out for her, took the dishes from

[031-072] her with a furtive, frightened glance at her stony face. Thea went slowly up

[031-073] the back stairs to her loft. Her legs seemed as heavy as lead as she climbed

[031-074] the stairs, and she felt as if everything inside her had solidified and grown

[031-075] hard.

[031-076] After shutting her door and locking it, she sat down on the edge of her bed.

[031-077] This place had always been her refuge, but there was a hostility in the house

[031-078] now which this door could not shut out. This would be her last summer in that

[031-079] room. Its services were over; its time was done. She rose and put her hand on

[031-080] the low ceiling. Two tears ran down her cheeks, as if they came from ice that

[031-081] melted slowly. She was not ready to leave her little shell. She was being

[031-082] pulled out too soon. She would never be able to think anywhere else as well as

[031-083] here. She would never sleep so well or have such dreams in any other bed; even

[031-084] last night, such sweet, breathless dreams—Thea hid her face in the

[031-085] pillow. Wherever she went she would like to take that little bed with her. When

[031-086] she went away from it for good, she would leave something that she could never

[031-087] recover; memories of pleasant excitement, of happy adventures in her mind; of

[031-088] warm sleep on howling winter nights, and joyous awakenings on summer mornings.

[031-089] There were certain dreams that might refuse to come to her at all except in a

[031-090] little morning cave, facing the sun—where they came to her so powerfully,

[031-091] where they beat a triumph in her!

[031-092] The room was hot as an oven. The sun was beating fiercely on the shingles

[031-093] behind the board ceiling. She undressed, and before she threw herself upon her

[031-094] bed in her chemise, she frowned at herself for a long while in her

[031-095] looking-glass. Yes, she and It must fight it out together. The thing that

[031-096] looked at her out of her own eyes was the only friend she could count on. Oh,

[031-097] she would make these people sorry enough! There would come a time when they

[031-098] would want to make it up with her. But, never again! She had no little

[031-099] vanities, only one big one, and she would never forgive.

[031-100] Her mother was all right, but her mother was a part of the family, and she was

[031-101] not. In the nature of things, her mother had to be on both sides. Thea felt

[031-102] that she had been betrayed. A truce had been broken behind her back. She had

[031-103] never had much individual affection for any of her brothers except Thor, but

[031-104] she had never been disloyal, never felt scorn or held grudges. As a little girl

[031-105] she had always been good friends with Gunner and Axel, whenever she had time to

[031-106] play. Even before she got her own room, when they were all sleeping and

[031-107] dressing together, like little cubs, and breakfasting in the kitchen, she had

[031-108] led an absorbing personal life of her own. But she had a cub loyalty to the

[031-109] other cubs. She thought them nice boys and tried to make them get their

[031-110] lessons. She once fought a bully who “picked on” Axel at school.

[031-111] She never made fun of Anna’s crimpings and curlings and beauty-rites.

[031-112] Thea had always taken it for granted that her sister and brothers recognized

[031-113] that she had special abilities, and that they were proud of it. She had done

[031-114] them the honor, she told herself bitterly, to believe that though they had no

[031-115] particular endowments, *they were of her kind*, and not of the Moonstone

[031-116] kind. Now they had all grown up and become persons. They faced each other as

[031-117] individuals, and she saw that Anna and Gus and Charley were among the people

[031-118] whom she had always recognized as her natural enemies. Their ambitions and

[031-119] sacred proprieties were meaningless to her. She had neglected to congratulate

[031-120] Charley upon having been promoted from the grocery department of

[031-121] Commings’s store to the drygoods department. Her mother had reproved her

[031-122] for this omission. And how was she to know, Thea asked herself, that Anna

[031-123] expected to be teased because Bert Rice now came and sat in the hammock with

[031-124] her every night? No, it was all clear enough. Nothing that she would ever do in

[031-125] the world would seem important to them, and nothing they would ever do would

[031-126] seem important to her.

[031-127] Thea lay thinking intently all through the stifling afternoon. Tillie whispered

[031-128] something outside her door once, but she did not answer. She lay on her bed

[031-129] until the second church bell rang, and she saw the family go trooping up the

[031-130] sidewalk on the opposite side of the street, Anna and her father in the lead.

[031-131] Anna seemed to have taken on a very story-book attitude toward her father;

[031-132] patronizing and condescending, it seemed to Thea. The older boys were not in

[031-133] the family band. They now took their girls to church. Tillie had stayed at home

[031-134] to get supper. Thea got up, washed her hot face and arms, and put on the white

[031-135] organdie dress she had worn last night; it was getting too small for her, and

[031-136] she might as well wear it out. After she was dressed she unlocked her door and

[031-137] went cautiously downstairs. She felt as if chilling hostilities might be

[031-138] awaiting her in the trunk loft, on the stairway, almost anywhere. In the

[031-139] dining-room she found Tillie, sitting by the open window, reading the dramatic

[031-140] news in a Denver Sunday paper. Tillie kept a scrapbook in which she pasted

[031-141] clippings about actors and actresses.

[031-142] “Come look at this picture of Pauline Hall in tights, Thea,” she

[031-143] called. “Ain’t she cute? It’s too bad you didn’t go to

[031-144] the theater more when you was in [[chicago|Chicago]]; such a good chance! Didn’t you

[031-145] even get to see Clara Morris or Modjeska?”

[031-146] “No; I didn’t have time. Besides, it costs money, Tillie,”

[031-147] Thea replied wearily, glancing at the paper Tillie held out to her.

[031-148] Tillie looked up at her niece. “Don’t you go and be upset about any

[031-149] of Anna’s notions. She’s one of these narrow kind. Your father and

[031-150] mother don’t pay any attention to what she says. Anna’s fussy; she

[031-151] is with me, but I don’t mind her.”

[031-152] “Oh, I don’t mind her. That’s all right, Tillie. I guess

[031-153] I’ll take a walk.”

[031-154] Thea knew that Tillie hoped she would stay and talk to her for a while, and she

[031-155] would have liked to please her. But in a house as small as that one, everything

[031-156] was too intimate and mixed up together. The family was the family, an integral

[031-157] thing. One couldn’t discuss Anna there. She felt differently toward the

[031-158] house and everything in it, as if the battered old furniture that seemed so

[031-159] kindly, and the old carpets on which she had played, had been nourishing a

[031-160] secret grudge against her and were not to be trusted any more.

[031-161] She went aimlessly out of the front gate, not knowing what to do with herself.

[031-162] Mexican Town, somehow, was spoiled for her just then, and she felt that she

[031-163] would hide if she saw Silvo or Felipe coming toward her. She walked down

[031-164] through the empty main street. All the stores were closed, their blinds down.

[031-165] On the steps of the bank some idle boys were sitting, telling disgusting

[031-166] stories because there was nothing else to do. Several of them had gone to

[031-167] school with Thea, but when she nodded to them they hung their heads and did not

[031-168] speak. Thea’s body was often curiously expressive of what was going on in

[031-169] her mind, and to-night there was something in her walk and carriage that made

[031-170] these boys feel that she was “stuck up.” If she had stopped and

[031-171] talked to them, they would have thawed out on the instant and would have been

[031-172] friendly and grateful. But Thea was hurt afresh, and walked on, holding her

[031-173] chin higher than ever. As she passed the Duke Block, she saw a light in Dr.

[031-174] Archie’s office, and she went up the stairs and opened the door into his

[031-175] study. She found him with a pile of papers and accountbooks before him. He

[031-176] pointed her to her old chair at the end of his desk and leaned back in his own,

[031-177] looking at her with satisfaction. How handsome she was growing!

[031-178] “I’m still chasing the elusive metal, Thea,”—he pointed

[031-179] to the papers before him,—“I’m up to my neck in mines, and

[031-180] I’m going to be a rich man some day.”

[031-181] “I hope you will; awfully rich. That’s the only thing that

[031-182] counts.” She looked restlessly about the consulting-room. “To do

[031-183] any of the things one wants to do, one has to have lots and lots of

[031-184] money.”

[031-185] [[dr-archie|Dr. Archie]] was direct. “What’s the matter? Do you need some?”

[031-186] Thea shrugged. “Oh, I can get along, in a little way.” She looked

[031-187] intently out of the window at the arc streetlamp that was just beginning to

[031-188] sputter. “But it’s silly to live at all for little things,”

[031-189] she added quietly. “Living’s too much trouble unless one can get

[031-190] something big out of it.”

[031-191] Dr. Archie rested his elbows on the arms of his chair, dropped his chin on his

[031-192] clasped hands and looked at her. “Living is no trouble for little people,

[031-193] believe me!” he exclaimed. “What do you want to get out of

[031-194] it?”

[031-195] “Oh—so many things!” Thea shivered.

[031-196] “But what? Money? You mentioned that. Well, you can make money, if you

[031-197] care about that more than anything else.” He nodded prophetically above

[031-198] his interlacing fingers.

[031-199] “But I don’t. That’s only one thing. Anyhow, I couldn’t

[031-200] if I did.” She pulled her dress lower at the neck as if she were

[031-201] suffocating. “I only want impossible things,” she said roughly.

[031-202] “The others don’t interest me.”

[031-203] Dr. Archie watched her contemplatively, as if she were a beaker full of

[031-204] chemicals working. A few years ago, when she used to sit there, the light from

[031-205] under his green lampshade used to fall full upon her broad face and yellow

[031-206] pigtails. Now her face was in the shadow and the line of light fell below her

[031-207] bare throat, directly across her bosom. The shrunken white organdie rose and

[031-208] fell as if she were struggling to be free and to break out of it altogether. He

[031-209] felt that her heart must be laboring heavily in there, but he was afraid to

[031-210] touch her; he was, indeed. He had never seen her like this before. Her hair,

[031-211] piled high on her head, gave her a commanding look, and her eyes, that used to

[031-212] be so inquisitive, were stormy.

[031-213] “Thea,” he said slowly, “I won’t say that you can have

[031-214] everything you want—that means having nothing, in reality. But if you

[031-215] decide what it is you want most, *you can get it*.” His eye caught

[031-216] hers for a moment. “Not everybody can, but you can. Only, if you want a

[031-217] big thing, you’ve got to have nerve enough to cut out all that’s

[031-218] easy, everything that’s to be had cheap.” Dr. Archie paused. He

[031-219] picked up a paper-cutter and, feeling the edge of it softly with his fingers,

[031-220] he added slowly, as if to himself:—

[031-221] “He either fears his fate too much,\

[031-222] Or his deserts are small,\

[031-223] Who dares not put it to the touch\

[031-224] To win...or lose it all.”

[031-225] Thea’s lips parted; she looked at him from under a frown, searching his

[031-226] face. “Do you mean to break loose, too, and—do something?”

[031-227] she asked in a low voice.

[031-228] “I mean to get rich, if you call that doing anything. I’ve found

[031-229] what I can do without. You make such bargains in your mind, first.”

[031-230] Thea sprang up and took the paper-cutter he had put down, twisting it in her

[031-231] hands. “A long while first, sometimes,” she said with a short

[031-232] laugh. “But suppose one can never get out what they’ve got in them?

[031-233] Suppose they make a mess of it in the end; then what?” She threw the

[031-234] paper-cutter on the desk and took a step toward the doctor, until her dress

[031-235] touched him. She stood looking down at him. “Oh, it’s easy to

[031-236] fail!” She was breathing through her mouth and her throat was throbbing

[031-237] with excitement.

[031-238] As he looked up at her, Dr. Archie’s hands tightened on the arms of his

[031-239] chair. He had thought he knew [[thea-kronborg|Thea Kronborg]] pretty well, but he did not know

[031-240] the girl who was standing there. She was beautiful, as his little Swede had

[031-241] never been, but she frightened him. Her pale cheeks, her parted lips, her

[031-242] flashing eyes, seemed suddenly to mean one thing—he did not know what. A

[031-243] light seemed to break upon her from far away—or perhaps from far within.

[031-244] She seemed to grow taller, like a scarf drawn out long; looked as if she were

[031-245] pursued and fleeing, and—yes, she looked tormented. “It’s

[031-246] easy to fail,” he heard her say again, “and if I fail, you’d

[031-247] better forget about me, for I’ll be one of the worst women that ever

[031-248] lived. I’ll be an awful woman!”

[031-249] In the shadowy light above the lampshade he caught her glance again and held it

[031-250] for a moment. Wild as her eyes were, that yellow gleam at the back of them was

[031-251] as hard as a diamond drill-point. He rose with a nervous laugh and dropped his

[031-252] hand lightly on her shoulder. “No, you won’t. You’ll be a

[031-253] splendid one!”

[031-254] She shook him off before he could say anything more, and went out of his door

[031-255] with a kind of bound. She left so quickly and so lightly that he could not even

[031-256] hear her footstep in the hallway outside. Archie dropped back into his chair

[031-257] and sat motionless for a long while.

[031-258] So it went; one loved a quaint little girl, cheerful, industrious, always on

[031-259] the run and hustling through her tasks; and suddenly one lost her. He had

[031-260] thought he knew that child like the glove on his hand. But about this tall girl

[031-261] who threw up her head and glittered like that all over, he knew nothing. She

[031-262] was goaded by desires, ambitions, revulsions that were dark to him. One thing

[031-263] he knew: the old highroad of life, worn safe and easy, hugging the sunny

[031-264] slopes, would scarcely hold her again.

[031-265] After that night Thea could have asked pretty much anything of him. He could

[031-266] have refused her nothing. Years ago a crafty little bunch of hair and smiles

[031-267] had shown him what she wanted, and he had promptly married her. To-night a very

[031-268] different sort of girl—driven wild by doubts and youth, by poverty and

[031-269] riches—had let him see the fierceness of her nature. She went out still

[031-270] distraught, not knowing or caring what she had shown him. But to Archie

[031-271] knowledge of that sort was obligation. Oh, he was the same old Howard Archie!

[031-272] That Sunday in July was the turning-point; Thea’s peace of mind did not

[031-273] come back. She found it hard even to practice at home. There was something in

[031-274] the air there that froze her throat. In the morning, she walked as far as she

[031-275] could walk. In the hot afternoons she lay on her bed in her nightgown, planning

[031-276] fiercely. She haunted the post-office. She must have worn a path in the

[031-277] sidewalk that led to the post-office, that summer. She was there the moment the

[031-278] mail-sacks came up from the depot, morning and evening, and while the letters

[031-279] were being sorted and distributed she paced up and down outside, under the

[031-280] cottonwood trees, listening to the thump, thump, thump of Mr. Thompson’s

[031-281] stamp. She hung upon any sort of word from Chicago; a card from Bowers, a

[031-282] letter from Mrs. Harsanyi, from Mr. Larsen, from her landlady,—anything

[031-283] to reassure her that Chicago was still there. She began to feel the same

[031-284] restlessness that had tortured her the last spring when she was teaching in

[031-285] Moonstone. Suppose she never got away again, after all? Suppose one broke a leg

[031-286] and had to lie in bed at home for weeks, or had pneumonia and died there. The

[031-287] desert was so big and thirsty; if one’s foot slipped, it could drink one

[031-288] up like a drop of water.

[031-289] This time, when Thea left Moonstone to go back to Chicago, she went alone. As

[031-290] the train pulled out, she looked back at her mother and father and Thor. They

[031-291] were calm and cheerful; they did not know, they did not understand. Something

[031-292] pulled in her—and broke. She cried all the way to Denver, and that night,

[031-293] in her berth, she kept sobbing and waking herself. But when the sun rose in the

[031-294] morning, she was far away. It was all behind her, and she knew that she would

[031-295] never cry like that again. People live through such pain only once; pain comes

[031-296] again, but it finds a tougher surface. Thea remembered how she had gone away

[031-297] the first time, with what confidence in everything, and what pitiful ignorance.

[031-298] Such a silly! She felt resentful toward that stupid, good-natured child. How

[031-299] much older she was now, and how much harder! She was going away to fight, and

[031-300] she was going away forever.
