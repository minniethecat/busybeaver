[011-001]At dinner that day Alexandra said she thought they must really manage to go

[011-002]over to the Shabatas’ that afternoon. “It’s not often I let three days go by

[011-003]without seeing Marie. She will think I have forsaken her, now that my old

[011-004]friend has come back.”

[011-005]After the men had gone back to work, Alexandra put on a white dress and her

[011-006]sun-hat, and she and Carl set forth across the fields. “You see we have kept up

[011-007]the old path, Carl. It has been so nice for me to feel that there was a friend

[011-008]at the other end of it again.”

[011-009]Carl smiled a little ruefully. “All the same, I hope it hasn’t been*quite*the same.”

[011-010]Alexandra looked at him with surprise. “Why, no, of course not. Not the same.

[011-011]She could not very well take your place, if that’s what you mean. I’m friendly

[011-012]with all my neighbors, I hope. But Marie is really a companion, some one I can

[011-013]talk to quite frankly. You wouldn’t want me to be more lonely than I have been,

[011-014]would you?”

[011-015]Carl laughed and pushed back the triangular lock of hair with the edge of his

[011-016]hat. “Of course I don’t. I ought to be thankful that this path hasn’t been worn

[011-017]by—well, by friends with more pressing errands than your little Bohemian is

[011-018]likely to have.” He paused to give Alexandra his hand as she stepped over the

[011-019]stile. “Are you the least bit disappointed in our coming together again?” he

[011-020]asked abruptly. “Is it the way you hoped it would be?”

[011-021]Alexandra smiled at this. “Only better. When I’ve thought about your coming,

[011-022]I’ve sometimes been a little afraid of it. You have lived where things move so

[011-023]fast, and everything is slow here; the people slowest of all. Our lives are

[011-024]like the years, all made up of weather and crops and cows. How you hated cows!”

[011-025]She shook her head and laughed to herself.

[011-026]“I didn’t when we milked together. I walked up to the pasture corners this

[011-027]morning. I wonder whether I shall ever be able to tell you all that I was

[011-028]thinking about up there. It’s a strange thing, Alexandra; I find it easy to be

[011-029]frank with you about everything under the sun except—yourself!”

[011-030]“You are afraid of hurting my feelings, perhaps.” Alexandra looked at him

[011-031]thoughtfully.

[011-032]“No, I’m afraid of giving you a shock. You’ve seen yourself for so long in the

[011-033]dull minds of the people about you, that if I were to tell you how you seem to

[011-034]me, it would startle you. But you must see that you astonish me. You must feel

[011-035]when people admire you.”

[011-036]Alexandra blushed and laughed with some confusion. “I felt that you were

[011-037]pleased with me, if you mean that.”

[011-038]“And you’ve felt when other people were pleased with you?” he insisted.

[011-039]“Well, sometimes. The men in town, at the banks and the county offices, seem

[011-040]glad to see me. I think, myself, it is more pleasant to do business with people

[011-041]who are clean and healthy-looking,” she admitted blandly.

[011-042]Carl gave a little chuckle as he opened the Shabatas’ gate for her. “Oh, do

[011-043]you?” he asked dryly.

[011-044]There was no sign of life about the Shabatas’ house except a big yellow cat,

[011-045]sunning itself on the kitchen doorstep.

[011-046]Alexandra took the path that led to the orchard. “She often sits there and

[011-047]sews. I didn’t telephone her we were coming, because I didn’t want her to go to

[011-048]work and bake cake and freeze ice-cream. She’ll always make a party if you give

[011-049]her the least excuse. Do you recognize the apple trees, Carl?”

[011-050]Linstrum looked about him. “I wish I had a dollar for every bucket of water

[011-051]I’ve carried for those trees. Poor father, he was an easy man, but he was

[011-052]perfectly merciless when it came to watering the orchard.”

[011-053]“That’s one thing I like about Germans; they make an orchard grow if they can’t

[011-054]make anything else. I’m so glad these trees belong to some one who takes

[011-055]comfort in them. When I rented this place, the tenants never kept the orchard

[011-056]up, and Emil and I used to come over and take care of it ourselves. It needs

[011-057]mowing now. There she is, down in the corner. Maria-a-a!” she called.

[011-058]A recumbent figure started up from the grass and came running toward them

[011-059]through the flickering screen of light and shade.

[011-060]“Look at her! Isn’t she like a little brown rabbit?” Alexandra laughed.

[011-061]Maria ran up panting and threw her arms about Alexandra. “Oh, I had begun to

[011-062]think you were not coming at all, maybe. I knew you were so busy. Yes, Emil

[011-063]told me about Mr. Linstrum being here. Won’t you come up to the house?”

[011-064]“Why not sit down there in your corner? Carl wants to see the orchard. He kept

[011-065]all these trees alive for years, watering them with his own back.”

[011-066]Marie turned to Carl. “Then I’m thankful to you, Mr. Linstrum. We’d never have

[011-067]bought the place if it hadn’t been for this orchard, and then I wouldn’t have

[011-068]had Alexandra, either.” She gave Alexandra’s arm a little squeeze as she walked

[011-069]beside her. “How nice your dress smells, Alexandra; you put rosemary leaves in

[011-070]your chest, like I told you.”

[011-071]She led them to the northwest corner of the orchard, sheltered on one side by a

[011-072]thick mulberry hedge and bordered on the other by a wheatfield, just beginning

[011-073]to yellow. In this corner the ground dipped a little, and the blue-grass, which

[011-074]the weeds had driven out in the upper part of the orchard, grew thick and

[011-075]luxuriant. Wild roses were flaming in the tufts of bunchgrass along the fence.

[011-076]Under a white mulberry tree there was an old wagon-seat. Beside it lay a book

[011-077]and a workbasket.

[011-078]“You must have the seat, Alexandra. The grass would stain your dress,” the

[011-079]hostess insisted. She dropped down on the ground at Alexandra’s side and tucked

[011-080]her feet under her. Carl sat at a little distance from the two women, his back

[011-081]to the wheatfield, and watched them. Alexandra took off her shade-hat and threw

[011-082]it on the ground. Marie picked it up and played with the white ribbons,

[011-083]twisting them about her brown fingers as she talked. They made a pretty picture

[011-084]in the strong sunlight, the leafy pattern surrounding them like a net; the

[011-085]Swedish woman so white and gold, kindly and amused, but armored in calm, and

[011-086]the alert brown one, her full lips parted, points of yellow light dancing in

[011-087]her eyes as she laughed and chattered. Carl had never forgotten little Marie

[011-088]Tovesky’s eyes, and he was glad to have an opportunity to study them. The brown

[011-089]iris, he found, was curiously slashed with yellow, the color of sunflower

[011-090]honey, or of old amber. In each eye one of these streaks must have been larger

[011-091]than the others, for the effect was that of two dancing points of light, two

[011-092]little yellow bubbles, such as rise in a glass of champagne. Sometimes they

[011-093]seemed like the sparks from a forge. She seemed so easily excited, to kindle

[011-094]with a fierce little flame if one but breathed upon her. “What a waste,” Carl

[011-095]reflected. “She ought to be doing all that for a sweetheart. How awkwardly

[011-096]things come about!”

[011-097]It was not very long before Marie sprang up out of the grass again. “Wait a

[011-098]moment. I want to show you something.” She ran away and disappeared behind the

[011-099]low-growing apple trees.

[011-100]“What a charming creature,” Carl murmured. “I don’t wonder that her husband is

[011-101]jealous. But can’t she walk? does she always run?”

[011-102]Alexandra nodded. “Always. I don’t see many people, but I don’t believe there

[011-103]are many like her, anywhere.”

[011-104]Marie came back with a branch she had broken from an apricot tree, laden with

[011-105]pale yellow, pink-cheeked fruit. She dropped it beside Carl. “Did you plant

[011-106]those, too? They are such beautiful little trees.”

[011-107]Carl fingered the blue-green leaves, porous like blotting-paper and shaped like

[011-108]birch leaves, hung on waxen red stems. “Yes, I think I did. Are these the

[011-109]circus trees, Alexandra?”

[011-110]“Shall I tell her about them?” Alexandra asked. “Sit down like a good girl,

[011-111]Marie, and don’t ruin my poor hat, and I’ll tell you a story. A long time ago,

[011-112]when Carl and I were, say, sixteen and twelve, a circus came to [[hanover|Hanover]] and we

[011-113]went to town in our wagon, with Lou and Oscar, to see the parade. We hadn’t

[011-114]money enough to go to the circus. We followed the parade out to the circus

[011-115]grounds and hung around until the show began and the crowd went inside the

[011-116]tent. Then Lou was afraid we looked foolish standing outside in the pasture, so

[011-117]we went back to Hanover feeling very sad. There was a man in the streets

[011-118]selling apricots, and we had never seen any before. He had driven down from

[011-119]somewhere up in the French country, and he was selling them twenty-five cents a

[011-120]peck. We had a little money our fathers had given us for candy, and I bought

[011-121]two pecks and Carl bought one. They cheered us a good deal, and we saved all

[011-122]the seeds and planted them. Up to the time Carl went away, they hadn’t borne at

[011-123]all.”

[011-124]“And now he’s come back to eat them,” cried Marie, nodding at Carl. “That IS a

[011-125]good story. I can remember you a little, Mr. Linstrum. I used to see you in

[011-126]Hanover sometimes, when Uncle Joe took me to town. I remember you because you

[011-127]were always buying pencils and tubes of paint at the drug store. Once, when my

[011-128]uncle left me at the store, you drew a lot of little birds and flowers for me

[011-129]on a piece of wrapping-paper. I kept them for a long while. I thought you were

[011-130]very romantic because you could draw and had such black eyes.”

[011-131]Carl smiled. “Yes, I remember that time. Your uncle bought you some kind of a

[011-132]mechanical toy, a Turkish lady sitting on an ottoman and smoking a hookah,

[011-133]wasn’t it? And she turned her head backwards and forwards.”

[011-134]“Oh, yes! Wasn’t she splendid! I knew well enough I ought not to tell Uncle Joe

[011-135]I wanted it, for he had just come back from the saloon and was feeling good.

[011-136]You remember how he laughed? She tickled him, too. But when we got home, my

[011-137]aunt scolded him for buying toys when she needed so many things. We wound our

[011-138]lady up every night, and when she began to move her head my aunt used to laugh

[011-139]as hard as any of us. It was a music-box, you know, and the Turkish lady played

[011-140]a tune while she smoked. That was how she made you feel so jolly. As I remember

[011-141]her, she was lovely, and had a gold crescent on her turban.”

[011-142]Half an hour later, as they were leaving the house, Carl and Alexandra were met

[011-143]in the path by a strapping fellow in overalls and a blue shirt. He was

[011-144]breathing hard, as if he had been running, and was muttering to himself.

[011-145]Marie ran forward, and, taking him by the arm, gave him a little push toward

[011-146]her guests. “Frank, this is Mr. Linstrum.”

[011-147]Frank took off his broad straw hat and nodded to Alexandra. When he spoke to

[011-148]Carl, he showed a fine set of white teeth. He was burned a dull red down to his

[011-149]neckband, and there was a heavy three-days’ stubble on his face. Even in his

[011-150]agitation he was handsome, but he looked a rash and violent man.

[011-151]Barely saluting the callers, he turned at once to his wife and began, in an

[011-152]outraged tone, “I have to leave my team to drive the old woman Hiller’s hogs

[011-153]out-a my wheat. I go to take dat old woman to de court if she ain’t careful, I

[011-154]tell you!”

[011-155]His wife spoke soothingly. “But, Frank, she has only her lame boy to help her.

[011-156]She does the best she can.”

[011-157]Alexandra looked at the excited man and offered a suggestion. “Why don’t you go

[011-158]over there some afternoon and hog-tight her fences? You’d save time for

[011-159]yourself in the end.”

[011-160]Frank’s neck stiffened. “Not-a-much, I won’t. I keep my hogs home. Other

[011-161]peoples can do like me. See? If that Louis can mend shoes, he can mend fence.”

[011-162]“Maybe,” said Alexandra placidly; “but I’ve found it sometimes pays to mend

[011-163]other people’s fences. Good-bye, Marie. Come to see me soon.”

[011-164]Alexandra walked firmly down the path and Carl followed her.

[011-165]Frank went into the house and threw himself on the sofa, his face to the wall,

[011-166]his clenched fist on his hip. Marie, having seen her guests off, came in and

[011-167]put her hand coaxingly on his shoulder.

[011-168]“Poor Frank! You’ve run until you’ve made your head ache, now haven’t you? Let

[011-169]me make you some coffee.”

[011-170]“What else am I to do?” he cried hotly in Bohemian. “Am I to let any old

[011-171]woman’s hogs root up my wheat? Is that what I work myself to death for?”

[011-172]“Don’t worry about it, Frank. I’ll speak to Mrs. Hiller again. But, really, she

[011-173]almost cried last time they got out, she was so sorry.”

[011-174]Frank bounced over on his other side. “That’s it; you always side with them

[011-175]against me. They all know it. Anybody here feels free to borrow the mower and

[011-176]break it, or turn their hogs in on me. They know you won’t care!”

[011-177]Marie hurried away to make his coffee. When she came back, he was fast asleep.

[011-178]She sat down and looked at him for a long while, very thoughtfully. When the

[011-179]kitchen clock struck six she went out to get supper, closing the door gently

[011-180]behind her. She was always sorry for Frank when he worked himself into one of

[011-181]these rages, and she was sorry to have him rough and quarrelsome with his

[011-182]neighbors. She was perfectly aware that the neighbors had a good deal to put up

[011-183]with, and that they bore with Frank for her sake.
