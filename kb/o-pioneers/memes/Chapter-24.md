[024-001]When [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]] came in from work at five o’clock that evening, old Moses

[024-002]Marcel, Raoul’s father, telephoned him that Amédée had had a seizure in the

[024-003]wheatfield, and that Doctor Paradis was going to operate on him as soon as the

[024-004][[hanover|Hanover]] doctor got there to help. Frank dropped a word of this at the table,

[024-005]bolted his supper, and rode off to Sainte-Agnes, where there would be

[024-006]sympathetic discussion of Amédée’s case at Marcel’s saloon.

[024-007]As soon as Frank was gone, Marie telephoned Alexandra. It was a comfort to hear

[024-008]her friend’s voice. Yes, Alexandra knew what there was to be known about

[024-009]Amédée. Emil had been there when they carried him out of the field, and had

[024-010]stayed with him until the doctors operated for appendicitis at five o’clock.

[024-011]They were afraid it was too late to do much good; it should have been done

[024-012]three days ago. Amédée was in a very bad way. Emil had just come home, worn out

[024-013]and sick himself. She had given him some brandy and put him to bed.

[024-014]Marie hung up the receiver. Poor Amédée’s illness had taken on a new meaning to

[024-015]her, now that she knew Emil had been with him. And it might so easily have been

[024-016]the other way—Emil who was ill and Amédée who was sad! Marie looked about the

[024-017]dusky sitting-room. She had seldom felt so utterly lonely. If Emil was asleep,

[024-018]there was not even a chance of his coming; and she could not go to Alexandra

[024-019]for sympathy. She meant to tell Alexandra everything, as soon as Emil went

[024-020]away. Then whatever was left between them would be honest.

[024-021]But she could not stay in the house this evening. Where should she go? She

[024-022]walked slowly down through the orchard, where the evening air was heavy with

[024-023]the smell of wild cotton. The fresh, salty scent of the wild roses had given

[024-024]way before this more powerful perfume of midsummer. Wherever those

[024-025]ashes-of-rose balls hung on their milky stalks, the air about them was

[024-026]saturated with their breath. The sky was still red in the west and the evening

[024-027]star hung directly over the Bergsons’ wind-mill. Marie crossed the fence at the

[024-028]wheatfield corner, and walked slowly along the path that led to Alexandra’s.

[024-029]She could not help feeling hurt that Emil had not come to tell her about

[024-030]Amédée. It seemed to her most unnatural that he should not have come. If she

[024-031]were in trouble, certainly he was the one person in the world she would want to

[024-032]see. Perhaps he wished her to understand that for her he was as good as gone

[024-033]already.

[024-034]Marie stole slowly, flutteringly, along the path, like a white night-moth out

[024-035]of the fields. The years seemed to stretch before her like the land; spring,

[024-036]summer, autumn, winter, spring; always the same patient fields, the patient

[024-037]little trees, the patient lives; always the same yearning, the same pulling at

[024-038]the chain—until the instinct to live had torn itself and bled and weakened for

[024-039]the last time, until the chain secured a dead woman, who might cautiously be

[024-040]released. Marie walked on, her face lifted toward the remote, inaccessible

[024-041]evening star.

[024-042]When she reached the stile she sat down and waited. How terrible it was to love

[024-043]people when you could not really share their lives!

[024-044]Yes, in so far as she was concerned, Emil was already gone. They couldn’t meet

[024-045]any more. There was nothing for them to say. They had spent the last penny of

[024-046]their small change; there was nothing left but gold. The day of love-tokens was

[024-047]past. They had now only their hearts to give each other. And Emil being gone,

[024-048]what was her life to be like? In some ways, it would be easier. She would not,

[024-049]at least, live in perpetual fear. If Emil were once away and settled at work,

[024-050]she would not have the feeling that she was spoiling his life. With the memory

[024-051]he left her, she could be as rash as she chose. Nobody could be the worse for

[024-052]it but herself; and that, surely, did not matter. Her own case was clear. When

[024-053]a girl had loved one man, and then loved another while that man was still

[024-054]alive, everybody knew what to think of her. What happened to her was of little

[024-055]consequence, so long as she did not drag other people down with her. Emil once

[024-056]away, she could let everything else go and live a new life of perfect love.

[024-057]Marie left the stile reluctantly. She had, after all, thought he might come.

[024-058]And how glad she ought to be, she told herself, that he was asleep. She left

[024-059]the path and went across the pasture. The moon was almost full. An owl was

[024-060]hooting somewhere in the fields. She had scarcely thought about where she was

[024-061]going when the pond glittered before her, where Emil had shot the ducks. She

[024-062]stopped and looked at it. Yes, there would be a dirty way out of life, if one

[024-063]chose to take it. But she did not want to die. She wanted to live and dream—a

[024-064]hundred years, forever! As long as this sweetness welled up in her heart, as

[024-065]long as her breast could hold this treasure of pain! She felt as the pond must

[024-066]feel when it held the moon like that; when it encircled and swelled with that

[024-067]image of gold.

[024-068]In the morning, when Emil came down-stairs, Alexandra met him in the

[024-069]sitting-room and put her hands on his shoulders. “Emil, I went to your room as

[024-070]soon as it was light, but you were sleeping so sound I hated to wake you. There

[024-071]was nothing you could do, so I let you sleep. They telephoned from Sainte-Agnes

[024-072]that Amédée died at three o’clock this morning.”
