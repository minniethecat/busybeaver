[003-001]One Sunday afternoon in July, six months after [[john-bergson|John Bergson]]’s death, Carl was

[003-002]sitting in the doorway of the Linstrum kitchen, dreaming over an illustrated

[003-003]paper, when he heard the rattle of a wagon along the hill road. Looking up he

[003-004]recognized the Bergsons’ team, with two seats in the wagon, which meant they

[003-005]were off for a pleasure excursion. Oscar and Lou, on the front seat, wore their

[003-006]cloth hats and coats, never worn except on Sundays, and Emil, on the second

[003-007]seat with Alexandra, sat proudly in his new trousers, made from a pair of his

[003-008]father’s, and a pink-striped shirt, with a wide ruffled collar. Oscar stopped

[003-009]the horses and waved to Carl, who caught up his hat and ran through the melon

[003-010]patch to join them.

[003-011]“Want to go with us?” Lou called. “We’re going to Crazy [[ivar|Ivar]]’s to buy a

[003-012]hammock.”

[003-013]“Sure.” Carl ran up panting, and clambering over the wheel sat down beside

[003-014]Emil. “I’ve always wanted to see Ivar’s pond. They say it’s the biggest in all

[003-015]the country. Aren’t you afraid to go to Ivar’s in that new shirt, Emil? He

[003-016]might want it and take it right off your back.”

[003-017]Emil grinned. “I’d be awful scared to go,” he admitted, “if you big boys

[003-018]weren’t along to take care of me. Did you ever hear him howl, Carl? People say

[003-019]sometimes he runs about the country howling at night because he is afraid the

[003-020]Lord will destroy him. Mother thinks he must have done something awful wicked.”

[003-021]Lou looked back and winked at Carl. “What would you do, Emil, if you was out on

[003-022]the prairie by yourself and seen him coming?”

[003-023]Emil stared. “Maybe I could hide in a badger-hole,” he suggested doubtfully.

[003-024]“But suppose there wasn’t any badger-hole,” Lou persisted. “Would you run?”

[003-025]“No, I’d be too scared to run,” Emil admitted mournfully, twisting his fingers.

[003-026]“I guess I’d sit right down on the ground and say my prayers.”

[003-027]The big boys laughed, and Oscar brandished his whip over the broad backs of the

[003-028]horses.

[003-029]“He wouldn’t hurt you, Emil,” said Carl persuasively. “He came to doctor our

[003-030]mare when she ate green corn and swelled up most as big as the water-tank. He

[003-031]petted her just like you do your cats. I couldn’t understand much he said, for

[003-032]he don’t talk any English, but he kept patting her and groaning as if he had

[003-033]the pain himself, and saying, ‘There now, sister, that’s easier, that’s

[003-034]better!’”

[003-035]Lou and Oscar laughed, and Emil giggled delightedly and looked up at his

[003-036]sister.

[003-037]“I don’t think he knows anything at all about doctoring,” said Oscar

[003-038]scornfully. “They say when horses have distemper he takes the medicine himself,

[003-039]and then prays over the horses.”

[003-040]Alexandra spoke up. “That’s what the Crows said, but he cured their horses, all

[003-041]the same. Some days his mind is cloudy, like. But if you can get him on a clear

[003-042]day, you can learn a great deal from him. He understands animals. Didn’t I see

[003-043]him take the horn off the Berquist’s cow when she had torn it loose and went

[003-044]crazy? She was tearing all over the place, knocking herself against things. And

[003-045]at last she ran out on the roof of the old dugout and her legs went through and

[003-046]there she stuck, bellowing. Ivar came running with his white bag, and the

[003-047]moment he got to her she was quiet and let him saw her horn off and daub the

[003-048]place with tar.”

[003-049]Emil had been watching his sister, his face reflecting the sufferings of the

[003-050]cow. “And then didn’t it hurt her any more?” he asked.

[003-051]Alexandra patted him. “No, not any more. And in two days they could use her

[003-052]milk again.”

[003-053]The road to Ivar’s homestead was a very poor one. He had settled in the rough

[003-054]country across the county line, where no one lived but some Russians,—half a

[003-055]dozen families who dwelt together in one long house, divided off like barracks.

[003-056]Ivar had explained his choice by saying that the fewer neighbors he had, the

[003-057]fewer temptations. Nevertheless, when one considered that his chief business

[003-058]was horse-doctoring, it seemed rather short-sighted of him to live in the most

[003-059]inaccessible place he could find. The Bergson wagon lurched along over the

[003-060]rough hummocks and grass banks, followed the bottom of winding draws, or

[003-061]skirted the margin of wide lagoons, where the golden coreopsis grew up out of

[003-062]the clear water and the wild ducks rose with a whirr of wings.

[003-063]Lou looked after them helplessly. “I wish I’d brought my gun, anyway,

[003-064]Alexandra,” he said fretfully. “I could have hidden it under the straw in the

[003-065]bottom of the wagon.”

[003-066]“Then we’d have had to lie to Ivar. Besides, they say he can smell dead birds.

[003-067]And if he knew, we wouldn’t get anything out of him, not even a hammock. I want

[003-068]to talk to him, and he won’t talk sense if he’s angry. It makes him foolish.”

[003-069]Lou sniffed. “Whoever heard of him talking sense, anyhow! I’d rather have ducks

[003-070]for supper than Crazy Ivar’s tongue.”

[003-071]Emil was alarmed. “Oh, but, Lou, you don’t want to make him mad! He might

[003-072]howl!”

[003-073]They all laughed again, and Oscar urged the horses up the crumbling side of a

[003-074]clay bank. They had left the lagoons and the red grass behind them. In Crazy

[003-075]Ivar’s country the grass was short and gray, the draws deeper than they were in

[003-076]the Bergsons’ neighborhood, and the land was all broken up into hillocks and

[003-077]clay ridges. The wild flowers disappeared, and only in the bottom of the draws

[003-078]and gullies grew a few of the very toughest and hardiest: shoestring, and

[003-079]ironweed, and snow-on-the-mountain.

[003-080]“Look, look, Emil, there’s Ivar’s big pond!” Alexandra pointed to a shining

[003-081]sheet of water that lay at the bottom of a shallow draw. At one end of the pond

[003-082]was an earthen dam, planted with green willow bushes, and above it a door and a

[003-083]single window were set into the hillside. You would not have seen them at all

[003-084]but for the reflection of the sunlight upon the four panes of window-glass. And

[003-085]that was all you saw. Not a shed, not a corral, not a well, not even a path

[003-086]broken in the curly grass. But for the piece of rusty stovepipe sticking up

[003-087]through the sod, you could have walked over the roof of Ivar’s dwelling without

[003-088]dreaming that you were near a human habitation. Ivar had lived for three years

[003-089]in the clay bank, without defiling the face of nature any more than the coyote

[003-090]that had lived there before him had done.

[003-091]When the Bergsons drove over the hill, Ivar was sitting in the doorway of his

[003-092]house, reading the Norwegian Bible. He was a queerly shaped old man, with a

[003-093]thick, powerful body set on short bow-legs. His shaggy white hair, falling in a

[003-094]thick mane about his ruddy cheeks, made him look older than he was. He was

[003-095]barefoot, but he wore a clean shirt of unbleached cotton, open at the neck. He

[003-096]always put on a clean shirt when Sunday morning came round, though he never

[003-097]went to church. He had a peculiar religion of his own and could not get on with

[003-098]any of the denominations. Often he did not see anybody from one week’s end to

[003-099]another. He kept a calendar, and every morning he checked off a day, so that he

[003-100]was never in any doubt as to which day of the week it was. Ivar hired himself

[003-101]out in threshing and corn-husking time, and he doctored sick animals when he

[003-102]was sent for. When he was at home, he made hammocks out of twine and committed

[003-103]chapters of the Bible to memory.

[003-104]Ivar found contentment in the solitude he had sought out for himself. He

[003-105]disliked the litter of human dwellings: the broken food, the bits of broken

[003-106]china, the old wash-boilers and tea-kettles thrown into the sunflower patch. He

[003-107]preferred the cleanness and tidiness of the wild sod. He always said that the

[003-108]badgers had cleaner houses than people, and that when he took a housekeeper her

[003-109]name would be Mrs. Badger. He best expressed his preference for his wild

[003-110]homestead by saying that his Bible seemed truer to him there. If one stood in

[003-111]the doorway of his cave, and looked off at the rough land, the smiling sky, the

[003-112]curly grass white in the hot sunlight; if one listened to the rapturous song of

[003-113]the lark, the drumming of the quail, the burr of the locust against that vast

[003-114]silence, one understood what Ivar meant.

[003-115]On this Sunday afternoon his face shone with happiness. He closed the book on

[003-116]his knee, keeping the place with his horny finger, and repeated softly:—

[003-117]He sendeth the springs into the valleys, which run among the hills;

[003-118]They give drink to every beast of the field; the wild asses quench their

[003-119]thirst.

[003-120]The trees of the Lord are full of sap; the cedars of Lebanon which he hath

[003-121]planted;

[003-122]Where the birds make their nests: as for the stork, the fir trees are her

[003-123]house.

[003-124]The high hills are a refuge for the wild goats; and the rocks for the conies.

[003-125]Before he opened his Bible again, Ivar heard the Bergsons’ wagon approaching,

[003-126]and he sprang up and ran toward it.

[003-127]“No guns, no guns!” he shouted, waving his arms distractedly.

[003-128]“No, Ivar, no guns,” Alexandra called reassuringly.

[003-129]He dropped his arms and went up to the wagon, smiling amiably and looking at

[003-130]them out of his pale blue eyes.

[003-131]“We want to buy a hammock, if you have one,” Alexandra explained, “and my

[003-132]little brother, here, wants to see your big pond, where so many birds come.”

[003-133]Ivar smiled foolishly, and began rubbing the horses’ noses and feeling about

[003-134]their mouths behind the bits. “Not many birds just now. A few ducks this

[003-135]morning; and some snipe come to drink. But there was a crane last week. She

[003-136]spent one night and came back the next evening. I don’t know why. It is not her

[003-137]season, of course. Many of them go over in the fall. Then the pond is full of

[003-138]strange voices every night.”

[003-139]Alexandra translated for Carl, who looked thoughtful. “Ask him, Alexandra, if

[003-140]it is true that a sea gull came here once. I have heard so.”

[003-141]She had some difficulty in making the old man understand.

[003-142]He looked puzzled at first, then smote his hands together as he remembered.

[003-143]“Oh, yes, yes! A big white bird with long wings and pink feet. My! what a voice

[003-144]she had! She came in the afternoon and kept flying about the pond and screaming

[003-145]until dark. She was in trouble of some sort, but I could not understand her.

[003-146]She was going over to the other ocean, maybe, and did not know how far it was.

[003-147]She was afraid of never getting there. She was more mournful than our birds

[003-148]here; she cried in the night. She saw the light from my window and darted up to

[003-149]it. Maybe she thought my house was a boat, she was such a wild thing. Next

[003-150]morning, when the sun rose, I went out to take her food, but she flew up into

[003-151]the sky and went on her way.” Ivar ran his fingers through his thick hair. “I

[003-152]have many strange birds stop with me here. They come from very far away and are

[003-153]great company. I hope you boys never shoot wild birds?”

[003-154]Lou and Oscar grinned, and Ivar shook his bushy head. “Yes, I know boys are

[003-155]thoughtless. But these wild things are God’s birds. He watches over them and

[003-156]counts them, as we do our cattle; Christ says so in the New Testament.”

[003-157]“Now, Ivar,” Lou asked, “may we water our horses at your pond and give them

[003-158]some feed? It’s a bad road to your place.”

[003-159]“Yes, yes, it is.” The old man scrambled about and began to loose the tugs. “A

[003-160]bad road, eh, girls? And the bay with a colt at home!”

[003-161]Oscar brushed the old man aside. “We’ll take care of the horses, Ivar. You’ll

[003-162]be finding some disease on them. Alexandra wants to see your hammocks.”

[003-163]Ivar led Alexandra and Emil to his little cave house. He had but one room,

[003-164]neatly plastered and whitewashed, and there was a wooden floor. There was a

[003-165]kitchen stove, a table covered with oilcloth, two chairs, a clock, a calendar,

[003-166]a few books on the window-shelf; nothing more. But the place was as clean as a

[003-167]cupboard.

[003-168]“But where do you sleep, Ivar?” Emil asked, looking about.

[003-169]Ivar unslung a hammock from a hook on the wall; in it was rolled a buffalo

[003-170]robe. “There, my son. A hammock is a good bed, and in winter I wrap up in this

[003-171]skin. Where I go to work, the beds are not half so easy as this.”

[003-172]By this time Emil had lost all his timidity. He thought a cave a very superior

[003-173]kind of house. There was something pleasantly unusual about it and about Ivar.

[003-174]“Do the birds know you will be kind to them, Ivar? Is that why so many come?”

[003-175]he asked.

[003-176]Ivar sat down on the floor and tucked his feet under him. “See, little brother,

[003-177]they have come from a long way, and they are very tired. From up there where

[003-178]they are flying, our country looks dark and flat. They must have water to drink

[003-179]and to bathe in before they can go on with their journey. They look this way

[003-180]and that, and far below them they see something shining, like a piece of glass

[003-181]set in the dark earth. That is my pond. They come to it and are not disturbed.

[003-182]Maybe I sprinkle a little corn. They tell the other birds, and next year more

[003-183]come this way. They have their roads up there, as we have down here.”

[003-184]Emil rubbed his knees thoughtfully. “And is that true, Ivar, about the head

[003-185]ducks falling back when they are tired, and the hind ones taking their place?”

[003-186]“Yes. The point of the wedge gets the worst of it; they cut the wind. They can

[003-187]only stand it there a little while—half an hour, maybe. Then they fall back and

[003-188]the wedge splits a little, while the rear ones come up the middle to the front.

[003-189]Then it closes up and they fly on, with a new edge. They are always changing

[003-190]like that, up in the air. Never any confusion; just like soldiers who have been

[003-191]drilled.”

[003-192]Alexandra had selected her hammock by the time the boys came up from the pond.

[003-193]They would not come in, but sat in the shade of the bank outside while

[003-194]Alexandra and Ivar talked about the birds and about his housekeeping, and why

[003-195]he never ate meat, fresh or salt.

[003-196]Alexandra was sitting on one of the wooden chairs, her arms resting on the

[003-197]table. Ivar was sitting on the floor at her feet. “Ivar,” she said suddenly,

[003-198]beginning to trace the pattern on the oilcloth with her forefinger, “I came

[003-199]to-day more because I wanted to talk to you than because I wanted to buy a

[003-200]hammock.”

[003-201]“Yes?” The old man scraped his bare feet on the plank floor.

[003-202]“We have a big bunch of hogs, Ivar. I wouldn’t sell in the spring, when

[003-203]everybody advised me to, and now so many people are losing their hogs that I am

[003-204]frightened. What can be done?”

[003-205]Ivar’s little eyes began to shine. They lost their vagueness.

[003-206]“You feed them swill and such stuff? Of course! And sour milk? Oh, yes! And

[003-207]keep them in a stinking pen? I tell you, sister, the hogs of this country are

[003-208]put upon! They become unclean, like the hogs in the Bible. If you kept your

[003-209]chickens like that, what would happen? You have a little sorghum patch, maybe?

[003-210]Put a fence around it, and turn the hogs in. Build a shed to give them shade, a

[003-211]thatch on poles. Let the boys haul water to them in barrels, clean water, and

[003-212]plenty. Get them off the old stinking ground, and do not let them go back there

[003-213]until winter. Give them only grain and clean feed, such as you would give

[003-214]horses or cattle. Hogs do not like to be filthy.”

[003-215]The boys outside the door had been listening. Lou nudged his brother. “Come,

[003-216]the horses are done eating. Let’s hitch up and get out of here. He’ll fill her

[003-217]full of notions. She’ll be for having the pigs sleep with us, next.”

[003-218]Oscar grunted and got up. Carl, who could not understand what Ivar said, saw

[003-219]that the two boys were displeased. They did not mind hard work, but they hated

[003-220]experiments and could never see the use of taking pains. Even Lou, who was more

[003-221]elastic than his older brother, disliked to do anything different from their

[003-222]neighbors. He felt that it made them conspicuous and gave people a chance to

[003-223]talk about them.

[003-224]Once they were on the homeward road, the boys forgot their ill-humor and joked

[003-225]about Ivar and his birds. Alexandra did not propose any reforms in the care of

[003-226]the pigs, and they hoped she had forgotten Ivar’s talk. They agreed that he was

[003-227]crazier than ever, and would never be able to prove up on his land because he

[003-228]worked it so little. Alexandra privately resolved that she would have a talk

[003-229]with Ivar about this and stir him up. The boys persuaded Carl to stay for

[003-230]supper and go swimming in the pasture pond after dark.

[003-231]That evening, after she had washed the supper dishes, Alexandra sat down on the

[003-232]kitchen doorstep, while her mother was mixing the bread. It was a still,

[003-233]deep-breathing summer night, full of the smell of the hay fields. Sounds of

[003-234]laughter and splashing came up from the pasture, and when the moon rose rapidly

[003-235]above the bare rim of the prairie, the pond glittered like polished metal, and

[003-236]she could see the flash of white bodies as the boys ran about the edge, or

[003-237]jumped into the water. Alexandra watched the shimmering pool dreamily, but

[003-238]eventually her eyes went back to the sorghum patch south of the barn, where she

[003-239]was planning to make her new pig corral.
