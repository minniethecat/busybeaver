[010-001]Alexandra did not find time to go to her neighbor’s the next day, nor the next.

[010-002]It was a busy season on the farm, with the corn-plowing going on, and even Emil

[010-003]was in the field with a team and cultivator. Carl went about over the farms

[010-004]with Alexandra in the morning, and in the afternoon and evening they found a

[010-005]great deal to talk about. Emil, for all his track practice, did not stand up

[010-006]under farmwork very well, and by night he was too tired to talk or even to

[010-007]practise on his cornet.

[010-008]On Wednesday morning Carl got up before it was light, and stole downstairs and

[010-009]out of the kitchen door just as old [[ivar|Ivar]] was making his morning ablutions at

[010-010]the pump. Carl nodded to him and hurried up the draw, past the garden, and into

[010-011]the pasture where the milking cows used to be kept.

[010-012]The dawn in the east looked like the light from some great fire that was

[010-013]burning under the edge of the world. The color was reflected in the globules of

[010-014]dew that sheathed the short gray pasture grass. Carl walked rapidly until he

[010-015]came to the crest of the second hill, where the Bergson pasture joined the one

[010-016]that had belonged to his father. There he sat down and waited for the sun to

[010-017]rise. It was just there that he and Alexandra used to do their milking

[010-018]together, he on his side of the fence, she on hers. He could remember exactly

[010-019]how she looked when she came over the close-cropped grass, her skirts pinned

[010-020]up, her head bare, a bright tin pail in either hand, and the milky light of the

[010-021]early morning all about her. Even as a boy he used to feel, when he saw her

[010-022]coming with her free step, her upright head and calm shoulders, that she looked

[010-023]as if she had walked straight out of the morning itself. Since then, when he

[010-024]had happened to see the sun come up in the country or on the water, he had

[010-025]often remembered the young Swedish girl and her milking pails.

[010-026]Carl sat musing until the sun leaped above the prairie, and in the grass about

[010-027]him all the small creatures of day began to tune their tiny instruments. Birds

[010-028]and insects without number began to chirp, to twitter, to snap and whistle, to

[010-029]make all manner of fresh shrill noises. The pasture was flooded with light;

[010-030]every clump of ironweed and snow-on-the-mountain threw a long shadow, and the

[010-031]golden light seemed to be rippling through the curly grass like the tide racing

[010-032]in.

[010-033]He crossed the fence into the pasture that was now the Shabatas’ and continued

[010-034]his walk toward the pond. He had not gone far, however, when he discovered that

[010-035]he was not the only person abroad. In the draw below, his gun in his hands, was

[010-036]Emil, advancing cautiously, with a young woman beside him. They were moving

[010-037]softly, keeping close together, and Carl knew that they expected to find ducks

[010-038]on the pond. At the moment when they came in sight of the bright spot of water,

[010-039]he heard a whirr of wings and the ducks shot up into the air. There was a sharp

[010-040]crack from the gun, and five of the birds fell to the ground. Emil and his

[010-041]companion laughed delightedly, and Emil ran to pick them up. When he came back,

[010-042]dangling the ducks by their feet, Marie held her apron and he dropped them into

[010-043]it. As she stood looking down at them, her face changed. She took up one of the

[010-044]birds, a rumpled ball of feathers with the blood dripping slowly from its

[010-045]mouth, and looked at the live color that still burned on its plumage.

[010-046]As she let it fall, she cried in distress, “Oh, Emil, why did you?”

[010-047]“I like that!” the boy exclaimed indignantly. “Why, Marie, you asked me to come

[010-048]yourself.”

[010-049]“Yes, yes, I know,” she said tearfully, “but I didn’t think. I hate to see them

[010-050]when they are first shot. They were having such a good time, and we’ve spoiled

[010-051]it all for them.”

[010-052]Emil gave a rather sore laugh. “I should say we had! I’m not going hunting with

[010-053]you any more. You’re as bad as Ivar. Here, let me take them.” He snatched the

[010-054]ducks out of her apron.

[010-055]“Don’t be cross, Emil. Only—Ivar’s right about wild things. They’re too happy

[010-056]to kill. You can tell just how they felt when they flew up. They were scared,

[010-057]but they didn’t really think anything could hurt them. No, we won’t do that any

[010-058]more.”

[010-059]“All right,” Emil assented. “I’m sorry I made you feel bad.” As he looked down

[010-060]into her tearful eyes, there was a curious, sharp young bitterness in his own.

[010-061]Carl watched them as they moved slowly down the draw. They had not seen him at

[010-062]all. He had not overheard much of their dialogue, but he felt the import of it.

[010-063]It made him, somehow, unreasonably mournful to find two young things abroad in

[010-064]the pasture in the early morning. He decided that he needed his breakfast.
