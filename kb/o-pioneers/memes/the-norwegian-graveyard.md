## Overview

The Norwegian Graveyard on the Divide is the novel's most enduring witness to the transformation of the Nebraska prairie. When John Bergson is buried there at the novel's opening, the graveyard sits in wild, untamed grassland — "the grass had, indeed, grown back over everything, shaggy and red, hiding even the wire fence" (001-192). By the novel's middle, it overlooks a checkerboard of prosperous farms. By the end, both John Bergson and his son Emil lie there, framing the pioneer story from death to death, from wilderness to cultivation.

## Geography

The graveyard occupies a high point on the Divide with a commanding view. "From the Norwegian graveyard one looks out over a vast checker-board, marked off in squares of wheat and corn; light and dark, dark and light" (006-005). From its gate, "one can count a dozen gayly painted farmhouses; the gilded weather-vanes on the big red barns wink at each other across the green and brown and yellow fields" (006-008).

The graveyard lies three miles from the Bergson farmhouse (028-068) and approximately half a mile east of the town (025-037). A wire fence encloses it, and in the early chapters, the wild prairie grass has overgrown this fence. The Catholic graveyard stands separately (006-074), marking the religious divisions that persist even in death.

## In the Novel

The Norwegian Graveyard bookends the novel's narrative. In [[Chapter-01|Chapter 1]], the dying John Bergson is carried past it, and Alexandra reflects on her father's impending death while looking at the overgrown burial ground. John Bergson is buried there, his grave dug from the shaggy prairie sod.

When Alexandra and Carl Linstrum visit the graveyard years later, the view has transformed: "The shaggy coat of the prairie, which they lifted to make him a bed, has vanished forever" (006-005). Where once there was only wild grass, now prosperous farms checker the landscape. Alexandra remarks: "We can remember the graveyard when it was wild prairie, Carl" (009-062), linking the graveyard to memory and the passage of time.

In Part IV, Emil rides past the graveyard on his way to the church fair (025-037, 025-093), and his passage past it on that joyful day prefigures his own burial there.

After [[the-orchard-tragedy|the Orchard Tragedy]], Emil is buried in the Norwegian Graveyard. Ivar walks three miles from the farmhouse to the graveyard to tend Emil's grave (028-068). Alexandra visits Emil's grave on a rainy morning (029-147). In the novel's closing pages, Carl recalls Alexandra's words about the graveyard (030-111), bringing the narrative full circle.

## Significance

The Norwegian Graveyard functions as Cather's most powerful symbol of the passage of time on the Divide. It marks the transformation from wild prairie to cultivated farmland more vividly than any other location because the dead cannot move — they witness change from a fixed point. "Could he rise from beneath it, he would not know the country under which he has been asleep" (006-005).

The graveyard also represents the erasure of the pioneer generation. John Bergson, like the wild prairie grass, is covered over by the new order. His grave becomes "a bed" made from the prairie itself, suggesting a return to the land that the pioneers both fought and loved.

As the burial place of both John and Emil Bergson, the graveyard links father and son across the novel's entire span. Both deaths are costs of the frontier — John's from the struggle to establish a farm, Emil's from the passions that cultivation cannot control. Their shared resting place unites the pioneer and the dreamer in the soil they helped transform.
