[001-001]One January day, thirty years ago, the little town of [[hanover|Hanover]], anchored on a

[001-002]windy Nebraska tableland, was trying not to be blown away. A mist of fine

[001-003]snowflakes was curling and eddying about the cluster of low drab buildings

[001-004]huddled on the gray prairie, under a gray sky. The dwelling-houses were set

[001-005]about haphazard on the tough prairie sod; some of them looked as if they had

[001-006]been moved in overnight, and others as if they were straying off by themselves,

[001-007]headed straight for the open plain. None of them had any appearance of

[001-008]permanence, and the howling wind blew under them as well as over them. The main

[001-009]street was a deeply rutted road, now frozen hard, which ran from the squat red

[001-010]railway station and the grain “elevator” at the north end of the town to the

[001-011]lumber yard and the horse pond at the south end. On either side of this road

[001-012]straggled two uneven rows of wooden buildings; the general merchandise stores,

[001-013]the two banks, the drug store, the feed store, the saloon, the post-office. The

[001-014]board sidewalks were gray with trampled snow, but at two o’clock in the

[001-015]afternoon the shopkeepers, having come back from dinner, were keeping well

[001-016]behind their frosty windows. The children were all in school, and there was

[001-017]nobody abroad in the streets but a few rough-looking countrymen in coarse

[001-018]overcoats, with their long caps pulled down to their noses. Some of them had

[001-019]brought their wives to town, and now and then a red or a plaid shawl flashed

[001-020]out of one store into the shelter of another. At the hitch-bars along the

[001-021]street a few heavy work-horses, harnessed to farm wagons, shivered under their

[001-022]blankets. [[About]] the station everything was quiet, for there would not be

[001-023]another train in until night.

[001-024]On the sidewalk in front of one of the stores sat a little Swede boy, crying

[001-025]bitterly. He was about five years old. His black cloth coat was much too big

[001-026]for him and made him look like a little old man. His shrunken brown flannel

[001-027]dress had been washed many times and left a long stretch of stocking between

[001-028]the hem of his skirt and the tops of his clumsy, copper-toed shoes. His cap was

[001-029]pulled down over his ears; his nose and his chubby cheeks were chapped and red

[001-030]with cold. He cried quietly, and the few people who hurried by did not notice

[001-031]him. He was afraid to stop any one, afraid to go into the store and ask for

[001-032]help, so he sat wringing his long sleeves and looking up a telegraph pole

[001-033]beside him, whimpering, “My kitten, oh, my kitten! Her will fweeze!” At the top

[001-034]of the pole crouched a shivering gray kitten, mewing faintly and clinging

[001-035]desperately to the wood with her claws. The boy had been left at the store

[001-036]while his sister went to the doctor’s office, and in her absence a dog had

[001-037]chased his kitten up the pole. The little creature had never been so high

[001-038]before, and she was too frightened to move. Her master was sunk in despair. He

[001-039]was a little country boy, and this village was to him a very strange and

[001-040]perplexing place, where people wore fine clothes and had hard hearts. He always

[001-041]felt shy and awkward here, and wanted to hide behind things for fear some one

[001-042]might laugh at him. Just now, he was too unhappy to care who laughed. At last

[001-043]he seemed to see a ray of hope: his sister was coming, and he got up and ran

[001-044]toward her in his heavy shoes.

[001-045]His sister was a tall, strong girl, and she walked rapidly and resolutely, as

[001-046]if she knew exactly where she was going and what she was going to do next. She

[001-047]wore a man’s long ulster (not as if it were an affliction, but as if it were

[001-048]very comfortable and belonged to her; carried it like a young soldier), and a

[001-049]round plush cap, tied down with a thick veil. She had a serious, thoughtful

[001-050]face, and her clear, deep blue eyes were fixed intently on the distance,

[001-051]without seeming to see anything, as if she were in trouble. She did not notice

[001-052]the little boy until he pulled her by the coat. Then she stopped short and

[001-053]stooped down to wipe his wet face.

[001-054]“Why, Emil! I told you to stay in the store and not to come out. What is the

[001-055]matter with you?”

[001-056]“My kitten, sister, my kitten! A man put her out, and a dog chased her up

[001-057]there.” His forefinger, projecting from the sleeve of his coat, pointed up to

[001-058]the wretched little creature on the pole.

[001-059]“Oh, Emil! Didn’t I tell you she’d get us into trouble of some kind, if you

[001-060]brought her? What made you tease me so? But there, I ought to have known better

[001-061]myself.” She went to the foot of the pole and held out her arms, crying,

[001-062]“Kitty, kitty, kitty,” but the kitten only mewed and faintly waved its tail.

[001-063]Alexandra turned away decidedly. “No, she won’t come down. Somebody will have

[001-064]to go up after her. I saw the Linstrums’ wagon in town. I’ll go and see if I

[001-065]can find Carl. Maybe he can do something. Only you must stop crying, or I won’t

[001-066]go a step. Where’s your comforter? Did you leave it in the store? Never mind.

[001-067]Hold still, till I put this on you.”

[001-068]She unwound the brown veil from her head and tied it about his throat. A shabby

[001-069]little traveling man, who was just then coming out of the store on his way to

[001-070]the saloon, stopped and gazed stupidly at the shining mass of hair she bared

[001-071]when she took off her veil; two thick braids, pinned about her head in the

[001-072]German way, with a fringe of reddish-yellow curls blowing out from under her

[001-073]cap. He took his cigar out of his mouth and held the wet end between the

[001-074]fingers of his woolen glove. “My God, girl, what a head of hair!” he exclaimed,

[001-075]quite innocently and foolishly. She stabbed him with a glance of Amazonian

[001-076]fierceness and drew in her lower lip—most unnecessary severity. It gave the

[001-077]little clothing drummer such a start that he actually let his cigar fall to the

[001-078]sidewalk and went off weakly in the teeth of the wind to the saloon. His hand

[001-079]was still unsteady when he took his glass from the bartender. His feeble

[001-080]flirtatious instincts had been crushed before, but never so mercilessly. He

[001-081]felt cheap and ill-used, as if some one had taken advantage of him. When a

[001-082]drummer had been knocking about in little drab towns and crawling across the

[001-083]wintry country in dirty smoking-cars, was he to be blamed if, when he chanced

[001-084]upon a fine human creature, he suddenly wished himself more of a man?

[001-085]While the little drummer was drinking to recover his nerve, Alexandra hurried

[001-086]to the drug store as the most likely place to find [[carl-linstrum|Carl Linstrum]]. There he was,

[001-087]turning over a portfolio of chromo “studies” which the druggist sold to the

[001-088]Hanover women who did china-painting. Alexandra explained her predicament, and

[001-089]the boy followed her to the corner, where Emil still sat by the pole.

[001-090]“I’ll have to go up after her, Alexandra. I think at the depot they have some

[001-091]spikes I can strap on my feet. Wait a minute.” Carl thrust his hands into his

[001-092]pockets, lowered his head, and darted up the street against the north wind. He

[001-093]was a tall boy of fifteen, slight and narrow-chested. When he came back with

[001-094]the spikes, Alexandra asked him what he had done with his overcoat.

[001-095]“I left it in the drug store. I couldn’t climb in it, anyhow. Catch me if I

[001-096]fall, Emil,” he called back as he began his ascent. Alexandra watched him

[001-097]anxiously; the cold was bitter enough on the ground. The kitten would not budge

[001-098]an inch. Carl had to go to the very top of the pole, and then had some

[001-099]difficulty in tearing her from her hold. When he reached the ground, he handed

[001-100]the cat to her tearful little master. “Now go into the store with her, Emil,

[001-101]and get warm.” He opened the door for the child. “Wait a minute, Alexandra. Why

[001-102]can’t I drive for you as far as our place? It’s getting colder every minute.

[001-103]Have you seen the doctor?”

[001-104]“Yes. He is coming over to-morrow. But he says father can’t get better; can’t

[001-105]get well.” The girl’s lip trembled. She looked fixedly up the bleak street as

[001-106]if she were gathering her strength to face something, as if she were trying

[001-107]with all her might to grasp a situation which, no matter how painful, must be

[001-108]met and dealt with somehow. The wind flapped the skirts of her heavy coat about

[001-109]her.

[001-110]Carl did not say anything, but she felt his sympathy. He, too, was lonely. He

[001-111]was a thin, frail boy, with brooding dark eyes, very quiet in all his

[001-112]movements. There was a delicate pallor in his thin face, and his mouth was too

[001-113]sensitive for a boy’s. The lips had already a little curl of bitterness and

[001-114]skepticism. The two friends stood for a few moments on the windy street corner,

[001-115]not speaking a word, as two travelers, who have lost their way, sometimes stand

[001-116]and admit their perplexity in silence. When Carl turned away he said, “I’ll see

[001-117]to your team.” Alexandra went into the store to have her purchases packed in

[001-118]the egg-boxes, and to get warm before she set out on her long cold drive.

[001-119]When she looked for Emil, she found him sitting on a step of the staircase that

[001-120]led up to the clothing and carpet department. He was playing with a little

[001-121]Bohemian girl, Marie Tovesky, who was tying her handkerchief over the kitten’s

[001-122]head for a bonnet. Marie was a stranger in the country, having come from [[omaha|Omaha]]

[001-123]with her mother to visit her uncle, Joe Tovesky. She was a dark child, with

[001-124]brown curly hair, like a brunette doll’s, a coaxing little red mouth, and

[001-125]round, yellow-brown eyes. Every one noticed her eyes; the brown iris had golden

[001-126]glints that made them look like gold-stone, or, in softer lights, like that

[001-127]Colorado mineral called tiger-eye.

[001-128]The country children thereabouts wore their dresses to their shoe-tops, but

[001-129]this city child was dressed in what was then called the “Kate Greenaway”

[001-130]manner, and her red cashmere frock, gathered full from the yoke, came almost to

[001-131]the floor. This, with her poke bonnet, gave her the look of a quaint little

[001-132]woman. She had a white fur tippet about her neck and made no fussy objections

[001-133]when Emil fingered it admiringly. Alexandra had not the heart to take him away

[001-134]from so pretty a playfellow, and she let them tease the kitten together until

[001-135]Joe Tovesky came in noisily and picked up his little niece, setting her on his

[001-136]shoulder for every one to see. His children were all boys, and he adored this

[001-137]little creature. His cronies formed a circle about him, admiring and teasing

[001-138]the little girl, who took their jokes with great good nature. They were all

[001-139]delighted with her, for they seldom saw so pretty and carefully nurtured a

[001-140]child. They told her that she must choose one of them for a sweetheart, and

[001-141]each began pressing his suit and offering her bribes; candy, and little pigs,

[001-142]and spotted calves. She looked archly into the big, brown, mustached faces,

[001-143]smelling of spirits and tobacco, then she ran her tiny forefinger delicately

[001-144]over Joe’s bristly chin and said, “Here is my sweetheart.”

[001-145]The Bohemians roared with laughter, and Marie’s uncle hugged her until she

[001-146]cried, “Please don’t, Uncle Joe! You hurt me.” Each of Joe’s friends gave her a

[001-147]bag of candy, and she kissed them all around, though she did not like country

[001-148]candy very well. Perhaps that was why she bethought herself of Emil. “Let me

[001-149]down, Uncle Joe,” she said, “I want to give some of my candy to that nice

[001-150]little boy I found.” She walked graciously over to Emil, followed by her lusty

[001-151]admirers, who formed a new circle and teased the little boy until he hid his

[001-152]face in his sister’s skirts, and she had to scold him for being such a baby.

[001-153]The farm people were making preparations to start for home. The women were

[001-154]checking over their groceries and pinning their big red shawls about their

[001-155]heads. The men were buying tobacco and candy with what money they had left,

[001-156]were showing each other new boots and gloves and blue flannel shirts. Three big

[001-157]Bohemians were drinking raw alcohol, tinctured with oil of cinnamon. This was

[001-158]said to fortify one effectually against the cold, and they smacked their lips

[001-159]after each pull at the flask. Their volubility drowned every other noise in the

[001-160]place, and the overheated store sounded of their spirited language as it reeked

[001-161]of pipe smoke, damp woolens, and kerosene.

[001-162]Carl came in, wearing his overcoat and carrying a wooden box with a brass

[001-163]handle. “Come,” he said, “I’ve fed and watered your team, and the wagon is

[001-164]ready.” He carried Emil out and tucked him down in the straw in the wagonbox.

[001-165]The heat had made the little boy sleepy, but he still clung to his kitten.

[001-166]“You were awful good to climb so high and get my kitten, Carl. When I get big

[001-167]I’ll climb and get little boys’ kittens for them,” he murmured drowsily. Before

[001-168]the horses were over the first hill, Emil and his cat were both fast asleep.

[001-169]Although it was only four o’clock, the winter day was fading. The road led

[001-170]southwest, toward the streak of pale, watery light that glimmered in the leaden

[001-171]sky. The light fell upon the two sad young faces that were turned mutely toward

[001-172]it: upon the eyes of the girl, who seemed to be looking with such anguished

[001-173]perplexity into the future; upon the sombre eyes of the boy, who seemed already

[001-174]to be looking into the past. The little town behind them had vanished as if it

[001-175]had never been, had fallen behind the swell of the prairie, and the stern

[001-176]frozen country received them into its bosom. The homesteads were few and far

[001-177]apart; here and there a windmill gaunt against the sky, a sod house crouching

[001-178]in a hollow. But the great fact was the land itself, which seemed to overwhelm

[001-179]the little beginnings of human society that struggled in its sombre wastes. It

[001-180]was from facing this vast hardness that the boy’s mouth had become so bitter;

[001-181]because he felt that men were too weak to make any mark here, that the land

[001-182]wanted to be let alone, to preserve its own fierce strength, its peculiar,

[001-183]savage kind of beauty, its uninterrupted mournfulness.

[001-184]The wagon jolted along over the frozen road. The two friends had less to say to

[001-185]each other than usual, as if the cold had somehow penetrated to their hearts.

[001-186]“Did Lou and Oscar go to the Blue to cut wood to-day?” Carl asked.

[001-187]“Yes. I’m almost sorry I let them go, it’s turned so cold. But mother frets if

[001-188]the wood gets low.” She stopped and put her hand to her forehead, brushing back

[001-189]her hair. “I don’t know what is to become of us, Carl, if father has to die. I

[001-190]don’t dare to think about it. I wish we could all go with him and let the grass

[001-191]grow back over everything.”

[001-192]Carl made no reply. Just ahead of them was the Norwegian graveyard, where the

[001-193]grass had, indeed, grown back over everything, shaggy and red, hiding even the

[001-194]wire fence. Carl realized that he was not a very helpful companion, but there

[001-195]was nothing he could say.

[001-196]“Of course,” Alexandra went on, steadying her voice a little, “the boys are

[001-197]strong and work hard, but we’ve always depended so on father that I don’t see

[001-198]how we can go ahead. I almost feel as if there were nothing to go ahead for.”

[001-199]“Does your father know?”

[001-200]“Yes, I think he does. He lies and counts on his fingers all day. I think he is

[001-201]trying to count up what he is leaving for us. It’s a comfort to him that my

[001-202]chickens are laying right on through the cold weather and bringing in a little

[001-203]money. I wish we could keep his mind off such things, but I don’t have much

[001-204]time to be with him now.”

[001-205]“I wonder if he’d like to have me bring my magic lantern over some evening?”

[001-206]Alexandra turned her face toward him. “Oh, Carl! Have you got it?”

[001-207]“Yes. It’s back there in the straw. Didn’t you notice the box I was carrying? I

[001-208]tried it all morning in the drug-store cellar, and it worked ever so well,

[001-209]makes fine big pictures.”

[001-210]“What are they about?”

[001-211]“Oh, hunting pictures in Germany, and Robinson Crusoe and funny pictures about

[001-212]cannibals. I’m going to paint some slides for it on glass, out of the Hans

[001-213]Andersen book.”

[001-214]Alexandra seemed actually cheered. There is often a good deal of the child left

[001-215]in people who have had to grow up too soon. “Do bring it over, Carl. I can

[001-216]hardly wait to see it, and I’m sure it will please father. Are the pictures

[001-217]colored? Then I know he’ll like them. He likes the calendars I get him in town.

[001-218]I wish I could get more. You must leave me here, mustn’t you? It’s been nice to

[001-219]have company.”

[001-220]Carl stopped the horses and looked dubiously up at the black sky. “It’s pretty

[001-221]dark. Of course the horses will take you home, but I think I’d better light

[001-222]your lantern, in case you should need it.”

[001-223]He gave her the reins and climbed back into the wagon-box, where he crouched

[001-224]down and made a tent of his overcoat. After a dozen trials he succeeded in

[001-225]lighting the lantern, which he placed in front of Alexandra, half covering it

[001-226]with a blanket so that the light would not shine in her eyes. “Now, wait until

[001-227]I find my box. Yes, here it is. Good-night, Alexandra. Try not to worry.” Carl

[001-228]sprang to the ground and ran off across the fields toward the Linstrum

[001-229]homestead. “Hoo, hoo-o-o-o!” he called back as he disappeared over a ridge and

[001-230]dropped into a sand gully. The wind answered him like an echo, “Hoo,

[001-231]hoo-o-o-o-o-o!” Alexandra drove off alone. The rattle of her wagon was lost in

[001-232]the howling of the wind, but her lantern, held firmly between her feet, made a

[001-233]moving point of light along the highway, going deeper and deeper into the dark

[001-234]country.
