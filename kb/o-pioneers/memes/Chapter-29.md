[029-001]Late in the afternoon of a brilliant October day, [[alexandra-bergson|Alexandra Bergson]], dressed in

[029-002]a black suit and traveling-hat, alighted at the Burlington depot in [[lincoln|Lincoln]].

[029-003]She drove to the Lindell Hotel, where she had stayed two years ago when she

[029-004]came up for Emil’s Commencement. In spite of her usual air of sureness and

[029-005]self-possession, Alexandra felt ill at ease in hotels, and she was glad, when

[029-006]she went to the clerk’s desk to register, that there were not many people in

[029-007]the lobby. She had her supper early, wearing her hat and black jacket down to

[029-008]the dining-room and carrying her handbag. After supper she went out for a walk.

[029-009]It was growing dark when she reached the university campus. She did not go into

[029-010]the grounds, but walked slowly up and down the stone walk outside the long iron

[029-011]fence, looking through at the young men who were running from one building to

[029-012]another, at the lights shining from the armory and the library. A squad of

[029-013]cadets were going through their drill behind the armory, and the commands of

[029-014]their young officer rang out at regular intervals, so sharp and quick that

[029-015]Alexandra could not understand them. Two stalwart girls came down the library

[029-016]steps and out through one of the iron gates. As they passed her, Alexandra was

[029-017]pleased to hear them speaking Bohemian to each other. Every few moments a boy

[029-018]would come running down the flagged walk and dash out into the street as if he

[029-019]were rushing to announce some wonder to the world. Alexandra felt a great

[029-020]tenderness for them all. She wished one of them would stop and speak to her.

[029-021]She wished she could ask them whether they had known Emil.

[029-022]As she lingered by the south gate she actually did encounter one of the boys.

[029-023]He had on his drill cap and was swinging his books at the end of a long strap.

[029-024]It was dark by this time; he did not see her and ran against her. He snatched

[029-025]off his cap and stood bareheaded and panting. “I’m awfully sorry,” he said in a

[029-026]bright, clear voice, with a rising inflection, as if he expected her to say

[029-027]something.

[029-028]“Oh, it was my fault!” said Alexandra eagerly. “Are you an old student here,

[029-029]may I ask?”

[029-030]“No, ma’am. I’m a Freshie, just off the farm. [[cherry-county|Cherry County]]. Were you hunting

[029-031]somebody?”

[029-032]“No, thank you. That is—” Alexandra wanted to detain him. “That is, I would

[029-033]like to find some of my brother’s friends. He graduated two years ago.”

[029-034]“Then you’d have to try the Seniors, wouldn’t you? Let’s see; I don’t know any

[029-035]of them yet, but there’ll be sure to be some of them around the library. That

[029-036]red building, right there,” he pointed.

[029-037]“Thank you, I’ll try there,” said Alexandra lingeringly.

[029-038]“Oh, that’s all right! Good-night.” The lad clapped his cap on his head and ran

[029-039]straight down Eleventh Street. Alexandra looked after him wistfully.

[029-040]She walked back to her hotel unreasonably comforted. “What a nice voice that

[029-041]boy had, and how polite he was. I know Emil was always like that to women.” And

[029-042]again, after she had undressed and was standing in her nightgown, brushing her

[029-043]long, heavy hair by the electric light, she remembered him and said to herself,

[029-044]“I don’t think I ever heard a nicer voice than that boy had. I hope he will get

[029-045]on well here. Cherry County; that’s where the hay is so fine, and the coyotes

[029-046]can scratch down to water.”

[029-047]At nine o’clock the next morning Alexandra presented herself at the warden’s

[029-048]office in the State Penitentiary. The warden was a German, a ruddy,

[029-049]cheerful-looking man who had formerly been a harness-maker. Alexandra had a

[029-050]letter to him from the German banker in [[hanover|Hanover]]. As he glanced at the letter,

[029-051]Mr. Schwartz put away his pipe.

[029-052]“That big Bohemian, is it? Sure, he’s gettin’ along fine,” said Mr. Schwartz

[029-053]cheerfully.

[029-054]“I am glad to hear that. I was afraid he might be quarrelsome and get himself

[029-055]into more trouble. Mr. Schwartz, if you have time, I would like to tell you a

[029-056]little about [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]], and why I am interested in him.”

[029-057]The warden listened genially while she told him briefly something of Frank’s

[029-058]history and character, but he did not seem to find anything unusual in her

[029-059]account.

[029-060]“Sure, I’ll keep an eye on him. We’ll take care of him all right,” he said,

[029-061]rising. “You can talk to him here, while I go to see to things in the kitchen.

[029-062]I’ll have him sent in. He ought to be done washing out his cell by this time.

[029-063]We have to keep ’em clean, you know.”

[029-064]The warden paused at the door, speaking back over his shoulder to a pale young

[029-065]man in convicts’ clothes who was seated at a desk in the corner, writing in a

[029-066]big ledger.

[029-067]“Bertie, when 1037 is brought in, you just step out and give this lady a chance

[029-068]to talk.”

[029-069]The young man bowed his head and bent over his ledger again.

[029-070]When Mr. Schwartz disappeared, Alexandra thrust her black-edged handkerchief

[029-071]nervously into her handbag. Coming out on the streetcar she had not had the

[029-072]least dread of meeting Frank. But since she had been here the sounds and smells

[029-073]in the corridor, the look of the men in convicts’ clothes who passed the glass

[029-074]door of the warden’s office, affected her unpleasantly.

[029-075]The warden’s clock ticked, the young convict’s pen scratched busily in the big

[029-076]book, and his sharp shoulders were shaken every few seconds by a loose cough

[029-077]which he tried to smother. It was easy to see that he was a sick man. Alexandra

[029-078]looked at him timidly, but he did not once raise his eyes. He wore a white

[029-079]shirt under his striped jacket, a high collar, and a necktie, very carefully

[029-080]tied. His hands were thin and white and well cared for, and he had a seal ring

[029-081]on his little finger. When he heard steps approaching in the corridor, he rose,

[029-082]blotted his book, put his pen in the rack, and left the room without raising

[029-083]his eyes. Through the door he opened a guard came in, bringing Frank Shabata.

[029-084]“You the lady that wanted to talk to 1037? Here he is. Be on your good

[029-085]behavior, now. He can set down, lady,” seeing that Alexandra remained standing.

[029-086]“Push that white button when you’re through with him, and I’ll come.”

[029-087]The guard went out and Alexandra and Frank were left alone.

[029-088]Alexandra tried not to see his hideous clothes. She tried to look straight into

[029-089]his face, which she could scarcely believe was his. It was already bleached to

[029-090]a chalky gray. His lips were colorless, his fine teeth looked yellowish. He

[029-091]glanced at Alexandra sullenly, blinked as if he had come from a dark place, and

[029-092]one eyebrow twitched continually. She felt at once that this interview was a

[029-093]terrible ordeal to him. His shaved head, showing the conformation of his skull,

[029-094]gave him a criminal look which he had not had during the trial.

[029-095]Alexandra held out her hand. “Frank,” she said, her eyes filling suddenly, “I

[029-096]hope you’ll let me be friendly with you. I understand how you did it. I don’t

[029-097]feel hard toward you. They were more to blame than you.”

[029-098]Frank jerked a dirty blue handkerchief from his trousers pocket. He had begun

[029-099]to cry. He turned away from Alexandra. “I never did mean to do not’ing to dat

[029-100]woman,” he muttered. “I never mean to do not’ing to dat boy. I ain’t had

[029-101]not’ing ag’in’ dat boy. I always like dat boy fine. An’ then I find him—” He

[029-102]stopped. The feeling went out of his face and eyes. He dropped into a chair and

[029-103]sat looking stolidly at the floor, his hands hanging loosely between his knees,

[029-104]the handkerchief lying across his striped leg. He seemed to have stirred up in

[029-105]his mind a disgust that had paralyzed his faculties.

[029-106]“I haven’t come up here to blame you, Frank. I think they were more to blame

[029-107]than you.” Alexandra, too, felt benumbed.

[029-108]Frank looked up suddenly and stared out of the office window. “I guess dat

[029-109]place all go to hell what I work so hard on,” he said with a slow, bitter

[029-110]smile. “I not care a damn.” He stopped and rubbed the palm of his hand over the

[029-111]light bristles on his head with annoyance. “I no can t’ink without my hair,” he

[029-112]complained. “I forget English. We not talk here, except swear.”

[029-113]Alexandra was bewildered. Frank seemed to have undergone a change of

[029-114]personality. There was scarcely anything by which she could recognize her

[029-115]handsome Bohemian neighbor. He seemed, somehow, not altogether human. She did

[029-116]not know what to say to him.

[029-117]“You do not feel hard to me, Frank?” she asked at last.

[029-118]Frank clenched his fist and broke out in excitement. “I not feel hard at no

[029-119]woman. I tell you I not that kind-a man. I never hit my wife. No, never I hurt

[029-120]her when she devil me something awful!” He struck his fist down on the warden’s

[029-121]desk so hard that he afterward stroked it absently. A pale pink crept over his

[029-122]neck and face. “Two, t’ree years I know dat woman don’ care no more ’bout me,

[029-123]Alexandra Bergson. I know she after some other man. I know her, oo-oo! An’ I

[029-124]ain’t never hurt her. I never would-a done dat, if I ain’t had dat gun along. I

[029-125]don’ know what in hell make me take dat gun. She always say I ain’t no man to

[029-126]carry gun. If she been in dat house, where she ought-a been—But das a foolish

[029-127]talk.”

[029-128]Frank rubbed his head and stopped suddenly, as he had stopped before. Alexandra

[029-129]felt that there was something strange in the way he chilled off, as if

[029-130]something came up in him that extinguished his power of feeling or thinking.

[029-131]“Yes, Frank,” she said kindly. “I know you never meant to hurt Marie.”

[029-132]Frank smiled at her queerly. His eyes filled slowly with tears. “You know, I

[029-133]most forgit dat woman’s name. She ain’t got no name for me no more. I never

[029-134]hate my wife, but dat woman what make me do dat—Honest to God, but I hate her!

[029-135]I no man to fight. I don’ want to kill no boy and no woman. I not care how many

[029-136]men she take under dat tree. I no care for not’ing but dat fine boy I kill,

[029-137]Alexandra Bergson. I guess I go crazy sure ’nough.”

[029-138]Alexandra remembered the little yellow cane she had found in Frank’s

[029-139]clothes-closet. She thought of how he had come to this country a gay young

[029-140]fellow, so attractive that the prettiest Bohemian girl in [[omaha|Omaha]] had run away

[029-141]with him. It seemed unreasonable that life should have landed him in such a

[029-142]place as this. She blamed Marie bitterly. And why, with her happy, affectionate

[029-143]nature, should she have brought destruction and sorrow to all who had loved

[029-144]her, even to poor old Joe Tovesky, the uncle who used to carry her about so

[029-145]proudly when she was a little girl? That was the strangest thing of all. Was

[029-146]there, then, something wrong in being warm-hearted and impulsive like that?

[029-147]Alexandra hated to think so. But there was Emil, in the Norwegian graveyard at

[029-148]home, and here was Frank Shabata. Alexandra rose and took him by the hand.

[029-149]“Frank Shabata, I am never going to stop trying until I get you pardoned. I’ll

[029-150]never give the Governor any peace. I know I can get you out of this place.”

[029-151]Frank looked at her distrustfully, but he gathered confidence from her face.

[029-152]“Alexandra,” he said earnestly, “if I git out-a here, I not trouble dis country

[029-153]no more. I go back where I come from; see my mother.”

[029-154]Alexandra tried to withdraw her hand, but Frank held on to it nervously. He put

[029-155]out his finger and absently touched a button on her black jacket. “Alexandra,”

[029-156]he said in a low tone, looking steadily at the button, “you ain’ t’ink I use

[029-157]dat girl awful bad before—”

[029-158]“No, Frank. We won’t talk about that,” Alexandra said, pressing his hand. “I

[029-159]can’t help Emil now, so I’m going to do what I can for you. You know I don’t go

[029-160]away from home often, and I came up here on purpose to tell you this.”

[029-161]The warden at the glass door looked in inquiringly. Alexandra nodded, and he

[029-162]came in and touched the white button on his desk. The guard appeared, and with

[029-163]a sinking heart Alexandra saw Frank led away down the corridor. After a few

[029-164]words with Mr. Schwartz, she left the prison and made her way to the

[029-165]street-car. She had refused with horror the warden’s cordial invitation to “go

[029-166]through the institution.” As the car lurched over its uneven roadbed, back

[029-167]toward Lincoln, Alexandra thought of how she and Frank had been wrecked by the

[029-168]same storm and of how, although she could come out into the sunlight, she had

[029-169]not much more left in her life than he. She remembered some lines from a poem

[029-170]she had liked in her schooldays:—

[029-171]Henceforth the world will only be

[029-172]A wider prison-house to me,—

[029-173]and sighed. A disgust of life weighed upon her heart; some such feeling as had

[029-174]twice frozen Frank Shabata’s features while they talked together. She wished

[029-175]she were back on the Divide.

[029-176]When Alexandra entered her hotel, the clerk held up one finger and beckoned to

[029-177]her. As she approached his desk, he handed her a telegram. Alexandra took the

[029-178]yellow envelope and looked at it in perplexity, then stepped into the elevator

[029-179]without opening it. As she walked down the corridor toward her room, she

[029-180]reflected that she was, in a manner, immune from evil tidings. On reaching her

[029-181]room she locked the door, and sitting down on a chair by the dresser, opened

[029-182]the telegram. It was from Hanover, and it read:—

[029-183]Arrived Hanover last night. Shall wait here until you come. Please hurry.

[029-184]CARLLINSTRUM.

[029-185]Alexandra put her head down on the dresser and burst into tears.
