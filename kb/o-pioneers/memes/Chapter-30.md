[030-001]The next afternoon Carl and Alexandra were walking across the fields from Mrs.

[030-002]Hiller’s. Alexandra had left [[lincoln|Lincoln]] after midnight, and Carl had met her at

[030-003]the [[hanover|Hanover]] station early in the morning. After they reached home, Alexandra

[030-004]had gone over to Mrs. Hiller’s to leave a little present she had bought for her

[030-005]in the city. They stayed at the old lady’s door but a moment, and then came out

[030-006]to spend the rest of the afternoon in the sunny fields.

[030-007]Alexandra had taken off her black traveling suit and put on a white dress;

[030-008]partly because she saw that her black clothes made Carl uncomfortable and

[030-009]partly because she felt oppressed by them herself. They seemed a little like

[030-010]the prison where she had worn them yesterday, and to be out of place in the

[030-011]open fields. Carl had changed very little. His cheeks were browner and fuller.

[030-012]He looked less like a tired scholar than when he went away a year ago, but no

[030-013]one, even now, would have taken him for a man of business. His soft, lustrous

[030-014]black eyes, his whimsical smile, would be less against him in the Klondike than

[030-015]on the Divide. There are always dreamers on the frontier.

[030-016]Carl and Alexandra had been talking since morning. Her letter had never reached

[030-017]him. He had first learned of her misfortune from a San Francisco paper, four

[030-018]weeks old, which he had picked up in a saloon, and which contained a brief

[030-019]account of [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]]’s trial. When he put down the paper, he had already

[030-020]made up his mind that he could reach Alexandra as quickly as a letter could;

[030-021]and ever since he had been on the way; day and night, by the fastest boats and

[030-022]trains he could catch. His steamer had been held back two days by rough

[030-023]weather.

[030-024]As they came out of Mrs. Hiller’s garden they took up their talk again where

[030-025]they had left it.

[030-026]“But could you come away like that, Carl, without arranging things? Could you

[030-027]just walk off and leave your business?” Alexandra asked.

[030-028]Carl laughed. “Prudent Alexandra! You see, my dear, I happen to have an honest

[030-029]partner. I trust him with everything. In fact, it’s been his enterprise from

[030-030]the beginning, you know. I’m in it only because he took me in. I’ll have to go

[030-031]back in the spring. Perhaps you will want to go with me then. We haven’t turned

[030-032]up millions yet, but we’ve got a start that’s worth following. But this winter

[030-033]I’d like to spend with you. You won’t feel that we ought to wait longer, on

[030-034]Emil’s account, will you, Alexandra?”

[030-035]Alexandra shook her head. “No, Carl; I don’t feel that way about it. And surely

[030-036]you needn’t mind anything Lou and Oscar say now. They are much angrier with me

[030-037]about Emil, now, than about you. They say it was all my fault. That I ruined

[030-038]him by sending him to college.”

[030-039]“No, I don’t care a button for Lou or Oscar. The moment I knew you were in

[030-040]trouble, the moment I thought you might need me, it all looked different.

[030-041]You’ve always been a triumphant kind of person.” Carl hesitated, looking

[030-042]sidewise at her strong, full figure. “But you do need me now, Alexandra?”

[030-043]She put her hand on his arm. “I needed you terribly when it happened, Carl. I

[030-044]cried for you at night. Then everything seemed to get hard inside of me, and I

[030-045]thought perhaps I should never care for you again. But when I got your telegram

[030-046]yesterday, then—then it was just as it used to be. You are all I have in the

[030-047]world, you know.”

[030-048]Carl pressed her hand in silence. They were passing the Shabatas’ empty house

[030-049]now, but they avoided the orchard path and took one that led over by the

[030-050]pasture pond.

[030-051]“Can you understand it, Carl?” Alexandra murmured. “I have had nobody but [[ivar|Ivar]]

[030-052]and [[signa|Signa]] to talk to. Do talk to me. Can you understand it? Could you have

[030-053]believed that of Marie Tovesky? I would have been cut to pieces, little by

[030-054]little, before I would have betrayed her trust in me!”

[030-055]Carl looked at the shining spot of water before them. “Maybe she was cut to

[030-056]pieces, too, Alexandra. I am sure she tried hard; they both did. That was why

[030-057]Emil went to Mexico, of course. And he was going away again, you tell me,

[030-058]though he had only been home three weeks. You remember that Sunday when I went

[030-059]with Emil up to the French Church fair? I thought that day there was some kind

[030-060]of feeling, something unusual, between them. I meant to talk to you about it.

[030-061]But on my way back I met Lou and Oscar and got so angry that I forgot

[030-062]everything else. You mustn’t be hard on them, Alexandra. Sit down here by the

[030-063]pond a minute. I want to tell you something.”

[030-064]They sat down on the grass-tufted bank and Carl told her how he had seen Emil

[030-065]and Marie out by the pond that morning, more than a year ago, and how young and

[030-066]charming and full of grace they had seemed to him. “It happens like that in the

[030-067]world sometimes, Alexandra,” he added earnestly. “I’ve seen it before. There

[030-068]are women who spread ruin around them through no fault of theirs, just by being

[030-069]too beautiful, too full of life and love. They can’t help it. People come to

[030-070]them as people go to a warm fire in winter. I used to feel that in her when she

[030-071]was a little girl. Do you remember how all the Bohemians crowded round her in

[030-072]the store that day, when she gave Emil her candy? You remember those yellow

[030-073]sparks in her eyes?”

[030-074]Alexandra sighed. “Yes. People couldn’t help loving her. Poor Frank does, even

[030-075]now, I think; though he’s got himself in such a tangle that for a long time his

[030-076]love has been bitterer than his hate. But if you saw there was anything wrong,

[030-077]you ought to have told me, Carl.”

[030-078]Carl took her hand and smiled patiently. “My dear, it was something one felt in

[030-079]the air, as you feel the spring coming, or a storm in summer. I didn’t*see*anything. Simply, when I was with those two young things, I felt my

[030-080]blood go quicker, I felt—how shall I say it?—an acceleration of life. After I

[030-081]got away, it was all too delicate, too intangible, to write about.”

[030-082]Alexandra looked at him mournfully. “I try to be more liberal about such things

[030-083]than I used to be. I try to realize that we are not all made alike. Only, why

[030-084]couldn’t it have been [[raul-marcel|Raoul Marcel]], or Jan Smirka? Why did it have to be my

[030-085]boy?”

[030-086]“Because he was the best there was, I suppose. They were both the best you had

[030-087]here.”

[030-088]The sun was dropping low in the west when the two friends rose and took the

[030-089]path again. The straw-stacks were throwing long shadows, the owls were flying

[030-090]home to the prairie-dog town. When they came to the corner where the pastures

[030-091]joined, Alexandra’s twelve young colts were galloping in a drove over the brow

[030-092]of the hill.

[030-093]“Carl,” said Alexandra, “I should like to go up there with you in the spring. I

[030-094]haven’t been on the water since we crossed the ocean, when I was a little girl.

[030-095]After we first came out here I used to dream sometimes about the shipyard where

[030-096]father worked, and a little sort of inlet, full of masts.” Alexandra paused.

[030-097]After a moment’s thought she said, “But you would never ask me to go away for

[030-098]good, would you?”

[030-099]“Of course not, my dearest. I think I know how you feel about this country as

[030-100]well as you do yourself.” Carl took her hand in both his own and pressed it

[030-101]tenderly.

[030-102]“Yes, I still feel that way, though Emil is gone. When I was on the train this

[030-103]morning, and we got near Hanover, I felt something like I did when I drove back

[030-104]with Emil from the river that time, in the dry year. I was glad to come back to

[030-105]it. I’ve lived here a long time. There is great peace here, Carl, and

[030-106]freedom.... I thought when I came out of that prison, where poor Frank is, that

[030-107]I should never feel free again. But I do, here.” Alexandra took a deep breath

[030-108]and looked off into the red west.

[030-109]“You belong to the land,” Carl murmured, “as you have always said. Now more

[030-110]than ever.”

[030-111]“Yes, now more than ever. You remember what you once said about the graveyard,

[030-112]and the old story writing itself over? Only it is we who write it, with the

[030-113]best we have.”

[030-114]They paused on the last ridge of the pasture, overlooking the house and the

[030-115]windmill and the stables that marked the site of [[john-bergson|John Bergson]]’s homestead. On

[030-116]every side the brown waves of the earth rolled away to meet the sky.

[030-117]“Lou and Oscar can’t see those things,” said Alexandra suddenly. “Suppose I do

[030-118]will my land to their children, what difference will that make? The land

[030-119]belongs to the future, Carl; that’s the way it seems to me. How many of the

[030-120]names on the county clerk’s plat will be there in fifty years? I might as well

[030-121]try to will the sunset over there to my brother’s children. We come and go, but

[030-122]the land is always here. And the people who love it and understand it are the

[030-123]people who own it—for a little while.”

[030-124]Carl looked at her wonderingly. She was still gazing into the west, and in her

[030-125]face there was that exalted serenity that sometimes came to her at moments of

[030-126]deep feeling. The level rays of the sinking sun shone in her clear eyes.

[030-127]“Why are you thinking of such things now, Alexandra?”

[030-128]“I had a dream before I went to Lincoln—But I will tell you about that

[030-129]afterward, after we are married. It will never come true, now, in the way I

[030-130]thought it might.” She took Carl’s arm and they walked toward the gate. “How

[030-131]many times we have walked this path together, Carl. How many times we will walk

[030-132]it again! Does it seem to you like coming back to your own place? Do you feel

[030-133]at peace with the world here? I think we shall be very happy. I haven’t any

[030-134]fears. I think when friends marry, they are safe. We don’t suffer like—those

[030-135]young ones.” Alexandra ended with a sigh.

[030-136]They had reached the gate. Before Carl opened it, he drew Alexandra to him and

[030-137]kissed her softly, on her lips and on her eyes.

[030-138]She leaned heavily on his shoulder. “I am tired,” she murmured. “I have been

[030-139]very lonely, Carl.”

[030-140]They went into the house together, leaving the Divide behind them, under the

[030-141]evening star. Fortunate country, that is one day to receive hearts like

[030-142]Alexandra’s into its bosom, to give them out again in the yellow wheat, in the

[030-143]rustling corn, in the shining eyes of youth!
