[021-001][[signa|Signa]]’s wedding supper was over. The guests, and the tiresome little Norwegian

[021-002]preacher who had performed the marriage ceremony, were saying good-night. Old

[021-003][[ivar|Ivar]] was hitching the horses to the wagon to take the wedding presents and the

[021-004]bride and groom up to their new home, on Alexandra’s north quarter. When Ivar

[021-005]drove up to the gate, Emil and [[marie-shabata|Marie Shabata]] began to carry out the presents,

[021-006]and Alexandra went into her bedroom to bid Signa good-bye and to give her a few

[021-007]words of good counsel. She was surprised to find that the bride had changed her

[021-008]slippers for heavy shoes and was pinning up her skirts. At that moment Nelse

[021-009]appeared at the gate with the two milk cows that Alexandra had given Signa for

[021-010]a wedding present.

[021-011]Alexandra began to laugh. “Why, Signa, you and Nelse are to ride home. I’ll

[021-012]send Ivar over with the cows in the morning.”

[021-013]Signa hesitated and looked perplexed. When her husband called her, she pinned

[021-014]her hat on resolutely. “I ta-ank I better do yust like he say,” she murmured in

[021-015]confusion.

[021-016]Alexandra and Marie accompanied Signa to the gate and saw the party set off,

[021-017]old Ivar driving ahead in the wagon and the bride and groom following on foot,

[021-018]each leading a cow. Emil burst into a laugh before they were out of hearing.

[021-019]“Those two will get on,” said Alexandra as they turned back to the house. “They

[021-020]are not going to take any chances. They will feel safer with those cows in

[021-021]their own stable. Marie, I am going to send for an old woman next. As soon as I

[021-022]get the girls broken in, I marry them off.”

[021-023]“I’ve no patience with Signa, marrying that grumpy fellow!” Marie declared. “I

[021-024]wanted her to marry that nice Smirka boy who worked for us last winter. I think

[021-025]she liked him, too.”

[021-026]“Yes, I think she did,” Alexandra assented, “but I suppose she was too much

[021-027]afraid of Nelse to marry any one else. Now that I think of it, most of my girls

[021-028]have married men they were afraid of. I believe there is a good deal of the cow

[021-029]in most Swedish girls. You high-strung Bohemian can’t understand us. We’re a

[021-030]terribly practical people, and I guess we think a cross man makes a good

[021-031]manager.”

[021-032]Marie shrugged her shoulders and turned to pin up a lock of hair that had

[021-033]fallen on her neck. Somehow Alexandra had irritated her of late. Everybody

[021-034]irritated her. She was tired of everybody. “I’m going home alone, Emil, so you

[021-035]needn’t get your hat,” she said as she wound her scarf quickly about her head.

[021-036]“Good-night, Alexandra,” she called back in a strained voice, running down the

[021-037]gravel walk.

[021-038]Emil followed with long strides until he overtook her. Then she began to walk

[021-039]slowly. It was a night of warm wind and faint starlight, and the fireflies were

[021-040]glimmering over the wheat.

[021-041]“Marie,” said Emil after they had walked for a while, “I wonder if you know how

[021-042]unhappy I am?”

[021-043]Marie did not answer him. Her head, in its white scarf, drooped forward a

[021-044]little.

[021-045]Emil kicked a clod from the path and went on:—

[021-046]“I wonder whether you are really shallow-hearted, like you seem? Sometimes I

[021-047]think one boy does just as well as another for you. It never seems to make much

[021-048]difference whether it is me or [[raul-marcel|Raoul Marcel]] or Jan Smirka. Are you really like

[021-049]that?”

[021-050]“Perhaps I am. What do you want me to do? Sit round and cry all day? When I’ve

[021-051]cried until I can’t cry any more, then—then I must do something else.”

[021-052]“Are you sorry for me?” he persisted.

[021-053]“No, I’m not. If I were big and free like you, I wouldn’t let anything make me

[021-054]unhappy. As old Napoleon Brunot said at the fair, I wouldn’t go lovering after

[021-055]no woman. I’d take the first train and go off and have all the fun there is.”

[021-056]“I tried that, but it didn’t do any good. Everything reminded me. The nicer the

[021-057]place was, the more I wanted you.” They had come to the stile and Emil pointed

[021-058]to it persuasively. “Sit down a moment, I want to ask you something.” Marie sat

[021-059]down on the top step and Emil drew nearer. “Would you tell me something that’s

[021-060]none of my business if you thought it would help me out? Well, then, tell me,*please*tell me, why you ran away with [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]]!”

[021-061]Marie drew back. “Because I was in love with him,” she said firmly.

[021-062]“Really?” he asked incredulously.

[021-063]“Yes, indeed. Very much in love with him. I think I was the one who suggested

[021-064]our running away. From the first it was more my fault than his.”

[021-065]Emil turned away his face.

[021-066]“And now,” Marie went on, “I’ve got to remember that. Frank is just the same

[021-067]now as he was then, only then I would see him as I wanted him to be. I would

[021-068]have my own way. And now I pay for it.”

[021-069]“You don’t do all the paying.”

[021-070]“That’s it. When one makes a mistake, there’s no telling where it will stop.

[021-071]But you can go away; you can leave all this behind you.”

[021-072]“Not everything. I can’t leave you behind. Will you go away with me, Marie?”

[021-073]Marie started up and stepped across the stile. “Emil! How wickedly you talk! I

[021-074]am not that kind of a girl, and you know it. But what am I going to do if you

[021-075]keep tormenting me like this!” she added plaintively.

[021-076]“Marie, I won’t bother you any more if you will tell me just one thing. Stop a

[021-077]minute and look at me. No, nobody can see us. Everybody’s asleep. That was only

[021-078]a firefly. Marie,*stop*and tell me!”

[021-079]Emil overtook her and catching her by the shoulders shook her gently, as if he

[021-080]were trying to awaken a sleepwalker.

[021-081]Marie hid her face on his arm. “Don’t ask me anything more. I don’t know

[021-082]anything except how miserable I am. And I thought it would be all right when

[021-083]you came back. Oh, Emil,” she clutched his sleeve and began to cry, “what am I

[021-084]to do if you don’t go away? I can’t go, and one of us must. Can’t you see?”

[021-085]Emil stood looking down at her, holding his shoulders stiff and stiffening the

[021-086]arm to which she clung. Her white dress looked gray in the darkness. She seemed

[021-087]like a troubled spirit, like some shadow out of the earth, clinging to him and

[021-088]entreating him to give her peace. Behind her the fireflies were weaving in and

[021-089]out over the wheat. He put his hand on her bent head. “On my honor, Marie, if

[021-090]you will say you love me, I will go away.”

[021-091]She lifted her face to his. “How could I help it? Didn’t you know?”

[021-092]Emil was the one who trembled, through all his frame. After he left Marie at

[021-093]her gate, he wandered about the fields all night, till morning put out the

[021-094]fireflies and the stars.
