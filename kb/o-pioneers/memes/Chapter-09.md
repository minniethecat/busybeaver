[009-001]Carl had changed, Alexandra felt, much less than one might have expected. He

[009-002]had not become a trim, self-satisfied city man. There was still something

[009-003]homely and wayward and definitely personal about him. Even his clothes, his

[009-004]Norfolk coat and his very high collars, were a little unconventional. He seemed

[009-005]to shrink into himself as he used to do; to hold himself away from things, as

[009-006]if he were afraid of being hurt. In short, he was more self-conscious than a

[009-007]man of thirty-five is expected to be. He looked older than his years and not

[009-008]very strong. His black hair, which still hung in a triangle over his pale

[009-009]forehead, was thin at the crown, and there were fine, relentless lines about

[009-010]his eyes. His back, with its high, sharp shoulders, looked like the back of an

[009-011]over-worked German professor off on his holiday. His face was intelligent,

[009-012]sensitive, unhappy.

[009-013]That evening after supper, Carl and Alexandra were sitting by the clump of

[009-014]castor beans in the middle of the flower garden. The gravel paths glittered in

[009-015]the moonlight, and below them the fields lay white and still.

[009-016]“Do you know, Alexandra,” he was saying, “I’ve been thinking how strangely

[009-017]things work out. I’ve been away engraving other men’s pictures, and you’ve

[009-018]stayed at home and made your own.” He pointed with his cigar toward the

[009-019]sleeping landscape. “How in the world have you done it? How have your neighbors

[009-020]done it?”

[009-021]“We hadn’t any of us much to do with it, Carl. The land did it. It had its

[009-022]little joke. It pretended to be poor because nobody knew how to work it right;

[009-023]and then, all at once, it worked itself. It woke up out of its sleep and

[009-024]stretched itself, and it was so big, so rich, that we suddenly found we were

[009-025]rich, just from sitting still. As for me, you remember when I began to buy

[009-026]land. For years after that I was always squeezing and borrowing until I was

[009-027]ashamed to show my face in the banks. And then, all at once, men began to come

[009-028]to me offering to lend me money—and I didn’t need it! Then I went ahead and

[009-029]built this house. I really built it for Emil. I want you to see Emil, Carl. He

[009-030]is so different from the rest of us!”

[009-031]“How different?”

[009-032]“Oh, you’ll see! I’m sure it was to have sons like Emil, and to give them a

[009-033]chance, that father left the old country. It’s curious, too; on the outside

[009-034]Emil is just like an American boy,—he graduated from the State University in

[009-035]June, you know,—but underneath he is more Swedish than any of us. Sometimes he

[009-036]is so like father that he frightens me; he is so violent in his feelings like

[009-037]that.”

[009-038]“Is he going to farm here with you?”

[009-039]“He shall do whatever he wants to,” Alexandra declared warmly. “He is going to

[009-040]have a chance, a whole chance; that’s what I’ve worked for. Sometimes he talks

[009-041]about studying law, and sometimes, just lately, he’s been talking about going

[009-042]out into the sand hills and taking up more land. He has his sad times, like

[009-043]father. But I hope he won’t do that. We have land enough, at last!” Alexandra

[009-044]laughed.

[009-045]“How about Lou and Oscar? They’ve done well, haven’t they?”

[009-046]“Yes, very well; but they are different, and now that they have farms of their

[009-047]own I do not see so much of them. We divided the land equally when Lou married.

[009-048]They have their own way of doing things, and they do not altogether like my

[009-049]way, I am afraid. Perhaps they think me too independent. But I have had to

[009-050]think for myself a good many years and am not likely to change. On the whole,

[009-051]though, we take as much comfort in each other as most brothers and sisters do.

[009-052]And I am very fond of Lou’s oldest daughter.”

[009-053]“I think I liked the old Lou and Oscar better, and they probably feel the same

[009-054]about me. I even, if you can keep a secret,”—Carl leaned forward and touched

[009-055]her arm, smiling,—“I even think I liked the old country better. This is all

[009-056]very splendid in its way, but there was something about this country when it

[009-057]was a wild old beast that has haunted me all these years. Now, when I come back

[009-058]to all this milk and honey, I feel like the old German song, ‘Wo bist du, wo

[009-059]bist du, mein geliebtest Land?’—Do you ever feel like that, I wonder?”

[009-060]“Yes, sometimes, when I think about father and mother and those who are gone;

[009-061]so many of our old neighbors.” Alexandra paused and looked up thoughtfully at

[009-062]the stars. “We can remember the graveyard when it was wild prairie, Carl, and

[009-063]now—”

[009-064]“And now the old story has begun to write itself over there,” said Carl softly.

[009-065]“Isn’t it queer: there are only two or three human stories, and they go on

[009-066]repeating themselves as fiercely as if they had never happened before; like the

[009-067]larks in this country, that have been singing the same five notes over for

[009-068]thousands of years.”

[009-069]“Oh, yes! The young people, they live so hard. And yet I sometimes envy them.

[009-070]There is my little neighbor, now; the people who bought your old place. I

[009-071]wouldn’t have sold it to any one else, but I was always fond of that girl. You

[009-072]must remember her, little Marie Tovesky, from [[omaha|Omaha]], who used to visit here?

[009-073]When she was eighteen she ran away from the convent school and got married,

[009-074]crazy child! She came out here a bride, with her father and husband. He had

[009-075]nothing, and the old man was willing to buy them a place and set them up. Your

[009-076]farm took her fancy, and I was glad to have her so near me. I’ve never been

[009-077]sorry, either. I even try to get along with Frank on her account.”

[009-078]“Is Frank her husband?”

[009-079]“Yes. He’s one of these wild fellows. Most Bohemians are good-natured, but

[009-080]Frank thinks we don’t appreciate him here, I guess. He’s jealous about

[009-081]everything, his farm and his horses and his pretty wife. Everybody likes her,

[009-082]just the same as when she was little. Sometimes I go up to the Catholic church

[009-083]with Emil, and it’s funny to see Marie standing there laughing and shaking

[009-084]hands with people, looking so excited and gay, with Frank sulking behind her as

[009-085]if he could eat everybody alive. Frank’s not a bad neighbor, but to get on with

[009-086]him you’ve got to make a fuss over him and act as if you thought he was a very

[009-087]important person all the time, and different from other people. I find it hard

[009-088]to keep that up from one year’s end to another.”

[009-089]“I shouldn’t think you’d be very successful at that kind of thing, Alexandra.”

[009-090]Carl seemed to find the idea amusing.

[009-091]“Well,” said Alexandra firmly, “I do the best I can, on Marie’s account. She

[009-092]has it hard enough, anyway. She’s too young and pretty for this sort of life.

[009-093]We’re all ever so much older and slower. But she’s the kind that won’t be

[009-094]downed easily. She’ll work all day and go to a Bohemian wedding and dance all

[009-095]night, and drive the hay wagon for a cross man next morning. I could stay by a

[009-096]job, but I never had the go in me that she has, when I was going my best. I’ll

[009-097]have to take you over to see her to-morrow.”

[009-098]Carl dropped the end of his cigar softly among the castor beans and sighed.

[009-099]“Yes, I suppose I must see the old place. I’m cowardly about things that remind

[009-100]me of myself. It took courage to come at all, Alexandra. I wouldn’t have, if I

[009-101]hadn’t wanted to see you very, very much.”

[009-102]Alexandra looked at him with her calm, deliberate eyes. “Why do you dread

[009-103]things like that, Carl?” she asked earnestly. “Why are you dissatisfied with

[009-104]yourself?”

[009-105]Her visitor winced. “How direct you are, Alexandra! Just like you used to be.

[009-106]Do I give myself away so quickly? Well, you see, for one thing, there’s nothing

[009-107]to look forward to in my profession. Wood-engraving is the only thing I care

[009-108]about, and that had gone out before I began. Everything’s cheap metal work

[009-109]nowadays, touching up miserable photographs, forcing up poor drawings, and

[009-110]spoiling good ones. I’m absolutely sick of it all.” Carl frowned. “Alexandra,

[009-111]all the way out from New York I’ve been planning how I could deceive you and

[009-112]make you think me a very enviable fellow, and here I am telling you the truth

[009-113]the first night. I waste a lot of time pretending to people, and the joke of it

[009-114]is, I don’t think I ever deceive any one. There are too many of my kind; people

[009-115]know us on sight.”

[009-116]Carl paused. Alexandra pushed her hair back from her brow with a puzzled,

[009-117]thoughtful gesture. “You see,” he went on calmly, “measured by your standards

[009-118]here, I’m a failure. I couldn’t buy even one of your cornfields. I’ve enjoyed a

[009-119]great many things, but I’ve got nothing to show for it all.”

[009-120]“But you show for it yourself, Carl. I’d rather have had your freedom than my

[009-121]land.”

[009-122]Carl shook his head mournfully. “Freedom so often means that one isn’t needed

[009-123]anywhere. Here you are an individual, you have a background of your own, you

[009-124]would be missed. But off there in the cities there are thousands of rolling

[009-125]stones like me. We are all alike; we have no ties, we know nobody, we own

[009-126]nothing. When one of us dies, they scarcely know where to bury him. Our

[009-127]landlady and the delicatessen man are our mourners, and we leave nothing behind

[009-128]us but a frock-coat and a fiddle, or an easel, or a typewriter, or whatever

[009-129]tool we got our living by. All we have ever managed to do is to pay our rent,

[009-130]the exorbitant rent that one has to pay for a few square feet of space near the

[009-131]heart of things. We have no house, no place, no people of our own. We live in

[009-132]the streets, in the parks, in the theatres. We sit in restaurants and concert

[009-133]halls and look about at the hundreds of our own kind and shudder.”

[009-134]Alexandra was silent. She sat looking at the silver spot the moon made on the

[009-135]surface of the pond down in the pasture. He knew that she understood what he

[009-136]meant. At last she said slowly, “And yet I would rather have Emil grow up like

[009-137]that than like his two brothers. We pay a high rent, too, though we pay

[009-138]differently. We grow hard and heavy here. We don’t move lightly and easily as

[009-139]you do, and our minds get stiff. If the world were no wider than my cornfields,

[009-140]if there were not something beside this, I wouldn’t feel that it was much worth

[009-141]while to work. No, I would rather have Emil like you than like them. I felt

[009-142]that as soon as you came.”

[009-143]“I wonder why you feel like that?” Carl mused.

[009-144]“I don’t know. Perhaps I am like Carrie Jensen, the sister of one of my hired

[009-145]men. She had never been out of the cornfields, and a few years ago she got

[009-146]despondent and said life was just the same thing over and over, and she didn’t

[009-147]see the use of it. After she had tried to kill herself once or twice, her folks

[009-148]got worried and sent her over to Iowa to visit some relations. Ever since she’s

[009-149]come back she’s been perfectly cheerful, and she says she’s contented to live

[009-150]and work in a world that’s so big and interesting. She said that anything as

[009-151]big as the bridges over the Platte and the Missouri reconciled her. And it’s

[009-152]what goes on in the world that reconciles me.”
