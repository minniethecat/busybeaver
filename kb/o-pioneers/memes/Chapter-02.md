[002-001]On one of the ridges of that wintry waste stood the low log house in which John

[002-002]Bergson was dying. The Bergson homestead was easier to find than many another,

[002-003]because it overlooked [[norway-creek|Norway Creek]], a shallow, muddy stream that sometimes

[002-004]flowed, and sometimes stood still, at the bottom of a winding ravine with

[002-005]steep, shelving sides overgrown with brush and cottonwoods and dwarf ash. This

[002-006]creek gave a sort of identity to the farms that bordered upon it. Of all the

[002-007]bewildering things about a new country, the absence of human landmarks is one

[002-008]of the most depressing and disheartening. The houses on the Divide were small

[002-009]and were usually tucked away in low places; you did not see them until you came

[002-010]directly upon them. Most of them were built of the sod itself, and were only

[002-011]the unescapable ground in another form. The roads were but faint tracks in the

[002-012]grass, and the fields were scarcely noticeable. The record of the plow was

[002-013]insignificant, like the feeble scratches on stone left by prehistoric races, so

[002-014]indeterminate that they may, after all, be only the markings of glaciers, and

[002-015]not a record of human strivings.

[002-016]In eleven long years [[john-bergson|John Bergson]] had made but little impression upon the wild

[002-017]land he had come to tame. It was still a wild thing that had its ugly moods;

[002-018]and no one knew when they were likely to come, or why. Mischance hung over it.

[002-019]Its Genius was unfriendly to man. The sick man was feeling this as he lay

[002-020]looking out of the window, after the doctor had left him, on the day following

[002-021]Alexandra’s trip to town. There it lay outside his door, the same land, the

[002-022]same lead-colored miles. He knew every ridge and draw and gully between him and

[002-023]the horizon. To the south, his plowed fields; to the east, the sod stables, the

[002-024]cattle corral, the pond,—and then the grass.

[002-025]Bergson went over in his mind the things that had held him back. One winter his

[002-026]cattle had perished in a blizzard. The next summer one of his plow horses broke

[002-027]its leg in a prairiedog hole and had to be shot. Another summer he lost his

[002-028]hogs from cholera, and a valuable stallion died from a rattlesnake bite. Time

[002-029]and again his crops had failed. He had lost two children, boys, that came

[002-030]between Lou and Emil, and there had been the cost of sickness and death. Now,

[002-031]when he had at last struggled out of debt, he was going to die himself. He was

[002-032]only forty-six, and had, of course, counted upon more time.

[002-033]Bergson had spent his first five years on the Divide getting into debt, and the

[002-034]last six getting out. He had paid off his mortgages and had ended pretty much

[002-035]where he began, with the land. He owned exactly six hundred and forty acres of

[002-036]what stretched outside his door; his own original homestead and timber claim,

[002-037]making three hundred and twenty acres, and the half-section adjoining, the

[002-038]homestead of a younger brother who had given up the fight, gone back to Chicago

[002-039]to work in a fancy bakery and distinguish himself in a Swedish athletic club.

[002-040]So far John had not attempted to cultivate the second half-section, but used it

[002-041]for pasture land, and one of his sons rode herd there in open weather.

[002-042]John Bergson had the Old-World belief that land, in itself, is desirable. But

[002-043]this land was an enigma. It was like a horse that no one knows how to break to

[002-044]harness, that runs wild and kicks things to pieces. He had an idea that no one

[002-045]understood how to farm it properly, and this he often discussed with Alexandra.

[002-046]Their neighbors, certainly, knew even less about farming than he did. Many of

[002-047]them had never worked on a farm until they took up their homesteads. They had

[002-048]been*handwerkers*at home; tailors, locksmiths, joiners, cigar-makers,

[002-049]etc. Bergson himself had worked in a shipyard.

[002-050]For weeks, John Bergson had been thinking about these things. His bed stood in

[002-051]the sitting-room, next to the kitchen. Through the day, while the baking and

[002-052]washing and ironing were going on, the father lay and looked up at the roof

[002-053]beams that he himself had hewn, or out at the cattle in the corral. He counted

[002-054]the cattle over and over. It diverted him to speculate as to how much weight

[002-055]each of the steers would probably put on by spring. He often called his

[002-056]daughter in to talk to her about this. Before Alexandra was twelve years old

[002-057]she had begun to be a help to him, and as she grew older he had come to depend

[002-058]more and more upon her resourcefulness and good judgment. His boys were willing

[002-059]enough to work, but when he talked with them they usually irritated him. It was

[002-060]Alexandra who read the papers and followed the markets, and who learned by the

[002-061]mistakes of their neighbors. It was Alexandra who could always tell about what

[002-062]it had cost to fatten each steer, and who could guess the weight of a hog

[002-063]before it went on the scales closer than John Bergson himself. Lou and Oscar

[002-064]were industrious, but he could never teach them to use their heads about their

[002-065]work.

[002-066]Alexandra, her father often said to himself, was like her grandfather; which

[002-067]was his way of saying that she was intelligent. John Bergson’s father had been

[002-068]a shipbuilder, a man of considerable force and of some fortune. Late in life he

[002-069]married a second time, a Stockholm woman of questionable character, much

[002-070]younger than he, who goaded him into every sort of extravagance. On the

[002-071]shipbuilder’s part, this marriage was an infatuation, the despairing folly of a

[002-072]powerful man who cannot bear to grow old. In a few years his unprincipled wife

[002-073]warped the probity of a lifetime. He speculated, lost his own fortune and funds

[002-074]entrusted to him by poor seafaring men, and died disgraced, leaving his

[002-075]children nothing. But when all was said, he had come up from the sea himself,

[002-076]had built up a proud little business with no capital but his own skill and

[002-077]foresight, and had proved himself a man. In his daughter, John Bergson

[002-078]recognized the strength of will, and the simple direct way of thinking things

[002-079]out, that had characterized his father in his better days. He would much

[002-080]rather, of course, have seen this likeness in one of his sons, but it was not a

[002-081]question of choice. As he lay there day after day he had to accept the

[002-082]situation as it was, and to be thankful that there was one among his children

[002-083]to whom he could entrust the future of his family and the possibilities of his

[002-084]hard-won land.

[002-085]The winter twilight was fading. The sick man heard his wife strike a match in

[002-086]the kitchen, and the light of a lamp glimmered through the cracks of the door.

[002-087]It seemed like a light shining far away. He turned painfully in his bed and

[002-088]looked at his white hands, with all the work gone out of them. He was ready to

[002-089]give up, he felt. He did not know how it had come about, but he was quite

[002-090]willing to go deep under his fields and rest, where the plow could not find

[002-091]him. He was tired of making mistakes. He was content to leave the tangle to

[002-092]other hands; he thought of his Alexandra’s strong ones.

[002-093]“*Dotter*,” he called feebly, “*dotter!*” He heard her quick step and

[002-094]saw her tall figure appear in the doorway, with the light of the lamp behind

[002-095]her. He felt her youth and strength, how easily she moved and stooped and

[002-096]lifted. But he would not have had it again if he could, not he! He knew the end

[002-097]too well to wish to begin again. He knew where it all went to, what it all

[002-098]became.

[002-099]His daughter came and lifted him up on his pillows. She called him by an old

[002-100]Swedish name that she used to call him when she was little and took his dinner

[002-101]to him in the shipyard.

[002-102]“Tell the boys to come here, daughter. I want to speak to them.”

[002-103]“They are feeding the horses, father. They have just come back from the Blue.

[002-104]Shall I call them?”

[002-105]He sighed. “No, no. Wait until they come in. Alexandra, you will have to do the

[002-106]best you can for your brothers. Everything will come on you.”

[002-107]“I will do all I can, father.”

[002-108]“Don’t let them get discouraged and go off like Uncle Otto. I want them to keep

[002-109]the land.”

[002-110]“We will, father. We will never lose the land.”

[002-111]There was a sound of heavy feet in the kitchen. Alexandra went to the door and

[002-112]beckoned to her brothers, two strapping boys of seventeen and nineteen. They

[002-113]came in and stood at the foot of the bed. Their father looked at them

[002-114]searchingly, though it was too dark to see their faces; they were just the same

[002-115]boys, he told himself, he had not been mistaken in them. The square head and

[002-116]heavy shoulders belonged to Oscar, the elder. The younger boy was quicker, but

[002-117]vacillating.

[002-118]“Boys,” said the father wearily, “I want you to keep the land together and to

[002-119]be guided by your sister. I have talked to her since I have been sick, and she

[002-120]knows all my wishes. I want no quarrels among my children, and so long as there

[002-121]is one house there must be one head. Alexandra is the oldest, and she knows my

[002-122]wishes. She will do the best she can. If she makes mistakes, she will not make

[002-123]so many as I have made. When you marry, and want a house of your own, the land

[002-124]will be divided fairly, according to the courts. But for the next few years you

[002-125]will have it hard, and you must all keep together. Alexandra will manage the

[002-126]best she can.”

[002-127]Oscar, who was usually the last to speak, replied because he was the older,

[002-128]“Yes, father. It would be so anyway, without your speaking. We will all work

[002-129]the place together.”

[002-130]“And you will be guided by your sister, boys, and be good brothers to her, and

[002-131]good sons to your mother? That is good. And Alexandra must not work in the

[002-132]fields any more. There is no necessity now. Hire a man when you need help. She

[002-133]can make much more with her eggs and butter than the wages of a man. It was one

[002-134]of my mistakes that I did not find that out sooner. Try to break a little more

[002-135]land every year; sod corn is good for fodder. Keep turning the land, and always

[002-136]put up more hay than you need. Don’t grudge your mother a little time for

[002-137]plowing her garden and setting out fruit trees, even if it comes in a busy

[002-138]season. She has been a good mother to you, and she has always missed the old

[002-139]country.”

[002-140]When they went back to the kitchen the boys sat down silently at the table.

[002-141]Throughout the meal they looked down at their plates and did not lift their red

[002-142]eyes. They did not eat much, although they had been working in the cold all

[002-143]day, and there was a rabbit stewed in gravy for supper, and prune pies.

[002-144]John Bergson had married beneath him, but he had married a good housewife. Mrs.

[002-145]Bergson was a fair-skinned, corpulent woman, heavy and placid like her son,

[002-146]Oscar, but there was something comfortable about her; perhaps it was her own

[002-147]love of comfort. For eleven years she had worthily striven to maintain some

[002-148]semblance of household order amid conditions that made order very difficult.

[002-149]Habit was very strong with Mrs. Bergson, and her unremitting efforts to repeat

[002-150]the routine of her old life among new surroundings had done a great deal to

[002-151]keep the family from disintegrating morally and getting careless in their ways.

[002-152]The Bergsons had a log house, for instance, only because Mrs. Bergson would not

[002-153]live in a sod house. She missed the fish diet of her own country, and twice

[002-154]every summer she sent the boys to the river, twenty miles to the southward, to

[002-155]fish for channel cat. When the children were little she used to load them all

[002-156]into the wagon, the baby in its crib, and go fishing herself.

[002-157]Alexandra often said that if her mother were cast upon a desert island, she

[002-158]would thank God for her deliverance, make a garden, and find something to

[002-159]preserve. Preserving was almost a mania with Mrs. Bergson. Stout as she was,

[002-160]she roamed the scrubby banks of Norway Creek looking for fox grapes and goose

[002-161]plums, like a wild creature in search of prey. She made a yellow jam of the

[002-162]insipid ground-cherries that grew on the prairie, flavoring it with lemon peel;

[002-163]and she made a sticky dark conserve of garden tomatoes. She had experimented

[002-164]even with the rank buffalo-pea, and she could not see a fine bronze cluster of

[002-165]them without shaking her head and murmuring, “What a pity!” When there was

[002-166]nothing more to preserve, she began to pickle. The amount of sugar she used in

[002-167]these processes was sometimes a serious drain upon the family resources. She

[002-168]was a good mother, but she was glad when her children were old enough not to be

[002-169]in her way in the kitchen. She had never quite forgiven John Bergson for

[002-170]bringing her to the end of the earth; but, now that she was there, she wanted

[002-171]to be let alone to reconstruct her old life in so far as that was possible. She

[002-172]could still take some comfort in the world if she had bacon in the cave, glass

[002-173]jars on the shelves, and sheets in the press. She disapproved of all her

[002-174]neighbors because of their slovenly housekeeping, and the women thought her

[002-175]very proud. Once when Mrs. Bergson, on her way to Norway Creek, stopped to see

[002-176]old [[mrs-lee|Mrs. Lee]], the old woman hid in the haymow “for fear Mis’ Bergson would

[002-177]catch her barefoot.”
