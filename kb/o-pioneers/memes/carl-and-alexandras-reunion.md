## Overview

After learning of Alexandra Bergson's tragedy from a newspaper in San Francisco, Carl Linstrum rushes back to the Divide by the fastest boats and trains available. He meets Alexandra at the Hanover station and they spend the afternoon walking across the familiar fields, talking through the events of the past year. By the end of their conversation, they decide to marry, bringing the novel to its hopeful, life-affirming conclusion.

## Background

Carl Linstrum had left the Divide for the Klondike gold rush a year earlier, having been driven away by the hostility of Alexandra's brothers Lou and Oscar, who resented his closeness to their sister (030-016). While in Alaska, Carl established a promising mining venture with an honest partner. When he picked up a four-week-old San Francisco newspaper in a saloon and read about Frank Shabata's trial, he immediately set out to return to Alexandra, traveling day and night (030-020).

Meanwhile, Alexandra had just returned from Lincoln, where she had visited Frank Shabata in prison and extended her forgiveness. The emotional weight of that visit was still fresh as she met Carl at the station.

## Account

Carl met Alexandra at the Hanover station early in the morning. After reaching home, Alexandra changed out of her black traveling suit into a white dress—a symbolic shedding of mourning. They visited Mrs. Hiller briefly, then spent the rest of the afternoon walking in the sunny fields (030-001).

As they talked, Alexandra asked Carl about his business in the Klondike, and he assured her that his honest partner could manage things and that he planned to stay through the winter (030-028). Carl then asked Alexandra directly: "You do need me now, Alexandra?" She put her hand on his arm and replied, "I needed you terribly when it happened, Carl. I cried for you at night" (030-043).

They discussed the orchard tragedy, with Carl urging Alexandra not to be too hard on Emil and Marie, recalling how he had seen them together at the pond—young, charming, and full of grace (030-064). Walking past the Shabatas' empty house and the pasture pond, they talked through all that had happened.

## Aftermath

Carl and Alexandra's reunion transforms the novel's emotional landscape from tragedy to renewal. Their decision to marry—to face the future together on the land Alexandra has spent her life building—affirms the novel's central theme of endurance and regeneration. The final scene, in which they walk across the fields under the wide Nebraska sky, echoes the novel's opening and closes the narrative circle with a sense of hard-won peace.
