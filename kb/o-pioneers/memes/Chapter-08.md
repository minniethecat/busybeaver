[008-001]Alexandra was to hear more of [[ivar|Ivar]]’s case, however. On Sunday her married

[008-002]brothers came to dinner. She had asked them for that day because Emil, who

[008-003]hated family parties, would be absent, dancing at Amédée Chevalier’s wedding,

[008-004]up in the French country. The table was set for company in the dining-room,

[008-005]where highly varnished wood and colored glass and useless pieces of china were

[008-006]conspicuous enough to satisfy the standards of the new prosperity. Alexandra

[008-007]had put herself into the hands of the [[hanover|Hanover]] furniture dealer, and he had

[008-008]conscientiously done his best to make her dining-room look like his display

[008-009]window. She said frankly that she knew nothing about such things, and she was

[008-010]willing to be governed by the general conviction that the more useless and

[008-011]utterly unusable objects were, the greater their virtue as ornament. That

[008-012]seemed reasonable enough. Since she liked plain things herself, it was all the

[008-013]more necessary to have jars and punchbowls and candlesticks in the company

[008-014]rooms for people who did appreciate them. Her guests liked to see about them

[008-015]these reassuring emblems of prosperity.

[008-016]The family party was complete except for Emil, and Oscar’s wife who, in the

[008-017]country phrase, “was not going anywhere just now.” Oscar sat at the foot of the

[008-018]table and his four tow-headed little boys, aged from twelve to five, were

[008-019]ranged at one side. Neither Oscar nor Lou has changed much; they have simply,

[008-020]as Alexandra said of them long ago, grown to be more and more like themselves.

[008-021]Lou now looks the older of the two; his face is thin and shrewd and wrinkled

[008-022]about the eyes, while Oscar’s is thick and dull. For all his dullness, however,

[008-023]Oscar makes more money than his brother, which adds to Lou’s sharpness and

[008-024]uneasiness and tempts him to make a show. The trouble with Lou is that he is

[008-025]tricky, and his neighbors have found out that, as Ivar says, he has not a fox’s

[008-026]face for nothing. Politics being the natural field for such talents, he

[008-027]neglects his farm to attend conventions and to run for county offices.

[008-028]Lou’s wife, formerly [[annie-lee|Annie Lee]], has grown to look curiously like her husband.

[008-029]Her face has become longer, sharper, more aggressive. She wears her yellow hair

[008-030]in a high pompadour, and is bedecked with rings and chains and “beauty pins.”

[008-031]Her tight, high-heeled shoes give her an awkward walk, and she is always more

[008-032]or less preoccupied with her clothes. As she sat at the table, she kept telling

[008-033]her youngest daughter to “be careful now, and not drop anything on mother.”

[008-034]The conversation at the table was all in English. Oscar’s wife, from the

[008-035]malaria district of Missouri, was ashamed of marrying a foreigner, and his boys

[008-036]do not understand a word of Swedish. Annie and Lou sometimes speak Swedish at

[008-037]home, but Annie is almost as much afraid of being “caught” at it as ever her

[008-038]mother was of being caught barefoot. Oscar still has a thick accent, but Lou

[008-039]speaks like anybody from Iowa.

[008-040]“When I was in Hastings to attend the convention,” he was saying, “I saw the

[008-041]superintendent of the asylum, and I was telling him about Ivar’s symptoms. He

[008-042]says Ivar’s case is one of the most dangerous kind, and it’s a wonder he hasn’t

[008-043]done something violent before this.”

[008-044]Alexandra laughed good-humoredly. “Oh, nonsense, Lou! The doctors would have us

[008-045]all crazy if they could. Ivar’s queer, certainly, but he has more sense than

[008-046]half the hands I hire.”

[008-047]Lou flew at his fried chicken. “Oh, I guess the doctor knows his business,

[008-048]Alexandra. He was very much surprised when I told him how you’d put up with

[008-049]Ivar. He says he’s likely to set fire to the barn any night, or to take after

[008-050]you and the girls with an axe.”

[008-051]Little [[signa|Signa]], who was waiting on the table, giggled and fled to the kitchen.

[008-052]Alexandra’s eyes twinkled. “That was too much for Signa, Lou. We all know that

[008-053]Ivar’s perfectly harmless. The girls would as soon expect me to chase them with

[008-054]an axe.”

[008-055]Lou flushed and signaled to his wife. “All the same, the neighbors will be

[008-056]having a say about it before long. He may burn anybody’s barn. It’s only

[008-057]necessary for one property-owner in the township to make complaint, and he’ll

[008-058]be taken up by force. You’d better send him yourself and not have any hard

[008-059]feelings.”

[008-060]Alexandra helped one of her little nephews to gravy. “Well, Lou, if any of the

[008-061]neighbors try that, I’ll have myself appointed Ivar’s guardian and take the

[008-062]case to court, that’s all. I am perfectly satisfied with him.”

[008-063]“Pass the preserves, Lou,” said Annie in a warning tone. She had reasons for

[008-064]not wishing her husband to cross Alexandra too openly. “But don’t you sort of

[008-065]hate to have people see him around here, Alexandra?” she went on with

[008-066]persuasive smoothness. “He IS a disgraceful object, and you’re fixed up so nice

[008-067]now. It sort of makes people distant with you, when they never know when

[008-068]they’ll hear him scratching about. My girls are afraid as death of him, aren’t

[008-069]you, [[milly|Milly]], dear?”

[008-070]Milly was fifteen, fat and jolly and pompadoured, with a creamy complexion,

[008-071]square white teeth, and a short upper lip. She looked like her grandmother

[008-072]Bergson, and had her comfortable and comfort-loving nature. She grinned at her

[008-073]aunt, with whom she was a great deal more at ease than she was with her mother.

[008-074]Alexandra winked a reply.

[008-075]“Milly needn’t be afraid of Ivar. She’s an especial favorite of his. In my

[008-076]opinion Ivar has just as much right to his own way of dressing and thinking as

[008-077]we have. But I’ll see that he doesn’t bother other people. I’ll keep him at

[008-078]home, so don’t trouble any more about him, Lou. I’ve been wanting to ask you

[008-079]about your new bathtub. How does it work?”

[008-080]Annie came to the fore to give Lou time to recover himself. “Oh, it works

[008-081]something grand! I can’t keep him out of it. He washes himself all over three

[008-082]times a week now, and uses all the hot water. I think it’s weakening to stay in

[008-083]as long as he does. You ought to have one, Alexandra.”

[008-084]“I’m thinking of it. I might have one put in the barn for Ivar, if it will ease

[008-085]people’s minds. But before I get a bathtub, I’m going to get a piano for

[008-086]Milly.”

[008-087]Oscar, at the end of the table, looked up from his plate. “What does Milly want

[008-088]of a pianny? What’s the matter with her organ? She can make some use of that,

[008-089]and play in church.”

[008-090]Annie looked flustered. She had begged Alexandra not to say anything about this

[008-091]plan before Oscar, who was apt to be jealous of what his sister did for Lou’s

[008-092]children. Alexandra did not get on with Oscar’s wife at all. “Milly can play in

[008-093]church just the same, and she’ll still play on the organ. But practising on it

[008-094]so much spoils her touch. Her teacher says so,” Annie brought out with spirit.

[008-095]Oscar rolled his eyes. “Well, Milly must have got on pretty good if she’s got

[008-096]past the organ. I know plenty of grown folks that ain’t,” he said bluntly.

[008-097]Annie threw up her chin. “She has got on good, and she’s going to play for her

[008-098]commencement when she graduates in town next year.”

[008-099]“Yes,” said Alexandra firmly, “I think Milly deserves a piano. All the girls

[008-100]around here have been taking lessons for years, but Milly is the only one of

[008-101]them who can ever play anything when you ask her. I’ll tell you when I first

[008-102]thought I would like to give you a piano, Milly, and that was when you learned

[008-103]that book of old Swedish songs that your grandfather used to sing. He had a

[008-104]sweet tenor voice, and when he was a young man he loved to sing. I can remember

[008-105]hearing him singing with the sailors down in the shipyard, when I was no bigger

[008-106]than Stella here,” pointing to Annie’s younger daughter.

[008-107]Milly and Stella both looked through the door into the sitting-room, where a

[008-108]crayon portrait of [[john-bergson|John Bergson]] hung on the wall. Alexandra had had it made

[008-109]from a little photograph, taken for his friends just before he left Sweden; a

[008-110]slender man of thirty-five, with soft hair curling about his high forehead, a

[008-111]drooping mustache, and wondering, sad eyes that looked forward into the

[008-112]distance, as if they already beheld the New World.

[008-113]After dinner Lou and Oscar went to the orchard to pick cherries—they had

[008-114]neither of them had the patience to grow an orchard of their own—and Annie went

[008-115]down to gossip with Alexandra’s kitchen girls while they washed the dishes. She

[008-116]could always find out more about Alexandra’s domestic economy from the

[008-117]prattling maids than from Alexandra herself, and what she discovered she used

[008-118]to her own advantage with Lou. On the Divide, farmers’ daughters no longer went

[008-119]out into service, so Alexandra got her girls from Sweden, by paying their fare

[008-120]over. They stayed with her until they married, and were replaced by sisters or

[008-121]cousins from the old country.

[008-122]Alexandra took her three nieces into the flower garden. She was fond of the

[008-123]little girls, especially of Milly, who came to spend a week with her aunt now

[008-124]and then, and read aloud to her from the old books about the house, or listened

[008-125]to stories about the early days on the Divide. While they were walking among

[008-126]the flower beds, a buggy drove up the hill and stopped in front of the gate. A

[008-127]man got out and stood talking to the driver. The little girls were delighted at

[008-128]the advent of a stranger, some one from very far away, they knew by his

[008-129]clothes, his gloves, and the sharp, pointed cut of his dark beard. The girls

[008-130]fell behind their aunt and peeped out at him from among the castor beans. The

[008-131]stranger came up to the gate and stood holding his hat in his hand, smiling,

[008-132]while Alexandra advanced slowly to meet him. As she approached he spoke in a

[008-133]low, pleasant voice.

[008-134]“Don’t you know me, Alexandra? I would have known you, anywhere.”

[008-135]Alexandra shaded her eyes with her hand. Suddenly she took a quick step

[008-136]forward. “Can it be!” she exclaimed with feeling; “can it be that it is Carl

[008-137]Linstrum? Why, Carl, it is!” She threw out both her hands and caught his across

[008-138]the gate. “Sadie, Milly, run tell your father and Uncle Oscar that our old

[008-139]friend [[carl-linstrum|Carl Linstrum]] is here. Be quick! Why, Carl, how did it happen? I can’t

[008-140]believe this!” Alexandra shook the tears from her eyes and laughed.

[008-141]The stranger nodded to his driver, dropped his suitcase inside the fence, and

[008-142]opened the gate. “Then you are glad to see me, and you can put me up overnight?

[008-143]I couldn’t go through this country without stopping off to have a look at you.

[008-144]How little you have changed! Do you know, I was sure it would be like that. You

[008-145]simply couldn’t be different. How fine you are!” He stepped back and looked at

[008-146]her admiringly.

[008-147]Alexandra blushed and laughed again. “But you yourself, Carl—with that

[008-148]beard—how could I have known you? You went away a little boy.” She reached for

[008-149]his suitcase and when he intercepted her she threw up her hands. “You see, I

[008-150]give myself away. I have only women come to visit me, and I do not know how to

[008-151]behave. Where is your trunk?”

[008-152]“It’s in Hanover. I can stay only a few days. I am on my way to the coast.”

[008-153]They started up the path. “A few days? After all these years!” Alexandra shook

[008-154]her finger at him. “See this, you have walked into a trap. You do not get away

[008-155]so easy.” She put her hand affectionately on his shoulder. “You owe me a visit

[008-156]for the sake of old times. Why must you go to the coast at all?”

[008-157]“Oh, I must! I am a fortune hunter. From Seattle I go on to Alaska.”

[008-158]“Alaska?” She looked at him in astonishment. “Are you going to paint the

[008-159]Indians?”

[008-160]“Paint?” the young man frowned. “Oh! I’m not a painter, Alexandra. I’m an

[008-161]engraver. I have nothing to do with painting.”

[008-162]“But on my parlor wall I have the paintings—”

[008-163]He interrupted nervously. “Oh, water-color sketches—done for amusement. I sent

[008-164]them to remind you of me, not because they were good. What a wonderful place

[008-165]you have made of this, Alexandra.” He turned and looked back at the wide,

[008-166]map-like prospect of field and hedge and pasture. “I would never have believed

[008-167]it could be done. I’m disappointed in my own eye, in my imagination.”

[008-168]At this moment Lou and Oscar came up the hill from the orchard. They did not

[008-169]quicken their pace when they saw Carl; indeed, they did not openly look in his

[008-170]direction. They advanced distrustfully, and as if they wished the distance were

[008-171]longer.

[008-172]Alexandra beckoned to them. “They think I am trying to fool them. Come, boys,

[008-173]it’s Carl Linstrum, our old Carl!”

[008-174]Lou gave the visitor a quick, sidelong glance and thrust out his hand. “Glad to

[008-175]see you.”

[008-176]Oscar followed with “How d’ do.” Carl could not tell whether their offishness

[008-177]came from unfriendliness or from embarrassment. He and Alexandra led the way to

[008-178]the porch.

[008-179]“Carl,” Alexandra explained, “is on his way to Seattle. He is going to Alaska.”

[008-180]Oscar studied the visitor’s yellow shoes. “Got business there?” he asked.

[008-181]Carl laughed. “Yes, very pressing business. I’m going there to get rich.

[008-182]Engraving’s a very interesting profession, but a man never makes any money at

[008-183]it. So I’m going to try the goldfields.”

[008-184]Alexandra felt that this was a tactful speech, and Lou looked up with some

[008-185]interest. “Ever done anything in that line before?”

[008-186]“No, but I’m going to join a friend of mine who went out from New York and has

[008-187]done well. He has offered to break me in.”

[008-188]“Turrible cold winters, there, I hear,” remarked Oscar. “I thought people went

[008-189]up there in the spring.”

[008-190]“They do. But my friend is going to spend the winter in Seattle and I am to

[008-191]stay with him there and learn something about prospecting before we start north

[008-192]next year.”

[008-193]Lou looked skeptical. “Let’s see, how long have you been away from here?”

[008-194]“Sixteen years. You ought to remember that, Lou, for you were married just

[008-195]after we went away.”

[008-196]“Going to stay with us some time?” Oscar asked.

[008-197]“A few days, if Alexandra can keep me.”

[008-198]“I expect you’ll be wanting to see your old place,” Lou observed more

[008-199]cordially. “You won’t hardly know it. But there’s a few chunks of your old sod

[008-200]house left. Alexandra wouldn’t never let [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]] plough over it.”

[008-201]Annie Lee, who, ever since the visitor was announced, had been touching up her

[008-202]hair and settling her lace and wishing she had worn another dress, now emerged

[008-203]with her three daughters and introduced them. She was greatly impressed by

[008-204]Carl’s urban appearance, and in her excitement talked very loud and threw her

[008-205]head about. “And you ain’t married yet? At your age, now! Think of that! You’ll

[008-206]have to wait for Milly. Yes, we’ve got a boy, too. The youngest. He’s at home

[008-207]with his grandma. You must come over to see mother and hear Milly play. She’s

[008-208]the musician of the family. She does pyrography, too. That’s burnt wood, you

[008-209]know. You wouldn’t believe what she can do with her poker. Yes, she goes to

[008-210]school in town, and she is the youngest in her class by two years.”

[008-211]Milly looked uncomfortable and Carl took her hand again. He liked her creamy

[008-212]skin and happy, innocent eyes, and he could see that her mother’s way of

[008-213]talking distressed her. “I’m sure she’s a clever little girl,” he murmured,

[008-214]looking at her thoughtfully. “Let me see—Ah, it’s your mother that she looks

[008-215]like, Alexandra. Mrs. Bergson must have looked just like this when she was a

[008-216]little girl. Does Milly run about over the country as you and Alexandra used

[008-217]to, Annie?”

[008-218]Milly’s mother protested. “Oh, my, no! Things has changed since we was girls.

[008-219]Milly has it very different. We are going to rent the place and move into town

[008-220]as soon as the girls are old enough to go out into company. A good many are

[008-221]doing that here now. Lou is going into business.”

[008-222]Lou grinned. “That’s what she says. You better go get your things on. Ivar’s

[008-223]hitching up,” he added, turning to Annie.

[008-224]Young farmers seldom address their wives by name. It is always “you,” or “she.”

[008-225]Having got his wife out of the way, Lou sat down on the step and began to

[008-226]whittle. “Well, what do folks in New York think of William Jennings Bryan?” Lou

[008-227]began to bluster, as he always did when he talked politics. “We gave Wall

[008-228]Street a scare in ninety-six, all right, and we’re fixing another to hand them.

[008-229]Silver wasn’t the only issue,” he nodded mysteriously. “There’s a good many

[008-230]things got to be changed. The West is going to make itself heard.”

[008-231]Carl laughed. “But, surely, it did do that, if nothing else.”

[008-232]Lou’s thin face reddened up to the roots of his bristly hair. “Oh, we’ve only

[008-233]begun. We’re waking up to a sense of our responsibilities, out here, and we

[008-234]ain’t afraid, neither. You fellows back there must be a tame lot. If you had

[008-235]any nerve you’d get together and march down to Wall Street and blow it up.

[008-236]Dynamite it, I mean,” with a threatening nod.

[008-237]He was so much in earnest that Carl scarcely knew how to answer him. “That

[008-238]would be a waste of powder. The same business would go on in another street.

[008-239]The street doesn’t matter. But what have you fellows out here got to kick

[008-240]about? You have the only safe place there is. Morgan himself couldn’t touch

[008-241]you. One only has to drive through this country to see that you’re all as rich

[008-242]as barons.”

[008-243]“We have a good deal more to say than we had when we were poor,” said Lou

[008-244]threateningly. “We’re getting on to a whole lot of things.”

[008-245]As Ivar drove a double carriage up to the gate, Annie came out in a hat that

[008-246]looked like the model of a battleship. Carl rose and took her down to the

[008-247]carriage, while Lou lingered for a word with his sister.

[008-248]“What do you suppose he’s come for?” he asked, jerking his head toward the

[008-249]gate.

[008-250]“Why, to pay us a visit. I’ve been begging him to for years.”

[008-251]Oscar looked at Alexandra. “He didn’t let you know he was coming?”

[008-252]“No. Why should he? I told him to come at any time.”

[008-253]Lou shrugged his shoulders. “He doesn’t seem to have done much for himself.

[008-254]Wandering around this way!”

[008-255]Oscar spoke solemnly, as from the depths of a cavern. “He never was much

[008-256]account.”

[008-257]Alexandra left them and hurried down to the gate where Annie was rattling on to

[008-258]Carl about her new dining-room furniture. “You must bring Mr. Linstrum over

[008-259]real soon, only be sure to telephone me first,” she called back, as Carl helped

[008-260]her into the carriage. Old Ivar, his white head bare, stood holding the horses.

[008-261]Lou came down the path and climbed into the front seat, took up the reins, and

[008-262]drove off without saying anything further to any one. Oscar picked up his

[008-263]youngest boy and trudged off down the road, the other three trotting after him.

[008-264]Carl, holding the gate open for Alexandra, began to laugh. “Up and coming on

[008-265]the Divide, eh, Alexandra?” he cried gayly.
