[014-001]On Sunday afternoon, a month after [[carl-linstrum|Carl Linstrum]]’s arrival, he rode with Emil

[014-002]up into the French country to attend a Catholic fair. He sat for most of the

[014-003]afternoon in the basement of the church, where the fair was held, talking to

[014-004][[marie-shabata|Marie Shabata]], or strolled about the gravel terrace, thrown up on the hillside

[014-005]in front of the basement doors, where the French boys were jumping and

[014-006]wrestling and throwing the discus. Some of the boys were in their white

[014-007]baseball suits; they had just come up from a Sunday practice game down in the

[014-008]ballgrounds. Amédée, the newly married, Emil’s best friend, was their pitcher,

[014-009]renowned among the country towns for his dash and skill. Amédée was a little

[014-010]fellow, a year younger than Emil and much more boyish in appearance; very lithe

[014-011]and active and neatly made, with a clear brown and white skin, and flashing

[014-012]white teeth. The Sainte-Agnes boys were to play the Hastings nine in a

[014-013]fortnight, and Amédée’s lightning balls were the hope of his team. The little

[014-014]Frenchman seemed to get every ounce there was in him behind the ball as it left

[014-015]his hand.

[014-016]“You’d have made the battery at the University for sure, ’Médée,” Emil said as

[014-017]they were walking from the ball-grounds back to the church on the hill. “You’re

[014-018]pitching better than you did in the spring.”

[014-019]Amédée grinned. “Sure! A married man don’t lose his head no more.” He slapped

[014-020]Emil on the back as he caught step with him. “Oh, Emil, you wanna get married

[014-021]right off quick! It’s the greatest thing ever!”

[014-022]Emil laughed. “How am I going to get married without any girl?”

[014-023]Amédée took his arm. “Pooh! There are plenty girls will have you. You wanna get

[014-024]some nice French girl, now. She treat you well; always be jolly. See,”—he began

[014-025]checking off on his fingers,—“there is Sévérine, and Alphosen, and Joséphine,

[014-026]and Hectorine, and Louise, and Malvina—why, I could love any of them girls! Why

[014-027]don’t you get after them? Are you stuck up, Emil, or is anything the matter

[014-028]with you? I never did know a boy twenty-two years old before that didn’t have

[014-029]no girl. You wanna be a priest, maybe? Not-a for me!” Amédée swaggered. “I

[014-030]bring many good Catholics into this world, I hope, and that’s a way I help the

[014-031]Church.”

[014-032]Emil looked down and patted him on the shoulder. “Now you’re windy, ’Médée. You

[014-033]Frenchies like to brag.”

[014-034]But Amédée had the zeal of the newly married, and he was not to be lightly

[014-035]shaken off. “Honest and true, Emil, don’t you want ANY girl? Maybe there’s some

[014-036]young lady in [[lincoln|Lincoln]], now, very grand,”—Amédée waved his hand languidly before

[014-037]his face to denote the fan of heartless beauty,—“and you lost your heart up

[014-038]there. Is that it?”

[014-039]“Maybe,” said Emil.

[014-040]But Amédée saw no appropriate glow in his friend’s face. “Bah!” he exclaimed in

[014-041]disgust. “I tell all the French girls to keep ’way from you. You gotta rock in

[014-042]there,” thumping Emil on the ribs.

[014-043]When they reached the terrace at the side of the church, Amédée, who was

[014-044]excited by his success on the ball-grounds, challenged Emil to a jumping-match,

[014-045]though he knew he would be beaten. They belted themselves up, and [[raul-marcel|Raoul Marcel]],

[014-046]the choir tenor and Father Duchesne’s pet, and Jean Bordelau, held the string

[014-047]over which they vaulted. All the French boys stood round, cheering and humping

[014-048]themselves up when Emil or Amédée went over the wire, as if they were helping

[014-049]in the lift. Emil stopped at five-feet-five, declaring that he would spoil his

[014-050]appetite for supper if he jumped any more.

[014-051]Angélique, Amédée’s pretty bride, as blonde and fair as her name, who had come

[014-052]out to watch the match, tossed her head at Emil and said:—

[014-053]“’Médée could jump much higher than you if he were as tall. And anyhow, he is

[014-054]much more graceful. He goes over like a bird, and you have to hump yourself all

[014-055]up.”

[014-056]“Oh, I do, do I?” Emil caught her and kissed her saucy mouth squarely, while

[014-057]she laughed and struggled and called, “’Médée! ’Médée!”

[014-058]“There, you see your ’Médée isn’t even big enough to get you away from me. I

[014-059]could run away with you right now and he could only sit down and cry about it.

[014-060]I’ll show you whether I have to hump myself!” Laughing and panting, he picked

[014-061]Angélique up in his arms and began running about the rectangle with her. Not

[014-062]until he saw Marie Shabata’s tiger eyes flashing from the gloom of the basement

[014-063]doorway did he hand the disheveled bride over to her husband. “There, go to

[014-064]your graceful; I haven’t the heart to take you away from him.”

[014-065]Angélique clung to her husband and made faces at Emil over the white shoulder

[014-066]of Amédée’s ball-shirt. Emil was greatly amused at her air of proprietorship

[014-067]and at Amédée’s shameless submission to it. He was delighted with his friend’s

[014-068]good fortune. He liked to see and to think about Amédée’s sunny, natural, happy

[014-069]love.

[014-070]He and Amédée had ridden and wrestled and larked together since they were lads

[014-071]of twelve. On Sundays and holidays they were always arm in arm. It seemed

[014-072]strange that now he should have to hide the thing that Amédée was so proud of,

[014-073]that the feeling which gave one of them such happiness should bring the other

[014-074]such despair. It was like that when Alexandra tested her seed-corn in the

[014-075]spring, he mused. From two ears that had grown side by side, the grains of one

[014-076]shot up joyfully into the light, projecting themselves into the future, and the

[014-077]grains from the other lay still in the earth and rotted; and nobody knew why.
