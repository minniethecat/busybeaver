## Overview

Albert Tovesky is the father of [[Marie Shabata]] (née Tovesky), a prominent figure in Omaha's Bohemian immigrant community. Described as "one of the more intelligent Bohemians who came West in the early seventies," (012-001) he plays a pivotal role in Marie's backstory, attempting to protect his youngest daughter from what he correctly perceives as a disastrous match with [[Frank Shabata]].

## Biography

Albert Tovesky arrived in the American West during the wave of Central European immigration in the 1870s, settling in Omaha where he established himself as "a leader and adviser among his people." (012-001) By his second wife, Marie was his youngest child and the undisputed favorite — "the apple of his eye."

When the dashing Frank Shabata arrived from Bohemia and set his sights on sixteen-year-old Marie, Albert Tovesky recognized the danger. He was infuriated by the engagement, declaring: "Engaged, indeed! You aren't fit to be out of school, and that's what's the matter with you." (012-038) His solution was decisive — he sent Marie to a convent school in St. Louis, the Sisters of the Sacred Heart, hoping distance and discipline would break the attachment.

But the attempt failed. The enforced separation only intensified Frank's determination, and Marie pined in the convent for a year. On her eighteenth birthday, she met Frank at the Union Station in St. Louis and ran away with him. (012-038) Albert Tovesky's response reveals both his character and his circumstances — he "forgave his daughter because there was nothing else to do, and bought her a farm in the country that she had loved so well as a child." (012-038)

## Relationships

Albert Tovesky's defining relationship is with his daughter **Marie**. His decisions — the convent exile, the eventual forgiveness and purchase of her farm — shape the trajectory of the novel's central tragedy. He is both protective and ultimately powerless against the forces of youthful passion and Frank Shabata's obsessive pursuit.

He is the brother of [[Joe Tovesky]], another Bohemian immigrant who shares the family's deep affection for Marie. Together they represent a stable, established immigrant family whose careful planning is undone by the romantic impulsiveness of the next generation.

## Significance

Albert Tovesky embodies the immigrant father archetype in American frontier fiction — a man who has built respectability through intelligence and effort, only to find that the New World's freedoms also bring the freedom to make devastating choices. His story illustrates the tension between Old World patriarchal authority and American individual liberty.

The farm he buys for Marie and Frank becomes both a gift and a prison. The land that should have been a dowry of love becomes, over five years, the stage for Frank's resentment, Marie's isolation, and ultimately the double murder in the orchard. Albert Tovesky's well-intentioned paternalism thus carries an ironic weight throughout the novel.
