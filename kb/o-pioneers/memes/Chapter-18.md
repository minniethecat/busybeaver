[018-001]Winter has settled down over the Divide again; the season in which Nature

[018-002]recuperates, in which she sinks to sleep between the fruitfulness of autumn and

[018-003]the passion of spring. The birds have gone. The teeming life that goes on down

[018-004]in the long grass is exterminated. The prairie-dog keeps his hole. The rabbits

[018-005]run shivering from one frozen garden patch to another and are hard put to it to

[018-006]find frost-bitten cabbage-stalks. At night the coyotes roam the wintry waste,

[018-007]howling for food. The variegated fields are all one color now; the pastures,

[018-008]the stubble, the roads, the sky are the same leaden gray. The hedgerows and

[018-009]trees are scarcely perceptible against the bare earth, whose slaty hue they

[018-010]have taken on. The ground is frozen so hard that it bruises the foot to walk in

[018-011]the roads or in the ploughed fields. It is like an iron country, and the spirit

[018-012]is oppressed by its rigor and melancholy. One could easily believe that in that

[018-013]dead landscape the germs of life and fruitfulness were extinct forever.

[018-014]Alexandra has settled back into her old routine. There are weekly letters from

[018-015]Emil. Lou and Oscar she has not seen since Carl went away. To avoid awkward

[018-016]encounters in the presence of curious spectators, she has stopped going to the

[018-017]Norwegian Church and drives up to the Reform Church at [[hanover|Hanover]], or goes with

[018-018][[marie-shabata|Marie Shabata]] to the Catholic Church, locally known as “the French Church.” She

[018-019]has not told Marie about Carl, or her differences with her brothers. She was

[018-020]never very communicative about her own affairs, and when she came to the point,

[018-021]an instinct told her that about such things she and Marie would not understand

[018-022]one another.

[018-023]Old [[mrs-lee|Mrs. Lee]] had been afraid that family misunderstandings might deprive her of

[018-024]her yearly visit to Alexandra. But on the first day of December Alexandra

[018-025]telephoned Annie that to-morrow she would send [[ivar|Ivar]] over for her mother, and

[018-026]the next day the old lady arrived with her bundles. For twelve years Mrs. Lee

[018-027]had always entered Alexandra’s sitting-room with the same exclamation, “Now we

[018-028]be yust-a like old times!” She enjoyed the liberty Alexandra gave her, and

[018-029]hearing her own language about her all day long. Here she could wear her

[018-030]nightcap and sleep with all her windows shut, listen to Ivar reading the Bible,

[018-031]and here she could run about among the stables in a pair of Emil’s old boots.

[018-032]Though she was bent almost double, she was as spry as a gopher. Her face was as

[018-033]brown as if it had been varnished, and as full of wrinkles as a washerwoman’s

[018-034]hands. She had three jolly old teeth left in the front of her mouth, and when

[018-035]she grinned she looked very knowing, as if when you found out how to take it,

[018-036]life wasn’t half bad. While she and Alexandra patched and pieced and quilted,

[018-037]she talked incessantly about stories she read in a Swedish family paper,

[018-038]telling the plots in great detail; or about her life on a dairy farm in

[018-039]Gottland when she was a girl. Sometimes she forgot which were the printed

[018-040]stories and which were the real stories, it all seemed so far away. She loved

[018-041]to take a little brandy, with hot water and sugar, before she went to bed, and

[018-042]Alexandra always had it ready for her. “It sends good dreams,” she would say

[018-043]with a twinkle in her eye.

[018-044]When Mrs. Lee had been with Alexandra for a week, Marie Shabata telephoned one

[018-045]morning to say that Frank had gone to town for the day, and she would like them

[018-046]to come over for coffee in the afternoon. Mrs. Lee hurried to wash out and iron

[018-047]her new cross-stitched apron, which she had finished only the night before; a

[018-048]checked gingham apron worked with a design ten inches broad across the bottom;

[018-049]a hunting scene, with fir trees and a stag and dogs and huntsmen. Mrs. Lee was

[018-050]firm with herself at dinner, and refused a second helping of apple dumplings.

[018-051]“I ta-ank I save up,” she said with a giggle.

[018-052]At two o’clock in the afternoon Alexandra’s cart drove up to the Shabatas’

[018-053]gate, and Marie saw Mrs. Lee’s red shawl come bobbing up the path. She ran to

[018-054]the door and pulled the old woman into the house with a hug, helping her to

[018-055]take off her wraps while Alexandra blanketed the horse outside. Mrs. Lee had

[018-056]put on her best black satine dress—she abominated woolen stuffs, even in

[018-057]winter—and a crocheted collar, fastened with a big pale gold pin, containing

[018-058]faded daguerreotypes of her father and mother. She had not worn her apron for

[018-059]fear of rumpling it, and now she shook it out and tied it round her waist with

[018-060]a conscious air. Marie drew back and threw up her hands, exclaiming, “Oh, what

[018-061]a beauty! I’ve never seen this one before, have I, Mrs. Lee?”

[018-062]The old woman giggled and ducked her head. “No, yust las’ night I ma-ake. See

[018-063]dis tread; verra strong, no wa-ash out, no fade. My sister send from Sveden. I

[018-064]yust-a ta-ank you like dis.”

[018-065]Marie ran to the door again. “Come in, Alexandra. I have been looking at Mrs.

[018-066]Lee’s apron. Do stop on your way home and show it to Mrs. Hiller. She’s crazy

[018-067]about cross-stitch.”

[018-068]While Alexandra removed her hat and veil, Mrs. Lee went out to the kitchen and

[018-069]settled herself in a wooden rocking-chair by the stove, looking with great

[018-070]interest at the table, set for three, with a white cloth, and a pot of pink

[018-071]geraniums in the middle. “My, a-an’t you gotta fine plants; such-a much flower.

[018-072]How you keep from freeze?”

[018-073]She pointed to the window-shelves, full of blooming fuchsias and geraniums.

[018-074]“I keep the fire all night, Mrs. Lee, and when it’s very cold I put them all on

[018-075]the table, in the middle of the room. Other nights I only put newspapers behind

[018-076]them. Frank laughs at me for fussing, but when they don’t bloom he says,

[018-077]‘What’s the matter with the darned things?’—What do you hear from Carl,

[018-078]Alexandra?”

[018-079]“He got to Dawson before the river froze, and now I suppose I won’t hear any

[018-080]more until spring. Before he left California he sent me a box of orange

[018-081]flowers, but they didn’t keep very well. I have brought a bunch of Emil’s

[018-082]letters for you.” Alexandra came out from the sitting-room and pinched Marie’s

[018-083]cheek playfully. “You don’t look as if the weather ever froze you up. Never

[018-084]have colds, do you? That’s a good girl. She had dark red cheeks like this when

[018-085]she was a little girl, Mrs. Lee. She looked like some queer foreign kind of a

[018-086]doll. I’ve never forgot the first time I saw you in Mieklejohn’s store, Marie,

[018-087]the time father was lying sick. Carl and I were talking about that before he

[018-088]went away.”

[018-089]“I remember, and Emil had his kitten along. When are you going to send Emil’s

[018-090]Christmas box?”

[018-091]“It ought to have gone before this. I’ll have to send it by mail now, to get it

[018-092]there in time.”

[018-093]Marie pulled a dark purple silk necktie from her workbasket. “I knit this for

[018-094]him. It’s a good color, don’t you think? Will you please put it in with your

[018-095]things and tell him it’s from me, to wear when he goes serenading.”

[018-096]Alexandra laughed. “I don’t believe he goes serenading much. He says in one

[018-097]letter that the Mexican ladies are said to be very beautiful, but that don’t

[018-098]seem to me very warm praise.”

[018-099]Marie tossed her head. “Emil can’t fool me. If he’s bought a guitar, he goes

[018-100]serenading. Who wouldn’t, with all those Spanish girls dropping flowers down

[018-101]from their windows! I’d sing to them every night, wouldn’t you, Mrs. Lee?”

[018-102]The old lady chuckled. Her eyes lit up as Marie bent down and opened the oven

[018-103]door. A delicious hot fragrance blew out into the tidy kitchen. “My, somet’ing

[018-104]smell good!” She turned to Alexandra with a wink, her three yellow teeth making

[018-105]a brave show, “I ta-ank dat stop my yaw from ache no more!” she said

[018-106]contentedly.

[018-107]Marie took out a pan of delicate little rolls, stuffed with stewed apricots,

[018-108]and began to dust them over with powdered sugar. “I hope you’ll like these,

[018-109]Mrs. Lee; Alexandra does. The Bohemians always like them with their coffee. But

[018-110]if you don’t, I have a coffee-cake with nuts and poppy seeds. Alexandra, will

[018-111]you get the cream jug? I put it in the window to keep cool.”

[018-112]“The Bohemians,” said Alexandra, as they drew up to the table, “certainly know

[018-113]how to make more kinds of bread than any other people in the world. Old Mrs.

[018-114]Hiller told me once at the church supper that she could make seven kinds of

[018-115]fancy bread, but Marie could make a dozen.”

[018-116]Mrs. Lee held up one of the apricot rolls between her brown thumb and

[018-117]forefinger and weighed it critically. “Yust like-a fedders,” she pronounced

[018-118]with satisfaction. “My, a-an’t dis nice!” she exclaimed as she stirred her

[018-119]coffee. “I yust ta-ake a liddle yelly now, too, I ta-ank.”

[018-120]Alexandra and Marie laughed at her forehandedness, and fell to talking of their

[018-121]own affairs. “I was afraid you had a cold when I talked to you over the

[018-122]telephone the other night, Marie. What was the matter, had you been crying?”

[018-123]“Maybe I had,” Marie smiled guiltily. “Frank was out late that night. Don’t you

[018-124]get lonely sometimes in the winter, when everybody has gone away?”

[018-125]“I thought it was something like that. If I hadn’t had company, I’d have run

[018-126]over to see for myself. If you get down-hearted, what will become of the rest

[018-127]of us?” Alexandra asked.

[018-128]“I don’t, very often. There’s Mrs. Lee without any coffee!”

[018-129]Later, when Mrs. Lee declared that her powers were spent, Marie and Alexandra

[018-130]went upstairs to look for some crochet patterns the old lady wanted to borrow.

[018-131]“Better put on your coat, Alexandra. It’s cold up there, and I have no idea

[018-132]where those patterns are. I may have to look through my old trunks.” Marie

[018-133]caught up a shawl and opened the stair door, running up the steps ahead of her

[018-134]guest. “While I go through the bureau drawers, you might look in those

[018-135]hat-boxes on the closet-shelf, over where Frank’s clothes hang. There are a lot

[018-136]of odds and ends in them.”

[018-137]She began tossing over the contents of the drawers, and Alexandra went into the

[018-138]clothes-closet. Presently she came back, holding a slender elastic yellow stick

[018-139]in her hand.

[018-140]“What in the world is this, Marie? You don’t mean to tell me Frank ever carried

[018-141]such a thing?”

[018-142]Marie blinked at it with astonishment and sat down on the floor. “Where did you

[018-143]find it? I didn’t know he had kept it. I haven’t seen it for years.”

[018-144]“It really is a cane, then?”

[018-145]“Yes. One he brought from the old country. He used to carry it when I first

[018-146]knew him. Isn’t it foolish? Poor Frank!”

[018-147]Alexandra twirled the stick in her fingers and laughed. “He must have looked

[018-148]funny!”

[018-149]Marie was thoughtful. “No, he didn’t, really. It didn’t seem out of place. He

[018-150]used to be awfully gay like that when he was a young man. I guess people always

[018-151]get what’s hardest for them, Alexandra.” Marie gathered the shawl closer about

[018-152]her and still looked hard at the cane. “Frank would be all right in the right

[018-153]place,” she said reflectively. “He ought to have a different kind of wife, for

[018-154]one thing. Do you know, Alexandra, I could pick out exactly the right sort of

[018-155]woman for Frank—now. The trouble is you almost have to marry a man before you

[018-156]can find out the sort of wife he needs; and usually it’s exactly the sort you

[018-157]are not. Then what are you going to do about it?” she asked candidly.

[018-158]Alexandra confessed she didn’t know. “However,” she added, “it seems to me that

[018-159]you get along with Frank about as well as any woman I’ve ever seen or heard of

[018-160]could.”

[018-161]Marie shook her head, pursing her lips and blowing her warm breath softly out

[018-162]into the frosty air. “No; I was spoiled at home. I like my own way, and I have

[018-163]a quick tongue. When Frank brags, I say sharp things, and he never forgets. He

[018-164]goes over and over it in his mind; I can feel him. Then I’m too giddy. Frank’s

[018-165]wife ought to be timid, and she ought not to care about another living thing in

[018-166]the world but just Frank! I didn’t, when I married him, but I suppose I was too

[018-167]young to stay like that.” Marie sighed.

[018-168]Alexandra had never heard Marie speak so frankly about her husband before, and

[018-169]she felt that it was wiser not to encourage her. No good, she reasoned, ever

[018-170]came from talking about such things, and while Marie was thinking aloud,

[018-171]Alexandra had been steadily searching the hat-boxes. “Aren’t these the

[018-172]patterns, Maria?”

[018-173]Maria sprang up from the floor. “Sure enough, we were looking for patterns,

[018-174]weren’t we? I’d forgot about everything but Frank’s other wife. I’ll put that

[018-175]away.”

[018-176]She poked the cane behind Frank’s Sunday clothes, and though she laughed,

[018-177]Alexandra saw there were tears in her eyes.

[018-178]When they went back to the kitchen, the snow had begun to fall, and Marie’s

[018-179]visitors thought they must be getting home. She went out to the cart with them,

[018-180]and tucked the robes about old Mrs. Lee while Alexandra took the blanket off

[018-181]her horse. As they drove away, Marie turned and went slowly back to the house.

[018-182]She took up the package of letters Alexandra had brought, but she did not read

[018-183]them. She turned them over and looked at the foreign stamps, and then sat

[018-184]watching the flying snow while the dusk deepened in the kitchen and the stove

[018-185]sent out a red glow.

[018-186]Marie knew perfectly well that Emil’s letters were written more for her than

[018-187]for Alexandra. They were not the sort of letters that a young man writes to his

[018-188]sister. They were both more personal and more painstaking; full of descriptions

[018-189]of the gay life in the old Mexican capital in the days when the strong hand of

[018-190]Porfirio Diaz was still strong. He told about bull-fights and cock-fights,

[018-191]churches and*fiestas*, the flower-markets and the fountains, the music

[018-192]and dancing, the people of all nations he met in the Italian restaurants on San

[018-193]Francisco Street. In short, they were the kind of letters a young man writes to

[018-194]a woman when he wishes himself and his life to seem interesting to her, when he

[018-195]wishes to enlist her imagination in his behalf.

[018-196]Marie, when she was alone or when she sat sewing in the evening, often thought

[018-197]about what it must be like down there where Emil was; where there were flowers

[018-198]and street bands everywhere, and carriages rattling up and down, and where

[018-199]there was a little blind boot-black in front of the cathedral who could play

[018-200]any tune you asked for by dropping the lids of blacking-boxes on the stone

[018-201]steps. When everything is done and over for one at twenty-three, it is pleasant

[018-202]to let the mind wander forth and follow a young adventurer who has life before

[018-203]him. “And if it had not been for me,” she thought, “Frank might still be free

[018-204]like that, and having a good time making people admire him. Poor Frank, getting

[018-205]married wasn’t very good for him either. I’m afraid I do set people against

[018-206]him, as he says. I seem, somehow, to give him away all the time. Perhaps he

[018-207]would try to be agreeable to people again, if I were not around. It seems as if

[018-208]I always make him just as bad as he can be.”

[018-209]Later in the winter, Alexandra looked back upon that afternoon as the last

[018-210]satisfactory visit she had had with Marie. After that day the younger woman

[018-211]seemed to shrink more and more into herself. When she was with Alexandra she

[018-212]was not spontaneous and frank as she used to be. She seemed to be brooding over

[018-213]something, and holding something back. The weather had a good deal to do with

[018-214]their seeing less of each other than usual. There had not been such snowstorms

[018-215]in twenty years, and the path across the fields was drifted deep from Christmas

[018-216]until March. When the two neighbors went to see each other, they had to go

[018-217]round by the wagon-road, which was twice as far. They telephoned each other

[018-218]almost every night, though in January there was a stretch of three weeks when

[018-219]the wires were down, and when the postman did not come at all.

[018-220]Marie often ran in to see her nearest neighbor, old Mrs. Hiller, who was

[018-221]crippled with rheumatism and had only her son, the lame shoemaker, to take care

[018-222]of her; and she went to the French Church, whatever the weather. She was a

[018-223]sincerely devout girl. She prayed for herself and for Frank, and for Emil,

[018-224]among the temptations of that gay, corrupt old city. She found more comfort in

[018-225]the Church that winter than ever before. It seemed to come closer to her, and

[018-226]to fill an emptiness that ached in her heart. She tried to be patient with her

[018-227]husband. He and his hired man usually played California Jack in the evening.

[018-228]Marie sat sewing or crocheting and tried to take a friendly interest in the

[018-229]game, but she was always thinking about the wide fields outside, where the snow

[018-230]was drifting over the fences; and about the orchard, where the snow was falling

[018-231]and packing, crust over crust. When she went out into the dark kitchen to fix

[018-232]her plants for the night, she used to stand by the window and look out at the

[018-233]white fields, or watch the currents of snow whirling over the orchard. She

[018-234]seemed to feel the weight of all the snow that lay down there. The branches had

[018-235]become so hard that they wounded your hand if you but tried to break a twig.

[018-236]And yet, down under the frozen crusts, at the roots of the trees, the secret of

[018-237]life was still safe, warm as the blood in one’s heart; and the spring would

[018-238]come again! Oh, it would come again!
