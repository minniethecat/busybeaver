## Overview

Joe Tovesky is a Bohemian immigrant living in Omaha who appears in the novel primarily as the devoted uncle of [[Marie Shabata]] (née Tovesky). His role, though brief in direct appearances, frames Marie's earliest connection to the Nebraska Divide and provides one of the novel's most poignant echoes at its tragic close.

## Biography

Joe Tovesky first appears during the novel's opening scene in the general store at Hanover, where the young Marie Tovesky has come from Omaha with her mother to visit him. (001-123) Marie, then a dark-haired child with striking golden-brown eyes, captures the attention of everyone around her, and Joe Tovesky's pride in his niece is immediate and demonstrative.

When Joe Tovesky enters the store, he makes a noisy, joyful entrance, "picking up his little niece, setting her on his shoulder for every one to see." (001-135) His own children are all boys, and he adores this little girl with unreserved affection. His cronies form a circle around him, admiring and teasing the child who would grow up to become central to the novel's tragedy.

At the novel's end, after Marie's death at the hands of her husband [[Frank Shabata]], Alexandra visits Frank in prison and reflects on the incomprehensible arc of Marie's life. She thinks of "poor old Joe Tovesky, the uncle who used to carry her about so proudly when she was a little girl." (029-144) This echo of the novel's first chapter — from pride and joy to destruction and sorrow — encapsulates the novel's meditation on fate and the land.

## Relationships

Joe Tovesky's most significant relationship is with his niece, [[Marie Shabata]]. As the uncle of a beloved only niece among a household of boys, his affection for Marie is one of the novel's few depictions of uncomplicated familial love. His pride in carrying her on his shoulder in the Hanover general store (001-135) becomes, in retrospect, an emblem of everything that will be lost.

Joe Tovesky is also the brother of [[Albert Tovesky]], Marie's father, who settled in Omaha and became a leader among the Bohemian immigrant community there. (012-001)

## Significance

Joe Tovesky functions in the novel as an emotional bookend. His exuberant introduction of Marie in Chapter 1 establishes her as a treasured, almost magical child — the "little Bohemian girl" whose golden eyes fascinate everyone she meets. Alexandra's memory of him at the novel's close (029-144) transforms this early scene into an elegy, asking implicitly how such a beloved child could come to such a violent end.

As a minor character, Joe Tovesky also illustrates the Bohemian immigrant community's presence on the Divide. Unlike the Swedish Bergsons or the Norwegian Lee family, the Toveskys represent the Central European strand of the Great Plains settlement, with its own distinct cultural traditions and social networks centered in Omaha.

## Key Passages

> "Joe Tovesky came in noisily and picked up his little niece, setting her on his shoulder for every one to see. His children were all boys, and he adored this little creature. His cronies formed a circle about him, admiring and teasing the little girl."
> (001-135)

> "And why, with her happy, affectionate nature, should she have brought destruction and sorrow to all who had loved her, even to poor old Joe Tovesky, the uncle who used to carry her about so proudly when she was a little girl?"
> (029-144)
