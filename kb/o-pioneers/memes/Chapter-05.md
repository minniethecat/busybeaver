[005-001]Alexandra and Emil spent five days down among the river farms, driving up and

[005-002]down the valley. Alexandra talked to the men about their crops and to the women

[005-003]about their poultry. She spent a whole day with one young farmer who had been

[005-004]away at school, and who was experimenting with a new kind of clover hay. She

[005-005]learned a great deal. As they drove along, she and Emil talked and planned. At

[005-006]last, on the sixth day, Alexandra turned Brigham’s head northward and left the

[005-007]river behind.

[005-008]“There’s nothing in it for us down there, Emil. There are a few fine farms, but

[005-009]they are owned by the rich men in town, and couldn’t be bought. Most of the

[005-010]land is rough and hilly. They can always scrape along down there, but they can

[005-011]never do anything big. Down there they have a little certainty, but up with us

[005-012]there is a big chance. We must have faith in [[the-divide|the high land]], Emil. I want to

[005-013]hold on harder than ever, and when you’re a man you’ll thank me.” She urged

[005-014]Brigham forward.

[005-015]When the road began to climb the first long swells of the Divide, Alexandra

[005-016]hummed an old Swedish hymn, and Emil wondered why his sister looked so happy.

[005-017]Her face was so radiant that he felt shy about asking her. For the first time,

[005-018]perhaps, since that land emerged from the waters of geologic ages, a human face

[005-019]was set toward it with love and yearning. It seemed beautiful to her, rich and

[005-020]strong and glorious. Her eyes drank in the breadth of it, until her tears

[005-021]blinded her. Then the Genius of the Divide, the great, free spirit which

[005-022]breathes across it, must have bent lower than it ever bent to a human will

[005-023]before. The history of every country begins in the heart of a man or a woman.

[005-024]Alexandra reached home in the afternoon. That evening she held a family council

[005-025]and told her brothers all that she had seen and heard.

[005-026]“I want you boys to go down yourselves and look it over. Nothing will convince

[005-027]you like seeing with your own eyes. The river land was settled before this, and

[005-028]so they are a few years ahead of us, and have learned more about farming. The

[005-029]land sells for three times as much as this, but in five years we will double

[005-030]it. The rich men down there own all the best land, and they are buying all they

[005-031]can get. The thing to do is to sell our cattle and what little old corn we

[005-032]have, and buy the Linstrum place. Then the next thing to do is to take out two

[005-033]loans on our half-sections, and buy Peter Crow’s place; raise every dollar we

[005-034]can, and buy every acre we can.”

[005-035]“Mortgage the homestead again?” Lou cried. He sprang up and began to wind the

[005-036]clock furiously. “I won’t slave to pay off another mortgage. I’ll never do it.

[005-037]You’d just as soon kill us all, Alexandra, to carry out some scheme!”

[005-038]Oscar rubbed his high, pale forehead. “How do you propose to pay off your

[005-039]mortgages?”

[005-040]Alexandra looked from one to the other and bit her lip. They had never seen her

[005-041]so nervous. “See here,” she brought out at last. “We borrow the money for six

[005-042]years. Well, with the money we buy a half-section from Linstrum and a half from

[005-043]Crow, and a quarter from Struble, maybe. That will give us upwards of fourteen

[005-044]hundred acres, won’t it? You won’t have to pay off your mortgages for six

[005-045]years. By that time, any of this land will be worth thirty dollars an acre—it

[005-046]will be worth fifty, but we’ll say thirty; then you can sell a garden patch

[005-047]anywhere, and pay off a debt of sixteen hundred dollars. It’s not the principal

[005-048]I’m worried about, it’s the interest and taxes. We’ll have to strain to meet

[005-049]the payments. But as sure as we are sitting here to-night, we can sit down here

[005-050]ten years from now independent landowners, not struggling farmers any longer.

[005-051]The chance that father was always looking for has come.”

[005-052]Lou was pacing the floor. “But how do you*know*that land is going to go

[005-053]up enough to pay the mortgages and—”

[005-054]“And make us rich besides?” Alexandra put in firmly. “I can’t explain that,

[005-055]Lou. You’ll have to take my word for it. I*know*, that’s all. When you

[005-056]drive about over the country you can feel it coming.”

[005-057]Oscar had been sitting with his head lowered, his hands hanging between his

[005-058]knees. “But we can’t work so much land,” he said dully, as if he were talking

[005-059]to himself. “We can’t even try. It would just lie there and we’d work ourselves

[005-060]to death.” He sighed, and laid his calloused fist on the table.

[005-061]Alexandra’s eyes filled with tears. She put her hand on his shoulder. “You poor

[005-062]boy, you won’t have to work it. The men in town who are buying up other

[005-063]people’s land don’t try to farm it. They are the men to watch, in a new

[005-064]country. Let’s try to do like the shrewd ones, and not like these stupid

[005-065]fellows. I don’t want you boys always to have to work like this. I want you to

[005-066]be independent, and Emil to go to school.”

[005-067]Lou held his head as if it were splitting. “Everybody will say we are crazy. It

[005-068]must be crazy, or everybody would be doing it.”

[005-069]“If they were, we wouldn’t have much chance. No, Lou, I was talking about that

[005-070]with the smart young man who is raising the new kind of clover. He says the

[005-071]right thing is usually just what everybody don’t do. Why are we better fixed

[005-072]than any of our neighbors? Because father had more brains. Our people were

[005-073]better people than these in the old country. We*ought*to do more than

[005-074]they do, and see further ahead. Yes, mother, I’m going to clear the table now.”

[005-075]Alexandra rose. The boys went to the stable to see to the stock, and they were

[005-076]gone a long while. When they came back Lou played on his*dragharmonika*and Oscar sat figuring at his father’s secretary all evening. They said nothing

[005-077]more about Alexandra’s project, but she felt sure now that they would consent

[005-078]to it. Just before bedtime Oscar went out for a pail of water. When he did not

[005-079]come back, Alexandra threw a shawl over her head and ran down the path to the

[005-080]windmill. She found him sitting there with his head in his hands, and she sat

[005-081]down beside him.

[005-082]“Don’t do anything you don’t want to do, Oscar,” she whispered. She waited a

[005-083]moment, but he did not stir. “I won’t say any more about it, if you’d rather

[005-084]not. What makes you so discouraged?”

[005-085]“I dread signing my name to them pieces of paper,” he said slowly. “All the

[005-086]time I was a boy we had a mortgage hanging over us.”

[005-087]“Then don’t sign one. I don’t want you to, if you feel that way.”

[005-088]Oscar shook his head. “No, I can see there’s a chance that way. I’ve thought a

[005-089]good while there might be. We’re in so deep now, we might as well go deeper.

[005-090]But it’s hard work pulling out of debt. Like pulling a threshing-machine out of

[005-091]the mud; breaks your back. Me and Lou’s worked hard, and I can’t see it’s got

[005-092]us ahead much.”

[005-093]“Nobody knows about that as well as I do, Oscar. That’s why I want to try an

[005-094]easier way. I don’t want you to have to grub for every dollar.”

[005-095]“Yes, I know what you mean. Maybe it’ll come out right. But signing papers is

[005-096]signing papers. There ain’t no maybe about that.” He took his pail and trudged

[005-097]up the path to the house.

[005-098]Alexandra drew her shawl closer about her and stood leaning against the frame

[005-099]of the mill, looking at the stars which glittered so keenly through the frosty

[005-100]autumn air. She always loved to watch them, to think of their vastness and

[005-101]distance, and of their ordered march. It fortified her to reflect upon the

[005-102]great operations of nature, and when she thought of the law that lay behind

[005-103]them, she felt a sense of personal security. That night she had a new

[005-104]consciousness of the country, felt almost a new relation to it. Even her talk

[005-105]with the boys had not taken away the feeling that had overwhelmed her when she

[005-106]drove back to the Divide that afternoon. She had never known before how much

[005-107]the country meant to her. The chirping of the insects down in the long grass

[005-108]had been like the sweetest music. She had felt as if her heart were hiding down

[005-109]there, somewhere, with the quail and the plover and all the little wild things

[005-110]that crooned or buzzed in the sun. Under the long shaggy ridges, she felt the

[005-111]future stirring.
