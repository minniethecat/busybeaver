[026-001]When [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]] got home that night, he found Emil’s mare in his stable.

[026-002]Such an impertinence amazed him. Like everybody else, Frank had had an exciting

[026-003]day. Since noon he had been drinking too much, and he was in a bad temper. He

[026-004]talked bitterly to himself while he put his own horse away, and as he went up

[026-005]the path and saw that the house was dark he felt an added sense of injury. He

[026-006]approached quietly and listened on the doorstep. Hearing nothing, he opened the

[026-007]kitchen door and went softly from one room to another. Then he went through the

[026-008]house again, upstairs and down, with no better result. He sat down on the

[026-009]bottom step of the box stairway and tried to get his wits together. In that

[026-010]unnatural quiet there was no sound but his own heavy breathing. Suddenly an owl

[026-011]began to hoot out in the fields. Frank lifted his head. An idea flashed into

[026-012]his mind, and his sense of injury and outrage grew. He went into his bedroom

[026-013]and took his murderous 405 Winchester from the closet.

[026-014]When Frank took up his gun and walked out of the house, he had not the faintest

[026-015]purpose of doing anything with it. He did not believe that he had any real

[026-016]grievance. But it gratified him to feel like a desperate man. He had got into

[026-017]the habit of seeing himself always in desperate straits. His unhappy

[026-018]temperament was like a cage; he could never get out of it; and he felt that

[026-019]other people, his wife in particular, must have put him there. It had never

[026-020]more than dimly occurred to Frank that he made his own unhappiness. Though he

[026-021]took up his gun with dark projects in his mind, he would have been paralyzed

[026-022]with fright had he known that there was the slightest probability of his ever

[026-023]carrying any of them out.

[026-024]Frank went slowly down to the orchard gate, stopped and stood for a moment lost

[026-025]in thought. He retraced his steps and looked through the barn and the hayloft.

[026-026]Then he went out to the road, where he took the foot-path along the outside of

[026-027]the orchard hedge. The hedge was twice as tall as Frank himself, and so dense

[026-028]that one could see through it only by peering closely between the leaves. He

[026-029]could see the empty path a long way in the moonlight. His mind traveled ahead

[026-030]to the stile, which he always thought of as haunted by [[emil-bergson|Emil Bergson]]. But why

[026-031]had he left his horse?

[026-032]At the wheatfield corner, where the orchard hedge ended and the path led across

[026-033]the pasture to the Bergsons’, Frank stopped. In the warm, breathless night air

[026-034]he heard a murmuring sound, perfectly inarticulate, as low as the sound of

[026-035]water coming from a spring, where there is no fall, and where there are no

[026-036]stones to fret it. Frank strained his ears. It ceased. He held his breath and

[026-037]began to tremble. Resting the butt of his gun on the ground, he parted the

[026-038]mulberry leaves softly with his fingers and peered through the hedge at the

[026-039]dark figures on the grass, in the shadow of the mulberry tree. It seemed to him

[026-040]that they must feel his eyes, that they must hear him breathing. But they did

[026-041]not. Frank, who had always wanted to see things blacker than they were, for

[026-042]once wanted to believe less than he saw. The woman lying in the shadow might so

[026-043]easily be one of the Bergsons’ farm-girls.... Again the murmur, like water

[026-044]welling out of the ground. This time he heard it more distinctly, and his blood

[026-045]was quicker than his brain. He began to act, just as a man who falls into the

[026-046]fire begins to act. The gun sprang to his shoulder, he sighted mechanically and

[026-047]fired three times without stopping, stopped without knowing why. Either he shut

[026-048]his eyes or he had vertigo. He did not see anything while he was firing. He

[026-049]thought he heard a cry simultaneous with the second report, but he was not

[026-050]sure. He peered again through the hedge, at the two dark figures under the

[026-051]tree. They had fallen a little apart from each other, and were perfectly

[026-052]still—No, not quite; in a white patch of light, where the moon shone through

[026-053]the branches, a man’s hand was plucking spasmodically at the grass.

[026-054]Suddenly the woman stirred and uttered a cry, then another, and another. She

[026-055]was living! She was dragging herself toward the hedge! Frank dropped his gun

[026-056]and ran back along the path, shaking, stumbling, gasping. He had never imagined

[026-057]such horror. The cries followed him. They grew fainter and thicker, as if she

[026-058]were choking. He dropped on his knees beside the hedge and crouched like a

[026-059]rabbit, listening; fainter, fainter; a sound like a whine; again—a

[026-060]moan—another—silence. Frank scrambled to his feet and ran on, groaning and

[026-061]praying. From habit he went toward the house, where he was used to being

[026-062]soothed when he had worked himself into a frenzy, but at the sight of the

[026-063]black, open door, he started back. He knew that he had murdered somebody, that

[026-064]a woman was bleeding and moaning in the orchard, but he had not realized before

[026-065]that it was his wife. The gate stared him in the face. He threw his hands over

[026-066]his head. Which way to turn? He lifted his tormented face and looked at the

[026-067]sky. “Holy Mother of God, not to suffer! She was a good girl—not to suffer!”

[026-068]Frank had been wont to see himself in dramatic situations; but now, when he

[026-069]stood by the windmill, in the bright space between the barn and the house,

[026-070]facing his own black doorway, he did not see himself at all. He stood like the

[026-071]hare when the dogs are approaching from all sides. And he ran like a hare, back

[026-072]and forth about that moonlit space, before he could make up his mind to go into

[026-073]the dark stable for a horse. The thought of going into a doorway was terrible

[026-074]to him. He caught Emil’s horse by the bit and led it out. He could not have

[026-075]buckled a bridle on his own. After two or three attempts, he lifted himself

[026-076]into the saddle and started for [[hanover|Hanover]]. If he could catch the one o’clock

[026-077]train, he had money enough to get as far as [[omaha|Omaha]].

[026-078]While he was thinking dully of this in some less sensitized part of his brain,

[026-079]his acuter faculties were going over and over the cries he had heard in the

[026-080]orchard. Terror was the only thing that kept him from going back to her, terror

[026-081]that she might still be she, that she might still be suffering. A woman,

[026-082]mutilated and bleeding in his orchard—it was because it was a woman that he was

[026-083]so afraid. It was inconceivable that he should have hurt a woman. He would

[026-084]rather be eaten by wild beasts than see her move on the ground as she had moved

[026-085]in the orchard. Why had she been so careless? She knew he was like a crazy man

[026-086]when he was angry. She had more than once taken that gun away from him and held

[026-087]it, when he was angry with other people. Once it had gone off while they were

[026-088]struggling over it. She was never afraid. But, when she knew him, why hadn’t

[026-089]she been more careful? Didn’t she have all summer before her to love Emil

[026-090]Bergson in, without taking such chances? Probably she had met the Smirka boy,

[026-091]too, down there in the orchard. He didn’t care. She could have met all the men

[026-092]on the Divide there, and welcome, if only she hadn’t brought this horror on

[026-093]him.

[026-094]There was a wrench in Frank’s mind. He did not honestly believe that of her. He

[026-095]knew that he was doing her wrong. He stopped his horse to admit this to himself

[026-096]the more directly, to think it out the more clearly. He knew that he was to

[026-097]blame. For three years he had been trying to break her spirit. She had a way of

[026-098]making the best of things that seemed to him a sentimental affectation. He

[026-099]wanted his wife to resent that he was wasting his best years among these stupid

[026-100]and unappreciative people; but she had seemed to find the people quite good

[026-101]enough. If he ever got rich he meant to buy her pretty clothes and take her to

[026-102]California in a Pullman car, and treat her like a lady; but in the mean time he

[026-103]wanted her to feel that life was as ugly and as unjust as he felt it. He had

[026-104]tried to make her life ugly. He had refused to share any of the little

[026-105]pleasures she was so plucky about making for herself. She could be gay about

[026-106]the least thing in the world; but she must be gay! When she first came to him,

[026-107]her faith in him, her adoration—Frank struck the mare with his fist. Why had

[026-108]Marie made him do this thing; why had she brought this upon him? He was

[026-109]overwhelmed by sickening misfortune. All at once he heard her cries again—he

[026-110]had forgotten for a moment. “Maria,” he sobbed aloud, “Maria!”

[026-111]When Frank was halfway to Hanover, the motion of his horse brought on a violent

[026-112]attack of nausea. After it had passed, he rode on again, but he could think of

[026-113]nothing except his physical weakness and his desire to be comforted by his

[026-114]wife. He wanted to get into his own bed. Had his wife been at home, he would

[026-115]have turned and gone back to her meekly enough.
