[012-001]Marie’s father, Albert Tovesky, was one of the more intelligent Bohemians who

[012-002]came West in the early seventies. He settled in [[omaha|Omaha]] and became a leader and

[012-003]adviser among his people there. Marie was his youngest child, by a second wife,

[012-004]and was the apple of his eye. She was barely sixteen, and was in the graduating

[012-005]class of the Omaha High School, when [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]] arrived from the old country

[012-006]and set all the Bohemian girls in a flutter. He was easily the buck of the

[012-007]beer-gardens, and on Sunday he was a sight to see, with his silk hat and tucked

[012-008]shirt and blue frock-coat, wearing gloves and carrying a little wisp of a

[012-009]yellow cane. He was tall and fair, with splendid teeth and close-cropped yellow

[012-010]curls, and he wore a slightly disdainful expression, proper for a young man

[012-011]with high connections, whose mother had a big farm in the Elbe valley. There

[012-012]was often an interesting discontent in his blue eyes, and every Bohemian girl

[012-013]he met imagined herself the cause of that unsatisfied expression. He had a way

[012-014]of drawing out his cambric handkerchief slowly, by one corner, from his

[012-015]breast-pocket, that was melancholy and romantic in the extreme. He took a

[012-016]little flight with each of the more eligible Bohemian girls, but it was when he

[012-017]was with little Marie Tovesky that he drew his handkerchief out most slowly,

[012-018]and, after he had lit a fresh cigar, dropped the match most despairingly. Any

[012-019]one could see, with half an eye, that his proud heart was bleeding for

[012-020]somebody.

[012-021]One Sunday, late in the summer after Marie’s graduation, she met Frank at a

[012-022]Bohemian picnic down the river and went rowing with him all the afternoon. When

[012-023]she got home that evening she went straight to her father’s room and told him

[012-024]that she was engaged to Shabata. Old Tovesky was having a comfortable pipe

[012-025]before he went to bed. When he heard his daughter’s announcement, he first

[012-026]prudently corked his beer bottle and then leaped to his feet and had a turn of

[012-027]temper. He characterized Frank Shabata by a Bohemian expression which is the

[012-028]equivalent of stuffed shirt.

[012-029]“Why don’t he go to work like the rest of us did? His farm in the Elbe valley,

[012-030]indeed! Ain’t he got plenty brothers and sisters? It’s his mother’s farm, and

[012-031]why don’t he stay at home and help her? Haven’t I seen his mother out in the

[012-032]morning at five o’clock with her ladle and her big bucket on wheels, putting

[012-033]liquid manure on the cabbages? Don’t I know the look of old Eva Shabata’s

[012-034]hands? Like an old horse’s hoofs they are—and this fellow wearing gloves and

[012-035]rings! Engaged, indeed! You aren’t fit to be out of school, and that’s what’s

[012-036]the matter with you. I will send you off to the Sisters of the Sacred Heart in

[012-037]St. Louis, and they will teach you some sense,*I*guess!”

[012-038]Accordingly, the very next week, Albert Tovesky took his daughter, pale and

[012-039]tearful, down the river to the convent. But the way to make Frank want anything

[012-040]was to tell him he couldn’t have it. He managed to have an interview with Marie

[012-041]before she went away, and whereas he had been only half in love with her

[012-042]before, he now persuaded himself that he would not stop at anything. Marie took

[012-043]with her to the convent, under the canvas lining of her trunk, the results of a

[012-044]laborious and satisfying morning on Frank’s part; no less than a dozen

[012-045]photographs of himself, taken in a dozen different love-lorn attitudes. There

[012-046]was a little round photograph for her watch-case, photographs for her wall and

[012-047]dresser, and even long narrow ones to be used as bookmarks. More than once the

[012-048]handsome gentleman was torn to pieces before the French class by an indignant

[012-049]nun.

[012-050]Marie pined in the convent for a year, until her eighteenth birthday was

[012-051]passed. Then she met Frank Shabata in the Union Station in St. Louis and ran

[012-052]away with him. Old Tovesky forgave his daughter because there was nothing else

[012-053]to do, and bought her a farm in the country that she had loved so well as a

[012-054]child. Since then her story had been a part of the history of the Divide. She

[012-055]and Frank had been living there for five years when [[carl-linstrum|Carl Linstrum]] came back to

[012-056]pay his long deferred visit to Alexandra. Frank had, on the whole, done better

[012-057]than one might have expected. He had flung himself at the soil with savage

[012-058]energy. Once a year he went to Hastings or to Omaha, on a spree. He stayed away

[012-059]for a week or two, and then came home and worked like a demon. He did work; if

[012-060]he felt sorry for himself, that was his own affair.
