[027-001]When old [[ivar|Ivar]] climbed down from his loft at four o’clock the next morning, he

[027-002]came upon Emil’s mare, jaded and lather-stained, her bridle broken, chewing the

[027-003]scattered tufts of hay outside the stable door. The old man was thrown into a

[027-004]fright at once. He put the mare in her stall, threw her a measure of oats, and

[027-005]then set out as fast as his bow-legs could carry him on the path to the nearest

[027-006]neighbor.

[027-007]“Something is wrong with that boy. Some misfortune has come upon us. He would

[027-008]never have used her so, in his right senses. It is not his way to abuse his

[027-009]mare,” the old man kept muttering, as he scuttled through the short, wet

[027-010]pasture grass on his bare feet.

[027-011]While Ivar was hurrying across the fields, the first long rays of the sun were

[027-012]reaching down between the orchard boughs to those two dew-drenched figures. The

[027-013]story of what had happened was written plainly on the orchard grass, and on the

[027-014]white mulberries that had fallen in the night and were covered with dark stain.

[027-015]For Emil the chapter had been short. He was shot in the heart, and had rolled

[027-016]over on his back and died. His face was turned up to the sky and his brows were

[027-017]drawn in a frown, as if he had realized that something had befallen him. But

[027-018]for [[marie-shabata|Marie Shabata]] it had not been so easy. One ball had torn through her right

[027-019]lung, another had shattered the carotid artery. She must have started up and

[027-020]gone toward the hedge, leaving a trail of blood. There she had fallen and bled.

[027-021]From that spot there was another trail, heavier than the first, where she must

[027-022]have dragged herself back to Emil’s body. Once there, she seemed not to have

[027-023]struggled any more. She had lifted her head to her lover’s breast, taken his

[027-024]hand in both her own, and bled quietly to death. She was lying on her right

[027-025]side in an easy and natural position, her cheek on Emil’s shoulder. On her face

[027-026]there was a look of ineffable content. Her lips were parted a little; her eyes

[027-027]were lightly closed, as if in a day-dream or a light slumber. After she lay

[027-028]down there, she seemed not to have moved an eyelash. The hand she held was

[027-029]covered with dark stains, where she had kissed it.

[027-030]But the stained, slippery grass, the darkened mulberries, told only half the

[027-031]story. Above Marie and Emil, two white butterflies from Frank’s alfalfa-field

[027-032]were fluttering in and out among the interlacing shadows; diving and soaring,

[027-033]now close together, now far apart; and in the long grass by the fence the last

[027-034]wild roses of the year opened their pink hearts to die.

[027-035]When Ivar reached the path by the hedge, he saw Shabata’s rifle lying in the

[027-036]way. He turned and peered through the branches, falling upon his knees as if

[027-037]his legs had been mowed from under him. “Merciful God!” he groaned.

[027-038]Alexandra, too, had risen early that morning, because of her anxiety about

[027-039]Emil. She was in Emil’s room upstairs when, from the window, she saw Ivar

[027-040]coming along the path that led from the Shabatas’. He was running like a spent

[027-041]man, tottering and lurching from side to side. Ivar never drank, and Alexandra

[027-042]thought at once that one of his spells had come upon him, and that he must be

[027-043]in a very bad way indeed. She ran downstairs and hurried out to meet him, to

[027-044]hide his infirmity from the eyes of her household. The old man fell in the road

[027-045]at her feet and caught her hand, over which he bowed his shaggy head.

[027-046]“Mistress, mistress,” he sobbed, “it has fallen! Sin and death for the young

[027-047]ones! God have mercy upon us!”
