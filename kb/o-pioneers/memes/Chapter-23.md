[023-001]The next morning Angélique, Amédée’s wife, was in the kitchen baking pies,

[023-002]assisted by old Mrs. Chevalier. Between the mixing-board and the stove stood

[023-003]the old cradle that had been Amédée’s, and in it was his black-eyed son. As

[023-004]Angélique, flushed and excited, with flour on her hands, stopped to smile at

[023-005]the baby, [[emil-bergson|Emil Bergson]] rode up to the kitchen door on his mare and dismounted.

[023-006]“’Médée is out in the field, Emil,” Angélique called as she ran across the

[023-007]kitchen to the oven. “He begins to cut his wheat to-day; the first wheat ready

[023-008]to cut anywhere about here. He bought a new header, you know, because all the

[023-009]wheat’s so short this year. I hope he can rent it to the neighbors, it cost so

[023-010]much. He and his cousins bought a steam thresher on shares. You ought to go out

[023-011]and see that header work. I watched it an hour this morning, busy as I am with

[023-012]all the men to feed. He has a lot of hands, but he’s the only one that knows

[023-013]how to drive the header or how to run the engine, so he has to be everywhere at

[023-014]once. He’s sick, too, and ought to be in his bed.”

[023-015]Emil bent over Hector Baptiste, trying to make him blink his round, bead-like

[023-016]black eyes. “Sick? What’s the matter with your daddy, kid? Been making him walk

[023-017]the floor with you?”

[023-018]Angélique sniffed. “Not much! We don’t have that kind of babies. It was his

[023-019]father that kept Baptiste awake. All night I had to be getting up and making

[023-020]mustard plasters to put on his stomach. He had an awful colic. He said he felt

[023-021]better this morning, but I don’t think he ought to be out in the field,

[023-022]overheating himself.”

[023-023]Angélique did not speak with much anxiety, not because she was indifferent, but

[023-024]because she felt so secure in their good fortune. Only good things could happen

[023-025]to a rich, energetic, handsome young man like Amédée, with a new baby in the

[023-026]cradle and a new header in the field.

[023-027]Emil stroked the black fuzz on Baptiste’s head. “I say, Angélique, one of

[023-028]’Médée’s grandmothers, ’way back, must have been a squaw. This kid looks

[023-029]exactly like the Indian babies.”

[023-030]Angélique made a face at him, but old Mrs. Chevalier had been touched on a sore

[023-031]point, and she let out such a stream of fiery*patois*that Emil fled from

[023-032]the kitchen and mounted his mare.

[023-033]Opening the pasture gate from the saddle, Emil rode across the field to the

[023-034]clearing where the thresher stood, driven by a stationary engine and fed from

[023-035]the header boxes. As Amédée was not on the engine, Emil rode on to the

[023-036]wheatfield, where he recognized, on the header, the slight, wiry figure of his

[023-037]friend, coatless, his white shirt puffed out by the wind, his straw hat stuck

[023-038]jauntily on the side of his head. The six big work-horses that drew, or rather

[023-039]pushed, the header, went abreast at a rapid walk, and as they were still green

[023-040]at the work they required a good deal of management on Amédée’s part;

[023-041]especially when they turned the corners, where they divided, three and three,

[023-042]and then swung round into line again with a movement that looked as complicated

[023-043]as a wheel of artillery. Emil felt a new thrill of admiration for his friend,

[023-044]and with it the old pang of envy at the way in which Amédée could do with his

[023-045]might what his hand found to do, and feel that, whatever it was, it was the

[023-046]most important thing in the world. “I’ll have to bring Alexandra up to see this

[023-047]thing work,” Emil thought; “it’s splendid!”

[023-048]When he saw Emil, Amédée waved to him and called to one of his twenty cousins

[023-049]to take the reins. Stepping off the header without stopping it, he ran up to

[023-050]Emil who had dismounted. “Come along,” he called. “I have to go over to the

[023-051]engine for a minute. I gotta green man running it, and I gotta to keep an eye

[023-052]on him.”

[023-053]Emil thought the lad was unnaturally flushed and more excited than even the

[023-054]cares of managing a big farm at a critical time warranted. As they passed

[023-055]behind a last year’s stack, Amédée clutched at his right side and sank down for

[023-056]a moment on the straw.

[023-057]“Ouch! I got an awful pain in me, Emil. Something’s the matter with my insides,

[023-058]for sure.”

[023-059]Emil felt his fiery cheek. “You ought to go straight to bed, ’Médée, and

[023-060]telephone for the doctor; that’s what you ought to do.”

[023-061]Amédée staggered up with a gesture of despair. “How can I? I got no time to be

[023-062]sick. Three thousand dollars’ worth of new machinery to manage, and the wheat

[023-063]so ripe it will begin to shatter next week. My wheat’s short, but it’s gotta

[023-064]grand full berries. What’s he slowing down for? We haven’t got header boxes

[023-065]enough to feed the thresher, I guess.”

[023-066]Amédée started hot-foot across the stubble, leaning a little to the right as he

[023-067]ran, and waved to the engineer not to stop the engine.

[023-068]Emil saw that this was no time to talk about his own affairs. He mounted his

[023-069]mare and rode on to Sainte-Agnes, to bid his friends there good-bye. He went

[023-070]first to see [[raul-marcel|Raoul Marcel]], and found him innocently practising the “Gloria” for

[023-071]the big confirmation service on Sunday while he polished the mirrors of his

[023-072]father’s saloon.

[023-073]As Emil rode homewards at three o’clock in the afternoon, he saw Amédée

[023-074]staggering out of the wheatfield, supported by two of his cousins. Emil stopped

[023-075]and helped them put the boy to bed.
