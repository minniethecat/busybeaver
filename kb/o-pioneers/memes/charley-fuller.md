## Overview

Charley Fuller is a real estate man operating in the Hanover area during the early years of the Bergson family's settlement on the Divide. He takes advantage of the hard times—when many American farmers are abandoning their claims—to buy up land at low prices. Alexandra Bergson regards him with a mix of admiration for his business instincts and wariness of his opportunistic approach.

## Biography

Fuller appears during a conversation between Alexandra, Carl Linstrum, and Lou Bergson in which the difficulties of farming the high country are discussed. Lou reports that another settler, Chris Arnson, has traded his half-section to Fuller. Alexandra immediately recognizes Fuller as "the real estate man" and notes his strategy: "He's buying and trading for every bit of land he can get up here. It'll make him a rich man, some day" (004-163). Lou counters that Fuller "is rich now, that's why he can take a chance" (004-166).

Later in the same conversation, the Bergsons learn that another neighbor, Percy Adams, is also planning to sell out to Fuller. Alexandra exclaims, "There's Fuller again! I wish that man would take me for a partner. He's feathering his nest!" (004-177). She admires his business sense—"If only poor people could learn a little from rich people!" (004-178)—but her own strategy is the opposite of his speculative approach: she believes in holding onto the land for the long term.

## Relationships

- **[[Alexandra Bergson]]**: Alexandra admires Fuller's business instincts but refuses to sell land to him. She sees him as a man who understands that the land will one day be valuable, though she disagrees with his method of profiting from others' discouragement.
- **[[Lou Bergson]]**: Lou is the one who brings news of Fuller's transactions to the family discussion, seeing Fuller's success as evidence that only the already-wealthy can afford to speculate.

## Significance

Fuller represents the speculative, profit-driven relationship to the land that stands in contrast to Alexandra's patient, cultivating approach. Both recognize the land's future value, but Fuller acquires it by exploiting others' despair, while Alexandra holds and improves it. He embodies the commercial forces that surround the agrarian community, foreshadowing the eventual transformation of the frontier into real estate.
