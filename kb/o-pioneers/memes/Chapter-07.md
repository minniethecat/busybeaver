[007-001]Emil reached home a little past noon, and when he went into the kitchen

[007-002]Alexandra was already seated at the head of the long table, having dinner with

[007-003]her men, as she always did unless there were visitors. He slipped into his

[007-004]empty place at his sister’s right. The three pretty young Swedish girls who did

[007-005]Alexandra’s housework were cutting pies, refilling coffeecups, placing platters

[007-006]of bread and meat and potatoes upon the red tablecloth, and continually getting

[007-007]in each other’s way between the table and the stove. To be sure they always

[007-008]wasted a good deal of time getting in each other’s way and giggling at each

[007-009]other’s mistakes. But, as Alexandra had pointedly told her sisters-in-law, it

[007-010]was to hear them giggle that she kept three young things in her kitchen; the

[007-011]work she could do herself, if it were necessary. These girls, with their long

[007-012]letters from home, their finery, and their love-affairs, afforded her a great

[007-013]deal of entertainment, and they were company for her when Emil was away at

[007-014]school.

[007-015]Of the youngest girl, [[signa|Signa]], who has a pretty figure, mottled pink cheeks, and

[007-016]yellow hair, Alexandra is very fond, though she keeps a sharp eye upon her.

[007-017]Signa is apt to be skittish at mealtime, when the men are about, and to spill

[007-018]the coffee or upset the cream. It is supposed that Nelse Jensen, one of the six

[007-019]men at the dinner-table, is courting Signa, though he has been so careful not

[007-020]to commit himself that no one in the house, least of all Signa, can tell just

[007-021]how far the matter has progressed. Nelse watches her glumly as she waits upon

[007-022]the table, and in the evening he sits on a bench behind the stove with his

[007-023]DRAGHARMONIKA, playing mournful airs and watching her as she goes about her

[007-024]work. When Alexandra asked Signa whether she thought Nelse was in earnest, the

[007-025]poor child hid her hands under her apron and murmured, “I don’t know, ma’m. But

[007-026]he scolds me about everything, like as if he wanted to have me!”

[007-027]At Alexandra’s left sat a very old man, barefoot and wearing a long blue

[007-028]blouse, open at the neck. His shaggy head is scarcely whiter than it was

[007-029]sixteen years ago, but his little blue eyes have become pale and watery, and

[007-030]his ruddy face is withered, like an apple that has clung all winter to the

[007-031]tree. When [[ivar|Ivar]] lost his land through mismanagement a dozen years ago,

[007-032]Alexandra took him in, and he has been a member of her household ever since. He

[007-033]is too old to work in the fields, but he hitches and unhitches the work-teams

[007-034]and looks after the health of the stock. Sometimes of a winter evening

[007-035]Alexandra calls him into the sitting-room to read the Bible aloud to her, for

[007-036]he still reads very well. He dislikes human habitations, so Alexandra has

[007-037]fitted him up a room in the barn, where he is very comfortable, being near the

[007-038]horses and, as he says, further from temptations. No one has ever found out

[007-039]what his temptations are. In cold weather he sits by the kitchen fire and makes

[007-040]hammocks or mends harness until it is time to go to bed. Then he says his

[007-041]prayers at great length behind the stove, puts on his buffalo-skin coat and

[007-042]goes out to his room in the barn.

[007-043]Alexandra herself has changed very little. Her figure is fuller, and she has

[007-044]more color. She seems sunnier and more vigorous than she did as a young girl.

[007-045]But she still has the same calmness and deliberation of manner, the same clear

[007-046]eyes, and she still wears her hair in two braids wound round her head. It is so

[007-047]curly that fiery ends escape from the braids and make her head look like one of

[007-048]the big double sunflowers that fringe her vegetable garden. Her face is always

[007-049]tanned in summer, for her sunbonnet is oftener on her arm than on her head. But

[007-050]where her collar falls away from her neck, or where her sleeves are pushed back

[007-051]from her wrist, the skin is of such smoothness and whiteness as none but

[007-052]Swedish women ever possess; skin with the freshness of the snow itself.

[007-053]Alexandra did not talk much at the table, but she encouraged her men to talk,

[007-054]and she always listened attentively, even when they seemed to be talking

[007-055]foolishly.

[007-056]To-day Barney Flinn, the big red-headed Irishman who had been with Alexandra

[007-057]for five years and who was actually her foreman, though he had no such title,

[007-058]was grumbling about the new silo she had put up that spring. It happened to be

[007-059]the first silo on the Divide, and Alexandra’s neighbors and her men were

[007-060]skeptical about it. “To be sure, if the thing don’t work, we’ll have plenty of

[007-061]feed without it, indeed,” Barney conceded.

[007-062]Nelse Jensen, Signa’s gloomy suitor, had his word. “Lou, he says he wouldn’t

[007-063]have no silo on his place if you’d give it to him. He says the feed outen it

[007-064]gives the stock the bloat. He heard of somebody lost four head of horses,

[007-065]feedin’ ’em that stuff.”

[007-066]Alexandra looked down the table from one to another. “Well, the only way we can

[007-067]find out is to try. Lou and I have different notions about feeding stock, and

[007-068]that’s a good thing. It’s bad if all the members of a family think alike. They

[007-069]never get anywhere. Lou can learn by my mistakes and I can learn by his. Isn’t

[007-070]that fair, Barney?”

[007-071]The Irishman laughed. He had no love for Lou, who was always uppish with him

[007-072]and who said that Alexandra paid her hands too much. “I’ve no thought but to

[007-073]give the thing an honest try, mum. ’T would be only right, after puttin’ so

[007-074]much expense into it. Maybe Emil will come out an’ have a look at it wid me.”

[007-075]He pushed back his chair, took his hat from the nail, and marched out with

[007-076]Emil, who, with his university ideas, was supposed to have instigated the silo.

[007-077]The other hands followed them, all except old Ivar. He had been depressed

[007-078]throughout the meal and had paid no heed to the talk of the men, even when they

[007-079]mentioned cornstalk bloat, upon which he was sure to have opinions.

[007-080]“Did you want to speak to me, Ivar?” Alexandra asked as she rose from the

[007-081]table. “Come into the sitting-room.”

[007-082]The old man followed Alexandra, but when she motioned him to a chair he shook

[007-083]his head. She took up her workbasket and waited for him to speak. He stood

[007-084]looking at the carpet, his bushy head bowed, his hands clasped in front of him.

[007-085]Ivar’s bandy legs seemed to have grown shorter with years, and they were

[007-086]completely misfitted to his broad, thick body and heavy shoulders.

[007-087]“Well, Ivar, what is it?” Alexandra asked after she had waited longer than

[007-088]usual.

[007-089]Ivar had never learned to speak English and his Norwegian was quaint and grave,

[007-090]like the speech of the more old-fashioned people. He always addressed Alexandra

[007-091]in terms of the deepest respect, hoping to set a good example to the kitchen

[007-092]girls, whom he thought too familiar in their manners.

[007-093]“Mistress,” he began faintly, without raising his eyes, “the folk have been

[007-094]looking coldly at me of late. You know there has been talk.”

[007-095]“Talk about what, Ivar?”

[007-096]“[[About]] sending me away; to the asylum.”

[007-097]Alexandra put down her sewing-basket. “Nobody has come to me with such talk,”

[007-098]she said decidedly. “Why need you listen? You know I would never consent to

[007-099]such a thing.”

[007-100]Ivar lifted his shaggy head and looked at her out of his little eyes. “They say

[007-101]that you cannot prevent it if the folk complain of me, if your brothers

[007-102]complain to the authorities. They say that your brothers are afraid—God

[007-103]forbid!—that I may do you some injury when my spells are on me. Mistress, how

[007-104]can any one think that?—that I could bite the hand that fed me!” The tears

[007-105]trickled down on the old man’s beard.

[007-106]Alexandra frowned. “Ivar, I wonder at you, that you should come bothering me

[007-107]with such nonsense. I am still running my own house, and other people have

[007-108]nothing to do with either you or me. So long as I am suited with you, there is

[007-109]nothing to be said.”

[007-110]Ivar pulled a red handkerchief out of the breast of his blouse and wiped his

[007-111]eyes and beard. “But I should not wish you to keep me if, as they say, it is

[007-112]against your interests, and if it is hard for you to get hands because I am

[007-113]here.”

[007-114]Alexandra made an impatient gesture, but the old man put out his hand and went

[007-115]on earnestly:—

[007-116]“Listen, mistress, it is right that you should take these things into account.

[007-117]You know that my spells come from God, and that I would not harm any living

[007-118]creature. You believe that every one should worship God in the way revealed to

[007-119]him. But that is not the way of this country. The way here is for all to do

[007-120]alike. I am despised because I do not wear shoes, because I do not cut my hair,

[007-121]and because I have visions. At home, in the old country, there were many like

[007-122]me, who had been touched by God, or who had seen things in the graveyard at

[007-123]night and were different afterward. We thought nothing of it, and let them

[007-124]alone. But here, if a man is different in his feet or in his head, they put him

[007-125]in the asylum. Look at Peter Kralik; when he was a boy, drinking out of a

[007-126]creek, he swallowed a snake, and always after that he could eat only such food

[007-127]as the creature liked, for when he ate anything else, it became enraged and

[007-128]gnawed him. When he felt it whipping about in him, he drank alcohol to stupefy

[007-129]it and get some ease for himself. He could work as good as any man, and his

[007-130]head was clear, but they locked him up for being different in his stomach. That

[007-131]is the way; they have built the asylum for people who are different, and they

[007-132]will not even let us live in the holes with the badgers. Only your great

[007-133]prosperity has protected me so far. If you had had ill-fortune, they would have

[007-134]taken me to Hastings long ago.”

[007-135]As Ivar talked, his gloom lifted. Alexandra had found that she could often

[007-136]break his fasts and long penances by talking to him and letting him pour out

[007-137]the thoughts that troubled him. Sympathy always cleared his mind, and ridicule

[007-138]was poison to him.

[007-139]“There is a great deal in what you say, Ivar. Like as not they will be wanting

[007-140]to take me to Hastings because I have built a silo; and then I may take you

[007-141]with me. But at present I need you here. Only don’t come to me again telling me

[007-142]what people say. Let people go on talking as they like, and we will go on

[007-143]living as we think best. You have been with me now for twelve years, and I have

[007-144]gone to you for advice oftener than I have ever gone to any one. That ought to

[007-145]satisfy you.”

[007-146]Ivar bowed humbly. “Yes, mistress, I shall not trouble you with their talk

[007-147]again. And as for my feet, I have observed your wishes all these years, though

[007-148]you have never questioned me; washing them every night, even in winter.”

[007-149]Alexandra laughed. “Oh, never mind about your feet, Ivar. We can remember when

[007-150]half our neighbors went barefoot in summer. I expect old [[mrs-lee|Mrs. Lee]] would love to

[007-151]slip her shoes off now sometimes, if she dared. I’m glad I’m not Lou’s

[007-152]mother-in-law.”

[007-153]Ivar looked about mysteriously and lowered his voice almost to a whisper. “You

[007-154]know what they have over at Lou’s house? A great white tub, like the stone

[007-155]water-troughs in the old country, to wash themselves in. When you sent me over

[007-156]with the strawberries, they were all in town but the old woman Lee and the

[007-157]baby. She took me in and showed me the thing, and she told me it was impossible

[007-158]to wash yourself clean in it, because, in so much water, you could not make a

[007-159]strong suds. So when they fill it up and send her in there, she pretends, and

[007-160]makes a splashing noise. Then, when they are all asleep, she washes herself in

[007-161]a little wooden tub she keeps under her bed.”

[007-162]Alexandra shook with laughter. “Poor old Mrs. Lee! They won’t let her wear

[007-163]nightcaps, either. Never mind; when she comes to visit me, she can do all the

[007-164]old things in the old way, and have as much beer as she wants. We’ll start an

[007-165]asylum for old-time people, Ivar.”

[007-166]Ivar folded his big handkerchief carefully and thrust it back into his blouse.

[007-167]“This is always the way, mistress. I come to you sorrowing, and you send me

[007-168]away with a light heart. And will you be so good as to tell the Irishman that

[007-169]he is not to work the brown gelding until the sore on its shoulder is healed?”

[007-170]“That I will. Now go and put Emil’s mare to the cart. I am going to drive up to

[007-171]the north quarter to meet the man from town who is to buy my alfalfa hay.”
