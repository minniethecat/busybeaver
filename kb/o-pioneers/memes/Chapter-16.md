[016-001]Emil came home at about half-past seven o’clock that evening. Old [[ivar|Ivar]] met him

[016-002]at the windmill and took his horse, and the young man went directly into the

[016-003]house. He called to his sister and she answered from her bedroom, behind the

[016-004]sitting-room, saying that she was lying down.

[016-005]Emil went to her door.

[016-006]“Can I see you for a minute?” he asked. “I want to talk to you about something

[016-007]before Carl comes.”

[016-008]Alexandra rose quickly and came to the door. “Where is Carl?”

[016-009]“Lou and Oscar met us and said they wanted to talk to him, so he rode over to

[016-010]Oscar’s with them. Are you coming out?” Emil asked impatiently.

[016-011]“Yes, sit down. I’ll be dressed in a moment.”

[016-012]Alexandra closed her door, and Emil sank down on the old slat lounge and sat

[016-013]with his head in his hands. When his sister came out, he looked up, not knowing

[016-014]whether the interval had been short or long, and he was surprised to see that

[016-015]the room had grown quite dark. That was just as well; it would be easier to

[016-016]talk if he were not under the gaze of those clear, deliberate eyes, that saw so

[016-017]far in some directions and were so blind in others. Alexandra, too, was glad of

[016-018]the dusk. Her face was swollen from crying.

[016-019]Emil started up and then sat down again. “Alexandra,” he said slowly, in his

[016-020]deep young baritone, “I don’t want to go away to law school this fall. Let me

[016-021]put it off another year. I want to take a year off and look around. It’s

[016-022]awfully easy to rush into a profession you don’t really like, and awfully hard

[016-023]to get out of it. Linstrum and I have been talking about that.”

[016-024]“Very well, Emil. Only don’t go off looking for land.” She came up and put her

[016-025]hand on his shoulder. “I’ve been wishing you could stay with me this winter.”

[016-026]“That’s just what I don’t want to do, Alexandra. I’m restless. I want to go to

[016-027]a new place. I want to go down to the City of Mexico to join one of the

[016-028]University fellows who’s at the head of an electrical plant. He wrote me he

[016-029]could give me a little job, enough to pay my way, and I could look around and

[016-030]see what I want to do. I want to go as soon as harvest is over. I guess Lou and

[016-031]Oscar will be sore about it.”

[016-032]“I suppose they will.” Alexandra sat down on the lounge beside him. “They are

[016-033]very angry with me, Emil. We have had a quarrel. They will not come here

[016-034]again.”

[016-035]Emil scarcely heard what she was saying; he did not notice the sadness of her

[016-036]tone. He was thinking about the reckless life he meant to live in Mexico.

[016-037]“What about?” he asked absently.

[016-038]“[[About]] [[carl-linstrum|Carl Linstrum]]. They are afraid I am going to marry him, and that some of

[016-039]my property will get away from them.”

[016-040]Emil shrugged his shoulders. “What nonsense!” he murmured. “Just like them.”

[016-041]Alexandra drew back. “Why nonsense, Emil?”

[016-042]“Why, you’ve never thought of such a thing, have you? They always have to have

[016-043]something to fuss about.”

[016-044]“Emil,” said his sister slowly, “you ought not to take things for granted. Do

[016-045]you agree with them that I have no right to change my way of living?”

[016-046]Emil looked at the outline of his sister’s head in the dim light. They were

[016-047]sitting close together and he somehow felt that she could hear his thoughts. He

[016-048]was silent for a moment, and then said in an embarrassed tone, “Why, no,

[016-049]certainly not. You ought to do whatever you want to. I’ll always back you.”

[016-050]“But it would seem a little bit ridiculous to you if I married Carl?”

[016-051]Emil fidgeted. The issue seemed to him too far-fetched to warrant discussion.

[016-052]“Why, no. I should be surprised if you wanted to. I can’t see exactly why. But

[016-053]that’s none of my business. You ought to do as you please. Certainly you ought

[016-054]not to pay any attention to what the boys say.”

[016-055]Alexandra sighed. “I had hoped you might understand, a little, why I do want

[016-056]to. But I suppose that’s too much to expect. I’ve had a pretty lonely life,

[016-057]Emil. Besides Marie, Carl is the only friend I have ever had.”

[016-058]Emil was awake now; a name in her last sentence roused him. He put out his hand

[016-059]and took his sister’s awkwardly. “You ought to do just as you wish, and I think

[016-060]Carl’s a fine fellow. He and I would always get on. I don’t believe any of the

[016-061]things the boys say about him, honest I don’t. They are suspicious of him

[016-062]because he’s intelligent. You know their way. They’ve been sore at me ever

[016-063]since you let me go away to college. They’re always trying to catch me up. If I

[016-064]were you, I wouldn’t pay any attention to them. There’s nothing to get upset

[016-065]about. Carl’s a sensible fellow. He won’t mind them.”

[016-066]“I don’t know. If they talk to him the way they did to me, I think he’ll go

[016-067]away.”

[016-068]Emil grew more and more uneasy. “Think so? Well, Marie said it would serve us

[016-069]all right if you walked off with him.”

[016-070]“Did she? Bless her little heart! SHE would.” Alexandra’s voice broke.

[016-071]Emil began unlacing his leggings. “Why don’t you talk to her about it? There’s

[016-072]Carl, I hear his horse. I guess I’ll go upstairs and get my boots off. No, I

[016-073]don’t want any supper. We had supper at five o’clock, at the fair.”

[016-074]Emil was glad to escape and get to his own room. He was a little ashamed for

[016-075]his sister, though he had tried not to show it. He felt that there was

[016-076]something indecorous in her proposal, and she did seem to him somewhat

[016-077]ridiculous. There was trouble enough in the world, he reflected, as he threw

[016-078]himself upon his bed, without people who were forty years old imagining they

[016-079]wanted to get married. In the darkness and silence Emil was not likely to think

[016-080]long about Alexandra. Every image slipped away but one. He had seen Marie in

[016-081]the crowd that afternoon. She sold candy at the fair.*Why*had she ever

[016-082]run away with [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]], and how could she go on laughing and working and

[016-083]taking an interest in things? Why did she like so many people, and why had she

[016-084]seemed pleased when all the French and Bohemian boys, and the priest himself,

[016-085]crowded round her candy stand? Why did she care about any one but him? Why

[016-086]could he never, never find the thing he looked for in her playful, affectionate

[016-087]eyes?

[016-088]Then he fell to imagining that he looked once more and found it there, and what

[016-089]it would be like if she loved him,—she who, as Alexandra said, could give her

[016-090]whole heart. In that dream he could lie for hours, as if in a trance. His

[016-091]spirit went out of his body and crossed the fields to [[marie-shabata|Marie Shabata]].

[016-092]At the University dances the girls had often looked wonderingly at the tall

[016-093]young Swede with the fine head, leaning against the wall and frowning, his arms

[016-094]folded, his eyes fixed on the ceiling or the floor. All the girls were a little

[016-095]afraid of him. He was distinguished-looking, and not the jollying kind. They

[016-096]felt that he was too intense and preoccupied. There was something queer about

[016-097]him. Emil’s fraternity rather prided itself upon its dances, and sometimes he

[016-098]did his duty and danced every dance. But whether he was on the floor or

[016-099]brooding in a corner, he was always thinking about Marie Shabata. For two years

[016-100]the storm had been gathering in him.
