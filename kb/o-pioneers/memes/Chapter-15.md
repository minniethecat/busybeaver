[015-001]While Emil and Carl were amusing themselves at the fair, Alexandra was at home,

[015-002]busy with her account-books, which had been neglected of late. She was almost

[015-003]through with her figures when she heard a cart drive up to the gate, and

[015-004]looking out of the window she saw her two older brothers. They had seemed to

[015-005]avoid her ever since [[carl-linstrum|Carl Linstrum]]’s arrival, four weeks ago that day, and she

[015-006]hurried to the door to welcome them. She saw at once that they had come with

[015-007]some very definite purpose. They followed her stiffly into the sitting-room.

[015-008]Oscar sat down, but Lou walked over to the window and remained standing, his

[015-009]hands behind him.

[015-010]“You are by yourself?” he asked, looking toward the doorway into the parlor.

[015-011]“Yes. Carl and Emil went up to the Catholic fair.”

[015-012]For a few moments neither of the men spoke.

[015-013]Then Lou came out sharply. “How soon does he intend to go away from here?”

[015-014]“I don’t know, Lou. Not for some time, I hope.” Alexandra spoke in an even,

[015-015]quiet tone that often exasperated her brothers. They felt that she was trying

[015-016]to be superior with them.

[015-017]Oscar spoke up grimly. “We thought we ought to tell you that people have begun

[015-018]to talk,” he said meaningly.

[015-019]Alexandra looked at him. “What about?”

[015-020]Oscar met her eyes blankly. “[[About]] you, keeping him here so long. It looks bad

[015-021]for him to be hanging on to a woman this way. People think you’re getting taken

[015-022]in.”

[015-023]Alexandra shut her account-book firmly. “Boys,” she said seriously, “don’t

[015-024]let’s go on with this. We won’t come out anywhere. I can’t take advice on such

[015-025]a matter. I know you mean well, but you must not feel responsible for me in

[015-026]things of this sort. If we go on with this talk it will only make hard

[015-027]feeling.”

[015-028]Lou whipped about from the window. “You ought to think a little about your

[015-029]family. You’re making us all ridiculous.”

[015-030]“How am I?”

[015-031]“People are beginning to say you want to marry the fellow.”

[015-032]“Well, and what is ridiculous about that?”

[015-033]Lou and Oscar exchanged outraged looks. “Alexandra! Can’t you see he’s just a

[015-034]tramp and he’s after your money? He wants to be taken care of, he does!”

[015-035]“Well, suppose I want to take care of him? Whose business is it but my own?”

[015-036]“Don’t you know he’d get hold of your property?”

[015-037]“He’d get hold of what I wished to give him, certainly.”

[015-038]Oscar sat up suddenly and Lou clutched at his bristly hair.

[015-039]“Give him?” Lou shouted. “Our property, our homestead?”

[015-040]“I don’t know about the homestead,” said Alexandra quietly. “I know you and

[015-041]Oscar have always expected that it would be left to your children, and I’m not

[015-042]sure but what you’re right. But I’ll do exactly as I please with the rest of my

[015-043]land, boys.”

[015-044]“The rest of your land!” cried Lou, growing more excited every minute. “Didn’t

[015-045]all the land come out of the homestead? It was bought with money borrowed on

[015-046]the homestead, and Oscar and me worked ourselves to the bone paying interest on

[015-047]it.”

[015-048]“Yes, you paid the interest. But when you married we made a division of the

[015-049]land, and you were satisfied. I’ve made more on my farms since I’ve been alone

[015-050]than when we all worked together.”

[015-051]“Everything you’ve made has come out of the original land that us boys worked

[015-052]for, hasn’t it? The farms and all that comes out of them belongs to us as a

[015-053]family.”

[015-054]Alexandra waved her hand impatiently. “Come now, Lou. Stick to the facts. You

[015-055]are talking nonsense. Go to the county clerk and ask him who owns my land, and

[015-056]whether my titles are good.”

[015-057]Lou turned to his brother. “This is what comes of letting a woman meddle in

[015-058]business,” he said bitterly. “We ought to have taken things in our own hands

[015-059]years ago. But she liked to run things, and we humored her. We thought you had

[015-060]good sense, Alexandra. We never thought you’d do anything foolish.”

[015-061]Alexandra rapped impatiently on her desk with her knuckles. “Listen, Lou. Don’t

[015-062]talk wild. You say you ought to have taken things into your own hands years

[015-063]ago. I suppose you mean before you left home. But how could you take hold of

[015-064]what wasn’t there? I’ve got most of what I have now since we divided the

[015-065]property; I’ve built it up myself, and it has nothing to do with you.”

[015-066]Oscar spoke up solemnly. “The property of a family really belongs to the men of

[015-067]the family, no matter about the title. If anything goes wrong, it’s the men

[015-068]that are held responsible.”

[015-069]“Yes, of course,” Lou broke in. “Everybody knows that. Oscar and me have always

[015-070]been easy-going and we’ve never made any fuss. We were willing you should hold

[015-071]the land and have the good of it, but you got no right to part with any of it.

[015-072]We worked in the fields to pay for the first land you bought, and whatever’s

[015-073]come out of it has got to be kept in the family.”

[015-074]Oscar reinforced his brother, his mind fixed on the one point he could see.

[015-075]“The property of a family belongs to the men of the family, because they are

[015-076]held responsible, and because they do the work.”

[015-077]Alexandra looked from one to the other, her eyes full of indignation. She had

[015-078]been impatient before, but now she was beginning to feel angry. “And what about

[015-079]my work?” she asked in an unsteady voice.

[015-080]Lou looked at the carpet. “Oh, now, Alexandra, you always took it pretty easy!

[015-081]Of course we wanted you to. You liked to manage round, and we always humored

[015-082]you. We realize you were a great deal of help to us. There’s no woman anywhere

[015-083]around that knows as much about business as you do, and we’ve always been proud

[015-084]of that, and thought you were pretty smart. But, of course, the real work

[015-085]always fell on us. Good advice is all right, but it don’t get the weeds out of

[015-086]the corn.”

[015-087]“Maybe not, but it sometimes puts in the crop, and it sometimes keeps the

[015-088]fields for corn to grow in,” said Alexandra dryly. “Why, Lou, I can remember

[015-089]when you and Oscar wanted to sell this homestead and all the improvements to

[015-090]old preacher Ericson for two thousand dollars. If I’d consented, you’d have

[015-091]gone down to the river and scraped along on poor farms for the rest of your

[015-092]lives. When I put in our first field of alfalfa you both opposed me, just

[015-093]because I first heard about it from a young man who had been to the University.

[015-094]You said I was being taken in then, and all the neighbors said so. You know as

[015-095]well as I do that alfalfa has been the salvation of this country. You all

[015-096]laughed at me when I said our land here was about ready for wheat, and I had to

[015-097]raise three big wheat crops before the neighbors quit putting all their land in

[015-098]corn. Why, I remember you cried, Lou, when we put in the first big

[015-099]wheat-planting, and said everybody was laughing at us.”

[015-100]Lou turned to Oscar. “That’s the woman of it; if she tells you to put in a

[015-101]crop, she thinks she’s put it in. It makes women conceited to meddle in

[015-102]business. I shouldn’t think you’d want to remind us how hard you were on us,

[015-103]Alexandra, after the way you baby Emil.”

[015-104]“Hard on you? I never meant to be hard. Conditions were hard. Maybe I would

[015-105]never have been very soft, anyhow; but I certainly didn’t choose to be the kind

[015-106]of girl I was. If you take even a vine and cut it back again and again, it

[015-107]grows hard, like a tree.”

[015-108]Lou felt that they were wandering from the point, and that in digression

[015-109]Alexandra might unnerve him. He wiped his forehead with a jerk of his

[015-110]handkerchief. “We never doubted you, Alexandra. We never questioned anything

[015-111]you did. You’ve always had your own way. But you can’t expect us to sit like

[015-112]stumps and see you done out of the property by any loafer who happens along,

[015-113]and making yourself ridiculous into the bargain.”

[015-114]Oscar rose. “Yes,” he broke in, “everybody’s laughing to see you get took in;

[015-115]at your age, too. Everybody knows he’s nearly five years younger than you, and

[015-116]is after your money. Why, Alexandra, you are forty years old!”

[015-117]“All that doesn’t concern anybody but Carl and me. Go to town and ask your

[015-118]lawyers what you can do to restrain me from disposing of my own property. And I

[015-119]advise you to do what they tell you; for the authority you can exert by law is

[015-120]the only influence you will ever have over me again.” Alexandra rose. “I think

[015-121]I would rather not have lived to find out what I have to-day,” she said

[015-122]quietly, closing her desk.

[015-123]Lou and Oscar looked at each other questioningly. There seemed to be nothing to

[015-124]do but to go, and they walked out.

[015-125]“You can’t do business with women,” Oscar said heavily as he clambered into the

[015-126]cart. “But anyhow, we’ve had our say, at last.”

[015-127]Lou scratched his head. “Talk of that kind might come too high, you know; but

[015-128]she’s apt to be sensible. You hadn’t ought to said that about her age, though,

[015-129]Oscar. I’m afraid that hurt her feelings; and the worst thing we can do is to

[015-130]make her sore at us. She’d marry him out of contrariness.”

[015-131]“I only meant,” said Oscar, “that she is old enough to know better, and she is.

[015-132]If she was going to marry, she ought to done it long ago, and not go making a

[015-133]fool of herself now.”

[015-134]Lou looked anxious, nevertheless. “Of course,” he reflected hopefully and

[015-135]inconsistently, “Alexandra ain’t much like other women-folks. Maybe it won’t

[015-136]make her sore. Maybe she’d as soon be forty as not!”
