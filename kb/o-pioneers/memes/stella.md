## Overview

Stella is the younger daughter of [[Annie Lee]] and [[Lou Bergson]], appearing briefly in Chapter 8 during [[Alexandra Bergson]]'s visit to the Lee household. Though a minor presence in the novel, she and her sister Milly represent the second generation of immigrant children growing up on the Nebraska Divide — the inheritors of the land their grandparents and parents struggled to tame.

## Biography

Stella appears in a single domestic scene in Chapter 8, when Alexandra visits the home of her brother Lou and his wife Annie Lee. During the visit, Alexandra brings up the subject of music and inheritance, recalling how her father [[John Bergson]] had a sweet tenor voice. She gestures to Stella while speaking: "I can remember hearing him singing with the sailors down in the shipyard, when I was no bigger than Stella here." (008-106)

Later in the same scene, "Milly and Stella both looked through the door into the sitting-room, where a crayon portrait of John Bergson hung on the wall." (008-107) The portrait, made from a photograph taken just before John Bergson left Sweden, shows a slender man of thirty-five with wondering, sad eyes — an image of the immigrant generation that Stella and her sister will never fully know.

## Relationships

Stella is the daughter of [[Lou Bergson]] and [[Annie Lee]], and the granddaughter of [[John Bergson]] through Lou. Her sister **Milly** appears alongside her in their brief scene together. (008-107) She is also the niece of [[Alexandra Bergson]], whose visit provides the occasion for Stella's only appearance in the narrative.

Through her mother Annie, Stella connects to the larger network of Swedish immigrant families who settled the Divide. Her uncle is [[Oscar Bergson]], Lou's brother, who farms his own land nearby.

## Significance

Stella's presence in the novel, though minimal, serves a structural purpose. She represents the generation that will inherit the Divide — children who know only the transformed landscape their elders wrested from wild prairie. When Alexandra compares herself at Stella's age to Stella now, the contrast underlines how much has changed: Alexandra's childhood was spent in the desperate early years of settlement, while Stella grows up in a world of established farms, pianos, and crayon portraits of ancestors.

The scene also reinforces the novel's central concern with inheritance — not just of land, but of memory. Stella and Milly looking "through the door into the sitting-room" at their grandfather's portrait (008-107) suggests the distance between generations, a gap that the novel both acknowledges and attempts to bridge through its acts of remembrance.
