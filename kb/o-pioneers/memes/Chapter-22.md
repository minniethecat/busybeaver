[022-001]One evening, a week after [[signa|Signa]]’s wedding, Emil was kneeling before a box in

[022-002]the sitting-room, packing his books. From time to time he rose and wandered

[022-003]about the house, picking up stray volumes and bringing them listlessly back to

[022-004]his box. He was packing without enthusiasm. He was not very sanguine about his

[022-005]future. Alexandra sat sewing by the table. She had helped him pack his trunk in

[022-006]the afternoon. As Emil came and went by her chair with his books, he thought to

[022-007]himself that it had not been so hard to leave his sister since he first went

[022-008]away to school. He was going directly to [[omaha|Omaha]], to read law in the office of a

[022-009]Swedish lawyer until October, when he would enter the law school at Ann Arbor.

[022-010]They had planned that Alexandra was to come to Michigan—a long journey for

[022-011]her—at Christmas time, and spend several weeks with him. Nevertheless, he felt

[022-012]that this leave-taking would be more final than his earlier ones had been; that

[022-013]it meant a definite break with his old home and the beginning of something

[022-014]new—he did not know what. His ideas about the future would not crystallize; the

[022-015]more he tried to think about it, the vaguer his conception of it became. But

[022-016]one thing was clear, he told himself; it was high time that he made good to

[022-017]Alexandra, and that ought to be incentive enough to begin with.

[022-018]As he went about gathering up his books he felt as if he were uprooting things.

[022-019]At last he threw himself down on the old slat lounge where he had slept when he

[022-020]was little, and lay looking up at the familiar cracks in the ceiling.

[022-021]“Tired, Emil?” his sister asked.

[022-022]“Lazy,” he murmured, turning on his side and looking at her. He studied

[022-023]Alexandra’s face for a long time in the lamplight. It had never occurred to him

[022-024]that his sister was a handsome woman until [[marie-shabata|Marie Shabata]] had told him so.

[022-025]Indeed, he had never thought of her as being a woman at all, only a sister. As

[022-026]he studied her bent head, he looked up at the picture of [[john-bergson|John Bergson]] above the

[022-027]lamp. “No,” he thought to himself, “she didn’t get it there. I suppose I am

[022-028]more like that.”

[022-029]“Alexandra,” he said suddenly, “that old walnut secretary you use for a desk

[022-030]was father’s, wasn’t it?”

[022-031]Alexandra went on stitching. “Yes. It was one of the first things he bought for

[022-032]the old log house. It was a great extravagance in those days. But he wrote a

[022-033]great many letters back to the old country. He had many friends there, and they

[022-034]wrote to him up to the time he died. No one ever blamed him for grandfather’s

[022-035]disgrace. I can see him now, sitting there on Sundays, in his white shirt,

[022-036]writing pages and pages, so carefully. He wrote a fine, regular hand, almost

[022-037]like engraving. Yours is something like his, when you take pains.”

[022-038]“Grandfather was really crooked, was he?”

[022-039]“He married an unscrupulous woman, and then—then I’m afraid he was really

[022-040]crooked. When we first came here father used to have dreams about making a

[022-041]great fortune and going back to Sweden to pay back to the poor sailors the

[022-042]money grandfather had lost.”

[022-043]Emil stirred on the lounge. “I say, that would have been worth while, wouldn’t

[022-044]it? Father wasn’t a bit like Lou or Oscar, was he? I can’t remember much about

[022-045]him before he got sick.”

[022-046]“Oh, not at all!” Alexandra dropped her sewing on her knee. “He had better

[022-047]opportunities; not to make money, but to make something of himself. He was a

[022-048]quiet man, but he was very intelligent. You would have been proud of him,

[022-049]Emil.”

[022-050]Alexandra felt that he would like to know there had been a man of his kin whom

[022-051]he could admire. She knew that Emil was ashamed of Lou and Oscar, because they

[022-052]were bigoted and self-satisfied. He never said much about them, but she could

[022-053]feel his disgust. His brothers had shown their disapproval of him ever since he

[022-054]first went away to school. The only thing that would have satisfied them would

[022-055]have been his failure at the University. As it was, they resented every change

[022-056]in his speech, in his dress, in his point of view; though the latter they had

[022-057]to conjecture, for Emil avoided talking to them about any but family matters.

[022-058]All his interests they treated as affectations.

[022-059]Alexandra took up her sewing again. “I can remember father when he was quite a

[022-060]young man. He belonged to some kind of a musical society, a male chorus, in

[022-061]Stockholm. I can remember going with mother to hear them sing. There must have

[022-062]been a hundred of them, and they all wore long black coats and white neckties.

[022-063]I was used to seeing father in a blue coat, a sort of jacket, and when I

[022-064]recognized him on the platform, I was very proud. Do you remember that Swedish

[022-065]song he taught you, about the ship boy?”

[022-066]“Yes. I used to sing it to the Mexicans. They like anything different.” Emil

[022-067]paused. “Father had a hard fight here, didn’t he?” he added thoughtfully.

[022-068]“Yes, and he died in a dark time. Still, he had hope. He believed in the land.”

[022-069]“And in you, I guess,” Emil said to himself. There was another period of

[022-070]silence; that warm, friendly silence, full of perfect understanding, in which

[022-071]Emil and Alexandra had spent many of their happiest half-hours.

[022-072]At last Emil said abruptly, “Lou and Oscar would be better off if they were

[022-073]poor, wouldn’t they?”

[022-074]Alexandra smiled. “Maybe. But their children wouldn’t. I have great hopes of

[022-075][[milly|Milly]].”

[022-076]Emil shivered. “I don’t know. Seems to me it gets worse as it goes on. The

[022-077]worst of the Swedes is that they’re never willing to find out how much they

[022-078]don’t know. It was like that at the University. Always so pleased with

[022-079]themselves! There’s no getting behind that conceited Swedish grin. The

[022-080]Bohemians and Germans were so different.”

[022-081]“Come, Emil, don’t go back on your own people. Father wasn’t conceited, Uncle

[022-082]Otto wasn’t. Even Lou and Oscar weren’t when they were boys.”

[022-083]Emil looked incredulous, but he did not dispute the point. He turned on his

[022-084]back and lay still for a long time, his hands locked under his head, looking up

[022-085]at the ceiling. Alexandra knew that he was thinking of many things. She felt no

[022-086]anxiety about Emil. She had always believed in him, as she had believed in the

[022-087]land. He had been more like himself since he got back from Mexico; seemed glad

[022-088]to be at home, and talked to her as he used to do. She had no doubt that his

[022-089]wandering fit was over, and that he would soon be settled in life.

[022-090]“Alexandra,” said Emil suddenly, “do you remember the wild duck we saw down on

[022-091]the river that time?”

[022-092]His sister looked up. “I often think of her. It always seems to me she’s there

[022-093]still, just like we saw her.”

[022-094]“I know. It’s queer what things one remembers and what things one forgets.”

[022-095]Emil yawned and sat up. “Well, it’s time to turn in.” He rose, and going over

[022-096]to Alexandra stooped down and kissed her lightly on the cheek. “Good-night,

[022-097]sister. I think you did pretty well by us.”

[022-098]Emil took up his lamp and went upstairs. Alexandra sat finishing his new

[022-099]nightshirt, that must go in the top tray of his trunk.
