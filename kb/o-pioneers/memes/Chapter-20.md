[020-001]The French Church, properly the Church of Sainte-Agnes, stood upon a hill. The

[020-002]high, narrow, red-brick building, with its tall steeple and steep roof, could

[020-003]be seen for miles across the wheatfields, though the little town of

[020-004]Sainte-Agnes was completely hidden away at the foot of the hill. The church

[020-005]looked powerful and triumphant there on its eminence, so high above the rest of

[020-006]the landscape, with miles of warm color lying at its feet, and by its position

[020-007]and setting it reminded one of some of the churches built long ago in the

[020-008]wheat-lands of middle France.

[020-009]Late one June afternoon [[alexandra-bergson|Alexandra Bergson]] was driving along one of the many

[020-010]roads that led through the rich French farming country to the big church. The

[020-011]sunlight was shining directly in her face, and there was a blaze of light all

[020-012]about the red church on the hill. Beside Alexandra lounged a strikingly exotic

[020-013]figure in a tall Mexican hat, a silk sash, and a black velvet jacket sewn with

[020-014]silver buttons. Emil had returned only the night before, and his sister was so

[020-015]proud of him that she decided at once to take him up to the church supper, and

[020-016]to make him wear the Mexican costume he had brought home in his trunk. “All the

[020-017]girls who have stands are going to wear fancy costumes,” she argued, “and some

[020-018]of the boys. Marie is going to tell fortunes, and she sent to [[omaha|Omaha]] for a

[020-019]Bohemian dress her father brought back from a visit to the old country. If you

[020-020]wear those clothes, they will all be pleased. And you must take your guitar.

[020-021]Everybody ought to do what they can to help along, and we have never done much.

[020-022]We are not a talented family.”

[020-023]The supper was to be at six o’clock, in the basement of the church, and

[020-024]afterward there would be a fair, with charades and an auction. Alexandra had

[020-025]set out from home early, leaving the house to [[signa|Signa]] and Nelse Jensen, who were

[020-026]to be married next week. Signa had shyly asked to have the wedding put off

[020-027]until Emil came home.

[020-028]Alexandra was well satisfied with her brother. As they drove through the

[020-029]rolling French country toward the westering sun and the stalwart church, she

[020-030]was thinking of that time long ago when she and Emil drove back from the river

[020-031]valley to the still unconquered Divide. Yes, she told herself, it had been

[020-032]worth while; both Emil and the country had become what she had hoped. Out of

[020-033]her father’s children there was one who was fit to cope with the world, who had

[020-034]not been tied to the plow, and who had a personality apart from the soil. And

[020-035]that, she reflected, was what she had worked for. She felt well satisfied with

[020-036]her life.

[020-037]When they reached the church, a score of teams were hitched in front of the

[020-038]basement doors that opened from the hillside upon the sanded terrace, where the

[020-039]boys wrestled and had jumping-matches. Amédée Chevalier, a proud father of one

[020-040]week, rushed out and embraced Emil. Amédée was an only son,—hence he was a very

[020-041]rich young man,—but he meant to have twenty children himself, like his uncle

[020-042]Xavier. “Oh, Emil,” he cried, hugging his old friend rapturously, “why ain’t

[020-043]you been up to see my boy? You come to-morrow, sure? Emil, you wanna get a boy

[020-044]right off! It’s the greatest thing ever! No, no, no! Angel not sick at all.

[020-045]Everything just fine. That boy he come into this world laughin’, and he been

[020-046]laughin’ ever since. You come an’ see!” He pounded Emil’s ribs to emphasize

[020-047]each announcement.

[020-048]Emil caught his arms. “Stop, Amédée. You’re knocking the wind out of me. I

[020-049]brought him cups and spoons and blankets and moccasins enough for an orphan

[020-050]asylum. I’m awful glad it’s a boy, sure enough!”

[020-051]The young men crowded round Emil to admire his costume and to tell him in a

[020-052]breath everything that had happened since he went away. Emil had more friends

[020-053]up here in the French country than down on [[norway-creek|Norway Creek]]. The French and

[020-054]Bohemian boys were spirited and jolly, liked variety, and were as much

[020-055]predisposed to favor anything new as the Scandinavian boys were to reject it.

[020-056]The Norwegian and Swedish lads were much more self-centred, apt to be

[020-057]egotistical and jealous. They were cautious and reserved with Emil because he

[020-058]had been away to college, and were prepared to take him down if he should try

[020-059]to put on airs with them. The French boys liked a bit of swagger, and they were

[020-060]always delighted to hear about anything new: new clothes, new games, new songs,

[020-061]new dances. Now they carried Emil off to show him the club room they had just

[020-062]fitted up over the post-office, down in the village. They ran down the hill in

[020-063]a drove, all laughing and chattering at once, some in French, some in English.

[020-064]Alexandra went into the cool, whitewashed basement where the women were setting

[020-065]the tables. Marie was standing on a chair, building a little tent of shawls

[020-066]where she was to tell fortunes. She sprang down and ran toward Alexandra,

[020-067]stopping short and looking at her in disappointment. Alexandra nodded to her

[020-068]encouragingly.

[020-069]“Oh, he will be here, Marie. The boys have taken him off to show him something.

[020-070]You won’t know him. He is a man now, sure enough. I have no boy left. He smokes

[020-071]terrible-smelling Mexican cigarettes and talks Spanish. How pretty you look,

[020-072]child. Where did you get those beautiful earrings?”

[020-073]“They belonged to father’s mother. He always promised them to me. He sent them

[020-074]with the dress and said I could keep them.”

[020-075]Marie wore a short red skirt of stoutly woven cloth, a white bodice and kirtle,

[020-076]a yellow silk turban wound low over her brown curls, and long coral pendants in

[020-077]her ears. Her ears had been pierced against a piece of cork by her great-aunt

[020-078]when she was seven years old. In those germless days she had worn bits of

[020-079]broom-straw, plucked from the common sweeping-broom, in the lobes until the

[020-080]holes were healed and ready for little gold rings.

[020-081]When Emil came back from the village, he lingered outside on the terrace with

[020-082]the boys. Marie could hear him talking and strumming on his guitar while Raoul

[020-083]Marcel sang falsetto. She was vexed with him for staying out there. It made her

[020-084]very nervous to hear him and not to see him; for, certainly, she told herself,

[020-085]she was not going out to look for him. When the supper bell rang and the boys

[020-086]came trooping in to get seats at the first table, she forgot all about her

[020-087]annoyance and ran to greet the tallest of the crowd, in his conspicuous attire.

[020-088]She didn’t mind showing her embarrassment at all. She blushed and laughed

[020-089]excitedly as she gave Emil her hand, and looked delightedly at the black velvet

[020-090]coat that brought out his fair skin and fine blond head. Marie was incapable of

[020-091]being lukewarm about anything that pleased her. She simply did not know how to

[020-092]give a half-hearted response. When she was delighted, she was as likely as not

[020-093]to stand on her tip-toes and clap her hands. If people laughed at her, she

[020-094]laughed with them.

[020-095]“Do the men wear clothes like that every day, in the street?” She caught Emil

[020-096]by his sleeve and turned him about. “Oh, I wish I lived where people wore

[020-097]things like that! Are the buttons real silver? Put on the hat, please. What a

[020-098]heavy thing! How do you ever wear it? Why don’t you tell us about the

[020-099]bull-fights?”

[020-100]She wanted to wring all his experiences from him at once, without waiting a

[020-101]moment. Emil smiled tolerantly and stood looking down at her with his old,

[020-102]brooding gaze, while the French girls fluttered about him in their white

[020-103]dresses and ribbons, and Alexandra watched the scene with pride. Several of the

[020-104]French girls, Marie knew, were hoping that Emil would take them to supper, and

[020-105]she was relieved when he took only his sister. Marie caught Frank’s arm and

[020-106]dragged him to the same table, managing to get seats opposite the Bergsons, so

[020-107]that she could hear what they were talking about. Alexandra made Emil tell Mrs.

[020-108]Xavier Chevalier, the mother of the twenty, about how he had seen a famous

[020-109]matador killed in the bull-ring. Marie listened to every word, only taking her

[020-110]eyes from Emil to watch Frank’s plate and keep it filled. When Emil finished

[020-111]his account,—bloody enough to satisfy Mrs. Xavier and to make her feel thankful

[020-112]that she was not a matador,—Marie broke out with a volley of questions. How did

[020-113]the women dress when they went to bull-fights? Did they wear mantillas? Did

[020-114]they never wear hats?

[020-115]After supper the young people played charades for the amusement of their

[020-116]elders, who sat gossiping between their guesses. All the shops in Sainte-Agnes

[020-117]were closed at eight o’clock that night, so that the merchants and their clerks

[020-118]could attend the fair. The auction was the liveliest part of the entertainment,

[020-119]for the French boys always lost their heads when they began to bid, satisfied

[020-120]that their extravagance was in a good cause. After all the pincushions and sofa

[020-121]pillows and embroidered slippers were sold, Emil precipitated a panic by taking

[020-122]out one of his turquoise shirt studs, which every one had been admiring, and

[020-123]handing it to the auctioneer. All the French girls clamored for it, and their

[020-124]sweethearts bid against each other recklessly. Marie wanted it, too, and she

[020-125]kept making signals to Frank, which he took a sour pleasure in disregarding. He

[020-126]didn’t see the use of making a fuss over a fellow just because he was dressed

[020-127]like a clown. When the turquoise went to Malvina Sauvage, the French banker’s

[020-128]daughter, Marie shrugged her shoulders and betook herself to her little tent of

[020-129]shawls, where she began to shuffle her cards by the light of a tallow candle,

[020-130]calling out, “Fortunes, fortunes!”

[020-131]The young priest, Father Duchesne, went first to have his fortune read. Marie

[020-132]took his long white hand, looked at it, and then began to run off her cards. “I

[020-133]see a long journey across water for you, Father. You will go to a town all cut

[020-134]up by water; built on islands, it seems to be, with rivers and green fields all

[020-135]about. And you will visit an old lady with a white cap and gold hoops in her

[020-136]ears, and you will be very happy there.”

[020-137]“Mais, oui,” said the priest, with a melancholy smile. “C’est L’Isle-Adam, chez

[020-138]ma mère. Vous êtes très savante, ma fille.” He patted her yellow turban,

[020-139]calling, “Venez donc, mes garçons! Il y a ici une véritable clairvoyante!”

[020-140]Marie was clever at fortune-telling, indulging in a light irony that amused the

[020-141]crowd. She told old Brunot, the miser, that he would lose all his money, marry

[020-142]a girl of sixteen, and live happily on a crust. Sholte, the fat Russian boy,

[020-143]who lived for his stomach, was to be disappointed in love, grow thin, and shoot

[020-144]himself from despondency. Amédée was to have twenty children, and nineteen of

[020-145]them were to be girls. Amédée slapped Frank on the back and asked him why he

[020-146]didn’t see what the fortune-teller would promise him. But Frank shook off his

[020-147]friendly hand and grunted, “She tell my fortune long ago; bad enough!” Then he

[020-148]withdrew to a corner and sat glowering at his wife.

[020-149]Frank’s case was all the more painful because he had no one in particular to

[020-150]fix his jealousy upon. Sometimes he could have thanked the man who would bring

[020-151]him evidence against his wife. He had discharged a good farm-boy, Jan Smirka,

[020-152]because he thought Marie was fond of him; but she had not seemed to miss Jan

[020-153]when he was gone, and she had been just as kind to the next boy. The farm-hands

[020-154]would always do anything for Marie; Frank couldn’t find one so surly that he

[020-155]would not make an effort to please her. At the bottom of his heart Frank knew

[020-156]well enough that if he could once give up his grudge, his wife would come back

[020-157]to him. But he could never in the world do that. The grudge was fundamental.

[020-158]Perhaps he could not have given it up if he had tried. Perhaps he got more

[020-159]satisfaction out of feeling himself abused than he would have got out of being

[020-160]loved. If he could once have made Marie thoroughly unhappy, he might have

[020-161]relented and raised her from the dust. But she had never humbled herself. In

[020-162]the first days of their love she had been his slave; she had admired him

[020-163]abandonedly. But the moment he began to bully her and to be unjust, she began

[020-164]to draw away; at first in tearful amazement, then in quiet, unspoken disgust.

[020-165]The distance between them had widened and hardened. It no longer contracted and

[020-166]brought them suddenly together. The spark of her life went somewhere else, and

[020-167]he was always watching to surprise it. He knew that somewhere she must get a

[020-168]feeling to live upon, for she was not a woman who could live without loving. He

[020-169]wanted to prove to himself the wrong he felt. What did she hide in her heart?

[020-170]Where did it go? Even Frank had his churlish delicacies; he never reminded her

[020-171]of how much she had once loved him. For that Marie was grateful to him.

[020-172]While Marie was chattering to the French boys, Amédée called Emil to the back

[020-173]of the room and whispered to him that they were going to play a joke on the

[020-174]girls. At eleven o’clock, Amédée was to go up to the switchboard in the

[020-175]vestibule and turn off the electric lights, and every boy would have a chance

[020-176]to kiss his sweetheart before Father Duchesne could find his way up the stairs

[020-177]to turn the current on again. The only difficulty was the candle in Marie’s

[020-178]tent; perhaps, as Emil had no sweetheart, he would oblige the boys by blowing

[020-179]out the candle. Emil said he would undertake to do that.

[020-180]At five minutes to eleven he sauntered up to Marie’s booth, and the French boys

[020-181]dispersed to find their girls. He leaned over the card-table and gave himself

[020-182]up to looking at her. “Do you think you could tell my fortune?” he murmured. It

[020-183]was the first word he had had alone with her for almost a year. “My luck hasn’t

[020-184]changed any. It’s just the same.”

[020-185]Marie had often wondered whether there was anyone else who could look his

[020-186]thoughts to you as Emil could. To-night, when she met his steady, powerful

[020-187]eyes, it was impossible not to feel the sweetness of the dream he was dreaming;

[020-188]it reached her before she could shut it out, and hid itself in her heart. She

[020-189]began to shuffle her cards furiously. “I’m angry with you, Emil,” she broke out

[020-190]with petulance. “Why did you give them that lovely blue stone to sell? You

[020-191]might have known Frank wouldn’t buy it for me, and I wanted it awfully!”

[020-192]Emil laughed shortly. “People who want such little things surely ought to have

[020-193]them,” he said dryly. He thrust his hand into the pocket of his velvet trousers

[020-194]and brought out a handful of uncut turquoises, as big as marbles. Leaning over

[020-195]the table he dropped them into her lap. “There, will those do? Be careful,

[020-196]don’t let any one see them. Now, I suppose you want me to go away and let you

[020-197]play with them?”

[020-198]Marie was gazing in rapture at the soft blue color of the stones. “Oh, Emil! Is

[020-199]everything down there beautiful like these? How could you ever come away?”

[020-200]At that instant Amédée laid hands on the switchboard. There was a shiver and a

[020-201]giggle, and every one looked toward the red blur that Marie’s candle made in

[020-202]the dark. Immediately that, too, was gone. Little shrieks and currents of soft

[020-203]laughter ran up and down the dark hall. Marie started up,—directly into Emil’s

[020-204]arms. In the same instant she felt his lips. The veil that had hung uncertainly

[020-205]between them for so long was dissolved. Before she knew what she was doing, she

[020-206]had committed herself to that kiss that was at once a boy’s and a man’s, as

[020-207]timid as it was tender; so like Emil and so unlike any one else in the world.

[020-208]Not until it was over did she realize what it meant. And Emil, who had so often

[020-209]imagined the shock of this first kiss, was surprised at its gentleness and

[020-210]naturalness. It was like a sigh which they had breathed together; almost

[020-211]sorrowful, as if each were afraid of wakening something in the other.

[020-212]When the lights came on again, everybody was laughing and shouting, and all the

[020-213]French girls were rosy and shining with mirth. Only Marie, in her little tent

[020-214]of shawls, was pale and quiet. Under her yellow turban the red coral pendants

[020-215]swung against white cheeks. Frank was still staring at her, but he seemed to

[020-216]see nothing. Years ago, he himself had had the power to take the blood from her

[020-217]cheeks like that. Perhaps he did not remember—perhaps he had never noticed!

[020-218]Emil was already at the other end of the hall, walking about with the

[020-219]shoulder-motion he had acquired among the Mexicans, studying the floor with his

[020-220]intent, deep-set eyes. Marie began to take down and fold her shawls. She did

[020-221]not glance up again. The young people drifted to the other end of the hall

[020-222]where the guitar was sounding. In a moment she heard Emil and Raoul singing:—

[020-223]“Across the Rio Grand-e

[020-224]There lies a sunny land-e,

[020-225]My bright-eyed Mexico!”

[020-226]Alexandra Bergson came up to the card booth. “Let me help you, Marie. You look

[020-227]tired.”

[020-228]She placed her hand on Marie’s arm and felt her shiver. Marie stiffened under

[020-229]that kind, calm hand. Alexandra drew back, perplexed and hurt.

[020-230]There was about Alexandra something of the impervious calm of the fatalist,

[020-231]always disconcerting to very young people, who cannot feel that the heart lives

[020-232]at all unless it is still at the mercy of storms; unless its strings can scream

[020-233]to the touch of pain.
