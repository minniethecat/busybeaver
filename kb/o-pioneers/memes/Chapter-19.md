[019-001]If Alexandra had had much imagination she might have guessed what was going on

[019-002]in Marie’s mind, and she would have seen long before what was going on in

[019-003]Emil’s. But that, as Emil himself had more than once reflected, was Alexandra’s

[019-004]blind side, and her life had not been of the kind to sharpen her vision. Her

[019-005]training had all been toward the end of making her proficient in what she had

[019-006]undertaken to do. Her personal life, her own realization of herself, was almost

[019-007]a subconscious existence; like an underground river that came to the surface

[019-008]only here and there, at intervals months apart, and then sank again to flow on

[019-009]under her own fields. Nevertheless, the underground stream was there, and it

[019-010]was because she had so much personality to put into her enterprises and

[019-011]succeeded in putting it into them so completely, that her affairs prospered

[019-012]better than those of her neighbors.

[019-013]There were certain days in her life, outwardly uneventful, which Alexandra

[019-014]remembered as peculiarly happy; days when she was close to the flat, fallow

[019-015]world about her, and felt, as it were, in her own body the joyous germination

[019-016]in the soil. There were days, too, which she and Emil had spent together, upon

[019-017]which she loved to look back. There had been such a day when they were down on

[019-018]the river in the dry year, looking over the land. They had made an early start

[019-019]one morning and had driven a long way before noon. When Emil said he was

[019-020]hungry, they drew back from the road, gave Brigham his oats among the bushes,

[019-021]and climbed up to the top of a grassy bluff to eat their lunch under the shade

[019-022]of some little elm trees. The river was clear there, and shallow, since there

[019-023]had been no rain, and it ran in ripples over the sparkling sand. Under the

[019-024]overhanging willows of the opposite bank there was an inlet where the water was

[019-025]deeper and flowed so slowly that it seemed to sleep in the sun. In this little

[019-026]bay a single wild duck was swimming and diving and preening her feathers,

[019-027]disporting herself very happily in the flickering light and shade. They sat for

[019-028]a long time, watching the solitary bird take its pleasure. No living thing had

[019-029]ever seemed to Alexandra as beautiful as that wild duck. Emil must have felt

[019-030]about it as she did, for afterward, when they were at home, he used sometimes

[019-031]to say, “Sister, you know our duck down there—” Alexandra remembered that day

[019-032]as one of the happiest in her life. Years afterward she thought of the duck as

[019-033]still there, swimming and diving all by herself in the sunlight, a kind of

[019-034]enchanted bird that did not know age or change.

[019-035]Most of Alexandra’s happy memories were as impersonal as this one; yet to her

[019-036]they were very personal. Her mind was a white book, with clear writing about

[019-037]weather and beasts and growing things. Not many people would have cared to read

[019-038]it; only a happy few. She had never been in love, she had never indulged in

[019-039]sentimental reveries. Even as a girl she had looked upon men as work-fellows.

[019-040]She had grown up in serious times.

[019-041]There was one fancy indeed, which persisted through her girlhood. It most often

[019-042]came to her on Sunday mornings, the one day in the week when she lay late abed

[019-043]listening to the familiar morning sounds; the windmill singing in the brisk

[019-044]breeze, Emil whistling as he blacked his boots down by the kitchen door.

[019-045]Sometimes, as she lay thus luxuriously idle, her eyes closed, she used to have

[019-046]an illusion of being lifted up bodily and carried lightly by some one very

[019-047]strong. It was a man, certainly, who carried her, but he was like no man she

[019-048]knew; he was much larger and stronger and swifter, and he carried her as easily

[019-049]as if she were a sheaf of wheat. She never saw him, but, with eyes closed, she

[019-050]could feel that he was yellow like the sunlight, and there was the smell of

[019-051]ripe cornfields about him. She could feel him approach, bend over her and lift

[019-052]her, and then she could feel herself being carried swiftly off across the

[019-053]fields. After such a reverie she would rise hastily, angry with herself, and go

[019-054]down to the bath-house that was partitioned off the kitchen shed. There she

[019-055]would stand in a tin tub and prosecute her bath with vigor, finishing it by

[019-056]pouring buckets of cold well-water over her gleaming white body which no man on

[019-057]the Divide could have carried very far.

[019-058]As she grew older, this fancy more often came to her when she was tired than

[019-059]when she was fresh and strong. Sometimes, after she had been in the open all

[019-060]day, overseeing the branding of the cattle or the loading of the pigs, she

[019-061]would come in chilled, take a concoction of spices and warm home-made wine, and

[019-062]go to bed with her body actually aching with fatigue. Then, just before she

[019-063]went to sleep, she had the old sensation of being lifted and carried by a

[019-064]strong being who took from her all her bodily weariness.
