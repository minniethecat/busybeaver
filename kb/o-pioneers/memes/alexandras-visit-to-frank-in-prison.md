## Overview

Alexandra Bergson travels to Lincoln to visit Frank Shabata in the State Penitentiary, where he is serving his sentence for the murder of Emil Bergson and Marie Shabata. Despite Frank having killed her brother, Alexandra shows him mercy and forgiveness, promising to advocate for his release. This visit marks a key moment of emotional resolution and moral grace in the novel's final section.

## Background

Earlier in Part IV, Frank Shabata discovered his wife Marie with Emil Bergson in the orchard and shot them both in a fit of jealous rage (029-047). Frank was tried, convicted, and sentenced to the State Penitentiary in Lincoln. Alexandra, devastated by the loss of her brother Emil, has been struggling to make sense of the tragedy and to find a way forward. She travels to Lincoln in October, dressed in black, still in mourning.

## Account

Alexandra arrives at the warden's office at nine in the morning. The warden, a German named Mr. Schwartz, greets her warmly after reading a letter of introduction from the German banker in Hanover (029-050). Alexandra tells the warden about Frank's history and character, explaining why she is interested in helping him (029-057).

When Frank is brought into the room, Alexandra is shocked by his appearance: he is dressed in prison stripes, his hair is shorn, and his once-handsome face is "bleached and vacant." Frank cannot meet Alexandra's eyes and breaks down in tears, expressing remorse for what he has done. Alexandra speaks gently to him, telling him she does not hold bitterness toward him and that she intends to do everything in her power to secure his release.

She promises to speak to the Governor and asks Frank to be patient and to behave well in prison. The visit ends with Alexandra having achieved a measure of peace through the act of forgiveness. As she leaves the prison, the weight of her mourning begins to lift.

## Aftermath

Alexandra's visit to Frank profoundly changes her emotional state. The act of forgiveness frees her from the bitterness that had been hardening within her. Upon returning from Lincoln, she is met by Carl Linstrum at the Hanover station, and their reunion—described in Chapter 30—marks the novel's movement toward hope and renewal. Alexandra's mercy toward Frank also sets the stage for the novel's final resolution, in which she and Carl decide to marry and build a future together on the Divide.
