[028-001][[ivar|Ivar]] was sitting at a cobbler’s bench in the barn, mending harness by the light

[028-002]of a lantern and repeating to himself the 101st Psalm. It was only five o’clock

[028-003]of a mid-October day, but a storm had come up in the afternoon, bringing black

[028-004]clouds, a cold wind and torrents of rain. The old man wore his buffalo-skin

[028-005]coat, and occasionally stopped to warm his fingers at the lantern. Suddenly a

[028-006]woman burst into the shed, as if she had been blown in, accompanied by a shower

[028-007]of rain-drops. It was [[signa|Signa]], wrapped in a man’s overcoat and wearing a pair of

[028-008]boots over her shoes. In time of trouble Signa had come back to stay with her

[028-009]mistress, for she was the only one of the maids from whom Alexandra would

[028-010]accept much personal service. It was three months now since the news of the

[028-011]terrible thing that had happened in [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]]’s orchard had first run like

[028-012]a fire over the Divide. Signa and Nelse were staying on with Alexandra until

[028-013]winter.

[028-014]“Ivar,” Signa exclaimed as she wiped the rain from her face, “do you know where

[028-015]she is?”

[028-016]The old man put down his cobbler’s knife. “Who, the mistress?”

[028-017]“Yes. She went away about three o’clock. I happened to look out of the window

[028-018]and saw her going across the fields in her thin dress and sun-hat. And now this

[028-019]storm has come on. I thought she was going to Mrs. Hiller’s, and I telephoned

[028-020]as soon as the thunder stopped, but she had not been there. I’m afraid she is

[028-021]out somewhere and will get her death of cold.”

[028-022]Ivar put on his cap and took up the lantern. “*Ja*,*ja*, we will

[028-023]see. I will hitch the boy’s mare to the cart and go.”

[028-024]Signa followed him across the wagon-shed to the horses’ stable. She was

[028-025]shivering with cold and excitement. “Where do you suppose she can be, Ivar?”

[028-026]The old man lifted a set of single harness carefully from its peg. “How should

[028-027]I know?”

[028-028]“But you think she is at the graveyard, don’t you?” Signa persisted. “So do I.

[028-029]Oh, I wish she would be more like herself! I can’t believe it’s Alexandra

[028-030]Bergson come to this, with no head about anything. I have to tell her when to

[028-031]eat and when to go to bed.”

[028-032]“Patience, patience, sister,” muttered Ivar as he settled the bit in the

[028-033]horse’s mouth. “When the eyes of the flesh are shut, the eyes of the spirit are

[028-034]open. She will have a message from those who are gone, and that will bring her

[028-035]peace. Until then we must bear with her. You and I are the only ones who have

[028-036]weight with her. She trusts us.”

[028-037]“How awful it’s been these last three months.” Signa held the lantern so that

[028-038]he could see to buckle the straps. “It don’t seem right that we must all be so

[028-039]miserable. Why do we all have to be punished? Seems to me like good times would

[028-040]never come again.”

[028-041]Ivar expressed himself in a deep sigh, but said nothing. He stooped and took a

[028-042]sandburr from his toe.

[028-043]“Ivar,” Signa asked suddenly, “will you tell me why you go barefoot? All the

[028-044]time I lived here in the house I wanted to ask you. Is it for a penance, or

[028-045]what?”

[028-046]“No, sister. It is for the indulgence of the body. From my youth up I have had

[028-047]a strong, rebellious body, and have been subject to every kind of temptation.

[028-048]Even in age my temptations are prolonged. It was necessary to make some

[028-049]allowances; and the feet, as I understand it, are free members. There is no

[028-050]divine prohibition for them in the Ten Commandments. The hands, the tongue, the

[028-051]eyes, the heart, all the bodily desires we are commanded to subdue; but the

[028-052]feet are free members. I indulge them without harm to any one, even to

[028-053]trampling in filth when my desires are low. They are quickly cleaned again.”

[028-054]Signa did not laugh. She looked thoughtful as she followed Ivar out to the

[028-055]wagon-shed and held the shafts up for him, while he backed in the mare and

[028-056]buckled the hold-backs. “You have been a good friend to the mistress, Ivar,”

[028-057]she murmured.

[028-058]“And you, God be with you,” replied Ivar as he clambered into the cart and put

[028-059]the lantern under the oilcloth lap-cover. “Now for a ducking, my girl,” he said

[028-060]to the mare, gathering up the reins.

[028-061]As they emerged from the shed, a stream of water, running off the thatch,

[028-062]struck the mare on the neck. She tossed her head indignantly, then struck out

[028-063]bravely on the soft ground, slipping back again and again as she climbed the

[028-064]hill to the main road. Between the rain and the darkness Ivar could see very

[028-065]little, so he let Emil’s mare have the rein, keeping her head in the right

[028-066]direction. When the ground was level, he turned her out of the dirt road upon

[028-067]the sod, where she was able to trot without slipping.

[028-068]Before Ivar reached the graveyard, three miles from the house, the storm had

[028-069]spent itself, and the downpour had died into a soft, dripping rain. The sky and

[028-070]the land were a dark smoke color, and seemed to be coming together, like two

[028-071]waves. When Ivar stopped at the gate and swung out his lantern, a white figure

[028-072]rose from beside [[john-bergson|John Bergson]]’s white stone.

[028-073]The old man sprang to the ground and shuffled toward the gate calling,

[028-074]“Mistress, mistress!”

[028-075]Alexandra hurried to meet him and put her hand on his shoulder. “*Tyst!*Ivar. There’s nothing to be worried about. I’m sorry if I’ve scared you all. I

[028-076]didn’t notice the storm till it was on me, and I couldn’t walk against it. I’m

[028-077]glad you’ve come. I am so tired I didn’t know how I’d ever get home.”

[028-078]Ivar swung the lantern up so that it shone in her face. “*Gud!*You are

[028-079]enough to frighten us, mistress. You look like a drowned woman. How could you

[028-080]do such a thing!”

[028-081]Groaning and mumbling he led her out of the gate and helped her into the cart,

[028-082]wrapping her in the dry blankets on which he had been sitting.

[028-083]Alexandra smiled at his solicitude. “Not much use in that, Ivar. You will only

[028-084]shut the wet in. I don’t feel so cold now; but I’m heavy and numb. I’m glad you

[028-085]came.”

[028-086]Ivar turned the mare and urged her into a sliding trot. Her feet sent back a

[028-087]continual spatter of mud.

[028-088]Alexandra spoke to the old man as they jogged along through the sullen gray

[028-089]twilight of the storm. “Ivar, I think it has done me good to get cold clear

[028-090]through like this, once. I don’t believe I shall suffer so much any more. When

[028-091]you get so near the dead, they seem more real than the living. Worldly thoughts

[028-092]leave one. Ever since Emil died, I’ve suffered so when it rained. Now that I’ve

[028-093]been out in it with him, I shan’t dread it. After you once get cold clear

[028-094]through, the feeling of the rain on you is sweet. It seems to bring back

[028-095]feelings you had when you were a baby. It carries you back into the dark,

[028-096]before you were born; you can’t see things, but they come to you, somehow, and

[028-097]you know them and aren’t afraid of them. Maybe it’s like that with the dead. If

[028-098]they feel anything at all, it’s the old things, before they were born, that

[028-099]comfort people like the feeling of their own bed does when they are little.”

[028-100]“Mistress,” said Ivar reproachfully, “those are bad thoughts. The dead are in

[028-101]Paradise.”

[028-102]Then he hung his head, for he did not believe that Emil was in Paradise.

[028-103]When they got home, Signa had a fire burning in the sitting-room stove. She

[028-104]undressed Alexandra and gave her a hot footbath, while Ivar made ginger tea in

[028-105]the kitchen. When Alexandra was in bed, wrapped in hot blankets, Ivar came in

[028-106]with his tea and saw that she drank it. Signa asked permission to sleep on the

[028-107]slat lounge outside her door. Alexandra endured their attentions patiently, but

[028-108]she was glad when they put out the lamp and left her. As she lay alone in the

[028-109]dark, it occurred to her for the first time that perhaps she was actually tired

[028-110]of life. All the physical operations of life seemed difficult and painful. She

[028-111]longed to be free from her own body, which ached and was so heavy. And longing

[028-112]itself was heavy: she yearned to be free of that.

[028-113]As she lay with her eyes closed, she had again, more vividly than for many

[028-114]years, the old illusion of her girlhood, of being lifted and carried lightly by

[028-115]some one very strong. He was with her a long while this time, and carried her

[028-116]very far, and in his arms she felt free from pain. When he laid her down on her

[028-117]bed again, she opened her eyes, and, for the first time in her life, she saw

[028-118]him, saw him clearly, though the room was dark, and his face was covered. He

[028-119]was standing in the doorway of her room. His white cloak was thrown over his

[028-120]face, and his head was bent a little forward. His shoulders seemed as strong as

[028-121]the foundations of the world. His right arm, bared from the elbow, was dark and

[028-122]gleaming, like bronze, and she knew at once that it was the arm of the

[028-123]mightiest of all lovers. She knew at last for whom it was she had waited, and

[028-124]where he would carry her. That, she told herself, was very well. Then she went

[028-125]to sleep.

[028-126]Alexandra wakened in the morning with nothing worse than a hard cold and a

[028-127]stiff shoulder. She kept her bed for several days, and it was during that time

[028-128]that she formed a resolution to go to [[lincoln|Lincoln]] to see Frank Shabata. Ever since

[028-129]she last saw him in the courtroom, Frank’s haggard face and wild eyes had

[028-130]haunted her. The trial had lasted only three days. Frank had given himself up

[028-131]to the police in [[omaha|Omaha]] and pleaded guilty of killing without malice and without

[028-132]premeditation. The gun was, of course, against him, and the judge had given him

[028-133]the full sentence,—ten years. He had now been in the State Penitentiary for a

[028-134]month.

[028-135]Frank was the only one, Alexandra told herself, for whom anything could be

[028-136]done. He had been less in the wrong than any of them, and he was paying the

[028-137]heaviest penalty. She often felt that she herself had been more to blame than

[028-138]poor Frank. From the time the Shabatas had first moved to the neighboring farm,

[028-139]she had omitted no opportunity of throwing Marie and Emil together. Because she

[028-140]knew Frank was surly about doing little things to help his wife, she was always

[028-141]sending Emil over to spade or plant or carpenter for Marie. She was glad to

[028-142]have Emil see as much as possible of an intelligent, city-bred girl like their

[028-143]neighbor; she noticed that it improved his manners. She knew that Emil was fond

[028-144]of Marie, but it had never occurred to her that Emil’s feeling might be

[028-145]different from her own. She wondered at herself now, but she had never thought

[028-146]of danger in that direction. If Marie had been unmarried,—oh, yes! Then she

[028-147]would have kept her eyes open. But the mere fact that she was Shabata’s wife,

[028-148]for Alexandra, settled everything. That she was beautiful, impulsive, barely

[028-149]two years older than Emil, these facts had had no weight with Alexandra. Emil

[028-150]was a good boy, and only bad boys ran after married women.

[028-151]Now, Alexandra could in a measure realize that Marie was, after all, Marie; not

[028-152]merely a “married woman.” Sometimes, when Alexandra thought of her, it was with

[028-153]an aching tenderness. The moment she had reached them in the orchard that

[028-154]morning, everything was clear to her. There was something about those two lying

[028-155]in the grass, something in the way Marie had settled her cheek on Emil’s

[028-156]shoulder, that told her everything. She wondered then how they could have

[028-157]helped loving each other; how she could have helped knowing that they must.

[028-158]Emil’s cold, frowning face, the girl’s content—Alexandra had felt awe of them,

[028-159]even in the first shock of her grief.

[028-160]The idleness of those days in bed, the relaxation of body which attended them,

[028-161]enabled Alexandra to think more calmly than she had done since Emil’s death.

[028-162]She and Frank, she told herself, were left out of that group of friends who had

[028-163]been overwhelmed by disaster. She must certainly see Frank Shabata. Even in the

[028-164]courtroom her heart had grieved for him. He was in a strange country, he had no

[028-165]kinsmen or friends, and in a moment he had ruined his life. Being what he was,

[028-166]she felt, Frank could not have acted otherwise. She could understand his

[028-167]behavior more easily than she could understand Marie’s. Yes, she must go to

[028-168]Lincoln to see Frank Shabata.

[028-169]The day after Emil’s funeral, Alexandra had written to [[carl-linstrum|Carl Linstrum]]; a single

[028-170]page of notepaper, a bare statement of what had happened. She was not a woman

[028-171]who could write much about such a thing, and about her own feelings she could

[028-172]never write very freely. She knew that Carl was away from post-offices,

[028-173]prospecting somewhere in the interior. Before he started he had written her

[028-174]where he expected to go, but her ideas about Alaska were vague. As the weeks

[028-175]went by and she heard nothing from him, it seemed to Alexandra that her heart

[028-176]grew hard against Carl. She began to wonder whether she would not do better to

[028-177]finish her life alone. What was left of life seemed unimportant.
