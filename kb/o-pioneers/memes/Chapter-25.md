[025-001][[the-church|The Church]] has always held that life is for the living. On Saturday, while half

[025-002]the village of Sainte-Agnes was mourning for Amédée and preparing the funeral

[025-003]black for his burial on Monday, the other half was busy with white dresses and

[025-004]white veils for the great confirmation service to-morrow, when the bishop was

[025-005]to confirm a class of one hundred boys and girls. Father Duchesne divided his

[025-006]time between the living and the dead. All day Saturday the church was a scene

[025-007]of bustling activity, a little hushed by the thought of Amédée. The choir were

[025-008]busy rehearsing a mass of Rossini, which they had studied and practised for

[025-009]this occasion. The women were trimming the altar, the boys and girls were

[025-010]bringing flowers.

[025-011]On Sunday morning the bishop was to drive overland to Sainte-Agnes from

[025-012][[hanover|Hanover]], and [[emil-bergson|Emil Bergson]] had been asked to take the place of one of Amédée’s

[025-013]cousins in the cavalcade of forty French boys who were to ride across country

[025-014]to meet the bishop’s carriage. At six o’clock on Sunday morning the boys met at

[025-015]the church. As they stood holding their horses by the bridle, they talked in

[025-016]low tones of their dead comrade. They kept repeating that Amédée had always

[025-017]been a good boy, glancing toward the red brick church which had played so large

[025-018]a part in Amédée’s life, had been the scene of his most serious moments and of

[025-019]his happiest hours. He had played and wrestled and sung and courted under its

[025-020]shadow. Only three weeks ago he had proudly carried his baby there to be

[025-021]christened. They could not doubt that that invisible arm was still about

[025-022]Amédée; that through the church on earth he had passed to the church

[025-023]triumphant, the goal of the hopes and faith of so many hundred years.

[025-024]When the word was given to mount, the young men rode at a walk out of the

[025-025]village; but once out among the wheatfields in the morning sun, their horses

[025-026]and their own youth got the better of them. A wave of zeal and fiery enthusiasm

[025-027]swept over them. They longed for a Jerusalem to deliver. The thud of their

[025-028]galloping hoofs interrupted many a country breakfast and brought many a woman

[025-029]and child to the door of the farmhouses as they passed. Five miles east of

[025-030]Sainte-Agnes they met the bishop in his open carriage, attended by two priests.

[025-031]Like one man the boys swung off their hats in a broad salute, and bowed their

[025-032]heads as the handsome old man lifted his two fingers in the episcopal blessing.

[025-033]The horsemen closed about the carriage like a guard, and whenever a restless

[025-034]horse broke from control and shot down the road ahead of the body, the bishop

[025-035]laughed and rubbed his plump hands together. “What fine boys!” he said to his

[025-036]priests. “The Church still has her cavalry.”

[025-037]As the troop swept past the graveyard half a mile east of the town,—the first

[025-038]frame church of the parish had stood there,—old Pierre Seguin was already out

[025-039]with his pick and spade, digging Amédée’s grave. He knelt and uncovered as the

[025-040]bishop passed. The boys with one accord looked away from old Pierre to the red

[025-041]church on the hill, with the gold cross flaming on its steeple.

[025-042]Mass was at eleven. While the church was filling, Emil Bergson waited outside,

[025-043]watching the wagons and buggies drive up the hill. After the bell began to

[025-044]ring, he saw [[frank-shabata|Frank Shabata]] ride up on horseback and tie his horse to the

[025-045]hitch-bar. Marie, then, was not coming. Emil turned and went into the church.

[025-046]Amédée’s was the only empty pew, and he sat down in it. Some of Amédée’s

[025-047]cousins were there, dressed in black and weeping. When all the pews were full,

[025-048]the old men and boys packed the open space at the back of the church, kneeling

[025-049]on the floor. There was scarcely a family in town that was not represented in

[025-050]the confirmation class, by a cousin, at least. The new communicants, with their

[025-051]clear, reverent faces, were beautiful to look upon as they entered in a body

[025-052]and took the front benches reserved for them. Even before the Mass began, the

[025-053]air was charged with feeling. The choir had never sung so well and Raoul

[025-054]Marcel, in the “Gloria,” drew even the bishop’s eyes to the organ loft. For the

[025-055]offertory he sang Gounod’s “Ave Maria,”—always spoken of in Sainte-Agnes as

[025-056]“the Ave Maria.”

[025-057]Emil began to torture himself with questions about Marie. Was she ill? Had she

[025-058]quarreled with her husband? Was she too unhappy to find comfort even here? Had

[025-059]she, perhaps, thought that he would come to her? Was she waiting for him?

[025-060]Overtaxed by excitement and sorrow as he was, the rapture of the service took

[025-061]hold upon his body and mind. As he listened to Raoul, he seemed to emerge from

[025-062]the conflicting emotions which had been whirling him about and sucking him

[025-063]under. He felt as if a clear light broke upon his mind, and with it a

[025-064]conviction that good was, after all, stronger than evil, and that good was

[025-065]possible to men. He seemed to discover that there was a kind of rapture in

[025-066]which he could love forever without faltering and without sin. He looked across

[025-067]the heads of the people at Frank Shabata with calmness. That rapture was for

[025-068]those who could feel it; for people who could not, it was non-existent. He

[025-069]coveted nothing that was Frank Shabata’s. The spirit he had met in music was

[025-070]his own. Frank Shabata had never found it; would never find it if he lived

[025-071]beside it a thousand years; would have destroyed it if he had found it, as

[025-072]Herod slew the innocents, as Rome slew the martyrs.

[025-073]San—cta Mari-i-i-a,

[025-074]wailed Raoul from the organ loft;

[025-075]O—ra pro no-o-bis!

[025-076]And it did not occur to Emil that any one had ever reasoned thus before, that

[025-077]music had ever before given a man this equivocal revelation.

[025-078]The confirmation service followed the Mass. When it was over, the congregation

[025-079]thronged about the newly confirmed. The girls, and even the boys, were kissed

[025-080]and embraced and wept over. All the aunts and grandmothers wept with joy. The

[025-081]housewives had much ado to tear themselves away from the general rejoicing and

[025-082]hurry back to their kitchens. The country parishioners were staying in town for

[025-083]dinner, and nearly every house in Sainte-Agnes entertained visitors that day.

[025-084]Father Duchesne, the bishop, and the visiting priests dined with Fabien

[025-085]Sauvage, the banker. Emil and Frank Shabata were both guests of old Moise

[025-086]Marcel. After dinner Frank and old Moise retired to the rear room of the saloon

[025-087]to play California Jack and drink their cognac, and Emil went over to the

[025-088]banker’s with Raoul, who had been asked to sing for the bishop.

[025-089]At three o’clock, Emil felt that he could stand it no longer. He slipped out

[025-090]under cover of “The Holy City,” followed by Malvina’s wistful eye, and went to

[025-091]the stable for his mare. He was at that height of excitement from which

[025-092]everything is foreshortened, from which life seems short and simple, death very

[025-093]near, and the soul seems to soar like an eagle. As he rode past the graveyard

[025-094]he looked at the brown hole in the earth where Amédée was to lie, and felt no

[025-095]horror. That, too, was beautiful, that simple doorway into forgetfulness. The

[025-096]heart, when it is too much alive, aches for that brown earth, and ecstasy has

[025-097]no fear of death. It is the old and the poor and the maimed who shrink from

[025-098]that brown hole; its wooers are found among the young, the passionate, the

[025-099]gallant-hearted. It was not until he had passed the graveyard that Emil

[025-100]realized where he was going. It was the hour for saying good-bye. It might be

[025-101]the last time that he would see her alone, and today he could leave her without

[025-102]rancor, without bitterness.

[025-103]Everywhere the grain stood ripe and the hot afternoon was full of the smell of

[025-104]the ripe wheat, like the smell of bread baking in an oven. The breath of the

[025-105]wheat and the sweet clover passed him like pleasant things in a dream. He could

[025-106]feel nothing but the sense of diminishing distance. It seemed to him that his

[025-107]mare was flying, or running on wheels, like a railway train. The sunlight,

[025-108]flashing on the window-glass of the big red barns, drove him wild with joy. He

[025-109]was like an arrow shot from the bow. His life poured itself out along the road

[025-110]before him as he rode to the Shabata farm.

[025-111]When Emil alighted at the Shabatas’ gate, his horse was in a lather. He tied

[025-112]her in the stable and hurried to the house. It was empty. She might be at Mrs.

[025-113]Hiller’s or with Alexandra. But anything that reminded him of her would be

[025-114]enough, the orchard, the mulberry tree... When he reached the orchard the sun

[025-115]was hanging low over the wheatfield. Long fingers of light reached through the

[025-116]apple branches as through a net; the orchard was riddled and shot with gold;

[025-117]light was the reality, the trees were merely interferences that reflected and

[025-118]refracted light. Emil went softly down between the cherry trees toward the

[025-119]wheatfield. When he came to the corner, he stopped short and put his hand over

[025-120]his mouth. Marie was lying on her side under the white mulberry tree, her face

[025-121]half hidden in the grass, her eyes closed, her hands lying limply where they

[025-122]had happened to fall. She had lived a day of her new life of perfect love, and

[025-123]it had left her like this. Her breast rose and fell faintly, as if she were

[025-124]asleep. Emil threw himself down beside her and took her in his arms. The blood

[025-125]came back to her cheeks, her amber eyes opened slowly, and in them Emil saw his

[025-126]own face and the orchard and the sun. “I was dreaming this,” she whispered,

[025-127]hiding her face against him, “don’t take my dream away!”
