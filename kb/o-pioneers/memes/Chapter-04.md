[004-001]For the first three years after [[john-bergson|John Bergson]]’s death, the affairs of his family

[004-002]prospered. Then came the hard times that brought every one on the Divide to the

[004-003]brink of despair; three years of drouth and failure, the last struggle of a

[004-004]wild soil against the encroaching plowshare. The first of these fruitless

[004-005]summers the Bergson boys bore courageously. The failure of the corn crop made

[004-006]labor cheap. Lou and Oscar hired two men and put in bigger crops than ever

[004-007]before. They lost everything they spent. The whole country was discouraged.

[004-008]Farmers who were already in debt had to give up their land. A few foreclosures

[004-009]demoralized the county. The settlers sat about on the wooden sidewalks in the

[004-010]little town and told each other that the country was never meant for men to

[004-011]live in; the thing to do was to get back to Iowa, to Illinois, to any place

[004-012]that had been proved habitable. The Bergson boys, certainly, would have been

[004-013]happier with their uncle Otto, in the bakery shop in Chicago. Like most of

[004-014]their neighbors, they were meant to follow in paths already marked out for

[004-015]them, not to break trails in a new country. A steady job, a few holidays,

[004-016]nothing to think about, and they would have been very happy. It was no fault of

[004-017]theirs that they had been dragged into the wilderness when they were little

[004-018]boys. A pioneer should have imagination, should be able to enjoy the idea of

[004-019]things more than the things themselves.

[004-020]The second of these barren summers was passing. One September afternoon

[004-021]Alexandra had gone over to the garden across the draw to dig sweet

[004-022]potatoes—they had been thriving upon the weather that was fatal to everything

[004-023]else. But when [[carl-linstrum|Carl Linstrum]] came up the garden rows to find her, she was not

[004-024]working. She was standing lost in thought, leaning upon her pitchfork, her

[004-025]sunbonnet lying beside her on the ground. The dry garden patch smelled of

[004-026]drying vines and was strewn with yellow seed-cucumbers and pumpkins and

[004-027]citrons. At one end, next the rhubarb, grew feathery asparagus, with red

[004-028]berries. Down the middle of the garden was a row of gooseberry and currant

[004-029]bushes. A few tough zenias and marigolds and a row of scarlet sage bore witness

[004-030]to the buckets of water that Mrs. Bergson had carried there after sundown,

[004-031]against the prohibition of her sons. Carl came quietly and slowly up the garden

[004-032]path, looking intently at Alexandra. She did not hear him. She was standing

[004-033]perfectly still, with that serious ease so characteristic of her. Her thick,

[004-034]reddish braids, twisted about her head, fairly burned in the sunlight. The air

[004-035]was cool enough to make the warm sun pleasant on one’s back and shoulders, and

[004-036]so clear that the eye could follow a hawk up and up, into the blazing blue

[004-037]depths of the sky. Even Carl, never a very cheerful boy, and considerably

[004-038]darkened by these last two bitter years, loved the country on days like this,

[004-039]felt something strong and young and wild come out of it, that laughed at care.

[004-040]“Alexandra,” he said as he approached her, “I want to talk to you. Let’s sit

[004-041]down by the gooseberry bushes.” He picked up her sack of potatoes and they

[004-042]crossed the garden. “Boys gone to town?” he asked as he sank down on the warm,

[004-043]sun-baked earth. “Well, we have made up our minds at last, Alexandra. We are

[004-044]really going away.”

[004-045]She looked at him as if she were a little frightened. “Really, Carl? Is it

[004-046]settled?”

[004-047]“Yes, father has heard from St. Louis, and they will give him back his old job

[004-048]in the cigar factory. He must be there by the first of November. They are

[004-049]taking on new men then. We will sell the place for whatever we can get, and

[004-050]auction the stock. We haven’t enough to ship. I am going to learn engraving

[004-051]with a German engraver there, and then try to get work in Chicago.”

[004-052]Alexandra’s hands dropped in her lap. Her eyes became dreamy and filled with

[004-053]tears.

[004-054]Carl’s sensitive lower lip trembled. He scratched in the soft earth beside him

[004-055]with a stick. “That’s all I hate about it, Alexandra,” he said slowly. “You’ve

[004-056]stood by us through so much and helped father out so many times, and now it

[004-057]seems as if we were running off and leaving you to face the worst of it. But it

[004-058]isn’t as if we could really ever be of any help to you. We are only one more

[004-059]drag, one more thing you look out for and feel responsible for. Father was

[004-060]never meant for a farmer, you know that. And I hate it. We’d only get in deeper

[004-061]and deeper.”

[004-062]“Yes, yes, Carl, I know. You are wasting your life here. You are able to do

[004-063]much better things. You are nearly nineteen now, and I wouldn’t have you stay.

[004-064]I’ve always hoped you would get away. But I can’t help feeling scared when I

[004-065]think how I will miss you—more than you will ever know.” She brushed the tears

[004-066]from her cheeks, not trying to hide them.

[004-067]“But, Alexandra,” he said sadly and wistfully, “I’ve never been any real help

[004-068]to you, beyond sometimes trying to keep the boys in a good humor.”

[004-069]Alexandra smiled and shook her head. “Oh, it’s not that. Nothing like that.

[004-070]It’s by understanding me, and the boys, and mother, that you’ve helped me. I

[004-071]expect that is the only way one person ever really can help another. I think

[004-072]you are about the only one that ever helped me. Somehow it will take more

[004-073]courage to bear your going than everything that has happened before.”

[004-074]Carl looked at the ground. “You see, we’ve all depended so on you,” he said,

[004-075]“even father. He makes me laugh. When anything comes up he always says, ‘I

[004-076]wonder what the Bergsons are going to do about that? I guess I’ll go and ask

[004-077]her.’ I’ll never forget that time, when we first came here, and our horse had

[004-078]the colic, and I ran over to your place—your father was away, and you came home

[004-079]with me and showed father how to let the wind out of the horse. You were only a

[004-080]little girl then, but you knew ever so much more about farm work than poor

[004-081]father. You remember how homesick I used to get, and what long talks we used to

[004-082]have coming from school? We’ve someway always felt alike about things.”

[004-083]“Yes, that’s it; we’ve liked the same things and we’ve liked them together,

[004-084]without anybody else knowing. And we’ve had good times, hunting for Christmas

[004-085]trees and going for ducks and making our plum wine together every year. We’ve

[004-086]never either of us had any other close friend. And now—” Alexandra wiped her

[004-087]eyes with the corner of her apron, “and now I must remember that you are going

[004-088]where you will have many friends, and will find the work you were meant to do.

[004-089]But you’ll write to me, Carl? That will mean a great deal to me here.”

[004-090]“I’ll write as long as I live,” cried the boy impetuously. “And I’ll be working

[004-091]for you as much as for myself, Alexandra. I want to do something you’ll like

[004-092]and be proud of. I’m a fool here, but I know I can do something!” He sat up and

[004-093]frowned at the red grass.

[004-094]Alexandra sighed. “How discouraged the boys will be when they hear. They always

[004-095]come home from town discouraged, anyway. So many people are trying to leave the

[004-096]country, and they talk to our boys and make them low-spirited. I’m afraid they

[004-097]are beginning to feel hard toward me because I won’t listen to any talk about

[004-098]going. Sometimes I feel like I’m getting tired of standing up for this

[004-099]country.”

[004-100]“I won’t tell the boys yet, if you’d rather not.”

[004-101]“Oh, I’ll tell them myself, to-night, when they come home. They’ll be talking

[004-102]wild, anyway, and no good comes of keeping bad news. It’s all harder on them

[004-103]than it is on me. Lou wants to get married, poor boy, and he can’t until times

[004-104]are better. See, there goes the sun, Carl. I must be getting back. Mother will

[004-105]want her potatoes. It’s chilly already, the moment the light goes.”

[004-106]Alexandra rose and looked about. A golden afterglow throbbed in the west, but

[004-107]the country already looked empty and mournful. A dark moving mass came over the

[004-108]western hill, the Lee boy was bringing in the herd from the other half-section.

[004-109]Emil ran from the windmill to open the corral gate. From the log house, on the

[004-110]little rise across the draw, the smoke was curling. The cattle lowed and

[004-111]bellowed. In the sky the pale half-moon was slowly silvering. Alexandra and

[004-112]Carl walked together down the potato rows. “I have to keep telling myself what

[004-113]is going to happen,” she said softly. “Since you have been here, ten years now,

[004-114]I have never really been lonely. But I can remember what it was like before.

[004-115]Now I shall have nobody but Emil. But he is my boy, and he is tender-hearted.”

[004-116]That night, when the boys were called to supper, they sat down moodily. They

[004-117]had worn their coats to town, but they ate in their striped shirts and

[004-118]suspenders. They were grown men now, and, as Alexandra said, for the last few

[004-119]years they had been growing more and more like themselves. Lou was still the

[004-120]slighter of the two, the quicker and more intelligent, but apt to go off at

[004-121]half-cock. He had a lively blue eye, a thin, fair skin (always burned red to

[004-122]the neckband of his shirt in summer), stiff, yellow hair that would not lie

[004-123]down on his head, and a bristly little yellow mustache, of which he was very

[004-124]proud. Oscar could not grow a mustache; his pale face was as bare as an egg,

[004-125]and his white eyebrows gave it an empty look. He was a man of powerful body and

[004-126]unusual endurance; the sort of man you could attach to a corn-sheller as you

[004-127]would an engine. He would turn it all day, without hurrying, without slowing

[004-128]down. But he was as indolent of mind as he was unsparing of his body. His love

[004-129]of routine amounted to a vice. He worked like an insect, always doing the same

[004-130]thing over in the same way, regardless of whether it was best or no. He felt

[004-131]that there was a sovereign virtue in mere bodily toil, and he rather liked to

[004-132]do things in the hardest way. If a field had once been in corn, he couldn’t

[004-133]bear to put it into wheat. He liked to begin his corn-planting at the same time

[004-134]every year, whether the season were backward or forward. He seemed to feel that

[004-135]by his own irreproachable regularity he would clear himself of blame and

[004-136]reprove the weather. When the wheat crop failed, he threshed the straw at a

[004-137]dead loss to demonstrate how little grain there was, and thus prove his case

[004-138]against Providence.

[004-139]Lou, on the other hand, was fussy and flighty; always planned to get through

[004-140]two days’ work in one, and often got only the least important things done. He

[004-141]liked to keep the place up, but he never got round to doing odd jobs until he

[004-142]had to neglect more pressing work to attend to them. In the middle of the wheat

[004-143]harvest, when the grain was over-ripe and every hand was needed, he would stop

[004-144]to mend fences or to patch the harness; then dash down to the field and

[004-145]overwork and be laid up in bed for a week. The two boys balanced each other,

[004-146]and they pulled well together. They had been good friends since they were

[004-147]children. One seldom went anywhere, even to town, without the other.

[004-148]To-night, after they sat down to supper, Oscar kept looking at Lou as if he

[004-149]expected him to say something, and Lou blinked his eyes and frowned at his

[004-150]plate. It was Alexandra herself who at last opened the discussion.

[004-151]“The Linstrums,” she said calmly, as she put another plate of hot biscuit on

[004-152]the table, “are going back to St. Louis. The old man is going to work in the

[004-153]cigar factory again.”

[004-154]At this Lou plunged in. “You see, Alexandra, everybody who can crawl out is

[004-155]going away. There’s no use of us trying to stick it out, just to be stubborn.

[004-156]There’s something in knowing when to quit.”

[004-157]“Where do you want to go, Lou?”

[004-158]“Any place where things will grow,” said Oscar grimly.

[004-159]Lou reached for a potato. “Chris Arnson has traded his half-section for a place

[004-160]down on the river.”

[004-161]“Who did he trade with?”

[004-162]“Charley Fuller, in town.”

[004-163]“Fuller the real estate man? You see, Lou, that Fuller has a head on him. He’s

[004-164]buying and trading for every bit of land he can get up here. It’ll make him a

[004-165]rich man, some day.”

[004-166]“He’s rich now, that’s why he can take a chance.”

[004-167]“Why can’t we? We’ll live longer than he will. Some day the land itself will be

[004-168]worth more than all we can ever raise on it.”

[004-169]Lou laughed. “It could be worth that, and still not be worth much. Why,

[004-170]Alexandra, you don’t know what you’re talking about. Our place wouldn’t bring

[004-171]now what it would six years ago. The fellows that settled up here just made a

[004-172]mistake. Now they’re beginning to see this high land wasn’t never meant to grow

[004-173]nothing on, and everybody who ain’t fixed to graze cattle is trying to crawl

[004-174]out. It’s too high to farm up here. All the Americans are skinning out. That

[004-175]man Percy Adams, north of town, told me that he was going to let Fuller take

[004-176]his land and stuff for four hundred dollars and a ticket to Chicago.”

[004-177]“There’s Fuller again!” Alexandra exclaimed. “I wish that man would take me for

[004-178]a partner. He’s feathering his nest! If only poor people could learn a little

[004-179]from rich people! But all these fellows who are running off are bad farmers,

[004-180]like poor Mr. Linstrum. They couldn’t get ahead even in good years, and they

[004-181]all got into debt while father was getting out. I think we ought to hold on as

[004-182]long as we can on father’s account. He was so set on keeping this land. He must

[004-183]have seen harder times than this, here. How was it in the early days, mother?”

[004-184]Mrs. Bergson was weeping quietly. These family discussions always depressed

[004-185]her, and made her remember all that she had been torn away from. “I don’t see

[004-186]why the boys are always taking on about going away,” she said, wiping her eyes.

[004-187]“I don’t want to move again; out to some raw place, maybe, where we’d be worse

[004-188]off than we are here, and all to do over again. I won’t move! If the rest of

[004-189]you go, I will ask some of the neighbors to take me in, and stay and be buried

[004-190]by father. I’m not going to leave him by himself on the prairie, for cattle to

[004-191]run over.” She began to cry more bitterly.

[004-192]The boys looked angry. Alexandra put a soothing hand on her mother’s shoulder.

[004-193]“There’s no question of that, mother. You don’t have to go if you don’t want

[004-194]to. A third of the place belongs to you by American law, and we can’t sell

[004-195]without your consent. We only want you to advise us. How did it use to be when

[004-196]you and father first came? Was it really as bad as this, or not?”

[004-197]“Oh, worse! Much worse,” moaned Mrs. Bergson. “Drouth, chince-bugs, hail,

[004-198]everything! My garden all cut to pieces like sauerkraut. No grapes on the

[004-199]creek, no nothing. The people all lived just like coyotes.”

[004-200]Oscar got up and tramped out of the kitchen. Lou followed him. They felt that

[004-201]Alexandra had taken an unfair advantage in turning their mother loose on them.

[004-202]The next morning they were silent and reserved. They did not offer to take the

[004-203]women to church, but went down to the barn immediately after breakfast and

[004-204]stayed there all day. When Carl Linstrum came over in the afternoon, Alexandra

[004-205]winked to him and pointed toward the barn. He understood her and went down to

[004-206]play cards with the boys. They believed that a very wicked thing to do on

[004-207]Sunday, and it relieved their feelings.

[004-208]Alexandra stayed in the house. On Sunday afternoon Mrs. Bergson always took a

[004-209]nap, and Alexandra read. During the week she read only the newspaper, but on

[004-210]Sunday, and in the long evenings of winter, she read a good deal; read a few

[004-211]things over a great many times. She knew long portions of the “Frithjof Saga”

[004-212]by heart, and, like most Swedes who read at all, she was fond of Longfellow’s

[004-213]verse,—the ballads and the “Golden Legend” and “The Spanish Student.” To-day

[004-214]she sat in the wooden rocking-chair with the Swedish Bible open on her knees,

[004-215]but she was not reading. She was looking thoughtfully away at the point where

[004-216][[the-divide|the upland]] road disappeared over the rim of the prairie. Her body was in an

[004-217]attitude of perfect repose, such as it was apt to take when she was thinking

[004-218]earnestly. Her mind was slow, truthful, steadfast. She had not the least spark

[004-219]of cleverness.

[004-220]All afternoon the sitting-room was full of quiet and sunlight. Emil was making

[004-221]rabbit traps in the kitchen shed. The hens were clucking and scratching brown

[004-222]holes in the flower beds, and the wind was teasing the prince’s feather by the

[004-223]door.

[004-224]That evening Carl came in with the boys to supper.

[004-225]“Emil,” said Alexandra, when they were all seated at the table, “how would you

[004-226]like to go traveling? Because I am going to take a trip, and you can go with me

[004-227]if you want to.”

[004-228]The boys looked up in amazement; they were always afraid of Alexandra’s

[004-229]schemes. Carl was interested.

[004-230]“I’ve been thinking, boys,” she went on, “that maybe I am too set against

[004-231]making a change. I’m going to take Brigham and the buckboard to-morrow and

[004-232]drive down to the river country and spend a few days looking over what they’ve

[004-233]got down there. If I find anything good, you boys can go down and make a

[004-234]trade.”

[004-235]“Nobody down there will trade for anything up here,” said Oscar gloomily.

[004-236]“That’s just what I want to find out. Maybe they are just as discontented down

[004-237]there as we are up here. Things away from home often look better than they are.

[004-238]You know what your Hans Andersen book says, Carl, about the Swedes liking to

[004-239]buy Danish bread and the Danes liking to buy Swedish bread, because people

[004-240]always think the bread of another country is better than their own. Anyway,

[004-241]I’ve heard so much about the river farms, I won’t be satisfied till I’ve seen

[004-242]for myself.”

[004-243]Lou fidgeted. “Look out! Don’t agree to anything. Don’t let them fool you.”

[004-244]Lou was apt to be fooled himself. He had not yet learned to keep away from the

[004-245]shell-game wagons that followed the circus.

[004-246]After supper Lou put on a necktie and went across the fields to court Annie

[004-247]Lee, and Carl and Oscar sat down to a game of checkers, while Alexandra read

[004-248]“The Swiss Family Robinson” aloud to her mother and Emil. It was not long

[004-249]before the two boys at the table neglected their game to listen. They were all

[004-250]big children together, and they found the adventures of the family in the tree

[004-251]house so absorbing that they gave them their undivided attention.
