## Overview

Uncle Otto is a member of the Bergson family who abandoned farming on the Divide and relocated to Chicago, where he ran a bakery shop. He serves primarily as a negative example in the novel—a figure invoked to warn against giving up on the land. His story illustrates the divide between those who persevere on the frontier and those who retreat to the comforts of city life.

## Biography

Uncle Otto is mentioned only through the recollections of other characters. On his deathbed, [[John Bergson]] explicitly warns his children not to follow Otto's path: "Don't let them get discouraged and go off like Uncle Otto. I want them to keep the land" (002-108). This invocation establishes Otto as the family's cautionary tale—the uncle who lacked the fortitude to endure the harsh conditions of the Divide.

The narrative later notes that the Bergson boys, [[Lou Bergson]] and [[Oscar Bergson]], "would have been happier with their uncle Otto, in the bakery shop in Chicago" (004-013), suggesting that Otto found a more congenial life in the city, away from the grueling demands of agriculture. Like most of the Bergson children, Lou and Oscar lacked their father's and Alexandra's commitment to the land.

Years later, during a conversation between Alexandra and [[Emil Bergson]], she reminds Emil of their family heritage: "Father wasn't conceited, Uncle Otto wasn't. Even Lou and Oscar weren't when they were boys" (022-082). This passing reference suggests Otto was remembered as a modest, unpretentious man.

## Relationships

- **[[John Bergson]]**: Likely Otto's brother. John uses Otto as the example of what he does not want his children to become—someone who gave up and left.
- **[[Lou Bergson]]** and **[[Oscar Bergson]]**: Otto's nephews, who would have preferred his city life to farming.
- **[[Alexandra Bergson]]**: Otto's niece, who remembers him as unpretentious and uses his memory to remind Emil of their family's character.

## Significance

Uncle Otto embodies the path not taken—the alternative of abandoning the frontier for the security of city life. He represents the many immigrants who found the Divide too harsh and retreated to more familiar, less demanding environments. By holding Otto up as a cautionary figure, John Bergson articulates the novel's central theme: that the land rewards only those who persist through hardship. Otto's absence from the main narrative underscores his symbolic function—he is defined entirely by his departure.
