[006-001]IT is sixteen years since [[john-bergson|John Bergson]] died. His wife now lies beside him, and

[006-002]the white shaft that marks their graves gleams across the wheat-fields. Could

[006-003]he rise from beneath it, he would not know the country under which he has been

[006-004]asleep. The shaggy coat of the prairie, which they lifted to make him a bed,

[006-005]has vanished forever. From the Norwegian graveyard one looks out over a vast

[006-006]checker-board, marked off in squares of wheat and corn; light and dark, dark

[006-007]and light. Telephone wires hum along the white roads, which always run at right

[006-008]angles. From the graveyard gate one can count a dozen gayly painted farmhouses;

[006-009]the gilded weather-vanes on the big red barns wink at each other across the

[006-010]green and brown and yellow fields. The light steel windmills tremble throughout

[006-011]their frames and tug at their moorings, as they vibrate in the wind that often

[006-012]blows from one week’s end to another across that high, active, resolute stretch

[006-013]of country.

[006-014][[the-divide|The Divide]] is now thickly populated. The rich soil yields heavy harvests; the

[006-015]dry, bracing climate and the smoothness of the land make labor easy for men and

[006-016]beasts. There are few scenes more gratifying than a spring plowing in that

[006-017]country, where the furrows of a single field often lie a mile in length, and

[006-018]the brown earth, with such a strong, clean smell, and such a power of growth

[006-019]and fertility in it, yields itself eagerly to the plow; rolls away from the

[006-020]shear, not even dimming the brightness of the metal, with a soft, deep sigh of

[006-021]happiness. The wheat-cutting sometimes goes on all night as well as all day,

[006-022]and in good seasons there are scarcely men and horses enough to do the

[006-023]harvesting. The grain is so heavy that it bends toward the blade and cuts like

[006-024]velvet.

[006-025]There is something frank and joyous and young in the open face of the country.

[006-026]It gives itself ungrudgingly to the moods of the season, holding nothing back.

[006-027]Like the plains of Lombardy, it seems to rise a little to meet the sun. The air

[006-028]and the earth are curiously mated and intermingled, as if the one were the

[006-029]breath of the other. You feel in the atmosphere the same tonic, puissant

[006-030]quality that is in the tilth, the same strength and resoluteness.

[006-031]One June morning a young man stood at the gate of the Norwegian graveyard,

[006-032]sharpening his scythe in strokes unconsciously timed to the tune he was

[006-033]whistling. He wore a flannel cap and duck trousers, and the sleeves of his

[006-034]white flannel shirt were rolled back to the elbow. When he was satisfied with

[006-035]the edge of his blade, he slipped the whetstone into his hip pocket and began

[006-036]to swing his scythe, still whistling, but softly, out of respect to the quiet

[006-037]folk about him. Unconscious respect, probably, for he seemed intent upon his

[006-038]own thoughts, and, like the Gladiator’s, they were far away. He was a splendid

[006-039]figure of a boy, tall and straight as a young pine tree, with a handsome head,

[006-040]and stormy gray eyes, deeply set under a serious brow. The space between his

[006-041]two front teeth, which were unusually far apart, gave him the proficiency in

[006-042]whistling for which he was distinguished at college. (He also played the cornet

[006-043]in the University band.)

[006-044]When the grass required his close attention, or when he had to stoop to cut

[006-045]about a head-stone, he paused in his lively air,—the “Jewel” song,—taking it up

[006-046]where he had left it when his scythe swung free again. He was not thinking

[006-047]about the tired pioneers over whom his blade glittered. The old wild country,

[006-048]the struggle in which his sister was destined to succeed while so many men

[006-049]broke their hearts and died, he can scarcely remember. That is all among the

[006-050]dim things of childhood and has been forgotten in the brighter pattern life

[006-051]weaves to-day, in the bright facts of being captain of the track team, and

[006-052]holding the interstate record for the high jump, in the all-suffusing

[006-053]brightness of being twenty-one. Yet sometimes, in the pauses of his work, the

[006-054]young man frowned and looked at the ground with an intentness which suggested

[006-055]that even twenty-one might have its problems.

[006-056]When he had been mowing the better part of an hour, he heard the rattle of a

[006-057]light cart on the road behind him. Supposing that it was his sister coming back

[006-058]from one of her farms, he kept on with his work. The cart stopped at the gate

[006-059]and a merry contralto voice called, “Almost through, Emil?” He dropped his

[006-060]scythe and went toward the fence, wiping his face and neck with his

[006-061]handkerchief. In the cart sat a young woman who wore driving gauntlets and a

[006-062]wide shade hat, trimmed with red poppies. Her face, too, was rather like a

[006-063]poppy, round and brown, with rich color in her cheeks and lips, and her dancing

[006-064]yellow-brown eyes bubbled with gayety. The wind was flapping her big hat and

[006-065]teasing a curl of her chestnut-colored hair. She shook her head at the tall

[006-066]youth.

[006-067]“What time did you get over here? That’s not much of a job for an athlete. Here

[006-068]I’ve been to town and back. Alexandra lets you sleep late. Oh, I know! Lou’s

[006-069]wife was telling me about the way she spoils you. I was going to give you a

[006-070]lift, if you were done.” She gathered up her reins.

[006-071]“But I will be, in a minute. Please wait for me, Marie,” Emil coaxed.

[006-072]“Alexandra sent me to mow our lot, but I’ve done half a dozen others, you see.

[006-073]Just wait till I finish off the Kourdnas’. By the way, they were Bohemians. Why

[006-074]aren’t they up in the Catholic graveyard?”

[006-075]“Free-thinkers,” replied the young woman laconically.

[006-076]“Lots of the Bohemian boys at the University are,” said Emil, taking up his

[006-077]scythe again. “What did you ever burn John Huss for, anyway? It’s made an awful

[006-078]row. They still jaw about it in history classes.”

[006-079]“We’d do it right over again, most of us,” said the young woman hotly. “Don’t

[006-080]they ever teach you in your history classes that you’d all be heathen Turks if

[006-081]it hadn’t been for the Bohemians?”

[006-082]Emil had fallen to mowing. “Oh, there’s no denying you’re a spunky little

[006-083]bunch, you Czechs,” he called back over his shoulder.

[006-084][[marie-shabata|Marie Shabata]] settled herself in her seat and watched the rhythmical movement

[006-085]of the young man’s long arms, swinging her foot as if in time to some air that

[006-086]was going through her mind. The minutes passed. Emil mowed vigorously and Marie

[006-087]sat sunning herself and watching the long grass fall. She sat with the ease

[006-088]that belongs to persons of an essentially happy nature, who can find a

[006-089]comfortable spot almost anywhere; who are supple, and quick in adapting

[006-090]themselves to circumstances. After a final swish, Emil snapped the gate and

[006-091]sprang into the cart, holding his scythe well out over the wheel. “There,” he

[006-092]sighed. “I gave old man Lee a cut or so, too. Lou’s wife needn’t talk. I never

[006-093]see Lou’s scythe over here.”

[006-094]Marie clucked to her horse. “Oh, you know Annie!” She looked at the young man’s

[006-095]bare arms. “How brown you’ve got since you came home. I wish I had an athlete

[006-096]to mow my orchard. I get wet to my knees when I go down to pick cherries.”

[006-097]“You can have one, any time you want him. Better wait until after it rains.”

[006-098]Emil squinted off at the horizon as if he were looking for clouds.

[006-099]“Will you? Oh, there’s a good boy!” She turned her head to him with a quick,

[006-100]bright smile. He felt it rather than saw it. Indeed, he had looked away with

[006-101]the purpose of not seeing it. “I’ve been up looking at Angélique’s wedding

[006-102]clothes,” Marie went on, “and I’m so excited I can hardly wait until Sunday.

[006-103]Amédée will be a handsome bridegroom. Is anybody but you going to stand up with

[006-104]him? Well, then it will be a handsome wedding party.” She made a droll face at

[006-105]Emil, who flushed. “Frank,” Marie continued, flicking her horse, “is cranky at

[006-106]me because I loaned his saddle to Jan Smirka, and I’m terribly afraid he won’t

[006-107]take me to the dance in the evening. Maybe the supper will tempt him. All

[006-108]Angélique’s folks are baking for it, and all Amédée’s twenty cousins. There

[006-109]will be barrels of beer. If once I get Frank to the supper, I’ll see that I

[006-110]stay for the dance. And by the way, Emil, you mustn’t dance with me but once or

[006-111]twice. You must dance with all the French girls. It hurts their feelings if you

[006-112]don’t. They think you’re proud because you’ve been away to school or

[006-113]something.”

[006-114]Emil sniffed. “How do you know they think that?”

[006-115]“Well, you didn’t dance with them much at [[raul-marcel|Raoul Marcel]]’s party, and I could

[006-116]tell how they took it by the way they looked at you—and at me.”

[006-117]“All right,” said Emil shortly, studying the glittering blade of his scythe.

[006-118]They drove westward toward [[norway-creek|Norway Creek]], and toward a big white house that

[006-119]stood on a hill, several miles across the fields. There were so many sheds and

[006-120]outbuildings grouped about it that the place looked not unlike a tiny village.

[006-121]A stranger, approaching it, could not help noticing the beauty and fruitfulness

[006-122]of the outlying fields. There was something individual about the great farm, a

[006-123]most unusual trimness and care for detail. On either side of the road, for a

[006-124]mile before you reached the foot of the hill, stood tall osage orange hedges,

[006-125]their glossy green marking off the yellow fields. South of the hill, in a low,

[006-126]sheltered swale, surrounded by a mulberry hedge, was the orchard, its fruit

[006-127]trees knee-deep in timothy grass. Any one thereabouts would have told you that

[006-128]this was one of the richest farms on the Divide, and that the farmer was a

[006-129]woman, [[alexandra-bergson|Alexandra Bergson]].

[006-130]If you go up the hill and enter Alexandra’s big house, you will find that it is

[006-131]curiously unfinished and uneven in comfort. One room is papered, carpeted,

[006-132]over-furnished; the next is almost bare. The pleasantest rooms in the house are

[006-133]the kitchen—where Alexandra’s three young Swedish girls chatter and cook and

[006-134]pickle and preserve all summer long—and the sitting-room, in which Alexandra

[006-135]has brought together the old homely furniture that the Bergsons used in their

[006-136]first log house, the family portraits, and the few things her mother brought

[006-137]from Sweden.

[006-138]When you go out of the house into the flower garden, there you feel again the

[006-139]order and fine arrangement manifest all over the great farm; in the fencing and

[006-140]hedging, in the windbreaks and sheds, in the symmetrical pasture ponds, planted

[006-141]with scrub willows to give shade to the cattle in fly-time. There is even a

[006-142]white row of beehives in the orchard, under the walnut trees. You feel that,

[006-143]properly, Alexandra’s house is the big out-of-doors, and that it is in the soil

[006-144]that she expresses herself best.
