[013-001]On the evening of the day of Alexandra’s call at the Shabatas’, a heavy rain

[013-002]set in. Frank sat up until a late hour reading the Sunday newspapers. One of

[013-003]the Goulds was getting a divorce, and Frank took it as a personal affront. In

[013-004]printing the story of the young man’s marital troubles, the knowing editor gave

[013-005]a sufficiently colored account of his career, stating the amount of his income

[013-006]and the manner in which he was supposed to spend it. Frank read English slowly,

[013-007]and the more he read about this divorce case, the angrier he grew. At last he

[013-008]threw down the page with a snort. He turned to his farm-hand who was reading

[013-009]the other half of the paper.

[013-010]“By God! if I have that young feller in de hayfield once, I show him someting.

[013-011]Listen here what he do wit his money.” And Frank began the catalogue of the

[013-012]young man’s reputed extravagances.

[013-013]Marie sighed. She thought it hard that the Goulds, for whom she had nothing but

[013-014]good will, should make her so much trouble. She hated to see the Sunday

[013-015]newspapers come into the house. Frank was always reading about the doings of

[013-016]rich people and feeling outraged. He had an inexhaustible stock of stories

[013-017]about their crimes and follies, how they bribed the courts and shot down their

[013-018]butlers with impunity whenever they chose. Frank and [[lou-bergson|Lou Bergson]] had very

[013-019]similar ideas, and they were two of the political agitators of the county.

[013-020]The next morning broke clear and brilliant, but Frank said the ground was too

[013-021]wet to plough, so he took the cart and drove over to Sainte-Agnes to spend the

[013-022]day at Moses Marcel’s saloon. After he was gone, Marie went out to the back

[013-023]porch to begin her butter-making. A brisk wind had come up and was driving

[013-024]puffy white clouds across the sky. The orchard was sparkling and rippling in

[013-025]the sun. Marie stood looking toward it wistfully, her hand on the lid of the

[013-026]churn, when she heard a sharp ring in the air, the merry sound of the whetstone

[013-027]on the scythe. That invitation decided her. She ran into the house, put on a

[013-028]short skirt and a pair of her husband’s boots, caught up a tin pail and started

[013-029]for the orchard. Emil had already begun work and was mowing vigorously. When he

[013-030]saw her coming, he stopped and wiped his brow. His yellow canvas leggings and

[013-031]khaki trousers were splashed to the knees.

[013-032]“Don’t let me disturb you, Emil. I’m going to pick cherries. Isn’t everything

[013-033]beautiful after the rain? Oh, but I’m glad to get this place mowed! When I

[013-034]heard it raining in the night, I thought maybe you would come and do it for me

[013-035]to-day. The wind wakened me. Didn’t it blow dreadfully? Just smell the wild

[013-036]roses! They are always so spicy after a rain. We never had so many of them in

[013-037]here before. I suppose it’s the wet season. Will you have to cut them, too?”

[013-038]“If I cut the grass, I will,” Emil said teasingly. “What’s the matter with you?

[013-039]What makes you so flighty?”

[013-040]“Am I flighty? I suppose that’s the wet season, too, then. It’s exciting to see

[013-041]everything growing so fast,—and to get the grass cut! Please leave the roses

[013-042]till last, if you must cut them. Oh, I don’t mean all of them, I mean that low

[013-043]place down by my tree, where there are so many. Aren’t you splashed! Look at

[013-044]the spider-webs all over the grass. Good-bye. I’ll call you if I see a snake.”

[013-045]She tripped away and Emil stood looking after her. In a few moments he heard

[013-046]the cherries dropping smartly into the pail, and he began to swing his scythe

[013-047]with that long, even stroke that few American boys ever learn. Marie picked

[013-048]cherries and sang softly to herself, stripping one glittering branch after

[013-049]another, shivering when she caught a shower of raindrops on her neck and hair.

[013-050]And Emil mowed his way slowly down toward the cherry trees.

[013-051]That summer the rains had been so many and opportune that it was almost more

[013-052]than Shabata and his man could do to keep up with the corn; the orchard was a

[013-053]neglected wilderness. All sorts of weeds and herbs and flowers had grown up

[013-054]there; splotches of wild larkspur, pale green-and-white spikes of hoarhound,

[013-055]plantations of wild cotton, tangles of foxtail and wild wheat. South of the

[013-056]apricot trees, cornering on the wheatfield, was Frank’s alfalfa, where myriads

[013-057]of white and yellow butterflies were always fluttering above the purple

[013-058]blossoms. When Emil reached the lower corner by the hedge, Marie was sitting

[013-059]under her white mulberry tree, the pailful of cherries beside her, looking off

[013-060]at the gentle, tireless swelling of the wheat.

[013-061]“Emil,” she said suddenly—he was mowing quietly about under the tree so as not

[013-062]to disturb her—“what religion did the Swedes have away back, before they were

[013-063]Christians?”

[013-064]Emil paused and straightened his back. “I don’t know. [[About]] like the Germans’,

[013-065]wasn’t it?”

[013-066]Marie went on as if she had not heard him. “The Bohemians, you know, were tree

[013-067]worshipers before the missionaries came. Father says the people in the

[013-068]mountains still do queer things, sometimes,—they believe that trees bring good

[013-069]or bad luck.”

[013-070]Emil looked superior. “Do they? Well, which are the lucky trees? I’d like to

[013-071]know.”

[013-072]“I don’t know all of them, but I know lindens are. The old people in the

[013-073]mountains plant lindens to purify the forest, and to do away with the spells

[013-074]that come from the old trees they say have lasted from heathen times. I’m a

[013-075]good Catholic, but I think I could get along with caring for trees, if I hadn’t

[013-076]anything else.”

[013-077]“That’s a poor saying,” said Emil, stooping over to wipe his hands in the wet

[013-078]grass.

[013-079]“Why is it? If I feel that way, I feel that way. I like trees because they seem

[013-080]more resigned to the way they have to live than other things do. I feel as if

[013-081]this tree knows everything I ever think of when I sit here. When I come back to

[013-082]it, I never have to remind it of anything; I begin just where I left off.”

[013-083]Emil had nothing to say to this. He reached up among the branches and began to

[013-084]pick the sweet, insipid fruit,—long ivory-colored berries, tipped with faint

[013-085]pink, like white coral, that fall to the ground unheeded all summer through. He

[013-086]dropped a handful into her lap.

[013-087]“Do you like Mr. Linstrum?” Marie asked suddenly.

[013-088]“Yes. Don’t you?”

[013-089]“Oh, ever so much; only he seems kind of staid and school-teachery. But, of

[013-090]course, he is older than Frank, even. I’m sure I don’t want to live to be more

[013-091]than thirty, do you? Do you think Alexandra likes him very much?”

[013-092]“I suppose so. They were old friends.”

[013-093]“Oh, Emil, you know what I mean!” Marie tossed her head impatiently. “Does she

[013-094]really care about him? When she used to tell me about him, I always wondered

[013-095]whether she wasn’t a little in love with him.”

[013-096]“Who, Alexandra?” Emil laughed and thrust his hands into his trousers pockets.

[013-097]“Alexandra’s never been in love, you crazy!” He laughed again. “She wouldn’t

[013-098]know how to go about it. The idea!”

[013-099]Marie shrugged her shoulders. “Oh, you don’t know Alexandra as well as you

[013-100]think you do! If you had any eyes, you would see that she is very fond of him.

[013-101]It would serve you all right if she walked off with Carl. I like him because he

[013-102]appreciates her more than you do.”

[013-103]Emil frowned. “What are you talking about, Marie? Alexandra’s all right. She

[013-104]and I have always been good friends. What more do you want? I like to talk to

[013-105]Carl about New York and what a fellow can do there.”

[013-106]“Oh, Emil! Surely you are not thinking of going off there?”

[013-107]“Why not? I must go somewhere, mustn’t I?” The young man took up his scythe and

[013-108]leaned on it. “Would you rather I went off in the sand hills and lived like

[013-109][[ivar|Ivar]]?”

[013-110]Marie’s face fell under his brooding gaze. She looked down at his wet leggings.

[013-111]“I’m sure Alexandra hopes you will stay on here,” she murmured.

[013-112]“Then Alexandra will be disappointed,” the young man said roughly. “What do I

[013-113]want to hang around here for? Alexandra can run the farm all right, without me.

[013-114]I don’t want to stand around and look on. I want to be doing something on my

[013-115]own account.”

[013-116]“That’s so,” Marie sighed. “There are so many, many things you can do. Almost

[013-117]anything you choose.”

[013-118]“And there are so many, many things I can’t do.” Emil echoed her tone

[013-119]sarcastically. “Sometimes I don’t want to do anything at all, and sometimes I

[013-120]want to pull the four corners of the Divide together,”—he threw out his arm and

[013-121]brought it back with a jerk,—“so, like a table-cloth. I get tired of seeing men

[013-122]and horses going up and down, up and down.”

[013-123]Marie looked up at his defiant figure and her face clouded. “I wish you weren’t

[013-124]so restless, and didn’t get so worked up over things,” she said sadly.

[013-125]“Thank you,” he returned shortly.

[013-126]She sighed despondently. “Everything I say makes you cross, don’t it? And you

[013-127]never used to be cross to me.”

[013-128]Emil took a step nearer and stood frowning down at her bent head. He stood in

[013-129]an attitude of self-defense, his feet well apart, his hands clenched and drawn

[013-130]up at his sides, so that the cords stood out on his bare arms. “I can’t play

[013-131]with you like a little boy any more,” he said slowly. “That’s what you miss,

[013-132]Marie. You’ll have to get some other little boy to play with.” He stopped and

[013-133]took a deep breath. Then he went on in a low tone, so intense that it was

[013-134]almost threatening: “Sometimes you seem to understand perfectly, and then

[013-135]sometimes you pretend you don’t. You don’t help things any by pretending. It’s

[013-136]then that I want to pull the corners of the Divide together. If you WON’T

[013-137]understand, you know, I could make you!”

[013-138]Marie clasped her hands and started up from her seat. She had grown very pale

[013-139]and her eyes were shining with excitement and distress. “But, Emil, if I

[013-140]understand, then all our good times are over, we can never do nice things

[013-141]together any more. We shall have to behave like Mr. Linstrum. And, anyhow,

[013-142]there’s nothing to understand!” She struck the ground with her little foot

[013-143]fiercely. “That won’t last. It will go away, and things will be just as they

[013-144]used to. I wish you were a Catholic. [[the-church|The Church]] helps people, indeed it does. I

[013-145]pray for you, but that’s not the same as if you prayed yourself.”

[013-146]She spoke rapidly and pleadingly, looked entreatingly into his face. Emil stood

[013-147]defiant, gazing down at her.

[013-148]“I can’t pray to have the things I want,” he said slowly, “and I won’t pray not

[013-149]to have them, not if I’m damned for it.”

[013-150]Marie turned away, wringing her hands. “Oh, Emil, you won’t try! Then all our

[013-151]good times are over.”

[013-152]“Yes; over. I never expect to have any more.”

[013-153]Emil gripped the hand-holds of his scythe and began to mow. Marie took up her

[013-154]cherries and went slowly toward the house, crying bitterly.
