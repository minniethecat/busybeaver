# Adèle's Dressing-Case

**Adèle's dressing-case** is a portable case containing the personal effects of [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]]. Though a mundane object in itself, the dressing-case plays a remarkably freighted role in the plot of *Perishable Goods*, serving successively as a token of care, a ransom condition imposed by the kidnappers, and ultimately a symbol of deliverance carried out of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] during Adèle's rescue.

## The Request

The dressing-case first appears during the planning conference at the Pleydell farm in Chapter II, after [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] and his party have arrived from England. Having interviewed the servants and examined the spot where [[adeles-glove|Adèle's glove]] was found, Mansel made a specific request: that Adèle's dressing-case "should be sent for and packed with such things as she would be glad to have" (002-109). This was not a sentimental gesture but a piece of operational foresight. Mansel instructed that the dressing-case was to be taken with the party wherever they went (002-109) — he anticipated that when Adèle was recovered, she would need her own clothes and belongings immediately, and there would be no time to return to the farm.

The original instruction captures both Mansel's foresight and the tenderness underlying his operational efficiency:

> Then he asked that Adèle's dressing-case should be sent for and packed with such things as she would be glad to have. This we were to take with us wherever we went.
>
> (002-109)

The packing of the case fell to Daphne Pleydell, who left the conference to arrange it (002-110). The dressing-case thus represented the party's absolute confidence that Adèle would be found alive — a confidence that stood in stark contrast to the despair of her husband, [[captain-pleydell|Captain Pleydell]], who lay upstairs with a broken leg, unable to believe that his wife could be recovered without a ransom demand.

## A Kidnapper's Condition

When the kidnappers' communication was finally discovered — concealed in a long-disused letter-box built into the gatepost at the Pleydell farm — the dressing-case featured explicitly in their instructions. The demand, conveyed through the intermediary of Rose Noble's network, required that Adèle's dressing-case be delivered as part of the conditions for her release. The kidnappers, it seemed, intended that their captive should be properly equipped for whatever destination they had in mind — a detail that simultaneously hinted at their organisational sophistication and at their assumption that Adèle would remain in their custody for some time.

The inclusion of the dressing-case in the kidnappers' demands gave it a curious dual status: it was simultaneously a token of Mansel's solicitude and an instrument of the criminals' control. That the same object should be requested by both the rescuer and the captor is one of those narrative symmetries that Dornford Yates handled with characteristic understatement.

## The Rescue

The dressing-case reappears at the climactic moment of Adèle's rescue from the Castle of Gath in Chapter VI. After Mansel and [[william-chandos|William Chandos]] had pulled Adèle from the underground water-channel into which she had plunged to escape her captors, Mansel directed her up a stairway into a room within the castle's secret passages. "Your dressing-case is there," he told her. "Change your clothes as quickly as ever you can" (006-087).

That the dressing-case was already positioned in the room ahead of the rescue is a testament to Mansel's meticulous planning: he had caused it to be placed there in advance, so that Adèle — drenched to the skin from her passage through the water-channel — could change into dry clothes immediately upon her liberation. The dressing-case thus became the first instrument of her return to dignity and comfort after days of captivity.

As the party made their escape through the castle's hidden apartments — the King's Closet, the King's Bedchamber, the Dining-room — Chandos "caught up her dressing-case and brought up the rear" (006-107). The dressing-case, packed at White Ladies days before and carried across Europe, was borne out of the Castle of Gath as tangible proof that the rescue was complete. Adèle had been recovered, and with her, the small private world of her personal belongings.

## Symbolic Weight

In a novel whose title — [[perishable-goods-phrase|"perishable goods"]] — reduces a human being to a commodity, the dressing-case stands as a quiet counter-symbol. It contains not valuables but *things she would be glad to have*: the intimate, irreplaceable articles of a woman's daily life that have no market value but are essential to personhood. Where Rose Noble and his network saw Adèle as an item of negotiable cargo, Mansel saw her as a person who would need her own clothes, her own brush, her own familiar things. The dressing-case is, in this sense, an assertion of Adèle's humanity against the brutal commodification that the novel's title encodes.
