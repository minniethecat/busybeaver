# George Hanbury

George Hanbury is introduced in the very first sentence of *Perishable Goods* as the narrator [[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s housemate and closest friend: "It was in October, 1926, that George Hanbury and I first set up house in [[wiltshire|Wiltshire]]." (001-001) Throughout the novel, he serves as a steadfast companion and trusted lieutenant in [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s dangerous undertakings.

## Role & Position

Hanbury occupies a position between the narrator [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and their leader [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]. He is present at every critical juncture — from the discovery of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s burglary to the final expedition. Along with [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] and [[william-chandos|Chandos]], "Hanbury and I had made our fortune in crime" (001-028), though always on the side of justice.

Hanbury's role is practical and loyal. He drives [[rolls-royce|the car]]s, handles logistics, and provides emotional support, often acting as a sounding board for the narrator's concerns. When [[william-chandos|Chandos]] shares disturbing news, he turns to Hanbury: "to [[maintenance|Maintenance]] I told George Hanbury my news, to find that he shared my concern" (001-030).

## Key Traits

**Loyalty**: Hanbury's devotion to [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is absolute. When [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is "possessed" with urgency, "Hanbury and I with him" (001-060) — he follows without question.

**Perceptiveness**: He quickly grasps the implications of cryptic messages. When a mysterious letter arrives, "It must be [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]], said Hanbury, looking up from the sheet" (001-075), correctly identifying their antagonist.

**Practical Competence**: Hanbury handles the mechanical and logistical aspects of their adventures. He "started our engine, as [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] took the wheel" (001-160) and is entrusted with critical tasks like changing travel arrangements (001-132).

**Emotional Honesty**: Unlike the stoic [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], Hanbury expresses shock and distress openly: "Oh, my God, said Hanbury" (001-103) when confronted with devastating news.

## Key Events

### The Revelation

When the letter from [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] arrives, Hanbury is among the first to read it and immediately recognizes the danger: "I still see no daylight," he says, acknowledging the gravity of the threat (001-082).

### The Expedition

Hanbury is integral to the planning and execution of the group's mission. He handles baggage logistics, drives [[rolls-royce|the car]]s, and manages the practical details that make their daring operation possible (001-132)(001-160).

### The Telegram

At a critical moment, Hanbury receives and opens an urgent telegram, demonstrating his role as a trusted handler of sensitive communications (001-170).

### Hanbury in the Field

Hanbury's role extends far beyond logistics. During the reconnaissance in
[[carinthia|Carinthia]], he drives the second car while [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] leads the convoy: "Mansel
was leading, and I was sitting with Hanbury, who was driving the second car"
(005-073). His presence in the field is constant and active: he is the one who
studies Lass's maps when they arrive — "Hanbury had the book in an instant and
was studying one of its maps" (004-125) — and his tactical judgment is trusted
by [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]].

When the party splits up during the pursuit of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s agents, Hanbury
operates independently: "Mansel and I took the left path, and Hanbury the right"
(003-143). This division of forces demonstrates [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s confidence that Hanbury
can handle himself alone — a significant trust, given the mortal danger of the
situation.

### Capture and Resilience

Hanbury endures one of the novel's most harrowing ordeals. During the infiltration
of [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], he is overpowered and taken prisoner. The narrator discovers him
"on the hearth lay George Hanbury, with his wrists and his ankles bound and a
gag in his mouth" (007-045). Despite this brutal treatment, Hanbury retains his
composure. Once freed, "George Hanbury told me his tale" (007-051), providing
critical intelligence about the enemy's movements and dispositions.

> And on the hearth lay George Hanbury, with his wrists and his ankles bound and a gag in his mouth.
>
> That George had been left there to live, if he could, but, if he could not, to die we had not much doubt. He had lain there, gagged and bound, for twenty-four hours: his bonds had never been loosened, he had been given no water, much less any food: he had not so much as been visited. And such as will so use a prisoner are scarcely like to be troubled to find him dead.
>
> (007-045--007-046)

His resilience under duress is matched by his physical competence. In the
fighting that follows, Hanbury proves himself capable of decisive violence:
"Thanks to Hanbury," the narrator explains, "He opened the passage door"
(008-128), and later, "Because Hanbury had laid him out" (008-135) — testament
to his willingness and capacity to fight when necessary.

### The Final Operation

Hanbury continues to play an essential role through the novel's conclusion.
He leads one of the tactical elements during the rescue of [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]],
and [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s planning repeatedly assigns him independent responsibilities:
"Mr. Hanbury with Bell and Rowley will go to the top of this tower" (007-144),
and "Mr. Hanbury, Rowley and Bell will find Mrs. Pleydell, release her and
carry her off" (007-145). In the final chapter, Hanbury facilitates the
surrender of the remaining gang members, and a character notes that "Hanbury
met you in the road at the end of the drive" (008-063), confirming his
continuing role in operations through the denouement.

## The Friendship with Chandos

The bond between Hanbury and the narrator [[william-chandos|Chandos]] is the emotional backbone of
the novel. They share a house — [[maintenance|Maintenance]] — and a life of hunting and
domesticity that predates the crisis. The narrator records that "for the next six
months we hunted four days a week, yet would commit to no one the pleasant task of
setting our home in order" (001-001). This is a friendship built on shared
rhythms: the hunt, the household, the mutual understanding that requires no
explanation.

Their companionship extends beyond England. When [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] announces the
European expedition, Hanbury's response is immediate and unqualified: "And
Hanbury and I with him" (001-060). The narrator does not record Hanbury asking
questions, weighing risks, or requesting time to consider. Hanbury's loyalty is
pre-verbal — it manifests as presence, as the simple fact of being there. When
the narrator offers his own observations during the crisis, he reliably turns
to Hanbury for confirmation: "I told George Hanbury my news, to find that he
shared my concern" (001-030). Hanbury functions as a mirror and a validator, the
person whose agreement makes a suspicion feel real.

## Hanbury as Comic Relief

Amid the novel's sustained tension, Hanbury provides moments of levity. His dry
wit surfaces at unexpected moments. When the party encounters a particularly
theatrical local informant, "Hanbury and I began to shake with laughter"
(003-017), and his murmured aside — "The man's mad" (003-019) — cuts through
the melodrama. His deadpan correction of a misunderstanding ("You must mean
fishing," said Hanbury (003-024)) demonstrates the English gift for deflating
absurdity with understatement. These moments are strategically placed: each
laugh Hanbury provokes releases the pressure that the narrative has built,
making the subsequent return to danger more effective by contrast.
