# The Oratory

The **oratory** at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] is a chamber that forms part of the castle's ecclesiastical architecture, situated between the day-chamber and the chapel. It features in the castle's guided tour and becomes a place of refuge during the novel's climactic confrontation.

## Location and Architecture

The oratory is part of the castle's interconnected sequence of state rooms documented in the guide-book description. It is accessed after passing through the Queen's day-chamber and an antechamber, before reaching the chapel:

> "The southeast tower is not shown. Now we come to the oratory. There is a door and stairs going down from the oratory into the chapel. There are many shrines in the mountains, but not like this. It is still here always, in the midst of the storm." (004-129)

The description emphasizes the oratory's atmosphere of permanence and sanctuary — "still here always, in the midst of the storm" — qualities that prove thematically significant when the room later serves as a place of safety.

## Refuge During the Confrontation

During the final confrontation with [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]], [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] and the servants — [[carson|Carson]], [[bell|Bell]], and [[rowley|Rowley]] — take refuge in the oratory. Rose Noble, after overpowering [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]], locks all the doors except those to the roof and the room where Mansel is held:

> "Well, that washed out George and the servants, for they were in the oratory, very properly biding their time." (008-276)

Hanbury's decision to wait in the oratory rather than attempt a premature intervention proves wise, preserving a force that can assist once the tide turns.

## The Search

After escaping from the roof, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] searches the castle systematically for Hanbury and the servants, trying the oratory last:

> "I had no idea where George and the servants were, and, as luck would have it, I tried the oratory last." (008-278)

The delay in finding them adds to the tension of the rescue sequence, but once located, Hanbury and the servants are freed to rejoin the operation.

## The Signal of Victory

After [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] has driven into the courtyard and sounded the horn to signal victory, the oratory becomes the point from which confirmation is given:

> "Before its echoes had died, a casement of the oratory was opened, and Hanbury put out his head. 'All over,' said Mansel simply. 'Open the doors.'" (008-291)

The oratory window thus frames the moment of triumph: Hanbury looking down from the casement as Mansel declares the operation complete.

## Significance

The oratory's role is modest but thematically resonant. A chamber designed for prayer and contemplation becomes, in the novel's climax, a place of strategic patience — those who wait within it survive and contribute to the final victory. Its "stillness in the midst of the storm" is realized not in spiritual terms but in tactical ones: a quiet room where loyal men bide their time while the storm of confrontation rages elsewhere in the castle.
