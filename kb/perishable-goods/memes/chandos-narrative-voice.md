# Chandos's Narrative Voice

William Chandos is not merely a character in *Perishable Goods* — he is the narrator, and his distinctive voice shapes every aspect of the reader's experience. The novel is told entirely in Chandos's first person, and his perspective as observer, participant, and loyal friend gives the story its particular tone: gentlemanly, understated, warm, and occasionally self-deprecating.

> Now neither Hanbury nor I would have dreamed of visiting London without calling on Jonathan Mansel, whose flat was in Cleveland Row; for we three had made our fortune together and together had proved the stuff of which friendship is made. That apart, Mansel was the very finest gentleman that ever I knew: his ways were quiet, and his address was simple; but there was a natural royalty about him such as, I think, few monarchs have been able to boast.
>
> (001-003)

## Characteristics

Chandos's narrative voice is defined by several consistent traits. His language is precise and cultivated — the vocabulary of an educated English gentleman of the 1920s — yet never pretentious. He describes action with clarity and economy, letting the events speak for themselves rather than embellishing them.

His perspective on [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] is particularly important. Chandos admires Mansel deeply but never descends into hero-worship. He records Mansel's decisions and speeches faithfully, but also notes his moments of doubt and his occasional errors of judgment. This balanced portrayal makes Mansel a believable hero rather than a caricature.

Chandos is also a notably honest narrator about his own limitations. He admits when he is frightened, confesses when he has misunderstood a situation, and never claims greater competence than he possesses. This candour builds trust with the reader and makes the story's most dramatic moments — the rescue on the terrace, the confrontation with Noble — feel immediate and real.
