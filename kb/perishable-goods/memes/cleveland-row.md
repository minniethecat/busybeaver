# Cleveland Row

Cleveland Row is a street in St. James's, London, and the location of
[[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s flat—the novel's urban anchor point and the
setting for the opening and pivotal planning scenes of Dornford Yates's *Perishable
Goods*. From its windows overlooking the royal parks to its role as the trio's
operational headquarters, Cleveland Row represents the civilized world that the
protagonists leave behind when adventure calls.

## Location & Setting

Cleveland Row lies in St. James's, one of London's most exclusive districts, adjacent
to St. James's Palace and Marlborough Gate. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] describes the approach:

> "I stepped out of Marlborough Gate into Cleveland Row. Except for my servant, Bell, I
> was alone." (001-004)

The flat itself is a gentleman's residence of the period—comfortable, well-appointed,
and staffed by [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s body-servant [[carson|Carson]]. It serves as a natural gathering
point for the trio of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], [[william-chandos|Chandos]], and
[[george-hanbury|Hanbury]], whose bond was forged in France before the events of the
novel (001-003).

## Narrative Significance

Cleveland Row serves as the novel's symbol of order and civilization. It is from this
secure London address that [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] first reveals [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s theft, and it is from its
doorstep that the protagonists depart into a world where—as events at the
[[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] demonstrate—the protections of law and society no
longer apply.

The flat's location in St. James's, within sight of royal palaces, places [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] and
his circle firmly within the British establishment. This positioning is thematically
important: the protagonists are not mercenaries or outlaws but gentlemen whose campaign
against [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] is, in their own view, a righteous restoration of order rather than a
criminal enterprise.

The contrast between Cleveland Row and the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]—London drawing-room versus
Alpine fortress—maps onto the novel's broader opposition between civilization and
lawlessness, the known and the unknown.
