# The Spur

The spur is the mountain ridge on which the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] is constructed, and it functions as both the fortress's foundation and its sole land approach. In *Perishable Goods*, the spur is far more than a geographical feature: it is contested ground, an observation post, a stage for confrontation, and the narrow causeway that governs every movement to and from the castle.

## The Nail Upon a Finger

[[william-chandos|William Chandos]] provides the definitive description of the spur's relationship to the castle:

> "We were now upon the spur of a mountain, and, I judged, some three thousand feet up. The whole of the end of the spur was masked by the castle's facade, and it was easy to see that the building had been set on the spur as the nail upon a man's finger, that is to say, moated on three of its sides by God knows what depths and accessible only on the fourth." (004-085)

The simile---"the nail upon a man's finger"---is precise and evocative. The castle caps the spur as a nail caps a fingertip, surrounded by precipice on three sides and connected to the world only by the narrow ridge of rock along which the drive runs. This geography makes the castle virtually impregnable to direct assault.

## The Exposed Approach

The spur's most defining characteristic is its exposure. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] notes: "there were not six trees on the spur and the wood lay two hundred yards from the castle wall" (004-100). The absence of cover makes approach impossible to conceal: "such as found it could not conceal their coming" (004-100). For [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s party, this means every visit must be accounted for; for [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s garrison, it means any approaching figure is visible long before reaching the gate.

The drive traverses the spur in a carefully engineered course: "The drive which led to the spur went to the heart of the thicket and there turned sharp to the left: from there it ran slightly up hill to the edge of the wood and then directly down to the castle gate" (004-120). The dramatic reveal is described in theatrical terms: "from anyone walking, the spur and the castle were hidden till he came to the edge of the wood, but then burst upon him in an instant, as though some curtain had suddenly been let fall" (004-120).

## Approach by Car

The party's first approach demonstrates the spur's function as a stage for performance. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] records: "At the sight of the Castle we stopped, as anyone would have done, and were plainly uncertain whether or no to proceed: but, after a little discussion, I drove the car down the spur and drew up before the gateway in a perfectly natural way" (005-004). The hesitation is part of the performance---the party presents itself as innocent tourists overwhelmed by the sight.

Later, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] describes the spur in deceptively peaceful terms: "The day was very fine, and a gentle breeze was blowing across the spur: except for the whisper of the engine, there was no sound: and I remember thinking how gay the greenwood looked in the brave sunshine and how black the clean-cut shadow which the battlements threw upon the turf" (005-007). The pastoral description belies the fact that the spur is the approach to a fortress holding a kidnapped woman.

## The Spur as Lookout

Throughout the operation, the spur serves as an observation post. The rescue party maintains a presence there, watching for movement to and from the castle. [[carson|Carson]] reports with military language: "We fairly picketed that spur" (007-058). The spur is the eyes of the operation; to leave it unwatched is to risk being seen without seeing in return. When [[bell|Bell]] must leave for other duties, he notes his reluctance: "I hated leaving the spur, but it didn't seem prudent for one to go out alone" (007-059).

A captured enemy is instructed to come "to the spur" by night and "there wait till one of us met him or else it was dawn" (007-112). The spur thus functions as a neutral meeting ground---a place where friend and foe alike must pass, and where clandestine encounters can be arranged under cover of darkness.

## The Escape Route

In extremis, the spur becomes the only escape route. When [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] flees: "With never a look behind him, Rose Noble passed up the spur and into the drive" (008-185). The party, waiting in ambush: "We were now on the fringe of the wood, and I could see the castle between the leaves: the drive was ten paces away and the spur but three" (008-186).

The spur's role as sole exit is dramatised when a fugitive faces a critical choice: "He had not rope enough to go by the cliff---the spur was his only way" (008-236). The spur is both the sole land route and, for those without ropes, the only possible path.

When [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] and [[bell|Bell]] must get [[adele-pleydell|Adele]] to safety: "Bell and I slid down from the roof to the spur" (008-283). In the novel's closing pages, the final departure: "Five minutes later the Rolls stole up the spur and into the wood" (009-189).

## A Sentry's View

The spur is watched from within the castle. In the porter's lodge, "on either side of the window, which was some way above the ground, were steps leading up to two stalls, cut out of the wall, from which the porters could comfortably watch the spur" (008-039). The architectural detail reveals the spur's historical function: it was designed to be watched, and the porter's stalls---permanent observation posts built into the wall centuries before---are now used by [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s sentries for the same purpose.

The bookseller of Lass, a respectable citizen well acquainted with Gath, visits the spur simply to look upon the castle: "when he kept holiday, therefore, that he should visit the spur was natural enough" (008-046). His appearance---an innocent visitor on the spur---nearly exposes the entire operation.

## The Spur in Retrospect

In the novel's closing pages, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] reflects on the spur as part of a landscape he is leaving forever: "Castle, spur and thicket, mountains and forests and the flash of the water below seemed all 'such stuff as dreams are made on,' and I could not shake off the feeling that we were about to quit a fantastic country which, search for it as we might, we never should see again" (009-167). The spur has become part of a dreamscape---a "fantastic country" that exists only in memory.
