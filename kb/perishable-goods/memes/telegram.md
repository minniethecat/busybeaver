# The Telegram

The telegram that arrives at [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s [[cleveland-square|Cleveland Square]] flat is the
pivot point of *Perishable Goods* — the moment when a London burglary becomes
a European manhunt. Delivered by [[carson|Carson]] during [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s council of war with
[[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]], its contents propel the entire narrative from England to
[[carinthia|Carinthia]].

## Delivery

The telegram arrives in the immediate aftermath of the burglary's discovery,
while [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is briefing his companions on the stolen letters. The narrator
records:

> Presently Carson entered, bearing a telegram. (001-091)

The timing is significant — [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] has just explained the nature of the stolen
papers and [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s blackmail scheme when this new intelligence arrives,
compounding the crisis.

## Contents

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] reads the telegram aloud to [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]]:

> He rose to his feet and handed the telegram to me: "Return Adèle disappeared
> Carinthia." (001-097)

The message is terse, in telegram style — but its implications are devastating.
[[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]], the young American wife of [[captain-pleydell|Captain Pleydell]], has vanished in
the Austrian province of [[carinthia|Carinthia]], the very region where [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s Castle
of Gath is located.

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s reaction is immediate: "Shall I call in the police, [[daphne-pleydell|Pleydell]]?"
(001-097). But the decision is already made — the party will pursue [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]
to [[carinthia|Carinthia]] themselves.

## Narrative Significance

The telegram transforms the stakes of the story. Before its arrival, the party
faces a theft in London; after it, they face a kidnapping abroad. [[william-chandos|Chandos]]
reflects on what might have been:

> Had Rose Noble's letter followed instead of preceding the telegram, the secret
> was ours. (001-111)

The telegram's arrival after the burglary rather than before means that [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]
and his companions are always one step behind — reacting rather than
anticipating.

[[william-chandos|Chandos]] also notes [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s emotional state: "I think that the depth of feeling
he showed, when the telegram was brought in, was because we might at that
moment have been starting for Austria" (001-111) — [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s fury is at the
lost opportunity, not at the news itself.

## The Double Blow: Letter and Telegram

The telegram does not arrive in isolation. It lands in the midst of a council already reeling from [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s letter, which has proposed a ruinous financial demand in exchange for the stolen papers. [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] immediately identifies the letter's author: "It must be Rose Noble, for nobody else would know you could raise such a sum" (001-075). The party sits in silence, unable to make useful comment without knowing the nature of the papers [[rose-noble|Noble]] holds (001-077).

The conjunction of letter and telegram is the cruel hinge of the plot. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] spells out the tragic arithmetic: had the letter arrived after the telegram, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] would never have revealed the secret of the papers, and "neither George Hanbury nor I would ever have suspected the truth" (001-111). Instead, the letter precedes the telegram, forcing [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s hand — he discloses his vulnerability to his friends, and the three are now irrevocably bound together in the mission.

## Mansel's Decision

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s response to the twin crises reveals his character. He refuses to act on the letter's suggestion of a payoff: "I am not going to act upon the suggestion here made, because, for one thing, such a sum is ruinous, and, for another, I do not trust Rose Noble" (001-113). He dismisses the blackmail letter as a document to be "counted out" (001-119). His decision is military: the party will not negotiate with a kidnapper and blackmailer but will pursue [[rose-noble|Noble]] directly into [[carinthia|Carinthia]].

The telegram also crystallizes the moral urgency of the mission. [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] is, as [[william-chandos|Chandos]] puts it, "in Rose Noble's hands — perishable goods" (001-107). The phrase gives the novel its title and defines the desperate stakes: [[rose-noble|Noble]] can destroy [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] at any moment, and the party must move with all speed.

## Departure and Secrecy

The party makes no secret of their departure for [[carinthia|Carinthia]] — it is "a movement we could not hide" (001-142). [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] observes pragmatically that it does not much matter if someone wires [[rose-noble|Noble]] of their coming: the chase is now open, and speed, not stealth, is the decisive factor. The telegram has transformed a discreet London affair into an overt European pursuit, and from this point forward the narrative never looks back.
