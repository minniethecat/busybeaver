# Pistols

Pistols are the primary weaponry of the characters in Dornford Yates's
*Perishable Goods*. As the action moves from London drawing-rooms to the
remote [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], firearms become essential equipment — distributed
to servants and gentlemen alike, and wielded on both sides of the conflict
between [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] and [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]].

## Arming the Party

When [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] prepares for the operation at [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], he ensures his
entire party is armed. The narrator records:

> Mansel told Carson and Bell to serve out a pistol apiece. (004-115)

This distribution of firearms to servants — [[carson|Carson]] ([[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s body-servant) and
[[bell|Bell]] ([[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s man) — marks the transition from civilian life to military-style
operation. The party crosses into Austria equipped for confrontation.

## Encounters with the Enemy

Pistols feature prominently in every armed encounter. During the confrontation
with [[jute|Jute]], [[casemate|Casemate]]'s enforcer, the party finds him "with his arms folded and a
pistol in his right hand" (005-052). After subduing [[jute|Jute]], "we took a pistol
from his pocket and put him out of action" (005-080).

The villains are heavily armed: [[casemate|Casemate]]'s men "carried a knife as well as a
pistol and wore a coil of rope" (005-163), suggesting professional preparation.

## The Underground Passage

In one of the novel's most tense sequences, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] finds himself unarmed in
the underground passages beneath [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]:

> "Mansel," I said. "Give me your pistol and torch." (006-016)

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] "gave me his pistol unwillingly enough" (006-019). When the arch of the
tunnel collapses, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] records: "I managed to save both pistols and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]]'s
torch" (006-021) — a small triumph in the face of disaster.

## The Final Confrontation

The pistols reach their narrative climax in the final confrontation with [[rose-noble|Rose]]
[[rose-noble|Noble]]. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] describes the scene:

> Rose Noble sat with his elbows upon the board and a pistol in either hand.
> (006-159)

And moments later:

> At that chair, sat Rose Noble. One pistol lay before him: the other was in
> his hand. (006-167)

The decisive moment comes when [[william-chandos|Chandos]] acts: "The left struck aside the pistol,
as [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] fired: the right crashed into his face" (006-220). After the
struggle, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] counts the remaining arsenal: "We had three pistols, each
holding seven rounds" (006-235).

## Throughout the Campaign

Pistols remain a constant presence through the later chapters. In Chapter VII,
[[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] is seen "covering the gap with his pistol and steady as any rock"
(007-131). In Chapter VIII, when [[william-chandos|Chandos]] is captured: "My pistol had been taken,
but not my knife" (008-005), and the captor "had me by the collar and a pistol
was hurting my ribs" (008-129). The final chapter sees [[bell|Bell]]'s pistol
redistributed: "I took [[bell|Bell]]'s pistol, posted [[george-hanbury|George]] and [[rowley|Rowley]]" (008-279),
and [[william-chandos|Chandos]] framed "in the passage doorway, pistol in hand" (008-297).
