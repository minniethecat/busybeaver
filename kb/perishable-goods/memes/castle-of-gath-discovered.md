# Castle of Gath Discovered

The discovery of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] is the climax of the search operation that occupies Chapters III and IV of *Perishable Goods*. Following [[rouse|Hannibal Rouse]]'s trail to the ancient town of [[lass|Lass]], [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s party identifies the remote fortress where [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]] is imprisoned, establishes visual contact with her, and begins the surveillance that will precede the rescue operation.

## The Pursuit to Lass

The trail began when [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s trap closed on [[rouse|Rouse]]. Having staged a false alert — [[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] arriving at the [[villach|Villach]] inn shouting that [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] had "sent word" (003-094) — [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] correctly predicted that [[rouse|Rouse]] would flee to report to [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] (003-110). The party's own cars had been subtly disabled by [[bell|Bell]] to prevent [[rouse|Rouse]] from driving (003-111).

[[rouse|Rouse]] boarded the Innsbruck train at Villach. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], [[william-chandos|William Chandos]], and [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] were aboard the same train in a sleeping-car compartment. From the corridor, they overheard [[rouse|Rouse]]'s voice — transformed, stripped of its buffoonery — demanding of the attendant: "What time do we get to [[lass|Lass]]?" (003-131).

That single word, [[lass|Lass]], was the key. A map study showed it lay fifty miles from Innsbruck, in highly mountainous country (003-136).

At [[lass|Lass]], however, plans went awry. A railway delay had allowed [[carson|Carson]] and [[bell|Bell]], following with the [[rolls-royce|cars]], to catch up to the train. [[rouse|Rouse]] spotted the two Rolls-Royces approaching as he walked from the station. In a brilliant piece of improvisation, he flagged down a small motor bus, engaged its driver in argument until he could board, and "under the eyes of us all was rapidly driven away" (003-151).

The near-disaster was partly retrieved by [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]], who sprinted after the bus and tracked [[rouse|Rouse]] to a close in the old quarter of [[lass|Lass]] (003-170). There, in a second-hand bookshop, the party regrouped.

## Bell's Recognition

As the party watched the close, uncertain which of its ancient houses sheltered [[rouse|Rouse]], a man emerged. [[bell|Bell]] spoke: "Sir, that's the man. *He was on the quay at Dieppe*" (003-178).

The identification was the payoff of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s hours of patient work with [[bell|Bell]]'s memory (002-020). The man — tall, loose-limbed, with a pasty face, walking "as though he liked the look of himself," known as [[casemate|Casemate]] (004-012) — was the spy who had planted the "perishable goods" note on [[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s car seat at Dieppe (001-176). His presence at [[lass|Lass]] proved that the trail was hot.

## The Pursuit of Casemate

What followed was an improvised street pursuit through the medieval maze of [[lass|Lass]]. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] discarded his coat, hat, collar, and tie, smeared himself with oil and dirt, and donned a mechanic's overalls to shadow [[casemate|Casemate]] through the town (004-005)(004-023). [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] wore the bookseller's coat and hat, green with age. [[bell|Bell]] cycled, his face and hands "filthy beyond compare" (004-010).

The pursuit led out of [[lass|Lass]], along a mountain road. When [[casemate|Casemate]] suddenly doubled back, trying to flush out a tail, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] was forced to leap for an oak branch overhanging the road (004-041). From this perch, he witnessed the encounter that identified the prison.

## The Meeting on the Road

A car approached at speed. [[casemate|Casemate]] signaled it to stop. The man who put out his head was [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] himself (004-049).

The exchange that followed — overheard by [[william-chandos|Chandos]] from the oak bough — was a torrent of information. [[casemate|Casemate]] relayed the news that [[rouse|Rouse]] ("Big Willie") had been drawn to [[lass|Lass]] by a false report that [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] had got word through to her family (004-051). [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] instantly recognized the ruse and erupted in fury: "Of course he brought him. You snake-faced idiot, what do you take me for?" (004-064).

The tirade revealed the criminal network's structure: [[rouse|Rouse]] as the head spy, [[casemate|Casemate]] as his subordinate, a man named [[jute|Jute]] who had compounded the error by following [[rouse|Rouse]] by train when his car would not start (004-064), and [[bunch|Bunch]] as [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s driver. More critically, [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s instructions to [[bunch|Bunch]] confirmed their violent intent: "If you see that cyclist, run him down" (004-080) — meaning [[bell|Bell]].

## The Ride to Gath

As [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s car turned and began to maneuver on the narrow road, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] saw his chance. The car passed directly beneath the oak branch. "In a flash I was sitting sideways upon the bough, and, as the car came back for the last time, I stepped gingerly on to its roof, trusting to my rubber-soled shoes to make no noise" (004-078).

Flat on the canopy roof, gripping the sun-visor above the driver, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] rode as [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s unwitting passenger up the mountain road. [[bunch|Bunch]] drove with dangerous speed; [[william-chandos|Chandos]] "had to fight like a madman to stay where I was" (004-083).

The car left the main road for a hidden drive shrouded in moss and woodland litter, "so artlessly... that a man might have passed it by" (004-084). In less than two minutes the wood fell behind and the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] burst into view.

## The Castle

The moment of identification, when Mansel confirms they have found their quarry:

> “Gath,” he said, after a moment. “This should be the Castle of Gath. And now for the text.”
>
> (004-126)

[[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s first sight of the castle was overwhelming: "We were heading for a castle which seemed to me to command all the kingdoms of the earth" (004-084).

The building stood on the spur of a mountain some three thousand feet up, moated on three sides by precipices and accessible only by the drive that ran down the spur's spine (004-085). The walls were "high and massive," rising into battlements, with a stout round tower at each end, their red conical roofs above corniced stone. A great gateway with heavy doors dominated the facade. The place had "an air of being old rather than ancient and more of an aerie than a keep" (004-086).

As the car halted before the gateway, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] was "trembling with excitement" — he was about to be carried within the walls (004-087). But the castle door was built in two halves; the upper half stayed fast while the lower swung open. With only three inches of headroom, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] had to leap from the roof. He landed on sloping turf, tumbled, and heard the door shut and the bolts shot home (004-096).

## Mansel's Reconnaissance

Reporting back to [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] at the crossroads, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] was crushed by his near-miss. But [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] grasped his arm: "William, she will thank you... If you had gone in, you'd have made a fatal mistake. Alone, unarmed and with no idea of the building, you wouldn't have stood an earthly: and, what would have been far worse, *we shouldn't have known where you were*" (004-108).

The party then approached the castle on foot. From the edge of the wood, the spur and fortress burst upon them "as though some curtain had suddenly been let fall" (004-120). [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] produced the bookseller's guide to [[lass|Lass]], which identified the building as the Castle of Gath, built by the Holy Roman Emperor Maximilian I in the late fifteenth century as a private retreat — "so as to be a tranquil castles where he can retire to rest sometimes from the cares of reign" (004-129).

The guide's description was invaluable, providing a room-by-room tour of the State Apartments: the antechambers, the King's Bedchamber, the Queen's Bedchamber, the oratory, the chapel, and — critically — "a pretty terrace, where flowers blow, and running all of the length of the South of the Castles" (004-129). The guide also noted that ownership was in legal dispute and the interior was not currently shown to visitors — giving [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] perfect cover (004-129).

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s comment on reading this was simple: "One has much to be thankful for. Let's hope they let her walk on 'the pretty terrace, where flowers blow'" (004-133).

## The Signal

The party then climbed an opposing mountain whose summit commanded the castle's south and east sides. With binoculars, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] studied the fortress.

"It was the binoculars that showed us [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]" (004-166).

She was standing on the little terrace, hands on the balustrade, alone, with the cascade leaping below her and an archway framing a shut door behind. Her head was up, her eyes lifted toward the peak where the party stood (004-167).

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] tore off his wristwatch, ripped open its case, and thrust it into [[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s hand. "Flash it in the sun. If she sees it, she'll understand" (004-169).

[[william-chandos|Chandos]] flashed the silver case while [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] watched through the binoculars. A coded exchange followed — six flashes, then one, then more — and [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] raised her hand. When the acknowledgment was complete, she moved to the tower window and showed a white cloth there, establishing her exact location within the vast building. "She's very wise — [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]" (004-183).

## The Strategy

With the prison located, contact established, and the layout known, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] laid out his strategy. A direct assault on a properly manned fortress was impossible. The alternative was deception: "There's only one thing to do, and that is to go on playacting" (004-136).

The party would make a show of scouring the countryside, appearing to search everywhere except [[castle-of-gath|Gath]]. On the following day they would "discover" the castle and drive up to ring the bell — letting [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] believe they had stumbled upon it by chance. "And, if all goes well, two or three days later we'll call again. But this time we'll come by night, and I think we'll let the bell go" (004-138).

The rationale was counter-intuitive but sound: so long as [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] believed the party was searching blindly, he would keep a sentry on the walls; but if he thought the party knew where [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] was, he would be "out of touch" and might relax his guard, not knowing whether they had given up or were preparing something (004-142).

## Significance

The discovery of the Castle of Gath is the novel's central revelation. It transforms the search from a blind casting across [[carinthia|Carinthia]] into a focused siege, and it demonstrates the peculiar genius of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s method — not the direct assault of a soldier but the layered deception of an intelligence officer.

[[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s ride on the roof of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s car is the most physically daring feat of the narrative, a cinematic sequence of improvised courage. And the signal from the mountain — [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] on the terrace, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] flashing his watchcase, the silent acknowledgment across the gulf — is the novel's emotional high point: the captive and her rescuer communicating in a "common language which others did not know" (004-185).

The discovery scene also cements the book's central thematic pairing. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] reflects: "For the first time I saw how well and truly she and [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] were matched. They were made of the same fine substance, a little higher than their fellows" (004-185).

That night, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] wrote to Captain [[daphne-pleydell|Pleydell]]: "I know where [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] is confined and saw her today from a distance. She seemed to be well. I am glad to say that I managed to attract her attention, and she now knows that help is at hand" (004-188).

Mansel identifies the fortress from his ancient text:

> "Gath," he said, after a moment. "This should be the Castle of Gath. And now for the text."
