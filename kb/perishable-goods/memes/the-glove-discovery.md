# The Glove Discovery

The Glove Discovery is a critical moment in Chapter II of *Perishable Goods*. During the briefing at [[poganec|Poganec]], [[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] reveals that Fitch, a servant, found one of [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]]'s gloves lying by the roadside eleven miles from the farm — not in the direction of [[sava|Sava]], where she was believed to have ridden (002-054)(002-055). The glove is the first and only physical evidence of the abduction, and [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] later interprets its position as proof that [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] was transferred between vehicles at that spot (002-154).

## The Disappearance

[[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] laid out the facts to the newly arrived rescue party: "Today is Thursday. At seven on Monday morning [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] went out for a ride. She rode in the direction of [[sava|Sava]], a village ten miles away. Her mare came in on her own at a quarter past eight. Everyone turned out at once, but found nothing. The mare was clean and unhurt" (002-054).

The initial assumption — that [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] had met misfortune on the road to [[sava|Sava]] — directed all searches in that direction. Inquiries were made at all farms in the vicinity of [[sava|Sava]]. They proved fruitless. "Nobody saw [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] on Monday morning" (002-054).

The day had been hot. [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] was wearing breeches and boots, a white silk shirt, and gloves. She had no hat (002-054).

## The Glove

Then, on Tuesday morning — the day after the disappearance — Fitch found the glove. It was "lying by the side of the road almost exactly eleven miles from here, *but not in the direction of Sava*" (002-055).

The discrepancy was glaring. If [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] had been riding toward [[sava|Sava]], her glove should not have been found on a road pointing elsewhere. The evidence suggested that her captors had doubled back or taken a different route after intercepting her.

## The Ransom Demand in the Gate-Box

The briefing at [[poganec|Poganec]] also revealed the devastating effect of the disappearance on Captain "Boy" [[daphne-pleydell|Pleydell]], [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]'s husband. Propped up with pillows in his bed, his leg broken, his thick dark hair turned "white as snow" (002-051), he spoke with a steady voice but a broken spirit.

He had prepared five thousand pounds in notes, but no demand had come. His conclusion was harrowing: "It never occurred to me that [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] had been taken for herself" (002-062) — meaning, not for ransom but as a sexual captive. "Face the facts, [[jonathan-mansel|Jonah]]: and, when you've had a good look at them, come back and try to tell me not to turn my face to the wall" (002-076).

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] refused to accept this. "I know they're out for money," he insisted (002-075). Then, in a flash of inspiration, he demanded: "Haven't you even looked in the box in the gate?" (002-087).

The party rushed down the drive. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], lame from an old war wound, ran ahead (002-089). The letter-box was "a doll's-house business, cut out of the gatepost itself, with a slit for letters before and a little iron door behind" (002-091). The door had not been opened for years. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] forced the lock with a chisel (002-092).

[[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] thrust in his hand and drew out a folded paper (002-094). It was the identical ransom demand that [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] had received in [[cleveland-square|Cleveland Row]]:

> "The stolen goods will be returned on the receipt by the Manager of the ——— Bank, Zurich, of your cheque for five hundred thousand pounds. This sum you can raise, if you please. No time should be wasted, for the goods are perishable. August 30th." (002-094)

The letter had been there since Monday — the day of the abduction. [[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] "bellowed" his news up the drive: "It's come! It's come! It's been here since Monday. The demand..." (002-096).

## Mansel's Reading of the Glove

Later, after the crisis had passed, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] returned to the site where the glove was found and directed the party to search for tire marks. His interpretation was precise: "here I am certain that she changed from one car to another and, while she did so, contrived to drop her glove, for they would have watched her too closely to let her throw it out as she went" (002-154).

He showed them where oil had dripped onto the grass at the edge of the road — "as is sometimes the way of a car which is standing still" (002-155). His reconstruction: "The relay was waiting, half on and half off the grass, close up to the hedge. The other ran up alongside, and the transfer was made" (002-156).

Despite an hour's search "as they do on a dairy farm," nothing more was found (002-157).

## Significance

The glove disclosure operates on two levels. On the surface, it is the first physical clue — a breadcrumb dropped by a captive who, watched too closely to throw it, nonetheless managed to leave a sign. On a deeper level, the scene exposes the psychological toll of the abduction: Captain [[daphne-pleydell|Pleydell]]'s white hair and near-despair, [[daphne-pleydell|Daphne]]'s sobs, and the dreadful silence when no ransom demand appeared. The discovery of the demand in the gate-box is a narrative reversal that restores hope — money, not malice, is the motive — and it is made possible only because [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] had not burned [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s earlier letter (001-120).

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s laconic lesson to [[william-chandos|Chandos]] as they walked back up the drive — "Learn of me. *Never burn anything*" (002-098) — encapsulates the forensic temperament that pervades the entire rescue operation.
