# Mansel Wounded

The wounding of [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] is the most shocking moment in *Perishable Goods*, occurring immediately after the triumph of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s death. Shot by [[casemate|Casemate]] in the "gallery of stone" in [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]], Mansel's injury transforms the narrative from a story of rescue and revenge into a desperate struggle for survival.

## The Setting

The shooting occurred in the southwest tower of [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]], moments after Mansel had returned triumphant from the wood. He had driven back with [[william-chandos|Chandos]], entered through the ground-floor window, and opened the great gate (008-290). [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]], on watch in the oratory, had been summoned to "open the doors" (008-292).

Everything seemed concluded. Chandos was following leisurely, "still thinking on the death of Rose Noble and of all that had passed" (008-294). He noted with wonder that the clock in the dashboard of the [[rolls-royce|Rolls]] read no more than half-past nine—the entire confrontation with Rose Noble, from the ambush to the burial, had taken mere hours.

## The Attack

As Chandos descended the winding stair to the guardroom, he came upon a scene of quiet intimacy that was about to be shattered. "The passage door was open, and Mansel was standing in the passage, fronting Adele. His back was towards me, but I saw that his head was bowed and he had her hand to his lips. The back of her other hand was across her eyes" (008-296).

Between them and Chandos, framed in the passage doorway, crouched [[casemate|Casemate]]—the man they had all forgotten. "And between them and me crouched Casemate framed in the passage doorway, pistol in hand" (008-297). Casemate, whom Mansel had knocked unconscious in the guardroom earlier that day, had recovered. Chandos had thought of him twice since the rescue but had been distracted each time (009-001).

Chandos let out a cry "that might have been heard in Lass" (008-298), but it was too late. Casemate fired, and Chandos "saw Mansel stagger a little and then sink down on his knees" (008-298). Before Casemate could turn, Chandos "had knocked him flat on his face and was kneeling upon his back. Then I took my knife and drove it into his spine" (008-299).

## The Wound

The verdict was grim. "But the mischief was done. Mansel was still alive, but the bullet had entered his stomach, and there was death in his face" [008-300–008-301].

The party carried Mansel into "the lovely bedchamber" and laid him upon the King's bed (009-003)—the same great bed of Maximilian that had figured in the earlier narrative. When they would have removed his coat, Mansel refused: "He's got me all right," he whispered: "so let me be" (009-005). To Chandos's tearful attempt at apology, Mansel responded with characteristic stoicism: "Rot. Luck of the battle—that's all" (009-008).

## The Vigil Begins

With Mansel on the King's bed, a death-watch commenced. [[adele-pleydell|Adele]] sat upon the great bed, "steady-eyed as ever, but very pale," her hand in Mansel's (009-009). One ring of the broken handcuffs was still about her slim leg—a brutal reminder of her recent captivity. On Mansel's other side lay [[tester|Tester]], "close up against his lord," his chin on his little paws and his eyes on Mansel's face (009-010).

The bloodstain spread slowly on the ancient silken quilt: "Upon this there was now a great bloodstain, very slowly spreading about his hips" (009-022). The terrible sight shocked Chandos, who covered his eyes.

## Chandos's Remorse

The wounding of Mansel haunted [[william-chandos|Chandos]] for the remainder of his life. His opening words in Chapter IX are self-accusatory: "I shall never forgive myself for forgetting that Casemate was still at large" (009-001). He had thought of Casemate twice—once by the brook awaiting Mansel's return, and again while Mansel was opening the castle gate—but each time "I had let some other matter thrust Casemate out of my mind" (009-001).

Chandos's guilt was compounded by the circumstances: he had been the one to knock Casemate down and had been told by Mansel that the man would be "safe for an hour" (008-286)—a prediction that proved accurate but insufficient, as more than an hour had passed. He had even, in Casemate's clothes, impersonated the man to deceive Rose Noble, yet somehow the danger Casemate still posed had slipped his mind.

## Significance

Mansel's wounding represents the cost of victory. The rescue of Adele, the killing of Rose Noble, the capture of Punter and Bunch—all of it had been accomplished. But victory was hollow while Mansel lay dying.

> I am free and safe and sound—at a terrible cost. Jonah has been wounded and lies at the point of death. Adèle.
>
> (009-099)

The wound transformed the story's remaining chapters, shifting focus from tactical operations to medical emergency, from combat to care, and ultimately to the moral reckoning that follows violence.
