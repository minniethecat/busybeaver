# Adele Tends Mansel

After [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] was shot by [[casemate|Casemate]] and underwent emergency surgery by [[buchinger|Dr. Buchinger]], the task of nursing him fell to [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]]. Her care of Mansel during the three critical days when his life hung in the balance is one of the most intimate and emotionally charged sequences in *Perishable Goods*, revealing the depth of the bond that had formed between them during their ordeal at [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]].

## The Deathbed Vigil

Chandos's description of Adele at Mansel's bedside is one of the novel's most moving images:

> Upon the great bed sat Adèle, steady-eyed as ever, but very pale. She might have been Eve, as Milton has pictured her, sitting upon the green bank, looking into the pool. Her left arm propped her, and she was sitting sideways, after the way of a child: one ring of the broken handcuffs was still about her slim leg. Her hand was in Mansel’s, and their two hands lay in her lap: her beautiful head was bowed, and her eyes never left the eyes of the man she loved.
>
> (009-009)

Before [[buchinger|Buchinger]]'s arrival, Adele kept vigil at Mansel's bedside in the King's bedchamber. The scene, as witnessed by [[william-chandos|Chandos]], was one of extraordinary composure amid devastation. "Upon the great bed sat Adele, steady-eyed as ever, but very pale. She might have been Eve, as Milton has pictured her, sitting upon the green bank, looking into the pool" (009-009).

One ring of the broken handcuffs was still about her slim leg—a remnant of her captivity under [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] (009-009). Her hand was in Mansel's, and "her beautiful head was bowed, and her eyes never left the eyes of the man she loved" (009-009).

On Mansel's other side lay [[tester|Tester]], the Sealyham terrier, "close up against his lord. Mansel's left hand was upon him; but, though, I think, he would have licked it, the poor scrap never moved, but lay as still as an image, with his chin on his little paws and his eyes upon Mansel's face" (009-010).

## Asking Tester's Permission

After the intimate conversation between Adele and Mansel in which he urged her to find strength within herself, Adele turned to the practical matter of his care. Her first act was characteristically humble and revealing: she asked Tester's permission to help.

Adele "seemed to brace herself. Then she took his hand and kissed it and held it against her heart" (009-045). Turning to Tester, she said quietly: "I'll try. If Tester will let me, I'll do what I can to help him; and, as soon as we've got our bearings, Tester and I will try" (009-046).

This small, tender moment encapsulates Adele's nature. She does not presume to take over Mansel's care; she seeks the approval even of his dog, recognizing that Tester's devotion to his master gives him a kind of claim upon the sickroom. It is an act of grace that acknowledges the bond between Mansel and his dog, and Adele's willingness to honour it by sharing the vigil rather than displacing it.

Chandos observed: "I saw the dog's ears lift, but he never stirred" (009-047)—a sign, perhaps, that Tester understood and accepted the arrangement.

## Mansel's Words to Tester

Mansel had already spoken to Tester himself, in one of the most heart-rending passages in the novel. "Poor little chap. I'm afraid it'll hit him hard" (009-048). Then Mansel told the dog directly: "he must look after Adele and that soon he was to be her dog, 'for you see, old fellow,' he murmured, 'I've got to go away. And it's not like the other times, for this time … I shan't come back'" (009-049).

The dog's reaction was immediate and devastating: "A tremor ran through the dog's frame, and he gave a little whimper that wrung my heart. As plainly as if he had spoken, he was acknowledging the sentence which the man that he worshipped had passed" (009-050). This was too much for Adele: "beneath this turn of the roller Adele broke down. She slipped from the bed to the floor, buried her face in her hands and shook like a leaf" (009-051).

## The Nursing Period

After Buchinger's surgery, Adele took on the primary nursing role during the three critical days when Mansel remained "standing still—on the very edge of death" (009-083). The strain on her was considerable. Buchinger expressed his concern directly: "I fear for the health of that beautiful girl" (009-084). He recommended that the night watch be shared, insisting that "if you do not want another patient upon your hands, I recommend you to insist that this is done" (009-102).

Adele's devotion, however, was absolute. When [[daphne-pleydell|Daphne Pleydell]] and [[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] later visited the castle by night, they witnessed the depth of her care. And when Mansel began to recover, it was under "the love of Adele" (009-114).

## Tester's Vigil

Throughout Mansel's recovery, Tester's conduct mirrored Adele's devotion. A box was set for the dog in the "gallery of stone," and "there he had lain, like a mouse, since the day on which Mansel was hit, never going further than the terrace, the door to which stood open, for him to use" (009-106).

Tester never tried to enter the sickroom, but "always rose and listened, whenever the door was opened by day or night, and anxiously scanned the faces of such as went" (009-106). This continued through the three dark days. Only after Mansel was out of danger did Tester "go out and about and took his ease" (009-106).

Chandos remarks on this extraordinary intuition: "I do not seek to magnify his instinct, but, well as we knew him, these things astonished us all, and, if speech was denied him, he had, I think, another and finer faculty" (009-107).

## Significance

The episode of Adele tending Mansel—and asking Tester's permission to do so—is central to the emotional architecture of the novel's final chapters. It transforms Adele from a damsel rescued into an active agent of Mansel's recovery, and it weaves the three figures—Mansel, Adele, and Tester—into a triangle of devotion and care. The dog's silent vigil and Adele's tireless nursing become twin testaments to the love that Mansel inspired in those around him.
