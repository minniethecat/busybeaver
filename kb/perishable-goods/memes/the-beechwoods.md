# The Beechwoods

The beechwoods are a stand of trees at the foot of the cliff below the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], occupying a critical position in the tactical geography of the rescue operation in *Perishable Goods*. The woods serve three distinct functions: as a concealed observation post, as the designated escape route for those lowered from the castle, and as the boundary between the exposed ground of the spur and the relative safety of the forest beyond.

## Location and Terrain

The beechwoods lie at the base of the cliff on the northeastern side of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]. The castle sits atop the cliff, and the woods below provide the nearest cover for anyone attempting to approach or depart unseen. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] identifies them by name and direction during a critical moment: "The moment you're down, unfasten the rope and run for your life to the beechwoods that lie northeast. Over there" (006-070). The directional precision---northeast---and the physical gesture underscore the woods' role as a predetermined objective in the escape plan.

The woods are distinguished from the general forest. The spur is "bare" with "not six trees" (004-100), but the beechwoods provide dense cover. This contrast between exposed spur and sheltered woods is fundamental to the operational geography: the spur is where you are seen; the beechwoods are where you hide.

## Observation Post

The beechwoods serve as a forward observation post for the rescue party. [[bell|Bell]] reports to [[carson|Carson]]: "Bell and I visited the beechwoods at three" (007-059). The visit is a patrol---a reconnaissance of the ground that will be used during the escape. The woods' position at the foot of the cliff makes them the closest concealed point from which the castle can be watched.

[[bell|Bell]] also reports his experience during an earlier phase: "When the car came out of the castle, I was down by the beechwoods at the foot of the cliff" (007-052). His concealment holds: "They didn't bother much about the foot of the cliff: they certainly went that way, for I saw them go by, but they passed at sixty and never so much as slowed up" (007-053). The enemy's speed and inattention confirm the beechwoods' value as a hiding place.

## The Escape Route

The beechwoods' most dramatic function is as the designated destination for those escaping the castle by rope. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s instruction is simple and absolute: run to the beechwoods. The command itself is one of the most urgent and directional moments in the narrative:

> "The moment you're down, unfasten the rope and run for your life to the beechwoods that lie northeast." He pointed the direction with his arm. "Over there. George or Carson will be there with one of the Rolls."
>
> (006-070)

The woods offer immediate concealment from anyone watching from the castle walls, and their position at the cliff's foot means that a person descending by rope need only cover a short distance across open ground.

The escape plan also involves vehicles: "according to plan, one of the cars must now leave for the foot of the cliff and stay there perhaps till dawn" (007-060). The car at the cliff's foot is the last link in the escape chain: castle to rope to beechwoods to car to safety. [[carson|Carson]] "decided to go alone to the foot of the cliff" (007-061) to ensure the escape route remains manned.

## The Wood as Frontier

The beechwoods function as a narrative boundary. The spur and castle grounds are territory controlled by [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]; the beechwoods and forest beyond are territory contested by [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s party. The drive leads "to the heart of the thicket and there turned sharp to the left: from there it ran slightly up hill to the edge of the wood and then directly down to the castle gate" (004-120). Reaching the wood means reaching relative safety.

This frontier quality is reinforced by the dramatic reveal: "from anyone walking, the spur and the castle were hidden till he came to the edge of the wood, but then burst upon him in an instant, as though some curtain had suddenly been let fall" (004-120). The wood is the curtain; passing through it reveals the stage. For the protagonists, moving in the opposite direction---from castle to wood---means drawing the curtain closed behind them.
