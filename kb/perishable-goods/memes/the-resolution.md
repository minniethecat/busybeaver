# The Resolution

The resolution of *Perishable Goods* is not a single dramatic event but a sequence of quiet, emotionally charged conversations on the terrace of [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]]. As [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] recovered from his wound, [[adele-pleydell|Adele]] and he, together with [[william-chandos|Chandos]], faced the moral implications of the love that had grown between them during their ordeal, and reached a resolution that would define the rest of their lives.

## Recovery

After the three-day crisis and [[buchinger|Dr. Buchinger]]'s announcement that Mansel would live (009-101), the atmosphere at the castle shifted from vigil to convalescence. The days became a "pleasant habit" and "Mansel grew steadily better under the love of Adele" (009-114). The visits of [[daphne-pleydell|Daphne Pleydell]] and [[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] were conducted discreetly, by night, and the world outside seemed none the wiser.

It was in this period of recovery that Adele sought Chandos out on the terrace, and the two had a conversation that laid bare the emotional and moral architecture of the novel's central relationship.

## Adele's Reflection

One morning, Chandos was sitting on the terrace, "on a cushion of one of the cars, looking at the exquisite prospect and finding much virtue in sloth," when Adele came to join him (009-115). "Care and her vigil had left her a little pale, but this only gave her beauty a delicate look which suited it very well" (009-116).

Adele began by recalling a question Chandos had asked her earlier: "Do you remember a question you asked me not very long ago? ... 'What about going back?'" [009-127–009-128]. She acknowledged that he had seen more clearly than she how difficult the return to ordinary life would be. "It would have been—awfully hard" (009-130).

> "Do you remember," she said, "a question you asked me not very long ago?"
>
> "Yes," said I. "'What about going back?'"
>
> (009-127–009-128)

But Adele had found the answer through suffering. She articulated a remarkable insight: "They say God tempers the wind to the shorn lamb. I'm sure He does—in His mysterious way. I've often wondered how such a thing could be done: but now I know. So soon as the lamb is shorn, He lets the wind blow for a moment *with ice in its breath*: and that moment's so dreadful that ever after that the lamb doesn't feel the wind" (009-130).

The "moment" of which she spoke was the half-hour when she believed Mansel was dying: "that half-hour was so dreadful that now the rest seems easy, and I'm not a bit afraid. It's the old question of contrast. I've lifted the load that I might have had to carry. It crushed me—for half an hour. And then for three days and nights I stood waiting to know my fate" (009-130).

## The Perfect Love-Affair

Adele's perspective on her relationship with Mansel was one of complete, almost serene acceptance. "I'm very lucky," she said. "My love-affair's been so perfect—from first to last. I was taken because he loved me, and then he came in his strength, to pull me out. I shared the rough with him; and, whilst we were under the shadow, he slept with his head in my lap: when he was hurt, I was there; and alone I've had the glory of helping him back to life" (009-133).

She recognized the extraordinary circumstances that made their love possible—and necessary. "It's all been out of the world: we've never had to use the back stairs, or whisper, or put out the light. There's been nothing sordid about it, and nothing cheap" (009-133).

Yet this perfection was, by its nature, finite. "When it's finished, it'll go, like a painting, into the quiet gallery of which you and he and I have the only keys" (009-133). The "quiet gallery" becomes the novel's central metaphor for memory—a private museum of shared experience accessible only to those who lived it.

## The Moral Question

Adele then raised the one issue that troubled her: her husband. "I'm upset about Boy. You can't get away from the fact that I'm letting him down. He is so good to me, and I love him so much. He's so proud of me and of Jonah, and he plays such a splendid game" (009-139).

She recounted a detail that spoke volumes about the character of her husband, [[captain-pleydell|Captain "Boy" Pleydell]]: when Chandos had taken her to [[poganec|Poganec]], the family had not told Boy she was coming, "because they knew he'd say that I mustn't leave Jonah's side. You can't beat that, can you?" (009-139).

Chandos's response was characteristically wholehearted: "As I live, I believe that he'd understand. Of course, you can't possibly tell him: but, if you did, I believe that he'd understand" (009-140).

## The Page and the Lady

Adele then asked the question at the heart of the novel's moral universe: "William, tell me. All the world would say that Jonah and I were doing a rotten thing. How is it you don't think so? How is it you understand?" (009-142).

Chandos's answer was simple and absolute: "There's no one like him" (009-143). And then: "And, then—there's no one like you" (009-145). He framed their love in a romantic, almost chivalric context: "The page and the lady. It's often happened before. And I shall survive" (009-147).

But his real explanation went deeper: "I can look at him with your eyes, and I can see you with his, and—well, Kings and Queens go together, and, oh, my dear, I'm so glad I shall have that key" (009-147). Chandos's acceptance of their love was rooted in his own love for both of them—a triangulation of affection that made moral judgment impossible.

Adele's response was to put out her hand, and when Chandos lifted her up, she left her fingers in his. "You're a lot like him," she said quietly. "Rowley told me who fetched the milk" (009-150)—revealing that Chandos himself had been driving through the night to bring fresh milk for Adele during Mansel's convalescence. Then she put her hand to his lips (009-151).

## Significance

The resolution on the terrace establishes the moral framework within which Mansel and Adele's love can be understood—not as a betrayal of [[captain-pleydell|Boy Pleydell]], but as a transcendent fact of nature that the participants themselves will honour by letting it go. Their love was real, it was beautiful, and it was over. It would survive only in the "quiet gallery" of memory, accessible only to those who had lived it and to Chandos, the witness who understood.
