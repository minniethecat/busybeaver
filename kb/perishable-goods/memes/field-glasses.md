# Field-Glasses

**Field-glasses** (binoculars) are among the most tactically significant pieces of equipment carried by [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s party during the Carinthian campaign in *Perishable Goods*. In an era before aerial reconnaissance, drones, or electronic surveillance, these optical instruments were the primary means by which the party observed enemy movements, reconnoitred terrain, and — most dramatically — established the first visual contact with the kidnapped [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]] at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]].

## Equipment and Specifications

The field-glasses carried by the party were of professional quality. Chandos notes that during the ascent of the observation peak overlooking Gath, "each of us carried a binocular of a considerable power" (004-161). These were not the compact opera-glasses of the drawing-room but serious optical instruments capable of resolving fine detail at substantial distances — essential for observing a fortress whose walls rose from a crag "that no man could ever scale" (004-164).

The glasses were part of a broader suite of equipment that the party carried or prepositioned for the campaign, alongside [[map|maps]], [[torch|electric torches]], and [[pistol|pistols]]. Their presence in the party's kit underscores Mansel's insistence on preparation: he had equipped his team not merely for armed confrontation but for the patient, methodical work of surveillance and reconnaissance.

## The Hog's Back Surveillance

The first deployment of field-glasses in the narrative occurs during the vehicle-tracking phase of Chapter II. Having traced the likely route of Adèle's abductors westward from the spot where [[adeles-glove|her glove]] was found, the party established an observation post on a great hog's-back ridge. From this vantage point, "four men with glasses could command a very great view" (002-161). Any car that was sighted was carefully watched, and if its line of travel could be determined, two members of the party would leave in pursuit.

> The others repaired to the hog's back, from which four men with glasses could command a very great view: if ever a car was sighted it was carefully watched, and two would leave in pursuit, so soon as the line it was taking could be fairly presumed.
>
> (002-161)

This systematic, glasses-aided surveillance of the road network was the operational method by which Mansel narrowed the search area and eventually identified the Castle of Gath as the kidnappers' destination.

## The First Sight of Adèle

The field-glasses achieved their most dramatic narrative function in Chapter IV, during the reconnaissance of the Castle of Gath. The party had climbed a wooded peak opposite the fortress and gained a view of the entire south and east faces. Chandos records that "the naked eye showed us" the general magnificence of the castle's emplacement, but it was the binoculars — specifically — that "showed us Adèle" (004-166).

Through the glasses, the party observed Adèle standing on a small terrace, her hands on the balustrade, with the castle's cascade leaping below her and an archway framing a shut door behind (004-167). She was standing very still, her head up, her eyes lifted to the very peak upon which the rescue party was concealed. This moment — the first confirmation that Adèle was alive and at Gath — is one of the most emotionally charged passages in the novel, and it was made possible entirely by the optical reach of the field-glasses.

## The Heliograph Signals

Immediately upon sighting Adèle, Mansel improvised a signalling system using the glasses in combination with his wristwatch. He tore off the watch, ripped open its case, and thrust it into Chandos's hand, instructing him to flash the silver case in the sun while Mansel watched through the glasses. "Flash it in the sun," he said, clapping the glasses to his eyes. "If she sees it, she'll understand" (004-169).

What followed was a tense and wordless exchange: Chandos flashed the case six times, covering it between flashes; Mansel watched through the glasses and relayed Adèle's responses; and at last, after what seemed an unbearably long time, Adèle raised her hand in acknowledgment and turned away (004-180). Mansel at last put down his glasses and wiped the sweat from his face (004-179). The entire episode — one of the most skilfully rendered scenes in the novel — depended absolutely on the field-glasses, which transformed a distant, silent figure on a terrace into a person with whom the rescue party could communicate.

## Throughout the Campaign

The field-glasses remained in constant use throughout the Castle of Gath operation. During the long hours of surveillance from the hillsides and roads around the fortress, the glasses were passed from hand to hand, each member of the party taking a turn at watching the comings and goings of Rose Noble's agents. They were as essential to the campaign's success as the Rolls-Royce or the pistols — an instrument not of violence but of knowledge, embodying Mansel's principle that the battle was won or lost in the gathering of intelligence long before any shot was fired.
