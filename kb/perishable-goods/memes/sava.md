# Sava

Sava is a village in [[carinthia|Carinthia]], Austria, located approximately ten miles from the main operational area (002-054). It serves as a geographical reference point in the planning and execution of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] operation in *Perishable Goods*, appearing primarily in Chapter II.

## Location & Setting

Sava is described as "a village ten miles away" from the protagonists' position (002-054). It is connected by a bridge on the road from St. Martin: "that bridge serves a road to Sava---the only road thereabouts" (002-121). The route through Sava offers an eastern approach to the operational area; [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s tactical plan includes driving "from St. Martin to Sava and come from the East" (002-125).

## Role in the Plot

Sava functions primarily as a directional marker and potential route in the planning of movements around [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]. During the early phase of the operation, a character is observed riding "in the direction of Sava" (002-054), raising suspicion because the movement did not align with expected patterns---"but not in the direction of Sava" (002-055) is noted as significant. One character had previously "ridden most of the way to Sava" during reconnaissance (002-108). The village reappears in a late-story recollection: "When they took me that day by Sava, I knew you'd come" (009-040), indicating Sava was a waypoint in a significant character movement.

## The Sava Bridge: A Tactical Chokepoint

The geography of the Sava road is dominated by a single bridge, which [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] identifies as a critical tactical feature. Standing with [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and surveying the operational area, Mansel points out the bridge and remarks that it "serves a road to Sava---the only road thereabouts" (002-121). The phrase "the only road thereabouts" is dense with strategic implication: in a landscape of mountain valleys where roads are few, control of a single bridge can determine the entire pattern of movement across a region. Any party approaching from the east must cross this bridge; any observer at the bridge can see traffic bound for Sava.

Mansel's tactical genius in the novel lies in his ability to read terrain not as scenery but as a chessboard. The bridge at Sava is not merely a crossing-point but a node in a network of observation, movement, and ambush. His plan to "drive from St. Martin to Sava and come from the East" (002-125) is designed to circumvent enemy observation posts positioned near the more obvious western approach. The eastern route, longer and less expected, exploits the single-road geography that Mansel has identified. That the bridge is described as situated south of [[poganec|Poganec]] (002-125) places it precisely in the reader's mental map of the operational theatre.

## Reconnaissance and Misdirection

Sava figures prominently in the novel's account of ground-level reconnaissance. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], setting an example of thoroughness for his party, "had already ridden most of the way to Sava and had driven with Major Pleydell to the spot where the glove had been found" (002-108). This dual inspection -- first on horseback for close observation, then by car with a senior colleague -- illustrates the methodical approach Mansel brings to every phase of the operation. He "had also seen and talked with all the servants employed about the farm" (002-108), and his conclusion that they were "all to be honest, though painfully unobservant" is a characteristically dry assessment of the local population's usefulness as witnesses.

The incident of the glove adds a layer of mystery to the Sava axis. A glove is discovered "lying by the side of the road almost exactly eleven miles from here, but not in the direction of Sava" (002-055). The specificity of the measurement -- "almost exactly eleven miles" -- and the scrupulous correction -- "but not in the direction of Sava" -- signal the narrator's attention to the kind of discrepancy that would escape a casual observer. The detail betrays a mind trained, like [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s, to treat every deviation from expectation as potential evidence. That the glove was *not* on the Sava road is as significant as its distance from base; it suggests an attempt by the quarry to misdirect pursuit by creating a false trail.

## Sava in Memory: The Late-Story Recollection

The single most emotionally charged reference to Sava occurs not during the operational planning of Chapter II but in a flashback near the novel's end. In Chapter IX, a character recalls the moment of capture: "When they took me that day by Sava, I knew you'd come" (009-040). The sentence, spoken in recollection, transforms Sava from a place-name on a tactical map into a site of ordeal and faith. The speaker's confidence -- "I knew you'd come" -- is a testament to the bond of loyalty among [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s party, a bond that the geography of Sava has tested and confirmed.

This retrospective invocation also alters the reader's understanding of the earlier, more clinical references to Sava. The reconnaissance, the careful measurement of distances, the tactical discussions at the bridge -- all these, in retrospect, are seen as the preparations that made the rescue possible. Sava is not merely a village on a map but the site where the party's competence and comradeship were put to the ultimate test.

## Inquiries in the Direction of Sava

The search for [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]] in the novel's early chapters involves a systematic canvass of the countryside, and Sava defines one axis of that search. "Inquiries were made at all farms in the direction of Sava" (002-054) -- the phrase, instinct with method, suggests the sweep of a search operation that leaves no farmstead unvisited, no potential witness unquestioned. The "direction of Sava" functions here not merely as a compass bearing but as a sector of responsibility, a defined zone of the operational area for which the searchers must account. That the inquiries yield no result -- the rider they were tracing had gone elsewhere -- only underscores the difficulty of the search and the skill of those who were attempting to evade it.

Mansel reconstructs Adèle Pleydell's last known movements, tracing her path toward Sava:

> "Today is Thursday. At seven on Monday morning Adèle went out for a ride. She rode in the direction of Sava, a village ten miles away. Her mare came in on her own at a quarter past eight. Everyone turned out at once, but found nothing. The mare was clean and unhurt. Inquiries were made at all farms in the direction of Sava. They proved fruitless. Nobody saw Adèle on Monday morning. The day was hot, and she was wearing breeches and boots, a white silk shirt and gloves. She had no hat on."
>
> (002-054)
