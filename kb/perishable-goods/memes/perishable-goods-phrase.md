# "Perishable Goods"

The phrase **"perishable goods"** is the title of Dornford Yates's 1928 novel and, within the narrative, the chilling term used by [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] to describe the kidnapped [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]]. It is the novel's thematic keystone: a phrase that reduces a woman to a commodity, encodes the brutal logic of the criminal enterprise that has taken her, and — in the hands of [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] — becomes a weapon turned back upon its author.

## The First Utterance

The phrase is first spoken by Mansel himself, not by Rose Noble, in one of the most striking passages of Chapter I. The party has just received the telegram informing them of Adèle's disappearance in Carinthia, and Mansel has been sitting in silence, absorbing the news. Chandos watches him:

> "'Perishable goods,' he said quietly, speaking as though he were alone. 'Yes. I suppose you might call Adèle in Rose Noble's hands — perishable goods.'"
>
> (001-107)

The attribution is significant. Although Mansel is speaking the words, he is *quoting* or *anticipating* Rose Noble's vocabulary — imagining, with a strategist's empathy for his enemy, how the criminal mastermind would categorise a kidnapped woman. The phrase is Mansel's act of entering Rose Noble's mind, and it tells the reader everything about the antagonist before Noble has even appeared in the narrative. To Rose Noble, Adèle is not a person with a family and a life; she is inventory, and like all perishable inventory, her value depends on her condition at the time of delivery.

Mansel's quiet, almost meditative delivery of the phrase — "speaking as though he were alone" — conveys both his horror at the thought and his acceptance of its accuracy as a tactical assessment. He understands precisely what kind of man he is dealing with.

## The Logic of Commodification

The phrase "perishable goods" encodes a specific economic logic. In commerce, perishable goods are items whose value diminishes or vanishes with the passage of time or with damage to the product. Fresh produce, cut flowers, live animals — all are perishable, all must be moved quickly from source to market, and all lose their worth if they are bruised, spoiled, or delayed.

Applied to Adèle, the term implies several things simultaneously. First, that she has been taken not for ransom in the ordinary sense but as an item of exchange — something to be traded, delivered, or sold. Second, that her value to her captors is conditional on her remaining "undamaged" — a term that, in the context of a kidnapped woman in the 1920s, carries unmistakable and dreadful connotations. Third, that time is the enemy of both the kidnappers and the rescuers: the longer she remains in captivity, the greater the risk that she will be harmed, and the less valuable she becomes to those who hold her.

## Mansel's Counter-Use

In Chapter VI, during the confrontation in the Dining-room of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], Mansel turns the phrase back upon Rose Noble with devastating effect. Cornered by Noble, who holds the party at gunpoint with a pistol in either hand, Mansel responds to Noble's threats with characteristic calm:

> "'Are getting the rough,' said Mansel. 'And there again I advise you to watch your step. "Perishable goods" have a market: but "damaged goods" make a very different price.'"
>
> (006-179)

The rejoinder is brilliant on multiple levels. First, it demonstrates that Mansel has understood Noble's worldview from the beginning — he is speaking Noble's own language, the language of commodification and market value. Second, it contains an implicit threat: if Noble harms Adèle, he destroys his own leverage. Third, it reframes the power dynamic: Noble, who thought he held all the cards, is being reminded by his captive that his position is constrained by the very economic logic he himself introduced.

Mansel's use of the phrase also introduces the variant "damaged goods" — a term that extends the commercial metaphor to its logical conclusion. If "perishable goods" are items at risk of spoilage, "damaged goods" are items that have already spoiled and are therefore worthless (or worth far less). The implication is clear: harm Adèle, and Noble destroys his own bargaining position.

## The Title's Resonance

As the novel's title, "Perishable Goods" works on at least three levels. Most immediately, it refers to the specific plot circumstance: Adèle is the "perishable good" around which the entire conflict revolves. More broadly, it gestures toward the novel's thematic preoccupation with value — the value of human life, the value of honour, the value of love — and the different systems (commercial, chivalric, romantic) by which value is measured. Most abstractly, it suggests that all the novel's characters, heroes and villains alike, are in some sense "perishable goods" — mortal, vulnerable, subject to damage and decay — and that the measure of a life is what one does with that perishability.
