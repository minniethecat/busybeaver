# Chapter I: Scene Setting

The opening pages of *Perishable Goods* efficiently establish the novel's world. [[william-chandos|William Chandos]], the narrator, introduces himself, his friend [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]], and the magnetic figure of [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]. In a few deft strokes, Dornford Yates creates the framework for the adventure to follow.

> It was in October, 1926, that George Hanbury and I first set up house in Wiltshire; and, since for the next six months we hunted four days a week, yet would commit to no one the pleasant task of setting our home in order, I do not think we slept out of Maintenance—for from time immemorial that has been the name of the place—more than seven times.
>
> (001-001)

## The World Established

The novel opens in October 1926, with [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] setting up house in [[wiltshire|Wiltshire]] (001-001). The domestic tranquility — hunting four days a week at [[maintenance|Maintenance]] — establishes a baseline of gentlemanly ease that the coming crisis will shatter.

The introduction of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is handled with characteristic understatement. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] notes that neither he nor [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] "would have dreamed of visiting London without calling on Jonathan Mansel" (001-003) — a detail that conveys [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s gravitational pull without hyperbole.

## The Burglary Discovery

The narrative accelerates when [[william-chandos|Chandos]] arrives at [[cleveland-row|Cleveland Row]] to find the wall-safe cut open (001-016). The discovery triggers the novel's central conflict: [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] has struck, and [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] must respond. The scene-setting transitions seamlessly into the adventure.
