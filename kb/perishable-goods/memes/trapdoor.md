# Trapdoor

The **trapdoor** in the King's Closet at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] is a concealed floor entrance discovered by [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] during reconnaissance. It provides the infiltration party with an alternative route into the castle, bypassing the guarded approaches, and becomes central to the operational planning of the [[castle-of-gath-operation|Castle of Gath operation]].

## Discovery

Mansel locates the trapdoor during a solo reconnaissance of the castle's lower passages. Crawling through the water channel beneath the archway, he finds a steep spiral staircase that terminates abruptly:

> "In the side of the archway I found a flight of steps — a very steep spiral staircase, that comes to a sudden end. I'm certain it serves a trapdoor. If the bookseller's guide is sound, that trapdoor should be in the King's Closet." (005-201)

The [[bookseller-of-lass|bookseller of Lass]] has supplied intelligence confirming the layout, and Mansel's instinct proves correct: the staircase leads to precisely the room the party most needs to access.

## Initial Access and Securing

On the first night of the operation, the trapdoor is found to be fastened from above. Mansel determines that the party must first enter the [[the-closet|King's Closet]] by another route — the terrace and windows — in order to unbar the trapdoor from inside:

> "We can't go that way tonight, because the trapdoor is fast. At least, I imagine it is, because I can't move the slab I found at the top of the stairs: but, if we can enter the Closet by some other way tonight, we can unbar the trapdoor, and then, when we come by the terrace, we shall have our way in." (005-202)

[[william-chandos|Chandos]] later uses this route to enter the castle, climbing the spiral staircase from the archway after wading through the water channel:

> "I had a vile wet passage, but I managed to save both pistols and Hanbury's torch. The archway was dank and draughty, so I lost no time in finding the little staircase that served the trapdoor and, when I had found it, in climbing as high as I could." (006-021)

## Operational Function

Once unbarred, the trapdoor becomes the party's primary entry and exit point for the King's Closet. It allows them to come and go silently, avoiding the exposed terrace route. After sending dispatches to [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]], the team secures the position:

> "These dispatches I sent alone, and, when they were gone, we finally closed the trapdoor and getting into our blanket, lay down on the carpet to sleep." (006-055)

At dusk the following day, the party reopens the closet windows and secures the trapdoor for the night's operations:

> "When it was dark, we opened the Closet windows and shut the trapdoor. Then Mansel set me at a window, with Adèle by my side, and himself to watch the Bedchamber until the moment should come." (006-294)

## The Second Trapdoor

Later in the operation, a second, much larger trapdoor is discovered — a section of flooring sawn through with a fine saw, surrounding a table:

> "I could scarcely believe that here was another trapdoor, for, for one thing only, the cleft was surrounding the table with a fair two inches to spare, and a trapdoor some eight feet by five seemed out of reason: yet, for some purpose or other, the floor had been sawn asunder." (007-018)

This second trapdoor proves to be the mechanism concealing [[the-lift|the lift]] — [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s private elevator connecting the King's Closet to the cellars below.

## Final Departure

After the successful resolution of the operation, the party departs through the trapdoor for the last time, leaving the windows open to the night air:

> "The windows of the Closet were still open, as was the trapdoor, and the room was full of sweet air and the murmur of the water below. Mansel sat down on the floor, and swung his legs into the trap." (007-187)

## Significance

The trapdoor exemplifies the layered, almost theatrical architecture of the Castle of Gath — a medieval fortress retrofitted with Renaissance chambers, secret staircases, and concealed passages. It also illustrates Mansel's methodical approach to infiltration: rather than forcing entry, he maps the castle's hidden geometry and turns it to his advantage.
