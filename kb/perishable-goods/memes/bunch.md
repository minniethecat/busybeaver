# Bunch

Bunch is one of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s hired men, serving as driver, guard,
and enforcer at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]. Paired with
[[punter|Punter]] throughout the novel's second half, Bunch forms one half of a
double-act that provides both menace and—in the final chapters—a strain of
Cockney fatalism that borders on the comic. A capable driver and a practical
man, Bunch navigates the collapse of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s criminal enterprise with a
mixture of shrewdness and self-preservation.

## Role in the Story

Bunch serves [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] in three distinct capacities:

- **The Driver (Ch. IV)**: Bunch is first seen at the wheel of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s car,
  navigating mountain roads with skill: "Bunch was a good driver and swooped
  down the winding road as though there were no precipitous bends or yawning
  gullies awaiting [[rolls-royce|the car]] that took a corner ill" (004-083). He brings the
  car expertly to rest at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] gateway (004-088).
- **The Pursuer (Ch. VI–VII)**: When [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]] is spirited away, Bunch
  joins the high-speed chase, racing alongside [[punter|Punter]] and [[casemate|Casemate]] to intercept
  [[rolls-royce|Mansel's Rolls-Royce]] at the hairpin bend. His driving is resourceful—"By this
  time Bunch had stolen a bit of a start" (007-055)—but ultimately outmatched
  by [[carson|Carson]] and [[bell|Bell]]'s pursuit.
- **The Rooftop Guard (Ch. VIII)**: Bunch and [[punter|Punter]] are posted as lookouts on
  the castle roof, guarding the captured [[william-chandos|Chandos]]. It is here that Bunch's
  character emerges most vividly—a man who passes the time by eating, drinking,
  and delivering sententious observations on the deteriorating situation.

Bunch's voice is distinctive: a Cockney-inflected commentary that cuts through
the tension with blunt, often sardonic assessments. When [[punter|Punter]] panics about the
gate being shut, Bunch is philosophical:

> "All right, all right," said Bunch soothingly. "But you ain't broke your
> back." (008-082)

When [[rose-noble-criminal-network|the gang]] debates [[jute|Jute]]'s fate, Bunch's diagnosis is characteristically
direct:

> "That's Jute," said Bunch. "'E would 'ave it Jute 'ad hopped the twig. But
> Jute ain't dead." (008-084)

## Quotations

Bunch's dialogue is rendered in a broad Cockney dialect that distinguishes him from
the novel's gentleman-protagonists. His speech mixes practicality, fatalism, and
unexpected flashes of sentiment:

On the gate being shut:

> "Come on," said Bunch. "Best shut that ——— gate." (008-092)

On [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s reputation:

> "'E ain't dead yet," said Bunch. (008-086)

On the transformed car that delivers the coup de grâce:

> "Complete with car," said Bunch, licking his lips. (008-197)

On the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] itself:

> "Suicide 'All," said Bunch. "But 'e won't 'ave a dart by moonlight." (008-203)

When violence is proposed against the prisoner, Bunch demurs with unexpected restraint:

> "I don' wan' to kill 'im," said Bunch. "If you—" (008-209)

His reaction to [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s promise of payment reveals the transactional nature of
his loyalty—"the intimation that no claim by [[jute|Jute]] would be paid was greeted by [[punter|Punter]]
and Bunch with the highest glee" (008-253)—and his final moments on the rooftop show
a man "all of a twitter" (008-257), reduced from enforcer to nervous spectator by
the implacable competence of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s party.

## Significance

Bunch functions as the novel's most vivid representative of the "ordinary" criminal:
not a mastermind like [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] or a cold professional like [[casemate|Casemate]], but a hired
hand who does his job, enjoys his food, and hopes to survive the night. His Cockney
commentary provides a counterpoint to the high-stakes drama unfolding around him—a
reminder that [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s empire rests on men for whom the operation is, ultimately,
just a job. His partnership with [[punter|Punter]] creates one of the novel's most effective
double acts, and his pragmatic, unheroic response to crisis—eat, drink, and wait to
see what happens—offers a darkly comic mirror to the derring-do of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s party.
