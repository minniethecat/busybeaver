# Lass

Lass is an old, labyrinthine town in the [[carinthia|Carinthia]]n mountains, where the group stays at the Three Kings Hotel while planning the rescue of [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]] from the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]].

## Description

The narrator describes Lass as possessing "the feature of any city I know, but Lass was so old and curious and had" a distinctive character (003-174). The town is noted for its maze-like layout: "Lass was no better than a maze" (004-002). It lies "in a trough of the mountains" (004-027), emphasizing its sheltered alpine setting.

## Role in the Story

Lass serves as the group's operational base for the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] mission. [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] suggests that "we should reach Lass not later than six o'clock" (003-136), indicating it is a planned destination. Before arriving, he insists on settling strategic details: "before we get to Lass we must settle one or two things" (003-137).

> It was then arranged that each should watch for one hour, while the others slept. I was to take the first watch, and Mansel the last; "although," said he, "I think that may be a short one, for, before we get to Lass we must settle one or two things. Still, I shall begin to get pensive at five o'clock, and so, if you two don't mind, I'll sleep till then."
>
> (003-137)

Upon arrival, the group checks into the Three Kings Hotel. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s familiarity with the town is noted --- he moves "as naturally as though he travelled to Lass every day of his life" (003-142). The hotel becomes both a refuge and a command post, as [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] warns that "when we get to Lass, there will be more people about" (003-140), suggesting the need for discretion.

## The Three Kings Hotel: Command Post

The Three Kings Hotel at Lass functions as far more than a lodging. It is the group's headquarters, the place where intelligence is gathered, plans are formulated, and operations are launched. The hotel's public rooms provide cover for the party's presence---they can pass as tourists while secretly coordinating a rescue mission. From the Three Kings, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] dispatches patrols, receives reports, and maintains the fiction of innocent travelers. The hotel's significance is underscored by its use as a reference point: when [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] encounters an ally during the operation, he instructs, "send him to The Three Kings at Lass" (005-032), establishing the hotel as the recognized rendezvous for the rescue party.

## A Town Big with Rumour

As the conflict intensifies, Lass transforms from a quiet base into a nexus of dangerous gossip. The narrator records that after [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s wounding, "Lass was big with rumour, and, though much of the gossip was wild, we were astounded to find how close some approached the truth" (009-110). The town's compact geography and close-knit community become liabilities: every stranger's movement is noticed, every unusual circumstance becomes the subject of speculation. The group is forced to take extreme precautions, drawing supplies "not from Lass, but from towns in other directions from twenty to fifty miles off" and ensuring that "since the day of Mansel's wounding, we had not been seen in Lass" (009-108).

## The Bookseller of Lass

One of the town's most significant inhabitants is the bookseller of Lass, a "respectable citizen" who is "well acquainted with Gath and with its ownership" (008-050). His apartment in Lass---used by the group and then precipitately abandoned---becomes a source of danger when he unexpectedly visits the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]. His appearance at the castle threatens to expose the entire operation, as his eyes "had only to light upon George Hanbury, Bell or myself, when his suspicions would be instantly quickened by that most lively curiosity which our use of his apartment at Lass and our strange and precipitate departure must have aroused" (008-050). The bookseller represents the tight social fabric of Lass---a town where everyone knows everyone else's business and where anonymity is impossible.

## The Maze That Hindered Pursuit

The town's labyrinthine character proves to be a double-edged sword. While the maze-like streets initially provide cover for the group's movements, they also frustrate their operations. When pursuing a suspect through Lass, the narrator laments that "Lass was no better than a maze" (004-002), the confusing layout allowing their quarry to slip away. The town's geography becomes an active participant in the plot: its narrow, winding streets and hidden approaches both shelter and betray the protagonists by turns.

## Medical Resources and Aftermath

In the novel's later stages, Lass provides an unexpected resource. When [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is gravely wounded, [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] locates Dr. Buchinger, "an Innsbruck surgeon, who happened to be at Lass" (009-062). The narrator admits he "hadn't much hope of Lass" for finding competent medical aid, paraphrasing Scripture: "Can there any good thing come out of Nazareth?" (009-068). Yet the town unexpectedly yields the surgeon whose skills prove critical to Mansel's survival. After the crisis, [[george-hanbury|George]] drives to Lass to retrieve the group's belongings from the Three Kings, "giving out that Mansel had crushed his foot and lay in a peasant's cabin" (009-095)---a cover story designed to quell the rumour mill that the group's presence had set in motion.

## Significance

Lass represents the threshold between the civilized world and the lawless territory of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]. It is the last safe haven before the group embarks on their dangerous rescue mission into the [[carinthia|Carinthia]]n wilds.
