# Final Confrontation

The Final Confrontation is the climactic event of Dornford Yates's *Perishable Goods*, occurring in Chapter IX, "Full Measure." In the aftermath of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s death, the forgotten [[casemate|Casemate]] shoots [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]], who dies over the following hours in the great bedchamber of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], attended by [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]], [[william-chandos|William Chandos]], and his dog [[tester|Tester]]. The title "Full Measure" refers to the completeness of the sacrifice: "in this supreme ordeal they gave full measure" (009-195).

## Context / Lead-up

[[castle-of-gath|The Castle of Gath]] Operation has reached its terminal phase. [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] has been killed—shot, as [[carson|Carson]] and [[bell|Bell]] discover: "[[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] lay still unburied, for [[carson|Carson]] and [[bell|Bell]] had left him and rushed to the castle directly they heard the shot." Yet in the chaos of victory, one threat has been forgotten. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] opens the chapter with a confession of guilt: "I shall never forgive myself for forgetting that [[casemate|Casemate]] was still at large" (009-001). He had thought of [[casemate|Casemate]] twice during the rescue operation, but each time "I had let some other matter thrust [[casemate|Casemate]] out of my mind" (009-001). He brands it "a monstrous negligence" and wonders if "[[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s familiar possessed me to let [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] down" (009-001).

The party carries the wounded [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] into the castle's great bedchamber and lays him on "the King's bed" (009-001). [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s first words confirm the gravity: "He's got me all right... so let me be" (009-001).

## Sequence of Events

1. **[[casemate|Casemate]]'s shot**: Forgotten in the aftermath of [[rose-noble|Noble]]'s death, [[casemate|Casemate]] fires the shot that mortally wounds [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] (009-001).
2. **The King's bed**: [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is carried to the great bedchamber of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] and laid upon a historic bed (009-001).
3. **[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s first words**: "He's got me all right," he whispers. "So let me be" (009-001). [[william-chandos|Chandos]] weeps but [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] only smiles: "Rot... Luck of the battle—that's all" (009-001).
4. **The vigil begins**: [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]], "steady-eyed as ever, but very pale," sits on the bed with [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s hand in hers (009-001). [[tester|Tester]] lies on [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s other side, "close up against his lord" (009-001).
5. **[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s last orders**: With remarkable composure, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] instructs [[william-chandos|Chandos]] to deal with [[punter-chase|Punter and Bunch]]—search them for money, buy tickets to London, "drop them outside the town" (009-001). He then closes his eyes, "well content" (009-001).
6. **The surgeon**: [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] brings a local surgeon, [[buchinger|Dr. Buchinger]], under an extraordinary arrangement—a thousand pounds for the operation, another four thousand "provided we'd reason to think that he'd held his tongue" (009-001). The deal is brokered with the help of a bookseller, [[buchinger|Buchinger]], who vouches for [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]]: "This gentleman is saving a lady's name."
7. **The death-watch**: [[buchinger|Buchinger]] operates but warns that [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] "might die any minute during the next three days" (009-001). [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is "standing still—on the very edge of death" (009-001).
8. **[[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] and [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]**: In the long hours of the vigil, [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] and [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] speak their love openly. "If we were in London," [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] murmurs, "they might have a shot: but I'm very comfortable, and it's a great relief to know that we're out of the wood" (009-001).
9. **"I'm terribly afraid"**: [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] breaks down: "When they took me that day by [[sava|Sava]], I knew you'd come. I wasn't afraid, because I knew you'd find me and pull me out... But now... you're going, my darling... and I must fend for myself. And I'm lost and beggared and beaten, and—oh, Jonah, I'm terribly afraid" (009-001).
10. **[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s reply**: "A fiction, my beauty, a fiction. You mustn't bow down to an image that you set up... [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] was a great lady, before ever she heard my name. So lift up your head, in the old familiar way. Look Fate in the eyes, my darling, and he'll always give you the wall" (009-001).
11. **The last commission**: [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] tells [[tester|Tester]] he must look after [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]—"for you see, old fellow... I've got to go away. And it's not like the other times, for this time... I shan't come back" (009-001). The dog gives "a little whimper that wrung my heart" (009-001).
12. **Death of [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]**: "Destiny will be served. For more than four hundred years that room had been swept and garnished, had stood ever ready and waiting for 'The Last of the Knights.' [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]], Gentleman, had come into his own" (009-001).

## Participants

- **[[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]** — the dying protagonist; faces death with the same composure he brought to every battle
- **[[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]]** — [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s love; keeps the death-watch with steady-eyed devotion and heartbroken terror
- **[[william-chandos|William Chandos]]** — narrator; consumed by guilt for forgetting [[casemate|Casemate]], serves as witness to Mansel's death
- **[[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]]** — secures the surgeon through a desperate and ingenious arrangement
- **[[casemate|Casemate]]** — [[rose-noble|Noble]]'s lieutenant, forgotten in the chaos; fires the fatal shot
- **[[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]** — already dead before the chapter opens, his body "lay still unburied" (009-001)

## Outcome / Consequences

The Final Confrontation is the novel's terminal event. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] dies, but his sacrifice achieves everything he set out to do: [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] is free, [[rose-noble|Noble]]'s network is destroyed, and his companions are safe. The chapter closes on the phrase "full measure"—the complete, ungrudging gift of a life for those he loved.

Mansel's death transforms the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] from a site of criminal operation into a shrine of sacrifice. [[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s narration frames it in chivalric terms: "The Last of the Knights" has fallen in a chamber that "for more than four hundred years" had been waiting for him. The medieval imagery is deliberate—[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is not merely a modern gentleman but the inheritor of an older code of honor, and his death is the fulfillment of that inheritance.

For the survivors, the cost is permanent. [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] will carry [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s last words and the memory of his touch; [[william-chandos|Chandos]] will carry the guilt of having forgotten [[casemate|Casemate]]; [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] will carry the knowledge that he did everything humanly possible to save his friend. The "passionate adventure" (009-194) at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] is over, but its consequences will shape every life it touched.
