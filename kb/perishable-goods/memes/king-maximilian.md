# King Maximilian

**King Maximilian** — Maximilian I (1459–1519), Holy Roman Emperor — is a historical figure whose presence permeates the latter chapters of *Perishable Goods*. Though he never appears as a character, his name, his legend, and his most famous epithet provide the symbolic framework through which Dornford Yates elevates [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s death from a mere plot outcome to an event of chivalric resonance.

## The Historical Maximilian

Maximilian I was one of the most significant figures of late-medieval Europe. Archduke of Austria, King of the Romans, and Holy Roman Emperor, he was renowned as a patron of the arts, a military reformer, and — most relevantly for Yates's purposes — a figure of knightly virtue in an age when chivalry was fading into legend. His nickname, "the Last of the Knights" (*der letzte Ritter*), was earned through his personal participation in tournaments, his cultivation of chivalric ideals, and his self-fashioning as the embodiment of a vanishing code of honour.

Yates's narrator, [[william-chandos|William Chandos]], refers to Maximilian and "the exquisite surname which he had won" (009-019) — the epithet "the Last of the Knights" — as a way of understanding what he is witnessing in the great bedchamber of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]].

## Maximilian's Presence at the Castle of Gath

The Castle of Gath itself is presented as a fortress with deep historical associations. Its secret passages — the King's Closet, the King's Bedchamber, the private staircases — are explicitly linked to Maximilian's era, and the great bed in which Mansel dies is understood to be a king's bed. The Dining-room where Rose Noble ambushes the rescue party is situated around "his private board" — the table at which Maximilian himself once dined (006-166).

Chandos's imagination repeatedly returns to Maximilian at moments of high tension. When the party is trapped by Rose Noble in the Dining-room, Chandos's wayward mind — even in the midst of mortal danger — conjures the image of King Maximilian:

> Desperately I tried to marshal my wits, for if ever a clear head was needed to save the game, it was needed now; but my brain was ever wayward, and I remember thinking how strange a picture we made and how much astonished King Maximilian would have been, could he have viewed such a company gracing his private board.
>
> (006-166)

The juxtaposition of Maximilian's courtly world with the brutal modernity of Rose Noble's gangsterism is one of the novel's most effective tonal contrasts.

## "The Last of the Knights"

The most significant invocation of Maximilian occurs in Chapter IX, as Mansel lies dying. Chandos sits in the great bedchamber, taking in the black oak, the gold and crimson, the tapestries, the four carved men-at-arms about the bed. "For once Fate had not bungled," he reflects, "but had laid a king upon a king's bed to die" (009-018). Then the thought crystallises: he thinks of Maximilian and of "the exquisite surname which he had won," and at last sees "the writing which had been all the time upon the wall" (009-019).

The passage that follows makes the analogy explicit: "For more than four hundred years that room had been swept and garnished, had stood ever ready and waiting for 'The Last of the Knights.' Jonathan Mansel, Gentleman, had come into his own" (009-020). The parallel is unmistakable: just as Maximilian was the last embodiment of medieval chivalry, so Mansel — gentleman, strategist, soldier — is the last embodiment of a code of honour that the twentieth century's mechanised cruelty has rendered obsolete.

## The Name in Every Mouth

In the novel's closing paragraphs, Chandos returns to Maximilian one final time. He reflects that the stone slab sealing the Closet entrance will one day be found, and the bloodstain on the quilt discovered, and "the name of Maximilian will be in every mouth" (009-198). Old annals will be reread, old narratives revised, and legends will be "brought to bed of fine new tales of wrath and mischief and murder saved or done." Yet Chandos doubts that these tales, for all their bravery, will compare with the truth: the true story of what happened in that room — the story that only he, Adèle, and Mansel shared — is beyond the reach of history.

The invocation of Maximilian thus serves a double purpose. On the surface, it provides historical texture and a sense of deep time, grounding the novel's climax in a real historical figure and a real location. On a deeper level, it enables Yates to make a claim for Mansel's significance that would otherwise seem grandiose: that this obscure English gentleman, dying in an Austrian fortress while conducting a private war against a criminal gang, was in fact the heir to a chivalric tradition stretching back four centuries.
