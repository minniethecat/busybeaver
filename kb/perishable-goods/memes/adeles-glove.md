# Adèle's Glove

**Adèle's glove** — a single riding glove belonging to [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]] — is the first physical piece of evidence discovered after her abduction and arguably the most important clue in the entire investigation that drives the plot of *Perishable Goods*. Found by the servant [[fitch|Fitch]] on the morning after the kidnapping, the glove enabled [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] to reconstruct the route of Adèle's captors and to begin the systematic tracking that would ultimately lead the rescue party to the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]].

## The Day of the Abduction

On Monday morning, Adèle went out for an early ride in the direction of [[sava|Sava]], a village some ten miles from the Pleydell farm. The day was hot, and she was dressed for riding: breeches and boots, a white silk shirt, and gloves (002-054). Her mare returned to the farm on its own at a quarter past eight, clean and unhurt. Despite an immediate search, nothing was found. Nobody had seen Adèle that morning (002-054).

The gloves are mentioned in the description of her attire as an unremarkable detail — simply part of a lady's riding kit. At this point in the narrative, no one yet knows that one of those gloves will become the thread from which the entire rescue operation will be unwound.

## The Discovery

On Tuesday morning, Fitch, a servant at the farm, found one of Adèle's gloves lying by the side of the road (002-055). The location was significant in two respects. First, the glove was found almost exactly eleven miles from the farm — roughly the distance Adèle would have ridden to Sava. Second, and critically, it was found *not* in the direction of Sava. Adèle had been taken in a different direction entirely, and the glove marked the point of divergence.

The glove's survival on the road for a full day — from Monday morning to Tuesday morning — suggests the remoteness of the Carinthian roads and the infrequency of traffic upon them. Had the road been busier, the glove might have been picked up, trampled, or blown away. As it was, it lay where it had fallen, waiting for Fitch's sharp eyes.

## Mansel's Reconstruction

When Mansel arrived at the Pleydell farm and learned of the glove, he immediately incorporated it into his investigation. He rode most of the distance to Sava and drove with [[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] to the spot where the glove had been found, interviewing all the farm servants along the way (002-108). But it was only after further reflection that he grasped the glove's full significance.

Driving the party at speed to the spot where the glove had lain, Mansel set everyone to searching for tyre marks. His reasoning was precise and devastating: "I am certain," he said, "that she changed from one car to another and, while she did so, contrived to drop her glove, for they would have watched her too closely to let her throw it out as she went" (002-154). The glove was not an accident but an act of deliberate resistance — Adèle, closely guarded, had found the one moment of transition between vehicles when she could let fall a clue, trusting that someone would find it and understand.

The tyre marks at the site confirmed Mansel's theory. From that point, the party began driving slowly westward, tracking the kidnappers' route by following roads and questioning anyone who might have seen the vehicle pass. By evening, they had reached a great hog's-back ridge some fifty miles from the spot where the glove had lain (002-158) — and from that ridge, using [[field-glasses|field-glasses]], they would eventually identify the Castle of Gath as the kidnappers' destination.

## The Glove as Symbol

Like [[dressing-case|Adèle's dressing-case]], the glove is a small, personal object that carries enormous narrative weight. It is simultaneously a piece of evidence, a message from a captive to her rescuers, and a token of Adèle's presence of mind under extreme duress. That she had the wit to drop the glove, and that Mansel had the wit to understand what she had done, establishes the unspoken bond between them — a bond that would deepen into love by the novel's end.

The glove also exemplifies the forensic method that distinguishes Mansel from the conventional detective-heroes of the period. He does not interrogate witnesses or search for fingerprints; he reads the physical landscape, reconstructs movements from the barest traces, and trusts his understanding of human behaviour under pressure. The glove, a mute object on a dusty road, speaks to him as clearly as any confession.


> With that, he drove very fast to where Adèle's glove had been found, and set us all to seeking some mark of a tire; "for here," he said, "I am certain that she changed from one car to another and, while she did so, contrived to drop her glove, for they would have watched her too closely to let her throw it out as she went."

> (002-154)
