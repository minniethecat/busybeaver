# Mansel's War Record

[[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s service in the Great War is the invisible foundation of his character in *Perishable Goods*. His tactical brilliance, his leadership style, his network of loyal comrades, and his physical limp all trace back to his wartime experience — a past that the novel references without fully narrating.

## Evidence in the Text

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] is "lame of a wound he had had in the War" (002-089), a physical reminder of his service that never impedes his effectiveness. His military experience informs every aspect of the rescue operation — from the surveillance discipline on [[the-spur|the spur]] to the tactical planning of the [[castle-of-gath-operation|Castle of Gath assault]].

> Mansel cried to Hanbury and me to bring some tools and a torch and, in spite of his limp—for he was lame of a wound he had had in the War—ran well ahead of his cousin down the drive.
>
> (002-089)

The war also forged the bonds between [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], [[william-chandos|William Chandos]], and [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]]: "we three had made our fortune together and together had proved our friendship" (001-003). The fortune they made — nine hundred thousand pounds — was earned during the war (001-029), and it is this shared history that makes their partnership unbreakable.

## Rose Noble's Perspective

[[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] recognizes [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] from before the war (002-147), and his recognition carries fear. [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] lost nine hundred thousand pounds because of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] (001-029), and the war record explains why: [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] was not merely a soldier but an effective operator who disrupted [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s criminal enterprises.
