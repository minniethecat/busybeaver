# Fitch

**Fitch** is a servant employed at the Pleydell family's farm in Carinthia, Austria. Though he appears only briefly in the narrative and is never described in person, his discovery of a single dropped glove on the road provides the first physical clue in the search for the kidnapped [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]]. His role is a small but significant example of how [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] wove every available thread — including the observations of the estate's humblest members — into his investigative fabric.

## The Discovery

On Tuesday morning, the day after Adèle's disappearance during her early-morning ride toward [[sava|Sava]], Fitch found one of her gloves lying by the side of the road (002-055). The glove was found almost exactly eleven miles from the Pleydell farm — but notably *not* in the direction of Sava, the village toward which Adèle had been riding when last seen. This discrepancy in direction was the first indication that Adèle had been taken not by chance but by design, her likely route toward Sava having been anticipated by her abductors and a trap laid elsewhere.

The glove's location was reported to [[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]], who relayed the information to Mansel when the rescue party arrived from England. The day of the discovery — a full day after the abduction — underscores the isolation of the Carinthian countryside and the slow pace at which news and search efforts could proceed in a remote farming community without modern communications.

> Early on Tuesday morning Fitch found one of her gloves. It was lying by the side of the road almost exactly eleven miles from here, but not in the direction of Sava.
>
> (002-055)

## Mansel's Investigation

Upon hearing of the glove, Mansel immediately incorporated the find into his investigative strategy. He rode most of the way to Sava and drove with Major Pleydell to the spot where the glove had been found. He also interviewed all the servants employed about the farm, including Fitch (002-108). Mansel's verdict on the farm staff was characteristic: he believed them all to be honest, "though painfully unobservant" (002-108).

The importance of Fitch's find became clear when Mansel later drove the party at speed to the spot where the glove had lain and set everyone to searching for tyre marks (002-154). Mansel was by then certain that Adèle had been forced to change from one car to another at that location and had contrived to drop her glove as she was moved — for her captors "would have watched her too closely to let her throw it out as she went" (002-154). The glove thus became not merely a confirmation of the direction of her abduction but the anchor point from which Mansel began tracking the kidnappers' vehicles westward toward the distant hog's-back ridge, some fifty miles away (002-158).

## Significance

Fitch's discovery is a textbook example of how Yates constructed his plots: a seemingly trivial piece of information, reported by an unnamed and otherwise invisible character, becomes the linchpin of a sophisticated manhunt. Fitch himself remains entirely offstage — the reader never meets him, never hears his voice, never learns his first name or his precise role at the farm. His contribution to the story is the single sentence in which his discovery is reported, and yet without that discovery Mansel would have had no starting point from which to trace Adèle's captors.

In the broader economy of the novel, Fitch represents the network of ordinary, honest local people — servants, farmhands, villagers — whose unremarkable observations, when gathered and interpreted by a mind like Mansel's, could be transformed into actionable intelligence. His inclusion also reinforces the texture of Yates's fictional Carinthia: a countryside populated not only by criminals and aristocrats but by a working rural community whose members go about their duties and, occasionally, find things that change the course of events.
