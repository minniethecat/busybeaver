# Maintenance

Maintenance is the country estate in [[wiltshire|Wiltshire]] shared by [[william-chandos|William Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] in Dornford Yates's *Perishable
Goods*. It serves as the party's rural base of operations---a counterpart to
[[cleveland-row|Cleveland Row]] in London---and the place from which the [[carinthia|Carinthia]]
expedition is launched.

## Location & Setting

Situated in [[wiltshire|Wiltshire]], Maintenance is a gentleman's country property with hunting
grounds and the quiet privacy essential to [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s planning. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] introduces it
in the novel's opening sentence:

> "I do not think we slept out of Maintenance---for from time immemorial that was the
> name of the house---from the moment we went there, in April, until the twelfth day of
> August, when we left it for Carinthia." (001-001)

The house is substantial enough to accommodate guests and staff, yet remote enough
that the trio's preparations for their European adventure go unobserved.

## Key Events

- **Preparation for [[carinthia|Carinthia]]**: "There was much to be done at Maintenance" before the
  expedition (001-057) --- the house serves as the staging ground for the party's
  departure.
- **Return and Reflection**: [[william-chandos|Chandos]] recalls winter evenings at Maintenance "looking
  into the fire of logs" (009-199), framing the adventure between the departure from
  and return to the English countryside.

## Narrative Significance

Maintenance functions as the novel's symbolic home---the point of departure and return
that brackets the adventure. Its name, suggesting upkeep and continuity, contrasts with
the disruption and violence the protagonists encounter in [[carinthia|Carinthia]] and at
the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]. The novel opens at Maintenance and closes with
[[william-chandos|Chandos]] seated by its hearth, giving the narrative a circular structure that emphasizes
the restoration of order.

## The Founding of the Household

The novel's very first sentence establishes Maintenance as the product of a deliberate act of settlement. In October 1926, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] "first set up house in Wiltshire" (001-001), marking the inception of a shared domestic life that predates the novel's action by nearly two years. Crucially, the two men "would commit to no one the pleasant task of setting our home in order" (001-001), preferring to arrange the house themselves -- a detail that reveals their investment in the property as something more than a mere hunting-box. The house is theirs by choice and by labour, not by inheritance, and this personal stake in the establishment gives Maintenance a warmth that distinguishes it from the storied but formal atmosphere of [[white-ladies|White Ladies]].

The name itself is explained with an aside -- "for from time immemorial that has been the name of the place" (001-001) -- that suggests a lineage preceding [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]]'s tenure. Yet the name "Maintenance" acquires a personal resonance through the novel. To maintain is to preserve, to keep in repair, to continue -- and the house becomes the engine of continuity in the protagonists' lives. Between October and April, the two men "hunted four days a week" (001-001), embedding themselves in the rhythms of the Wiltshire countryside, their new home already woven into the fabric of English rural life.

## A Base Between Two Worlds

Maintenance occupies a middle ground in the novel's geography of masculine friendship. It lies approximately fifty miles from [[white-ladies|White Ladies]], the Pleydell family estate in the New Forest where [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] resides as a permanent guest. On returning from a visit to [[white-ladies|White Ladies]], [[william-chandos|Chandos]] records that "the next day we left for Wiltshire promising soon to return. Yet we did not, though our homes were but fifty miles apart" (001-057). The fifty-mile gap is not great, but the demands of the estate are greater: "with the coming of summer there was much to be done at Maintenance, and, though the hunting was over, we had our hands full" (001-057).

This detail is significant. Maintenance is not a holiday cottage; it requires work. The estate's needs -- its "pleasant task of setting our home in order" -- tie [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] to the land even when the hunting season ends. The rhythm of the year at Maintenance alternates between sport (the four-day hunting weeks of winter) and stewardship (the summer's unspecified but absorbing duties), and both rhythms reinforce the house's character as a place of productive, purposeful life rather than mere leisure.

## The Hearth as Symbolic Anchor

The novel's closing image -- [[william-chandos|Chandos]] "looking into the fire of logs" at Maintenance (009-199) -- is carefully prepared by the opening. The fire of logs is the English hearth at its most traditional, and the phrase "giving my memory rein" (009-199) casts the entire intervening adventure as material for retrospection, assimilated from the safety of the Wiltshire country house. This retrospective framing is more than a narrative convenience; it is the novel's moral conclusion. The violence of [[carinthia|Carinthia]], the ordeal at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], the terror of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s criminality -- all these are reduced to fuel for recollection, safely contained within the domestic order that Maintenance represents.

The circular structure (departure from Maintenance in August, return and winter reflection at the same hearth) enacts the novel's deepest conviction: that adventure is a departure from order, undertaken in its service, and that the proper end of adventure is the restoration of the home that was left behind. Maintenance is not merely the starting-point of the plot but its telos -- the condition to which the narrative must return for the story to be complete.

## Key Scenes

- (001-001) The novel's opening sentence: [[william-chandos|Chandos]] establishes that he and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] "first set up house in Wiltshire" in October 1926, naming Maintenance as the home from which they "did not sleep out" more than seven times during the hunting season.
- (001-030) On returning to Maintenance, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] shares news of a robbery with [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]], beginning the chain of concern that draws them into [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s orbit.
- (001-057) The demands of summer keep the friends at Maintenance, preventing a return visit to [[white-ladies|White Ladies]] despite the short distance: "there was much to be done."
- (009-199) The novel's closing reflection: [[william-chandos|Chandos]] sits at Maintenance "looking into the fire of logs and giving my memory rein," recollecting the adventure from the safety of home.
