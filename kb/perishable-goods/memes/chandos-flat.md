# Chandos's Flat

[[william-chandos|William Chandos]]'s London flat is the starting point of the narrator's journey in *Perishable Goods*. It is from here that [[william-chandos|Chandos]] departs for [[cleveland-row|Cleveland Row]], where the news of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s burglary and the subsequent crisis unfolds.

## Role in the Story

The flat appears only briefly, but its position in the narrative is significant. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] "started betimes and travelled to London by road" (001-004), arriving at [[cleveland-row|Cleveland Row]] as the clock of St. James's Palace strikes nine. The flat represents the narrator's independent life — the domestic space he maintains separate from [[maintenance|Maintenance]], the country house he shares with [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]].

The flat's modest narrative presence is characteristic of [[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s narrative style. He is a self-effacing narrator who foregrounds the actions of others — [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]], [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]], [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] — while his own domestic arrangements remain mostly in the background.

> "I started betimes and travelled to London by road, and the clock of St. James's Palace was striking nine as I turned out of Marlborough Gate into Cleveland Row. Except for my servant, Bell, I was alone."
>
> (001-004)
