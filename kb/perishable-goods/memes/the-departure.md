# The Departure from Gath

The departure from [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]] constitutes the closing sequence of *Perishable Goods*, bringing to an end the extraordinary weeks during which [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] and his party had occupied the ancient fortress, rescued [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]], and defeated [[rose-noble-criminal-network|Rose Noble's criminal network]]. The departure was conducted with the same careful secrecy that had characterized the entire [[castle-of-gath-operation|operation]].

## Disposing of the Prisoners

The first act of departure was the removal of [[punter|Punter]] and [[bunch|Bunch]], the two surviving henchmen of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]. "When the dusk came in, George, with Bell and Rowley, went off with Punter and Bunch, to take them to Innsbruck, purchase their tickets for London and see them go" (009-160).

The prisoners, who had been held in the very kitchen they had once used as a gaol for [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]], comported themselves with a humility that might have been taken for chastened repentance—but was not. "The latter gave no trouble, comported themselves most humbly and touched their hats to their warders, if ever they spoke" (009-161). However, [[rowley|Rowley]], who watched to see that they actually took the train, reported a revealing detail: "Punter was burdened with a suitcase and Bunch with a dressing-bag, and, since, when they left the car, they had had no luggage and neither had upon him the price of a glass of beer, I fear that they were incorrigible and that honesty was, so to speak, beyond their element" (009-161).

The implication is clear: Punter and Bunch, even in defeat, had stolen luggage from somewhere—most likely the railway station or another traveller—confirming that their criminal natures were fixed and ineradicable. They were "incorrigible" (009-161).

## Preparing the Castle

Before the party's own departure, meticulous efforts were made to erase every sign of their occupation. [[carson|Carson]], [[tester|Tester]], and [[william-chandos|Chandos]] "went the rounds for the last time" (009-162), inspecting every room.

The result of their efforts was comprehensive: "No sign of our occupation was anywhere to be seen" (009-163). The damage done in the antechamber had been repaired, bullet-holes in the Dining-room stopped, broken window-glass replaced. "The rooms had all been cleaned, the floors shone like glass, the beds had been stripped" (009-164). The caretakers would wash and return the linen to store. "What rubbish and litter there was had been taken the night before and sunk in a river we knew of ten miles away" (009-164).

## Chandos's Last Walk

When the inspection was complete, Chandos sent Carson down with Tester to finish loading the [[rolls-royce|Rolls]] and "walked by myself on the roof for half an hour" (009-165). The night was "lovely," and though October had arrived, "the air was as still and gentle as that of a summer's eve. A fine moon was riding a cloudless sky and shedding enough pale light to lend the towers and bulwarks a fanciful air and all the world I could see the look of a fairytale" (009-166).

Chandos was seized by a sensation of unreality: "I do not believe in Enchantment: but, if there be such a thing, it stood at my shoulder that evening upon the ramparts of Gath. Castle, spur and thicket, mountains and forests and the flash of the water below seemed all 'such stuff as dreams are made on,' and I could not shake off the feeling that we were about to quit a fantastic country which, search for it as we might, we never should see again" (009-167).

## The Final Moments

At ten o'clock, Chandos went to the terrace as he had been asked, "to tell Adele and Mansel that it was time to be gone" (009-169). What he found was a tableau of farewell.

Adele and Mansel were standing together by the balustrade. "They had left the chairs, set for them, and were standing together up to the balustrade: his arm was about her, and her head was against his shoulder, and the two threw a single shadow upon the flags" (009-170). Chandos stood irresolute, then went heavily down the steps. They did not turn at his coming; only Adele stretched out a little hand. "I came and stood beside her, and her arm went about my shoulders and held me close" (009-173).

The three—Mansel, Adele, and Chandos—stood together, "all three together, looking unto the hills" (009-174). Mansel spoke the words that define their shared experience: "The very elements respect you. Was ever a night so lovely, to round a dream?" (009-176). Adele answered: "Our dream. Our beautiful, shining dream. You gave it us, darling. By keeping my letters, you gave it. And your strength has been its glory, your gentleness its life" (009-177).

## The Closing Embrace

Adele then turned to Chandos: "Dear William, you saw the beginning, and now you see the end. You've been with us in light and in shadow: you've given us freely the best that a man can give: and I can only tell you that the love that you have shown us is part and parcel of our dream" (009-185). She "put up her lips to be kissed, like a little child, and I kissed them a little fearfully, for my clay is common, but she and Mansel were spirits of another sort" (009-186).

Then Adele "returned to her lover, and I left them to the rustle of the water and the pale splendour of the moonlight and the motionless plumage of the opposing hills" (009-187).

## The Drive Away

"Very soon they came up to the 'gallery of stone': and I shut the door behind them and followed them down to the courtyard, without a word. Five minutes later the Rolls stole up the spur and into the wood" [009-188–009-189].

The departure was quiet, deliberate, and final. The Rolls-Royce, which had carried them through so much, now carried them away—up the spur, through the wood, and into the night. The Castle of Gath, scene of their triumph and their terror, receded into darkness.

## The Epilogue's Revelation

In the novel's closing reflections, Chandos reveals that two physical traces of their occupation remained—both beneath the King's bed. "Not for some days did I remember that we had never replaced the stone slab which had sealed the entrance to the Closet from the archway below. This will be found one day, where it lies beneath the King's bed: and one day the coverlet will be drawn, and men will find the bloodstain upon the quilt" [009-196–009-197].

Chandos imagines the speculation these relics will provoke: "The name of Maximilian will be in every mouth. Old annals will be reread, old narratives revised and legends brought to bed of fine new tales of wrath and mischief and murder saved or done. And yet I cannot think that, for all their bravery, they will compare with the truth" (009-198).

The truth—the "quiet gallery" of memory shared by Mansel, Adele, and Chandos—would remain theirs alone. The departure from Gath was not the end of that gallery but its completion: the final brushstroke on a painting that only three people would ever truly see.
