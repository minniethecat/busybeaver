# The Telegram Arrives

The arrival of the telegram is the pivotal dramatic moment of Chapter I of *Perishable Goods*. On the evening of 30 August, [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]], [[william-chandos|William Chandos]], and [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] are dining at [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s flat in [[cleveland-square|Cleveland Row]], laying plans for their journey to [[carinthia|Carinthia]], when [[carson|Carson]] brings in a telegram that transforms a puzzling extortion letter into a crisis of abduction (001-090)(001-097).

## Prelude: The Ransom Letter

Earlier that evening, after the cloth had been drawn, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] had shared "some unfortunate news" — that Captain "Boy" [[daphne-pleydell|Pleydell]], [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]'s husband, had broken his leg in [[carinthia|Carinthia]] (001-065). Moments later, [[carson|Carson]] entered with a note marked "Immediate," found by the porter on the front steps. The note contained the first ransom demand:

> "The stolen goods will be returned on the receipt by the Manager of the ——— Bank, Zurich, of your cheque for five hundred thousand pounds. This sum you can raise, if you please. No time should be wasted for the goods are perishable. August 30th." (001-070)

The demand was bewildering. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] questioned the word "perishable" (001-073). [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] could not understand why [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] had waited nine months (001-074). [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] identified the author — "It must be [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]], for nobody else would know you could raise such a sum" (001-075) — but the price was "fantastic." [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] valued the stolen papers, casual letters from a girl numbering ten or twelve with her photograph, at no more than a hundred pounds (001-076)(001-079).

The trio sat in silence. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] noted the curious phrasing: "'The goods are perishable.' That's a curious way of saying 'I'm going to send them to him'" (001-086). [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] agreed: "It's not at all like [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]. He always made himself clear" (001-087).

## The Telegram

Then a bell rang. The three men sat in "breathless silence" as a servant passed to the front door (001-090). [[carson|Carson]] returned bearing a telegram (001-091).

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] ripped open the envelope, glanced at the sheet, and "clapped his hands to his face" (001-092). After a moment of recovery, he told [[carson|Carson]] to have the messenger wait for an answer (001-095). Then he rose and handed the telegram to [[william-chandos|Chandos]]:

> "Return Adèle disappeared shall I call in police Pleydell." (001-097)

The words struck like a physical blow. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] cried out (001-098). [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] snatched the form from his hand (001-099).

## Mansel's Revelation

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s remark was devastating in its clarity: "You were quite right, [[william-chandos|Chandos]]. [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] has a way of making himself painfully clear" (001-100).

Then, with a short laugh, he made the confession that connected everything: **"The letters he took had been written to me by [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]"** (001-102).

The implications cascaded. "And, when he says 'stolen goods,' he's not referring to the letters, but to something more — more valuable, *something which 'disappeared' a few hours ago*" (001-104).

Only then did [[william-chandos|Chandos]] fully understand. He watched [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] pick up the earlier letter and read it again. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] spoke quietly, as though alone:

> "*'Perishable goods.' Yes. I suppose you might call Adèle in Rose Noble's hands — 'perishable goods.'*" (001-107)

## Significance

The moment is the hinge upon which the entire novel turns. The meaning of the title is spoken aloud: [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]] herself is the "perishable goods." The earlier theft of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s papers (001-016) is retroactively revealed as [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s reconnaissance — he learned from the letters not only that [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] loved [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]], but that she was the one "perishable" commodity for which [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] might pay half a million pounds.

The telegram also accomplishes a personal unmasking. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], the most reserved of men, is forced to reveal his deepest secret to his two closest friends: that he loves his cousin's wife. His flash of visible emotion is noted: "the flash of feeling he showed, when the telegram was brought in, was because when he read it, he knew that his secret was ours" (001-111).

[[william-chandos|Chandos]], in a moment of private agony, realizes that his own earlier indiscretion — telling [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] of the theft during their morning ride at [[white-ladies|White Ladies]] (001-050) — means that she herself must now know she is the woman [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] loves, and that she is being held as a weapon against him (001-125). This knowledge will haunt [[william-chandos|Chandos]] throughout the ensuing campaign.
