# Buchinger's Emergency Surgery

[[buchinger|Dr. Buchinger]]'s emergency surgery on [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] at [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]] is the medical climax of *Perishable Goods*, the operation that saves Mansel's life after [[casemate|Casemate]] shot him in the stomach. The surgery was made possible by a series of astonishing coincidences and the quick thinking of [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]].

## The Summoning

While [[william-chandos|Chandos]], [[adele-pleydell|Adele]], and the dying Mansel kept their vigil in the King's bedchamber, [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] had not been idle. Realizing the desperate nature of Mansel's wound—"the bullet had entered his stomach, and there was death in his face" (008-301)—Hanbury made a decision that would prove life-saving.

As Hanbury later recounted to Chandos: "I knew it was a chance in a million, but there was the car waiting, and the bookseller ready to help" (009-068). Hanbury understood the medical reality with the clarity of a doctor's son: "you don't die of the wound: you die of the loss of blood" (009-068). Time was the critical factor—every moment of haemorrhage brought Mansel closer to death.

Hanbury drove to [[lass|Lass]] in the [[rolls-royce|Rolls]], accompanied by the [[bookseller-of-lass|bookseller]]. Upon reaching the local surgeon's house, they encountered an extraordinary stroke of fortune. The door was open, and "by the grace of God," the local surgeon was just then showing [[buchinger|Dr. Buchinger]] out (009-071)—Buchinger, an Innsbruck surgeon of considerable reputation, had been visiting and was literally on the doorstep, about to catch his train.

## A Happy Mistake

Hanbury then made what he called "a most happy mistake": "I took Buchinger for the local, and the local for Buchinger's man" (009-071). This confusion proved fortuitous, for the local surgeon, [[rachel|Dr. Rachel]], was competent within his sphere but, as Hanbury observed, "take him out of his groove, and he loses his mind" (009-079). Buchinger, by contrast, "knows his job" (009-077).

Hanbury's pitch was direct. With no time for finesse, he "jolly well bunged it in": he offered to pay a thousand pounds for the operation, with another four thousand pounds three months later, "provided we'd reason to think that he'd held his tongue" (009-071). He laid the notes on the table before the surgeon's eyes. The bookseller added his word: "I will give you my word that there is nothing to fear. This gentleman is saving a lady's name" (009-071).

Still Buchinger hesitated. He insisted: "Dr. Rachel must help me," and looked at the man Hanbury had mistaken for his assistant (009-072). Realizing his error—that Buchinger must be "another *and bigger* pot" (009-073)—Hanbury added two hundred and fifty pounds to the offer. The two surgeons looked at the notes, and "Rachel made a noise like a siphon and picked his up" (009-076).

## The Operation

Buchinger proved his worth immediately. He "called for paper and pencil and made out a list. When he'd done, he checked it over: then he gave it to Rachel and told him to 'get those things into the car'" (009-077). Five minutes later they collected the necessary supplies from a chemist's two streets away.

The operation was performed in the makeshift conditions of the castle, with the equipment Rachel maintained at his clinic. Buchinger's skill, combined with Rachel's surgical supplies, was sufficient for the task. But the outcome was far from certain.

## The Prognosis

When the operation was over, Buchinger was direct about the gravity of the situation: "so far from his life being saved, Mansel might die any minute during the next three days" (009-082). He explained the medical reality with precision: "When he thought he was dying, he was perfectly right. He was dying—and dying fast. He is now no longer dying: Dr. Rachel and I have stopped that. But he is standing still—on the very edge of death" (009-083).

Buchinger could do no more. "Nothing that I can do can draw him back. If he should live for three days, he will himself draw back: and then a medical student could make him well. But if, before then, he slips—that is the end" (009-083).

## The Three-Day Wait

For three days, Mansel lay in the room [[adele-pleydell|Adele]] had once used in the southwest tower, suspended between life and death. Buchinger remained at the castle, as his holiday "does not end for another six days" (009-084)—another improbable coincidence that worked in their favour.

The strain on Adele was Buchinger's chief concern beyond Mansel himself. He observed: "I fear for the health of that beautiful girl" (009-084), recognizing that her vigil at Mansel's bedside was taking a toll that no medical intervention could address. He insisted that nursing duties be shared so that Adele would not become "another patient upon your hands" (009-102).

Finally, "the mists parted, and they said he would live" (009-101). Buchinger predicted "he will recover fast. If all goes well, he can leave his bed in a fortnight, and in another week you may drive him home. But not before that" (009-102).

## Significance

The Buchinger operation represents the convergence of luck, money, nerve, and surgical skill that saved Mansel's life. Without Hanbury's desperate gamble, without the bookseller's vouching, without the precisely-timed coincidence of Buchinger's doorstep departure, and without the vast sums of money Mansel kept available, the novel would have ended in tragedy. As Chandos put it simply to Hanbury: "You've saved his life" (009-078).

Hanbury's response—"if I have, it's pure fluke"—captures the truth of the matter. Chance, as much as design, kept Mansel alive. But Hanbury had seized that chance with both hands.
