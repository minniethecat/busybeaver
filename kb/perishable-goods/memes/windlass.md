# Windlass

The **windlass** is a mechanical hoist situated beside the massive cage of [[the-lift|the lift]] at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]]. It controls the great rope attached to a hook on the lift's underside, allowing the party to raise and lower the concealed elevator — and, critically, to rescue [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] and the servants during the operation's climax.

## Mechanism

The lift at the Castle of Gath operates on a system of pulleys and counterweights. The weights are so heavy that the platform cannot descend under its own weight:

> "Remember they said it was raised by a system of pulleys and weights. The weights weighed far more than the table, so it couldn't descend on its own or so much as budge, but had to be hauled down by a windlass between each course." (007-022)

The windlass is the manual control for this system, allowing an operator to winch the platform down against the counterweights. The mechanism stands to one side of the lift cage:

> "About us was the massive cage in which the lift ran, and at each of its corners were a pulley and a rope and a great counterweight of stone. A little to one side stood the windlass, as Mansel had said." (007-034)

## Reaching the Rope

The party's immediate problem is reaching the windlass rope, which is attached to a hook on the lift's underside — eight feet below their position. [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] devises a solution, lowering [[william-chandos|Chandos]] by the wrists to swing out and grasp the rope:

> "On the under side of the lift, right in its middle, was a hook: to this was attached the great rope which the windlass controlled. If we could reach this rope, the trick was ours. Mansel took my left wrist in his hands and lowered me clear of the lift. At once I swung to and fro until I could reach and lay hold of the rope." (007-038)

## The Rescue

Once the rope is secured, the windlass becomes the means of bringing the lift down and, more importantly, of hoisting people up from the cellars. After [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] is found alive in the cellar, the party uses the windlass to operate the lift:

> "By using the windlass, we had the lift down at once, but, when I would have gone up, Mansel put me aside. 'I'm going,' he said. 'And please give me full five minutes before you bring me down.'" (007-048)

The windlass thus becomes the mechanism by which the trapped servants and Hanbury are brought to safety, transforming a piece of castle infrastructure into a tool of rescue.

## Under Pressure

The windlass work is physically demanding and must be performed under the constant threat of discovery. As Chandos operates the mechanism, his mind races with the implications of their situation:

> "As I bent again to the windlass, my mind was full. Not only was Rose Noble's foresight a fearsome thing, but the fellow was very well served. Punter and Bunch and Casemate had done uncommonly well." (007-065)

## Significance

The windlass, like the [[ladder|ladders]] and [[rope|rope]], represents the novel's recurring theme of practical mechanism over brute force. [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] has installed an ingenious concealed elevator to serve his criminal operations; Mansel's team turns that very mechanism against him, using the windlass to undo the trap he has laid. It is a fitting emblem of the contest: Noble's theatrical villainy versus Mansel's operational competence.
