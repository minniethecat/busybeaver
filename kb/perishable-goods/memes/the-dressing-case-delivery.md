# The Dressing-Case Delivery

The Dressing-Case Delivery is a small but strategically loaded moment at the conclusion of the morning council at [[poganec|Poganec]]. As the rescue party prepares to depart, [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] makes an unexpected request: that [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]]'s dressing-case be sent for and packed with such things as she would be glad to have, and that the party take it with them wherever they go (002-109).

## Context

The request came at the end of the council held at a quarter to twelve on the morning after the party's arrival (002-101). Captain "Boy" [[daphne-pleydell|Pleydell]], having been restored by the discovery of the ransom demand in the gate-box (002-094), had agreed that the half-million-pound sum was "fantastic" and that the letter should be ignored (002-103). He had also accepted that [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s party would operate independently, not returning to [[poganec|Poganec]] (002-103).

Security arrangements had been established: the [[daphne-pleydell|Pleydells]] would take no action without first communicating with the party, would accept no message purporting to come from the party unless one of the principals personally delivered it, and would recognize no intermediary but [[major-pleydell|Major Pleydell]] or known servants (002-106).

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] had already conducted preliminary investigations — riding most of the way to [[sava|Sava]], visiting the spot where the glove was found, interviewing all the farm servants (002-108).

## The Request

Then, rising to leave, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] said: "And now please send for [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]'s dressing-case and pack it with such things as she would be glad to have. This we are to take with us wherever we go" (002-109).

> Then he asked that Adèle's dressing-case should be sent for and packed with such things as she would be glad to have. This we were to take with us wherever we went.
>
> (002-109)

The request was unusual. The party was embarking on a mobile search operation, planning to be "constantly moving in search or pursuit of [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]" (001-150). Carrying a woman's dressing-case — an inherently bulky and personal item — was an encumbrance. Yet [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] did not regard it as such.

[[daphne-pleydell|Daphne]] left to arrange the packing, and the party bade Captain [[daphne-pleydell|Pleydell]] goodbye. His final words to [[william-chandos|William Chandos]] were: "[[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]'s very lucky to have such good friends" (002-112). [[william-chandos|Chandos]] replied, with perfect sincerity: "Oh, the boot's on the other leg" (002-113).

Two minutes later the engines of the [[rolls-royce|cars]] were running, and the party was about to take their seats when [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] called [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] into a parlour to share his deduction about the enemy observation post (002-115).

## Strategic Purpose

While [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] does not state his reasons explicitly in the moment, the strategic logic of carrying the dressing-case emerges over the following chapters. The case serves multiple purposes.

First, it is a signal to the kidnappers that the family is prepared to cooperate — to deliver goods on demand. [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] had demanded the cheque for half a million; the family was ignoring that demand but demonstrating willingness to provide other items [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] might need. This ambiguity keeps open the possibility of negotiation and reduces the risk that [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] will harm his hostage out of frustration.

Second, the case provides a pretext for contact. Should the party encounter the kidnappers or their intermediaries, possession of [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]'s personal effects establishes their bona fides and creates a natural occasion for communication. In a kidnapping where direct contact has been minimal — only notes and a telegram — any established channel is valuable.

Third, the case is a practical contingency. If the rescue is successful, [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] will have been held captive for days, perhaps weeks. Clean clothes, personal toiletries, and familiar possessions will be a tangible comfort after her ordeal.

## The Fate of the Case

The dressing-case becomes part of the party's mobile equipment. It travels with them from [[poganec|Poganec]] to [[villach|Villach]] and eventually to [[lass|Lass]], always present as a token of the mission's purpose — not merely to defeat [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] but to restore [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] to safety and dignity.

The care taken over this small detail reflects [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s character. Even in the midst of a high-stakes pursuit, he thinks of [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]]'s comfort. It is the same quality that leads him, when he finally sights her through binoculars on the terrace of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], to flash a signal with his wristwatch case rather than take any action that might endanger her (004-168).

## Significance

The dressing-case delivery is a small scene but emblematic of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s method: every action serves multiple purposes, and nothing is done without tactical calculation. What appears to be a kindness is also a signal; what seems to be an encumbrance is also an asset. The case stands for the dual nature of the entire operation — at once a desperate rescue and a gentleman's errand, conducted with a care for the small decencies even as it prepares for "battle and murder" (002-073).
