# The Salzburg Drive

The Salzburg Drive is the high-speed cross-continental journey undertaken by [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s rescue party from Dieppe, France, to [[poganec|Poganec]] in [[carinthia|Carinthia]], Austria. Covering approximately a thousand miles in under twenty-four hours, the drive demonstrates both the mechanical excellence of the two [[rolls-royce|Rolls-Royce]] cars and [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s extraordinary endurance and navigational skill.

## The Night Leg: France

The drive began in darkness from the Dieppe quay, with [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] in the lead and [[william-chandos|William Chandos]] following at the wheel of the second car. "So long as the night lasted, we had the road to ourselves" (002-007). [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] drove "like the wind," and [[william-chandos|Chandos]] clung to his taillight by "more by luck than by cunning" (002-007).

The first hour imposed a continuous strain. [[william-chandos|Chandos]], unfamiliar with the road, feared both leaving it by driving too fast and losing his guiding light by slackening speed. "I wished for the day with a fervour which I think would have opened the eyes of St. Paul himself" (002-007).

When daylight broke, the trial proved to have been a valuable apprenticeship. With visibility restored, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] could see [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s Rolls "scudding before me like a gull," and following behind "seemed child's play" (002-008). Recent rain had laid the dust, and the country smelled sweet. "Our passage became every moment more agreeable" (002-009).

At a quarter to eight, a closed level-crossing gate provided the first definite check. The party halted for a quarter of an hour, eating the food [[bell|Bell]] had brought and letting [[tester|Tester]] exercise (002-012). [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], pleased with their pace, rotated the drivers — [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] took [[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s place at the wheel (002-013).

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] also pressed [[bell|Bell]] to try to recall the people he had seen at Dieppe, particularly anyone who had been walking up and down on the quay. "Bell is observant," [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] explained to [[william-chandos|Chandos]], "and I'll lay a hundred to one that he observed the fellow that left that note." He elaborated his theory of memory as a storeroom: "Some time ago I observed the way to [[salzburg|Salzburg]]: and now I'm producing it. [[bell|Bell]] undoubtedly observed the man who carried that note: let's hope he'll be able to produce him — when the moment comes" (002-020).

## The Daylight Push

With [[william-chandos|Chandos]] now riding beside [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], the cars pressed on through roads they had travelled in earlier adventures. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] drove "with the utmost confidence, never hesitating where the road forked or peering at any signpost, but whipping through town after town as though it were the place of his birth" (002-018).

When [[william-chandos|Chandos]] remarked on this, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] demurred that it was nothing — that [[william-chandos|Chandos]] knew the way equally well but was not using his memory as actively (002-019).

They reached Strasbourg at two o'clock and lunched on German soil half an hour later (002-025). The afternoon was "made dreadful by the heat and glare of the sun" (002-027). [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], who had had no respite, bore the burden with remarkable lightness. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] reflected that "how Mansel, who had had no respite, bore the burden of that day so lightly I never shall understand" (002-027).

At sundown they halted beside a little stream, plunging their heads and shoulders into the water. While the principals ate, the servants cleaned windscreens and sponged down the dust-caked cars — "by no means a fruitless exercise, for we were sick of the dust, and the sight of the glistening coachwork did us all good" (002-028).

## Into Austria

Night fell soon after eight, but a fine moon was westering. Two and a half hours later they entered Austria (002-029).

At [[salzburg|Salzburg]] they drove straight to the station, where [[rowley|Rowley]] lay "stretched on our luggage, fast asleep" (002-030). It took only a moment to rouse him and lift the trunks into the cars. The digression cost them less than five minutes (002-030).

Clear of the city, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] halted and called the servants together. He issued his first operational order: "Discuss this business with no one... I've had two letters already warning me not to proceed" (002-032). He then announced the dispositions: [[rowley|Rowley]] would be dropped at [[villach|Villach]] to take rooms and order supper; the rest would press on to [[poganec|Poganec]]; and at [[poganec|Poganec]], [[carson|Carson]] and [[bell|Bell]] were to stay with and sleep in the cars (002-033)(002-034).

## Arrival in Carinthia

Passing through [[villach|Villach]], they roused the innkeeper, who "began to cry to his household that at last his luck had turned, for the best guests he ever had were come again" (002-036). But the party was gone before he had opened the door, leaving [[rowley|Rowley]] amidst the luggage.

The final stage ran through "most handsome country, very mountainous and closely wooded, with streams and pastures lying in every dale" (002-038). The mountain air suited the engines, and "the cars sailed over the hills like giants refreshed" (002-038).

They swept through St. Martin to hear the church clock strike one (002-039). Ten minutes later they sighted "a long low house, lying in the lap of a meadow, with rising woods behind" — [[poganec|Poganec]]. Its windows were all open, and "lights burning in every room," a "fantastic" picture in the moonlight (002-040).

## Significance

The Salzburg Drive establishes the physical scale of the rescue operation and the capabilities of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s team. The sheer speed of the journey — from [[cleveland-square|Cleveland Row]] to the heart of [[carinthia|Carinthia]] in scarcely more than twenty-four hours — demonstrates the seriousness of the mission and the resources at [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s command. More subtly, the drive reveals [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s philosophy of memory and observation (002-020), which will prove decisive when [[bell|Bell]] later identifies the Dieppe spy [[casemate|Casemate]] at [[lass|Lass]] (003-178).

Mansel's philosophy of observation and memory drives the team's navigation through Europe:

> "And that," said he, "is why I'm badgering Bell. Bell is observant, and I'll lay a hundred to one that he observed the fellow that left that note. I don't think I did, for when he saw me coming, he probably made himself scarce. And you were behind the car. But Bell and he were both waiting for the boat to come in. Well, there's the making of a clue. It's slight enough in all conscience, but—well, I don't know where Adèle is, and Austria's quite a big place." After a little, he continued thoughtfully. "Memory's a storeroom, that's all: the storeroom of things you observe. If you're observant, so much the richer your store. But you must be a good storeman—able to produce the requisite, when the moment comes. Some time ago I observed the way to Salzburg: and now I'm producing it. Bell undoubtedly observed the man who carried that note: let's hope he'll be able to produce him—when the moment comes."
>
> (002-020)
