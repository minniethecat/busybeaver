# The Rosary

[[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s rosary appears in the novel's closing reflections, one of several objects that [[william-chandos|William Chandos]] imagines will acquire historical interest: "What curious interest will not these things arouse?" (009-198). The rosary speaks to dimensions of [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s character that the adventure narrative only hints at.

> What curious interest will not these things arouse? The name of Maximilian will be in every mouth. Old annals will be reread, old narratives revised and legends brought to bed of fine new tales of wrath and mischief and murder saved or done. And yet I cannot think that, for all their bravery, they will compare with the truth.
>
> (009-198)

## Role in the Story

The rosary is not mentioned during the action of the novel but appears in [[william-chandos|Chandos]]'s forward-looking epilogue. Its presence among the relics of the adventure — alongside references to [[king-maximilian|Maximilian]] and the annals of history — suggests a private, contemplative side to [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] that the narrator only partially reveals.

The rosary connects [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] to a tradition of faith and ritual that coexists with his identity as a man of action. It is a reminder that the novel's hero is more complex than the adventure genre typically allows — a man who carries prayer beads alongside his [[pistol|pistol]].
