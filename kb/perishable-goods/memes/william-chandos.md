# William Chandos

Richard William Chandos is the first-person narrator of Dornford Yates's 1928 novel
*Perishable Goods* and a central protagonist in the campaign against
[[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]. A gentleman of independent means, he shares
[[cleveland-row|Cleveland Row]] lodgings with [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] and
serves as [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s trusted lieutenant in a series of
increasingly dangerous operations across Europe.

## Role in the Story

Chandos occupies the dual role of participant and chronicler. Every event in the novel
reaches the reader through his observation, yet he is far from a passive witness. His
contributions to [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s campaign include:

- **Reconnaissance**: Shadowing [[casemate|Casemate]] through the countryside near the
  [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]], a feat [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] later calls "the trick" that made the
  operation possible (004-114).
- **Impersonation**: Assuming [[casemate|Casemate]]'s identity—clothing, manner, and role—to
  infiltrate [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s operation at the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] [008-019–008-022]. This
  deception is the novel's most audacious gambit.
- **Combat**: Fighting alongside [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] in the rescue of
  [[daphne-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]] (006-279) and the armed confrontation at the Castle
  of Gath.

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s trust in Chandos is absolute, as reflected in his frequent direct address:

> "You were quite right, Chandos," said Mansel. "Rose Noble has been here." (001-100)

## Biography

Chandos belongs to the English gentleman class of the 1920s—educated, independently
wealthy, and at leisure to pursue adventure. His full name, Richard William Chandos, is
revealed only once, in Chapter VIII, during the impersonation of [[casemate|Casemate]]:

> "Richard William Chandos, alive and in Casemate's clothes, would be infinitely
> preferable to Casemate, dead." (008-019)

He shares a flat with [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] on [[cleveland-row|Cleveland Row]], and the two
maintain [[maintenance|Maintenance]], their country house in [[wiltshire|Wiltshire]],
which serves as the trio's base of operations. His servant, [[carson|Bell]], accompanies
him on several missions.

Together with [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]], Chandos had "made [their] fortune in France" before
the events of the novel (001-028). This shared history of adventure—and presumably
wartime service—explains the deep trust and operational默契 among the three men.

## Quotations

Chandos's narrative voice—measured, self-deprecating, and occasionally wry—is the
novel's defining stylistic register. While he rarely quotes himself directly, his
observations reveal character:

> "Now neither Hanbury nor I would have dreamed of visiting London without calling on
> Jonathan Mansel, whose flat was in Cleveland Row; for we three had made our fortune in
> France, and to Mansel we owed our lives." (001-003)

His courage is demonstrated through action rather than declaration. When ordered to lead
the infiltration through the water passage:

> "Chandos first. Wait for me in the porch." (007-072)

## Significance

Chandos exemplifies the Yatesian gentleman-hero: capable of violence but preferring
cunning, loyal to his friends, and possessed of the emotional restraint that the
narrative voice itself performs. His first-person perspective gives *Perishable Goods*
its distinctive tone—part adventure story, part memoir—and his willingness to subsume
his own identity (literally, by wearing [[casemate|Casemate]]'s clothes) demonstrates the theme of
sacrificial friendship that runs through the novel.
