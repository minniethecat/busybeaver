# Wiltshire

Wiltshire is the English county where [[william-chandos|William Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] established their shared household. The novel opens with [[william-chandos|Chandos]] recounting how "I and I first set up house in Wiltshire" (001-001), establishing the location as the protagonists' domestic base before the adventure begins.

> It was in October, 1926, that George Hanbury and I first set up house in Wiltshire; and, since for the next six months we hunted four days a week, yet would commit to no one the pleasant task of setting our home in order, I do not think we slept out of Maintenance — for from time immemorial that has been the name of the place — more than seven times.

> (001-001)

## Location & Setting

Wiltshire is a rural county in southwestern England, known for its rolling chalk downlands and historic estates. In the context of *Perishable Goods*, it represents the quiet English countryside from which the protagonists are drawn into continental adventure. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]]'s residence---[[maintenance|Maintenance]]---is located in Wiltshire (001-001).

## Maintenance: The Named Residence

The house shared by [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] bears the name Maintenance, a designation that [[william-chandos|Chandos]] notes has been attached to the property "from time immemorial" (001-001). The name itself carries undertones of stability, upkeep, and permanence---qualities that stand in deliberate contrast to the perilous, unpredictable nature of the continental adventure that soon engulfs the household. Maintenance in Wiltshire represents the ordered English life that the protagonists are repeatedly forced to leave behind, and to which they ultimately hope to return. The fact that the name is presented as ancient and unchanging reinforces the novel's thematic division between the settled English countryside and the lawless Alpine territories where the central conflict unfolds.

## The Hunting Life

The opening sentence of the novel provides a glimpse into the rhythm of life at Maintenance: for six months after setting up house, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] "hunted four days a week" (001-001). This detail establishes the gentlemen's life of leisure and sport that characterizes their existence in Wiltshire. Despite their devotion to the hunt, the two men "would commit to no one the pleasant task of setting our home in order," suggesting a deep personal investment in their domestic arrangement. Chandos records that during this period they did not sleep away from Maintenance more than seven times (001-001), underscoring the intensity of their commitment to the house and to each other's company---a domestic contentment that makes their subsequent departure into danger all the more significant.

## Role in the Plot

Wiltshire serves as the starting point of the narrative. The opening line places the reader there, and the county functions as the departure point for the journey to [[white-ladies|White Ladies]]: "The next day we left for Wiltshire promising soon to return" is inverted---the protagonists leave Wiltshire for the [[new-forest|New Forest]] (001-057). The county thus anchors the English dimension of the novel's geography, contrasting with the Austrian locations that dominate the later chapters.

## The Departure and Return

The departure from Wiltshire is not a single event but a recurring pattern in the novel's geography. After the initial visit to [[white-ladies|White Ladies]], the protagonists return to Wiltshire "promising soon to return" (001-057)---a promise that sets in motion the chain of events leading to the continental crisis. The return to Wiltshire after White Ladies represents the last moment of English normality before [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s urgent summons draws [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] away from their county and toward the dangers of [[carinthia|Carinthia]]. Maintenance in Wiltshire thus functions as both the geographical and emotional point of origin---the home that must be left and the destination to which the characters aspire to return once the adventure is concluded.

## Thematic Significance

Wiltshire embodies the novel's pastoral English identity, standing in opposition to the wild and dangerous Austrian Alps. The county is not merely a setting but a value system: it represents order, domesticity, sport, tradition, and male companionship---all qualities that are threatened by the criminal forces operating in [[carinthia|Carinthia]]. The act of "setting up house" in Wiltshire, with all its connotations of permanence and joint enterprise, provides the emotional foundation that drives [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] to risk everything in the rescue of [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]]. Without Wiltshire---without Maintenance---the stakes of the continental adventure would be significantly diminished.
