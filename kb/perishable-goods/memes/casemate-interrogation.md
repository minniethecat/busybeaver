# Casemate Interrogation

The Casemate Interrogation was the moment when [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]] extracted from [[casemate|Casemate]]—or more accurately, from the coincidence of a chance encounter—the vital intelligence about the layout of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] and [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]]'s location within it. Although the novel does not depict a formal interrogation of [[casemate|Casemate]] by [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], the term encompasses the process by which Mansel's party gathered and synthesized the geographical and architectural intelligence upon which the entire rescue operation would depend.

## Context / Lead-up

After his hair-raising ride on the roof of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s car, [[william-chandos|William Chandos]] had been deposited on the spur outside the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] and, failing to enter with the car, had fled back through the wood to the crossroads where [[rowley|Rowley]] was waiting (004-104). He found [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] and [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] poring over a paper book while [[carson|Carson]] and [[bell|Bell]] attended to practical matters nearby (004-104).

The "paper book" was a souvenir guide to [[lass|Lass]] and its surroundings, pressed upon [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] by the bookseller whose overcoat and hat he had borrowed (004-118). Written by the bookseller himself in imperfect but enthusiastic English, the guide contained maps and a description of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] so detailed and so inadvertently useful that it became the blueprint for the entire operation.

## Sequence of Events

1. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] returns from his reconnaissance and tells his tale: the car ride, the castle sighting, the near-entry, and the escape (004-106).
2. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] reassures him that being shut out was in fact a blessing in disguise: "If you had gone in, you'd have made a fatal mistake. Alone, unarmed and with no idea of the building, you wouldn't have stood an earthly" (004-108).
3. After breakfasting on food picked up at [[lass|Lass]], [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]], [[william-chandos|Chandos]], and [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] set out on foot to survey the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] from the edge of the wood (004-114).
4. At the wood's edge, the castle bursts upon them in an instant—"as though some curtain had suddenly been let fall" (004-120).
5. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] asks for the guidebook. [[george-hanbury|Hanbury]] consults the maps and identifies the location: "This should be the Castle of Gath" (004-126).
6. The three men read the bookseller's description together, absorbing every detail of the castle's architecture: the courtyard with its water-basin, the "pretty terrace where flowers blow," the Grand Staircase, the State Apartments, the "galleries of stone," the southwest tower, the chapel, and the oratory (004-129).
7. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] looks up into the sky and says quietly: "One has much to be thankful for." Then: "Let's hope they let her walk on 'the pretty terrace, where flowers blow'" (004-132)(004-133).

## Participants

- **[[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]** — the operation's strategist; immediately grasps the tactical significance of the guidebook's architectural detail
- **[[william-chandos|William Chandos]]** — the first-hand observer who had seen the castle from the spur and could corroborate the guidebook's account
- **[[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]]** — the accidental possessor of the guidebook; his chance encounter with the bookseller in [[lass|Lass]] proves providential
- **[[casemate|Casemate]]** — though not physically present, his role in leading the party to the castle makes him the unwitting source of the intelligence

## Outcome / Consequences

The bookseller's guide provided the party with an architectural blueprint of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] so complete that it would guide every subsequent tactical decision. From this description, [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] deduced:

- The existence and layout of the State Apartments on the south front
- The "galleries of stone" that flanked the courtyard and served as guardrooms
- The southwest tower, where [[adele-pleydell|Adèle]] would later be confirmed to be lodged (005-171)
- The terrace and cascade, which would become the party's secret entry route
- The chapel and oratory, religious spaces that held strategic importance

[[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] immediately recognized that a direct assault was impossible: "Any chance of a snap division... can be written right off. When your enemy's that far away, you're apt to be careless... But to rush a position like this when it's properly manned is quite impossible" (004-136).

Instead, he devised the strategy of feigned ignorance—scouring the countryside openly while secretly studying the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] from every angle (004-137). This deception would buy the party the time and intelligence they needed to plan a nighttime assault.
