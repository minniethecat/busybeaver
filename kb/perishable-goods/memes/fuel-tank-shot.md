# Fuel Tank Shot

The fuel tank shot is a pivotal action sequence in Chapter VII of *Perishable Goods*, occurring during [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]]'s recounting of the mountain pursuit to [[william-chandos|Chandos]] and [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] in the old kitchen beneath [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]]. After the servants attacked, [[carson|Carson]] and [[bell|Bell]] gave chase to [[punter|Punter]], [[bunch|Bunch]], and [[casemate|Casemate]] in the [[rolls-royce|Rolls-Royce]] along the winding mountain roads of [[carinthia|Carinthia]].

> "This annoyed Carson and Bell, and I must say I'm not surprised. And, as soon as the Rolls was round, they put her along. By this time Bunch had stolen a bit of a start, but they gradually overhauled him, and, choosing a smooth bit of going, Bell took a shot at their tank. He didn't hit it that time, but he laid it open the next."

> (007-053)

## The Pursuit

The chase began when [[carson|Carson]] heard the enemy's car leaving [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]]. As [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] reported, "Carson heard the car coming, and the moment it passed he gave chase" (007-053). Hanbury, who was stationed by the beechwoods at the foot of the cliff with [[rowley|Rowley]], watched the enemy car pass at approximately sixty miles per hour without slowing (007-053). Carson, accompanied by Bell, decided the best course was to "cling to their heels" (007-053) rather than attempt an immediate interception with only two men against three.

For half an hour the enemy enjoyed what Hanbury described as "the devil's own luck—never a check" (007-053). The road wound through the mountainous terrain, presenting challenges for both pursuer and pursued. The situation changed dramatically when the enemy encountered a hairpin bend, followed two hundred yards later by a flock of sheep (007-053).

## The Tactical Manoeuvre

When [[carson|Carson]] rounded the corner, he found [[bunch|Bunch]] fifty yards ahead and already executing a skilful manoeuvre: "Bunch was fifty yards off and *turning his car*" (007-054). Despite the flock of sheep blocking the road, Bunch managed to reverse his direction. Hanbury acknowledged the move as excellent, noting that while Bunch's car had a shortish wheelbase, "a Rolls takes some getting round" (007-054).

Carson responded with equal skill, stopping and reversing toward a turning he had noted at the top of the hill (007-054). Before he could reach it, however, Bunch came roaring back. "There was Punter beside him, and Casemate was back in the car" (007-054). As they passed, [[punter|Punter]] fired at Carson, hitting "the brim of his hat" (007-054).

## The Shot

This near-miss infuriated Carson and Bell. Hanbury reported his lack of surprise at their reaction: "This annoyed Carson and Bell, and I must say I'm not surprised" (007-055). Once the [[rolls-royce|Rolls]] was turned around, "they put her along" (007-055), accelerating in pursuit. Though Bunch had stolen "a bit of a start," the Rolls-Royce gradually overhauled the enemy car (007-055).

The critical moment came when Bell, "choosing a smooth bit of going," took deliberate aim at the enemy's fuel tank (007-055). His first shot missed its mark, but his second found its target: "He didn't hit it that time, but he laid it open the next" (007-055). The shot did not ignite the tank but ruptured it, draining the fuel and effectively disabling the enemy car.

## Aftermath

With the enemy's fuel tank ruptured, the pursuit became "a matter of time and nothing else" (007-056). Hanbury estimated that Bunch might manage another three miles, "but he couldn't do more" (007-056). Carson wisely fell back to keep out of range, as the enemy had "fired again and made a hole in the screen" (007-056).

The crippled enemy car continued toward the foot of the cliff, now within six miles of its destination (007-057). Three miles on, Carson rounded a bend to discover the car abandoned, "doors open, by the side of the road" (007-057). He sped past at a racing pace while two more shots were fired harmlessly (007-057).

Hanbury initially interpreted the outcome favourably: "I assumed they'd gone out to get food: and now their car was done in, and they were cut off" (007-058). He and the others "fairly picketed that spur," believing "a ferret couldn't have passed the line we held" (007-058).

However, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] later recognized the deeper strategic significance. [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s men had resisted the temptation to seize the Rolls when they could have, leaving it outside the gate—a decision that prevented the rescuers from discovering that [[george-hanbury|George]] had been captured and that [[adele-pleydell|Adele]] was about to be delivered into enemy hands. As Chandos reflected: "This was the pink of strategy—the casting away of a sceptre to win a crown" (007-066).

Bell's precision shot was the defining moment of the pursuit, turning the tactical balance in favour of Mansel's party and demonstrating the marksmanship that made Bell an indispensable member of [[mansel-household|the Mansel household]].
