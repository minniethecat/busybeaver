# Castle of Gath Terrace

The terrace of the [[castle-of-gath|Castle of Gath]] is a stone platform on the cliff side of the fortress, serving as the stage for some of the most dramatic moments in *Perishable Goods*. It is here that [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]] is first seen by her rescuers
The first sight of Adèle on the terrace is one of the novel's most vivid images:

> She was standing on the little terrace, with her hands on the balustrade. Whether she was watched I cannot say, but she was alone. Below her was the leaping cascade, and, behind, an archway, framing a door that was shut. She was standing very still; her head was up, and her eyes lifted to the peak upon which we stood.
>
> (004-167)
, here that desperate escapes are made through an archway door, and here that the novel's closing scenes unfold in quiet contrast to the violence that preceded them.

## First Sight of Adele

The terrace provides the rescue party with their first visual confirmation of [[adele-pleydell|Adele]]'s presence at the castle. During reconnaissance, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] observes: "She was standing on the little terrace, with her hands on the balustrade" (004-167). The image is arrestingly serene---a woman at a balustrade, as though admiring a view---yet the context is one of imprisonment. The "little terrace" is both a privilege and a cage: it offers [[adele-pleydell|Adele]] fresh air and sunlight, but it is bounded by the castle walls and the precipitous drop of the cliff.

Earlier, even before the sighting, the terrace had entered the party's imagination through a half-quoted line of verse: "Let's hope they let her walk on 'the pretty terrace, where flowers blow'" (004-133). The quotation---a fragment of romantic poetry---invests the terrace with ironic pathos. It is a pretty place, but it is also a prison yard.

## Architecture and the Water Channel

The terrace is bisected by a channel of water that flows from a basin above, down across the stone platform, and over the cliff edge. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] describes "peering between the balusters" to see "the ripple of the water of which the bookseller had written and could hear it fall out of the basin on its way to the terrace and the cliff" (005-166). This water channel becomes critical: "this was ten inches deep, and its floor was as smooth as glass" (005-168), providing both concealment and a covert passage.

The archway door at the head of the terrace steps is the primary point of access between the terrace and the interior. Controlling this door means controlling movement between the outside and the inside. When [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] and his party cannot open it from within, they must find alternative routes; when they can, the terrace becomes their highway into the heart of the castle.

## The Terrace as Battleground

As the operation intensifies, the terrace becomes contested ground. [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]]'s tactical planning repeatedly uses the terrace as a reference point. In one of his most daring gambits, he plans to stage a fall onto the terrace: "The sentinel watching the courtyard will rush to the opposite wall, to see me piled up on the terrace, with a length of rope in my hand" (007-144). He predicts: "I think that Rose Noble will go to the terrace at once: and the others with him" (007-145). The terrace, in this conception, is a stage on which a performance of death will be mounted.

When the plan executes: "As they reached the terrace, a slim figure flashed in their wake" (007-202). The terrace becomes the pivot around which the rescue turns, the point at which deception becomes action and action becomes escape.

## The Whisper of the Water

Throughout the later chapters, the sound of water falling from the terrace becomes a recurring motif. In the tense silence following the [[final-confrontation|final confrontation]], "the whisper of the water, taking its leap from the terrace, was all the sound we heard" (009-056). The water, which earlier provided tactical cover, becomes an elegiac backdrop.

The channel beneath the archway door proves a vital escape route. When [[william-chandos|Chandos]] must flee: "as soon as my feet touched the terrace, I made my way to the water and scrambled under the door" (006-021). The water channel that decorates the terrace becomes, in extremity, a means of survival.

## The Terrace in Peacetime

After the operation's conclusion, the terrace becomes a place of rest. [[william-chandos|Chandos]] records: "I was sitting on the terrace one morning, on a cushion of one of the cars, looking at the exquisite prospect and finding much virtue in sloth, when a hand came to rest upon my shoulder, and there was Adele" (009-115). The transformation is complete: the terrace that was a prison and a battleground is now a place where one sits on a car cushion and admires the view.

In the novel's final pages, [[william-chandos|Chandos]] returns one last time: "Then I went to the terrace, as they had asked me to do, to tell Adele and Mansel that it was time to be gone" (009-169). The terrace is the last place visited before departure, the final station on the journey out of [[carinthia|Carinthia]] and back to England.
