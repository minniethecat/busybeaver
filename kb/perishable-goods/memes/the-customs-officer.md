# The Customs Officer

The **customs officer** (or more accurately, the French customs *official*) is an unnamed frontier functionary encountered at the port of Dieppe during the protagonists' urgent crossing from England to France in Chapter I of *Perishable Goods*. Though he appears only briefly and is never named, his efficient — and financially lubricated — processing of the party's papers and vehicles is a critical early demonstration of the operational methods that [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s team would employ throughout the campaign: money, preparation, and speed.

> Bell was to cross to France by a morning boat, there to buy food and petrol against our coming and to promise ten thousand francs to the officials concerned if we were clear of the Customs in half an hour.

> (001-134)

## The Crossing

The crossing itself was undertaken under extreme time pressure. The party had learned of [[adele-pleydell|Adèle Pleydell]]'s kidnapping on the evening of 30 August, and Mansel determined to sail the very next night (001-131). The crossing was from Newhaven to Dieppe: the party dined at Newhaven, saw the cars taken on board, and took what rest they could (001-143).

Crucially, [[bell|Bell]] had been sent ahead on a morning boat with a specific mission: to purchase food and petrol and — most importantly — "to promise ten thousand francs to the officials concerned if we were clear of the Customs in half an hour" (001-134). This was not bribery in the pejorative sense so much as an application of Mansel's philosophy that money, properly deployed, could purchase the most precious of operational commodities: time.

## The Arrival at Dieppe

As the steamer entered Dieppe harbour, the party came on deck and spotted Bell standing with three officials on the edge of the quay (001-151). Bell pointed the party out to his companions, and one of the officials — the customs officer in question — boarded the steamer before she was fairly at rest. [[william-chandos|William Chandos]] met him with the party's papers, and since those were exactly what the official had come for, "he took them without a word" (001-151).

What followed was a model of quiet efficiency bought with hard cash. The official took the papers ashore while the cars were being unloaded. Almost immediately, and in defiance of normal port procedure — which would have prioritised the registered baggage of the waiting boat-train — the cars were lifted off first. Mansel's [[rolls-royce|Rolls-Royce]] was ashore before anyone else (001-156). Moments later, the official came running out of his office with the documents properly stamped and signed, glanced at the number-plates, handed back the wallet, and raised his hat (001-156).

## The Price of Speed

The entire clearance was accomplished in under fifteen minutes — half the thirty-minute target Mansel had set. Bell had cleverly structured the incentive: five thousand francs if the job was done in half an hour, doubled to ten thousand if done in half that time (001-159). "Such is the power of money," Chandos observes dryly (001-155).

The customs officer himself is given no dialogue, no physical description, and no nationality beyond his professional function. He is simply "the man who had taken our papers" (001-153) and "the man who had taken our papers" (001-156). Yet his role is emblematic: he is the first of many functionaries — Austrian constables, German border guards, local officials — whom Mansel's party would either circumvent, bribe, or simply avoid during their extra-legal campaign across Central Europe.

## Significance

The customs officer episode serves several narrative functions. First, it establishes the operational tempo: the party's progress across Europe is a matter of life and death, and every delay — even a bureaucratic one — is a threat. Second, it demonstrates the resourcefulness of the servants, particularly Bell, whose advance work at Dieppe was as essential as any armed action later in the story. Third, it introduces the recurring motif of officialdom as an obstacle to be managed rather than an authority to be obeyed — a theme that runs throughout the novel, from Mansel's refusal to involve the Austrian police (002-073) to the party's careful avoidance of "official notice of the matter" (001-136).

The customs officer, like [[fitch|Fitch]], is a minor figure whose significance far exceeds his page-time. Without his efficient — and well-rewarded — processing of the party's papers, the twenty-four-hour dash from Dieppe to [[carinthia|Carinthia]] might have been lost to bureaucratic delay, and the slender window for recovering Adèle might have closed forever.
