# The Cliff Descent Plan

The cliff descent was [[jonathan-mansel|Jonathan Mansel]]'s contingency plan for extracting [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]] from [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]] if the rescue operation went awry. This plan, revealed through [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]]'s account after his rescue from the castle kitchen, demonstrates Mansel's characteristic foresight in preparing for the worst-case scenario.

## Context

The plan was established during the broader [[castle-of-gath-operation|Castle of Gath operation]], which involved Mansel and his party infiltrating the castle to rescue [[adele-pleydell|Adele]] from [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]. The southwest tower, where Adele was confined, stood atop a formidable cliff. As [[george-hanbury|George Hanbury]] reported to [[william-chandos|Chandos]]: "When the car came out of the castle, I was down by the beechwoods at the foot of the cliff. Rowley was with me, but Carson and Bell and Tester were on guard, within sound of the drive" (007-052).

## The Plan

Mansel conceived that if the rescue attempt failed and the party found themselves trapped or pursued, Adele could be lowered down the cliff face using ropes. The foot of the cliff was adjacent to [[the-beechwoods|the beechwoods]], a wooded area that provided cover for the servants positioned there.

The plan was not merely theoretical. Hanbury was stationed at the foot of the cliff precisely "according to plan" (007-060), with the specific instruction that "one of the cars must now leave for the foot of the cliff *and stay there perhaps till dawn*" (007-060). This arrangement meant that if Adele were lowered down the cliff, a vehicle would be waiting to spirit her away to safety.

> At last it began to grow dark, and I had to face two fresh facts. The first was this. According to plan, one of the cars must now leave for the foot of the cliff *and stay there perhaps till dawn*. That was awkward enough, but the second was worse. It was a yard of pearls to a bootlace that Punter and Casemate and Bunch would try to get home that night.
>
> (007-060)

## The Attack That Disrupted Everything

The plan was fatally disrupted on the night of the rescue. Hanbury, who had decided to go alone to the foot of the cliff—"I meant to take Tester, but at the last I forgot" (007-061)—found the rope gone, assuming Mansel had pulled it up. At ten o'clock, something fell near where he stood. "As I bent over it, somebody laid me out" (007-061).

Hanbury was bundled into the back of the [[rolls-royce|Rolls]] and hauled through a ground-floor window into the Castle of Gath (007-063). The servants, hearing the Rolls approach, assumed it was Hanbury returning, and "before they'd scented the trouble, the fat was burnt" (007-064).

Meanwhile, [[punter|Punter]], [[bunch|Bunch]], and [[casemate|Casemate]] had returned to the castle by car, and one of their first acts was to remove the rope from the cliff—the very means by which Adele could have been lowered. They also resisted the powerful temptation to take the Rolls inside the gate, a decision Chandos recognized as masterful strategy: "though their car had been ruined and the Rolls was at their gate, the temptation to take her inside had been withstood" (007-066).

## Unrealised Potential

The cliff descent plan, though never executed, reveals the thoroughness of Mansel's operational thinking. He had anticipated the possibility of failure and prepared accordingly, positioning personnel, transport, and equipment at the critical point. The plan failed only because [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]]'s foresight exceeded even Mansel's expectations—the enemy had not only captured Hanbury but had also removed the rope, sealing off the cliff as an escape route.

The irony is that [[bell|Bell]] and Hanbury had visited the beechwoods earlier that day: "Bell and I visited the beechwoods at three. I hated leaving the spur, but it didn't seem prudent for one to go out alone. We saw no one, and, when we got back, Carson had nothing to report" (007-059). The reconnaissance confirmed the area was clear, but the subsequent night attack rendered the entire cliff-descent contingency moot.

## Strategic Significance

The cliff descent plan exemplifies the strategic character of the [[castle-of-gath-operation|Castle of Gath operation]] as a whole: meticulously planned, with multiple contingencies, but ultimately trumped by Rose Noble's anticipatory cunning. The episode demonstrates why Mansel regarded Rose Noble as the most formidable adversary he had faced—a man whose "foresight was a fearsome thing" (007-066), capable of anticipating and neutralizing Mansel's contingencies before they could be brought to bear.

## Connection to the Rescue

The cliff descent plan was intimately linked with other elements of the operation. The servants' disposition—"Rowley was with me, but Carson and Bell and Tester were on guard, within sound of the drive" (007-052)—shows a layered defensive arrangement with the cliff foot as the innermost position. The motorcar stationed there was critical: if Adele were lowered successfully, immediate transport to safety at [[poganec|Poganec]] was essential.

The failure of the plan also created the conditions for Hanbury's capture and the subsequent crisis that nearly doomed the entire operation. Had Hanbury not been ambushed, the party would have retained a forward presence at the cliff foot, and the communication between the castle and the outside world would have remained intact. Instead, Hanbury's disappearance left Mansel and Chandos cut off and ignorant of the fate of their support.

The plan's failure thus cascaded: without Hanbury, the rope was removed; without the rope, Adele could not be lowered; without the cliff route, the only remaining option was the direct assault through the castle that culminated in the desperate ruse of Mansel's feigned fall and the final confrontation in the wood. The cliff descent was the road not taken—and its closure forced every subsequent action along a narrower and more dangerous path.
