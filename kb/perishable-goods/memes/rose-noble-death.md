# Rose Noble's Death

The death of [[rose-noble|Rose Noble]] is the climactic moment of Chapter VIII of *Perishable Goods*, bringing to an end the central antagonist of the [[castle-of-gath-operation|Castle of Gath operation]]. Shot by [[jonathan-mansel|Mansel]] in the wooded dell near the castle, Rose Noble's death was as sudden as it was decisive, collapsing his criminal enterprise and freeing his surviving henchmen into bewildered captivity.

## The Setting

The confrontation took place in a small hollow concealed within the wood that surrounded [[castle-of-gath|the Castle of Gath]]. Rose Noble, accompanied by [[punter|Punter]] and [[bunch|Bunch]], had taken up position there after retreating from the castle with [[william-chandos|Chandos]] as his prisoner. The hollow was cunningly chosen: "We were now on the fringe of the wood, and I could see the castle between the leaves: the drive was ten paces away and the spur but three, but, while we commanded both, a man must have stumbled upon us before he knew we were there" (008-186).

For hours, Rose Noble and his men waited in ambush, confident in their tactical position. Rose Noble believed he had Mansel trapped by circumstance—Chandos would have to be rescued, and the wood gave the concealed party every advantage. As he declared to his men: "He gave me this wood for the taking—just the kind of damfool error a squirt like Mansel would make. The poor trash couldn't see that, if ever it got to a dogfight, *the --- that had this thicket was bound to win*" (008-244).

## The Moment

The arrogance of the ambushers proved their undoing. As they debated whether to spread their positions and Rose Noble dismissed the suggestion—"What the hell's the use of spreading before they show up? Or even then?" (008-259)—Mansel had already arrived behind them.

The fatal exchange was brief. Bunch asked: "Rose, are you sure we'd better not spread? You know. Just in case—" Rose Noble sat up and answered dismissively. "None whatever," said Mansel (008-260).

> Then a shot was fired just behind me, and Rose Noble fell back, staring, with the blood running into his eyes.
>
> (008-261)

The shot was instantly fatal. Chandos records: "Rose Noble was stone dead, shot through the brain" (008-262). The man who had terrorized his subordinates, outmanoeuvred his enemies, and held [[adele-pleydell|Adele Pleydell]] for ransom was dead before he could react.

## The Burial

After the capture of Punter and Bunch and the tending of Chandos's wounds, Mansel gave the matter of Rose Noble's remains to [[carson|Carson]] and [[bell|Bell]]. "Carson and Bell are digging a certain grave, and I think we'll leave them to it—and him to them" (008-287).

Mansel chose not to witness the burial, expressing a complex mixture of feeling toward the fallen enemy: "To tell you the truth, I don't want to see him again. The sight of him rouses feelings that one shouldn't have against the dead" (008-287). His reluctance was not born of squeamishness but of a recognition that even in death, Rose Noble provoked a visceral reaction that Mansel considered unworthy.

## Mansel's Valediction

In the aftermath, Mansel offered what amounts to a eulogy for his enemy—a remarkable tribute from the man who had been hunted, wounded, and nearly broken by Rose Noble's machinations. "In his way, he was a great man, and, if he'd had the help I've had, he'd have wiped me off the map. He hadn't a servant worth having—they let him down right and left: he practically stood alone: and, even so, it took six of us all we knew to bring him down" (008-287).

Mansel's assessment identifies Rose Noble's fatal weakness: his subordinates. [[jute|Jute]] had been driven off or killed; [[casemate|Casemate]] was unreliable and openly nervous about the enterprise; Punter and Bunch, while loyal, lacked initiative. Against the coordinated efforts of six dedicated men—Mansel, Chandos, Hanbury, Carson, Bell, and Rowley—Rose Noble "practically stood alone."

Chandos, for his part, declined to pass judgment on Mansel's eulogy: "It is not for me to review that valediction: I heard it in silence, and in silence I leave it now. The quarrel was not mine, but Mansel's, and I will not pick over the blossoms he chose to lay upon the grave" (008-288).

## Significance

The death of Rose Noble brought the [[castle-of-gath-operation|Castle of Gath operation]] to its military conclusion. With the mastermind dead and Punter and Bunch captured, the rescue of Adele was complete. However, the danger was not entirely past—[[casemate|Casemate]] still lay stunned in the castle, and his recovery would shortly produce the novel's most devastating turn. Rose Noble's death was the victory; but the cost of that victory had yet to be fully reckoned.
