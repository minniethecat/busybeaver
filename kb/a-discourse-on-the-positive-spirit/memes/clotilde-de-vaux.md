**Clotilde de Vaux** (1815–1846) was a French writer and intellectual, celebrated in Auguste Comte's *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* as the embodiment of feminine virtue and a profound influence on the moral development of Positivism.

## Biography

Clotilde de Vaux was born in 1815 in France. She was gifted equally in mind and heart, and had already begun to feel the power of the new philosophy to raise feminine influence from the decline into which it had fallen under the revolutionary influences of modern times. Misunderstood everywhere, even by her own family, her nature was far too noble for bitterness. Her sorrows were as exceptional as they were undeserved; but her purity preserved her unscathed from all sophistical attacks on marriage, even before the true theory of marriage had come before her. (005-133)

In the only writing she published, she expressed herself characteristically on the subject of Woman's vocation: "Surely the true sphere of Woman is to provide Man with the comforts and delights of home, receiving in exchange from him the means of subsistence earned by his labours." She showed a marked antipathy to the subversive ideas prevalent in her day, and had intended to refute the attacks upon marriage contained in the works of [[george-sand]].

## Connection to Positivism

Comte describes her as "my Saint Clotilde," fully conscious of the moral value of Positivism, though she had only one year to give to its study. A few months before her death, she wrote to Comte: "If I were a man, I should be your enthusiastic disciple; as a woman, I can but offer you my cordial admiration." (005-134)

She described the intellectual anarchy of their time with the charming simile: "We are all standing as yet with one foot in the air over the threshold of truth." Her nature was of rare endowment, moved by noble impulse, and yet allowing its due influence to reason. She wrote to Comte: "No one knows better than myself how weak our nature is unless it has some lofty aim beyond the reach of passion." (005-133)

> "Our race is one which must have duties, in order to form its feelings." (005-133)

## Legacy

Clotilde de Vaux died in 1846 at the age of thirty-one. Her influence on Comte was profound — she became the living symbol of feminine moral superiority in his system, and her memory was immortalized in the Positivist [Calendar of Great Men]. Comte's devotion to her memory shaped the central role that Woman and [Feeling] occupy in the Religion of Humanity. See *Testament d'Auguste Comte*, p. 550. (006-007)
