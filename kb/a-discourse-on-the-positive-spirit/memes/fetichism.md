## Overview

**Fetichism** (also spelled Fetishism) is, in [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s evolutionary theory of human intellectual development, the most primitive phase of the [[theological-stage]]. It is characterized by the belief that individual material objects — trees, stones, rivers, animals — are themselves animate beings endowed with will and feeling, without any abstraction to invisible deities.

## Key Principles

Fetichism represents the starting point of all human intellectual development. In this phase, the mind explains natural phenomena by directly attributing life and volition to the objects themselves. Unlike the later phases of [[polytheism]] and [[monotheism]], fetichism involves no abstraction whatsoever — the object is the god, and its observed behavior is its own will (004-086).

Comte regards fetichism as an inevitable and necessary first step. The human mind could not have leaped directly to abstract theological conceptions; it required the concrete, tangible experience of fetichistic thinking as a foundation. The child, Comte argues, recapitulates this intellectual development, beginning with a fetichistic relationship to toys and familiar objects before progressing to more abstract forms of thought (004-088).

Despite its primitiveness, Comte identifies in fetichism certain enduring features that persist in more developed forms. Even in the positivist state, the emotional attachment to particular objects, places, and beings retains something of the fetichistic mode. Comte sees this as evidence that fetichism, while intellectually superseded, captures a genuine dimension of human experience — the affective relationship to the concrete and particular (002-068).

## Historical Context

In Comte's grand historical narrative, fetichism dominated the earliest phase of human prehistory. From this starting point, humanity progressed through polytheism and monotheism before reaching the [[metaphysics|metaphysical stage]] and, ultimately, the positive stage. Comte insists that each phase was necessary and that fetichism, far from being a mere error, was an indispensable stage in the education of the human mind (007-047).

The historical remnants of fetichism persist in what Comte calls "survivals" — practices, beliefs, and emotional dispositions that retain the structure of fetichistic thinking even in more advanced civilizations. Comte sees these survivals not as pathologies to be eliminated but as evidence of the continuity of human nature across historical stages (007-155).
