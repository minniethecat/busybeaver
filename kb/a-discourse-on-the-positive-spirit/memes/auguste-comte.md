# Auguste Comte

**Isidore Marie Auguste François Xavier Comte** (19 January 1798 – 5 September 1857) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and writer who formulated the doctrine of [[positivism]]. He is often regarded as the first philosopher of science in the modern sense and is the founder of the discipline of [[sociology]].

Comte's major works include the *Course of [[positive-philosophy|Positive Philosophy]]* (*Cours de Philosophie Positive*, 1830–1842) and the *System of Positive Polity* (*Système de Politique Positive*, 1851–1854). His ideas influenced 19th-century thought and laid the groundwork for modern social science.

## Major Works

Comte's most significant works reflect his ambitious intellectual program. The *Course of Positive Philosophy* (1830–1842) established the [[positivism|positivist]] framework and the law of the three stages. (P01-002)

His later *System of Positive Polity* (1851–1854) attempted to apply positivist principles to the reorganization of society. Comte himself regarded it as his principal achievement. (P01-007)

## Legacy

Comte's influence extended across philosophy, [[sociology]], and political thought. His law of the three stages became foundational in the [[scientific-spirit|scientific]] study of society. His coinage of terms such as "[[altruism]]" and "[[sociology]]" permanently enriched the modern intellectual vocabulary. (P01-005)
