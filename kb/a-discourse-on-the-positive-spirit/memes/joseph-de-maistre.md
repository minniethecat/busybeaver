## Biography

Joseph de Maistre (1753–1821) was a Savoyard philosopher, writer, and diplomat. A leading voice of the Counter-Enlightenment and the conservative reaction to the French Revolution, de Maistre argued for the restoration of hereditary monarchy and the authority of the Pope. His major works include *Considerations on France* (1797) and *The Pope* (1819). He served as ambassador to Russia from 1803 to 1817.

## De Maistre in Comte's Philosophy

[[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]] discussed Joseph de Maistre in *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* as an important figure in the intellectual reaction that followed the French Revolution, crediting his school with rendering a necessary service to the development of [[positivism|Positivism]] despite its retrograde intentions.

Comte situated de Maistre within the post-revolutionary intellectual landscape, observing that "the philosophical reaction, organized at the beginning of our century by the great De Maistre, was of material assistance in preparing the true theory of [[progress|Progress]]" (003-011). He acknowledged that de Maistre's school "was of brief duration, and it was no doubt animated by a retrograde spirit; but it will always be ranked among the necessary antecedents of the Positive system" (003-011). For Comte, de Maistre's historical contribution lay in re-establishing an appreciation for the Middle Ages, which the revolutionary spirit had obscured — a necessary corrective without which the full trajectory of human progress could not be understood.

In a later passage, Comte returned to de Maistre in the context of the post-revolutionary reaction. He described how "the historical and doctrinal evidence brought forward by De Maistre and his school, of the social inutility of modern metaphysics" (003-020) constituted one of the only permanent results of the entire reactionary period. Comte paired this contribution with the contemporary advances of Cabanis and Gall in extending the Positive method to biology, suggesting that de Maistre's critique of metaphysics complemented the constructive scientific work.

Comte went so far as to claim that [[positivism|Positivism]] had appreciated [[catholicism|Catholicism]] more fully than its own defenders, "not even excepting De Maistre himself" (003-049). He noted that "some of the more candid organs of the retrograde school have allowed" this, positioning his own philosophy as having absorbed and surpassed de Maistre's insights about [[social-statics|social order]].

Thus for Comte, de Maistre represented a paradoxical figure: a thinker whose aims were reactionary but whose work inadvertently advanced the intellectual conditions for [[positivism|Positivism]] by demonstrating the bankruptcy of [[metaphysics|metaphysical philosophy]] and by recovering an appreciation for the organic [[social-statics|social order]] of the Middle Ages.
