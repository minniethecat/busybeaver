**Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** (28 August 1749 – 22 March 1832) was a German poet, playwright, novelist, and scientist, widely regarded as the greatest figure of German literature.

## Biography

Born in Frankfurt, Goethe studied law before achieving early fame with *The Sorrows of Young Werther* (1774). His masterpiece *Faust* (Part I, 1808; Part II, 1832) is considered one of the pinnacles of world literature. Beyond literature, Goethe made contributions to morphology, colour theory, and geology. He spent most of his career in Weimar, where he served as a statesman and director of the court theatre.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte invokes Goethe in three passages of *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, each illustrating the positivist thesis that scientific and poetic genius are not organically distinct.

In Chapter 6, Comte pairs Byron and Goethe as examples of modern poetry that synthesizes the esthetic spirit with profound insight into the human condition:

> The most striking examples of it are the works of Byron and Goethe, the principle value of which consists in their having blended satire with elegy. (006-049)

In the same chapter, Comte deploys Goethe to support his argument that scientific and poetic faculties differ only in direction, not in kind:

> Diderot would no doubt have been a great poet in a time more favourable to art; and Goethe, under different political influences, might have been an eminent philosopher. (006-074)

In Chapter 7, Comte returns to Goethe's prophetic insight, asserting that the poet anticipated the oppressive weight of modern social organization:

> The danger of allowing the individual to be so completely subordinated to society as to oppress him, was foreseen by Goethe, and still more clearly by Byron. (007-041)

Through these references, Comte positions Goethe as evidence of the underlying unity of human intellectual faculties — a central tenet of positivist philosophy.
