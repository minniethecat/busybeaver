**Paul** (c. 5–67 CE), also known as Saint Paul or Paul the Apostle, was an early Christian missionary and theologian. In Comte's positivist framework, Paul holds a singular historical significance as the effective founder of the Christian religion, elevated above the position traditionally assigned to him by [[theology]] itself.

## Biography

Paul was a Roman citizen of Jewish origin who, after a conversion experience, became the most influential early Christian missionary. His epistles constitute a substantial portion of the New Testament and laid the doctrinal foundations for what would become Christianity (003-079).

## Connection to Positivism

Comte's assessment of Paul is distinctive and elevated. [[positivism|Positivism]] gives Paul "a still higher place than has been given him by Theology; for it looks upon him as historically the founder of the religion which bears the inappropriate name of Christianity" (003-079). This is a striking claim — Comte argues that the historical role of Paul in establishing the religious system that would dominate the Middle Ages is more significant than theological accounts acknowledge, precisely because [[theology]] obscures the human, historical origins of its own foundations.

Comte goes further by proposing that Paul be celebrated as one of the three greatest predecessors of Western civilization, alongside Caesar and [[charlemagne|Charlemagne]]. These three figures "are respectively the highest types of Greco-Roman civilization, of Mediaeval Feudalism, and of [[catholicism|Catholicism]], which forms the link between the two periods" (003-079). Paul, in this schema, represents the Catholic-Mediaeval synthesis.

A second reference to Paul in Comte's work illustrates a different dimension: the relationship between moral merit and official rank. Comte cites Paul recognizing the official superiority of [[st-peter|Saint Peter]], "of whose moral and mental inferiority to himself he must have been well aware" (007-070). This example demonstrates how the abstract order of merit can coexist with the concrete order of rank without subversive consequences — a principle Comte considered essential for social harmony.

## Contributions

Paul's primary historical contribution, in Comte's view, was the founding of the institutional structure of Christianity. His missionary journeys and epistolary writings transformed a regional Jewish movement into a universal religion that would structure European society for over a millennium (003-079).

Paul's deference to Peter's official authority, despite recognizing his own moral and intellectual superiority, also provided Comte with a model of how individual merit can be acknowledged without undermining institutional hierarchy — a key positivist principle of social organization (007-070).
