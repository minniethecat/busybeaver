**St Augustine of Hippo** (354–430) was a Roman African theologian, philosopher, and Doctor of the Church, referenced by Auguste Comte in the *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* as inaugurating a tradition of nobler spirits within Catholicism who recognized that Prayer transcends self-interested petition.

## Connection to Positivism

In his discussion of the Positivist understanding of Prayer, Comte invokes St Augustine as the historical origin of a more elevated conception of the practice. He observes that it is "a common but very palpable error to imagine that Prayer is inseparable from the chimerical motives of self-interest in which it first originated." He then traces a lineage of reform within Catholicism itself: (005-xxx)

> "From St. Augustine downwards, all the nobler spirits have felt more and more strongly, notwithstanding the self-absorbing tendencies of Christian doctrine, that Prayer did not necessarily imply petition." (005-xxx)

Comte argues that Positivism completes this trajectory by founding Prayer on a truer principle, where its moral efficacy is "no longer impaired by thoughts of personal recompense." Prayer becomes "simply a solemn out-pouring, whether in private or in public, of men's nobler feelings, inspiring them with larger and more comprehensive thoughts." St Augustine thus serves as a bridge figure, demonstrating that even within the theological framework, a movement toward the Positivist understanding was already underway.
