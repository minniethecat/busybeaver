# Charlemagne

**Charlemagne** (Charles the Great, 2 April 742 – 28 January 814) was King of the Franks from 768 and Emperor of the West from 800. In [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, Charlemagne appears as a pivotal figure in the emergence of medieval civilization — the historical period that Comte regarded as foundational to the development of the [[positive-spirit|Positive spirit]].

Comte's references to Charlemagne span multiple dimensions of his historical analysis: political, social, and cultural.

## Political and Civilizational Role

Comte invokes Charlemagne repeatedly as a symbol of the civilizing power that shaped Western Europe. He credits Charlemagne's reign with establishing France as a leading force in European civilization:

> France has always been the centre of European progress, especially from the time of Charlemagne, France has always been the centre of civilization.
> (003-042)

This assertion reflects Comte's broader view that modern European civilization emerged from the medieval synthesis achieved under Frankish rule, with Charlemagne's empire providing the political framework within which the Catholic-feudal system could develop.

> In spite of national differences, the incomparable genius of Charlemagne created the first great European synthesis.
> (003-037)

## Charlemagne in Comte's Historical Typology

Comte places Charlemagne within a select group of historical figures who, across different epochs, embodied the highest type of human achievement:

> His predecessors, Caesar, St. Paul and Charlemagne, who are respectively the highest types of Roman, medieval, and modern civilization.
> (003-079)

This passage is remarkable for its tripartite historical schema: Caesar represents classical antiquity, St. [[paul|Paul]] represents the spiritual foundation of the Middle Ages, and Charlemagne represents the political synthesis of medieval Europe. In Comte's view, these three figures together constitute the lineage from which modern civilization descends.

| Historical Epoch | Representative Figure | Contribution |
|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| Classical Antiquity | Caesar | Political unification of the Mediterranean world |
| Early Christianity | St. [[paul|Paul]] | Spiritual foundation of European civilization |
| Medieval Synthesis | Charlemagne | Political and cultural integration of Western Europe |

## The Symbolic Function

In a striking passage, Comte uses Charlemagne as the image of secular governance within the positivist system:

> The temporal power would be the image of the immortal Charlemagne, the founder of the Western Republic.
> (007-122)

This reference appears in the context of Comte's vision for the future organization of society. The positivist temporal power — responsible for material and practical governance — is to be modeled on Charlemagne's role as the political founder of Western Christendom. The "Western Republic" Comte envisions is not a return to medieval monarchy but a rational reorganization of society in which temporal authority is clearly distinguished from spiritual (intellectual and moral) authority.

## Connection to Positivism

Charlemagne's significance for Comte lies in his embodiment of the principle of [[order]] — the necessary complement to [[progress]]. The medieval system that Charlemagne helped establish provided the stable framework within which the intellectual developments leading to [[positivism]] could eventually unfold.

The separation of temporal and spiritual powers — for Comte, the great achievement of the medieval period — finds its archetype in Charlemagne's relationship with the Papacy. This separation would become a central principle of Comte's own positivist polity, in which the power of material regulation (temporal) is distinguished from the power of intellectual and moral guidance (spiritual).
