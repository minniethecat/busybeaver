## Overview

**Monotheism** is, in Comte's evolutionary classification, the third and culminating phase of the [[theological-stage]], succeeding [[polytheism]] and representing the highest form of theological abstraction before the emergence of [[metaphysical-stage|metaphysical]] and ultimately [[positive-stage|positive]] thinking. It unifies all divine agency into a single supreme being.

## Key Principles

Monotheism achieves the maximum intellectual consolidation possible within theological thinking. By reducing all divine causality to a single will, it provides a unified explanatory framework that polytheism, with its distributed pantheon, could not offer. This unification prepared the human mind for the even higher abstraction of a single natural law governing all phenomena — the central insight of [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] (002-078).

However, Comte offers a nuanced and often critical assessment of monotheism. While acknowledging its intellectual achievement, he identifies several regressive tendencies. Monotheism concentrates [[spiritual-power|spiritual authority]] more intensely than polytheism, tending toward theocratic despotism in which a single priestly class claims to interpret the single divine will (003-049). It also discourages intellectual independence, since deviation from orthodox monotheistic doctrine is framed as rebellion against the one true God.

The aesthetic consequences of monotheism are similarly ambivalent. Comte observes that monotheism, particularly in its medieval Christian form, suppressed the imaginative arts that had flourished under polytheism, redirecting creative energy toward otherworldly contemplation (006-047). Yet monotheism also purified moral sentiment, elevating love and devotion above the transactional relationships with deities that characterized polytheistic religion.

> Comte on the rise of the Positive spirit amid the decline of monotheistic theology:
>
> For the charge of Optimism there is even less ground than for that of Fatalism. The latter was, to a certain extent, connected with the rise of the Positive spirit.
>
> (002-078)

> Comte on Positive religion and the preponderance of Social feeling over Self-love:
>
> Positive religion brings before us in a definite shape the noblest of human problems, the permanent preponderance of Social feeling over Self-love.
>
> (007-152)

## Historical Context

Comte locates the dominance of monotheism in the medieval and early modern periods of Western history. Medieval [[catholicism|Catholicism]] represents, in his system, the most fully developed form of monotheism — a system that achieved a partial but imperfect separation of spiritual and temporal powers while organizing European society around a shared moral framework (007-067).

The corruption and eventual decomposition of monotheism, particularly through Protestantism's fragmentation of [[spiritual-power|spiritual authority]], created the conditions for the emergence of metaphysical and ultimately positive philosophy. Comte regards monotheism not as a permanent achievement but as a necessary transitional phase whose historical function is now exhausted (007-152).
