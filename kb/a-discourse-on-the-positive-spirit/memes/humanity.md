## Overview

**Humanity** (le Grand Être, the Great Being) is the central object of veneration in [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s Religion of Humanity. It designates the continuous collective existence of the human species across generations—not all human beings, but those who have contributed positively to moral and intellectual progress and whose influence continues through tradition and memory (001-003). Comte placed "the primary functions of humanity" at the summit of his philosophical system, making Humanity both the subject and object of positivist worship (001-010).

## Key Principles

**A collective being.** Humanity is not an abstraction but a real collective existence constituted by the ongoing contributions of individuals across time. Each generation inherits the accumulated intellectual and moral capital of its predecessors and adds to it—or fails to. Comte's conception gives ontological weight to the social bond that unites successive generations.


> Comte on the concept of Humanity in its normal state, dimly foreseen by Bacon:
>
> Now the investigation of the economy of nature here contemplated is evidently of the abstract kind. We decompose that economy into its primary phenomena, that is to say, into those which are not reducible to others. These we range in classes, each of which, notwithstanding the connexion that exists between all, requires a separate inductive process; for the existence of laws cannot be proved in any one of them by pure deduction.
>
> (002-056)


> Comte on preparing the advanced portion of humanity for the acceptance of true philosophy:
>
> Positivism consists essentially of a Philosophy and a Polity. These can never be dissevered; the former being the basis, and the latter the end of one comprehensive system, in which our intellectual faculties and our social sympathies are brought into close correlation with each other.
>
> (001-003)

**Selective inclusion.** Not every human being belongs to Humanity in Comte's sense. Only those who have contributed to progress—through scientific discovery, moral example, artistic creation, or social service—are incorporated into the Great Being. Those who live purely selfish lives are excluded from this collective immortality.

**Subjective immortality.** Comte's [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] replaces theological immortality of the soul with subjective immortality in the memory and gratitude of posterity. The dead live on through their contributions to Humanity, mediated by the priesthood's custodianship of tradition and commemoration (007-042).

**Object of worship.** The Religion of Humanity redirects religious sentiment from supernatural beings to the collective human achievement. Comte organized a complete positivist liturgy—festivals, sacraments, prayers—centered on the veneration of Humanity as the highest reality.

## Historical Context

Comte's concept of Humanity evolved throughout his career. In the *Course of [[positive-philosophy|Positive Philosophy]]* (1830–1842), Humanity appears primarily as the object of sociological study. In the later *System of Positive Polity* (1851–1854), Humanity becomes the central religious concept—the Great Being to whom positivist worship is directed. This evolution reflects Comte's broader shift from scientific philosophy to the founding of a new religion.

The "normal state of humanity" that Comte glimpsed in [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s foresight was one in which [[positive-science|scientific knowledge]] serves moral and social ends rather than merely technical ones (002-056).
