**Jacques-Bénigne Bossuet** (27 September 1627 – 12 April 1704) was a French bishop, theologian, and renowned orator, celebrated as one of the greatest pulpit orators in French history.

## Biography

Born in Dijon, France, Bossuet was educated by the Jesuits and ordained a priest in 1652. He served as Bishop of Meaux from 1681 until his death. His most famous works include *Discours sur l'histoire universelle* (1681) and *Politique tirée des propres paroles de l'Écriture sainte* (published posthumously). He was a staunch defender of Gallicanism and the divine right of kings.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte invokes Bossuet in Chapter 6 of *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* during his argument that scientific and poetic genius are not organically distinct but differ only in the direction of their combinations of thought. After noting Diderot's unrealized poetic potential, Comte observes: "Bossuet is even a more striking instance of a mind equally capable of the deepest philosophy and of the sublimest poetry, had the circumstances of his life given him a more definite impulse in either direction" (006-074). For Comte, Bossuet represents the synthetic capacity of the human mind — capable of both abstract philosophical reasoning and concrete esthetic expression — supporting the positivist thesis that these faculties differ in application, not in essence.
