## Overview

**Human Knowledge** in [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s positivist philosophy refers to the complete system of verified knowledge acquired by humanity through the [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]]. Comte held that all genuine knowledge must be organized into a coherent hierarchy, forming "the entire system of human knowledge" (004-082).

This systematic organization of knowledge was essential to Comte's project: only when the sciences were properly classified could their educational and social function be realized.

## Key Principles

Comte's [[classification-of-sciences]] organizes human knowledge into a hierarchy from the most general and abstract sciences (mathematics, astronomy) to the most complex and concrete (sociology, morals). Each science depends on the laws of those below it and adds new principles specific to its own phenomena.

All human knowledge, in Comte's view, must pass through the [[law-of-three-states]]: theological fictions, metaphysical abstractions, and finally positive laws. Genuine knowledge is positive knowledge — confined to observable phenomena and their invariable relations.

Comte insisted that "the entire system of human knowledge" (004-082) forms a unity. There are not separate realms of scientific and non-[[positive-science|scientific knowledge]]; there is only knowledge at different stages of development and different levels of generality.

## Historical Context

Comte wrote at a time when the sciences were increasingly specialized and fragmented. His call for systematic unity responded to the growing [[specialization|division of intellectual labor]], which he saw as necessary but potentially disorienting without a synthesizing philosophy.

The classification of human knowledge was not merely an academic exercise but a practical necessity for educational reform. Comte believed that teaching the sciences in their logical order would allow each individual to recapitulate the [[mental-evolution|intellectual evolution]] of the species.
