## Overview

The **Classification of Sciences** is [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s systematic arrangement of the fundamental sciences into a hierarchical order: mathematics, astronomy, physics, chemistry, biology, and sociology. This classification is not merely a convenience of organization; it reflects the objective logical and historical dependencies among the sciences. Each science in the series depends on the laws of those preceding it while introducing new phenomena irreducible to lower levels (002-045). Comte regarded this classification as one of his most important contributions to the philosophy of science.

## Key Principles

**Decreasing generality, increasing complexity.** The sciences are arranged from the most general and simplest (mathematics) to the most particular and complex (sociology). This ordering reflects both the logical dependence relations among the sciences and the historical order in which they achieved positivity (006-036).

**Logical dependence.** Each science presupposes the laws established by the sciences below it in the hierarchy. Biology cannot proceed without chemistry; sociology cannot proceed without biology (002-061). The classification thus provides a curriculum for scientific education: one must master each level before ascending to the next.

**Method unity.** Despite the diversity of phenomena, the same philosophical principle underlies the classification across all sciences. Comte saw the classification as itself a demonstration of the unity of the positive method (002-045). As he noted, "the philosophical principle of this analysis is precisely the same" regardless of the science to which it is applied.

**Culmination in sociology.** The classification culminates in sociology—the most complex and most recently developed science. Comte's innovation was to establish sociology as a genuine positive science, completing the hierarchy (006-033).

Comte stated the philosophical basis of his classification:

> The philosophical principle of this analysis is precisely the same as that on which I have based the Classification of the Sciences. In both cases the order followed is that of increasing generality and complexity in the phenomena.
>
> (003-092)

## Historical Context

Comte developed the classification in the *Course of [[positive-philosophy|Positive Philosophy]]* (1830–1842) as a response to the fragmented state of nineteenth-century knowledge. [[specialization|Specialization]] had produced isolated experts incapable of seeing the unity of science; the classification was meant to restore an encyclopedic perspective. It also served a political purpose: demonstrating that social phenomena are lawful and knowable, and that therefore a science of society—and a scientific politics—is possible.

Comte noted that earlier thinkers had attempted partial classifications, but he claimed originality for the systematic justification of the order and the inclusion of sociology as the terminal science (002-064).
