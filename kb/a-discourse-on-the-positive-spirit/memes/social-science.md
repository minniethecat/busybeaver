## Overview

**Social Science** (or sociology) is, in [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s positivist system, the highest and most complex of all the sciences. It is the science that applies the positive method—observation, comparison, and the search for invariable laws—to the study of collective human existence. Comte regarded the foundation of social science as the ultimate aim of his entire philosophical project, for it alone could provide the intellectual basis for ending the intellectual and moral anarchy of post-revolutionary Europe and guide the work of social regeneration.

## Key Principles

The foundation of social science rests on the prior development of all the preliminary sciences—mathematics, astronomy, physics, chemistry, and biology (003-006). Each lower science provides the methodological and conceptual basis for the one above it, and social science inherits the full apparatus of positive reasoning from this hierarchy. Without the preparatory work of the simpler sciences, the phenomena of society—the most complex, variable, and interdependent of all natural phenomena—could not be studied scientifically.

Social science is divided into two complementary branches: **social statics**, which studies the conditions of [[social-statics|social order]] and the laws of consensus, and **social dynamics**, which studies the laws of [[human-progress|social progress]] and historical development (007-035). These two branches correspond to the two fundamental aspects of every social phenomenon: its simultaneous coordination with other coexisting phenomena, and its sequential development through time.

A crucial feature of social science, when rightly understood, is its tendency to reconcile the claims of order and progress rather than to favour one at the expense of the other (003-046). By demonstrating that order is the condition of progress and that progress is the development of order, social science overcomes the sterile opposition between reactionary and revolutionary doctrines that had characterised European politics since the French Revolution.

## Historical Context

Comte introduced the term *sociology* (initially *social physics*) in his *Course of [[positive-philosophy|Positive Philosophy]]* (1830–1842), positioning it as the final science in his celebrated hierarchy. He argued that the [[scientific-spirit|positive spirit]] had progressively transformed each of the simpler sciences—mathematics first, then astronomy, physics, chemistry, and biology—and that the time had come for the same transformation to reach the study of society. The foundation of social science on the basis of the preliminary sciences constitutes, in Comte's view, the definitive intellectual achievement of the modern age (007-067). Comte's social science is closely connected with his broader program of social regeneration, for he believed that only a scientific understanding of society could indicate the normal state towards which social development tends (003-095).
