**George Sand** (1804–1876), born Amantine Lucile Aurore Dupin, was a French novelist, memoirist, and socialist. She is mentioned in Auguste Comte's *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* in connection with Clotilde de Vaux, who intellectually opposed Sand's views on marriage.

## Biography

George Sand was one of the most celebrated French novelists of the nineteenth century, known for her pastoral novels, her unconventional lifestyle, and her advocacy for women's rights and social reform. Her works include *Indiana*, *Lélia*, and *La Mare au Diable*.

## Connection to Positivism

Sand appears in the *Discourse* through the lens of Clotilde de Vaux. Comte records that de Vaux "had intended to refute the attacks upon marriage, contained in the works of George Sand, to whom she was intellectually no less than morally superior." (005-133)

> "In a large work which she did not live to finish, she had intended to refute the attacks upon marriage, contained in the works of George Sand, to whom she was intellectually no less than morally superior." (005-133)

This passage serves primarily to illustrate de Vaux's character and convictions, but it also reveals Comte's alignment with de Vaux's critique of Sand's progressive views on marriage. For Comte, de Vaux represented the true feminine ideal — one that preserved the institution of marriage while elevating Woman's moral influence — in contrast to Sand's more subversive approach.
