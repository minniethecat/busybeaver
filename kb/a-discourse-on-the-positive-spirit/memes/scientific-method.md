## Overview

The **Scientific Method**, in Comte's positivist philosophy, is the systematic procedure by which positive knowledge is acquired. It consists of observation, experimentation, and comparison—applied in an order appropriate to the complexity of the phenomena under study. Comte's distinctive contribution was to argue that the [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]] should be extended "to the complex and important phenomena of society" (007-082), giving rise to the science of sociology.

## Key Principles

Comte identified three principal modes of scientific investigation: **observation**, the direct examination of phenomena as they occur; **experimentation**, the controlled manipulation of conditions to isolate causal relations; and **comparison**, the systematic analysis of similarities and differences across cases. Each mode has greater or lesser applicability depending on the complexity of the subject matter.

Crucially, Comte rejected the notion that the [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]] could discover [[absolute-knowledge|absolute truth]]. He held that the method yields relative knowledge—knowledge of relations and laws—rather than knowledge of essences or ultimate causes. The positivist method is thus characterized by its modesty: "the [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]] rests on scientific laws" rather than on metaphysical claims about the nature of reality.

On the social and moral promise of the Positive method, Comte wrote:

> Of such immense consequence to society will it be to extend the scientific method to the complex and important phenomena of human nature. Man will no longer be the slave of man; he yields only to external Law; and to this those who demonstrate it to him are as submissive as himself.
>
> (007-082)

## Historical Context

Comte's advocacy of the [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]] for social inquiry was revolutionary in the early nineteenth century. He argued that sociology, as the most complex science, required the prior development of all other sciences—mathematics, astronomy, physics, chemistry, and biology—before its own method could be properly established. This hierarchical dependence is the basis of his [[classification-of-sciences|Classification of Sciences]].

Throughout the *Discourse*, Comte contrasts the [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]] with the "theological and metaphysical methods" that had previously dominated social and political thought. The [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]] alone, he argued, could provide the intellectual foundation for social consensus and progressive reform.
