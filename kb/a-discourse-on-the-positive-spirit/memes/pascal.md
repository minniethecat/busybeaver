**Pascal** (1623–1662) was a French mathematician, physicist, and religious philosopher. In Comte's historical schema, Pascal's thought prefigured the positivist conception of [[humanity|Humanity]] as a single collective being evolving through time.

## Biography

Blaise Pascal was born in Clermont-Ferrand, France, and demonstrated exceptional mathematical talent from an early age. He made foundational contributions to probability theory, fluid mechanics, and the philosophy of religion before his death at the age of thirty-nine.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte cites Pascal as an early precursor to the positivist conception of [[humanity|Humanity]]. In Comte's account, the idea of the [[humanity|Great Being]] — [[humanity|Humanity]] itself as a collective organism — was first distinctly anticipated in Pascal's insight that "the human race" could be conceived as "one Man" (007-033).

Comte situates Pascal's contribution within a broader historical progression: from ancient patriotism, through mediaeval universal brotherhood, to the gradual emergence of the concept of [[humanity|Humanity]]. In this trajectory, Pascal represents a key moment when the notion of humanity as a unified subject of development first arose in a distinct speculative and scientific form (007-033).

However, Comte notes that a full conception of [[humanity|Humanity]] as the basis for a new synthesis remained impossible until the crisis of the French Revolution, which both demonstrated the urgent necessity for social regeneration and gave birth to [[positivism|Positivism]] (007-033).
