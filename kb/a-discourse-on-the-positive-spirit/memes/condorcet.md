# Marquis de Condorcet

**Marie Jean Antoine Nicolas de Caritat, Marquis de Condorcet** (17 September 1743 – 29 March 1794) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and political scientist. In [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, Condorcet occupies a singular position as Comte's acknowledged predecessor — a thinker who anticipated the core tenets of [[positivism]] before the term itself existed.

Comte refers to Condorcet with a combination of reverence and critical distance, naming him no fewer than four times across the *Discourse*.

## Condorcet as Predecessor

Comte explicitly acknowledges Condorcet as his intellectual forerunner — the thinker who came closest to formulating the positivist philosophy before Comte himself:

> The view of my eminent predecessor Condorcet, that they should be taught to understand the methods of science.
> (005-101)

And elsewhere, with more personal emphasis:

> Condorcet, my principal predecessor, knew that the political future depended on the extension of the positive spirit.
> (003-025)

This acknowledgment is significant within Comte's system, which generally treats previous thinkers as having grasped partial truths. Condorcet is one of the few figures Comte credits with having approached a genuinely positivist outlook.

## The Tragic Figure

Comte's first reference to Condorcet is tinged with pathos, describing him as an "unfortunate predecessor":

> The great and unfortunate predecessor Condorcet.
> (003-012)

Condorcet's life ended in tragedy: a victim of the French Revolution he had supported, he died in prison under mysterious circumstances in 1794. Comte's description reflects his view that the revolutionary period, while necessary, consumed many of its most enlightened participants.

## The *Sketch* and the Idea of Progress

Comte's most extended engagement with Condorcet concerns the latter's most famous work, the *Sketch for a Historical Picture of the [[progress|Progress]] of the Human Mind* (1795), written while Condorcet was in hiding from the Revolutionary authorities:

> The essay of my great predecessor Condorcet on the progress of the human mind.
> (007-101)

This work, which traced human history as a narrative of intellectual and [[human-progress|social progress]] through ten epochs, is a direct ancestor of Comte's own law of three stages. Condorcet's *Sketch* demonstrated that the history of humanity could be understood as a rational progression — an idea that Comte would develop into his systematic sociology.

| Aspect | Condorcet's View | Comte's Development |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Historical method | Narrative of intellectual progress | Law of three stages (theological → metaphysical → positive) |
| Social science | Mathematics applied to society | [[sociology|Sociology]] as the queen of sciences |
| Political vision | Republican rationalism | Positivist polity with spiritual/temporal separation |

## Connection to Positivism

Condorcet's relationship to Comte's [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] is both genealogical and critical. Comte inherited from Condorcet the conviction that the [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]] could and should be applied to social phenomena, and that the history of human thought revealed a progressive development toward rationality.

However, Comte also criticized Condorcet's approach as insufficiently systematic. Condorcet's social mathematics, in Comte's view, remained bound to the metaphysical assumptions of the Enlightenment — particularly the belief in unlimited progress and the sovereignty of individual reason. Comte's [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] would replace these with a more organic conception of society and a recognition of the necessary limits of rational inquiry.

Comte's own system can be understood as the completion of the project Condorcet had begun: the extension of positive science to the study of society, grounded in historical observation rather than mathematical abstraction.
