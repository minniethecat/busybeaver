**Johannes Kepler** (27 December 1571 – 15 November 1630) was a German astronomer and mathematician, a key figure in the Scientific Revolution known for his laws of planetary motion.

## Biography

Kepler was born in Weil der Stadt, in the Holy Roman Empire. He studied at the University of Tübingen, where he was introduced to the Copernican system. He served as imperial mathematician to Emperor Rudolph II and later worked in Linz. His most celebrated contributions are the three laws of planetary motion, which described the elliptical orbits of planets and laid the groundwork for [[newton|Newton]]'s theory of universal gravitation.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte, in the introductory remarks to *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, invokes Kepler as an exemplar of the philosophical temperament that accepts slow recognition. Comte notes that philosophers have seldom had justice done them until generations have passed, citing the remark that "Kepler, they say, declared himself satisfied if he had one convert in a century" (P01-004). This allusion serves Comte's argument that the abstract and severe form of his own work should be judged with the same patience accorded to scientific pioneers.
