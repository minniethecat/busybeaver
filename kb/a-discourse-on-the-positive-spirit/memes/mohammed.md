**Mohammed** (c. 570–632 CE) was the founder of Islam and is regarded by Muslims as the final prophet of God. In Comte's positivist analysis of religious evolution, Mohammedan [[monotheism|Monotheism]] represents a significant stage in the progressive development of human belief systems.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte positioned Islam, or "Mohammedan [[monotheism|Monotheism]]," within his broader theory of religious development. He anticipated that the positivist movement would engage with Islamic societies, predicting that "the next step will be to Mohammedan [[monotheism|Monotheism]]; first in Turkey, afterwards in Persia" (007-139). Comte believed that [[positivism|Positivism]] would find points of sympathy with Islam that [[catholicism|Catholicism]] could not admit, and noted that these sympathies were already perceptible in his time.

A key element of Comte's favorable view was the historical role of Arab civilization in transmitting Greek science to the West. He acknowledged that "Arab civilization transmitted Greek science to us: and this will always secure for it an honourable place among the essential elements of the mediaeval system, regarded as a preparation for [[positivism|Positivism]]" (007-139).

In his discussion of global religious transformation, Comte also noted the influence of Mohammedan [[monotheism|Monotheism]] beyond its traditional regions, observing its spread alongside Christianity into regions dominated by [[polytheism|Polytheism]], thereby preparing the way for further intellectual development (007-142).
