**Virgil** (70–19 BCE) was a Roman poet of the Augustan period, best known for the epic poem the *Aeneid*.

## Connection to Positivism

[[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]], in his *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, mentions Virgil in passing during his discussion of the limitations of ancient epic poetry. Comte argues that [[homer|Homer]] would have preferred nobler subjects than military life, and notes that the only poetical tribute to Rome's more constructive policy of incorporation was:

> contained in a few beautiful lines of Virgil's Æneid, ending with the remarkable expression (006-045)

Comte cites Virgil's lines as a rare instance where ancient poetry captured the nobler aspect of Roman policy — the system of incorporation following conquest — rather than merely glorifying military exploits.

## Major Works

- *Aeneid* (epic poem, 29–19 BCE)
- *Georgics* (didactic poem, 29 BCE)
- *Eclogues* (pastoral poems, 42–39 BCE)
