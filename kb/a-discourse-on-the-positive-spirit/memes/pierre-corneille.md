**Pierre Corneille** (1606–1684) was a French dramatist, widely regarded as one of the founders of French classical tragedy.

## Corneille and the Positivist Theory of Art

[[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]], in his *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, elevates Corneille to the level of [[homer|Homer]] as the exemplary poet in the Western tradition. Comte uses the recurring formula "from [[homer|Homer]] to Corneille" to signify the continuity of genuine poetic genius:

> All poets of real genius, from Homer to Corneille, have always considered their work to be that of beautifying human life, and so far, of elevating it. (006-009)

## Historical Drama as Positivist Art

Comte particularly admires Corneille's Roman history dramas as precursors to the Positivist ideal of art that idealizes past phases of [[humanity|Humanity]]:

> Our great Corneille, for instance, is principally remembered for the series of dramas in which he has so admirably depicted various periods of Roman history. (006-064)

For Comte, Corneille exemplifies the spontaneous artistic impulse toward historical idealization that [[positivism|Positivism]] can systematize into a comprehensive program.

## Corneille and Racine

Comte acknowledges the critical debate between Corneille and [[jean-racine|Racine]], noting the historical preference for [[jean-racine|Racine]] while implicitly siding with those who value Corneille's moral grandeur:

> Preference is given for so long a time to Racine over Corneille. (006-029)

This observation appears in Comte's discussion of how critical judgments can be distorted by passing intellectual fashions.

## The Education of Artists

Comte returns to the Corneille-[[homer|Homer]] parallel in his argument that genuine art requires philosophical training:

> The greatest poets, from Homer to Corneille, have always participated largely in the best education of which their times admitted. (006-067)

Corneille thus serves as evidence for Comte's thesis that the Positivist program of universal education — applied equally to artists — would not diminish but rather enhance artistic achievement.

## Personal Resonance

Comte's appreciation of Corneille was evidently personal as well as theoretical. He mentions reading a translation of the *Imitation of Christ* by Corneille, suggesting familiarity with the dramatist's entire oeuvre:

> I often read a chapter of it; the beautiful translation of Corneille. (005-040)
