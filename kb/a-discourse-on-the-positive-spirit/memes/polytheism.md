## Overview

**Polytheism** is, in Comte's evolutionary schema of human intellectual development, the second phase of the [[theological-stage]], intermediate between [[fetichism]] and [[monotheism]]. It represents a significant advance in abstraction, in which natural phenomena are attributed to the wills of multiple distinct deities rather than to spirits inhabiting individual objects.

## Key Principles

Polytheism marks a crucial step in the progressive abstraction of theological explanation. While fetichism attributes life and will to individual material objects, polytheism consolidates these forces into a pantheon of gods, each governing a domain of nature or human activity. This consolidation represents the first systematic attempt to explain the world through consistent invisible agencies rather than through the direct animation of matter (004-088).

Comte notes that polytheism, particularly in its Greek and Roman forms, was more conducive to the separation of spiritual and temporal powers than the monotheism that succeeded it. Under polytheism, the priesthood could exercise moral authority without claiming direct political control, since no single divine will unified all aspects of existence (003-049). The plurality of gods encouraged a corresponding plurality of social functions and a degree of intellectual freedom absent from later monotheistic systems.

The aesthetic and imaginative dimensions of polytheism are also significant. Comte observes that polytheism stimulated the arts and provided a rich symbolic vocabulary for expressing human ideals — a capacity that monotheism, with its abstract singular deity, substantially diminished (005-051).

## Historical Context

In Comte's historical narrative, polytheism dominated the classical civilizations of Greece and Rome. It reached its highest development in these cultures before being gradually supplanted by monotheism through the rise of Christianity. Comte regards this transition as both progressive — in that monotheism achieved greater intellectual unification — and regressive — in that it concentrated [[spiritual-power|spiritual authority]] and reduced the scope for independent thought (005-090).

The decline of polytheism was, in Comte's view, an inevitable consequence of intellectual progress. As human understanding advanced, the multiplicity of gods appeared increasingly arbitrary, and the mind sought a single first cause. Yet Comte insists that polytheism was a necessary and valuable phase, without which the higher abstractions of monotheism — and ultimately of [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] — could not have been reached (004-093).
