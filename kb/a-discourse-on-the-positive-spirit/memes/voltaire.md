# Voltaire

**François-Marie Arouet**, known by his pen name **Voltaire** (21 November 1694 – 30 May 1778), was a French Enlightenment writer, historian, and philosopher. In [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, Voltaire represents the critical, destructive phase of intellectual development — a necessary but ultimately insufficient stage in the transition toward [[positivism]].

Comte's references to Voltaire are consistently critical, treating the great philosophe as the embodiment of the "metaphysical spirit" that [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] must overcome.

## Voltaire and the Revolutionary School

Comte associates Voltaire with the negative, critical tendency of the French Enlightenment — the movement that dismantled the old theological order without constructing a new positive one:

> The successors of the school of Voltaire and Danton have shown themselves incapable of constructing a new social order.
> (003-032)

For Comte, Voltaire's critical genius was effective at destruction but impotent at construction. The Enlightenment's critique of religion, monarchy, and tradition cleared the ground for a new order, but the philosophes themselves could not provide the positive doctrine that would replace what they had torn down.

## The Moral Critique

Comte's most pointed criticism of Voltaire concerns his treatment of historical figures — specifically, his disparagement of [[joan-of-arc|Joan of Arc]]:

> The disgrace of detracting, as Voltaire has done, from her character.
> (005-129)

This passage appears in the context of Comte's discussion of the moral role of women in the positivist system. Comte regarded Voltaire's satirical treatment of [[joan-of-arc|Joan of Arc]] — in the mock-epic poem *La Pucelle d'Orléans* — as an instance of the Enlightenment's failure to appreciate the moral value of religious sentiment, even when the theological framework that sustained it had been surpassed.

Comte's [[positive-philosophy|positivism]], unlike Voltaire's Enlightenment, sought not to mock religion but to understand its historical function and assimilate its moral achievements into a rational framework.

## From Criticism to Construction

Comte acknowledges the historical necessity of Voltaire's critical work:

> Under the impulse given by Voltaire, to a state of entire freedom of discussion.
> (005-137)

The freedom of thought that Voltaire championed was, in Comte's view, an essential precondition for the emergence of positive philosophy. Without the Enlightenment's liberation of the mind from theological authority, the scientific study of society could never have begun.

| Phase | Representative | Function | Comte's Assessment |
|-------|---------------|----------|-------------------|
| Critical/Destructive | Voltaire, Danton | Dismantle old order | Necessary but insufficient |
| Constructive/Positive | Comte, Condorcet | Build new synthesis | The completion of the critical phase |

## Connection to Positivism

Voltaire occupies an ambivalent position in Comte's historical schema. He belongs to the [[metaphysics|metaphysical stage]] — the transitional period between [[theology]] and positive science. His critical spirit was indispensable: without Voltaire's attacks on superstition and intolerance, the intellectual liberation required for [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] might not have occurred.

Yet Comte insists that criticism alone cannot sustain a society. The purely negative liberty that Voltaire championed — freedom *from* constraint — must be succeeded by the positive liberty of [[order]] and [[progress]]: freedom *for* rational participation in social life.

The positivist synthesis thus preserves Voltaire's commitment to intellectual freedom while embedding it within a constructive doctrine of social reorganization — a task that Voltaire himself could not perform but that his work made possible.
