**Louis XI** (1423–1483), called the Prudent, reigned as King of France from 1461 to 1483. He is referenced by Auguste Comte in the *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* as the monarch who sought the canonization of Joan of Arc, an act that Comte presents as evidence of progressive sentiment within the old order.

## Biography

Louis XI succeeded his father Charles VII and continued the consolidation of the French monarchy. Known for his diplomatic cunning and political pragmatism, he strengthened royal authority at the expense of the feudal nobility and laid the groundwork for the centralized French state that would reach its apex under [[louis-xiv]].

## Connection to Positivism

Comte mentions Louis XI in the context of his discussion of Woman's exceptional place in history. Reflecting on Joan of Arc, Comte notes that "our great king Louis XI applied very properly to the Pope for her canonization, and no objection was made to his request. Yet, practically, it was never carried into effect." (005-xxx)

> "Our great king Louis XI applied very properly to the Pope for her canonization, and no objection was made to his request." (005-xxx)

Comte observes that the canonization was gradually forgotten, and the clergy "soon came to feel a sort of dislike to her memory, which reminded them of nothing but their own social weakness." He attributes this to the inherent limitation of any absolute theological system, in which sanctioning the exception compromises the rule. Comte contrasts this with the relative character of Positivism, which "enables it to appreciate exceptional cases without weakening the rules" — and promises that Positivists will be the first to do justice to the extraordinary heroine.

Louis XI also appears in Comte's discussion of Republicanism, where the reign of Louis XIV is identified as the reference point against which Royalist reaction is measured. (003-028)
