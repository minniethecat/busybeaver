**St Bernard of Clairvaux** (1090–1153) was a French abbot, theologian, and Doctor of the Church, referenced by Auguste Comte in the *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* as a historical example of the distinction between personal merit and official rank within hierarchical institutions.

## Biography

St Bernard entered the Cistercian monastery of Cîteaux in 1113 and soon after founded the abbey of Clairvaux. He became one of the most influential churchmen of the twelfth century, preaching the Second Crusade, settling papal schisms, and authoring numerous theological treatises. He was canonized in 1174 and declared a Doctor of the Church in 1830.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte invokes St Bernard as part of his argument for distinguishing between the order of rank and the order of merit in social organization. He writes that "St Bernard was esteemed more highly than any of the Popes of his time; yet he remained in the humble position of an abbot, and never failed to show the most perfect deference for the higher functionaries of the Church." (006-xxx)

> "St Bernard was esteemed more highly than any of the Popes of his time; yet he remained in the humble position of an abbot, and never failed to show the most perfect deference for the higher functionaries of the Church." (006-xxx)

Comte pairs this example with that of [[paul|St Paul]], who recognized the official superiority of [[st-peter|St Peter]] while being aware of Peter's moral and mental inferiority. For Comte, these examples demonstrate that the abstract order of merit can coexist with the concrete order of rank without subversive consequences, and that the contrast between them is morally beneficial to all classes.
