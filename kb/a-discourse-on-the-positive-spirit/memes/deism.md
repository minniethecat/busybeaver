## Overview

**Deism** is the theological doctrine that posits a supreme being who created the universe but does not intervene in its operations through miracles or revelation. In [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s philosophy of history, deism represents a transitional phase between traditional monotheism and the fully positive (scientific) worldview.

Comte described it as "the shallow deism, which satisfies so many of our irreligious contemporaries" (004-109), viewing it as an intellectually unstable compromise that could not serve as a lasting foundation for [[social-statics|social order]].

## Key Principles

Deism retains the theological notion of a creator while abandoning the personal God of Christianity and the institutional authority of the Church. It preserves the idea of a first cause — a concept that Comte's [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] would ultimately reject as inaccessible to science.

In Comte's analysis, deism represents the final dilution of the theological spirit before its replacement by positive philosophy. Like the [[law-of-three-stages]], it illustrates the gradual weakening of supernatural explanations in favor of natural laws.

## Historical Context

Comte situated deism within the broader decline of the theological stage of intellectual development. By his time, deism had become widespread among educated Europeans who could no longer accept orthodox Christianity but were not yet prepared to embrace a fully scientific worldview.

The limitation of deism, according to Comte, was that it provided no positive organizing principle for society. While it freed individuals from ecclesiastical authority, "the shallow deism, which satisfies so many" (004-109) could not generate the moral consensus necessary for [[social-order]].

[[positivism|Positivism]], by contrast, replaces the deistic creator with [[humanity|Humanity]] as the true object of reverence and the source of moral obligation.
