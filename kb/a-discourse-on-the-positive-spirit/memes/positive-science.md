## Overview

**Positive Science** is the form of knowledge that defines the third and final stage of Comte's law of three stages.

Unlike theological knowledge, which explains phenomena by reference to [[supernatural|supernatural will]]s, and metaphysical knowledge, which appeals to abstract entities and forces, positive science restricts itself to the discovery of invariable laws through the systematic observation, classification, and correlation of phenomena.

For Comte, the establishment of positive science across all domains of knowledge — including the social realm — constitutes the defining intellectual achievement of modernity and the necessary foundation for rational social organization.

## Key Principles

**The abandonment of ultimate causes.** The defining characteristic of positive science is its renunciation of the search for first causes and ultimate explanations. Positive science does not ask why phenomena occur in any absolute sense but rather how they are related to one another through constant laws of succession and resemblance.

This methodological modesty is, in Comte's view, the source of positive science's unparalleled predictive and practical power.


> Comte explains that the Positive spirit must serve social sympathies, not pursue speculative digressions for their own sake:
>
> It will be felt, I think, that the principal difficulty of the Positive Synthesis was met by my discovery of the laws of development, if we bear in mind that while that theory completes and co-ordinates the objective basis of the system, it at the same time holds it in subordination to the subjective principle. It is under the influence of this moral principle that the whole philosophical construction should be carried on.
>
> (002-049)

**The unity of [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]].** Comte insists that all positive sciences, from astronomy to sociology, share a common method: the subordination of imagination to observation, the systematic classification of phenomena, and the formulation of increasingly general laws.

The apparent diversity of the sciences reflects differences in the complexity of their subject matter, not differences in fundamental epistemological approach.

**The hierarchy of the sciences.** Positive sciences are organized in a natural hierarchy based on the increasing complexity and decreasing generality of their phenomena.

Mathematics, as the most general and least complex science, forms the foundation; astronomy, physics, chemistry, and biology follow in order; sociology, as the most complex and least general science, crowns the hierarchy. This ordering reflects both the historical sequence in which the sciences attained positivity and the logical dependencies among them.

**The extension to social phenomena.** The decisive advance of [[positive-philosophy|positivism]] over earlier forms of positive science is its extension of [[scientific-spirit|scientific method]]s to the study of social phenomena. While the natural sciences had attained positivity by Comte's time, the study of society remained mired in theological and metaphysical modes of thought.

The creation of sociology as a positive science is thus the culminating achievement of intellectual development and the precondition for rational social reorganization.

## Historical Context

**The incomplete scientific revolution.** Comte's project of establishing positive science across all domains of knowledge responds to what he perceives as the incomplete character of the scientific revolution. While the natural sciences had successfully broken free from theological and metaphysical modes of explanation, the moral and social sciences had not.

This asymmetry produced the intellectual anarchy of the modern age, in which scientific and pre-scientific modes of thought coexist in unresolved tension. The completion of the [[philosophical-system|positive system]] through the establishment of sociology as a positive science would resolve this tension and provide the intellectual basis for social harmony.


> Comte on the social value of Positivism, demonstrating how it illuminates both future policy and present action:
>
> The object of this chapter has been to show the social value of Positivism. We have found that not merely does it throw light upon our Future policy, but that it also teaches us how to act upon the Present; and these indications have in both cases been based upon careful examination of the Past, in accordance with the fundamental laws of human development.
>
> (003-117)
