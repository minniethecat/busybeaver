## Overview

**Social Order** in [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]]'s positivist philosophy refers to the stable organization of society through shared principles, institutions, and moral consensus. Together with [[progress]], it forms the fundamental duality of Comte's social theory: order provides the static foundation upon which dynamic progress becomes possible.

Comte held that any philosophy "promoting views subversive of all [[social-statics|social order]]" (002-018) was destructive, and that [[positivism|Positivism]] alone could provide a systematic basis for social stability while simultaneously enabling progress.

## Key Principles

Social order depends upon shared intellectual and moral principles. Comte observed that "as the principles of [[social-statics|social order]] disappeared, artists and especially poets" (006-010) contributed to social dissolution by celebrating individual liberty without corresponding responsibility.

The restoration of order requires positive philosophy: "the first principles of [[social-statics|social order]] leads, is the success of medical art" (006-013), meaning that just as medicine requires knowledge of healthy organic functioning before treating disease, [[social-reorganization|social reconstruction]] requires understanding the normal conditions of [[social-statics|social order]] before attempting reform.

Comte distinguished his approach from both revolutionary doctrines that sought to destroy existing institutions and reactionary doctrines that sought merely to preserve them. [[positivism|Positivism]] reconstructs [[social-statics|social order]] on scientific foundations.

> Comte on the doctrine establishing harmony in our nature and the basis of social order:
>
> It is true that this doctrine, which is the only basis for establishing harmony in our nature, has been vaguely accepted by many thinkers in all ages.
>
> (002-018)

> Comte on the danger of self-love and its remedy in the Positivist conception of social order:
>
> The foolish vanity of the later poets of antiquity led some of them into errors much resembling those of the learned men of modern times.
>
> (006-010)

## Historical Context

In Comte's analysis, the French Revolution had destroyed the old theological basis of [[social-statics|social order]] without providing a new one. The resulting disorder demonstrated that "the principles of [[social-statics|social order]] disappeared" (006-010) when their traditional foundation was undermined.

The positivist solution was to rebuild [[social-statics|social order]] upon the immutable laws discovered by science, particularly sociology. Comte argued that [[positivism|Positivism]], "while systematically encouraging the growth of true liberty" (002-018), would not promote views subversive of [[social-statics|social order]] — unlike the critical doctrines of the eighteenth century.
