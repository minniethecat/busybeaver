**Denis Diderot** (5 October 1713 – 31 July 1784) was a French philosopher, art critic, and writer, best known as co-founder and chief editor of the *Encyclopédie* (1751–1772), one of the most ambitious works of the Enlightenment.

## Biography

Born in Langres, France, Diderot was educated by the Jesuits and later moved to Paris, where he became a prominent figure in Enlightenment circles. The *Encyclopédie*, edited jointly with Jean le Rond d'Alembert, aimed to compile and disseminate all human knowledge. Diderot also wrote novels, plays, and art criticism.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte, in Chapter 6 of *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, discusses the relationship between scientific and poetic genius, arguing that there is "no organic distinction" between the two faculties. He uses Diderot as a prime illustration: "Diderot would no doubt have been a great poet in a time more favourable to art" (006-074). Comte's argument is that the direction of genius — whether abstract (scientific) or concrete (poetic) — depends on the circumstances of the era, not on any inherent incompatibility. Diderot exemplifies a mind that, under different historical conditions, could have realized its full esthetic potential.
