**Maximilien Robespierre** (6 May 1758 – 28 July 1794) was a French lawyer and politician, one of the most influential figures of the French Revolution and the Reign of Terror.

## Biography

Robespierre was born in Arras, France, and trained as a lawyer before being elected to the Estates-General in 1789. He rose to prominence as a leader of the Jacobin Club and the Committee of Public Safety. His advocacy of revolutionary virtue, popular sovereignty, and the Cult of the Supreme Being marked the most radical phase of the Revolution. He was overthrown and executed during the Thermidorian Reaction in July 1794.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte references Robespierre at four distinct points in *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, consistently portraying him as a transitional figure whose influence spanned both the destructive and retrograde phases of the revolutionary era.

In Chapter 3, Comte identifies Robespierre as the origin of "official [[deism|Deism]]," the state-sanctioned metaphysical religion that marked the reaction against revolutionary atheism:

> Dating from the official Deism introduced by Robespierre, it reached its height under the aggressive system of Bonaparte, and it was feebly protracted, in spite of the peace of 1815, by his insignificant successors. (003-020)

Comte links Robespierre to Rousseau's intellectual lineage, criticizing "the disciples, that is, of Rousseau, who profess to adopt Robespierre's policy" as politically incoherent and "totally devoid of political coherence" (003-032).

In Chapter 4, Comte situates Robespierre at the origin of "theological hypocrisy" that has persisted through modern French politics:

> Theological hypocrisy, which, from Robespierre downwards, has been the refuge of retrograde policy. (004-109)

Finally, in Chapter 7, Comte places Robespierre at the beginning of the Counter-revolutionary sequence:

> The Counter-revolution, begun by Robespierre, carried to its full length by Bonaparte, and continued by the Bourbons. (007-104)

For Comte, Robespierre embodies the contradictions of the [[metaphysics|metaphysical stage]]: a revolutionary who inaugurated reaction, a deist who prepared the ground for theological restoration.
