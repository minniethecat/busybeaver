## Biography

Joan of Arc (c. 1412–1431), also known as the Maid of Orléans, was a French peasant girl who, claiming divine guidance, led French forces to several pivotal victories during the Hundred Years' War. Captured by the Burgundians and handed over to the English, she was tried for heresy and burned at the stake in 1431 at the age of about nineteen. She was canonized as a saint by the Catholic Church in 1920.

## Joan of Arc in Comte's Philosophy

[[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]] devoted extended attention to Joan of Arc in *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, presenting her as the archetypal example of an exceptional woman whose life transcended the ordinary social functions of her sex while remaining worthy of the highest veneration.

In his discussion of women's social role, Comte identified Joan of Arc under the heading "Exceptional women. Joan of Arc" (005-128) as a case demanding special consideration. He acknowledged that "there have been remarkable instances of women whose life has been one of speculation, or even, what is in most cases still more foreign to their nature, of political activity" (005-129).

Comte used Joan of Arc's treatment by the Catholic Church to illustrate a broader critique of theological absolutism. He noted that "the Maid of Orleans, whose heroism saved France in the fifteenth century," had her canonization requested by King Louis XI, and "no objection was made to his request. Yet, practically, it was never carried into effect" (005-129). Comte explained that the clergy came to fear her memory because "it was feared, not without reason, that to consider Joan of Arc as a saint might have the effect of spreading false and dangerous ideas of feminine duty" (005-129). For Comte, this demonstrated the limitation of absolute systems: "The difficulty was insuperable for any absolute system, in which to sanction the exception is to compromise the rule" (005-129).

In contrast, Comte argued that [[positivism|Positivism]], as a relative system, could fully honor Joan of Arc while simultaneously acknowledging the anomalous nature of her career. He declared that "Positivists will be the first to do justice to this extraordinary heroine, whom theologians have been afraid to recognize, and whom metaphysicians, even in France, have had the hardihood to insult" (005-129). He proposed that the anniversary of her martyrdom become "a solemn festival, not only for France, but for Western Europe" (005-129), emphasizing that her work was of European significance because the enslavement of France would have meant the loss of French influence as the center of advanced European nations.

Thus for Comte, Joan of Arc served as a proof of [[positivism|Positivism]]'s superior capacity over both [[theology]] and metaphysics to recognize genuine human merit without being bound by rigid categorical rules.
