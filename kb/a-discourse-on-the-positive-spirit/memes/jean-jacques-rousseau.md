**Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (28 June 1712 – 2 July 1778) was a Genevan philosopher, writer, and composer whose political philosophy influenced the Enlightenment and the French Revolution.

## Biography

Born in Geneva, Rousseau spent much of his life in France. His major works include *The Social Contract* (1762), *Emile, or On [[education|Education]]* (1762), and *Discourse on Inequality* (1755). His ideas on popular sovereignty and the general will were radical for their time and deeply influenced subsequent political thought.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte treats Rousseau's intellectual legacy with sustained criticism in Chapter 3 of *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*. He identifies Rousseau's followers — "the disciples, that is, of Rousseau, who profess to adopt Robespierre's policy" — as members of the metaphysical school that obstructs social reorganization (003-032). Comte argues that these Deists, despite their lack of governmental power, retain a "hold upon the people" while being "totally devoid of political coherence." Their "wild anarchy," Comte contends, is incompatible with the industrial, scientific, and esthetic spirit of modern life. In Comte's historical schema, Rousseauism represents the [[metaphysics|metaphysical stage]] that must give way to positive philosophy.
