**Frederick the Great** (1712–1786) was King of Prussia from 1740 to 1786. An enlightened despot and military leader, he is referenced in Auguste Comte's *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* as a historical standard of statesmanship against which the leaders of the French Revolution are measured.

## Biography

Frederick II, known as Frederick the Great, ascended the Prussian throne in 1740. His reign was marked by military expansion, administrative reform, and patronage of the arts and philosophy. He corresponded with [[voltaire]] and cultivated an image of enlightened rule, though his primary legacy remained Prussia's emergence as a major European power.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte invokes Frederick the Great in the context of evaluating the statesmanship of the French Revolutionary Convention of 1793. He asserts that the true philosopher will always look with respectful admiration on the men of the Convention, who "not only had no rational theory to guide them, but were encumbered with false metaphysical notions; and who yet notwithstanding proved themselves the only real statesmen that Western Europe can boast of since the time of Frederick the Great." (003-097)

> "The true philosopher will always look with respectful admiration on these men... the only real statesmen that Western Europe can boast of since the time of Frederick the Great." (003-097)

Comte argues that the wisdom of the Convention's policy was almost unaccountable, explicable only by the urgent circumstances that suggested it. The comparison to Frederick the Great serves to highlight the exceptional nature of the Convention's provisional governance, which, unlike the Prussian monarch's, operated without any intellectual or moral foundation yet achieved lasting political significance.
