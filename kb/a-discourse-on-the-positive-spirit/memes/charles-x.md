**Charles X** (1757–1836) reigned as King of France from 1824 to 1830. He is referenced by Auguste Comte in the *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* as the monarch whose reign represented the terminal phase of Royalist reaction in post-Revolutionary France.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte situates the reign of Charles X within his historical analysis of the progressive and retrograde forces in French politics. He describes the post-Convention era as one in which "the successors of the Convention, men unworthy of their task, degraded the progressive dictatorship entrusted to them by the circumstances of the time into a retrograde tyranny." He then identifies the reign of Charles X as the decisive turning point: (003-xxx)

> "During the reign of Charles X, which was the last phase of the reaction, the central power was thoroughly undermined by the legal opposition of the parliamentary or local power." (003-xxx)

According to Comte, the central government under Charles X still refused to recognize any limits to its authority, but the growth of free thought made its claims to spiritual jurisdiction increasingly untenable, leaving it merely the temporal authority requisite for public order. The subsequent neutral period saw the dictatorship legally destroyed, with parliamentary power replacing central power.

The overthrow of Charles X in the July Revolution of 1830 thus marks, in Comte's framework, the definitive end of the retrograde reaction and the beginning of the restoration of the republic and the progressive spirit.
