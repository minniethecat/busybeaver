**Homer** (c. 8th century BCE) was an ancient Greek epic poet, traditionally regarded as the author of the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey*.

## Role in Art and Society

[[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]], in his *Discourse on the Positivist Spirit*, uses Homer as a central example throughout Chapter 6 to illustrate the proper function of Art in society. Comte argues that the greatest poets have always understood their role as beautifying and elevating human life, rather than seeking to govern it:

> All poets of real genius, from Homer to Corneille, have always considered their work to be that of beautifying human life, and so far, of elevating it. Government of human life they had never supposed to fall within their province. (006-009)

Comte repeatedly invokes the formula "from Homer to [[pierre-corneille|Corneille]]" to establish a continuity of genuine poetic genius across Western history.

## Art and Social Hierarchy in Antiquity

Comte examines Homer's place in ancient society to support his argument that artists were never the leading class, even in esthetically-oriented Polytheistic cultures:

> If proofs were necessary, Homer's poems, especially the Odyssey, would show how secondary the influence of the fine arts was upon society, even when the priesthood had ceased to control them. (006-010)

This observation grounds Comte's broader theory that the faculties of Representation and Expression must remain subordinate to those of Conception and Co-ordination.

## The Limitations of Ancient Epic

Comte notes that Homer's subject matter — military life — reflected the limited scope available to artists in antiquity, before the development of domestic affections:

> Even in such a case as that of Homer, we feel that he would hardly have spent his extraordinary powers upon descriptions of military life, had there been nobler subjects for his genius. (006-045)

## Homer in Positivist Education

For Comte, Homer exemplifies the kind of artistic idealization of the past that [[positivism|Positivism]] can systematize. The Positivist poet of the future will be able to idealize all historical phases with the same mastery that Homer brought to his own era:

> All phases of the Past will be recalled to life with the same distinctness with which some of them have been already idealized by Homer and Corneille. (006-064)

## Artistic Education

Comte draws a parallel between Homer's time and the Positivist future regarding the education of artists:

> The greatest poets, from Homer to Corneille, have always participated largely in the best education of which their times admitted. The artist must have clear conceptions before he can exhibit true pictures. (006-067)
