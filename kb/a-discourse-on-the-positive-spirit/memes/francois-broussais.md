**François-Joseph-Victor Broussais** (17 December 1772 – 17 November 1838) was a French physician and physiologist known for his controversial medical theories and his advocacy of physiological medicine.

## Biography

Born in Saint-Malo, France, Broussais served as a naval surgeon before studying medicine in Paris. He became a professor at the Val-de-Grâce military hospital. His major work, *Examen des doctrines médicales* (1816), critiqued prevailing medical systems and championed a physiological approach centred on irritation and inflammation. Though his theories were later discredited, his emphasis on pathological anatomy influenced the development of modern medicine.

## Connection to Positivism

Comte mentions Broussais in Chapter 4 of *A Discourse on the Positivist Spirit* during a discussion of Communism and the necessity of proposing solutions alongside problems. Comte argues that merely stating a problem without venturing a solution yields no progress: "Suppose, for instance, that Gall and Broussais had limited themselves to a simple statement of their great problems without venturing on any solution; their principles, however incontestable, would have been barren of result" (004-062). This pairing of Broussais with Gall — both pioneers in cerebral physiology — reflects Comte's broader respect for the positive method in the life sciences as precursors to sociology.
