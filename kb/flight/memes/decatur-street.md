Decatur Street is a thoroughfare in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta, Georgia]]. In the novel *Flight*, it is depicted as part of the city's slum district and serves as the setting for the racial violence that upends the Daquin family's life.

## The Slum District

Decatur Street and neighboring [[ivy-street|Ivy Street]] are identified early in the novel as [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s slum district. A cab driver tells a passenger that he "would have carried you through Decatur and [[ivy-street|Ivy Street]]s — that's the slum district" ([001-007]), marking these streets as the most impoverished and dangerous part of the city.

> He'd have carried you through Decatur and Ivy Streets — that's the slum district. A lot of folks are afraid to go through there at night. [001-007]

## The Race Riot

During the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] race riot of 1906, Decatur Street becomes a scene of terror. From the Daquin home on [[peachtree-street|Peachtree Street]], the sounds of the mob reach Mimi: "From nearby Decatur Street, thoroughfare of saloons" ([005-074]), the violence of the white mob echoes through the night. The proximity of Decatur Street's chaos to the Daquin home underscores the inescapability of racial danger in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]].

## Significance

Decatur Street represents the underside of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] — the slum conditions, the saloon culture, and ultimately the eruption of mob violence that reveals the fragility of Black life in the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] South. The events on and around Decatur Street become the catalyst for the Daquin family's decision to leave [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], reshaping the trajectory of Mimi's entire life.
