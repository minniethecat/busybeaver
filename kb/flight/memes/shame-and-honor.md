## Definition

Shame and social honor in *Flight* function as powerful, often destructive mechanisms through which the Black community regulates its members' behavior. The novel documents how reputation — particularly women's sexual reputation — is policed through gossip networks that can destroy a person's standing overnight. For [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]], the experience of being shamed and the subsequent decision to escape shame through racial passing form one of the novel's central arcs.

## Gossip as Social Control

From the moment the Daquin family arrives in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], gossip precedes and surrounds them. Mrs. Plummer and Mrs. King, examining the Daquins' furniture to "speculate on its probable cost" (001-038), model the community's investment in scrutinizing newcomers. Their discussion of a local woman's scandal — "A body'd think she'd have mo' sense than to do her dirt so bold like!" (001-026) — establishes gossip as the primary mechanism through which women's behavior is policed.

The novel's narrator observes that gossip is persistent and regenerative: "rumour, apparently dead and forgotten, was many lived and any misstep of the future could and would resurrect all previous reports" (009-007). This quality of scandal — its ability to lie dormant and then resurge — creates a constant pressure toward conformity.

## The Scandal of Unmarried Pregnancy

Mimi's pregnancy by [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] transforms her from a respected newcomer into a social pariah. The community's response is characterized by cruel speculation: "Some had it that the father of the child was uncertain — every man under seventy years of age who had ever been on the most casual terms of friendship with Mimi was discussed as the possible father" (016-041). The novel captures the gendered asymmetry of scandal: Carl's reputation suffers far less than Mimi's.

Mimi's decision to leave Atlanta — to flee — is driven not by guilt but by the impossibility of remaining. "The wicked fleeth when no man pursueth," the community declares (016-042), but the novel makes clear that the community itself is the pursuer. Only [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]] and her mother [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] "championed the cause of Mimi" (016-042).

## Honor and Respectability

The novel contrasts Mimi's internal sense of honor — "she had given of herself freely, without thought of consequences and without any sense of shame" (012-019) — with the community's external, punitive honor code. Mimi's refusal to feel shame about her pregnancy is perhaps her most radical act. Dr. Adams is puzzled by her response: "Her smile puzzled Dr. Adams" (012-019). In a society where a woman's value is measured by her sexual reputation, Mimi's refusal to internalize shame represents a form of resistance.
