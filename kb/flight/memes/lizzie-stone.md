## Overview

Lizzie Stone represents the working-class Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] that exists outside [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club's refined drawing rooms. While the novel focuses primarily on the middle-class and elite Black characters — the Hunters, the Daquins, the Robertsons — figures like Lizzie Stone remind the reader that most Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]ns in the early 20th century lived lives of hard labor, economic precarity, and limited horizons.

The character serves as a counterweight to the novel's interest in the color-conscious elite. Where [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]] polices the boundaries of respectability from her turreted mansion, Lizzie Stone navigates a different set of constraints — the demands of white employers, the vulnerability of poverty, and the daily negotiation of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] from a position of economic weakness rather than relative privilege.
