## Overview

Wu Hseh-Chuan is a Chinese diplomat and intellectual who appears as the guest of honor at a dinner party hosted by Mrs. Crosby in the later chapters of *Flight*. Although his presence in the novel is brief — limited to a handful of mentions across Chapters 21 through 23 — his single recorded observation has an outsized impact on the protagonist Mimi Daquin (now Mimi Forrester). His remark that "only your Negroes have successfully resisted mechanization" [023-024] becomes the catalyst for Mimi's long-dormant racial consciousness to reawaken after nearly a decade of passing for white.

Mimi's encounter with Wu Hseh-Chuan challenges her internalized stereotypes about Chinese people. She reflects on "the concept which had always sprung to her mind when she had heard the word Chinese or Chinaman — it always had been of a slant-eyed Oriental shuffling across a laundry floor or a bestial, treacherous villain upon the stage or moving-picture screen." Against this backdrop, Hseh-Chuan's calm dignity and "farsighted wisdom" stand out in "bold relief" [023-005].

## In the Story

### The Dinner Party (Chapters 21–22)

Mrs. Crosby announces the dinner in a note to the Forresters: "wants us to come to dinner Friday night — they're having a few people in to meet some Chinaman — Wu Hseh-Chuan" [021-009]. As is typical of Mrs. Crosby, her "few people" proves to mean a crowd of forty guests, "a heterogeneous assemblage" that Jimmie suspects was assembled by writing "the names of every person she ever met, put them in a hat, and then pulled out the first forty" [022-004].

### Mimi's Racial Awakening (Chapter 23)

It is not the dinner itself but its aftermath that proves significant. Reflecting the morning after, Mimi contrasts the "calm dignity, the farsighted wisdom of the Chinese" against "the background of the tawdry, shallow people around him" — including Mrs. Crosby, Bert Bellamy, and her own husband [023-005]. Hseh-Chuan's observation about Black resistance to mechanization lodges in her mind, and later, when an impulse draws her toward Harlem, she realizes: "It was the statement Wu Hseh-Chuan had made — '… only your Negroes have successfully resisted mechanization …' was the way he put it" [023-024].

At a Harlem nightclub, watching Black dancers move with "unconscious grace, a rhythmic sweep to their bodies that made the others seem awkward and graceless," Mimi begins "to see for the first time what Wu Hseh-Chuan had meant — it had taken an Oriental from halfway around the world to make her see things she had seen all her life and yet had never seen" [023-029]. His outsider's perspective enables Mimi to see her own people — and her own flight from them — with fresh eyes.
