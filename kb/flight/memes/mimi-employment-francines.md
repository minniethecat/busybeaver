## Overview

Mimi's employment at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] — the most exclusive modiste in [[new-york-city|New York]] — is the practical realization of her decision to pass. To her surprised delight she was successful at the first place to which she applied (017-012). Her French name, her Creole French accent, and her sewing skills — all products of her [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] upbringing — enable her to secure a position that would be unavailable to a known Black woman.

The employment represents both liberation and imprisonment. Mimi earns more money than she could in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], and she is free from the daily humiliations of the color line. But she is also trapped in a performance that requires constant vigilance. She lives in fear of discovery, unable to reveal her true self to even her closest coworker. The employment at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] is the beginning of a double life that the novel refuses to romanticize.
