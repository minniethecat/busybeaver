## Overview

Dolores d'Aubigny is an aspiring Broadway actress who appears at the Crosbys' dinner party in Chapter 22 of *Flight*. Her real name, according to Bert Bellamy's gossip, is Mary Mason — "though I've heard she was christened Mary Mason" [022-019]. She is the mistress of Horace Crosby, the wealthy Chicago businessman whose fortune from grain and stockyard speculation funds his wife Eulalia Crosby's lavish social life. Dolores functions in the narrative less as a developed character than as a symbol: she represents the empty, sordid diversions — affairs, ambition, and the commodification of beauty — that Mimi Daquin observes with growing disillusionment in the New York society she has entered through her marriage to Jimmie Forrester.

## In the Story

### The Crosbys' Dinner Party (Chapter 22)

Dolores d'Aubigny makes her sole appearance at the Crosbys' dinner party for Wu Hseh-Chuan. Bert Bellamy points her out to Mimi as "the stunning-looking girl dressed in red," and explains that she is the reason Horace Crosby "developed so suddenly a keen interest in the dram-mer" [022-019]. Bellamy identifies her as a performer slated for the Broadway "Futilities" — a system, he explains, in which producer Thorne requires showgirls to have wealthy patrons who will finance productions [022-023].

Mimi observes the triangulated tension as Dolores greets Mrs. Crosby while Horace looks on: "She fancied there was a shrewd appraisal of the relative merits of his wife, fat and ungainly and too heavily rouged and powdered, and of her who called herself Dolores d'Aubigny, slim and graceful and beautiful" [022-020]. The girl's "marked casualness" in glancing at Horace and walking away reveals the choreography of the clandestine affair. Mimi feels "a flood of pity for the bungling, ludicrous old woman" — Mrs. Crosby — who remains "wholly unaware of the little drama being enacted of which she was one of the principals" [022-020]. Bellamy predicts Dolores "will cost Horace a pretty penny before she's through with him" [022-021].

### Symbolic Weight (Chapter 23)

The morning after the dinner, Mimi reflects on the meaning of what she observed. In a passage that serves as the novel's moral taxonomy of modern emptiness, she catalogs the diversions people use to escape the "huge, insatiable machine" of modern life: "Horace Crosby seeking release and forgetfulness in an empty, sordid affair with a woman, beautiful though she was, like Dolores d'Aubigny" [023-006]. The affair is listed alongside Bert Bellamy's gossip addiction, Eulalie Crosby's trivial busyness, and Jimmie Forrester's booster clubs — all futile attempts to "forget the implacable demands of the forces that drove them on" [023-006].
