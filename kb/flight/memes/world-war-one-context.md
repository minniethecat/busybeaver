## Overview

[[world-war-i|World War I]] forms the distant but palpable backdrop to the novel's later chapters. There had been vague rumblings of discord and war in Europe, but all that seemed very far away, too distant ever to affect America (017-009). The war's effects are felt indirectly: in the economic boom that creates demand for workers at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], in the patriotic rhetoric that intensifies the pressure toward Anglo-conformity, and in the service of African American soldiers who would return from [[french-government|France]] with new expectations of racial equality.

The war's significance for the novel lies in what it portends. The generation of Black Americans who served in [[world-war-i|World War I]] — and who experienced a [[french-government|France]] where racial barriers were far less rigid — would become the vanguard of the New Negro movement and the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] Renaissance. Mimi's generation, caught between the old world of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] and the emerging possibilities of modernity, would be transformed by [[world-war-i|the war]] and its aftermath.
