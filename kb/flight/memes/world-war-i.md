# World War I

The First World War (1914–1918), whose interruption of European immigration created unprecedented labor demand in northern industries and accelerated the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]] of African Americans from the South.

## Overview

World War I is a background presence in *Flight*, never directly named but implicit in the economic and social conditions that shape [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s opportunities in the North. The war's demand for industrial labor, combined with the cessation of European immigration, opened factory and service jobs to African Americans that had previously been reserved for white workers. This labor market transformation is the economic engine driving the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]] that structures the novel's geography.

For Mimi, the war years coincide with her transition from the South to the North — from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], then to [[new-york-city|New York]]. The wartime economy's appetite for labor, particularly in the garment industry, creates the conditions in which a young Black woman with sewing skills can find employment, whether in the Black community or, eventually, by passing for white in [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's dress shop]].

## Narrative Context

The war is not directly depicted in the novel, but its effects are visible throughout Mimi's northern experience. The labor shortages that brought African American women into industrial and service work explain why Mimi's "training at the [[manhattan-trade-school|Manhattan Trade School]]" (017-020) can translate into employment at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]]. The war economy also explains the prosperity of [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] during the years of Mimi's residence there — the neighborhood's growth was directly fueled by wartime migration.

The war's demographic impact also shapes the novel's treatment of gender relations. With men deployed overseas, women entered the workforce in unprecedented numbers, creating both economic opportunity and social disruption. The absence of men — and the presence of women earning their own incomes — forms part of the context for the scandal that destroys Mimi's reputation in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]].

## Aftermath

The war's end did not reverse the demographic transformation it had accelerated. [[the-great-migration|The Great Migration]] continued through the 1920s, and the Black communities established in northern cities — like the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] where Mimi sought refuge — became permanent features of American urban life. The war thus represents an invisible but decisive force in the novel's architecture of displacement and opportunity.
