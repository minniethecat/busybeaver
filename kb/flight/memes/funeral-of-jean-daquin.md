## Overview

The death and funeral of [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] mark a major turning point in the novel. Jean has been Mimi's protector, her connection to Creole heritage, and the embodiment of the philosophy that a Negro is known by his ability and not by the colour of his skin (002-096). With his death, Mimi loses not only her father but the moral and cultural framework that has sustained her identity.

The funeral brings [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]] from [[new-york-city|New York]] to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], introducing the character who will become Mimi's next source of support. Jean's sister offers Mimi a new home, setting in motion the final phase of her geographical and racial journey. The funeral thus functions as both an ending and a beginning — the closure of the Creole chapter and the opening of the [[new-york-city|New York]] chapter.
