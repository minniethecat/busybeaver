## Location

Pryor Street runs through downtown [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], intersecting with [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue at the eastern edge of the city's Black business and residential district. In the early 1900s, the area around Pryor Street marked a racial boundary: [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Avenue]] to its west was the thriving heart of Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], while [[decatur-street|Decatur Street]] and [[ivy-street|Ivy Street]] nearby were — in the words of [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]] — "the slum district — saloons and houses" frequented by the "lowest kind of Negroes" (`001-007`). Pryor Street itself occupied an interstitial position, a contested space where the respectable Black world of [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue and the violent white world beyond met.

## In the Novel

> "If he'd gone that way he'd have carried you through Decatur and Ivy Streets -- that's the slum district -- saloons and houses --" He paused significantly, looking at Mimi. "Pretty bad," he added. "Lowest kind of Negroes." `001-007`

Pryor Street first appears in the novel's opening chapter, when [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]] insists that the cab carrying the Daquin family from [[atlanta-union-station|Union Station]] take the long way around: "Go around by Pryor Street and from there down [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue" (`001-007`). He explains that the direct route would take them through [[decatur-street|Decatur and Ivy Streets]], the slum district. This routing decision is Mimi's introduction to the racialized geography of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], a city where every street carries a moral and racial valence.

Pryor Street returns in the novel's most violent episode — the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta race riot]]. As Mimi and [[jean-daquin|Jean]] flee the mob sweeping down [[peachtree-street|Peachtree Street]] to [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Avenue]], they reach the corner of Pryor Street just as "a shout welling into a roar made them draw back into the shelter of a doorway" (`005-082`). From their hiding place they witness a young Black man running for his life: "Up Pryor Street sped a young, well-dressed Negro. Inch by inch, foot by foot, yard by yard he gained on his pursuers" (`005-082`). When the lead pursuer closes in, the fleeing man plunges a pocketknife "to its hilt in the breast of his pursuer, who gasped, groaned and ludicrously sank to the ground" (`005-082`). The man escapes into the shadows of [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue, and the mob, "peered indecisively down the street where the Negro had fled, discussed and voted against following him as the street led to the Negro section" (`005-083`).

The intersection of Pryor Street and [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Avenue]] thus becomes for Mimi the site where the logic of [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] is laid bare: a Black man hunted for his life, fighting back, surviving — but only because the mob is unwilling to enter the Black quarter. Mimi and Jean flee home, "weak with fright and the horror of the scenes they had witnessed" (`005-083`), and the memory of what she saw at Pryor Street haunts Mimi for weeks afterward: "In the still hours of the night she would awaken from a sound sleep screaming with terror" (`005-084`).
