## Definition

Racial violence in *Flight* operates at two levels: the spectacular violence of the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot, which destroys [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s business and drives the family from the city, and the quotidian violence of segregation — the daily humiliations, exclusions, and threats that structure life under [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]]. The novel traces how both forms of violence shape [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s consciousness and ultimately drive her decision to pass.

The concept of trauma, while not available to Walter White in 1926 in its modern clinical sense, is rendered vividly in the novel through Mimi's psychological responses to racial violence. Her \"intense consciousness of race which her experiences in [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] at [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] had engendered in her\" (017-006) represents what we would now recognize as traumatic hypervigilance — a heightened awareness of racial danger that persists long after the immediate threat has passed.

## Historical Context

The [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot of September 1906 was one of the most devastating episodes of racial violence in American history. Over three days, white mobs killed at least 25 Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]ns (some estimates range much higher) and destroyed Black homes and businesses. The riot exposed the fragility of Black economic achievement and the complicity of white authorities in racial violence.

The psychological effects of racial violence on Black communities were profound but underdocumented in the early 20th century. The concept of racial trauma — the cumulative psychological impact of racism, discrimination, and violence on individuals and communities — would not be formally recognized for decades. However, the lived experience of this trauma shaped every aspect of Black life.

## In the Novel

The [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot is the novel's pivotal traumatic event. Mimi witnesses violence that destroys her father's business and her family's sense of safety. The trauma of [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] reverberates through the rest of the novel: Mimi's fear of white mobs, her distrust of the safety promised by respectability, and her eventual decision to leave the South altogether all trace back to this foundational experience.

The novel also documents the quieter violence of daily life under [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]]. The segregated train cars, the white theater that ejects [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], the constant negotiation of spaces and boundaries — these accumulate over time, wearing down Mimi's resistance. When she finally decides to pass, it is not a single event but the cumulative weight of racial violence that drives her decision.

Mimi's moments of despair — her thoughts of suicide, her sense that she is trapped in a series of rooms from which there is no exit — represent the novel's most direct engagement with the psychological cost of racial trauma. The novel refuses to present passing as a solution to this trauma; rather, it shows passing as yet another form of violence, this time self-inflicted (017-001).
