## Overview

The [[georgia-piedmont|Georgia Piedmont]] — the rolling upland region between the coastal plain and the Appalachian Mountains — provided the agricultural hinterland that sustained [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s growth. In the early 20th century, the region was dominated by cotton cultivation, much of it still worked by Black sharecroppers and tenant farmers whose lives were only marginally improved from the conditions of slavery. The rural poverty of the Piedmont stood in stark contrast to the urban prosperity of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black business district.

In *Flight*, the [[georgia-piedmont|Georgia countryside]] appears primarily through the train window in the novel's opening scene. The train passes through cotton fields and small towns before arriving at [[atlanta-union-station|Union Station]], and the landscape — with its plantations and its history — establishes the spatial and historical context for the racial drama that will unfold in the city. The countryside is the world that the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]] was fleeing.
