Philadelphia, the largest city in Pennsylvania, lies on the Delaware River roughly halfway between [[new-york-city|New York]] and Washington. In the early twentieth century it was a major industrial center and home to one of the oldest free African American communities in the North.

> She could give no reason why she chose that city to which to go. The nearest she could explain her choice was that Philadelphia was large, she knew no one there and she was sure she could lose herself in its vastness. [012-067]

The city's colored population was concentrated in several neighborhoods, with a social hierarchy built around church affiliation and professional status. The "old Philadelphia coloured families" formed an elite stratum [014-050].

## Location

Philadelphia, positioned between the South and [[new-york-city|New York]], was a natural way station for migrants of the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]] moving northward. Its industrial economy offered employment in factories, domestic service, and the small trades that sustained the colored working class.

For Mimi, the city was chosen almost at random — a train boarded with no connections and no plan beyond the conviction that its size would grant her anonymity. Because she knew no one in Philadelphia, she experienced the city from its margins: a single mother navigating social codes.

## In the Novel

[[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] arrives in Philadelphia after [[atlanta-departure|fleeing Atlanta]] in the wake of the race riot. The Mannings, an elderly colored couple, become the first stable presence she has known since Jean's death.

> To her neighbours she said that Mimi was a young widow grieving for her dead husband, which explanation, in view of the mourning garments Mimi yet wore for Jean, was accepted in good faith. [013-002]

Yet Philadelphia proves to be a place of grinding hardship. For more than eight months, she knows almost no one beyond the Mannings' circle [013-023]. When she finally secures work, it is intermittent and poorly paid.

> One of these lasted more than a week, the making of the trousseau of a girl who belonged to one of the old Philadelphia coloured families. [014-050]

The city's colored community offers no real path upward. She comes to see [[new-york-city|New York]] as "the only field in which any marked advance could be made" [014-052]. When her [[mrs-rogers|Aunt Sophie]] sends for her, she departs without regret.

> Even her years in Philadelphia began to fade into the land of half-remembered, half-forgotten things. [016-002]

## Significance

Philadelphia functions as a way station between the trauma of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] and the reinvention Mimi undertakes in [[new-york-city|New York]]. The city's old, established colored society cannot accommodate a woman in Mimi's position: young, ambitious, and alone.

> There was little chance of her making any great progress in Philadelphia. New York to her seemed the only field in which any marked advance could be made. [014-052]

Philadelphia offers an important contrast to the world Mimi eventually chooses — the white world accessed through [[racial-passing]]. In Philadelphia she struggles to survive as a colored woman; in [[new-york-city|New York]] she will discover that passing opens every door.

Mimi's experience represents what the novel suggests was the common lot of many colored women in the urban North: not the violence of the South but the slow, grinding poverty of respectability without opportunity.
