## Overview

Hilda Adams is a young woman in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Negro elite society and [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s first close friend in Walter White's *Flight*. As the daughter of [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] -- a member of [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club once ejected from a white theatre for passing -- Hilda grows up in the shadow of the color line.

Her friendship with Mimi provides both girls with a rare experience of genuine peer connection in a social world dominated by adult anxieties about status. Hilda is characterized by her directness, her sense of humor, and her willingness to speak frankly about the social world around her.

She serves as Mimi's informal guide to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Negro society, explaining its codes, its personalities, and its dangers through whispered conversations on porch steps and at club gatherings.

## In the Story

### First Meeting

Hilda is introduced at a [[fleur-de-lis-meeting|Fleur-de-Lis Club meeting]] where [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] has brought her daughter at [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]]'s invitation (004-001). When Mimi first looks "around the room for Hilda Adams," she finds "no younger people" besides herself (004-006).

When they finally meet, Hilda "stood silent just beyond" her mother's presence until introduced (004-011). Her simple approach -- "Let's go out on the steps and talk" -- cuts through the formality of the adult gathering (004-013).

Mimi and Hilda "sat there and talked," beginning a friendship that [[jean-daquin|Jean]] would later recognize: "I'm happy you've found Hilda" (004-014, 004-031). Through all her fourteen years, Mimi "had had no confidante other than her father," and with Hilda she found a rare peer connection (004-014).

### Guide to Atlanta Society

Sitting on the steps during the club meeting, Hilda provides Mimi with whispered commentary on the attendees, "telling in whispers about the women" (004-019). She is interrupted by her own curiosity, "asking suddenly" whether Mimi had met [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]], revealing romantic preoccupations beneath the club's formal surface (004-024).

### The Theatre Incident

Hilda shares with Mimi the defining story of her family's experience. She tells Mimi "in whispers about the" ejection of her mother, [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], from the white theatre where she had been passing (004-019).

When recounting the incident, Hilda identifies the gossiper as [[mrs-watkins|Mrs. Watkins]] and adds the intimate detail that "Dr. Watkins is still in love with mamma and it makes Mrs. Watkins furious" (004-020).

### Loyal Friendship

Hilda's friendship deepens over time. She defends the Daquin family when others gossip, noting that "there are lots of folks like Mrs. Adams, for example, and Hilda, but the trouble is they're the kind who attend to their own affairs" (006-053).

When Mary Daquin falls ill at a Fleur-de-Lis Club meeting, Hilda telephones for the doctor, demonstrating practical loyalty (005-057).

## Relationships

Hilda's relationship with her mother, [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], is close and protective. Hilda has internalized the lesson of her mother's theatre ejection and carries its implications into her social navigation.

Her friendship with [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] is the most significant peer relationship in the novel's [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] section. Hilda's connection to [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] -- "the most thrilling boy in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]" -- places her at the intersection of romantic and social currents (004-025).

Her father, Dr. Adams, appears in moments of crisis, summoned to attend the ailing Mary Daquin and later to Jean Daquin during his final illness.
