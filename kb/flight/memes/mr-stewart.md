## Overview

Mr. Stewart is a white bank cashier in Atlanta who appears in Chapter 5 of *Flight*. Jean Daquin visits him regarding some financial notes, but their conversation quickly turns from business to the charged topic of racial violence and media sensationalism. Through their exchange, Walter White dramatizes the chasm between white and Black perspectives on lynching, mob violence, and the reporting of alleged assaults in the Jim Crow South.

Stewart initially confronts Jean with hostility, asserting that "You Negroes have got to stop these real attacks" [005-037]. When Jean challenges him to consider the issue from the Black perspective, Stewart admits with some surprise that he "hadn't given much time to it" [005-042]. He ultimately retreats to a position of passive resignation — "We can only hope for the best" [005-044] — embodying the white moderate's refusal to take meaningful action against racial terror.

## In the Story

### The Bank Confrontation (Chapter 5)

Jean Daquin arrives at Stewart's bank intending to discuss business notes, but is taken aback by Stewart's uncharacteristic hostility. Jean had known Stewart as "usually so pleasant," and asks why he has changed [005-032]. The answer lies in the racial hysteria gripping Atlanta: Stewart, like many white citizens, has been inflamed by newspaper reports of alleged Black assaults on white women.

Jean pushes back, pointing out that many reported "attacks" are later found to be cases of mistaken identity or hysteria — yet corrections receive minimal coverage compared to the inflammatory initial reports. He argues that "there's trouble ahead if these inflammatory stories aren't stopped" and appeals to Stewart as a man of sense to intervene [005-036].

### Confronting the Other Side

When Jean asks Stewart whether he has ever tried to see the issue from "our side of the fence" [005-041], Stewart's reflective admission that he has not reveals a man whose racial worldview has never been challenged. Jean tells the story of a friend's son — a young Black man who accidentally brushed against a white woman and ran in terror, knowing that a single scream could mean his death [005-043]. Stewart responds with a platitude about sitting "on a keg of dynamite" and hoping for the best [005-044].

After Jean leaves, a group of men demand that Stewart reveal what Jean said. Stewart refuses, not out of principle but because "he did not know how Jean's words and thoughts might be taken" [005-045] — a small act of discretion that reveals the precariousness of any cross-racial exchange.
