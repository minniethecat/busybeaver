# Meeting of the Fleur-de-Lis Club at Mrs. Hunter's Home

The April afternoon social gathering of [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club that introduces [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black elite society, produces her lifelong friendship with [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]], and reveals the color and class dynamics that will shape her life.

## Overview

The Fleur-de-Lis Club meeting at [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]]'s home in April is a richly observed set piece. Mimi, dressed in her "best dress, a pale yellow confection of lawn" (004-002), accompanies her stepmother to the gathering, where she observes the rituals of status, appearance, and gossip that define Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s elite.

The meeting produces Mimi's first encounter with [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]]. Through all her fourteen years, Mimi "had had no confidante other than her father" (004-014). With Hilda she finds peer connection: "Mimi felt tender affection surge through her for this newfound friend" (004-014).

[[jean-daquin|Jean]] later affirms the friendship: "I'm happy you've found Hilda. You need the companionship of young people" (004-031).

The meeting also introduces the story of [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]]'s ejection from the [[grand-opera-house-atlanta|Grand Opera House]], presenting Mimi with the practice of racial passing and the community's punitive response. The afternoon becomes a compressed education in the sociology of Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]].

## Narrative Context

The meeting occupies chapter 4. [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]] carefully orchestrates their entrance, arriving at "twenty minutes of five" rather than the stated "four o'clock sharp" (004-003).

The guests, seated "in a circle whose circumference was only a little less than that of the room" (004-004), present a uniform lightness of complexion. Mimi immediately notices that "none of the women present were darker than a light brown" (004-006).

The meeting's central subplot involves [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]]. Her story of ejection from the [[grand-opera-house-atlanta|Grand Opera House]] after "passing for white" (004-008) provokes the club women's satisfied condemnation rather than sympathy.

Mimi's reaction -- "she felt a deep warmth within her for this woman" (004-010) -- marks her first identification with passing. The meeting also introduces [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] through Hilda's description of him as "the most thrilling boy in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]" (004-025), planting a seed that will later complicate Mimi's life.

## Aftermath

The meeting launches multiple narrative threads: Mimi's friendship with Hilda, her identification with [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], her awareness of passing as a strategy, and her introduction to [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]].

The club's display of gossip, color consciousness, and social cruelty also provides the template for the community dynamics that later destroy Mimi's reputation in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]].
