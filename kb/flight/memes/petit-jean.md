## Overview

Petit Jean — Little Jean — is [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s infant son, named for her father [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]].

He is born in [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] during Mimi's difficult years after [[atlanta-departure|leaving Atlanta]]. His presence fundamentally shapes every subsequent decision Mimi makes. The child represents both Mimi's deepest love and her most profound vulnerability: a visible connection to Black identity that she cannot erase.

The novel treats Petit Jean with characteristic restraint — he appears primarily as a motivation rather than as a developed character. Mimi's need to provide for him drives her to seek better-paying work; her fear for his future keeps her from despair.

## Birth and Early Months

Petit Jean is born in [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], where Mimi has fled after the [[atlanta-race-riot|Atlanta race riot]]. The pregnancy and birth are not dramatized; the novel simply notes that Mimi must "supply the needs of herself and 'Petit Jean,' as she called the baby" (013-020).

The infant's presence transforms Mimi's world. Despite the poverty and isolation of her Philadelphia years, the child brings moments of joy:

> But when she saw "Petit Jean" cooing and kicking in the old market basket which served as his crib, she felt anew the thrill of his presence and forgot the mean little room, the struggle for existence, the unlovely prospect of life. (013-027)

Mimi arranges for Petit Jean to stay "by day at a nursery" while she works (013-028). The practical demands of childcare, combined with the exhaustion of factory work, define the rhythms of her life.

## Illness and Crisis

The novel's most sustained treatment of Petit Jean comes during a medical crisis. The child falls seriously ill, requiring surgery:

> She was terrified as the thought of her Petit Jean going under the knife. She feared she would go mad. (014-027)

Mimi's response is visceral, primal. In her imagination, she sees "Petit Jean lifeless, never again to coo and gurgle at her, never again to twist her heart with his quaint, choking laugh" (014-032). The passage reveals the depth of her attachment and the precariousness of her situation — a single mother with no family network, facing a child's illness alone.

The doctor who treats Petit Jean is "one of the leading coloured physicians of Philadelphia" (014-020). The operation succeeds, but the experience deepens Mimi's awareness of her vulnerability. She realizes "that she could not continue at this rate for the future — for Petit Jean's future, she amended" (014-040).

## The Orphanage Decision

Mimi's decision to place Petit Jean in an orphanage is one of the novel's most painful moments. It represents the ultimate cost of her passing — not only the loss of community and identity, but the severing of the most intimate human bond.

Following the operation, Petit Jean seems "to feel the impending change and clung more closely than ever to his mother" (014-059). The detail is devastating: the child senses what is coming, even if he cannot understand it.

Mimi justifies the decision as temporary. She will earn more money passing as a white woman and retrieve him sooner. But "night after night she cried herself to sleep in her anxiety and her aching need of Petit Jean" (015-023). The novel does not offer reassurance that the plan will succeed.

## Petit Jean as Anchor

Throughout her New York years, Petit Jean remains a presence in Mimi's consciousness. He serves as "a link and a reminder" (016-002) of the life she has left behind:

> Thoughts of Petit Jean sustain Mimi through her moments of deepest despair. In her moments of greatest depression she thought of suicide but these morbid thoughts did not remain with her long. Petit Jean and thoughts of his future would have driven them away. (017-001)

The child functions as both burden and anchor — the reason Mimi cannot fully escape her past and the reason she continues to survive. Her goal, consistently stated, is "the accumulation of enough money to bring Petit Jean to her" (017-050).

Even at the novel's close, Petit Jean remains the thread connecting Mimi to her former life. When she reflects on what has sustained her, she counts "Jean and Petit Jean, her work, the love of people" (020-034) among her compensations. The child is absent yet present, a promise deferred but never forgotten.

## Symbolic Role

Petit Jean embodies the novel's central tension between individual aspiration and collective identity.

He is the living evidence of Mimi's Blackness — a fact that cannot be hidden, explained away, or passed over. In a novel structured around the choice to pass, Petit Jean represents the impossibility of complete escape. Mimi can change her name, her address, her presentation — but she cannot unmake her son.

The orphanage represents an intermediate space: not abandonment, but suspension. Petit Jean is placed in institutional care, waiting for a mother who promises to return. Whether she will — and what the child will become if she does — are questions the novel leaves unanswered.
