## Overview

Auburn Avenue in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] was known in the early 20th century as "the richest Negro street in the world."

Home to banks, insurance companies, real estate offices, newspapers, and retail establishments, it represented the pinnacle of Black economic achievement under [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]]. The businesses that lined Auburn Avenue — including Alonzo Herndon's Atlanta Life Insurance Company — served a community that white-owned businesses refused to serve.

In *Flight*, Auburn Avenue is the world of the Hunter family and the Black business elite. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s observation that a "company operated by Negroes in Atlanta" has succeeded validates his belief in Black economic potential — a belief that will be destroyed by the race riot (002-055).

## Auburn Avenue in the Novel

Auburn Avenue is the first Atlanta street the Daquins encounter upon arrival. [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]] directs the driver from the train station:

> "No—No," shouted Mr. Robertson as the driver started down the gleaming length of Peachtree Street. "Down Decatur Street and from there down Auburn Avenue." (001-007)

The route is deliberate. Mr. Robertson wants to impress his Creole in-laws with the spectacle of Black economic achievement. To Jean and Mimi, who have "used to the Creole dilution of the Negro songs" (001-047), the raw vitality of Auburn Avenue is overwhelming:

> The automobile turned from the massive leading business houses into Auburn Avenue lined with blowzy boarding-houses and in the next block on into the Negro business section. (001-009)

The street embodies the ambition of Atlanta's Black elite. Law offices, insurance companies, and banks project an image of respectability and power that stands in deliberate contrast to the Jim Crow restrictions that surround them.

## The Business Elite of Sweet Auburn

The businesses of Auburn Avenue represented an economic ecosystem that was self-contained and self-sustaining.

Mr. Robertson makes this explicit to Jean: "Gene thinks his Creole crowd's stuck-up and exclusive — these Atlanta Negroes'll show him a trick or two" (001-013). The businessmen of Auburn Avenue could "turn a dollar and give you back ninety-nine cents change" (001-007), as Mr. Robertson admiringly put it.

The street's prosperity was intimately linked to segregation. Because white businesses refused to serve Black customers, Black entrepreneurs built parallel institutions — banks, insurance companies, pharmacies, newspapers — that kept capital circulating within the community.

## Jean's Encounter with Auburn Avenue

For [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]], Auburn Avenue offers a vision of Black achievement entirely different from the languid Creole world of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]].

He is particularly struck by the Atlanta-based Negro insurance company: "His judgment vindicated, Mr. Robertson launched into a description of the company operated by Negroes in Atlanta" (002-055). The company's success represents proof of a concept Jean barely dared to believe in — that Black economic independence was possible.

Yet Jean's encounter with Auburn Avenue is also the beginning of his undoing. The aggressive commercialism represented by the street and embodied by Mr. Robertson clashes with everything Jean values. The same economic forces that built Auburn Avenue will, in the novel's logic, draw the Daquins into the path of the race riot.

## A Street of Contradictions

Auburn Avenue is presented with characteristic ambivalence. It is a monument to Black achievement and a product of segregation. It is both inspiring and, in its raw commercial energy, slightly vulgar to Creole sensibilities.

The avenue reappears in quieter moments. Mimi, walking the street alone, encounters Carl Hunter: "she had seen him approaching her on Auburn Avenue" (009-006). The street becomes a stage for the dramas of Atlanta's Black elite — their romances, their rivalries, their fragile prosperity.

## The Riot and Aftermath

During the [[atlanta-race-riot|Atlanta race riot]], the geography of Auburn Avenue becomes a map of destruction. The violence surges "down Peachtree Street, to Auburn Avenue, down Auburn Avenue to Decatur Street" (005-082), sweeping through the heart of the Black business district.

The riot's path along Auburn Avenue is symbolically charged. The street that represented Black economic achievement becomes a corridor of violence, demonstrating the fragility of prosperity under [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]].
