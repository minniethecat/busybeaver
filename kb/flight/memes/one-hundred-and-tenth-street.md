## 1. Geographic Significance

One Hundred and Tenth Street (110th Street) forms the southern boundary of [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] in upper [[new-york-city|Manhattan]]. In the early twentieth century, this street served as a de facto racial dividing line: below it lay the white sections of Manhattan — the [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] mansions, the fashionable shops, the carefully segregated world of white privilege — and above it stretched Black [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], a community in the midst of its cultural and demographic transformation.

## 2. Symbolic Boundary

For [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]], 110th Street represents more than a geographic marker. It is the line between two worlds, two identities, two versions of herself. When she passes as white, she lives below 110th Street in the white sections of the city. When she returns to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], she crosses back above it, re-entering the Black community she had left behind.

> Years later, returning to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] after her years passing as white, Mimi feels the old pull of the neighborhood she had once called home. She crosses 110th Street and "felt the old attraction which had come over her that first night" (`023-024`).

## 3. Fifth Avenue and 110th Street

[[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] runs the length of [[new-york-city|Manhattan]], but its character changes dramatically at 110th Street. Below it, Fifth Avenue is "synonymous with wealth, fashion, and the white upper class — the avenue of mansions, department stores, and the kind of 'carefully groomed and tolerably handsome young men' to be seen 'any afternoon on Fifth Avenue'" (`019-031`). Above it, the avenue enters [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], where it serves a different community with different institutions and a different relationship to the city's power structures.

## 4. A Marker of Racial Geography

The significance of 110th Street as a racial boundary was not unique in early-twentieth-century America. Similar dividing lines existed in other American cities — streets, railroad tracks, parks — that marked the edges of Black neighborhoods and enforced the spatial logic of segregation. In *Flight*, 110th Street embodies this pattern: a line that [[mimi-daquin|Mimi]] crosses in both directions, each crossing marking a transition between two racial identities that the novel explores in depth.
