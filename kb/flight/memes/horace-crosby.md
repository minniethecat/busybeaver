## Overview

Horace Crosby is a wealthy Chicago businessman whose fortune — built through grain market manipulation and stockyard ownership — enables the lavish lifestyle of his wife, Eulalia Crosby. He is described as a Rotary Club member who "had manipulated certain markets in grain, very much to the advantage of the Crosby exchequer" [019-015]. His wealth funds his wife's Paris shopping sprees at Francine's dress shop and her elaborate social entertaining.

Though Horace Crosby remains largely in the background of the narrative, his business relationship with Jimmie Forrester provides the structural link connecting the Crosbys to the Forrester social circle. His extramarital affair with Dolores d'Aubigny, observed by Mimi at his wife's own dinner party, exposes the moral emptiness beneath the surface of the fashionable world Mimi has entered.

## In the Story

### The Crosby Fortune (Chapters 19–22)

Horace Crosby's wealth is the engine of Mrs. Crosby's social life. The "huge Crosby monogram in gilt" on the lavender motorcar [019-017], the Paris shopping expeditions, and the lavish dinner parties all flow from his business success. His brokerage accounts are handled by Jimmie Forrester's firm — "Jimmie does practically all of Horace's buying and selling of stocks or bonds" [019-029] — making the Forrester-Crosby connection a blend of business and social obligation.

### The Dinner Party and the Affair (Chapter 22)

At the Crosbys' dinner for Wu Hseh-Chuan, Mimi observes Horace Crosby with Dolores d'Aubigny, a slim, graceful, beautiful woman who has positioned herself near him. Mimi sees "a shrewd appraisal of the relative merits of his wife, fat and ungainly and too heavily rouged and powdered, and of her who called herself Dolores d'Aubigny, slim and graceful and beautiful" [022-020]. The scene enacts a quiet drama of which Mrs. Crosby remains "wholly unaware" — her husband's wandering eye observed by guests at her own party.

### Mimi's Judgment (Chapter 23)

In a moment of broad moral reflection, Mimi catalogs Horace Crosby among those seeking "release and forgetfulness in an empty, sordid affair with a woman, beautiful though she was, like Dolores d'Aubigny" [023-006]. He represents one more example of the spiritual emptiness Mimi perceives in the world of wealth and whiteness — driven by forces he does not understand, seeking distraction in betrayal.
