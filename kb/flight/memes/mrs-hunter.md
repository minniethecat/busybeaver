## Overview

Mrs. Hunter is a prominent member of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Negro elite in Walter White's *Flight*. As wife of [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]] -- president of the [[lincoln-club|Lincoln Mutual Life Insurance Company]] -- she presides over the social ceremonies of the city's most prestigious colored women's club.

[[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]] calls [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] "the eating brigade" (003-022). Mrs. Hunter is described as "willowy" and embodies the careful management of reputation and class anxiety that defines [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black elite (003-004).

Through Mrs. Hunter, White examines cultural aspirations, insecurities, and elaborate systems of mutual evaluation. She is both a gatekeeper and a prisoner of the social order she helps maintain.

## In the Story

### Hostess of the Fleur-de-Lis Club

Mrs. Hunter first appears presiding over "elaborate ceremonials" (003-004). As hostess, she manages complex social dynamics with practiced grace, though her occasional embarrassment betrays insecurity.

When [[mimi-daquin|Mimi]] mentions the opera *La Boheme* -- the source of her nickname -- Mrs. Hunter is "somewhat embarrassed" and answers that she does not know it (003-007). She then feels "it necessary that she justify herself before this queer infant" (003-009).

### Cultural Aspirations and Anxieties

Mrs. Hunter's cultural world is revealed when she admits that "the only play I've seen was Ben Hur." She confesses that "Mr. Hunter has never forgiven me for climbing up the back stairs to the peanut gallery at the Grand Opera House where the coloured people sit" (003-010).

Her self-deprecating admission, followed by a beaming look at her husband, captures both vulnerability and her performance of wifely devotion (003-011). This mixture of ambition and insecurity defines the class she represents.

### Social Management

Throughout social gatherings, Mrs. Hunter works to manage conversations and maintain decorum. She deflects her husband's more aggressive argumentation, "tired of the argument over" contentious topics (003-016).

When she determines to change the subject, "her eyebrows making a rapid ascent" signal her disapproval and reassert control (003-019). She "turned and surveyed Jean and" Mimi with the appraising gaze of a woman who must place every new face in the social hierarchy (003-020).

## Relationships

Mrs. Hunter's marriage to [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]] is an alliance of social ambition. He serves as president of the [[lincoln-club|Lincoln Mutual Life Insurance Company]] while she runs [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]].

Her relationship with her son, [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]], is strained by generational difference. She defends him to her husband: "Carl's nothing but a child as yet and he and Mildred are all we've got. He'll come round all right" (003-014).

With the Daquin family, Mrs. Hunter is welcoming but always appraising, treating Jean and Mary as additions to be evaluated.
