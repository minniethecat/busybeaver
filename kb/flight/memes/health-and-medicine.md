## Definition

Health and medicine in *Flight* illuminate the precariousness of Black life in early twentieth-century America, where access to medical care was shaped by both racial exclusion and economic constraint. The novel presents medical encounters — pregnancy, childbirth, illness, and death — as sites where the vulnerability of Black bodies is most starkly revealed, while also documenting the emergence of a Black medical profession that served communities excluded from white healthcare institutions.

## Black Physicians and Community Care

The novel features Black physicians as respected community figures. Dr. Adams, who examines [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] when she falls ill and discovers her pregnancy, is described gravely breaking the news: "A few questions, an examination, and then Dr. Adams looked gravely at Mimi" (012-017). His grave expression reflects not medical concern alone but awareness of the social catastrophe that an unmarried pregnancy represented for a young Black woman in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]].

The response of [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] to Mimi's pregnancy — "tomorrow I'll take you to a doctor who'll fix you up" (012-037) — invokes the underground economy of abortion providers that existed in the shadows of respectable medical practice. Mimi's refusal — "I hate him — and if I live to be a thousand years old I'll hate him more every day I live" (012-066) — is simultaneously a moral stand and an assertion of bodily autonomy.

## Childbirth and Public Hospitals

Mimi's delivery of [[petit-jean|Petit Jean]] takes place in a public hospital maternity ward, where she endures "two days and two nights of pain which tore Mimi's body with its burning shafts of agony" (013-015). Her inability to afford a private room — "she had spent her money faster than she had realized" (013-015) — reflects the economic constraints that determined the quality of medical care available to poor Black women. The "lack of privacy and the other inconveniences of the public ward" (013-016) are accepted as the cost of survival.

## Illness as Social Metaphor

The novel uses physical illness as a register for social and emotional distress. Mimi experiences unwanted male attention as "a revulsion within her which swept through her like a physical illness" (013-031), linking bodily vulnerability to the broader condition of being a Black woman in a predatory social world. Conversely, Petit Jean's health — "singularly free from the maladies of childhood" (014-004) — serves as a counterpoint to his mother's suffering, a small mercy in an otherwise unsparing world.

## Health Disparities and Racial Inequality

The novel's medical episodes collectively document the health disparities produced by Jim Crow. Black Atlantans relied on Black physicians because white hospitals and doctors routinely excluded them. Mr. Manning's broken hip, "set" by a doctor who "announced that he would be confined to his bed for many weeks" (013-028), illustrates the economic precariousness that injury could produce in households without savings or insurance. Health in *Flight* is never merely biological — it is always economic, always racial, always political.
