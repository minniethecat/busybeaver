Canal Street is a major thoroughfare in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans, Louisiana]]. In the novel *Flight*, it functions as a powerful social and racial boundary line that divides the city's Creole and American populations.

## Racial Boundary

The street marks a fundamental division in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] society. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]], Mimi's father, makes this explicit when he tells his daughter to "look at the Americans north of Canal Street and you'll see I am right" ([002-053]). His statement reflects the Creole perception of Canal Street as the line separating their refined, French-influenced culture from what they viewed as coarser American society.

> Look at the Americans north of Canal Street and you'll see I am right. They have got the land and the money, but we have the culture and the blood. [002-053]

## Real Estate and Commerce

Canal Street also figures in the economic life of the Creole community. Mr. Laroux, "the real-estate man on Canal Street" ([002-073]), represents the business transactions that take place along this commercial corridor. His telephone call to [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] signals the connection between property, commerce, and the street's role as a hub of Creole economic activity.

## Social Distinction

The women who lived "north of Canal Street" ([010-042]) are referenced as belonging to a different social category — Americans rather than Creoles. This geographic marker encodes the racial and cultural hierarchies that structure [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] life and that ultimately drive Mimi's family to leave the city.
