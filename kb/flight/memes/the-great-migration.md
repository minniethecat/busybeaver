## Definition

The Great Migration refers to the mass movement of African Americans from the rural South to northern cities between approximately 1910 and 1940. In *Flight*, it captures the geographic displacement shaping [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s life.

Mimi moves from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], and finally into white [[new-york-city|New York]]. Each move represents both escape and loss.

The novel presents migration as a recurring structure in Black life. The "railroad flats" of [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] become Mimi's metaphor. She feels she is "always opening the door of another room, passing through it, then opening and closing behind her, never to be reopened, the door of the next cubicle" (017-001).

## Historical Context

The Great Migration was driven by push and pull forces. The violent enforcement of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] in the South pushed Black families northward, as exemplified by the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot of 1906]]. Economic opportunity in northern industrial cities pulled them. The novel captures both forces in the Robertson family history.

Mary Robertson's father accumulated considerable wealth through speculation in "Chicago's fast-growing Negro settlement on the south side" (002-031). The migration created both wealth and precariousness for Black families, as real estate values soared while living conditions frequently remained poor.

The novel also portrays the cultural consequences of migration. [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], with its Creole traditions, gives way to the more rigid racial order of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], which in turn contrasts with the relative freedom of [[new-york-city|New York]]. Each move forces Mimi to renegotiate her identity in a new social landscape.

## In the Novel

Mimi's multiple migrations structure the novel's geography and emotional arc. The opening scene establishes the pattern immediately: "the long train rumbled and swayed, whistle blowing intermittently, screeching discordantly" past segregated neighborhoods (001-001). The train, with its segregated cars and abrupt transitions between landscapes, becomes the vehicle of Black geographic mobility.

Each destination carries its own social logic. [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] represents Creole cosmopolitanism and childhood innocence, a world where Creole identity offered an alternative to the Black-white binary.

[[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] is the crucible of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] violence -- the race riot that drives the family away and scars Mimi's racial consciousness. It is also where she first encounters the intraracial color hierarchy of the Black elite.

[[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] represents a failed attempt at respectability and the perils of visibility as a light-skinned Black woman. There Mimi struggles alone with work and motherhood.

[[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] offers community and possibility but ultimately subjects Mimi to the same intraracial policing she experienced in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. Through her aunt, she witnesses "the transition she saw going on around her in Harlem -- gradually yet surely there was being builded a Negro city here in northern Manhattan" (015-042).

[[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]], Jean's sister in [[new-york-city|New York]], represents the promise and reality of the migration. She has built a comfortable life in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], becoming part of its social fabric. When she urges Mimi to come to [[new-york-city|New York]] -- "I've been pretty lonely since Henry died" (011-051) -- she offers both escape and new constraints.

Mimi's life trajectory embodies the migration's central paradox: each new destination promises freedom but delivers its own form of confinement. The novel's closing movement -- Mimi passing into white New York, cut off from her family and community -- dramatizes the ultimate cost of geographic displacement.
