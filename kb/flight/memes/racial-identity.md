## Definition

Racial identity in *Flight* is presented not as a fixed biological category but as a social construction that characters must continually negotiate. The novel traces the formation of [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s racial consciousness from her childhood in [[new-orleans-louisiana|Creole New Orleans]], through the trauma of the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot, to her ultimate decision to abandon her racial identity altogether by passing for white. Each stage of this journey reveals racial identity as something produced by social pressures rather than inherent in the individual.

The novel's understanding of racial identity is deeply influenced by the Creole perspective that [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] represents: the belief that \"a Negro is known by his ability and not by the colour of his skin\" (002-096). This philosophy challenges the American racial binary from within, suggesting that identity is a matter of achievement rather than ascription — a view that the novel both celebrates and complicates through Mimi's experiences.

## Historical Context

The early 20th century was a period of intense contestation over racial identity in America. The \"one-drop rule\" — the legal principle that any person with any known African ancestry was classified as Black — was being codified into law across the South. At the same time, the emerging discipline of anthropology was challenging scientific racism, and the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] Renaissance was producing new forms of Black cultural self-expression.

Within Black communities, identity was negotiated along multiple axes: skin color, class, education, region, and culture. The contrast between [[new-orleans-louisiana|Creole New Orleans]] and Anglo [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] illustrates this complexity. Where Jean sees cultural achievement, Mary sees exclusion; where Mary sees opportunity, Jean sees color-consciousness and social climbing (001-013).

## In the Novel

Mimi's racial identity develops in three stages. In childhood, she absorbs her father's Creole pride without fully understanding the racial logic that surrounds her. She is \"used to the Creole dilution of the Negro songs\" (001-047) — her cultural world is hybrid, Creole rather than simply Black or white.

The [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot shatters this innocence. Mimi's \"intense consciousness of race which her experiences in [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] at [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] had engendered in her\" (017-006) marks the beginning of a new stage in which racial identity is experienced as vulnerability and threat. Her light skin, once a source of Creole pride, becomes a potential escape route.

The final stage — passing — represents both the culmination and the negation of Mimi's racial identity journey. By presenting herself as white, she achieves a kind of freedom, but the novel refuses to present this as liberation. Her passing is framed as a forced choice, not a voluntary transformation: \"the very intolerance of her own people had driven her from them\" (017-007).
