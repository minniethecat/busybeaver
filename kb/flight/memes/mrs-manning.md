Mrs. Manning is an elderly caterer in Philadelphia who, together with her husband [[mr-manning|Mr. Manning]], takes [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] into their home after the birth of her son Petit Jean. She embodies maternal kindness and practical generosity during one of the most vulnerable periods of Mimi's life.

## In the Story

### Welcoming Mimi

Mrs. Manning first appears glancing significantly at Mimi's left hand, gently trying to learn more about the young woman's circumstances [013-009]. When Mimi confesses her financial difficulties, Mrs. Manning reassures her without hesitation:

> "Don't you worry one little bit, honey. You'll never have to worry about rent or food — so long's we've got anything to eat and a roof over our heads you've them too." [013-019]

### Daily Care

Mrs. Manning and her husband devised plans to help Mimi earn a living as a seamstress, but on various pretexts they deferred her start of work, reluctant to push her toward independence before she was ready [013-023]. Mrs. Manning cared for Petit Jean during the day when Mimi needed to be away, and she accidentally touched a painful nerve when she innocently referred to Mimi's "late husband" — never with malice, but with a lack of suspicion that made Mimi's hidden truth all the more difficult to bear [013-026].

### After the Accident

When Mr. Manning slipped on ice and broke his hip, Mrs. Manning became the primary caregiver for both her husband and her household [013-028]. Mimi stayed as long as she was needed, unwilling to desert Mrs. Manning when her help was most required, but eventually found a boarding place and independent work [013-028]. Mrs. Manning's kindness became the measure against which Mimi judged subsequent hardships — "Mimi realized now how kind the Mannings had really been to her" [013-029].

## Key Moments
