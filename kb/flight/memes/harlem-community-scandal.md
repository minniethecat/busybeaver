## Overview

The scandal over [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s relationship with [[jerry-reed|Jerry Reed]], a married man, is the event that drives her from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]]. The novel treats the scandal with restraint — it is narrated primarily through its effects rather than its details — but its consequences are devastating. Mimi's reputation is destroyed, her social position becomes untenable, and she concludes that there is no path forward within the Black community.

The scandal illustrates the novel's critique of respectability politics and intraracial community policing. Mimi is judged harshly for a transgression that [[jerry-reed|Jerry]] — as a man — seems to weather more easily. The gendered double standard compounds the injustice, and Mimi's bitterness is directed not at white racism but at her own people: what's happened here will just keep on happening every place I go (016-060).
