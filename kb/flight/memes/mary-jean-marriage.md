## Overview

The marriage of [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] — a proudly Creole tailor from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] — to Mary Robertson — a light-skinned Black woman from [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] — is a union that bridges two very different racial worlds. To the marriage there arose storms of protest from the relatives and friends of both Mary and Jean (002-044). The Creole community viewed Mary as an outsider, not of Creole blood, while Mary's [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] family regarded Jean's Creole pride as pretentious and exclusionary.

The marriage embodies the novel's interest in the collision of different racial cultures. Jean's Creole philosophy — that ability matters more than color — clashes with Mary's Anglo-American sensibility, which is more attuned to the practical realities of the color line. Their union produces the blended family at the novel's center, with Mimi caught between her father's Creole world and her stepmother's American pragmatism.
