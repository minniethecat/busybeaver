## Definition

Gender and race in *Flight* are not separate categories of experience but intersecting systems that shape every aspect of the characters' lives. The novel's focus on [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] — a woman navigating the color line — reveals how racial passing carried different meanings and consequences for women than for men. For a woman like Mimi, passing offered not only economic opportunity but also protection from the sexual vulnerability that accompanied Black womanhood in the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] era.

The novel presents Black women's experience as doubly constrained: by the sexism of both white and Black society, and by the racism that made Black women particularly vulnerable to exploitation. [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]]'s ejection from the theater, the gossip about Hilda's mother, and ultimately Mimi's own scandal all demonstrate how the intersection of race and gender policed Black women's behavior with particular intensity.

## Historical Context

The early 20th century saw the emergence of organized Black women's activism through the National Association of Colored Women, which adopted the motto \"Lifting as We Climb.\" Black women reformers like Mary Church Terrell and Ida B. Wells-Barnett fought simultaneously against racism and sexism, developing an analysis that anticipated later theories of intersectionality.

The [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]] in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], despite the novel's critical portrayal of its members' pettiness, represents a real historical institution: the Black women's club movement. These clubs provided spaces for Black women to assert their respectability and build community, even as they policed the boundaries of acceptable behavior.

## In the Novel

[[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s journey through the novel is shaped at every turn by her gender. The scandal that destroys her reputation in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] — a relationship with a married man — would not have had the same consequences for a Black man. Her decision to pass is framed in specifically gendered terms: she will work as a finisher at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], a feminized occupation that enables her to pass undetected (016-060).

The novel's treatment of [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] reveals the intersectional policing of Black women's bodies and behavior. [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] is punished twice: first by the white theater manager who ejects her, then by her own community's gossip. The women who mock her are themselves subject to the same racial logic — their cruelty toward [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] can be read as displaced anger at a system they cannot directly challenge (004-009).

[[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson Daquin]], Mimi's stepmother, illustrates another dimension of gender and race. Her [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] background makes her an outsider in [[new-orleans-louisiana|Creole New Orleans]]; her desire for social status in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] makes her vulnerable to the pettiness of [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club. She is judged both for her racial position (not Creole enough) and her gender performance (not genteel enough) (002-044).
