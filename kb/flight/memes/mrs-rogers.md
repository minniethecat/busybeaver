## Overview

Mrs. Rogers — called Aunt Sophie by Mimi — is [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s sister, living in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] after the death of her husband.

She enters the novel after Jean's death, arriving in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] for the funeral and offering Mimi a new home in [[new-york-city|New York]]. Her invitation — "Won't you go back to New York with me?" (011-047) — opens the door to Mimi's final migration.

Mrs. Rogers represents the family networks that sustained the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]]. Like countless other Black women, she provided housing, emotional support, and practical assistance to relatives making the journey north. Her [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] home becomes Mimi's sanctuary.

## Arrival in Atlanta

Mrs. Rogers arrives in Atlanta the morning after Jean's death. The scene is marked by quiet dignity and restrained grief:

> Early the next morning Mrs. Rogers came. As she entered the house she glanced swiftly about. Then she looked keenly at Mimi. "Good morning, Mimi," she said quietly, and walked over and kissed Mimi lightly on the forehead. (011-039)

She does not weep or wail. Instead, she sizes up the situation with practical clarity. Her first concern is for Mimi's welfare, not for displays of grief. This composure — the ability to face loss without breaking — is one of her defining traits.

## Life in Harlem

In [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], Mrs. Rogers provides the stability Mimi has lacked since leaving [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]]. Her home becomes a gathering place for Harlem's respectable middle class:

> The room was comfortably furnished, the dinner was deliciously cooked, and the guests were well-dressed, prosperous in appearance. All of them were pleasant to Mimi. (016-003)

Mrs. Rogers is well-regarded in the community. Her niece's presence attracts attention: "That's Miss Daquin — she's Mrs. Rogers' niece and she's very popular" (016-032). Through Mrs. Rogers's connections, Mimi gains access to a social world that combines respectability with a measure of freedom.

Mrs. Rogers has lived near the Marshall — Harlem's famous Black hotel and theater — and she takes Mimi on tours of the neighborhood (015-043). She introduces Mimi to the institutions of Harlem life: churches, social clubs, the network of mutual aid that sustained the community.

## Wisdom and Worldliness

Mrs. Rogers possesses a practical wisdom earned through experience. She offers Mimi counsel on men, marriage, and the compromises of adult life:

> Mrs. Rogers smiled. "You say that now. But wait until you fall in love — you'll forget all about freedom and independence." (015-031)

She speaks frankly about her own marriage, acknowledging its difficulties:

> "Take men, for example. Mr. Rogers and I had our times of disagreement." (015-035)

And yet she affirms the value of the life she has lived: "Yes, I suppose it can," Mrs. Rogers frankly admitted. "But life's that way — a mixture of good and bad and you've got to take the mixture" (015-039).

## The Crisis of Passing

When [[harlem-scandal|Mimi's scandal]] breaks, Mrs. Rogers is the person to whom Mimi confides her decision to pass. The scene is one of the novel's most emotionally complex passages:

> She had not wanted to leave Harlem. Mrs. Rogers had argued, pleaded, almost wept, but Mimi was firm. (017-005)

Mrs. Rogers's love for Mimi is evident, but so is her powerlessness. She cannot protect her niece from the community's judgment, cannot offer an alternative to the course Mimi has chosen. Her final gesture is one of sorrowful witness:

> Mrs. Rogers watched Mimi with worry that she would not admit even to herself. Younger people came and she preferred associating with young people — it keeps me young, too — but Mimi had become a part and a very important part of the household. (016-059)

## Significance

Mrs. Rogers is one of the few consistently benevolent figures in the novel. She asks nothing of Mimi except her presence, offers stability without demanding conformity.

Yet her benevolence has limits. The community whose respect she has earned is also the community that will drive Mimi out. Mrs. Rogers embodies the strengths of Black institutional life — family, church, mutual aid — but she cannot transcend its constraints.

Her final appearance in the novel is as a memory, a figure from the life Mimi has abandoned. The break is irrevocable, and Mrs. Rogers, like [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] itself, becomes part of the past Mimi cannot reclaim.
