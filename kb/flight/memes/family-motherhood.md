## Definition

Family and motherhood in *Flight* function as central structuring forces that both sustain and constrain the novel's characters. The concept encompasses biological and step-relationships, Creole family traditions, the protective bond between fathers and daughters, and the experience of single motherhood. Across the novel, family is depicted not as a simple source of comfort but as a site of tension — between tradition and modernity, between blood ties and social ambition, and between the desire to protect and the need to let go.

## Family Structures in the Novel

The Daquin family household in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] embodies the tension between Creole tradition and American social climbing. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] represents the old Creole values — pride in ancestry, contentment with modest means, and a philosophy that "a Negro is known by his ability and not by the colour of his skin" (002-025). His second wife, [[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson Daquin]], brought from [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] an entirely different set of values: social ambition, material display, and what Jean saw as "her petty shopkeeper attitude, her scorn for traditions so dear to Jean and herself" (002-085).

[[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s biological mother died when Mimi was very young (002-025), and Mary Robertson's arrival as stepmother fundamentally altered the household. Mimi's resistance to calling Mary "mother" — "Stop calling her my mother! She's only a stepmother!" (002-018) — reflects not merely adolescent rebellion but a deeper conflict between Creole identity and the assimilationist values Mary represented.

## Fatherhood and Protection

Jean Daquin's relationship with Mimi exemplifies the protective fatherhood that permeates the novel. Gazing at his sleeping daughter, Jean felt his "eyes half filled with joy at her warm beauty, half with troubled anxiety" (002-008). His daily prayer — "Holy Mother, keep me alive — not for life alone, sweet as it is for me, but that I may be with her to guide her steps and protect her!" (002-009) — articulates the central anxiety of Black parenthood in the Jim Crow era: the fear of being unable to shield one's child from racial violence.

Jean's protective intentions, however, could not prevent Mimi's subsequent experiences. After his death, the family unit dissolved — Mary returned to Chicago, and Mimi was left to navigate [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] society alone, pregnant and ostracized (012-017).

## Motherhood Across Generations

The novel presents motherhood in multiple registers. [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]]'s fierce protectiveness over her son [[carl-hunter|Carl]] — "His father and I are terribly worried about him" (009-027) — illustrates maternal anxiety in a respectable Black family confronting a son's waywardness. Her plea to Mimi — "Oh, please, Mimi, ask him again and again until he does come. You can save him — if you will" (009-040) — reveals the desperation of a mother who has exhausted her own authority.

Mimi's own experience of motherhood reverses this dynamic. After giving birth to [[petit-jean|Petit Jean]], she becomes a single mother, placing her son in an orphanage and later with a family in Westchester County, directing "the training he should receive" (019-008). Her "ceaseless longing to have him near — aching and unappeasable yearning for him that could not be stilled" (019-008) echoes Jean Daquin's earlier prayer, completing a generational cycle of parental love thwarted by circumstance.
