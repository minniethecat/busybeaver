## Definition

Fashion and self-presentation in *Flight* serve as visible markers of class position, cultural identity, and social aspiration. Clothing communicates belonging — or its absence — in a society where racial identity is visually ambiguous but class must be performed. For [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]], dressmaking becomes both a means of economic survival and a medium of creative self-expression, linking her Creole aesthetic heritage to the commercial world of [[new-york-city|New York]] fashion.

## Clothing as Class Performance

The novel's attention to clothing is precise and diagnostic. [[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson Daquin]]'s elaborate costume for her first [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]] meeting — "her lightly fitting waist with high neck supported by whalebone stays, her long skirt flaring wide and with a train were all of the latest design" (004-002) — reveals her determination to establish herself as "one of the elect" through sartorial display. Her "modified leg-o'-mutton sleeves" and "half-mittens of black lace" (004-002) constitute a kind of armor against the scrutiny of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black elite.

The observer's gaze is reciprocal: the Fleur-de-Lis women examine each other with the same scrutiny. [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]] catalogues the attendees by their appearance — "that skinny one over there," "that one with the gold tooth who laughs all the time" (004-022) — while Mimi herself becomes an object of admiration: "a pale yellow confection of lawn" (004-002).

## Dressmaking as Vocation

Mimi's sewing skills, initially a genteel accomplishment, become the foundation of her economic independence. "She could sew rather well" (013-021) — this modest claim opens the path to survival in [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]]. Her gift is described sensuously: "She loved the feel of silks and satins and chiffons; the finer the fabric, the more she liked to handle it and make it into alluring garments" (015-041).

Her trajectory from day seamstress to design student to forewoman "in one of the most exclusive shops in New York" (016-012) traces the transformation of a domestic skill into professional expertise. The novel's attention to the materiality of fabrics — the "silks and satins and chiffons" — suggests that aesthetic pleasure is not frivolous but sustaining, a form of beauty that resists the ugliness of racial oppression.

## Fashion and Racial Passing

Clothing plays a crucial role in Mimi's eventual passing as white. Appearance — the right dress, the right hat, the right presentation — enables the crossing of the color line. The novel suggests that identity is not merely worn but produced through dress; the clothes make the woman visible in a particular way, determining which self the world sees.
