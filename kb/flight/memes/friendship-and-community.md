## Definition

Friendship and community in *Flight* constitute the social infrastructure through which characters navigate the challenges of racial oppression, economic precarity, and personal crisis. The novel traces relationships of varying depth and authenticity — from the strategic socializing of the [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]] to the genuine bond between [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] and [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]] — revealing community as both a source of sustenance and a site of surveillance.

## Adolescent Friendship: Mimi and Hilda

The friendship between Mimi and Hilda Adams provides one of the novel's few portraits of uncalculated affection. Hilda, "who stood silent just beyond her mother" (004-002), becomes Mimi's guide to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] society, explaining the intricate social hierarchies at the Fleur-de-Lis Club gathering. Their bond deepens through shared experience: "the long hours they spent together deepened the love they had for each other" (005-009), a phrase the novel uses to describe both Mimi's friendship with Hilda and her romance with [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]], suggesting the porous boundary between friendship and romantic attachment in adolescence.

Yet even this friendship is strained by the social dynamics that structure Black Atlanta. Hilda's unrequited love for Carl — "she shrank in terror from her own thoughts" (009-004) — creates an unspoken tension that neither girl can address directly.

## Community as Surveillance

The novel's title, *Flight*, names a central dynamic: the impossibility of escaping community judgment. Gossip functions as a mechanism of social control, particularly over women's behavior. Mrs. Plummer and Mrs. King's discussion of the newly arrived Daquin family — "A body'd think she'd have mo' sense than to do her dirt so bold like!" (001-026) — establishes the community's appetite for scandal before Mimi has even settled in Atlanta.

After Mimi's pregnancy becomes known, the community's judgment is merciless. "Scandal thrives only when it is retailed to those with open ears and credulous minds" (016-042), the narrator observes, and only Hilda and Mrs. Adams champion Mimi's cause. The novel thus presents a double-edged portrait of community: those who sustain are few, while those who judge are many.

## Mutual Aid and Survival

Against this backdrop, the novel documents genuine acts of community care. The Mannings in [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] take Mimi in during her pregnancy, and Aunt Sophie in [[new-york-city|New York]] provides the practical support that enables Mimi's economic recovery. These relationships, grounded in kinship and shared circumstance rather than social performance, represent the community at its best.
