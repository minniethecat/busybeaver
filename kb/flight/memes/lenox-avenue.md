## Location

Lenox Avenue is the principal north-south artery of [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], running through the heart of the neighborhood from Central Park North to the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] River. In the early decades of the twentieth century, it was the commercial and cultural spine of what was rapidly becoming the largest Black urban community in the United States. By the time [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] arrived in [[new-york-city|New York]], [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] -- which "first had been Dutch 'Haarlem,' then Irish, afterwards Jewish and German" -- was "in the flood-tide of transition to a Negro city within a city" (`015-017`). The avenue was lined with shops, churches, beauty parlors, and theaters, and its sidewalks were a constant "shifting panorama of life" (`015-017`).

The intersection of Lenox Avenue and 135th Street marked the epicenter of [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], with the subway station at that corner serving as the gateway for tens of thousands of Southern and Caribbean migrants arriving in uptown [[new-york-city|Manhattan]].

## In the Novel

> Down Lenox Avenue they walked. Harlem, which first had been Dutch "Haarlem," then Irish, afterwards Jewish and German, was in the flood-tide of transition to a Negro city within a city. `015-017`

Lenox Avenue is Mimi's introduction to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] and, through [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], to a new conception of what it means to be a Negro in America. She first walks down the avenue on the evening of her arrival in [[new-york-city|New York]], accompanied by her [[mrs-rogers|Aunt Sophie]] and [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]], having taken the subway to [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue and 135th Street]] (`015-017`). The scene overwhelms her: "The colourful scenes fascinated her, made her want to stay there and watch the comedies, the tragedies, the shifting panorama of life as it flowed, now swiftly, now slowly, by" (`015-017`). From a side street doorway near the bustling avenue, she hears "a resonant flooding swell of music" -- a baritone voice leading a group of singers, "chopping off of the words like little sausages, setting the beat like a metronome" (`015-017`). This is her first direct encounter with the living culture of [[harlem-new-york|Black Harlem]], and it stirs something in her that [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] and [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] never did.

She would live near Lenox Avenue, in a "small flat" that her [[mrs-rogers|Aunt Sophie]]'s sister-in-law kept (`015-024`), and the street became part of the geography of her new [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] life -- a life of work, study, and the thrill of being part of a community where colored people ran their own institutions, published their own newspapers, and built their own world.

Years later, returning to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] after her years passing as white in [[westchester-county|Westchester]] and the city below [[one-hundred-and-tenth-street|110th Street]], Mimi feels the old pull of Lenox Avenue. She debarks at "[[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue and 135th Street]]" (`023-024`) and "felt the old attraction which had come over her that first night" (`023-024`). In that moment she realizes why she was drawn back to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]]: it was the statement of a Chinese scholar, [[wu-hseh-chuan|Wu Hseh-Chuan]], who had told her that "only your Negroes have successfully resisted mechanization" (`023-024`). Lenox Avenue thus becomes not only a physical destination but the symbol of a racial authenticity that Mimi, after her long exile in the white world, yearns to reclaim.
