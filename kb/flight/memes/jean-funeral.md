# Funeral of Jean Daquin

The death and funeral of [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]], Mimi's father and primary emotional anchor, whose passing in late October leaves Mimi without her protector and alters her place in the Daquin [[home|household]] and [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] community.

## Overview

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s death in the late autumn of an unspecified year is the novel's most profound personal [[death|loss]] for Mimi. After years of decline — physical, emotional, and spiritual — Jean succumbs to illness, his passing marked by the peaceful smile that remains on his face (011-004). His death strips Mimi of her strongest advocate, leaving her alone with her stepmother, Mary Daquin, with whom she has a strained relationship.

The funeral becomes a stage on which the tensions and hypocrisies of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black community are displayed. The whispering gossips at the wake, [[mrs-plummer|Mrs. Plummer]] and Mrs. King, demonstrate the cruelty Mimi will face from the very community that should support her in grief. Mimi's eruption at them — "Get out! Get out! Get out!" (011-033) — is her first open confrontation with the community that her father had navigated with quiet dignity.

## Narrative Context

The death and funeral occupy chapter 11. The event occurs in the context of Jean's long decline — he had never fully recovered from the trauma of the 1906 [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot and had grown increasingly isolated from his wife and community. His final conversation with Mimi, in which he tells her "You'll come through all right" (011-008), provides the emotional foundation for her survival after his death.

The funeral also introduces Mrs. Sophie Rogers, Jean's sister from [[new-york-city|New York]], who arrives to provide both family representation and a new possibility for Mimi's future. [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]] offers Mimi a home in [[new-york-city|New York]], predicting that "up North you'd have a much better chance than here" (011-051). This offer will become crucial when Mimi's life in the South becomes untenable.

## Aftermath

Jean's death has immediate and long-term consequences. Mimi uses part of her inheritance to purchase stock in [[lincoln-mutual-life|Lincoln Mutual Life Insurance Company]] (012-007), demonstrating her financial prudence. More importantly, the [[death|loss]] of Jean's moderating influence accelerates the deterioration of Mimi's relationship with Mary Daquin and sets the stage for her eventual departure from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]].
