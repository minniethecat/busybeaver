## Overview

The Chicago Defender was the most influential Black newspaper in the United States during the early 20th century. Founded by Robert S. Abbott in 1905, the Defender reached a national readership through its network of Pullman porters who distributed the paper throughout the South. It was the leading journalistic voice advocating for the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]], encouraging African Americans to leave the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] South for economic opportunity and greater personal freedom in northern cities.

In *Flight*, the Defender is part of the broader context of Black journalism that countered the inflammatory narratives of white newspapers like the [[atlanta-constitution-newspaper|Atlanta Constitution]]. The Defender's call for migration resonates with the novel's geographical trajectory, as [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] and her family — like millions of other African Americans — move from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], then to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] and [[new-york-city|New York]], tracing the migration routes the paper championed.

## In the Novel

The Chicago Defender is not named directly in the novel, but its influence and perspective form part of the novel's historical context. The paper's advocacy for the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]] provides an implicit framework for understanding Mimi's repeated displacements — from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] after the race riot, from [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] after scandal, and finally from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] itself.

The Defender's role in publicizing the atrocities of the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot of 1906]] and similar events across the South gave Black southerners concrete information about both the dangers of staying and the possibilities of leaving. The paper's [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] headquarters also represents a northern urban center where Black political and economic power was beginning to coalesce, offering an alternative to the [[new-york-city|New York]] that Mimi ultimately chooses.

The novel's attention to the role of newspapers — both white sensationalist papers and the emerging Black press — reflects the early 20th-century understanding that media was a crucial battleground in the fight for racial justice. The Defender embodied the counter-narrative that [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] could only articulate in private conversations.
