# The Harlem Scandal

The community scandal in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] that destroys [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s social standing after her relationship with [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] — the married man she followed from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] — becomes public knowledge through gossip and the press.

## Overview

The Harlem scandal is the crisis that precipitates [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s decision to pass for white. After Mimi reconnects with [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]], who is now married, the Black community that had embraced her turns against her with devastating swiftness. The scandal is amplified by *The Blabber*, a weekly gossip publication "modelled upon Town Topics" that devotes its pages to "not too cryptic references, almost invariably scandalous ones, to persons of prominence" (016-045).

The experience teaches Mimi the ferocity of community judgment. "The averted glances of those who had been her friends, the ostentatious turning of heads when she passed on the street those with whom she had been on terms of intimate acquaintanceship, the sudden death of conversations at her approach" (016-053) — these small cruelties become unbearable. The scandal transforms Mimi from an accepted member of [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] society into a pariah, demonstrating the community's capacity for cruelty that the novel has been documenting since [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club meeting.

## Narrative Context

The scandal unfolds in chapter 16. Mimi's aunt, [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]] — "in business, well known, and with enemies gained through the frank comment and eyes with which Mimi's aunt saw readily through pretence and sham" (016-044) — is collateral damage, her enemies using Mimi's disgrace to strike at her. [[mrs-plummer|Mrs. Plummer]], the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] gossip who reemerges in [[new-york-city|New York]], provides "a complete, more than complete, account of Mimi and her life" (016-035).

The scandal's most painful dimension is its public character. The *Blabber* publishes a paragraph announcing that "All [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] is agog" over "hitherto hidden chapters" in Mimi's life, asking "what the charming miss has done with the baby" (016-047). The reference to [[petit-jean|Petit Jean]] — Mimi's illegitimate son — makes the scandal irrevocable. No private apology or discreet withdrawal can undo what has been printed and circulated.

Mimi's response is decisive: "I'm leaving [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], leaving coloured people for good" (016-062). The scandal thus becomes the catalyst for the novel's climactic action — Mimi's decision to cross the color line permanently.

## Aftermath

The scandal drives Mimi to apply to [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], secure employment as a white woman, and relocate to a rooming house far from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]]. Her career success and romantic life as a white woman — the novel's final act — are direct consequences of the community's rejection. The scandal thus represents both a traumatic rupture and the condition of possibility for Mimi's subsequent life.
