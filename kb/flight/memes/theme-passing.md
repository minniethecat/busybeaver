# Passing as a Spectrum of Racial Strategies

## Overview

In Walter White's *Flight*, racial passing is not a binary choice but a spectrum of strategies through which African Americans navigated the American color line in the early twentieth century. Mimi Daquin's trajectory encompasses the full range: from her Creole family's insistence on visible respectability in New Orleans, to her eventual "passing from the race" [017-007] as a white woman in Manhattan and Westchester, to her ultimate return to Harlem. The novel depicts passing not merely as individual deception but as a structural response to Jim Crow segregation — a survival mechanism that carries profound psychological costs, including isolation from the Black community and constant fear of exposure. Simultaneously, the novel presents the Creole alternative as a form of visible Black identity maintained alongside class distinction, suggesting that passing exists on a continuum of racial self-presentation strategies rather than as a discrete act.

## The Creole Baseline: Visible Identity with Class Distinction

Before Mimi ever considers passing, she is formed by the Creole community of New Orleans, which had developed its own distinctive racial strategy: acknowledged Black ancestry combined with fierce pride in cultural heritage and social standing. Jean Daquin, Mimi's father, instilled in her a deep pride in Creole lineage. As the novel explains:

> "The white Louisianian will tell you the Creole is white with ancestry of French or Spanish origin... the Negro will tell you the Creole is a Negro of French or Spanish ancestry but whose skin, by some alchemy, is white" [002-096]

This ambiguous racial positioning created a community that "militantly proud of their Creole and Negro ancestry, yielded not an inch to the Anglo-Saxon" [002-046]. Jean Daquin himself traced his descent from "a Negro from San Domingo" [002-050] with pride. However, this Creole strategy was place-bound. As Jean warned Mimi:

> "You can afford to laugh here in Creole New Orleans... But away from New Orleans I don't know — I don't know" [002-088]

The Creole approach — living visibly as people of color while maintaining cultural and class distinction — formed Mimi's baseline understanding of racial identity and made both the attractions and the costs of passing more visible to her.

## The Structural Context: Jim Crow and the Logic of Passing

The novel carefully establishes that passing is not an individual moral failing but a rational response to systematic oppression. The Jim Crow regime permeated every institution: "in the street cars white people should sit from the front of the car towards the rear and coloured people should start occupying seats from the rear towards the front" [005-010]. In Atlanta, the Grand Opera House relegated "coloured people" to the peanut gallery [003-010]. Even churches enforced segregation — when Mimi's stepmother inquired about a Catholic church in Atlanta, she was told "there's no coloured Catholic church here" [003-025], and Jean feared they "might be forced to sit in a segregated section of the church" [004-048].

Given this context, passing represented access to life's ordinary dignities — to sit anywhere in a theater, to shop without humiliation, to work according to one's abilities rather than one's race. The novel presents Mimi's decision to pass not as racial betrayal but as a response to a society that made Black identity a barrier to full participation. As the narrator observes of Mimi's state of mind before her transition: "Thus her passing from the race seemed to Mimi" a natural consequence rather than a deliberate choice [017-007].

## Mimi's Passing Journey: From Visibility to Whiteness

Mimi's passage into the white world occurs gradually rather than as a single decisive break. After her failed effort to establish herself in Harlem, where she had embraced the Black community under the guidance of her aunt Mrs. Rogers, she enters lower Manhattan's commercial world. A key moment comes when a former Harlem acquaintance recognizes her at work and asks: "isn't that the same girl from Atlanta who was working down town, passing for white?" [016-060]

The novel describes how complete her transformation becomes: after her passing, "she was not far distant from Harlem in miles but in other ways she might as well have been a thousand miles away" [017-002]. She enters the workroom at Francine's, a high-end dressmaking establishment, where her new identity is never questioned. Madame Francine's establishment becomes her gateway into white society — a world where her "ivory-white" complexion [016-009] and reddish-brown hair rendered her racial origins invisible to the casual observer.

Her marriage to Jimmie Forrester — a white businessman she meets in Paris — represents the apotheosis of her passing strategy. The narrator describes Jimmie's introduction: "Jimmie Forrester — James H. Forrester was the name on his calling card" [019-031]. Through this marriage, Mimi achieves what passing ultimately promises: complete absorption into the white world, financial security, and social position. She places her son "with a family in Westchester County" [019-008], the quintessential white suburb, ensuring his future would never be constrained by race.

## The Psychological Cost of Passing

Throughout the novel, White emphasizes the psychological toll of racial passing. Even at the height of her success, Mimi lives in fear of discovery. When she encounters someone who knew her from Harlem, she experiences acute anxiety:

> "Mrs. Rogers had pleaded, argued, almost commanded her not to go... she had on numerous occasions narrowly missed being exposed" [019-011]

The fear extends to social situations where Mimi cannot fully relax. At dinner parties, "all the while she searched the faces of the guests for some one who might have known or seen her when she lived in Harlem and who might start talking" [019-010]. The requirement of constant vigilance — of never being able to speak freely about one's past, one's family, one's childhood — constitutes a form of ongoing psychological confinement.

The novel also depicts the isolating effect of passing on Mimi's relationship with the Black community. She becomes "cut off from her own people" [016-014] and experiences the peculiar loneliness of living among people who would reject her if they knew her true identity. The narrator captures this erasure of personal history:

> "Even her years in Philadelphia began to fade into the land of things that had never been" [016-002]

Passing, in this light, does not merely obscure the past — it annihilates it, severing the continuity of self that memory provides.

## Passing's Failure and the Return to Harlem

Despite the apparent success of Mimi's passing — financial security, social position, a husband who adores her — the novel does not endorse passing as a sustainable or fulfilling life strategy. Jimmie Forrester's death represents the collapse of the entire edifice of her white identity. Without him, the Westchester life, the Paris shopping trips, the security of white respectability all dissolve.

Mimi's return to Harlem in the final chapter represents not defeat but reconciliation. When asked by her white companions about visiting "a Negro cabaret in Harlem" [023-022], Mimi's response is complex. She had suggested Harlem herself, and the narrator notes:

> "It had been nearly ten years since she had seen a Negro cabaret in Harlem" [023-023]

This return is not the triumphant homecoming of a prodigal daughter but something more subtle — an acknowledgment that the self she had constructed through passing was ultimately unsustainable, and that her authentic identity required reconnection with the community she had left behind.

## The Creole Alternative in Retrospect

Throughout Mimi's journey, the novel implicitly contrasts her passing strategy with the Creole approach of her New Orleans origins. Creole identity — as practiced by Jean Daquin and his circle — involved asserting one's dignity and worth while remaining visibly within the colored community. This strategy had its own costs: the Creoles were perceived as "stuck-up and exclusive" [001-013] by other Black communities, and their strategy was geographically limited, unable to survive the journey from New Orleans to Atlanta.

Yet the novel suggests that the Creole model — of pride in identity rather than flight from it — contains wisdom that Mimi ultimately rediscovers. Her return to Harlem represents a form of reconciliation that mirrors, in its own way, the Creole insistence on being fully present in one's own community while maintaining personal dignity.

## Conclusion

*Flight* presents passing as a phenomenon far more complex than simple racial deception. Through Mimi Daquin's journey across the color line and back, Walter White maps a spectrum of racial self-presentation strategies available to African Americans in the Jim Crow era. The Creole strategy of visible identity with class distinction, Mimi's complete absorption into whiteness, and her ultimate return to Harlem represent different points on this spectrum — each with its own costs and its own incomplete rewards. The novel's enduring contribution to American racial discourse lies in its refusal to moralize about passing, instead presenting it as a deeply human response to an inhuman social order, while also demonstrating through Mimi's story that no strategy of racial self-presentation can fully resolve the contradictions imposed by a society built on color prejudice.
