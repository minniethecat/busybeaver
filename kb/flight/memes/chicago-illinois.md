## Overview

Chicago appears in *Flight* primarily as a place of origin — the city Mary Robertson left behind when she married [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]].

Her father had built wealth in Chicago's "fast-growing Negro settlement on the south side" (002-031), benefiting from the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]] that brought tens of thousands of Black southerners to the industrial North.

Mary's Chicago background makes her an outsider in [[creole-identity|New Orleans Creole]] society. She is "American for many generations" (002-025), a distinction that matters in the [[creole-quarter|Creole quarter]], where French and Spanish ancestry carried prestige.

Her Baptist faith, midwestern directness, and discomfort with Creole social codes mark her as different, foreshadowing tensions that will define her marriage.

## Mary Robertson's Chicago

The Chicago that shaped Mary Robertson was one of ambitious striving within the confines of segregation. Her father, [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]], embodied the city's aggressive spirit:

> Mary Robertson did not herself know why she had been attracted by Jean Daquin. She knew that, in part, she had been drawn to him by the difference between the eager, almost domineering aggressiveness of her own Chicago and the ease and languorous charm of the Creole quarter (002-031)

Mary had "no recollection of a time when she had not resented keenly the prejudice because of colour which she had encountered throughout her life" (002-032). Chicago, for all its opportunities, was not immune to the color line.

Her Baptist faith marked her as different in Catholic New Orleans. As she explained to [[mrs-plummer|Mrs. Plummer]]: "You see, in Chicago I was a Baptist — and you know Baptists always believe in immersion" (003-018). The detail encapsulated the cultural gulf between midwestern Protestantism and Creole traditions.

## Chicago and the Great Migration

Chicago was the preeminent destination of the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]], the mass movement of African Americans from the rural South to northern industrial cities.

New arrivals found work in stockyards, steel mills, and domestic service. They built a dense community on the South Side supporting Black-owned businesses, churches, newspapers, and civic organizations.

Mr. Robertson profited directly — "her father had made a comfortable fortune as a real-estate agent in Chicago's fast-growing Negro settlement on the south side" (002-031). His wealth, built on migrant housing needs, enabled Mary's settlement-work mission and later the family's move to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]].

## Chicago as Counterpoint to New Orleans

The novel sets Chicago and [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] in implicit opposition. New Orleans is old, Catholic, Creole, rooted in ancestry. Chicago is new, Protestant, commercial, defined by ambition.

Mary's distaste for her life in Chicago grew "daily as she stayed on in New Orleans long after she had originally planned to return" (002-036). The Creole quarter offered something Chicago could not: "for the first time in her life absence of the humiliation and prejudice she felt as a Negro elsewhere in America" (002-036).

Mimi never visits Chicago. When Mr. Robertson urges her to return after Jean's death, Mimi refuses: "she had no intentions of returning to Chicago as he wished" (012-007). Her trajectory points eastward, toward [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] and [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]].

## Later References

Chicago surfaces at the novel's close in a bitter irony. Mrs. Horace Crosby is introduced as "late of Chicago, where her Rotary Club husband had made a fortune in real estate" (019-015).

The description echoes Mr. Robertson's own path to wealth, collapsing the distance between Black Chicago and the white Chicago of Mimi's new life.
