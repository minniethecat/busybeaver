## Overview

The [[fleur-de-lis-meeting|Fleur-de-Lis Club meeting]] at [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]]'s home is the novel's most vivid depiction of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black elite society. Mimi, dressed in her best pale yellow dress, accompanies her stepmother to a gathering of carefully dressed women whose complexions vary from light brown to indistinguishable from white (004-006). The scene functions as Mimi's introduction to the color-conscious social world that her father disdains.

The meeting's central drama is the gossip about [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], who has been caught passing at the [[grand-opera-house-atlanta|Grand Opera House]]. The women's reaction — sweetly cruel, welcoming [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] to her face while savaging her behind her back — reveals the club as a site of intraracial policing as much as community solidarity. Young Mimi, witnessing this, feels a deep sympathy for [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] and a burning detestation of her detractors (004-010).
