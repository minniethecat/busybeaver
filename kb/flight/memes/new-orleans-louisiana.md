New Orleans at the turn of the 20th century occupied a unique position in the American racial landscape. Its French and Spanish colonial heritage had produced a tripartite racial system — white, free people of color, and enslaved Black people — that was fundamentally different from the Anglo-American binary of Black and white.

The Creole community represented a hybrid identity that challenged American racial categories.

> The quiet and languorous charm of the Creole quarter was being dissipated. [002-036]

For [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]], New Orleans is the site of childhood innocence and Creole pride. The novel presents the city through the nostalgic lens of memory, suggesting that this world was already being lost even as Mimi lived in it.

The old Creole families were dying off, poverty was eroding traditional boundaries, and the Anglo-American racial order was encroaching.

## Geography and Character

New Orleans in *Flight* is not merely a backdrop but an active force shaping character and destiny. White maps the city with precision, distinguishing the Creole quarter from the newer American sectors.

> The old places disappeared. Graceful lines of sloping roofs were replaced by harshly severe brick or wooden eaves, leaded glass dim with years was ruthlessly removed for plain sashes turned out by thousands by unimaginative factory hands. [002-059]

The architectural decline mirrors the social transformation of the city. As tenants of six months replace the old Creole families, the built environment registers the loss: the new people were "as cheap and noisy and brazen as their homes" [002-060].

## The Daquin Family in New Orleans

The Daquin family's New Orleans history is central to their identity. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] traces his lineage to a Haitian refugee who fled the revolution of 1791 and later fought under Andrew Jackson at the Battle of New Orleans [002-099].

> "We Daquins trace our history a long ways back — back to the early days of the convent Louis XV founded here in 1727 — the Ursalines — to teach the Negro and Indian girls." [002-090]

Jean's stories connect the family to the city's foundational mythology. The Battle of New Orleans becomes a family story as much as a historical event — an inheritance passed down through names and narratives.

## The Creole Racial Order

New Orleans's distinctive racial system is the novel's starting point for exploring the mutability of racial identity. The Creole community's tripartite structure created a social space where racial boundaries were more permeable than in the Anglo-American South.

| Aspect | Creole New Orleans | Anglo-American South |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Racial categories | Tripartite (white, free people of color, enslaved) | Binary (white, Black) |
| Cultural basis | French, Spanish, African, Caribbean synthesis | Anglo-Protestant |
| Legal framework | Code Noir (French colonial law) | [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] segregation |
| Social mobility | Some permeability for light-skinned Creoles | One-drop rule, rigid boundaries |

> "You can afford to laugh here in Creole New Orleans. But away from here it's a different matter. Here in the place we know I want to tell you of some of the stock from which we Daquins come." [002-088]

The warning is prophetic. In Creole New Orleans, Mimi's mixed ancestry is legible within a known social framework. In [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], that framework collapses into the binary logic of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]].

## New Orleans in Memory

After the Daquin family departs for [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], New Orleans exists in the novel only as memory — a lost world that grows more golden with distance.

> Jean, immersed in his painfully nostalgic memories of his New Orleans, did not notice. [001-009]

[[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson Daquin]]'s dismissal of New Orleans — "that dead old hole of New Orleans" [001-044] — represents a competing view: that the city's Creole exceptionalism is a dead end, not a paradise lost. Her investment in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black middle-class aspirations requires repudiating the Creole past.

Mimi, caught between her father's nostalgia and her mother's ambition, belongs fully to neither.
