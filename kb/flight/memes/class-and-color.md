## Definition

In the social world of early 20th-century Black America, class and skin color were deeply intertwined. The novel *Flight* reveals a community in which lighter skin and closer approximation to white standards of beauty and behavior conferred social status. This intraracial color hierarchy — often called \"colorism\" — operated alongside economic class distinctions to produce a complex social order (004-006).

The novel portrays this system not as a simple binary between light and dark, but as a graduated scale in which proximity to whiteness in color, culture, and comportment determined one's place. At the same time, Walter White refuses to present this as a simple morality tale — characters navigate these hierarchies with a mixture of opportunism, resentment, pride, and pragmatic accommodation.

## Historical Context

The color hierarchy within Black communities has deep historical roots. During slavery, lighter-skinned enslaved people — often the children of slaveholders — were frequently assigned to domestic work rather than field labor, creating an association between light skin and higher status. After emancipation, the \"old free Negro families\" — many of whom were light-skinned and had been free for generations — formed an elite class distinct from the recently emancipated masses (006-012).

In cities like [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] and [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], this class system was reinforced by churches, social clubs, and fraternal organizations that policed boundaries of respectability and color. The [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]], which [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]] so eagerly joins, is one such institution — its members range in complexion from light brown to white-passing, and its social rituals revolve around maintaining an image of gentility and refinement (004-006).

## In the Novel

The novel's treatment of class and color is most vivid in [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club scene of Chapter 4. Mimi observes that \"none of the women present were darker than a light brown, their complexions varying from that shade to one indistinguishable from white\" (004-006). This observation is immediately followed by the gossip about [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], who has been discovered \"passing\" for white at the [[grand-opera-house-atlanta|Grand Opera House]] — a violation of both racial and social protocol.

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] stands apart from this color hierarchy. His Creole philosophy — that a Negro is known by his ability, not his skin color — represents an alternative value system. But even Jean is not immune to class concerns: he proudly notes his descent from \"the famous Major [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]\" who fought under Andrew Jackson at the Battle of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] (002-099). Class pride rooted in family history serves as Jean's counter to the color-consciousness of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] society.

Mimi's trajectory through the novel traces a descent and re-ascent through this class-color hierarchy. Her childhood in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] is marked by relative privilege and Creole pride; her years in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] and [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] involve downward mobility and exposure to the vulnerabilities of dark-skinned Black life; her final decision to pass for white in [[new-york-city|New York]] represents a tragic resolution — she escapes the color hierarchy by leaving it entirely, but at the cost of her identity and community (017-007).
