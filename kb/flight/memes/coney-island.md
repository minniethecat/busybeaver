## Location

Coney Island is a peninsula and beachfront neighborhood at the southernmost edge of [[new-york-city|Brooklyn]], facing the Atlantic Ocean. By the early twentieth century, it had become the most famous amusement destination in the United States — a democratic playground of roller coasters, boardwalks, beaches, and cheap entertainments that drew working-class [[new-york-city|New York]]ers of every race and ethnicity. For [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]]'s Black residents, a trip to Coney Island was one of the few affordable excursions beyond the boundaries of the neighborhood, accessible by the subway and offering a temporary escape from the cramped quarters and long working hours of city life.

## In the Novel

> The only variations she permitted to her regular routine of work were trips to Coney Island with her aunt and some of their friends or rides down town and back of an evening on the Fifth Avenue buses. `015-047`

Coney Island appears in the novel as the emblem of Mimi's modest, hard-won happiness during her early years in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]]. After the trauma of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] and the grinding poverty of [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], her life in [[new-york-city|New York]] settles into a quiet routine of sewing by day, designing in the evenings, and saving every spare dollar for Jean's return. The "only variations she permitted to her regular routine of work were trips to Coney Island with her aunt and some of their friends or rides down town and back of an evening on the [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue buses]]" (`015-047`).

These trips represent the narrow but real pleasures of Mimi's [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] life — a day at the beach, the company of friends, the sensation of being part of a crowd enjoying itself. That Coney Island, with its noisy boardwalk, its hot dogs and sideshows, is the outer limit of her leisure says something about the austerity of her existence: everything is subordinated to the goal of reclaiming her son.

Later, after Mimi has established herself at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's dress shop]], her coworker [[sylvia-smith|Sylvia]] "invited her frequently to go to Coney Island, to the movies, to dances, but these Mimi always declined" (`018-002`). By this point, Mimi has narrowed her life even further — she no longer allows herself even the modest escapes she once enjoyed. Coney Island thus marks a contrast between the two phases of her [[new-york-city|New York]] life: the early years, when she could still take pleasure in simple things, and the later years, when her discipline has hardened into self-denial.
