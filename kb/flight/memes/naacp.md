## Overview

The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP), founded in 1909, was the preeminent national organization fighting against racial discrimination, lynching, and the legal apparatus of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]]. Headquartered in [[new-york-city|New York City]] — the same city to which [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] migrates — the NAACP pursued a strategy of legal challenges, publicity campaigns, and political advocacy to dismantle segregation and secure civil rights for African Americans.

In *Flight*, the NAACP is not directly named but is implicit in the novel's racial politics. The organization's founding in 1909 coincides with the years immediately following the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot of 1906]] — the catalytic event that drives the Daquin family from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. The NAACP's mission to challenge racial injustice legally and through the press represents the organized resistance that characters like [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] could only articulate in private conversations (005-033).

## In the Novel

The NAACP operates as a contextual presence rather than a named entity in the narrative. The novel's treatment of disenfranchisement, press sensationalism, and mob violence — all issues the NAACP actively fought — gives the organization implicit relevance. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s argument with the bank cashier, Mr. Stewart, about how newspapers inflame racial hatred through false reporting echoes the NAACP's early campaigns against media incitement (005-033).

The organization's legal strategy against segregation also provides the invisible counterpoint to the novel's depiction of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s streetcar segregation laws. When Jean is advised that "protest will do no good and you haven't a chance in the courts" (005-024), the NAACP's work to make such challenges possible represents the emerging organized resistance that the novel's characters cannot yet fully access.

The NAACP's headquarters in [[new-york-city|New York]] — where Mimi ultimately settles and passes as white — creates a geographic irony: the organization fighting for Black rights operates in the very city where Mimi chooses to abandon her racial identity.
