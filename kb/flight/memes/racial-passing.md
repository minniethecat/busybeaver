# Racial Passing

## Definition

Racial passing is the practice by which a person classified as a member of one racial group seeks to be accepted as a member of a different group. In early 20th-century America, the term most commonly refers to individuals of partial African ancestry who presented themselves as white to escape [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] restrictions.

Passing was not merely a matter of physical appearance — it required severing family ties, abandoning community networks, and maintaining constant vigilance against discovery (017-007).

The phenomenon emerged from American racial classification, where any known African ancestry defined one as Black. This created a population of light-skinned individuals who could, under the right circumstances, move across the color line.

As the novel explores, passing was both a survival strategy and a form of loss — the passer gained access to opportunities denied by segregation, at the cost of estrangement from family and community.

## Historical Context

Following Reconstruction, the United States erected a system of legal segregation known as [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]]. Southern states such as Georgia enforced strict separation in public accommodations and education.

The color line was policed by law, custom, and violence — the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot of 1906]] demonstrated the lethal consequences of transgressing racial boundaries.

Within Black communities, attitudes toward passing were deeply ambivalent. Many saw it as a betrayal of racial solidarity.

Booker Washington likened upwardly mobile African Americans to "crabs in a basket" — when one attempts to climb out, the others pull it back down (017-005). Others viewed passing pragmatically, as a necessary response to a system denying Black people dignity and opportunity.

In northern cities like [[new-york-city|New York]], the racial landscape was more fluid. [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] was a center of Black cultural life, yet its residents lived within sharp boundaries. The mere accusation of passing could bring social ruin, as [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] illustrates (004-008).

## In the Novel

Racial passing is the central organizing concept of *Flight*. The novel traces [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s life from proud Creole identification in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to her ultimate decision to pass for white in [[new-york-city|New York]].

Mimi's passing is not racial self-hatred but a desperate response to persecution: "the very intolerance of her own people had driven her from them" (017-007).

The first explicit treatment of passing occurs at a Fleur-de-Lis Club meeting in Chapter 4, where [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] is publicly shamed for attending a white theater while passing (004-008).

Mimi's turn toward passing crystallizes late in the novel. Confiding in [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]], she articulates her reasoning (016-060).

At [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], where Mimi works as a finisher, she lives in constant fear of discovery (017-011). When a coworker mentions the previous fitter was "a coloured girl," alarm creeps into Mimi's voice (017-030).

The novel refuses to romanticize passing — it shows the psychological toll of concealment and the loss of spontaneity (017-007).
