## Overview

When Mimi decides to pass for white, she moves from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] to the lower Nineties in Manhattan — a physical distance of only a few miles but a social distance as vast as the color line itself. Her new neighborhood is described as a world of anonymous apartment buildings, rushing commuters, and the relentless pursuit of material success. Here there was little of that softness of speech to which she was accustomed. Here there was an obsession with material things that crowded out the naturalness that made life tolerable (017-007).

The novel uses the contrast between [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] and lower Manhattan to dramatize what Mimi loses when she passes. The warmth, spontaneity, and community of Black life are replaced by morose, worried faces and the cold efficiency of the white working world. Her building — one of the huge rabbit warrens that house countless families — embodies the anonymity of her new existence (017-004).
