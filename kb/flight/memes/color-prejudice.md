## Definition

Color prejudice in *Flight* operates at multiple levels: the white supremacist prejudice that structures American society through [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]], and the intraracial color prejudice that divides Black communities along gradients of skin tone. The novel treats both forms of prejudice as manifestations of the same underlying logic — the belief that human worth can be read from physical appearance.

The novel's genius lies in its refusal to treat intraracial color prejudice as a simple mirror of white racism. The women of [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club who gossip about [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] are not merely internalizing white standards — they are navigating a complex social field in which proximity to whiteness confers real advantages, and deviation from community norms carries real costs (004-008).

## Historical Context

Color prejudice within Black communities has a long and painful history in America. The \"brown paper bag test\" — a lighter-than-a-paper-bag skin color requirement for admission to certain churches, clubs, and social organizations — was a common practice in the early 20th century. Light-skinned elites often formed exclusive social circles that replicated the exclusionary practices of white society.

The politics of color were complicated by the fact that lighter skin often correlated with greater access to education, employment, and social mobility. As the novel shows through [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club scene, light-skinned women like [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]] could aspire to social recognition denied to darker-skinned Black women — but their position remained precarious, always subject to challenge and gossip.

## In the Novel

The [[fleur-de-lis-meeting|Fleur-de-Lis Club meeting]] in Chapter 4 is the novel's most concentrated treatment of color prejudice. Mimi's observation that no woman present is \"darker than a light brown\" (004-006) is immediately followed by Mrs. Watkins's attack on [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] for passing — a sequence that links color hierarchy to community policing.

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s Creole philosophy offers an explicit alternative to color prejudice. His insistence that worth is determined by ability, not color, represents a moral challenge to the color hierarchy. But the novel does not present this philosophy as triumphant — Jean's world is being destroyed by forces that his principles cannot withstand (002-059).

Mimi's experience of color prejudice from her own community ultimately drives her to pass: \"what's happened here will just keep on happening every place I go\" (016-060). The irony is that escaping color prejudice requires embracing an even more radical form of color-based identity — passing as white.
