## Overview

The novel opens with the Daquin family's arrival at [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s [[atlanta-union-station|Union Station]]. The long train rumbled and swayed, whistle blowing intermittently, screeching discordantly, past smoky factory and office buildings, through rows of one-storied, two- or three-room cottages (001-001). The scene establishes the novel's central motifs: the train as vehicle of migration, the city as destination and threat, and the racial landscape of the [[new-south-ideology|New South]].

The arrival scene introduces the family's dynamics: Jean's Creole pride, Mary's midwestern directness, and young Mimi's alert observation of the world around her. The grinning Negro porter who announces their arrival — Fo Atlanty! (001-002) — embodies the limited roles available to Black people in the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] South, while the porter's deferential performance contrasts with Jean's refusal to accommodate the racial order.
