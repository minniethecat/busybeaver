# The Gendered Moral Economy of *Flight*

## Overview

In Walter White's *Flight*, the regulation of women's conduct through gossip, scandal, and moral judgment constitutes a pervasive social mechanism that operates across every community depicted in the novel. From Mrs. Plummer's surveillance of the Daquin family in Atlanta, to the church-based moral authority of figures like Mrs. Adams, to Mrs. Rogers' role as Harlem society's gatekeeper, to the shallow morality of white New York socialites like Dolores d'Aubigny, the novel reveals a consistent pattern: women's social standing is policed primarily by other women through the informal economy of reputation. This gendered moral economy transcends racial boundaries — it operates with equal force in the Creole community of New Orleans, Atlanta's Black elite, Harlem's Negro society, and white Manhattan — suggesting that the policing of women's moral conduct is not an artifact of any particular racial culture but a structural feature of patriarchal societies in which women's primary power lies in their ability to shape and enforce communal judgment.

## New Orleans: Mrs. Plummer's Gossip Network and Creole Respectability

The novel introduces the gendered moral economy in its opening pages, through the character of Mrs. Plummer, the novel's archetypal gossip — she knows everyone's business, judges everyone's conduct, and serves as the informal conduit through which community reputation is made and destroyed. The narrator describes her with predatory precision:

> "Mrs. Plummer's ears seemed to stretch out" [001-020]

Her first interaction with the Daquin family establishes the pattern. She immediately sizes up the newcomers, asking pointed questions about their racial identity and social standing. The narrator notes that she "called her Mrs. Day-queen" [001-039], a deliberate mispronunciation that signals her refusal to grant the Creole family the social deference they expect. When she observes Mimi's behavior, Mrs. Plummer's judgment carries real weight in the community: "ev'rybody knows you don't gossip" [001-024] she is told, a statement that ironically reinforces the very gossip network it pretends to disavow.

Mrs. Plummer's partnership with Mrs. King forms a two-woman surveillance apparatus. Together they watch Mimi's comings and goings, particularly her relationship with Carl Hunter. When they spot Mimi and Carl together:

> "Ain't that that Day-quinn girl?" whispered Mrs. Plummer. "Ain't that that Day-quinn girl and that Hunter boy?" [007-026]

The two women then rush to spread the news, and the narrator describes how they "hurried through the little kitchen" [011-034] — the domestic space becoming the headquarters of moral enforcement.

The Creole community of New Orleans that the Daquins left behind had its own version of this moral economy, though it operated through different channels. Jean Daquin's obsession with "high Creole society" [002-044] and his marriage to Mary Robertson, a woman "not of Creole blood" [002-044], had already exposed the family to social judgment before their departure. The novel thus establishes that the gendered moral economy is not peculiar to any one place but is portable, following women wherever they go.

## Atlanta: Mrs. Adams and Church-Based Moral Authority

In Atlanta, the gendered moral economy finds its institutional anchor in the church and its most powerful enforcer in Mrs. Adams. As the mother of Hilda, Mimi's closest friend in Atlanta, Mrs. Adams occupies a position of considerable moral authority within the city's Black elite. Her influence extends from the domestic sphere into the public realm — she is "invited to all the parties" [004-050] and her opinions carry weight in determining who belongs and who does not in Atlanta's Negro society.

The church serves as the primary arena for moral enforcement. The narrator notes that churches "attended in the main only by coloured people who were mulattoes or lighter" [004-041] functioned as sites where class and color hierarchies were maintained and policed. When scandal erupts around Mimi and Carl Hunter, the church becomes the stage for community judgment. Carl is "seen no more at church" [010-001] as a result of the controversy, and Mimi's reputation is systematically destroyed through the machinery of church-based gossip.

Mrs. Adams' role in this drama is complex. She initially champions Mimi's cause — "only Hilda and Mrs. Adams championed the cause of Mimi" [016-042] — but her defense is itself an exercise of moral authority, a decision about who deserves community protection and who does not. When her own daughter Hilda is involved in the scandal, having been the object of Carl's prior romantic interest, Mrs. Adams' position becomes even more fraught, demonstrating how women's moral authority is always entangled with their own family's social standing.

The narrator captures the essential nature of this economy with a sweeping observation that universalizes the phenomenon:

> "women here gossip — that's no practice peculiar of this or that race" [006-059]

This explicit statement refuses to attribute the gossip network to any particular community's values and instead recognizes it as a cross-cutting social phenomenon.

The consequences of scandal in Atlanta are severe. Mimi becomes pregnant out of wedlock, an event that would destroy any young woman's reputation in the early twentieth century regardless of race. The community's response — the withdrawal of social support, the whispered judgments, the doors that close — forces Mimi onto a path that leads ultimately to Philadelphia, widowhood, and the radical independence that will characterize her later life.

## Harlem: Mrs. Rogers as Moral Arbiter

Mrs. Rogers, Mimi's aunt in Harlem, represents the most sympathetic version of the moral arbiter figure in the novel. Unlike Mrs. Plummer, whose gossip is malicious, or Mrs. Adams, whose moral authority is entangled with social ambition, Mrs. Rogers exercises her judgment with wisdom and genuine concern for her niece's welfare.

When Mimi first arrives in Harlem, it is Mrs. Rogers who educates her about the community's moral expectations. Mrs. Rogers tells her with worldly wisdom:

> "there are gossips everywhere — and in every race" [015-029]

Mrs. Rogers acknowledges the universality of the moral economy while helping her niece navigate it. Her own social position — she runs her own business and is well-known in Harlem — gives her the standing to serve as both protector and guide.

Mrs. Rogers' shrewdest insight into the moral economy comes when she discusses with Mimi the women who "had fixed on nothing but dress and gossip and petty, unimportant social activities" [015-045]. She recognizes that the same gossip network that can destroy a woman's reputation can also trap women in triviality, making them "no less shrewd" [015-045] than men in business but directing their intelligence toward the policing of other women rather than toward their own liberation.

When Mimi decides to pass for white and leave Harlem, it is Mrs. Rogers who "pleaded, argued, almost commanded her not to go" [017-005]. This is not, in the novel's terms, a simple defense of racial loyalty. It is also Mrs. Rogers' recognition that in the white world, Mimi will have no female protector, no moral arbiter who has her genuine interests at heart. The gendered moral economy, for all its oppressive aspects, also provides a form of social insurance — a community of women who, however judgmental, would rally around one of their own in a true crisis.

## The Harlem Community Judgment of Passing

The novel reveals a particularly sharp form of moral judgment directed at women who pass for white. A former acquaintance from Harlem recognizes Mimi in Manhattan and voices the unmistakable note of condemnation:

> "isn't that the same girl from Atlanta who was working down town, passing for white?" [016-060]

The question carries layers of moral condemnation. Passing is judged not merely as racial betrayal but as a specifically female moral failing — a form of deception, of sexual and social irregularity, of abandoning one's proper place.

The scandal around Mimi functions as a feast for the Harlem gossip networks. The narrator describes how:

> "All Harlem is agog," the paragraph ran, "over the report that one of our fairest daughters has crossed the colour line" [016-047]

The narrative also notes the gendered nature of this enforcement: "the wise ones" [016-042] — invariably women — are the keepers and disseminators of moral judgment. The Black press amplifies the informal moral economy of gossip through formal institutional channels.

In this context, Mimi's decision to pass represents not just a racial choice but a gendered one: she is choosing to place herself beyond the reach of the Black community's moral economy, a radical act of self-determination that the novel presents as both brave and deeply costly.

## White Society: The Shallowness of Dolores d'Aubigny

The novel extends its analysis of the gendered moral economy beyond the Black community into white society, particularly through the character of Dolores d'Aubigny. When this character appears in the narrative, she is introduced with immediate moral judgment that mirrors precisely the gossip that pursued Mimi:

> "Her name's Dolores d'Aubigny — though I've heard some say that's not her real name" [022-019]

The gossip about her identity — who she really is, where she really comes from — is structurally the same mechanism that followed Mimi across communities.

Dolores d'Aubigny represents a purely performative version of white femininity. She is described as "slim and graceful" [022-020] and noted for her beauty, but the narrative treats her as fundamentally hollow. The moral judgment directed at her comes not from church-based authority or community concern but from competitive social positioning — women evaluating other women as rivals for male attention and social status.

The novel suggests that among white New York socialites, the moral economy has been stripped of any genuine ethical content. Gossip serves not to maintain communal standards but to advance individual social ambitions. The narrator observes with clinical precision:

> "possession of all the latest gossip was the most valuable asset" [022-010]

In this world, moral discourse is reduced to a commodity traded for social advantage. Women's judgments of one another become purely instrumental — weapons in the competition for status rather than instruments of genuine moral community.

The contrast with the Black community's moral economy is revealing. While Mrs. Plummer's gossip and Mrs. Adams' church-based authority serve to maintain community standards and protect collective interests, Dolores d'Aubigny's world reduces moral judgment to pure social performance. The novel thus suggests that the gendered moral economy, while oppressive in any form, takes on different qualities depending on the broader social context in which it operates.

## Cross-Cutting Analysis: Women Enforcing Norms on Women

Across all four settings — New Orleans Creole, Atlanta's Black elite, Harlem, and white Manhattan — a consistent pattern emerges: women are the primary enforcers of social norms on other women. Mrs. Plummer watches Mimi's comings and goings; Mrs. Adams determines who belongs in Atlanta society; Mrs. Rogers educates her niece about community expectations; Dolores d'Aubigny's circle polices its members through competitive gossip. In every case, the mechanism is informal, the enforcers are women, and the primary targets are women.

This pattern reflects a structural reality of early twentieth-century America: in a patriarchal society where women lacked formal institutional power, their influence operated through the informal economy of reputation. A woman's social standing, marriage prospects, and economic security all depended on her reputation, and that reputation was largely controlled by other women. The church provided one institutional locus for this power; the gossip network of neighborhoods and social circles provided another.

The novel does not simply condemn this system. It also shows that the gendered moral economy served protective functions, particularly in Black communities under constant threat from white violence. Mrs. Adams' concern for Mimi's reputation is not merely priggishness — in a world where Black women were vulnerable to sexual exploitation, a young woman's reputation was a form of armor. Mrs. Rogers' guidance to Mimi is not merely controlling — it is the wisdom of a woman who has survived and prospered within a system she did not create.

The tragedy of Mimi's journey is that she loses the protection of the Black community's moral economy without gaining the protection of the white community's. Her passing places her in a zone of moral limbo — judged by Black women for abandoning the race, yet never fully accepted by white women whose acceptance was always conditional and superficial. The novel's final vision — Mimi returned to Harlem — represents a kind of reconciliation with the gendered moral economy, an acknowledgment that imperfect as it is, community judgment is preferable to the isolation of passing.

## Conclusion

The gendered moral economy that Walter White depicts in *Flight* is both oppressive and protective, universal and culturally specific. It constrains women's freedom through constant surveillance and judgment, yet it also provides the fabric of community that makes survival possible. The novel's refusal to take a single stance toward this phenomenon — condemning the gossip of Mrs. Plummer while celebrating the wisdom of Mrs. Rogers, exposing the hypocrisy of church-based moral authority while acknowledging its protective function — reflects the complexity of women's experience in a society that offered them few sources of power and many sources of danger. Through Mimi's journey across multiple communities and her eventual return to Harlem, the novel suggests that the gendered moral economy, for all its flaws, is inseparable from community itself — and that the isolation of passing, however liberating it may initially appear, ultimately offers no escape from the fundamental human need for belonging.
