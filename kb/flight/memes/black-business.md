## Definition

Black business enterprise in the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] era represented both economic necessity and racial pride. Excluded from white-owned establishments and denied access to mainstream capital, African Americans built a parallel economy of insurance companies, banks, funeral homes, newspapers, and retail businesses. In *Flight*, the world of Black business is personified by figures like [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]], who owns the [[lincoln-mutual-life|Lincoln Mutual Life Insurance Company]], and [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]], who dreams of establishing a tailoring business in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] (002-055).

The novel portrays Black business as a site of both aspiration and limitation. Black entrepreneurs could achieve significant wealth and community standing, but their success was constrained by the broader framework of segregation. As the novel shows, the very existence of Black businesses was a response to exclusion — a form of economic self-defense that could easily become a form of economic imprisonment.

## Historical Context

The early 20th century saw the rise of a significant Black business class, particularly in southern cities like [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. [[education-and-uplift|Booker T. Washington]]'s National Negro Business League, founded in 1900, promoted entrepreneurship as central to racial advancement. Insurance companies like North Carolina Mutual and [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Life became pillars of Black economic life, providing not only financial services but also employment for college-educated African Americans who were barred from white firms.

[[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue became known as \"the richest Negro street in the world,\" home to banks, insurance companies, real estate offices, and retail establishments. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s observation that a Negro-owned insurance company had succeeded in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] confirms his judgment that the city offered economic opportunity, however constrained by the color line (002-055).

## In the Novel

The Hunter family represents the Black business elite of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]]'s [[lincoln-mutual-life|Lincoln Mutual Life Insurance Company]] provides the family with a substantial home — \"a huge pile of towers and turrets and bulging bay windows\" (004-003) — and his wife presides over [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club with the authority that wealth confers. Yet the novel also suggests the limits of this prosperity: [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] chafes against his father's expectation that he study \"insurance and [[bank|banking]],\" desiring a different kind of life (004-025).

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s trajectory offers a counterpoint. His tailoring business in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] is modest, and his move to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] is motivated by the hope of greater opportunity. But the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot destroys those hopes, demonstrating the fragility of Black economic achievement in the face of white mob violence. Jean, who had \"in his pride of Creole ancestry\" been content with his modest dreams, finds those dreams shattered by forces beyond his control (002-025).

The novel's economic geography maps the constraints on Black entrepreneurship. In [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], the Creole community provided a protected market and a network of mutual support. In [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], Black business was more exposed to the vicissitudes of white power, as [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] demonstrated. In [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], the sheer density of Black population created unprecedented economic opportunity — but also fierce competition. Mimi's work at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], a white establishment where she can earn more than in Black-owned businesses, underscores the economic logic of passing.
