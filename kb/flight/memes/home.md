Home in Walter White's *Flight* is never simply a place of shelter. It is the primary site where racial identity is formed and contested — where [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s Creole pride is nurtured, where [[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson Daquin]]'s domestic labor sustains the family's fragile middle-class status, and where Mimi first learns that the self displayed at home and the self presented to the world may be fundamentally different.

The novel traces a progressive [[death|loss]] of home across Mimi's journey: from the "quiet and languorous charm of the [[creole-quarter|Creole quarter]]" (`002-036`) to the Robertson home in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], from [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], and finally from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] to the white world where home becomes a fiction maintained at enormous psychic cost. Each displacement represents not just a change of address but a transformation of identity.

## Home as Racial Formation

The Daquin home in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] is the novel's archetype of domestic racial identity. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s pride in his ancestry fills the house with stories of the Haitian Revolution and the Battle of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] (`002-099`). Mary, by contrast, "preferred to stay at home" (`002-048`), learning to cook Creole dishes and maintaining the domestic rituals that define the family's cultural distinctiveness.

The Robertson home in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], offered as a gift to the newly arrived Daquins — "[[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]] offered them a home in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], furnished and ready for occupancy" (`002-055`) — represents both generosity and obligation. The house is "furnished and ready," suggesting that the Robertsons have not simply provided shelter but have pre-shaped the Daquins' domestic environment. The new home comes with expectations.

> She was interested in sizing up her new home. She was perched in a huge chair in the living room. (`001-043`)

The verb "sizing up" captures Mary's active assessment of the space that will define her family's new life. Home is not passively received but actively evaluated — a domain where a woman's judgment holds sway even as broader social forces constrain her choices.

## Home and Gendered Labor

White is attentive to the gendered dimensions of domestic space. Mary Daquin's labor — sewing, cooking, managing the household — is the invisible infrastructure that sustains the family's social position. When Jean prefers "spending his time at home" (`004-046`), Mary's "constant" efforts to make the home presentable are the precondition for his comfort.

> I stays at home and tends to my own bus'ness. (`001-023`)

This declaration, spoken by a secondary character, articulates a philosophy of domestic containment that the novel both respects and interrogates. Staying home is presented as a form of self-protection in a hostile racial environment — a refusal to expose oneself to the humiliations of public space. But it is also a limitation, a confinement that falls disproportionately on women.

## The Loss of Home

Mimi's journey is a progressive estrangement from domestic belonging. The Robertson home, for all its comforts, is never fully hers. After the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] race riot, the home becomes a site of terror: "Down the street and home scurried Jean and Mimi, weak with fear" (`005-083`). The domestic sphere cannot protect against racial violence; the mob penetrates even the quietest street.

After Jean's death, home becomes untenable. Mimi moves to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], then to [[new-york-city|New York]], each relocation taking her further from the Creole domestic world of her childhood. By the time she passes, she has become homeless in the deepest sense — not without shelter, but without any place where her whole self can be present.

## Home and the Return

The novel's final movement — Mimi's tentative return to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] — is a return to the possibility of home. The cabaret scene, with its "music of barbaric sweetness and rhythm" (`024-009`), offers a collective domesticity: not a private house but a shared Black space where Mimi might belong again. Whether she can actually rebuild a home there is the question the novel leaves open.
