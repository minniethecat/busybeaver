The bank in Walter White's *Flight* is more than a financial institution — it is a symbol of the economic dimensions of racial inequality. White's characters measure their standing by "bank balance" (`002-025`), aspire to positions in banking, and navigate a financial world where access to capital is mediated by race. The novel's treatment of banking reveals the economic infrastructure beneath the social drama of passing and racial identity.

## The Bank as Class Marker

Early in the novel, [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s philosophy is captured in a question that links money to human worth: "Does that mean they are happier than we?—have more money in the bank?" (`002-053`). The question is rhetorical — Jean's Creole pride insists that lineage and culture matter more than wealth — but its very asking reveals the centrality of financial standing to social status in the [[new-south-ideology|New South]].

Jean's daily visits "to the bank uptown" (`005-031`) establish him as a man of affairs, someone whose business requires a relationship with financial institutions. Yet his banking is modest — the deposits of a working man, not an investor. The distinction matters: banking in *Flight* is about respectability as much as wealth.

## Banking as Racial Opportunity

For Black men in the novel, a position in a bank represents the pinnacle of respectable employment. [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] is directed to "study insurance and banking" (`004-025`) as a path to middle-class stability. The banker who sits "at Mimi's right" at a social gathering comes "from a town in Iowa" (`022-024`) — his profession conferring a status that transcends his geographic origins.

> He'd been for a number of years before his death a responsible position in a bank down town. (`015-024`)

This description of a character's late husband uses banking as shorthand for achievement. The modifier "responsible" and the location "down town" together signal solidity, probity, and connection to the commercial heart of the city. Banking, in this formulation, is the opposite of precarious Black labor — it is security, permanence, the promise that one's children will rise further.

## The Racial Limits of Finance

Yet the novel is clear about the racial boundaries of American finance. The Black bank employee is "assistant cashier" (`016-016`), not president — the glass ceiling is visible even in the novel's silences about it. White's passing protagonist Mimi circulates in a white world where financial opportunity is presumed, not fought for; the contrast with the constrained banking careers of Black characters is part of the novel's critique.

> You know how it is — men who have stolen railroads and banks but who've never been caught. (`016-054`)

This bitter observation, spoken by a Black character about white wealth, cuts to the heart of the novel's economic analysis. White banking is built on theft — of land, of labor, of life — while Black banking is built on thrift and respectability. The asymmetry is structural, not personal, and the novel refuses to pretend otherwise.
