## Overview

Atlanta in the early 1900s was the symbolic capital of the [[new-south-ideology|New South]] — a city that had rebuilt itself after Civil War destruction into a regional hub of commerce, transportation, and industry.

The Daquin family's [[atlanta-arrival|arrival in Atlanta]] opens the novel, establishing the city as the stage on which [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s racial education will unfold (001-001).

The novel presents Atlanta through the eyes of newcomers — Jean, Mary, and [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] — who must learn the city's racial codes. The city's Black elite, with their insurance companies and social clubs, stands in stark contrast to the violence that will erupt in the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|1906 race riot]].

But the Atlanta that Mimi comes to know is defined above all by that riot, which destroys her father's business and reveals the violence underlying the [[new-south-ideology|New South]]'s promises.

> "Your Pa's a mighty smart man to come all the way to Atlanta," gushed Mary. "He says he's going to put New Orleans on the map by showing these stuck-up and exclusive Atlanta Negroes a trick or two." (001-011)

## In the Novel

Atlanta is the novel's crucible — the place where Mimi's childhood innocence is destroyed and her racial consciousness forged.

The Daquin family's move from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to Atlanta represents a transition from the fluid racial order of Creole Louisiana to the rigid binary of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] Georgia.

Mary Robertson, Mimi's stepmother, greets the city with enthusiasm, telling her friend that she "just adored Atlanta" (004-030), but her embrace of Atlanta society comes at the cost of accommodating its color hierarchy.

The [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]], which meets in [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]]'s "huge pile of towers and turrets and bulging bay windows" (004-003), embodies Atlanta's Black elite society.

Here, the novel's critique of intraracial color prejudice is most sharply drawn — the club's members, none darker than "a light brown," police the boundaries of respectability with ferocity rivaling white racism (004-006).

The [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot of 1906]] is the novel's central traumatic event. White mobs rampage through Black neighborhoods, destroying businesses and homes — including [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s tailoring shop. The riot drives the family from Atlanta, demonstrating that Black economic achievement offers no protection against white violence.

Mimi's "intense consciousness of race which her experiences in [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] at Atlanta had engendered in her" (017-006) shapes every subsequent choice she makes.

> "So engrossed had she been in Mrs. Adams, Mimi had not seen Hilda, who in the few days she had been in Atlanta had heard enough to know there were some few people who were mulattoes or quadroons, others only by those whose colour was of a light brown." (004-041)

## Significance

Atlanta represents the promise and the failure of the [[new-south-ideology|New South]]. The city's Black business district, centered on [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Avenue]], with the Lincoln Mutual Life Insurance Company and other enterprises, offered economic opportunity for African Americans — but that opportunity was built on a foundation of rigid segregation enforced by violence.

The novel portrays Atlanta as a city of surfaces: elaborate Victorian homes, carefully dressed women, genteel conversation — all masking the brutal reality of the color line.
