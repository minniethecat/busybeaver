## Definition

Jim Crow was the system of legal and customary racial segregation that dominated the American South from the end of Reconstruction (1877) through the mid-20th century. The name, derived from a racist minstrel show character, came to describe a comprehensive regime of racial subordination enforced through law, custom, economic pressure, and violence.

The novel portrays Jim Crow as a lived reality: the segregated train that brings the Daquin family to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], the white theater that ejects [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], and the mob violence that drives the family from the city. Jim Crow determines where characters can go, what they can do, and who they can be.

> "Union Station—Atlanty. All out for Atlanty," bawled a grinning Negro porter as the train rolled into a long, dingy shed with low-hung roof. (001-002)

## Historical Context

The legal foundation of Jim Crow was the Supreme Court's 1896 decision in Plessy v. Ferguson, which upheld "separate but equal" accommodations as constitutional. In practice, facilities for Black Americans were consistently inferior. A system of racial etiquette also governed every interaction: Black people were expected to defer to whites in speech, gesture, and movement.

The enforcement of Jim Crow was never solely legal — it depended on the constant threat and occasional exercise of violence. Race riots, economic retaliation, and extra-legal terror policed the color line. The [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot of 1906]], which drives the Daquin family from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], was one of many such eruptions (005-030).

## In the Novel

Jim Crow structures the novel's opening: the Daquin family arrives by train from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], entering the segregation regime of [[georgia-piedmont|Georgia]]. The porter embodies the limited roles available to Black people under Jim Crow, while the neighbors' gossip reveals how segregation ideology permeates daily life (001-027).

> "Lord, Mis' Plummer, I don't know what's gettin' into these coloured folks—they gettin' mo' like white folks ev'ry day." (001-027)

The novel reveals segregation's geography through movement. Black characters navigate [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] constrained by law and custom. The white theater [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] enters by passing marks one boundary (004-008); the white mob rampaging through Black neighborhoods during [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] marks another (006-003).

Jim Crow's internalization by Black characters is a painful theme. When [[sylvia-smith|Sylvia]] mentions the previous fitter was "a coloured girl," the alarm in Mimi's voice shows how thoroughly she has absorbed segregation's logic — even while escaping it through passing.

> "A coloured girl?" Mimi asked, alarm creeping into her voice. (017-030)
