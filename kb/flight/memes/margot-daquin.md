## Overview

Margot Daquin is the deceased mother of Mimi Daquin and the beloved first wife of Jean Daquin. Though she dies before the main action of *Flight* begins — when Mimi was nine years old — her memory pervades the novel's early chapters. She represents the old Creole world of New Orleans that Jean longs for and that Mary Robertson displaces: a world of quiet elegance, gentle guidance, and racial accommodation within the French Quarter's distinctive culture.

Margot is characterized primarily through Jean's grief-stricken memories. "Margot with quiet efficiency had guided him, consoled him, upheld him," and "with gentle self-effacement she had suggested solutions to the problems which arose in his little sick-and-accident insurance company" [002-028]. Her death plunges Jean into alcoholism and a grief "that bordered on coma" for more than a year [002-029].

## In the Story

### Marriage and Family Life

Margot and Jean Daquin lived together in the old family house on Dumaine Street in New Orleans, raising their daughter Mimi. She managed their household with quiet competence while also subtly guiding Jean's small insurance business [002-028]. The family's life followed the rhythms of Creole society: they dined at the old French Quarter restaurants — Antoine's, Delatoire's, Mme. Begue's — without trouble despite Jean's known Negro ancestry [002-057].

Mimi, christened "Annette Angela Daquin," retained few direct memories of her mother but absorbed the family's reverence for her. The photograph of Margot in her wedding gown was one of two objects Jean kept on his table during his years of grieving, alongside a wine glass [002-029].

### Death and Aftermath

Margot died when Mimi was nine years old [002-027]. Her death devastated Jean, who "floundered about, terrified by loneliness, panic-stricken when he found himself the sole keeper and guardian and nurse of a perturbingly active child of nine" [002-027]. For more than a year, Jean lived in a state bordering on coma, drinking heavily at night while staring at Margot's photograph [002-029].

The loss shaped the family's future in two crucial ways: Jean's vulnerability led him into marriage with Mary Robertson, who swept into New Orleans "like the digging of a channel that linked the sluggish bayou with the pellmell hurtling stream nearby" [002-027]; and Mimi grew up in the shadow of a mother she barely knew, with others constantly noting her resemblance to Margot — "Every day you look more like Margot did when I first knew her," Jean tells her proudly [010-072].

### Legacy

Margot's memory creates an unspoken standard against which Mary Robertson Daquin is measured and found lacking. Family friends observe that Mary is "not Margot's type at all" [011-045]. The tension between the old Creole world Margot embodied — gentle, self-effacing, tradition-bound — and the aggressive, money-driven modernity Mary represents becomes one of the novel's central conflicts. Jean's inability to move past Margot's death leaves him ill-equipped for the changed world Mary and Mr. Robertson force upon the family.
