# Mimi Daquin's Decision to Pass

The climactic moment when [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] resolves to leave "coloured people for good" and cross the color line, abandoning her racial community in response to its betrayal of her.

## Overview

[[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s decision to pass for white permanently is the climax of her character arc in *Flight*. After the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] community that she had embraced and that had embraced her turns against her — following a scandal involving a married man — Mimi makes the deliberate choice to abandon her racial identity. Her determination to leave "coloured people for good" (016-060) represents both a strategic response to social destruction and a profound psychological break.

The decision is not made casually. It is the culmination of years of displacement — from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] after [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]], from [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], and now from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] itself. Mimi sees passing as her only remaining option for survival with dignity, a conclusion driven by what she perceives as the collective failure of the Black community to protect and sustain her.

## Narrative Context

The decision unfolds across chapters 16 and 17. Following the scandal in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], Mimi seeks employment first in the Black community and finds all doors closed to her. Only when she turns to the white world does she find opportunity. She applies to [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], secures a position as a finisher, and begins the deliberate process of constructing a new white identity.

[[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]], who had earlier offered Mimi a home in [[new-york-city|New York]] and promised that "up North you'd have a much better chance" (011-051), represents the supportive family network that Mimi is now abandoning. Her aunt's offer of community proves insufficient to counter the damage done by the [[harlem-scandal|Harlem scandal]].

Mimi's subsequent employment at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] — where she observes wealthy white patrons and begins to imagine her own place among them — represents the beginning of her new life on the other side of the color line. The decision is both a liberation and a [[death|loss]], a theme the novel explores throughout its latter chapters.

## Aftermath

The decision sets in motion the final act of the novel: Mimi's life as a white woman in [[new-york-city|New York]], her professional success, her romantic relationships, and the constant tension between the freedom of her new identity and what she has surrendered to achieve it. The decision also creates the novel's central ethical question — whether the cost of passing is ultimately bearable.
