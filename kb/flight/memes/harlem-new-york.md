## Overview

Harlem in the 1910s and 1920s was the cultural capital of Black America — the destination of the [[great-migration-event|Great Migration]], the birthplace of the Harlem Renaissance, and a symbol of Black possibility in a society structured by white supremacy.

For [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]], Harlem represents the promise of community after years of displacement, and ultimately the site of a betrayal that drives her to cross the color line permanently.

> Harlem, which first had been Dutch "Haarlem," then Irish, afterwards Jewish and German, was in the flood-tide of transition to a Negro city within a city. [015-017]

The novel portrays Harlem as a world of extraordinary vitality and complexity. [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]], Mimi's aunt, has built a comfortable life there. But Harlem is also bounded by the color line, and its internal social dynamics prove as constraining to Mimi as the white racism she fled.

## Historical Context

By the time [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] arrives in [[new-york-city|New York]], Harlem had already undergone its transformation from a white middle-class suburb to the largest Black urban community in America.

Originally settled by the Dutch as Nieuw Haarlem in 1658, the neighborhood saw waves of European immigration before a real estate collapse in the early 1900s opened it to Black settlement. Between 1910 and 1930, the Black population of Harlem surged from roughly 10,000 to over 200,000.

> From the doorway of a former private dwelling in a side street near bustling Lenox Avenue came a resonant flooding swell of music. High above the rest sounded a heavy baritone, chopping off of the words like little sausages. [015-017]

This transformation was engineered by Black real estate entrepreneurs like [[philip-payton|Philip A. Payton]], who leased white-owned buildings and rented them to Black tenants at higher rates. The concentration of Black talent, capital, and ambition in a single neighborhood produced an unprecedented cultural efflorescence, but it also made Harlem a captive market.

## Arrival and Belonging

Mimi arrives in Harlem as a refugee from [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], seeking the protection of her aunt, [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]]. The neighborhood offers her a community she has not known since [[new-orleans-exodus|leaving New Orleans]].

> The new life she was ushered into through Aunt Sophie seemed by comparison with her experiences since she left Atlanta almost idyllic to Mimi. [015-041]

The vibrancy of Harlem life — the street-corner orators, the church congregations, the club gatherings — envelops Mimi. She becomes a reader of the Harlem press, and copies were seldom left on newsstands "more than a few hours after it appeared on Thursday mornings" [016-045].

> Most interesting to her was the transition she saw going on around her in Harlem. Gradually yet surely there was being builded a Negro city here in northern Manhattan. [015-042]

## The Internal Dynamics of Community

But Harlem's internal dynamics prove Mimi's undoing. The same density that creates community also enables surveillance. When her relationship with a married man becomes known, the neighborhood turns against her.

> "All Harlem is agog," the paragraph ran, "with some hitherto hidden chapters in the life of one of our most attractive and popular young women. But it seems there has been a coloured gentleman in the firewood." [016-047]

The scandal sheet devotes its pages to "not too cryptic references, almost invariably scandalous ones, to persons of prominence" [016-045].

> "Three-fourths of the men in Harlem" — so one character estimates the proportion of philandering husbands, a bitter observation about the community's hypocrisy. [016-012]

Harlem's moral economy is revealed as a double standard: the community polices women's behavior with ferocious intensity while excusing men's. This gendered hypocrisy pervades the novel but in Harlem it acquires the lethal density of a neighborhood where everyone knows everyone else's business.

## The Decision to Leave

The scandal drives Mimi to the desperate conclusion:

> "I'm leaving Harlem, leaving coloured people for good. I'll live my own life, make more money than I can here, I'll be able sooner to have Jean with me — well, there's no other way out." [016-062]

This line marks the novel's decisive turn. Harlem was supposed to be the destination; instead it becomes another point of departure. The "railroad flats" of Harlem become Mimi's metaphor for her life:

> Her life to her was like one of those Harlem apartments of seemingly interminable length, with no hall and with each room opening into the next one. Railroad flats, she had heard them called. [017-001]

## Geographic Boundaries

Harlem's southern boundary, [[one-hundred-and-tenth-street|110th Street]], serves as a symbolic racial dividing line. Below it lies the white Manhattan. Above it lies the Black world that Mimi first embraces and then flees.

> It came to her suddenly why the impulse to go to Harlem had sprung to her mind. Now that she was once again headed towards Harlem, she felt the old attraction which had come over her that first night in New York. [023-024]

> She came to the surface again in Harlem and there she wandered through the streets. She wondered why she had never come to Harlem as she had walked through the streets where French and Italian and Gipsy and Jewish people lived. [023-032]

## Cultural and Literary Significance

In *Flight*, Harlem functions as both setting and symbol. It is the living proof of Black institution-building — churches, newspapers, clubs, businesses — that the novel celebrates. But it is also a closed system of surveillance and moral enforcement that the novel critiques.

The novel's Harlem is not the Harlem of the Renaissance's triumphant self-presentation. It is seen from the margins — from the perspective of a woman whose survival depends on escaping the very community that ought to sustain her. In this sense, *Flight* anticipates more ambivalent treatments of Harlem in later African American fiction.
