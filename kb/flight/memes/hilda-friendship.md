# Mimi and Hilda's First Meeting

The encounter between fourteen-year-old [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] and [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]] at [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club party, which blossoms into an immediate and powerful friendship.

## Overview

Mimi and Hilda's first meeting is the novel's most enchanted encounter. The two girls, both fourteen, are brought together at [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club gathering at [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]]'s home. Their connection is instantaneous and inexplicable: "Hilda and Mimi stood looking at each other, each caught and held fast by some power, neither of them knew what nor even that there was such a power holding them. For a minute — an hour — a century they stood there unable either to move or speak" (004-011).

The friendship that forms in that moment will be the novel's most enduring relationship between equals. Unlike Mimi's relationships with men — her father, [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]], the men she encounters while passing — her bond with Hilda is based in mutual recognition rather than dependence or transaction. Their friendship survives the novel's many displacements: the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] race riot, Jean's death, Mimi's flight to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], and though they are separated by geography and [[mimi-passing-decision|Mimi's decision to pass]], the bond remains.

## Narrative Context

The meeting occurs in chapter 4, during [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club gathering. Mimi has been observing the adult women's gossip and cruelty when Hilda, "who stood silent just beyond her mother," appears (004-011). The two girls immediately retreat from the adult world: "'Come on, let's sit over there on the steps and talk,' said Hilda simply, taking Mimi's hand" (004-013).

Their conversation is described in language of almost mystical intensity. "They began with questions of each other, about school, about their childhood, revealing little intimacies that subtly wove between them a gossamer band of friendship, fragile and almost invisible, yet with the strength of piano wire" (004-014). Mimi, who "through all of her fourteen years, had had no confidante other than her father," finds in Hilda the companion she has been missing.

Jean later tells Mimi: "I'm happy you've found Hilda. You need the companionship of young people — when you're as old as I it won't be so easy to find somebody whose ideas fit in with your own" (004-031).

## Aftermath

Hilda is Mimi's anchor through the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] years. They walk to school together, confide in each other, and weather the community's judgments together. Hilda is present at [[jean-funeral|Jean's funeral]], providing comfort. Even after Mimi leaves [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], the friendship endures in memory — Hilda and [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] "championed the cause of Mimi" during the [[harlem-scandal|Harlem scandal]] (016-042), offering the loyalty that the broader community denied.
