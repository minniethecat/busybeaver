# Mimi's Employment at Francine's

[[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s first day as a finisher at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] exclusive dress shop in [[new-york-city|New York City]], the practical commencement of her new life passing as a white woman.

## Overview

Mimi's employment at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] is the practical implementation of her decision to pass for white. After the [[harlem-scandal|Harlem scandal]] destroyed her reputation in the Black community, Mimi applies to [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] — "the only place to which she applied" (017-011) — drawing on her [[manhattan-trade-school|Manhattan Trade School]] training and her skill as a seamstress to secure a position as a finisher.

The employment is both a professional achievement and an existential gamble. Mimi must construct and maintain a white identity — a French identity, leveraging her Creole background and language skills — in a workplace where discovery would mean ruin. [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] represents the economic opportunity that the white world offers and the Black world, at this point in Mimi's life, has denied her.

## Narrative Context

The employment is described in chapter 17, which details Mimi's transition from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] to her new life. She has secured a small room "with a private family, living in the lower Nineties, respectable but commonplace" (017-002), far from the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] community she has left behind.

At [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], Mimi joins four other floor workers. She is "diffident" despite her training, but her skill at the [[manhattan-trade-school|Manhattan Trade School]] gives her confidence (017-020). The shop operates under strict discipline: "A whisper from the fitter or a very rare visit from [[francines-dress-shop|Francine]] herself hushed their whispered conversations" (017-021).

[[madame-francine|Madame Francine]] herself is "the high priestess" of the establishment (017-013), a figure who has carefully constructed the shop's aura of French exclusivity. Mimi studies her, observing how she manages both clients and workers, and begins to imagine "herself sitting as [[francines-dress-shop|Francine]] now did in a delicately beautiful little office" (017-047). The employment is thus not only a practical necessity but the seed of Mimi's professional ambition.

## Aftermath

Mimi's work at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] is the foundation of her new life. It provides her with income, a professional identity, and daily practice in the performance of whiteness. Her success at the shop — earning the respect of colleagues and the satisfaction of clients — validates her decision to pass and sets the stage for the novel's final act: her romantic life as a white woman and the ultimate question of whether passing can provide a sustainable life.
