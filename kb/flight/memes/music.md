Music runs through Walter White's *Flight* like a second narrative voice, speaking what the characters cannot say aloud. From the "distinctive Negro music" (`007-001`) of the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] church to the "barbaric sweetness and rhythm" (`024-009`) of the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] cabaret, music marks the key moments of [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s journey. It appears at moments of revelation, transformation, and crisis — a force that bypasses the conscious mind and speaks directly to what White calls "the unspoiled child within" (`024-007`).

The novel's treatment of music reflects a broader understanding in early 20th-century African American thought: that Black music was not merely entertainment but a repository of racial memory and a form of cultural resistance. The spirituals, ragtime, and early jazz that punctuate *Flight* are presented as expressions of a "soul of the race" (`006-060`) that white America could imitate but never truly possess.

## Music as Cultural Expression

White emphasizes the distinctive character of Black musical performance throughout the novel. At the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] church service Mimi attends early in the story, she is stirred by "the music which had a distinctive Negro quality — a plaintive mournful something that reached the depths" (`007-001`). This quality is presented as innate, unteachable: "None of its members had ever taken a music lesson, none of them knew anything of the technique of music, yet it was all there" (`007-018`).

The passage makes an implicit argument about authenticity and cultural inheritance. The musicians' power comes not from formal training but from lived experience — "a distinctive Negro quality" that White connects to the history of enslavement and survival. This is music as racial testimony.

> None of its members had ever taken a music lesson, none of them knew anything of the technique of music, yet it was all there — beautiful, moving, compelling — the essence of a people's sorrow and joy and aspiration. (`007-018`)

The novel contrasts this authentic Black expression with the commercialized music consumed by white audiences. When Mimi passes into the white world, music becomes a commodity — "a music hall air she had heard the night before" (`019-035`) — stripped of its spiritual dimension.

## Music and Mimi's Inner Life

Music serves as the novel's most reliable indicator of Mimi's true emotional state. At moments when her racial passing demands emotional suppression, music breaks through the facade:

> Under the spell of the music other doors opened one by one and forms stepped out — some from the past that were graven deep in her memory, some that were of the present, but each one of that company of whom she had been for some time past unexpectedly reminded. (`024-013`)

At the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] cabaret — a setting that functions as the novel's emotional climax — music becomes a portal to Mimi's suppressed self. The "music of barbaric sweetness and rhythm" (`024-009`) unlocks memories she has spent years repressing, forcing a confrontation with the identity she has tried to leave behind.

The novel's closing invocation of music — quoting John Keats's line "The music yearning like a God in pain" (`024-016`) — elevates the theme to a metaphysical register. Music yearns toward wholeness, toward the integration of self that Mimi's passing has made impossible.

## Music in the Social World

Beyond its psychological function, music in *Flight* maps the novel's social geography:

| Setting | Musical Style | Social Meaning |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] church | Negro spirituals and hymnody | Communal racial identity, sacred tradition |
| [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] home gatherings | "Occasional musical evenings" (`005-028`) | [[respectability-politics|Respectability]], middle-class aspiration |
| [[creole-quarter|Creole quarter]] dances | "Three or four-piece orchestra" (`007-015`) | Creole cultural distinctiveness |
| [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] cabaret | Ragtime, early jazz, "barbaric" rhythms | Liberation, modernity, racial crossing |
| White [[new-york-city|New York]] | "Music hall airs" (`019-035`) | Commercialized culture, emotional emptiness |

Each musical setting carries a distinct social meaning. The transition from sacred music (church) to secular music (cabaret) mirrors Mimi's own passage from the protected world of [[new-orleans-louisiana|Creole New Orleans]] to the anonymous world of white [[new-york-city|New York]]. That the novel's final musical scene occurs in a [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] cabaret — with Mimi returning, however tentatively, to Black space — is no accident.

## Cross-Cultural Appeal and Appropriation

White is attentive to how Black music attracted white audiences even as white society maintained racial segregation. The [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] cabaret Mimi visits is frequented by white patrons seeking exoticism:

> It was the rendezvous of Negro and white musicians and writers and bohemians who sought something different from the conventional places of amusement. (`015-043`)

This observation captures a central tension of the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] Renaissance: Black art was celebrated by white audiences while Black artists and citizens remained second-class. The music crossed the color line even when the musicians could not. Mimi's own fate — appreciated for her beauty and style while her racial identity remains a secret — recapitulates this pattern.
