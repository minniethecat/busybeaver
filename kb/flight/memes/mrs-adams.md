## Overview

Mrs. Adams is a member of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Negro elite in Walter White's *Flight*. She is the mother of [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]] — [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s first close friend — and the wife of Dr. Adams, a physician.

Mrs. Adams is defined within the novel's social world by a single scarring incident: her ejection from a white theatre where she had been passing for white to sit in the orchestra section. This episode encapsulates the dangers of crossing the color line.

The incident marks her permanently within Atlanta's Negro society, where she is "invited to all the parties" but carries the weight of having been publicly humiliated.

## In the Story

### The Theatre Incident

Mrs. Adams's defining moment is narrated rather than dramatized. Hilda tells Mimi that Mrs. Adams had been "buying seats in the orchestra, 'passing' for white, and seeing all the" performances from the best seats (004-008).

The scheme worked until she was recognized and ejected — a public shaming that reverberated through Atlanta's Negro society. The woman who witnessed her ejection "is Mrs. Watkins. Her husband's a doctor" (004-020).

The incident becomes a moral lesson within the community about the dangers of aspiring beyond one's assigned place. Mimi, watching this scene, feels deep sympathy for Mrs. Adams, "to the same degree that she felt a yearning to touch, to smile at Mrs. Adams" (004-010).

### Social Standing

Despite the theatre incident, Mrs. Adams maintains her place in Atlanta's elite Negro society. She is "invited to all the parties" (004-050). When she "entertained the last time, Mrs. Adams served simple but well-prepared" refreshments (004-051).

Her continued inclusion suggests both the community's capacity for forgiveness and its pragmatic recognition of how common passing was among light-skinned Negroes in Atlanta.

### The Daquin Connection

Mrs. Adams becomes particularly close to the Daquin family. She is present at [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club meeting where Mary Daquin falls ill, staying behind "after the women had gone" to help (005-056).

When [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] asks frantically about Mary's condition, "Mrs. Adams' calm reply" serves as a steadying force (005-057). She tells Jean that she and Hilda will stay to help (005-062).

Mrs. Adams takes her turn entertaining the Fleur-de-Lis Club in October. Mimi worries "Mrs. Adams will feel badly" if they do not attend (010-008).

## Relationships

Mrs. Adams's relationship with her daughter [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]] is protective. Her marriage to Dr. Adams provides the family with professional standing.

Her friendship with [[mary-robertson|Mary Daquin]] is cemented by club membership and the crisis of Mary's illness. Her relationship with the broader society is defined by the theatre incident — a mark of vulnerability.

## Key Moments

**The Theatre Incident:** Discussed by club members for having passed at the [[grand-opera-house-atlanta|Grand Opera House]] (004-008). Hilda tells Mimi about the ejection (004-020).

**Social Position:** Is "invited to all the parties" despite the scandal (004-050). Serves "simple but well-prepared" refreshments (004-051).

**Daquin Connection:** Stays to care for the ill Mary Daquin (005-056). Gives "calm reply" to Jean's fearful question (005-057). Agrees with Hilda to stay and help (005-062). Takes her turn entertaining the club in October (010-007).
