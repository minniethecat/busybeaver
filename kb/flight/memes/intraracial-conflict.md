## Definition

Intraracial conflict in *Flight* refers to the tensions, rivalries, and betrayals that occur within Black communities — as distinct from the conflict between Black people and white racism. The novel explores this theme through [[education-and-uplift|Booker T. Washington]]'s famous metaphor of the crab basket: when one Black person climbs toward freedom, others reach up with their claws and pull them back down to their own level (017-005).

The novel presents intraracial conflict not as a moral failing of Black people but as a structural consequence of oppression. In a world where opportunities for advancement are artificially limited, competition for those opportunities becomes fierce. The gossip about [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] at [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club, the scandal that destroys Mimi's reputation in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] — these are presented as symptoms of a system that channels frustration inward rather than outward.

## Historical Context

[[education-and-uplift|Booker T. Washington]]'s \"crabs in a basket\" metaphor, which Mimi recalls in a moment of despair, captured a widely recognized phenomenon in Black community life. The metaphor suggested that Black progress was often obstructed not only by white racism but also by jealousy, gossip, and sabotage from within the community itself.

The social dynamics of respectability politics exacerbated these internal tensions. Those who had achieved middle-class status policed the boundaries of their class position, while those who transgressed community norms — by passing, by sexual impropriety, by social ambition — became targets for collective punishment. The [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]] scene illustrates this dynamic with painful clarity.

## In the Novel

The novel's central narrative arc turns on an act of intraracial conflict: the scandal that destroys Mimi's reputation in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], after her relationship with a married man is discovered. This scandal, driven by gossip and moral judgment from within her own community, is what ultimately drives Mimi to pass for white. She tells [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]]: \"I never thought I'd want to leave my own people. I wouldn't leave them now but they've driven me away\" (016-060).

The [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]] scene presents a microcosm of intraracial conflict. The women's gossip about [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] — discussed with evident satisfaction and envy — reveals how color prejudice, class anxiety, and personal rivalry combine to produce a particularly toxic form of community policing. The fact that the women greet [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] warmly to her face only underscores the duplicity that Mimi finds so repellent (004-009).

The crab basket metaphor, when Mimi recalls it, has transformed from an oratorical device into lived experience. What had once seemed \"merely an effective story coined for oratorical purposes\" now is \"forced upon her with painful truth\" (017-005). The novel suggests that this internal conflict is inseparable from the external pressure of white racism — each reinforces the other in a cycle that makes solidarity difficult and escape appealing.
