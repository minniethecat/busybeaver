## Location

Peachtree Street runs through the heart of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], serving as the city's principal commercial and civic artery. In the early twentieth century it was lined with banks, department stores, office buildings, and churches, representing the concentrated wealth and power of white [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. The street was the center of the city's retail district and the spine from which other major thoroughfares -- including [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue, the corresponding main street of black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] -- branched eastward. The "Sacred Heart" church, the Catholic parish mentioned derisively by [[mrs-plummer|Mrs. Plummer]] (`001-031`), stood along its length, a marker of white religious and institutional presence.

Peachtree Street was, by law and custom, white space. Colored people might walk its sidewalks, shop in its stores if they observed the strict protocols of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]], and work in its buildings, but they could not claim it as their own. Its very name in the novel serves as shorthand for the world that excluded them.

## In the Novel

Peachtree Street appears at two critical junctures in the novel, both during the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] chapters. [[mrs-plummer|Mrs. Plummer]], the salty-tongued cook in the Daquin [[home|household]], invokes the street to ridicule what she sees as colored people's misplaced aspiration: "Is you got a picture, [[mrs-plummer|Mis' Plummer]], of any coloured folks no matter what kinda religion they got stickin' their heads in that 'Sacred Heart' church out Peachtree Street?" (`001-031`). In [[mrs-plummer|Mrs. Plummer]]'s view, the white Jesus of Peachtree Street's churches has nothing to offer colored people, and to seek it is to forget who you are.

The street's most dramatic appearance comes during the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] race riot. As the mob sweeps through the city, the fleeing car speeds "down Peachtree Street, to [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue, down [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue to [[pryor-street|Pryor Street]]," where a "shout welling into a roar" forces them back (`005-082`). The geography is precise and telling: Peachtree Street, the domain of white commerce, gives way to [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue, the Negro thoroughfare, and then to [[pryor-street|Pryor Street]], where the violence of the mob is at its worst. The movement from white space to black space, from commerce to chaos, charts [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]]'s trajectory and the vulnerability of the colored community.

## Significance

Peachtree Street embodies the racial geography of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. It is the avenue of white prosperity, the physical manifestation of a social order that denied colored people full participation in the city's economic and civic life. The street's juxtaposition with [[auburn-avenue]] -- one the seat of white power, the other of black enterprise -- maps the segregated city. [[mrs-plummer|Mrs. Plummer]]'s mockery of Peachtree Street religion and [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]]'s path down the same street together suggest that white [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s institutions, whether sacred or commercial, offered no protection to colored citizens when racial violence erupted.

The Daquin family's [[atlanta-arrival|arrival in Atlanta]] was conditioned from the start by this racial geography. [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]], Mary's brother, carefully routed their carriage to avoid certain streets, explaining that "if he'd gone that way he'd have carried you through Decatur and [[ivy-street|Ivy Street]]s -- that's the Nigger district" (`001-007`). Peachtree Street, by contrast, was the proper public face of the city -- the streets through which respectable white [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] moved. The "laws of [[georgia-piedmont|Georgia]]" that governed streetcar segregation (`005-010`) ensured that even this public thoroughfare was racially ordered: white passengers from the front, colored from the rear.

The street also functions as a symbol of the larger Southern order the Daquins left [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to escape. [[jean-daquin]] had hoped [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] would prove more tolerant; [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] that rolled down Peachtree Street proved otherwise, setting in motion Mimi's long journey north. As Mimi later gazes from the train window at "the bare red hills of [[georgia-piedmont|Georgia]]" (`012-067`), she is leaving behind not just [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] but the whole [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] geography -- Peachtree Street included -- that had destroyed her family.
