## Overview

Jerry Reed is the married man whose relationship with [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] becomes public scandal in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]]. The novel treats this relationship with characteristic restraint — Jerry appears only in fragments, through the gossip and judgment of others. What matters is not the relationship itself but its consequences: the destruction of Mimi's reputation, her social death in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], and her desperate conclusion that she must leave colored people for good.

The Jerry Reed scandal illustrates the novel's critique of intraracial community policing. Mimi is judged harshly for the relationship, while Jerry — if he faces consequences at all — appears to suffer far less. The gendered double standard is clear: a woman's sexual transgression destroys her, while a man's is overlooked. This injustice is part of what drives Mimi to conclude that the community that should protect her instead persecutes her (016-060).
