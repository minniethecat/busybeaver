# The Daquin Family's Departure from New Orleans

The relocation of the Daquin family from [[new-orleans-louisiana|Creole New Orleans]] to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta, Georgia]], driven by Mary Daquin's ambition and [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s reluctant consent to move his family into a new and unfamiliar world.

## Overview

The Daquin family's departure from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] is the novel's opening movement, establishing the conflict between [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s Old World Creole sensibilities and the forces of economic ambition and racial pragmatism that will define the family's experience in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. Mary Daquin, supported by her [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] businessman father, [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]], has long agitated for the move — [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], she insists, is "a dead old hole" (001-044) compared to the opportunities awaiting in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s thriving Black business district.

For Jean, the departure is an act of profound sacrifice. The novel depicts his "delightfully painful nostalgia for his [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]]" (001-009) and his foreboding about the world he is entering. "Leaving [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], the old houses, the old friends, the old customs," he reflects — these are the things he is surrendering (002-080). The departure is not just a journey but a rupture: the end of a Creole identity that had its own complex relationship to race and culture, distinct from the binary racial order of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]].

## Narrative Context

The departure is narrated across chapters 1 (the train journey and arrival) and 2 (flashback to the decision and final days in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]]). Chapter 1 opens with the train "rumbled and swayed" through the southern landscape, arriving at [[atlanta-union-station|Union Station]] in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] (001-001). Chapter 2 returns to [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to recount Jean's courtship of Mary, his resistance to leaving, and his eventual capitulation.

[[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]]'s role is crucial: he "rushed to [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], stormed, denounced, ridiculed" Jean's resistance (002-044), leveraging his economic power and his daughter's determination. Jean's final acceptance of the move — driven by the failure of his distillery and ice plant businesses (002-049) — is presented as a defeat forced by economic reality and marital pressure.

## Aftermath

The departure sets the novel's trajectory in motion. The family's [[atlanta-arrival|arrival in Atlanta]] places them directly in the path of the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|1906 race riot]], which will destroy Jean's fragile adaptation to his new world. The Creole identity that Jean and Mimi brought from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] — fluid, French, Catholic — will prove both a liability and a resource in the racial landscape of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] and, later, [[new-york-city|New York]].
