## Location

Fifth Avenue is the central spine of [[new-york-city|Manhattan]], running from [[washington-square-nyc|Washington Square]] Park in Greenwich Village north to [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]]. In the early twentieth century, it was already established as the city's most prestigious commercial and residential boulevard. Below [[one-hundred-and-tenth-street|110th Street]], Fifth Avenue was synonymous with wealth, fashion, and the white upper class — the avenue of mansions, department stores, and the kind of "carefully groomed and tolerably handsome young men" to be seen "any afternoon on Fifth Avenue or in the fashionable streets near Piccadilly" (`019-031`).

Above the park, however, as the avenue entered [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], it became the western boundary of the Black quarter. The Fifth Avenue buses, "lumbering" through the winter snow (`023-002`), transported passengers between these two worlds — [[harlem-new-york|Black Harlem]] and white Manhattan — and for Mimi, the avenue became a recurring threshold.

## In the Novel

> All day she worked and in the evenings she either stayed at home and made dresses and other garments for herself to replace the worn things she had brought to New York, or with her aunt she walked through the streets of Harlem or rode on the bus down Fifth Avenue. `015-041`

Fifth Avenue appears repeatedly in the novel as a point of crossing between Mimi's two lives. During her early years in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], she "rode on the bus down Fifth Avenue" (`015-041`) on evening outings with her [[mrs-rogers|Aunt Sophie]], and the "rides down town and back of an evening on the Fifth Avenue buses" (`015-047`) were among the few diversions from her routine of sewing and saving every dollar to reclaim her son Jean. In this period, Fifth Avenue was a place of modest pleasure — the view from the bus window, the spectacle of the city unfolding — and the boundary between the [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] she lived in and the [[new-york-city|New York]] she was learning to navigate.

During the [[world-war-i|First World War]], the avenue took on a more ominous cast: "khaki-clad Americans marched up Fifth Avenue and were whisked mysteriously away" (`018-005`), as [[world-war-i|the war]] became a "grim, horrible reality" felt even in the quiet of [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's dress shop]].

When Mimi later passes as white and lives in [[westchester-county|Westchester]], Fifth Avenue becomes part of her husband Jimmie Forrester's world. She sees Jimmie "crossing perilously Fifth Avenue, dodging taxicabs and buses by a hair's breadth" (`020-026`), and she dodges into a shop to avoid him. The avenue here is the terrain of her deception — a place where she must evade the man who does not know her real identity.

In the novel's final section, after Mimi hears the [[music|jazz]] concert and recognizes the truth of her own alienation, she and Jimmie "silently drove home through the quiet emptiness of lower Fifth Avenue" (`024-016`). The silence and emptiness of the avenue at this moment mirrors the hollow space between them — she has realized she "did not know" this man she had married, and Fifth Avenue, for all its grandeur, is for her a road through a world that is not her own.
