# The Great Migration

The mass movement of approximately six million African Americans from the rural South to the urban North between 1910 and 1970, with the first wave occurring during the novel's timeframe.

## Overview

[[the-great-migration|The Great Migration]] was the largest internal population movement in American history. Millions left the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] South for industrial cities in the North. Push factors included racial violence, economic exploitation, and legalized segregation. Pull factors included [[world-war-i|World War I]] jobs and the relative safety of northern cities.

*Flight* traces the personal dimension of this movement. The Daquin family's journey from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], and ultimately [[new-york-city|New York]] and [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] mirrors the broader migration. Each displacement is driven by violence, economic need, and the search for community.

## Narrative Context

The novel spans the first wave of the Great Migration. The Daquins' move from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] precedes the main wave but anticipates its logic — movement toward urban centers with Black economic institutions. The flight from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] after the 1906 riot (chapters 5-6) represents racial violence as a migration catalyst.

Mimi's later moves reflect the migration's complexity. After Jean's death, she "boarded a train for Philadelphia" (012-067), choosing a city where "she knew no one." Her trajectory — from [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] to [[new-york-city|New York]], then from [[harlem-new-york|Black Harlem]] to white Manhattan — shows relocation offered opportunity without guaranteeing safety.

> "A few weeks later Mimi boarded a train for Philadelphia. She could give no reason why she chose that city to which to go." (012-067)

## Key Characters and the Migration

[[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]], established in [[new-york-city|New York]] before Mimi's arrival, represents the migration success story: economic stability, independence, and new social networks in the North. She tells Mimi:

> "Won't you go back to New York with me? ... And up North you'd have a much better chance than here where all you can do is to teach school or get married." (011-051)

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] is the reluctant migrant. He mourns the old Creole world of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] (002-092). His displacement to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] expresses the forces that would drive millions northward. He was "the follower of an older day, of an age that was passing even in Louisiana, that definitely had passed here in Georgia" (004-043).

> "If only I could get some money saved and secure a steady income, perhaps start a small dressmaking establishment of my own, I could see ahead." (014-052)

## Aftermath

[[the-great-migration|The Great Migration]] transformed American society, creating Black urban communities that became centers of cultural production and political power. For Mimi, the migration culminates in an ironic extreme: passing for white fulfills the migration's promise of self-transformation while repudiating the community the migration created.
