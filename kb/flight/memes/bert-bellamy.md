## Overview

Bert Bellamy is a wealthy socialite who moves in the same New York circles as Jimmie and Mimi Forrester. The son of a Chicago stockyard magnate — "Bellamy, Senior, had made untold amounts through the stockyards he owned in Chicago and Kansas City" — Bellamy is a bachelor of independent means whose principal occupations are socializing and gathering gossip about the prominent and near-prominent [022-009].

Physically, Bellamy is described as "short and rotund, his face cherubic and ruddy like that of a healthy child" and "always faultlessly dressed," giving the impression that "he kept at his bachelor apartments a barber, a tailor, a manicurist and a haberdasher, all constantly busy" [022-009]. He shudders when anyone brings up the origin of the Bellamy fortune in stockyards, preferring to affect the manner of old money rather than acknowledge his family's industrial roots.

## In the Story

### The Crosby Dinner Party (Chapter 22)

Bellamy first appears at Mrs. Crosby's dinner party for the Chinese diplomat Wu Hseh-Chuan. He greets Mimi with a characteristically flattering remark: "Mimi, my dear, those orchids look, compared to their wearer, like scrub cabbages!" [022-008]. Mimi responds with gentle mockery — "That sounds nice — even if it is a bit silly" — and Bellamy, accustomed to gratitude for his polished phrases, is intrigued by her resistance [022-009].

Bellamy's defining trait is his insatiable appetite for personal information. He "went everywhere, knew everybody and, to him most fascinating, he knew everything about everybody" [022-011]. He had previously made a veiled advance toward Mimi, suggesting dinner when Jimmie was away on business, but Mimi recognized his intentions and declined with a gentle laugh. Bellamy gracefully withdrew, calling her a "little mid-Victorian," and they settled into an uneasy friendship.

### Escort and Racial Tensions (Chapter 23)

When Jimmie must travel to Chicago on short notice, he arranges for Bellamy to take Mimi to the theatre [023-014]. During their outing, they visit a Harlem nightclub where Bellamy sings "the latest 'Mammy' song in what he fondly imagined to be Negro dialect" [023-024]. More troubling, he casually uses the word "nigger" in conversation, which causes Mimi to "suddenly hate him" [023-029]. These moments crystallize for Mimi the chasm between her hidden identity and the casually racist world she has entered.

### Mimi's Assessment

In a moment of reflection, Mimi catalogs Bellamy among those who "drug themselves with the opiate" of empty diversions: "Bert Bellamy drugging himself with the opiate of inquisitive prying into the affairs of the prominent and the near-prominent" [023-006]. He represents, for Mimi, the spiritual emptiness of the sophisticated white world — a man of charm and surfaces whose life is devoted to the trivial.
