## Overview

Jean Daquin is [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s father and one of the most pivotal figures in Walter White's *Flight*. A Creole tailor from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], Jean embodies the proud but precarious world of Creole identity at the turn of the twentieth century.

He traces his lineage to a San Domingan quadroon, Major Jean Daquin, who fought alongside Andrew Jackson at the Battle of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] (002-099). This heritage gives him immense personal dignity but little protection against [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]].

Jean is "improvident, oblivious of material advantage" (002-033), content in the Creole quarter. His marriage to Mary Robertson of [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] brings the outside world's racial binary into his Creole universe.

As a father, Jean invests Mimi with the Daquin family philosophy. This inheritance becomes both strength and burden for Mimi.

## In the Story

### Creole New Orleans

Jean Daquin lives in the Creole quarter of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], working as a tailor in the old French style. His world is one of "the romance and languorous charm of the Creole quarter" (002-036).

He ran his own "little sick-and-accident insurance company" in New Orleans, which furnished sufficient revenue to satisfy their simple needs (002-028).

### Marriage to Mary Robertson

Jean's marriage to Mary Robertson represents the collision of two worlds. "To the marriage there arose storms of protest," for Mary, though "respectable and worthy, yet was not of Creole blood" (002-044).

Despite opposition, Jean marries her, and she begins to reshape their lives, steering them toward [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] (002-036).

### Atlanta and the Color Line

Jean moves to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], where Mr. Robertson had invested heavily in an industrial insurance company operated by Negroes (002-055). Through this influence, Jean received an offer to work there.

But the city's rigid color line proves alien. At gatherings, he and Mimi, "used to the Creole dilution of the Negro songs, sat straining their ears" to understand the spirituals (001-047).

After Jean's death, his cash had been "invested largely in purchasing stock in the Lincoln Mutual Insurance Company" (012-007).

### Passing on the Daquin Legacy

Jean tells Mimi of their ancestor "Major Jean Daquin, San Domingan, quadroon whose blood" runs in their veins and who fought with "Negro troops under Andrew Jackson" (002-099).

His refrain — that "the Negro blood in you set you aside" but should be a source of pride — forms the moral center of the novel (002-086).

## Relationships

Jean's relationship with Mimi is the emotional core of his character. His marriage to Mary Robertson brings ambition and energy but also the racial framework that uproots the family.

His connection to his sister, [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]], provides the network into which Mimi later retreats. Jean's philosophical nature contrasts with the aggressive practicality of his father-in-law, [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]].

## Key Moments

**New Orleans:** Awakens Mimi on the train, calling her "petite Mimi" (001-003). Sits bewildered on the surrey ride through [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] (001-009).

**Family History:** Traces ancestry back to San Domingo (002-088/002-090). Tells of Major Jean Daquin at the Battle of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] (002-099).

**Philosophy:** Teaches that "ability matters more than color" (002-046). Warns Mimi she cannot "afford to laugh here in Creole New Orleans" elsewhere (002-088).

**Atlanta:** Works at the insurance company in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] (002-055). Objects to entertaining [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club at home (010-007).
