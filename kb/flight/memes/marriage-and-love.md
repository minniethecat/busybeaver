## Definition

Marriage and romantic love in *Flight* are shaped by the intersection of personal desire with racial, cultural, and class constraints. The novel presents marriage not simply as a private bond but as a public institution through which communities regulate identity, enforce social boundaries, and negotiate the tension between tradition and aspiration. From [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s two marriages to [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s eventual passing marriage, the novel traces how romantic choices both reflect and reshape racial identity.

## Jean Daquin's Two Marriages

Jean's first marriage to Margot, Mimi's biological mother, represents the Creole ideal: an endogamous union within the tight-knit Creole community of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]]. After Margot's death, Jean spent more than a year "in a state that bordered on coma" (002-029), grieving nightly with "a faded photograph of Margot in her wedding gown" (002-029) beside a wineglass. Mimi imagined that had her mother lived, she would have "married some Creole whose ideas about life had dovetailed with those of the Daquin family" (002-026).

Jean's second marriage to [[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson]] of [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] violated the Creole community's unwritten rules. They were married in both Catholic and Baptist ceremonies (002-043), yet Mary remained an outsider — "despite her marriage to Jean she yet was and would ever remain an outsider" (002-046). The Creole families objected because Mary was not of Creole blood; Mary's father objected because Jean was both Creole and darker-skinned. The novel describes the dual rejection: "He was alternately reviled and pitied for marrying an outsider" while Mary's father "stormed, denounced, ridiculed, pleaded, but in vain" (002-044).

## Love Across the Color Line

Mary's love for Jean, despite their incompatibility, is described with genuine tenderness: "She loved gentle, irresponsible Jean with her whole heart, with a woman's inconsistency, though he offended in almost every particular the canons of efficiency and progress which were a part of her very being" (002-047). Yet the marriage deteriorated under the pressure of their differing values — Mary's social ambition versus Jean's Creole contentment — until Mary's nagging and Jean's withdrawal became the pattern of their domestic life (002-055).

This deterioration reveals the novel's broader commentary: romantic love, however genuine, is vulnerable to the structural pressures of racial hierarchy and class aspiration. Jean's hope that Mimi would "be married before he joined Margot" (006-013) reflects the parental desire to see children securely placed within the institution, even as that institution proved inadequate to protect them.

## Romantic Love and Adolescent Awakening

Mimi's relationship with [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] introduces the theme of adolescent romance shaped by social surveillance. Their "long hours they spent together deepened the love they had for each other" (005-009), but the relationship was policed by both families and the gossip networks of Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] society. The novel traces how romantic love in a racially surveilled society is never purely private — every relationship carries the weight of community judgment and the constant threat of racial violence.
