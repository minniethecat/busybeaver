## Overview

Sylvia Smith is Mimi's coworker at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], the exclusive [[new-york-city|New York]] dress shop where Mimi works as a finisher while passing for white. Sylvia is the only person at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] with whom Mimi develops anything approaching a friendship, though Mimi keeps her at a careful distance. Their adjoining work tables create a proximity that both threatens and sustains Mimi — threatening because Sylvia might discover her secret, sustaining because her chatter provides a thin thread of human connection in Mimi's isolated new life.

Sylvia represents the white working-class world that Mimi enters when she passes. She complains about the fitter (whom she nicknames Old Faithful), gossips about the other girls, and shares her lunch with Mimi — ordinary interactions that would be impossible if she knew Mimi's true racial identity. The novel uses Sylvia to dramatize the precarity of passing: every casual conversation carries the risk of exposure.

## In the Novel

Sylvia's most significant moment comes when she casually mentions that the previous fitter at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] was a coloured girl. The remark sends alarm through Mimi, who fears that this information might somehow lead to her own discovery (017-030). Sylvia does not notice Mimi's reaction — she has no reason to suspect the truth — but the moment crystallizes the constant vigilance that passing requires.

Sylvia's function in the novel is to show what Mimi has gained and lost by passing. She has gained access to a world of economic opportunity and freedom from racial harassment. But she has lost the ability to be honest with even her closest coworker — to share her real name, her real history, her real self. Sylvia's unknowing friendship is a reminder of the human connections that passing forecloses.
