## Definition

Education was at the center of debates about racial uplift in early 20th-century Black America. The question was not whether education was valuable but what kind: industrial education aimed at economic self-sufficiency (associated with Booker T. Washington and Tuskegee Institute), or liberal arts education aimed at developing a leadership class (associated with W.E.B. Du Bois). In *Flight*, [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s trade school education — she is trained at the [[manhattan-trade-school|Manhattan Trade School]] — represents a practical resolution of this debate.

The novel's treatment of education is pragmatic rather than ideological. Mimi's sewing skills, her French language ability, and her training all serve as tools of survival and mobility. Education in *Flight* is not presented as a path to liberation but as a form of capital that can be leveraged within the constraints of the racial order.

## Historical Context

The Washington-Du Bois debate dominated discussions of Black education in the early 20th century. Washington's 1895 [[new-south-ideology|Atlanta Compromise]] speech argued that Black people should focus on economic self-improvement rather than agitating for political rights, a position that won him support from white philanthropists but criticism from Black intellectuals who saw it as accommodationist.

Trade schools for Black students proliferated in this period, providing training in industrial and domestic skills. The [[manhattan-trade-school|Manhattan Trade School]], where Mimi trains, was part of this movement. For Black women, sewing — the skill Mimi uses to secure employment at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] — was one of the few occupations that offered a path to economic independence, however modest.

## In the Novel

Mimi's trade school education proves crucial to her ability to pass. Her training as a finisher gives her the skills to secure employment at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], the most exclusive modiste in [[new-york-city|New York]]. Her French language ability — a product of her Creole upbringing and Jean's insistence on her education — reinforces her cover story as a French woman rather than a Black one (016-060).

The novel's treatment of education reflects [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s broader philosophy: that ability, not color, should determine worth. Mimi's skills at the sewing machine demonstrate her ability, but the novel insists that in a society structured by race, ability alone cannot overcome the color line. Mimi's skills enable her to pass — which is to say, they enable her to conceal rather than transcend her racial identity.

The limit of education as an uplift strategy is revealed by Mimi's predicament. She has the education, the skills, and the cultural capital to succeed — yet she can only do so by abandoning her community and her identity. The novel suggests that individual achievement, however impressive, cannot substitute for structural transformation.
