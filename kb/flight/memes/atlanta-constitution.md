## Overview

The Atlanta Constitution was the major white daily newspaper in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta, Georgia]], during the period of *Flight*. In the summer of 1906, the Constitution played a central role in inciting the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot through its sensationalist coverage of alleged — and largely fabricated — assaults on white women by Black men.

The newspaper's role in fomenting racial violence is explicitly addressed in the novel. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]], observing the mounting tension in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] during the summer of 1906, recognizes that the "circulation-seeking newspapers" are deliberately inflaming public opinion with "extra" editions and screaming headlines (005-030). The Constitution's coverage was part of a broader pattern of media incitement that the [[naacp|NAACP]] would later make a target of its anti-lynching campaigns.

## In the Novel

The Atlanta Constitution is not named directly by the novel, but its practices are described in detail through [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s observations. The novel reports that "circulation-seeking newspapers brought out 'extra' after 'extra,' screaming in twelve-inch headlines, 'Third Assault,' 'Fourth Assault,' 'Negro Attempts to Assault Mrs. Mary Chafin Near Sugar Creek Bridge,' 'Angry Citizens Pursue Black Brute'" (005-030). This sensationalism is explicitly linked to the violence that follows.

Jean confronts the bank cashier, Mr. Stewart, about the media's role: "They spread the story of an alleged or a reported attack all over the front page — but when they found the woman was mistaken or hysterical from reading all these other wild tales, they stick it in an insignificant item on the inside, if they mention it at all" (005-036). He warns that "there's trouble ahead if these inflammatory stories aren't stopped" (005-036).

The novel later notes that "it was to be discovered that all but two of these reports were without foundation in truth" (005-030), confirming Jean's analysis and indicting the newspapers for their role in manufacturing a crisis that led to mob violence.
