## Overview

[[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s childhood in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] is the novel's emotional and cultural baseline — the world of Creole identity, French language, and paternal devotion that she will spend the rest of the novel trying to recover. Born around 1892 to [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] and his first wife (who died when Mimi was young), she grows up in the Creole Quarter, absorbing her father's pride in his ancestry and his philosophy that worth is measured by ability, not color.

The childhood section of the novel is suffused with nostalgia. The golden sunset of that age was fading into the twilight (002-059) — even as the narrator describes Mimi's early years, the language of [[death|loss]] predominates. [[creole-quarter|The Creole Quarter]] is already changing, the old families dying off, the Anglo-American racial binary encroaching. Mimi's childhood happiness is inseparable from the awareness that it cannot last.
