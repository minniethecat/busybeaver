## Overview

[[atlanta-constitution|The Atlanta Constitution]] was the city's leading white newspaper during the period of the novel. Under the editorship of figures like Henry Grady and Clark Howell, the paper promoted the [[new-south-ideology|New South]] ideology while also publishing the sensationalized coverage of alleged Black criminality that helped trigger the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|1906 race riot]]. The paper's circulation of inflammatory stories — particularly around allegations of Black men assaulting white women — created the conditions for mob violence.

In the novel's world, [[atlanta-constitution|the Constitution]] represents the institutional power of white media to shape racial narratives. Its coverage of Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]ns alternated between the paternalistic (celebrating respectable Negroes who knew their place) and the inflammatory (sensationalizing alleged Black crimes). This dual discourse — uplift for the compliant, violence for the transgressive — structured the discursive environment in which [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|the riot]] occurred.
