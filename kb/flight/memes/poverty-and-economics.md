## Definition

Poverty and economic class in *Flight* operate as both material conditions and moral markers. The novel maps a complex economic landscape within the Black community: from the slums of Decatur and Ivy Streets to the aspiring middle class of [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Avenue]] Atlanta, from Creole gentility to American commercial ambition. The tension between two value systems — the Creole aristocracy's contempt for money-grubbing and the American gospel of financial striving — structures much of the novel's social commentary.

## The Creole Critique of Commerce

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] articulates the Creole position most clearly: "Happiness cannot be bought with dollars — look at the Americans north of Canal Street and you'll see I am right. They scramble and fight and scheme to gain a few dollars and when they have them what do they do? They fight and scramble and scheme for more!" (002-053). For Jean, the Creole community's cardinal sin is "avarice or stinginess," while "poverty — all these are minor faults to be forgiven and forgotten" (002-096).

This philosophy places Jean at odds with his father-in-law [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]], whose every thought revolves around "Money — money — money — how much is it worth? — how much can I make out of it?" (001-015). The novel's critique of Robertson's commercialism is sharp: Jean resolves he "will never let my soul be turned into a moneygrubber's" (001-015), framing the conflict as a spiritual rather than merely economic choice.

## Class Within the Black Community

The novel documents pronounced class stratification within Black Atlanta. [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]]'s instruction to avoid "Decatur and Ivy Streets — that's the slum district — saloons and houses" (001-007), characterizing its residents as the "lowest kind of Negroes," reveals an intra-racial class hierarchy that mirrors white society's structures. The [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]] enforces class boundaries through elaborate social performances, while Mrs. Plummer and Mrs. King examine each new piece of furniture "to speculate on its probable cost" (001-038).

Jean observes this class performance with dismay: "Here are these coloured people with the gifts from God of laughter and song and of creative instincts — and what are they doing with it? They are aping the white man — becoming a race of moneygrubbers with ledgers and money tills for brains and Shylock hearts" (004-036). His daughter [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] offers the pragmatic rejoinder: "But, papa Jean, they've got to do it! They're living in a world where they must either make money or else perish" (004-037).

## Economic Precarity and Violence

The novel connects economic conditions to racial violence directly. The [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot]] of 1906 is preceded by "a period of unemployment" that "caused a marked loafing of whites and Negroes followed by a long series of petty crimes" (005-030). Jean observes with growing alarm "the glances of suspicion, of hostility, of hatred" from white tradesmen and bank officials (005-031). The novel insists that economic precarity is not merely a condition of Black life but a mechanism through which racial terror is produced.
