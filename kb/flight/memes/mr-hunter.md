# Mr. Hunter

Mr. Hunter is the president of the [[lincoln-club|Lincoln Club]] and husband of [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]], appearing as a prominent figure in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black business community.

## Personality and Character

Mr. Hunter is portrayed as a hustling, jovial businessman who enjoys telling stories and jokes. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] liked him better than most men he met in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], though Jean noted that his one flaw was an inability to resist telling a new joke when he had heard one [004-044][004-045]. He is described as "the best of the lot" among [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black business leaders [004-033].

Mr. Hunter calls [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis Club]] "[[lincoln-club|the eating brigade]]," showing his irreverent humor toward his wife's social ambitions [003-022].

## Relationship with Jean Daquin

Mr. Hunter becomes Jean's closest male friend in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. He introduces himself at the [[atlanta-union-station|train station]] on the day the Daquins arrive [001-044]. The two men engage in extended conversations about race and business, with Mr. Hunter arguing the pragmatic, accommodationist position while Jean holds to his Creole ideals [003-012][003-015].

When [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] returns home after being expelled from college, Mr. Hunter confides his disappointment in his son to Jean, revealing the generational tension between father and son [007-031].

## Role in Jean's Funeral

After Jean's death, Mr. Hunter takes charge of the practical arrangements, volunteering to handle the estate since Jean died without a will [012-007]. He offers [[mimi-daquin|Mimi]] money to help her after the funeral, though she refuses [012-067]. He participates alongside [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]], and others in planning the funeral service [011-019].

## Significance

Mr. Hunter exemplifies the established generation of Black businessmen in early 20th-century [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] who built institutions like the [[lincoln-club|Lincoln Club]] and navigated [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] through economic power and respectability politics. His friendship with Jean and his practical, unsentimental approach to life contrast with Jean's Creole idealism, creating a dynamic that illuminates the different strategies Black men employed to survive and thrive in the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|post-riot]] South.
