# Mimi's Flight from Atlanta to Philadelphia

[[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s solitary journey from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] following the death of her father and her discovery that [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] — whom she loved and planned to marry — has been unfaithful.

## Overview

Mimi's departure from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] is the second great displacement of her life, following the family's earlier move from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]]. This departure, however, is her own decision: she leaves not as a child carried by parental choices but as a young woman choosing exile over compromise. The precipitating event is her discovery of [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]]'s betrayal — the man she loved has been unfaithful, destroying the future she had imagined for herself.

"A few weeks later Mimi boarded a train for [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]]. She could give no reason why she chose that city to which to go. The nearest she could explain her choice was that [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] was large, she knew no one there and she was sure she could lose herself in its vastness" (012-067). Her departure is deliberately anonymous: she refuses [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]]'s offer of additional money, taking only what is rightfully hers from the sale of Jean's stock, and rejects [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]]'s pleas that she marry Carl to salvage her reputation.

## Narrative Context

The departure closes chapter 12, following the aftermath of Jean's death. Mimi has been betrayed by Carl and abandoned by the social world she trusted. Her stepmother's solution — marrying Carl despite everything — represents the pragmatic accommodation to patriarchy that Mimi refuses. [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]] "pleaded with her to the hour of departure to marry Carl, but Mimi's determination grew stronger with her pleading" (012-067).

Mimi's choice of [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] is significant partly for its randomness — she wants anonymity above all — and partly for its symbolic resonance. [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], the "City of Brotherly Love," represents the promise of northern freedom that had drawn the Daquins from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] in the first place. But this second journey north carries no family, no introductions, no [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]] to orchestrate her arrival. Mimi travels alone.

## Aftermath

The departure initiates Mimi's [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] years, described in chapters 13-15: her solitary pregnancy and the birth of [[petit-jean|Petit Jean]], her struggle for economic survival, her dependence on the kindness of the Mannings, and her ultimate decision to seek a new life in [[new-york-city|New York]]. The flight from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] is the threshold between Mimi's life as a daughter in a family and her life as a woman alone.
