## Overview

Carl Hunter is the son of Mr. and [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]], prominent figures in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Negro elite and hosts of [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club. In Walter White's *Flight*, Carl represents the younger generation of Black Atlantans who are growing restless with respectability politics.

He is characterized by his skepticism toward the social climbing and pretension that define his parents' world. Hilda Adams describes him as "the most thrilling boy in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]," seventeen and "not a bit like the other boys" (004-025).

Carl's complex relationship with his family, his ambivalence about his social position, and his frankness make him one of the novel's more nuanced minor characters.

## In the Story

### First Impressions

Carl is first mentioned by his mother, [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]], who notes defensively that "Carl's nothing but a child as yet," minimizing expectations placed on him (003-014). The discussion around Carl at [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club reveals generational tensions in Black Atlanta society.

### Meeting Mimi

Mimi meets Carl when Hilda brings him to her at a social gathering (005-001). Their initial conversation captures Carl's attraction to and discomfort with the social world of the club.

He is described as different from the other young men Mimi has met — more direct, less concerned with appearances.

### The Return from School

Carl reappears significantly when he comes home from school for the summer. "It was that summer that Carl Hunter came home" and his arrival stirs social dynamics (006-016).

Mimi sees him again in late June and remembers their earlier meeting (006-020). Carl grins at her recognition, and she realizes his time away has "not changed Carl" — his disregard for society's pretensions remains intact (006-023, 006-026).

### Generational Conflict

Carl dismisses the social performances of [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club and speaks openly about wanting a different kind of life. His father, Mr. Hunter, represents the establishment Carl reflexively rejects.

Mr. Hunter calls the Fleur-de-Lis "the eating brigade" because he is jealous (003-022). Mrs. Hunter's remark that Carl is "nothing but a child" reveals both maternal protection and the generational gap.

Later in the novel, Carl tells Mimi he was "wasting" himself, declaring "I never will be an insurance man" — rejecting his father's career path (009-018).

## Relationships

Carl's relationship with his mother, [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]], is defined by her protectiveness and his resistance to her world. His father, Mr. Hunter, represents institutional authority that Carl questions.

His connection to [[hilda-adams|Hilda Adams]] serves as a bridge to Mimi. His interaction with Mimi hints at romantic possibility complicated by his restlessness.

## Key Moments

**Introduction:** Discussed by his mother, who calls him "nothing but a child as yet" (003-014). Described by Hilda as "the most thrilling boy in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]," seventeen (004-025).

**Meeting Mimi:** Meets [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]] when Hilda brings him (005-001). Mimi says he was "real nice" (005-007).

**Return:** Comes home from school for the summer (006-016). Sees Mimi in late June; his disregard for society remains unchanged (006-020/006-023/006-026).

**Rebellion:** Declares "I never will be an insurance man" (009-018). Later tells Mimi about his father forcing him into the insurance business (006-043).
