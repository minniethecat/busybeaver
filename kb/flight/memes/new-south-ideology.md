## Definition

The New South ideology emerged after Reconstruction as a vision for the economic modernization of the former Confederacy. Promoters like Henry Grady of the [[atlanta-constitution-newspaper|Atlanta Constitution]] argued that the South should move beyond its agrarian past — and the racial conflict of the Civil War era — toward industrialization, urbanization, and reconciliation with the North. In practice, the New South preserved the racial hierarchy of the Old South while modernizing its economic structure.

In *Flight*, the New South is the context for the Daquin family's move to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. The city's booming economy, its Black business district on [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue, and its self-image as a progressive Southern metropolis all reflect New South ideology. But the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot of 1906]] reveals the violent racial logic that the New South rhetoric concealed.

## Historical Context

[[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] in the early 1900s embodied the contradictions of the New South. The city had rebuilt rapidly after its destruction in the Civil War, becoming a regional center of commerce, transportation, and manufacturing. [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue was home to a thriving Black business district, including Alonzo Herndon's [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Life Insurance Company — one of the most successful Black-owned businesses in the country.

But [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] was also the site of one of the most devastating race riots in American history. In September 1906, white mobs rampaged through Black neighborhoods, killing dozens and destroying homes and businesses. The riot was fueled by sensational newspaper coverage alleging Black men had assaulted white women — a pattern of racialized sexual panic that recurred throughout the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] era.

## In the Novel

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s move to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] is motivated by New South ideology — the belief that the city offered economic opportunity that [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] no longer could. His judgment is vindicated when a Negro-owned insurance company succeeds in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] (002-055). The city's Black business community, with its banks and insurance companies and real estate offices, represents the promise of the New South.

The [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot shatters this promise. The violence that destroys Jean's business and ultimately drives the family from the city reveals the fragility of Black economic achievement under [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]]. The New South, the novel suggests, was always a false promise — economic modernization without racial justice was merely a more efficient form of exploitation.

The novel's geography traces the failure of New South ideology: from [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] (where the promise proves false) to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]] (where respectability proves insufficient) to [[new-york-city|New York]] (where Mimi must pass to escape the racial logic that the New South codified). Each move represents a search for a place where economic opportunity and racial justice might coexist.
