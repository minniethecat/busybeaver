# Atlanta Race Riot of 1906

The four-day racial massacre in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta, Georgia]], beginning September 22, 1906, in which white mobs attacked Black residents, destroyed Black-owned property, and killed at least two dozen African Americans.

## Overview

The Atlanta Race Riot of 1906 began on September 22, 1906. Armed white mobs rampaged through Black neighborhoods, destroying homes and businesses and killing residents. Months of sensationalist newspaper coverage alleging assaults by Black men against white women, combined with a bitter gubernatorial campaign, had inflamed tensions to the breaking point (005-030).

In *Flight*, the riot is the catalytic event that transforms [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s world. The violence shatters the relative security of her family's life in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] and forces their flight to [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], initiating the cycle of displacement that will define her life trajectory.

> "A bitter political campaign was waged, its central issue the question of disenfranchisement of Negroes and 'Negro domination.' A play, The Clansmen, a distorted picture of Reconstruction filled with venom and hatred, came to Atlanta and stayed during this time of strain, and whipped into fury bitterness between white and black." (005-030)

## Narrative Context

The riot occurs in chapters 5 and 6. Chapter 5 chronicles the mounting tension: the inflammatory newspaper coverage, the political campaign against "Negro domination," and [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s growing unease. A confrontation with a white businessman who demands that "better-class Negroes" stop attacks on white women reveals the impossibility of rational dialogue under Jim Crow (005-033).

Mimi's experience of the riot alters her perception of race and safety. "Scenes she had witnessed in the rioting" (006-003) crystallize her awareness of racial vulnerability, causing "the forced growth of her racial consciousness" (007-010). The trauma endures years later when she reflects on "her experiences in the riot at [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]" (017-006).

> "...scenes she had witnessed in the rioting, she began to take these acquaintanceships as a matter of course, as part of the day's work, as an accepted and usual thing." (006-003)

## Aftermath

The riot's consequences are enduring. [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] is shattered — his faith in peaceful coexistence between Black and white southerners is destroyed. The family flees [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] for [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], beginning Mimi's displacements that will lead her to pass for white in [[new-york-city|New York City]] (019-011).

> "That terrible night during the riot. After that I hated all white people and began to think every Negro who wasn't for us was against us." (008-039)
