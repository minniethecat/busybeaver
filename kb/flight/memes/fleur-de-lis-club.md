## Overview

The Fleur-de-Lis Club is the premier social organization for elite Black women in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. Its members are predominantly light-skinned, mirroring the color hierarchy of the city's Black society. [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]] mockingly calls it "[[lincoln-club|the eating brigade]]" (003-022), referencing the elaborate refreshments at its gatherings.

> "I'm entertaining my club, the Fleur-de-Lis—Mr. Hunter calls it 'the eating brigade' but that's because he's jealous." (003-022)

The club functions as a gatekeeping institution. Admission signals social acceptance, while exclusion marks one as an outsider. [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]]'s invitation represents her successful assimilation; the club's treatment of [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] reveals its role in enforcing both color and class boundaries (004-046).

## In the Novel

Mimi attends the Fleur-de-Lis Club meeting at [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]]'s grand home, where she observes the club's dynamics firsthand (004-002). She notices that everyone present is light-skinned, ranging from "a light brown" to "one indistinguishable from white" (004-006).

The club's conversation turns to [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]], ejected from the [[grand-opera-house-atlanta|Grand Opera House]] for passing as white. The women's "Serves her right" reveals the club's sordid inner life behind its veneer of gentility (004-009).

> "Serves her right," sweetly commented one of the informative one's companions, satisfaction in her tone. "Going where she isn't wanted." (004-009)

[[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]]'s acceptance marks a turning point. "Under Mrs. Hunter's dexterous management," she "had been extended and had accepted an invitation to join" (004-046). She soon "spoke pityingly of those who were not of the elect" (004-046). The club holds its October meeting at the Daquin home, despite Jean's objections (010-007).
