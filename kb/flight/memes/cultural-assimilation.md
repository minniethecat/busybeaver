## Definition

Cultural assimilation in the context of *Flight* refers to the process by which members of marginalized groups adopt the language, customs, and social norms of the dominant (white) culture. The novel explores this theme at two levels: the broader cultural assimilation of [[new-orleans-louisiana|Creole New Orleans]] into the Anglo-American racial binary, and Mimi's personal assimilation into white society through passing.

The novel presents assimilation as a [[death|loss]] as much as a gain. When Mimi passes, she gains access to better-paying work, freedom of movement, and relief from racial harassment — but she loses the spontaneity, the ready laughter, the naturalness of her own people. She sees \"morose, worried faces\" in her new world, and she misses [[world-war-i|the war]]mth of Black community life (017-007).

## Historical Context

The question of assimilation has been central to African American political thought since emancipation. From Frederick Douglass to [[education-and-uplift|Booker T. Washington]] to W.E.B. Du Bois, Black intellectuals debated whether the goal was full integration into American society or the development of a distinct Black national identity. The Creole community of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] represented a third path — a hybrid cultural formation that predated and challenged the American racial binary.

The early 20th century saw intense pressure toward Anglo-conformity for all immigrant and minority groups. The rhetoric of \"100% Americanism\" during and after [[world-war-i|World War I]] reinforced the expectation that difference was suspect and should be suppressed. For African Americans, this pressure was compounded by the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] system, which simultaneously demanded assimilation to white norms and enforced segregation.

## In the Novel

Mimi's passing represents the most extreme form of assimilation available to a light-skinned Black person in early 20th-century America. Her French language ability, her sewing skills, and her light complexion make assimilation possible — but the novel emphasizes what is lost in the process. She \"missed the spontaneity, the ready laughter, the naturalness of her own\" (017-007).

The novel also explores assimilation at the community level. [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]]'s eagerness to join [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club, her careful performance of middle-class respectability, represents a form of assimilation to bourgeois norms that is shared across the color line. The club's rituals — the recipes, the card games, the visiting etiquette — mirror white middle-class practices of the same period.

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] resists assimilation on his own terms. His Creole pride, his insistence on the value of French language and culture, and his refusal to accommodate [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] represent an alternative model of cultural survival. But the novel suggests that this resistance, however admirable, may be unsustainable in the face of overwhelming social and economic pressure (002-059).
