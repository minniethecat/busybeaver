## Location

Paris, the capital of [[french-government|France]], was in the early twentieth century the undisputed center of Western fashion, art, and culture. Its haute couture houses -- Worth, Paquin, Poiret, and their rivals -- defined the standard of feminine elegance to which wealthy women on both sides of the Atlantic aspired. The city's boulevards, shops, and theatres (`018-040`) represented the summit of cosmopolitan sophistication, and for American modistes like [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], the Paris buying trip was an essential ritual of the trade.

For Americans of color, Paris carried an additional significance: it was a place where the American racial order did not apply. Black writers, artists, musicians, and intellectuals had been traveling to Paris since the nineteenth century, finding there a freedom of movement and social acceptance unimaginable at home. The city's famous indifference to American racial categories made it a symbolic counterpoint to [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] -- a place where a person might be judged by their accomplishments rather than their ancestry.

## In the Novel

Paris figures in the novel not as a directly depicted setting but as an idealized destination and a cultural benchmark. [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] Dress Shop models itself on Parisian standards; its name, its atmosphere, and its claim to exclusivity all rest on the premise that it brings Paris to [[new-york-city|New York]] (`017-011`, `017-012`). [[madame-francine|Madame Francine]] herself "remembered her French" (`018-023`) when the occasion demanded, and Mimi's French name and fluent accent -- genuine inheritances of her Creole upbringing -- authenticate the shop's Gallic pretensions.

The Paris buying trip is the summit of Mimi's professional ascent. When [[madame-francine|Madame Francine]] asks, "How'd you like to go with me to Paris next month to get new styles?" (`018-020`), the question marks Mimi's elevation from workroom finisher to trusted associate. The trip itself, undertaken twice a year in June and December (`018-026`), involves visiting the Paris shops, memorizing models "for later reproduction in [[new-york-city|New York]]" (`018-027`), and absorbing the atmosphere of the boulevards. Mimi's ability to "convince even a Parisian of her Gallicism" (`018-031`) -- that is, to pass not merely as white but as French -- represents the highest proof of her capacity for self-reinvention.

The Paris trips are also moments of pure exhilaration for Mimi. "She was glad she had done nothing so foolish" as to refuse the offer when she stands on the deck watching "the serrated skyline of [[new-york-city|New York]]" recede (`018-040`). In Paris, "it was all too marvellous, too intoxicatingly beautiful" (`018-042`). Even [[madame-francine|Madame Francine]]'s crotchetiness cannot diminish the wonder of being there. The city represents the life that Mimi has built for herself -- glamorous, sophisticated, and utterly contingent on her secret.

## Significance

Paris functions in the novel as both a real destination and a symbolic space. As a real city, it is the source of the fashion that sustains [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]] business and the reward for Mimi's professional competence. As a symbol, it represents a world beyond American racial classifications -- a place where Mimi's Frenchness, which in America is a mask, might be the simple truth. The novel never shows us Mimi in Paris interacting with other African Americans who have found refuge there; but the possibility hovers at the edges of the narrative, an alternative path Mimi might have taken had circumstances been different.

The Paris-[[french-government|France]] that Walter White constructs is also a commentary on the ironies of [[racial-passing]]. Mimi's Frenchness, the quality that enables her to pass in [[new-york-city|New York]], would be less remarkable in Paris itself. In this sense, Paris deconstructs the racial performance at the heart of the novel: what America treats as a daring imposture might simply be, in another context, the unremarkable fact of a Creole woman's heritage.
