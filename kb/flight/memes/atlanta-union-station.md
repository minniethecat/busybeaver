## Location

Atlanta Union Station, also known as the Terminal Station, stood on Mitchell Street in downtown [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], serving as the city's principal railroad depot through the early decades of the twentieth century. Built in 1905 and designed in the Beaux-Arts style, it was a major hub for Southern rail traffic, connecting [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] to [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], the Atlantic coast, and the industrial North. The station's architecture -- a "long, dingy shed with low-hung roof" (`001-002`) -- was less grand than its function, but it was nonetheless the gateway through which thousands of migrants, travelers, and commercial goods entered and departed the city each day.

Crucially, the station was a [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] space. Its waiting rooms were segregated, labeled "For Coloured" and "For White" (`001-006`), and its very architecture encoded the racial order that governed every aspect of Southern public life. For the Daquin family, stepping off the train from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], the station was their first encounter with the formal segregation regime of [[georgia-piedmont|Georgia]] -- a regime more rigid and punitive than the informal codes of Creole Louisiana.

## In the Novel

The novel opens at Union Station, with the long train from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] rumbling into the shed and a grinning Negro porter bellowing "Union Station -- Atlanty. All out for Atlanty" (`001-001`, `001-002`). Mimi awakes with a start as Jean shakes her, calling her "[[mimi-daquin|petite Mimi]]" (`001-003`), and the family gathers their boxes and bundles. The opening sequence is dense with sensory detail: the screech of the whistle, the swaying of the car, the "towheaded white children" playing in the red earth beyond the tracks (`001-001`), and the sudden "grinning, laughing, chattering crowd" of Negro porters, cabmen, and relatives surging onto the platform (`001-002`).

[[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]], Mary's brother, hustles them through the waiting room "labeled 'For Coloured'" to the sidewalk (`001-006`), demonstrating the practiced navigation of [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] space that the Daquins -- accustomed to a different racial regime in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] -- have yet to learn. Robertson's brisk efficiency, his command of the proper routes through the segregated city, contrasts with the Daquins' uncertain wonder. He insists on taking them the long way around, down [[pryor-street|Pryor Street]] and [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue, avoiding Decatur and [[ivy-street|Ivy Street]]s as the route "that's the Nigger district" (`001-007`). Union Station is thus established as the point of entry into a racialized geography that the novel will spend its first five chapters mapping.

The station is also where Mimi first experiences the sheer novelty of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. She does not hear the newsboy shouting headlines because to her "it was terra incognita" (`001-008`) -- an unknown land full of possibility and strangeness. For Mimi, the station is the first step into a new life, though she cannot yet know the violence and tragedy that await her in the city beyond.

## Significance

Atlanta Union Station functions as the novel's symbolic threshold. The Daquins' arrival marks a crossing not merely between South and South but between worlds -- from the insular, French-inflected Creole culture of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to the starkly bifurcated racial order of [[georgia-piedmont|Georgia]]. Every character the station introduces -- the grinning porter, the efficient Robertson, the bewildered Daquins -- embodies an aspect of the Southern racial system: accommodation, mastery, and dislocation respectively. The station's architecture of segregation (the separate waiting rooms, the prescribed routes through the city) prefigures the constraints that will shape, and ultimately destroy, the Daquin family's life in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]].

The opening scene also establishes the [[the-great-migration]] context that frames the entire novel. The Daquins are not poor rural migrants fleeing the cotton fields; they are the colored bourgeoisie, educated, French-speaking, and proud. But they are migrants nonetheless, and Union Station is the portal through which they enter the drama that will scatter them across the cities of the North.
