## Definition

Creole identity in early 20th-century America was a complex and contested category, particularly in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]]. The term referred to people of mixed French, Spanish, West Indian, and African ancestry who formed a distinct cultural community.

The novel *Flight* portrays Creole identity through the Daquin family. Jean traces their lineage to the early days of Louisiana, including a San Domingan ancestor (002-099). He rejected the American racial binary.

Jean explained that white Louisianians claimed the Creole was purely European. He instead embraced the mixture: "from that very mixture comes the delightful colourfulness which is their greatest charm" (002-096). "The cardinal sin is avarice or stinginess," he added. This placed him at odds with both white supremacists and Black color hierarchies.

## Historical Context

The Creole community of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] held a unique position. After the Louisiana Purchase of 1803, the colonial racial order -- recognizing free people of color -- was supplanted by the Anglo-American binary.

The [[creole-quarter|Creole quarter]] maintained distinctiveness through the 19th century. By the early 20th century, the old families were dying off and poverty forced sales. "One by one the old houses were razed by boisterous, unfeeling house-wreckers" (002-059).

The term "Creole" became politically contested. White Creoles distanced themselves from African ancestry, while Creoles of color insisted on their full heritage.

Jean's pride in Creole ancestry sustained him (002-025). This identity carried particular values: education, French culture, and resistance to the American racial binary. His insistence on Mimi's French education and his disdain for [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black elite both stem from this worldview.

## In the Novel

Creole identity in *Flight* is most prominently embodied by [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]. His fierce pride colors the family's life in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]].

When Mary Robertson, a light-skinned woman from [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]], marries Jean, the Creole community resists. She is an outsider, "though respectable and worthy, yet was not of Creole blood" (002-044).

The tension escalates after the family moves to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]. Mary complains Jean's Creole crowd is "stuck-up and exclusive," while Jean says Atlanta Negroes will "show him a trick or two for fair" (001-013). The Creole focus on culture over skin color collides with Atlanta's color-consciousness.

Mimi inherits her father's Creole sensibility even as she rejects the community. Her decision to pass for white in [[new-york-city|New York]] can be read as both betrayal and extension of the Creole refusal of American racial categories.

Jean warned her that outside New Orleans "it's a different matter" (002-088). The demolition of the [[creole-quarter|Creole quarter]] mirrors the fragility of alternative racial formations.
