## Overview

[[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s decision to pass for white is the novel's central dramatic turning point. After the scandal of her relationship with [[jerry-reed|Jerry Reed]] destroys her reputation in [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]], Mimi concludes that she can no longer remain among her own people. She tells [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]]: I never thought I'd want to leave my own people. I wouldn't leave them now but they've driven me away (016-060). The decision is framed not as ambition or self-hatred but as a desperate response to persecution from within her own community.

The scene of the decision is one of the novel's most emotionally complex. [[mrs-rogers|Mrs. Rogers]] argues, pleads, almost weeps — but cannot dissuade Mimi. The older woman's love and worry are palpable, but so is her powerlessness. Mimi has been pushed beyond the point where love and community can hold her. The decision represents both a personal tragedy and a structural indictment: the community that should have protected Mimi has instead made her position within it impossible.
