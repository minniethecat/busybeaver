## Definition

Memory and the past in *Flight* function as powerful forces that shape the present and constrain the future. The novel is structured around acts of remembrance — [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s reverent preservation of Creole history, [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s compartmentalized recollection of her multiple lives, and the collective memory of racial trauma that the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot]] etches into consciousness. Memory is never neutral in *Flight*: it is simultaneously a source of strength and a burden from which characters seek to escape.

## Jean Daquin's Creole Memory

Jean Daquin embodies the novel's most deliberate relationship with the past. His extensive recitation of Daquin family history — "the record we're proud of — we've helped build this Louisiana of ours" (002-088) — serves as both inheritance and armor for Mimi. He tells her of Jeannot, the enslaved ancestor who chopped off his own hand rather than become an executioner (002-098), transforming historical trauma into a narrative of honor and resistance.

Jean's attachment to memory is explicitly contrasted with [[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson]]'s forward-looking ambition. Where Mary sees "draughty, moth-eaten old places" to be torn down for profit, Jean sees layered history — "the old families were dying off, poverty was forcing others to sell their homes" (002-059). His Creole reverence for the past becomes, in the novel's framing, both a moral virtue and a practical liability in a world that rewards ruthless forgetting.

## Mimi's Compartmentalized Selves

Mimi experiences her life as a series of discrete, sealed-off chapters. She conceptualizes her existence as "railroad flats" — "each room opening into the next one," each door "closing behind her, never to be reopened" (017-001). [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], [[new-york-city|New York]] — each city represents a separate self that must be left behind.

This compartmentalization is Mimi's survival strategy, but it also represents a form of loss. The novel suggests that the ability to forget is both necessary for survival and a kind of violence against the self. When Mimi passes for white, she must erase not only her racial identity but her entire personal history — her father, her Creole heritage, even her son.

## Memory and Racial Trauma

The novel's depiction of the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot]] insists on memory as political act. The riot's details — "extra after extra, screaming in twelve-inch headlines" (005-030) — are preserved as evidence against forgetting. The novel itself functions as a memory project, recovering and transmitting the experience of racial violence that official history would prefer to erase.
