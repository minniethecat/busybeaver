## Location

Baltimore is the largest city in Maryland, a major port on the Chesapeake Bay roughly a hundred miles south of [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]]. In the early twentieth century, it had one of the largest Catholic populations in the United States, a legacy of its colonial founding and successive waves of Irish, German, and Italian immigration. The city's substantial network of Catholic charitable institutions — orphanages, hospitals, and parish schools — made it a destination for families seeking care for children they could not support.

## In the Novel

> The bleak November day when she carried Jean there was no more cold and dreary than Mimi's heart. Petit Jean seemed to feel the impending separation. He clung to Mimi, showering little affections upon her that tore her heart with pain. `014-059`

Baltimore enters the novel at Mimi's moment of greatest despair. After her failed struggle for survival in [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], where she could neither feed herself nor care for [[petit-jean|Petit Jean]], she "decided on a home in Baltimore, for she felt that there where so many Catholics lived there would be greater advantages for the unfortunate children of that faith" (`014-059`). The logic is characteristically pragmatic — Mimi, herself raised Catholic in [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]], seeks the best possible institutional care for her son within her religious tradition.

The separation is devastating. "The bleak November day when she carried Jean there was no more cold and dreary than Mimi's heart" (`014-059`). [[petit-jean|Petit Jean]] clings to her, "showering little affections upon her that tore her heart with pain" (`014-059`), and she rushes away from the orphanage "as though it were plague-stricken" to catch the train to [[new-york-city|New York]] (`014-059`). The city of Baltimore is thus associated with the most painful act of Mimi's life — the surrender of her child.

Yet Baltimore also becomes the place where Mimi measures her progress. From [[new-york-city|New York]], she visits "once or twice a year" (`018-001`), watching Jean grow from an infant into "a sturdy, intelligent child" with "Mimi's reddish hair and her brown eyes" and a mouth "sensitive like Carl's" (`018-001`). Each visit "cheered, saddened and comforted her" (`018-001`), and each return to [[new-york-city|New York]] filled her with "determination to work twice — three times as hard as ever until she could bring Jean back" (`018-001`). Baltimore is both her wound and her motivation — the place that holds her son until she can earn enough to reclaim him.

Eventually Mimi moves Jean from Baltimore to [[westchester-county|Westchester County]], placing him with a French family where she could see him more frequently (`019-008`). The "change from Baltimore to [[westchester-county|Westchester County]] made her happier," but Baltimore remains the emotional nadir of the novel — the city where Mimi had to leave her child behind.
