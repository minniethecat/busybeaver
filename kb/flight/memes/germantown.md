Germantown is a historic neighborhood in northwest [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia, Pennsylvania]]. In the novel *Flight*, it is the setting for some of [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s most difficult experiences as a young domestic worker.

## Domestic Service

After leaving the South, Mimi finds work in Germantown, where she is hired to do domestic work for a family that "regularly every Thursday lived in Germantown" ([013-032]). The work is demanding — "often the work was so great" that it consumed her days.

## The Germantown Household

Mimi is "expected on Thursdays at the Germantown home" ([014-001]), a regular schedule that becomes a source of dread. What begins as a seemingly ordinary domestic position gradually turns into something more troubling. The "unpleasant ones in Germantown and [[new-york-city|New York]]" ([018-003]) are later referenced as experiences that made Mimi fearful and contributed to her growing determination to escape domestic service entirely.

## A Turning Point

Germantown becomes associated with a decisive moment in Mimi's psychological development. When confronted with inappropriate advances from the man of the house, she reacts with visceral rejection. Her bitter smile signals the hardening of her resolve — she will no longer endure the vulnerabilities of domestic work.

> A thousand times no! The man in Germantown? A bitter smile came over her face. She thought of the unspeakable indignities she had endured. [014-040]

This moment marks a turning point that eventually leads her to [[new-york-city|New York City]] and her career as a dressmaker.
