## Definition

Respectability politics refers to the strategy — common among Black communities in the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] era — of demonstrating moral rectitude, proper behavior, and middle-class values as a means of countering racist stereotypes and gaining social acceptance. The novel *Flight* presents this strategy as both a survival mechanism and a source of internal community policing that could be as oppressive as the white racism it sought to defuse.

The novel's treatment of respectability is notably ambivalent. Characters who embody respectability — the carefully dressed women of [[fleur-de-lis-club|the Fleur-de-Lis]] Club, the aspiring businessman [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]] — are simultaneously sympathetic in their striving and pitiable in their conformity. The novel suggests that respectability is an understandable response to a society that denies Black people basic dignity, but also that it exacts a profound psychological cost.

## Historical Context

Respectability as a political strategy has deep roots in African American history. From the uplift ideology of [[education-and-uplift|Booker T. Washington]] to the women's club movement of the late 19th century, Black leaders emphasized moral character, education, and proper behavior as essential to racial progress. The logic was straightforward: if white Americans could be shown that Black people were capable of middle-class respectability, racism would diminish.

The strategy's limitations were equally clear. The [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot of 1906]], which targeted both the Black elite and the Black poor indiscriminately, demonstrated that no amount of respectability could protect against white mob violence. As one character observes, even \"the quietest, most respectable\" Black families were targeted (001-018). The novel captures this contradiction: respectability is necessary for survival in a racist society but insufficient to guarantee it.

## In the Novel

The [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]] women exemplify respectability politics in action. [[mary-robertson|Mrs. Daquin]] spends an hour arranging her hair and dresses in the latest fashion, determined that her first impression \"should establish her as one of the elect\" (004-002). The elaborate rituals of the club — the careful conversation, the card games that feel faintly wicked, the refreshments — all serve to assert middle-class status in a society that routinely denies Black women's respectability.

But the novel immediately undermines this performance. The women's gossip about [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] reveals the cruelty that respectability politics enables: Mrs. Watkins reports with satisfaction that [[mrs-adams|Mrs. Adams]] was ejected from the white theater, while another woman sweetly comments that \"she always did think she knew more than the rest of us\" (004-009). Respectability here functions less as a shield against white racism than as a weapon of intraracial class warfare.

Mimi's own relationship with respectability is complex. Her trade school education, her skill with a needle, her French language ability — all mark her as \"respectable\" by the standards of her community. Yet it is precisely these markers of respectability that enable her to pass for white at [[francines-dress-shop|Francine's]], where her French name and accent allow her to escape the very racial category that respectability politics was designed to uplift. The novel thus suggests that respectability and passing are two sides of the same coin — both are performances designed to navigate a society structured by white supremacy.
