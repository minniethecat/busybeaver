The Lincoln Club is a social organization for Black businessmen in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta, Georgia]]. In the novel *Flight*, it appears as an important institution within [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black elite, presided over by [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]].

> Note: "Lincoln Club" does not appear as a phrase in the novel's corpus. The institution is reconstructed from contextual references to Mr. Hunter's social position, his presidency of the club, and its relationship to the Fleur-de-Lis Club as described in the character pages for Mr. Hunter, Mrs. Hunter, and Carl Hunter.

## The Institution

The Lincoln Club represents the institutional infrastructure of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black business community in the early twentieth century. As a gathering place for the city's most prominent Black men, it served both social and professional functions — a venue for networking, political discussion, and the performance of respectability that was central to the community's survival strategy under [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]]. [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]]'s role as president is documented in his character page, where he is described as a man who befriended [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] and "helped arrange his funeral."

## Mr. Hunter's Presidency

[[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]], husband of [[mrs-hunter|Mrs. Hunter]], serves as president of the Lincoln Club. His position marks him as a leading figure in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black establishment — a man of institutional authority whose influence extends beyond business into the social and political life of the community. As a character, [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]] is depicted as "a hustling, jovial man" who represents "the established Black business class of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] who navigated racial politics through respectability and institutional power."

## "The Eating Brigade"

The Lincoln Club has a playful rivalry with the [[fleur-de-lis-club|Fleur-de-Lis Club]], whose members refer to it mockingly as "the eating brigade." This nickname, used by [[mr-hunter|Mr. Hunter]] himself, suggests that the club's gatherings centered on elaborate meals and socializing. The gentle mockery between the two institutions reveals the intimate, sometimes competitive dynamics within [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]'s Black elite. [[carl-hunter|Carl Hunter]], the son of the Lincoln Club's president, dismissively refers to both institutions as part of the social world he wishes to escape.

## Significance

The Lincoln Club embodies the self-organized institutional life that Black communities built in the [[jim-crow-era|Jim Crow]] South. Alongside churches, mutual aid societies, and women's clubs, organizations like the Lincoln Club provided spaces where Black men could exercise leadership, build networks, and maintain dignity in a society that denied them full citizenship.
