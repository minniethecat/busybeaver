Miss Lawrence is a Black fitter who worked at Francine's dress shop in New York before establishing her own independent dressmaking business in Harlem.

## At Francine's Dress Shop

Miss Lawrence was placed in a supervisory position over the other girls at Francine's establishment, which provoked racial hostility from some of the white workers:

> Some of the girls tried to get nasty when they put Miss Lawrence over us — Miss Lawrence was the coloured woman — but I liked her and she soon had 'em under her thumb, believe me. [017-037]

Sylvia, a Jewish coworker passing as French, defended Miss Lawrence and noted her professional competence. Despite the prejudice, Miss Lawrence maintained authority through skill and force of personality.

## Independent Business in Harlem

After leaving Francine's, Miss Lawrence established her own dressmaking shop in Harlem, serving a clientele that included former coworkers from Francine's:

> They go all the way up there to get Miss Lawrence to do their clothes. [017-043]

Her success demonstrated that an independent, Black-owned business could thrive outside the predominantly white fashion establishment. She served as an alternative model for [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]], who later considered her path when contemplating her own options for financial independence [018-039].

## Significance

Miss Lawrence represents one of the few examples in *Flight* of a Black woman achieving economic independence through skill and entrepreneurship rather than racial passing. Her trajectory — forced out of white-dominated spaces, then building something successful in a Black community — parallels and contrasts with Mimi's own strategy of passing for white to access opportunity.
