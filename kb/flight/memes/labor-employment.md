## Definition

Labor and employment in *Flight* define the economic boundaries within which Black characters pursue survival, dignity, and advancement. The novel presents a stratified occupational landscape: at the bottom, domestic service and manual labor; in the middle, skilled trades and Black-owned businesses; and at the aspirational top, the precarious hope of financial independence through individual enterprise. For [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]], the journey from financial dependence to self-supporting seamstress traces one of the novel's central arcs — the transformation of labor from necessity to identity.

## Black Enterprise and Insurance

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s employment at the [[lincoln-mutual-life|Lincoln Mutual Life Insurance Company]] of [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] introduces the novel's portrayal of Black enterprise. The company, presided over by [[mr-hunter|William Hunter]], is described by [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]] as "a gold mine insuring all the coloured folks here in the South" (001-044). Robertson's injunction — "I want you to stick to the job — no monkey business or I'm through" (001-044) — frames Black employment as conditional on white patronage, even within ostensibly autonomous Black institutions.

The novel contrasts this commercial ethos with [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]]'s Creole values: "We are not a nation of shopkeepers, thank God" (002-096). Jean's disdain for the "shopkeeper attitude" of his wife [[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson]] marks a cultural fault line — the Creole aristocracy's contempt for commerce versus the American gospel of economic striving.

## Women's Work

For Black women in the novel, occupational choices are severely constrained. After Jean's death, Mimi finds herself "trained to do no work which would bring her appreciable income or which she could have accepted without lowering their social status," facing "the Hobson's choice of teaching school or enduring the loss of many little comforts" (012-007). Teaching represented the sole respectable profession for educated Black women; domestic service was both more available and more degrading.

Mimi's eventual turn to sewing — "she could sew rather well" (013-021) — becomes the foundation of her economic independence. From day work as a seamstress in [[philadelphia-pennsylvania|Philadelphia]], she progresses to a design course in [[new-york-city|New York]], and eventually to a position as forewoman "in one of the most exclusive shops in New York" (016-012). Her trajectory from penniless single mother to skilled professional illustrates both the constraints and the possibilities of Black women's labor in the early twentieth century.

## Unemployment and Racial Violence

The novel connects labor conditions directly to racial violence. The summer of 1906 in Atlanta saw "a period of unemployment" that "caused a marked loafing of whites and Negroes followed by a long series of petty crimes" (005-030). This economic precarity, combined with political demagoguery and sensationalist journalism, created the tinder for the [[atlanta-race-riot-1906|Atlanta Race Riot]]. The novel thus insists that labor is not merely an economic question but a matter of physical survival — unemployment breeds the conditions in which racial violence flourishes.
