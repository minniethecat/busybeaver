## Definition

Religious life — centered on the Black church — was the institutional backbone of African American communities in the early 20th century. Churches served not only as places of worship but as centers of social life, political organizing, mutual aid, and cultural expression. In *Flight*, religious institutions appear at key moments, reflecting their importance to the novel's characters even when those characters are not particularly devout.

The novel treats religion with a characteristic ambivalence. Religious language and imagery appear throughout — Mimi's baptism, the funeral of [[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]], the moral judgment that drives Mimi from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] — but the novel does not present faith as a solution to the problems it depicts. Rather, religion is another site of community, conflict, and constraint.

## Historical Context

The Black church emerged from slavery as the most important independent institution in African American life. Denominations like the African Methodist Episcopal Church and the National Baptist Convention supported schools, published newspapers, and provided platforms for political leadership. The church was also a crucial site of women's organizing, though women were often excluded from formal leadership positions.

In [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]], the intersection of church life and racial politics was particularly intense. Many of the city's Black elite were church leaders, and churches were involved in everything from education to the temperance movement. The respectability politics that dominated Black middle-class life had deep roots in church culture.

## In the Novel

Religious institutions appear in *Flight* primarily through the character of Mimi's stepmother Mary and her Baptist background. When Mary explains that in [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] she was a Baptist, she reveals both her religious roots and her cultural distance from the Creole Catholic world of Jean's [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] (003-018). This religious difference is one more dimension of Mary's outsider status in the Daquin family.

The moral judgment that drives Mimi from [[harlem-new-york|Harlem]] has religious overtones. The community that condemns her relationship with a married man does so in the name of respectability and proper behavior — values deeply rooted in church culture. Mimi's bitterness about this judgment reflects a broader critique of religious moralism as a form of community policing.

The novel's spiritual imagery is not confined to institutional religion. Mimi's description of the song's passage — \"her soul had taken flight from her own body to that of the singer\" — suggests a form of transcendence that is aesthetic rather than doctrinal, and that exists outside the institutional framework of the church (007-013).
