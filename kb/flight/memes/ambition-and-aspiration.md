## Definition

Ambition and aspiration in *Flight* are presented as contested values, celebrated by some characters as the engine of progress and condemned by others as a betrayal of cultural authenticity. The novel maps this conflict onto its central generational and cultural divide: the Creole aristocracy's contempt for commercial striving versus the American gospel of self-improvement. [[mimi-daquin|Mimi Daquin]]'s journey from dependent daughter to self-supporting professional traces a third path — ambition not as social climbing but as survival.

## Mary Robertson and the Gospel of Getting Ahead

[[mary-robertson|Mary Robertson Daquin]] is the novel's most vivid embodiment of American ambition. Raised in [[chicago-illinois|Chicago]] where she heard "from morning to night endless discussions of money and of politics" (002-032), Mary brings to [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] a restless dissatisfaction with the Creole contentment she encounters. Her father [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]] personifies the extreme of this ethos: "Money — money — money — how much is it worth? — how much can I make out of it?" (001-015).

Mary's campaign to move the family to [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] — a "more 'progressive' town where folks get ahead faster" (002-017) — represents the triumph of ambition over tradition. The novel treats this ambition ambivalently: Mary's energy is both admirable and destructive, her "growing surliness and quarrelsomeness" (002-085) a direct product of ambitions that can never be fully satisfied.

## Jean's Creole Critique

[[jean-daquin|Jean Daquin]] articulates the counter-position: "Happiness cannot be bought with dollars — look at the Americans north of Canal Street and you'll see I am right. They scramble and fight and scheme to gain a few dollars and when they have them what do they do? They fight and scramble and scheme for more!" (002-053). Jean sees the "race of moneygrubbers" (004-036) as a spiritual death, a loss of the "gifts from God of laughter and song and of creative instincts" (004-036).

Yet the novel does not simply endorse Jean's position. Mimi's rebuttal — "But, papa Jean, they've got to do it! They're living in a world where they must either make money or else perish" (004-037) — acknowledges the material realities that make ambition not a luxury but a necessity for Black Americans.

## Mimi's Ambition as Survival

Mimi's trajectory represents a synthesis of these positions. Her drive is not for status but for independence — the freedom to reclaim her son and control her own life. From the "Hobson's choice of teaching school or enduring the loss of many little comforts" (012-007) to her enrollment in design school and eventual position as forewoman, Mimi's ambition is framed as both necessity and liberation. She achieves what Mary Robertson could not: advancement without the loss of self.
