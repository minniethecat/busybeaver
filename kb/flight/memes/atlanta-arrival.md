# The Daquins' Arrival in Atlanta

The family's arrival at [[atlanta-union-station|Union Station]] in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] in early spring, their drive through the city's segregated geography, and their immediate exposure to the racial and social order they have entered.

## Overview

The Daquin family's arrival at [[atlanta-union-station|Union Station]] in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] opens the novel with a scene of disorientation and anticipation. The train "rolled into a long, dingy shed with low-hung roof" as the porter called out, "[[atlanta-union-station|Union Station]] — Atlanty. All out for Atlanty" (001-002). The arrival is a threshold moment: the Daquins cross from the familiar Creole world of [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] into the binary racial order of the [[new-south-ideology|New South]].

[[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]] meets the family and immediately takes control, directing the driver to "go around by [[pryor-street|Pryor Street]] and from there down [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue" rather than through "Decatur and [[ivy-street|Ivy Street]]s — that's the slum district" (001-007). This routing is itself a lesson in the geography of respectability that governs Black [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]]: the "good" neighborhoods are distinct from the "lowest kind of Negroes" (001-007). [[mr-robertson|Mr. Robertson]]'s careful management of first impressions signals the social stakes of the new environment.

## Narrative Context

The arrival occupies chapter 1. Mimi, refreshed from her train nap, observes everything eagerly. "Because it was terra incognita to her she tried to see everything as eagerly as she had watched the land from the car window in the long ride from [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]]" (001-008). She notices the shift from white faces to "black and brown and yellow faces" as the carriage moves along [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue, finding the change "pleasant, warming, inviting" (001-009).

Jean, by contrast, is despondent. "Sunk dejectedly in the seat beside the driver so that only the top of his black, crumpled felt hat showed above the high seat, was too engrossed in delightfully painful nostalgia for his [[new-orleans-louisiana|New Orleans]] to notice anything" (001-009). The divergence between Mimi's openness and Jean's resistance establishes the generational tension that will define their experiences in [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]].

The neighborhood gossips, [[mrs-plummer|Mrs. Plummer]] and Mrs. King, immediately begin speculating about the new arrivals: their Catholicism, their French names, and Jean's employment at [[lincoln-mutual-life|Lincoln Mutual Life Insurance Company]] (001-028). This gossip presages the community surveillance that will later plague Mimi.

## Aftermath

The arrival initiates the sequence of events that leads to the [[atlanta-georgia|Atlanta]] Race Riot, Jean's death, and Mimi's eventual flight north. The immediate impressions formed on this day — the segregation of space, the social hierarchy of [[auburn-avenue|Auburn Ave]]nue, the watchful eyes of the community — establish the conditions under which the family will live and the forces that will ultimately destroy it.
