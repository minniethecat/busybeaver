# Bandit's Roost

## Description

Bandit's Roost was one of the most notorious alleys in [[the-bend|the Bend]], the densely packed slum district at the heart of New York's tenement crisis in the 1890s. Located at 59-1/2 Mulberry Street in the [[sixth-ward|Sixth Ward]], Bandit's Roost achieved iconic status when [[jacob-riis|Jacob Riis]] photographed it in 1888 — an image so powerful that Riis selected it as the frontispiece of *How the Other Half Lives*. (P05-001) The alley embodied the physical and moral degradation that Riis sought to expose: a dark, narrow passageway where overcrowding, poverty, and crime converged in the shadow of New York's most celebrated wealth. The name itself — "Bandit's Roost" — conveyed its reputation as a lair for criminals and outcasts, a place where, as Riis wrote, "the Bend" was "the foul core of New York's slums." (006-002)

The photograph that made Bandit's Roost famous is one of the most enduring images in the history of American documentary photography. Shot using Riis's pioneering flash-powder technique, it shows residents of the alley — men and women in rough street clothes — staring directly at the camera from the narrow lane, with laundry hanging overhead and the cramped tenement walls closing in on either side. The image captured the human density of the tenement world in a way that statistics alone could not, and it helped establish Riis's reputation as a visual storyteller as much as a journalist. The frontispiece caption in the book reads simply: "[Frontispiece illustration: 'Bandit's Roost' — photograph by Jacob A. Riis]." (P05-001)

## Location and Physical Layout

Bandit's Roost was situated in the block at the center of the Bend district, bounded by Bayard, Park, Mulberry, and Baxter Streets — what Riis called "'the Bend' proper." (006-009) The Bend itself was defined by the curve in [[mulberry-street|Mulberry Street]], which "crooks like an elbow within hail of the old depravity of the Five Points." (006-002) Within this district, Bandit's Roost was, in Riis's words, "one of the most notorious of these" alleyways, part of "the scores of back alleys, of stable lanes and hidden byways, of which the rent collector alone can keep track." (006-007)

The physical entrance to Bandit's Roost was notably narrow. On either side of it, the desperate economics of the Bend had produced a peculiar form of commerce: even the hallways of the adjacent tenements had been converted into shops. These improvised retail spaces, as Riis described them, were "three feet wide by four deep, they have just room for one, the shopkeeper, who, himself within, does his business outside, his wares displayed on a board hung across what was once the hall door." (006-007) Behind the rear wall of one such shop, "a hole has been punched from the hall into the alley and the tenants go that way" — the former doorway having been entirely given over to commerce. (006-007)

One of these shops was a "tobacco bureau" presided over by a painted saint; the other, a fish-stand "full of slimy, odd-looking creatures, fish that never swam in American waters." (006-007) These improvised storefronts captured the inventiveness born of extreme necessity that characterized all of the Bend, where "trucks and ash-barrels had provided four distinct lines of shops that are not down on the insurance maps, to accommodate the crowds." (006-007)

## Mortality and Living Conditions

The human cost of life in and around Bandit's Roost was staggering. Riis marshaled mortality statistics from the [[tenement-house-commission|Tenement House Commission]] to demonstrate the lethality of the district. In a single specimen year (1882), the block containing Bandit's Roost recorded 155 deaths of children. (006-009) The proportion of child deaths to total mortality in the block was 68.28 percent — dramatically higher than the citywide figure of 46.20 percent. (006-009) Riis noted that "the infant mortality in any city or place as compared with the whole number of deaths is justly considered a good barometer of its general sanitary condition" — and the barometer in the Bend pointed to crisis. (006-009)

The tenement at No. 59-1/2, directly adjacent to Bandit's Roost, was particularly deadly. "Here, in this tenement, No. 59-1/2, next to Bandits' Roost, fourteen persons died that year, and eleven of them were children; in No. 61 eleven, and eight of them not yet five years old." (006-009) According to the records of the Bureau of Vital Statistics, only thirty-nine people lived in No. 59-1/2 in the year 1888, nine of them little children — yet "there were five baby funerals in that house the same year." (006-009) Out of the alley itself, at No. 59, "nine dead were carried in 1888, five in baby coffins." (006-009)

The broader mortality data for the Bend block that Riis reproduced from the Registrar of Vital Statistics, Dr. Roger S. Tracy, showed a general death rate of 35.75 per thousand for the block as a whole — compared to 26.27 for the entire city. (006-009, 006-011) On Mulberry Street specifically, the death rate for children under five was a catastrophic 136.70 per thousand, as recorded in the mortality table Riis reproduced from the Registrar of Vital Statistics. (006-009) The contrast with the [[model-tenement|model tenement]] across the way at Nos. 48 and 50, built by Christian reformers, could not have been sharper: there, despite housing 161 people "under good management, and in decent quarters," the hearse called only twice that year, once for a baby. (006-012) Riis drew the pointed conclusion: "The agent of the Christian people who built that tenement will tell you that Italians are good tenants, while the owner of the alley will oppose every order to put his property in repair with the claim that they are the worst of a bad lot. Both are right, from their different standpoints. It is the standpoint that makes the difference — and the tenant." (006-012)

## Landlord and Ownership

One of the most damning revelations in Riis's treatment of Bandit's Roost concerned the identity of its owners. Riis disclosed that the alley and other infamous tenement property in the Bend "stood associated on the tax-books all through the long struggle to make its owners responsible" with "the name of an honored family, one of the 'oldest and best,' rich in possessions and in influence, and high in the councils of the city's government." (006-015) The connection between elite respectability and slum exploitation was central to Riis's argument: the conditions in Bandit's Roost were not an accident of poverty but the direct product of a system in which the powerful profited from the misery of the powerless.

Riis emphasized that this was not an isolated case — "nor would it be the only instance by very many that stand recorded on the Health Department's books of a kind that has come near to making the name of [[landlord|landlord]] as odious in New York as it has become in Ireland." (006-015) The property-owners of the Bend resisted every order to repair their buildings, while the [[board-of-health|Health Department]] and sanitary police fought an exhausting battle to enforce even minimal standards. (006-025, 006-015) The overcrowding issue was complicated by the official response: under the pressure of the [[italian-immigrants|Italian]] influx, "the standard of breathing space required for an adult by the health officers has been cut down from six to four hundred cubic feet" — a concession that Riis considered a damning admission of failure. (006-025)

## Significance

Bandit's Roost occupies a unique place in the documentary record of Gilded Age New York. The 1888 photograph that Riis took of the alley became the visual signature of *How the Other Half Lives* — the first thing a reader saw upon opening the book. This editorial choice was deliberate: Riis understood that his statistical arguments about overcrowding, disease, and child mortality (006-009) would only gain their full force when anchored to a concrete image of human life in the tenements. The faces staring out from the alley — Italian immigrants, ragged children, working women — gave the anonymous statistics of the Tenement House Commission a human face.

The alley also illustrated the interconnected nature of the slum problem that Riis documented across the entire book. Bandit's Roost was physically adjacent to [[bottle-alley|Bottle Alley]], around the corner in [[baxter-street|Baxter Street]], which Riis described as "a fair specimen of its kind, wherever found" — every house filled with "piles of rags, of malodorous bones and musty paper." (006-016) It sat within the Bend district, which Riis described as the central hub from which all the city's tenement pathologies radiated: [[overcrowding]], [[crime-and-vice|crime]], the [[padrone-system]], and the [[stale-beer-dive|stale-beer dives]] that served as "the real home of trampdom." (007-012) The Bend itself would eventually be demolished and replaced by Mulberry Bend Park (now Columbus Park) in 1897, one of the few direct victories of the reform movement that Riis helped inspire. Riis predicted this outcome: "The Mulberry Street Bend will go the way of the Five Points when all the red tape that binds the hands of municipal effort has been unwound." (025-017)

Bandit's Roost endures in the historical imagination less as a specific place — its physical structure was swept away with the rest of the Bend — than as a symbol. In Riis's hands, a single alley off [[mulberry-street|Mulberry Street]] became the emblem of an entire system of housing exploitation, and the power of his photography ensured that the place would be remembered long after the tenements themselves had vanished.
