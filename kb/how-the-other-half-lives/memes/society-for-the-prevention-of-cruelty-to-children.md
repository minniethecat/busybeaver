The Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children (SPCC) was a pioneering child welfare organization in late 19th-century New York City. Founded in 1875, it was the first organization in the world dedicated specifically to the legal protection of children from abuse and neglect. In Riis's account, the SPCC represented the institutional response to the tenement system's most vulnerable victims — the infants and young children who were abandoned, exploited, or abused in the city's poorest districts.

## Mission and Methods

The SPCC operated through direct legal intervention, using the courts to prosecute adults who abused or exploited children. Riis documents one of its most significant cases — the prosecution of David Smith, the "New York Fagin," who was "convicted and sent to prison last year through the instrumentality of the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children" [008-005]. Smith had been exploiting a fourteen-year-old boy, forcing him to beg after deliberately injuring him — burning his feet with chemicals — to elicit sympathy from passersby. The case was a landmark in establishing that the state had both the right and the obligation to intervene on behalf of abused children, even against their parents or guardians.

The SPCC's methods were confrontational by design. Rather than relying on voluntary compliance or moral suasion, the Society used the threat of prosecution to compel changes in behavior. This legalistic approach distinguished it from earlier charitable organizations that had focused on providing services rather than enforcing rights.

## Key Figures

- **Elbridge T. Gerry**: Founder of the SPCC and its chief legal strategist, who pioneered the use of habeas corpus to remove children from abusive homes
- **David Smith** ("New York Fagin"): The SPCC's most famous prosecution — a child exploiter convicted through the Society's intervention [008-005]

## Role in the Welfare Ecosystem

Riis describes the SPCC as occupying a specific niche in the child welfare landscape. "What the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children is to the baby-waif, the Children's Aid Society is to the older boy" [017-010]. This division of labor — the SPCC handling the most vulnerable infants and young children through legal intervention, the Children's Aid Society focusing on older youth through vocational placement and the orphan trains — reflected the specialized needs of different age groups among the city's homeless children.

## Connection to Tenement Reform

The SPCC's work was inextricably linked to the tenement reform movement. The conditions the society fought against — child labor, abandonment, exploitation — were products of the tenement system that Riis sought to expose. Every case the SPCC prosecuted was evidence that the housing conditions, wages, and social disorganization of the tenement districts were producing systematic harm to children. In this sense, the SPCC was both a remedy for and an indictment of the tenement system.
