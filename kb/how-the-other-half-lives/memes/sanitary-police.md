## Definition

The sanitary police were the enforcement arm of the New York City [[health-department|Board of Health]], a corps of inspectors empowered to enter tenements, document violations of the sanitary code, and remove people from dangerously overcrowded dwellings. Unlike ordinary police, their beat was public health: they measured cubic feet of air per occupant, searched for hidden fever-nests, vaccinated tramps against smallpox, and confiscated decayed food from street markets. Jacob Riis presents them as both the city's most effective weapon against slum conditions and a tragic illustration of how even the most energetic inspection regime could not keep pace with the scale of the tenement problem.

The term "sanitary police" appears throughout *How the Other Half Lives* in contexts that range from the grim to the darkly ironic. They are the men who patrol "the Bend" at midnight, the officials whose reports supply much of the statistical evidence for reform, and the agents whose backs, once turned, allow every abuse to resume.

## Duties and Operations

The sanitary police operated on several fronts simultaneously, each of which Riis documents in detail.

**Midnight inspections.** The sanitary police's most intrusive and effective tool was the midnight raid on overcrowded tenements. Riis describes the scene:

> From midnight till far into the small hours of the morning the policeman's thundering rap on closed doors is heard, with his stern command, "Apri port'!" on his rounds gathering evidence of illegal overcrowding. (006-026)

The scale of these operations was staggering. In the first six months of the year in which Riis was writing, "1,068 persons were turned out of not quite two hundred tenements below Houston Street by the sanitary police on their midnight inspections, and this covered only a very small part of that field" (024-013). The results were quickly undone: "The policeman's back was probably no sooner turned than the room was reopened for business" (006-029).

**Market patrols.** The sanitary police also regulated the street markets of the tenement quarters. Riis describes the health officers' cart descending on the Jewish quarter: "preceded and followed by stalwart policemen, who shovel up with scant ceremony the eatables—musty bread, decayed fish and stale vegetables—indifferent to the curses that are showered on them from stoops and windows, and carry them off to the dump" (010-026). It took "more than ten years of persistent effort on the part of the sanitary authorities to drive the trade in live fowl from the streets to the fowl-market on Gouverneur Slip" (010-022).

**Disease surveillance.** The sanitary police maintained "constant and sharp lookout for hidden fever-nests" (010-008). In the Tenth Ward, which health officers called "the typhus ward" (010-009), they hunted for concealed cases of infectious disease among immigrant families who "hide their sick lest the authorities carry them off to the hospital to be slaughtered, as they firmly believe" (010-008). When diphtheria or scarlet fever was discovered, "the health officers exercised summary powers of removal to the hospital where proper treatment could be had, and the result was a low death-rate" (014-020).

**Vaccination.** In midwinter, "on the coldest nights, the sanitary police corral the tramps here and in their lodging-houses and vaccinate them, despite their struggles and many oaths that they have recently been 'scraped'" (007-019).

## Geographic Focus and Scale

The sanitary police were concentrated in the worst districts of the lower city. Riis states bluntly: "It is in 'the Bend' the sanitary policeman locates the bulk of his four hundred, and the sanitary reformer gives up the task in despair" (006-026). The "four hundred" refers to the official estimate of overcrowded tenements—a figure Riis treats with some skepticism, noting the "standing quarrel between the professional—I mean now the official—sanitarian and the unsalaried agitator for sanitary reform over the question of overcrowded tenements" (006-025). The official sanitarian put the number "a little vaguely at four or five hundred, while the other asserts that there are thirty-two thousand" (006-025).

Within "[[the-bend|the Bend]]," the sanitary police confronted conditions that were nearly unmanageable. Riis describes a Crosby Street house where "the sanitary police report counting 101 adults and 91 children" (002-007). On Ludlow Street, "the sanitary policeman whose beat this is will tell you that it contains thirty-six families, but the term has a widely different meaning here and on the avenues" (010-003). In a single seven-story tenement where smallpox had been reported, "there were fifty-eight babies and thirty-eight children that were over five years of age" (010-003).

The sanitary police also contended with the ragpickers' settlements around the city dumps. "Whenever the back of the sanitary police is turned, he will make his home in the filthy burrows where he works by day, sleeping and eating his meals under the dump" (005-006). Raids on these unlicensed dump settlements were conducted "at intervals," but "the temptation of having to pay no rent is too strong, and they are driven from one dump only to find lodgement under another" (005-006).

Beyond these worst districts, enforcement was thin. Riis notes that during the same period when over a thousand people were evicted from tenements below Houston Street, "the uptown tenements were practically left to take care of themselves in this respect" (024-013).

## Limits of Enforcement

Riis is clear-eyed about what the sanitary police could and could not achieve. The fundamental obstacle was that of scale: "To remedy the overcrowding, with which the night inspections of the sanitary police cannot keep step, tenements may eventually have to be licensed, as now the lodging-houses, to hold so many tenants, and no more" (025-017). The inspection force was simply too small for a tenement population that had swelled past a million.

A second limitation was the constant pressure from landlords and their political allies. Under the pressure of the Italian influx, "the standard of breathing space required for an adult by the health officers has been cut down from six to four hundred cubic feet" (006-025)—a concession that Riis presents as evidence of how the "needs of the situation" could erode sanitary standards.

The sanitary police also faced resistance from the tenants themselves. Riis describes how ragpickers' homes contained "piles of rags, of malodorous bones and musty paper, all of which the sanitary police flatter themselves they have banished to the dumps and the warehouses" (006-016)—the word "flatter" capturing Riis's assessment of how permanently effective such enforcement actually was.

Yet Riis does not dismiss the sanitary police as futile. The biggest tenements, built in "the last ten years of sanitary reform rule," had been "brought, in all but the crowding, under its laws" (014-021). The sanitary police represented the principle—however imperfectly realized—that housing conditions were a proper object of government regulation and that the health of the poor could not be left to the mercy of the market.

## Relationship to Sanitary Reform

The sanitary police were the implementers of [[sanitary-reform|sanitary reform]], the men who translated policy into midnight knocks on tenement doors. Riis uses them to personify the tension between official optimism and the grim reality of the slums. The "standing quarrel" between the official sanitarian and the unsalaried reformer (006-025) was, at bottom, a dispute about what the sanitary police could honestly report: the official counted four hundred overcrowded tenements; the reformer counted the whole thirty-two thousand.

This tension plays out across the book. In "[[the-bend|the Bend]]," the sanitary policeman locates the bulk of his four hundred while "the sanitary reformer gives up the task in despair" (006-026). The sanitary police report on ragpickers' materials they have "banished"—even as those same materials pile up in the tenements (006-016). The health officers' midnight raids turn out a thousand people in six months—from "only a very small part" of the field (024-013). In each case, Riis uses the sanitary police as a measuring stick: they show what the city was willing to do, and by their limitations, what it was not.
