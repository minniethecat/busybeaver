# Children's Aid Society

## Overview

The Children's Aid Society (CAS) was the most important child-welfare organization in 19th-century New York. Founded by the minister Charles Loring Brace, the Society operated a network of lodging-houses, industrial schools, reading rooms, and summer camps for the "street Arabs" — homeless and working children — whom Riis documented in Chapter XVII. The Society's most famous (and controversial) program was the "orphan train" movement, which relocated over 100,000 New York children to rural families in the Midwest and West between 1854 and 1929.

## The Lodging-House Network

The CAS operated a tiered system of lodging-houses designed to reach children at different stages of need. The Newsboys' Lodging House, the most famous, charged six cents for a bed and a meal — a sum within reach of even the poorest street child. "What the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children is to the baby-waif, the Children's Aid Society is to the homeless boy at this real turning-point in his career" (017-010), Riis explained, marking the CAS's role as an intervention at the last moment before a boy slipped permanently into vagrancy or crime. Beyond the central lodging-house, the Society operated facilities "farther uptown than any reared by the Children's Aid Society, in Sixty-seventh Street" (017-023), serving older boys who had outgrown the downtown shelters. Through this network, Riis reported, "sixty thousand children have been rescued by them from the streets and had their little feet set in the better way" (016-009).

## The Orphan Train Movement

The orphan train program — officially the "Emigrant Aid Plan" — was the CAS's most ambitious and most debated initiative. The premise was that any home in rural America was better than no home in the New York slums. Brace argued that removing children from the corrupting environment of the tenement districts and placing them with farm families would break the cycle of urban poverty. Riis witnessed a departure himself: "I helped once to see a party off for Michigan, the last sent out by that great friend of the homeless children, Mrs. Astor, before she died" (017-021). In that party was a boy from the Five Points House of Industry whose sole possessions were "two little white mice of the friendliest disposition" (017-021) — a detail that captured both the pathos of the children's condition and the hope the program represented. Critics, both contemporary and modern, have pointed to the program's disruption of immigrant families, its insufficient vetting of receiving households, and the cultural erasure it often entailed. The orphan trains remain one of the most complex legacies in American child welfare history.

## In Riis's Account

The CAS appears throughout *How the Other Half Lives* as the primary institutional response to child homelessness. Riis describes the Society's lodging-houses as essential but insufficient: they served thousands of children but could not keep pace with the number of children the tenements produced. The Society's work was "farther uptown than any reared by the Children's Aid Society" — meaning that even its farthest reach could not escape the tenement districts. (017-023)

Riis's relationship to the CAS was supportive but critical. He endorsed its work while arguing that no charity could substitute for housing reform. The CAS treated the symptoms of the tenement crisis (homeless children); Riis wanted to treat the cause (the tenement itself).

## Legacy

The Children's Aid Society remains active in New York today, and its historical role in the development of American child welfare — both the positive aspects (founding the first free school-lunch program, pioneering vocational education) and the negative (the orphan trains' disruption of families, the organization's role in removing children from poor immigrant homes) — makes it one of the most historically significant organizations in Riis's book.
