# Tenement Reform

Tenement reform was the Progressive-era movement to improve housing conditions in New York's immigrant districts through legislation, enforcement, and model construction. Riis positioned himself as both chronicler and advocate of this movement, dedicating the final chapters of *How the Other Half Lives* to documenting reform efforts and arguing for their extension. He praised "nearly all the practical tenement reformers I have known" (024-017) as individuals who combined moral conviction with pragmatic knowledge of tenement conditions.

## The Reformers

Riis identified several categories of tenement reformers. There were the sanitary inspectors who enforced existing laws against landlord resistance; the charity workers and missionaries who lived among the poor and documented conditions; the architects and builders who designed model tenements as alternatives to the slum; and the legislators who passed laws restricting landlord prerogatives. The reformer was, for Riis, the counterweight to the landlord — the force that pushed back against the profit motive that drove tenement degradation.

The "practical" quality that Riis emphasized was crucial. He distinguished between sentimental reformers who offered charity without structural change and the practical reformers who understood that only legislation and enforcement could alter the economic incentives that produced slums. This distinction was central to Riis's own self-presentation as a journalist whose vivid descriptions of tenement life were meant to drive legislative action.

## Challenges and Opposition

Reform was never simply a matter of passing laws. Riis documented the resistance that reformers faced at every level: landlords who controlled the political machinery of their wards, tenants who feared eviction if they reported violations, and politicians who depended on landlord support for reelection. The "early reformers" lacked the evidence base that Riis's book provided — it "has since been fully demonstrated" (024-015) that the conditions they had alleged were real, but in their own time they were dismissed as alarmists.

The reformers also faced immense obstacles of scale. Riis acknowledged that even the most dedicated reformers confronted numbers that overwhelmed their efforts — "four hundred" tenements to inspect, and "the sanitary reformer gives up the task in despair" (006-026). The scale of the problem far exceeded the capacity of the enforcement apparatus, a structural imbalance that Riis argued could only be corrected by stronger laws and more inspectors.

Riis also warned against the "impatient reformer" who demanded immediate, sweeping change. He cautioned that the reformer who demanded too much too quickly would be dismissed as impractical, while the reformer who compromised too readily would achieve nothing. The effective reformer, in Riis's view, was one who could maintain moral urgency while accepting the incremental pace of legislative change — a balance that was itself a political skill (024-003).

## What the Law Accomplished

Riis offered a measured assessment of legislative progress by 1890. "The law has done what it could. That was not always a great deal, seldom more than barely sufficient for the moment" (024-003). Key achievements included the prohibition of barracks-style tenements covering entire lots, the legal claim of air and sunlight, and the end of rear tenements as new construction. "It is no longer lawful to construct barracks to cover the whole of a lot. Air and sunlight have a legal claim, and the day of rear tenements is past" (024-003). Two years before his writing, a hundred thousand people had still burrowed in rear tenements; "but some have been torn down since. Their number will decrease steadily until they shall have become a bad tradition of a heedless past" (024-003).

The dark, unventilated bedroom was going, along with the open sewer. Riis looked forward to the day when "the greatest of all evils that now curse life in the tenements — the dearth of water in the hot summer days — will also have been remedied, and a long step taken toward the moral and physical redemption of their tenants" (024-003). This was cautious optimism, grounded in the recognition that legal reform was necessary but insufficient without enforcement and cultural change.

## Legislative Achievements

Riis's book was published in 1890, at the beginning of a decade of intense housing reform activity. The Tenement House Act of 1901 — passed after Riis's book had created public demand for action — mandated light, air, and sanitation requirements for new construction and established enforcement mechanisms that previous laws had lacked. Riis's documentation of conditions under the old laws provided the factual basis for the new legislation.

## The On-Site Agent Principle

A key insight that Riis drew from successful reform experiments was the necessity of competent on-site management. "It has since been fully demonstrated that a competent agent on the premises, a man of the best and the highest stamp, who knows how to instruct and guide with a firm hand, is a prerequisite to the success of any reform tenement scheme" (024-015). This was not a moral claim but "a plain business proposition" — the presence of responsible management transformed tenant behavior and building conditions in ways that laws alone could not achieve.

Riis illustrated this principle with an account of a "towering barrack in the Tenth Ward" that had been the site of two domestic tragedies in three years. After a new housekeeper — "a tidy, mild-mannered, but exceedingly strict little body" — took charge, the character of the building was transformed. "The worst elements had been banished from the house in short order under her management, and for the rest a new era of self-respect had dawned" (024-016). This change, Riis noted with emphasis, had been accomplished "in the short space of a single year" (024-016).

## The Tenant's Readiness

Riis was emphatic in arguing that tenants were not inherently resistant to improved conditions — they were simply denied access to them. He cited a veteran reformer who insisted: "I hold that not ten percent of the people now living in tenements would refuse to avail themselves of the best improved conditions offered, and come fully up to the use of them, properly instructed; but they cannot get them" (024-017). This reformer, who had built homes for more than five hundred families over fourteen years, reported "getting daily more faith in human nature from my work among the poor tenants" (024-017).

The 1885 Tenement House Commission had reached the same conclusion, declaring that "the condition of the tenants is in advance of the houses which they occupy" (024-017) — a finding that Riis considered "quite the severest arraignment of the tenement that had yet been uttered" (024-017).
