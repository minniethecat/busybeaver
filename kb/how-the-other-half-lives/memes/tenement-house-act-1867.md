# Tenement-House Act of 1867

## Origins and Political Context

The Tenement-House Act of 1867 was born from fear. The close of the Civil War brought renewed attention to the overcrowded, unsanitary conditions of New York's slums, but it was the "dread of advancing cholera, with the guilty knowledge of the harvest field that awaited the plague in New York's slums" that finally "pricked the conscience of the community into action" (002-002). The memory of earlier cholera epidemics, which had killed thousands and spread from tenement districts to wealthier neighborhoods, gave public health arguments an urgency that moral appeals alone had failed to produce.

A citizens' movement coalesced around the findings of the Council of Hygiene, a body of physicians and civic leaders who conducted the first systematic survey of tenement conditions. The Council's characterization of the tenement evil as "the result of forgetfulness of the poor" (002-003) captured both the problem and the proposed solution: the city could no longer afford to forget. The movement's twin achievements were the organization of a [[board-of-health|Board of Health]] — established in 1866 with sanitary inspection powers — and the passage of the Tenement-House Act in 1867, which Riis called "the first step toward remedial legislation" (002-002).

The legislative committee that preceded the act was, in Riis's telling, "the first legislative committee ever appointed to probe this sore," and it "went deeper down and uncovered its roots" (P04-005). Its conclusion — "that certain conditions and associations of human life and habitation are the prolific parents of corresponding habits and morals" — linked housing conditions to social outcomes in a way that was novel for its time and that would become the intellectual foundation of the [[sanitary-reform|sanitary reform]] movement (P04-005).

## Provisions and Early Enforcement

Although Riis does not enumerate the act's provisions in detail, the historical record establishes that the 1867 law defined the [[tenement]] in legal terms for the first time — as a building occupied by three or more families living independently — and established minimum requirements for fire escapes, ventilation, and sanitation, including a mandate of one toilet per twenty occupants. These were elementary standards by later measures, but they represented a radical assertion of the principle that housing conditions were a legitimate subject of public regulation.

Enforcement began with data. A thorough canvass of the tenements had already begun in 1866, but "the cholera first, and next a scourge of smallpox, delayed the work, while emphasizing the need of it, so that it was 1869 before it got fairly under way and began to tell" (002-002). When enforcement began in earnest, the Board of Health targeted the most egregious condition first: the windowless interior bedroom. "The dark bedroom fell under the ban first," Riis records (002-002). In 1869, the Board ordered the cutting of more than forty-six thousand windows in interior rooms, "chiefly for ventilation — for little or no light was to be had from the dark hallways" (002-002). Air-shafts were unknown at the time; the idea that fresh air and sunlight were necessities rather than luxuries was itself a novelty that the act's enforcement helped establish.

The work encountered resistance from both sides of the tenement equation. Landlords "saw in every order to repair or clean up only an item of added expense to diminish their income from the rent," while tenants themselves, "who had sunk, after a generation of unavailing protest, to the level of their surroundings," considered official interference "an infringement of personal rights, and a hardship" (002-002). The enforcement of the act was therefore as much a campaign of public education as of regulatory coercion — an effort to establish, against the resistance of both property and poverty, that the state had a legitimate interest in how the poor were housed.

## The Cellar Campaign

The act's most dramatic early enforcement victory was the campaign against [[cellar-dwelling|cellar dwellings]]. These subterranean living spaces — rooms below street level, lacking light, air, and drainage — were the lowest rung of the tenement hierarchy and the most lethal. Many were below tidewater, meaning they flooded regularly with sewage and runoff.

Five years after the act's passage, the Board of Health succeeded "at last in ousting the 'cave-dwellers' and closing some five hundred and fifty cellars south of Houston Street, many of them below tidewater, that had been used as living apartments" (002-002). The clearance was not gentle: "In many instances the police had to drag the tenants out by force" (002-002). The image of police dragging families from underground rooms encapsulates the paradox of early tenement reform — an act of humanitarian compulsion that was experienced by its beneficiaries as an eviction. The cellar dwellers, dispossessed of their underground homes, were pushed into the above-ground tenements, where they joined the already overcrowded population. The reform solved one problem by intensifying another — a dynamic that would recur throughout the history of tenement legislation.

## Limitations and Legacy

The 1867 act demonstrated both what legislation could achieve and what it could not. By 1879, a decade after enforcement began, official reports read in churches still "characterized the younger criminals as victims of low social conditions of life and unhealthy, overcrowded lodgings, brought up in 'an atmosphere of actual darkness, moral and physical'" (002-003). Riis underlined the reformers' frustration: "This after the saw had been busy in the dark corners ten years!" (002-003).

The structural limitation of the act was its differential application. New construction was subject to sanitary oversight — "Over the new houses sanitary law exercises full control" — but existing buildings were largely exempt: "the old remain" (002-004). This grandfathering of substandard housing created a permanent two-tier system: buildings constructed after 1867 met minimum standards, while tens of thousands of families continued to live in tenements that the law had already declared unfit. As Riis observed, "the new tenements, that have been recently built, have been usually as badly planned as the old, with dark and unhealthy rooms, often over wet cellars, where extreme overcrowding is permitted" (002-003).

The 1867 act's most enduring legacy was constitutional rather than physical. It established the legal principle — contested then, accepted now — that housing conditions are a matter of public concern subject to legislative regulation. Every subsequent tenement law, from the [[tenement-house-act|Tenement House Act of 1879]] (the "Old Law") to the comprehensive New Law of 1901, built upon the foundation laid in 1867. The act did not solve the tenement problem — Riis's entire book is testimony to how much remained undone — but it made solving it legally conceivable.
