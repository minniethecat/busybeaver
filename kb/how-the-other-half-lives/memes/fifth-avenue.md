# Fifth Avenue

## The Other Half

Fifth Avenue represented everything the tenement districts were not. While Riis spent most of *How the Other Half Lives* documenting the squalor of the Lower East Side, Fifth Avenue was the implicit counterpoint — the "half" that did not live in tenements, that enjoyed light, air, space, and the amenities that wealth could buy. Riis did not describe Fifth Avenue in detail; he did not need to. Its existence as the emblem of New York wealth was sufficient to frame his argument about inequality.

## The Distance Between

The distance between Fifth Avenue and the tenement districts — measured in city blocks but experienced as a chasm of class — was the subject of Riis's title. "How the other half lives" was a question his wealthy readers, living on or near Fifth Avenue, had never needed to ask. Riis's book was designed to bridge that distance, bringing the reality of tenement life into the parlors of the comfortable classes.
