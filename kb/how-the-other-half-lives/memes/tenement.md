# Tenement

## Definition

In Jacob Riis's era, the *tenement* was a legally defined housing form: "a house 'occupied by three or more families, living independently and doing their cooking on the premises; or by more than two families on a floor, so living and cooking and having a common right in the halls, stairways, yards, etc.'" (002-007) This legal definition, however, captured only the surface — Riis used the word in a narrower, more damning sense, invoking the typical slum tenement of the Lower East Side with its dark, unventilated rooms and overcrowded conditions.

The tenement originated not as purpose-built slum housing but as a repurposing of the old Knickerbocker mansions:

> "The first tenement New York knew bore the mark of Cain from its birth, though a generation passed before the writing was deciphered. It was the 'rear house,' infamous ever after in our city's history. There had been tenant-houses before, but they were not built for the purpose." (001-002)

## Historical Origins

The tenement evolved through a process of subdivision and infill that transformed single-family homes into overcrowded warrens. When the city's merchant class moved uptown, their downtown mansions were subdivided into apartments, and the backyard gardens were filled in with rear tenements that blocked light and air from the original structures. "The shell-paved walk had become an alley; what the rear house had left of the garden, a 'court.'" (001-004) Plenty of these original rear tenements were still to be found in the Fourth Ward in Riis's day. (001-004)

The demand for cheap housing was insatiable. As one official report put it: "Such is the lack of houseroom in the city that any kind of tenement can be immediately crowded with lodgers, if there is space offered." (001-010) Thousands were living in cellars, and even churches were converted: some fifteen years before 1869, the old Baptist Church in Mulberry Street had been sold, and the rear half of the frame structure was converted into tenements "that with their swarming population became the scandal even of that reckless age." (001-010)

## Manifestation in the Tenements

By 1890, tenements had spread across the entire city:

> "Where are the tenements of today? Say rather: where are they not? In fifty years they have crept up from the Fourth Ward slums and the Five Points the whole length of the island, and have polluted the Annexed District to the Westchester line." (002-008)

The physical conditions were grim. Landlords subdivided old houses into windowless interior rooms — what Riis called "the dark bedroom, prolific of untold depravities." (001-003) By mid-century, overcrowding had become severe:

> "Crazy old buildings, crowded rear tenements in filthy yards, dark, damp basements, leaking garrets, shops, outhouses, and stables converted into dwellings, though scarcely fit to shelter brutes, are habitations of thousands of our fellow-beings in this wealthy, Christian city." (001-011)

The tenement was also an economic engine of exploitation. In the Bohemian quarter, for instance, landlords doubled as employers, forcing tenants into cigar-making labor as a condition of their lease:

> "Not content with simply robbing the tenant, the owner, in the dual capacity of landlord and employer, reduces him to virtual serfdom by making his becoming *his* tenant, on such terms as he sees fit to make, the condition of employment at wages likewise of his own making." (012-002)

Riis documented the staggering scale of tenement poverty. **Chapter XXI** reports that in eight years 135,595 families were registered as asking or receiving charity, and for five years past one person in every ten who died in the city was buried in the Potter's Field. (021-002)

## Disease and Mortality

The tenement was a deadly environment, particularly for children. Overcrowding and poor sanitation made it impossible to contain infectious diseases:

> "Under the most favorable circumstances, an epidemic, which the well-to-do can afford to make light of as a thing to be got over or avoided by reasonable care, is excessively fatal among the children of the poor, by reason of the practical impossibility of isolating the patient in a tenement." (014-020)

Summer heat transformed the tenements into death traps. "Life in the tenements in July and August spells death to an army of little ones whom the doctor's skill is powerless to save." (014-019) In response, the Board of Health dispatched fifty "summer doctors" into the tenements each year, offering free advice and medicine. (014-019) But the structural conditions overwhelmed medical intervention: "Tread it ever so lightly on the avenues, in the tenements it kills right and left." (014-020)

The old-style tenements, improvised from private dwellings, were the worst offenders: "The old houses that from private dwellings were made into tenements, or were run up to house the biggest crowds in defiance of every moral and physical law, can be improved by no device short of demolition." (014-021) A census comparing tenement height to mortality tables revealed a surprising pattern — "as the buildings grew taller the death-rate fell" — because the biggest tenements had been constructed under the sanitary reform laws of the previous decade. (014-021)

## Pauperism in the Tenements

Beyond disease, the tenement was an incubator of chronic poverty. "The truth is that pauperism grows in the tenements as naturally as weeds in a garden lot. All the surroundings of tenement-house life favor its growth, and where once it has taken root it is harder to dislodge than the most virulent of physical diseases." (021-007)

The charitable investigations of the era revealed the scope of the problem: 135,595 families receiving aid were found in no fewer than 31,000 different tenements. (021-006) More than a hundred and twenty pauper families were reported living in a single tenement. (021-006) Riis cited a physician's grim assessment: "In nine years I have known of but a single case of permanent improvement in a poor tenement family." (021-007)

The tenement, Riis argued, was not merely a backdrop to poverty but its cause — "the destroyer of individuality and character everywhere." (021-007) He located the solution in housing reform itself: "Tenement-house reform holds the key to the problem of pauperism in the city. We can never get rid of either the tenement or the pauper." (021-017) The ultimate remedy was "to remove the cause — the tenement that was built for 'a class of whom nothing was expected,' and which has come fully up to the expectation." (021-017)

## Tenement and the Law

The legal response to the tenement crisis began slowly. A citizens' movement in the 1860s resulted in the organization of the Board of Health and the passage of the Tenement-House Act of 1867, which Riis called "the first step toward remedial legislation." (002-002) The act established minimal requirements for light, ventilation, and sanitation, and empowered health inspectors to enforce them. (002-002)

The Health Board's "determined effort to head it off by laying a strong hand upon the tenement builders" became its chief business in subsequent years. (002-004) Yet the law's reach was limited. The tenement defeated regulatory efforts through loopholes and evasion — the sweater system in Jewtown exploited the fact that "the bulk of the sweater's work is done in the tenements, which the law that regulates factory labor does not reach." (011-006)

The legal definition itself remained problematic. It drew no distinction between the tenement and the "flat" — a term that, in practice, designated a dwelling with a private hall and a door that closed, marking the boundary between public squalor and private decency. (014-002) As Riis observed: "The hall that is a highway for all the world by night and by day is the tenement's proper badge. There may be a tenement behind a closed door; but never a 'flat' without it." (014-002)

## Significance

The tenement is the book's central organizing idea — it is both the physical setting and the moral framework of *How the Other Half Lives*. Riis uses the term to connect every social ill he documents: disease, crime, child mortality, political corruption, and ethnic conflict all trace back, in his telling, to the conditions of tenement life.

The book's opening chapters establish the tenement's history and legal definition, while its remaining chapters move through the tenement districts group by group — Italians in the Bend, Chinese in Mott Street, Jews in the Tenth Ward, Bohemians in the East Side — showing how each community adapted to and was shaped by the same brutal housing environment. Riis's ultimate argument is that moral reform must begin with physical reform of the tenement itself:

> "What the tenements are and how they grow to what they are, we shall see hereafter. The story is dark enough, drawn from the plain public records, to send a chill to any heart." (P04-004)

By 1890, the tenements housed three-fourths of New York's population. (002-008) The tenement was New York itself, and its reform was the central task of the age.
