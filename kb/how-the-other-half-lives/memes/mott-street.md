# Mott Street

## Description

Mott Street was — and remains — the heart of Manhattan's Chinatown. In Riis's era, it was a dense strip of tenements, stores, and basement rooms occupied almost exclusively by Chinese immigrants. The street was less than half a mile long, but for Riis and his readers it represented the most foreign, least accessible part of New York. A fire in a Mott Street rear house opens Riis's narrative of the tenement's physical dangers. (001-006)

Mott Street ran through the Sixth Ward, one of the most densely populated districts in the city, and stood at the boundary where Chinatown met the larger slum of the Bend. Its physical character was that of the conventional tenement district — tall barracks with rear houses wedged into every available space — but Riis observed that it was "clean to distraction," stamped by the ubiquitous Chinese laundry trade. The houses, though of standard tenement construction, bore the marks of their Chinese occupants: red and yellow signs hung endways with streamers of red flannel, announcing in Chinese characters that Dr. Chay Yen Chong sold herb medicines, or that Won Lung and Company took in washing and dealt out tea and groceries. (009-003) A church in Mott Street stood at the entrance to Chinatown as a barrier between it and the tenements beyond, its congregation waging what Riis described as "unceasing war upon the monstrous wickedness" of the district. (009-006)

## In Riis's Account

Chapter IX ("Chinatown") is built around Mott Street. Riis describes it as a street where "the Chinese colony" was concentrated almost entirely, with a population that few outsiders ever saw because the Chinese avoided contact with the white city around them. The opium den, the gambling house, and the joss house (temple) were the institutions Riis documented, and his descriptions combined genuine curiosity with the racial exoticism of his era.

The fire Riis describes in Chapter I — "the burning of a rear house in Mott Street" that "made homeless ten families" — was on this street. (001-006) The fire serves as the book's opening anecdote of tenement danger, making Mott Street literally the first place where the tenement's lethal consequences are dramatized. The owner had insured the building for eight hundred dollars, though it brought him six hundred dollars a year in rent from tenants paying an average of five dollars a month for their "mean little cubbyholes." (001-006) The fire made homeless ten families who had been paying those rents. The owner, Riis noted with irony, "evidently considered himself especially entitled to be pitied for losing such valuable property." (001-006)

Mott Street was also economically significant. Chinese businesses — laundries, restaurants, import houses — operated from the same tenement buildings that housed families. The street economy was insular, with Chinese workers paid in scrip redeemable only at Chinese-owned stores. Riis notes that the Chinese "are content to live among themselves" and that this isolation made the street an object of fascination and fear. (009)

### The Opium Dens and Their Victims

Mott Street was the epicenter of the opium trade that Riis considered the most insidious threat emanating from Chinatown. Cellar-ways opened directly onto the street, and from them came "the pungent odor of burning opium and the clink of copper coins on the table." (009-004) Fan tan, the Chinese gambling game, was played in these underground rooms, and every Chinese home or burrow on Mott Street had its bunk and its opium layout, easily enjoyed safe from police interference. (009-004) Riis claimed that opium smoking was so pervasive that "in Mott Street there is no need" of dedicated opium joints — the drug was part of domestic life. (009-004)

The true horror, in Riis's telling, was the fate of the white women and girls drawn into the Chinese dens. There were houses on Mott Street "literally jammed, from the 'joint' in the cellar to the attic" with victims of opium addiction. (009-006) These women, once addicted, rarely or never returned to their former lives. Riis wrote that "Mott Street gives up its victims only to the Charity Hospital or the Potter's Field." (009-006) Once a woman had "put the pipe to their lips," she had, in Riis's phrase, "given Mott Street a mortgage upon their souls for all time." (009-007) He recounted intervening in a Mott Street cellar where a Chinaman was beating his white "wife" with a broom-handle, and the man's response — "S'ppose your wifee bad, you no lickee her?" — captured for Riis the impossibility of bridging the cultural gulf. (009-011)

### The Joss House and the Telegraph Pole

The Joss House, the Chinese temple, stood on Mott Street and was one of the street's most distinctive landmarks. Riis described an expedition to photograph Joss, after which a rumor spread that the picture was wanted for the Rogues' Gallery at police headquarters. Two roast pigs were offered as propitiation to "his offended majesty of Mott Street" and his attendant priests. (009-013) The priests, Riis observed, bore "a very practical hand in the worship by serving as the divine stomach." (009-013)

The telegraph pole near the Joss House, in full view from Chatham Square, served as what Riis called "the real official organ of Chinatown." (009-016) On it, unseen hands posted yellow and red notices daily, announcing where fan tan games would run that night or warning the faithful of impending police raids. This telegraph pole was at the center of the colony's "real life" — its gambling news and the constant "stream of plotting and counterplotting" that constituted Chinese social and political existence on Mott Street. (009-016)

### The Big Flat

One of the most notorious tenements on Mott Street was the "Big Flat," originally built as a model tenement but which "speedily came to rival the Court in foulness." (024-009) It became a regular hotbed of thieves and peace-breakers, making constant trouble for the police. The immediate cause of its failure, beyond the lack of proper supervision, was its open access to two streets in a neighborhood where criminals abounded — an arrangement the builders had intended as a ventilation advantage. (024-009) By the time Riis wrote Chapter XXIV, the Big Flat had been demolished and replaced by a six-story carriage factory, one of the rare instances where commercial development eliminated a tenement plague-spot. (024-008)

### The Landlord

Mott Street landlords appear repeatedly in Riis's account as exemplars of tenement profiteering. In Chapter XXV, Riis described watching a Mott Street landlord, the owner of "a row of barracks that have made no end of trouble for the health authorities for twenty years," execute his idea of reform by giving the wretched pile a coat of paint and putting up a "gorgeous tin cornice" with the year 1890 in letters a yard long. (025-011) From his vantage point, Riis looked down on "the same dirty crowds camping on the roof, foremost among them an Italian mother with two stark-naked children who had apparently never made the acquaintance of a washtub." (025-011)

### Disease and Destitution

Mott Street's tenements were repeatedly the scene of disease outbreaks and desperate poverty. A measles epidemic that ravaged Elizabeth Street also "overshadowed in part the contiguous tenements in Mott Street," its death-track as clearly defined "as the track of a tornado through a forest district." (014-020) In one Mott Street tenement, Riis was summoned to find a child dying from peritonitis brought on by starvation — the father crippled from lead poisoning, the mother and one son nearly blind from an untreated eye disease, the family subsisting on two dollars a week from the priest. (014-022)

The street was also a site of organized charity fraud. Riis described a Mott Street den from which a cry went up that a family — a "sick" husband, a despairing mother, and half a dozen children in rags — were destitute of the "first necessities of life." The newspaper appeal brought a "stream of gold," but investigation revealed the "sickness" to be laziness and the destitution to be the family's stock in trade. (021-012) Before the day was three hours old, two "toughs" had blocked the alley and were exacting toll of a silver quarter from each tearful sympathizer heading to the attic. (021-013)

The street was also on the route of the police raids Riis accompanied. On the way to raid the stale-beer dives of the Bend, the police column passed "the heathen temples of Mott Street," and the officers remarked that "there was some fun to be gotten out of a raid there." (007-002)

## Significance

Mott Street represents Riis's engagement with the most segregated community in New York. The Chinese were excluded from white neighborhoods by law and custom, and they responded by building an autonomous economic and social world within a few blocks of Mott Street. Riis's account is among the earliest detailed descriptions of American Chinatown life, and while its racial attitudes are problematic, its documentary value — street-level detail about businesses, housing, and community institutions — remains significant.

Riis's conclusion about the street was dire: "To the peril that threatens there all the senses are alert, whereas the poison that proceeds from Mott Street puts mind and body to sleep, to work out its deadly purpose in the corruption of the soul." (009-017) He called for "the severest official scrutiny, the harshest repressive measures" in Chinatown, arguing that the case was "infinitely more urgent" than even the Bend. (009-017) Yet in the same chapter, he also proposed a more humane remedy: rather than banish the Chinaman, open the door wider — for his wife — so that he might not remain "a homeless stranger among us." (009-018)
