The Newsboys' Lodging House on Duane Street was the flagship institution of the Children's Aid Society's effort to shelter and reform the city's homeless children. Founded in 1855, it provided beds, meals, and education to thousands of newsboys, bootblacks, and street children who had no other home. Riis documented it in his chapter on "The Street Arab" as a rare bright spot in the tenement landscape — an institution that worked, and a rebuttal to the pessimism that often attended discussions of the slum poor.

## A Home for the Homeless

Riis described the population of the lodging house as "an odd mixture of small humanity as could hardly be got together in any other spot" [017-002]. The boys came from every background — orphans, runaways, the abandoned, and those whose families were too poor to keep them. "We wuz six," one boy told Riis, "and we ain't got no father" [017-009]. These were the city's most vulnerable children, the ones who sold newspapers on street corners by day and slept in doorways by night before the lodging house gave them a bed.

The lodging house was not a charity in the traditional sense of free relief. Boys paid six cents for a bed and four cents for a meal — a pricing structure designed to preserve dignity and self-respect while keeping the service within reach of even the poorest street child. This principle of nominal payment distinguished the Children's Aid Society's approach from the almshouse model: the boys were customers, not charity cases, and the institution's discipline reflected that distinction.

## Rules and Reform

The house was run on strict principles. Over the door, a notice proclaimed: "Boys who swear and chew tobacco cannot sleep here" [017-010]. There was another unwritten condition — that the boy be truly homeless — "but upon this the managers wisely do not insist too obstinately, accepting without too close inquiry his account of himself where that seems advisable, well knowing that many a home that sends forth such lads far less deserves the name than the one they are able to give them" [017-010]. This pragmatic leniency was a deliberate feature of the institution's design: the managers understood that rigid eligibility tests would exclude precisely the children who most needed shelter.

The lodging house offered more than beds. It provided baths, clean clothes, and evening school instruction. The boys were organized into a quasi-military corps with its own officers, uniforms, and drills — a structure that instilled discipline while satisfying the boys' appetite for display and competition. For many, the lodging house was their first experience of order, cleanliness, and adult attention that was neither exploitative nor indifferent.

## Scale and Impact

The numbers were remarkable. Riis reported that "the Duane Street Lodging House alone has sheltered since its foundation in 1855 nearly a quarter of a million different boys, at a total expense of a good deal less than half a million dollars" [017-020]. Across all its lodging houses, the Society sheltered and taught 12,153 boys and girls in the year prior to Riis's writing [017-020]. The efficiency was striking — each boy cost roughly two dollars to shelter, an extraordinary return on charitable investment.

This cost-effectiveness was central to Riis's argument for the lodging house model. In an era when critics of charity warned against creating dependency, the Newsboys' Lodging House demonstrated that well-designed institutions could lift the poor without demoralizing them. The boys who passed through its doors included future businessmen, professionals, and at least one member of Congress — proof, in Riis's view, that the street child was not inherently criminal but was made so by an environment that the lodging house successfully counteracted.

## Reform Philosophy

The Newsboys' Lodging House embodied a distinct philosophy of poverty relief that Riis championed throughout his work: charity should not merely alleviate suffering but should change the conditions that produced it. The lodging house did not simply feed and shelter boys — it taught them to read, trained them in habits of cleanliness and punctuality, and, crucially, connected many of them with placements in the West through the Children's Aid Society's emigration program. This last element — removing children from the tenement environment entirely — represented the most radical version of Riis's environmental determinism: if the tenement made criminals, the only certain cure was to leave it behind.
