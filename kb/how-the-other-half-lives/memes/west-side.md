# West Side

## Location and Character

The West Side of Lower Manhattan was, in Jacob Riis's geography of poverty, one of the most densely packed and degraded tenement districts in the city. Riis described it as stretching from the [[Fourth Ward]] up through the tenement belt, with the Irish predominating in the "West Side tenement districts" near the Battery (003-011). The area was notorious for its stale-beer dives — Riis reported that the West Side was "the stronghold of the African on the West Side, a share of the stale-beer traffic" (007-012), where Black and Irish populations competed in a shared economy of cheap intoxication.

## The "West Side Emerald"

Riis used a striking metaphor to capture the neighborhood's symbolic degradation: "Down near the Battery the West Side emerald would be soiled by a dirty stain" (003-013). This image — of something supposedly green and precious being sullied — reflected his broader argument that the immigrant poor were not inherently degraded but were made so by their environment. The proximity to the Battery, the historic heart of the city, made the contrast between civic aspiration and tenement reality especially stark.

## Social Conditions

The conditions Riis documented on the West Side were among the worst in the city. In March of an unspecified year, he recorded the case of "mothers in West Side tenements, who, in their drunkenness, lay upon the floor" while their infants crawled to open windows (014-029). The West Side was also associated with the city's "social" statistics — Riis noted that "less startling titles" attached to the "West Side to the wealth and 'social' standing of its patrons" (018-005), a sardonic observation about how statistical categories masked human suffering.

## In the Tenement Landscape

The West Side was part of the larger tenement geography that Riis mapped in *How the Other Half Lives*. It appeared alongside other notorious districts — the [[Fourth Ward]], the [[Seventh Ward]], the Bend, and Jewtown — in his catalog of places where housing had failed the poor. The area's proximity to the Hudson River and to the commercial center of the city made it a natural destination for immigrant populations seeking work on the docks and in the service industries of lower Manhattan, but the housing stock available to them was among the oldest and most degraded in the city.
