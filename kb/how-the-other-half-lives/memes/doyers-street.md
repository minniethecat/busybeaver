# Doyers Street

## The Bloody Angle

Doyers Street was one of the most notorious thoroughfares in 1890s New York. A short, sharply curved lane in the heart of what is now Chinatown, it earned the nickname "the Bloody Angle" for the frequency of violent confrontations — particularly hatchet murders associated with Chinese tong (association) rivalries. The street's unusual bend made it a natural ambush point, and its location within the tenement district Riis described in Chapter IX, "Chinatown," placed it at the intersection of immigrant poverty and organized vice.

## In Riis's Chinatown

Riis's chapter on Chinatown focused primarily on [[mott-street|Mott Street]] and the opium dens and gambling establishments that defined the neighborhood in the public imagination. Doyers Street, just around the corner from Mott, was part of the same ecosystem — a dense warren of tenement housing, basement gambling rooms, and the "joss houses" (temples) that Riis viewed with a mixture of anthropological curiosity and nativist suspicion.

## Geography of the Bend

Doyers Street was also geographically significant as part of the larger [[the-bend|Bend]] district — the notorious slum centered on Mulberry Street that Riis described as "the foul core of New York's slums." The street's proximity to both the Bend and Chinatown meant that it was a zone of ethnic transition, where Irish, Italian, and Chinese populations overlapped and sometimes clashed.
