## Overview

Pell Street lay at the center of what Riis called the "Bloody Sixth" Ward in 1890s New York, running through the Chinatown district alongside Mott Street. It was notorious for its concentration of opium dens, cheap lodging-houses, and tenements that Riis described as "literally jammed, from the 'joint' in the cellar to the attic" [009-006]. Together with Mott and Doyers streets, Pell formed the core triangle of Chinatown — a district that Riis portrayed as simultaneously exotic, threatening, and, in his reformist view, urgently in need of Christian intervention.

## Opium and Vice

Riis portrayed Pell Street as a center of the opium trade that he considered "a subtler poison than ever the stale-beer dives knew" [009-006]. Together with Mott Street, Pell Street housed "dozens" of establishments where victims of opium addiction were packed into every level of the tenement — from cellar "joints" to attics. The opium dens served a cross-ethnic clientele: Riis observed that while the Chinese operated the establishments, the customers included "the young scions of respectable New York families" as well as "the harlots of the lowest dens" [009-006]. This blurring of class and ethnic boundaries in the pursuit of vice made Chinatown especially threatening to Riis's middle-class readers.

Police raids targeted "the Chinese fan-tan games" and "opium joints of Mott and Pell Street" [007-002], but enforcement had limited effect against a trade deeply embedded in the district's economy. The raids produced a grim cycle: Chinese immigrants arrested in sweeps were "emptied upon Blackwell's Island" [009-006], served their sentences, and returned to the same streets and the same trade.

## Lodging-Houses

Pell Street was also home to some of the city's cheapest lodging-houses. Riis documented "Bunks in a seven-cent lodging-house, Pell Street" [008-011] — among the lowest tier of accommodation available, below even the five- and ten-cent spots that characterized other parts of the Bowery district. These establishments catered to the most destitute population of the city, providing a plank or bunk for the price of a few cents. The seven-cent lodging-house stood at the absolute bottom of a housing hierarchy that descended from the tenement flat, through the five-cent spot, to this last refuge of the utterly penniless.

## In the Context of Chinatown's Economy

Pell Street's vice economy was part of a larger structure of ethnic exclusion. Chinese immigrants, barred by the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act from bringing wives or families, and excluded from most legitimate trades, had few economic options beyond the service and entertainment industries that catered to the bachelor society. The opium dens, fan-tan parlors, and cheap lodging-houses of Pell Street were at once symptoms of racist immigration policy and evidence — in Riis's telling — of Chinese moral inferiority. Riis himself was unable or unwilling to see this structural connection, treating the vice of Pell Street as a product of Chinese culture rather than of the legal and economic marginalization that produced it.

## Street Life

The street's notoriety extended beyond its organized vice. Riis related an anecdote, reported with laughter at the Elizabeth Street police station, about "a dead goat" found "lying in Pell Street" that "was mysteriously missing by the time the offal-cart came to take it away" — an Italian had "carried it off in his sack to a wake or feast" in a nearby alley [006-004]. The story, told as dark comedy, illustrated the informal economy, the improvisational food practices, and the ethnic boundaries that shaped daily life on Pell Street. Italians, Chinese, and Irish shared the street's physical space but occupied separate moral universes in Riis's narrative.
