# Charity

## Definition

In *How the Other Half Lives*, "charity" refers to the organized philanthropic response to urban poverty — primarily the work of the Charity Organization Society (COS) and related religious and civic bodies. Riis treats charity as a double-edged institution: essential for immediate relief, but also a symptom of the society's failure to address the root causes of destitution. He saw that charity could enable as well as relieve: "Ill-applied charity and idleness answer the first." (007-018)

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The scale of need dwarfed the charitable response. Riis reports a staggering figure:

> "In eight years 135,595 families in New York were registered as asking or receiving charity." (021-002)

The Charity Organization Society's statistical work provided Riis with some of his most powerful data. A classification of 5,169 cases investigated in a single year broke down as follows: only 6.4% were worthy of continuous relief (orphans, cripples, the very aged); 24.4% needed temporary relief; 52.2% needed work rather than relief; and 17% were judged unworthy — professional beggars. (021-002)

The "unworthy" category particularly concerned Riis and the COS. He describes a "veritable 'tribe of Ishmael,' tightening its grip on society as the years pass," referring to families that trained their children in professional begging. (021-003)

Riis devotes significant attention to what he considered the destructive effects of undiscriminating charity. Impulsive giving, he argued, did more harm than good:

> "More than all the wretchedness of the slums is brought about by this sort of ill-directed, impulsive but thoughtless charity." (021-012)

The police station lodging-rooms offered another form of informal charity — a "charity drink" and a bite to eat for the homeless and destitute. (007-014) But even this police-administered relief raised more questions than it answered: "Ill-applied charity and idleness answer the first." (007-018)

## Significance

Riis's treatment of charity reflects the central tension of Progressive-era reform. He was deeply embedded in the charitable institutions of his time — his own work was sponsored by charitable organizations — yet he recognized that charity treated symptoms, not causes. His solution, articulated in Chapter XXV, was structural: enforce sanitary standards, build model tenements, and tear down the worst slums. Charity was the safety net; reform was the cure.

The Charity Organization Society's data-driven approach — classifying cases, tracking outcomes, distinguishing the "deserving" from the "undeserving" poor — also prefigures modern social work. Riis's extensive use of their statistics in the book reflects his conviction that moral arguments alone were insufficient; reform required data.
