# Caroline Astor

## Biography

Caroline Webster Schermerhorn Astor (1830–1908) was the undisputed queen of Gilded Age New York society. As the wife of William Backhouse Astor Jr., she presided over the "Four Hundred" — the elite social list that defined Manhattan's upper class. But Riis mentions her not for her balls and receptions but for her charitable work with tenement children.

## Role in the Tenements

Riis invokes Mrs. Astor in Chapter XVII ("The Street Arab"), his account of homeless and working children in the tenement districts. In a passage discussing the efforts of the Children's Aid Society to provide lodging, schools, and industrial training for street children, Riis notes:

> "Mrs. Astor set an example during her noble life of practical charity." (017-022)

The reference is brief but significant. Mrs. Astor represented the absolute peak of wealth and social status in New York — and she chose to direct some of that wealth toward tenement children. Riis uses her as an implicit rebuke to other wealthy New Yorkers who gave nothing: if even Mrs. Astor could find time for tenement charity, what excuse did others have?

Her involvement was channeled through the Children's Aid Society, the primary organization working with homeless and working children in the tenement districts. The Society maintained lodging-houses, industrial schools, and summer camps that served thousands of children each year. Mrs. Astor's patronage gave the Society social legitimacy and fundraising power.

## Legacy

Mrs. Astor's appearance in Riis's book is strategic. By this point in the narrative, the reader has been exposed to twenty chapters of tenement horrors. The mention of Mrs. Astor — the most socially elevated person in the book — offers a model of engagement: the wealthy can and should direct their resources toward the poor. Riis is careful not to present her as a saint; she is simply an example of what was possible.

Her inclusion also reveals Riis's own social position. He was not a radical calling for the abolition of wealth but a reformer calling for its responsible use. The Astor model — private philanthropy by the wealthy, channeled through organized charities — was exactly the approach Riis advocated.
