Homelessness was one of the most visible and pervasive social crises of late 19th-century New York City. Jacob Riis documented a staggering scale of shelterlessness, in which the city itself — through its police station-houses and municipal dormitories — became the primary provider of nightly shelter for a vast and unacknowledged population.

## Definition

In Riis's New York, homelessness was not a marginal condition but a structural feature of the tenement economy. The "homeless army" he described was composed of displaced veterans, transient laborers, families evicted from tenements, and children cast adrift by poverty and parental death. Homelessness cut across every demographic category — men, women, children, native-born and immigrant alike — and was sustained by a system in which the cheapest tenement rooms were still beyond the reach of the poorest.

Riis distinguished carefully between the homeless and the merely poor, as between the pauper and the honestly destitute. "I speak of the pauper, not of the honestly poor," he wrote, recognizing that while many of the homeless were victims of economic forces beyond their control, others had been absorbed into a permanent culture of dependency [021-007]. The Charity Organization Society found that "more than half were destitute because they had no work and were unable to find any, and one-sixth were frauds, professional beggars, training their children to follow in their footsteps" [021-003].

## The Tramp Army

The core of the homeless population was the "army of tramps" — a term Riis used to describe the vast population of homeless men who cycled through the city's lodging-houses, police stations, and street encampments. This population had its origins in the post-Civil War period: "The army of tramps that grew up after the disbandment of the armies in the field, and has kept up its muster roll, together with the inrush of the Italian tide, have ever since opposed a stubborn barrier to all efforts at permanent improvement" [006-003].

The tramp was not simply unemployed; he operated according to a distinct philosophy. "Tramps and toughs profess the same doctrine, that the world owes them a living, but from standpoints that tend in different directions" [007-018]. Riis observed that "the tough does not become a tramp, save in rare instances, when old and broken down" — the tramp represented a separate species of urban poverty, defined more by passivity than by aggression [007-018]. "Of the tough the tramp doctrine that the world owes him a living makes a thief; of the tramp a coward" [007-019].

The tramp population was not drawn from any single class. Riis noted that "nor is the tramps' army recruited from any certain class" [007-018]. Once a man entered the tramp circuit, however, escape was difficult: "Once started on the career of a tramp, the man keeps to it because it is the laziest" [007-018].

## The Cheap Lodging-House System

The infrastructure of homelessness was the cheap lodging-house, a commercial institution that Riis regarded as a primary driver of urban destitution. A police-court justice told Riis that "the ten-cent lodging-houses more than counterbalance the good done by the free reading-room, lectures, and all other agencies of reform" [008-002]. The same magistrate concluded that "such lodging-houses have caused more destitution, more beggary and crime than any other agency I know of" [008-002].

The lodging-houses operated at multiple price tiers. The twenty-five-cent lodging-house "keeps up the pretence of a bedroom, though the head-high partition enclosing a space just large enough to hold a cot and a chair and allow the man room to pull off his clothes is the shallowest of all pretences" [008-009]. Below this tier, "there are no licensed lodging-houses known to me which charge less than seven cents for even such a bed as this canvas strip, though there are unlicensed ones enough where one may sleep on the floor for five cents a spot, or squat in a sheltered hallway for three" [008-013]. At the bottom was the police station lodging-house, "where the soft side of a plank is the regulation couch" [008-013].

Inspector Byrnes of the police department reported that "in the last two or three years at least four hundred young men have been arrested for petty crimes that originated in the lodging-houses, and that in many cases it was their first step in crime" [008-004]. The Bowery lodging-houses functioned as schools of criminal apprenticeship: one thief "went to Johnstown after the flood and was shot and killed there while robbing the dead" [008-004].

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The scale of the crisis was captured in police statistics that Riis cited with alarm. According to police figures, "4,974,025 separate lodgings were furnished last year by these dormitories," and combined with police station lodgings, "the total of the homeless army was 5,121,659, an average of over fourteen thousand homeless men for every night in the year" [008-014]. This was not a transient crisis but a permanent feature of urban life.

More than half of the lodging-houses were concentrated in the Bowery district — the Fourth, Sixth, and Tenth Wards — and "they harbor nearly three-fourths of their crowds" [008-014]. Riis calculated "that more than nine thousand homeless young men lodge nightly along Chatham Street and the Bowery, between the City Hall and the Cooper Union" [008-014].

The crisis touched all levels of tenement society. A fire "made homeless ten families, who had paid an average of $5 a month for their mean little rooms" [001-006], showing how even the employed poor lived one disaster away from the street. In the worst cases, the density of homelessness produced scenes of extreme overcrowding: "Two hundred and seventy-five tramps had been jammed into the cellar" of a single tenement [007-008].

A significant component of the homeless population was the "army of tramps that grew up after the disbandment of the armies" following the Civil War [006-003]. These displaced veterans and transient laborers drifted into the city seeking work and found themselves trapped in the lodging-house circuit, unable to afford permanent quarters.

The Eleventh Precinct, covering the Tenth Ward, "harbored one-third of the army of homeless lodgers and fourteen percent of all the prisoners of the year" [018-004]. Below Fourteenth Street, 234 cheap lodging-houses were counted, generating "a total of four millions and a half of lodgers for the twelvemonth" [018-004].

## The Stale-Beer Dives

At the lowest stratum of the homeless economy were the stale-beer dives — unlicensed cellar establishments that served as de facto shelters for the most degraded tramps. Located primarily in "the Bend" and its back alleys, these dives operated on a simple principle: for the price of a drink of stale beer, a tramp could sit through the night in a crowded, stifling cellar. "Their customers, alike homeless and hopeless in their utter wretchedness, are the professional tramps, and these only" [007-011].

On one police raid described by Riis, "seventy-five tramps, male and female, were arrested in the four small rooms" of a single cellar dive [007-007]. These raids were routinized; the police sergeant observed "as if it were the most natural thing in the world, that he had more than once seen the same tramp sent to Blackwell's Island twice in twenty-four hours for six months at a time" [007-008]. The stale-beer dive functioned as a waystation in a perpetual circuit between the streets, the cellar, and the prison.

On hot summer nights, those who could not afford even the stale-beer admission fee occupied tenement hallways: "it is no rare experience when exploring the worst of the tenements in 'the Bend' to find the hallways occupied by rows of 'sitters,' tramps whom laziness or hard luck has prevented from earning enough by their day's 'labor' to pay the admission fee to a stale-beer dive" [007-013]. At such times, "the woman tramp predominates" [007-013].

## Homeless Children and Waifs

Homelessness was not limited to adults. Riis documented a parallel crisis of homeless children — "waifs" — who occupied a distinct position in the urban ecology. "In a score of years an army of twenty-five thousand of these forlorn little waifs have cried out from the streets of New York in arraignment of a Christian civilization under the blessings of which the instinct of motherhood even was smothered by poverty and want" [016-002].

The Newsboys' Lodging Houses, operated by the Children's Aid Society, served as the primary refuge for homeless boys. "The Duane Street Lodging House alone has sheltered since its foundation in 1855 nearly a quarter of a million different boys, at a total expense of a good deal less than half a million dollars" [017-020]. Across all of the Society's lodging-houses, "12,153 boys and girls were sheltered and taught last year" [017-020].

The causes of child homelessness were rooted in the tenement economy itself. "Grinding poverty and hard work beyond the years of the lad; blows and curses for breakfast, dinner, and supper; all these are recruiting agents for the homeless army" of boys [017-008]. A twelve-year-old boy in the Newsboys' Lodging House told Riis: "We wuz six and we ain't got no father" [017-009].

Philanthropic intervention provided the only counterweight. In thirty-seven years, the Children's Aid Society "has sheltered quite three hundred thousand outcast, homeless, and orphaned children in its lodging-houses, and has found homes in the West for seventy thousand that had none" [015-013]. Mrs. Astor personally "had sent out no less than thirteen hundred when she died, and left funds to carry on her work" [017-022].

## Pauperism and the Tenement Connection

Homelessness was inseparable from the broader problem of pauperism that Riis saw as endemic to the tenement districts. "The truth is that pauperism grows in the tenements as naturally as weeds in a garden lot" [021-007]. The Charity Organization Society recorded that "in eight years 135,595 families in New York were registered as asking or receiving charity" [021-002], a figure Riis translated into human terms: "in a population of a million and a half, very nearly, if not quite, half a million persons were driven, or chose, to beg for food, or to accept it in charity at some period of the eight years" [021-002].

Riis estimated that "New York spends in public and private charity every year a round $8,000,000" [021-017]. Yet he argued that the expenditure was often counterproductive: "They frequently aggravate it, degrading and pauperizing where true help should aim at raising the sufferer to self-respect and self-dependence" [021-017]. The Charity Organization Society had demonstrated an alternative model, "raising, in eight years, 4,500 families out of the rut of pauperism into proud, if modest, independence, without alms, but by a system of 'friendly visitation'" [021-017].

The connection between homelessness and pauperism ran through the very structure of tenement housing. "Tenement-house reform holds the key to the problem of pauperism in the city," Riis concluded. "We can never get rid of either the tenement or the pauper" [021-017]. The two were locked together: the tenement produced homelessness, and homelessness sustained the tenement economy by ensuring a perpetual supply of desperate tenants willing to pay premium rents for the meanest accommodations.

## Significance

Riis's treatment of homelessness served a dual purpose in his larger argument. First, the sheer numbers demonstrated that the tenement system was not merely inadequate but actively destructive — it produced homelessness on an industrial scale. Second, his observation that the census "takes no account" of the "vast homeless crowds" [006-026] pointed to a deliberate blindness in official statistics. By rendering the homeless invisible to policymakers, the census allowed the problem to fester unaddressed, generation after generation.

The homeless crisis was also a direct indictment of the free market in housing. When the cheapest tenement room was beyond the reach of working families, and when the only alternatives were police station-houses and underground lodging-cellars, the system had failed in its most basic function. Riis's documentation of homelessness was, in this sense, the strongest quantitative argument in *How the Other Half Lives* for public intervention in the housing market.

The political dimension of homelessness could not be ignored. "If tramps have nothing else to call their own they have votes, and votes that are for sale cheap for cash" [007-008]. The lodging-houses became political machines during elections: "About election time, especially in Presidential elections, the lodging-houses come out strong on the side of the political boss who has the biggest 'barrel'" [008-015]. In this way, the homeless population was not merely a social problem but an instrument of political corruption — a captive electorate whose votes could be purchased by the ward boss who controlled access to shelter.
