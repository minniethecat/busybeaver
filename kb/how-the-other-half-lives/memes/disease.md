# Disease

## Definition

Disease in Riis's narrative is both a physical reality — the epidemics that swept through overcrowded tenements — and a metaphor for the moral contagion of slum life. The sanitary reform movement was built on the medical observation that cholera, typhus, and tuberculosis followed the geography of the tenements. Riis uses disease statistics not primarily as a public health argument but as a moral one: the tenement was literally killing its inhabitants, and a society that allowed this was complicit in murder.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The link between tenement conditions and disease was well documented by Riis's time. Measles, "ordinarily a harmless disease," became a killer in the tenements:

> "Measles, ordinarily a harmless disease, furnishes a familiar example." (014-020)

The disease thrived on the very conditions the tenement guaranteed: overcrowding, poor ventilation, inadequate sanitation, and malnutrition. Riis describes a child "dying from some unknown disease" whom he visited with the charity doctor. (014-022) The phrase "unknown disease" captures the medical ignorance of the era — diseases were often diagnosed too late because tenement dwellers could not afford doctors.

In Jewtown, Riis observed that dirt and disease were inseparable:

> "These people are wedded everywhere to dirt and disease, and Jewtown is no exception." (010-008)

The Board of Health conducted inspections and kept statistics, but Riis's point was that disease was not a medical problem but a housing problem. You could not cure tenement tuberculosis with medicine; you had to cure the tenement. He compared physical disease to pauperism explicitly:

> "The truth is that pauperism grows in the tenements as naturally as weeds in a garden. It is as catching as the most virulent of physical diseases." (021-007)

## Significance

Disease occupies a strategic position in Riis's argument because it was the one consequence of tenement life that even the most callous property owner could not ignore. Epidemic disease did not respect neighborhood boundaries — cholera in the tenements threatened the whole city. This made public health the entering wedge for tenement reform: sanitary inspection, mandatory ventilation, and plumbing requirements all entered law as disease-control measures before they were recognized as housing-rights measures.

The disease narrative also bridges Riis's two audiences: middle-class readers who feared contagion, and reformers who sought justice. By framing the tenement as a disease vector, Riis made housing reform a matter of self-interest for the wealthy, not just charity for the poor.
