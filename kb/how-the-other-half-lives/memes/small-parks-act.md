# Small Parks Act (1887)

The Small Parks Act of 1887 was New York State legislation that authorized the city to acquire and demolish slum blocks for conversion into small public parks. Its most celebrated result was Mulberry Bend Park — built on the site of what Riis called the "foul core of New York's slums" — which demonstrated that environmental improvement could transform neighborhood character. Riis cited the act and its results as proof that tenement reform was possible, noting that the park "put an end for good and all to the Bread and Blood riots of which it used to be the scene."

## Background

The small parks movement emerged from the recognition that the tenement districts were almost entirely devoid of public open space. Children played in the streets and alleys; families had no access to fresh air or recreation. Reformers argued that parks were not luxuries but necessities — public health interventions that could reduce disease, crime, and social unrest in the overcrowded slums.

## Mulberry Bend Park

The act's most significant achievement was the demolition of the Mulberry Bend slum and its replacement with a public park. Riis described the transformation as the conversion of a "foul core" into a space of civic pride. The park was opened in 1897, providing the densest neighborhood in the city with its first significant green space. Riis photographed both the slum before demolition and the park afterward, using the before-and-after contrast as his strongest visual argument for environmental reform.
