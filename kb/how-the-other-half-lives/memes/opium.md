# Opium

Opium dens were a fixture of Riis's portrait of Chinatown. He located the "opium joints of Mott and Pell Streets" (007-002) as central to the neighborhood's illicit economy, alongside gambling houses and fan-tan games. The opium den was for Riis both a literal place and a symbol of the vice he believed was inseparable from tenement poverty.

## In the Tenement Districts

The opium trade was concentrated in Chinatown but its effects radiated outward. Visitors to the Chinese quarter encountered "the pungent odor of burning opium and the clink of copper coins on the table" (009-004), sensory markers that Riis used to establish the alienness of the district. The opium den was one of several institutions — along with the gambling house and the lodging-house — that Riis saw as trapping the poor in cycles of dependency.

Riis grouped the opium habit with "similar grievous" afflictions (014-036), placing it alongside alcoholism and other forms of narcotic dependency that he documented throughout the tenement districts. His treatment of opium use reflected the broader anti-vice framework of Progressive-era reform: individual moral failing was understood as both cause and consequence of environmental degradation.

## Inside the Opium Den

Riis offered a vivid portrait of the opium den's interior, describing a cellar-way in Mott Street from which issued "the pungent odor of burning opium and the clink of copper coins on the table" (009-004). The scene was dominated by fan tan, "their ruling passion" — "The average Chinaman, the police will tell you, would rather gamble than eat any day" (009-004). Only the opium smoker remained apart from the gaming: "Only the fellow in the bunk smokes away, indifferent to all else but his pipe and his own enjoyment" (009-004).

Riis corrected a common misperception about the prevalence of opium dens in Chinatown: "It is a mistake to assume that Chinatown is honeycombed with opium 'joints.' There are a good many more outside of it than in it" (009-004). The Chinese, he noted, did not monopolize the pipe. But within Chinatown, "Not a Chinese home or burrow there, but has its bunk and its layout, where they can be enjoyed safe from police interference" (009-004). While the Chinaman "smokes opium as Caucasians smoke tobacco, and apparently with little worse effect upon himself," Riis warned with characteristic alarm: "woe unto the white victim upon which his pitiless drug gets its grip" (009-004).

## Racial Dimensions

Riis's account of opium use was inseparable from his racial characterization of Chinese immigrants. The opium den functioned in his narrative as evidence of Chinese otherness — an exotic vice that distinguished Chinatown from the European immigrant quarters. This framing reinforced the racial hierarchy that structured *How the Other Half Lives*, where Chinese immigrants occupied the lowest rung of Riis's civilizational ladder.

The racial logic extended to a broader warning about the Chinese presence. Riis contrasted the visible peril of the Bend — against which "all the senses are alert" — with the insidious threat from Mott Street: "the poison that proceeds from Mott Street puts mind and body to sleep, to work out its deadly purpose in the corruption of the soul" (009-017). The opium den was thus not merely a site of individual degradation but a metaphor for the supposed danger that Chinese immigrants posed to the moral health of the city.
