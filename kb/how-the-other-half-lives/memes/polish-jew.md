## Overview

Polish Jews formed a significant segment of the Jewish immigrant population on Manhattan's Lower East Side during the late nineteenth century. Jacob Riis distinguished them from Russian Jews in *How the Other Half Lives*, noting differences in character, occupation, and social standing that shaped the internal dynamics of the Jewish quarter.

## Settlement and Demographics

Polish Jews, along with Russian Jews, had "overrun the district" of the Lower East Side, displacing earlier immigrant groups in the city's relentless pattern of ethnic succession (003-012). They settled predominantly in the eastern reaches of the Jewish quarter, pushing into neighborhoods that had previously been dominated by German and Irish immigrants.

## Occupation and Education

Riis observed that even "the children of the most ignorant Polish Jew" kept "fairly abreast" of their educational opportunities (010-019). This remark, characteristically ambivalent, reflected Riis's complex attitude toward Jewish immigrants — simultaneously admiring their educational ambition while trafficking in ethnic stereotypes. Polish Jews worked across a range of trades, from pushcart peddling to garment work in the tenement sweatshops.

## Social Position

In the racial and ethnic hierarchy that Riis documented, Polish Jews occupied a distinct rung. They were classified below whites but above Italians and Chinese immigrants in the social order Riis described (013-005). Police beats in the quarters of the Polish Jews, particularly in the vicinity of Mulberry Bend, were among the busiest in the city (010-022).

## Discrimination and Stereotyping

The Polish Jew was subjected to virulent stereotyping in Riis's narrative. The police officer quoted by Riis compared the danger posed by "the cutter's long knife" and "the Polish Jew," treating both as threats to public order (011-003). This conflation of ethnicity with criminality was typical of the era's nativist discourse, even within reform-minded works like Riis's.
