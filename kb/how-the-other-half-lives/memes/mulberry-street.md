## Overview

Mulberry Street was the spine of the Italian immigrant district in 1890s New York, running through the heart of what had once been the Five Points and what had become, by Riis's time, the city's densest concentration of Italian tenement life. Its most famous feature was the Mulberry Bend — a sharp crook in the street that gave its name to the surrounding district and that Riis characterized as "one of the worst plague-spots" in the entire tenement landscape [025-017].

The street's geography was inseparable from its social character. "Where Mulberry Street crooks like an elbow within hail of the depravity of the Five Points" lay the Bend, a warren of alleys and rear tenements that concentrated poverty into a space Riis described with almost theatrical dread [006-002]. A census of one small tenement at the lower end of Mulberry Street enumerated a hundred Irish, thirty-eight Italian, and two German speakers [003-012] — a snapshot of the rapid ethnic succession that defined the street's history.

## The Bend

The Mulberry Bend was simultaneously a geographic feature (the curve in the street), a neighborhood (the district around it), and a symbol (of everything Riis believed was wrong with the tenement system). The Bend's physical configuration — a blind curve lined with rear tenements accessible only through narrow alleys — created what Riis called "foul courts and alleys" where "the two races, differing hopelessly" competed for space [003-012][006-002].

The street's role in the Italian immigrant experience was central. The "tramp from the Mulberry Street Bend" [013-014] functioned in Riis's narrative as an archetype of the worst elements of Italian immigration — the padrone, the exploitative landlord, the career criminal. Yet the street also housed the industrious ragpickers who had transformed New York's waste stream into a viable, if meager, economy [006-024].

## Reform and Demolition

Riis's campaign against the Mulberry Bend became a centerpiece of his broader tenement reform advocacy. He celebrated the planned demolition of the Bend to make way for what would become Mulberry Bend Park (later Columbus Park), writing: "The Mulberry Street Bend will go the way of the Five Points" [025-017]. The park's creation in 1897 — one of the earliest uses of eminent domain for the explicit purpose of slum clearance — was a direct result of the reform movement that *How the Other Half Lives* helped catalyze.
