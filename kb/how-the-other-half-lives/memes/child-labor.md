# Child Labor

## Definition

Child labor in Riis's New York encompassed both formal employment in tenement industries (cigar-making, garment work) and the informal street economy of newsboys, bootblacks, and messengers. Riis treats it as one of the most damning consequences of the tenement system: families so poor that every member, including children as young as five or six, had to work to survive. "Men, women and children work together seven days in the week," he reports of the Bohemian cigar-making tenements. (012-005)

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The tenement was both home and factory. Children worked alongside their parents in the same apartments where they slept. Riis describes a sweater's shop where a family of "man, wife, three children, and a boarder" all labored at knee-pants production. (011-012) The children's role was often the finishing work — trimming threads, turning garments, and running errands that kept the whole family production line moving.

The economic logic was brutal. An operator with four children "works on the machine for his sweater twelve hours a day, turning out three dozen 'knee-pants'...for which he received forty-two cents." (011-013) The children's labor was not supplemental — it was essential to family survival at those wages.

On the streets, children worked as newsboys, bootblacks, and messengers. Riis devotes an entire chapter (XVII, "The Street Arab") to this population of homeless and semi-homeless children. The Children's Aid Society maintained lodging-houses and schools for them, but the demand for cheap child labor in the street trades kept the system going. (017-023)

The conditions took a measurable toll. Board of Health statistics showed extraordinary infant and child mortality in the tenement districts. Riis describes "six grown people and five children" crowded into single rooms, and the inevitable spread of disease that followed. (006-017)

## The Street Trades

Beyond the tenement workshops, thousands of children earned their living on the streets of New York. Riis devoted an entire chapter to "The Street Arab," the homeless and semi-homeless boys who slept in doorways, coal chutes, and under docks, and supported themselves by selling newspapers, shining shoes, and running errands. "The Street Arab is as much of an institution in New York as Newspaper Row, to which he gravitates naturally, following his Bohemian instinct," Riis wrote. (017-002)

The Newsboys' Lodging House provided one of the few institutional lifelines for these children. A census there on any night would show "such an odd mixture of small humanity as could hardly be got together in any other spot." (017-002) The Lodging House offered not just shelter but a path to self-sufficiency: a boy could "even have the loan of the small capital required to start him in business with a bootblack's kit, or an armful of papers." (017-013) But these boys were not helpless; Riis described them as "bright and sharp as the weasel," fiercely independent and resourceful. (017-003)

The origins of these street children were almost always the tenement. One twelve-year-old urchin told Riis, "We wuz six, and we ain't got no father." (017-009) Riis observed that "there is very little to hold the boy who has never known anything but a home in a tenement" — the overcrowding and poverty simply pushed children out into the streets, where they formed gangs and subsisted by their wits. (017-009)

Girls were also drawn into the street economy, though their work was less visible. Riis described "two little girls whose father had 'busted up the house' and put them on the street after their mother died." (015-012) The Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children reported that four of eighteen "professional cracksmen" — children between nine and fifteen caught with burglars' tools — were girls, "quite as bad as the worst." (019-014)

## The Legal Framework and Its Failure

Factory legislation existed on paper to protect children. The law stipulated that "forty-five minutes at least must be allowed for dinner, and children under sixteen must not be employed unless they can read and write English; none at all under fourteen." (011-006) But these protections applied only to factories — not to the tenement workshops where the most exploitative child labor occurred. In the tenement, Riis noted, "the child works unchallenged from the day he is old enough to pull a thread." (011-006)

Riis witnessed the consequences firsthand. In a Ludlow Street sweater's shop, he observed "two young girls, not fifteen, and a boy who says unasked that he is fifteen, and lies in saying it, are at the machines sewing knickerbockers." (011-008) A "sweet-faced little Italian girl" worked as a finisher for "a dollar and a half" a week. (011-012) The garment trades notoriously absorbed child labor: Riis noted that these facts "give a slight idea of the hardships and the poor pay of a business that notoriously absorbs child-labor." (020-008)

The cash-girl system in department stores was another avenue of child exploitation. Riis recorded the case of an Elizabeth Street family where the oldest child's earnings as a cash-girl — "$2.50 a week" — was the only income for seven children during a winter when the father had been laid off. (014-028)

## Reform Efforts and the Children's Aid Society

The scale of the crisis prompted organized responses. "In thirty-seven years the Children's Aid Society, that came into existence as an emphatic protest against the tenement corruption of the young, has sheltered quite three hundred thousand outcast, homeless, and orphaned children in its lodging-houses, and has found homes in the West for seventy thousand that had none." (015-013)

The Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children, led by Elbridge T. Gerry, had "thrown its protection around more than twenty-five thousand helpless children, and has convicted nearly sixteen thousand wretches of child-beating and abuse." (015-013) Riis added that "a standing army of fifteen thousand dependent children" filled New York's asylums and institutions, giving "some idea of the crop that is garnered day by day in the tenements." (015-013)

Despite these efforts, Riis acknowledged the limits of institutional charity. The problem was structural, rooted in the tenement economy itself. "Nothing is now better understood than that the rescue of the children is the key to the problem of city poverty," he wrote, "that character may be formed where to reform it would be a hopeless task." (015-014)

## Child Mortality and the Tenement

The tenement's toll on child life was measurable in mortality statistics. The late Tenement House Commission "counted 155 deaths of children in a specimen year (1882)" in the Bend alone. (006-009) In a single tenement at 59-1/2 Mulberry Street, "fourteen persons died that year, and eleven of them were children; in No. 61 eleven, and eight of them not yet five years old." (006-009) Riis considered infant mortality "a good barometer of its general sanitary condition." (006-009)

Disease spread unchecked through the overcrowded tenements. "Under the most favorable circumstances, an epidemic, which the well-to-do can afford to make light of as a thing to be got over or avoided by reasonable care, is excessively fatal among the children of the poor, by reason of the practical impossibility of isolating the patient in a tenement." (014-020) Riis found "houses in which as many as eight little children had died in five months." (014-020)

The most harrowing cases combined poverty, illness, and child exploitation. In a Mott Street tenement, Riis described "a child dying from some unknown disease" — which the doctor diagnosed as "improper nourishment," meaning starvation — while "the whole family, father, mother, and four ragged children, sat around looking on with the stony resignation of helpless despair." (014-022) A mother in the middle of the night "got up to murder her child, who was crying for food." (014-028)

## Significance

Child labor is the moral center of Riis's argument about the tenement's effect on families. It is the clearest example of his thesis that bad housing is not merely uncomfortable but profoundly corrupting — it turns parents into exploiters of their own children and children into wage earners before they can read. His photographs of working children, which accompanied the book's original edition, were among the most influential images of the Progressive era.

Chapter XV ("The Problem of the Children") explicitly frames children as the victims of the tenement system, not its perpetrators. The chapter title's use of "problem" is deliberately ambiguous — are the children the problem, or is the society that allows them to live in such conditions? Riis's answer is unequivocal: "With such human instincts and cravings, forever unsatisfied, turned into a haunting curse; with appetite ground to keenest edge by a hunger that is never fed, the children of the poor grow up in joyless homes to lives of wearisome toil that claims them at an age when the play of their happier fellows has but just begun." (015-004)
