## Overview

Ludlow Street was, by Jacob Riis's account, one of the most densely populated streets in the most densely populated district on earth. Located in the heart of the Tenth Ward — "Jewtown" — Ludlow Street exemplified the extreme crowding that defined the Jewish immigrant quarter of the 1890s.

Riis's description emphasized vertical density: "The average five-story tenement adds a story or two to its stature in Ludlow Street and an extra building on the rear lot, and yet the sign 'To Let' is the rarest of all there" [010-003]. A seven-story tenement on the street contained thirty-six families — a term that Riis noted "has a widely different meaning here and on the avenues" [010-003].

## Sweatshop Economy

Ludlow Street was a center of the garment sweatshop system that Riis documented in Chapter XI, "The Sweaters of Jewtown." The street's tenements housed both the workers and the workshops, with sweatshop operators — "sweaters" — renting apartments where teams of Jewish immigrants sewed garments at piece rates. Riis recorded the distinctive dialect of the trade: "knee-pants" became "knerbockers" in "the Ludlow Street dialect" [011-008], a mark of the linguistic isolation that characterized the community.

A "towering double tenement in Ludlow Street, owned by a well-known Jewish liquor dealer and politician" served as one of Riis's case studies of the sweater system, where workers labored in conditions he described in grim detail [011-015]. The economics reflected the brutal logic of the trade: workers paid forty-five cents a dozen for garments that retailed for many times that amount [011-011].

## Living Conditions

The street's living conditions reflected the broader health crisis of Jewtown. The Eastern Dispensary, serving the Ludlow Street population, attributed the community's diseases "not to intemperance or immorality, but to ignorance, want of suitable food, and the foul air in which they live and work" [010-008]. A photograph captioned "A tramp's nest in Ludlow Street" showed a man sleeping curled on the ground with his feet in a box [010-005], capturing the street-level destitution that accompanied the vertical crowding above.
