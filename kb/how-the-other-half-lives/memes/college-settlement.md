# College Settlement

## The Settlement House Movement

The College Settlement was part of the settlement house movement that brought educated, middle-class volunteers — predominantly women — to live in poor neighborhoods and provide social services. Riis described it as "the 'College Settlement' of courageous girls" (024-018), pairing it with the [[neighborhood-guild|Neighborhood Guilds]] as examples of the new model of charitable work that emphasized presence over distant philanthropy.

## Riis's Assessment

Riis positioned the College Settlement within his catalog of reform efforts in Chapter XXIV, "What Has Been Done." Unlike traditional charity, which Riis often criticized as paternalistic and ineffective, the settlement model brought reformers into daily contact with the communities they served. The "courageous girls" who staffed the settlement lived in the same tenement conditions as their neighbors, giving their work an authenticity that Riis admired.

## Impact and Limitations

While Riis praised the settlement model, he also recognized its limitations. The settlement could serve a few hundred people; the tenements housed hundreds of thousands. Individual initiative, however admirable, could not substitute for systemic reform. The College Settlement was, in Riis's argument, a proof of concept — evidence that reform was possible, not a scalable solution in itself.
