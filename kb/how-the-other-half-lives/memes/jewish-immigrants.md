## Immigration Background

The Jewish immigration to New York City in the late nineteenth century was driven by persecution in Eastern Europe and shaped, in Jacob Riis's account, by the imperative of survival through thrift. Jews arrived "in droves from Eastern Europe to escape persecution, from which freedom could be bought only with gold" [010-006]. By the 1890s, an estimated twenty-five thousand Jewish immigrants arrived annually, of whom more than seventy percent remained in New York City [010-007].

The scale and concentration of this migration were unprecedented. Riis recorded that "half of them require and receive aid from the Hebrew Charities from the very start, lest they starve" [010-007]. The Hebrew Charities—a Jewish mutual-aid organization—functioned as a critical buffer between the newest arrivals and utter destitution, though Riis framed even this as evidence of a community that arrived already dependent.

The Jewish community of the 1890s was not monolithic. Riis distinguished between the "Russian Jew" and the "Polish Jew" [003-012], and the community contained significant internal stratification between earlier German-Jewish immigrants (who had arrived mid-century and were often more established) and the newer Eastern European arrivals who populated the tenements of the Tenth Ward.

The Jewish migration was fundamentally reactive to events abroad: "every fresh persecution of the Russian or Polish Jew on his native soil starts greater hordes hitherward to confound economical problems, and recruit the sweater's phalanx" [011-005]. This chain of causation—persecution driving migration, migration feeding the sweater system—was, in Riis's view, a self-perpetuating cycle that the New York economy absorbed but could not break. Riis observed that the Russian and Polish Jew had "overrun the district between Rivington and Division Streets, east of the Bowery, to the point of suffocation" and was "filling the tenements of the old Seventh Ward to the river front, and disputing with the Italian every foot of available space in the back alleys of Mulberry Street" [003-012].

## Settlement Patterns

The Tenth Ward—called "Jewtown" by Riis and his contemporaries—was, by Riis's account, the most densely populated place on earth:

> It is said that nowhere in the world are so many people crowded together on a square mile as here. The average five-story tenement adds a story or two to its stature in Ludlow Street and an extra building on the rear lot, and yet the sign "To Let" is the rarest of all there. [010-003]

The physical geography of Jewtown extended from Baxter Street—with its "interminable rows of old clothes shops and its brigades of pullers-in"—through Bayard Street "with its synagogues and its crowds," to Ludlow Street where seven-story tenements packed thirty-six families into a single building [010-002][010-003]. The area abutted [[chinatown]] to the south and the Italian districts of [[mulberry-bend]] to the west, forming a dense patchwork of immigrant enclaves.

The Jewish quarter's internal character was defined by the intersection of religious life and commerce. Synagogues among the poorest Jews "frequently consist of a scantily furnished room in a rear tenement, with a few wooden stools or benches for the congregation" [010-014]. Attached to many were Talmudic schools that absorbed a share of the community's youth, sometimes taught by "a man of some attainments who has been stranded there" [010-014]. The Hebrew letters on store signs, the "old clo'es man" with his pushcart of used clothing, and the Friday market scenes combined to create what Riis described, with characteristic ambivalence, as a "veritable Babel of confusion" in which "an English word falls upon the ear almost with a sense of shock" [010-026].

The Jewish population was expanding beyond the Tenth Ward into adjoining districts. By the time of Riis's writing, Penitentiary Row—a block of Cherry Street tenements in the Seventh Ward—had "become peopled wholly with Hebrews, the overflow from Jewtown adjoining, pedlars and tailors, all of them" [004-020]. This overflow produced nicknames from the existing population: one alley was called "Jews' Alley" [004-020]. Bayard Street served as "the high road to Jewtown across the Bowery, picketed from end to end with the outposts of Israel," its storefronts bearing "Hebrew faces, Hebrew signs, and incessant chatter in the queer lingo that passes for Hebrew on the East Side" [006-004].

On the color-coded ethnic map that Riis drew in "The Mixed Crowd," the "dull gray of the Jew, his favorite color" was "squeezed in" between "the Italian red" and "a sharp streak of yellow, marking the narrow boundaries of Chinatown" [003-012]. Chinatown, Riis wrote, had been "cheekily planted" by "Joss" "between the tabernacles of Jewry and the shrines of the Bend" [009-002], a geographic description that emphasized how tightly these communities abutted one another.

## Housing and Domestic Economy

The Jewish tenement differed from its Italian counterpart in a crucial respect: it was simultaneously a home and a workplace. "The homes of the Hebrew quarter are its workshops also," Riis wrote [010-008], naming the defining fact of tenement life in Jewtown. The tenement housed not just families but the sewing machines on which the garment trade depended, circumventing the factory-inspection law that regulated commercial workspaces but did not reach into private dwellings [011-006].

The boarder system was central to the domestic economy. "The boarder plays as important a part in the domestic economy of Jewtown as the lodger in the Mulberry Street Bend" [010-003]. With rents that Riis described as extortionate, families offset their housing costs by taking in paying boarders—sometimes "half a score" sleeping "on cots, or on the floor" [011-018]. The resulting density erased any concept of privacy: "It is idle to speak of privacy in these 'homes.' The term carries no more meaning with it than would a lecture on social ethics to an audience of Hottentots" [011-018].

The tenement itself was subject to constant physical modification. Landlords in Jewtown added extra stories and rear buildings to maximize occupancy [010-003]. In Ludlow Street, "two hallways side by side in adjoining tenements, would be sinful waste in Jewtown, when one would do as well by knocking a hole in the wall" [011-015]. Riis noted a "towering double tenement in Ludlow Street, owned by a well-known Jewish liquor dealer and politician, a triple combination that bodes ill for his tenants" [011-015]. The landlord-employer who was also a political operator represented a concentration of power over tenants that Riis found especially troubling.

Real estate ownership was the universal ambition. "The Jew runs to real estate as soon as he can save up enough for a deposit to clinch the bargain," Riis observed [004-020]. "As fast as the old houses are torn down, towering structures go up in their place, and Hebrews are found to be the builders" [004-020]. The savings of Jewtown, accumulated through the sweater system, were "universally invested" in tenement property, and "with the natural talent of its people for commercial speculation the investment is enormously profitable" [011-009].

The suspender-maker's household on Broome Street illustrated the domestic economy in microcosm. A family of six slept and worked in two rooms: the old folks in a "miserable little coop of a bedroom," the daughter on the lounge, "the big boys and the children on the floor" [011-016]. For three months of the year, when work was brisk, the family made "as high as fourteen and fifteen dollars a week" by united effort; the other nine months, "it averages from three to four dollars" [011-016]. Meat at twelve cents a pound, coal at ten cents a small pail, "milk at four and five cents a quart, 'according to quality'"—these were the items of a Jewtown housekeeping account [011-016].

Even apparent destitution was often misleading. "Yet even the direst apparent poverty in Jewtown, unless dependent on absolute lack of work, would, were the truth known, in nine cases out of ten have a silver lining in the shape of a margin in bank" [011-018]. This hidden savings—the product of the thrift that Riis both admired and condemned—distinguished the Jewish poor, in his analysis, from the improvident destitution he attributed to other immigrant groups.

## In Riis's Account

Jacob Riis devoted two chapters to the Jewish community—Chapter X, "Jewtown," and Chapter XI, "The Sweaters of Jewtown"—making the Jewish quarter the most extensively covered topic in *How the Other Half Lives* after the general tenement survey. His portrait was deeply contradictory: admiration for Jewish industriousness and family cohesion mixed with anti-Semitic tropes about money-worship and clannishness.

**Thrift as virtue and vice.** The organizing theme of Riis's Jewtown chapter was thrift, which he described in terms that captured both his respect and his revulsion:

> Thrift is the watchword of Jewtown, as of its people the world over. It is at once its strength and its fatal weakness, its cardinal virtue and its foul disgrace. Become an overmastering passion with these people who come here in droves from Eastern Europe to escape persecution, from which freedom could be bought only with gold, it has enslaved them in bondage worse than that from which they fled. Money is their God. Life itself is of little value compared with even the leanest bank account. [010-006]

The passage is remarkable for its structure: Riis began by acknowledging persecution as the context for Jewish thrift, then immediately converted that acknowledgment into condemnation. The mechanism—persecution produced thrift, thrift became obsessive, obsession became a new form of enslavement—was characteristic of his method: structural conditions were noted, then converted into moral failings.

Riis claimed to have "over and over again" encountered cases of Polish and Russian Jews "deliberately starving themselves to the point of physical exhaustion, while working night and day at a tremendous pressure to save a little money" [010-006]. These were the extreme cases, but Riis presented them as emblematic rather than exceptional. He reinforced the theme with the observation that "the world as a debtor has no credit in Jewtown" [010-003], suggesting a community in which every relationship was ultimately reducible to a balance sheet.

**Disease and the "suicide ward."** The health consequences of Jewtown's density were severe. The health officers called the Tenth the "typhus ward"; the death registry called it the "suicide ward"; and the police called it the "crooked ward" for its population of petty thieves [010-009]. The managers of the Eastern Dispensary attributed the community's diseases "not to intemperance or immorality, but to ignorance, want of suitable food, and the foul air in which they live and work" [010-008]—an unusual acknowledgment of environmental causation in a book that typically moralized illness.

"Penury and poverty are wedded everywhere to dirt and disease," Riis wrote, "and Jewtown is no exception" [010-008]. Yet he acknowledged that the Eastern Dispensary—"in the very heart of their district"—found a different explanation than the moral one Riis himself typically preferred. The Dispensary's conclusion that Jewish disease stemmed from environmental conditions rather than moral failings was one of the few moments in *How the Other Half Lives* when an authoritative institutional voice directly contradicted Riis's own interpretive framework.

**Religious life.** Riis's treatment of Jewish religious practice was shaped by Christian supersessionism. He described the synagogues and Talmudic schools in detail, but framed Jewish religious life as closed, alien, and—in the case of Christian missionary efforts in the district—impervious to the "new doctrine" of Christianity [010-013]. The Sabbath preparations that brought "latent color and picturesqueness" to the quarter on Friday [010-022] were, in Riis's telling, the one moment when Jewtown's grinding routine of industry gave way to something like the perpetual holiday atmosphere he attributed (with equal ambivalence) to the Italians.

He wrote of the East Side Jews that "in all matters pertaining to their religious life that tinges all their customs, they stand, these East Side Jews, where the new day that dawned on Calvary left them standing, stubbornly refusing to see the light" [010-013]. A visit to a Jewish house of mourning, he claimed, was "like bridging the gap of two thousand years" [010-013]. The two or three Christian missions in the district conducted what Riis called "a hopeless propagandism for the Messiah whom the Tenth Ward rejects," their crowds coming "to hear the Christian preacher as the Jews of old gathered to hear the apostles expound the new doctrine"—with results, he noted with irony, that were "often strikingly similar" [010-013].

**Comparative framing.** Riis positioned the Jewish community in relation to its neighbors in ways that revealed his ethnic hierarchy. "Hardly less aggressive than the Italian," he wrote, the Russian and Polish Jew was "disputing with the Italian every foot of available space" [003-012]. "Unlike the German, who begins learning English the day he lands as a matter of duty, or the Polish Jew, who takes it up as soon as he is able as an investment, the Italian learns slowly, if at all" [005-003]. In this single sentence, Riis ranked three immigrant groups by their relationship to the dominant language, with the Jew occupying a middle position—not as dutiful as the German, but more calculatingly rational than the Italian.

The Bohemian, Riis noted, was "unlike the Polish Jew, whose example of untiring industry he emulates" but "has seldom much laid up against a rainy day" [012-004]. Even in the matter of food economy, the Jew served as the standard of thrift: "The Polish Jew fed as big a family on one pound of meat a day" [012-007]. Across chapters, the Jewish immigrant functioned as Riis's benchmark of economic discipline against which other groups were measured and found wanting.

## Economic Role

The Jewish immigrant economy was dominated by the garment trade, which Riis examined in detail in Chapter XI, "The Sweaters of Jewtown." The sweater system operated as a hierarchy of exploitation: the manufacturer contracted with a "sweater" (a subcontractor), who hired workers—typically fellow Jewish immigrants—to sew garments in tenement apartments at piece rates. Riis recorded wages of "five cents a dozen, paying his Jewish workers from twenty to thirty cents an hour" [020-012], sums that barely sustained life even by tenement standards.

The economic structure of Jewtown was distinctive among immigrant communities:

- It was a **complete internal economy**: Jewish manufacturers employed Jewish subcontractors who employed Jewish workers, creating a system in which, as Riis noted with disapproval, "the Jew having swept the field, battle with the Jew for the field's crumbs" [010-006]
- It was **highly stratified**: the sweater might be only marginally better off than his workers, but the system created a ladder of exploitation that allowed each level to extract surplus from the level below
- It employed **women and children** extensively: Riis documented "the kindergarten at the sewing machine" and the "up-to-date sweater" who employed only workmates who could also pay rent to him as their landlord

The dancing schools that Riis observed—"generally kept by some man who works in the daytime at tailoring, cigarmaking, or something else" [010-010]—offered one of the few forms of recreation in a community where work dominated waking life. Even here, Riis noted, the young people carried "their business preferences" into their leisure [010-010].

| Economic Sector | Characteristics |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Garment sweatshops | Dominant employer; piece-rate system; tenement-based production |
| Used clothing trade | Baxter Street "old clo'es" shops; pushcart commerce |
| Small retail | Suspender pedlars, sidewalk shops, food stalls |
| Cigarmaking | Secondary trade; competed with Bohemian producers |
| Professional/skilled | Small but growing cohort of Talmudic scholars, teachers, clerks |

The sweater system was self-perpetuating in its internal logic. The sweater "knows well that the isolation of the workman in his helpless ignorance is his sure foundation, and he has done what he could—with merciless severity where he could—to smother every symptom of awakening intelligence in his slaves" [011-005]. Yet the system also generated its own competition: "The workman growls, not at the hard labor, or poor pay, but over the pennies another is coining out of his sweat, and on the first opportunity turns sweater himself, and takes his revenge by driving an even closer bargain than his rival tyrant, thus reducing his profits" [011-004]. This was the economic engine of Jewtown—a treadmill in which upward mobility for one Jewish worker came at the expense of working conditions for those he then employed.

The speed with which new arrivals entered the system was striking. "The sun sets upon the day of the arrival of many a Polish Jew, finding him at work in an East Side tenement, treading the machine and 'learning the trade'" [011-004]. Often two or three sets of sweaters worked on a single job—a subcontracting chain in miniature that pushed piece rates ever downward. A typical garment worker in a Ludlow Street shop "works on the machine for his sweater twelve hours a day, turning out three dozen 'knee-pants,' for which he receives forty-two cents a dozen" [011-013]. Meanwhile, the sweater himself might clear "as much as fifty percent at least on their labor" [011-014].

## Social Mobility and Education

Despite—and in some respects because of—the grinding conditions of the tenement economy, the Jewish community displayed patterns of social mobility that Riis found noteworthy. Education was central to this trajectory, and Riis recorded the striking academic performance of Jewish children with grudging admiration.

"As scholars, the children of the most ignorant Polish Jew keep fairly abreast of their more favored playmates, until it comes to mental arithmetic, when they leave them behind with a bound" [010-019]. Riis attributed this to an innate commercial aptitude: "It is surprising to see how strong the instinct of dollars and cents is in them. They can count, and correctly, almost before they can talk" [010-019]. The public schools of the Tenth Ward provided objective evidence of the community's demographic dominance: "when the great Jewish holidays come around every year, the public schools in the district have practically to close up" [010-002], so thoroughly had Jewish children come to constitute the student body.

The trajectory from poverty to property was well-established. A sweater's family, Riis calculated, could hoard "thirty dollars a month, and in a few years will own a tenement somewhere and profit by the example set by their landlord in rent-collecting" [011-009]. This was "the way the savings of Jewtown are universally invested," and it produced patterns of property ownership that altered the physical landscape of lower Manhattan [011-009].

The impulse to save was so deeply embedded that it defined even the most marginal forms of existence. The lodger of Jewtown could "live like a lord"—as Riis quoted him saying—"for twenty-five cents a day, including the price of his bed, that ranges all the way from thirty to forty and fifty cents a week, and save money, no matter what his earnings" [011-017]. Riis grudgingly conceded: "He does it, too, so long as work is to be had at any price, and by the standard he sets up Jewtown must abide" [011-017].

The institutional framework that supported Jewish social mobility included the United Hebrew Charities, which Riis praised as "an organization that gathers to its work the best thought and effort of many of our most public-spirited citizens" [011-019]. The Baron de Hirsch fund, which disbursed "ten thousand dollars a month for the relief of the Jewish poor in New York" [011-019], presented a philanthropic challenge that Riis observed with ambivalence: how to pour money into the community "so that it shall effect its purpose without pauperizing, and without perpetuating the problem it is sought to solve, by attracting still greater swarms" [011-019].

Trade schools for manual training had proved "most excellent" for the young under the management of the United Hebrew Charities, though they could "hardly be made to reach the adults" [011-019]. Riis identified the key obstacle—and the key remedy—as the language barrier: "the ignorance of the immigrants. They must be taught the language of the country they have chosen as their home, as the first and most necessary step" [011-019]. This was a rare moment in which Riis offered a concrete, structural prescription rather than a moral judgment: "That done, it may well be that the case in its new aspect will not be nearly so hard to deal with" [011-019].

## Daily Life: Food, Markets, and the Sabbath

The rhythms of daily life in Jewtown revolved around the intersection of religious observance and market commerce. The week's commercial climax came on Thursday night and Friday morning, "bargain days in the 'Pig-market'"—so named "probably in derision, for pork is the one ware that is not on sale" [010-022]. The Pig-market stretched along Hester Street from Ludlow, "extending either way...and up and down the side streets two or three blocks, as the state of trade demands" [010-022]. Its wares included "bandanas and tin cups at two cents, peaches at a cent a quart, 'damaged' eggs for a song, hats for a quarter, and spectacles, warranted to suit the eye, at the optician's who has opened shop on a Hester Street doorstep, for thirty five cents" [010-022]. Frowsy chickens and half-plucked geese, "hung by the neck and protesting with wildly strutting feet even in death against the outrage," were the "great staple" [010-022]. Half or quarter chickens could be purchased by those who could not afford a whole bird.

Kashrut observance shaped both commerce and public health. It took "more than ten years of persistent effort on the part of the sanitary authorities to drive the trade in live fowl from the streets to the fowl-market on Gouverneur Slip, where the killing is now done according to Jewish rite by priests detailed for the purpose by the chief rabbi" [010-022]. This reform, hard-won, produced its own controversies: "Since then they have had a characteristic rumpus, that involved the entire Jewish community, over the fees for killing and the mode of collecting them" [010-022].

Food culture in Jewtown was distinctive. "Pickles are favorite food in Jewtown" [011-012], Riis observed, noting the ready availability of cheap, preserved food that required no cooking in tenement kitchens that doubled as workshops. Restaurants at the lower end of Orchard Street catered to Polish Jews "who remember the injunction that the ox that treadeth out the corn shall not be muzzled" [011-017]—a biblical warrant for eating well. The competitive dynamic was fierce: one restaurant offered "soup, meat-stew, bread, pie, pickles, and a 'schooner' of beer for thirteen cents," while its rival charged fifteen cents for the same but with "two schooners of beer and a cigar, or a cigarette, as the extra inducement" [011-017]. The thirteen-cent establishment was winning the price war and "about to spread out into the adjoining store to accommodate the crowds of customers" [011-017].

The Sabbath transformed Jewtown. From Friday evening until sunset on Saturday, "the orthodox Jew can do no work of any sort" [021-009], creating a vacuum that was filled by a peculiar figure: the "scrub," a female tramp-beggar who was "willing to work at least one day in the week, generally the Jewish Sabbath" [021-009]. The scrub performed the labor that Jewish residents could not, receiving "a pittance" that "buys her rum for at least two days of the week at one of the neighborhood 'morgues'" [021-009]. There were "distilleries in Jewtown, or just across its borders, that depend almost wholly on her custom" [021-009]. When one Hester Street distillery was raided due to complaints of "boisterous hilarity," thirty-two aged scrubs were marched to the station-house [021-009].

The contrast between Jewtown's weekday industry and Friday's transformation was one of the few moments of color Riis allowed the quarter. Friday, he noted, "brings out all the latent color and picturesqueness of the Italians, as of these Semites" [010-022], uniting the two communities in a shared aesthetic release from the workweek. "A common pulse beats in the quarters of the Polish Jews and in the Mulberry Bend, though they have little else in common" [010-022]. The crowds and "the common poverty" were "the bonds of sympathy between them" [010-022].

The street commerce of Jewtown had its own vocabulary. "Old coats are hawked for fifty cents, 'as good as new,' and 'pants'—there are no trousers in Jewtown, only pants—at anything that can be got" [010-022]. The suspender—a staple of the Jewish peddling trade—was both ubiquitous and mysteriously absent: "Millions of suspenders thus perambulate Jewtown all day on a sort of dress parade," yet "the 'pants' of Jewtown hang down with a common accord, as if they had never known the support of suspenders" [010-025]. Riis closed his chapter on Jewtown with a half-comic observation that captured the opacity he projected onto the Jewish quarter: "No one has ever been able to tell me what becomes of the suspenders of Jewtown" [010-025].
