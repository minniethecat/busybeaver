# Five Points

## Description

Five Points was the original American urban slum — the district that gave New York its notoriety for tenement misery decades before Riis's book. Named for the five-cornered intersection of Anthony, Cross, Orange, and Little Water Streets (today's Worth, Mosco, Baxter, and Park), Five Points was built on filled-in marshland over the Collect Pond. The unstable ground and poor drainage made the area undesirable from the start, and by the 1830s it was the most densely populated, poorest neighborhood in the city.

## Ethnic Succession and Migration

Five Points was the archetype of the ethnic succession pattern that Riis would later observe across the entire city. The neighborhood passed through successive waves of settlement: old-stock Americans, then Irish immigrants fleeing the famine, followed by Italians, and later by Chinese immigrants. Riis described this process in visual terms, writing that "a map of the city, colored to designate nationalities, would show more stripes than on the skin of a zebra, and more colors than any rainbow" (003-011). The Five Points was one of the earliest and most dramatic sites of this kaleidoscopic shift.

The African American presence in Five Points was violently terminated by the Civil War draft riots of 1863. A church in Mulberry Street, "built originally for the worship of staid New Yorkers of the 'old stock,' it was engulfed by the colored tide, when the draft-riots drove the negroes out of reach of Cherry Street and the Five Points" (003-011). Within a decade, the Italian advance wave had reached the same church, and "today the arms of United Italy adorn its front" (003-011). The Italians who displaced the African American population were, in Riis's view, part of a larger pattern: "Little Italy already rivals its parent, the 'Bend,' in foulness" (003-012), demonstrating that displacement alone, without housing reform, simply transplanted the slum.

By Riis's time, the Chinese had established themselves in the Six Ward as well, though he noted with characteristic irony that "withal the police give the Chinese the name of being the 'quietest people down there,' meaning in the notoriously turbulent Sixth Ward; and they are" (009-017). The Italian immigrant, by contrast, was "welcomed as a tenant who 'makes less trouble' than the contentious Irishman or the order-loving German, that is to say: is content to live in a pigsty and submits to robbery at the hands of the rent-collector without murmur" (005-002). The ethnic succession of Five Points was never a story of upward mobility — it was a story of one desperate group replacing another in the same exploited conditions.

## In Riis's Account

Riis references Five Points as historical background — the slum that preceded and was eventually absorbed by the larger tenement crisis. The tenement system that Riis documents in the 1880s and 1890s "crept up from the Fourth Ward slums and the Five Points the whole length of the island." (002-008) The Five Points was the origin point, the first cluster of overcrowded tenement blocks from which the crisis radiated outward.

By Riis's time, much of Five Points had been demolished or absorbed into the expanding commercial district, but its legacy shaped the geography of poverty he documented. The rear tenement, the cellar dwelling, the damp basement apartment — all the building types Riis condemns — were perfected in Five Points before spreading to the Bend, Cherry Street, and beyond.

The proximity of Five Points to the Bend was a continuing source of worry. Riis described Mulberry Street where it "crooks like an elbow within hail of the old depravity of the Five Points" as the site of "the Bend, foul core of New York's slums" (006-002). The contagion of slum conditions was not contained by the demolition of individual buildings; instead, "the Five Points had been cleansed, as far as the immediate neighborhood was concerned, but the Mulberry Street Bend was fast outdoing it in foulness not a stone's throw away, and new centres of corruption were continually springing up and getting the upper hand whenever vigilance was relaxed for ever so short a time" (002-003).

## Gangs and Political Corruption

The Sixth Ward, encompassing Five Points, was infamous for its gangs, which Riis described as "the ripe fruit of tenement-house growth" (019-003). The most notorious was "the infamous Whyo Gang until a few years ago absorbed the worst depravity of the Bend and what is left of the Five Points" (019-014). The gang was "finally broken up when its leader was hanged for murder after a life of uninterrupted and unavenged crimes, the recital of which made his father confessor turn pale" (019-014). As Riis explained, "the great Whyo had been a 'power in the ward,' handy at carrying elections for the party or faction that happened to stand in need of his services and was willing to pay for them in money or in kind" (019-014). The gang system was not separate from the political system — it was an extension of it.

Even after the Whyos were broken up by the hanging of their leader Driscoll, "the exploits of the Paradise Park Gang in the way of highway robbery showed last summer that the embers of the scattered Whyo Gang, upon the wreck of which it grew, were smouldering still" (019-015). The gang was an institution bred by the tenement itself: "every corner has its gang, not always on the best of terms with the rivals in the next block, but all with a common programme: defiance of law and order" (019-003). Riis noted that "his boy occasionally shows, as a pickpocket, the results of his training with the toughs of the Sixth Ward slums" (005-008), tracing a direct line from neighborhood conditions to criminal career. The Sixth Ward tenement did not merely house poverty — it manufactured criminality.

## The Mission and Institutional Response

Five Points was not only the original slum — it was also the site of the earliest organized efforts to combat slum conditions through charitable institutions. "The Five Points Mission and the Five Points House of Industry have accomplished what no machinery of government availed to do" (016-009), Riis wrote, crediting these private charities with achievements that public authorities had failed to match. He described them as "two powerful agents that were among the pioneers in this work of moral and physical regeneration" that "stand in Paradise Park today as milestones on the rocky, uphill road" (016-009).

The House of Industry operated a nursery, industrial training programs, and residential facilities for children. "The House of Industry is an enormous nursery-school with an average of more than four hundred day scholars and constant boarders — 'outsiders' and 'insiders.' Its influence is felt for many blocks around in that crowded part of the city" (016-009). Riis was particularly moved by the scene of the nursery at bedtime: "it is one of the most touching sights in the world to see a score of babies, rescued from homes of brutality and desolation, where no other blessing than a drunken curse was ever heard, saying their prayers in the nursery at bedtime" (016-009). The House of Industry's reach extended to the orphan train movement: one boy Riis saw departing for Michigan "had been an 'Insider' at the Five Points House of Industry, and brought along as his only baggage a padlocked and iron-bound box that contained all his wealth, two little white mice of the friendliest disposition" (017-021). The missions at Five Points established a model — private charity filling the void left by public neglect — that would characterize American poverty relief throughout the Gilded Age.

## The Five Points Transformation

The eventual demolition of Five Points and its replacement by Paradise Park was one of the few clear victories Riis could point to. "Another Paradise Park will take its place and let in sunlight and air to work such transformation as at the Five Points, around the corner of the next block" (006-002), he wrote of Mulberry Bend, holding up Five Points as proof that slum clearance could succeed. The transformation from America's most notorious slum to a public park demonstrated that the built environment — not the character of the residents — determined neighborhood conditions. Yet Riis was also aware that clearance alone was insufficient: "whenever vigilance was relaxed for ever so short a time, new centres of corruption were continually springing up" (002-003).

The Five Points lesson was bittersweet: slums could be demolished, but unless the underlying housing market was reformed, new ones would simply appear elsewhere. Writing in his final chapter, Riis was hopeful but cautious: "the Mulberry Street Bend will go the way of the Five Points when all the red tape that binds the hands of municipal effort has been unwound" (025-017). The transformation of Five Points was a demonstration that change was possible, but also a warning that demolition alone — without sustained reform of the tenement system — was only a temporary fix.

## Significance

Five Points is historically significant as the template for American slum formation. Every pattern Riis documents — ethnic succession, landlord exploitation, political machine corruption, sanitary neglect — was first observed in Five Points. Charles Dickens's visit in 1842 produced one of the most famous accounts of the district, and by the time Riis wrote, Five Points was already a legendary name. Riis's contribution was to show that the conditions Dickens found incredible in 1842 had, by 1890, spread across the entire island.

The district's significance extends beyond its notoriety. Five Points demonstrated the causal relationship between physical environment and social outcomes — a central thesis of Riis's entire project. The gang, the political machine, the exploitative landlord, the overburdened charity, and the immigrant family trapped in a cycle of poverty: all these archetypes of Gilded Age urban life were formed in Five Points before they became general features of the American city. As Riis warned, "in fifty years they have crept up from the Fourth Ward slums and the Five Points the whole length of the island, and have polluted the Annexed District to the Westchester line" (002-008). What began in Five Points did not stay in Five Points — it became the defining condition of New York.
