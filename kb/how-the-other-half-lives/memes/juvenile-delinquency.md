# Juvenile Delinquency

## The Tenement's Children

Juvenile delinquency was, in Riis's analysis, not a moral failing of individual children but a structural product of the tenement environment. Children who grew up in overcrowded single-room apartments spent their days on the streets, where they learned survival skills that shaded easily into criminal behavior. Riis documented the progression: truancy led to vagrancy, vagrancy to petty theft, petty theft to the reformatory — and the reformatory, far from rehabilitating its inmates, often served as a finishing school for adult criminals.

## The Gerry Society's Work

The primary institutional response to juvenile delinquency was the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children, led by [[elbridge-t-gerry|Elbridge T. Gerry]]. The "Gerry Society" intervened in cases of child abuse and neglect, but its approach — removing children from tenement homes and placing them in institutions — was a blunt instrument. Riis acknowledged the necessity of the Society's work while also recognizing that no institution could substitute for the stable home that tenement conditions systematically destroyed.
