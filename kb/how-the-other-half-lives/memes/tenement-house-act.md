# Tenement House Act

## Definition

The Tenement House Acts were a series of New York State laws that established minimum standards for tenement construction and sanitation. The three landmark acts — 1867, 1879, and 1901 — represent the legal framework within which Riis's entire reform argument operates. The 1867 act was the first to define the tenement in law, requiring fire escapes and one toilet per twenty occupants. The 1879 act mandated that every room have access to outside air, which inadvertently produced the notorious "dumbbell" design. The 1901 "New Law" act, passed after Riis's book, required much more generous light courts and effectively ended the old-law tenement era.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The legal definition of a tenement emerged from this legislative process. Riis quotes it directly:

> "The law defines it as a house 'occupied by three or more families, living independently and doing their cooking on the premises; or by more than two families on a floor, so living and cooking and having a common right in the halls, stairways, yards, etc.'" (002-007)

The acts gave sanitary inspectors their authority. Riis notes the asymmetry of enforcement: new buildings were subject to sanitary oversight, but existing structures were largely exempt:

> "Over the new houses sanitary law exercises full control. But the old are untouched while they stand." (002-004)

This grandfathering of substandard buildings was the central failure Riis identified in the legislative approach. The law could regulate what landlords built tomorrow but could not fix what they had built yesterday. The result was that tens of thousands of families continued to live in conditions the law had already declared unfit.

## Significance

The Tenement House Act saga is the legal backbone of Riis's narrative. Every horror he documents — the dark bedrooms, the overflowing privies, the fire traps — was known to legislators who had passed laws against them. The gap between law on the books and conditions on the ground is Riis's central indictment.

In his concluding chapter, Riis proposes a three-point reform program, all of which assume stronger legislation: demolish the worst slums, enforce sanitary standards on the rest, and encourage model tenement construction. (025-016) The 1901 act, passed a decade after the book's publication, incorporated many of these recommendations and became the template for American housing codes for generations.
