# Crime

Crime runs through *How the Other Half Lives* as both fact and framework — a measurable consequence of tenement conditions and a moral argument for reform. Riis consistently portrayed criminal behavior not as individual depravity but as the predictable output of an environment that "fostered" and "shielded" it (018-009), a position that aligned him with Progressive-era environmental determinism.

## Environmental Determinism

Riis's framework was explicitly environmental: crime was "a moral distemper" that found "its most fertile soil" in the tenements (021-007). The 1863 Draft Riots were cited as historical proof that the tenement districts were "nurseries of crime" that revealed themselves "by immediate and active participation in the operations of the mob" during moments of civil disorder (P04-002).

This environmental view underwrote Riis's reform argument: fix the tenements, and you would reduce crime. The logic was direct — remove the "nurseries" and the criminal crop would diminish. This was consistent with the broader Progressive belief that housing reform was crime prevention.

## Nurseries of Crime

Riis identified specific tenement institutions as what the chief of the Secret Police called "nurseries of crime" (008-002). The cheap lodging-houses, where men slept in rows on the floor for a few cents, were singled out by Inspector Thomas Byrnes as having produced at least four hundred arrests for petty crimes in two or three years — "in many cases it was their first step in crime" (008-004). The tenements themselves were "stamped as nurseries of crime" because they provided "the sort of crime that feeds especially on idleness and lies ready to the hand of fatal opportunity" (008-002).

Cherry Street was emblematic: "One cannot take many steps in Cherry Street without encountering some relic of past or present prominence in the ways of crime" (004-017). The back-alleys like Bottle Alley and Bandits' Roost were especially notorious as breeding grounds for criminal activity.

## Juvenile Crime

Juvenile delinquency was a particular concern. Riis noted that "juvenile crime increased fearfully year by year" (001-011), a trend he attributed to tenement conditions. The progression from street child to criminal was described as a pipeline: homeless boys, having "never known anything but a home in a tenement," were easily drawn into the "wild life in the streets" until — "very soon" — they found "a place in the police books" (017-009).

The gang member's first serious crime was typically robbing a "lush" — a drunken man asleep in a hallway (019-008). From there, the half-grown boy could advance to more serious offenses, with the gang providing both social structure and cover. The Whyo gang, led by the infamous McGloin, was cited as the archetype, finally broken only when its leader was hanged for murder "after a life of uninterrupted and unavenged crimes" (019-014).

## The Gang System

Riis described the gang as a ladder of criminal advancement. The young tough began by robbing copper-bottom wash-boilers and progressed to his first real robbery — typically a "lush," a drunken man found asleep in a hallway. This first venture "moves him up into full fellowship at once. He is no longer a 'kid,' though his years may be few, but a tough with the rest" (019-008).

The gang operated with territorial precision. By day they loafed in corner-groggeries; by night they plundered stores along the avenues or lay in wait at the river for unsteady feet. "The man who is sober and minds his own business they seldom molest, unless he be a stranger inquiring his way, or a policeman and the gang twenty against the one" (019-015). The river figured prominently in their operations — "Floaters come ashore every now and then with pockets turned inside out" (019-015). When pursued by police on the water, the gang members had a ready escape: "they simply upset the boat. They swim, one and all, like real rats; the lost plunder can be recovered at leisure the next day by diving or grappling" (019-015).

The scale of the problem was documented in police statistics. Of the 82,200 persons arrested in 1889, 10,505 were under twenty years old. The Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children enumerated eighteen "professional cracksmen" between nine and fifteen years old who had been caught with burglars' tools or in the act of robbery. Four of them, "hardly yet in long trousers, had 'held up' a wayfarer in the public street and robbed him of $73" (019-014). One sixteen-year-old "was the leader of a noted gang of young robbers in Forty-ninth Street. He committed murder, for which he is now serving a term of nineteen years in State's Prison" (019-014).

## The Chinese Exception

Riis's treatment of crime in Chinatown departed from his environmental framework. Chinese crime was portrayed as inscrutable and organized — "the difficulty of tracing the motive of the crime and the murderer is extreme, and it is the rarest of all results that the police get on the track of either" (009-016). The Chinese community was said to "rise up as one man to shield the criminal" (009-016), a characterization that reinforced racial stereotypes rather than the environmental analysis Riis applied to European immigrant neighborhoods.
