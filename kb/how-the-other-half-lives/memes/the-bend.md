# The Bend

## Description

The Bend was the most famous slum in Gilded Age New York — a dense cluster of tenement blocks near Mulberry Street in the Sixth Ward, known to every police officer, sanitary inspector, and reformer in the city. Riis uses the Bend as the setting for some of his most vivid reportage: the stale-beer dive raid (Chapter VII), the sanitary policeman's beat (Chapter VI), and the street life of the Italian colony. The name "the Bend" came from the curve in Mulberry Street where it bent eastward, and the neighborhood's geography — narrow alleys, blind courtyards, and buildings wedged into every available foot of land — embodied the physical logic of the tenement.

### Geography

Riis locates the Bend precisely: "Where Mulberry Street crooks like an elbow within hail of the old depravity of the Five Points, is 'the Bend,' foul core of New York's slums." (006-002) The district proper lay in the block between Bayard, Park, Mulberry, and Baxter Streets. (006-009) Long before it became a slum, cows coming home from pasture trod a path over the hill; by Riis's time, "echoes of tinkling bells" proclaimed only "the homecoming of the ragpicker's cart." (006-002) The whole district was "a maze of narrow, often unsuspected passageways — necessarily, for there is scarce a lot that has not two, three, or four tenements upon it, swarming with unwholesome crowds." (006-003) Riis declares: "There is but one 'Bend' in the world, and it is enough." (006-002)

The Bend's borders touched several distinct neighborhoods. Bayard Street formed "the high road to Jewtown across the Bowery, picketed from end to end with the outposts of Israel." (006-004) The Italian element was "trailing across Chatham Street from their stronghold in 'the Bend' in ever increasing numbers" into the Fourth Ward. (004-013) Little Italy itself "already rivals its parent, the 'Bend,' in foulness." (003-012)

## In Riis's Account

Riis opens Chapter VI with a description of the Bend as the archetypal tenement district. It was "the foul core of New York's slums" where every social problem the book documents converged: overcrowding, disease, crime, and ethnic conflict. The sanitary policeman "locates the bulk of his work" in the Bend. (006-026) The stale-beer dives — the lowest form of drinking establishment — were "the home-product of the slums, the stale-beer dive, is known about 'the Bend'" as if it were the neighborhood's signature export. (007-011)

The racial geography of the Bend was complex. Italians occupied the worst housing, but the district also contained Irish, Chinese, and Black residents, each in distinct sub-neighborhoods. Riis's description of the street life — "crowds of half-naked children tumble in the street" — captures the density that made the Bend both vibrant and lethal. (011-020)

The Bend was also the site of reform efforts. Riis describes the sanitary police's midnight inspections and the ongoing struggle between landlords, politicians, and reformers. The district's visibility — every New Yorker knew the name "the Bend" — made it both a source of civic shame and a target for reform.

### The Italian Colony

By 1890 the Bend had become overwhelmingly Italian. Three-fourths of the population of the "Bloody Sixth" Ward had been Irish only twenty years earlier, but "the inrush of the Italian tide" had since transformed it completely. (006-003) When the sun shone, "the entire population seeks the street, carrying on its household work, its bargaining, its lovemaking on street or sidewalk." (006-004) Women sat in rows along the curb haggling over "baskets of frowsy weeds, some sort of salad probably, stale tomatoes, and oranges not above suspicion," using ash-barrels as counters. (006-004) The street hosted a perpetual market: "Hucksters and pedlars' carts make two rows of booths in the street itself, and along the houses is still another." (006-004) The women did "all the carrying, all the work one sees going on in 'the Bend,'" while the men "sit or stand in the streets, on trucks, or in the open doors of the saloons smoking black clay pipes, talking and gesticulating as if forever on the point of coming to blows." (006-007)

Red bandanas and yellow kerchiefs were everywhere, as were "the ristorantes of innumerable Pasquales; half of the people in 'the Bend' are christened Pasquale, or get the name in some other way." (006-008) The neighborhood included "steamship agencies, employment offices, and savings-banks, all in one" — the "banks" that hung out shingles on every hand. (006-008)

Within this colony violence was endemic. "No Sunday has passed in New York since 'the Bend' became a suburb of Naples without one or more of these murderous affrays coming to the notice of the police." (005-007) The Italian padrone system had its headquarters in "the Mulberry Street Bend," where "these vile dens flourish and gather about them all the wrecks, the utterly wretched, the hopelessly lost, on the lowest slope of depraved humanity." (005-008)

### The Stale-Beer Dives

The Bend was the undisputed capital of the stale-beer dive, "the real home of trampdom, just as Fourteenth Street is its limit." (007-012) These establishments, euphemistically called "two-cent restaurants," operated in cellars opening onto back alleys. (007-011) The beer was "collected from the kegs put on the sidewalk by the saloonkeeper to await the brewer's cart, and is touched up with drugs to put a froth on it." (007-011) The privilege to "sit all night on a chair, or sleep on a table, or in a barrel" went with each round of drinks. (007-011) Riis notes that "the meanest thief is infinitely above the stale-beer level. Once upon that plane there is no escape. To sink below it is impossible; no one ever rose from it." (007-011)

Riis accompanied the police on a midnight raid through the Bend's hidden alleys. Officers groped "in single file through the narrow rift between slimy walls to the tenements in the rear," stumbling over tramps asleep in the passage. (007-002) In one cellar they found "a room perhaps a dozen feet square" covered with "a brown crust that, touched with the end of a club, came off in shuddering showers of crawling bugs." (007-004) Grouped around a beer-keg, "a foul and ragged host of men and women" drank from tomato cans. (007-004) In four small rooms, seventy-five tramps were arrested; across the whole operation, two hundred and seventy-five were jammed into cells. (007-008, 007-007) In one dive they found "a woman, a mother with a newborn babe on a heap of dirty straw." (007-007)

The business was brutally profitable. One former dive-keeper became "an extensive manufacturer of macaroni, the owner of several big tenements and other real estate; and the capital, it is said, has all come out of his old business." (007-012) As Riis put it, "a stale-beer trust on a Mulberry Street scale may yet be among the possibilities." (007-012)

### Mortality and Public Health

The Bend's death toll was staggering. The Tenement House Commission counted 155 deaths of children in a single year (1882) in the block between Bayard, Park, Mulberry, and Baxter Streets. (006-009) Child deaths were 68.28 percent of total mortality in the block, compared to 46.20 percent for the whole city. (006-009) In one tenement, No. 59-1/2 next to Bandits' Roost, fourteen persons died that year, eleven of them children; at No. 61, eleven died, eight of them not yet five years old. (006-009) There were five baby funerals in No. 59-1/2 in 1888 alone. (006-009)

The sanitary police patrolled the Bend through the long summer days, "scattering disinfectants in streets and lanes, in sinks and cellars, and hidden hovels where the tramp burrows." (006-026) From midnight until the small hours, the policeman's "thundering rap on closed doors" and command of "Apri port'!" rang through the tenements as he gathered evidence of overcrowding. (006-026) Riis describes one raid where, in a room not thirteen feet either way, "twelve men and women slept, two or three in bunks set in a sort of alcove, the rest on the floor." (006-026) Most of the men were lodgers paying five cents a spot. (006-026)

Against this grim backdrop, the contrast with the model tenement at Nos. 48 and 50 was stark. The model tenement housed 161 people "under good management, and in decent quarters" — yet the hearse called only twice that year, once for a baby. (006-012) Riis notes the paradox: "The agent of the Christian people who built that tenement will tell you that Italians are good tenants, while the owner of the alley will oppose every order to put his property in repair with the claim that they are the worst of a bad lot. Both are right, from their different standpoints. It is the standpoint that makes the difference — and the tenant." (006-012)

### The Tenant-Landlord Struggle

The Bend was notorious not only for its living conditions but for the identities of its property owners. Riis exposed the fact that tenement property in the Bend, "all of it notorious for years as the vilest and worst to be found anywhere," stood associated on the tax-books "with the name of an honored family, one of the 'oldest and best,' rich in possessions and in influence, and high in the councils of the city's government." (006-015) He adds that this was "not the only instance by very many" that had "come near to making the name of landlord as odious in New York as it has become in Ireland." (006-015)

The overcrowding debate pitted the official sanitarian against the reform agitator. The former estimated four or five hundred overcrowded tenements; the latter insisted on thirty-two thousand. (006-025) "It depends on the angle from which one sees it which is right." (006-025) Under pressure from Italian immigration, the breathing-space standard had been cut from six to four hundred cubic feet per adult — the health officers' plea being "the needs of the situation," which Riis found "no more perfect argument" for the reformer's position. (006-025)

### The Street Arab and the Tramp

The Bend was "the home of the tramp as well as the ragpicker." (006-002) Its vast homeless crowds, whose "instinct to shun the light" meant they could hardly be counted, sheltered in its hidden byways. (006-026) The census found that in the Bend district between Broadway and the Bowery, of 4,367 apartments only nine were vacant. (006-026) Nearly a hundred and fifty lodgers were driven out of two adjoining Mulberry Street tenements — one aptly named "the House of Blazes" — during that census. (006-026)

On hot summer nights, hallways were occupied by rows of "sitters" — tramps who had not earned enough for a stale-beer dive fee and "have their reasons for declining the hospitality of the police station lodging-rooms." (007-013) The woman tramp predominated in summer, while men "take to the railroad track and to camping out when the nights grow warm, returning in the fall to prey on the city." (007-013) Riis observed: "Like a foul loadstone, 'the Bend' attracts and brings them back, no matter how far they have wandered. For next to idleness the tramp loves rum; next to rum stale beer, its equivalent of the gutter." (007-013)

The Bend's famous alleys included Bandits' Roost and Bottle Alley. At Bandits' Roost, even the hallways had been converted into shops — "three feet wide by four deep, they have just room for one, the shopkeeper, who, himself within, does his business outside." (006-007) Bottle Alley, around the corner in Baxter Street, was "a fair specimen of its kind, wherever found" — every house filled with "piles of rags, of malodorous bones and musty paper." (006-016)

### Connections to Other Neighborhoods

The Bend was not an isolated pocket but connected to a network of slum districts. "A common pulse beats in the quarters of the Polish Jews and in the Mulberry Bend, though they have little else in common." (010-022) The boarder played "as important a part in the domestic economy of Jewtown as the lodger in the Mulberry Street Bend." (010-003) The observer who crossed from the Bend into Jewtown was struck by the contrast: the Jew "coops himself up in his den with the thermometer at stewing heat," while the Italian "seeks the street" when the sun shines. (006-004, 010-008)

The infamous Whyo Gang had, until a few years before Riis wrote, "absorbed the worst depravity of the Bend and what is left of the Five Points." (019-014) The Mulberry Street Bend was described as "scarce dirtier than Little Italy in Harlem," though a priest working in the Bend observed that "inherent purity revolts instinctively from the naked brutality of vice as seen in the slums" — "the one gleam of hope in an otherwise hopeless desert." (014-005, 014-006)

## Significance

The Bend functions in Riis's book as the symbolic center of the tenement world. While individual chapters document specific neighborhoods (Jewtown, Chinatown, the Italian colony), the Bend is where all these strands converge. It is the negative reference point against which every reform proposal is measured: if you can fix the Bend, you can fix the tenement problem. The district was eventually demolished and replaced by Mulberry Bend Park (now Columbus Park) in 1897 — one of the few direct, tangible victories of the reform movement Riis helped launch.

Riis himself closes his book with the conviction that the Bend would be swept away: "The Mulberry Street Bend will go the way of the Five Points when all the red tape that binds the hands of municipal effort has been unwound." (025-017) Earlier, he had noted that the city authorities, "moved by the angry protests of ten years of sanitary reform effort, have decided that it is too much and must come down. Another Paradise Park will take its place and let in sunlight and air to work such transformation as at the Five Points, around the corner of the next block." (006-002) This prediction proved correct, and the transformation of the Bend into a public park became one of the most celebrated outcomes of the Progressive Era housing reform movement — the model tenement in the Bend standing as proof that "the change in the character" of a population could follow directly from improvement in its physical environment. (024-016)
