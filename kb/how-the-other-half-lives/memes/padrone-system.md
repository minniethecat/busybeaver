The padrone system was the labor recruitment and control mechanism that structured Italian immigrant employment in Riis's New York. The padrone — whom Riis called "the 'banker,' nothing else" [005-003] — functioned as a labor broker who connected Italian immigrants with employers, charging fees for his services and maintaining control through debt and language barriers.

## Definition

The padrone system was a form of labor intermediation in which a single figure — the padrone — recruited Italian immigrant workers, contracted their labor to employers in bulk, and extracted a commission from every wage earned. The padrone combined the roles of employer, creditor, translator, and landlord, creating a web of dependency from which the immigrant worker could not easily escape. Riis characterized the system as a uniquely Italian form of exploitation, distinct from the garment-trade sweaters who exploited Jewish labor or the subcontracting systems used in other immigrant trades.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

Riis described the padrone's pitch: Italian laborers were promised "princely wages" [005-003] to lure them into the system. Upon arrival, the immigrant worker discovered that the padrone controlled every aspect of his economic life — where he worked, what he was paid, where he lived, and whom he could communicate with. The padrone's power rested on the immigrant's isolation: no English, no local contacts, no knowledge of prevailing wages or legal rights.

The system concentrated Italian workers in specific occupations — construction, excavation, and street work — where the padrone could contract with employers for gang labor. This concentration made Italian immigrants highly visible in the city's manual labor force while simultaneously isolating them from other paths to employment and advancement.

Riis viewed the padrone system with particular contempt because it exploited the most vulnerable of the new immigrants. He noted that "it was left to the genius of the padrone to develop the full resources" [005-006] of immigrant exploitation, suggesting that the system had been refined over time to extract maximum profit from Italian labor while providing minimum return to the workers themselves.

## Significance

The padrone system represented, for Riis, the darkest side of laissez-faire immigration policy. Without regulation, without oversight, and without any mechanism for newly arrived immigrants to learn their rights, the free market in immigrant labor became a rigged system in which the labor broker held all the cards. The padrone was not an anomaly but the logical product of a system that treated immigrant labor as a commodity to be bought, sold, and consumed.

The system also illustrated the layered nature of tenement exploitation. The padrone was often himself an Italian immigrant who had risen through the ranks — a pattern that complicated simple narratives of native-born Americans exploiting newcomers. The exploitation was intra-ethnic, driven by the same information asymmetries and power imbalances that structured the tenement housing market.
