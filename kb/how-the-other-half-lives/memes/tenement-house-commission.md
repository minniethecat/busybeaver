The Tenement House Commission was a government investigative body that documented conditions in New York's tenement districts during the 1880s, providing reformers like Riis with the statistical ammunition they needed to argue for legislative action. The Commission's findings — especially its mortality statistics for tenement children — became central evidence in Riis's case for housing reform. As a government body, the Commission represented the beginning of official acknowledgment that tenement conditions were a public problem requiring public intervention.

## Mortality Findings

The Commission's most damning finding was its child mortality count. Riis reported that "the late Tenement House Commission counted 155 deaths of children in a specimen year (1882)" [006-009] in the block between Bayard, Park, Mulberry, and Baxter Streets — "the Bend" proper, the most notorious slum in the city. In a single city block, in a single year, 155 children died. The statistic was deployed by Riis as an indictment of the entire tenement system: these deaths were not natural or inevitable but the direct result of overcrowding, poor sanitation, and landlord neglect.

## Sanitary Survey Methods

The Commission's investigative approach combined quantitative mortality data with qualitative sanitary inspections. Its inspectors visited tenement buildings to assess ventilation, plumbing, crowding, and structural condition — documenting not just the number of deaths but the environmental conditions that produced them. This dual approach, pairing death statistics with physical inspections, created a methodology that later investigative bodies — including the Tenement House Commission of 1900 — would adopt and refine.

The Commission's reports were among the first official documents to map mortality to specific buildings and blocks, creating a geography of death that made the pattern of tenement mortality visible to policymakers. A tenement on a given block might have a death rate three or four times that of a building on the next block — a variance that could not be explained by the character of the residents but only by the physical conditions of the building itself. This finding — that mortality followed buildings, not populations — was the factual foundation for the argument that tenement reform was an environmental, not a moral, project.

## The 1889 Declaration

Five years before Riis published *How the Other Half Lives*, the Commission had delivered an official verdict that Riis quoted as validating his entire project: "the condition of the tenement houses in New York is worse than that of any other city in the civilized world" [024-017]. That this assessment came from a government body, not a muckraking journalist, gave it institutional weight. Riis cited it to demonstrate that his own reporting was not sensationalist exaggeration but the factual confirmation of what official investigators had already established.

## Legacy

The Tenement House Commission's work bridged the gap between early sanitary reform and the comprehensive housing legislation of the early 20th century. Its reports created a paper trail of official concern that later reformers could cite, building on precedent to argue for more aggressive regulation. The Commission model — a temporary investigative body with fact-finding powers — would be repeated by subsequent reform efforts, including the Tenement House Commission of 1900 that led directly to the Tenement House Act of 1901.

The Commission also established an important precedent: government bodies could — and should — investigate housing conditions as a matter of public health, not merely as questions of private property. This principle, that the state had a legitimate interest in how landlords maintained their buildings, was contested throughout the Gilded Age but became settled law through the regulatory frameworks that the Commission's work helped to justify.
