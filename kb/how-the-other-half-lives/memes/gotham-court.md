# Gotham Court

## Description

Gotham Court was a tenement block on Cherry Street in the Fourth Ward — a double row of five-story tenements built back-to-back under a common roof with a narrow alley between them. Riis describes the policeman "always on duty in Gotham Court, half a stone's throw away" from the neighboring tenements, implying that the Court required permanent police presence. (004-013) Built by a speculator named Silas, "the builder of Gotham Court," the building was a monument to the profit motive in tenement construction. (004-015)

## In Riis's Account

Gotham Court appears in Chapter IV ("The Downtown Back-Alleys") as the definitive example of failed tenement reform. The building was originally constructed as a model tenement — one of the first attempts to build decent housing for the poor:

"It was originally built as a model tenement, but speedily came to rival the worst slums in the city." (024-009)

The failure of Gotham Court was structural. The building's design — double row, back-to-back, common roof — guaranteed inadequate light and ventilation regardless of intentions. Within a few years of construction, a sanitary official counted 146 cases of sickness in the building. (004-014) The model tenement had become a death trap.

Riis uses Gotham Court to illustrate his argument that good intentions without good regulation were insufficient. The builder may have intended a model tenement, but the market incentives — maximize rentable space, minimize maintenance — pushed the building toward slum conditions regardless.

## Significance

Gotham Court is the most important single building in Riis's narrative. It appears in multiple chapters and serves as his go-to example of reform failure. The building's trajectory — from model tenement to notorious slum — encapsulates Riis's entire argument: the tenement problem cannot be solved by private charity or individual good intentions. Only systemic regulation, enforced by law, can break the cycle.
