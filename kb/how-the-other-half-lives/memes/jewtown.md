# Jewtown

## Description

"Jewtown" — the Tenth Ward, roughly between Division, Houston, and Clinton Streets — was the most densely populated place on earth in the 1890s. Riis reports that the Tenth Ward housed over 300,000 people per square mile in some blocks, a density that exceeded Bombay and rivaled the worst quarters of London's East End. The population was overwhelmingly Eastern European Jewish, with smaller clusters of Germans, Poles, and other groups. The housing stock was the notorious "double-decker" tenement — five or six stories, built to lot-line, with minimal light and air.

## In Riis's Account

Chapters X ("Jewtown") and XI ("The Sweaters of Jewtown") document the Tenth Ward in exhaustive detail. Riis describes the garment industry that dominated the district's economy: "The bulk of the sweater's work is done in the tenements" (011-006). Women and children worked alongside men at sewing machines that ran twelve hours a day, the sound of machinery filling the street from "every open window of the big tenements" (011-007).

The extraordinary density produced extraordinary commercial vitality. Riis describes streets lined with pushcarts, basement shops, and impromptu markets. The Jewish tenement economy was the most sophisticated of any ethnic group Riis documented — a complex web of manufacturers, sweaters, workers, and retailers that kept money circulating within the district even as it kept workers in poverty.

## Religious Life

Religious observance was woven through every aspect of Jewtown life. Bayard Street, with "its synagogues and its crowds," was a landmark of the quarter (010-002). Among the poorest Jews, a synagogue could be "a scantily furnished room in a rear tenement, with a few wooden stools or benches for the congregation" (010-014). Attached to many of these makeshift synagogues were Talmudic schools "that absorb a share of the growing youth," ensuring that religious instruction continued despite material poverty (010-014).

The most striking marker of Jewish religious distinctiveness was the reversal of the Christian Sabbath. On Sundays, while the rest of New York rested, "under the rule of Mosaic law, the week of work is under full headway, its first day far spent" (011-007). The hucksters' wagons stood idle only because Jewish law, not Christian custom, had halted them the day before. A tin sign advertising a "Sunday School" for "stray children of the new dispensation" was "beyond these things there is little to suggest the Christian Sabbath" (011-007).

Riis found Jewish mourning rites particularly moving: "A visit to a Jewish house of mourning is like bridging the gap of two thousand years. The inexpressibly sad and sorrowful wail for the dead, as it swells and rises in the hush of all sounds of life, comes back from the ages like a mournful echo of the voice of Rachel 'weeping for her children and refusing to be comforted'" (010-013).

## Domestic Economy: Boarders and Marriage

The boarder system was as fundamental to Jewtown's domestic economy as the lodger was to the Mulberry Street Bend: "The boarder plays as important a part in the domestic economy of Jewtown as the lodger in the Mulberry Street Bend" (010-003). Taking in boarders was how families made rent, and the presence of strangers in overcrowded apartments was a structural feature of tenement life rather than an exceptional circumstance.

Marriage carried its own economic imperatives. Riis noted that a Jewish suspender-maker "will hardly be able to save up the dot for his daughter, without which she stands no chance of marrying in Jewtown" (011-016). The dowry was an economic prerequisite for marriage, linking the district's family structure directly to its labor economy.

## Food and Diet

The diet of Jewtown reflected both religious observance and extreme poverty. Pickles occupied a special place: "Pickles are favorite food in Jewtown. They are filling, and keep the children from crying with hunger" (011-012). The practical function of the pickle — as a hunger suppressant for children — captured the intersection of cultural tradition and material deprivation.

The kosher meat supply generated its own community-wide conflict. It took "more than ten years of persistent effort on the part of the sanitary authorities to drive the trade in live fowl from the streets to the fowl-market on Gouverneur Slip, where the killing is now done according to Jewish rite by priests detailed for the purpose by the chief rabbi" (010-022). Even after this reform, the community was convulsed by "a characteristic rumpus, that involved the entire Jewish community, over the fees for killing and the mode of collecting them" (010-022).

## The Hester Street Market

The Hester Street open-air market — known ironically as the "Pig-market" since pork was the one commodity conspicuously absent — was Jewtown's commercial heart. Riis catalogued its offerings: "Bandanas and tin cups at two cents, peaches at a cent a quart, 'damaged' eggs for a song, hats for a quarter, and spectacles, warranted to suit the eye, at the optician's who has opened shop on a Hester Street doorstep, for thirty five cents; frowsy-looking chickens and half-plucked geese, hung by the neck and protesting with wildly strutting feet even in death against the outrage, are the great staple of the market" (010-022).

## Recreation: The Dancing-Schools

Despite the crushing work schedules, Jewtown had a vibrant social life centered on dancing-schools. "The young people in Jewtown are inordinately fond of dancing, and after their day's hard work will flock to these 'schools' for a night's recreation" (010-010). These dancing-schools were "scattered among the synagogues, often keeping them company in the same tenement" — sacred and profane sharing the same overcrowded building (010-010).

## Significance

Jewtown is the most densely documented individual district in Riis's book, occupying two full chapters. It represents the peak of his method — combining statistical data, personal observation, and dramatic narrative to create a total portrait of a community. The Tenth Ward was also the district where the labor argument and the housing argument most clearly intersected: the tenement was the workplace, and the workplace was the tenement.
