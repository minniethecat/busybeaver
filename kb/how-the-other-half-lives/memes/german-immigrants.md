## Immigration Background

German immigration to New York City represented one of the oldest and largest immigrant streams of the nineteenth century. By the 1890s, the German community was well established, forming what contemporaries called *Kleindeutschland* (Little Germany) on the Lower East Side. Unlike the [[Italian Immigrants|Italians]] or [[Jewish Immigrants|Russian Jews]], who were arriving in accelerating numbers during Riis's time, German immigration had peaked earlier in the century, and the German tenement population in 1890 was more settled, more assimilated, and—in Riis's estimation—more respectable than many of the newer arrivals.

Riis recorded a moment of demographic transition in one Mulberry Street tenement block, where census data showed "two that spoke the German tongue" among a population dominated by a hundred Irish and thirty-eight Italian residents [003-002]. The Germans, once the dominant immigrant group in many tenement districts, were being displaced by newer waves from southern and eastern Europe.

## Settlement Patterns

German settlement in New York centered on the East Side, particularly the area below Houston Street and stretching along Second Avenue. The Seventh Ward housed a significant German population, and Riis documented one family there as "honest, hardworking Germans, scrupulously neat, but poor"—nine people living in two rooms on a rent of seven dollars and a half [004-028].

The German presence in the tenements was distinctive for its domestic character. Unlike the [[Bohemian Immigrants|Bohemians]], whose homes doubled as cigar factories, or the Italians, whose ragpicking blurred the line between residence and waste-processing, German tenement households were noteworthy for their efforts to maintain standards of cleanliness and order despite crushing poverty. The "solid German population" that Riis observed on the East Side [012-003] represented a community that had been shaped by decades of tenement life, with established churches, benevolent societies, and mutual-aid organizations that newer immigrant groups were only beginning to develop.

## In Riis's Account

The Germans occupy an unusual position in *How the Other Half Lives*. Unlike the Italians, Chinese, Jews, Irish, and Bohemians—each of whom received a dedicated chapter—the Germans appear primarily as a point of comparison, a reference group against which other immigrant communities are measured. This relative invisibility is itself significant: the Germans were, in Riis's framework, the "old immigrants" who had already begun the process of assimilation and upward mobility that the newer groups had yet to achieve.

Riis's most sustained portrait of German tenement life appears in his account of the Seventh Ward family:

> There were nine in the family: husband, wife, an aged grandmother, and six children; honest, hardworking Germans, scrupulously neat, but poor. All nine lived in two rooms, one about ten feet square that served as parlor, bedroom, and eating-room, the other a small hall-room made into a kitchen. [004-028]

The adjectives are carefully chosen: "honest, hardworking, scrupulously neat"—the German family represented, in Riis's taxonomy, the deserving poor. This contrasts sharply with his portrayals of the "swarthy Italian" [005-008] or the Chinese in their "gamut of passion" [009-001].

In Chapter XIV, "The Common Herd," Riis offered what amounts to his thesis on German tenement character:

> The German has an advantage over his Celtic neighbor in his strong love for flowers, which accompanied him from the fatherland. But the Irish are fond of flowers too, and the geranium is the one touch of nature that is to be found in nearly every tenement home. [014-015]

The passage is revealing: Riis credited the German with a "strong love for flowers" as a mark of civilization, but immediately acknowledged that the Irish shared the same trait. The distinction served less to describe an actual cultural difference than to establish a rhetorical hierarchy in which Germans occupied a rung above the Irish on the ladder of tenement respectability.

## Economic Role

By the 1890s, German immigrants in New York occupied a broader economic spectrum than the newer immigrant groups. While many remained in the tenement districts, working as laborers and in skilled trades, a significant number had moved into small business ownership, the skilled crafts, and the lower rungs of the professions. The German community supported a network of institutions—churches, schools, singing societies, and *Turnvereine* (gymnastic clubs)—that reflected a level of economic stability beyond the reach of most Italian or Bohemian tenement dwellers.

In the tenement economy specifically, Germans appeared in Riis's account primarily as garment workers and skilled laborers, competing with and sometimes employing members of newer immigrant groups. The wage statistics that Riis cited in Chapter XXI showed Germans at eight percent of the tenement workforce [021-010]—a smaller share than their proportion in the city's overall population, reflecting their gradual movement out of the tenement districts and into more prosperous neighborhoods.
