# Charity Organization Society

## Overview

The Charity Organization Society (COS) was founded in 1882 to coordinate and rationalize New York's chaotic charitable sector. Before the COS, hundreds of independent charities operated without coordination, leading to duplication, fraud, and the "indiscriminate almsgiving" that reformers blamed for creating a permanent pauper class. The COS introduced systematic case investigation, cross-charity coordination, and the famous distinction between the "deserving" and "undeserving" poor.

The New York COS was modeled on the London Charity Organisation Society, established in 1869, which pioneered the "scientific charity" movement on both sides of the Atlantic (021-002). The movement's central tenet was that charity must be based on careful investigation of each applicant's circumstances rather than on sentimental impulse. By the time Riis published *How the Other Half Lives* in 1890, the COS had accumulated eight years of systematic data on poverty in New York, making it the most comprehensive statistical authority on the subject available to any reformer.

## In Riis's Account

The COS is Riis's single most important source of quantitative data. His statistics on poverty — 135,595 families registered as asking or receiving charity in eight years, 5,169 cases classified into four categories — come directly from COS investigations (021-002). The COS's finding that 17% of relief applicants were "unworthy" (professional beggars), 52.2% needed work rather than relief, and only 6.4% were "worthy of continuous relief" structures Riis's entire argument about the difference between charity and reform.

Riis emphasized that the COS figures were conservative: "To be well within the limits, the Society's statisticians allow only three and a half to the family, instead of the four and a half that are accepted as the standard" (021-002). Even with this undercount, the data indicated that "in a population of a million and a half, very nearly, if not quite, half a million persons were driven, or chose, to beg for food, or to accept it in charity at some period of the eight years" (021-002). The COS estimated that "eighty-five percent of the registry are still within, or lingering upon, the borders of dependence" (021-002), underscoring the chronic nature of tenement poverty.

The COS's philosophy — that charity should be "scientific," based on investigation rather than sentiment — aligned with Riis's own method. Both believed that documenting poverty was the first step toward eliminating it.

## The COS Classification System

The COS's most influential contribution to Riis's argument was its four-part classification of relief applicants, based on the investigation of 5,169 cases in a single year (021-002). The breakdown revealed that only 327 cases (6.4%) were "worthy of continuous relief" — the orphans, cripples, and very aged whom "true charity accounts it a gain to care for" (021-003). Another 1,269 (24.4%) were "worthy of temporary relief," people who "needed just a lift to start them on the road of independence, or of permanent pauperism, according to the wisdom with which the lever was applied" (021-003).

The largest category — 2,698 cases, or 52.2% — were those "in need of work, rather than relief" (021-002). These were able-bodied people whose poverty stemmed not from moral failing but from the absence of employment. This finding was central to Riis's thesis that charity, no matter how well organized, could never substitute for economic opportunity. The final category — 875 cases, or 17% — were "unworthy of relief," professional beggars whom Riis described as "a veritable 'tribe of Ishmael,' tightening its grip on society as the years pass" (021-003).

Riis noted that "almost precisely the same results followed a similar investigation in Boston" (021-003), lending national credibility to the COS methodology and suggesting that New York's poverty pattern was structural rather than idiosyncratic.

## Geographical Reach of COS Cases

The COS's registry revealed the pervasive spread of poverty through the tenement districts. The 135,595 registered families "inhabited no fewer than 31,000 different tenements" (021-006). Riis emphasized that "at least ninety-nine percent were found in the big barracks, the rest in shanties scattered here and there" (021-006), confirming that tenement housing was the primary habitat of New York poverty. The geographical concentration was so intense that "more than a hundred and twenty pauper families have been reported from time to time as living in one such tenement" (021-006).

The COS data also revealed the contagion effect of pauperism within tenement buildings: "There are houses that have been corrupted through and through by this pestilence, until their very atmosphere breathes beggary" (021-006). This spatial analysis of poverty — mapping its concentration in specific buildings — was a methodological innovation that distinguished the COS from previous charitable record-keeping and provided ammunition for Riis's call to reform the tenements themselves rather than merely their inhabitants (021-007).

## Enforcement and the Black List

The COS was not only a data-gathering body but an active enforcer against fraudulent begging. Riis reported that "the Charity Organization Society alone, in five years, caused the taking up of 2,594 street beggars, and the arrest and conviction of 1,474 persistent offenders" (021-009). A key tool was the COS's "black list" — a registry of known fraudulent charity seekers — which Riis described as "justly dreaded only by the frauds" (021-012). A critical inquiry or a check against the black list would often reveal that claimed "sickness" was laziness, and that "destitution" was a family's "stock in trade," delivering what Riis called "a shock that for once is downright wholesome" (021-012).

Riis illustrated the necessity of the black list with numerous examples from COS case files. One family, the Masonis, were exposed as "regular pauper capitalists, able to draw their check for three thousand dollars" while begging on the streets with a story of poverty and hunger (021-008). In another case, "the tenement woke up early to the gold mine that was being worked under its roof," and before long "the stream of callers who responded to the newspaper appeal found the alley blocked by a couple of 'toughs,' who exacted toll of a silver quarter from each tearful sympathizer" (021-013). These cases demonstrated how undiscriminating charity, far from relieving suffering, actually created cottage industries of fraud within the tenements.

The COS's enforcement work complemented police efforts. While "the police report only 19 arrests for begging during the year 1889," the real scale of street mendicancy was reflected in 2,633 vagrancy charges, 947 of them against women (021-009). The COS filled the enforcement gap that the police — lacking a systematic intelligence apparatus — could not address.

## Friendly Visitation and Success Metrics

The COS's signature operational method was "friendly visitation" — sending trained volunteers into tenement homes to assess genuine need and provide moral guidance alongside material assistance. Riis celebrated the COS's record of "raising, in eight years, 4,500 families out of the rut of pauperism into proud, if modest, independence, without alms, but by a system of friendly visitation" (021-017). This approach distinguished the COS from traditional charity by making investigation and personal relationship the prerequisite for aid, rather than mere distribution of relief.

The friendly visitor was expected to become a mentor and moral guide to the family, teaching habits of thrift, cleanliness, and self-discipline. Riis endorsed this philosophy, arguing that "alms do not meet the emergency at all" and that they "frequently aggravate it, degrading and pauperizing where true help should aim at raising the sufferer to self-respect and self-dependence" (021-017). The COS's success with 4,500 families demonstrated, in Riis's view, "what can be done by well-directed effort" (021-017), and he grouped the COS's achievements with "the work of the Society for Improving the Condition of the Poor and kindred organizations along the same line" (021-017).

Yet Riis also recognized the limits of even the best-organized charity. He cited a "wise and charitable physician" who lamented that "in nine years I have known of but a single case of permanent improvement in a poor tenement family" (021-007). This sobering assessment reinforced Riis's argument that charity alone, however scientific, could not overcome the structural degradation imposed by the tenement environment.

## The $8,000,000 Question

Riis placed the COS's work within the larger context of New York's staggering charitable expenditure. He reported that "New York spends in public and private charity every year a round $8,000,000" (021-017) — an enormous sum for the era. The direct cost of "maintaining our standing army of paupers, criminals, and sick poor, by direct taxation, was last year $7,156,112.94" (022-014). In the Department of Charities and Correction's institutions alone, "the average daily census of all the prisons, hospitals, workhouses, and asylums … was about 14,000" (022-014).

Riis's argument, built on COS data, was that this massive expenditure was almost entirely palliative. "A small part of this sum intelligently invested in a great labor bureau, that would bring the seeker of work and the one with work to give together under auspices offering some degree of mutual security, would certainly repay the amount of the investment in the saving of much capital now worse than wasted" (021-017). The COS's own findings — that 52.2% of applicants needed work, not charity — supplied the empirical foundation for this proposal.

## Philosophical Foundations

The COS operated within a larger institutional landscape that Riis found deeply problematic. He noted that "Pauperdom is to blame for the unjust yoking of poverty with punishment, 'charities' with 'correction,' in our municipal ministering to the needs of the Nether Half" (022-002) — referencing the city's Department of Charities and Correction, which treated the poor and the criminal under the same administrative roof. The COS's philosophy was that any charity scheme "merely turns him into a pauper, however it may be disguised, and drowns him hopelessly in the mire out of which it proposed to pull him" (024-007). This anti-almsgiving principle — that indiscriminate charity creates paupers — was the intellectual core of the scientific charity movement.

The COS's worldview drew a sharp line between the pauper and the honestly poor. Riis articulated this distinction forcefully: "The thief is infinitely easier to deal with than the pauper, because the very fact of his being a thief presupposes some bottom to the man. Granted that it is bad, there is still something, a possible handle by which to catch him. To the pauper there is none. He is as hopeless as his own poverty" (021-007). The COS saw its mission as preventing the honestly poor — those temporarily without work — from sliding into the permanent moral degradation of pauperism.

The ultimate failure of even the best-intentioned charity, Riis argued, was that it could not reach the root cause. "The ultimate and greatest need, however, the real remedy, is to remove the cause — the tenement that was built for 'a class of whom nothing was expected'" (021-017). The COS's data, paradoxically, proved that charity could only succeed if it rendered itself unnecessary by reforming the housing conditions that produced poverty in the first place. "Tenement-house reform holds the key to the problem of pauperism in the city. We can never get rid of either the tenement or the pauper. The two will always exist together in New York. But by reforming the one, we can do more toward exterminating the other than can be done by all other means together" (021-017).

## Parallel Organizations

Riis also cited the United Hebrew Charities as a parallel model of organized, scientific charity that gathered "the best thought and effort of many of our most public-spirited citizens" (011-019). The existence of such parallel organizations demonstrated that the COS approach — coordination over chaos, investigation over sentiment — was becoming the dominant charitable paradigm across New York's diverse communities.

The Society for Improving the Condition of the Poor (SICP), another kindred organization, worked "along the same line" as the COS in applying systematic methods to poverty relief (021-017). The Children's Aid Society, founded in 1854 as "an emphatic protest against the tenement corruption of the young," had "sheltered quite three hundred thousand outcast, homeless, and orphaned children in its lodging-houses, and has found homes in the West for seventy thousand that had none" (015-013). These organizations collectively represented what Riis saw as the best of private charity — but also its inherent limits when confronting problems rooted in the physical environment of the tenements.

## The COS in Historiography

The Charity Organization Society's "scientific charity" philosophy has been subject to significant historical reappraisal. While Riis embraced the COS as an ally in his campaign for tenement reform, later historians have critiqued the COS's moralizing distinction between the "deserving" and "undeserving" poor, its hostility to government relief, and the class-bound assumptions of its "friendly visitors" — typically middle-class women entering immigrant households as moral arbiters. Nevertheless, in the context of Riis's 1890 narrative, the COS stood as the indispensable empirical foundation for the argument that poverty in New York was not a matter of individual failing but of structural conditions that demanded structural solutions.
