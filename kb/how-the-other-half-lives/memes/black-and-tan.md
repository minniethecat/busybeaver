# Black-and-Tan Saloons

The "black-and-tan" saloon was a racially integrated drinking establishment where Black and white (usually Irish) patrons mixed in the tenement districts of 1890s New York. Riis mentioned these establishments in the context of the "borderland where the white and black races meet in common debauch" (013-014) — a phrase that captured both his moral disapproval of the saloon and his unease with racial mixing. The black-and-tan saloon was, in Riis's telling, the one place where the "color line" that governed housing was temporarily suspended — though only, he insisted, at the cost of mutual degradation.
