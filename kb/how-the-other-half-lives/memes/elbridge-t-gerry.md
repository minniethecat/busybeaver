# Elbridge T. Gerry

Elbridge T. Gerry was a New York lawyer, reformer, and the founding president of the [[society-for-the-prevention-of-cruelty-to-children|Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children]] (SPCC), the first organization in the world dedicated to the legal protection of children from abuse and neglect. In Jacob Riis's account of tenement life, Gerry appears as the institutional architect of the legal machinery that fought against the exploitation and murder of the city's most vulnerable children -- the infants, waifs, and street urchins whom the tenement system ground up by the thousands.

## Leadership of the SPCC

Riis identifies Gerry as "the President of the Society charged with the execution of the law that was passed through his efforts" (016-006). The Society was the SPCC, founded in 1875 as a pioneering child-protection agency that used the courts to prosecute adults who abused, neglected, or exploited children. Gerry's legal background -- he was a lawyer by training -- equipped him for a strategy that distinguished the SPCC from earlier charitable organizations: rather than relying on moral suasion or voluntary compliance, the Society wielded the threat of prosecution and pioneered the use of habeas corpus to remove children from abusive homes.

Under Gerry's leadership, the SPCC became "what the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children is to the baby-waif" -- the first line of defense for the youngest and most helpless victims of tenement poverty (017-010). While the [[childrens-aid-society|Children's Aid Society]] focused on older street boys, providing lodging-houses and placing them in Western homes, the SPCC under Gerry concentrated on the infants and young children whom nobody else would protect. The scope of the work was staggering: "In the last fifteen years of this tireless battle for the safety of the State the intervention of the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children has been invoked for 138,891 little ones: it has thrown its protection around more than twenty-five thousand helpless children, and has convicted nearly sixteen thousand wretches of child-beating and abuse" (015-013).

## The Campaign Against Baby-Farming

Gerry's most prominent appearance in *How the Other Half Lives* comes in the chapter "Waifs of the City's Slums," where Riis quotes him at length on the practice of [[baby-farming]] -- the systematic starvation of unwanted infants for profit. The quote reveals Gerry as both a clear-eyed analyst of a gruesome criminal economy and a frustrated legislator whose reforms had been only partially effective.

The practice Gerry described was a form of child-murder dressed in the language of domestic boarding. "Baby-farms," Gerry told Riis, "are concerns by means of which persons, usually of disreputable character, eke out a living by taking two, or three, or four babies to board. They are the charges of outcasts, or illegitimate children. They feed them on sour milk, and give them paregoric to keep them quiet, until they die, when they get some young medical man without experience to sign a certificate to the [[health-department|Board of Health]] that the child died of inanition, and so the matter ends. The baby is dead, and there is no one to complain" (016-006).

Gerry had been instrumental in passing legislation to combat this trade. "The law has fought this most heinous of crimes by compelling the registry of all baby-farms," Riis explains, identifying Gerry as "the President of the Society charged with the execution of the law that was passed through his efforts" (016-006). The 1876 New York State law required the licensing and inspection of all lodging facilities for infants. However, Riis was scathing about the law's effectiveness, noting that a registration requirement for murderers-by-neglect was as useful as requiring "all persons intending murder to register their purpose with time and place of the deed under the penalty of exemplary fines" (016-006). Gerry himself appears to have shared this frustration; his detailed description of how the system was evaded -- with compliant doctors signing false death certificates -- suggests a man who understood the limits of the law he had helped to write. By the time of Riis's writing, only "a handful of baby-farms have been registered and licensed by the Board of Health with the approval of the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children in the last five years, but none of this kind" (016-006).

## Broader Enforcement and Reform Work

The SPCC's reach under Gerry extended far beyond the baby-farming trade. The Society's officers operated throughout the tenement districts and on the streets of New York, intervening wherever children were being exploited. Their methods were aggressive and confrontational, backed by Gerry's legal strategy of using the courts as instruments of child protection.

One of the Society's most significant achievements during Gerry's tenure was the destruction of the [[padrone-system|padrone system]], a form of child slave trafficking that imported Italian children to beg on American streets. Riis records that "the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children... had broken up the dreadful padrone system, a real slave trade in Italian children, who were bought of poor parents across the sea and made to beg their way on foot through France to the port whence they were shipped to this city, to be beaten and starved here by their cruel masters and sent out to beg, often after merciless mutilation to make them 'take' better with a pitying public" (021-011). The children were purchased from impoverished Italian parents and forced to beg their way to America, where they were beaten, starved, and sometimes deliberately mutilated to make them more pitiable to donors. Breaking this criminal network required not just investigation and prosecution, but the coordination of multiple agencies across international borders -- a scale of operation that demonstrated the institutional capacity Gerry had built at the SPCC.

The Society also targeted fraudulent street begging that exploited children. In one celebrated case, "the officers of Mr. Gerry's society" arrested a French schoolmaster who posed as a blind veteran, pretending to have been "blinded by a shell at the siege of Paris" while using children to solicit sympathy and donations. The man "miraculously recovered his sight when arrested and deprived of his children" (021-011). In another case, SPCC officers arrested an Italian woman begging along Madison Avenue with a "poor little wreck of a girl, whose rags and pinched face were calculated to tug hard at the purse-strings of a miser" -- only to discover upon investigation that the woman was a "pauper capitalist" with "bankbooks... able to draw their check for three thousand dollars" (021-008). The woman was fined $250. The SPCC's agents also patrolled the tenement districts, searching for children too young for street work. In the newsboys' district around [[park-row|Park Row]], "the agent of the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children finds those he thinks too young for 'business,' but does not always capture them" (017-004).

The Society's criminal prosecutions established precedents that Gerry's legal strategy depended on. One of its landmark cases was the prosecution of David Smith, the "New York Fagin," who was "convicted and sent to prison last year through the instrumentality of the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children" (008-005). Smith had taken a fourteen-year-old runaway boy, trained him to steal, and then -- when the boy proved an inept thief -- deliberately burned his arms with a hot iron and applied acid to the wounds, forcing him to beg with the fabricated story of an industrial accident. The case established that the state could and would intervene against adults who exploited children for criminal purposes, even when the children were not their own.

## Legacy and Significance in Riis's Narrative

In Riis's portrait of tenement New York, Gerry occupies a distinctive position: he is the institutional reformer who fights the system's worst abuses from within the legal framework, complementing the work of direct-service charities like the [[sister-irene|Foundling Asylum]] and the [[childrens-aid-society|Children's Aid Society]]. His approach -- law-making, prosecution, and enforcement -- represented a departure from earlier models of charity that had relied on voluntary compliance. By treating child abuse as a crime rather than a misfortune, Gerry's SPCC transformed the legal status of children from property to persons with rights that the state had an obligation to protect.

Yet Riis's account also reveals the limits of Gerry's approach. The baby-farm registration law that Gerry championed proved largely ineffective against the clandestine operators it was designed to stop. "The devil keeps the only complete register to be found anywhere," Riis concluded grimly (016-006). The tenement system was so vast, so anonymous, and so economically driven that even the most aggressive enforcement strategy could only reach a fraction of its victims. Gerry's work was necessary but not sufficient -- a point that Riis made by arguing that "tenement-house reform holds the key to the problem of pauperism in the city" (021-017). The SPCC could rescue individual children, but it could not change the housing conditions, wages, and social disorganization that produced a steady supply of new victims. Gerry's legacy, as Riis presents it, was the creation of a legal framework for child protection that would require generations of additional reform to fulfill its promise.
