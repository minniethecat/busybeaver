## Overview

The Bohemian Quarter was a district on Manhattan's Upper East Side settled by immigrants from Bohemia (present-day Czech Republic). It was distinctive among immigrant quarters for its concentration of tenement-house cigarmaking, a trade that Jacob Riis documented extensively in *How the Other Half Lives*.

## Location and Character

The Bohemian quarter lay on the East Side, stretching for about a mile and a half along the avenues. As Riis observed, "scarce a Bohemian is to be found, until" one reached its boundaries (012-004). The quarter served as the setting for Chapter XII of Riis's study, which examined the intersection of immigrant labor, housing, and exploitation under the sweating system.

## Tenement-House Cigarmaking

The Bohemian quarter was the center of tenement-house cigarmaking, an industry in which entire families worked together in their apartments. Riis described one Bohemian cigarmaker producing four thousand cigars a week at $3.75, supporting a large family (012-007). Another Bohemian with six children worked alongside them at the bench (012-012). In the Bohemian quarter, cigarmaking was "made the vehicle for enforcing the misery" of poverty (012-002).

## Political and Religious Character

The Bohemian community included a notable anarchist and freethinking element. Riis recorded one Bohemian cigarmaker who was "an Anarchist" and "an infidel" — a political identity that Riis viewed with the suspicion characteristic of his era (012-013). This radicalism distinguished the Bohemian quarter from more politically conservative immigrant neighborhoods.

## Living Conditions

The Bohemian quarter, like all East Side tenement districts, suffered from overcrowding, poor sanitation, and the exploitation of the sweating system. Riis noted that Bohemians followed "his Bohemian instinct" toward natural clustering, crowded out of more desirable quarters (017-002).
