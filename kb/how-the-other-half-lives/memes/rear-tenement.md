# Rear Tenement

## Definition

The *rear tenement* was the first form of purpose-built tenement housing in New York. Unlike the later "double-decker" and dumbbell tenements that filled entire lots, the rear tenement was built behind an existing house, in what had been the backyard or garden:

> "The first tenement New York knew bore the mark of Cain from its birth, though a generation passed before the writing was deciphered. It was the 'rear house,' infamous ever after in our city's history." (001-002)

The name captured both its physical placement — at the rear of the lot — and its social status as the hidden face of the city. Where the street-front houses presented a respectable facade, the rear tenement was invisible from the street, accessible only by narrow alleys.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

Rear tenements were built opportunistically by individual homeowners. Riis describes the process:

> "In its beginning, the tenant-house became a real blessing to that class of industrious poor whose small earnings limited their expenses." (001-003)

But the design was inherently flawed. Cut off from the street, rear tenements shared a single narrow alley with the building in front. Light, air, and escape routes were minimal. A fire in a Mott Street rear house left ten families homeless and illustrated the lethal consequences of this design. (001-006)

Riis documented a rear tenement on Roosevelt Street with a photograph captioned simply "An old rear-tenement in Roosevelt Street" — the image showing a weather-beaten wooden structure wedged between brick buildings, with no visible windows on the street-facing side. (004-025)

By the time Riis wrote, the rear tenement was being replaced by larger, purpose-built tenement blocks (the famous "double-decker"), but many still stood:

> "The double-deckers that are taking the place of the rear tenements in rapidly growing numbers are perhaps even worse." (011-006)

Conditions in the surviving rear tenements were predictably grim. Riis describes "three rear rooms on the sixth floor" as one family's cheapest apartment option. (011-015) A Bohemian family living in a rear attachment "without windows" embodied the worst of the type. (012-006)

The rear tenement was not isolated to a single neighborhood. Riis observed "crazy old buildings, crowded rear tenements in filthy yards, dark, damp, and ill-smelling" (001-011) across the city, and described a "scantily furnished room in a rear tenement, with a few wooden stools" (010-014) as typical of the immigrant poor's living conditions.

## Significance

The rear tenement is historically significant as the first link in the chain of housing degradation Riis documents. The pattern it established — exploit every square foot of the lot, maximize density, minimize light and air — became the template for all subsequent tenement construction. Riis uses the rear tenement's origin story in Chapter I to show that the housing crisis was not accidental but engineered: a logical outcome of landlords responding to market incentives with no regulatory constraint.

The rear tenement also illustrates Riis's method of using specific, named buildings to make systemic arguments. By showing readers the actual structure on Roosevelt Street rather than abstract statistics, he made the argument for reform emotionally legible.

## Historical Aftermath

The rear tenement's elimination was one of the first concrete achievements of the tenement reform movement. The Tenement House Act of 1879, known as the "Old Law," did not ban rear tenements outright, but it did require that new tenements provide windows in every habitable room — a standard that rear tenements, facing the back of a lot and cut off from street light, could not meet. The 1879 law effectively prohibited new rear tenement construction.

The Tenement House Act of 1901, the "New Law" that Riis's book helped bring about, went further: it mandated minimum court sizes and set coverage limits that made the rear tenement's extreme lot exploitation illegal. Existing rear tenements were not demolished overnight — some persisted into the 1910s and 1920s — but no new ones could be built, and the surviving ones gradually fell to demolition or condemnation.

The rear tenement's architectural legacy can be seen in the "dumbbell" tenement that replaced it: a building that filled the lot from street to rear, with narrow air shafts cut into the sides rather than a gap at the back. The dumbbell solved the legal problem (every room had a window) while preserving the economic logic (maximum lot coverage), and in doing so created its own set of problems — dark air shafts, minimal ventilation, and new fire hazards. The pattern Riis identified — each reform creating the conditions for the next distortion — would repeat throughout the history of New York housing policy.
