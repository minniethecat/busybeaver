# Absentee Landlord

## The Root of the Problem

Jacob Riis identified the absentee landlord as a primary cause of tenement degradation. Unlike the resident landlord who lived among tenants and had a stake in conditions, the absentee owner "lives in the country, has an agent who collects the rent, and hears of it in the newspapers" (024-014). The agent's only incentive was to maximize rent collection; maintenance, sanitation, and tenant welfare were someone else's problem.

## The Agency Problem

The economic structure Riis described was a classic principal-agent problem. The landlord delegated to an agent whose compensation depended on rent revenue, not building condition. Repairs that would preserve the building's long-term value were neglected because neither the agent (who would not benefit) nor the landlord (who was unaware) had an incentive to make them. Riis saw this as one of the structural reasons that the free market failed to produce decent housing for the poor.

## Reform Implications

The absentee landlord problem pointed toward one of Riis's key reform arguments: that the housing market for the poor was not a functioning market at all. When ownership was separated from responsibility by distance and delegation, the normal mechanisms of supply, demand, and quality competition broke down. Riis argued that only regulatory intervention — in the form of stronger tenement house laws and effective enforcement — could bridge the gap that absentee ownership created.
