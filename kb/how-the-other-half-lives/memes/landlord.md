# The Landlord

## Biography

The landlord in *How the Other Half Lives* is less a single person than a structural position — the property owner whose profit motive drove the tenement system. Riis distinguishes between several landlord types: the Irish immigrant who climbed the economic ladder by buying tenement property, the absentee speculator who never visited his buildings, and the "home article" who lived among his tenants and fought every reform measure personally.

He also documents the ethnic succession of property ownership. In the Irish tenement districts, the landlord was frequently Irish — "the Irish landlord" who, Riis notes with characteristic irony, had risen from tenement poverty himself. (003-006) The pattern was consistent: each ethnic group's most entrepreneurial members became landlords to the next wave of poorer immigrants.

## Role in the Tenements

Riis portrays the landlord as the central obstacle to reform. When sanitary inspectors attempted to enforce the law, "the landlord opened the door with alacrity" — and immediately claimed the violations were the tenants' fault. (006-029) The landlord's political power was substantial; ward-level politicians depended on landlord support, and the landlord could deliver or withhold tenant votes.

The absentee landlord was a particular target of Riis's anger. Unlike the resident owner who at least saw the conditions, the absentee "has done his share" — meaning his share of the damage — by extracting rent while ignoring maintenance. (024-007) The resident landlord who fought reform was worse in a different way, described as "the home article, who fights every measure of reform with the desperation of a man defending his only property." (024-014)

Riis documents the economic logic that made the landlord both victim and villain. The tenement was a rational investment given the city's housing shortage, and many small landlords were only marginally wealthier than their tenants. But the system they collectively created — "this landlord despotism" — was, in Riis's view, the primary cause of tenement misery. (013-009)

The color line was drawn and redrawn by landlords. Riis describes how the landlord "does the drawing" of racial boundaries in tenement neighborhoods, segmenting the population by ethnicity and race to maximize control and profit. (013-002)

## Multiple Perspectives

The landlord was the most vilified figure in the tenement drama, but the moral calculus shifted depending on whose perspective one adopted. To the tenement reformer — Riis included — the landlord was the structural villain of the housing crisis, a profiteer who squeezed maximal rent from minimal housing. The reform literature of the 1880s and 1890s, from the Tenement House Commission reports to the sermons of urban clergymen, cast the landlord as a moral failure, an exploiter of human misery who chose profit over decency.

To the tenants themselves, the landlord was a more immediate and personal presence. For families facing eviction, the landlord was a figure of terror — the person who could put children on the street with a marshal's warrant. Yet the small-scale immigrant landlord, who often lived in the same building as his tenants, could also be a neighbor, a creditor willing to extend a week's grace on the rent, or the only person in the building with connections to the ward boss who could find a tenant's son a job. Tenant-landlord relationships in the tenement were intimate and coercive at the same time, governed by mutual dependence as much as extraction.

To the political machine, the landlord was an indispensable ally. Landlords controlled tenant votes and delivered them to the ward boss; in exchange, the machine protected landlords from enforcement of housing codes. This landlord-politician alliance was the structural foundation of tenement corruption, and it persisted because both parties profited — the landlord avoided regulation, and the politician secured a reliable voting bloc. Reformers who attacked the landlord were, whether they realized it or not, attacking the machine's electoral base.

To economists and housing analysts of the era, the landlord was less a moral agent than a rational actor responding to market incentives. The tenement was an extraordinarily profitable investment precisely because the housing supply was constricted — the city's geography (an island with limited buildable land) and its regulatory framework (weak or unenforced building codes) combined to create a seller's market in housing. Progressive-era housing economists, including those associated with the Tenement House Committee, argued that the only lasting solution was to change the incentive structure — to make decent housing more profitable than slum housing, rather than to rely on moral suasion or sporadic enforcement.

Historians of immigration have offered yet another perspective: the immigrant landlord was a success story. For an Irish or German immigrant who arrived penniless and worked his way up to property ownership, the tenement was an achievement, not a sin. The moral condemnation of the landlord in the reform literature implicitly condemned the aspirations of the very immigrant communities it claimed to help — a tension that Riis, himself an immigrant who had risen from poverty, navigated with visible discomfort.

## Legacy

The landlord typology in Riis's book became a template for Progressive-era housing analysis. His recognition that landlords were not a monolithic class — that the small immigrant landlord, the absentee speculator, and the corporate owner faced different incentives and required different regulatory approaches — was sophisticated for its time and prefigured modern housing policy.

The "home article" landlord also complicates the book's moral narrative. Riis admits that some landlords were trapped in the same economic logic as their tenants. Reform could not simply punish landlords; it had to change the economic incentives that made slum ownership profitable.
