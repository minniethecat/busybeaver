Silas Wood was a nineteenth-century New York property owner remembered in Jacob Riis's *How the Other Half Lives* for an act of architectural vengeance. His brother John owned the lot upon which [[gotham-court|Gotham Court]] was built on Cherry Street, and a quarrel between the brothers — the cause of which Riis does not record, though the bitterness of its consequences suggests a deep and irreparable rift — led Silas to construct a tenement on the western side of the alley with the express purpose of destroying the value and livability of his brother's property.

## The Wood Brothers' Feud

Riis describes the resulting building as "a curious monument to a Quaker's revenge, a living illustration of the power of hate to perpetuate its bitter fruit beyond the grave" [004-015]. The tenement, facing the main Gotham Court building, was deliberately placed to block light and air from the original structure. According to Riis, Silas Wood's hatred outlived him: the tenement remained standing long after both brothers were dead, continuing to blight the lives of generations of tenants who had no part in the quarrel. As Riis noted, the building's very existence was "a monument to a feud between brothers that outlasted both their lives."

The story took on a particular irony given that John Wood had "sold the Cherry Street front" [004-015] in a transaction that may itself have been connected to the brothers' falling-out. The tenement that Silas built was not originally part of Gotham Court proper but stood as a separate and intentionally hostile addition — an architectural antagonist facing the main block across the narrow alley. Its presence worsened the already-deplorable conditions in the court, which by the 1880s had become synonymous with tenement squalor and had acquired the distinction of a permanent police post: "the policeman always on duty in Gotham Court, half a stone's throw away" [004-013], was a testament to the disorder bred by such overcrowded spaces.

## Gotham Court Context

Gotham Court itself was one of the most notorious tenement blocks in the Fourth Ward. Its Cherry Street frontage and the rear buildings that filled the lot behind it represented the worst features of the pre-1867 tenement era — buildings constructed before the first tenement house law imposed any meaningful regulation on light, air, or sanitation. Riis used the court repeatedly in his journalism as a symbol of everything the tenement reform movement sought to eradicate. The fact that its physical form had been shaped not by economics alone but by a personal vendetta made Gotham Court, in Riis's telling, uniquely emblematic of the moral dimension of the housing crisis.

## Legacy

The story of Silas Wood's revenge tenement exemplifies the moral dimension Riis brought to his housing journalism. For Riis, the tenement was not merely a physical structure but a carrier of human intention — and in this case, of human malice. The Wood brothers' feud, long forgotten by the time Riis documented its architectural consequences, continued to shape the lives of the impoverished families who inhabited Gotham Court. Later tenement reformers would point to buildings like Gotham Court and note, as Riis did, that "like Gotham Court, it was originally built as a" speculation [024-009] — a pattern in which the profit motive and, in this case, sheer vindictiveness combined to produce housing that was almost designed to fail.
