# Labor

## The Tenement Economy

Labor in the tenements of 1890s New York took forms that blurred the line between domestic life and industrial production. Riis documented a labor landscape where "the law that regulates factory labor does not reach" (011-006) — the tenement sweatshop, where entire families worked fourteen-hour days making garments in the same single rooms where they slept. The [[sweatshop|sweating system]] was the most visible form of tenement labor, but Riis also described cigarmakers, ragpickers, day laborers, and street vendors.

## The Economics of Desperation

Riis recognized that the labor conditions he documented were not the product of individual moral failing but of an economic system that trapped workers in poverty. "The wages of many a hard day's work are wasted" (013-011) was his description of the [[policy-game|policy game]], but the observation applied more broadly: tenement labor paid so little that even full-time work could not lift a family out of the tenement. The missionary's field for "labors beside which most others seem easy" (008-002) was, in Riis's telling, the struggle for economic survival itself.
