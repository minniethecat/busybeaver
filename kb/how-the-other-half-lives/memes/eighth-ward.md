# Eighth Ward

## The Center of Black Settlement

The Eighth Ward contained the Thompson Street neighborhood that Riis identified as the center of African-American life in 1890s New York — the area known as "Africa." In Chapter XIII, "The Color Line in New York," Riis described the ward as the domain of the "Eighth Ward barrack" (013-010), a term that conveyed the military-industrial quality of the housing stock available to Black tenants.

## Landlord Despotism

The Eighth Ward was where Riis observed the mechanics of the "color line" most clearly. Black tenants in the ward paid premium rents for inferior housing, a system enforced by landlord collusion. Riis documented how landlords used the ward's demographics to inflate rents, charging Black families substantially more than white families for comparable — or worse — accommodations. The ward was, in effect, a laboratory for the racial economics of the tenement system.

## Transition and Displacement

By the time of Riis's writing, the Eighth Ward was undergoing demographic change. Italian immigrants were pushing north into the area from the [[fourth-ward|Fourth Ward]] and the Mulberry Bend, displacing Black residents who had nowhere else to go in a city where the "color line" restricted their housing options to a handful of blocks. Riis noted that "even in the Eighth Ward, the stronghold of the colored race, the Italian is elbowing his way in" (013-014).
