# Immigration

## Definition

Immigration was the demographic engine behind New York's tenement crisis. Riis reports that in thirty-five years, "the city of less than a hundred thousand came to harbor half a million souls, for whom homes had to be found." (001-003) The war of 1812 triggered the first great wave, and successive waves from Ireland, Germany, Italy, Eastern Europe, and China continuously replenished the tenement population throughout the nineteenth century.

Riis identifies the war of 1812 as the inflection point: "It was the stir and bustle of trade, together with the tremendous immigration that followed upon the war of 1812 that dislodged them." (001-003) The pre-war city of modest wooden houses was rapidly converted to tenements as landlords subdivided old single-family homes to meet the surging demand. Each fresh wave of immigration intensified the pressure on the housing stock, creating the conditions that by 1890 had produced over thirty-seven thousand tenement houses sheltering more than twelve hundred thousand persons. (P04-003)

## Manifestation in the Tenements

Immigrants came to New York with diverse expectations but faced a common housing reality. Riis observes that even the poorest immigrant arrived with economic ambition:

> "The poorest immigrant comes here with the purpose and ambition to better himself." (003-009)

But the tenement system absorbed them all. Each new group inherited the worst housing, and each in turn was blamed for the conditions in which it lived. Riis structures his book as a tour through ethnic neighborhoods — Italians in the Bend, Chinese in Mott Street, Jews in the Tenth Ward, Bohemians on the East Side, Black New Yorkers in the West Side tenements — showing that the housing was the constant while the occupants changed.

### Ethnic Succession

A recurring pattern in Riis's account is ethnic succession: each immigrant wave occupied the lowest rung, then climbed as the next wave arrived behind it. "The once unwelcome Irishman has been followed in his turn by the Italian, the Russian Jew, and the Chinaman, and has himself taken a hand at opposition, quite as bitter and quite as ineffectual, against these later hordes." (003-003)

This succession was visible in occupational mobility as well as housing. Riis catalogs the progression:

> "The Irish hod-carrier in the second generation has become a bricklayer, if not the Alderman of his ward, while the Chinese coolie is in almost exclusive possession of the laundry business." (003-009)

The German ragpicker of thirty years before was by Riis's day "the thrifty tradesman or prosperous farmer of today," (003-008) while the Italian scavenger was "fast graduating into exclusive control of the corner fruit-stands." (003-009) Yet this ladder was not available equally to all groups; the Bohemian remained "the only foreigner with any considerable representation in the city who counts no wealthy man of his race." (003-012)

### The Ethnic Map

Riis describes the distribution of immigrant groups across the city with striking visual precision:

> "A map of the city, colored to designate nationalities, would show more stripes than on the skin of a zebra, and more colors than any rainbow." (003-011)

On such a map, the city would divide into "two great halves, green for the Irish prevailing in the West Side tenement districts, and blue for the Germans on the East Side." (003-011) Between the "dull gray of the Jew" and the "Italian red" would appear "a sharp streak of yellow, marking the narrow boundaries of Chinatown." (003-012) The Bohemian colonies appeared "in wedge-like streaks that relieve the monotony of the solid German population by their strong contrasts." (003-012)

Riis also noted the relentless pressure of new arrivals on older settlements. "Within the past decade the advance wave of the Italian onset reached" areas previously held by other groups, (003-011) and "the Russian and Polish Jew, having overrun the district between Rivington and Division Streets, east of the Bowery, to the point of suffocation, is filling the tenements of the old Seventh Ward to the river front, and disputing with the Italian every foot of available space in the back alleys of Mulberry Street." (003-012)

## The Padrone System and "Assisted" Immigration

Not all immigration was voluntary. Riis documents the exploitative padrone system that trapped Italian laborers in cycles of debt. Recent Congressional inquiries exposed "the nature of the 'assistance' he receives from greedy steamship agents and 'bankers,' who persuade him by false promises to mortgage his home, his few belongings, and his wages for months to come for a ticket to the land where plenty of work is to be had at princely wages." (005-003)

The padrone then received the immigrant at the landing and "turns him to double account as a wage-earner and a rent-payer," contracting for his lodging "in the vilest tenements at extortionate rents." (005-003) The promised princely wages vanished upon arrival: "in their place hardships and a dollar a day, beheft with the padrone's merciless mortgage, confront him." (005-003)

The padrone's reach extended across multiple industries. He "hires him out to the railroad contractor, receiving a commission from the employer as well as from the laborer, and repeats the performance monthly, or as often as he can have him dismissed." (005-003) In the city's ash-barrel trade, Italian contractors paid the city over eighty thousand dollars annually for the privilege of sorting refuse, and the consequent consolidation of power "vastly increased the power of the padrone, or his ally, the contractor, by giving him exclusive control of the one industry in which the Italian was formerly an independent 'dealer.'" (005-006)

### Comparative Language Acquisition

Riis used language learning as a barometer of immigrant ambition and integration. "Unlike the German, who begins learning English the day he lands as a matter of duty, or the Polish Jew, who takes it up as soon as he is able as an investment, the Italian learns slowly, if at all." (005-003) This linguistic isolation reinforced the padrone's grip, since the laborer had no direct access to the English-speaking economy.

## The Sweater Economy and Immigrant Labor

Immigration directly fed the sweater system, the exploitative garment manufacturing network that operated within tenement apartments. "Every shipload from German ports brings them to his door in droves, clamoring for work," Riis reports of the sweater's workforce. (011-004) A Polish Jew might arrive in the morning and be "at work in an East Side tenement, treading the machine and 'learning the trade'" by sunset. (011-004)

This perpetual supply of new immigrant labor kept wages at starvation levels. The sweater was "above all, of the hungry hordes of immigrants to whom no argument appeals save the cry for bread." (011-005) Persecution abroad amplified the crisis at home: "Every fresh persecution of the Russian or Polish Jew on his native soil starts greater hordes hitherward to confound economical problems, and recruit the sweater's phalanx." (011-005)

## Riis as Immigrant

Riis was himself a Danish immigrant who experienced tenement poverty firsthand upon arriving in New York. This autobiographical dimension gives the book its distinctive viewpoint. Riis's central argument is that immigration magnified a pre-existing housing crisis — the fault lay in the tenement system, not in the people forced to inhabit it. As he demonstrates through his exhaustive survey of ethnic neighborhoods, the housing was the constant; the occupants changed. His insistence on treating each group with specificity — with distinct chapters for Italians, Jews, Chinese, Bohemians, Germans, Irish, and Black New Yorkers — was both an acknowledgment of the ethnic segregation enforced by the tenement districts and an argument that the housing problem transcended ethnic boundaries.

The book documents at least eighteen distinct ethnic groups across the Lower East Side and beyond. These included the major populations — Irish, German, Italian, Jewish (Russian and Polish), Chinese, Bohemian, and Black American — as well as smaller communities of Finns, Greeks, Swiss, French, Spanish, Scandinavians, and Arabs. (003-002) (003-013) Each group, Riis argued, confronted the same corrupting environment, and each, given adequate housing, proved capable of improvement.

## Intergroup Relations

The tenement system bred ethnic tensions, but Riis noted that these were often manufactured by landlords and political operators. The Irish landlord who collected extortionate rents from Italians "was merely showing forth the result of the schooling he had received." (003-006) The Italian immigrant, for his part, was welcomed "as a tenant who 'makes less trouble' than the contentious Irishman or the order-loving German" — meaning "he is content to live in a pigsty and submits to robbery at the hands of the rent-collector without murmur." (005-002)

Yet Riis also recorded the ethnic competition that immigration produced. The Fourth Ward alley he surveyed contained "one hundred and forty families, one hundred Irish, thirty-eight Italian, and two that spoke the German tongue." (003-002) And strikingly, Riis noted that "the one thing you shall vainly ask for in the chief city of America is a distinctively American community." (003-002) In the entire court, "barring the agent herself, there was not a native-born individual." (003-002)

## Significance

Immigration is the demographic thread running through every chapter of *How the Other Half Lives*. Riis was himself a Danish immigrant who lived in tenement poverty when he first arrived, and his book is in part a rebuke to nativists who blamed immigrants for the slums. His argument, repeated throughout, is that immigration magnified a pre-existing housing crisis — the fault was in the tenement system, not in the people forced to live in it.

The book's ethnic geography also makes it an invaluable historical document. Riis mapped the distribution of eighteen ethnic groups across the Lower East Side and beyond, creating a snapshot of American urban demography at the peak of mass European immigration. His insistence on treating each group separately — with distinct chapters for Italians, Jews, Chinese, Bohemians, Germans, Irish, and Black New Yorkers — reflected both the segregation of the tenement districts and his belief that the housing problem transcended ethnic boundaries.

By 1890, the fifteen thousand tenement houses of a previous generation had swelled to thirty-seven thousand, sheltering over twelve hundred thousand persons. (P04-003) More than half of New York's population lived in tenements, and the vast majority of these were immigrants or their children. Riis's book was thus, at its core, a portrait of the immigrant city and an argument that America's promise depended on decent housing for all who arrived at its shores.
