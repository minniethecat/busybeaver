## Overview

Hamilton Street appears briefly but memorably in *How the Other Half Lives* as the site of "The Ship," a tenement whose name and eccentric character made it a local landmark. The street stood in a district east of the Bowery, not far from the East River waterfront, in a neighborhood that had once been closer to the water's edge before landfill and development reshaped the shoreline. Riis paused his tour of Cherry Street's alley tenements to describe this anomalous building, treating it as a curiosity that revealed something essential about the tenement world: its capacity to absorb and preserve the odd, the old, and the inexplicable.

## The Ship

Riis paused his tour of Cherry Street's alley tenements to describe a building on Hamilton Street that defied easy categorization. "The queer old building they have halted in front of is 'The Ship,' famous for fifty years as a ramshackle tenement filled with the oddest crowd" [004-021]. The name itself was unexplained: "No one knows why it is called 'The Ship'" — though Riis relayed a striking piece of local lore as the best available explanation: "there is a tradition that once the river came clear up here to Hamilton Street" [004-021].

The image was suggestive. If the tradition was accurate, Hamilton Street had once been a waterfront — or nearly so — and "The Ship" might have been an old maritime building, a former boarding house for sailors, or simply a tenement whose shape and isolation reminded residents of a vessel. Over fifty years, the building had accumulated its population as a ship accumulates barnacles, becoming "filled with the oddest crowd" — a phrase that Riis left deliberately unglossed, inviting readers to imagine the eccentrics, outcasts, and marginal characters who would inhabit a building so named.

The Ship, unlike the other alleys and courts Riis catalogued on the same tour, was not described as a site of particular misery or danger. It was simply strange — a building that did not fit the typologies of the tenement reformer's inventory. In this sense, it represented a different dimension of the pre-reform tenement world: not its capacity for suffering but its sheer unpredictability, the way unregulated construction over decades produced forms and names that defied rational analysis.

## The Retreating Waterfront

The tradition that the East River had once reached Hamilton Street reflected the physical transformation of the Lower East Side over the nineteenth century. Landfill projects had pushed the shoreline eastward, converting marsh and tidal flats into buildable lots. Streets that had once been near the water were now blocks inland, their maritime origins preserved only in fragmentary local memory. Hamilton Street exemplified this process: physically ordinary by 1890, it nonetheless retained a trace connection to the earlier geography of the island — a connection preserved not in official records but in the name of a single tenement whose residents may not have known why it was called what it was.

This transformation of the waterfront was itself a driver of tenement construction. As new land was created east of the original shoreline, developers built tenements on the freshly filled lots, creating the dense residential fabric that Riis would later document. The irony was that Hamilton Street, which local memory still associated with the water, had become entirely landlocked — its maritime past surviving only as a ghost in the name of "The Ship."

## Cherry Street Context

Hamilton Street's position near Cherry Street placed it within one of Riis's most intensively documented tenement districts. Cherry Street was, in Riis's account, a corridor of alley tenements — buildings tucked behind buildings, accessible through passages barely wide enough for a person, invisible from the street and unknown to the wider city. Hamilton Street shared in this geography of concealment, its tenements part of a hidden city that existed behind the respectable facades of the Lower East Side. The Ship, for all its eccentricity, was part of this larger pattern: a building that had survived for fifty years precisely because it was invisible, tucked away on a side street in a district the wider city preferred not to see.
