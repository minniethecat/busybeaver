The "colony" in Riis's vocabulary described the ethnic enclaves that segmented New York's immigrant districts into distinct territories. Each immigrant group established its own colony — a geographic concentration where language, customs, and institutions from the homeland were reproduced in the tenement environment. Riis mapped these colonies street by street, treating them as the organizing principle of the Lower East Side's human geography.

## Definition

In the context of *How the Other Half Lives*, a colony was a self-contained ethnic settlement within the tenement districts — bounded geographically, defined linguistically and culturally, and reproduced through chain migration. The colony was simultaneously a source of social support for newcomers and a barrier to assimilation, providing the familiar while insulating immigrants from American institutions and the English language.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

Riis described the colony as more than a residential concentration — it was the nerve center of immigrant life. Within each colony, "the real life of the colony, its gambling news" [009-016] circulated through informal networks of communication and commerce. These internal networks gave the colony its cohesion, but also made it opaque to outsiders, including reformers and public health officials.

The Bohemian colony illustrated the complexity of settlement patterns. Riis questioned whether "colony" was even "the proper name to give to the colony, for though it has distinct boundaries" [012-003] it was internally diverse. Bohemians were scattered in wedge-like bands rather than concentrated in a single block, suggesting that even Riis's own mapping vocabulary struggled to capture the fluidity of immigrant settlement.

Riis mapped a hierarchy of colonies across the Lower East Side. The German and Irish colonies were treated as established and relatively orderly; the Jewish colony was dense, industrious, but insular; the Italian colony was the most recent, poorest, and most degraded; the Chinese colony was treated as utterly foreign and dangerous. This mapping of colonies onto a civilizational scale was central to the book's argument about which immigrants could be assimilated and which could not.

## Significance

The colony concept is essential to understanding Riis's method. By organizing the immigrant districts into colonies, he transformed the chaotic diversity of the Lower East Side into a legible map — one that served both descriptive and political purposes. Descriptively, the colony framework captured real patterns of chain migration and linguistic clustering. Politically, it allowed Riis — and his readers — to rank immigrant groups according to their perceived distance from American norms, turning geography into a moral argument.

The colony also functioned as a lens on the limits of reform. Colonies that were most self-contained — the Jewish, the Chinese — were also the hardest for outside agencies to penetrate, raising questions that remain relevant about the relationship between ethnic solidarity, social isolation, and public health in immigrant communities.
