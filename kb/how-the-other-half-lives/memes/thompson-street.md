## Overview

Thompson Street was a Lower East Side thoroughfare whose reputation for vice and degradation made it emblematic of the district's worst conditions. Jacob Riis referenced it in *How the Other Half Lives* as a byword for urban moral collapse.

## Reputation

The moral turpitude of Thompson Street had been notorious for years (013-014). It stood among the most disreputable addresses in a district already known for poverty and overcrowding. Its proximity to the Bend — the infamous Mulberry Street curve — placed it at the center of what Riis described as the city's most dangerous and degraded quarter.

## Context in the Bend District

Thompson Street lay within the broader zone of the Bend, a heavily policed area characterized by rear tenements, stale-beer dives, and lodging houses that catered to the city's most desperate population. The street shared in the district's pattern of overcrowded tenements and minimal enforcement of sanitary regulations, conditions that reformers cited in their campaigns for tenement house legislation.

## Legacy

Like many Lower East Side streets of its era, Thompson Street passed through cycles of notoriety and gradual improvement as immigrant populations shifted and tenement reform took hold in the decades following Riis's documentation.
