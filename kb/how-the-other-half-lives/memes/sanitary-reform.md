# Sanitary Reform

## Definition

Sanitary reform in Riis's New York was the intersection of public health, housing regulation, and Progressive-era activism. It encompassed the efforts of health inspectors, "sanitary police," civic organizations, and individual agitators to impose minimum standards — light, air, plumbing, and cleanliness — on the tenement districts. The movement emerged from the growing recognition that epidemic disease, particularly cholera and typhus, originated in the overcrowded, unventilated tenements.

The term "sanitary reform" as Riis uses it denotes not merely the regulatory framework of the Health Department, but a broader civic awakening in which data collection, investigative journalism, and organized political pressure each played a part. The sanitary question touched every dimension of tenement life: from the depth of air shafts to the enforcement of street-cleaning, from the licensing of lodging-houses to the regulation of the milk supply that nourished the tenement's infants.

## Origins of the Sanitary Reform Movement

Riis traces the organized sanitary reform movement to a citizens' campaign that arose from the findings of the Council of Hygiene, a body of physicians and civic leaders who conducted the first systematic survey of tenement conditions. The survey's revelations of overcrowding, filth, and epidemic mortality shocked the city's conscience. A citizens' movement resulted in the organization of a Board of Health and the adoption of the Tenement-House Act of 1867, the first step toward remedial legislation. (002-002)

The Council of Hygiene itself had diagnosed the tenement problem as "the result of forgetfulness of the poor," a phrase Riis repeats with pointed irony, noting how that forgetfulness had a way of avenging itself. (002-003) The reformers' logic was straightforward: epidemic disease did not respect the boundary between rich and poor neighborhoods. As Riis puts it in his introduction, the tenements were "the hotbeds of the epidemics that carry death to rich and poor alike." (P04-004)

By 1879, a decade after the first Tenement-House Act, official reports still characterized younger criminals as "victims of low social conditions of life and unhealthy, overcrowded lodgings, brought up in an atmosphere of actual darkness, moral and physical." Riis noted the exasperation in the reformers' voice: "This after the saw had been busy in the dark corners ten years!" (002-003)

The first legislative committee ever appointed to probe the roots of poverty went "deeper down and uncovered its roots," concluding that "certain conditions and associations of human life and habitation are the prolific parents of corresponding habits and morals," and recommending "the prevention of drunkenness by providing for every man a clean and comfortable home." (P04-005) A subsequent sanitary inquiry revealed that "more than one-half of the tenements with two-thirds of their population were held by owners who made the keeping of them a business, generally a speculation." The typical owner was seeking fifteen to over thirty percent return on his investment. (P04-005)

## The Sanitary Police and Their Work

Riis documents the sanitary reform effort as an ongoing battle, not a settled victory. The sanitary police — a corps of health department inspectors — were on the front lines. Their beat was the worst housing in the city:

> "It is in 'the Bend' the sanitary policeman locates the bulk of his work." (006-026)

The conditions they confronted were staggering. Riis describes a tenement where "a sanitary official counted 146 cases of sickness" and another where "musty paper" accumulated in layers, "all of which the sanitary police flatter themselves they have scraped off." (006-016)

The sheer scale of their task was overwhelming. In the first six months of the year in which Riis was writing, the sanitary police on their midnight inspections turned 1,068 persons out of "not quite two hundred tenements below Houston Street," and this covered "only a very small part of that field." (024-013) The night inspections could not keep pace with the overcrowding; Riis observed that the sanitary police routinely found a hundred and one adults and ninety-one children in a single Crosby Street house, and the public no longer found such numbers remarkable. (002-007)

The sanitary police also patrolled the markets and streets. Riis describes the health officers' cart coming down a Jewish quarter street, "preceded and followed by stalwart policemen, who shovel up with scant ceremony the eatables—musty bread, decayed fish and stale vegetables—indifferent to the curses that are showered on them from stoops and windows, and carry them off to the dump." (010-026) Elsewhere, he notes that the health officers called the Tenth Ward "the typhus ward" and maintained "constant and sharp lookout for hidden fever-nests." (010-009, 010-008)

Some idea of what a sanitary "cleaning up" entailed in these districts could be gained from Riis's own photographic expeditions, during which the police had to beat back crowds of enraged tenants who believed the flash-powder was an explosion and attacked the party with fists and improvised weapons. (004-009)

## Gotham Court: The Test Case

No single tenement better illustrated the promise and failure of sanitary reform than Gotham Court, a block of barracks on Cherry Street that Riis called the tenement that "has challenged public attention more than any other in the whole city and tested the power of sanitary law and rule for forty years." (004-013) It was here that mortality rose during the last great cholera epidemic to the unprecedented rate of 195 in 1,000 inhabitants. (004-013)

Gotham Court had been built in 1852 as a supposedly model tenement. "How long it continued a model tenement is not on record," Riis observes dryly. "It could not have been very long, for already in 1862, ten years after it was finished, a sanitary official counted 146 cases of sickness in the court, including 'all kinds of infectious disease,' from smallpox down, and reported that of 138 children born in it in less than three years 61 had died, mostly before they were one year old." (004-014)

The Board of Health eventually compelled the owners of the worst buildings to make repairs, "under threat of driving out the tenants and locking the doors behind them." The work was accomplished "against the old man's angry protests." (004-006) This pattern — official compulsion met by landlord resistance — recurs throughout Riis's account of the sanitary reform era.

## Epidemics, Infant Mortality, and the Data of Reform

Infant mortality served the sanitary reformers as their most powerful statistical weapon. Riis quotes the accepted public-health maxim: "The infant mortality in any city or place as compared with the whole number of deaths is justly considered a good barometer of its general sanitary condition." (006-009) In the tenements, that barometer was permanently in the red.

Under the most favorable circumstances, an epidemic that the well-to-do could afford to dismiss as a manageable inconvenience was "excessively fatal among the children of the poor, by reason of the practical impossibility of isolating the patient in a tenement." (014-020) The disease that could be kept at bay in the brownstone was lethal in the barracks: "Tread it ever so lightly on the avenues, in the tenements it kills right and left." (014-020)

The Board of Health deployed fifty "summer doctors" — physicians especially trained for tenement work — who were sent into the districts with free advice and medicine for the poor. But despite fresh-air excursions and all the other palliatives, "the gravediggers in Calvary work overtime, and little coffins are stacked mountains high on the deck of the Charity Commissioners' boat when it makes its semiweekly trips to the city cemetery." (014-019)

The reformers also cited the scientific evidence on light and air. Riis mentions "an English doctor who experimented with the sunlight in the soldiers' barracks, and found that on the side that was shut off altogether from the sun the mortality was one hundred percent greater than on the light side, where its rays had free access." (004-018) This finding gave physiological weight to what the sanitary police already knew from experience: that dark bedrooms killed. (004-022)

## The Struggle Between Reformers and Property

The reformers faced constant resistance from landlords who saw sanitary laws as an attack on their property rights. One key figure Riis mentions is "the unsalaried agitator for sanitary reform" — a volunteer activist who kept public pressure on the authorities over ventilation standards. Riis frames this as a "standing quarrel between the professional—I mean now the official—sanitarian and the unsalaried agitator for sanitary reform over the question of overcrowded tenements." (006-025)

Behind this quarrel lay a fundamental tension: official sanitarian reports tended toward optimism in matters touching their jurisdiction, while the volunteer agitators accused them of complicity with landlord interests. The health officers, Riis noted elsewhere, were "professional optimists always in matters that trench upon their official jurisdiction." (008-014)

The reformers had to contend with landlords who blocked alleyways and shut out their neighbors' light and air as revenge for personal quarrels — Riis relates the story of one owner who erected "an immense blank wall right in front of the windows" of a rival's tenements, "shutting out effectually light and air from them." (004-015) Another large owner of tenement property "once undertook to fit out his houses with stationary tubs, sanitary plumbing, wood-closets, and all the latest improvements" but abandoned the effort when his tenants, unused to the fixtures, broke them. (024-010)

## Results and Limitations

By the time Riis wrote, reform had made tangible progress. New construction was subject to sanitary oversight in a way old buildings were not:

> "Over the new houses sanitary law exercises full control. But the old are untouched while they stand." (002-004)

He notes that tenements built in the last decade "of sanitary reform rule" were of markedly better quality. (014-021) A census of tenements compared with mortality tables had yielded "no little surprise and congratulation" with the discovery "that as the buildings grew taller the death-rate fell." The biggest tenements, built under sanitary reform laws, had been "brought, in all but the crowding, under its laws." (014-021)

Yet the scope of the challenge remained immense. The old houses, "that from private dwellings were made into tenements, or were run up to house the biggest crowds in defiance of every moral and physical law, can be improved by no device short of demolition." (014-021) A nighttime inspection of tenements below Houston Street by the sanitary police still turned up conditions that Riis called "a revelation of the condition of the other half." (024-013)

Riis was candid about the limits of what sanitary regulation alone could achieve. He questioned "whether all the improvements made under the sanitary regulations of recent years deserve the name." (024-003) In ten years he had seen "plague-spots disappear before its onward march, with which health officers, police, and sanitary science had struggled vainly since such struggling began as a serious business." (024-008) But the fundamental condition — overcrowding driven by poverty — resisted all regulatory remedies.

## The Path Forward

In his concluding chapter, Riis lays out the unfinished agenda of sanitary reform. It rested on three approaches: the enforcement of law, the building of model tenements, and — as a last resort — demolition. (025-013) Reform by law, he argued, "must aim at making it unprofitable to own a bad tenement." (025-017)

To remedy overcrowding, Riis proposed that tenements might eventually have to be licensed — as lodging-houses already were — to hold a fixed maximum number of tenants. More radically, he suggested that "the State may have to bring down the rents that cause the crowding, by assuming the right to regulate them as it regulates the fares on the elevated roads." (025-017) He also urged "the arrest and summary punishment of landlords, or their agents, who persistently violate law and decency." (025-017)

Riis saw tenement-house reform as the master key to every other social ill: "Tenement-house reform holds the key to the problem of pauperism in the city." (021-017) The sanitary reformer's cause was, in his final formulation, nothing less than the proposition "that the security of the one no less than of the other half demands, on sanitary, moral, and economic grounds, that it be decently housed." (025-008)

## Significance

Sanitary reform is the bureaucratic and scientific backbone of Riis's narrative. Where his photographs and prose create moral urgency, the sanitary inspectors and their reports provide the factual basis. Riis frequently cites official reports, health statistics, and police testimony — a rhetorical strategy that grounds his emotional appeals in institutional authority.

The chapter "The Awakening" (Chapter II) traces the origin of the reform impulse specifically to a sanitary survey of the tenements, arguing that data collection was the first step toward action. This reflects Riis's own method: the book itself is a kind of sanitary survey, gathering evidence across the city to make a case that no single report could.

The achievement of the sanitary reform movement, as Riis measures it, was not the eradication of tenement evils — a goal that remained distant — but the establishment of the principle that housing conditions were a matter of legitimate public concern, subject to law, inspection, and the organized pressure of an informed citizenry. The fifteen thousand tenant houses that were "the despair of the sanitarian in the past generation" had, by the time of Riis's writing, swelled to thirty-seven thousand, housing more than twelve hundred thousand persons. (P04-003) The sanitary reformer's work had not ended; it had only grown more urgent.
