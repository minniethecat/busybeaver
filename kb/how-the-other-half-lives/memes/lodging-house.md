# Lodging House

## Definition

Cheap lodging-houses were the bottom rung of New York's housing ladder — establishments charging seven to twenty-five cents per night for a bunk or a spot on the floor. Riis described the Bowery lodging-house district as a "caravansary" harboring "nightly a population as large as that of many a thriving town." (008-002) Unlike tenements, which at least implied permanent residence, lodging-houses served a transient population of tramps, newly arrived immigrants, and unemployed young men.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The scale of the lodging-house system was staggering. In one year, Riis reports, nearly five million separate lodgings were furnished by these establishments:

> "According to the police figures, 4,974,025 separate lodgings were furnished last year by the registered lodging-houses." (008-014)

The human cost was severe. Young men arriving in the city with honest hopes were drawn into the lodging-house orbit and often into crime. Inspector Byrnes, the police chief, testified:

> "At least four hundred young men have been arrested for petty crimes that originated in the lodging-houses, and in many cases it was their first step in crime." (008-004)

The seven-cent lodging-house was the lowest tier — Riis describes bunks in a Pell Street establishment where men slept in conditions worse than the street. (008-011) The twenty-five cent houses maintained a pretense of respectability but were only marginally better. (008-009)

Riis also documents the role of the lodging-house in the "street Arab" economy. Newsboys and bootblacks, many homeless, gravitated to these establishments, forming an underclass that the city's charity and reform networks struggled to reach. (017-010)

## Significance

The lodging-house serves as a kind of economic warning in Riis's narrative. If the tenement was the trap that held the working poor, the lodging-house was the pit below it — a way station between poverty and total destitution. Riis places it at the center of his chapter on "The Cheap Lodging-Houses" (Chapter VIII) because he sees it as a linchpin in the cycle of urban poverty: housing instability leads to unemployment, which leads to crime, which fills the prisons and Potter's Field.

The lodging-house also highlighted the limits of private charity and municipal regulation. Despite police licensing, the establishments flourished because the demand for cheap shelter was inexhaustible — a structural condition that no amount of enforcement could eliminate without addressing the root economic causes.
