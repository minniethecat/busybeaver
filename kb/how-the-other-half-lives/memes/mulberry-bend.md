# Mulberry Bend

Mulberry Bend was one of the most notorious slum districts in 1880s New York City, located at a sharp curve in Mulberry Street in the Lower East Side. Jacob Riis devoted extensive attention to "the Bend" in *How the Other Half Lives*, using it as the definitive example of tenement overcrowding and its social consequences. The district was bounded by Bayard, Park, Mulberry, and Baxter Streets, occupying the heart of the "Bloody Sixth" Ward (006-009). Riis described it memorably as the place where "the boundary line of the Other Half lies through the tenements" (014-002).

## The Slum Landscape

The Bend's physical configuration -- a tight curve in the street creating a hidden pocket -- made it a natural enclave for the city's poorest residents. "Where Mulberry Street crooks like an elbow within hail of the old depravity of the Five Points, is 'the Bend,' foul core of New York's slums" (006-002). The district's notorious Bottle Alley and Bandits' Roost became emblematic of urban decay in the Gilded Age. The whole district was "a maze of narrow, often unsuspected passageways -- necessarily, for there is scarce a lot that has not two, three, or four tenements upon it, swarming with unwholesome crowds" (006-003). What a birds-eye view of the Bend would be like, Riis wrote, was "a matter of bewildering conjecture" (006-003).

The Bend had deep historical roots. "Long years ago the cows coming home from the pasture trod a path over this hill" (006-002). The cow-path had been transformed into a "vast human pigsty" within living memory (006-002). Its everyday appearance from the corner of Bayard Street on a sunny day, Riis declared, was "one of the sights of New York" (006-003). Yet for all its notoriety, Riis insisted that "there is but one 'Bend' in the world, and it is enough" (006-002).

Around the Bend clustered "the bulk of the tenements that are stamped as altogether bad, even by the optimists of the Health Department" (006-002). Incessant raids could not keep down the crowds, and in "the scores of back alleys, of stable lanes and hidden byways, of which the rent collector alone can keep track, they share such shelter as the ramshackle structures afford with every kind of abomination rifled from the dumps and ash barrels of the city" (006-002). The Bend was the home of the tramp as well as the ragpicker -- "Here, too, shunning the light, skulks the unclean beast of dishonest idleness" (006-002).

## Daily Life in the Bend

Riis painted a vivid picture of street life in the Bend, where the boundary between private and public space dissolved. "All 'the Bend' must be, if not altogether, at least half out of doors when the sun shines" (006-008) -- a description that captured both the vibrant street culture and the suffocating overcrowding that drove residents outside. The Italian immigrant community transformed the streets into extensions of their homes: "half of the people in 'the Bend' are christened Pasquale, or get the name in some other way" (006-008). When the police did not know the name of an escaped murderer, Riis wryly observed, "they guess at Pasquale and send the name out on alarm; in nine cases out of ten it fits" (006-008).

The street market of the Bend was a spectacle unto itself. "Hucksters and pedlars' carts make two rows of booths in the street itself, and along the houses is still another -- a perpetual market doing a very lively trade in its own queer staples, found nowhere on American ground save in 'the Bend'" (006-004). Women sat in rows along the curb, "haggling over baskets of frowsy weeds, some sort of salad probably, stale tomatoes, and oranges not above suspicion" (006-004). Ash-barrels served as counters, and the arrival of the official refuse cart caused "a temporary suspension of trade until the barrels have been emptied and restored" (006-004).

Yet this outdoor life carried constant danger. "No Sunday has passed in New York since 'the Bend' became a suburb of Naples without one or more of these murderous affrays coming to the notice of the police" (005-007), Riis reported, linking Italian immigration to violence in a manner characteristic of his ethnic stereotypes. The surrounding streets were lined with the "vile dens" of the padrone and tramp landlord: "Where his headquarters is, in the Mulberry Street Bend, these vile dens flourish and gather about them all the wrecks, the utterly wretched, the hopelessly lost, on the lowest slope of depraved humanity" (005-008).

The gendered division of labor was stark: "The women do all the carrying, all the work one sees going on in 'the Bend.' The men sit or stand in the streets, on trucks, or in the open doors of the saloons smoking black clay pipes, talking and gesticulating as if forever on the point of coming to blows" (006-007). This observation, while colored by Riis's cultural biases, documented the real economic arrangements of immigrant families in which women's labor -- both paid and domestic -- sustained households while men faced irregular employment.

## Immigration and Ethnic Succession

By the 1880s, Mulberry Bend had become predominantly Italian, part of the larger pattern of ethnic succession in Lower East Side neighborhoods. Earlier Irish and German residents -- three-fourths of the "Bloody Sixth" Ward had been Irish just twenty years before (006-003) -- had moved upward and outward, replaced by newer Italian immigrants who faced discrimination in housing and employment. The shift was driven by "the disbandment of the armies in the field" after the Civil War, which swelled the tramp population, and the "inrush of the Italian tide" (006-003). Together these forces "opposed a stubborn barrier to all efforts at permanent improvement" (006-003).

Riis noted the formation of a distinct "Little Italy" centered on the Bend (013-008). The Italian presence was so dominant that Bayard Street -- the high road to Jewtown -- would change character the moment one turned the corner onto Mulberry: "Before him lies spread out what might better be the marketplace in some town in Southern Italy than a street in New York" (006-004). The Bend stood at the ethnic crossroads of the Lower East Side: Hebrew faces and signs attended the wanderer "to the very corner of Mulberry Street" (006-004), while Chinatown was "next-door neighbor to the Bend" (009-003).

The Italian influx did not confine itself to the Bend. Riis observed that Italians were "trailing across Chatham Street from their stronghold in 'the Bend' in ever increasing numbers, seeking, according to their wont, the lowest level" (004-013). The "tramp from the Mulberry Street Bend" (013-014) became an archetype in Riis's racial mythology, and the infamous Whyo Gang until a few years before his writing "absorbed the worst depravity of the Bend and what is left of the Five Points" (019-014).

## Mortality and Public Health

The Bend was a deathtrap. In the block between Bayard, Park, Mulberry, and Baxter Streets -- "the Bend" proper -- the late Tenement House Commission counted 155 deaths of children in a specimen year, 1882 (006-009). Their percentage of the total mortality in the block was 68.28, while for the whole city the proportion was only 46.20 (006-009). The infant mortality rate functioned, as Riis pointed out, as "a good barometer of its general sanitary condition" (006-009).

Individual tenements told an even grimmer story. In one building, No. 59 1/2 next to Bandits' Roost, fourteen persons died that year and eleven of them were children; in the adjoining No. 61, eleven died and eight were not yet five years old (006-009). According to the records in the Bureau of Vital Statistics, only thirty-nine people lived in No. 59 1/2 in the year 1888, nine of them little children, yet there were five baby funerals from that house in a single year (006-009). Out of the alley itself, No. 59, nine dead were carried in 1888, five in baby coffins (006-009).

Riis's most harrowing vignette came from a health inspector's visit on a July day when the thermometer outside was climbing into the nineties but inside, "in that awful room, with half a dozen persons washing, cooking, and sorting rags, lay the dying baby alongside the stove, where the doctor's thermometer ran up to 115 degrees" (006-021). It was a child, Riis wrote, "perishing for the want of a breath of fresh air in this city of untold charities" (006-021).

The Bend was also the site of brutal abuse. "Right here, in this tenement on the east side of the street, they found little Antonia Candia, victim of fiendish cruelty, 'covered,' says the account found in the records of the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children, 'with sores, and her hair matted with dried blood'" (006-009). As Riis put it: "Abuse is the normal condition of 'the Bend,' murder its everyday crop, with the tenants not always the criminals" (006-009).

## Crime, Vice, and the Tramp Economy

The Bend was the "real home of trampdom" (007-012) and the headquarters of the stale-beer dive, that "home-product of the slums" that went by the more dignified name of the two-cent restaurant (007-011). On hot summer nights it was "no rare experience when exploring the worst of the tenements in 'the Bend' to find the hallways occupied by rows of 'sitters,' tramps whom laziness or hard luck has prevented from earning enough by their day's 'labor' to pay the admission fee to a stale-beer dive" (007-013). Huddled together in "loathsome files," they squatted overnight or "until an inquisitive policeman breaks up the congregation with his club, which in Mulberry Street has always free swing" (007-013). Like "a foul loadstone, 'the Bend' attracts and brings them back, no matter how far they have wandered" (007-013).

The lodging economy was brutally efficient. A kerosene lamp burned dimly as twelve men and women slept "in a room not thirteen feet either way, two or three in bunks set in a sort of alcove, the rest on the floor" (006-026). Most were lodgers "who slept there for five cents a spot" (006-026). The overcrowding was so pervasive that when the sanitary police conducted a midnight census, "nearly a hundred and fifty 'lodgers' were driven out of two adjoining Mulberry Street tenements, one of them aptly named 'the House of Blazes'" (006-026).

Half a dozen blocks up Mulberry Street there was a ragpicker's settlement, "a sort of overflow from 'the Bend,' that exists today in all its pristine nastiness" (006-024). Something like forty families were packed into five old houses built to hold five, and out in the yards additional crowds were "accommodated in sheds built of all sorts of old boards and used as drying racks for the Italian tenants' 'stock'" (006-024).

## Reform and Demolition

The Bend became a focal point for tenement reform efforts. Riis's photographs and descriptions helped galvanize public opinion, leading to the demolition of the worst buildings in the 1890s. A census taken "not much more than twenty years" before Riis's writing "returned only twenty-four of the six hundred and nine tenements as in decent condition" (006-003), a statistic that underlined both the scale of the problem and the reform movement's achievement. The area was eventually converted into Mulberry Bend Park (opened 1897, now Columbus Park), authorized under the Small Parks Act of 1887, representing one of the early victories of the progressive housing reform movement. Riis wrote confidently: "The Mulberry Street Bend will go the way of the Five Points when all the red tape that binds the hands of municipal effort has been unwound" (025-017). The city authorities, "moved by the angry protests of ten years of sanitary reform effort, have decided that it is too much and must come down" (006-002).

The most powerful argument for reform came from a direct comparison within the Bend itself. In the model tenement across the way at Nos. 48 and 50, "where the same class of people live in greater swarms (161, according to the record), but under good management, and in decent quarters, the hearse called that year only twice, once for a baby" (006-012). The contrast between the model tenement and the barracks across the way was cited by Riis as one of the strongest arguments for tenement reform (024-016). The agent of the Christian people who built that tenement could accurately say "that Italians are good tenants, while the owner of the alley will oppose every order to put his property in repair with the claim that they are the worst of a bad lot" (006-012). Riis concluded: "Both are right, from their different standpoints. It is the standpoint that makes the difference -- and the tenant" (006-012).

Bottle Alley, around the corner in Baxter Street, was "a fair specimen of its kind, wherever found" (006-016). Looking into any of its houses, one saw "everywhere the same piles of rags, of malodorous bones and musty paper, all of which the sanitary police flatter themselves they have banished to the dumps and the warehouses" (006-016). A typical "flat" consisted of a "parlor" and two pitch-dark coops called bedrooms -- "Truly, the bed is all there is room for" (006-016). The family teakettle did double duty as a wash-boiler by day, returning to its proper use by night, "a practical illustration of how poverty in 'the Bend' makes both ends meet" (006-016). One, two, three beds -- "if the old boxes and heaps of foul straw can be called by that name" (006-016) -- a broken stove, a table propped on boxes, piles of rubbish in the corner. "The closeness and smell are appalling" (006-016). Eleven people slept there: "Six grown people and five children" (006-017), and the tenant paid nine and a half dollars a month.
