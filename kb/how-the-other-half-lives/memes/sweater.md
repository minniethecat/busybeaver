# Sweater

## Definition

The *sweater* in Riis's New York was a labor subcontractor who took raw materials from manufacturers and farmed out piecework to tenement families at the lowest possible wages, pocketing the difference. The term encompassed both the middleman and the system he operated — what contemporaries called "the sweating system." The sweater did not himself employ workers in a factory; instead, he exploited the isolation of tenement workers to prevent collective bargaining:

> "The sweater knows well that the isolation of the workman in his home puts him at a hopeless disadvantage." (011-005)

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The sweater's work pervaded the tenement districts, particularly the Jewish quarter. Riis describes walking through the sweaters' district and seeing the evidence everywhere:

> "The bulk of the sweater's work is done in the tenements, which the law that regulates factory labor does not reach." (011-006)

The conditions were brutal. A single tenement apartment might house multiple workers operating sewing machines for twelve hours a day:

> "[He] works on the machine for his sweater twelve hours a day, turning out three dozen 'knee-pants'...for which he received forty-two cents." (011-013)

The sweaters' district was unmistakable — Riis could identify it by sound alone, with the hum of sewing machines audible from the street:

> "Every open window of the big tenements, that stand in endless rows, has its own sewing-machine, with its family of human attachments." (011-007)

The sweater was not always a large-scale operator. Riis documented small-scale sweaters as well:

> "Five men and a woman, two young girls, not fifteen, and a boy who says unasked that he is fifteen, and lies in saying it, are at the machines sewing knickerbockers, 'knee-pants' in the Ludlow Street sweater's shop." (011-011)

## Significance

The sweater system represents Riis's central argument that the tenement was not merely a housing problem but an economic trap. Landlord and employer often overlapped — in the Bohemian quarter especially, "the owner, in the dual capacity of landlord and employer, reduces him to virtual serfdom." (012-002) The sweater was the mechanism that turned housing exploitation into labor exploitation, making the tenement both a home and a sweatshop.

Riis's exposé of the sweating system was part of a broader Progressive-era push for factory regulation. The key vulnerability he highlighted was jurisdictional: factory inspectors could enter workplaces, but when work moved into private tenement apartments, the law broke down. Reformers argued that tenement-house cigar making and garment work must either be regulated or banned outright.

## Historical Aftermath

The anti-sweating movement that Riis helped catalyze gained momentum in the 1890s. The sweating system became a central focus of Progressive-era labor reformers, culminating in a series of legislative efforts aimed at banning tenement-house manufacturing. In New York, the Tenement House Act of 1901 prohibited manufacturing in tenement apartments, closing the jurisdictional loophole that Riis had documented: "the law that regulates factory labor does not reach" the tenement. (011-006)

The sweater system's exposure also shaped American labor journalism for generations. The 1911 Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire — twenty-one years after Riis's book — reignited the same arguments about subcontracted garment labor, though by then the locus had shifted from tenement apartments to unsafe factory lofts. Riis's methodological legacy is visible in the pattern: a journalist enters a hidden workplace, documents conditions with both words and images, and uses the evidence to drive legislative change.

The term "sweater" itself fell out of common usage after the First World War as tenement manufacturing was largely eliminated in American cities, but the economic structure it described — subcontracting piecework to isolated, unorganized workers — persisted in garment industries worldwide. Modern labor historians regard Riis's chapters on the sweating system as among the earliest systematic English-language accounts of what is now recognized as a global pattern of labor exploitation in decentralized supply chains.
