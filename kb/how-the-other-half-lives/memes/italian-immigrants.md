## Immigration Background

The Italian immigration to New York City in the late nineteenth century was remarkable for its scale, its speed, and the degree to which it reshaped the city's tenement districts. By the 1890s, Italians had become the most numerous single immigrant group in the city's slums. Census data from one small tenement block at the lower end of Mulberry Street—just below [[mulberry-bend]]—recorded a hundred Irish, thirty-eight Italian, and only two German speakers [003-002], a striking reversal of the demographic patterns of earlier decades.

The Italians came overwhelmingly from the impoverished rural districts of southern Italy and Sicily, lured by promises of plentiful work at high wages. Riis was sharply critical of the mechanism that brought them:

> Recent Congressional inquiries have shown the nature of the "assistance" he receives from greedy steamship agents and "bankers," who persuade him by false promises to mortgage his home, his few belongings, and his wages for months to come for a ticket to the land where plenty of work is to be had at princely wages. [005-003]

The padrone—the Italian term for this labor broker and banker—collected his percentage both on the voyage and on arrival, turning the immigrant into what Riis called a "doubly accounted" wage-earner and rent-payer with little agency over his own fate [005-003].

## Settlement Patterns

Italian settlement concentrated overwhelmingly in the [[Fourth Ward]] and the lower reaches of Mulberry Street, where the notorious [[mulberry-bend]] district formed the core of Italian immigrant life. The area's physical character—crooked streets, dark alleys, and aging tenement buildings—had been shaped by earlier waves of Irish and German immigration, but the Italian influx gave it a distinctive new complexion.

Unlike the [[Irish Immigrants|Irish]], who had gradually dispersed outward from the Five Points area as their economic position improved, or the [[Bohemian Immigrants|Bohemians]], whose settlement followed the geography of cigar factories, the Italians clustered with extraordinary density. This concentration was driven by both economic factors—the padrone system kept workers tethered to specific landlords and employers—and by the linguistic and cultural isolation that characterized the community. Riis observed that the Italian tongue was "infinitely sweeter than the harsh German" but equally isolating for those who never learned English [006-008].

The physical conditions of Italian tenements were among the worst in the city. Riis recorded scenes of families living in converted stables, occupied by "a swarthy Italian and his family of wife and children" in spaces never intended for human habitation [006-024]. The Italian tenant was nonetheless described by at least one landlord as a good renter, "while the less said about the Irish the better"—though Riis attributed this to the Irish tenant's greater willingness to complain [006-012].

## In Riis's Account

Jacob Riis devoted Chapter V—"The Italian in New York"—almost entirely to this community, and his portrait is as revealing of his own attitudes as it is of Italian life. His tone oscillates between fascination, condescension, and occasional admiration.

Riis described the Italian presence in vividly aesthetic, and sometimes dismissive, terms:

> A picturesque, if not very tidy, element has been added to the population in the "assisted" Italian immigrant who claims so large a share of public attention, partly because he keeps coming at such a tremendous rate, but chiefly because he elects to stay in New York, or near enough for it to serve as his base of operations, and here promptly reproduces conditions of destitution and disorder which, set in the framework of Mediterranean exuberance, are the delight of the artist, but in a measure not. [005-002]

The phrase "conditions of destitution and disorder" was characteristic of Riis's framing: the Italian immigrant was both a subject of reformist concern and an aesthetic spectacle for the middle-class viewer.

Three themes dominate Riis's account of the Italian community:

**The padrone system.** Riis saw the padrone as the key to understanding Italian poverty in New York. The system of labor contracting that began on the docks of Naples or Palermo extended deep into the tenement economy, controlling both employment and housing. The Italian immigrant who paid the padrone for passage, for lodging, and for the privilege of ragpicking was caught in a web of dependency from which escape was nearly impossible [005-006].

**The ragpicking economy.** Riis credited the Italians, or rather the padrone, with transforming New York's garbage into a profitable enterprise. "The discovery was made by earlier explorers that there is money in New York's ash-barrel, but it was left to the genius of the padrone to develop the full resources of the mine that has become the exclusive preserve of the Italian immigrant" [005-006]. Before the Italian domination of ragpicking, the city had paid crews to level out the garbage scows; afterward, the trash itself was systematically sorted and sold.

**Character judgments.** Riis's assessments of Italian character were contradictory. He declared the Italian "as honest as he is hotheaded," noting that "there are no Italian burglars in the Rogues' Gallery" [005-008]. Yet in the same chapter he described the Italian as a "born gambler" whose "soul is in the game from the moment the cards are on the table, and very frequently his knife is in it too" [005-007]. This dual portrait—honest laborer but passionate gambler—reflected both the authentic observations of a police reporter and the ethnic stereotyping endemic to 1890s journalism.

## Economic Role

The Italian immigrant economy in 1890s New York was dominated by the ragpicking trade, which Riis estimated employed the overwhelming majority of Italian men in the tenement districts. The work was organized through the padrone, who controlled both access to the waste stream and the sale of recovered materials.

The economics of ragpicking were brutally simple. The padrone charged rent for the tenement apartment and took a percentage of whatever the ragpicker recovered and sold. The city's garbage—from rags and paper to metal scraps and bones—provided the raw material, while the padrone's connections to recycling markets provided the outlet. Everything between, including the labor itself, was the immigrant's burden.

> Italian cheap labor has come of late to compete with the Hebrew, so that the price of making three shirts, that are styled in the trade "thirteens," is now only fifteen cents. [020-013]

Beyond ragpicking, Italians competed with other immigrant groups—particularly [[Jewish Immigrants|Jews]]—in the tenement garment trades, driving wages down through their willingness to accept less. Riis recorded the tension this created, noting that the Italian was "hardly less aggressive than the Italian, the Russian and Polish Jew" in the competition for tenement work [003-012]. The Italians also featured prominently in Riis's account of the [[stale-beer dive]] economy, where the poorest immigrants spent their meager earnings on cheap, often adulterated alcohol.

The Italian immigrant's economic position was paradoxical: he had captured a near-monopoly on a waste trade no one else wanted, but the padrone system ensured that the profits flowed upward, not to the ragpicker himself. Riis closed his chapter with the observation that the Italian, "who in his own country turns every available scrap of ground to account," was capable of far more than the tenement economy allowed him to achieve [021-010].
