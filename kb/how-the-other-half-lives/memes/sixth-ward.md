The Sixth Ward was one of the most notorious districts in late 19th-century New York City, known for its extreme poverty, crime, and ethnic tensions. Located in lower Manhattan, it encompassed parts of what is now Chinatown and the Lower East Side, bordering the infamous Five Points neighborhood. Riis treated the Sixth Ward as a laboratory of criminal ecology — a place where the failures of housing, policing, and immigration policy converged to produce an environment of sustained violence.

## In Riis's Account

Riis describes the Sixth Ward as a breeding ground for criminal activity, where immigrant children were drawn into the city's underworld. The ward's slums served as training grounds for young pickpockets, as "the Italian boy occasionally shows, as a pickpocket, the results of his training with the toughs of the Sixth Ward slums" [005-008]. This observation was not merely sensational — it reflected Riis's environmental determinism, in which criminal behavior was produced by place rather than by innate character.

The ward's reputation for turbulence was well-established among the police. Riis notes that the police gave the Chinese residents the name of being the "quietest people down there," meaning specifically "in the notoriously turbulent Sixth Ward; and they are" [009-017]. This observation highlights how exceptionally violent and chaotic the ward was — that a community the police otherwise regarded with suspicion was considered peaceful simply by comparison with the ambient violence of the ward.

The Sixth Ward was also the home base of the Whyo Gang, one of the most feared criminal organizations of the era. Riis records that "this was in the Sixth Ward, where the infamous Whyo Gang until a few years ago absorbed the worst depravity of the Bend and what is left of the Five Points" [019-014]. The gang's reign of terror ended only when its leader, Driscoll, was hanged for murder — an event that marked a turning point in the ward's history.

## Geography and Demographics

The Sixth Ward covered the area south of Canal Street, including sections of Mott Street and Pell Street that later formed the core of Chinatown. During Riis's time, it was a dense mixture of Italian, Chinese, and Irish immigrants, with the Italian population concentrated in the rear tenements and the Chinese establishing their presence along Mott Street. The ward's location placed it at the intersection of multiple immigrant colonies, making it a crucible of ethnic tension and cultural exchange.

The ward's geography reinforced its social character. Narrow streets, rear tenements, and cellar dwellings created a physical environment that was nearly impossible to police effectively — a fact that criminal organizations like the Whyo Gang exploited to the fullest.

## Legacy

The dissolution of the Whyo Gang marked a turning point for the Sixth Ward, though Riis observed that "the embers of the scattered Whyo Gang, upon the wreck of which it grew, were smouldering still" [019-015]. This pattern — one gang dispersing only to spawn another — was characteristic of the ward's criminal history. The ward's notoriety as the "Bloody Sixth" persisted into the early 20th century until urban renewal and demographic shifts gradually transformed the area into the Chinatown known today.
