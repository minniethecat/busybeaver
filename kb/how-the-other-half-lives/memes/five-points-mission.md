The Five Points Mission was a pioneering charitable and religious organization established in the heart of New York's most notorious slum district. Founded in the former Old Brewery building — which had been a symbol of the Five Points' depravity — the mission represented one of the earliest organized efforts to bring moral and material relief to the neighborhood's destitute residents. Its work stood in the lineage of slum philanthropy that would eventually reshape New York's approach to urban poverty.

## The Old Brewery

The Old Brewery, before its conversion into the Mission, had been a tenement of extraordinary density and vice. Riis reports it once housed over a thousand people in conditions of unimaginable squalor, making it one of the most densely packed residential buildings in the nineteenth-century city. The transformation of this building — from a symbol of the Five Points' degeneracy into a site of organized charity — was, for Riis, emblematic of the larger possibility of slum regeneration. The building's very name had become shorthand for urban depravity, and its repurposing represented what reformers saw as the triumph of moral order over chaos.

## In Riis's Account

Riis identifies the Five Points Mission, alongside the [[five-points-house-of-industry|Five Points House of Industry]], as one of "two powerful agents that were among the pioneers in this work of moral and physical regeneration" that "stand in Paradise Park today as milestones on the rocky, uphill road" [016-009]. The mission was founded by "a handful of noble women, who braved the foul depravity of the Old Brewery to rescue its child victims," and who "rolled away the first and heaviest boulder, which legislatures and city councils had tackled in vain."

Riis's account places the Mission within a larger narrative of progress: "Prayer-time in the nursery — Five Points House of Industry" [016-011], he captioned one of his photographs, showing the kind of institutional work that had replaced the old chaos. The Mission's work was primarily focused on children, who were seen as both the most vulnerable victims of the slum and the most promising candidates for moral regeneration. This emphasis on children as the lever of slum reform — rescue the child, and you break the cycle of poverty — would become a dominant theme in Progressive-era philanthropy.

## The Five Points District

The Five Points itself had long been the symbolic center of New York's slum problem. Riis traced its geography from "the Fourth Ward slums and the Five Points the whole length of the island" [002-008], depicting the district as the anchor of a corridor of poverty that ran the length of Manhattan. By the time Riis wrote, "all of the old depravity of the Five Points" had migrated to "the Bend" [006-002], but the Five Points remained, in the public imagination, synonymous with urban vice. The Mission's location in this notorious district was both its challenge and its rationale — it placed charity at the point of greatest need.

Riis would later predict that "the Bend will go the way of the Five Points when all the red tape that binds" was cut [025-017], suggesting that the trajectory of the Five Points — from depravity through mission work to eventual clearance — was a model for other slum districts. The Five Points Mission and the House of Industry had demonstrated, in his view, that organized intervention could succeed where legislation alone had failed.

## Relationship to Other Charities

The Five Points Mission operated alongside a network of charitable institutions that emerged in the Five Points during the mid-nineteenth century. While the Five Points House of Industry focused on industrial education and day schooling, the Mission emphasized religious conversion and rescue work. Together, they represented a broader shift in slum philanthropy from mere almsgiving toward organized, institutional intervention — a model that would influence later efforts such as the [[childrens-aid-society|Children's Aid Society]], the [[fresh-air-fund|Fresh Air Fund]], and the [[five-points-mission|Five Points Mission]] itself, whose work with destitute children set a pattern that later child-saving organizations would follow. The Mission also anticipated the work of figures like [[sister-irene|Sister Irene]], founder of the Foundling Asylum, whose rescue of abandoned infants extended the Mission's child-centered logic to its most extreme case.
