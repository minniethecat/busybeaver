# The Sweater Worker

## Biography

The sweater worker was the human unit of the tenement labor system. Typically a Jewish, Italian, or Bohemian immigrant, the sweater worker labored in the same apartment where they slept, ate, and raised children. Riis describes the archetypal case: an operator with four children who "works on the machine for his sweater twelve hours a day, turning out three dozen 'knee-pants'...for which he received forty-two cents." (011-013) The economics were merciless: the sweater took his cut, the worker bore all costs (rent, machine maintenance, thread), and the manufacturer at the end of the chain paid the lowest price the market would bear.

## Role in the Tenements

The sweater worker's apartment was both home and factory. A typical configuration: "man, wife, three children, and a boarder" all working at knee-pants production in two or three rooms. (011-012) The boarder was "really only a lodger, getting his meals with the family" — eating at the same table where garments were sewn. (011-012) The children trimmed threads and turned finished garments; the wife operated a second machine; the husband managed the relationship with the sweater.

Earnings were shockingly low. A worker described being paid "fifteen cents a yard" for children's suits. (011-019) Another family of "man, wife and two children" lived on "nine dollars and a half a week" including an extra dollar from the Children's Aid Society. (011-014)

Disease was occupational. A doctor attributed consumption among Bohemian cigar-makers to "the tobacco-fumes" that filled tenement apartments where whole families rolled cigars twelve hours a day. (012-012) The isolation of home work prevented collective bargaining: "The sweater knows well that the isolation of the workman in his home puts him at a hopeless disadvantage." (011-005)

## Legacy

The sweater worker became a central figure in Progressive-era labor reform. The anti-sweatshop movement that emerged in the 1890s — led by the Consumers' League and the Women's Trade Union League — used Riis's documentation to argue for minimum-wage laws, factory inspection, and the prohibition of tenement manufacturing. The Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire of 1911, which killed 146 garment workers, brought the sweating system to national attention and led to the first comprehensive labor regulations.
