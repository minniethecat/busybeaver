# Improved Dwellings Association

The Improved Dwellings Association was a pioneering philanthropic housing corporation in late nineteenth-century New York City that constructed and operated a block of model tenements on the Upper East Side. Jacob Riis, in his 1890 book *How the Other Half Lives*, identified the Association as one of the two preeminent efforts to build decent, profitable housing for the city's working poor:

> "From the managers of the two best-known experiments in model tenement building in the city, the Improved Dwellings Association and the Tenement-house Building Company, I have letters dated last August, declaring their enterprises eminently successful." (025-023)

## Founding and Corporate Structure

The Improved Dwellings Association was organized as a stock corporation, but its philanthropic character was embedded in its charter from the outset. By the terms of subscription agreed upon before incorporation, dividends to shareholders were strictly limited to five percent per annum. (025-025) This self-imposed cap distinguished the Association from purely commercial real estate ventures and reflected the philosophy that model tenement building could serve both conscience and capital -- a view that Riis himself championed throughout his reform advocacy.

The Association's president was [[w-bayard-cutting|W. Bayard Cutting]], a wealthy New York merchant and philanthropist who provided Riis with a detailed letter assessing the enterprise's financial performance. (025-024) His personal involvement exemplified what Riis considered essential to the success of model tenement projects: the direct, sustained engagement of proprietors rather than passive investment.

## The East Seventy-second Street Block

The Association erected its flagship project -- a block of thirteen houses on [[east-seventy-second-street|East Seventy-second Street]] -- approximately nine years before Riis published his book, placing its construction around 1881. (025-024) The total cost, originally estimated at roughly $240,000 including land, rose to $285,000 due to disputes with the contractor engaged to build the structures. (025-024) The neighborhood at the time was new and sparsely settled, adding to the financial risk of the undertaking.

The buildings incorporated design features that were notably advanced for their era. Apartments, all "outer" rooms with windows facing the street or yard, ranged from two rooms on the top floor (renting for $6.25 per month) to four rooms on the ground floor ($14 per month). (025-024) The block provided accommodations for 218 families. Amenities included coal lifts, ash-chutes, common laundry facilities in the basement, and free baths -- innovations that Riis observed were "then new enough to be looked upon with suspicion by the doubting Thomases who predicted disaster." (025-024) When Riis inspected the property, all but nine apartments were occupied, and one of the nine was rented during his visit. (025-024)

The superintendent told Riis that disorderly tenants were rare, even though "the buildings shelter all sorts of people." (025-024)

## Financial Performance and Reinvestment

Despite the cost overruns and an unpromising start in an undeveloped neighborhood, the Association's financial results validated its model. The five-percent dividend was paid consistently -- at two percent every six months -- from the first half-year of the buildings' operation onward. (025-025) All surplus earnings beyond the dividend were plowed back into the property. The Association installed new and expensive roofs for tenants' comfort, completely repainted the buildings inside and out beyond what was originally planned, and added a costly set of fire-escapes at the direction of the [[fire|Fire Department]], along with numerous other improvements. (025-025)

Cutting emphasized that the five-percent return was all the more impressive because the Association's charter prohibited the sale of liquor on its premises, deliberately forgoing the substantially higher rents that a saloon tenant would have paid. (025-025) He concluded his assessment to Riis with the judgment that "an investment at five percent net upon real estate security worth more than the principal sum, ought to be considered desirable." (025-025)

## Place in the Model Tenement Movement

The Improved Dwellings Association held a distinctive position in the broader [[model-tenement]] movement that Riis promoted as the third pillar of [[tenement-reform]], alongside legal regulation and the rehabilitation of existing tenements. (025-016) Because its buildings were, in Cutting's words, "the first erected in this city upon anything like a large scale, where it was proposed to meet the architectural difficulties that present themselves in the tenement-house problem," the Association served as a proof of concept for the proposition that model tenement construction could succeed in New York. (025-025)

Riis placed the Association's experience alongside that of the Tenement House Building Company in [[cherry-street|Cherry Street]], the Riverside Buildings of [[a-t-white|A. T. White]] in Brooklyn, and the rehabilitated tenements of [[ellen-collins|Ellen Collins]] on [[water-street|Water Street]] -- together forming the evidentiary backbone of his argument in Chapter XXV that decent housing for the poor could be made to pay. Cutting himself expressed confidence that "the experiment could be tried today with the improved knowledge which has come with time, and a much larger return be shown upon the investment." (025-025)

Riis concluded from the Association's example and others like it that "model tenements can be built successfully and made to pay in New York," provided the owner accepted a moderate return of five or six percent. (025-035) He further argued that "the erection of a single good tenement has the power to change, gradually but surely, the character of a whole bad block" -- a claim for which the Association's transformation of a vacant East Seventy-second Street neighborhood into a thriving community of 218 families offered compelling evidence. (025-035)
