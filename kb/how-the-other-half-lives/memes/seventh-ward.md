# Seventh Ward

## A Tenement Stronghold

The Seventh Ward was one of the most notorious tenement districts in Jacob Riis's geography of poverty. Described as a place where the tenements were "filling the tenements of the old Seventh Ward to the river front, and disputing every foot of space" (003-012), the ward represented the extreme density and overcrowding that characterized immigrant housing in 1890s New York. The Italian push into the ward was displacing the established Irish population, creating ethnic friction in an already unstable housing environment.

## Penitentiary Row

Within the Seventh Ward, Riis documented one of the most evocatively named locations in the tenement landscape: Penitentiary Row. He crossed "the boundary of the Seventh Ward" to find it — "Penitentiary Row, suggestive name, that harbored within its double row of tenements a contingent of Italian immigrants" (004-020). The name itself — whether official or colloquial — spoke to the carceral quality of tenement life, where families were warehoused in spaces that Riis explicitly compared to prisons.

## Living Conditions

The conditions in the Seventh Ward were representative of the worst tenement abuses. Riis described a typical scene in "a Seventh Ward tenement" where the neglect of basic sanitation was "typical enough to serve as a sample of the whole" (004-028). The ward's proximity to the river meant that it was subject to dampness and flooding, while its old building stock — some of the oldest tenements in the city — made rehabilitation nearly impossible under the existing economic incentives.

## Ethnic Transition

By the 1890s, the Seventh Ward was in ethnic transition. The Irish, who had dominated the ward for decades, were being displaced by newer Italian immigrants moving north from the [[fourth-ward|Fourth Ward]] and the Bend area. Riis observed this demographic shift with characteristic anxiety about the "new" immigration, but he also noted that the housing conditions that deformed Irish life were being passed, unimproved, to the new arrivals.
