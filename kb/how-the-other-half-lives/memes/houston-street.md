Houston Street was more than a major crosstown thoroughfare in late 19th-century Manhattan. In Riis's geography of poverty, it was a fault line — the northern boundary of the worst tenement districts and the site of some of the most dramatic episodes in the city's struggle with housing reform. The street's cellars, rear tenements, and lodging-houses concentrated the failures of the free market in housing into a single visible corridor.

## A Boundary of Misery

Riis treated Houston Street as a demarcation between the tenement belt and the more prosperous sections of the city to the north. South of Houston, the tenement density was highest, the immigrant colonies were most concentrated, and the housing conditions were worst. The street was, in Riis's mapping, the geographic line where respectable New York ended and the "other half" began.

## Cellar Clearances and Housing Reform

Houston Street figured prominently in the early history of tenement regulation. The Health Department's cellar clearances — one of the first systematic efforts to improve tenement conditions — were concentrated along Houston Street and its cross streets. These clearances forced landlords to seal underground dwellings that lacked light, ventilation, and drainage, displacing their occupants but also eliminating some of the most dangerous housing in the city.

The clearances were controversial. Landlords argued they destroyed property value without compensation; reformers argued they were the minimum necessary to prevent epidemic disease. Riis, characteristically, sided with the reformers, treating the cellar clearances as evidence that public health regulation could meaningfully improve tenement conditions when enforced.

## Tenement Evictions and Lodging-Houses

Houston Street was also the scene of mass evictions that Riis documented as emblematic of the tenement dweller's precarious existence. Families who had paid rent for years could be turned out with minimal notice when landlords chose to sell or redevelop. The street's concentration of cheap lodging-houses meant that evicted families often found their first temporary shelter within blocks of their former homes, beginning the downward spiral from tenement apartment to lodging-house to homelessness.
