# Alfred T. White

## Who Was Alfred T. White

Alfred Tredway White was a Brooklyn-based builder and philanthropist who devoted much of his adult life to the construction of model tenement housing for the working poor. Jacob Riis introduces him in Chapter XXV of *How the Other Half Lives* as "Mr. A. T. White," describing him as a man "who has devoted a life of beneficent activity to tenement building" (025-033).

Though his work was conducted "altogether across the East River" in Brooklyn, Riis insisted that White's experience was "justly applying to New York as well" (025-033). White himself argued that "there is not the slightest reason to doubt that the financial result of a similar undertaking in any tenement-house district of New York City would be equally good" (025-033).

Unlike many philanthropists who approached tenement reform through organized [[charity]], White operated as a builder-investor who sought to prove that decent housing for the poor could also be a sound business proposition—a thesis central to Riis's concluding argument in the book.

## The Riverside Buildings

White's crowning achievement was the Riverside Buildings, a model tenement complex in Brooklyn that housed approximately three hundred families under a single unified roof. Riis reproduced the general plan and a detailed floor plan of the Riverside Buildings as the centerpiece illustrations of his concluding chapter (025-028 through 025-032), presenting them as visual proof that decent tenement architecture was achievable.

White's design was architecturally innovative in ways that directly addressed the evils of the conventional [[tenement]]. The buildings eliminated the common hallway entirely—"each has his private front-door" and "the common hall, with all that it stands for, has disappeared" (025-034). The fireproof staircases were placed on the exterior of the building, functioning as perfect fire-escapes.

Most significantly, White's plan required no [[air-shaft|air-shafts]]: "There are no air-shafts, for they are not needed. Every room, under the admirable arrangement of the plan, looks out either upon the street or the yard" (025-034). The central yard was landscaped as "a great park with a playground set apart for the children, where they may dig in the sand to their heart's content," and weekly concerts were performed by a brass band (025-034).

Riis explicitly framed the Riverside Buildings as an improvement on the London model tenement plans of Sir Sydney Waterlow, noting that White's design "embodies all the good features of Sir Sydney Waterlow's London plan, with improvements suggested by the builder's own experience" (025-034).

## Profit-Sharing and Tenant Relations

One of White's most distinctive innovations was his profit-sharing scheme for tenants. Riis reports that White "has made it pay well enough to allow good tenants a share in the profits, averaging nearly one month's rent out of the twelve, as a premium upon promptness and order" (025-034).

This mechanism transformed the landlord-tenant relationship from one of extraction into something approaching partnership, rewarding responsible tenancy with tangible financial benefit. White's approach anticipated by several years a similar plan conceived but never implemented by the [[tenement-house-building-company|Tenement House Building Company]] for its Cherry Street properties (025-026).

The results, by Riis's account, were remarkable: "Mr. White never has any trouble with his tenants, though he gathers in the poorest; nor do his tenements have anything of the 'institution character' that occasionally attaches to ventures of this sort, to their damage" (025-034).

Riis's choice of the phrase "institution character" is deliberate—it was a common criticism of philanthropic housing that it felt regimented, paternalistic, and barracks-like. White's buildings avoided this entirely, becoming "like a big village of contented people, who live in peace with one another because they have elbow-room even under one big roof" (025-034).

White himself emphasized that his tenants were drawn from "the humblest classes," noting that "the proportion of day-laborers and sewing-women in his houses is greater than in any of the London model tenements" (025-033).

## White in Riis's Reform Argument

Riis positioned White as the capstone of his evidentiary case for model tenement construction. Chapter XXV, "How the Case Stands," is structured as an eight-point logical argument (025-002 through 025-010) followed by case studies of successful tenement reform.

The case studies proceed in escalating ambition: [[ellen-collins|Ellen Collins]]'s rehabilitation of old tenements on Water Street, the [[improved-dwellings-association|Improved Dwellings Association]] on East Seventy-second Street, the [[tenement-house-building-company|Tenement House Building Company]] on Cherry Street, and finally White's Riverside Buildings.

Each case escalates in ambition and scale, and White's is presented as the apogee—"the beau ideal of the model tenement for a great city like New York" (025-034).

Riis was responding to skeptics who argued that model tenements could not succeed in New York, either because land costs were too high or Manhattan's tenant population was too degraded. White refuted both objections: on land costs, he argued that "high cost of land is no detriment, provided the value is made by the pressure of people seeking residence there" (025-033); on tenant quality, he rebutted the claim that Brooklyn had better tenants by pointing to the socioeconomic profile of his own buildings.

By Riis's close, White's experience was presented as conclusive: "Enough has been said to show that model tenements can be built successfully and made to pay in New York" (025-035).

## Legacy

White built homes for five hundred poor families over the course of his career (025-034). His Riverside Buildings represented a synthesis of the practical and the ideal—profitable enough to sustain themselves as a business investment, humane enough to provide genuine homes rather than mere shelter.

In Riis's taxonomy of tenement reform, White belonged to the third and most ambitious category of intervention: "By building new, model tenements" (025-016), alongside the parallel efforts of the [[improved-dwellings-association|Improved Dwellings Association]] and the [[tenement-house-building-company|Tenement House Building Company]].

Where [[ellen-collins|Ellen Collins]] demonstrated that old tenements could be rehabilitated, White demonstrated that new construction, designed from first principles around the needs of working-class families, could be both architecturally beautiful and financially sustainable. His work provided the affirmative counterpoint to the grim catalog of tenement horrors that fills Riis's earlier chapters.

The proof that the housing crisis was not inevitable was, as Riis wrote, that "nothing but our own slothfulness is in the way" (025-007). White's legacy in the movement is that of the builder-reformer who showed that the [[tenement-reform|tenement reform]] agenda was not utopian but achievable through the disciplined application of capital, architectural ingenuity, and genuine concern for the welfare of tenants.
