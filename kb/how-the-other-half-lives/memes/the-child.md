# The Tenement Child

## Biography

"The Problem of the Children" (Chapter XV) is the moral center of *How the Other Half Lives*. Riis frames the tenement child not as a demographic category but as the living refutation of American optimism. Children were born into conditions that predetermined their futures: crowded into dark rooms with sick adults, put to work at age five or six, and exposed to vice in the streets before they could read. Riis describes "six grown people and five children" crowded into single rooms, and the inevitable spread of disease (006-017).

## Role in the Tenements

The tenement child lived a life that Riis presents as a systematic repudiation of every middle-class assumption about childhood. There was no nursery, no schoolroom, no playground — only the street, the tenement apartment, and the factory. Children worked alongside their parents from the earliest possible age, their labor essential to family survival: "Men, women and children work together seven days in the week" (012-005).

Infant mortality rates in the tenement districts were catastrophic. The Board of Health documented death rates several times higher than in middle-class neighborhoods. The defining experience of tenement childhood was the death of siblings — a normalizing of mortality that Riis found as damaging as the deaths themselves.

Yet Riis also finds resilience. Children played in the streets with improvised toys, formed their own social hierarchies, and developed survival skills that shocked middle-class observers. This was not a sentimental portrait of childhood innocence lost but a documentary account of children adapting to conditions that no child should face.

## School and the Street

The scale of educational deprivation was staggering. In one tenement, Riis counted 170 children living in a single building, yet the sanitary inspector "reported that he found only seven in the whole house who owned that they went to school. The rest gathered all the instruction they received running for beer for their elders" (015-002). Even honest play was structurally impossible: "Honest play is interdicted in the streets. The policeman arrests the ball-tossers, and there is no room in the backyard" (019-002). Riis recorded one backyard notice between two enormous tenements: "All boys caught in this yard will be delt with accorden to law."

The children's physical world was as confined as their educational one. Riis found that "out of forty-eight boys twenty had never seen the Brooklyn Bridge that was scarcely five minutes' walk away, three only had been in Central Park, fifteen had known the joy of a ride in a horsecar" (015-004). Their entire domain was "the street, with its ash-barrels and its dirt, the river that runs foul with mud" (015-004).

## The Saloon and the Growler

Riis identified the saloon as the most destructive force in the tenement child's life. "All the evil the saloon does in breeding poverty and in corrupting politics; all the suffering it brings into the lives of its thousands of innocent victims, the wives and children of drunkards... it is all as nothing to this, its worst offence" — the corruption of the child (018-009). The "growler" — the beer pail children were sent to fill at the corner saloon — was, in Riis's phrase, "at the cradle of the tough." "From the moment he, almost a baby, for the first time carries the growler for beer, he is never out of its reach, and the two soon form a partnership that lasts through life" (019-002).

## Baby-Farming and Child Insurance

Beyond the general conditions of poverty, Riis documented industries that preyed specifically on tenement children. Baby-farming — the practice of taking in infants for a fee and then starving them — flourished as a form of "systematic child murder." The farmers "feed them on sour milk, and give them paregoric to keep them quiet, until they die, when they get some young medical man without experience to sign a certificate to the Board of Health that the child died of inanition, and so the matter ends" (016-006). A parallel industry of child life insurance created perverse incentives: Riis discovered that three companies had issued "not less than a million such policies" on children's lives, with premiums as low as ten cents, effectively placing a cash value on a child's death (016-007).

## The Reformatory Trap

The institutional response to the crisis of tenement childhood could be as damaging as the conditions it sought to address. Riis described the reformatory trap: a child "caught in the street by the truant officer, or by the agents of the Children's Societies, peddling, perhaps, or begging, to help out the family resources, he runs the risk of being sent to a reformatory, where contact with vicious boys older than himself soon develop the latent possibilities for evil" (015-003). The city had no Truant Home — no intermediate institution between the street and the prison — so efforts to enforce school attendance were "paralyzed" by the lack of appropriate facilities.

## Institutional Response

Riis documented the scale of charitable intervention: "In thirty-seven years the Children's Aid Society... has sheltered quite three hundred thousand outcast, homeless, and orphaned children in its lodging-houses, and has found homes in the West for seventy thousand that had none" (015-013). Meanwhile, the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children had been invoked for 138,891 children and had "convicted nearly sixteen thousand wretches of child-beating and abuse" (015-013). These numbers measured both the scale of the crisis and the limits of the response.

## Legacy

Riis's focus on children was strategically brilliant. While adults could be blamed for their poverty (as lazy, drunken, or immoral), children were unambiguously innocent. By centering his reform argument on the child, Riis made opposition to tenement reform a position against children. This framing would be adopted by virtually every subsequent Progressive-era reform campaign, from child-labor laws to public schooling to playground construction.
