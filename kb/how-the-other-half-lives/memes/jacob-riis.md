# Jacob Riis

## Biography

Jacob August Riis (1849–1914) arrived in New York from Denmark in 1870 as a penniless immigrant. He spent his first years in America living in the very tenements and lodging-houses he would later document, working as a carpenter, miner, and farmhand before finding his vocation as a police reporter for the *New York Tribune* and later the *New York Evening Sun*. His firsthand experience of poverty gave his journalism an authority that purely observational reporters lacked.

By the late 1880s, Riis had become the most prominent chronicler of slum conditions in New York. His innovation was to pair written reportage with flash photography — a new technology that allowed him to capture images in the dark interiors of tenement apartments where no photographer had worked before. The result was *How the Other Half Lives*, published in 1890, which combined text, photographs, and statistical evidence into a single polemical work.

The book's title page identifies Riis as the author with a photograph caption:

> "'The Street Arab — photograph by Jacob A. Riis.'" (P05-001)

The book's imprint page gives the full title and author credit. (P01-001)

## Role in the Tenements

Riis was both participant in and chronicler of the tenement world. His chapters are structured as guided tours through neighborhoods he knew intimately — not as a distant observer but as someone who had lived among the people he described. His Danish accent and immigrant background gave him access to communities that nativist reporters could not reach.

He accompanied the police on raids (Chapter VII, the stale-beer dives), visited families in their tenement apartments with charity doctors, and walked the streets at all hours. His method was immersive journalism decades before the term existed. The book's argument is inseparable from his personal credibility: he had been poor in New York, and he had survived.

Riis's reportorial method is visible throughout the text. In Chapter VI, he describes standing in the Bend and watching the street life unfold below — a perspective that only someone who had spent countless nights in the district could command. In Chapter XI, walking through the sweaters' district, his ear is tuned to the hum of sewing machines:

> "Every open window of the big tenements, that stand in endless rows, has its own sewing-machine, with its family of human attachments." (011-007)

This sensory detail — the sound of the district, not just its appearance — reflects a reporter who had learned to read the tenement world through all five senses.

Riis's influence extended beyond the book. He became close to Theodore Roosevelt, then New York's police commissioner, and his advocacy helped shape the Tenement House Act of 1901. His lantern-slide lectures brought images of tenement life to middle-class audiences across the country, creating the visual vocabulary of urban poverty that would define Progressive-era reform.

## The Photographer's Eye

Riis's flash photography was revolutionary. Before his work, the interiors of tenement apartments were literally invisible — too dark for conventional photography. His magnesium flash powder, which produced a blinding burst of light followed by acrid smoke, allowed him to capture images that no one outside the tenement world had ever seen.

Riis described the photographic method in his own words:

> "The statement that eternal vigilance is the price of liberty applies just as fitly to the tenement-house inspection." (024-010)

This language — "eternal vigilance" — echoes the photographer's own practice. Riis's raids, whether with police or with camera, were acts of exposure in both the literal and figurative sense. His photographs made the tenement visible to audiences who had never entered one, and the shock of that visibility was itself a political act.

## Multiple Perspectives

Jacob Riis was perceived in radically different ways by the various constituencies his work touched. To the middle-class reform audience that bought his book and attended his lectures, Riis was a hero — the immigrant-made-good who had returned to the slums bearing the light of truth. His narrative of personal ascent from penniless Danish carpenter to celebrated author and confidant of Theodore Roosevelt resonated with the self-made mythology of the era, and his moral authority rested on the claim that he had experienced poverty firsthand. Reformers like Lawrence Veiller and the Tenement House Committee invoked Riis's photographs as incontrovertible evidence, treating his work less as journalism than as a legal brief against the tenement system.

To the tenement dwellers he photographed, Riis was a more complicated presence. His magnesium flash, which erupted without warning in the darkness of a tenement apartment, was an intrusion into lives that had no expectation of privacy. We have no direct testimony from the subjects of Riis's photographs — they were not asked for their names, let alone their consent. The power asymmetry was absolute: a white, English-speaking, professionally credentialed man with a police escort and a blinding light, entering homes whose occupants could not refuse. Contemporary critics have noted that Riis's photographic practice was a form of surveillance as much as sympathy, capturing images of the poor for the consumption of the comfortable.

To the political establishment of Tammany Hall, Riis was a threat and an annoyance — a reporter who named names, published damning statistics, and built a public constituency for reforms that would disrupt the patronage networks on which machine power depended. Yet Riis's relationship with the machine was not purely adversarial. His police access, which enabled the stale-beer dive raid and many of his most famous photographs, depended on cooperation from police officials who were themselves Tammany appointees. And his alliance with Theodore Roosevelt, who as police commissioner sought to professionalize the force and weaken Tammany's hold, positioned Riis as an ally of reformist elements within the Republican Party who were fighting the Democratic machine.

To later generations of journalists and historians, Riis's legacy is profoundly contested. He is celebrated as a founding figure of investigative journalism and documentary photography, but criticized for his racial essentialism, his reliance on ethnic stereotypes, and his paternalistic framing of the poor as objects of charity rather than agents of their own lives. The Jacob Riis Houses on the Lower East Side bear his name, but the public-housing model they represent — large-scale, government-built, institution-managed — raises questions about whether the reform tradition Riis helped launch ultimately produced better housing or new forms of institutional control. His place in the canon is secure; his meaning within it remains fiercely debated.

## Legacy

Riis's legacy is complex. His photographs remain among the most powerful images of 19th-century urban poverty ever made, and his book is a landmark of investigative journalism. But his racial and ethnic characterizations — particularly of Chinese, Italian, and Black New Yorkers — reflect the prejudices of his era, using stereotypes that modern readers find jarring. He was both a reformer who fought for the poor and a nativist who described immigrants in language that dehumanized them.

His method — using data, photography, and personal narrative in combination — became the template for 20th-century muckraking journalism. The Jacob Riis Houses on the Lower East Side, built in 1949, bear his name as a monument to the housing reform movement he helped create.
