# Police Headquarters

## 300 Mulberry Street

The Police Headquarters at 300 Mulberry Street was more than an administrative building — in Riis's reporting, it was a microcosm of the city's failure to protect its most vulnerable citizens. Located in the heart of the tenement district, the headquarters became an unofficial receiving station for lost, abandoned, and runaway children.

## Lost Children

Riis described the "flotsam and jetsam stranded at Police Headquarters one day last summer" (015-005) — children of all ages who had been picked up on the streets or brought in by officers. The scene Riis painted was one of confusion and pathos: "No one knew where they belonged. They themselves could not tell. They had forgotten" (015-005). The children ranged from toddlers who had wandered away from tenement apartments to teenagers who had fled abusive homes.

## The Year-Round Parade of Mothers

The headquarters was also the destination for "the mothers that go out and in at Police Headquarters the year round, inquiring for their lost little ones" (017-008). Riis used this image — the perpetual stream of desperate parents — to underscore the human cost of tenement conditions that made it impossible for working parents to supervise their children. The children who ended up at Police Headquarters were, in Riis's telling, the inevitable product of housing that offered no safe space for family life.

## Riis's Relationship with the Police

As a police reporter for the New York Tribune, Riis knew the Mulberry Street headquarters intimately. It was from here that he launched many of his nighttime expeditions into the tenements with the sanitary police, and it was here that he developed the relationships with police officials that gave him access to the statistics and stories that populate *How the Other Half Lives*.
