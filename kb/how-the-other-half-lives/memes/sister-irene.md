# Sister Irene

Sister Irene was the founder and head of the New York Foundling Asylum, a Catholic charitable institution that cared for abandoned infants and young children in the tenement districts of late-nineteenth-century New York. Jacob Riis cited her in *How the Other Half Lives* as an exemplar of charitable dedication, noting her "twenty-one years of loving work" among the city's most vulnerable population.

## The Foundling Asylum

The Foundling Asylum, under Sister Irene's direction, provided care for infants abandoned by parents who could not support them — a common tragedy in the tenement districts, where family poverty and overcrowding made child-rearing impossible for many. The asylum operated on principles of Catholic charity, offering shelter, medical care, and placement services for foundlings. Riis, though himself Protestant, praised Sister Irene's work without sectarian qualification, treating the asylum as a model of what dedicated charitable intervention could achieve in the tenement environment.

## In Riis's Account

Riis presented Sister Irene as a counterweight to the darker figures in his narrative — the baby farmers who profited from child death, the landlords who exploited family desperation, the officials who ignored infant mortality. Her twenty-one years of service represented, in Riis's framing, the possibility of redemption within the tenement world — proof that individuals of goodwill could make a measurable difference even within a broken system.
