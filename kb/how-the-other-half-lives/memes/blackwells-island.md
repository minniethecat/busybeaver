Blackwell's Island, strung along the East River, was the institutional dumping ground of late 19th-century New York — the place where the city sent its poor, its criminals, its insane, and its inconvenient. Riis identified the island as the spatial expression of a city that preferred its poverty invisible, writing that "over on the islands that lie strung along the river and far up the Sound the Nether Half hides its deformity, except on show-days" [022-003]. On Blackwell's Island clustered the penitentiary, the almshouse, the workhouse, and the lunatic asylum — each an institution nominally dedicated to reform or relief, each in Riis's account a monument to municipal failure.

## The Institutions

### The Penitentiary

The Blackwell's Island penitentiary received a wide spectrum of the city's offenders — from petty thieves and disorderly persons to the leaders of criminal gangs. It was built of granite quarried from the island itself by convict labor, a detail that Riis appreciated for its grim irony. The penitentiary's revolving-door relationship with the city's slums was a recurring theme in Riis's analysis: the same individuals cycled repeatedly through arrest, conviction, imprisonment, and release back into the conditions that produced their crimes.

### The Almshouse

The almshouse on Blackwell's Island was the endpoint for many of the destitute elderly and disabled who had exhausted every other option in the city's patchwork system of poor relief. Riis documented how "the tramp" was sent "to Blackwell's Island twice in twenty-four hours for six months at a time" [007-008], a sentence that demonstrated both the city's reliance on the island as a catch-all solution and the fundamental inadequacy of incarceration as a response to poverty. The [[almshouse]] represented the city's grudging acknowledgment that some of its residents could not support themselves — and its determination that this acknowledgment should be as invisible as possible.

### The Lunatic Asylum

The lunatic asylum housed the city's mentally ill in conditions that Riis described with particular horror. He observed the women patients taking their afternoon walks on the island's grounds — "a hundred or more women, walking with a dreamy, uncertain step, in single file" [022-009] — and used the image to indict a system that warehoused human beings without hope of treatment or release.

## A Destination for the Desperate

The island appeared throughout Riis's book as a recurring endpoint for those who fell through the city's cracks. Chinese immigrants swept up in police raids on Chinatown opium dens were "emptied upon Blackwell's Island" [009-006]. Tramps were sentenced to the island's workhouse for terms of six months, released, and rearrested within a day — a revolving door that Riis presented as evidence of the futility of punitive approaches to homelessness. "[The tramp] goes to the island, where he is set to work at some trade that he may or may not know, and returns with his new graft, but the same old tramp" [007-008].

## The Geography of Concealment

Riis understood the island's location as deliberate policy, not accident. The city had removed its most troubling populations from sight, concentrating them in facilities that could be ignored by the respectable classes on Manhattan. "Show-days" — periodic occasions when distinguished visitors toured the institutions — were the exception, when "the sore is uncovered by the authorities with due municipal pride in the exhibit" [022-003]. The rest of the time, the island and its residents remained invisible, a parallel city hidden in plain view across the narrow channel of the East River.

The geography of concealment was made possible by the geography of proximity: Blackwell's Island was close enough to Manhattan to be convenient, yet separated by water — a moat of respectability that kept the "Nether Half" at a safe distance from the city that produced them. Riis's exposé of the island was an attempt to collapse that distance, to force his readers to see what the East River was designed to hide.
