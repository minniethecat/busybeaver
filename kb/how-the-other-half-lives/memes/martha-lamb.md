# Martha Lamb

## Biography

Martha J. Lamb (1826–1893) was a prominent American historian and editor of the *Magazine of American History*. Her two-volume *History of the City of New York* (1877), published when Riis was beginning his career as a reporter, provided the most comprehensive account of the city's development then available. The work documented New York from its Dutch origins through the post-Civil War era, with particular attention to the physical growth of the city and the emergence of the tenement system.

## Role in the Tenements

Lamb appears in Riis's text as an authoritative historical voice. In Chapter I, when Riis needs to establish that the tenement crisis reached its peak in the mid-19th century, he turns to her:

> "'The city,' says its historian, Mrs. Martha Lamb, commenting on the era of aqueduct construction and the filling-in of the Collect Pond." (001-011)

Riis uses Lamb's testimony to anchor his argument in historical authority. She was not a reformer but a historian — her account of the city's growth could not be dismissed as partisan. When she described "crazy old buildings, crowded rear tenements in filthy yards" as "habitations of thousands of our fellow-beings in this wealthy, Christian city," it was not polemic but historical record. (001-011)

Lamb's *History* also provided the demographic context Riis needed. She documented the transformation of Manhattan from a merchant town of a hundred thousand to an industrial city of half a million, tracing the same waves of immigration, construction, and displacement that Riis's chapters would detail in journalistic form.

## Legacy

Lamb's importance to Riis's project extends beyond her direct quotation. Her method — using documentary evidence, maps, and statistical tables to tell the story of urban growth — was a model for Riis's own approach. Where Lamb wrote history, Riis wrote journalism, but both relied on the same evidentiary logic: the city's physical record, properly read, told its moral history.

Her later volumes covered the city's institutions, charities, and cultural life — topics that Riis addressed from the reformer's perspective rather than the historian's. The two bodies of work are complementary, with Lamb providing the long view and Riis the immediate exposé.
