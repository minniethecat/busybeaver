## Overview

Water Street occupied the eastern waterfront of Lower Manhattan in the 1890s, running through a district that Riis characterized as among the most degraded in the city. It was a street of old tenements alongside the East River, part of the working waterfront economy. But Water Street also became one of the most important sites in Riis's narrative of reform — the location of Ellen Collins's model tenement experiment that demonstrated slum property could be improved profitably.

## Ellen Collins's Model Tenements

The most significant passage about Water Street in *How the Other Half Lives* concerns the reform work of Ellen Collins. Riis wrote that "it is quite ten years since she bought three old tenements at the corner of Water and Roosevelt Streets, then as now one of the lowest localities in the city" [025-019]. Collins subsequently leased three more adjoining properties, creating a block of improved tenements that stood as "a decisive answer to all doubts" about whether it was possible "to make it pay to improve and make the most of the worst tenement property, even in the most wretched locality" [025-019]. The Water Street experiment was central to Riis's argument that tenement reform did not require charity — it could be made financially viable.

## Waterfront Tenement Life

The street's character reflected its proximity to the river. Riis described Water Street alongside Hamilton Street as part of a zone so close to the waterfront that "there is a tradition that once the river came clear up here to Hamilton Street, and boats were moored alongside it" [004-021]. The tenement known as "The Ship" — "famous for fifty years as a ramshackle tenement filled with the oddest crowd" — stood in the vicinity, its name possibly derived from its bewildering internal layout of "ups and downs of ladders parading as stairs, and its unexpected pitfalls" [004-021].

## Reform Significance

The Water Street model tenements represented a key piece of evidence in Riis's concluding argument that private capital, properly directed, could solve the tenement problem. By presenting Collins's investment as profitable, Riis sought to persuade the property-owning class that reform was in their financial interest — not merely a moral imperative. The corner of Water and Roosevelt Streets became, in his narrative, a demonstration site for the entire tenement reform movement.
