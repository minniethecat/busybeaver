The "tramp army" was Riis's term for the vast transient homeless population that flooded New York City in the decades after the Civil War. Composed largely of displaced veterans, itinerant laborers, and economic refugees, this population filled the city's lodging-houses and police station dormitories, forming a permanent underclass of the unhoused. Riis devoted the bulk of Chapter VII — "A Raid on the Stale-Beer Dives" — to documenting not only the dives themselves but the tramp population that sustained them.

## Origins

Riis traced the tramp population directly to the Civil War's aftermath. "The army of tramps that grew up after the disbandment of the armies" [006-003] was, at its origin, largely Irish — veterans who had fought for the Union and found themselves, after demobilization, without homes, jobs, or prospects. Over time the demographic composition diversified, but the connection between mass military mobilization, demobilization without social support, and mass homelessness remained central to Riis's analysis.

Riis addressed the origins question directly: "Whence these tramps, and why the tramping? are questions oftener asked than answered. Ill-applied charity and idleness answer the first query. They are the whence, and to a large extent the why also" [007-018]. His diagnosis was characteristically double-edged: charity was at fault for enabling dependency, but the tramp was also at fault for choosing idleness. "Once started on the career of a tramp, the man keeps to it because it is the laziest" [007-018]. This moral framing — the tramp as both victim of flawed charity and agent of his own degradation — reflected the contradictions of late-nineteenth-century reform thought.

## The Photographic Encounter

Riis included a revealing anecdote about photographing a tramp:

> "On one of my visits to 'the Bend' I came across a particularly ragged and disreputable tramp, who sat smoking his pipe on the rung of a ladder with such evident philosophic contentment in the busy labor of a score of ragpickers all about him, that I bade him sit for a picture, offering him ten cents for the job. He accepted the offer with hardly a nod, and sat patiently watching me from his perch until I got ready for work. Then he took the pipe out of his mouth and put it in his pocket, calmly saying: 'You mustn't think I'm smoking, if it's a pose you want. It wouldn't look well, you know.'" [007-015]

The tramp's self-consciousness about his photographic representation — his concern that smoking "wouldn't look well" — revealed an unexpected concern with respectability from a figure Riis otherwise portrayed as beyond shame. The anecdote complicated Riis's own framing, suggesting that even the tramp participated in the performance of social norms that Riis believed the tenement had destroyed.

## The Tramp and the Tough

Riis drew a sharp distinction between the tramp and the tough — two populations that shared the same tenement geography but represented different pathologies:

> "Tramps and toughs profess the same doctrine, that the world owes them a living, but from standpoints that tend in different directions. The tough does not become a tramp, save in rare instances, when old and broken. Of the tough the tramp doctrine that the world owes him a living makes a thief; of the tramp a coward." [007-018]

The tough was active, aggressive, and dangerous; the tramp was passive, indolent, and merely pitiable. "Numbers only make him bold unless he has to do with defenceless women" [007-019] — the tramp was a coward individually but could be dangerous in a crowd. This taxonomy was essential to Riis's reform program, which required distinguishing between populations that could be rehabilitated (tramps, through work) and those that required suppression (toughs, through policing).

## Scale and Institutional Response

The scale of the tramp population was staggering by contemporary standards. In a single raid on the stale-beer dives, Riis recorded that "two hundred and seventy-five tramps had been jammed into the cells to be arraigned next morning in the police court on the charge of vagrancy, with the certain prospect of six months 'on the Island'" [007-008]. The automatic sentence — six months on Blackwell's Island for vagrancy — was the city's primary mechanism for managing the tramp population, and it was a mechanism that, Riis noted, did nothing to address the underlying causes of tramping.

The conviction that the sentence was a foregone conclusion ("Of the sentence at least they were sure") [007-008] suggested that the criminal justice system had simply absorbed the tramp population into a perpetual cycle of arrest, incarceration, release, and re-arrest. The tramp was not being reformed; he was being warehoused.

## The Tramp's Economy

Riis documented a sophisticated informal economy that sustained the tramp population. The "sitters" — tramps who could not afford even the stale-beer dive's two-cent admission — "occasionally find a job in the saloons about Chatham and Pearl Streets on cold winter nights" [007-014], where they served as living charity props: "The barkeeper permits them to sit about the stove and by shivering invite the sympathy of transient customers. The dodge works well, especially about Christmas and election time" [007-014]. The tramp's poverty was, in this account, itself a commodity — his visible suffering could be monetized, however meagerly.

The tramp landlord was another figure in this ecology: "the Italian tramp landlord" [007-012] who specialized in housing the most desperate, extracting profit from a population that had nothing. The geographical boundary of trampdom was precisely defined: "Fourteenth Street is its limit. No real tramp crosses that frontier after nightfall and in the daytime only to beg" [007-012]. This invisible border — Fourteenth Street as the northern limit of the tramp's world — was one of the rigid spatial boundaries that organized Riis's tenement geography.
