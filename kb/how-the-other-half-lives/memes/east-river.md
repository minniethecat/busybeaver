# East River

## Geographical Boundary

The East River functioned in *How the Other Half Lives* as more than a geographical feature — it was the dividing line between two worlds. On the Manhattan side lay the tenement districts Riis spent twenty-five chapters documenting; on the Brooklyn side lay the hopeful counter-examples of model tenement construction that formed his concluding argument. Riis explicitly acknowledged this divide when discussing the work of [[alfred-t-white|Alfred T. White]]: though White's work "has been altogether across the East River, I regard as justly applying to New York as well" (025-033).

## The Waterfront

The East River waterfront was a zone of marginal existence. Riis described "an old boiler at the East River" where homeless boys gathered, and from [[first-avenue|First Avenue]] down to the river, "the Rag Gang and its allies have no need of sleeping quarters" (019-010). The ferry system — "some eighty-six thousand children and poor persons ferried back and forth across the East River" annually (022-014) — was a lifeline connecting the tenement poor to the islands where public institutions (hospitals, asylums, workhouses) were located.

## Symbolic Significance

In Riis's imagination, the East River was also a witness. Gazing across it from Brooklyn, he wrote: "Could its stones speak, what tales they might tell of the suffering of the tenement-house poor, of the struggle for existence" (022-002). The river served as a natural boundary between the problem (Manhattan's tenements) and the solution (Brooklyn's model housing), a geographical metaphor for the gap between the city as it was and the city as it could be.
