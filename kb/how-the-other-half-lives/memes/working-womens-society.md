The Working Women's Society was a labor reform organization that exposed the economic exploitation of women workers in late 19th-century New York. Its investigations into wages, working conditions, and employer practices provided Riis with some of his most damning evidence about the economic structure of tenement poverty. Unlike charitable organizations that offered relief, the Working Women's Society addressed root causes, arguing that women's poverty was not a moral failing but a structural outcome of systematically depressed wages.

## The Wage Investigation

The Society's signature contribution was its investigation into women's wages. At a public meeting shortly before Riis wrote, the Society reported a finding that he quoted at length: "It is a known fact that men's wages cannot fall below a limit upon which they can exist, but woman's wages have no limit, since the paths of shame are always open to her" [020-005]. This was a radical argument: women's economic vulnerability was not incidental but structural, maintained by the implicit threat that women who refused starvation wages could always turn to prostitution. The "paths of shame" functioned as a wage subsidy for employers, allowing them to pay women less than subsistence because the alternative — however degrading — set a floor that was lower than starvation.

## The Saleswomen's Case

The Society documented a specific case that Riis reproduced: saleswomen earning "wages averaging from $2 to $4.50 a week" had their pay "reduced by excessive fines, the employers placing a value upon time lost that is not given to services rendered" [020-006]. A woman who arrived five minutes late would be fined far more than she could earn in five minutes of work. The system of fines was a mechanism for employers to reclaim wages they had nominally promised, reducing actual take-home pay well below even the inadequate stated wage. Riis saw in this a parallel to the tenement rent system — both extracted maximum value from those least able to resist.

## Connection to the Tenement Economy

The Working Women's Society's findings illuminated a chain of economic exploitation that connected the department store counter to the tenement flat. Women who could not survive on the wages documented by the Society had limited options: they could supplement their income through [[sweater|home piece-work]] — sewing garments in tenement rooms for rates even lower than factory wages — or they could turn to the "paths of shame" that the Society identified as the unspoken wage floor. In either case, the tenement system was the backdrop: overcrowded flats served as sweatshops, and the anonymity of the tenement districts made possible both the exploitation and the alternative.

The Society's work also exposed the gendered dimension of tenement poverty. While male workers could, in theory, organize through craft unions — though in practice, immigrant laborers in the tenement districts had little access to such organizations — women workers faced the additional barrier of exclusion from male-dominated labor institutions. The Working Women's Society was, in part, an answer to this exclusion: an organization that took women's economic experience seriously on its own terms.

## Labor Advocacy and Reform

The Working Women's Society was part of a broader labor reform movement that included the Knights of Labor and the emerging trade union movement. Its focus on women workers was significant because women were largely excluded from the craft unions that organized skilled male workers. By documenting wage conditions and holding public meetings, the Society created pressure for legislative reform and built public support for labor organizing among the city's most exploited workers.
