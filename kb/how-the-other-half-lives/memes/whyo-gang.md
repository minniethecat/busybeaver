The Whyo Gang was one of the most infamous criminal organizations operating in late 19th-century New York City. Based in the Sixth Ward, the gang "absorbed the worst depravity of the Bend and what is left of the Five Points" [019-014], controlling a territory that encompassed some of the city's most impoverished and crime-ridden districts. The Whyos represented the apex of the gang ecology that Riis documented — not the street-level growler gangs of children, but a fully organized criminal enterprise with territorial control, internal hierarchy, and political influence.

## Criminal Dominance

Under its leader Driscoll, the Whyo Gang established a reign of terror marked by uninterrupted criminal activity. Riis records that Driscoll led "a life of uninterrupted and unavenged crimes, the recital of which made his father confessor turn pale, listening in the shadow of the scaffold, though many years of labor as chaplain of the Tombs had hardened him to such rehearsals" [019-014]. The detail of the prison chaplain — a man hardened by years of hearing criminals' confessions — turning pale at Driscoll's recitation conveys the exceptional scale of the Whyo leader's violence.

Driscoll had been a "power in the ward" [019-014], exercising influence through violence and intimidation that extended beyond the gang itself into the political and economic life of the Sixth Ward. This fusion of criminal power with local political influence was characteristic of Gilded Age urban machines, and the Whyos were among its most extreme manifestations.

## Key Figures

- **Driscoll**: Leader of the Whyo Gang, described by Riis as an "incomparable master-spirit" [019-015] whose execution for murder broke the gang's cohesion
- **The Paradise Park Gang**: Successor organization that arose from "the embers of the scattered Whyo Gang" [019-015], demonstrating the pattern of gang regeneration

## Dissolution

The gang's downfall came with the execution of Driscoll for murder. Riis explains that "the hanging of Driscoll broke up the Whyos because they were a comparatively small band, and, with the incomparable master-spirit gone, were unable to resist the angry rush of public indignation that followed the crowning outrage" [019-015]. The gang's dependence on a single leader made it vulnerable — without Driscoll's coordinating intelligence and force of personality, the Whyos could not maintain their territorial control against the combined pressure of police and public opinion.

## Aftermath

Despite the breakup, the Whyos' influence persisted through successor gangs. Riis notes that "the exploits of the Paradise Park Gang in the way of highway robbery showed last summer that the embers of the scattered Whyo Gang, upon the wreck of which it grew, were smouldering still" [019-015]. This pattern — one gang dispersing only to spawn another from its remnants — was, according to Riis, "the history of the passing away of famous gangs from time to time" [019-015]. The Whyos' story was not an exception but an illustration of the tenement districts' capacity to regenerate criminal organizations from the same environmental conditions that had produced the original gang.
