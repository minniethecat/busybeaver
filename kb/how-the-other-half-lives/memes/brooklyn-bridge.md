# Brooklyn Bridge

## The Great Bridge

The Brooklyn Bridge, completed in 1883, was one of the engineering marvels of the nineteenth century. By the time Jacob Riis published *How the Other Half Lives* in 1890, the bridge had been carrying traffic between Manhattan and Brooklyn for seven years. Riis referenced it as a geographical marker: "The road where it dives under the Brooklyn Bridge at Franklin Square" (004-003) was one of the routes into the tenement districts.

## A Bridge Between Two Worlds

In the symbolic geography of *How the Other Half Lives*, the Brooklyn Bridge connected two distinct housing realities. On the Manhattan side lay the tenement districts — the [[fourth-ward|Fourth Ward]], the [[seventh-ward|Seventh Ward]], and the other neighborhoods of overcrowding and deprivation that occupied most of the book's chapters. On the Brooklyn side lay the hopeful alternatives: [[alfred-t-white|Alfred T. White]]'s Riverside Buildings, the model tenements, and the argument that decent housing could be built at a profit. The bridge was, in effect, the physical connection between the problem and the solution.

## Franklin Square and the Tenement Border

The area around the Manhattan approach to the bridge — Franklin Square, where the elevated railroad "dives under the Brooklyn Bridge" (004-003) — was described by Riis as a borderland where the commercial prosperity of Lower Manhattan gave way to the tenement poverty of the Lower East Side. The contrast between the grandeur of the bridge and the squalor beneath it was a visual metaphor for the inequality that *How the Other Half Lives* was written to expose.
