## Immigration Background

The African-American community of 1890s New York was shaped not by transatlantic immigration but by internal migration from the Southern states following the Civil War. Riis opened his chapter on this community—Chapter XIII, "The Color Line in New York"—with the observation that "ever since the war New York has been receiving the overflow of colored population from the Southern cities," and that the migration had accelerated so rapidly in the last decade that the Black population was estimated to have doubled since the 1880 Census [013-004].

Unlike the European immigrant groups whose stories dominate *How the Other Half Lives*, Black New Yorkers were not foreigners arriving at Ellis Island but internal migrants crossing what Riis called a different kind of boundary: the color line.

Riis framed this distinction starkly, noting that "the name of the negro, alone among the world's races, is spelled with a small *n*"—a typographical convention he presented as evidence of a deeper social devaluation [013-003].

The economic context of this migration was grim. Riis observed that "trades of which he had practical control in his Southern home are not open to him here," and that while there was no formal industrial proscription, the practical barriers to skilled employment were nearly absolute [013-004]. The promise of Northern freedom had not translated into economic opportunity.

## Settlement Patterns

Black settlement in 1890s New York was defined by landlord-enforced segregation. Riis described this system with unusual directness:

> The color line must be drawn through the tenements to give the picture its proper shading. The landlord does the drawing, does it with an absence of pretence, a frankness of despotism, that is nothing if not brutal. The Czar of all the Russias is not more absolute upon his own soil than the New York landlord in his dealings with colored tenants. Where he permits them to live, they go; where he shuts the door, stay out. [013-002]

The primary area of Black settlement was the Thompson Street district, known colloquially—and disparagingly—as "Africa." The neighborhood centered on the Eighth Ward and included Sullivan Street and Bleecker Street, forming an enclave within the larger tenement landscape.

Riis noted that by the time of his writing, the neighborhood was already in transition: [[Italian Immigrants|Italians]] from the Mulberry Street Bend were moving in, creating a volatile mix of populations in what Riis termed "the borderland where the white and black races meet in common debauch" [013-014].

The housing economics of segregation were particularly perverse. Black tenants consistently paid higher rents than white tenants for inferior accommodations. Riis documented one case where "an increased rental of $17 per month, or $204 a year, and an advance of nearly thirteen and one-half percent on the gross rental 'in favor' of the colored tenant" was charged for the poorest class of rooms [013-008].

Despite this, real estate agents were "practically unanimous in the endorsement of the negro as a clean, orderly, and 'profitable' tenant" [013-006].

## In Riis's Account

Riis's chapter on Black New Yorkers is one of the most complex and contradictory in *How the Other Half Lives*. It contains some of his most pointed critiques of structural injustice, alongside some of his most troubling racial stereotyping.

**On structural discrimination.** Riis was unusually direct in condemning the landlord system that exploited Black tenants. He traced the mechanism of the "color line" to "this landlord despotism that has purposely confused the public mind, and for its own selfish ends is propping up a waning prejudice" [013-009].

This passage stands out in the book for naming the economic motive behind racial segregation—a rare moment of systemic analysis in a work that typically attributed poverty to individual or cultural failings.

**On cleanliness and character.** Riis drew a sharp contrast between Black tenants and the "lowest of the whites":

> Cleanliness is the characteristic of the negro in his new surroundings, as it was his virtue in the old. In this respect he is immensely the superior of the lowest of the whites, the Italians and the Polish Jews, below whom he has been classed in the past in the tenant scale. [013-005]

This comparison, while superficially praising Black cleanliness, served Riis's larger rhetorical purpose of establishing a hierarchy of "deserving" and "undeserving" poor—a framework that pervades the entire book.

**On cheerfulness and gambling.** Riis's portrait of Black character was shaped by contradictory stereotypes. He praised what he called the negro's "imperturbable cheerfulness" and philosophy "that has no room for repining," even in the face of poverty and injustice [013-010].

But he immediately pivoted to a condemnation of the "passion for gambling," specifically the game of policy—an illegal lottery that "reaps for its backers rich fortunes wherever colored people congregate" [013-011]. The juxtaposition—cheerful victim, irresponsible gambler—was characteristic of the era's paternalistic racial discourse.

**Signs of progress.** Riis closed his chapter on a cautiously hopeful note, reporting "evidence, simultaneous with the colored hegira from the low downtown tenements, of a movement toward the better sort of dwellings" [013-009]. He cited this as a sign that the color line was beginning to waver, though "the landlord deserves no credit" for the change [013-003].

The progress he observed was, in his telling, driven by Black agency and an awakening public conscience, not by any improvement in landlord behavior.

## Economic Role

The economic position of Black New Yorkers in the 1890s was defined by occupational exclusion. Trades that Black workers had dominated in Southern cities were largely closed to them in New York, not through formal legal barriers but through a combination of union exclusion, employer prejudice, and competition from European immigrant groups.

The real estate industry's assessment of Black tenants was revealing in its cynicism. A major real estate firm testified: "We would rather have negro tenants in our poorest class of tenements than the lower grades of foreign white people. We find the former cleaner than the latter, and they do not destroy the property so much" [013-006].

This preference, however, did not translate into fair treatment—it meant instead that Black tenants were desirable precisely because they could be charged premium rents for substandard housing while being less likely to complain or damage the property.

The most accessible forms of income for Black residents were service work, day labor, and domestic employment. The game of policy, while condemned by Riis, functioned as an informal economic institution within the community—a form of gambling that was also, for some, a last-ditch attempt at financial escape.

Riis acknowledged that "the wages of many a hard day's work are wasted by the negro" on policy and fortune-telling, but he also recorded the structural conditions that made such desperate gambles appealing [013-011].
