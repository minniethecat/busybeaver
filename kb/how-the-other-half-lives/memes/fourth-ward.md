## Overview

The Fourth Ward, located in Lower Manhattan near [[chatham-street|Chatham Square]] and the East River waterfront, was one of the original crucibles of the New York tenement system. Jacob Riis identified it as the historic birthplace of the housing crisis that would eventually consume the entire Lower East Side, calling it "the cradle of the tenement" [004-002]. In the decades before Riis wrote, the Fourth Ward had already become synonymous with the worst excesses of urban poverty—a dense warren of alleyways, rear tenements, and cellar dwellings that housed successive waves of immigrants in conditions of extraordinary degradation.

## Historical Significance

The Fourth Ward occupied a pivotal place in the geography of New York poverty. It was here, Riis argued, that the tenement first evolved from single-family homes subdivided for the poor into purpose-built barracks designed to extract maximum rent from minimum space. The ward's proximity to the docks and to the commercial center of the city made it a natural landing point for immigrants, who found work on the waterfront and housing in the ward's multiplying tenements.

> Down near Chatham Square, in the old Fourth Ward, where the cradle of the tenement stood, we shall find that we have arrived just in time to take a leaf from the landlord's book. [004-002]

The ward's decline was driven by the same forces that created slums throughout the city: landlords who subdivided old single-family houses into tenements, then built rear tenements in the backyards, then packed tenants into every available space at extortionate rents. By the time Riis investigated the district, its original buildings had been "improved" into some of the worst housing in the Western world.

### Origins of the Tenement System

The Fourth Ward was the proving ground for the architectural forms that would define the New York tenement. The transformation began with the old [[knickerbocker|Knickerbocker]] mansions along [[cherry-street|Cherry Street]] and the East River front — homes built for the city's Dutch and English elite in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. As trade expanded after the War of 1812 and immigration swelled the city's population, these comfortable dwellings were abandoned by their original owners and fell into the hands of real-estate agents. "Their comfortable dwellings in the once fashionable streets along the East River front fell into the hands of real-estate agents and boardinghouse keepers" [001-003]. The large rooms were partitioned into smaller ones without regard to light or ventilation, and the houses filled "from cellar to garret with a class of tenantry living from hand to mouth, loose in morals, improvident in habits, degraded, and squalid as beggary itself" [001-003].

The next stage was the construction of [[rear-tenement|rear tenements]] in the old gardens. Where the stolid Dutch burgher had grown his tulips, landlords built wooden rear houses, first two stories high, then carried up to three or four. The garden gate disappeared; the shell-paved walk became an alley; the remaining yard became a "court." Riis noted that "plenty such are yet to be found in the Fourth Ward, with here and there one of the original rear tenements" [001-004]. These structures, set behind the original street-facing houses and accessible only through narrow passages, were the original tenement form — darker, fouler, and more crowded than anything that had come before.

## The Slum Ecosystem

The Fourth Ward exemplified the self-reinforcing character of slum conditions. Its housing stock, degraded by decades of overcrowding and neglect, attracted the poorest tenants. These tenants, in turn, could pay only the lowest rents, which gave landlords no incentive to invest in maintenance or improvements. The cycle spiraled downward, generation after generation, spreading outward to infect the entire East Side:

> From the Fourth Ward slums had crept up, and the Five Points the whole of the East Side, to bone and muscle and sinew of the city, its wealth. [002-008]

The ward was also a center of the vice economy that flourished in slum districts. Saloons multiplied on every corner; [[lodging-house|lodging-houses]] packed the desperate into fetid common rooms; the streets themselves became markets for every variety of informal and illicit commerce. When Riis inquired of an agent about one particularly notorious Fourth Ward alley, he was told: "One hundred and forty families, one hundred Irish, thirty-eight Italian, and two that spoke the German tongue" [003-002]. Not a single native-born American lived in the entire court.

### Crime and Violence

The Fourth Ward had an extraordinary reputation for criminality. Superintendent Murray, who as captain of the Oak Street squad had secured convictions aggregating five hundred and thirty years of penal servitude in seven months, held the opinion that "the Fourth Ward, even in the last twenty years, has turned out more criminals than all the rest of the city together" [004-017]. The ward's sewers — enormous vaulted tunnels in which "a man may walk upright the full distance of the block and into the Cherry Street sewer" [004-014] — served as runways and storehouses for the Swamp Angels, a notorious gang of thieves who used the underground passages to evade police pursuit. The Cherry Street tough, Riis wrote, was "all-pervading" [004-017].

The violence was routine and often deadly. Riis describes a "can racket" — a drunken brawl — in one Cherry Street alley that left a man "cut to pieces" and dead in the hospital, with preparations for a wake underway upstairs [004-016]. Another "can racket" a week earlier, just blocks away, ended in a "general fusillade" during which one of the combatants fell off a roof and was killed. There was "the usual wake and nothing more was heard of it" [004-016].

### Gotham Court and Reform Failure

At the heart of the ward's housing stock stood [[gotham-court|Gotham Court]], a double row of five-story tenements built back-to-back under a common roof, extending two hundred and thirty-four feet back from Cherry Street [004-013]. Originally built in 1851 by a benevolent Quaker as a model tenement, it rapidly degraded. By 1862, a sanitary official counted 146 cases of sickness in the court including "all kinds of infectious disease," and of 138 children born there in less than three years, 61 had died, mostly before age one [004-014]. During the last great cholera epidemic, the mortality rate in Gotham Court reached 195 per 1,000 inhabitants [004-013].

The court became an emblem of the limits of private benevolence. Good intentions, Riis argued, could not overcome the structural incentives of the tenement market: maximize rentable space, minimize maintenance. The policeman was "always on duty in Gotham Court, half a stone's throw away" [004-013], a permanent acknowledgment that the model tenement had become something else entirely.

## Ethnic Succession

The Fourth Ward's ethnic composition shifted with each wave of immigration. Originally home to native-born Americans and earlier German and Irish settlers, by the 1890s it had become overwhelmingly immigrant. The Irish had largely moved on—some to better housing on the West Side, some up the economic ladder as landlords and saloon-keepers. In their place came Italians, who reproduced the pattern: the poorest taking the worst housing, beginning the slow climb upward that their predecessors had made.

This ethnic succession was a defining feature of the ward's history and of the tenement system more broadly. Each group arrived at the bottom and, if successful, moved out and up—leaving their former housing to be filled by the next, even poorer group. The housing stock itself, meanwhile, only deteriorated further with each transition.

| Period | Dominant Group | Housing Condition |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Pre-1840 | Native-born Americans | Single-family homes |
| 1840s–1860s | Irish, German immigrants | Converted tenements |
| 1870s–1880s | Mixed immigrant population | Purpose-built tenements, rear houses |
| 1890s | Irish, Italian, mixed | Overcrowded tenements, "cradle of the tenement" |

Gotham Court exemplified the ethnic dynamics of the ward in Riis's time. By then its population was "about equally divided between the Celtic and the Italian stock" [004-013], and the building's iron bars and permanent police presence were attributed by Irish tenants to the two German families who "made trouble all the time." A Chinese passerby offered a different view: "Lem Ilish velly bad" [004-013]. Gotham Court, Riis noted, had been "the entering wedge for the Italian element, who until recently had not attained a foothold in the Fourth Ward, but are now trailing across Chatham Street from their stronghold in 'the Bend' in ever increasing numbers, seeking, according to their wont, the lowest level" [004-013].

### The Saloon Economy

The Fourth Ward's saloons were central to its social and political life. Riis documents that the ward "points with pride to the honorable record of the conductors of its 'Tub of Blood,' and a dozen barrooms with less startling titles" [018-005]. These establishments were not marginal businesses but pillars of local power, their proprietors often transitioning into political office. The saloon was, Riis argued, "the one bright and cheery and humanly decent spot to be found" in many tenement blocks [018-003], a bitter admission that underscored the absence of any competing civic institution. The Irish landlord class that emerged in the ward owed its prosperity directly to the saloon trade, collecting rents from tenement property purchased with liquor profits [003-003].

## Legacy

By the time of Riis's writing, the Fourth Ward had largely been superseded in notoriety by newer and denser slum districts—[[five-points|the Five Points]], [[the-bend|the Bend]], the lower East Side tenement blocks. But its historical importance remained. It was in the Fourth Ward that the pattern was first established: the conversion of housing into a speculative commodity, the extraction of maximum rent from minimum space, the creation of a permanent underclass housed in conditions that made health, privacy, and dignity impossible.

The ward's legacy extended beyond its own boundaries. The model of tenement exploitation pioneered there spread uptown and across the East River, replicating itself in every district where immigrants settled and landlords saw profit: "In fifty years they have crept up from the Fourth Ward slums and the Five Points the whole length of the island, and have polluted the Annexed District to the Westchester line" [002-008]. Understanding the Fourth Ward, Riis believed, was essential to understanding the entire tenement problem.
