# Hester Street

## In the Heart of Jewtown

Hester Street ran through the center of what Riis called "Jewtown" — the densely packed Jewish quarter of the Lower East Side. "Turning the corner into Hester Street," Riis wrote, "we stumble upon a nest of cloakmakers" (011-014). The street was synonymous with the [[sweatshop|sweatshop]] economy that dominated the neighborhood's tenements.

## The Pushcart Economy

Hester Street was famous for its pushcart markets — a sprawling outdoor bazaar where everything from food to clothing to household goods was sold from handcarts. For Riis, the pushcart market was both a sign of immigrant entrepreneurial energy and a symptom of the overcrowding that made formal retail impossible. The street-level chaos of Hester Street reflected the tenement chaos above it.

## A Nest of Cloakmakers

The "cloakmakers" Riis found on Hester Street were part of the garment manufacturing system that turned Jewish tenement apartments into factories. The cloak trade was one of the most exploitative branches of the [[sweatshop|sweating system]], with seasonal demand producing cycles of intense overwork followed by unemployment. Riis's visit to Hester Street was part of his broader documentation of the economic conditions that the [[tenement|tenement]] system enabled.
