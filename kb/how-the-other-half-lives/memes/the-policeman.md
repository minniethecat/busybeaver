# The Policeman

## Biography

The policeman appears in *How the Other Half Lives* in two guises: the ordinary patrolman walking his beat through the tenement streets, and the "sanitary policeman" — a specialized health-department officer tasked with inspecting tenement conditions. Both were products of the same political system that produced the ward boss, and Riis is careful to distinguish between individual officers who tried to do their duty and a system that made honest policing nearly impossible.

## Role in the Tenements

The sanitary policeman was Riis's frontline reformer. These officers conducted the inspections that generated the statistics Riis relied on, and their reports were the raw material of the reform argument. Riis describes how "the sanitary policeman locates the bulk of his work" in the Bend, the worst slum district, and documents their midnight inspections of tenements below Houston Street. (006-026, 024-013)

The ordinary beat patrolman had a more complex role. He was the most visible representative of city government in the tenement districts, and his relationship with residents ranged from protective to predatory. The police station lodging-rooms, where homeless men could sleep on the floor for free, were an informal charity administered by patrolmen. But the police also collected graft from saloons, ignored sanitary violations in buildings whose owners paid tribute, and participated in the same corrupt political machine that frustrated reform.

Riis accompanied the police on the stale-beer dive raid (Chapter VII), and his account is carefully balanced — he admires the officers' courage and despairs at the conditions they enforce. The policeman was trapped in the same system as the tenement dweller: doing a necessary job in impossible conditions.

## Multiple Perspectives

The policeman was seen through starkly different lenses by the different populations of the tenement world. To the middle-class reformer, the patrolman was the natural ally of reform — the boots on the ground who could enforce housing codes and close illegal dives. Organizations like the Charity Organization Society and the Board of Health relied on police cooperation for their inspections, and the sanitary policeman was created precisely to bridge the gap between police authority and public-health expertise.

To the tenement dweller, the policeman was a more ambiguous figure. For families threatened by eviction or street crime, the patrolman could be a protector — the only representative of law in neighborhoods where formal institutions were scarce. But for the saloon-keeper forced to pay monthly tribute, or the pushcart peddler shaken down for a license fee he could not afford, the policeman was a predator in uniform. Riis captures this duality without resolving it: the same officer who let a homeless man sleep in the station-house corridor might, the next night, arrest a tenement mother for selling beer without a license.

The ward boss and the political machine saw the policeman as a patronage asset. Police assignments, promotions, and disciplinary actions were all controlled by Tammany Hall, and an officer's career depended on his loyalty to the ward boss. The sanitary policeman, detached from the regular force and reporting to the Health Department, was resented by the political machine precisely because he operated outside the patronage network.

From the landlord's perspective, the policeman — especially the sanitary policeman — was a threat. Every inspection could generate violations, fines, and adverse publicity. Landlords cultivated relationships with local precinct captains to ensure that inspections were announced in advance or, better yet, not conducted at all. The alliance between landlords and the police was a structural feature of tenement governance that frustrated every reform effort.

## Legacy

The dual image of the policeman — part protector, part predator — prefigures recurring debates about policing in poor communities. Riis's account is notable for resisting both the romanticization of the beat cop and the demonization of the police. His reform prescription — professionalize the force, break its ties to the political machine, and give officers the legal tools to enforce housing codes — became the template for Progressive-era police reform.
