# Pauperism

## Definition

In Riis's vocabulary, *pauperism* was not mere poverty but a distinct social condition: chronic, self-perpetuating dependency on charity, often transmitted across generations. The Charity Organization Society, whose statistics Riis relied on, distinguished carefully between the "worthy" poor (temporarily destitute due to illness or unemployment) and "paupers" (those who had made dependency a way of life). Riis reports that of 5,169 cases investigated, "875 were unworthy of relief, or 17 percent" — the professional pauper class. (021-002) This categorization, which reads harshly to modern eyes, reflected the Progressive-era conviction that indiscriminate charity created as much misery as it relieved.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

Riis devotes Chapter XXI ("Pauperism in the Tenements") to documenting the scale and character of chronic dependency. The statistics are staggering: in eight years, 135,595 families registered as asking or receiving charity, and "for five years past one person in every ten who died in this city was buried in the Potter's Field." (021-002)

The pauper barracks on West Thirty-eighth Street embodied the institutional response — a kind of warehouse for the destitute that Riis described as "a flat in the pauper barracks." (021-005) The barracks concentrated paupers in conditions that, Riis argued, made escape nearly impossible.

The structural driver was clear to Riis:

> "The truth is that pauperism grows in the tenements as naturally as weeds in a garden." (021-007)

He saw pauperism not as a moral failing but as a predictable output of the tenement environment — overcrowding, disease, irregular employment, and the constant visibility of degradation combined to make dependency seem like the only rational choice.

Riis also documented the corrupting effect of pauperism on families. In one case study, he describes "how thoroughly corrupting pauperism is in such a setting" — a family where children were trained to beg, perpetuating dependency into the next generation. (021-013) This was precisely what the Charity Organization Society meant by "pauperism" as distinct from poverty: it was the condition that reproduced itself.

## Significance

Pauperism is the endpoint of Riis's argument about the tenement's moral effects. If the tenement bred disease, crime, and vice, its ultimate product was the pauper — the person so broken by the environment that they could no longer imagine an alternative. Riis's statistical approach (COS data, burial records, case classification) was itself a reform tool: by quantifying pauperism, he made it a social problem rather than an individual moral failing.

The chapter on pauperism also contains Riis's most pointed critique of private charity. Ill-directed giving, he argued, was a cause of pauperism, not its cure. The 17 percent of cases classified as "unworthy" represented not irredeemable human beings but a system failure — charity that enabled rather than relieved.
