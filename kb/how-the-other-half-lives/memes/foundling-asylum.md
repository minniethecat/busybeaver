# Foundling Asylum

## Founding and Mission

The New York Foundling Asylum was established in 1869 by the Sisters of Charity, a Catholic religious order, to care for abandoned infants and young children. By the time Riis wrote *How the Other Half Lives*, the asylum had become one of the city's largest child welfare institutions. Riis described it as one of the first lines of defense for tenement children: "Foremost among these barriers is the Foundling Asylum. It stands at the very outset of life's journey" (016-002).

## Riis's Assessment

Riis positioned the Foundling Asylum within his broader catalog of institutions that dealt with the consequences of tenement poverty. Unlike the almshouse or the workhouse, which he regarded with deep suspicion, the asylum represented a genuinely humane intervention — though Riis also noted that the sheer volume of abandoned children (many left on the asylum's doorstep by mothers who could not afford to keep them) testified to the inadequacy of preventive measures.

## The Scale of the Problem

The asylum's very existence pointed to one of Riis's central arguments: that tenement conditions destroyed families. Mothers who left infants at the asylum were not, in Riis's telling, heartless — they were driven by economic desperation and the impossibility of caring for a child in a single-room tenement while working twelve-hour days. The asylum was a necessary institution, but Riis insisted that its necessity was itself an indictment of the housing conditions that made it so.
