## Overview

Ellen Collins was a nineteenth-century New York housing reformer active during the 1880s, whose work on Water Street provided Jacob Riis with the central evidentiary case for his argument that tenement reform could succeed on purely business terms. Riis presented her as the decisive counterexample to the prevailing belief that the slums were irredeemable — a woman who bought properties in one of the worst neighborhoods in the city, improved them thoroughly, treated her tenants fairly, and earned a steady return on her capital. Her experiment predated the organized model-tenement movement in New York and stands as one of the earliest documented demonstrations that philanthropic housing could be financially sustainable.

## The Water Street Experiment

Approximately ten years before the publication of *How the Other Half Lives*, Collins purchased three old tenements at the corner of [[water-street|Water Street]] and [[roosevelt-street|Roosevelt Street]], a location Riis described as "one of the lowest localities in the city" (025-019). She subsequently leased three more adjoining properties, bringing her holdings to six houses containing forty-five families (025-019). The buildings had previously been a "reputed haunt of thieves," but under her management they became "the most orderly in the neighborhood" (025-019). Riis wrote unequivocally: "The example set by Miss Ellen Collins in her Water Street houses will always stand as a decisive answer to all doubts on this point" — the point being whether it was possible to make money by improving the worst tenement property (025-019).

Collins's first physical intervention was to let light into the hallways, a simple act that Riis credited with causing the disappearance, "as if by magic," of the refuse heaps that had accumulated beside the sinks (025-019). The houses were then thoroughly refitted with proper plumbing, marking an investment in sanitation that exceeded the bare minimum expected of tenement landlords of the period (025-019). She installed a competent janitor to maintain order when she was not present, and over time the properties required so little intervention that "the houses seemed to run themselves in the groove once laid down" (025-019).

## Philosophy and Business Model

Collins articulated her governing principle in a phrase that Riis elevated to the status of a reform mantra: "fair play between tenant and landlord" (025-019). She set rents as low as was consistent with the idea of a business investment that must return a reasonable interest — explicitly rejecting the charity model in favor of reciprocal obligation (025-019). In a direct interview with Riis in August of the year before publication, she reported her financial results: "I have had six and even six and three-quarters percent on the capital invested; on the whole, you may safely say five and a half percent. This I regard as entirely satisfactory" (025-019).

Notably, Collins refused to rent the corner store as a [[saloon]], even though doing so would have increased her income considerably. The space was instead occupied by a butcher (025-019). This decision aligned her business practice with the temperance-reform sensibility that ran through much of the tenement reform movement and echoed the policy of the [[landlord|Improved Dwellings Association]], which also prohibited liquor sales on its premises (025-025). The tenants themselves contributed to the physical improvement of the property: each spring they provided flowers for the courtyard beds, "and take all the more pride in them because they are their own" (025-019).

## A Deliberate Experiment on the Poorest Class

A critical dimension of Collins's work was its deliberate targeting of the most challenging tenant population. Riis noted that "Miss Collins's tenants are distinctly of the poorest" and that "her purpose was to experiment with this class, and her experiment has been more than satisfactory" (025-019). This was not an accident of location — Collins had chosen to prove that even the poorest tenants, often blamed by landlords for the degradation of tenement property, would respond to decent conditions and fair treatment with orderly behavior.

The results bore out her hypothesis. Clothes could be left hanging on lines all night without fear of theft, and the flowerbeds in the shared yard — used by children from the six houses and from the whole block — remained undisturbed (025-019). Collins told Riis that there "has never been any need of putting up a bill," meaning she had never needed to post eviction notices or rules enforcement placards (025-019). While some of "the most refractory tenants" had left when the new regime was instituted, "a very considerable proportion stayed, conforming readily to the new rules, and are there yet" at the time of Riis's writing (025-019).

## Legacy and Significance

Riis positioned Collins's experience as the capstone evidence in his concluding chapter, "How the Case Stands." He argued that her work demonstrated "what can be accomplished with the worst possible material, by the sort of personal interest in the poor that alone will meet their real needs" (025-020). He contrasted this personal investment with the inadequacy of charity alone: "All the charity in the world, scattered with the most lavish hand, will not take its place" (025-020). He elevated "fair play" to a universal principle: "the key, too long mislaid, that unlocks the door to success everywhere as it did for Miss Collins" (025-020).

Collins stood alongside [[gotham-court|Gotham Court]]'s reform-minded owners and the later model-tenement builders as a member of what Riis called the "pioneers" — wealthy owners who made it "the personal business of wealthy owners with time to spare to look after their tenants" (025-021). Riis noted that she "has not lacked imitators whose experience has been akin to her own," suggesting her Water Street experiment had already begun to influence practice beyond her own properties by the time he wrote (025-020). Her work on Water Street represented one prong of Riis's three-part reform strategy — the remodeling and improvement of existing old houses — which he argued alongside new model-tenement construction and legal enforcement as the effective paths out of the tenement crisis (025-015, 025-016).
