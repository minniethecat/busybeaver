## Immigration Background

The Irish were the first great wave of mass immigration to transform New York City in the nineteenth century. Fleeing the Great Famine of 1845–1852 and generations of British colonial rule, Irish immigrants arrived in numbers that dwarfed all subsequent groups. By the 1890s, the Irish had ascended from their initial status as despised newcomers to become the dominant political force in the city, even as newer immigrant groups—Italians, Russian Jews, Chinese—crowded into the tenement districts the Irish had pioneered.

The era of "No Irish Need Apply" was still within living memory when Riis wrote. He recorded a conversation with an old inhabitant who was "sighing for the 'good old days' when the legend 'no Irish need apply' was familiar in the advertising columns of the newspapers" [003-002]. That man, representative of the vanished native-born population of lower New York, could not say where his countrymen had gone: "Some went to California in '49, some to the war and never came back. The rest, I expect, have gone to heaven, or somewhere. I don't see them 'round here" [003-002]. In their place, Riis observed, had come "this queer conglomerate mass of heterogeneous elements, ever striving and working like whiskey and water in one glass, and with the like result: final union and a prevailing taint of whiskey" [003-003].

The [[draft-riots|Draft Riots]] of 1863 marked a violent turning point. The riots, fueled largely by Irish working-class anger at conscription laws that allowed the wealthy to buy exemption, drove the city's African American population out of their lower Manhattan neighborhoods. Riis noted that a church on Mulberry Street, "built originally for the worship of staid New Yorkers of the 'old stock,' was engulfed by the colored tide, when the draft-riots drove the negroes out of reach of Cherry Street and the Five Points" [003-011]. Within a decade, the Italian wave had overtaken that same church, and "today the arms of United Italy adorn its front" [003-011].

Jacob Riis captured the Irish trajectory with characteristic irony. The Irish, he observed, had once faced the same nativist hostility they now directed at newer arrivals: "The once unwelcome Irishman has been followed in his turn by the Italian, the Russian Jew, and the Chinaman, and has himself taken a hand at opposition, quite as bitter and quite as ineffectual, against these later hordes" [003-003]. Where the Irish had been pushed out of the worst slums by denser immigrant swarms, they had exacted a distinctive form of revenge: "Victorious in defeat over his recent as over his more ancient foe, the one who opposed his coming no less than the one who drove him out, he dictates to both their politics, and, secure in possession of the offices, returns the native his greeting with interest, while collecting the rents of the Italian whose house he has bought with the profits of his saloon" [003-003].

## Settlement Patterns

On Riis's imagined ethnic map of New York, the Irish colored the city green—and green dominated the West Side tenement districts, just as blue (German) prevailed on the East Side [003-011]. This West Side concentration was not absolute; Irish communities interspersed with every other ethnic group, reflecting what Riis called "the true cosmopolitan immigrant":

> The Irishman is the true cosmopolitan immigrant. All-pervading, he shares his lodging with perfect impartiality with the Italian, the Greek, and the "Dutchman," yielding only to sheer force of numbers, and objects equally to them all. [003-011]

The Irish presence spanned the entire class spectrum of tenement life. At the bottom was the "low Irish"—the "sediment, the product of more than a generation in the city's slums, that, as distinguished from the larger body of his class, justly ranks at the foot of tenement dwellers" [003-006]. At the top were Irish landlords and saloon-keepers who had climbed the economic ladder within the tenement system. Between them lay "the great body of hardworking Irish and German immigrants and their descendants, who accept naturally the conditions of tenement life, because for them there is nothing else in New York" [014-013].

| Settlement Area | Character |
|-----------------|-----------|
| West Side tenement districts | Primary concentration, "green" on Riis's ethnic map |
| Fourth Ward | One hundred and forty families, one hundred Irish [003-002] |
| Sixth Ward ("Bloody Sixth") | Historic Irish stronghold, later ceded to Italians |
| Along both rivers | Wherever "the Irish brogue is heard" [014-015] |
| Hell's Kitchen | West Side slum district with heavy Irish population |

The Sixth Ward, known as the "Bloody Sixth," represented the historic heart of Irish tenement life. "Three-fourths of the population of the 'Bloody Sixth' Ward were then Irish," Riis recorded, looking back some twenty years [006-003]. The ward contained both the infamous [[five-points|Five Points]] slum—the original Irish immigrant ghetto—and the Mulberry Street Bend, "foul core of New York's slums" [006-002]. By the time Riis wrote, the Italian influx had transformed the Sixth Ward. "The army of tramps that grew up after the disbandment of the armies in the field, and has kept up its muster roll, together with the inrush of the Italian tide, have ever since opposed a stubborn barrier to all efforts at permanent improvement" [006-003].

The Irishman's housing trajectory was distinctive. Unlike the German, who saved money and fled the tenement forever, the successful Irishman "soon assumes to manage without improving things," remaining within the tenement world as a landlord or political operator [014-015]. Riis traced this pattern to the Irishman's unique vulnerability: "As already said, the Celt falls most readily victim to tenement influences since shantytown and its original free-soilers have become things of the past" [014-015]. The shantytowns—makeshift communities of squatters' shacks that once ringed the city—had offered the Irish a rough independence. Their disappearance forced the Irish wholly into the tenement system, with corrosive effects on the national character.

## In Riis's Account

Riis's portrayal of the Irish is the most ambivalent of any ethnic group in *How the Other Half Lives*. He was himself a Danish immigrant, and his view of the Irish mixes genuine admiration for their political skill and resilience with a Protestant reformer's dismay at their saloon culture and resistance to the domestic virtues he prized.

**The political Irish.** Riis acknowledged without reservation Irish dominance of the city's political machinery. The Irishman's "genius runs to public affairs rather than domestic life; wherever he is mustered in force the saloon is the gorgeous centre of political activity" [003-013]. This political ascendancy, built through Tammany Hall and the ward system, made the Irish the brokers of power for every subsequent immigrant group. Even the courts reflected Irish electoral power: "In the civil courts, the judges of which are elected in their districts, the tenant-voter has solid ground to stand upon at last" [014-033]. With forty voters in a single tenement house and the landlord having only one vote, the balance of power in eviction cases tilted decisively toward the tenant.

**The landlord Irish.** One of Riis's most memorable anecdotes concerns an Irish landlord's response to an Italian tenant who dared to ask for a rent reduction:

> "'Damma, man!' he said; 'if you speaka thata way to me, I fira you and your things in the streeta.'" And the frightened Italian paid the rent. [003-005]

Riis was quick to contextualize this behavior: "In justice to the Irish landlord it must be said that like an apt pupil he was merely showing forth the result of the schooling he had received, reenacting, in his own way, the scheme of the tenements. It is only his frankness that shocks" [003-006]. The Irish landlord was doing exactly what had been done to him.

The landlord system extended to individual Health Department cases. An inspector found "an Italian family paying a man with a Celtic name twenty-five dollars a month for three small rooms in a ramshackle rear tenement—more than twice what they were worth" [003-003]. The Sicilian tenant had already tried asking for a reduction, with predictable results.

**The tenement's toll.** Riis believed the Irish suffered disproportionately from tenement life: "The Irishman does not naturally take kindly to tenement life, though with characteristic versatility he adapts himself to its conditions at once. It does violence, nevertheless, to the best that is in him, and for that very reason of all who come within its sphere soonest corrupts him" [003-006].

**The "low Irish."** Riis drew a sharp distinction between the mass of hardworking Irish immigrants and what he called the "sediment"—those broken by generations of slum life [003-006]. This lowest stratum, he believed, represented not the Irish character but its corruption by the tenement environment.

**Tragic cases.** In Chapter XIV, Riis recounted the story of an Irish laborer driven insane by poverty after sewer gases destroyed his health. With seven children and only $2.50 a week coming in, the man sharpened a hatchet, determined that "the town should not take the children" [014-028]. Riis's telling emphasizes both the desperation of tenement poverty and the resilience of the wife, who recounted the story "with unconscious eloquence."

**The funeral tradition.** Riis identified the expensive funeral as "a distinctively Irish inheritance" that had taken root among all tenement dwellers [014-036]. He noted with exasperation that "the hoards of a whole lifetime of hard work and self denial" were often "squandered on the empty show of a ludicrous funeral parade," and that the practice persisted despite decades of condemnation by press and pulpit [014-036]. Even the political machine exploited the tradition: "the genius of politics has seen its way clear to make capital out of the dead voter as well as of the living, by making him the means of a useful 'show of strength' and count of noses" [014-036].

## The Tenement's Unique Corruption of the Celt

Riis advanced a specific and controversial theory: that the tenement system was uniquely destructive to the Irish character. While other immigrant groups suffered from tenement conditions, the Irish were uniquely vulnerable. This argument appears most forcefully in the chapter on street beggars:

> The tenement, especially its lowest type, appears to possess a peculiar affinity for the worse nature of the Celt, to whose best and strongest instincts it does violence, and soonest and most thoroughly corrupts him. [021-010]

The statistics Riis cited bore out this grim assessment. Among the city's street beggars, "the Irish head the list with fifteen percent, and the native American is only a little way behind with twelve percent, while the Italian, who in his own country turns beggary into a fine art, has less than two percent" [021-010]. The "native" twelve percent, Riis explained, represented "the result of this process, the hereditary beggar of the second or third generation in the slums" [021-010]—a direct legacy of the Irish degradation through tenement life.

The contrast with the Italian was instructive. The tenement, Riis argued, "has no power to corrupt the Italian, who comes here in almost every instance to work—no beggar would ever emigrate from anywhere unless forced to do so. He is distinctly on its lowest level from the start" [021-010]. The Irishman, by contrast, arrived with higher aspirations and a more sensitive nature; the tenement broke something in him that it could not break in those who had never hoped for better.

In Gotham Court, one of the most notorious tenements in the Fourth Ward, the population was "about equally divided between the Celtic and the Italian stock," a circumstance that "accounts for the iron bars and the policeman" [004-013]. A Chinese resident summed up the ethnic dynamics with blunt economy: "Lem Ilish velly bad" [004-013].

## Economic Role

The Irish economic trajectory in New York was one of gradual ascent from unskilled labor to political and commercial power. Riis charted this progression with a memorable observation: "The Irish hod-carrier in the second generation has become a bricklayer, if not the Alderman of his ward" [003-009].

The Irish economic profile was diverse:

| Occupation | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| Unskilled labor (hod-carriers, dock workers) | First-generation entry point |
| Skilled trades (bricklayers) | Second-generation advancement [003-009] |
| Saloon-keeping | Economic and political base |
| Landlording | "Bought with the profits of his saloon" [003-003] |
| Political office (Aldermen, ward bosses) | Ultimate aspiration for the successful |
| Domestic service | Women's employment |
| Police and fire departments | Traditional Irish-American career paths |

The saloon was the key institution of Irish economic mobility. It served simultaneously as a business, a political clubhouse, and a community center. The profits from the saloon purchased tenement buildings; the tenement rents funded further political advancement. Riis noted dryly that "the German struggles vainly to learn his trick; his Teutonic wit is too heavy, and the political ladder he raises from his saloon usually too short or too clumsy to reach the desired goal" [003-012].

The German immigrant provided the essential counterpoint to Irish economic patterns. Where the Irishman's thrift and shrewdness led him "along the plane of the tenement," the German "makes the most of his tenement, and it should be added that whenever and as soon as he can save up money enough, he gets out and never crosses the threshold of one again" [003-013]. The German's "strong love for flowers, which not all the tenements on the East Side have power to smother" [014-015], symbolized a domestic virtue that the Irish, with their genius for public affairs, had failed to cultivate.

Irish women found work in domestic service and the garment trades, while Irish men dominated the unskilled labor markets—the docks, the sewers, the construction gangs. The danger of this work was a constant theme. The sewer laborer driven mad by "poisonous gases" [014-028] was not an isolated case but representative of the physical toll exacted by the only work available to unskilled Irish immigrants. As Riis observed, the tenement economy ran on a brutal calculus: "In the home the child's place is usurped by the lodger, who performs the service of the Irishman's pig—pays the rent" [010-008].

## Comparisons with Other Immigrant Groups

Riis used the Irish as a benchmark against which he measured every other immigrant group. The [[bohemian-immigrants|Bohemian]] was described as "the Irishman of Central Europe, with all his genius and his strong passions, with the same bitter traditions of landlord-robbery, perpetuated here where he thought to forget them; like him ever and on principle in the opposition, 'agin the government' wherever he goes" [012-013]. The comparison was both a compliment—acknowledging the Irishman's passionate nature and political instincts—and a warning about transplanted grievances.

The Italian tenant was prized by landlords precisely because he "makes less trouble" than "the contentious Irishman or the order-loving German" [005-002]. This passivity, however, came at a cost: the Italian was "content to live in a pigsty and submits to robbery at the hands of the rent-collector without murmur" [005-002]. The Irishman's contentiousness, however troublesome to landlords, reflected a refusal to accept degradation that Riis grudgingly respected.

Among the city's juvenile street gangs, the Irish-American presence was significant. The gangs of the nineteenth ward were "made up of the American-born sons of English, Irish, and German parents" [019-003], representing the second generation's drift from immigrant striving to street criminality.

In the tenement districts, "the picture is faithful enough to stand for its class wherever along both rivers the Irish brogue is heard" [014-015]. For Riis, the Irish tenement experience was not merely one ethnic story among many; it was the archetype, the original pattern from which all other immigrant housing miseries derived.
