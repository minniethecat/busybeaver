Baby farming was the practice of taking in unwanted infants for a fee and then systematically neglecting or starving them to death — a form of child-murder that Riis identified as one of the most horrifying byproducts of tenement poverty in late nineteenth-century New York.

## In Riis's Account

Riis describes baby farming in stark terms, calling it "an infinitely more fiendish, if to surface appearances less deliberate, plan of child-murder than desertion" that "has flourished in New York for years under the title of baby-farming." He translates the euphemism bluntly: "The name, put into plain English, means starving babies to death" [016-006]. The practice was driven by women who took in infants — often from unwed mothers or families too poor to keep them — collected a lump-sum payment, and then provided so little care that the children died of neglect.

The law attempted to combat the practice by requiring the registration of all baby farms, but Riis was scathing about the effectiveness of this approach: "As well might it require all persons intending murder to register" [016-006]. The enforcement problem was structural — baby farms operated in the same unregulated tenement spaces where overcrowding, anonymity, and official indifference made it nearly impossible to monitor what happened to individual children.

Riis also documents the related phenomenon of insurance fraud involving children's lives. He reports the case of a woman tried for "incredible cruelty in her treatment of a stepchild," where "a strong suspicion" existed "that a pitifully small amount of insurance on the child's life" had motivated the abuse [016-007]. This connection between baby farming and the nascent life insurance industry — parents insuring children they could not afford to raise — exposed a dark intersection of market incentives and desperate poverty.

## The Economics of Infant Death

Baby farming was, at root, an economic transaction shaped by the constraints of tenement poverty. Unwed mothers in the tenement districts — often domestic servants or factory workers who had been dismissed upon discovery of their pregnancy — faced a brutal calculus: they could not keep a child without losing the wages on which their survival depended, and they could not surrender it to any institution that would guarantee its care. The baby farmer offered a solution: for a one-time payment — typically between ten and fifty dollars — she would "adopt" the infant, relieving the mother of a burden she could not carry.

The farmer's economic incentive was obvious. A lump-sum payment of even ten dollars was more than a tenement woman could earn in a month of work. But the ongoing cost of feeding and caring for an infant — even at the minimal standard of tenement life — would exhaust that payment within weeks. The only way the transaction could be profitable was if the infant did not survive. The baby farming economy was, in this sense, structurally homicidal: the farmer's financial interest was directly opposed to the child's survival.

## The Legal and Institutional Response

The registration requirement that Riis dismissed as ineffective was part of a broader legislative effort — the 1876 New York State law requiring the licensing and inspection of all lodging facilities for infants. The law's failure was not in its intent but in its enforcement mechanism: the agencies charged with inspection — typically underfunded municipal health departments — could not monitor the thousands of tenement rooms where babies might be boarded in conditions indistinguishable from ordinary family arrangements.

More effective were the institutional alternatives developed by child-welfare organizations. The New York [[sister-irene|Foundling Asylum]], established in 1869, accepted abandoned infants and placed them with foster families — a model that, while imperfect, offered a genuine alternative to the baby farmer. The [[society-for-the-prevention-of-cruelty-to-children|SPCC]], founded in 1875, prosecuted cases of child abuse and neglect, creating a legal deterrent that — combined with institutional alternatives — gradually reduced the economic viability of baby farming.

## Reform and Legacy

The campaign against baby farming was part of a broader child-welfare movement that included the work of the [[society-for-the-prevention-of-cruelty-to-children|SPCC]], the [[childrens-aid-society|Children's Aid Society]], and institutions like the [[sister-irene|Foundling Asylum]]. These organizations offered alternatives to abandonment — foundling hospitals, orphanages, and foster placement — that gradually reduced the economic logic driving desperate mothers to baby farmers.
