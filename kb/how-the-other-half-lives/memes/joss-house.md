The Joss House was the Chinese temple on Mott Street that served as both a religious shrine and a social club for the Chinese immigrant men of Chinatown. Riis documented it in "Chinatown" (Chapter IX) with the tone of exotic discovery that characterized much of his writing about Chinese immigrants — simultaneously fascinated by and dismissive of their religious practices. "Between the tabernacles of Jewry and the shrines of the Bend, Joss has cheekily planted his pagan worship of idols, chief among which are the celestial worshipper's own gain and lusts" [009-002], he wrote, situating the temple within his broader ethnic taxonomy of the Lower East Side.

## A Combined Church and Club

Riis observed that "the Joss House is both" church and club [009-013]. The walls were covered with Chinese characters that he was told represented "the names of the pillars of the church or club — that they might have their reward in this world, no matter what happened to them in the next" [009-013]. This dual function — sacred space and fraternal lodge — reflected the immigrant bachelor society's need for institutions that could provide community, mutual aid, and spiritual comfort in a single location, since Chinese women were almost entirely absent from Chinatown due to exclusionary immigration laws.

The Joss House's role as a social center was as important as its religious function. In a community where men lived without families, worked in laundries and cigar factories by day, and faced systematic discrimination from the wider society, the temple provided one of the few spaces where Chinese immigrants could gather without the hostile scrutiny of the police or the sensationalizing gaze of journalists like Riis himself.

## Religious Practice and the Joss

At the center of the temple's worship stood the figure of Joss — a term that Riis used as both the name of the deity and as a metonym for Chinese religion as a whole. The temple contained an altar with offerings of food, incense, and paper money, alongside the inscribed tablets bearing the names of benefactors. The priest, "a yellow-robed, mild-mannered little man," guided Riis through the space, explaining the rituals with what Riis described as an air of patient condescension toward the uncomprehending visitor.

The Joss House represented a form of religious practice that was fundamentally illegible to Riis's Protestant moral framework. Where Riis expected religion to produce moral improvement — sobriety, industry, sexual restraint — the Joss House seemed to him to sanctify the very vices he attributed to the Chinese: gambling (through fan-tan, which had its own associated rituals), opium use, and what he saw as a mercenary approach to the sacred.

## The "Melican Joss"

One detail that particularly struck Riis was the presence of the American motto "In God We Trust" on the temple wall, copied from the trade dollar. The priest "pointed to it with undisguised pride and attempted an explanation, from which I gathered that the inscription was intended as a diplomatic courtesy, a delicate international compliment to the 'Melican Joss,' the almighty dollar" [009-013]. Riis interpreted this as evidence that everything in Chinatown — religion included — was for sale: "It will buy anything in Chinatown, Joss himself included, as indeed, why should it not?" [009-003].

This conflation of religious practice with venality was typical of Riis's treatment of Chinese culture, which he could not see except through the lens of his own Protestant moral framework. The temple's syncretic openness to American symbols — what the priest may have intended as genuine interfaith diplomacy — was received by Riis as cynical opportunism. The "Melican Joss" anecdote reveals more about Riis's inability to imagine genuine Chinese religiosity than it does about the actual beliefs of Chinatown's residents.

## Location and Visibility

The Joss House stood on Mott Street, not far from the "telegraph pole" that Riis identified as "the real official organ of Chinatown" — the public notice board where the bachelor community posted its communications — "in full view from Chatham Square" [009-016]. Its visibility was a provocation to Riis's readers, a sign that non-Christian worship had established itself openly in the heart of the city. For Chinese immigrants, that same visibility was a claim to permanence and presence in a city that had systematically tried to exclude them.
