## Immigration Background

Chinese immigration to New York City in the late nineteenth century represented a small fraction of the city's total immigrant population—perhaps two to three thousand people concentrated in a few blocks of lower Manhattan—but the Chinese presence attracted a degree of public attention, regulation, and moral panic entirely disproportionate to its size. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 had already barred most new Chinese immigration by the time Riis wrote *How the Other Half Lives*, and the community he described was predominantly male, isolated, and subject to intense police surveillance.

Riis located Chinatown geographically as "between the tabernacles of Jewry and the shrines of the Bend," where "Joss has cheekily planted his pagan worship of idols, chief among which are the celestial worshipper's own gain and lusts" [009-002]. The framing—Chinese religion as pagan idolatry motivated by greed—established the tone for a chapter that mixed genuine observation with unrestrained cultural hostility.

## Settlement Patterns

Chinatown in the 1890s was concentrated on Mott Street and Pell Street, within the Sixth Ward, adjacent to the [[mulberry-bend]] district that housed the [[Italian Immigrants|Italian]] community. Riis's physical description of the neighborhood was uncharacteristically restrained:

> Chinatown as a spectacle is disappointing. Next-door neighbor to the Bend, it has little of its outdoor stir and life, none of its gayly-colored rags or picturesque filth and poverty. Mott Street is clean to distraction: the laundry stamp is on it. [009-003]

The cleanliness that Riis observed he attributed not to civic virtue but to racial character: "It is not altogether by chance the Chinaman has chosen the laundry as his distinctive field. He is by nature as clean as the cat, which he resembles in his traits of cruel cunning, and savage fury when aroused" [009-007]. This passage—praising cleanliness while invoking animalistic comparisons—exemplified the double-edged nature of Riis's ethnic portraiture.

The internal geography of Chinatown centered on the joss house (temple), the fan-tan gambling parlors in basements and back rooms, and the opium dens that Riis described in lurid detail. The telegraph pole on Mott Street served, in Riis's memorable phrase, as "the real official organ of Chinatown," where yellow and red notices posted daily conveyed gambling news to a community that had "got hold of the contrivance by the wrong end" [009-016].

## In Riis's Account

Riis devoted Chapter IX—"Chinatown"—to the Chinese community, and his account is among the most hostile in the entire book. Where his chapters on Italians and Bohemians balanced criticism with moments of sympathy, the Chinatown chapter offered virtually no redeeming portrait of Chinese life.

**Opium and vice.** The central charge Riis leveled against Chinatown was that it functioned as a machinery of moral corruption, particularly of young white women:

> From the teeming tenements to the right and left of it come the white slaves of its dens of vice and their infernal drug, that have infused into the 'Bloody Sixth' Ward a subtler poison than ever the stale-beer dives knew. [009-006]

He described houses "literally jammed, from the 'joint' in the cellar to the attic, with these hapless victims of a passion which, once acquired, demands the sacrifice of every instinct of decency" [009-006]. The racial and sexual panic underlying these passages—Chinese men as predators of white women—drew on tropes that had fueled the Chinese Exclusion Act and would continue to shape anti-Chinese discourse for decades.

**The "quiet" community.** Despite this condemnation, Riis recorded that "the police give the Chinese the name of being the 'quietest people down there,' meaning in the notoriously turbulent Sixth Ward; and they are" [009-017]. This acknowledgment complicated his narrative: the Chinese were simultaneously the source of a "subtler poison" and the least troublesome population in the ward. Riis resolved this tension by redefining quietness itself as sinister—"in their very exclusiveness and reserve they are a constant and terrible threat to society" [009-017].

**White women and Chinese men.** The presence of white women in Chinatown—as wives, companions, or opium users—troubled Riis deeply. He recounted an episode in which he and a policeman intervened to stop a Chinese man from beating his white "wife" with a broom-handle. When the man protested—"S'ppose your wifee bad, you no lickee her?"—Riis recorded his own response and the man's reaction:

> My assurance that I did not, that such a thing could not occur to me, struck him dumb with amazement. He eyed me a while in stupid silence, poked the linen in his tub, stole another look, and made up his mind. A gleam of intelligence shone in his eye, and pity and contempt struggled in his voice. "Then, I guess, she lickee you," he said. [009-012]

The anecdote, presented as comedy, reinforced Riis's framing of Chinese domestic life as alien and brutal, while also—perhaps unintentionally—capturing a moment of genuine cross-cultural incomprehension.

## Economic Role

The Chinese economic niche in 1890s New York was overwhelmingly defined by the laundry trade. Riis noted that "the laundry stamp is on" Chinatown [009-003], and the hand-laundry business employed the vast majority of Chinese men in the city. The work was labor-intensive, required minimal capital to start, and could be conducted in small storefront spaces—characteristics that made it accessible to a population barred from most other forms of employment by language barriers, legal discrimination, and social hostility.

Beyond laundries, Chinatown supported a secondary economy of gambling (fan-tan), opium distribution, and the small-scale retail of imported goods—watermelon seed, sugarcane cut to length—that Riis observed from peddlers who "disdain to offer the barbarian their wares" [009-005]. The economic isolation of the Chinese community was nearly total: they served few non-Chinese customers, employed no non-Chinese workers, and participated minimally in the broader tenement economy that connected Italian ragpickers, Jewish garment workers, and Bohemian cigarmakers.
