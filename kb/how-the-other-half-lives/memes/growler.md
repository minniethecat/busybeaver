The "growler" was both an object and a social institution in Riis's tenement world. As an object, it was the tin can used by children to fetch beer from corner saloons for their parents or for their own consumption. As an institution, it gave rise to the "growler gang" — groups of youths whose shared practice of beer-fetching evolved into more organized forms of street crime and territorial violence.

## The Growler Economy

The growler was one of the most routine features of tenement life. Riis described how the growler was an ever-present element of the tenement streets, noting that it "wanders at all hours and all seasons unmolested" [004-016] through the alleys and rear courts. The practice was so commonplace that it was practically invisible — children carrying beer cans through tenement hallways was simply part of the urban landscape. The failure of the excise law to reach these back alleys was a structural feature of tenement governance: "The sway of the excise law is not extended to these back alleys. It would matter little if it were. There are secret byways, and some it is not held worth while to keep secret, along which the 'growler' wanders" [004-016].

## The Growler as Cradle of the Gang

The growler trade was the starting point for the gang careers that Riis documented with alarm. He traced a direct causal line from the innocent errand of fetching beer to a life of crime:

> "The 'growler' stood at the cradle of the tough. It bosses him through his boyhood apprenticeship in the 'gang,' and leaves him, for a time only, at the door of the jail that receives him to finish his training and turn him loose upon the world a thief, to collect by stealth or by force the living his philosophy tells him that it owes him, and will not voluntarily surrender without an equivalent in the work which he hates." [019-002]

The progression Riis described was deterministic: "From the moment he, almost a baby, for the first time carries the growler" [019-002], the trajectory was set. This was one of Riis's most controversial arguments — the claim that a quotidian tenement practice was the first step on an irreversible path to criminality. Modern readers may find the causality overstated, but the observation that the growler run placed unsupervised children in saloon environments was empirically grounded.

Riis described what he called "A growler gang in session" [019-007], providing photographic evidence of youths gathered around their shared beer can. The ritual of collective drinking served as the social glue that bound the group, creating the bonds that would later organize around more serious activities — theft, extortion, and territorial violence.

## The Gang Ecology

The gang culture that emerged from the growler was, in Riis's account, an indigenous institution of the tenement streets. "Every corner has its gang, not always on the best of terms with the rivals in the next block, but all with a common programme: defiance of law and order, and with a common ambition: to get 'pinched,' i.e., arrested, so as to pose as heroes before their fellows" [019-003]. Arrest was not a deterrent but a status marker — a perverse incentive that Riis argued made conventional policing ineffective.

Riis acknowledged the gang's social function even as he condemned it: "The gang is an institution" [019-003] in the tenement districts, filling the void left by absent schools, playgrounds, and adult supervision. The growler gang was, in this sense, the tenement's answer to the question of what adolescent boys would do with their unstructured time.

## The Irony of the Almshouse Growlers

Riis employed the "growler" motif with a characteristic twist in his chapter on the city's institutional poor. At the almshouse on Blackwell's Island, he encountered "a 'growler-gang' of another sort" — rows of old women "grumbling and growling from sunrise to sunset, at the weather, the breakfast, the dinner, the supper" [022-005]. The pun was deliberate: the growler of the tenement streets had its echo in the institutional setting, where the grumbling of the aged poor was the only form of protest available to them. The "growler-gang" of the almshouse was, like its tenement counterpart, a product of institutional failure — in this case, the failure of the city's charitable apparatus to provide dignified care.

## In Riis's Argument

The growler chapter served a specific function in Riis's broader argument about environmental determinism. By tracing the gang member's career from "almost a baby" carrying a beer can to the "door of the jail" [019-002], Riis was making the case that crime was not a moral failing but a predictable outcome of the tenement environment. The growler was, in this framework, an environmental hazard — as dangerous to the child's development as the physical conditions of the tenement were to his health.

The geography of the growler was also significant. Riis noted that "the Bend" was the epicenter of the growler trade, just as it was the epicenter of the dive, the tramp, and the stale-beer economy. The concentration of these institutions in a single neighborhood was, for Riis, evidence that the tenement system was not a collection of isolated problems but an integrated ecology of degradation — one in which the child sent to fetch a growler of beer was as much a victim of the system as the family living in a windowless room.
