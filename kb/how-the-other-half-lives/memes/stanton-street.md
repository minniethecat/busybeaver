## Overview

Stanton Street appears in *How the Other Half Lives* as a representative tenement block in the garment district of the Lower East Side. It housed the families of sweatshop workers — machine operators, finishers, and ironers — whose labor produced the cheap clothing sold in New York's retail stores, and whose wages almost never matched the cost of rent and subsistence.

## The Knee-Pants Operator's Home

Riis's primary reference to Stanton Street is embedded in a chapter-length portrait of the garment sweatshop economy. He described a machine operator "who works on the machine for his sweater twelve hours a day, turning out three dozen 'knee-pants,' for which he receives forty-two cents a dozen" [011-013]. The finisher working alongside him earned ten cents a dozen, and the ironer eight. "This operator has four children at his home in Stanton Street, none old enough to work, and a sick wife. His rent is twelve dollars a month" [011-013].

The arithmetic was crushing. A full week's labor at this rate produced roughly nine dollars, of which twelve dollars went to rent — a monthly deficit that could only be covered by sending the children to work as soon as they were old enough, by taking in boarders, or by falling into debt. The sick wife, unable to contribute her labor, was both a financial liability and a measure of the family's vulnerability. Riis offered no resolution to the Stanton Street operator's story, leaving the reader with the raw numbers.

## The Sweatshop Geography

Stanton Street sat within a dense network of garment-production streets. Ludlow Street, Hester Street, and the surrounding blocks housed similar operations. The "sweater" — a subcontractor who took orders from manufacturers and then subcontracted the actual production to pieceworkers operating out of tenement apartments — formed the economic backbone of the district. The knee-pants trade was one segment of a larger garment economy that Riis estimated employed thousands of tenement dwellers in conditions that blurred the line between home and factory.
