# Neighborhood Guild

## Grassroots Reform

The Neighborhood Guilds were among the earliest settlement house organizations in the United States, predating even the more famous Hull House in Chicago. Riis mentioned them in his survey of reform efforts: "the Neighborhood Guilds, through the efforts of the King's Daughters" (024-018), positioning them alongside the [[college-settlement|College Settlement]] as demonstrations of what dedicated, locally-engaged reform could accomplish.

## The Guild Model

Unlike the religious charity organizations that Riis often criticized as out of touch with tenement realities, the guilds operated on a principle of neighborly mutual aid. They provided educational programs, recreational activities, and practical assistance to tenement residents while advocating for broader housing reform. The guild model emphasized the agency of the poor themselves, rather than casting them as passive recipients of charity.

## Riis's Reform Taxonomy

In Chapter XXIV's catalog of "what has been done," the Neighborhood Guilds represented the second tier of reform — below systemic solutions like the [[tenement-house-act-1867|Tenement-House Act of 1867]] and new construction like [[alfred-t-white|Alfred T. White]]'s Riverside Buildings, but above the traditional charitable model. The guilds showed that reform was socially sustainable; the model tenements showed it was economically sustainable.
