# The Italian Immigrant

## Biography

The Italian immigrant occupies a particularly harsh position in Riis's urban geography. Unlike the Germans and Irish, who had established themselves over decades, and unlike the Jews, who were economically ascendant through the garment trade, the Italians were the newest large group and occupied the bottom of every hierarchy — the worst housing, the worst jobs, and the worst reputation. Riis documents their concentration in "the Bend," the Mulberry Street district that was the most notorious slum in the city.

## Role in the Tenements

Italian immigrants were brought to New York through the "padrone" system — labor contractors who advanced passage money and then controlled the immigrant's employment until the debt was repaid. Riis describes this as a form of indentured servitude, noting that the "assisted" Italian immigrant claimed "so large a share of public attention" precisely because the padrone system was so exploitative. (005-002)

Once in the city, Italians worked as rag-pickers, ditch-diggers, and day laborers — the lowest-paid, least stable employment. Their housing reflected their economic position: the oldest, most decrepit rear tenements in the most crowded blocks. Riis describes Italian neighborhoods as "a couple of blocks" that were the "exclusive preserve of the Italian immigrant," where conditions were worse than in any other district. (005-006)

Riis's ethnic characterization of Italians is among his most prejudiced, yet he also defends them against nativist attacks. He notes that Italians had "redeeming traits" — thrift, family loyalty, and ambition — and that "only a few years ago, when railroads were building, Italians were the most sought-after laborers." (005-008) His account captures the tension between his commitment to documenting conditions honestly and the ethnic stereotypes he shared with his readership.

## Multiple Perspectives

The Italian immigrant was perceived through fundamentally incompatible lenses by the different constituencies of Gilded Age New York. To the native-born Protestant elite, the Italian was the quintessential "undesirable" immigrant — Catholic, dark-skinned in the racial taxonomy of the era, illiterate in English, and concentrated in occupations that seemed parasitic rather than productive. The Immigration Restriction League and its allies used images of Italian tenement life — often drawn directly from Riis's own photographs — to argue for literacy tests and quota restrictions.

To the Irish immigrants who preceded them, the Italians were competitors for the bottom rung. Irish landlords rented to Italian tenants, Irish foremen hired Italian laborers, and Irish politicians courted Italian votes, but the relationship was adversarial at every level. The Irish-dominated Catholic Church in New York struggled to accommodate Italian worshippers, who found the American church cold and unwelcoming. Riis documents the "Irish landlord" and the "Italian tenant" as archetypes of ethnic succession in the tenement, each group occupying roles in a hierarchical urban order.

Within the Italian community itself, the padrone was the most visible authority figure, but his relationship to fellow immigrants was deeply ambivalent. The padrone provided work, housing, and a bridge to the English-speaking economy, but at exploitative terms that many immigrants bitterly resented. The Italian-language press and mutual-aid societies that emerged in the 1890s were, in part, organized opposition to the padrone system, seeking to replace personal dependency with institutional support.

The labor movement and the socialist left saw the Italian immigrant as both a victim of capitalism and a threat to native-born workers. Italian strikebreakers were imported by employers during labor disputes, deepening the hostility of organized labor toward the new arrivals. Yet by the 1890s, Italian workers were forming their own unions and participating in labor actions, complicating the narrative of the "scab" immigrant. The Italian immigrant's relationship to organized labor went through a transformation that Riis's snapshot in 1890 captures at its most vulnerable moment, before collective organization had taken root.

## Legacy

The Italian immigrant's experience as documented by Riis became a canonical account of the immigrant journey through the tenement system. The Italian-American narrative of escape from the slums — from Mulberry Street to the suburbs — mirrors the trajectory of every earlier wave, and Riis's book captured the Mulberry Street phase at its most intense.
