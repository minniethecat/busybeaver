## Overview

Sebastopol was the nickname of a notoriously squalid tenement block in 1890s Manhattan, immortalized by Jacob Riis as the subject of the first photographic illustration in *How the Other Half Lives*. Riis paired it with [[hells-kitchen|Hell's Kitchen]] in a single caption — "Hell's Kitchen and Sebastopol" [P04-008] — placing it in the front rank of New York's most infamous slum landmarks before the reader had even reached the first chapter. The photograph, positioned at the close of the Introduction, depicts the tenement as a grim, institutional-looking structure whose blank side wall and barracks-like massing embody the architectural inhumanity of the tenement system Riis set out to expose.

The name "Sebastopol" is an ironic loan from the Crimean War: Sevastopol was the Russian fortress city that endured the epic eleven-month siege of 1854–1855, one of the defining military engagements of the nineteenth century. Transposing that name onto a New York tenement was a bitter joke — the building, like the fortress, was a place where people huddled under siege, besieged not by cannons but by overcrowding, darkness, and disease.

## The Photograph

The Sebastopol photograph (illustration-1.jpg) stands as the book's visual introduction to the tenement world. The image shows a street scene dominated by a five-story tenement building set on the left, its front facade punctuated by rows of windows while its side wall is entirely blank — no windows, no ornament, a dead wall rising five stories to the cornice. Another multistory building appears in the background. The scene is bisected by tall telegraph poles, and the street is dotted with vehicles and small dwellings. [P04-008]

The placement of this image at the end of the Introduction is deliberate and rhetorically charged. Riis has just finished his opening argument: the tenements are "the hotbeds of the epidemics that carry death to rich and poor alike; the nurseries of pauperism and crime that fill our jails and police courts" [P04-004]. The reader, having absorbed this indictment, turns the page to confront the physical reality — "Hell's Kitchen and Sebastopol" — rendered in the stark documentary style that made Riis's photography revolutionary. The blank side wall of the Sebastopol tenement becomes a visual metaphor for the indifference of the "upper half" described in the Introduction's opening sentence: those who "did not know because it did not care" [P04-001].

## In the Context of the Tenement System

Sebastopol takes its place within the larger geography of slum nicknames that Riis catalogued. Like [[gotham-court|Gotham Court]] on Cherry Street, "the Dirty Spoon" in the Fourth Ward, and "Blind Man's Alley" with its colony of blind beggars, Sebastopol belonged to a taxonomy of notorious addresses that every New Yorker recognized and every reformer cited. These nicknames were not merely colorful street slang; they were a parallel map of the city, a folk geography that tracked the locations of the worst suffering and the highest mortality. [004-009]

The tenement system that produced Sebastopol was, in Riis's arithmetic, staggering in scale. By the time he wrote, three-fourths of New York's population lived in tenements, and the fifteen thousand tenant houses of a generation earlier had swollen to thirty-seven thousand. [P04-003] A single block might contain several such buildings, each packed with hundreds of tenants. The Illustration of Sebastopol — paired with Hell's Kitchen, a West Side slum district heavily populated by [[irish-immigrants|Irish immigrants]] — suggests that these nicknamed landmarks spanned the city, East Side and West Side alike.

The sanitary conditions that defined places like Sebastopol were systemic. Riis cited the finding that "more than one-half of the tenements with two-thirds of their population were held by owners who made the keeping of them a business, generally a speculation" [P04-005]. Landlords sought returns of fifteen to thirty percent and responded to tenant complaints with the ultimatum: "pay their rent or leave" [P04-005]. It was this economic structure, not the vices of individual tenants, that converted buildings like Sebastopol from ordinary housing into engines of misery.

## Significance in Riis's Narrative

The choice to open the book's photographic program with Sebastopol and Hell's Kitchen is significant. Before Riis takes the reader through the genesis of the [[tenement]] in Chapter I, before the tours of [[cherry-street|Cherry Street]] and [[the-bend|the Bend]], before the ethnic portraits of Italians, Jews, and Chinese — he shows them the result. The photograph functions as a visual thesis statement: this is what the system produces.

Riis's pairing of the two nicknames also performs a narrative function. Hell's Kitchen, on the West Side, was already notorious for gang violence and Irish poverty. By coupling it with Sebastopol, Riis asserts that the tenement problem was not confined to a single neighborhood or a single ethnic group. The pairing says: the East Side has its own Hell's Kitchen. The crisis is citywide.

Sebastopol thus belongs to the documentary strategy that made *How the Other Half Lives* a landmark of American reform journalism. Like the later photographs of [[bandits-roost|Bandit's Roost]] and the [[lodger|lodging houses]] of the Bowery, the Sebastopol image was an argument disguised as evidence — a picture that, in Riis's hands, demanded that the viewer answer the question with which he closed the Introduction: "What are you going to do about it?" [P04-005]
