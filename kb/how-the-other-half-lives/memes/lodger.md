# Lodger

## Definition

The *lodger* was a ubiquitous figure in the tenement economy — a person who rented space within someone else's tenement apartment, often sharing a room with the family or sleeping in a corner partitioned by a curtain. The lodger system was the final intensification of tenement density: families who could not afford their own apartments took in lodgers to help pay the rent, and lodgers who could not afford rooms of their own took beds or floor space in those same apartments. Riis noted that a family of "man, wife, three children, and a boarder" was a typical configuration. (011-012)

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The lodger system was driven by simple economics. Rents were high relative to wages, so every tenement apartment was under pressure to sublet. Riis documents a sweater's household where the worker "is really only a lodger, getting his meals with the family." (011-012) In other cases, the lodger was a stranger paying for floor space in an already crowded flat.

The scale of lodging was enormous:

> "Thousands upon thousands of lodgers who help to pay its extortions." (011-018)

The lodger economy had a direct relationship to overcrowding. The 1879 Tenement House Act's dumbbell design was supposed to guarantee light and air to every room, but lodgers occupied spaces never intended as bedrooms. Early tenement builders justified the practice by pointing to demand:

> "Such is the lack of houseroom in the city that any kind of tenement can be immediately crowded with lodgers, if there is space offered." (001-010)

In the cheapest lodging-houses, the lodger relationship was formalized into a commercial transaction. A notice posted "on the wall of the lodgers' room" in one establishment suggested at least a pretense of rules. (008-012)

## Significance

The lodger represents the informal economy of the tenement — the ways in which market pressure pushed density beyond even the limits of the law. Every lodger was both a symptom (the primary tenant could not afford the rent) and a cause (the lodger's presence intensified overcrowding and its associated problems). Riis's attention to the lodger reflects his broader method of tracing systemic problems down to individual economic decisions.

The lodger also complicates the moral narrative of the tenement. Landlords were not the only exploiters — tenants themselves became petty landlords, subletting space they could barely afford. This chain of exploitation, from property owner to primary tenant to lodger, was the economic structure of the tenement in microcosm.
