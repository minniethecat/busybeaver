## Overview

The Lower East Side was the most densely populated immigrant quarter in nineteenth-century New York City, and the primary geographic focus of Jacob Riis's *How the Other Half Lives*. It served as the entry point for successive waves of European immigration and became synonymous with tenement crowding, poverty, and the social ills that reformers sought to address.

Defined broadly as the tenement districts east of the Bowery, the East Side stretched from the Fourth Ward in the south, through the Sixth and Seventh Wards, to the Tenth Ward in the north. The Bowery itself formed the great western boundary -- "the great democratic highway of the city" (008-003) -- beyond which a different social world began: "It is Sunday evening west of the Bowery" (011-007). The area contained some of the most notorious slum districts in the Western world, including the Mulberry Street Bend, Jewtown, the Bohemian quarter, and Chinatown.

## Geography and Boundaries

The East Side encompassed the tenement districts east of the Bowery, stretching from the Jewish quarter in the south to the German and Irish sections further north. Riis mapped the district's passage vividly: "The tenements grow taller, and the gaps in their ranks close up rapidly as we cross the Bowery and, leaving Chinatown and the Italians behind, invade the Hebrew quarter" (010-002). The Russian and Polish Jews had "overrun the district between Rivington and Division Streets, east of the Bowery, to the point of suffocation" (003-012). Specific streets defined the character of the district: Bayard Street was "the high road to Jewtown across the Bowery, picketed from end to end with the outposts of Israel" (006-004); Ludlow Street was the center of the sweating industry; Cherry Street was synonymous with crime and dilapidation; and Mulberry Street marked the heart of "the Bend."

The district was characterized by block after block of overcrowded tenement houses, rear tenements, and alleyway dwellings that housed the city's poorest residents. A bird's-eye view of an East Side tenement block was recorded in drawings by Charles F. Wingate (014-008). The blocks themselves were mazes: "In some localities, where the interior of a block is filled with rear tenements, often set at all sorts of odd angles, surprise alone is practicable" (019-011).

## The Ethnic Mosaic

The Lower East Side was home to a kaleidoscope of immigrant communities. Riis envisioned a color-coded map of the city: "The city on such a map would fall into two great halves, green for the Irish prevailing in the West Side tenement districts, and blue for the Germans on the East Side" (003-011). Dovetailed into the German majority, "the poor but thrifty Bohemian might be picked out by the sombre hue of his life as of his philosophy, struggling against heavy odds in the big human beehives of the East Side" (003-012). The Bohemian was unique among foreign-born groups: "The Bohemian is the only foreigner with any considerable representation in the city who counts no wealthy man of his race, none who has not to work hard for a living, or has got beyond the reach of the tenement" (003-012).

Between the German blue and the Italian red on Riis's hypothetical map "would be seen squeezed in on the map a sharp streak of yellow, marking the narrow boundaries of Chinatown" (003-012). The colored population also had a presence: "The negroes have made a stand at several points along Seventh and Eighth Avenues; but their main body, still pursued by the Italian foe, is on the march yet, and the black mark will be found overshadowing today many blocks on the East Side, with One Hundredth Street as the centre" (003-011). A notable exception to the squalor was recorded: "There is no more clean and orderly community in New York than the new settlement of colored people that is growing up on the East Side from Yorkville to Harlem" (013-004).

## The Jewish Quarter (Jewtown)

The Tenth Ward was the heart of the Jewish East Side -- what Riis called "Jewtown." The Jewish population had so thoroughly displaced earlier residents that "when the great Jewish holidays come around every year, the public schools in the district have practically to close up" (010-002). The density was staggering: "These are samples of the packing of the population that has run up the record here to the rate of three hundred and thirty thousand per square mile" (010-003), far exceeding "the densest crowding of Old London" which "never got beyond a hundred and seventy-five thousand" (010-003). In Essex Street, "two small rooms in a six-story tenement were made to hold a 'family' of father and mother, twelve children, and six boarders" (010-003).

The ward earned grim nicknames from municipal officials: "The health officers call the Tenth the typhus ward; in the office where deaths are registered it passes as the 'suicide ward,' for reasons not hard to understand; and among the police as the 'crooked ward'" (010-009). The Eastern Dispensary, located "in the very heart of their district," reported that "the diseases these people suffer from are not due to intemperance or immorality, but to ignorance, want of suitable food, and the foul air in which they live and work" (010-008). Thrift was nevertheless "the watchword of Jewtown, as of its people the world over" (010-006), and "the world as a debtor has no credit in Jewtown" (010-003).

## The Mulberry Street Bend

At the southern end of the East Side lay "the Bend," centered where Mulberry Street crooked "like an elbow within hail of the old depravity of the Five Points" (006-002) -- the "foul core of New York's slums" (006-002). "Around 'the Bend' cluster the bulk of the tenements that are stamped as altogether bad, even by the optimists of the Health Department" (006-002). Only twenty-four of six hundred and nine tenements in the district were rated as decent condition a generation earlier (006-003). The area between Bayard, Park, Mulberry, and Baxter Streets -- "the Bend proper" -- saw 155 child deaths in a single specimen year (1882) (006-009). Bandits' Roost and Bottle Alley were among the most notorious of its passageways (006-007, 006-016).

The Italian population dominated the Bend. "No Sunday has passed in New York since 'the Bend' became a suburb of Naples without one or more of these murderous affrays coming to the notice of the police" (005-007). "All 'the Bend' must be, if not altogether, at least half out of doors when the sun shines" (006-008). The women did "all the carrying, all the work one sees going on in 'the Bend'" while the men "sit or stand in the streets, on trucks, or in the open doors of the saloons smoking black clay pipes" (006-007). The stale-beer dives made the Bend "the real home of trampdom" (007-012).

## Living Conditions

East Side poverty manifested in every aspect of daily life. Families crowded into airless tenement apartments, with the packing of the child population reaching extreme densities (015-002). "I have in mind an alley -- an inlet rather to a row of rear tenements -- that is either two or four feet wide according as the wall of the crazy old building that gives on it bulges out or in" (015-002). In the Bend, the sanitary policeman located "the bulk of his four hundred" worst tenements, and "the sanitary reformer gives up the task in despair" (006-026).

The "stoop," or front steps, served as the first floor in East Side parlance (011-016). Conventional proprieties held no sway; as Riis observed, "nothing counts that cannot be counted in hard cash" (011-007). "Proprieties do not count on the East Side; nothing counts that cannot be converted into hard cash" (011-007). Expedients born of East Side crowding were endless: "Two hallways side by side in adjoining tenements, would be sinful waste in Jewtown, when one would do as well by knocking a hole in the wall" (011-015).

Rear tenements housed staggering numbers. "A hundred thousand people lived in rear tenements in New York last year" (004-026). The sweating system turned tenement homes into factories: "the homes of the Hebrew quarter are its workshops also" (010-008). On the streets the whir of machines was inescapable: "You are made fully aware of it before you have travelled the length of a single block in any of these East Side streets, by the whir of a thousand sewing-machines, worked at high pressure from earliest dawn till mind and muscle give out together" (010-008).

## Criminal Activity

The East Side harbored organized criminal elements. In one notorious case, police captured a band of firebugs who made a business of setting fire to tenements for insurance payouts (010-020). The moral turpitude of streets like Thompson Street was known citywide (013-014). Murder was "as congenial to Cherry Street or to Battle Row, as quiet and order to Murray Hill" (019-003). The Cherry Street tough was "all-pervading" (004-017), and "one cannot take many steps in Cherry Street without encountering some relic of past or present prominence in the ways of crime" (004-017).

Gang warfare was endemic. The Sixth Ward hosted "the infamous Whyo Gang" which "until a few years ago absorbed the worst depravity of the Bend and what is left of the Five Points" (019-014). When one side of the city erupted, the other waited: "When the West Side is in a state of eruption, the East Side gangs 'lie low,' and when the toughs along the North River are nursing broken heads at home, or their revenge in Sing Sing, fresh trouble breaks out in the tenements east of Third Avenue" (019-004). The nearness of the Bowery, "the great 'thieves' highway,'" sustained the supply of criminals (010-009).

## Industry and Labor

The East Side was home to the sweating system, where garment workers toiled in tenement rooms converted to workshops. The "sweater of the East Side" operated flannel workshops that exploited immigrant labor (020-012). Young girls pulling threads in East Side factories earned subsistence wages -- the average day's earnings of thirty cents for "the poor little girl who pulls threads in an East Side factory" was offset by the two dollars a day earned by the stylish "cashier," and "if anything, the average is probably too high" (020-011). The battle between East Broadway and Broadway "ended in a complete victory for the East Side and cheap labor, and transferred to it the control of the trade in cheap clothing" (011-003).

In Ludlow Street tenements, "five men and a woman, two young girls, not fifteen, and a boy who says unasked that he is fifteen, and lies in saying it, are at the machines sewing knickerbockers" (011-008). The sun would set "upon the day of the arrival of many a Polish Jew, finding him at work in an East Side tenement, treading the machine and 'learning the trade'" (011-004). The Bohemian quarter, scattered across the East Side, specialized in tenement-house cigarmaking (012-003).

## Lodging-Houses and the Bowery District

The Bowery district, which overlapped the western edge of the Lower East Side, was the center of the cheap lodging-house trade. "More than half of the lodging-houses are in the Bowery district, that is to say, the Fourth, Sixth, and Tenth Wards, and they harbor nearly three-fourths of their crowds" (008-014). The calculation that "more than nine thousand homeless young men lodge nightly along Chatham Street and the Bowery, between the City Hall and the Cooper Union, is probably not far out of the way" (008-014). These institutions were a nursery of crime: Inspector Byrnes observed that "in the last two or three years at least four hundred young men have been arrested for petty crimes that originated in the lodging-houses" (008-004).

## Institutions

The East Side contained numerous charitable and religious institutions. Foundlings were frequently abandoned there by young mothers unable to care for them -- "most of the foundlings come from the East Side, where they are left by young mothers without wedding-rings or other name than their own to bestow upon the baby" (016-004). Even the relatively low death-rate of the Foundling Asylum, at nineteen percent, "was equal to the startling death-rate of Gotham Court in the cholera scourge" (016-004). The district's synagogues, missions, and charity organizations formed a patchwork of aid and evangelism that Riis both documented and critiqued. Among the poorest Jews, synagogues "frequently consist of a scantily furnished room in a rear tenement, with a few wooden stools or benches for the congregation" (010-014). The Charity Ball, an annual fundraising event, was a recognized East Side institution (019-018).

## Public Health

The East Side was ground zero for the city's public health crises. The Tenth Ward bred "typhus fever and smallpox" (010-008). Infant mortality served as "a good barometer of its general sanitary condition" (006-009), and the East Side's barometer pointed relentlessly to crisis. The dread of cholera repeatedly spurred municipal action: "the last cholera scare made people willing to listen to reason" (014-013). In the busiest East Side court, known as the "Poor Man's Court," "fully five thousand dispossess warrants are issued in a year, but probably not fifty evictions take place in the district" (014-033), a testament both to overcrowding and to the practical limits of enforcement. Yet the area was also the focus of reform: "the erection of a single good tenement has the power to change, gradually but surely, the character of a whole bad block" (025-035).
