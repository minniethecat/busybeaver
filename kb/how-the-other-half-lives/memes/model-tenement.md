# Model Tenement

## Definition

The *model tenement* was a housing reform concept — purpose-built apartment buildings designed to provide decent, sanitary living conditions while still returning a modest profit to investors. Riis saw the model tenement as the crucial third pillar of reform, alongside legal restriction on old-law tenements and the demolition of the worst slums:

> "By building new, model tenements." (025-016)

## Manifestation in the Tenements

Riis documents the checkered history of model tenement experiments. The earliest efforts were often failures. Gotham Court, for example, was originally built as a model tenement:

> "Originally built as a model tenement, it speedily came to rival the worst slums in the city." (024-009)

The problem was structural. Model tenements that charged market rents could not house the poorest tenants, while those that charged less could not attract private capital. Reformers struggled with the tension between philanthropy and profitability. The Improved Dwellings Association and the Tenement House Building Company were two of the most prominent efforts, and Riis describes their financial returns with careful optimism:

> "Enough has been said to show that model tenements can be built successfully and as an investment." (025-035)

Even in better buildings, conditions could deteriorate rapidly. Riis reports that in one model tenement, a sanitary official counted 146 cases of sickness within a few years of its opening. (004-014) And across the street from another:

> "In the model tenement across the way at Nos. 48 and 50, the record of sickness and death is almost as bad." (006-012)

Nevertheless, Riis was unwavering in his advocacy. He listed model tenement construction as the third of his three-point reform program, and concluded that "the beau ideal of the model tenement for a great city like New York" had not yet been achieved but was a necessary goal. (025-034)

## Significance

The model tenement occupies a pivotal place in Riis's reform vision. While Chapters I–XXIV document the horrors of the old-law tenement, Chapter XXV ("How the Case Stands") lays out the path forward, and model tenements are at its center. Riis was unusual among Progressive-era reformers in insisting that the housing problem could not be solved by law or charity alone — private capital, properly guided, was essential.

The model tenement debate also connects to the larger question Riis poses throughout the book: can the free market solve the housing crisis, or must the state intervene? His answer — that market mechanisms must be harnessed but strictly regulated — became the template for American housing policy in the decades that followed.
