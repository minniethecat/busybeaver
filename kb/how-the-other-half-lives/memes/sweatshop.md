# Sweatshop

## The Sweating System

The sweatshop was central to Jacob Riis's portrait of immigrant labor in 1890s New York. Chapter XI, "The Sweaters of Jewtown," was devoted to the garment workers who turned tenement apartments into makeshift factories. Riis described the system: "The sweater is the middleman, the contractor, who takes the work from the manufacturer and distributes it among the families who do the actual making up" (011-003). The economics of the system meant that entire families — men, women, and children — worked fourteen to sixteen hours a day to earn subsistence wages.

## The Tenement as Factory

What distinguished the New York sweatshop from the factory was its location: the work happened in the same single-room apartments where families ate, slept, and lived. Riis described walking into a tenement apartment to find "a family of eight — father, mother, and six children — living, or rather existing, in a space of less than ten feet square, making 'pants'" (011-006). The law that regulated factory labor did not reach tenement house production.

## Cigarmaking and Beyond

While garment manufacturing — coats, pants, vests, shirts — was the most visible form of tenement labor, Riis also documented Bohemian cigarmakers who rolled tobacco leaves in tenement rooms shared with their families: "In the tenement the Bohemian cigarmaker works and lives with his family in a single room" (012-004). The health consequences were severe, with tobacco dust filling living spaces and contributing to the sky-high rates of tuberculosis that Riis documented throughout the tenement chapters.

## Riis as Labor Reporter

Riis's documentation of sweatshop conditions was among his most influential reporting. Unlike some of his contemporaries, he did not blame workers for the conditions in which they labored. He recognized the sweatshop as a structural phenomenon — the product of a supply chain that squeezed contractors who, in turn, squeezed workers. His solution, characteristically, focused on housing: if the tenement were reformed, the sweatshop-in-the-home would disappear with it.
