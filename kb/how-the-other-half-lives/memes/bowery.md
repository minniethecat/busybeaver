# Bowery

## Description

The Bowery was the grand boulevard of working-class New York — a wide street running north from Chatham Square through the Lower East Side, lined with theaters, beer gardens, pawnshops, and cheap lodging-houses. In Riis's geography, the Bowery was a borderland: east of it lay the tenement districts of the Lower East Side; west of it lay the more prosperous commercial city. The street itself was neither fully slum nor fully respectable — it was the space where the two worlds met.

## In Riis's Account

### The Lodging-House District

The Bowery figures prominently in Chapter VIII ("The Cheap Lodging-Houses"). Riis describes "the caravansaries that line Chatham Street and the Bowery" as housing "nightly a population as large as that of many a thriving town." (008-002) The lodging-houses were concentrated on and near the Bowery because it was the natural arrival point for young men coming to the city — the first street they saw, and often the last they would ever know. Riis called it "the great democratic highway of the city, where the twenty-five-cent lodging-houses take them in." (008-003) Young newcomers who arrived with little money and no knowledge of the city's traps gravitated to the Bowery instinctively, "in search of crowds, of 'life.'" (008-003)

Within the Bowery lodging-houses, Riis identified three distinct classes of lodgers: the majority were adventurers waiting for something to turn up; a smaller class consisted of respectable clerks or mechanics too poor or too lonely to maintain homes of their own, living from year to year in the barracks; and lastly, "the thief in search of recruits for his trade." (008-003) Riis traced a predictable downward trajectory: the young stranger, his money spent with no work in sight, descended from the twenty-five-cent house to the fifteen-cent lodging-house, where he encountered ex-convicts "returning from the prisons after having served out their sentences for robbery or theft" and was primed for recruitment into crime. (008-003)

The statistical concentration was overwhelming. Riis calculated that more than half of the city's lodging-houses lay within the Bowery district — the Fourth, Sixth, and Tenth Wards — and they harbored nearly three-quarters of the lodging-house population. (008-014) He estimated that "more than nine thousand homeless young men lodge nightly along Chatham Street and the Bowery, between the City Hall and the Cooper Union." (008-014)

### The "Thieves' Highway"

Riis tells the story of a boy's father searching for his child "in the Bowery" and falling under the influence of the same criminal networks that had ensnared the son. (008-008) The Bowery's lodging-houses were, in Inspector Byrnes's testimony, the origin point for hundreds of petty crimes. (008-004) Byrnes reported that in the preceding two or three years, at least four hundred young men had been arrested for petty crimes originating in the lodging-houses, "and that in many cases it was their first step in crime." (008-004) He added that three-fourths of the young offenders in the courts were under twenty years of age, poorly clad, and without means. (008-004)

The Bowery's criminal reputation is reinforced in Chapter X, where Riis calls it "the great 'thieves' highway'" whose nearness sustained the supply of criminals infiltrating Jewtown. (010-009) Riis recounts the case of a well-known thief who lived at the Windsor, a notorious Bowery lodging-house, and who "went to Johnstown after the flood and was shot and killed there while robbing the dead." (008-004) The Bowery was also the setting for the recruitment of young Edward, whom a criminal named Smith met on the Bowery, led to "a vile lodging-house," introduced to his "pals," and paraded around "all over the Bowery and Grand Street, showed him the sights and drew his attention to the careless way the ladies carried their bags and purses and the easy thing it was to get them." (008-006)

### Political Corruption

The Bowery was also a stage for political manipulation. Riis describes how the two political "machines, intrenched in their strongholds, outbid each other across the Bowery in open rivalry as to who shall commit the most flagrant frauds at the polls." (008-015) The lodging-house keepers served both machines by delivering the votes of their floating populations — a trade so entrenched that only "semi-occasionally" was a major offender caught, as happened "not long ago" when "the proprietor of one of the biggest Bowery lodging-houses" was prosecuted. (008-015)

### A Geographic Threshold

The Bowery served as Riis's most important geographic reference line. Moving eastward across it signaled entry into the immigrant slums; moving westward, a return to the respectable city. In Chapter III, Riis notes that the Russian and Polish Jews had "overrun the district between Rivington and Division Streets, east of the Bowery, to the point of suffocation." (003-012) Crossing the Bowery becomes a narrative device: in Chapter X, he writes that "the tenements grow taller, and the gaps in their ranks close up rapidly as we cross the Bowery and, leaving Chinatown and the Italians behind, invade the Hebrew quarter." (010-002) In Chapter VI, Bayard Street is described as "the high road to Jewtown across the Bowery, picketed from end to end with the outposts of Israel." (006-004) The "Bend" district itself was bounded between Broadway and the Bowery and Canal and Chatham Streets — a four-block band of the most notorious tenements in the city, where of 4,367 apartments, only nine were vacant at the last census. (006-026)

### The Bowery as Commercial Artery

Beyond lodging-houses and crime, the Bowery was a working commercial street. In Chapter XI, Riis reports that a single Bowery clothing firm "last year sold fifteen thousand suits at $1.95 that averaged in cost $1.12½" — a testament to the sweatshop economy that surrounded it. (011-019) The street marked the boundary between the Christian and Jewish calendars: "It is Sunday evening west of the Bowery," Riis writes in the same chapter, but east of it the Jewish workweek continued under full headway. (011-007) In Chapter XIII, Riis describes the policy-gambling trade that operated through cigar stores and candy shops, with the slips printed in "some cunning Bowery 'broker's' back office, where the slips are printed and the 'winnings' apportioned daily with due regard to the backer's interests." (013-011)

The Bowery also represented the entertainments available to the working poor. Its theaters (where admission cost a nickel), beer gardens, and dime museums were the tenement dweller's alternatives to the saloon. Riis's attitude toward these entertainments was characteristically mixed — they were better than the stale-beer dive but still a symptom of a city that offered the poor nothing better.

## Significance

The Bowery's role as a liminal space — between slum and city, between poverty and prosperity — makes it one of the most richly documented places in Riis's book. It was the space where the forces he documented (immigration, housing shortage, vice, reform) collided most visibly. The street's name became internationally synonymous with urban poverty and vice, and its decline from fashionable boulevard to skid row was itself a miniature of the tenement story. Riis used the Bowery not only as a geographic marker but as a narrative device: to cross it was to cross a threshold between worlds, and for thousands of young men arriving in New York, it was the last place they would ever know.
