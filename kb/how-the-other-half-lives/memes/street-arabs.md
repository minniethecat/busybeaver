# Street Arabs

## Who Were the Street Arabs

"Street Arabs" was Jacob Riis's term for the homeless children who populated the streets of 1890s New York. Chapter XVII of *How the Other Half Lives*, titled "The Street Arab," was devoted entirely to these children. Riis found them "all over the city, these Street Arabs, where the neighborhood offers a chance of picking up a living in the daytime and of 'turning in' at night with a newsboy or bootblack" (017-005). The term — a racialized analogy to nomadic Bedouin — reflected the era's tendency to frame social problems in ethnic and cultural terms, but it also captured the rootlessness that defined these children's lives.

## Sleeping Quarters

The Street Arabs' sleeping arrangements were a catalog of urban desperation. Riis documented children sleeping in "an old boiler at the East River" (017-005), in the doorways of printing houses, in hallways, and in the barrows of street vendors. The photograph "Street Arabs in sleeping quarters" (017-012) was one of Riis's most famous images, showing two boys huddled in a makeshift shelter under a stoop.

## Economic Survival

Street Arabs survived through a combination of legitimate and illegitimate economic activities. The legitimate side included selling newspapers, shining shoes, running errands, and collecting and selling rags. The illegitimate side included petty theft, serving as lookouts for adult criminals, and occasional prostitution. Riis described the economic ladder: "The newsboy is the aristocrat of the street Arabs" (017-007), while the bootblack occupied a lower rung and the outright vagrant the lowest.

## The Gerry Society and Reform

The [[elbridge-t-gerry|Gerry Society]] (Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children) was the primary institutional response to the Street Arab phenomenon. Riis credited [[elbridge-t-gerry|Elbridge T. Gerry]] with rescuing thousands of children from the streets, though he also noted that institutional solutions — orphanages, truant schools, and reformatories — were imperfect substitutes for stable homes.
