# The Immigrant

## Biography

The immigrant is the protagonist of *How the Other Half Lives* as much as the tenement is its setting. Riis documents eighteen distinct ethnic groups across Manhattan's wards, tracing their arrival patterns, housing conditions, labor markets, and assimilation trajectories. He was himself an immigrant, and his most sympathetic passages are those that describe immigrant striving against impossible odds. He notes that even the poorest arrived with ambition: "The poorest immigrant comes here with the purpose and ambition to better himself and, given half a chance, might be reasonably expected to make the most of it." (003-009)

Chapter 3, "The Mixed Crowd," opens with a visit to a Fourth Ward alley where "one hundred and forty families, one hundred Irish, thirty-eight Italian, and two that spoke the German tongue" lived — and "barring the agent herself, there was not a native-born individual in the court." (003-002) Riis observes that "one may find for the asking an Italian, a German, a French, African, Spanish, Bohemian, Russian, Scandinavian, Jewish, and Chinese colony" (003-002), yet "the one thing you shall vainly ask for in the chief city of America is a distinctively American community." (003-002) The old inhabitants are gone: "some went to California in '49, some to the war and never came back. The rest, I expect, have gone to heaven, or somewhere." (003-002) In their place "has come this queer conglomerate mass of heterogeneous elements, ever striving and working like whiskey and water in one glass, and with the like result: final union and a prevailing taint of whiskey." (003-003)

## Arrival and Settlement

Riis describes immigration as following a geographic logic: "As emigration from east to west follows the latitude, so does the foreign influx in New York distribute itself along certain well-defined lines that waver and break only under the stronger pressure of a more gregarious race or the encroachments of inexorable business." (003-010) Immigrant clustering was not mere preference but survival: "A feeling of dependence upon mutual effort, natural to strangers in a strange land, unacquainted with its language and customs, sufficiently accounts for this." (003-010)

Riis offers the metaphor of an ethnic map: "A map of the city, colored to designate nationalities, would show more stripes than on the skin of a zebra, and more colors than any rainbow." (003-011) The Irish dominated the West Side tenement districts, the Germans the East Side. The Italian red "would be seen forcing its way northward along the line of Mulberry Street," (003-011) while "between the dull gray of the Jew, his favorite color, and the Italian red, would be seen squeezed in on the map a sharp streak of yellow, marking the narrow boundaries of Chinatown." (003-012) Each ethnic group arrived from distinct push factors: "Every fresh persecution of the Russian or Polish Jew on his native soil starts greater hordes hitherward to confound economical problems, and recruit the sweater's phalanx." (011-005)

## Role in the Tenements

Each immigrant group occupies a distinct ecological niche in Riis's urban geography. The Italians cluster in the Bend, working as rag-pickers and laborers. The Jews concentrate in the Tenth Ward, dominating the garment trade. The Chinese are confined to a few blocks of Mott Street. The Bohemians work in tenement cigar-making, the Germans spread across the city as the most assimilated group, and the Irish — once the poorest — have climbed into landlord and political-boss roles.

This ethnic succession is one of Riis's key analytical frameworks. The tenement population is not static; each group moves through it, leaving the worst housing to the newest arrivals. The Irish, who occupied the Fourth Ward slums in the 1840s, now own tenement property and run the political machines. The Italians, the newest large group, inherit the worst conditions. Riis traces the pattern explicitly: "The once unwelcome Irishman has been followed in his turn by the Italian, the Russian Jew, and the Chinaman, and has himself taken a hand at opposition, quite as bitter and quite as ineffectual, against these later hordes." (003-003) The Irishman's ascent is described with dark irony: "Victorious in defeat over his recent as over his more ancient foe, the one who opposed his coming no less than the one who drove him out, he dictates to both their politics, and, secure in possession of the offices, returns the native his greeting with interest, while collecting the rents of the Italian whose house he has bought with the profits of his saloon." (003-003)

The economic progression is similarly documented: "The German ragpicker of thirty years ago, quite as low in the scale as his Italian successor, is the thrifty tradesman or prosperous farmer of today. The Italian scavenger of our time is fast graduating into exclusive control of the corner fruit-stands, while his black-eyed boy monopolizes the boot-blacking industry in which a few years ago he was an intruder. The Irish hod-carrier in the second generation has become a bricklayer, if not the Alderman of his ward, while the Chinese coolie is in almost exclusive possession of the laundry business." (003-008, 003-009)

Riis's ethnic typology is detailed and often prejudiced — he describes Italians as "content to live in a pig-sty," Jews as "the thriftiest of the thrifty," and Chinese as "inscrutable" — reflecting the racial science of his era. Yet his fundamental argument cuts against nativism: the housing is the problem, not the people. Every group that escaped the tenements became, in his telling, respectable citizens. He insists that "New York's wage-earners have no other place to live, more is the pity. They are truly poor for having no better homes; waxing poorer in purse as the exorbitant rents to which they are tied, as ever was serf to soil, keep rising." (003-008)

## Exploitation and Labor

The newly arrived immigrant was uniquely vulnerable to economic exploitation, and Riis documents two principal systems of immigrant labor bondage. The Italian was ensnared by the padrone, who "having made his ten percent out of him en route, receives him at the landing and turns him to double account as a wage-earner and a rent-payer." (005-003) The "princely wages" promised before departure vanish: "in their place hardships and a dollar a day, beheft with the padrone's merciless mortgage, confront him." (005-003)

The Jewish immigrant fell into the sweater system, where the subcontractor exploited the isolation and desperation of newly arrived garment workers: "The sweater knows well that the isolation of the workman in his helpless ignorance is his sure foundation, and he has done what he could — with merciless severity where he could — to smother every symptom of awakening intelligence in his slaves." (011-005) The supply of labor seemed inexhaustible to Riis: "In this effort to perpetuate his despotism he has had the effectual assistance of his own system and the sharp competition that keep the men on starvation wages; of their constitutional greed, that will not permit the sacrifice of temporary advantage, however slight, for permanent good, and above all, of the hungry hordes of immigrants to whom no argument appeals save the cry for bread." (011-005) The Bohemian cigar-worker was similarly trapped by the dual-role landlord-employer who "reduces him to virtual serfdom by making his becoming his tenant, on such terms as he sees fit to make, the condition of employment at wages likewise of his own making." (012-002)

Among the eighteen groups, Riis draws subtle distinctions about immigrant resilience. He judges that the German "makes the most of life thus mortgaged" and "knows how to drag even the barracks upward a part of the way at least toward the ideal plane of the home." (003-013) By contrast: "The Italian and the poor Jew rise only by compulsion. The Chinaman does not rise at all; here, as at home, he simply remains stationary. The Irishman's genius runs to public affairs rather than domestic life; wherever he is mustered in force the saloon is the gorgeous centre of political activity." (003-013)

## The Immigrant's Children

The second generation occupies an ambiguous position in Riis's narrative. Children of immigrants assimilate faster than their parents but face the street's corruption. The "street Arab" — overwhelmingly the child of immigrants — represents both the promise of Americanization and its ugliest perversion. Riis describes the "assimilation" of Europe's oppressed children as "perfect" (019-003), but this assimilation takes the form of gang membership and petty crime rather than upward mobility. The Children's Aid Society, which Riis praises, "has sheltered quite three hundred thousand outcast, homeless, and orphaned children in its lodging-houses, and has found homes in the West for seventy thousand that had none." (015-013)

## Legacy

The immigrant's role in Riis's book anticipates the central insight of 20th-century urban sociology: the city is a mechanism for processing successive waves of newcomers, and the housing market is the mechanism's engine. The Chicago School sociologists — Park, Burgess, Wirth — would formalize this insight in the 1920s, but Riis had already documented it in journalistic form three decades earlier.

Riis's own argument, threaded throughout his ethnic portraits, is that the immigrant is not innately degraded but degraded by the tenement. "Were the question raised who makes the most of life thus mortgaged," he asks, the palm goes to the German — the immigrant who most successfully resists "its levelling tendency." (003-013) The book's insistence that environment, not heredity, determines character made it a foundational text of Progressive-era housing reform. And so, Riis concludes, "to the end of the long register, all toiling together in the galling fetters of the tenement." (003-013)
