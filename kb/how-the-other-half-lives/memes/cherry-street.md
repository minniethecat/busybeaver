# Cherry Street

## Description

Cherry Street runs along the East River waterfront in the Fourth Ward of Manhattan's Lower East Side. In the late 18th century, it was a fashionable residential street — George Washington lived on Cherry Hill during the early days of the republic. By Riis's era, it had become the cradle of tenement New York, its old mansions subdivided into dark, crowded apartments and its rear lots filled with the first rear tenements.

## In Riis's Account

Riis traces the tenement's origin to Cherry Street specifically. The old Knickerbocker mansions "along the East River front fell into the hands of real-estate agents and boardinghouse keepers," and from this transformation the rear tenement was born. (001-003) Within memory, Washington had "moved from his house on Cherry Hill as too far out of town to be easily reached." (001-003) The irony that the president's old neighborhood had become the archetypal slum was not lost on Riis.

Cherry Street tenements figure prominently in Chapter IV. Riis describes a block of them in detail — the fire that made ten families homeless, the rear tenement buildings wedged behind the street-front houses, and the constant presence of the sanitary policeman. (004-013) A block of Cherry Street tenements was demolished during Riis's lifetime, and their demolition became a small but significant reform victory. (004-020)

Gotham Court, the book's most notorious single building, stood on Cherry Street. (004-013) The street thus contained in microcosm the entire history Riis was telling: from aristocratic mansion to subdivided tenement to rear-tenement construction to model tenement to slum to demolition.

## Significance

Cherry Street is historically significant as the birthplace of the New York tenement. The first rear house, the first subdivided mansion, the first overcrowded cellar dwelling — all appeared here before spreading across the city. Riis's detailed attention to Cherry Street in both Chapter I (historical origin) and Chapter IV (contemporary conditions) reflects its dual role as origin point and ongoing crisis.
