## Overview

Rivington Street served as the southern boundary line of the densest concentration of Jewish settlement on Manhattan's Lower East Side in the 1890s. Together with Division Street, it framed the district that Riis described as having been colonized by Russian and Polish Jewish immigrants to a degree of extreme crowding unmatched elsewhere in the city.

## The Jewish Quarter's Southern Frontier

Riis identified Rivington Street as the lower edge of a zone dominated by Eastern European Jewish immigrants. "The Russian and Polish Jew, having overrun the district between Rivington and Division Streets, east of the Bowery, to the point of suffocation" [003-012], he wrote, using language that simultaneously recorded the geographic extent of the settlement and expressed the alarm with which native-born New Yorkers viewed the concentration. The phrase "to the point of suffocation" was characteristic of Riis's rhetoric — it conveyed both literal overcrowding (rooms designed for families of four housing twelve or more) and a broader anxiety about demographic transformation.

## A District Under Pressure

The Rivington-to-Division corridor was not static. Riis observed that the Jewish population was "filling the tenements of the old Seventh Ward to the river front, and disputing with the Italian every foot of available space in the back alleys of Mulberry Street" [003-012]. The Jewish settlement was expanding under its own internal pressure — the sheer number of new arrivals meant that the Rivington-Division core could not contain the population, and families spilled southward toward the river and westward into territory that Italian immigrants had already claimed. The result was a zone of friction in which ethnic succession occurred block by block, alley by alley.
