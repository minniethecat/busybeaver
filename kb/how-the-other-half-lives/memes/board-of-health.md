The Board of Health (later the Health Department) was the municipal authority responsible for public health in late 19th-century New York City. It played a critical role in the early regulation of tenement housing, as its sanitary jurisdiction gave it authority to inspect and condemn the worst dwellings.

## Origins

The Board was established in direct response to epidemic disease. Riis notes that the organization of a "Board of Health and the adoption of the 'Tenement House Act'" were direct results of public health crises [002-002], linking the birth of tenement regulation to the fight against infectious disease. The connection was logical: overcrowded, unsanitary tenements were breeding grounds for cholera, typhus, and tuberculosis, and public health authorities could not ignore the housing conditions that spread disease across class boundaries.

## Sanitary Authority

The Board's power derived from its sanitary mandate. It could inspect tenements, order improvements, and in extreme cases condemn buildings as unfit for habitation. The scope of the problem it inherited was immense: Riis documented that there were "three hundred underground lodging-houses in the city when the Health Department was organized" [001-010], indicating that below-ground dwellings — lacking light, air, and drainage — were widespread and entirely unregulated before the Board's creation.

The Board's inspections revealed conditions that shocked the public conscience. Cellar dwellings where families lived in standing water, rear tenements with no ventilation, and rooms where multiple families shared a single windowless space all came under the Board's purview. The Board's reports provided the factual basis for tenement reform legislation throughout the late 19th century.

## Limitations and Constraints

Despite its formal authority, the Board of Health was constrained by political considerations, limited resources, and the sheer scale of the housing crisis. Landlord influence and corrupt local politics often undermined enforcement efforts. Even when the Board issued orders, property owners frequently ignored them or secured political intervention to block enforcement. This gap between legal authority and practical capability was a recurring theme in Riis's critique of tenement reform — laws existed on the books but remained unenforced.

The Board also faced a fundamental tension in its dual mandate. It was expected to protect public health while respecting property rights, and in an era when the latter was nearly absolute, the former often lost. Riis's documentation of the Board's work was, implicitly, an argument for strengthening its enforcement powers and insulating it from political pressure.
