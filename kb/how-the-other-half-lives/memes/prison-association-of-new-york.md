# Prison Association of New York

The Prison Association of New York was a 19th-century criminal justice reform organization that investigated the causes of crime and the conditions of prisons in New York State. In Jacob Riis's *How the Other Half Lives*, the Association appears at a critical juncture in the Introduction, where its Secretary's testimony before a state legislative committee provided the analytical foundation for Riis's central argument: that the tenement districts were the primary engines of crime in the city.

## Testimony Before the Legislative Committee

The Prison Association's most significant appearance in Riis's account comes through the testimony of its Secretary before "a legislative committee appointed to investigate causes of the increase of crime in the State twenty-five years ago" (P04-002). From Riis's vantage point in 1890, this places the testimony around 1865, in the immediate aftermath of the Civil War and the 1863 New York City Draft Riots. The Secretary's testimony invoked the Draft Riots as a revelatory moment: "When the great riot occurred in 1863, every hiding-place and nursery of crime discovered itself by immediate and active participation in the operations of the mob" (P04-002).

The testimony drew a direct causal line between housing conditions and criminal behavior. The Secretary stated that "those very places and domiciles, and all that are like them, are today nurseries of crime, and of the vices and disorderly courses which lead to crime" (P04-002). This language -- "nurseries of crime" -- became a recurring motif throughout Riis's book, deployed to characterize the [[lodging-house|cheap lodging-houses]], the back-alley tenements, and the gang-ridden streets of the [[five-points|Five Points]] district.

## The Environmental Theory of Crime

The Prison Association's testimony articulated what would become a core tenet of Progressive-era criminal justice reform: that crime was not primarily a matter of individual moral failing but an environmental product of degraded living conditions. The Secretary offered quantitative evidence for this claim, testifying that "by far the largest part -- eighty percent at least -- of crimes against property and against the person are perpetrated by individuals who have either lost connection with home life, or never had any, or whose homes had ceased to be sufficiently separate, decent, and desirable to afford what are regarded as ordinary wholesome influences of home and family" (P04-002).

This statistical claim -- that four-fifths of all crime traced back to broken or inadequate home environments -- represented a significant departure from the prevailing moralistic theories of criminality. The Association's analysis implicitly argued that prisons were filled not by innately criminal individuals but by victims of the [[tenement]] system, people whose housing conditions had deprived them of the socializing influences of decent family life. The Secretary further specified that "the younger criminals seem to come almost exclusively from the worst tenement house districts, that is, when traced back to the very places where they had their homes in the city here" (P04-002), extending the environmental argument to [[crime|juvenile delinquency]] specifically.

## Connection to Tenement Reform

The Prison Association's testimony functioned within Riis's narrative as a bridge between criminal justice reform and the [[tenement-reform|tenement reform movement]]. By establishing that the tenement districts were not merely unpleasant places to live but active producers of crime, the Association's analysis made prison reform inseparable from housing reform. Riis drew the explicit conclusion: "Of one thing New York made sure at that early stage of the inquiry: the boundary line of the Other Half lies through the tenements" (P04-002).

This framing was strategic. The legislative committee before which the Association testified was apparently "the first legislative committee ever appointed to probe this sore" (P04-005) -- the "sore" being the interconnected problems of poverty, [[pauperism]], drunkenness, and crime that plagued the tenement districts. The committee's investigation, informed by the Prison Association's evidence, "went deeper down and uncovered its roots," concluding that "certain conditions and associations of human life and habitation are the prolific parents of corresponding habits and morals" (P04-005). This official governmental endorsement of the environmental theory of crime lent institutional weight to the reform arguments that Riis would amplify in his book.

## Role in the Reform Ecosystem

The Prison Association occupied a distinct niche within the network of 19th-century reform organizations operating in New York. Unlike the [[childrens-aid-society|Children's Aid Society]], which provided direct services to homeless children, or the [[society-for-the-prevention-of-cruelty-to-children|Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children]], which pursued legal intervention on behalf of abused minors, the Prison Association operated primarily through investigation, documentation, and legislative advocacy. Its method was to gather systematic evidence about the conditions that produced crime and to present that evidence to policymakers in forums -- such as the legislative committee on the increase of crime -- where it might shape public policy.

The Association also functioned as a bridge between the worlds of [[charity|philanthropy]] and government. Its Secretary's testimony represented a transfer of reform knowledge from the voluntary sector into the official record of state legislative proceedings, a mechanism by which the observations of prison reformers and [[sanitary-reform|sanitary investigators]] could be converted into actionable legislative findings. Riis himself performed a similar bridging function, translating the testimony of experts like the Prison Association's Secretary into language accessible to a broad public audience.

## Legacy in Riis's Account

In *How the Other Half Lives*, the Prison Association's testimony serves as the evidentiary anchor for the book's introduction. Riis placed the Secretary's words at the very beginning of his argument -- in the Introduction, before the first chapter -- establishing the environmental theory of crime as the lens through which all subsequent descriptions of tenement life should be understood. The recurring phrase "nurseries of crime" (P04-002), drawn directly from the Association's testimony, appears throughout the book to describe the [[lodging-house|cheap lodging-houses]] of the Bowery, the back-alley rookeries of Cherry Street, and the gang territories of the Fourth Ward.

By invoking the Prison Association's testimony, Riis positioned his own work not as novel social criticism but as the continuation of a law enforcement and reform consensus that had been building for a quarter century. The Association had established the facts; Riis's contribution was to give those facts a human face through his documentary photography and narrative journalism.
