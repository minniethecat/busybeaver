**Elizabeth Street** was a street on Manhattan's [[lower-east-side|Lower East Side]],
documented by [[jacob-riis]] in *How the Other Half Lives* as a site of tenement
sweatshops, immigrant labor, and police enforcement.

## Police and Surveillance

The street held an important police presence. Riis observed the midnight roll-call
at the Elizabeth Street police-station, a nightly ritual in which the "reserve"
force was mustered for the evening patrol of the immigrant wards (007-002).

The Elizabeth Street police station also served as a repository of local-color
anecdotes about immigrant life. Officers "will tell you with a laugh" that a
dead goat reported lying in Pell Street had mysteriously vanished before the
offal-cart arrived — apparently carried off by an Italian for a wake or feast in
a back alley (006-004).

## Immigrant Life and Labor

Elizabeth Street was home to many immigrant workers. Riis described women "sewing
and starving in an Elizabeth Street attic," capturing the overlap of tenement
living and garment industry exploitation that characterized the street (020-010).
Women of Riis's acquaintance lived in an Elizabeth Street tenement, providing him
with direct accounts of tenement conditions (014-032).

## Public Health Crisis

Elizabeth Street was the epicenter of a devastating measles epidemic that Riis
documented in harrowing detail. "Such an epidemic ravaged three crowded blocks in
Elizabeth Street on the heels of the grippe last winter, and, when it had spent
its fury, the death-maps in the Bureau of Vital Statistics looked as if a black
hand had been laid across those blocks" (014-020). The epidemic's death map
"overshadowed" neighboring tenements in Mott Street and covered a "particularly
packed settlement" on Mulberry Street, graphically illustrating how overcrowding
turned contagious disease into mass casualty events.

## Starvation and Madness

The extreme poverty of Elizabeth Street produced cases of psychological collapse
that Riis used to illustrate the human cost of tenement conditions. He recounted
the case of "an Elizabeth Street truck-driver whom the newspapers never heard
of" — a man who, unable to work for months with a family to provide for, lost his
mind when there was "neither food, nor a scrap of anything upon which money could
be raised" (014-028).
