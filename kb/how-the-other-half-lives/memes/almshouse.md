The almshouse (or poorhouse) on Blackwell's Island was the final destination for the most desperate of New York's tenement poor — the elderly, the disabled, and those who had exhausted family support, charity, and their own capacity to work. Riis depicted the almshouse not as a place of refuge but as a grim terminal point in the life cycle of tenement poverty. He placed it within a broader institutional landscape that included the penitentiary, the workhouse, and the lunatic asylum, all concentrated on the islands strung along the East River.

## The Institutional Landscape

Riis introduced his discussion of the almshouse with a powerful architectural metaphor. He wrote that "the shadow of the workhouse points like a scornful finger toward its neighbor, the almshouse, when the sun sets behind the teeming city across the East River, as if, could its stones speak, it would say before night drops its black curtain between them: 'You and I are brothers. I am not more bankrupt in moral purpose than you'" [022-002]. This personification — the workhouse scorning the almshouse, yet acknowledging their shared moral bankruptcy — was one of Riis's most rhetorically sophisticated passages, compressing his entire critique of the city's institutional approach to poverty into a single sunset image.

The concentration of institutions on the islands was deliberate: "Over on the islands that lie strung along the river and far up the Sound the Nether Half hides its deformity, except on show-days, when distinguished visitors have to be entertained and the sore is uncovered by the authorities with due municipal pride in the exhibit" [022-003]. The isolation of the poor on islands — Blackwell's, Ward's, Hart's — was the spatial expression of a city that preferred its poverty invisible.

## Causes of Almshouse Admission

Riis identified several pathways to the almshouse. The most damning was familial abandonment: "many of these old people have been sent to the almshouse to die by their heartless children, for whom they had worked faithfully as long as they were able" [022-008]. This was a reversal of the expected generational contract — parents who had sacrificed for their children were discarded when they could no longer contribute.

A second pathway was immigration: "Very many of them are old-country poor, sent, there is reason to believe, to America by the authorities to get rid of the obligation to support them" [022-008]. Riis was alleging that European authorities were deliberately exporting their indigent elderly to America, a claim that reflected both the real phenomenon of assisted emigration for the poor and Riis's nativist anxieties about the costs of immigration.

A third pathway was simple poverty: "Bitter poverty threw them all 'on the county,' often on the wrong county at that" [022-008]. The bureaucratic cruelty of being assigned to the wrong county — which could mean being cut off from whatever family or community ties remained — was another layer of institutional indifference.

## The Almshouse Population

Riis's visual description of the almshouse residents emphasized the slow, terminal rhythm of institutional life:

> "Rows of old women, some smoking stumpy, black clay-pipes, others knitting or idling, all grumbling, sit or stand under the trees that hedge in the almshouse, or limp about in the sunshine, leaning on crutches or bean-pole staffs." [022-005]

The "growler-gang" pun Riis employed here — calling them "a 'growler-gang' of another sort" [022-005] than the beer-fetching youth gangs of the tenement — was characteristic of his rhetorical method. The old women's grumbling was the almshouse equivalent of the tenement's growler culture: a collective ritual of complaint that was, simultaneously, a form of social bonding and a sign of institutional failure. "They grumble and growl from sunrise to sunset, at the weather, the breakfast, the dinner, the supper; at pork and beans as at corned beef and cabbage" [022-005].

## The Keeper's Perspective

Riis included a darkly comic encounter with the almshouse keeper that revealed the institutional perspective on its charges. When Riis expressed interest in the old women, the keeper erupted in enthusiasm: "'Take them?' he yelled. 'Take the thousand of them and be welcome. They will never be still, by ———, till they are sent up on Hart's Island in a box, and I'll be blamed if I don't think they will growl then at the style of the funeral'" [022-006].

When Riis clarified that he merely wanted to photograph them rather than relieve the keeper of his burden, "he turned away in speechless disgust, and to his dying day, I have no doubt, remembered my call as the day of the champion fool's visit to the island" [022-007]. The anecdote served multiple purposes: it humanized the keeper even as it exposed his callousness, and it revealed Riis's own self-awareness about the limits of his documentary project — he was there to observe and photograph, not to relieve.

## The Revolving Door

The almshouse was not a terminal destination for all who entered. Riis documented a population of repeaters who cycled through the island's institutions: "The commonest keeper soon learns to pick out almost at sight the 'cases' that will leave the penitentiary, the workhouse, the almshouse, only to return again and again, each time more hopeless, to spend their wasted lives in the bondage of the island" [022-013]. The word "bondage" was deliberate — Riis was equating the institutional circuit with a form of captivity, a life sentence served in installments.

The scale of this revolving population was documented with statistical precision. The workhouse alone registered 22,477 admissions in 1889, "of whom some had been there as many as twenty times before" [022-004]. The almshouse was part of a system that processed, warehoused, and recycled the poor without ever addressing the conditions that produced them.

## In Riis's Argument

The almshouse chapter served as the book's culminating institutional critique. By placing it in the penultimate chapter (XXII, "The Wrecks and the Waste"), Riis positioned the almshouse as the tenement's end point — the destination toward which every earlier chapter's pathologies (overcrowding, disease, child labor, crime) ultimately led. The almshouse was what happened when the tenement system had used up a human being and discarded the remains.

Riis's specific reform proposals focused on separating the functions that had been collapsed together on the islands: the sick belonged in hospitals, the insane in asylums, the aged poor in decent almshouses, and criminals in penitentiaries. The current system's failure to maintain these distinctions was, in his view, a moral indictment as much as an administrative one.
