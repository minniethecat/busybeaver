# Fire

## Definition

Fire was the most dramatic and lethal consequence of tenement construction. Wooden buildings, inadequate fire escapes, blocked stairwells, and overcrowding combined to make tenement fires a recurring catastrophe. Riis treats fire not merely as an accident but as a predictable outcome of the same conditions that produced disease and crime — a housing system that maximized density at the expense of safety.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

Riis opens his documentation of tenement fires with a recent example that made the danger vivid:

> "The fire made homeless ten families, who had paid their fifty cents a week rent almost to the day of the fire." (001-006)

The building was a rear house on Mott Street — one of the original tenant-houses. Its destruction by fire was the endpoint of the degradation Riis traces in Chapter I from the first rear house to the contemporary slum.

The "fire-escape" — in quotes because Riis considered them a cruel joke — was a particular target of his anger. The iron balconies bolted to tenement facades were designated fire escapes, but in practice they served as storage, playgrounds, and sleeping porches:

> "Under these iron bridges called fire-escapes, but loaded down, despite the law, with lumber and useless household goods." (004-026)

A fire that broke out on the upper floors of a tenement trapped the residents above the flames, and the overloaded fire escapes were worse than useless:

> "When a fire that broke out after the men had gone to their work trapped the women and children on the upper floors, the fire-escapes were loaded with the accumulated stuff of years." (004-019)

The firebug phenomenon — arson for insurance or revenge — added a criminal dimension to the fire problem. Riis reports that "a band of firebugs who made a business of setting fire to tenements" operated on the East Side. (010-020) A fire that "swept up with sudden fury through the tenement in the middle of the night" left residents with nothing. (010-021)

## Significance

Fire is the most visceral of Riis's arguments because it required no statistics to understand. A photograph of a burned-out tenement, or of a family huddled on the street with their salvaged belongings, made the case for reform more powerfully than any report. Riis uses fire as the climactic example in his chapter on the tenement's physical dangers because it combined every failure of the system: bad construction, inadequate regulation, corrupt enforcement, and human tragedy.

The fire-escape scandal was also one of the Tenement House Act's major regulatory targets. The 1901 "New Law" required enclosed, fireproof stairwells — a direct response to the deaths Riis and other reformers had documented.
