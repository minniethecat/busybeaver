# Street Arab

The Street Arab was, in Riis's words, "as much of an institution in New York as Newspaper Row" (017-002). These homeless boys — newsboys, bootblacks, vagrants, and runaways — populated the streets of the city, sleeping in steam gratings, sandboxes, and boiler rooms, and surviving by their wits. Riis devoted an entire chapter to them, treating the Street Arab as both a symptom of tenement failure and an object of charitable intervention.

## Origins

The Street Arab population was fed by multiple sources. "Whence this army of homeless boys?" Riis asked, and answered with a catalogue of causes: orphans whose parents were imprisoned, boys fleeing drunken fathers, and children turned out by grinding poverty. One urchin of twelve explained simply: "We wuz six, and we ain't got no father. Some on us had to go" (017-009). The going was "easy enough," Riis observed — "There is very little to hold the boy who has never known anything but a home in a tenement" (017-009).

A census of the Newsboys' Lodging House on any night showed "an odd mixture of small humanity as could hardly be got together in any other spot" (017-002), including runaways from across the United States and abroad, drawn to New York "with a queer fascination" (017-002).

## Character

Riis characterized the Street Arab with ambivalence. The boy was a "vagabond," "acknowledging no authority and owing no allegiance to anybody or anything," with his "grimy fist raised against society whenever it tries to coerce him" (017-003). Yet he was also "as bright and sharp as the weasel," possessing "sturdy independence, love of freedom and absolute self-reliance, together with his rude sense of justice" (017-003). Riis noted that successful bankers, clergymen, lawyers, and even statesmen "of national repute" had emerged from these streets, bearing "evidence in their lives to the potency of such missionary efforts" (017-003).

The Street Arab "puts his whole little soul into what interests him for the moment" (017-014), whether it was pulverizing a rival or attending the "gospel shop" on Sundays. This intensity was such that when the boys in the Duane Street Lodging House bought boxing gloves, "the trapeze suffered a temporary eclipse until this new toy had been tested to the extent of several miniature black eyes" (017-014).

## Life on the Streets

The Street Arab's headquarters was Newspaper Row, around Park Row and Printing House Square, where boys gathered near the newspaper offices to hawk papers and pick up odd jobs. In winter they fought for warm spots around the grated vent-holes that let out steam from the press-rooms; in summer they played craps on the curb (017-004).

Their sleeping arrangements were improvised and precarious: a truck in the street, a convenient outhouse, a hay-barge at the wharf, an old boiler at the East River, or the end of an iron pipe by the Harlem Bridge (017-005). Two preferences emerged in an overheard discussion among boys at the lodging-house: the steam-grating versus the sandbox — the latter winning because "you could curl all up in it" (017-005).

The boys lived in constant vigilance against "their enemy, the policeman, whose chief business in life is to move them on, and of the agent bent on robbing them of their cherished freedom" (017-004). They knew every open door, hidden turn, and secret passage, making pursuit "like chasing the fleet-footed mountain goat in his rocky fastnesses" (017-004).

## The Children's Aid Society

The principal institutional response to the Street Arab was the Children's Aid Society, which Riis described as the "strongest of all its defences for itself and for the boy" (017-010). The Society operated five boys' lodging-houses and one for girls in the city. The Duane Street Lodging House alone had sheltered nearly a quarter of a million different boys since 1855, at a total expense of less than half a million dollars — of which the boys themselves contributed over $172,000 through their earnings (017-020).

The lodging-house operated on principles of self-help. The rules were succinct: "Boys who swear and chew tobacco cannot sleep here" (017-010). Rates were six cents for a bed, six for breakfast, and six for supper — "as much as he can eat" (017-014). Boys could borrow capital to start a bootblack's kit or buy an armful of papers, but "every cent is charged to him as carefully as though the transaction involved as many hundreds of dollars" (017-013). Riis noted with approval that "no pauper was ever bred within these houses" (017-013).

The Society also maintained twenty-one industrial schools, two free reading-rooms, a dressmaking school, a laundry, a sick-children's mission, and a brush factory for crippled boys. Its largest impact, however, came through its emigration program, sending "battalions of young emigrants" to homes in the Far West "to grow up self-supporting men and women safe from the temptations and the vice of the city" (017-021).

## The Trade School Movement

Late in the chapter, Riis turned to the New York Trade Schools, established by Colonel Richard Auchmuty, as a longer-term solution. He lamented that out of twenty-three million dollars paid annually to mechanics in the building trades in New York, "less than six millions go to the workman born in New York, while his boy roams the streets with every chance of growing up a vagabond" (017-023). The trade school model, in which "a young man may acquire the theory as well as the practice of a trade in a few months at a merely nominal outlay" (017-023), was beginning to spread to other cities. Riis expressed hope that such efforts would "put an end to the existence of the New York Street Arab, of the native breed at least" (017-023).

He further observed that the union men who had once opposed such training were beginning to send their own sons to the trade schools — "one may perhaps be permitted to hope that an era of better sense is dawning that shall witness a rescue work upon lines which, when the leaven has fairly had time to work, will put an end to the existence of the New York Street Arab, of the native breed at least" (017-023).
