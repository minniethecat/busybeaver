# Potter's Field

## Description

The Potter's Field on Hart Island, in Long Island Sound off the Bronx, was the final destination for New York's poorest dead. Those who could not afford a private burial — paupers, unidentified bodies, stillborn infants, and the unclaimed dead of the city's hospitals and prisons — were interred here in mass graves. Riis reports that "for five years past one person in every ten who died in this city was buried in the Potter's Field," a statistic that condensed the human cost of the tenement system into a single devastating number. (021-002)

## In Riis's Account

The Potter's Field appears in Riis's text not as a described place but as a statistical endpoint — the number that made the tenement's consequences undeniable. One in ten deaths ending in a mass grave was not a marginal phenomenon but a measure of how many New Yorkers lived and died in conditions of absolute destitution. The statistic appears in Chapter XXI ("Pauperism in the Tenements") alongside the Charity Organization Society's case data, reinforcing Riis's argument that poverty was not an individual failing but a structural condition with measurable, fatal outcomes.

Riis does not visit the Potter's Field or describe it; its power in his narrative comes precisely from its absence as a described place. It is the destination too grim to photograph, the statistic that speaks for itself.
