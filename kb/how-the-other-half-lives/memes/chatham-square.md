**Chatham Square** was a major transit junction and public square in Lower
Manhattan's [[fourth-ward|Fourth Ward]], described by [[jacob-riis]] as a
vantage point into the dense immigrant neighborhoods that surrounded it.

## Location and Transit

Chatham Square served as a key transit node, with the "Third Avenue Elevated
Railroad" stopping at the square, allowing passengers to "ride up half a mile
through" the tenement district for a panoramic view of immigrant life (011-007).
From this elevated vantage, the "real life" of Mott Street's Chinatown was in
full view (009-016).

## The Old Fourth Ward

Riis situated Chatham Square as the northern boundary of the old Fourth Ward
slums. "Down below Chatham Square, in the old Fourth Ward," lay the "cradle of
the tenement" — the original slum district where the tenement house evil first
took root in New York (004-002).

## Lodging Houses and the Homeless Population

The streets radiating from Chatham Square — particularly Chatham Street and the
Bowery — were lined with cheap lodging houses, or "caravansaries," that Riis
estimated harbored "nightly a population as large as that of many a thriving
town" (008-002). By Riis's calculation, "more than nine thousand homeless young
men lodge nightly along Chatham Street and the Bowery, between the City Hall and
the Cooper Union" (008-014). These lodging houses formed a corridor of transient
poverty that connected Chatham Square to the wider downtown homeless geography.

## Charity Fraud at the Square

Riis used Chatham Square as the setting for one of his most memorable lessons in
the pitfalls of indiscriminate charity. He described giving money regularly to
"an old woman who sat in Chatham Square with a baby done up in a bundle of rags,
moaning piteously in sunshine and rain, 'Please, help the poor'" (021-008). One
night he discovered the "baby" was nothing but rags, and the woman was "dead
drunk" — a cautionary tale he deployed to argue for the "scientific charity"
approach of the [[charity-organization-society|Charity Organization Society]].
