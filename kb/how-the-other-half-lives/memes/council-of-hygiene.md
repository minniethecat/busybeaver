# Council of Hygiene

The Council of Hygiene was a citizens' reform body active in New York City during the post-Civil War era. Composed largely of physicians and civic leaders, the Council undertook the first systematic survey of tenement living conditions in the city, producing findings that helped catalyze the organized sanitary reform movement and the creation of New York's first public health infrastructure.

## Origins and Formation

The Council of Hygiene emerged from the civic alarm that followed the Civil War, when New York's ruling class recognized that epidemic disease originating in the slums threatened the entire city. Jacob Riis places its work in the immediate postwar period, noting that "the dread of advancing cholera, with the guilty knowledge of the harvest field that awaited the plague in New York's slums, pricked the conscience of the community into action soon after the close of the war." (002-002) This atmosphere of epidemiological fear provided the political energy for organized sanitary investigation.

The Council was part of a broader citizens' movement — a voluntary association of reform-minded professionals who believed that data collection and public exposure could force government action. Unlike later official bodies such as the [[board-of-health|Board of Health]], the Council had no statutory authority; its power derived entirely from the credibility of its members and the persuasiveness of its findings.

## The Tenement Survey

The Council's principal contribution was a comprehensive sanitary survey — what Riis calls "a thorough canvass of the tenements" (002-002) — that documented conditions in the city's worst housing districts. This survey was among the first attempts to apply systematic, empirical investigation to the problem of urban poverty. The Council's investigators entered [[cellar-dwelling|cellar dwellings]], rear tenements, and overcrowded barracks, cataloguing the absence of light, ventilation, and sanitation that characterized [[tenement]] life.

The survey revealed conditions that shocked the public conscience: families living in windowless interior rooms, cellar apartments below tidewater level flooded with sewage, and multiple households crammed into spaces designed for single families. These findings established a factual baseline against which all subsequent reform efforts would be measured. As Riis notes, the data collection "had been begun already in the previous year" — that is, before the legislative response of 1867 — but was delayed by successive epidemics of cholera and smallpox, events that "emphasized the need of it" even as they slowed the work. (002-002) It was not until 1869 that the survey "got fairly under way and began to tell." (002-002)

## Influence on Legislation

The Council of Hygiene's survey provided the evidentiary foundation for New York's first generation of tenement reform legislation. Riis traces a direct line from the citizens' investigation to institutional action: "A citizens' movement resulted in the organization of a Board of Health and the adoption of the 'Tenement-House Act' of 1867, the first step toward remedial legislation." (002-002)

The legislative response was immediate and concrete. The [[board-of-health|Board of Health]], established in 1866, was empowered to inspect tenements, order sanitary improvements, and condemn uninhabitable dwellings. The [[tenement-house-act|Tenement-House Act of 1867]] codified minimum standards for light and ventilation — beginning with the banning of the "dark bedroom," the windowless interior room that the Council's survey had identified as a primary health hazard. In the first year of enforcement, the Board ordered "the cutting of more than forty-six thousand windows in interior rooms, chiefly for ventilation." (002-002)

The reach of reform extended to the most desperate form of housing: by 1871, "the department succeed at last in ousting the 'cave-dwellers' and closing some five hundred and fifty cellars south of Houston Street, many of them below tidewater, that had been used as living apartments." (002-002) These achievements, slow and incomplete as they were, traced their origins to the Council's initial survey.

## The Council's Diagnosis

The Council of Hygiene's most enduring rhetorical contribution was its phrasing of the tenement problem. In its official report, the Council characterized the housing crisis as "the result of forgetfulness of the poor" (002-003) — a formulation that framed the tenements not as a natural disaster or an inevitable feature of urban life, but as a failure of civic attention and moral responsibility.

Riis quotes this phrase with pointed irony in "The Awakening," his chapter on the origins of the reform movement. He notes that the evil the Council diagnosed had "a way of avenging itself" (002-003), spreading outward from the tenement districts in the form of epidemic [[disease]], crime, and social disorder. The "forgetfulness" the Council had described was not merely passive neglect; it was, in Riis's telling, a form of willful ignorance that carried a cost for the entire city.

The Council's language — characteristically restrained, even euphemistic — reflected the civic culture of its time. Riis describes the Council as having "mildly put it" (002-003), suggesting that even the reformers' own rhetoric understated the severity of the conditions they had documented. The gap between the brutal facts of the survey and the moderate language of its authors was, for Riis, itself a symptom of the problem: a society that could not bring itself to speak plainly about the suffering it tolerated.

## Legacy

The Council of Hygiene's influence extended well beyond its immediate legislative achievements. By establishing the principle that tenement conditions were susceptible to systematic investigation and public accountability, the Council created a template that later reformers — including Riis himself — would follow. The book *How the Other Half Lives* can be understood, in part, as a photographic and journalistic extension of the sanitary survey methodology the Council pioneered.

The Council's work also established the intellectual framework that would govern tenement reform for decades: the conviction that data, publicly presented, could mobilize political will; that epidemic disease was a problem of housing rather than individual morality; and that the conditions of the poor were a matter of legitimate concern for the entire community. As the [[sanitary-reform|sanitary reform]] movement matured, it built directly on the survey methodology and civic-organizing model the Council had demonstrated.

Yet Riis's retrospective assessment is ambivalent. By the time he wrote in 1890, the "fifteen thousand tenant houses that were the despair of the sanitarian in the past generation have swelled into thirty-seven thousand" (P04-003), and the Council's mild diagnosis of "forgetfulness" had been overtaken by a darker recognition: that the tenement system was not a temporary aberration but a permanent, profit-driven feature of the urban landscape. The Council had awakened the city's conscience, but the evil it named proved more durable than the remedies it inspired.
