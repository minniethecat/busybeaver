# Daniel Murphy

Daniel Murphy was the blind landlord of Blind Man's Alley, a notorious slum court in the Fourth Ward documented by Jacob Riis in *How the Other Half Lives*. Murphy's blindness — he could not see the conditions of the properties he owned — became, in Riis's telling, a metaphor for the moral blindness of the entire landlord class. Riis noted that the alley's name came from its population of blind beggars, but the blind landlord who owned it added a layer of grim irony to the story.

## In Riis's Account

Riis described Blind Man's Alley as a court of tenements housing blind beggars, owned by a blind landlord. The convergence of blind owner and blind tenants was, for Riis, an almost too-perfect illustration of the tenement system's indifference. Murphy collected rents from tenants whose blindness forced them into begging, while his own blindness meant he never had to confront the conditions of the property he owned. Riis used the example to argue that the landlord-tenant relationship in the tenement districts was fundamentally broken — based on extraction rather than any mutual obligation.
