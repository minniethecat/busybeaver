# Saloon

## Definition

The *saloon* in Riis's New York was the working-class alternative to the middle-class parlor — a neighborhood drinking establishment that served as social club, political headquarters, employment agency, and sometimes crime scene, all in one. Riis estimates that the tenement districts contained one saloon for every ten families (018-003), and he treats the saloon as the most visible symptom of the tenement's failure to provide decent common space. The saloon thrived, in his analysis, because the tenement apartment was too small and too grim to serve as a social venue, driving men — and sometimes women and children — into the street and the barroom.

## Manifestation in the Tenements

The saloon was central to the political economy of the slums (018-004). Riis documents its role in the "stale-beer" trade (Ch. VII, 007-014), where legitimate saloons sold their keg dregs to basement dives. But the regular saloon was a far more pervasive institution, present on nearly every block and deeply entangled with the ward politics that governed the tenement districts.

The saloon-keeper was often a figure of local influence — a natural ally of the ward boss who could deliver votes, cash checks, and extend credit. This political function was inseparable from the saloon's economic one. The police tolerated saloons that paid the right tribute, raided those that did not, and the resulting system of graft became the subject of repeated reform campaigns.

Riis also notes the saloon's role in the "stale-beer" economy. The legitimate saloon sold its keg dregs to basement "dives" that served them to customers who could not afford the regular price. The chapter on the stale-beer dive raid (Ch. VII) traces this supply chain from the respectable saloon to the criminal dive.

## Significance

The saloon occupies an ambiguous position in Riis's moral geography. Unlike the stale-beer dive, which he condemns without reservation, the saloon is for him a symptom rather than a cause — a business that exists because the tenement offers no decent alternative for social life. His reform program does not propose prohibition but rather the creation of parks, reading rooms, and settlement houses that might compete with the saloon for the tenement dweller's leisure time.

This ambivalence makes the saloon one of Riis's more nuanced treatments. He understood that the saloon was, for many immigrants, the only accessible public space — the one place where a working man could sit, talk, and read a newspaper without paying more than the price of a beer. The solution was not to close the saloons but to build a city where people did not need them.
