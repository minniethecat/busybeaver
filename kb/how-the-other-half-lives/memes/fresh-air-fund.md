# Fresh Air Fund

The Fresh Air Fund was a charitable organization that arranged summer excursions for tenement children, taking them from the overcrowded, airless streets of the Lower East Side to the countryside for brief respites of fresh air and green space. Jacob Riis mentioned the organization in *How the Other Half Lives* as an example of the practical charity that addressed, however modestly, one of the tenement's most fundamental deprivations: access to nature.

## Mission

The Fund operated on the principle that exposure to fresh air and rural environments was a health intervention for children whose lives were otherwise spent entirely within the polluted, overcrowded tenement districts. Children were taken by train or boat to farms and camps upstate or in New Jersey, where they spent days or weeks in environments radically different from the slum streets. For many, Riis noted, these excursions provided their first experience of grass, trees, and unpolluted air.

## In Riis's Account

Riis cited the Fresh Air Fund alongside the Children's Aid Society's emigration program and the trade school movement as evidence that practical, non-sectarian charity could make a measurable difference in tenement children's lives. The Fund addressed a specific problem — the absence of nature and fresh air in the tenement environment — with a specific, achievable intervention. Unlike the large-scale housing reforms Riis advocated, the Fresh Air Fund required no legislation and overcame no landlord opposition; it simply gave children a temporary escape.
