## Location and Reputation

Hell's Kitchen was a West Side Manhattan tenement district that Jacob Riis described as one of the most wicked neighborhoods in all of New York City. Writing in *How the Other Half Lives*, Riis declared flatly: "There is nothing below that line that can outdo in wickedness Hell's Kitchen, in the region of three-cent whiskey" (014-005). The "line" Riis referred to was Fourteenth Street, which many New Yorkers erroneously believed "fenced off the good from the bad" (014-005). Hell's Kitchen stood as living proof that the boundary was a fiction -- the West Side slums above Fourteenth Street were every bit as dangerous and degraded as the more famous [[five-points|Five Points]] or [[mulberry-street|Mulberry Bend]] below it.

The district lay along the North (Hudson) River waterfront, where the docks, slaughterhouses, and gas-houses attracted "the roughest crowds and support the vilest saloons," which Riis identified as the forces that "invariably form slum-centres" (014-005). Its geographic counterpoise was at the other end of Thirty-ninth Street on the East River, home of the infamous Rag Gang, though Riis made clear that neither gang district had a monopoly on violence: "the gangs belt the city like a huge chain from the Battery to Harlem" (019-004).

The neighborhood's population was overwhelmingly Irish, consistent with the West Side's status as the primary Irish [[irish-immigrants|immigrant]] settlement zone. On Riis's ethnic map, the Irish colored the West Side green (003-011). The Irish had once been the despised newcomers of the mid-nineteenth century; by the 1890s, they had risen to political dominance through Tammany Hall and the ward system, even as newer immigrant waves crowded into the tenement districts they had pioneered (003-003).

## The Three-Cent Whiskey Economy

Hell's Kitchen's defining feature, as Riis repeatedly emphasized, was its association with "three-cent whiskey" (014-005) (018-005). The district was saturated with [[saloon|saloons]] that sold cheap, potent liquor to the working poor at ruinous prices for public health and morality. Among the establishments Riis singled out by name were the "Witches' Broth" and the "Plug Hat" -- "names ominous of the concoctions brewed there and of their fatally generous measure" (018-005). These saloons were not merely drinking establishments; they were the political engine rooms of the district. Riis observed that "the rumshop turns the political crank in New York. The natural yield is rum politics" (018-003). Saloon-keepers stepped directly from their bars into city and state councils (018-004), and the saloon provided the political protection that kept gang members out of jail: "The saloon, in ninety-nine cases out of a hundred, stands behind the bargain" (019-010).

The connection between low wages, squalid housing, and heavy drinking was not accidental in Riis's analysis. He argued that "the scandalous scarcity of water in the hot summer when the thirst of the million tenants must be quenched, if not in that in something else, has in the past years more than all other causes encouraged drunkenness among the poor" (014-029). The three-cent whiskey of Hell's Kitchen was the poison that the tenement system forced upon its victims.

## The Hell's Kitchen Gang

The Hell's Kitchen Gang was among the most formidable of New York's street gangs and gave the district its fearsome reputation. In Riis's account, no other neighborhood was as thoroughly unified behind its criminal element: "When it comes to Hell's Kitchen, or to its compeers at the other end of Thirty-ninth Street over by the East River ... the Rag Gang and its allies have no need of fearing treachery in their periodical battles with the police. The entire neighborhood takes a hand on these occasions, the women in the front rank, partly from sheer love of the 'fun,' but chiefly because husbands, brothers, and sweethearts are in the fight to a man and need their help" (019-010).

Gang battles with [[the-policeman|police]] were waged with military tactics and weaponry. "Chimney-tops form the staple of ammunition then, and stacks of loose brick and paving-stones, carefully hoarded in upper rooms as a prudent provision against emergencies" (019-010). The police responded by establishing "regular patrol posts ... on the housetops in times of trouble in these localities" but even with rooftop patrols, officers did not "escape whole-skinned, if, indeed, with their lives; neither does the gang" (019-010). Riis noted that the only cure the policeman knew for the tough was "the club, and he lays it on without stint whenever and wherever he has the chance" (019-010).

The gang's repertoire extended beyond street brawling to organized extortion and terror bombing. "The Hell's Kitchen Gang and the Rag Gang have both distinguished themselves within recent times by blowing up objectionable stores with stolen gunpowder" (019-018). Storekeepers who refused to pay protection money to the gangs' annual "picnic" or "ball" -- a fiction of a social club that served as a blackmail racket -- could expect a visit on the first dark night and count themselves lucky if their premises escaped being "altogether wrecked" (019-018).

The political dimension was inseparable from the criminal one. Gang members operated with near impunity because of their political connections. Indictments piled up by the basketful, yet no jail held the gang leaders for long, because "the political backing that has saved him in the past can do it again" (019-010). Riis understood this as a commodity "only exchangeable 'for value received'" (019-010) -- the value being the gang's services at election time, intimidating voters and turning out the ward for the right candidate.

## Physical Appearance: The Deceptive Facade

One of Riis's most pointed observations about Hell's Kitchen was the disconnect between its physical appearance and its true character. He wrote: "Neither Hell's Kitchen, nor Murderers' Row bears its true character stamped on the front. They are not quite old enough, perhaps" (023-007). Unlike the ancient rookeries of London, New York's worst tenement districts were relatively young and did not yet look as menacing as they actually were. Some even had "brownstone fronts" (023-006), giving them an air of respectability that concealed the misery within.

This deception, Riis argued, was itself dangerous, because it allowed the well-meaning but uninstructed citizens -- "who are always in the majority" (023-008) -- to convince themselves that conditions were not as bad as reformers claimed. "Many an apple has a fair skin and a rotten core," Riis warned (023-007). The lesson of Hell's Kitchen was that "to get at the pregnant facts of tenement-house life one must look beneath the surface" (023-007).

## Sebastopol and the Riis Photograph

In the list of illustrations for *How the Other Half Lives*, Riis paired Hell's Kitchen with a single, evocative name: "Hell's Kitchen and Sebastopol" (P04-008). The illustration, a photograph of a street scene showing a five-story tenement building with a blank windowless side wall, suggests that Sebastopol was the name of a particular building or block within the Hell's Kitchen district -- likely named by its inhabitants after the Crimean War siege city, a grimly ironic borrowing typical of tenement nicknaming. The photograph accompanied the Introduction to Riis's book, positioning Hell's Kitchen at the outset of his argument as an emblem of the crisis he sought to expose.

The pairing of Hell's Kitchen with Sebastopol in the book's front matter was strategic. Before any chapter had been read, the reader encountered these two names as a kind of challenge: places that sounded like battlefields, located not in some distant war zone but in the heart of America's greatest city. Riis meant them to stand as proof that "the boundary line of the Other Half lies through the tenements" (P04-002).
