## Overview

Russian Jews constituted the largest and most visible segment of the Jewish population on Manhattan's Lower East Side during the period Jacob Riis documented in *How the Other Half Lives*. Distinguishable from their Polish Jewish neighbors by dialect, custom, and the harsh gutturals of their speech, Russian Jews were the subject of some of Riis's most sustained — and most ambivalent — ethnographic description.

## Settlement Patterns

Russian Jews, along with Polish Jews and Italians, participated in the ethnic succession that reshaped the Lower East Side in the late nineteenth century. They displaced earlier German and Irish residents, and were in turn pressed by newly arriving Italians (003-003). Russian Jews clustered in specific tenement blocks, their houses sheltering large families in conditions that Riis described as inimical to domestic order (025-026).

## Language and Culture

The "harsh gutturals of the Russian Jew" were a marked presence in the Jewish quarter (006-008). Riis's description, while pejorative in tone, reflected the linguistic distinctiveness of Russian Yiddish speakers within the broader Jewish immigrant community. Russian Jews maintained synagogues, charitable societies, and mutual aid organizations that structured daily life in the quarter.

## Living Conditions and Poverty

The difficulties of maintaining cleanliness and order in tenement houses that sheltered Russian Jews were noted by Riis as a consequence of overcrowding rather than cultural deficiency (025-026). Russian Jews, like other immigrant groups, were packed into tenement apartments with inadequate water supply, ventilation, and sanitary facilities — conditions that reformers cited in their campaigns for tenement house legislation.

## Distinction from Polish Jews

Riis maintained a distinction between Russian and Polish Jews throughout his study, reflecting contemporary perceptions of difference within the Jewish immigrant community. Russian Jews were often perceived as more recently arrived and less acculturated than their Polish counterparts, though both groups shared the experience of flight from persecution in the Pale of Settlement and the challenges of tenement life in New York.
