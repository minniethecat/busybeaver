# First Avenue

## The East Side Corridor

First Avenue runs along the eastern edge of Manhattan, parallel to the [[east-river|East River]]. In the 1890s, it marked the boundary between the dense tenement districts of the Lower East Side and the industrial waterfront. Riis documented the avenue as a zone where the worst extremes of tenement life were on display — from sweatshop labor to child homelessness.

## A Scene of Overcrowding

One of the most striking scenes Riis recorded on First Avenue was a tenement interior: "I shudder at the recollection of one such scene in a First Avenue tenement. It was in the middle of the night" (010-021). The description that followed detailed an apartment so overcrowded that the residents slept in shifts, with boarders paying for the privilege of sleeping on the floor. This scene exemplified the "lodger evil" that Riis identified as one of the principal drivers of tenement degradation — the economic pressure that forced families to pack strangers into already cramped quarters.

## The Rag Gang's Territory

Further downtown, where First Avenue approached "the Village," Riis placed the territory of the [[rag-gang|Rag Gang]]: "Further down First Avenue in 'the Village,' the Rag Gang and its allies have no need of sleeping quarters other than those provided by the street" (019-010). The area was a gathering point for the city's homeless children — the "street Arabs" who survived by collecting and selling rags, bones, and other refuse.
