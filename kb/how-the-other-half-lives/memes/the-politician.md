# The Politician

## Biography

The ward boss was the most powerful figure in tenement politics — the local representative of Tammany Hall who controlled jobs, relief, and police protection in exchange for votes. Riis devotes scattered references throughout the book to the political machine that governed the slums, not as a formal institution but as a network of personal relationships built on mutual obligation. The ward boss found jobs for the unemployed, paid rent for the evicted, and intervened with the police for the arrested — and on election day, the debt was collected at the ballot box.

Riis described how, during election seasons, "the lodging-houses come out strong on the side of the political boss who has the biggest 'barrel'" (008-015). The "colonization of voters" — rounding up lodging-house residents and marching them to the polls — was, in Riis's words, "an evil of the first magnitude, none the less because both parties smirch their hands with it" (008-015). This electoral machinery was the engine of the machine's power, converting tenement poverty into political currency.

## Role in the Tenements

Riis's treatment of the ward boss is more nuanced than might be expected from a reform-minded journalist. He understood that the machine filled a genuine need in immigrant communities that the city's formal institutions ignored. When a family was evicted, the landlord called the marshal; the ward boss called the landlord. The relationship was exploitative but functional — a form of informal governance that existed because the legitimate government had failed.

The landlord-politician alliance was a structural feature of tenement life. Riis documented a building in Ludlow Street "owned by a well-known Jewish liquor dealer and politician, a triple combination that bodes ill for his tenants" (011-015). The fusion of landlord, saloon-keeper, and political operative in a single person was not unusual — it was the logical endpoint of a system in which housing, alcohol, and votes were the three currencies of power in the tenement districts (011-015).

The saloon was the politician's operational base — the place where votes were counted, favors distributed, and ward-level power consolidated. The saloon-keeper was often a ward heeler himself, and the saloon's back room was the real seat of local government in the tenement districts (018-004). As Riis observed, the saloon was "the one bright and cheery spot" in many tenement blocks, making it the natural center of political organization (018-008).

## Multiple Perspectives

The ward boss was the most divisive figure in tenement society, seen as a savior or a villain depending entirely on one's position in the urban hierarchy. To the immigrant tenement dweller, the ward boss was often the most reliable source of help in a world where formal institutions offered nothing. When the breadwinner was arrested, the ward boss posted bail. When the rent came due and the family had nothing, the ward boss paid — or persuaded the landlord to wait. When a son needed a job on the docks or in the street-cleaning department, the ward boss arranged it. The price — a vote, and loyalty — was modest compared to the alternative, which was destitution with no recourse at all.

To the middle-class reformer, the ward boss was the embodiment of everything wrong with urban politics: corruption, graft, the subversion of the public good by private interest. The civil-service reform movement of the 1880s, which sought to replace patronage appointments with competitive examinations, was aimed directly at the machine's control of public employment. Reformers like Theodore Roosevelt, who served as Civil Service Commissioner before becoming New York's police commissioner, saw the ward boss as the enemy of honest government and worked to dismantle the spoils system that sustained him. Yet reformers were haunted by an uncomfortable fact: the machine provided services that the reformers' ideal of limited, meritocratic government did not.

To the political scientist and the urban historian, the ward boss represents a functional response to a structural problem. The American city of the late 19th century had no welfare state, no unemployment insurance, no public housing, and a police force and court system that served property interests first. The machine emerged to fill the void, providing a shadow welfare system funded by graft and organized through ethnic solidarity. This "functionalist" interpretation, articulated by later scholars, recasts the ward boss not as a criminal but as an entrepreneurial politician who solved a coordination problem — aggregating immigrant votes and distributing city resources in the absence of formal democratic institutions that served the poor.

From the landlord's perspective, the politician was both partner and threat. The landlord needed the machine to block housing-code enforcement, and the machine needed the landlord to deliver tenant votes. But the relationship was inherently unstable: the ward boss's need to maintain credibility with immigrant constituents meant that he sometimes intervened against landlords on behalf of tenants, forcing concessions that cut into rental income. The landlord-politician alliance was a marriage of convenience, not affection, and each party was constantly calculating whether the other remained useful.

Within the immigrant community, attitudes toward the ward boss were stratified by generation. First-generation immigrants, struggling with English and unfamiliar with American institutions, were the most dependent on the machine and the most loyal. Their American-born children, educated in public schools and more fluent in the language of reform and civic obligation, were more likely to see the machine as an embarrassment — a relic of the old country's patronage politics that had no place in a modern democracy. This generational split within immigrant communities prefigured the machine's eventual decline, as the second generation moved into the middle class and abandoned the machine for the reform politics of the Progressive Era.

## Legacy

Riis's portrait of tenement politics influenced the Progressive-era push for civil-service reform, nonpartisan municipal elections, and professional city management — all designed to break the machine's hold on immigrant voters. The tension between Riis's reform agenda and his recognition that the machine met real needs remains unresolved in the book, and it prefigures later debates about whether reform should work through or around existing community power structures.
