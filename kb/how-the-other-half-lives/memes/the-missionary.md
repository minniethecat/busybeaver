# The Missionary

## Biography

The city missionary was a fixture of the tenement districts — typically a Protestant minister or lay worker who lived in or near the slums and devoted their career to serving the poor. Unlike the distant charity patron (represented by figures like Mrs. Astor), the missionary was present in the tenements daily, visiting families, running Sunday schools, and sometimes operating small relief programs. Riis writes from the perspective of someone who worked alongside these figures; his own employer, the *New York Tribune*, had missionary connections through its editor.

## Role in the Tenements

Riis describes the lodging-house district as a particular focus of missionary work:

> "The cheap lodging-houses offer a field for the missionary's labors beside which most other fields seem tame." (008-002)

The lodging-houses concentrated the most desperate population — young men newly arrived in the city, homeless, unemployed, and vulnerable to the criminal networks that preyed on them. The missionary's task was to intercept these men before they fell into crime, offering food, shelter, and moral guidance as alternatives to the seven-cent lodging-house.

Riis's treatment of missionaries is characteristically ambivalent. He respects their dedication but questions their efficacy. The problem was not a shortage of missionaries but a shortage of decent housing. As long as the tenement existed, missionaries would be overwhelmed by the scale of need. His reform program — demolish the worst slums, enforce sanitary law, build model tenements — was designed to make missionaries less necessary, not more effective.

## Legacy

The city missionary movement was a precursor to the settlement house movement that emerged in the 1890s. Settlement houses like Hull House (Chicago) and the University Settlement (New York) sent educated, middle-class workers to live in poor neighborhoods — a model that combined the missionary's personal presence with the reformer's structural analysis. Riis's book appeared at the transition point between these two approaches, and his ambivalence about missionaries reflects that historical moment.
