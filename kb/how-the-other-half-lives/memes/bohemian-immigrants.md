## Immigration Background

The Bohemian immigration to New York City in the late nineteenth century brought Czechs from the rugged borderlands of the Austro-Hungarian Empire to the tenement districts of Manhattan's East Side. Unlike the mass migrations of Irish or Germans that preceded them, the Bohemian influx was smaller in scale but distinctive in character—a proud Slavic people whose history of resistance to Habsburg rule shaped their identity in the New World.

Riis estimated that by 1890, more than half of all Bohemians in New York worked as [[sweater|cigarmakers]], making it the defining occupation of the community [012-004]. The population was scattered in wedge-like bands across the East Side rather than concentrated in a single district. As Riis observed, "for a mile and a half scarce a Bohemian is to be found, until Thirty-eighth Street is reached," with additional colonies at Fifty-fourth and Seventy-third Streets [012-004].

The Bohemians came to America with few material resources. In Chapter III, "The Mixed Crowd," Riis noted pointedly that "the Bohemian is the only foreigner with any considerable representation in the city who counts no wealthy man of his race, none who has not to work hard for a living, or has got beyond the reach of the [[tenement]]" [003-012]. This economic precarity, combined with linguistic isolation, created a community defined simultaneously by fierce pride and grinding poverty. He described them as "the poor but thrifty Bohemian" who "might be picked out by the sombre hue of his life as of his philosophy, struggling against heavy odds in the big human beehives of the East Side" [003-012]. Their colonies extended "northward, with long lapses of space, from below the Cooper Institute more than three miles" [003-012].

## Settlement Patterns

Bohemian settlement in New York followed the geography of the cigar trade. Unlike the Irish, who dispersed broadly across the city, or the Italians, who clustered densely in the [[mulberry-bend]] area, the Bohemians' residential choices were dictated by proximity to the tenement factories where they worked.

The main Bohemian colonies were strung discontinuously up the East Side:

|-----------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Lower East Side | Fifth Street near Houston | First colony, mixed with German population |
| Mid-East Side | Thirty-eighth Street | Manufacturing hub, dense tenement factories |
| Upper East Side | Fifty-fourth Street | Residential settlement, family-oriented |
| Upper East Side | Seventy-third Street | Factory-tenement district, ash-barrels filled with tobacco stems |

> The two races mingle no more on this side of the Atlantic than on the rugged slopes of the Bohemian mountains; the echoes of the thirty years' war ring in New York, after two centuries and a half, with as fierce a hatred as the gigantic combat bred among the vanquished Czechs. [012-003]

The Bohemians' housing conditions were inseparable from their working conditions. The "tenement factory" system meant that landlord and employer were often the same person—typically a Polish Jew who owned both the cigar shop and the surrounding tenements [012-002]. Workers paid extortionate rents ($10–$12.25 per month for two or three rooms, often with dark interior bedrooms) as a condition of employment, creating what Riis called a system of "virtual serfdom" [012-002]. The manufacturer who owned "from three or four, to a dozen or more tenements contiguous to his shop" filled them with Bohemian families, "charging them outrageous rents, and demanding often even a preliminary deposit of five dollars 'key money'" [012-004]. This dual grip over both shelter and livelihood gave the landlord-employer extraordinary power over the Bohemian tenant.

## In Riis's Account

Jacob Riis devoted an entire chapter—Chapter XII, "The Bohemians—Tenement-House Cigarmaking"—to this community, and his portrayal reveals a complex mixture of sympathy, admiration, and the ethnic stereotyping characteristic of his era.

Riis was at pains to defend the Bohemians against charges of anarchism and criminality that dogged them in the American press. He marshalled statistical evidence: "the last census (1880) shows his people to have the fewest criminals of all in proportion to numbers. In New York a Bohemian criminal is such a rarity that the case of two firebugs of several years ago is remembered with damaging distinctness" [012-003]. He dismissed the anarchist label as largely a slander spread by those who benefited from cheap Bohemian labor. "I greatly mistrust that the Bohemian on our shores is a much-abused man," he wrote, noting that the accusation the Bohemian "lives like the 'rat' he is, cutting down wages by his underpaid labor" was thrown back "in the teeth of the trades unions with the countercharge that they are the first cause of his attitude to the labor question" [012-003].

His account emphasizes several themes that recur throughout the chapter:

**Linguistic isolation.** Riis found household after household where adults had lived in America for nine, thirteen, even fifteen years without learning English [012-006][012-007]. He attributed this to the difficulty of the Czech language and the all-consuming nature of tenement labor, which left no time for education. A blacksmith of nine years' residence could not practice his trade because he knew no "Engliska" [012-008]. Riis identified "his singularly harsh and unattractive language, which he can neither easily himself unlearn nor impart to others" as one of the primary causes of Bohemian isolation [012-003].

**The landlord-employer system.** The central grievance Riis identified was not sanitary conditions (which he found "greatly exaggerated" by union critics) but the economic bondage created when a single person controlled both housing and employment:

> Not content with simply robbing the tenant, the owner, in the dual capacity of landlord and employer, reduces him to virtual serfdom by making his becoming *his* tenant, on such terms as he sees fit to make, the condition of employment at wages likewise of his own making. [012-002]

When a tenant rebelled against these terms, "he is given the alternative of submission, or eviction with entire loss of employment" [012-004]. The bohemian cigarmaker "has seldom much laid up against a rainy day. He is fond of a glass of beer, and likes to live as well as his means will permit" — making him more vulnerable to the landlord's pressure than the frugal [[polish-jew|Polish Jew]] [012-004]. In the worst cases, rebellion resulted in "the throwing upon the street of a small army of people, with pitiful cases of destitution and family misery" [012-004].

**Pride and resilience.** Despite their poverty, Riis found the Bohemians "not uncleanly, rather the reverse" [012-005]. Their homes were cleanly kept, their families devoted, and their aspirations for a better life—though constantly thwarted—genuinely held. The wife of one cigarmaker, patting her husband's hand gently, expressed the quiet dream that he might work at his original trade as a blacksmith [012-008].

**The cigar-making economy.** The chapter provides detailed wage data: a husband-and-wife team could produce 3,000 cigars per week at $3.75 per thousand, earning $11.25 [012-006]. Adding a child raised output to 4,000 cigars and earnings to $15.00 [012-007]. The workday ran from dawn until 9 or 11 p.m., seven days a week [012-011]. A strike at the time of Riis's visit demanded a raise to $5.00 per thousand—a demand the manufacturer flatly refused [012-006].

## Working Conditions and Daily Life

The daily rhythm of Bohemian tenement life was dictated entirely by the cigar bench. "Men, women and children work together seven days in the week in these cheerless tenements to make a living for the family, from the break of day till far into the night" [012-005]. Riis documented working hours with grim precision: one cigarmaker reported working "from they can see till bedtime," with bedtime proving to be eleven o'clock — "Seventeen hours a day, seven days in the week, at thirteen cents an hour for the two, six cents and a half for each!" [012-011].

The tenement factory environment was unmistakable even from the street. On Seventy-first and Seventy-third Streets, "the very ash-barrels that stand in front of the big rows of tenements...advertise the business that is carried on within. They are filled to the brim with the stems of stripped tobacco leaves" [012-011]. The rank smell of tobacco "waited for us on the corner of the block" and "follows us into the hallways, penetrates every nook and cranny of the houses" [012-011]. Every room contained its workbench "with its stumpy knife and queer pouch of bed-tick, worn brown and greasy, fastened in front the whole length of the bench to receive the scraps of waste" [012-011].

The seasonal nature of the trade added another dimension of hardship. In winter, the manufacturer reduced tobacco supplies, cutting a family's income "at least one-fourth" while the rent remained fixed [012-011]. One cigarmaker reported that in winter they received "tobacco for only two thousand, the rent of $10 for two rooms...has nevertheless to be paid in full, and six mouths to be fed" [012-008]. Tobacco leaves were stored in the bedroom, "kept there, under cover lest they dry and crack, from Friday to Friday, when an accounting is made and fresh supplies given out" — and the family slept there too, though "the smell, offensive to the unfamiliar nose, does not bother them. They are used to it" [012-011].

The toll on health was evident, though Riis was careful not to overstate it. He found "several cases of consumption, of which one at least was said by the doctor to be due to the constant inhalation of tobacco fumes," but noted that examination of Health Department death records "does not support the claim that the Bohemian cigarmakers are peculiarly prone to that disease. On the contrary, the Bohemian percentage of deaths from consumption appears quite low" [012-005]. He left open "the more involved problem whether the falling off in the number of children, sometimes quite noticeable in the Bohemian settlements, is, as has been suggested, dependent upon the character of the parents' work" [012-005].

One particularly affecting case study involved a shoemaker by trade who had spent thirteen years helping his wife make cigars in the manufacturer's tenement [012-012]. She was a very good hand, "and until his health gave out two years ago they were able to make from $17 to $25 a week, by lengthening the day at both ends" [012-012]. After his health collapsed and the family moved away from the tobacco smell under doctor's orders, the burden fell entirely on the wife at eight dollars a week in the shop [012-012]. Their meals consisted of "breakfast of coffee and hardtack, or black bread, at twenty cents for the whole eight," dinner at thirty cents for the family, and supper of "sausage and bread. For ten cents they can eat all they want" — but "the week the rent is due, they have to shorten rations to pay the landlord" [012-012].

## Economic Role

The Bohemian economy in New York was almost synonymous with tenement-house cigarmaking. Riis estimated that "probably more than half of all the Bohemians in this city are cigarmakers" [012-004]. This concentration created a vulnerable, single-industry community.

The cigarmaking process was a family enterprise. Teams of two or three—typically husband, wife, and sometimes an older child—worked together at a single bench. The husband stripped tobacco leaves and made the filler; the wife rolled the wrapper and finished each cigar. The work was paid by the piece (per thousand cigars), not by the hour, and the manufacturer supplied the raw tobacco weekly [012-005].

The economic logic of the system was brutally efficient:

- A family team of two earned approximately $11.25 per week for 3,000 cigars
- Rent consumed $10–$12.25 of this total
- Weekly food budgets averaged $2.50–$3.00, compared to the Polish Jew's $0.60 for a family of comparable size [012-007]
- In winter, when the manufacturer reduced tobacco supplies, income fell by at least one-fourth while rent remained fixed [012-011]

The Bohemians' position at the bottom of the cigar trade was enforced by the hostility of the trades unions, which refused to admit women workers—a critical issue since many Bohemian families depended on the wife's cigarmaking skill brought from the old country [012-005]. Riis explained the union's grievance: "As they state the cause of the bitter hostility of the trades unions, she was the primary bone of contention in the day of the early Bohemian immigration" [012-005]. The manufacturer exploited this division: "The manufacturer has ever since industriously fanned the antagonism between the unions and his hands, for his own advantage" [012-005]. The [[tenement-house-act|Tenement House Act]] that attempted to prohibit cigarmaking in tenements was struck down as unconstitutional by the Court of Appeals, and with its defeat, "the victory rests with" the manufacturer [012-005].

Despite these conditions, Riis documented instances of successful labor action. One cigarmaker in the upper East Side settlement reported having "just come out successful of a strike that brought the pay of these tenements up to $4.50 per thousand cigars. Notice to quit had already been served on them, when the employer decided to give in, frightened by the prospective loss of rent" [012-011]. The interconnectedness of the landlord-employer system meant that rent revenue could occasionally act as leverage in the workers' favor.

Beyond cigarmaking, Riis noted that Bohemians who tried to practice skilled trades brought from Europe—blacksmithing, shoemaking—found themselves blocked by the language barrier [012-008]. The community's economic horizons were thus doubly constrained: by the factory-tenement system on one side and by linguistic isolation on the other.

## Religious and Political Identity

The Bohemian community's religious and political character was a subject of considerable public anxiety in late-nineteenth-century New York, and Riis devoted substantial attention to it in his chapter.

The charge of anarchism was the most persistent accusation leveled against the Bohemians. Riis acknowledged the charge directly: "But what of his being an Anarchist, this Bohemian—an infidel—I hear somebody say" [012-013]. His response was characteristically nuanced. He argued that "almost one might be persuaded by such facts as these—and they are everyday facts, not fancy—to retort: what more natural? With every hand raised against him in the old land and the new, in the land of his hoped-for freedom, what more logical than that his should be turned against society that seems to exist only for his oppression?" [012-013]. Yet he insisted "the charge is not half true. Naturally the Bohemian loves peace, as he loves music and song" [012-013].

Riis drew a revealing parallel between the Bohemian and the [[irish-immigrants|Irishman]]: "The Czech is the Irishman of Central Europe, with all his genius and his strong passions, with the same bitter traditions of landlord-robbery, perpetuated here where he thought to forget them; like him ever and on principle in the opposition, 'agin the government' wherever he goes" [012-013]. He acknowledged that "among such a people, ground by poverty until their songs have died in curses upon their oppressors, hopelessly isolated and ignorant of our language and our laws, it would not be hard for bad men at any time to lead a few astray" [012-013]. But he returned to his statistical defense: "even with the occasional noise made by the few, the criminal statistics already alluded to quite dispose of the charge that they incline to turbulence and riot" [012-013].

On the question of religion, the Bohemian community was fractured by centuries of European conflict. Riis described the "infidel propaganda" as "the legacy perhaps of the fierce contention through hundreds of years between Catholics and Protestants on Bohemia's soil, of bad faith and savage persecutions in the name of the Christians' God that disgrace its history" [012-013]. He quoted the Bohemian clergyman who spoke at the Christian Conference held in Chickering Hall, who characterized his people in a memorable formulation: "They are Roman Catholics by birth, infidels by necessity, and Protestants by history and inclination" [012-013]. Despite the presence of Freethinker schools established to counteract church influence, the clergyman reported that his flock "had grown in a few years from a mere handful at the start to proportions far beyond his hopes, gathering in both Anarchists and Freethinkers, and making good church members of them" [012-013].

This complex spiritual landscape reflected the broader Bohemian experience: a people shaped by persecution who remained suspicious of institutional authority in all its forms, yet who proved responsive to genuine community-building when it was offered.

## Riis's Concluding Assessment

Riis closed his chapter on a somber, urgent note. The Bohemians' plight, he argued, was at root "a question of education, all the more urgent because these people are poor, miserably poor almost to a man" [012-014]. He recorded the testimony of one community member who "knew thoroughly what he was speaking of": "There is not one of them all, who, if he were to sell all he was worth tomorrow, would have money enough to buy a house and lot in the country" [012-014].

This final observation crystallized the Bohemians' structural position in the urban economy — not merely poor, but locked into a system that made escape nearly inconceivable. Unlike the [[german-immigrants|German]] ragpicker who might become a "thrifty tradesman or prosperous farmer" within a generation, or the [[the-italian-immigrant|Italian]] scavenger who graduated to "exclusive control of the corner fruit-stands," the Bohemian found no such ladder [003-008][003-009]. The factory-tenement system, linguistic isolation, and absence of co-ethnic wealth combined to produce what Riis saw as a uniquely trapped community.
