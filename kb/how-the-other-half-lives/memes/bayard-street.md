Bayard Street was one of the most significant boundary lines in Riis's mapping of immigrant New York. Running east-west through the Lower East Side, it marked the dividing line between the Jewish colony to the south and the Italian colony to the north — a geographic fault line where the ethnic tensions and economic competition that structured tenement life were most visible.

## The Jewish–Italian Boundary

Riis used Bayard Street as his primary example of how immigrant colonies were separated by clear geographic boundaries. The street functioned as a social membrane: Jews lived on one side, Italians on the other, and crossing Bayard Street meant crossing from one immigrant world into another. This was not merely a matter of residential preference but of economic and social organization — each colony had its own labor markets, its own institutions, and its own internal hierarchies.

## Tenement Conditions

The tenements on Bayard Street exemplified the housing conditions of the Lower East Side's most densely packed blocks. Rear tenements, cellar dwellings, and overcrowded apartments were the norm. The street's location at the intersection of two major immigrant colonies made its housing stock particularly valuable to landlords, who could extract maximum rent from the constant demand generated by chain migration.

The street-level economy of Bayard Street reflected its role as a border zone. Pushcart vendors, small shops, and street trades catered to both communities, creating a commercial space where Italians and Jews — who otherwise lived in largely separate worlds — negotiated daily economic exchanges.

## Significance

Bayard Street mattered to Riis's argument because it made the abstract concept of the "immigrant colony" physically concrete. The colony was not a vague cultural zone but a territory with boundaries as real as any national border — and Bayard Street was one of those borders. Understanding the tenement problem required understanding this ethnic geography, because housing reform, public health, and policing all had to operate within a landscape partitioned by language, custom, and mutual suspicion.
