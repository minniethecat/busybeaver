# Chatham Street

## Description

Chatham Street (today's Park Row south of City Hall) was the Bowery's southern gateway — the first street many immigrants and job-seekers encountered when they arrived in the city. The street was dominated by cheap lodging-houses that charged seven to twenty-five cents per night, pawnshops that bought what little the lodgers possessed, and employment agencies that connected men to day-labor jobs. Riis describes "the caravansaries that line Chatham Street and the Bowery" as the city's primary processing mechanism for newcomers. (008-002)

## In Riis's Account

Chatham Street's lodging-houses were, in Riis's analysis, the first step on the path to crime for many young men. Inspector Byrnes testified that "at least four hundred young men" arrested for petty crimes began their descent in these establishments. (008-004) The street was a filter: those who found work moved on to tenement apartments; those who did not descended to the seven-cent lodging-houses, the stale-beer dives, and eventually the Potter's Field.

Riis documents how lodging-house keepers on Chatham Street collaborated with employment agents to extract maximum profit from transient workers. A man would arrive, pay for a night's lodging, pay an agent for a day's work, and return with barely enough to repeat the cycle. The system was designed to keep men exactly where they were — one step above destitution, one step below stability.
