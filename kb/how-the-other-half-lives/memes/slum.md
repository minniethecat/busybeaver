## Definition

In *How the Other Half Lives*, the "slum" is more than a collection of dilapidated buildings—it is a self-perpetuating ecosystem of poverty, shaped by the interaction of [[tenement]] housing, exploitative economics, and municipal neglect. Riis uses the term to describe neighborhoods where the physical decay of buildings, the density of population, and the concentration of vice mutually reinforce one another, creating districts that reproduce misery across generations.

The slum, for Riis, is distinguished from mere poverty by its institutional character. It has its own economy (the [[stale-beer-dive|stale-beer dive]], the [[lodging-house]], the pawnshop), its own social hierarchy (the [[landlord|landlord]], the [[the-politician|politician]], the gang leader), and its own relationship to the wider city—one of mutual dependence and mutual denial.

## Historical Context

The New York slum of the 1890s was the product of half a century of explosive growth. Waves of immigration—first Irish and German, then Italian, Jewish, Bohemian, and Chinese—poured into housing stock originally built for a much smaller and wealthier population. Single-family homes were subdivided into tenements; backyards were filled with rear tenements; cellars and attics became dwelling spaces.

Riis traced the genealogy of the slum from the Fourth Ward and [[five-points|Five Points]] outward:

> It was said that in the Seventh Ward, over which the shadow of this great church fell, five thousand families had no homes in which they could be sheltered. From the Fourth Ward slums had crept up, and the Five Points the whole of the East Side, to bone and muscle and sinew of the city, its wealth. [002-008]

The slum was not static—it migrated. As one immigrant group achieved enough prosperity to move uptown or outward, another, poorer group replaced it, often at higher densities. The Italian pushed out the Irish from the Sixth Ward; the Russian Jew crowded the Italian from Mulberry Street. Each transition reinforced the physical decay of the housing stock while changing the ethnic character of the neighborhood [003-012].

## Characteristics

Riis identified several defining characteristics of the slum district:

**Physical decay.** The slum tenement bore "the worn and greasy look" that came from generations of overcrowding without repair [004-006]. Buildings constructed for single families now held dozens. Rear tenements, built in former backyards, created "runways" rather than hallways—narrow passages "for the thieves and toughs of the neighborhood" [014-013].

**The open door.** Riis used the unlocked street door as the slum's defining architectural feature: "The hall that is a highway for all the world by night and by day is the tenement's proper badge. The Other Half ever receives with open doors" [014-002]. The absence of privacy was both a symptom and a cause of slum conditions.

**Vice infrastructure.** Every slum had its complement of [[saloon|saloons]], [[stale-beer-dive|stale-beer dives]], and [[lodging-house|lodging-houses]]. Riis noted that "gas-houses, slaughterhouses and the docks, that attract the roughest crowds and support the vilest saloons, invariably form slum-centres" [014-005]. The dive was "this real home-product of the slums" [007-011].

**Self-perpetuation.** The slum reproduced itself. Children raised in tenement conditions, Riis observed, had few paths out. The [[street arab]] and the gang member were "home-product[s] of the slums" [007-011], shaped by an environment that offered no alternative.

**Ethnic succession.** The slum was racialized in Riis's telling—each wave of immigrants brought "their slums with them wherever they go, if allowed to do it" [003-012]. This controversial formulation reflected Riis's belief that slum conditions were not innate to any group but were produced by the intersection of poverty with an exploitative housing system.

## Reform and Resistance

The slum was not passively accepted. The [[sanitary-reform|sanitary reform]] movement of the late nineteenth century—spurred by cholera epidemics and the efforts of organizations like the [[charity-organization-society|Charity Organization Society]]—achieved measurable improvements in building codes, ventilation requirements, and water access. The "small parks" movement, which converted slum blocks into public green space, demonstrated that environmental improvement could transform neighborhood character: "The changing of Tompkins Square from a sand lot into a beautiful park put an end for good and all to the Bread and Blood riots of which it used to be the scene" [014-015].

But Riis was under no illusion that these reforms were sufficient. The slum, he believed, would persist as long as the tenement system remained profitable. The economic incentives that packed human beings into dark, airless rooms at extortionate rents were stronger than any building code. Real reform required addressing the underlying economics of land, housing, and poverty—a conclusion Riis approached but never fully articulated.

## In Riis's Analysis

Riis's treatment of the slum as a concept reflects the central tension in his work: between environmental determinism (the slum makes the slum-dweller) and moral responsibility (the slum-dweller can, with effort, escape).

His most famous slum district was **the Bend**—the Mulberry Street Bend in the Sixth Ward—which he called the "foul core of New York's slums" [006-002]. The Bend exemplified everything Riis believed about slum ecology: the densest population, the worst housing, the highest concentration of dives and lodging-houses, and the most desperate poverty. It was also, in his telling, the most resistant to reform.

> Long years ago the cows coming home from the pasture trod a path over this hill. Echoes of this mooing lingered in the name of a street that survived the disappearance of the herd, and the Sixth Ward became "The Bend." [006-002]

Yet Riis also documented the persistence of decency within the slum. He found "sweet and innocent girls, singularly untouched by the evil around them, true wives and faithful mothers" in the worst tenements [014-006]. This tension between environmental determinism and individual moral agency runs throughout the book and was never fully resolved.
