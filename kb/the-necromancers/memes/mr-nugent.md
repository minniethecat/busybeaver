# Mr. Nugent

**Mr. Nugent** is the village grocer of the unnamed hamlet near [[stantons|Stantons]], the father of [[amy-nugent|Amy Nugent]], and a Baptist. He represents the respectable Dissenting working class against which the old Anglican/Catholic gentry define themselves in Robert Hugh Benson's *The Necromancers*.

## Social Position

Mr. Nugent runs the village shop with his name emblazoned in capitals "as bright and flamboyant as on the depot of a merchant king" ([005-032]). When [[maggie-deronnais|Maggie]] passes the shop, she can see him "in white shirtsleeves and an apron, busied at a pile of cheeses" — the image of honest if unglamorous trade. His shop is the commercial center of the hamlet, open into the evening when villagers gather for gossip and beer.

Mrs. Baxter's disdain for the Nugent family is largely class-based. She refers to Amy's father dismissively as "a grocer — and a Baptist" ([001-011]), the conjunction implying that both facts are equally disqualifying. The novel does not share this snobbery; Mr. Nugent is portrayed as a decent, hardworking man caught up in circumstances beyond his understanding.

## Amy's Death and Aftermath

When [[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]] fell in love with Amy Nugent, he called upon Mr. and Mrs. Nugent "in state to disclose his intentions" ([015-003]). For the Nugents, it seemed the beginning of a social transformation — their daughter would be mistress of the great house. Her sudden death shattered that prospect.

Mr. Nugent appears most vividly in the aftermath of his wife's terrifying experience in Amy's bedroom. Returning from the cricket match, he finds his wife insensible. When she recovers enough to demand that the forgotten candle be retrieved and the bedroom door locked, Mr. Nugent performs the task himself, "cloth cap and all" ([015-018]). He examines the room carefully and finds "all was entirely as usual" — the bed "white and innocent in the candlelight." His verdict is brisk and dismissive: "A pack of nonsense!" ([015-022]).

## Character

Mr. Nugent is pragmatic and theological in equal measure. After his wife's fright, "the theological discussion waxed late that night" ([015-023]), with Mr. Nugent supplying rational explanations: stooping caused a rush of blood; the watercress eaten on the previous afternoon; the unoccupied morning. By the end of the evening, his wife is persuaded — or at least silenced.

His habits are those of village normality. At the end of the evening he "step[s] round to the Wheatsheaf for an hour with the landlord after the bar was shut up" ([015-024]). This detail — the working man's pint at the pub — places him in a social world entirely separate from the drawing-room séances and theological debates of the gentry.

## Thematic Significance

Mr. Nugent embodies the "ordinary" world that the novel's spiritual drama displaces. He is neither Catholic nor spiritualist; his religion is practical Nonconformity, his concerns are trade and cricket. When the supernatural intrudes into his life — through his wife's terror in Amy's room — he handles it with the tools available to him: common sense, domestic authority, and a pint at the Wheatsheaf. That these tools prove adequate is perhaps Benson's quiet endorsement of the unreflective but sturdy world Mr. Nugent represents.
