"A few days before Laurie's arrival she went to the greenhouse to see the chrysanthemums. There was an excellent show of them" [005-098]. This brief scene, occupying only a handful of paragraphs in Chapter 5, is one of the novel's characteristic domestic interludes — an apparently trivial errand that, on closer reading, reveals the emotional state [[maggie-deronnais|Maggie Deronnais]] is working to conceal.

## The Errand

The greenhouse visit is framed as a practical task: Maggie needs flowers, and the chrysanthemums are in season. The gardener provides the details: "Mrs. Baxter doesn't like them hairy ones" [005-099]. Maggie's response is brisk and managerial: "On the nineteenth I shall want a big bunch of them. You'd better take those — those hairy ones. And some maidenhair. Is there plenty?" [005-100].

The nineteenth, the reader understands, is the date of [[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]]'s return. The chrysanthemums are for his arrival. Maggie's practical tone — choosing the flowers Mrs. Baxter dislikes, checking quantities, issuing instructions — is a performance of emotional neutrality that the date betrays. She is preparing for Laurie's return with an attention to domestic detail that is indistinguishable, in its thoroughness, from care.

## The Emotional Subtext

The greenhouse visit occurs at a specific point in Maggie's emotional trajectory. She has already diagnosed herself as "in danger of" falling in love with Laurie [005-059], and she has concluded that awareness of the danger is sufficient protection. The greenhouse scene tests this theory: the flowers are a material expression of an emotional investment that Maggie's conscious mind is refusing to acknowledge.

The narrator's restraint mirrors Maggie's own. Nothing is said about her feelings; everything is shown through action. The chrysanthemums — "hairy ones" chosen despite Mrs. Baxter's dislike — suggest an assertion of taste that is also an assertion of presence: Maggie is selecting the flowers because she is the one who will arrange them, because she has taken on the role of managing Laurie's homecoming, and because the domestic labor of preparation is the only channel available for emotions she cannot speak.

## Narrative Function

The greenhouse visit functions as a hinge between Maggie's private emotional reckoning and the practical arrangements for Laurie's return. It is immediately preceded by the narrator's observation that Maggie "would not for an instant allow to herself that any of their smoothness arose from the fact that this boy would be here again in a few weeks" [005-080], and it is followed by the practical arrangements for the Christmas shooting and Mrs. Baxter's scheme involving the Vicar.

The scene thus occupies a structural position in the chapter's emotional architecture: Maggie's self-diagnosis (interior) → the greenhouse visit (exterior action concealing interior feeling) → the household's practical and spiritual preparations (social). The greenhouse is the point at which private emotion is translated into domestic action, visible to the reader if not to the character herself.

## Significance

Small domestic scenes of this kind are the novel's primary means of psychological characterization. Maggie's greenhouse visit belongs to a category of narrative moments — the letter written, the flower arranged, the dinner organized — that carry the novel's emotional weight beneath a surface of ordinary activity. The scene also establishes a motif of preparation and anticipation that will be echoed in later chapters as the spiritual crisis builds and the domestic surface begins to crack.
