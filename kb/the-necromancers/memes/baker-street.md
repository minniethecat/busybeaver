## Overview

Baker Street, in the novel *The Necromancers*, is primarily the location of Mr. Vincent's establishment — the rooms where the medium conducts his professional séances and where Laurie Baxter becomes increasingly entangled in spiritualist practices. The address carries literary resonance, sharing a street with Sherlock Holmes's fictional residence, though Benson's Baker Street is a site of supernatural mystery of a darker kind.

## Description

Mr. Vincent's Baker Street rooms are notably unremarkable in their physical appearance. The séance room is described as "singularly unsuggestive of supernatural mystery" [004-001] — a deliberate writerly choice that heightens the contrast between the ordinary setting and the extraordinary claims. Rather than the theatrical occultism one might expect, the space is professional, almost clinical.

The establishment includes both living quarters and the public rooms where séances are held. It has a drawing room — "Mr. Vincent's drawing room" [002-157] — where participants gather before sessions, and a dedicated séance room. Mrs. Stapleton is among those who frequent the premises, indicating the social respectability of the gatherings.

The atmosphere of domestic normality is part of the novel's warning: spiritual danger does not announce itself with Gothic trappings but wears the face of professional respectability. The contrast between the mundane setting and the spiritual peril within structures the novel's critique of spiritualism.

## Role in the Story

Baker Street functions as the novel's counter-space to the Baxter house. Where the country house in Hertfordshire represents domestic order, Anglican piety, and (however imperfectly) genuine faith, the Baker Street rooms represent urban sophistication, spiritual experimentation, and hidden danger. Laurie's movement between these two spaces — the country house and the Baker Street séance room — maps his spiritual trajectory.

The séance room at Baker Street is where Laurie believes he sees Amy Nugent's face "four months after her death" [009-023], a vision that binds him more tightly to the medium's circle. It is also where the medium communicates with what he claims to be "the disembodied spirit of a certain 'Amy Nugent'" [007-053]. The Catholic characters' interpretation is darker: these communications come not from Amy but from deceptive spirits.

## Atmosphere

Despite the clinical appearance of the rooms, Benson invests Baker Street with an atmosphere of mounting dread. The narrator describes phenomena that unsettle observers: people are "very much upset at séances, more than once" [008-013]. The tension between the respectable exterior and the disturbing interior creates an atmosphere of spiritual infection — the sense that something dangerous lurks beneath the ordinary.
