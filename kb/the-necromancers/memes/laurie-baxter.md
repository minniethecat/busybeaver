## Overview

Laurie Baxter is the male protagonist of *The Necromancers*, a young man of ardent temperament whose emotional and spiritual instability drives the novel's central conflict. A convert to Catholicism while at Oxford, Laurie briefly considered the priesthood before becoming entangled in both a romance with Amy Nugent and an interest in spiritualist séances. His grief following Amy's sudden death makes him vulnerable to supernatural deception, culminating in a spiritual crisis that requires sacramental intervention.

## Background

Laurie is the son of Mrs. Baxter of the Baxter estate in Hertfordshire. He was educated at Oxford, where he underwent a conversion to Roman Catholicism — "Laurie's becoming a Catholic at Oxford" [001-018] — which initially led him toward the priesthood. His mother recalls that "he thought he was going to be a priest at first" [001-020], but Father Mahon discouraged this path, and Laurie's religious fervor subsequently cooled.

His temperament is established early: "Dear Laurie is so impetuous and sensitive" [001-006], his mother observes. The narrator describes him as having "more than his share of brains" [001-030] alongside a "tenderhearted to a degree" nature. He is presented as physically appealing, with a "well-bred swagger" and distinctive dress — "his swallowtail coat and white shirt, and the knee breeches and buckled shoes that he wore by reason of the touch of picturesque and defiant romanticism" [001-028].

## Role in the Story

Laurie's arc follows a pattern of romantic idealism, loss, and spiritual danger. His affair with Amy Nugent, the Baptist grocer's daughter, represents both genuine feeling and profound selfishness. Maggie Deronnais, observing from within the household, recognizes "the astonishing selfishness of this very boy's actions" [001-027] — he pursued Amy with passion but without consideration of the consequences for either her or his family.

Amy's sudden death — symbolically occurring on the day Laurie was away — becomes the catalyst for his spiritualist involvement. Drawn into the Baker Street séance circle through Lady Laura, he becomes a regular participant in Mr. Vincent's mediumship sessions. Father Mahon observes the spiritual danger and intervenes pastorally, aware that Laurie "possessed in a very high degree that kind of temperament" [007-052] that made him susceptible.

The crisis reaches its peak when Laurie experiences what the novel presents as genuine demonic assault — "the appearances Laurie saw" [019-011] — manifestations that Mr. Cathcart interprets as an evil spirit attempting possession. "It is not possession yet" [016-101]: the qualified language marks the precipice from which the sacraments, administered by Father Mahon, ultimately pull him back.

The epilogue, set four months later, shows Laurie recovering with Maggie's support, though the experience has left its mark. Maggie recalls "the extraordinary effect on Laurie — the physical and moral and spiritual effect" [019-011], a holistic description of the damage done.

## Character Traits

Laurie is defined by intensity and inconsistency — "his self-will, his moodiness, and his persistence" [001-028]. He combines genuine tenderness with profound self-absorption, authentic religious feeling with emotional volatility. His romanticism is both his charm and his vulnerability: the same quality that makes him fall in love with a grocer's daughter makes him susceptible to spiritualist deception.

## Relationships

- **Mrs. Baxter**: His adoring but anxious mother, who sees him as a perpetual source of worry
- **Amy Nugent**: His first love, whose death precipitates his spiritual crisis
- **Maggie Deronnais**: The Catholic woman his mother hopes he will marry; their relationship develops through shared experience of the spiritualist crisis
- **Father Mahon**: His confessor and spiritual director

## Significance

Laurie represents the danger of undisciplined spirituality. His conversion to Catholicism was sincere but shallow — an aesthetic and intellectual change rather than a moral transformation. The novel suggests that spiritualism preys on precisely this kind of religious instability, offering supernatural experience without moral demands. Laurie's recovery through the sacraments affirms the Catholic conviction that the Church's rites provide genuine spiritual protection.
