## Overview

Loss is the novel's emotional foundation. Laurie Baxter's bereavement, Mrs. Baxter's grief, and the wider community of mourners who gather at Baker Street are all drawn to spiritualism by the same force: the intolerable absence of the beloved dead. Spiritualism in the novel is not an intellectual position but a grief response — it promises what grief demands, which is continued contact.

## In the Novel

Laurie's loss is introduced through imagery of burial: his beloved lies "under a pile of blossoms as pink and white as her own face" [001-025], the flowers both commemorating and concealing the body. His "sense of loss" [002-102] is described as a wound that spiritualism promises to dress, not heal — the séance does not end grief but transforms it.

The loss is specified in chapter 2 as "the loss of the one soul in whom his own soul found rest" [002-199]. This language of soul-loss anticipates the spiritualist solution: if the soul persists, the loss of the body may be temporary.

Mrs. Baxter's grief takes a more practical form — she seeks evidence rather than experience, verification rather than consolation. The locked room, the moving objects, the accurate messages: these are her counter-arguments against loss, her forensic rebuttal of death's finality.

## The Language of Loss

Benson uses a distinctive vocabulary to articulate loss in the novel. The image of the jewel and its setting recurs: "now that the jewel was lost the setting was worse than empty" [001-071] — loss is figured not as absence alone but as the painful prominence of what remains. The bereaved person is like a setting from which the stone has been pried, the empty space more conspicuous than the surrounding gold.

This imagery gives loss a physical, almost architectural quality. Loss is not merely felt; it inhabits spaces, reshapes faces, alters the geometry of rooms. Laurie, in his grief, moves through the Baxter house like a man in a building from which the structural supports have been removed — still standing, but precarious.

## Loss and the Séance Economy

The novel depicts spiritualism as an economy built on loss. The medium's livelihood depends on the bereaved's willingness to pay for contact; the bereaved's willingness depends on the intensity of their loss. This creates a perverse incentive: the medium benefits from unresolved grief, and the séance, by offering temporary consolation, may actually prolong the mourning it claims to relieve.

The spiritualist promise — that "she was not lost; his relations with her were the same as ever" [009-029] — is both consoling and troubling. If the dead are not lost, then the work of mourning is unnecessary. But the novel's Catholic framework suggests that acceptance of loss is spiritually necessary, that the refusal to let the dead go is itself a form of disordered attachment.

## The Objects of Loss

Loss in the novel attaches to objects as well as persons. Laurie's piano stands "glossy and mysterious in the gloom" [007-073], a mute witness to the music that Amy will never play again. The material world becomes charged with absence — things remain, but their meaning has drained away. This object-oriented grief gives the novel a distinctly Edwardian texture, the mourning of a culture that invested deep emotional significance in pianos, letters, locked rooms, and the physical traces of the departed.

## Significance

Loss in *The Necromancers* is never overcome or resolved. Characters do not "get over" their grief; they adapt to it, incorporating spirit communication into their ongoing relationship with the dead. The novel thus treats loss as a permanent condition that spiritualism temporarily alleviates but never erases — a more honest treatment than the consolatory conventions of the period typically allowed.
