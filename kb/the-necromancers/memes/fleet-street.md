Fleet Street appears in the novel as a geographical marker rather than a developed setting — a London thoroughfare whose significance lies in its position between [[mitre-court|Mitre Court]] and the séance rooms of [[baker-street|Baker Street]]. It is a boundary, a seam in the urban fabric that separates — or fails to separate — the rational world of law and journalism from the spiritualist world of mediums and trance-states.

## A Geographical Hinge

The narrator locates a key location as "not twenty yards from Mitre Court and scarcely fifty from the public séance rooms" [011-001] — a description that positions Fleet Street at the intersection of Laurie's two worlds. The proximity is unsettling because it is so ordinary: the distance between reason and the occult is measured in yards, not miles, and the walk from law books to trance is shorter than a morning's stroll.

"Outside roared Oxford Street in one long rolling sound" [007-192] — the London streets form a continuous auditory background to the novel's interior dramas. The roar is indifferent to the spiritual crisis unfolding in the rooms above; the city's public life continues regardless, and the street that connects the Temple to Baker Street connects them without discrimination.

## Narrative Function

Fleet Street's narrative function is cartographic rather than dramatic. It appears in descriptions of movement — characters walk along it, live near it, cross it — but no scene is set on it. Its role is to establish the physical intimacy of the novel's opposed worlds: the rational and the occultic, the masculine-professional and the feminine-spiritualist, the public and the hidden.

This cartographic intimacy is part of the novel's argument. Spiritualism is not a remote cult accessible only to initiates; it operates on Fleet Street, twenty yards from the law courts, in rooms that anyone can enter. The horror is not that the séance rooms are hidden but that they are visible — and ordinary, and close.

## Significance

Fleet Street embodies the novel's geographical thesis: that the supernatural is not elsewhere but adjacent. The London of the novel is a city in which different epistemologies occupy the same streets, separated by doors rather than districts. Fleet Street — the historic home of English journalism, the place where facts are gathered and printed — is also the street on which séances are conducted. The juxtaposition is the novel's comment on the insufficiency of the factual to contain the real.
