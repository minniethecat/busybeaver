# Medium

A **medium** is a person who acts as an intermediary between the living and the spirits of the dead, facilitating communication through trance states, physical phenomena, or automatic writing. In *The Necromancers*, the medium is the central figure of the séance room, and Mr. Vincent serves as the novel's primary example of the type. The novel's portrayal of mediumship is double-edged: it grants the medium genuine power while questioning whether that power is ever benign.

## The Medium's Role

In the spiritualist framework presented by the novel, the medium provides the "vehicle" through which spirits can manifest. Mr. Vincent explains to Laurie Baxter that the personality of the medium directly shapes the nature of the communication:

> "...the personality of the medium affects the vehicle through which..." [002-040]

The medium enters a trance state during which spirits may "gain control" and speak through them. As Maggie Deronnais describes to Laurie:

> "...sometimes they gain control of the medium who is in a trance, and speak..." [002-086]

The quality of the medium determines the quality of the phenomena. A good medium, under favorable conditions, might enable a spirit to take on visible form:

> "...were very favorable, and the medium a very good one, the soul that..." [002-081]

The novel's theology, however, insists that the very premise is corrupt. The medium does not simply "facilitate" communication; he opens a channel that, in the novel's Catholic framework, can only lead to deception or spiritual harm. "To go to séances with good intentions" [019-029] is not sufficient protection — the medium's role is structurally dangerous regardless of motive.

## Mr. Vincent

Mr. Vincent is introduced as "certainly the greatest medium in London, if not in Europe" [003-058]. He is brought to the Baxter household by Mrs. Stapleton to conduct séances aimed at contacting the deceased Amy Nugent. His professional reputation is formidable; he is not a charlatan in the crude sense but something more dangerous — a skilled practitioner who believes in his own powers.

During séances, Mr. Vincent sits within the cabinet — an enclosed wooden structure — while the sitters form a circle. His manner is calm, professional, and authoritative. The medium's physical presence is carefully managed: he chooses where each sitter sits, placing Laurie "on one side" while he takes "the other" [004-100], a spatial arrangement that maximizes the medium's control over the proceedings.

> The medium paused an instant, looking in... [004-059]

> "...that she possesses upon that medium — I mean matter — through which..." [004-068]

He speaks of the materialization process with scientific detachment, describing the spirit's ability to draw "matter" from the medium's body as though it were a normal physiological event. His composure contrasts sharply with the emotional reactions of the sitters. When Laurie expresses nervousness, the medium's response is calibrated reassurance — the question "You are sure, Mr. Baxter, that you are not at all nervous?" [004-094] simultaneously diagnoses and manufactures the vulnerability it claims to address.

## The Medium as Predator

Beneath Mr. Vincent's tranquil surface, the novel constructs a portrait of spiritual predation. The medium's solicitousness after séances — coming "to see how he was after the séance" [014-041] — presents itself as care but functions as surveillance. He monitors Laurie's condition, managing his recovery with the same authority with which he managed the séance itself.

The medium's speech patterns reveal his technique. He speaks "tranquilly" [004-163], "kindly" [004-204], with smiles that disarm and nods that confirm — a repertoire of calm authority that makes his pronouncements feel like established fact rather than contested claims. When he nods [004-158] or smiles [004-190], the gestures are not warmth but technique — the medium's toolkit for disarming scepticism and manufacturing consent.

The novel's Catholic framework identifies the medium as an instrument of forces he may not fully understand. His own personality — his calm professionalism, his apparent sincerity — becomes itself a danger. The "very favorable" conditions he requires, the "very good" medium he is [002-081], all serve to make the spiritual peril more acute, not less. A fraudulent medium would be less dangerous, because the fraud would be detectable; a genuine medium, the novel implies, is a genuine conduit for genuinely destructive forces.

## Trance and Control

The medium's trance state is essential to the process. In this condition, the medium becomes passive, allowing spirits to use their vocal organs or to draw "matter" from their body for materialization. Mr. Vincent's trances are described as deep and controlled — he can enter and exit them at will, and he retains awareness of what transpires.

The relationship between medium and spirit is one of "control" — a term used in spiritualist circles to describe the spirit entity that guides or speaks through the medium. The medium's own personality and physical constitution affect which spirits can communicate and how clearly. The trance is presented as a state of radical passivity: the medium "opens" himself as a channel, and what enters depends on who — or what — takes the opportunity.

## Physical Phenomena

Through the medium, various physical effects are produced: rapping sounds, the movement of objects, and ultimately the visible materialization of spirit forms. Mr. Vincent's séances progress through stages — from simple rapping and automatic writing to the more ambitious goal of complete materialization. The progression is deliberate: each successful phenomenon builds confidence and deepens investment, making the sitter harder to extract from the process.

The medium's tranquil demeanor throughout these extraordinary events is the novel's most effective horror device. When he nods at the completion of a phenomenon [004-158], or inquires "an hour and a half?" as if timing a medical procedure rather than a spiritual violation [004-163], the clinical detachment itself becomes evidence of the séance's inhumanity. The medium who remains "tranquil" while the bereaved are reduced to spiritual wreckage is the novel's portrait of evil in its most civilised form.
