## Overview

Fear in *The Necromancers* is more than an emotion; it is a spiritual barometer. What a character fears reveals what they hold most dear, and the novel tracks fear as a diagnostic of the soul's attachments. Catholic theology distinguishes between servile fear (fear of punishment) and filial fear (fear of offending a beloved father); the novel's characters mostly exhibit the former, and their failure to reach the latter marks their spiritual immaturity.

## In the Novel

Laurie Baxter's fear is the novel's central case. After Amy's death, "he had been filled with unreasoning fear" [006-003] while walking through the countryside, and the fear is not attached to any specific threat — it is the diffuse anxiety of a soul that has lost its bearings. When he encounters the spiritualist phenomena, "he almost feared the next words" [004-056] of the medium, sensing dimly that what comes next may compromise something he cannot afford to lose.

The seance room generates fear as a collective experience. Mrs. Baxter, though drawn to spiritualism for comfort, "hardly knew what she feared" [002-183] during the proceedings. The ambiguity of spiritualist phenomena — are they from the dead or from something else? — makes fear the appropriate response, but Mrs. Baxter lacks the theological vocabulary to interpret her own fear. She feels the danger without understanding it.

Fear carries an ambiguous pleasure in the novel, characteristic of the gothic mode. The narrator observes that "the very shudder of fear had had in it a touch of delight" [002-212], and this mixed quality — fear as thrill — is precisely what the novel distrusts. Fear that titillates rather than warns is not a guide to safety but a lure toward danger.

Maggie Deronnais, by contrast, does not fear the seances in the same way. Her Catholic formation gives her a different framework: she distrusts spiritualism not because it frightens her but because she recognizes it as false. The absence of fear in Maggie is the novel's signal that she is the character most spiritually secure. The murdered person's spirit, the medium explains, is "fearfully excited — anger, fear, an agony of desire" [019-015], a state Maggie would never mistake for peace.

## Fear as Spiritual Warning: The Gift of Holy Fear

The novel presents a paradox: Laurie's fear is genuine and well-founded — he is in real spiritual danger — yet his fear does not protect him. It drives him "onwards" [007-095] into further involvement rather than away from it. This is the mark of what Catholic theology calls servile fear: fear that recognizes a threat but lacks the wisdom to respond rightly.

The seance exploits this dynamic. Laurie "did not shrink from experiment for fear of risk" [007-051] because spiritualism has reframed his fear as a challenge to be overcome rather than a warning to be heeded. The medium's rhetoric — "what was there to fear in that?" [007-073] — dismisses fear as superstition, while the novel suggests that fear, properly interpreted, is a form of spiritual intelligence.

The Catholic tradition's gift of holy fear (*timor Domini*) is the corrective the novel implies but never directly states. Unlike servile fear, which is self-regarding (fear of punishment or harm), filial fear is love-regarding: it fears above all the loss of communion with God. No character in the novel fully exhibits this gift, but Maggie's quiet confidence — her immunity to the seance's allure — suggests its presence as an unspoken norm. She does not fear the phenomena because her fear is rightly ordered toward its proper object.

## Context

The Catholic tradition's analysis of fear draws on Aquinas, who distinguishes *timor servilis* (servile fear, fearing punishment) from *timor filialis* (filial fear, fearing separation from God). The latter is a gift of the Holy Spirit; the former, while not sinful, marks an imperfect relationship with God. The novel's world is populated by characters experiencing servile fear of various objects — the dead, the unknown, social disapproval — and almost none who exhibit the ordered fear that Catholic theology recommends.
