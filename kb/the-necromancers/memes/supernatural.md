## Overview

The supernatural in *The Necromancers* is not a settled category but a contested terrain. Characters deploy the term to mean different things: for spiritualists, it designates phenomena that "one by one had been explained" by science in the past but which remain genuinely anomalous [003-072]; for skeptics, it marks the residue of credulity that has not yet been eliminated; and for Father Mahon, it is the proper domain of the demonic. The novel's drama lies not in proving or disproving the supernatural but in tracing how different characters *decide* what counts.

## In the Novel

The novel's opening seance scene is "singularly unsuggestive of supernatural mystery" [004-001] -- a deliberate choice. Benson begins not with wonders but with domestic ordinariness, suggesting that the supernatural enters through the mundane rather than replacing it.

Lady Laura's mediumship straddles the boundary: her trance states are framed as supernatural by believers, natural (psychological, physiological) by critics. The narrator refuses to arbitrate, presenting both readings with equal plausibility.

Father Mahon's intervention introduces a third framework: the supernatural as *preternatural* -- not divine miracle but demonic counter-miracle. This complicates the binary, transforming the question from "is it real?" to "what *kind* of real is it?" The faithful maintain "a reserve" of belief "in the supernatural" [007-064], a store of credulity that the seance progressively draws down.

In the novel's climactic possession, Maggie "leaned simply upon the supernatural, like a tired child" [017-084] -- a posture neither skeptical nor credulous, but *dependent*.

## The Reserve of Belief

Benson's phrase "a reserve of belief in the supernatural" [007-064] names a psychological phenomenon that the novel traces with care. Characters do not encounter the supernatural from a position of neutrality; they bring to the seance room a pre-existing store of credulity, an accumulated willingness to accept what exceeds nature.

This reserve is finite. As the novel progresses, characters' reserves are drawn down -- exhausted by phenomena that demand belief but offer no certainty. Laurie's initial reserve is large (he is the novel's most enthusiastic convert to spiritualist explanation); Maggie's is more carefully husbanded; Father Mahon's is directed entirely toward the divine supernatural, leaving none for the ambiguous phenomena of the seance. The novel's drama is partly an accounting of who has how much reserve left, and at what cost it was spent.

## Leaning on the Supernatural

Maggie's posture in the possession scene -- "she leaned simply upon the supernatural, like a tired child" [017-084] -- is the novel's most theologically suggestive image. To "lean upon" the supernatural is neither to prove it nor to merely believe in it; it is to *depend* on it, to make it the ground of one's action.

This posture differs from both Laurie's investigative enthusiasm and Father Mahon's doctrinal certainty. Maggie leans because she must -- the supernatural has become, for her, not an object of inquiry but a condition of survival. The simile "like a tired child" strips the posture of theological ambition and restores it to its simplest form: trust.

## Significance

The supernatural in *The Necromancers* is both the novel's ostensible subject and the term the novel most carefully refuses to define. This refusal is structural: by declining to settle whether the phenomena are divine, demonic, psychological, or fraudulent, Benson makes the reader participate in the characters' own uncertainty.

## Key Quotations

> (003-072)

> It was a mellow October afternoon, glowing towards sunset, as Laurie came across the south end of the park to his appointment next day; and the effect of it upon his mind was singularly unsuggestive of supernatural mystery.
> (004-001)

> All he knew was that it had become uninteresting. True? Oh, yes, he supposed so. He retained it still as many retain faith in the supernatural--a reserve that could be drawn upon in extremities.
> (007-064)

> Nor had all Laurie's arguments prevailed to shake in the smallest degree her resolute conviction that there was nothing whatever preternatural in his certainly queer story.
> (006-161)

> (017-084)

> (006-161)
