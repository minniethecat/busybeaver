Mrs. Stapleton is a New Thought enthusiast and close friend of [[mrs-baxter|Mrs. Baxter]], who introduces the Baxter household to the world of séances and psychical research. Her fervent but intellectually shallow approach to spiritualism serves as a foil to the more serious supernatural themes of the novel, and her influence on Laurie paradoxically pushes him toward skepticism rather than belief.

## Character and Beliefs

Mrs. Stapleton is introduced as "a New Thought kind of person" [002-009], a member of a loose spiritual movement that blended Christian faith with mind-cure metaphysics and an openness to psychic phenomena. She arrives at the Baxter home full of conviction, radiating a self-conscious certainty about the "Higher Light" that had supposedly dawned upon her [002-021].

> Mrs. Stapleton did not quite say this outright; she was not of those who claim ecstatic experience; yet the impression conveyed was that some Higher Light had dawned. [002-021]

Her enthusiasm is genuine enough, yet as the narrative observes, there is something fundamentally unconvincing about her — "the extreme unconvincingness of Mrs. Stapleton" [002-198] is a recurring note, and Laurie in particular finds her "feverish, self-conscious theory" [002-213] deeply off-putting.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Stapleton functions as a catalyst for the novel's central conflict between credulity and skepticism. Her introduction into the Baxter household — an invitation extended because of a personal "singularity" [002-025] — brings the séance practice into Laurie's world, directly precipitating his crisis of belief.

> She's — she's the wife of Colonel Stapleton. She goes in for what I think they call New Thought. [001-050]

Her luncheon at the Baxter house in Chapter 2 is a set-piece for the novel's treatment of spiritualism's social dimension — the polite but earnest table talk of psychical matters, where Mrs. Stapleton "laid her knife and fork down" [002-045] to deliver pronouncements with the gravity of revelation, and "flashed a radiant look of helpless innocence" [002-048] when challenged.

## Relationship with Other Characters

Mrs. Stapleton is described as Mrs. Baxter's "dearest friend" [003-001], and their friendship is the conduit through which spiritualist practices enter the Baxter household.

> She had gone to stay with her dearest friend, Mrs. Stapleton, for a few days of psychical research. [003-001]

She interacts frequently with [[mr-vincent]], the medium at the center of the séance circle, and her claim that Laurie "isn't a Christian any longer" [002-112] because he attends séances illustrates the social-religious tension she introduces.

She also engages [[mr-cathcart]] in theological debate, and in the climactic séance at [[the-convent|Saint Mary's Convent]], Mrs. Stapleton is among the group who believe they have achieved genuine communication:

> Mr. Vincent, Mrs. Stapleton, and the rest, really think they have come into communication with the other world. [019-022]

## Connection to Laurie Baxter

Laurie's reaction to Mrs. Stapleton is sharply drawn. Far from being drawn in by her New Thought fervor, he finds her "more elementary" than he expected [002-079] and is put off by what he perceives as a shallow enthusiasm untethered to genuine intellectual or spiritual depth.

> He had expected to be able to exchange impressions with Mrs. Stapleton; but she was more elementary than he had supposed. [002-079]

Her influence on him is thus paradoxical: by embodying a version of spiritualism that strikes him as unconvincing, she drives him not toward belief but toward the rationalist skepticism of [[james-morton]] and [[mr-rymer]].

The book Laurie reads in Chapter 2 is contrasted with "Mrs. Stapleton's feverish, self-conscious theory" [002-213], setting up a dialectic between systematic inquiry and emotional credulity that structures much of the novel's intellectual framework.

## Significance

Mrs. Stapleton embodies the social-spiritual milieu that the novel both satirizes and treats with a degree of sympathy. She is not a villain — her intentions are kindly, her friendship with Mrs. Baxter genuine — but her shallow conviction makes her an unwitting instrument of the deeper and more dangerous spiritual forces that the novel gradually unveils.

In the taxonomy of belief positions the novel maps out, she occupies the quadrant of sincere but uncritical credulity, a necessary counterpoint to the hardened skepticism of Mr. Rymer, the rationalist inquiry of James Morton, and the tragic supernatural reality experienced by [[laurie-baxter]] and [[maggie-deronnais]].
