## Overview

Vision in *The Necromancers* denotes a spectrum of visual spiritual perceptions: from the collective "ocular visions" witnessed at séances [004-116], through the vivid memories of the bereaved seeing "vision after vision" of the dead [001-066], to the private "waking visions" that Maggie Deronnais experiences as a byproduct of her heightened sensitivity [016-155]. The novel distinguishes between public visions (verifiable by multiple witnesses) and private ones (accessible only to the seer), a distinction that maps onto the broader tension between spiritualist empiricism and individual religious experience.

## In the Novel

The motif first appears in Laurie Baxter's grief: "he was seeing vision after vision" of his dead son, turning again and again in memory [001-066]. These are psychological visions—products of mourning, not mediumship—but they establish the baseline for what the séance promises to make literal.

Lady Laura's sessions produce "ocular visions" visible to all attendees [004-116], fulfilling the promise that private grief can become public verification. When vision crosses from the personal to the collective, it becomes evidence—the séance room as laboratory.

Maggie's "little waking visions" [016-155] occupy an intermediate category: private but not pathological, spontaneous but interpretable. She describes them to Laurie not as symptoms but as communications, a position that aligns with the Catholic tradition of private revelation while avoiding the spiritualist framework of mediumship.

## Context

The novel's treatment of vision reflects competing Edwardian frameworks: the spiritualist séance as a technology for producing shared visions; the Catholic mystical tradition of private revelation (Bernadette at Lourdes, the children of Fatima); and the emerging psychological discourse that reclassified visions as hallucinations. Benson's characters navigate all three, and the novel refuses to settle the question.

## Character Connections

- **Laurie Baxter** — experiences visions of dead loved ones as memory-turned-perception
- **Maggie Deronnais** — the novel's most consistent visionary, her "waking visions" are central to her character
- **Lady Laura Bethell** — the medium through whom collective vision becomes possible
- **Father Mahon** — interprets vision through the Catholic lens of discernment (divine vs. demonic)

## Significance

Vision in *The Necromancers* is the novel's epistemology made sensory—the question of *how we know* translated into *what we see*. By refusing to collapse all visions into a single category (hallucination, revelation, or trickery), Benson preserves the ambiguity that gives the novel its distinctive theological suspense.
