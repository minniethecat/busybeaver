# Sensitive

A **sensitive** is a person endowed with an unusual degree of psychic receptivity — someone who perceives spiritual influences, atmospheres, and presences more acutely than ordinary individuals. In *The Necromancers*, the concept of sensitivity is central to understanding why certain characters, particularly Laurie Baxter, are vulnerable to the dangers of séances.

## Laurie Baxter as a Sensitive

Laurie Baxter is repeatedly identified as a sensitive of exceptional degree. His mother is the first to note the trait:

> "...dear Laurie is so impetuous and sensitive," murmured his mother, drawin... [001-006]

Mr. Vincent, upon meeting Laurie, immediately recognizes his condition and warns of the implications:

> "He is extraordinarily sensitive. I almost wish he were not so..." [007-005]

> "...danger. He is extraordinarily sensitive, you know. There was that gir..." [007-025]

Later, Vincent diagnoses him directly:

> "...you are aware that you are a sensitive of a very high order. Now I d..." [008-066]

And again to Lady Laura:

> "...doubt, that he is exceptionally sensitive. Probably you have seen for y..." [012-061]

Lady Laura herself comes to recognize this quality in her brother:

> "...But he's particularly sensitive, isn't he?" [012-121]

> ...which she regarded the boy's sensitive faculties. [013-017]

## The Danger of Sensitivity

The novel treats sensitivity as a genuine gift, but one that carries profound peril. Mr. Vincent recounts a cautionary case:

> "...a very awful case. A girl — a sensitive — broke down altogether under ..." [008-028]

When Lady Laura questions whether she herself might be at risk, Vincent clarifies:

> "Yes; but you see we're not sensitives. And even I—" [008-030]

The implication is clear: sensitives are a distinct class, and their openness to spiritual influence makes them vulnerable in ways that ordinary people are not. Laurie's sensitivity is what draws him toward the séances, but also what places him in mortal danger when the forces invoked prove hostile.

## Sensitivity as a Double-Edged Gift

The novel portrays sensitivity as morally neutral but practically dangerous. A sensitive may be drawn to spiritualism precisely because of their genuine gift, yet that same gift opens doors that cannot easily be closed. Maggie Deronnais, who loves Laurie, intuits this from the beginning:

> "...He is extraordinarily sensitive..." [007-005]

Her concern is prescient. As the séances escalate, Laurie's sensitivity ceases to be a curiosity and becomes a channel for forces that threaten his sanity and his soul.

## Theological Context

From Father Mahon's Catholic perspective, sensitivity is a natural human faculty that spiritualism corrupts. Rather than cultivating this receptivity through séances — which he regards as a form of necromancy — the proper response to the unseen world is prayer and the sacraments. The tragedy of the novel is in part that Laurie's genuine gift is exploited rather than protected.
