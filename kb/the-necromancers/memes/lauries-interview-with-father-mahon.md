Laurie Baxter's interview with [[father-mahon|Father Mahon]] is one of the novel's central dramatic encounters, recurring as a reference point across multiple chapters and culminating in a direct confrontation that reshapes Laurie's understanding of his spiritual predicament. Unlike the polite spiritualism of [[mrs-stapleton|Mrs. Stapleton]]'s drawing-room, the encounter with the priest forces Laurie to confront theology as a living force with real consequences.

## Prelude

Laurie's relationship with the priest is strained from the outset. His mother observes early on: "I don't think he gets on with Father Mahon very well" [001-020]. The tension between them prefigures the larger conflict between Laurie's instinctive skepticism and the Catholic framework that surrounds [[maggie-deronnais]] and her world. Father Mahon appears only occasionally in the novel's early stretches — "still more occasionally Father Mahon" [005-009] — but his gravity exerts a pull on the narrative that intensifies as the supernatural events mount.

## The Interview

The interview itself is set in motion by Laurie's own initiative: "I rather want to see Father Mahon about something" [010-037]. He arrives at the rectory and is confronted with the physical reality of the priest: "Father Mahon came in, a large, crimson-faced" [001-089] figure, an embodiment of ecclesiastical authority that stands in stark contrast to the ethereal spiritualism Laurie has encountered in séances.

The priest is described as conscientious — "Father Mahon was a conscientious priest" [010-040] — and his questioning is searching rather than accusatory. The interrogation is intellectual and theological: the priest's theory "fits in with what Father Mahon says" [019-027] about the nature of spiritual phenomena, and he argues from a position of doctrinal clarity that séances are, at best, dangerous spiritual recklessness.

The encounter "precipitated her half-formed" [010-092] — a reference to Maggie's state of mind but indicative of the interview's wider catalytic effect. Father Mahon gives Maggie "a point to work" [010-091], a framework for understanding her situation in Catholic terms, but Laurie leaves the interview with his own framework destabilized.

## Aftermath

The interview's consequences ripple outward through the remaining chapters. Laurie is forced to reckon with a theological interpretation of events that he cannot simply dismiss as superstition, because it comes from a source — the priest — whose intellectual seriousness he cannot question. "He thought about his interview for a few minutes as he walked" [010-084] captures the lingering effect: the priest's words do not dissolve on contact with rationalism but demand sustained consideration.

Mrs. Baxter, for her part, wonders whether she has "enough to justify her telling Father Mahon" [006-125] about the events at home, underscoring the priest's role as a moral and spiritual arbiter whose judgment carries weight. The urgency of the supernatural crisis is such that Laurie eventually sends for the priest: "I have written to Father Mahon to come" [016-087]. Even the skeptic, in extremity, turns to the man of God.

## Significance

The interview with Father Mahon marks the point at which Laurie's intellectual resistance to Catholicism begins to weaken, not because he is converted by argument but because the priest's diagnosis of his condition proves more accurate than the rationalist explanations he prefers. The event functions as a hinge in the novel's structure: before the interview, Laurie treats spiritual matters as objects of detached inquiry; after it, he is implicated in them.

The interview also establishes Father Mahon as more than a background authority figure. His role in the narrative is not merely pastoral — he is diagnostician and exorcist, the one who recognizes the nature of the spiritual threat before anyone else. Laurie's interview with him is the first of several confessions in the novel, a sacramental encounter that carries the weight of mortal peril.
