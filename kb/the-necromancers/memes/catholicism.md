## Overview

Catholicism provides the theological and moral framework of Robert Hugh Benson's *The Necromancers*. As a Catholic priest himself, Benson portrays the Roman Catholic Church as the guardian of authentic spiritual truth, standing in direct opposition to the deceptive practices of spiritualism. The novel uses Catholic doctrine — particularly the sacraments, priestly authority, and the Church's teaching on demonic influence — as the standard against which all supernatural claims are measured.

## In the Novel

Catholicism enters the narrative primarily through Laurie Baxter's conversion while at Oxford. Mrs. Baxter recounts that "Laurie's becoming a Catholic at Oxford has brought you two together" [001-018], referring to Maggie Deronnais, who was raised in the faith. Laurie's initial fervor led him to consider the priesthood: "he thought he was going to be a priest at first" [001-020], though Father Mahon discouraged him.

The sacraments carry decisive narrative weight. In a critical scene, Laurie, under spiritual attack, calls for Father Mahon and receives the last rites: "Sit down, my dear boy," said the priest, and he impelled him gently toward a chair [001-090]. Father Mahon administers the sacrament with matter-of-fact authority: "The priest took up the coins and slipped them away" [001-095], a ritual moment that anchors the supernatural chaos in sacramental order.

Father Mahon embodies the institutional Church's perspective. He is described as "a conscientious priest" who "said his Mass at eight o'clock" and "sang his office always, and remembered the English saints in the evening" [010-040]. His theology is simple but firm: "the Catholic Church seems the one thing they don't attack" [019-031], observing that even spiritualists respect the Church's spiritual authority.

Maggie Deronnais represents the lived Catholic faith — not merely doctrinal but formative. She had "approved of his conversion to Catholicism, yet she was not sure that his motives were pure" [001-029], demonstrating the novel's nuanced treatment of conversion as both an intellectual and moral transformation.

## Sacramental Life and the Mass

The Mass serves as the novel's quiet counterpoint to the séance — one a legitimate encounter with the divine, the other a counterfeit. Attendance at Mass marks the rhythm of the faithful: Maggie "had gone up as usual to Mass that morning, and had been as" [002-006] regular in her observance as in her daily habits. After Mass she spent her mornings in domestic routine — "After Mass and breakfast she spent the morning" [005-009] — the sacrament integrated seamlessly into ordinary life rather than sought as spectacle.

The request for Mass intentions carries particular weight in the novel's economy of prayer. Laurie asks Father Mahon, "Will you say Mass for my intention, please?" [001-094], a request that acknowledges both the priest's sacramental role and the efficacy attributed to the Mass in Catholic devotion. Later, after Amy's death, Laurie "had had Mass said for Amy this morning" [002-198], demonstrating how the ritual of Mass intention becomes a tangible act of love for the dead — a licit alternative to the illicit contact promised by spiritualism. Mrs. Baxter, though not Catholic herself, tells Maggie "I'd have a Mass said for Amy" [019-062], showing how even non-Catholics in the novel recognize the Mass as a meaningful act of remembrance.

Father Mahon's daily rhythm structures the parish's sacramental life: "He said his Mass at eight o'clock; he breakfasted" [010-040]. This regularity — Mass at eight, breakfast after — presents the priest's life as one of ordered devotion rather than mystical transport, reinforcing the novel's implicit argument that true religion is steady, institutional, and sacramental rather than ecstatic and uncontrolled.

## The Convert's Experience

Laurie Baxter's conversion to Catholicism is the novel's central religious drama, and Benson treats it with the psychological acuity of one who knew the convert's path intimately. The conversion occurs at Oxford, during the period of intellectual awakening that the novel associates with religious seriousness. Maggie recognizes that Laurie is "a Catholic at Oxford" [001-018], the university setting lending intellectual credibility to his decision.

Yet the novel does not present conversion as a simple victory. Maggie "approved of his conversion to Catholicism, yet she was not sure that his motives were pure" [001-029], introducing a note of psychological complexity. Laurie's initial impulse toward the priesthood — "he thought he was going to be a priest at first" [001-020] — suggests an enthusiasm that Father Mahon wisely tempers. The priest's discouragement of this vocation hints that Laurie's faith, however sincere, remains immature — more emotional than settled.

This portrait of the convert as spiritually earnest but psychologically vulnerable becomes crucial to the plot. Laurie's genuine religious feeling does not immunize him against spiritualist temptation; if anything, his hunger for transcendent experience makes him more susceptible. Benson's convert is not a saint but a soul in formation, whose Catholic identity must be tested and deepened through suffering.

## Context

Benson wrote during the Catholic Literary Revival in England, a period following the Oxford Movement when prominent converts like John Henry Newman and Gerard Manley Hopkins brought Catholicism into English letters. The novel reflects post-Emancipation Catholic confidence while also dramatizing the Church's perennial warnings against occult practices.

## Character Connections

- **Laurie Baxter**: A convert whose faith is tested by grief and spiritualist temptation
- **Maggie Deronnais**: A cradle Catholic whose steady faith contrasts with Laurie's volatility
- **Father Mahon**: The parish priest who provides sacramental and spiritual guidance
- **Mrs. Baxter**: Laurie's Anglican mother, representing the established English religious sensibility

## Significance

Catholicism functions as the novel's normative religious center. The sacraments — particularly confession and the last rites — provide the only effective defense against the spiritual dangers the characters face. Benson's apologetic purpose is clear: authentic Catholic practice protects while unauthorized spiritual exploration destroys.

## Key Quotations

> "Laurie's becoming a Catholic at Oxford has brought you two together," Mrs. Baxter observed, noting how shared faith forged connections among the novel's characters. (001-018)

> Father Mahon represented the orthodox Catholic view: "He believes in the devil in quite an extraordinary way," treating spiritual warfare as an urgent present reality rather than historical doctrine. (019-028)

> "That particular thing is the devil, or has actually happened" — Maggie's summary of the theological position that refused to reduce supernatural evil to metaphor. (019-027)

> The Catholic Church "seems the one thing they don't like," Maggie noted of the spirits, observing that "you can be almost anything else, if you're a Spiritualist; but you can't be a Catholic." (019-031)

> "It is not possession yet: he is still partly conscious," Father Mahon assessed, the qualified language marking both the danger and the remaining hope. (016-101)

> The sacraments administered by the priest represented the Church's answer to spiritual danger — "the Catholic Church seems the one thing" the spirits could not abide. (019-031)
