The Temple, London's historic legal district, is the professional world [[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]] inhabits when he is not at Stantons or Oxford. It is mentioned far less frequently than his rooms in [[mitre-court|Mitre Court]] — which is "very convenient to the Temple" [006-009] — but its symbolic function in the novel's geography is distinct: the Temple represents law, evidence, and procedural rationality, the very values that spiritualism subverts.

## The World of Law

Laurie "had come down from Oxford just a year ago, and had determined to take things as they came" [001-062]. The move from Oxford to the Temple is a move from intellectual formation to professional practice — from thinking about the world to acting in it. The Temple, as the seat of English law, represents the institutional framework within which Laurie expects to build his adult life.

That framework is built on evidence. The law proceeds by proof, by testimony that can be tested, by facts that can be established. The medium [[mr-vincent|Mr. Vincent]] understands this perfectly: his strategy for overcoming Laurie's resistance is to provide evidence that meets legal standards — a notebook with dated entries, a verifiable claim about a grave's condition. The Temple has trained Laurie to demand evidence; Vincent supplies it in a form the Temple would recognize.

## The Insufficiency of Procedure

The novel's point, however, is that legal procedure is inadequate to spiritual reality. The Temple's methods — cross-examination, corroboration, the weighing of probabilities — cannot adjudicate between competing explanations of Laurie's trance-utterances. If the information about Amy's grave was correct, was its source spiritual or psychic? The Temple offers no procedure for answering that question, and Laurie, trained in law, finds himself without legal resources for the crisis he faces.

The Temple's adjacency to the séance district — Mitre Court is "very convenient to the Temple" [006-009] — maps the insufficiency geographically. The law and the occult are neighbors; their proximity is not a threat to either but a comment on both. The law cannot expel the occult from its neighborhood, and the occult does not need to hide from the law. They coexist on the same streets, in the same district, and the men who walk between them are not entirely sure which world they belong to.

## Significance

The Temple is the novel's representation of rational institutional authority — the world of law, evidence, and professional identity — and its narrative function is to be found wanting. Laurie's legal training equips him to cross-examine a witness but not to diagnose a spiritual assault; it gives him the vocabulary of skepticism but not the categories of resistance. The Temple, like Oxford, like Stantons, is a place whose apparent solidity the novel's action dissolves.
