## Overview

Passion in *The Necromancers* is not romance-novel sentiment but a theologically loaded category. The novel inherits the Catholic tradition's suspicion of ungoverned passion — affects that overwhelm reason and will, rendering the soul vulnerable to deception. Characters are shown in states of passionate intensity: Laurie's grief for Amy, the spiritualist circle's collective fervor, and even Mrs. Baxter's tenderness are all examined through the lens of whether passion serves or subverts the soul's proper orientation toward God.

## In the Novel

Laurie Baxter embodies passion as a condition of the bereaved lover. His "clean-cut, rather passionate lips were set now" [001-065] with the discipline of grief — passion held in check but not extinguished. The narrator describes him looking at a dark future "without life or love or hope" [001-066], and the intensity of his feeling is precisely what makes him susceptible to the spiritualists' promises.

The distinction between passion and genuine love is drawn repeatedly. Laurie's feelings for Maggie are described as something that "resembled respect rather than passion, and need rather than desire" [001-074] — a stable attachment rather than the consuming fire of his grief for Amy. The novel's implicit judgment is that respect and need are safer foundations for relationship than passion, which burns bright but offers no light by which to see.

The spiritualist mediums exploit passion directly. The unnamed medium tells Laurie about "some soul that you have loved passionately, who has crossed over" [004-065] — the appeal is not to reason or evidence but to the intensity of his feeling. When Laurie responds, "his whole being glowed passionately in his voice, even in his movements" [004-066], we see passion as a state of reduced agency, where feeling drives the man rather than the man governing his feelings.

Mrs. Baxter's compassion is a gentler form of the same dynamic. The old lady's "eyes filled with compassionate tears" [001-016] at Laurie's sorrow — her feeling is genuine, but like all passion in the novel, it operates below the level of judgment. Compassion moves her to comfort but not to discern. Mr. Morton, watching the séance proceedings, is described "with something of interested compassion in his heavy eyes" [006-058], another instance of feeling that observes but does not intervene.

## Context

The Catholic moral tradition distinguishes between passions (emotions that arise spontaneously) and the rational will that must govern them. Passions are not sins in themselves, but they become occasions of sin when reason abdicates its governing role. The novel applies this framework dramatically: Laurie's passion for Amy was morally neutral while she lived, but after her death, ungoverned and unredirected, it becomes the door through which spiritual deception enters.
