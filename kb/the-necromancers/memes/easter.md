## Overview

Easter in *The Necromancers* is more than a calendar date — it is the liturgical season against which the novel's spiritual drama unfolds. Laurie Baxter's return from London, the climactic possession crisis, and the novel's resolution are all anchored to Easter. The coincidence is theologically deliberate: the feast of the Resurrection provides the framework for a story about spiritual death and the possibility of deliverance.

## In the Novel

The Easter timeline is established early and maintained with precision. Mrs. Baxter asks: "He's coming down in Easter week, isn't he?" [003-061] — referring to Laurie's planned visit. Maggie presses: "Why shouldn't he come next week instead, Auntie, and be with us for Easter? You'd like that, wouldn't you?" The anticipation of Laurie's return is folded into the anticipation of the feast.

The narrative reaches its crisis on Easter Eve itself. "And now the day was come — Easter Eve, as it happened — and she would see him before night" [Chapter 15]. The timing is not incidental. In the Catholic liturgical calendar, Easter Eve (Holy Saturday) is the day of the Harrowing of Hell — when Christ descended to the dead to liberate the righteous souls. The novel's possession crisis, occurring on this night, echoes the descent: a soul is contested, and deliverance hangs in the balance.

## Easter Day as Resolution

The morning after the crisis brings not triumph but exhaustion. Maggie whispers: "It is Easter Day, Laurie" [Chapter 17]. The simple statement carries the weight of the liturgical calendar — the day of Resurrection has dawned, but the characters are too spent to feel its joy. The novel's restraint here is characteristic: it refuses the easy correlation between liturgical meaning and emotional experience.

Maggie's own recovery is narrated in retrospect. She "looked marvelously well, considering the nervous strain that had come upon her about Easter-time. She had collapsed altogether, it seemed, in Easter week itself" [Chapter 19]. The Easter season, which should be a time of joy, has become a marker of trauma. She reflects on the experience from a distance, describing "a kind of thick hedge between me and Easter" [Chapter 19] — the illness and the nuns' prohibition on discussing her experiences have created a barrier between her present self and the events of that night.

## Maggie's Easter Reflection

Maggie's retrospective account of Easter Eve is the novel's most sustained theological reflection. She describes her state during the crisis: "suddenly and, as it appeared, inevitably, without movement or sound, she began internally to pray" [Chapter 17]. Her prayer is wordless, an act of the will rather than the intellect: "behind the steady movement of her will — sufficient at least to elicit acts of petition — her intellect observed a thousand images and thoughts." She "relinquished all plans, all intentions and hopes: she leaned simply upon the supernatural, like a tired child."

This is prayer as pure dependence — the *act of faith* that the novel's theology has been building toward. And it occurs on Easter Eve, the night the Church keeps vigil for the Resurrection. The liturgical timing is not decorative but structural: Maggie's act of supernatural trust coincides with the Church's commemoration of the foundational supernatural event.

In her later reflection, Maggie is uncertain about what really happened: "at the time, on the night of Easter Eve, I mean, I was absolutely certain that the thing was real, that he was actually obsessed, that the thing — the Personality, I mean — came at me instead, and that somehow I won" [Chapter 19]. The uncertainty is theological as well as psychological: even after deliverance, the memory of spiritual combat resists rationalization.

## Context

Benson's use of the liturgical calendar as narrative structure reflects the Catholic understanding of time as sanctified — not an empty succession of moments but a cycle of feasts and fasts that make present the events of salvation history. Easter is the center of that cycle. By placing the novel's crisis on Easter Eve and its resolution on Easter Day, Benson insists that the spiritual combat depicted in the novel is not merely personal but participates in the cosmic drama of death and resurrection.
