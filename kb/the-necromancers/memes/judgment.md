## Overview

Judgment in *The Necromancers* is the natural virtue of discernment — the capacity to evaluate evidence, weigh competing claims, and reach conclusions that correspond to reality. The novel is interested in judgment under pressure: what happens to a person's capacity to judge when desire, grief, and social pressure are all pushing toward a particular conclusion? Laurie Baxter's failure of judgment is the novel's central case study, but judgment is tested across the cast. The novel's Catholic framework suggests that natural judgment, however acute, is insufficient for spiritual discernment without the supernatural gift of counsel.

## In the Novel

Laurie's judgment is compromised from the start. The narrator notes his "sustained will to suspend his judgment" [006-070] — a deliberate withholding of the critical faculty, not its absence. Laurie is not stupid; he is choosing not to judge, and the choice is motivated by the hope that the séance offers. The suspension of judgment is presented as a moral act, not merely a cognitive one — the will intervenes to protect a desired belief from scrutiny.

Mrs. Baxter exhibits a parallel failure. She notes "the extraordinary coincidence of this man's judgment with her own" [012-066] — finding her own views confirmed by an authority figure, she mistakes agreement for validation. The coincidence that ought to prompt suspicion instead produces trust. Her judgment operates on the principle that those who agree with her are likely to be right, a cognitive bias the novel diagnoses without naming.

Father Mahon provides the novel's standard for judgment. His advice — "You must use your judgment" [016-097] — assumes that judgment is a faculty to be exercised, not a feeling to be trusted. "Your object is to fight on" [016-097] despite uncertainty, and judgment is the weapon. The priest's confidence in Laurie's judgment is qualified by his knowledge of Laurie's spiritual condition — judgment can function well or poorly depending on the state of the soul that wields it.

Maggie's judgment is the novel's model of discernment in action. She "held her judgment in suspense" [017-009] rather than rushing to conclusion. "One thing only" [017-009] mattered — and her judgment identifies the essential while setting aside the inessential. This is not the suspension of judgment that Laurie practices (refusal to judge) but the disciplined delay of judgment that Catholic moral theology recommends — holding the mind in equipoise until sufficient evidence and proper disposition converge.

## Context

The Catholic tradition treats judgment as both a natural virtue (prudence, the recta ratio agibilium) and a supernatural gift (counsel, which perfects the act of prudence under divine guidance). The novel's characters who lack supernatural counsel — Laurie, Mrs. Baxter, Lady Laura — exercise natural judgment that ought to be sufficient but proves inadequate to the spiritual complexity they face. The implication is that natural judgment, unaided by grace, cannot reliably navigate encounters with the preternatural.
