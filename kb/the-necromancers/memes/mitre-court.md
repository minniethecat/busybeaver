Mitre Court, in the Temple district of London, is [[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]]'s private domain — "two rooms opening into one another, and communicating with the staircase" [006-009] — a space of masculine independence where he reads law, plays his grand piano, and attempts to maintain the rationalist detachment that his spiritualist encounters have already begun to erode. The rooms are the stage for one of the novel's pivotal scenes: the visit of [[mr-vincent|Mr. Vincent]], the medium, who crosses Laurie's threshold uninvited and, in doing so, breaches the psychological defenses the rooms are meant to represent.

## Description

The chambers are described with economy: "Laurie's rooms were in Mitre Court, very convenient to the Temple — two rooms opening into one another, and communicating with the staircase" [006-009]. The convenience to the Temple is practical but also symbolic — Laurie is a law student, a man of reason in training, and his chambers reflect this identity. The "grand piano, that occupied a third of his sitting room" [006-010] signals cultivation and leisure, the accoutrements of a young gentleman of means.

The open door — "I found your outer door open" [006-011] — is the detail that enables Vincent's entry and, metaphorically, Laurie's vulnerability. Mitre Court is a space Laurie believes he controls, but the open door suggests a permeability he has not acknowledged. The spiritualist world does not need to break in; it can walk through an entrance Laurie has left unguarded.

## Vincent's Visit

The visit of Mr. Vincent to Mitre Court is the novel's central scene of intellectual and psychological seduction. Laurie has twice refused Lady Laura's invitations to séances, but Vincent's personal call transforms the dynamic. The medium enters unannounced — "the porter's boy told me to come straight up" [006-011] — and proceeds to dismantle Laurie's rationalist objections with the professionalism of a man who has done this before.

The chambers provide the setting for an intimate intellectual duel. Laurie "set the results of his meditations forth at length" [006-024], rehearsing the arguments that have satisfied him: hypnotic suggestion, the subconscious self, the absence of conclusive proof. Vincent listens, then produces his notebook — "a few extracts from some of the things you said during your trance" [006-052] — and reads the sentence about Amy's grave that Laurie cannot explain. The notebook, small enough to fit in a breast pocket, is the counter-evidence that Laurie's Oxford-trained mind cannot process.

## Aftermath

The visit transforms Mitre Court from a place of retreat into a place breached. Laurie's rooms, which had represented his independent, rational, masculine life, are now associated with the moment his defenses collapsed. Vincent leaves, but the notebook's sentence remains, and Laurie's rooms in Mitre Court are no longer the sanctuary they were.

The location's proximity to both the Temple and Baker Street maps Laurie's position in the novel's moral geography. "Not twenty yards from Mitre Court and scarcely fifty from the public séance rooms" is how the narrator describes a nearby street [011-001], and the spatial proximity encodes the spiritual proximity Laurie is trying to deny. His chambers and the séance rooms are neighbors; the distance he imagines between his rational self and the spiritualist world is an illusion of cartography.

## Significance

Mitre Court is the novel's representation of male professional space — law books, grand piano, rooms of one's own — and its violation by Vincent's visit prefigures the larger violation Laurie will experience. The open door, the uninvited guest, the notebook extracted from the breast pocket: the scene is a seduction in the language of professional consultation, and its setting — "very convenient to the Temple" — underlines the irony. The law cannot protect Laurie from what Vincent brings, because what Vincent brings operates in a jurisdiction the law does not recognize.
