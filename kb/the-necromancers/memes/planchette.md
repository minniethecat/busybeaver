# Planchette

A **planchette** is a small, typically heart-shaped board mounted on casters, used in spiritualist practice as a tool for spirit communication. Participants place their fingers lightly upon it, and the device is believed to move under spirit influence, either tracing letters on a surface or pointing to characters on a lettered board. It is one of the mechanical aids associated with mediumship, alongside table-rapping and similar physical phenomena. In *The Necromancers*, the planchette is more than a prop — it is a conceptual hinge between the novel's critique of spiritualism as pseudo-science and its theological case against necromancy.

## In the Novel

The planchette appears in *The Necromancers* during a pivotal conversation about the legitimacy of spiritualist apparatus. When Laurie Baxter visits the medium and examines the paraphernalia of the séance room, he asks about various devices. The medium responds directly: "'Yes, that's planchette,' said the medium" [007-130]. Laurie's follow-up question — "For… for automatic writing?" [007-131] — reveals his layman's attempt to connect the device to its function, a connection that the medium treats as obvious but that Laurie, still tethered to ordinary categories, finds unsettling.

The planchette becomes a symbol of the broader tension between mechanism and meaning in the novel. Mr. Vincent discusses it not as a sacred object but as part of a continuum of human technological progress, placing it alongside "the production of radium, and of planchette as of wireless telegraphy — as" phenomena that were once mysterious but might one day be explained [011-082]. This framing is characteristic of Vincent's rhetorical strategy: acknowledging the strangeness of spiritualist practice while insisting it is no stranger than other modern discoveries. The planchette is a spiritualist's answer to the telegraph — a technology for transmitting messages across distances that materialist science declares impassable.

He further argues that the planchette, like all spiritualist apparatus, is merely a physical aid to a spiritual reality. "More than all the paraphernalia of planchette, spinning mirrors, or even" more elaborate devices, he suggests, the true key to spirit communication lies in the affinity between medium and spirit [007-262]. The downplaying of apparatus is itself a rhetorical move: by diminishing the importance of the physical tools, Vincent makes the spiritual claims harder to dispute. You cannot disprove a planchette; you can only question whether the hands that guide it are moved by spirits or by unconscious muscular impulses.

## Mechanism and the Supernatural

The novel's treatment of the planchette reflects a broader nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century anxiety about the relationship between technology and the supernatural. Spiritualism flourished in an age of telegraphs, telephones, and wireless communication — technologies that made action at a distance seem not merely possible but ordinary. The planchette, in this context, was the domestic equivalent of the wireless receiver: a device for picking up signals from beyond the normal range of human perception.

Benson's novel is alert to this parallel and deeply suspicious of it. By having Vincent compare the planchette to radium and wireless telegraphy, the novel exposes the spiritualist strategy of borrowing scientific authority for unscientific claims. The planchette is not a scientific instrument; it is a tool for opening channels that, in the novel's Catholic framework, should remain closed. The medium's rhetorical move — "this is just like radio, just like radium" — is the move the novel wants its readers to recognise and resist.

## Theological Reading

Within the novel's moral economy, the planchette illustrates the principle that the means of spirit communication are spiritually irrelevant. Whether one uses a planchette, a pencil, a spinning mirror, or no apparatus at all, the act of seeking communication with the dead is the same act. The Catholic framework does not distinguish between "high-tech" and "low-tech" necromancy. Maggie's retrospective summary traces the progression "from the pencil and the rappings and the automatic writing, right up to the appearances" [019-016] — the pencil (planchette) comes first, the appearances last, but they are stages of the same journey.

The planchette's very ordinariness — a small wooden board on casters — is part of the novel's warning. Spiritual danger does not announce itself with thunder and lightning; it arrives in the form of a parlour game, a curiosity, a device no more threatening than a child's toy. That is precisely what makes it dangerous.
