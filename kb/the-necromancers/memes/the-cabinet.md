# The Cabinet

The **cabinet** is an enclosed wooden structure used in spiritualist séances, within which the medium sits during attempts at materialization. In *The Necromancers*, the cabinet is a recurring physical object that marks the transition from ordinary conversation to supernatural experiment, and its presence in the room signals the seriousness of the proceedings.

## Physical Description

The cabinet is a raised wooden enclosure, open at the front but curtained, large enough to hold a single seated person. When Mr. Vincent prepares for a séance, the cabinet is positioned in the room as a focal point:

> "Kindly just examine the cabinet and the whole room first, gentlemen." [008-074]

## Function in the Séance

The cabinet serves a dual purpose. Practically, it provides a dark, enclosed space that spiritualists believed was necessary for the concentration of "ectoplasm" or spirit matter. Symbolically, it is a threshold — what happens inside the cabinet is hidden from the sitters, and what emerges from it may be genuine spirit communication or, as skeptics in the novel suggest, fraud.

## Theological Significance

Within the novel's Catholic critique of spiritualism, the cabinet is more than furniture. It is a kind of confessional inverted — not a place where sins are absolved, but where dangerous spiritual forces are invited in. Father Mahon's warnings about necromancy implicitly condemn the entire apparatus of which the cabinet is the physical center.
