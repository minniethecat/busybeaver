# Cardinal Newman

**Cardinal John Henry Newman** (1801–1890) was a towering figure of nineteenth-century English Catholicism — a theologian, convert, and eventually a cardinal of the Roman Catholic Church. In Robert Hugh Benson's *The Necromancers*, Newman appears in an unexpected role: as a spirit contact allegedly channeled through the medium [[mr-vincent|Mr. Vincent]] at the drawing-room séances of London spiritualist circles.

## In the Novel

Newman is first mentioned by [[mrs-stapleton|Mrs. Stapleton]] during her visit to [[stantons|Stantons]], the Baxter family home. In conversation with [[maggie-deronnais|Maggie Deronnais]] and [[mrs-baxter|Mrs. Baxter]], Mrs. Stapleton casually remarks that she had "such an interesting conversation" with Cardinal Newman "scarcely six weeks ago" — meaning through a medium. She explains that "on the Other Side they see things very differently" and that Newman, when Catholics are present, "does not scruple to give them a Latin blessing" ([002-029]–[002-038]).

Mrs. Baxter is horrified. Mrs. Stapleton's claim that the great Catholic cardinal now attends spiritualist drawing rooms and dispenses Latin blessings from beyond the grave strikes her as "extremely tiresome and upsetting" ([002-078]). The implication — that even the most devout Catholic souls abandon Christian orthodoxy after death — is a direct challenge to everything she believes.

## Maggie's Response

[[maggie-deronnais|Maggie Deronnais]] handles the situation with characteristic composure and irony. When Mrs. Stapleton offers to stop if Maggie is offended, Maggie replies with deadpan solemnity: "I am always glad to receive new light" ([002-036]). Later, in private conversation with Laurie, she expresses her real view: "how could Cardinal Newman possibly appear in a drawing room? Particularly when Mrs. Stapleton says he isn't a Christian any longer" ([002-112]).

For Maggie, the Newman claim is the clearest illustration of spiritualism's absurdity. She does not need theological arguments to refute it; the thing is simply untrue. This epistemological confidence — the Catholic instinct that some claims are so ludicrous they require no disproof — is exactly what [[laurie-baxter|Laurie]], in his grief-clouded state, has lost.

## Narrative Function

The Newman episode serves three purposes in the novel:

1. **Comic relief**: Mrs. Stapleton's earnest delivery of increasingly preposterous claims, met by Maggie's dry irony, provides one of the novel's few genuinely humorous sequences.

2. **Theological boundary-marking**: By invoking the most respected Catholic intellect of the age as a spirit-guide, spiritualism reveals its fundamental incompatibility with Catholic orthodoxy. Benson draws a bright line: the real Newman spent his life defending doctrinal truth; the "spirit Newman" of the séance room has abandoned Christianity altogether.

3. **Character development for Laurie**: Laurie's ambivalent response — he claims "I have not studied the evidence for Cardinal Newman" in a "head-voice" ([002-160]) — shows him trying to maintain intellectual detachment while being drawn deeper into the spiritualist worldview.

## Historical Note

The real John Henry Newman was canonized as a saint by the Catholic Church in 2019. Benson, writing in 1909, could not have known this, but his choice of Newman as the spirit-contact is pointed: if the greatest Catholic mind of the century could be so casually reimagined by spiritualists, what protection did ordinary believers have?

The invocation of Newman also reflects a real phenomenon of Victorian spiritualism, which frequently claimed the spirits of famous historical figures as communicants. The absurdity Benson satirizes — a cardinal of the Church attending suburban séances — was not his invention but a feature of the spiritualist culture he opposed.
