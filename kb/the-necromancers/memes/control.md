# Control

In the spiritualist practice depicted in *The Necromancers*, a **control** is a discarnate spirit entity that assumes direction of a medium's faculties during a séance, speaking through the medium's voice or guiding the medium's hand. The control acts as an intermediary — a spirit that has crossed over and now facilitates communication between the living participants and other spirits.

## In the Novel

The concept of controls is most directly articulated by Maggie Deronnais when she describes the spiritualist worldview to Laurie Baxter. Speaking of the spirits who communicate through the medium Mr. Vincent, she refers to "The 'controls,' she believed — the spirits" who directed the proceedings [012-011]. This formulation — with the word "controls" placed in quotation marks — suggests both familiarity with the technical vocabulary of spiritualism and a degree of personal conviction.

Mr. Vincent himself describes the mechanism more technically. He explains that spirits "gain control of the medium who is in a trance, and speak through his lips" [002-086]. The control is not the ultimate object of communication but the enabling agent — the spirit who has learned to manipulate the medium's physical organism.

At a later séance, Laurie Baxter, adopting a more confrontational stance, demands "that the control should give some unmistakable" sign of its identity [006-052]. This reflects the persistent tension in the novel between belief and skepticism: the control is simultaneously the most intimate point of contact with the spirit world and the hardest to verify, since everything it says is filtered through the medium's own body and voice.

The question of whether a medium is genuinely controlled by an external spirit or merely acting under self-control — a word that recurs throughout the novel in contexts of emotional restraint [001-065], [004-002], [013-025] — forms one of the central ambiguities of the narrative.
