[[lady-laura-bethell|Lady Laura Bethell]]'s waking vision in Chapter 16 is one of the novel's most quietly unnerving supernatural events. Occurring late in the narrative, when the spiritual crisis around [[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]] has built to its highest pressure, the vision is presented with an ambiguity characteristic of the novel's treatment of the supernatural: it may be a genuine premonition, a fever-dream, or something in between. What is certain is its effect on [[maggie-deronnais|Maggie Deronnais]], who receives Lady Laura's report and recognizes in it a confirmation of her own fears.

## The Setting

The vision occurs as Lady Laura dozes in her chair, hovering at the boundary between waking and sleep. She is "one of those persons whose moments between sleeping and waking, especially during a little attack of feverishness, are occupied in contemplating a number of little vivid pictures of all kinds that present themselves to the mental vision" [016-140].

The narrator describes the hypnagogic imagery in detail: "little scenes in which Maggie and Charlotte and medicine bottles and Chinese faces and printed pages of a book all moved together in a sort of convincing incoherence" [016-140]. This is the ordinary furniture of the half-sleeping mind — random, associative, meaningless. Lady Laura is "just beginning to lose herself in the depths of sleep" [016-140] when something changes.

## The Vision

"A little sound came, and she opened, as she thought, her eyes" [016-140]. The qualification — "as she thought" — is important: the narrative maintains the ambiguity between waking perception and dream throughout. What Lady Laura sees is her son Laurie, but not as she expects him:

"But the fear did not go immediately; though she told herself again and again that it was just one of those little waking visions that she knew so well" [016-143].

The fear is the vision's signature. Lady Laura's "little waking visions" are familiar to her — she has experienced them before and knows their character. This one is different not in kind but in intensity. It carries an emotional charge that her rational self-diagnosis cannot discharge.

## Lady Laura's Report

When Lady Laura reports the vision to Maggie, her language is carefully minimized: "I thought I saw Laurie look in, just for an instant. But he looked awful, somehow. It was just one of my little waking visions I've told you of, I suppose" [016-155].

The structure of the report is revealing. First the factual claim ("I thought I saw Laurie"), then the emotional content ("he looked awful"), then the rationalizing retreat ("just one of my little waking visions"). Lady Laura is trying to contain the experience within a familiar category, but the word "awful" — carrying its older weight of awe and dread — resists containment.

## Maggie's Response

Maggie's response is wordless but eloquent: "The girl was silent; but the old lady saw her suddenly straighten herself" [016-156]. The straightening is a physical manifestation of recognition. Maggie knows something Lady Laura does not — the full extent of the spiritual danger surrounding Laurie — and the vision, which Lady Laura dismisses as a neurological curiosity, confirms for Maggie that the crisis has reached a new intensity.

The silence between the two women after Lady Laura's report is charged with unshared knowledge. Lady Laura has the vision but not the context to interpret it; Maggie has the context but not the vision. Each possesses half of a truth that neither can fully articulate to the other.

## Significance

Lady Laura's waking vision occupies a strategic position in the novel's supernatural economy. Unlike the séances, which are orchestrated events with a professional medium, the vision is spontaneous, private, and apparently unmotivated. It belongs to the category of paranormal experience that resists both proof and disproof — phenomena that occur at the margins of consciousness, in the hypnagogic zone where the distinction between perception and imagination breaks down.

The novel does not resolve the vision's ontological status. It may be a genuine premonition — Lady Laura's latent mediumistic sensitivity registering a real spiritual disturbance. It may be a coincidence amplified by Maggie's anxiety. Or it may be a third thing: a natural psychological phenomenon — the half-sleeping mind's capacity for vivid imagery — that happens, in this instance, to correspond to a real crisis unfolding elsewhere. The novel's refusal to adjudicate between these possibilities is central to its method, which is to describe supernatural experiences without either endorsing or debunking them, leaving the reader suspended in the same uncertainty that afflicts its characters.
