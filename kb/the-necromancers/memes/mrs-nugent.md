# Mrs. Nugent

**Mrs. Nugent** (née Maria) is the wife of the village grocer [[mr-nugent|Mr. Nugent]] and the mother of [[amy-nugent|Amy Nugent]] in Robert Hugh Benson's *The Necromancers*. She is the novel's most striking example of an ordinary person encountering the supernatural unprepared — and the only character to experience what appears to be a genuine paranormal event without any involvement in spiritualism.

## Appearance and Character

Mrs. Nugent is "a full-bodied lady, in clothes rather too tight for her" who "panted a little after the ascent of the stairs" ([015-005]). She is practical, house-proud, and not given to imagination. Benson is careful to establish her complete lack of superstition: she "was utterly without a trace of what is known as superstition; for the whole evidential value of what follows, such as it is, depends upon that fact" ([015-007]). Her views on death are simple and orthodox: Amy's body is in the churchyard, Amy's soul is in heaven, and "there was no more to account for" ([015-007]).

This is what makes what happens to her so significant.

## The Supernatural Encounter

On Easter Saturday, Mrs. Nugent decides to sort through Amy's bedroom — the room where her daughter died. The room has become "a sort of melancholy sanctuary, symbolical of glories that might have been" ([015-003]), a shrine to the social elevation that Laurie Baxter's courtship had promised.

As she kneels to examine a drawer of linen, she hears the sound of a trotting horse approaching from the station. What follows is described with remarkable psychological precision:

"It seemed to her, she said, that there was something coming towards her that set her a-tremble; and when, a moment later, the trotting hoofs rang out sharp and near, she positively relapsed into a kind of sitting position on the floor, helpless and paralyzed by a furious up-rush of terror" ([015-012]).

The terror has a double quality: the external approach of the horse and an internal rising of something within the room itself, which "filled with some atmosphere, swift as water in a rising lock, that held her there motionless, blind and dumb with horror" ([015-013]). She loses consciousness as the sound passes.

When Mr. Nugent finds her and carries her downstairs, she can only express "grotesque expressions of fear — rolling eyes and exclamations" ([015-017]).

## Narrative Significance

Mrs. Nugent's experience is one of the novel's most ambiguous episodes. Unlike the séances conducted by [[mr-vincent|Mr. Vincent]], which are mediated, staged, and suspect, this event happens to a woman with no interest in spiritualism, no expectation of the supernatural, and no desire for attention. The horse and trap that pass outside are real — Mr. Nugent later confirms that Laurie Baxter passed at that hour ([015-026]) — but the terror that overcomes Mrs. Nugent has no natural explanation.

Benson does not resolve the ambiguity. Mr. Nugent's rationalizing explanations (stooped too fast, too much watercress, idle morning) are left to contend with Mrs. Nugent's wordless horror. For the reader, the episode suggests that the boundary between the natural and supernatural worlds is thinner than either the spiritualists or the rationalists claim — and that crossing it is not a matter of séance-room technique but of proximity to the dead.

The room where Amy died, the wardrobe still full of her belongings, the approaching sound of the young man who had loved her — these elements combine to create what [[maggie-deronnais|Maggie]] might call a genuine haunting, as opposed to the manufactured phenomena of the spiritualist drawing room.

## Aftermath

By evening, under "the influence of an excellent supper and a touch of stimulant," Mrs. Nugent allows herself to be persuaded that nothing supernatural occurred ([015-023]). But the practical consequence is absolute: the bedroom door is locked, the key kept on the table, and the servant-girl "registered a private vow that for no consideration under heaven would she enter the bedroom on the right at the top of the stairs" ([015-023]).
