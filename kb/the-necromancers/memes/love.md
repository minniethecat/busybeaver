## Overview

Love in *The Necromancers* operates on multiple registers: romantic love between Laurie Baxter and the deceased Amy Nugent, the enduring maternal love of Mrs. Baxter, the potential love between Laurie and Maggie Deronnais, and the divine love that the novel's Catholic framework presents as the only fully reliable form of attachment. Benson uses love as a diagnostic tool: the quality of a character's love reveals the quality of their soul, and disordered love — love that has lost its proper object — becomes a vector of spiritual danger.

## In the Novel

Romantic love is presented as both powerful and perilous. Laurie's love for Amy Nugent is described in terms of intensity that borders on idolatry. He "had honestly and simply fallen in love with her, with a romance usually" [001-024] associated with youthful passion. The aftermath of her death reveals the instability of romantic love when unmoored from religious framework: Laurie stands "seeing the last of his love, under a pile of blossoms" [001-025], and the image of burial carries spiritual as well as emotional weight. His grief makes him vulnerable — it is precisely his unresolved love for Amy that draws him to the seances where he believes he might contact her.

Maggie's relationship to love is characterized by composure. She "had fallen in love with the place from the instant" [001-058] — a love for the Baxter house and its surroundings that suggests stability rather than passion. Her feelings for Laurie are present but controlled, a contrast to the consuming quality of his feelings for Amy. The narrator notes that Laurie, in his grief, perceived "a dark future without life or love or hope" [001-066], and his vision of Amy's face "alight with love and fear and sudden wonder" [001-068] captures the compound nature of romantic love in the novel — it is never pure joy but always mixed with apprehension.

Maternal love, embodied in Mrs. Baxter, is sincere but limited. Her love for Laurie is genuine — no one doubts it — but it lacks the discernment to recognize or oppose spiritual danger. The novel's argument is implicit: natural love, however sincere, requires supernatural completion to be adequate to the soul's real needs.

## Love as Discernment: Maggie's Ordered Affections

The novel presents Maggie Deronnais as the model of properly ordered love. Unlike Laurie, whose love for Amy has become disordered — redirected from the living person to the impossible object of seance communication — Maggie's affections are integrated with her judgment. She has "recognized and certainly loved" [001-083] what is worthy of love, and her love does not blind her to spiritual reality but sharpens her perception of it.

This is a specifically Catholic understanding of love. The tradition teaches that love (*caritas*) is the form of all the virtues: it orders the soul's attachments toward their proper objects, with God as the ultimate end. Maggie's love for Laurie — quiet, steady, expressed through vigilance rather than display — exemplifies this ordering. She does not need to renounce her love to be discerning; her love *is* her discernment. The contrast with Laurie's romantic passion is deliberate: his love for Amy, severed from its human object by death and not elevated toward God, becomes an appetite that spiritualism can exploit.

## The Seance as Counterfeit of Loving Union

The spiritualist seance presents itself as a means of reuniting the living with the dead they love. The medium appeals directly to this desire: "Picture to yourself some soul that you have loved passionately, who has crossed over" [004-065]. The promise is of continued communion — love that death cannot sever. But the novel treats this promise as a deception, and the deception works precisely because the love is real.

The seance exploits the fact that Laurie "had honestly and simply fallen in love" [001-024]. His love is genuine; what is false is the apparatus that claims to satisfy it. The novel's critique is not of romantic love itself but of the refusal to accept its earthly limits. By seeking Amy through the medium rather than through prayer — by demanding experience rather than accepting faith — Laurie transforms his love from a virtue into a vulnerability.

## Context

The novel's treatment of love reflects the Catholic tradition's careful distinctions between different forms of love: *eros* (romantic love), *storge* (familial affection), *philia* (friendship), and *agape* (divine charity). The tradition warns that lower forms of love, when not ordered toward the higher, become disordered and potentially destructive. Laurie's romantic love for Amy, severed from its object by death and not redirected toward God, becomes an opening for spiritual deception.

## Key Quotations

> Laurie had met, spoken to, and ultimately kissed this Amy Nugent, on a certain summer evening as the stars came out; but, with a chivalry not so common in such cases, had also sincerely and simply fallen in love with her, with a romance usually reserved for better-matched affections. (001-024)

> He needed Amy herself, or, at least, some hint or sound or glimpse to show him that she indeed was as she had always been; whether in earth or heaven, he did not care; that there was somewhere something that was herself, some definite personal being of a continuous consciousness with that which he had known, characterized still by those graces which he thought he had recognized and certainly loved. (001-083)

> "Now, Mr. Baxter, imagine to yourself some soul that you have loved passionately, who has crossed over to the other side... Do you think that her first thought, or at least her second, will not be of you? In all that she sees, she will desire you to see it also. She will strive, crave, hunger for you." (004-065)

> Maggie had fallen in love with the place from the instant that she had entered it. There was here a sense of peace and sheltered security that she had hardly known even at school. (001-058)

> As he lay now, with lax hands at his side, he was turning now and again to the contemplation of a dark future without life or love or hope. (001-066)

> "Oh, my dear, why can't we be all just sensible and normal? I love doing just ordinary little things — the garden, and the chickens, and the cat and dog, and complaining to the butcher." (019-043)
