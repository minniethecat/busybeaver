## Overview

The French convent where Maggie Deronnais received her education is a significant off-stage presence in *The Necromancers*. Though never directly depicted in a scene, the convent functions as the formative space of Maggie's Catholic sensibility — the institution that shaped her moral perception, her spiritual discipline, and her immunity to the spiritualist allure that ensnares Laurie Baxter. It represents, in the novel's symbolic geography, everything that London's drawing-room seances are not: order, tradition, and genuine spiritual authority.

## Description

The convent is evoked through Maggie's memories and the narrator's observations about her character. She "had come to her, upon leaving her convent school three years before" [001-023], arriving at the Baxter household with "a pleasant little income of her own" and, more importantly, a fully formed Catholic consciousness. The physical details are sparse — it is a "French convent" [019-004] and a "convent-school" [004-002] — but the formative effects are richly detailed.

Maggie "had been warned in her French convent of the giddy gaieties of the world" [001-058], an education that included explicit preparation for the moral dangers of secular society. The warning proved prescient: the "giddy gaieties" she encounters in England include not just social frivolity but the more serious spiritual peril of the seance room. The convent's formation equipped her to recognize danger that the Anglican Mrs. Baxter could not.

The narrator notes that "she was convent-bred" [005-058] as an explanation for her reserve, her composure, and her capacity for moral judgment. The phrase "convent-bred" carries a double valence in the Edwardian context — it signals both refinement and suspected foreignness, both cultivation and papist otherness. Maggie's Frenchness and her Catholicism are inseparable from her convent education, marking her as both insider and outsider in the English Baxter household.

## Role in the Story

The convent functions as a moral reference point rather than a setting. When Laurie dismisses Maggie's concerns about spiritualism, his internal monologue registers her as "just a convent-bred girl who couldn't understand" [006-075] — a telling dismissal that the narrative itself refutes. The convent-bred girl understands far more than the Oxford-educated young man, and her formation proves more reliable than his sophistication.

The convent also represents a counter-trajectory to Laurie's. Where his education at Oxford and his London social life have exposed him to fashionable ideas without equipping him to evaluate them, Maggie's convent education has given her a fixed moral framework. She does not need to reason her way to the conclusion that seances are dangerous — she has been taught it, and the teaching has been internalized as instinct. This formation is what enables her to guide Laurie toward recovery during the events of Laurie's Return, providing the spiritual discernment that makes reconciliation possible.

In the novel's resolution, when Maggie contemplates the possibility of "burying herself in a convent or the foreign mission-field" [005-059], the convent reappears as a vocational possibility — a life of total religious commitment that represents one extreme of the Catholic option. She does not take this path, but its availability as a genuine alternative to the secular life she leads in the Baxter household underscores the novel's argument for Catholicism's institutional completeness.

## Atmosphere

The convent is characterized by qualities of order, enclosure, and discipline — the antithesis of the fluid, boundary-dissolving atmosphere of the seance room. Where the trance state blurs the line between living and dead, self and other, will and surrender, the convent maintains clear distinctions: sacred and profane, virtue and sin, truth and error. Its atmosphere is one of clarity, and clarity, in Benson's theological imagination, is the condition of spiritual safety.

## Key Quotations

> "She had been warned in her French convent of the giddy gaieties of the world and its temptations; and yet it seemed to her after a week in her new home that the world was very much maligned." (001-058)

> "It must be remembered that she was convent-bred." (005-058)

> "She came to the conclusion, arrived at after much heart-searching, that after all she was not yet actually in love with Laurie, but was in danger of being so, and that therefore now that she knew the danger, and could guard against it, she need not actually withdraw from her home, and bury herself in a convent or the foreign mission-field." (005-059)

> "Yet, after all, he asked himself indignantly, what affair was it of hers? She was not his confessor; she was just a convent-bred girl who couldn't understand." (006-075)

> "Maggie looked marvelously well, considering the nervous strain that had come upon her about Easter-time. She had collapsed altogether, it seemed, in Easter week itself, and had been for a long rest -- one at her own dear French convent until a week ago, being entirely forbidden by the nuns to speak of her experiences at all." (019-004)

> "The Church gives me just as much of all that as is good for me; and, for the rest, just tells me to be quiet and not bother -- above all, not to peep or pry." (019-050)
