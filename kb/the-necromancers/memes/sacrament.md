# Sacrament

In Catholic theology, a **sacrament** is an outward sign instituted by Christ to confer inward grace. In Robert Hugh Benson's *The Necromancers*, the sacramental economy of the Catholic Church stands as the theological alternative to the counterfeit "mysteries" of spiritualism — one offering genuine contact with the divine, the other a deceptive simulation.

## The Sacramental vs. the Séance

The novel's central theological argument is structured around a contrast between two ways of accessing the supernatural. Spiritualism offers direct, sensory evidence: tables that rap, tambourines that sound, faces that appear in cabinets. Catholicism offers the sacraments: baptism, confession, the Eucharist — outward signs whose power lies not in sensory drama but in divine institution.

[[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]], in his crisis of faith, experiences this contrast acutely. "Religion bade him approach in one way, Spiritualism in the other," Benson writes. "The senses had nothing to do with one; they were the only ultimate channels of the other" ([011-092]). The sacrament demands [[faith]]; the séance demands only attendance.

## Confession and the Confessional

The sacrament most directly invoked in the novel is **Penance** (Confession). Laurie's relationship with [[father-mahon|Father Mahon]] is structured around the confessional — or rather, around Laurie's avoidance of it. The confessional box that appears in [[mr-vincent|Mr. Vincent]]'s séance room — "a curious erection, not unlike a confessional, seated within for one, roofed, walled, and floored with thin wood" ([008-051]) — is the novel's most pointed visual symbol. The "cabinet" where mediums sit is a dark parody of the confessional: one offers absolution from a priest acting *in persona Christi*; the other offers messages from spirits whose identity and intentions are uncertain.

Mr. Vincent himself reinforces the parallel, telling Laurie that "all that has passed here is, of course, as sacred as in the confessional" ([004-185]). The claim is both reassuring and blasphemous — it borrows the Church's language of sacred confidentiality while dealing in phenomena the Church condemns.

## The Sacrament of the Sick

The novel's climactic turn toward Catholic restoration centers on another sacrament: the **Anointing of the Sick** (Extreme Unction). When Laurie, broken by his experiences, finally returns to the Church, it is through the sacraments — not through argument or emotional appeal, but through the objective channels of grace that the Church administers. This return is the novel's answer to spiritualism: not disproof but supersession.

## Benson's Sacramental Vision

Benson, a Catholic priest as well as a novelist, consistently frames the spiritual life in sacramental terms. The material world is not opposed to the spiritual but is its vehicle — wood and water, bread and wine, oil and words become channels of divine action. Spiritualism, in this view, is not wrong because it believes in the supernatural, but because it seeks the supernatural *outside* the channels God has established.

The novel's final position is not that the supernatural does not exist — Benson was perfectly willing to believe in preternatural phenomena — but that the proper response to the supernatural is not the séance circle but the confessional, not the medium but the priest, not "spirit communication" but sacramental grace.
