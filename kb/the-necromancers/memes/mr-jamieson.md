## Overview

Mr. Jamieson is a young clergyman who appears in the novel primarily as a participant in the Baker Street séances. Described as "just a young clergyman" [003-013] by Lady Laura Bethell, he represents the religious professional drawn to spiritualism — a figure the novel treats with a mixture of sympathy and warning. Jamieson is not a villain or a fool; he is a sincere clergyman whose religious formation has not equipped him to recognize the spiritual danger latent in the séance room. His presence in Vincent's circle demonstrates the permeability of the boundary between orthodox Christianity and spiritualist practice.

## Background

Jamieson's institutional identity is established economically: he is a clergyman — presumably Anglican, given the novel's setting and his association with the non-Catholic characters — whose interest in spiritualism is presented as professional curiosity rather than personal crisis. Lady Laura introduces him to Laurie: "Mr. Baxter — Mr. Jamieson. Mr. Jamieson is so anxious to know all that" [004-005] — the incomplete sentence leaves open what exactly Jamieson is anxious to know, but the implication is that his clerical identity makes the séance phenomena theologically interesting rather than merely sensational.

His physical manner is characterized by throat-clearing. "Mr. Jamieson cleared his throat in a virile" [004-010] fashion, and he "cleared his throat in gentle" [004-046] acknowledgment at key moments. The tic suggests a man who is uncomfortable without being aware of his discomfort — the body registers what the mind has not yet admitted.

## Role in the Story

Jamieson appears primarily in the séance scenes of Chapters 4, 7, and 8. At his first séance, he "sat back and swallowed perceptibly" [004-075] — the adverb "perceptibly" inviting the reader to observe what Jamieson himself may not recognize: that he is swallowing his own doubt. The medium addresses him directly: "And you, Mr. Jamieson?" [004-090], incorporating him into the performance and rendering his clerical authority irrelevant.

Jamieson returns to Laurie after the séance: "Mr. Jamieson came to see me again a few days" [007-036] later, and his persistence suggests that the séance has unsettled him in ways he cannot articulate. On his final visit, "Mr. Jamieson wore an expression usually associated" [008-052] with spiritual disturbance — a visual register of the cumulative effect of the séances on a man whose theological training gave him no categories for what he was experiencing.

The novel never resolves Jamieson's story. He disappears from the narrative after Chapter 8, his fate left ambiguous. This narrative ellipsis is itself a judgment: Jamieson is a case study in spiritual danger without resolution, a warning about what happens to religious professionals who lack the theological resources to resist spiritualist seduction.

## Character Traits

Jamieson is sincere, curious, and theologically under-equipped. His throat-clearing and perceptible swallowing suggest a body that knows what the mind denies — physical symptoms of spiritual unease. He is not presented as weak or foolish but as representative: many clergymen of the period were drawn to spiritualism, and Jamieson's story is the novel's acknowledgment of this historical reality.

## Significance

Jamieson represents the failure of conventional Anglicanism to provide adequate spiritual protection. His clerical identity, which ought to have equipped him to recognize and resist spiritual danger, instead makes him a more interesting participant in the séance — the medium can use him to demonstrate that even clergymen are convinced. The novel's Catholic argument is implicit: only a church with sacramental authority and a developed theology of the preternatural can protect its members from what Jamieson encounters.
