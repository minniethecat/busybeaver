# Rapping

**Rapping** refers to the knocking or tapping sounds that occur during spiritualist séances, traditionally understood as a form of communication from spirits. In *The Necromancers*, rapping is presented as the most elementary of the physical phenomena produced through mediumship — the first rung on a ladder that ascends toward materialization, and the point at which the skeptical participant's defenses begin to erode.

## The Phenomenon

Rapping is the simplest form of spirit communication: spirits are believed to affect matter by producing audible knocks in response to questions. Maggie Deronnais explains this to Laurie as part of her introduction to spiritualist practice:

> "...generally by things like table-rapping, or making blue lights. Then ..." [002-086]

Mr. Vincent elaborates on the principle during a discussion with Laurie, positioning rapping within a spectrum of spirit-matter interaction:

> "...they can affect matter — in table-rapping, for instance, and still more..." [004-036]

The "still more" is crucial — rapping is never presented as an end in itself but as evidence that greater phenomena are possible. The phenomenon is treated by spiritualists as proof that consciousness survives death and retains the ability to interact with the physical world. But the novel's framing treats it differently: rapping is the bait, the seemingly innocuous demonstration that makes the more dangerous phenomena thinkable.

## At the Séances

Rapping features prominently in the Baker Street séances. The sound is described as mechanical, rhythmic, and unmistakeable — a sensory experience that resists rational dismissal:

> "And the rapping, loud, continuous, unmistakab..." [006-035]

> There was the loud continuous rapping, at the beginning and the end... [004-200]

The physicality of the sound is what makes it convincing. It is not a whisper or a suggestion but a percussive event, as real as a knock on a door. Even after witnessing more dramatic phenomena, the rapping remains a persistent element — a constant that grounds the more spectacular manifestations in a baseline of apparent physical reality:

> ...remained the rapping and what the medium had called... [006-004]

In the climactic sessions, the sound acquires an eerie precision that transforms it from crude knocking into something more disturbing:

> ...perfectly calculated, as the rapping of some minute electric hamme... [013-022]

The simile — "minute electric hammer" — captures the novel's technique: the supernatural is described in mechanical terms, making it more credible by making it less mystical. The rapping is not ghostly; it is calculated, precise, industrial. This is the aesthetic of spiritualism the novel dissects: phenomena presented as technology, the séance room as laboratory.

## Rapping in the Novel's Structure

The novel uses rapping as a baseline phenomenon — it is the first evidence offered to convince the skeptical, and it establishes the premise that spirits can affect the material world. From this baseline, each subsequent séance escalates: from rapping to automatic writing, from writing to partial materialization, and finally to the attempted complete materialization that brings disaster.

Maggie, recounting the full arc of events to Mr. Cathcart in the Epilogue, traces the progression precisely:

> "...from the pencil and the rappings and the automatic writing, right up to the appearances ..." [019-016]

The sequence is significant: "pencil" (the planchette), then "rappings," then "automatic writing," then "appearances." Each term names a stage in a journey whose endpoint was visible from the beginning to anyone who understood what spiritualism actually was. The retrospective summary frames rapping as the entry point — seemingly harmless, even quaint — that led step by step into mortal peril.

## Theological Reading

Within the novel's Catholic framework, rapping is not innocent. Father Mahon's citation of Sabetti classifies all spirit communication as potential necromancy. The rapping that the characters initially treat as a curiosity is, from this perspective, the first knock of a door that should never have been opened. The mechanical precision that makes rapping convincing as "evidence" also makes it dangerous — it is precisely because the phenomenon appears real that it cannot be dismissed as harmless.

The novel's theology treats the natural order as a protection. The dead do not normally communicate with the living because they are not meant to. Any phenomenon that breaches this boundary — no matter how small, no matter how seemingly benign — is an act of violence against the order God has established. Rapping is not dangerous because it might be fraudulent; it is dangerous because it might be real.
