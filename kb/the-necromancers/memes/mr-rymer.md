Mr. Rymer is a solicitor and family adviser to the Baxters who serves as the novel's voice of rational-materialist skepticism. His blunt dismissal of Spiritualism — "it's all nonsense" [002-105] — provides the counterweight to [[mrs-stapleton|Mrs. Stapleton]]'s New Thought enthusiasm, and his arguments shape Laurie's initial resistance to the supernatural claims that surround him.

## Character and Role

Mr. Rymer is introduced through reported speech — other characters relay his opinions, establishing him as an off-stage intellectual authority whose views carry weight in the Baxter household. He is likely the family's legal adviser; his involvement with "letters" [005-081] suggests a professional relationship with the Baxters that extends beyond casual acquaintance.

> Mr. Rymer tells me it's all nonsense. [002-105]

His defining characteristic is his unwavering skepticism toward Spiritualism. He goes further, asserting that "things were permitted" [002-117] — a theological framing that initially seems to concede supernatural possibility but actually functions as dismissal.

> Mr. Rymer tells me Spiritualism is all nonsense. [002-108]

His wife, Mrs. Rymer, appears in passing [002-099], though she is a minor figure. The crucial question Rymer poses transcends mere rejection:

> What's going on underneath? That's what Mr. Rymer said to Mrs. Baxter. [010-062]

## Relationship with Laurie Baxter

Mr. Rymer's influence on Laurie is most directly captured in this question — "What's going on underneath?" — which becomes one of the novel's structuring inquiries. Laurie, caught between his mother's credulity and the inexplicable events he witnesses, finds in Rymer's rationalism a framework for resistance, even as that framework proves increasingly inadequate to the unfolding reality.

Laurie's relationship with Rymer is mediated through his mother: "Mr. Rymer tells me" [002-105] is Mrs. Baxter's report of Rymer's views, suggesting that Rymer is a figure of adult authority whose opinions are transmitted to Laurie rather than directly debated.

This mediation is significant — Laurie receives Rymer's skepticism as received wisdom rather than reasoned conclusion, leaving him vulnerable when direct experience challenges that wisdom.

## The Skeptic's Function

| Position | Representative | Stance |
|----------|---------------|--------|
| Credulous enthusiasm | [[mrs-stapleton]] | Spiritualism as revelation |
| Systematic rationalism | [[james-morton]] | Inquiry through evidence |
| Hard materialism | **Mr. Rymer** | "It's all nonsense" |
| Theological orthodoxy | [[mr-cathcart]] | Spiritualism as theological error |
| Tragic experience | [[laurie-baxter]] | Belief forced by events |

Mr. Rymer occupies the farthest pole of the novel's spectrum of belief: the position that spiritualist claims are not merely unproven but categorically false. Unlike [[james-morton]], who approaches the question with the tools of rational inquiry, Rymer has already decided. His skepticism is not investigative but declarative — a settled worldview rather than an open question.

"Does Mr. Rymer know very much about it, do you?" [002-111] — Mrs. Baxter leaves this question rhetorically hanging. The implication is that Rymer's skepticism is not grounded in investigation but in presupposition.

Yet his question "what's going on underneath?" [010-062] remains pertinent throughout the novel, as the surface phenomena of séances give way to darker realities that demand explanation.

## Significance

Mr. Rymer represents the default skepticism of the educated late-Victorian gentleman — a position that the novel neither wholly endorses nor dismisses. His function is to articulate the rational objection to Spiritualism in its bluntest form, so that the more nuanced positions (Morton's investigative rationalism, Cathcart's theological critique, Laurie's existential struggle) can emerge in relief.

He is the baseline against which other responses to the supernatural are measured: if Rymer is wrong that it is "all nonsense," then *how* is he wrong, and *what* is the truth? The novel's answer — that some of it is nonsense, and some of it is far more dangerous than nonsense — vindicates Rymer's instinct to look underneath while demonstrating the inadequacy of his dismissive framework.
