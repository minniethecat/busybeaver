## Overview

Hope in *The Necromancers* appears in two incompatible forms: theological hope, grounded in divine promise and sustained by faith, and natural hope, which attaches to human objects and collapses when those objects fail. The novel traces both forms through its characters. Laurie Baxter's hope for reunion with Amy through spiritualism is natural hope misdirected — it looks toward a human object through a counterfeit supernatural means. Maggie Deronnais's hope is quieter, a disposition rather than a demand, grounded in her Catholic formation rather than in any particular outcome. The novel's argument is that only theological hope is reliable, because only its object is immune to loss.

## In the Novel

Mrs. Baxter embodies natural hope in its most sympathetic form. She "had hoped that Laurie and Maggie might" [001-023] form an attachment — a mother's reasonable hope for her son's happiness. She "had hoped indeed that the Church, with its" [001-029] resources might help Laurie through his grief — hope placed in an institution rather than in God, well-meaning but second-order. Her hopes are what any good mother would feel, and the novel never condemns them. But they are hopes bounded by this world, and the novel's spiritual drama operates beyond those bounds.

Laurie confronts hope's limits directly. His mother asks him: "You know I once hoped" [001-034], and the past tense is devastating — hope that has become past tense is hope that has died. After Amy's death, Laurie sees "a dark future without life or love or hope" [001-066], and hope's absence is presented as the worst of the three losses. Laurie, "crushed, broken, and hopeless, like young ivy after a" [001-071] storm, is an image of natural hope destroyed. The séances rekindle his hope, but the novel treats this rekindling as dangerous: hope restored to the wrong object is not restoration but redirection toward illusion.

The word "hopeless" appears strategically. "Oh! it's hopeless" [019-008] — the exclamation carries theological weight beyond the immediate context. To be without hope, in Catholic understanding, is the sin of despair, the mirror-image of presumption. The novel's characters who lack hope are not merely unhappy but spiritually compromised.

Maggie's hope operates differently. She says, "I hope now that she'll spend the rest" [019-060] of her days in peace — a modest hope, appropriate to its object, without the grandiosity of Laurie's demands on the supernatural. Maggie hopes for ordinary goods; Laurie hoped for the impossible. The novel's implicit theology is that proper hope is proportionate to its object and grounded in the God who is its ultimate guarantor.

Mr. Vincent tells Laurie, "I hope you will allow me to say how" [001-099] — hope reduced to social formula, the word hollowed out. The contrast between theological hope, natural hope, and formulaic hope traces the word's degradation across the novel's social spectrum.

## Context

Hope is the second theological virtue in Catholic doctrine, positioned between faith (which gives the intellect its object) and charity (which gives the will its object). Hope is the virtue of the will directed toward future goods that are difficult but possible — ultimately, eternal beatitude. The sin against hope is despair (refusing to trust God's mercy) or presumption (expecting salvation without conversion). Laurie's hope for reunion through spiritualism is natural hope distorted into something approaching presumption: the expectation of supernatural consolation without supernatural means.
