Maggie Deronnais's interview with Mrs. Baxter in the garden of Maxwell Grange is the novel's introductory character scene, establishing Maggie's origins, temperament, and the shadow of her mother's death that hangs over her. Conducted on the day of [[amy-nugents-death|Amy Nugent's]] funeral, the interview sets in motion the emotional currents that will define the Baxter household's relationship with its new resident.

## Prelude

Mrs. Baxter has arranged to take in Maggie — the daughter of a deceased French mother and a Baptist grocer — following the death of Maggie's mother three years earlier at her convent school. The arrangement is charitable but also strategic: Mrs. Baxter wants companionship for her son [[laurie-baxter]] and hopes Maggie's presence will distract him from his grief over Amy Nugent's death. "She wanted to see the girl, and the interview in the garden had left her" [001-025] — the phrasing suggests both curiosity and appraisal.

## The Interview

The garden setting is significant: it is the space between the house and the outside world, a liminal zone where Maggie's past and future meet. Mrs. Baxter, "a small, delicate-looking old lady" [001-002], interviews the girl with a mixture of kindness and sharp observation. She notes the French convent education [001-023], the mother's death, and the "manner of life" [001-023] that has shaped Maggie's character.

The interview is punctuated by the visible signs of Amy Nugent's funeral — Laurie "was now at the funeral" [001-031] — creating a spectral backdrop. The juxtaposition of the living girl in the garden and the dead girl being buried sharpens the thematic tension: Maggie is being installed, however unwittingly, as a replacement.

Mrs. Baxter comes away from the interview both impressed and unsettled. The girl's French refinement and convent training mark her as socially acceptable, but something in her bearing — a depth, a gravity beyond her years — troubles the older woman's calculations. "The interview in the garden had left her more" [001-025] than merely satisfied; she is disturbed in ways she cannot yet articulate.

## Aftermath

The interview results in Maggie's permanent installation at Maxwell Grange. Mrs. Baxter's decision seals the novel's central domestic arrangement, bringing Maggie into Laurie's orbit and into the house where supernatural forces are already beginning to stir. The "funeral scene this morning" [001-071] — witnessed by Mrs. Baxter from the garden — casts its shadow over the domestic scene, a memento mori that frames Maggie's arrival as something more than simple hospitality.

## Significance

The garden interview is the novel's first event in the strict sense — a bounded encounter with fixed participants, a defined setting, and consequential outcomes. It establishes the pattern of interviews and confrontations that will structure the narrative, and it introduces Maggie not through her own voice but through the lens of Mrs. Baxter's assessment, a technique of mediated characterization that the novel uses repeatedly for its female characters.

The interview also introduces the novel's key structuring absence: the dead. Amy Nugent is present only as a name and a funeral, Maggie's mother only as a death that occurred three years prior. The garden interview assembles the living in a space bordered by the dead, a spatial metaphor for the novel's central argument — that the boundary between living and dead is thinner than anyone in the sunlit garden yet understands.
