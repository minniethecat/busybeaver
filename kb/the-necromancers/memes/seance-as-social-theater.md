# Séance as Social Theater: Performance, Class, and Gender in the Drawing Room

## Introduction

The séances in *The Necromancers* are not merely supernatural events; they are social performances. Benson situates his séances in the drawing rooms of Edwardian London — first Lady Laura Bethell's Queen's Gate residence, then Mr. Vincent's Baker Street establishment — and in doing so transforms the séance from a purely occult ritual into a piece of Victorian social theater. In these gatherings, class hierarchy, gender dynamics, professional authority, and the spectator's relation to the performance combine to create an experience in which the social and the supernatural are inseparable. The novel's insight is that the séance's power depends as much on its social staging as on any genuine supernatural agency — and that its danger lies partly in how skillfully that staging disguises itself as neutral inquiry.

## The Drawing Room as Stage

The physical settings of the séances are the novel's first argument about their nature. Lady Laura's Queen's Gate house is unmistakably upper-class: the "broad opulent pavement of Queen's Gate" [012-017], the "Queen Anne doorway" [014-006], the drawing room where breakfast things remain scattered [003-004]. The setting signals respectability and wealth — this is not a back-alley fortune-teller's parlor but the home of a titled woman of independent means. When Lady Laura "had just returned to her house in Queen's Gate, with her dearest friend, Mrs. Stapleton, for a few days of psychical orgy" [003-001], the casual reference to "psychical orgy" framed as a weekend entertainment captures precisely the tension Benson exploits: spiritual danger dressed in the costume of aristocratic leisure.

Mr. Vincent's Baker Street rooms operate differently but with equal theatrical intelligence. The séance room is "singularly unsuggestive of supernatural mystery" [004-001] — a deliberate strategy of deflation that makes the phenomena seem all the more credible. Rather than velvet curtains and crystal balls, the medium offers a professional, almost clinical space. This is the theater of science, not mysticism: the medium in his broadcloth [004-014] resembles a medical consultant more than a conjurer. The two settings — Queen's Gate's aristocratic domesticity and Baker Street's professional quasi-science — between them cover the full social range of Edwardian respectability, ensuring that no participant can dismiss the proceedings as low fakery.

## The Medium as Performer and Director

Mr. Vincent is both the star performer and the director of the séance as social drama. His professional reputation precedes him: he is "certainly the greatest medium in London, if not in Europe" [003-058], and this reputation functions as advance billing — the audience arrives primed to experience something extraordinary. His technique is calibrated throughout: he speaks "tranquilly" [004-163], smiles reassuringly [004-190], nods at confirmations [004-158], maintains a kindly demeanor [004-204]. Every gesture is measured, every response modulated — this is not spontaneous spiritual transport but controlled theatrical presentation.

The medium's physical placement of participants is a directorial act. Laurie "sat on one side and the medium on the other" [004-100], an arrangement that establishes spatial control while creating the intimacy of a private consultation. When the medium asks "You are sure, Mr. Baxter, that you are not at all nervous?" [004-094], the question performs multiple functions simultaneously: it diagnoses vulnerability, manufactures it if absent, and positions the medium as the calm authority managing the sitter's emotional state. His framing of the trance as "a very deep and satisfactory trance. There is nothing to be frightened of" [004-152] completes the performance of benevolent expertise — the professional who interprets the experience for the client, defining what it means before the client can form an independent judgment.

The séance protocol itself borrows from both theater and liturgy. "At the beginning of the séance the medium had repeated his warnings against levity, and his recommendations of a 'calm and reverent attitude'" [013-018]. The instructions mimic the ritual framing of a religious service — the call to reverence, the warning against profanation — while the content is purely dramatic. The medium establishes the emotional conditions under which the performance will succeed, making the audience complicit in the theatrical frame: if you do not feel the right way, the fault is yours, not the medium's.

## Class and Gender Dynamics

The séance circle in *The Necromancers* is shaped by the class and gender hierarchies of Edwardian society, and Benson exploits these hierarchies to deepen his critique. Lady Laura Bethell, the aristocratic hostess, occupies a position of social authority that lends credibility to the proceedings. Her house, her title, her network of connections — all confer legitimacy on the spiritualist practice that occurs under her roof. That a woman of her standing should host "psychical orgies" is the scandal the novel insists upon: the devil does not need to work through disreputable channels when the drawing rooms of the respectable are available.

The gender dynamics are equally significant. Spiritualism in the novel is a feminized space: the hostess is a woman, her companion Mrs. Stapleton is a woman, and the emotional register of the séance — grief, longing, the desire for contact with the dead — is coded as feminine. Mr. Vincent, as the male medium, occupies an ambiguous position: professionally authoritative yet operating in a sphere associated with female emotionalism. His calm professionalism can be read as a masculine performance designed to reassure participants that the proceedings are rational and controlled, not hysterical or uncontrolled. The medium's power lies partly in his ability to combine feminine receptivity (the trance state, the passivity required for spirit communication) with masculine authority (the clinical language, the professional setting, the directorial control).

Maggie Deronnais provides the counter-example. Her Catholic formation at [[the-convent|The Convent]], her steady piety, and her immunity to the séance's appeal represent a femininity that needs no theatrical mediation of the supernatural because it has access to the sacraments. She is the woman who does not need the drawing-room drama because she possesses the reality it counterfeits.

## The Spectator's Complicity

Benson's most sophisticated social insight is that the séance makes spectators into participants. Lady Laura "sat as one in trance herself" [004-209] while observing Laurie's trance, suggesting that mere spectatorship is not passive but transformative — exposure to the performance alters the observer's own consciousness. The séance room produces a shared altered state in which skepticism is gradually dissolved by the collective experience. "I have seen people very much upset at séances, more than once" [008-013] — the understatement registers the cumulative effect. People are not merely entertained by the séance; they are changed by it.

The progression from public to private séances deepens the spectator's complicity. Laurie begins with "public séances" [011-035] that leave him unimpressed [011-096]. It is the private, domestic séances at Queen's Gate that affect him profoundly, and the medium reinforces this by advising that Laurie should not attend public séances "at present" [007-008]. The move from public to private mirrors the move from spectator to participant, from observer to subject, from one who watches to one who is watched — and acted upon.

## Conclusion

The séances in *The Necromancers* succeed as vehicles of spiritual danger precisely because they succeed as social theater. Benson's anatomy of the séance — the drawing-room setting that confers respectability, the medium's calibrated performance of professional authority, the class and gender dynamics that structure the circle, the spectator's gradual transformation into participant — reveals the séance as a carefully constructed social ritual that exploits its participants' trust in the forms of respectable society. The novel's Catholic critique is not merely that spiritualism is theologically wrong but that it is socially insidious: it uses the drawing room, the titled hostess, the professional gentleman, and the language of science to disguise what is, in the novel's framework, a transaction with the demonic. The final irony is that the medium's most effective performance is not the materialization of spirits but the performance of harmlessness — and it is this performance, more than any supernatural phenomenon, that makes the séance truly dangerous.
