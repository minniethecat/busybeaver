The séance at [[the-convent|Saint Mary's Convent]] is the novel's supernatural climax — the event at which the polite drawing-room spiritualism of earlier chapters crosses definitively into genuine and dangerous contact with the other world. Unlike the domestic séances at Baker Street and Queen's Gate, the convent séance occurs in a space already thick with religious significance, and the forces it unleashes exceed the participants' capacity for control.

## Prelude

The convent has been a presence throughout the novel: Maggie's French convent education [001-023], her "dear French convent" [019-004] where she spent her formative years. The return to the convent for a séance is symbolically charged — it brings the novel's two spiritual systems, Catholicism and Spiritualism, into direct spatial contact. Father Mahon's theological warnings about possession hover over the gathering.

## The Séance

The séance is conducted by [[mr-vincent]], the medium who has presided over the earlier gatherings at Queen's Gate and Baker Street. But the convent atmosphere transforms the proceedings. The space itself — "on two sides rose masses of August greenery" [019-003] — is at once pastoral and monastic, the lushness of late summer pressing against the ancient stones of the convent walls.

The critical moment comes when the contact moves beyond the trance communications of earlier séances. Father Mahon's earlier warning that spiritual entities "want to get possession of human beings" [019-034] takes on immediate force. The boundary that the earlier séances merely touched — between communication and possession — is crossed. "Mr. Vincent, Mrs. Stapleton, and the rest, really think they have come into communication with the other world" [019-022] — the narrator's careful "really think" maintains ambiguity about what has happened while acknowledging the participants' conviction.

## Aftermath

The séance's aftermath transforms the novel's stakes. What was previously a matter of belief versus skepticism becomes a question of survival. The entities contacted at the convent are not the benign presences of spirit-rapping lore but something predatory. The "providential" nature of Amy Nugent's death — "think me brutal, please — how providential her death was!" [019-062] — is reconsidered in light of what the séance has revealed about the reality of the afterlife and its dangers.

## Significance

The Saint Mary's Convent séance is the novel's answer to the spiritualist question that has driven its plot from the beginning. Spiritualism, the event demonstrates, is neither the "nonsense" Mr. Rymer claims nor the "Higher Light" Mrs. Stapleton celebrates — it is a real contact with real forces, and the forces are malevolent. The convent setting makes the theological point explicit: the forces contacted through séances are what Catholic theology has always called demons, and to seek their communication is to invite their possession.
