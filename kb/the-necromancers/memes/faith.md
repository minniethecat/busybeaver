## Overview

Faith in *The Necromancers* is not a generic religious sentiment but a specific theological virtue — the supernatural gift by which the intellect assents to divine revelation on the authority of God.

The novel distinguishes sharply between Catholic faith (which protects), Anglican religiosity (which is sincere but insufficient), and spiritualist "faith" (which is credulity masquerading as belief). Characters are measured by the quality of their faith, and spiritual disaster befalls those whose faith is misplaced.

## In the Novel

The novel's religious landscape is defined by different modes of faith. Maggie Deronnais represents Catholic faith as a fully integrated habit of mind and soul. She was "educated in the same faith" [001-023] as the Catholic characters who form the novel's moral center.

Her faith expresses itself not in display but in steady discernment. She does not need to reason her way to conclusions about spiritualism — her faith has formed her instincts.

Mrs. Baxter represents Anglican faith — genuine but incomplete. Her piety is "humble, tender, and sincere" [001-002] but lacks the doctrinal clarity and institutional authority that Catholic faith provides. When spiritual crisis erupts, her faith offers comfort but not guidance.

Lady Laura Bethell represents a different case: a person of religious inclinations who has not yet "completely emancipated from what her friends sometimes called the graveclothes of so-called Revelation" [003-002]. Her faith is in transition — not yet abandoned, but no longer operational. This liminal state makes her vulnerable to spiritualist claims that fill the space orthodox faith once occupied.

The spiritualists themselves appeal to faith — but a faith redefined as openness to evidence rather than assent to authority. Mr. Vincent tells Laurie: "We do not ask for blind faith, Mr. Baxter — only for believing" [003-061]. The distinction is a rhetorical sleight of hand: the medium's "believing" is faith without content, trust without object, credulity without creed.

Laurie's Catholic faith is tested throughout the novel. The medium assures him: "I would not ask you to accept anything contrary to your faith" [004-047], and the assurance is technically true — the medium asks only for experiences, not doctrinal revision. But experiences, the novel argues, are precisely how faith is undone. Laurie's faith is real but weak; it requires the supplementation of authority he is tempted to reject.

## The Crisis of Faith: Experience Versus Authority

The novel's central theological drama concerns the relationship between faith and personal experience. Laurie Baxter begins with a faith that is genuine but largely inherited — he "had been educated in the same faith" [001-023] but his religion had become, as the narrator observes, a matter of "almost a superstitious rather than a religious instinct" [002-198]. This is faith as cultural inheritance rather than personal conviction, and it proves catastrophically vulnerable to the experiential appeals of spiritualism.

The crisis unfolds precisely at the point where experience appears to supersede authority. After his first seance, Laurie perceives that "so long as a man walks by faith, by the acceptance of authority, human or divine, there is always psychologically possible the assertion of self, the instinct that what one has not personally experienced may just conceivably be untrue" [009-026]. The seance has given him something faith could not: the apparent evidence of his senses. The tragedy is that this evidence — manufactured by fraud or demonic agency — destroys the foundation of belief precisely by appearing to confirm it.

The effect on Laurie's religious life is immediate and devastating. "As he looked at the Catholic religion, he perceived that the whole thing had changed. It no longer seemed august and dominant" [009-027]. The Mass he attended the previous morning now appears "rather absurd" [009-027]. This is not intellectual doubt but something more corrosive — a loss of the sense of the sacred, brought about by substituting sensory experience for the assent of faith.

The theological point is precise. Father Mahon, consulting his manual of moral theology, reads aloud the crucial distinction: spiritualist practices are sinful "even if it is openly protested that no commerce with the Demon is intended," and can be excused from mortal sin only "on account of simplicity or ignorance or a lack of certain faith" [010-069]. The phrase "lack of certain faith" is diagnostic: Laurie's faith, however sincere, lacks the certainty that would have protected him from seeking supernatural knowledge outside the Church's authority.

## Faith as Protection: The Final Crisis

The novel's climax turns on whether faith can be recovered after it has been compromised by false experience. Maggie Deronnais, confronting the spiritual danger that has engulfed the Baxter household, understands that "she must make that act of faith" [017-006] — a phrase that echoes the theological definition of faith as an act of the will, not merely an intellectual state. The locked door, the secretive visit, the sense of something terrible beneath the ordinary — all require Maggie to exercise faith as a deliberate choice rather than a passive inheritance.

This final emphasis on faith as act rather than sentiment completes the novel's theology. Catholic faith protects not because it provides better feelings or more consoling beliefs, but because it commands obedience to authority when experience contradicts doctrine. The spiritualists offer experience without authority; the Church offers authority that judges experience. Laurie's tragedy is that he chose the former, and by the time he understood the choice, his capacity for the latter had been fatally weakened.

## Context

The novel's theology of faith draws on the Catholic understanding of faith as a theological virtue infused at baptism — distinct from mere belief or opinion, and requiring the Church's teaching authority for its proper exercise. This contrasts with both the Protestant emphasis on individual faith and the secular-modern reduction of faith to personal preference.
