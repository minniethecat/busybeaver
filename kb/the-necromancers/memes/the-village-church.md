The village church near Stantons is a minor but symbolically charged location in the novel's geography of the sacred. It represents the Church of England — established, respectable, and tasteful in a slightly oppressive way — and its churchyard contains the grave of Amy Nugent, the dead young woman whose posthumous communications through [[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]]'s mediumship drive the novel's supernatural plot.

## Description

The church is introduced through [[maggie-deronnais|Maggie Deronnais]]'s skeptical eye: "It was a typical little country church, with that odor of the respectable and rather stuffy sanctity peculiar to the class; she had wrinkled her nose at it more than once in Laurie's company" [005-035]. The description is precise in its social criticism — "respectable and rather stuffy sanctity" is a phrase that pins the Church of England to its class function as neatly as any in the novel.

Maggie's reaction — "wrinkled her nose" — aligns her Catholic sensibility against the established church's comfortable worldliness. For Maggie, sanctity should not be "stuffy"; the very word suggests upholstery rather than sacrifice. The village church is a place where religion has been domesticated, its dangerous edges smoothed into respectability.

## The Churchyard

The churchyard is the more significant location. It is here that Amy Nugent is buried, and here that Mr. Vincent's mediumistic revelation is tested. "Tell Laurie that the ground has sunk a little above my grave; and that cracks are showing at the sides" [006-049] — the sentence from Vincent's notebook refers to this churchyard, and its verification would confirm the reality of the communications Laurie has been receiving.

Maggie visits the churchyard alone: "She passed by the door of it now, and, stepping among the wet grasses, came down the little slope among the headstones to where a very white marble angel clasped an equally white marble cross" [005-035]. The "very white marble angel" and "equally white marble cross" are objects of Maggie's aesthetic disapproval — "the intolerable taste of the thing" [005-035] — but they mark the physical location that anchors the novel's supernatural claims to the material world.

Later, Mr. Vincent suggests Laurie should verify the grave's condition: "You'd come to the Protestant churchyard with me for a moment after Mass" [006-090]. The proposal brings together the Catholic and Protestant geographies — Mass in one place, the churchyard in another — and the grave of Amy Nugent stands at their intersection, a physical fact that belongs to neither and both.

## Significance

The village church and its churchyard embody the novel's treatment of established religion: not hostile but insufficient. The church offers "stuffy sanctity" when what is needed is exorcism; its churchyard offers marble angels when what is needed is an accounting with the dead. The Church of England, like Stantons itself, is part of the established order that spiritualism disrupts — and, like Stantons, it proves incapable of mounting an effective defense.
