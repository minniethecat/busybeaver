## Overview

Lady Laura Bethell is the novel's embodiment of spiritualism as a fashionable pursuit of the leisured class. A spinster of means, she hosts private séances at her home, patronizes the medium Mr. Vincent, and serves as Laurie Baxter's entry point into the spiritualist world. Her enthusiasm for the supernatural is at once earnest and dangerous — she means no harm, but her patronage enables harm.

## Character

"Lady Laura Bethell, spinster, had just returned" [003-001] from some social engagement when the reader first meets her. She is "one of those" [002-025] enthusiasts whose interest in psychic phenomena is genuine but uncritical. Her speech is marked by a breathless quality — "he is such a man, you know" [003-006] — and a tendency to "deprecate and soothe" [003-015] when challenged. She is not malicious; she is credulous, and her credulity is the medium's resource.

## Role in the Story

Lady Laura functions as facilitator and enabler. It is she who sends "a nervous little note" [006-006] reminding Laurie of a séance, she who provides the private venue when the Baker Street rooms become unavailable, and she who vouches for Mr. Vincent's authenticity. Her social position gives the séances a veneer of respectability — if Lady Laura hosts them, how dangerous can they be?

She also serves as a conduit of information about Amy Nugent. She speaks of "the village girl" [003-048] with the casual condescension of her class, and has "received information about the village girl, more or less correct" [003-027] — the qualification "more or less" hinting at the gossip's unreliability.

## Significance

Lady Laura represents the social dimension of the novel's critique. Spiritualism, in Benson's portrayal, flourishes among those with too much leisure and too little theological formation. Lady Laura's harmlessness makes her dangerous — she would never intend spiritual damage, but her drawing-room séances open doors she does not understand.
