## Overview

Mr. Parker is the butler at the Baxter household, a minor but carefully observed character who represents the ordinary, professional servant class perspective on the extraordinary events of the novel. His role is limited to a single chapter, but his presence anchors the domestic reality against which the séance phenomena are measured.

## In the Novel

Mr. Parker appears principally in Chapter 13, on the night of a séance at the Baxter house. He is first seen after the evening's proceedings, permitted his small ritual: "It was already after ten o'clock; and Mr. Parker was permitted to smoke a small cigar" [013-003]. This detail — the cigar as permission, not entitlement — establishes his position in the household hierarchy.

Laurie's interaction with him is minimal: "He had not said one word to Mr. Parker this evening as he took off his coat" [013-004]. The silence is noted; Laurie is distracted, preoccupied, already under the influence of the séance's aftermath. Mr. Parker, as a servant, notices but does not comment.

## The Butler as Observer

Mr. Parker's most significant function in the narrative is as a register of atmosphere. He "had not thought that he looked very well" [013-004] — observing Laurie's condition — and his repeated remarks about the weather serve as a counterpoint to the spiritual drama. "It was a very wild night, as Mr. Parker observed four times: he only did so once more before going to bed" [013-010]. The comic precision of this — five observations total — humanizes him and grounds the supernatural in the mundane.

He had "once been under-butler" [013-007] to Old Lady Carraden, a detail that places him in the established hierarchy of domestic service. His professional identity gives him a perspective the other characters lack: he sees the Baxter household from below, observing its rhythms and disturbances without the emotional investment of family members.

Mr. Parker also "had once more helped to erect this morning" [013-006] the cabinet used for the séance. He is complicit in the apparatus of spiritualism without understanding or believing in it. The novel's implicit point is that the machinery of the occult relies on ordinary people doing ordinary tasks — setting up cabinets, serving tea, smoking cigars — without questioning the purpose.

Laurie, "as Mr. Parker had noticed, had been 'quiet-like'" [013-017]. The butler's vernacular observation — "quiet-like" — carries the novel's understated technique: the most important spiritual developments are registered through the most ordinary idioms.

## Significance

Mr. Parker is one of several minor characters (along with Mr. Cathcart, Mr. Jamieson, Mr. Rymer) who populate the novel's social world without driving its plot. His function is atmospheric and structural: he represents the uncomprehending but observant world outside the spiritual drama, the ordinary life that continues while souls are contested.
