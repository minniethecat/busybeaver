[[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]]'s departure for Oxford, occurring between the novel's opening chapters and the events of Chapter 5, is a deceptively quiet narrative event. It is mentioned only in retrospect, yet its emotional resonance shapes the interior lives of the novel's central characters — his mother, Mrs. Baxter, and [[maggie-deronnais|Maggie Deronnais]] — during the crucial weeks when the spiritualist crisis is building.

## The Departure

The departure itself is treated with characteristic understatement. After the events of the opening chapters — Laurie's first séance experience at Queen's Gate, his confused return home — "Laurie had gone back to Oxford as an avowed proselyte" [001-022]. The narrator does not dramatize the leave-taking; instead, the departure functions as a structural hinge, removing Laurie from the narrative foreground so that the effects of his absence can be observed in those who remain.

## Mrs. Baxter's Uneasiness

The emotional impact of Laurie's departure registers first through his mother. "That uneasiness had culminated a few days after Laurie's departure one evening as she sat with the old lady after tea — in a sudden touch of terror at she knew not what" [005-002]. Mrs. Baxter's distress is notable precisely because she cannot name its source. The terror she experiences is not about Oxford or about Laurie's safety in any ordinary sense; it is a premonitory dread that the narrator leaves unexplained, allowing it to hover as an atmospheric pressure over the chapters that follow.

When Maggie asks what is troubling her, Mrs. Baxter deflects: "Nothing. I was just thinking of Laurie; I don't know why" [005-006]. The denial itself is telling — the mother senses danger but cannot articulate it, a pattern that will recur as the supernatural threat intensifies.

## Maggie's Self-Discovery

For Maggie, Laurie's absence is the catalyst for a discovery she had not anticipated. With Laurie gone, she finds herself "beginning to study, rather to her own surprise… the character of Laurie" [005-012]. The physical distance allows her to observe her own feelings from a safer vantage, and what she finds disturbs her:

"She began to become a little astonished at the frequency with which, during a silent drive, or some mild mechanical labor in the gardens, the image of that young man would rise before her" [005-012].

This is the beginning of Maggie's recognition that her feeling for Laurie may be more than professional regard. The narrator tracks her mental negotiations with precision: she reads Laurie's letters home with an attention that exceeds mere politeness, she registers his mother's comments about his activities at Oxford, and she finds herself "angry" at his perceived neglect of his mother [005-027].

## Maggie's Emotional Negotiation

By the time Laurie's answer to her own letter arrives, "poor Maggie had arranged her emotions fairly satisfactorily" [005-059]. She has concluded that she is "not yet actually in love with Laurie, but was in danger of being so" [005-059], and that awareness of the danger is sufficient protection against it. The narrator's tone is gently ironic — Maggie's careful self-diagnosis is a rational construction imposed on an irrational development.

The letter Laurie sends back from Oxford, with its "neat Oxford-looking hand, the artistic appearance of the paragraphs" [005-075], becomes an object of intense but controlled scrutiny. Maggie reads it as evidence for her theory of non-attachment, even as the very attention she pays to its material features — the handwriting, the paragraph breaks — betrays the attachment she is trying to deny.

## Aftermath

The weeks of Laurie's absence settle into a "serene peace" that Maggie attributes to her emotional discipline: "She would not for an instant allow to herself that any of their smoothness arose from the fact that this boy would be here again in a few weeks" [005-080]. She arranges the Christmas shooting — "Master Laurie would be home for Christmas as usual" [005-080] — "all in an unemotional manner," a phrase whose very insistence on emotional neutrality signals the feeling it suppresses.

Mrs. Baxter, meanwhile, has concluded during Laurie's absence that "Laurie was unhappy in his religion" [005-081], a worry that will prove prescient. She resolves that the Vicar, Mr. Rymer, must "set it right" [005-081] when Laurie returns, unaware that the spiritual dangers gathering around her son are of a kind that parish clergymen are not equipped to address.

## Significance

Laurie's departure for Oxford is the novel's first deployment of absence as a narrative strategy. By removing the protagonist from view, the author allows the secondary characters — Mrs. Baxter and Maggie — to develop their independent relationships to the crisis that is forming, and allows the reader to understand that Laurie's spiritual predicament is not merely a private matter but a disturbance that radiates outward through the emotional field of the household.
