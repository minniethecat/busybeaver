## Overview

Maggie Deronnais (full name Margaret Marie Deronnais) is the female protagonist of Robert Hugh Benson's *The Necromancers*. Of half-French parentage and Catholic upbringing, she is the adopted daughter of Mrs. Baxter and a steady moral presence in the Baxter household. Where Laurie Baxter is impulsive, emotional, and spiritually unstable, Maggie is characterized by composure, perceptiveness, and a quiet but firm Catholic faith. She functions as both the novel's moral center and its romantic resolution.

## Background

Maggie is the daughter of a French parent and was educated in a convent school. She "had come to her, upon leaving her convent school three years before, with a pleasant little income of her own" [001-023], arriving at the Baxter household by an arrangement made before her mother's death. Her French Catholic heritage distinguishes her from the Anglican English setting of the Baxter home, giving her a perspective that is both insider and outsider.

Her physical description emphasizes strength and composure: "regular features, a slightly cleft rounded chin, a quantity of dark coiled hair, and large, steady, serene brown eyes" [001-004]. The narrator compares her to "a well-bred sheepdog eyeing an Angora cat" [001-004] when contrasted with Mrs. Baxter — a telling image of capability and watchfulness.

## Role in the Story

Maggie serves as the narrative's anchoring consciousness. From her first appearance — "The girl who sat upright with her hands on her lap" [001-004] — she is positioned as an observer and judge of character. She sees through Laurie's romantic posturing about Amy Nugent: "It seemed to her very odd that neither the boy himself nor Mrs. Baxter in the least seemed to realize the astonishing selfishness of this very boy's actions" [001-027].

Her religious faith is steady rather than demonstrative. While she "approved of his conversion to Catholicism, yet she was not sure that his motives were pure" [001-029], showing a critical intelligence about religious matters that contrasts with Laurie's emotional extremes.

In the novel's final section, set four months after the main events, Maggie reflects on the experience with characteristic composure: "like a very bad dream" [019-005]. She has emerged from the spiritual crisis with her faith intact and her understanding deepened. Her final conversation reveals the novel's thematic resolution, as she articulates the Catholic position on spiritualism with clarity: "to go to séances with good intentions is like going into a plague-stricken house" [019-029].

## Character Traits

Maggie's defining quality is her composure — "she had an air of repose, strength, and all-round competence" [001-004]. She is perceptive, able to see through self-deception in others. Her faith is internalized and practical rather than performative. She combines French cultural sophistication with English common sense, making her the most balanced character in the novel.

## Relationships

- **Mrs. Baxter**: Her adopted mother, toward whom Maggie shows patient affection and gentle protectiveness
- **Laurie Baxter**: The man Mrs. Baxter hopes she will marry; Maggie's feelings are complex — she sees his flaws clearly yet remains drawn to him
- **Father Mahon**: Her spiritual advisor and confessor, representing ecclesiastical authority

## Significance

Maggie embodies the novel's ideal of Catholic womanhood — faithful, intelligent, composed, and perceptive. She is the standard against which Laurie's spiritual instability is measured, and her eventual union with him (implied in the epilogue) represents the resolution of spiritual disorder into domestic and religious order.

## Key Quotations

> Maggie Deronnais was "a Catholic to her fingertips," her faith informing every aspect of her character and judgment. (001-007)

> "I'm not in the least sure of what it is that I believe even now," Maggie confessed, four months after the crisis, revealing the enduring psychological cost. (019-001)

> "Courage and love" — just two words came over and over again before her consciousness as she prepared to confront the darkness that had overtaken Laurie. (017-003)

> "I was absolutely certain that the thing was real, that he was actually obsessed, that the Personality came at me instead, and that somehow I won." (019-011)

> She recognized "the astonishing selfishness of this very boy's actions" — her moral clarity extending even to the man she would eventually love. (001-027)

> "There's a kind of thick hedge between me and Easter — I suppose it's the illness," she reflected, describing the psychological barrier the experience had erected. (019-008)
