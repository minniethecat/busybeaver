## Overview

Darkness in *The Necromancers* operates at multiple registers: as literal séance-room atmosphere, as metaphor for the hidden realms of spirit communication, and as a paradoxical site of revelation rather than concealment. The novel repeatedly constructs darkness as a condition for spiritual perception — light is associated with ordinary consciousness, while darkness enables extraordinary sight.

## In the Novel

The most concentrated darkness imagery appears during the séance scenes. Laurie Baxter experiences a moment of retreat into "this warm and fragrant darkness, hidden from all eyes" [002-210], where the absence of light becomes a shelter for intimacy with the unseen. The séance room is consistently described in terms of dimness: lowered gas, drawn curtains, the deliberate creation of twilight as the medium's working condition.

Darkness also functions as the counterpoint to revelation. Characters enter the séance in darkness and emerge with knowledge — the pattern inverts the conventional light/dark = knowledge/ignorance binary. What the characters learn in darkness is more real, more consequential, than anything they know in daylight.

The novel's climactic possession scenes intensify the motif: the entity manifests in obscurity, its presence felt before it is seen, its darkness a quality of spiritual weight rather than mere absence of light.

## Darkness and Intimacy

The séance darkness creates a particular kind of social space. In "this warm and fragrant darkness, hidden from all eyes" [002-210], the normal protocols of Edwardian propriety are suspended. Hands may touch in the dark; voices may speak with an unguardedness forbidden in lamplight. Laurie's experience of darkness as "warm" — a sensory adjective usually applied to light — suggests that darkness in the séance room is not cold absence but inhabited presence.

The intimacy of darkness is also its danger. What passes between sitters in the dark may be genuine spiritual communion, or it may be something less innocent: the medium's manipulation, the sitter's projection, the devil's counterfeit. The novel refuses to separate these possibilities cleanly, leaving darkness as a morally ambiguous medium.

## Outdoor Darkness

Benson extends the darkness motif beyond the séance room. The London streets are described at night with their own quality of obscurity [007-087], the "darkness of the garden in some square" suggesting that the supernatural operates outside as well as within. A "ring of light shone out into the darkness to explain that sudden withdrawal" [008-094] — the interplay of light and dark in the novel's exterior scenes mirrors the séance room's controlled illumination.

In chapter 13, "heavy red curtains shut out the darkness" [013-009] — the verb "shut out" implies an active effort to exclude what presses in from outside. Darkness in these exterior passages is not the warm, fragrant interior darkness of the séance but something wilder, less domesticated, more threatening. The garden in "the gusty darkness" [013-010] is the alternative to the curtained room: nature's own séance, without a medium to control it.

## Context

Benson's use of darkness draws on multiple traditions: the mystical "dark night" of St. John of the Cross, where spiritual progress requires the abandonment of sensory light; Victorian Gothic conventions where darkness signals the supernatural; and the practical reality of Edwardian séances, which were conducted in dim rooms to facilitate trance states.

## Significance

Darkness in the novel is never merely decorative. Each darkened room is a laboratory: the conditions under which the boundary between worlds becomes permeable. Benson's insistence on describing darkness in sensory detail — its warmth, its fragrance, its texture — transforms it from an absence into a presence, something actively *felt* rather than simply not seen.
