## Overview

Heaven appears in *The Necromancers* primarily as a theological reference point rather than a detailed cosmology. It functions as the implicit opposite of spirit communication — the "gift from heaven" [001-081] that confirms divine order — and as the presumed dwelling-place of the dead who are *not* contacted through séances. The novel's reticence about heaven itself underscores its focus on the intermediate zone between death and final judgment.

## In the Novel

The novel's most explicit reference to heaven appears early, in the narrator's description of spiritual gifts arriving "as a gift from heaven after the bargain of summer had been fulfilled" [001-081]. This framing positions heaven as a source of benevolent dispensation, distinct from the morally ambiguous phenomena of the séance room.

When characters contact the dead through Lady Laura's mediumship, questions inevitably arise about *which* dead are accessible. Father Mahon's theological framework implies that the saved — those in heaven — would not be the ones manifesting at Baker Street; the spirits contacted are either souls in purgatory or, worse, demonic impostures.

The novel's climactic exorcism scene makes heaven's role explicit in its liturgical language: the rite invokes divine authority against the possessing entity, positioning heaven as the ultimate power over whatever dwells in darkness.

## Purgatory and the Séance

The Catholic doctrine of purgatory — an intermediate state of purification between death and heaven — is crucial to the novel's implicit theology of the séance. A character asks, with striking bluntness: "what's purgatory, then, or heaven?" [006-127]. The question, posed in the context of spirit communication, exposes the theological problem: if the dead are accessible, are they in heaven, hell, or somewhere in between?

The novel's answer, though never stated explicitly, is implied by the séance phenomena themselves. The spirits who manifest are confused, repetitive, sometimes trivial — not the blessed souls of Catholic eschatology but something more ambiguous. They belong, the novel suggests, to a zone of incompletion rather than to the beatific vision. Heaven remains, throughout, the place from which no spirit returns — and this silence is the novel's most orthodox Catholic gesture.

## Heaven's Silence

Heaven in *The Necromancers* is defined primarily by its absence from the narrative. No character claims to have contacted a soul confirmed to be in heaven; no séance message carries the unmistakable mark of beatitude. This silence is theologically precise: in Catholic doctrine, the blessed in heaven have no need to communicate with the living, for they rest in the direct vision of God.

The novel thus uses heaven as a limit-concept. Spirit communication may be real, but it never reaches the highest register of the afterlife. The séance can contact the troubled dead, the wandering dead, perhaps the damned — but not the saved. This distinction, never argued but consistently implied, is Benson's quiet affirmation of Catholic orthodoxy within a narrative that otherwise entertains spiritualist possibilities.

## Significance

Heaven in *The Necromancers* is defined mostly by its absence — as the place spirit communication cannot reach. This absence creates the novel's theological tension: if the dead are accessible, what does that imply about the state of their souls?
