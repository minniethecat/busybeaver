## Overview

Confession — the Sacrament of Penance — appears in *The Necromancers* not as a depicted event but as an implied theological norm against which spiritualist practice is measured. The novel draws an implicit parallel between the confessional and the séance room: both involve private disclosure, both promise relief, but one operates within the Church's authority and the other outside it.

## In the Novel

The word "confessional" appears when Mr. Vincent assures Laurie that the proceedings of the séance are "as sacred as in the confessional" [004-185]. This comparison is theologically audacious — it claims for the séance the same seal of confidentiality that Catholic theology reserves for the sacrament. The novel's Catholic framework treats this as a category error: the séance may mimic the form of sacred confidence, but it lacks the grace of the sacrament.

The séance room itself is described as having an arrangement "not unlike a confessional" [008-051] — a curtained enclosure with seating for one within. The architectural parallel reinforces the thematic one: spiritualism builds structures that resemble Catholic ones, but the resemblance is superficial.

## The Séance as Counterfeit Confession

The parallel between confessional and séance is the novel's sharpest theological critique of spiritualism. In Catholic practice, the confessional is a place of truthful self-disclosure before God, mediated by a priest who acts *in persona Christi*. The seal of confession is absolute — the priest may never reveal what he hears.

Mr. Vincent's invocation of this seal — "as sacred as in the confessional" [004-185] — appropriates the language of sacramental theology for a practice that the novel regards as spiritually dangerous. The séance promises confidentiality, but what it actually offers is exposure: the medium does not absolve but manipulates. The architectural resemblance only deepens the deception.

## Laurie and the Priesthood

Laurie's relationship to confession is complicated by his early history. His mother recalls that "he was going to be a priest at first" [001-020], and his "intention to enter the priesthood" [001-023] had shaped his early formation. That Laurie once considered the priesthood — the office that confers the power to absolve — and later finds himself in a counterfeit confessional is one of the novel's quiet ironies.

Father Mahon, the "conscientious priest" who "said his Mass at eight o'clock" [010-040], represents the steady, unglamorous practice of the Catholic priesthood that Laurie rejected. The novel does not develop Father Mahon's character at length, but his presence serves as a normative benchmark: the real priest against whom the medium-priest is measured.

## Confession as Narrative Structure: Truth-Telling and Plot Revelation

The novel uses confession — understood broadly as the act of truthful disclosure — as a structural principle. The moments when characters tell the truth about what they have experienced function as narrative turning points. Maggie's conversations with Father Mahon, Laurie's halting admissions about the séances, Mrs. Baxter's eventual acknowledgment of the danger — each of these is a form of confession in the narrative sense, even when the Sacrament of Penance is not explicitly invoked.

This narrative pattern draws its power from the sacramental model it echoes. In Catholic theology, confession is not merely cathartic but effective: it restores the soul to a state of grace. The novel's secular confessions lack this supernatural efficacy — characters tell the truth but are not thereby healed — and the gap between disclosure and restoration is part of the novel's somber vision. Spiritualism offers counterfeit confession without absolution; the real sacrament, implied but never directly shown, remains the novel's unspoken standard.

The contrast is sharpest in the figure of the medium. Mr. Vincent promises confidentiality — "as sacred as in the confessional" [004-185] — and receives Laurie's confidences. But he is not a priest and cannot absolve. The information Laurie discloses in the séance room does not liberate him; it binds him more tightly to the medium's influence. The novel's implicit argument is that confession without grace is merely exposure, and exposure to the wrong confessor is a form of surrender.

## Theological Significance

In Catholic sacramental theology, confession requires contrition, confession of sins, and absolution by a priest acting *in persona Christi*. The séance, by contrast, offers disclosure without absolution — the medium may hear secrets but cannot forgive sins. The novel treats this distinction as central: the spiritualist promise is a counterfeit of the Church's promise.

The sacrament of penance is "preached by word and symbol and sacrament" [011-092] — it belongs to the visible, institutional life of the Church. Spiritualism, by contrast, operates in darkened rooms and behind curtains, offering a privacy that mimics sacred confidentiality but is actually secrecy without grace.
