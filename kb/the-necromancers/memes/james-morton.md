## Overview

James Morton is Laurie Baxter's friend and rowing coach at Oxford, and the novel's most articulate skeptic. He is "a square-built man" [009-034] with rooms "conveniently situated" [009-032] and a temperament that distrusts spiritualism on principle. Morton appears primarily in Chapter 9, where he listens to Laurie's account of the séances and delivers the novel's most memorable verdict: "Blasted rot" [009-055]. His function in the narrative is to voice the rationalist objection to spiritualism — an objection the novel treats as correct in its conclusion but insufficient in its grounds.

## Background

Morton is a product of "a second-rate college" [009-033] whose rooms reflect a man of action rather than contemplation. "Mr. James Morton's taste did not redeem the character" [009-033] of his surroundings — the detail suggests a man who has not cultivated aesthetic sensibility, who judges by results rather than appearances. His relationship with Laurie is established before the novel's action: he is Laurie's coach, a role that implies both authority and care, the older hand guiding the younger. Mrs. Baxter "asked after Mr. Morton, Laurie's coach, with proper" [006-080] concern, confirming his established place in the Baxter household's awareness.

## Role in the Story

Morton's narrative function is concentrated in Chapter 9, a dialogue chapter devoted almost entirely to his conversation with Laurie. Laurie comes to him with the story of the séances — the phenomena, the revelations, the medium's persuasiveness — and Morton listens, questions, and dismisses. His method is not theological argument but commonsense reduction: he does not debate the possibility of spirits but judges the phenomena by their effects.

"About half-past twelve Mr. Morton shut his own book with a slap" [009-040] — the gesture conveys impatience with ideas that do not survive daylight. "Mr. Morton waited" [009-046] for Laurie to finish, and his silence is a form of judgment. When Laurie has concluded, Morton delivers his assessment in three words: "Blasted rot" [009-055]. The repetition — "Blasted rot, said Mr. Morton again" [009-065] — reinforces the verdict, and when Laurie presses for explanation, Morton's response is epistemological: "I don't explain. The thing's simply not worth" [009-070] explaining. For Morton, spiritualism does not merit intellectual engagement; it disqualifies itself by its content.

Yet Morton is not presented as entirely adequate to the situation. His rationalism cannot account for the phenomena Laurie has witnessed, and his dismissal, however commonsensical, leaves Laurie without an alternative framework. Morton reluctantly concedes that Laurie "is all right" [009-079], but the concession is "grudging" — he knows something is wrong without being able to name it. The novel's Catholic framework suggests that Morton's rationalism, like Mrs. Baxter's sentiment, is a partial truth mistaken for a complete one.

## Character Traits

Morton is blunt, loyal, and intellectually honest within his limits. His vocabulary is physical and dismissive — "blasted rot" — and he distrusts complexity that cannot be reduced to clear terms. He is a good friend to Laurie: he listens, he warns, and he does not abandon his friend even when he cannot understand his predicament. His limitation is not dishonesty but incompleteness: rationalism cannot diagnose spiritual danger because it denies the category in which spiritual danger exists.

## Significance

Morton represents the secular-rationalist pole of the novel's spiritual spectrum. Between Father Mahon's Catholicism (supernatural reality governed by authority) and Mr. Vincent's spiritualism (supernatural reality governed by experience), Morton occupies the third position: supernatural reality denied. The novel treats all three positions as coherent but only one as adequate. Morton's dismissal of spiritualism is correct; his grounds for dismissing it — that it is simply nonsense — are insufficient for anyone who has witnessed what Laurie has witnessed.
