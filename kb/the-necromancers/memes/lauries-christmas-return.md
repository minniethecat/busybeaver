[[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]]'s return to Stantons for Christmas is the structural counterpart to his [[lauries-departure-for-oxford|departure for Oxford]] earlier in the same chapter. Where the departure was narrated retrospectively as an event whose emotional effects were observed in those left behind, the return is narrated prospectively — as anticipation, preparation, and emotional self-management — giving it a different narrative texture that reveals the interior states of [[maggie-deronnais|Maggie Deronnais]] and [[mrs-baxter|Mrs. Baxter]] at a critical juncture in the novel's development.

## Anticipation

The anticipation of Laurie's return is established through Maggie's management of domestic arrangements. She "informed the acting keeper that Master Laurie would be home for Christmas as usual — all in an unemotional manner" [005-080]. The phrase "unemotional manner" is characteristic of Maggie's technique of self-regulation: by treating the preparations as practical tasks, she attempts to neutralize the emotional charge that Laurie's return carries.

The narrator undercuts this performance of detachment in the same sentence: "She would not for an instant allow to herself that any of their smoothness arose from the fact that this boy would be here again in a few weeks" [005-080]. The very intensity of the denial reveals the feeling it is meant to suppress.

## Maggie's Emotional Preparation

Maggie's emotional preparation for Laurie's return has been underway since his departure. She has arrived, after "much heart-searching," at the conclusion that she is "not yet actually in love with Laurie, but was in danger of being so" [005-059]. The diagnosis is meant to be prophylactic: by naming the danger, she believes she can guard against it.

The return tests this hypothesis. "The days went pleasantly enough after that," the narrator reports [005-080], and Maggie attributes her serenity to emotional discipline. But the carefulness of her preparations — the shooting, the chrysanthemums, the social arrangements — suggests an investment that exceeds the requirements of a household companion. She is preparing not merely for a guest's arrival but for an emotional encounter she has been rehearsing in her mind for weeks.

## Mrs. Baxter's Spiritual Agenda

For Mrs. Baxter, Laurie's return is an opportunity for intervention. During his absence, she has "come to the conclusion that Laurie was unhappy in his religion — certainly references to it had dropped out of his letters — and that Mr. Rymer must set it right" [005-081].

She announces her plan at breakfast: "The Vicar must dine here at least twice while Laurie is here. He has a great influence with young men" [005-082]. Maggie, who knows Laurie's opinion of the clergyman, reflects on "a remark or two, extremely unjust, made by Laurie with regard to the clergyman" [005-083] but withholds her knowledge. The scene establishes a pattern that will recur throughout the novel: Mrs. Baxter's well-intentioned interventions consistently misdiagnose the nature of Laurie's spiritual crisis, mistaking supernatural assault for religious doubt.

## Significance

Laurie's Christmas return marks the point at which the domestic and the supernatural plot lines begin to converge. The household's surface rhythms — shooting parties, Christmas dinners, the Vicar's visits — will provide the ordinary backdrop against which extraordinary events unfold. Maggie's emotional preparation and Mrs. Baxter's spiritual scheming both prove inadequate to the reality of what Laurie brings home with him.
