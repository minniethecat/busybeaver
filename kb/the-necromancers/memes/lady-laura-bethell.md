## Overview

Lady Laura Bethell is a central supporting character in *The Necromancers*, an aristocratic spinster whose Queen's Gate house in London functions as a salon for psychical investigation and spiritualist practice. She is the character who introduces Laurie Baxter to the séance circle, acting as the social bridge between respectable society and the dangerous world of mediumship. Her enthusiasm for spiritualism, combined with her incomplete emancipation from conventional religion, makes her a complex figure: neither villain nor innocent, but a well-meaning facilitator of spiritual harm.

## Background

Lady Laura is introduced as "Lady Laura Bethell, spinster," who "had just returned to her house in Queen's Gate, with her dearest friend, Mrs. Stapleton, for a few days of psychical orgy" [003-001]. The phrase "psychical orgy" is striking — it suggests both the intensity of her engagement with spiritualism and the narrative's moral judgment of it. Her house is described as "as much as in any in London" [003-001] a center for spiritualist activity, indicating her prominence in this subculture.

Her religious position is carefully delineated. The narrator notes that "Lady Laura herself was not yet completely emancipated from what her friends sometimes called the graveclothes of so-called Revelation" [003-002]. She occupies an intermediate space: too sophisticated for orthodox Christianity, yet not fully committed to the spiritualist worldview that would replace it. This theological indeterminacy makes her an effective facilitator — she can move between Catholic, Anglican, and spiritualist circles without fully belonging to any.

Mrs. Baxter describes Lady Laura as "one of those very fascinating, emotional women" [002-025] whose charm masks a lack of spiritual discernment. She is wealthy, well-connected, and genuinely curious about psychic phenomena — qualities that make her a perfect patron for a medium like Mr. Vincent.

## Role in the Story

Lady Laura's primary narrative function is to connect Laurie Baxter to the séance world. She first appears through reputation — Maggie overhears her speaking with Mrs. Baxter, describing "how very psychical" [002-016] the Baxter house is — and then through action when she "reminded him of the séance to be held in Baker Street" [006-006]. Her invitation is casual, social, almost inevitable — the kind of thing one does in her circle.

Her own engagement with spiritualism is presented as intellectually serious but morally naive. At the Queen's Gate séances, she participates with evident emotional investment. At the session where Laurie enters trance, "Lady Laura sat as one in trance herself" [004-209], her absorption in the proceedings blurring the line between observer and participant. The medium's control over the gathering extends to her as much as to Laurie.

She is also portrayed as a woman of genuine kindness within her limitations. When Maggie visits Queen's Gate, Lady Laura is solicitous: "she would fetch Lady Laura, she said; she thought she might be ready now" [003-068]. Her manner is gracious, her hospitality impeccable — which makes the spiritual danger of her household all the more insidious. Evil in Benson's novel does not announce itself with menace; it arrives with tea and polite conversation.

The narrative does not punish Lady Laura directly — she is not possessed, not dramatically ruined. Her fate is more subtle: she remains in her intermediate state, fascinated by forces she does not understand, facilitating dangers she cannot perceive. She is a cautionary figure precisely because she suffers no obvious catastrophe.

## Character Traits

Lady Laura is characterized by emotionalism, social grace, and theological shallowness. She possesses "the silvery hair of the devout widow" type but applies her devotional instincts to psychical research rather than religion. Her enthusiasm is genuine but undisciplined, her curiosity broad but uncritical. She represents a particular Edwardian type: the aristocratic woman who has lost her faith but not her need for the transcendent, finding in spiritualism a substitute that satisfies without demanding.

## Relationships

- **Mrs. Baxter**: A family friend of long standing, whose country house Lady Laura visits and whose son she introduces to spiritualism
- **Mr. Vincent**: The medium whose professional services she patronizes and whose Baker Street establishment she frequents
- **Laurie Baxter**: The young man she innocently guides into spiritual danger
- **Mrs. Stapleton**: Her dearest friend and companion in psychical investigation

## Significance

Lady Laura embodies the novel's critique of Edwardian spiritualism as a social phenomenon. She is not wicked — she is cultivated, generous, and genuinely seeking — but her spiritual discernment has been eroded by the very sophistication she values. Her Queen's Gate house, with its drawing-room séances and its fashionable guests, is the novel's image of a civilization that has replaced the authority of the Church with the excitement of the séance room, and cannot tell the difference.
