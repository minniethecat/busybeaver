## Overview

Awe in *The Necromancers* represents wonder intensified to the point of religious recognition. Where wonder opens the mind to the possibility of the supernatural, awe registers the presence of the sacred as a reality that demands acknowledgment — not merely intellectual assent but a reorientation of the whole person. The novel is sparing with awe; it appears rarely, and when it does, it marks characters who are responding to something genuine rather than something staged.

## In the Novel

The novel's most telling instance of awe arrives late, in Laurie's early perception of the supernatural. Laurie, looking at Amy, felt "that curious awed admiration that delighted him" [001-066] — the awe is directed at a person, not a phenomenon, and the adjective "curious" marks it as an emotion he does not fully understand. This is characteristic of the novel's treatment of awe: it arrives unbidden, resists analysis, and points beyond its immediate object.

The spiritualist séances occasionally generate awe in their participants. The clergyman who witnesses something unexpected emerges with "the awestruck face" [008-074], a reaction that bypasses his theological training and clerical identity. The novel does not mock this response — the clergyman's awe is genuine, a reaction to something real. Whether the reality is divine or diabolical is the question the novel leaves open, but the awe itself is not a mistake.

The medium commands a certain awe from those around him. Laurie, in his early encounters, is described as "sufficiently in awe of him to pay attention to his words" [009-035], and the awe here shades into deference — a recognition of authority, whether earned or merely projected. The novel distinguishes between awe at a person (which can be misplaced) and awe at the sacred (which is properly ordered). Laurie's awe at the medium is the former, a case of arrested spiritual development.

Mr. Morton, the rationalist observer, experiences something closer to authentic awe near the novel's climactic revelations. There was "a strange awe mingled with his old rather skeptical" [011-095] disposition, and the mingling is significant: awe does not expel skepticism but coexists with it, complicating the rationalist's tidy categories. This is perhaps the novel's most theologically acute moment — awe that survives skepticism is harder to dismiss than awe that replaces it.

## Context

Rudolf Otto's *The Idea of the Holy* (1917), roughly contemporary with Benson's writing, analyzed the religious experience of the *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* — the mystery that both repels and attracts, generating awe as its characteristic response. The novel's treatment of awe aligns with Otto's phenomenology: awe is the subjective correlate of encounter with the numinous, prior to doctrinal interpretation. Whether the numinous presence in the séance room is divine or demonic is a secondary question; the primary fact is that it is real, and awe is the soul's involuntary testimony to that reality.
