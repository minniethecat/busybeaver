## Overview

Mrs. Baxter is the matriarch of the Baxter household in Robert Hugh Benson's *The Necromancers*, a "small, delicate-looking old lady, very true to type indeed, with the silvery hair of the devout widow" [001-002]. She is Laurie Baxter's mother and Maggie Deronnais's adopted guardian, a woman whose sincere but shallow piety provides the domestic background against which the novel's spiritual drama unfolds. Her inability to perceive the moral dimensions of her son's behavior — and the spiritual danger of his pursuits — makes her an unwitting enabler of the crisis that follows.

## Background

The novel introduces Mrs. Baxter in her drawing room, "the room in which she sat reflected perfectly her personality" [001-003]. The furniture is "early Victorian," suggesting a sensibility formed in an earlier era and not fully adapted to the Edwardian present. She is a widow, and her identity is defined primarily through her relationship to her son: "They were talking now about Laurie Baxter" [001-006] is the second substantive sentence of the novel.

Her piety is genuine but of a particular kind — "humble, tender, and sincere, but as persistent as the" [001-002] conventions of her class and generation. She attends church, says her prayers, and observes the forms of Anglican devotion, but her faith lacks the intellectual and moral rigor that the novel's Catholic characters possess. When crisis comes, her piety provides comfort but not discernment.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Baxter's narrative function is to represent the inadequacy of conventional Anglican piety in the face of genuine spiritual evil. She is present at the novel's opening, murmuring "I am very much distressed about it all" [001-001] — a refrain that captures her characteristic response to difficulty: distress without effective action.

Her relationship with Laurie is marked by maternal indulgence that slides into moral blindness. When the subject of Laurie's behavior after Amy Nugent's death arises, "Mrs. Baxter laid down her embroidery" [001-010] and begins a defense that reveals the limits of her moral perception. Maggie observes that "neither the boy himself nor Mrs. Baxter in the least seemed to realize the astonishing selfishness of this very boy's actions" [001-027]. The embroidery is a telling detail — Mrs. Baxter processes moral disturbance through the consolations of domestic routine, and her judgments are shaped more by maternal love than by moral principle.

She hopes for a marriage between Laurie and Maggie, and her matchmaking instincts are among her more perceptive qualities. She recognizes Maggie's worth — "her own adopted daughter" [001-023] — and wishes to secure her permanently within the family. This hope makes her, in her own way, an ally of the novel's resolution, even if she does not fully understand the spiritual dimensions of what has occurred.

When Laurie becomes entangled in spiritualism, Mrs. Baxter's response is characteristically inadequate. She worries, she frets, she talks to Maggie, but she lacks the theological framework to name the danger or the moral authority to intervene. The "rough outline" [019-004] she later receives of events suggests that she remains at the periphery of the spiritual drama, a loving but ineffectual witness.

## Character Traits

Mrs. Baxter embodies a particular type of Victorian-Anglican matriarch: delicate, devout, domestically competent, and morally well-intentioned but fundamentally unequipped for spiritual warfare. Her physical delicacy — "a small, delicate-looking old lady" [001-002] — mirrors her spiritual fragility. She is kind, genuinely loving, and incapable of the kind of hard moral perception that the Catholic characters exercise. She is not a hypocrite — her faith is real — but it is a faith of comfort rather than contestation.

## Relationships

- **Laurie Baxter**: Her beloved son, whose spiritual danger she senses but cannot name
- **Maggie Deronnais**: Her adopted daughter, whom she loves and hopes to see married to Laurie
- **Lady Laura Bethell**: A family friend whose spiritualist enthusiasm Mrs. Baxter neither shares nor effectively opposes

## Significance

Mrs. Baxter is the novel's portrait of the Anglican via media — the middle way that, in Benson's Catholic perspective, provides no defense against spiritual evil. Her piety is sincere but insufficient; her love is deep but undiscerning; her distress is real but impotent. She is not condemned by the narrative — she is pitied as a representative of a Christianity that has preserved the forms of devotion while losing the substance of spiritual authority.
