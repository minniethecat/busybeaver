## Overview

Conversion — specifically Laurie Baxter's conversion to Catholicism — is a foundational fact in *The Necromancers*. It precedes the novel's action but shapes every relationship within it.

His mother approved of his conversion "to Catholicism" [001-029] yet doubted his motives were pure, hoping the Church might effect "a moral conversion, as well as an artistic and intellectual change of view." The novel treats conversion not as a settled event but as an unstable condition, and it uses the figure of the convert to explore the gap between intellectual assent and moral transformation.

## Laurie's Conversion

Laurie's conversion is treated by other characters with ambivalence. His mother distinguishes between intellectual assent and moral transformation — Laurie has achieved the former but not, she fears, the latter. Mrs. Baxter had "approved of his conversion to Catholicism, yet she was not sure that it was more than an artistic and intellectual change of view" [001-029]. The phrase "these converts" recurs as a pejorative: "What strange people these converts were! There was so much ardor at one time, so much chilliness at another" [002-008]. The convert's instability — his tendency toward "some novelty or other" [010-088] — makes him vulnerable to spiritualist seduction.

## The Unfinished Conversion

The novel suggests that Laurie's conversion is incomplete. He has embraced Catholic ideas but not Catholic habits of mind. His grief over Amy Nugent reveals the gap: rather than entrusting her to prayer and the communion of saints, he seeks her through the medium's table. The spiritualist episode is thus framed as a detour in a conversion that has not yet reached the will.

## Converts as a Social Type

Benson's novel presents the convert as a recognizable social type, viewed with suspicion by both Catholics and non-Catholics alike. Mrs. Baxter, though a Catholic herself, regards her son's convert status with unease: "What strange people these converts were! There was so much ardor at one time, so much chilliness at another; there was so little of that steady workaday acceptance of religious facts that marked the born Catholic" [002-008]. The convert is perceived as emotionally volatile, prone to extremes of enthusiasm and indifference that the "born Catholic" does not exhibit.

This typology extends beyond the Baxter household. Maggie Deronnais, herself a Catholic from birth, "found converts a little trying" [005-090], sensing in them an "inevitable atmosphere" of performance. The novel's English setting sharpens the critique: "English converts were indescribably trying" [010-051], their zeal marked by a particularly national form of awkwardness. The convert, in Benson's rendering, is never quite at home — too Catholic for Protestant England, too freshly minted for the Catholic old guard.

## The State of Religious Unreality

Benson names a specific psychological condition that afflicts the recent convert: "that state of religious unreality which occasionally comes upon converts within a year or two of the change of their faith. The impetus of old association is absent, and the force of novelty has died" [002-198]. This diagnosis is crucial to the novel's moral architecture. Laurie has had Mass said for Amy's soul, but "by almost a superstitious rather than a religious instinct" [002-198] — his Catholic practice has become mechanical, a shell emptied of the conviction that once animated it.

This religious unreality makes Laurie susceptible to spiritualism. When Mrs. Stapleton speaks of communicating with the dead, Laurie's "train of thought" is ignited not by belief but by the vacuum left by belief's retreat [002-198]. The séance becomes a substitute for sacrament — a corrupted form of the communion of saints that his conversion should have taught him to trust.
