## Overview

The Baker Street séances are the central supernatural events of *The Necromancers*, conducted by the professional medium Mr. Vincent at his London establishment. These gatherings, which Laurie Baxter attends at the invitation of Lady Laura, represent the novel's dramatization of Edwardian spiritualist practice and serve as the primary vector through which Laurie becomes entangled in spiritual danger.

## Prelude

Laurie's introduction to the séance circle comes through Lady Laura, a family connection who "reminded him of the séance to be held in Baker Street" [006-006]. His interest is presented as initially intellectual — a Catholic interested in the boundaries between orthodox and heterodox spirituality — but his emotional vulnerability following Amy Nugent's death makes him more than a detached observer.

## The Event

Mr. Vincent's séances follow established spiritualist protocol. The medium operates from fixed premises: his Baker Street rooms serve as both residence and séance venue. The atmosphere is professional rather than theatrical, with the séance room described as "singularly unsuggestive of supernatural mystery" [004-001] — a deliberate contrast that makes the phenomena more unsettling.

The medium enters a trance state during which, according to the spiritualist framework, spirits speak through him. The novel describes the mechanism in clinical terms: "the personality of the medium affects the vehicle through which the messages come" [002-040]. Mr. Vincent explains that "sometimes they gain control of the medium who is in a trance, and speak through him" [002-086], describing a process in which the medium's consciousness is displaced by an external intelligence.

The séances are held on a regular schedule, including "the Sunday séances" [007-010] which become a recurring feature of Laurie's weekly routine. The gatherings have a social dimension — participants include various London figures interested in spiritualist phenomena — lending them an air of fashionable respectability.

Laurie's experience evolves from curiosity to deeper involvement. As he becomes a regular, the medium begins to take a personal interest in him. Father Mahon, learning of Laurie's attendance, becomes concerned. The priest's intervention reflects the Catholic view that even well-intentioned participation in séances exposes one to spiritual danger.

## Aftermath

The séances contribute directly to Laurie's spiritual crisis. The manifestations he experiences — "the appearances Laurie saw" [019-011] — are interpreted by the Catholic characters as demonic phenomena masked as spirit communication. Mr. Cathcart articulates the theological interpretation: at genuine séances, "it's the devil" [019-030] who answers, not the dead.

The medium eventually advises Laurie to step back: "you had better not come to the public séances at present" [007-008]. This withdrawal, however, comes too late — the spiritual damage is already done. The séances have opened a door that requires sacramental intervention to close.

## Significance

The Baker Street séances represent the novel's central cautionary tableau. Benson presents spiritualism not as fraud but as genuine — and therefore more dangerous. The séance room becomes a site of spiritual infection, where the desire to contact the dead exposes the living to forces they cannot control. The contrast between Mr. Vincent's professional respectability and the demonic reality beneath structures the novel's moral argument.
