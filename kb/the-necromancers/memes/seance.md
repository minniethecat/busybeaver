## Overview

The séance is the dramatic center of *The Necromancers*. It is the mechanism by which Laurie Baxter seeks contact with the dead Amy Nugent, and the ritual that the novel's Catholic framework condemns as a dangerous counterfeit of prayer. Séances occur both publicly in Baker Street and privately at Lady Laura's house, each setting intensifying the novel's argument about the peril of spiritualist practice. The novel presents séances not as harmless curiosities but as spiritual traps that exploit the bereaved.

## The Ritual

A séance involves a small group seated around a table, often in dim light, attempting to communicate with spirits through a medium. In the novel, Mr. Vincent serves as the medium, and the séances are described as occasions of heightened emotion and psychological manipulation. The medium's "powers" are spoken of in terms that blend theatricality with apparent sincerity [003-008]. During séances, the participants sit in a circle — Laurie observes that Mrs. Stapleton watched the proceedings "with the same strange interest as the medium" [004-122], suggesting an atmosphere in which even the ostensibly sceptical are drawn in.

The novel portrays the séance as a structured ritual. "At the beginning of the séance the medium had repeated his warnings against levity, and his recommendations of a 'calm and reverent attitude'" [013-018]. This framing — part scientific demonstration, part religious ceremony — is central to the novel's critique: spiritualism borrows the forms of genuine religious practice while inverting their purpose. Rather than humbly petitioning God, the séance "deliberately approach[es] those powers in a friendly way" [019-030].

## Public and Private Séances

The novel distinguishes between "public séances" [007-008] and private gatherings. The public séances in Baker Street are large enough to be semi-anonymous; Lady Laura reminds Laurie of "the séance to be held in Baker Street" [006-006] as if it were a social engagement. Laurie himself attends "some public séances" [011-035], but they leave him unimpressed. "The public séances had not impressed him" [011-096] — the large-group setting, with its theatrical character, fails to provide the personal hook the medium needs.

The private séances at Lady Laura's house, by contrast, are intimate affairs where the medium can target individual vulnerabilities. It is at the private séances that Laurie has his most disturbing experiences. The medium suggests Laurie should not attend public séances "at present" [007-008] — a tactic that isolates him and deepens his dependence. The Sunday séances in particular are a fixture: "the Sunday séances will have to cease for the present" [007-010] is said as a temporary measure, but the rhythm of weekly attendance is itself a form of habituation.

## The Danger

The novel's position on séances is unequivocal. Mr. Cathcart articulates the central warning: "At séances, if they're genuine… you deliberately approach those powers in a friendly way, and by the sort of passivity which you've got to get yourself into, you open yourself as widely as possible" [019-030]. The passivity required for participation is understood as a relinquishment of rational and moral agency. The séance-goer does not actively seek truth; he opens himself, passively, to whatever force presents itself.

The novel's restrained tone makes its warnings more chilling. "I have seen people very much upset at séances, more than once" [008-013] — the understatement is characteristic, but the pattern of harm is clear. The séance is presented as a gateway: "one visit more or less to a séance would carry any great harm" [014-004] is the rationalisation of the already-hooked, and the novel treats such thinking as self-deception. "To go to séances with good intentions is like" [019-029] — the sentence is left incomplete in the text, but the implication is that no good intention can sanctify the act.

After Laurie's most intense séance, Mr. Vincent comes "to see how he was after the séance" [014-041]. The medium's solicitousness reads, in context, as predatory — the wolf checking on the sheep. Laurie reports "that the séance had been a success" [014-043], but the novel's moral framework insists that a successful séance is a successful act of spiritual violence.

## Laurie's Séances

Laurie's attendance at séances follows a pattern of deepening involvement. He begins with curiosity, continues with the hook of a message from Amy, and ends in a state of spiritual crisis. The séance scheduled for "Sunday evening next" [011-096] represents his point of no return — a convergence of psychological pressure and spiritual peril. His diary records with "very neat" precision "two days before the séance — the entry of Morton — his own state of mind" [011-039], evidence of a rational mind attempting to track its own undoing.

The novel makes clear that Laurie is not merely an unlucky participant but a targeted victim. He sits "on one side" of the table while "the medium [sits] on the other" [004-100] — a physical arrangement that mirrors the spiritual polarity the novel constructs. The medium's question — "You are sure, Mr. Baxter, that you are not at all nervous?" [004-094] — is the kind of faux-solicitous inquiry that simultaneously diagnoses and creates the vulnerability it pretends to address.

## Aftermath

The effects of séance attendance do not end when the circle breaks. Laurie is described as feeling "tired" after one séance [014-043], but the fatigue is spiritual as much as physical. The novel traces a trajectory from fatigue to obsession to spiritual crisis, with each séance drawing the participant deeper. The medium, having established influence, extends his reach: Laurie is told he should rest "a little longer" [004-204], his recovery managed and monitored by the very man who caused his distress.

The séance thus functions in the novel as what Catholic moral theology would call a "proximate occasion of sin" — a situation which, by its nature, places the soul in peril. The novel does not merely describe séances; it anatomizes them as instruments of spiritual predation, and Laurie Baxter's story becomes a cautionary tale of what happens when grief is exploited by those who claim to bridge the gap between the living and the dead.
