## Overview

The trance state is the operational mechanism of mediumship in *The Necromancers* — the altered consciousness through which Mr. Vincent claims spirits communicate with the living. In the novel's Catholic critique of spiritualism, the trance represents the point of maximum spiritual vulnerability: the moment when the medium's will is suspended and an external intelligence gains access. Benson presents the trance not as fraud or psychological dissociation but as a genuine altered state with genuine spiritual consequences.

## In the Novel

The trance is first described in detail during Laurie Baxter's initial private sitting with Mr. Vincent. The medium explains the process clinically: "sometimes they gain control of the medium who is in a trance, and speak through him, or write through his hand" [002-086]. This description presents the trance as instrumental — the medium is a vehicle, and the trance is the condition under which the vehicle becomes available to external operators.

Laurie's own experience of trance is carefully documented. At his first session, the medium notes: "You passed into trance about five minutes past six" [004-150], describing it as "a very deep and satisfactory trance. There is nothing to be frightened of" [004-152]. The reassurance is characteristic of Mr. Vincent's professional manner, but the narrative context undercuts it. The trance state is presented as perilous precisely because the subject's will is suspended. "You nearly passed into trance a little earlier" [004-154], the medium observes, indicating that the boundary between normal consciousness and trance is permeable and unpredictable.

Lady Laura Bethell, present at the session, "sat as one in trance herself" [004-209], blurring the line between mediumistic trance and fascinated spectatorship. The novel suggests that the trance state is contagious — that exposure to it alters the consciousness of observers as well as the medium.

The theological danger crystallizes when Father Mahon learns of Laurie's involvement. The medium later acknowledges concern: Laurie "must not be allowed to go into trance if it could be prevented. It was dangerous" [007-053]. Mr. Vincent's own warning, coming from within the spiritualist framework, carries ironic weight: even the medium recognizes that the trance state exposes the subject to forces beyond control.

The Catholic interpretation reframes the trance entirely. Mr. Cathcart explains that evil spirits seek "to get possession of human beings and move them about like pieces on a chessboard" [019-034], and the trance is the doorway through which they enter. "Very often they can't get in" [019-030] without such an opening, but the trance — a willful relinquishment of conscious control — provides exactly that. In contrast, the disciplined formation Maggie received at The Convent specifically inoculates against such vulnerability — a contrast the novel draws between the openness of trance and the enclosure of Catholic education.

## Context

The phenomenon of mediumistic trance was widely discussed in late Victorian and Edwardian psychical research. Figures like Frederic Myers and the Society for Psychical Research approached trance states as psychological phenomena worthy of scientific investigation, while the Catholic Church condemned them as occasions of demonic influence. Benson's portrayal draws on both discourses: the clinical description of trance induction and the theological warning about its spiritual consequences.

## Character Connections

- **Father Mahon**: The priest who identifies trance states as spiritually dangerous

## Significance

The trance is the novel's central metaphor for spiritual vulnerability. To enter trance is to abdicate the rational will — the faculty that Catholic moral theology identifies as essential to moral agency. Benson's portrayal insists that the trance is not neutral: it is a state that something fills, and what fills it depends on the context in which it is induced. The medium's "deep and satisfactory trance" and the priest's warning about possession are two descriptions of the same phenomenon from incommensurable frameworks, and the novel's dramatic structure vindicates the priest's. The eventual undoing of trance's influence — Laurie's gradual awakening and return — is documented in the events surrounding Laurie's Return.
