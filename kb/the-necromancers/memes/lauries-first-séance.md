[[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]]'s first séance is the novel's central threshold event: the moment at which the protagonist passes from detached skepticism to experiential entanglement with spiritualist phenomena. Spanning three chapters, the event is carefully structured as a sequence of approach, resistance, capitulation, and aftermath — a narrative arc that mirrors the psychological arc of seduction and conversion that the novel anatomizes.

## Prelude: The Nervous Letter

The approach begins with a piece of correspondence. "When therefore he received a nervous little note from Lady Laura, reminding him of the séance to be held in Baker Street, and begging his attendance, he wrote a most proper letter back again, thanking her for her kindness, but saying that he had come to the conclusion that this kind of thing was not good for him or his work" [006-006]. Laurie's refusal is carefully reasoned, polite, and final — or so he believes.

Lady Laura writes again, and again he refuses. The pattern of refusal is essential to the séance's narrative function: Laurie does not drift passively into the experience; he is pursued, and his eventual consent is the product of a strategic campaign waged by [[mr-vincent|Mr. Vincent]], the medium whose professional interest in Laurie's "sensitivity" overrides all social niceties.

## Mr. Vincent's Visit

The campaign intensifies when Mr. Vincent calls on Laurie in person one evening. Laurie's rooms in Mitre Court — "two rooms opening into one another, and communicating with the staircase" [006-009] — provide a setting of masculine domesticity that Vincent's presence immediately destabilizes. The medium enters unannounced: "I beg your pardon, Mr. Baxter; the porter's boy told me to come straight up. I found your outer door open" [006-011].

Vincent's method is not persuasion but demonstration. When Laurie rehearses his rationalist objections — hypnotic sleep, the subconscious self, the absence of conclusive proof — Vincent listens patiently and then produces his notebook. From it he reads a sentence uttered by Laurie during his trance at Queen's Gate: "Tell Laurie that the ground has sunk a little above my grave; and that cracks are showing at the sides" [006-049].

The effect on Laurie is immediate and physical. "What is that book?" he demands hoarsely [006-050]. Vincent's reply is serenely procedural: the notebook contains "a few extracts from some of the things you said during your trance" [006-052], transcribed by [[mrs-stapleton|Mrs. Stapleton]]. The verification is simple, brutal, and irrefutable: Laurie has not seen the grave since he left the country in September, and no one has mentioned it to him. The information, if correct, can only have come from a source beyond his conscious knowledge.

## The Séance Night

The séance itself takes place on a Sunday evening at Lady Laura's house. Laurie arrives at half-past eight and is shown into the dining room, where "the table was still littered with dessert-plates and napkins" [008-003]. The domestic detail is deliberate — the ordinariness of the setting heightens the strangeness of what is to come.

[[lady-laura-bethell|Lady Laura]] intercepts him before the séance begins, visibly anxious: "I felt I must just speak to you. I wasn't sure whether you quite realized the dangers of all this" [008-008]. When Laurie presses her to explain what she means by "risks," she can only gesture vaguely: "There are always risks, you know… nerves, and so on. I have seen people very much upset at séances, more than once" [008-011, 008-013].

Laurie's response is a performance of bravado that the reader recognizes as hollow: "I'm ashamed to say that, if anything, I'm rather bored" [008-015]. Lady Laura's reply — "You know there are other risks" [008-020] — hangs in the air, unexplained and ominous.

## The Medium's Apparatus

Before the séance proper, Mr. Vincent shows Laurie his collection of spiritualist instruments in what amounts to a private induction into the medium's craft. There is "a curious windmill-shaped affair, its sails lined with looking-glass" [007-120] — a hypnotic induction device — and a planchette, "a tiny heart-shaped table on three wheeled legs" through which "ran a pencil perpendicularly of which the point just touched the tablecloth" [007-129].

The medium's manner during this tour is professional rather than mystical. "It's a little machine for inducing sleep," he explains of the windmill device [007-125], and when Laurie looks dazed, Vincent smiles: "Why, you're looking dazed yourself, already, Mr. Baxter" [007-125]. The effect is of a man who regards his apparatus with the pragmatic detachment of a scientist — an impression that the narrator will later complicate.

## The Experience

The séance experience itself is described through Laurie's altered perception. The room becomes "extraordinarily vivid and clear-cut" [007-082]; the firelight "wavered and sank again in billows of soft color about the shadowed walls" [007-082]. Crucially, Laurie realizes that "he saw no longer with eyes, but with that faculty of perception to which sight is only analogous — that faculty which underlies and is common to all the senses alike" [007-082].

This is the novel's most explicit description of the spiritualist sensorium: a mode of perception that bypasses the physical senses and accesses reality through a medium — in both senses of the word — that the rationalist framework cannot account for. Laurie's first séance is thus not merely an event but an epistemological rupture.

## Aftermath

When the séance ends, Laurie is described as feeling "tired" [014-043]. "He said that the séance had been a success" [014-043] — the blandness of the report is characteristic of the novel's restraint, but the reader understands that "success" here means the destruction of Laurie's rationalist defenses. He has seen something unmistakable, and the novel's remaining chapters trace the consequences.

The medium, for his part, has achieved his objective. He had told Lady Laura that Laurie "should see something unmistakable as soon as possible" [007-032], and he has delivered. Whether the delivery was spiritual or theatrical — and the novel maintains an ambiguity on this point — is less important than its effect: Laurie Baxter will never again be able to dismiss séances as harmless diversions.

## Significance

Laurie's first séance functions as the novel's conversion narrative in reverse. Where a religious conversion moves from doubt to belief through grace, Laurie's séance experience moves from skepticism to a state that is neither belief nor disbelief but a third condition — experiential entanglement — in which the question of whether spiritualist phenomena are "real" becomes secondary to the question of what they do to the person who experiences them. The séance is thus the engine that drives the novel's remaining action; everything that follows — Laurie's spiritual crisis, his interviews with Father Mahon, his eventual return — is a consequence of what happened in that room.
