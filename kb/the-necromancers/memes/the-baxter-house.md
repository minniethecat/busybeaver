## Overview

The Baxter house, the family's country estate in Hertfordshire, is the novel's primary domestic setting. It serves as the physical embodiment of Mrs. Baxter's world — a space of exquisite taste, Anglican piety, and social order — against which the disruptions of Laurie's Catholicism, his romance with Amy Nugent, and his spiritualist involvements are measured. The house's drawing room, in particular, functions as a stage for the novel's key domestic conversations.

## Description

The house reflects Mrs. Baxter's personality with unusual precision. The narrator describes the drawing room as mirroring her character: "In spite of the early Victorian date of the furniture, there was in its arrangement and selection a taste so exquisite as to deprive it of even a suspicion of Philistinism" [001-003].

Key features include a "rosewood table on which the September morning sun fell with serene beauty" [001-003], Tudor paneling that contrasts unexpectedly but harmoniously with the Victorian furnishings, and "a tapestry screen veiled the door into the hall" [001-003]. Windows lead to the garden, hung with "soft curtains of velvety gold" [001-003]. The room is furnished with "low chairs, a tapestry couch, a multitude of little leather-covered books on every table, and two low carved bookshelves on either side of the door filled with poetry and devotion" [001-003].

The hall provides access to the upper floors: "the gentle hall gave entrance to the drawing room on the right" [001-057], and stairs lead to Laurie's rooms above. His quarters on the first floor are described as "a big place, with two rooms" [002-130], a space that reflects his personality — larger, more dramatic, and less ordered than his mother's domain below.

## Role in the Story

The house functions as a symbolic space where the novel's conflicts are enacted. The drawing room is where Mrs. Baxter and Maggie discuss Laurie's situation while he stands at Amy Nugent's grave — the contrast between domestic comfort and mortal grief is deliberately stark.

It is here that Father Mahon arrives in the middle of the night to administer the last rites, transforming the domestic space into a site of sacramental drama. The house becomes a battlefield: "It was as pathetic as an amiable cat trying to go to sleep on a rifle range; she was developing the jumps" [001-021], the narrator observes of Mrs. Baxter, capturing the sense of domestic peace under perpetual threat.

## Atmosphere

Benson gives the house a distinctive atmosphere of piety and refinement. Mrs. Baxter carries "about with her always an atmosphere of piety, humble, tender, and sincere, but as persistent as the gentle sandalwood aroma which breathed from her dress" [001-002]. This sensory detail — piety as fragrance — extends to the house itself, whose "sanctuary of Mrs. Baxter's prayers" [010-002] suggests a domestic religion as carefully maintained as the furniture.

The garden serves as a liminal space — it is where Laurie brought Amy Nugent, unwilling to bring her indoors, and where characters retreat for conversations that cannot happen in the formality of the drawing room.
