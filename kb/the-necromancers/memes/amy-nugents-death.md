## Overview

The death of Amy Nugent is the pivotal event of *The Necromancers*, occurring before the novel's main action begins and setting its entire plot in motion. Amy, the "little and fair-haired" [001-013] daughter of a village grocer and a Baptist, was Laurie Baxter's improbable love interest. Her sudden death — occurring on a Thursday while Laurie was away — shatters him and creates the emotional vulnerability that draws him into the spiritualist séances of Mr. Vincent.

## Prelude

Amy Nugent's relationship with Laurie developed over the summer preceding the novel's opening. Laurie "had met, spoken to, and ultimately kissed this Amy Nugent, on a certain summer evening as the stars came out" [001-024]. Despite her humble origins — her father was a grocer and a Baptist — Laurie's feelings were genuine: "with a chivalry not so common in such cases, had also sincerely and simply fallen in love with her" [001-024].

The relationship met fierce opposition from Mrs. Baxter, who found the match socially impossible. She describes Amy as "like a picture on a chocolate-box" [001-013], dismissing her appearance and noting her lower-class accent: "she left out an aitch, and then she said the word over again quite right" [001-013]. The class tension between the Baxter estate and the village shop is explicit and painful.

## The Event

Amy's death — the novel never specifies the exact cause — occurs on a Thursday. Mrs. Baxter, recounting the event to Maggie, says that Laurie "was away from home, you know, on Thursday, when it happened; but he was back here on Friday, and has been like — like a madman ever since" [001-009]. The temporal detail — Laurie was absent when she died — carries symbolic weight: he was not there for her in her final moments.

The funeral takes place on the day the novel opens. Mrs. Baxter and Maggie sit in the drawing room discussing the situation while Laurie, "in a black suit, rent by sorrow, at this very hour at which the two ladies sat and talked in the drawing room, was standing by an open grave in the village churchyard" [001-025]. The narrative juxtaposition — the drawing room conversation and the graveside scene — emphasizes the distance between Laurie's private grief and his mother's social calculations.

Amy is buried "under a pile of blossoms as pink and white as her own complexion, within four elm-boards with a brass plate upon the cover" [001-025]. The description mingles sentiment and finality in the novel's characteristic manner.

## Aftermath

Amy's death reshapes every relationship in the novel. For Mrs. Baxter, it is a providential deliverance from an unsuitable daughter-in-law: "When I first heard of the poor girl's death, it seemed to me so providential" [001-009]. For Laurie, it is devastation that neither his mother's comfort nor his Catholic faith adequately addresses.

His grief drives him toward spiritualism — the desire to contact Amy's spirit becomes entangled with the séance circle's promises. The medium reports making contact with "the disembodied spirit of a certain 'Amy Nugent'" [007-053]. In a critical scene, Laurie believes he sees "the face of Amy Nugent four months after her death" [009-023] during a séance, an experience that binds him further to the medium's circle.

Maggie Deronnais's retrospective assessment is measured. She reflects that it was "really and truly, poor dear Amy Nugent who led Laurie such a dance" [019-022], acknowledging Amy's agency while maintaining sympathy for her fate. The epilogue reveals that Laurie "had had a Mass said for Amy Nugent" [010-084], suggesting that his Catholic faith ultimately provides a healthier channel for his grief than the séances offered.

## Significance

Amy's death functions as the novel's narrative engine and its moral test case. It reveals the selfishness beneath Laurie's romanticism, the social prejudices of Mrs. Baxter, and the inadequacy of spiritualism as a response to genuine grief. The Catholic alternative — prayer for the dead, the Mass — is presented as the authentic means of honoring the deceased, in contrast to the séance's attempt to summon them.
