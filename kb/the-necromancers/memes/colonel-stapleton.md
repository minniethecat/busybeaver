# Colonel Stapleton

**Colonel Tom Stapleton** is the husband of [[mrs-stapleton|Mrs. Stapleton]], the enthusiastic spiritualist and New Thought devotee who plays a significant role in drawing [[laurie-baxter|Laurie Baxter]] into the world of séances.

## "Dear Tom"

Mrs. Stapleton refers to her husband as "dear Tom," and what little we learn of him comes filtered through her perspective and the narrator's sardonic asides. He is a military man — a colonel — and we are told that his wife "kept 'dear Tom,' the Colonel, not crushed or beaten, for that was beyond the power of man to do, but at least silently acquiescent in her program" ([003-015]). The formulation is telling: the Colonel is not broken by his wife's spiritualist enthusiasms, but he has learned to keep quiet about them.

He allows his wife "to entertain her prophetical friends at his expense, now and then" and "even when among men, refrained from too bitter speech" ([003-015]). The portrait is of a man who has made his peace with a situation he finds faintly ridiculous — the practical soldier married to a woman who communes with spirits and wears emblematic jewelry.

## Absence and Presence

The Colonel is strategically absent for most of the novel's action. During the autumn when Mrs. Stapleton first visits [[stantons|Stantons]] and introduces Laurie to [[mr-vincent|Mr. Vincent]], the Colonel is in Scotland. His absence enables his wife's "refreshment of a week or two among the illuminated" ([003-015]). By Christmas, however, he has returned, and "his wife's duties had recalled her for the present from those spiritual conversations which she had enjoyed in the autumn" ([007-003]).

The implication is clear: the Colonel's presence is a brake on his wife's spiritualist activities. When he is home, Mrs. Stapleton must attend to domestic duties. When he is away, she slips into "the purer atmosphere" of mediums and messages from the Other Side.

## Narrative Role

Colonel Stapleton is a minor but thematically significant figure. Like [[mr-jamieson|Mr. Jamieson]], the young Anglican clergyman who nervously attends a séance and quickly retreats, the Colonel represents the sensible, establishment Englishman who knows that spiritualism is nonsense but lacks the theological framework to explain why.

His silence in the face of his wife's enthusiasms is not weakness but a kind of weary wisdom. He has learned, as the novel seems to suggest, that some battles are not worth fighting — that a woman who believes Cardinal Newman attends her friend's drawing room cannot be argued back to reason. The Colonel's strategy is simple: be present when possible, absent when necessary, and "refrain from too bitter speech" at all times.

The narrator notes that "it was said by the Colonel's friends that Mrs. Colonel had a tongue of her own" ([003-015]) — a wry acknowledgment that the Colonel's silence may be less philosophical than practical. In the Stapleton household, the gender dynamics of Victorian marriage intersect with the epistemological chaos of spiritualism, and the Colonel has chosen the path of least resistance.
