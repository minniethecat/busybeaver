## Overview

Amy Nugent is the absent center of *The Necromancers* — a character who dies before the novel's action begins but whose death shapes everything that follows. The Baptist grocer's daughter with whom Laurie Baxter fell in love, Amy is described as "possessed of every grace of body, mind, and soul" [001-024], and her sudden death, occurring on a day Laurie was away, leaves him with unresolved grief that the spiritualists exploit. She appears in the novel only through memory, séance, and the physical trace of her family's shop — a presence constituted entirely by absence.

## Background

Amy is the daughter of Amos and Maria Nugent, who keep a grocer's shop bearing the name "Nugent" in "capitals as bright and flaring" [005-032] as their class position is unambiguous. The relationship between Laurie, the Catholic gentleman, and Amy, the Baptist grocer's daughter, was socially asymmetrical from the start. Laurie's mother regarded "this final mad episode of Amy Nugent" [001-029] as a romantic folly, and Maggie Deronnais recognized "the astonishing selfishness" of Laurie's pursuit. The class dimension is never moralized but always present: Laurie's love was genuine, but his capacity to act on it with honor was limited by the social gap between them.

Amy's death is recounted as a sudden event: "on a certain summer evening" [001-024] Laurie kissed her, and shortly thereafter she died — "symbolically occurring on the day Laurie was away." The novel declines to specify the cause of death, treating it as brute fact rather than narrative event. What matters is the rupture it creates in Laurie's emotional life.

## Role in the Story

Amy's narrative function is to be the object of grief that makes Laurie vulnerable. The séance medium channels "the purified spirit of a certain 'Amy Nugent.' His communication with her" [007-053] is the bait that draws Laurie deeper into spiritualist practice. The medium asks whether Laurie was ever "questioned gave the name of Amy Nugent" [004-174] — the dead beloved's name is weaponized, converted from memory into leverage.

The Nugent shop itself becomes a narrative presence. Laurie "did not glance out at Mr. Nugent's shop, but it was of no use" [006-081] — the shop is unavoidable, a physical reminder of what he has lost. The name in bright capitals is a judgment on Laurie's failure to honor the girl who bore it. Maggie, visiting the Nugent establishment, observes the parents — "Amos and Maria Nugent" [005-041] — whose grief is the novel's quiet counterpoint to Laurie's more demonstrative sorrow.

The epilogue reports Maggie's retrospective assessment: "poor dear Amy Nugent who led Laurie such a dance" [019-022]. The phrase "led Laurie such a dance" is ambiguous — was Amy the agent of Laurie's trouble, or its occasion? The novel leaves the question open, but the Catholic framework suggests that the danger lay not in Amy herself but in Laurie's disordered attachment to her memory.

## Character Traits

Amy is characterized almost entirely through others' descriptions — she has no dialogue, no scenes of her own. She is "possessed of every grace" and represented as beautiful, sincere, and socially vulnerable. The novel's refusal to give her interiority is itself a narrative choice: Amy exists in the novel only as she exists in Laurie's memory, which is to say, as an image distorted by desire and grief.

## Significance

Amy Nugent is the novel's case study in a theological category: the beloved dead who becomes, through disordered grief, an occasion of spiritual danger. She is not responsible for what happens to Laurie — the novel is careful not to blame her — but her absence creates the void into which spiritualism pours its promises. The Catholic doctrine of prayer for the dead, mentioned in passing, is the novel's implicit alternative: ordered remembrance within the Church's communion of saints, as against the spiritualist attempt to breach the barrier between living and dead.
