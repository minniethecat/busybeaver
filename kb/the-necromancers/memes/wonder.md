## Overview

Wonder in *The Necromancers* occupies a specific theological position: it is the proper response to the genuinely supernatural, as distinct from the false marvels of spiritualism. Catholic thought has long treated wonder (*admiratio*) as the beginning of philosophy and, in its higher form, as the soul's response to divine action. The novel tracks wonder as it appears in characters' faces and voices, treating it as an involuntary witness to the real — a sign that something beyond nature has entered the room.

## In the Novel

Laurie's memory of Amy's face provides the novel's emblematic image of wonder. He remembered her "alight with love and fear and sudden wonder" [001-068], and the triad is significant: love, fear, and wonder compounded in a single expression. Wonder here is not a detached intellectual response but an intensification of the whole person — love, fear, and wonder together suggest an encounter too full for any single emotion to contain it.

The novel distinguishes wonder from mere curiosity. Characters frequently "wondered" about practical matters — Maggie "wondered now and again as to the fate" [001-082] of those involved, and she "wondered what sorrow would do for him" [001-031] when observing Laurie. These are the ordinary operations of an inquiring mind. But the compound "love and fear and sudden wonder" is something different: an affective response to presence, not a cognitive response to absence of information.

The spiritualist phenomena provoke wonder in their witnesses, but the novel is careful to distinguish between authentic wonder and manipulated credulity. When Laurie encounters the medium's revelations, he is described as affected "with an overwhelming desire, that it is little wonder that he was affected by them" [002-202]. The phrase "little wonder" is subtly ironic — what appears as wonder is better described as desire finding its object, emotion authorizing belief rather than belief disciplining emotion.

Mr. Vincent, the spiritualist organizer, is described as "a model of dignity and grace, interiorly a raging, self-conscious" [005-057] personality — the appearance of dignified wonder at spiritual phenomena masks an ungoverned interior. Lady Laura, by contrast, wonders "what he wants" [003-025] when approached, a healthy skepticism that contrasts with the spiritualists' demand for uncritical openness.

## Context

In the Catholic intellectual tradition, wonder is the virtue that opens the mind to realities beyond its ordinary scope — it is the beginning of philosophy and, in its supernatural elevation, the beginning of contemplation. The *donum intellectus* (gift of understanding) is closely related to wonder: it is the capacity to see truths that reason cannot demonstrate. The novel implicitly invokes this tradition when it distinguishes the wonder on Amy's remembered face — involuntary, total, authentic — from the manufactured "wonder" that spiritualist performances aim to produce.
