## Overview

Sorrow in *The Necromancers* is not an episode but a condition — the atmosphere in which the novel's central characters live after Amy Nugent's death. Laurie Baxter wears sorrow in his face: his "clean-cut, rather passionate lips were set now, with lines" [001-065] etched by grief, and his features are "pulled in lines of grotesque sorrow" [001-071]. The novel treats sorrow as a test: what a character does with sorrow reveals the quality of their soul. Mrs. Baxter's sorrow is tender and helpless; Maggie's is composed and watchful; Laurie's is consuming and, in its intensity, spiritually dangerous.

## In the Novel

Laurie is introduced in his grief: he stands "in a black suit, rent by sorrow" [001-025] at the very hour of Amy's funeral. The phrase "rent by sorrow" suggests violence done to the self — sorrow does not merely affect Laurie but tears him open. The novel follows his sorrow through its phases: the acute pain of the funeral, the duller ache of return to ordinary life, and the dangerous resurgence of feeling that the séances provoke.

Mrs. Baxter sees Laurie's sorrow and is moved: "she saw that his agony of sorrow was acute beneath all his attempts" [002-179] at composure. The word "agony" is deliberate — sorrow as a kind of passion in the theological sense, a suffering undergone rather than an action chosen. Laurie's "crossness" is explained by the narrator as a symptom of sorrow. His heart was "stirred to its depths by his sorrow" [002-197], and the image suggests that sorrow has penetrated beyond the surface self into regions where it cannot be managed by will.

Maggie observes sorrow with the distance that her temperament provides. She "wondered what sorrow would do for him" [001-031] — the question is diagnostic rather than sympathetic, and it signals the novel's own analytical interest in sorrow. Sorrow, Maggie concludes, "would be his test" [001-032] — not a condition to be cured but a proving ground for the soul. Her respect for Laurie's sorrow is genuine: "She at least respected his sorrow, he told himself. She bore herself" [001-080] with a restraint that acknowledged its weight without trying to lift it.

The novel tracks sorrow's resolution, or its failure to resolve. Laurie recalls, "for the first time without sorrow since ten days ago" [004-110], a moment of relief that the séance provides. But the relief is temporary and purchased at a spiritual cost the novel makes clear. He begins "to recover himself after his sorrow" [005-007] through the spiritualist community rather than through the Church — a recovery that the novel's Catholic framework treats as illusory.

## Context

The Catholic tradition distinguishes between sorrow as an emotion (a passion of the soul) and sorrow as a spiritual condition. The *donum scientiae* (gift of knowledge) enables the sorrow that leads to repentance — sorrow directed toward sin rather than toward loss. Laurie's sorrow is directed at the wrong object: not at sin but at death, not toward God but toward a human beloved. It is natural sorrow, not spiritual sorrow, and its naturalness is precisely its limitation.
