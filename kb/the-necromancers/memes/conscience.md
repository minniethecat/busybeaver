## Overview

Conscience in *The Necromancers* functions as an internal moral compass that characters either follow or suppress. It appears most prominently in domestic and interpersonal contexts — moments of self-reproach about small deceits or failures of candor — rather than in the grand theological confrontations. The novel's conscience is Augustinian: an interior voice that cannot be silenced, only ignored at increasing psychological cost.

## In the Novel

Conscience first surfaces in a domestic scene: a character's "conscience smote her again, shrewdly" [005-077], a phrase that captures the piercing, uncomfortable quality of moral self-awareness. The adverb "shrewdly" suggests that conscience is intelligent — it knows exactly where to strike.

Laurie's "bad conscience" [010-030] about his engagement with spiritualism is observed by another character who notes he would have confessed sooner "or he would have mentioned it." Conscience here produces concealment rather than confession — an inversion of the expected dynamic that reveals Laurie's ambivalence about his own investigation.

In chapter 7, the medium claims that those "who have nothing on their conscience" [007-196] are better subjects for spirit communication — a claim that moralizes the séance in ways that complicate the spiritualist emphasis on neutral observation. If moral state affects spiritual receptivity, then the séance room is never the neutral laboratory its practitioners claim.

## Conscience and Concealment

One of the novel's most acute psychological observations is that conscience does not always produce confession. Laurie's "bad conscience" [010-030] about his involvement with spiritualism leads him to hide rather than disclose — he would have spoken "or he would have mentioned it," but the conscience that should prompt candor instead provokes evasion.

This pattern is theologically significant. In Catholic moral theology, conscience is ordered toward confession: the recognition of sin should lead to the sacrament of penance. Laurie's conscience short-circuits this sequence. He knows something is wrong, but instead of bringing that knowledge to a confessor, he buries it deeper. The séance, which Mr. Vincent compares to the confessional [004-185], becomes the substitute — a place where secrets are disclosed without absolution, where conscience speaks but is not healed.

## The Conscience of the Medium

The medium's claim about a clear conscience [007-196] introduces a criterion of moral fitness into spiritualist practice. If "those who have nothing on their conscience" make better subjects, then spiritualism — which styled itself as scientific investigation open to all — imposes a moral prerequisite that science would not recognize. The séance ceases to be a laboratory and becomes a kind of moral trial, where the state of one's soul determines the quality of the evidence one can gather.

## Significance

Conscience in *The Necromancers* is the counterweight to spiritual ambition. The novel's characters seek contact with the dead, knowledge of the beyond, proof of immortality — but conscience keeps pulling them back to the moral obligations of the living: honesty, candor, accountability. This tension between spiritual aspiration and moral obligation is one of the novel's most understated but persistent themes.
