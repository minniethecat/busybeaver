## Overview

Evil in *The Necromancers* is a contested concept. Spiritualist practitioners describe the phenomena they encounter as "not necessarily evil" [007-051]; Catholic observers, particularly Father Mahon, insist on the demonic hypothesis. The novel sustains this ambiguity, never conclusively settling whether the intelligence behind possession is morally neutral, morally evil, or something beyond human moral categories entirely.

## In the Novel

The debate crystallizes in chapter 10, when Mrs. Baxter asks directly: "Then--then that's the evil of it?" [010-062]. The reply she receives is qualified: "don't get thinking that the devil's in everything" [010-072]. When pressed further--"Then it's not the devil?"--the answer is again hesitant: "it's not likely to get mixed up with the devil in that way" [010-074, 010-076].

Father Mahon takes the opposite position. He "believes in the devil in quite an extraordinary way" [019-028], interpreting all spirit manifestations through a demonological framework. His perspective sharpens during the possession crisis: "the thing is wholly evil" [016-099], though he allows that exorcism may exploit the evil entity's own limitations: "it may, perhaps, be turned against itself" [016-099].

Maggie Deronnais arrives at a subtler understanding. She had "known evil only in its effects--in sins" [017-123], but the possession case forces her to confront evil as an active, intelligent force rather than a passive absence of good.

## The Banality of Evil in Spiritualist Practice

The novel's most unsettling insight about evil is that it operates through ordinary, respectable channels. The seance takes place in a comfortable drawing room, conducted by well-dressed, articulate people who speak of "science" and "evidence." Mr. Vincent is "a big man" [004-014] in broadcloth, not a sinister figure in a cloak. The medium Lady Laura is a woman of social standing. Evil in *The Necromancers* does not announce itself with melodramatic signs; it presents itself as reasonable inquiry.

This is the theological point the novel makes through indirection. When Mrs. Baxter nervously suggests the devil's involvement and is told "don't get thinking that the devil's in everything" [010-072], the dismissal sounds sensible. But the novel's Catholic framework treats this very sensibility as the danger: evil works best when it persuades its victims that they are not dealing with evil at all. The devil, as Father Mahon understands, does not need to announce himself -- "of course the devil could do extraordinary things" [010-087] -- and the most effective demonic strategy is to convince people that the demonic does not exist.

## The Chessboard Metaphor: Evil as Strategic Intelligence

When Father Mahon observes that the evil entity "may, perhaps, be turned against itself" [016-099], he articulates a view of evil as strategic intelligence rather than blind force. This is the traditional Catholic understanding of the demonic: a fallen but still intelligent will, capable of tactics and vulnerable to counter-tactics. The exorcist does not overpower evil but outmaneuvers it -- using its own nature against it.

This view contrasts sharply with the spiritualist position that phenomena are "not necessarily evil" [007-051] -- merely neutral forces that may be used for good or ill. The novel does not resolve this debate, but the weight of its sympathy lies with Father Mahon's position. Evil in the novel is personal, not impersonal; it intends harm, not merely causes it; and the appropriate response is not scientific investigation but spiritual combat.

## Context

The novel's treatment of evil reflects the theological currents of Edwardian Catholicism. Father Mahon's position echoes the traditional Catholic doctrine of personal evil--Satan as a fallen angel, demons as active agents. The spiritualist counter-position aligns with a more diffuse, theosophical view where discarnate intelligences may be misguided or ignorant rather than malevolent.

## Character Connections

- **Father Mahon** -- primary advocate of the demonic interpretation
- **Maggie Deronnais** -- evolves from abstract to experiential understanding of evil
- **Laurie Baxter** -- uncertain; his Protestant formation leaves him without the categories to process the question
- **Lady Laura Bethell** -- the medium, who warns against "mixing up" phenomena with the devil

## Significance

The "is it evil?" question is the novel's theological center. It locates *The Necromancers* within a broader Edwardian debate about the moral status of occult practice. Benson refuses to resolve the question, presenting both the Catholic position (formal demonology) and the spiritualist position (neutral energies) as intellectually defensible--and leaving the reader, like the characters, to weigh the evidence.
