# The Travelling Show

The Travelling Show is the itinerant Punch and Judy exhibition operated by [[codlin-and-short|Codlin and Short]], encountered by [[nell-trent|Nell]] and her grandfather early in their wanderings. The show — a portable puppet theatre carried on Codlin's back, with a set of wooden Punch figures and a brazen trumpet — embodies the precarious, hand-to-mouth existence of Victorian travelling entertainers, a marginal subculture Dickens knew intimately and depicted with affectionate precision.

## The Showmen

The show is a two-man operation. Short (Harris Trotters) leads the way, blowing a "brazen trumpet" to announce their arrival in each town and singing "a fragment of a song in that hilarious tone common to Punches and their consorts" [015-014]. Codlin follows behind, bearing the heavy puppet theatre on his back and cursing his fate — "and all the hollow things of earth (but Punch especially)" [015-014].

The Punch figure itself is a character in its own right. When Nell first sees it, "perched cross-legged upon a tombstone," the puppet's "long nose and chin, hooked extremities, and goggle eyes" give it a grotesque vitality [016-004]. The Grandfather, whose mind is failing, is immediately captivated: "Are you going to show 'em tonight? are you?" he asks eagerly [015-010].

## Encounter in the Churchyard

Nell and her grandfather first meet the showmen in a country churchyard where Codlin and Short have set down their theatre to rest. The juxtaposition is characteristically Dickensian: the sacred space of the dead, populated by the profane figure of Mr. Punch and his wooden companions. Nell observes the scene with her customary quiet perceptiveness, while her grandfather's enthusiasm reveals his increasingly childlike state of mind.

## Travels with the Show

The travellers join forces, and the party makes its way toward the race town. Along the road they observe "gipsy camps, carts laden with gambling booths and their appurtenances, itinerant showmen of various kinds, and beggars and trampers of every degree" — all streaming toward the same destination [018-001]. The journey culminates in the memorable gathering at [[the-jolly-sandboys|the Jolly Sandboys]] inn, where the Punch men mingle with performing dogs, a giant, a limbless lady, and a card-sharp, creating one of the novel's most vivid and crowded tableaux.

## Narrative Function

The travelling show serves a double narrative purpose. On the surface, it provides cover for Nell and her grandfather's flight — the showmen are "two poor travelling showmen" who unwittingly shelter the runaways [069-055]. More deeply, the show represents a liminal world between respectability and vagrancy, between art and commerce, between life and the mechanical imitation of life — themes that resonate throughout the novel. The Punch and Judy show, with its grotesque violence and carnival humour, is the shadow side of [[mrs-jarleys-waxwork|Mrs. Jarley's Waxwork]] with its "air of coldness and gentility."
