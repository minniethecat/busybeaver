## In the Novel

Progress in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] operates on multiple registers: the physical progress of Nell and her grandfather through the English landscape, the moral progress (or regress) of characters confronting their natures, and the social progress that the novel both celebrates and questions. The word "progress" recurs in transitional moments: "while the meal was in progress" (025-006), "while they were in progress, Mr. Quilp withdrew" (005-018), marking the narrative's self-consciousness about movement and change.

The journey itself is the novel's master trope for progress. Nell and her grandfather's "progress through the streets of the town" (028-005) mirrors a larger pilgrimage structure — each town, each encounter, each hardship marks a station on the way. As the narrator observes, "the harder the gusts, the better progress they appeared to make" (069-035), suggesting that adversity itself is the engine of forward movement, a characteristically Dickensian paradox in which suffering produces advancement.

Yet progress is also ironized in the novel. The grandfather's gambling is described as a "sickly eagerness" that drives him forward without actually advancing — a parody of progress in which motion becomes mere compulsion. Kit Nubbles, by contrast, "would at least have made very good progress towards that end" (049-010) in his modest ambition, embodying a form of advancement that is incremental, honest, and ultimately rewarded.

## Key Examples

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: Nell's physical progress across England charts her moral and spiritual development — each step away from London is a step toward an idealized pastoral death.
- **Nell's grandfather**: His gambling represents a false progress — a "progress" toward ruin that masquerades as advancement, its "sickly eagerness" (036-016) a corruption of genuine forward movement.
- **[[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]]**: Swiveller "progressing so favourably in his recovery" (066-078) marks one of the novel's few unambiguous instances of positive progress — a moral reformation figured as physical healing.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of progress is ambivalent, reflecting the contradictions of Victorian society itself. The Industrial Revolution promised material progress; the novel's landscapes of industrial blight and rural poverty expose the human cost. The railway, emblem of Victorian progress, appears in the novel as a site of danger and death — the grandfather "had no thought of the railway" (008-054) as he wanders, and Quilp's association with the wharf and its industrial apparatus aligns technological power with moral degradation.

Yet Dickens does not reject progress outright. Nell's journey, for all its suffering, is genuinely transformative. Kit's modest advancement in life is presented as wholly admirable. The novel distinguishes between the false progress of acquisition (the grandfather's gambling, Quilp's accumulation) and the true progress of moral growth and human connection.
