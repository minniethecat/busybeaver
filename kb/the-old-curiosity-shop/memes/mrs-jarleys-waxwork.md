# Mrs. Jarley's Waxwork

Mrs. Jarley's Waxwork is a travelling exhibition of wax figures that appears in the middle chapters of the novel, serving as both a place of refuge for [[nell-trent|Nell Trent]] and her grandfather and a symbolic representation of the blurred line between the living and the lifeless, the real and the artificial.

## In the Novel

Nell and her grandfather encounter [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]'s waxwork after fleeing London. The waxwork becomes the centre of their lives for a significant portion of their journey. Nell works for [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]] as a guide, pointing out the figures to visitors and reciting the catalogue descriptions [027-013]. The waxwork travels from town to town, a self-contained world on wheels that Dickens describes as "a smart little house upon wheels" [026-013].

The exhibition includes figures of historical personages, murderers, and celebrities, all rendered in wax and dressed in period costume. [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]] markets it as educational and morally uplifting: according to her promotional materials, "waxwork refined the mind, cultivated the taste, and enriched the heart" [028-036].

## Description

Dickens portrays the waxwork with a mixture of genuine wonder and ironic detachment. The figures are described as startlingly lifelike: "so like life, that if waxwork only spoke and walked about" it would be indistinguishable from reality [027-020]. [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]] herself boasts that her collection is "the stupendous collection of real waxwork in the world" [027-013].

Nell's initial reaction captures the uncanny quality of the exhibition:

> "I never saw any waxwork ma'am," said Nell. "Is it funnier than Punch?"
>
> [027-017]

The waxwork carriage itself is vividly depicted — not "a shabby, dingy, dusty cart, but a smart little house upon wheels" [026-013], reflecting [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]'s pride in her profession and the respectable image she cultivates.

## Symbolic Meaning

The waxwork functions as a key Dickensian symbol on multiple levels. It embodies the **relationship between artifice and authenticity** that pervades the novel: the wax figures imitate the living, just as social performances imitate genuine feeling. Characters throughout the story — Quilp's theatrical villainy, the Brasses' legal charade, Codlin and Short's puppet show — inhabit similar spaces between real and performed identity.

The waxwork also offers a commentary on **death and mortality**, a central theme of the novel. The figures are simultaneously lifelike and lifeless, hovering in an uncanny middle ground. Nell's work among these silent, still figures foreshadows her own fate; she moves among the wax dead before joining the actual dead. Dickens makes this connection explicit when he notes that one character "was somehow connected with the waxwork, or was waxwork himself, or was [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]" [027-064].

Finally, the waxwork represents **the commodification of wonder** — the transformation of history, tragedy, and celebrity into a paid spectacle. [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]'s business model treats curiosity as currency, a theme that resonates with the novel's broader critique of commercial society.

## Associated Characters

- **[[Mrs. Jarley]]**: The proprietor of the waxwork, a practical businesswoman who employs Nell with a mixture of kindness and self-interest [027-044]
- **[[Nell Trent]]**: Finds employment as the waxwork's guide, pointing out figures to visitors and earning her keep [027-050]
- **[[Grandfather Trent]]**: Accompanies Nell at the waxwork but is uneasy with the sedentary, performative life
- **[[Thomas Codlin]]** and **[[Short Trotters]]**: The [[punch-and-judy|Punch and Judy]] men who precede the waxwork on the road and reappear throughout the travelling chapters [019-052]
