# The Brass Plate

The brass plate on the door of [[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]'s law office at [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]], inscribed with the attorney's name, is a small but telling object — a symbol of the thin veneer of professional respectability that masks the corruption within, and a recurring point of attention in the novel's satirical treatment of the legal profession.

## In the Novel

The brass plate is introduced when [[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]] first arrives at [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]] to take up his position as Brass's clerk. It is one of the first things he notices about the establishment: a physical marker of the professional status that Brass claims but does not earn. The plate appears at the "little habitation, which is so close upon the footway that the passenger who takes the wall must needs turn his back on the window" [033-003].

Throughout the novel's [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]] chapters, the brass plate serves as an ironic counterpoint to the moral squalor within. The name "Brass" itself is Dickens's joke — brass, a cheap metal that looks like gold, perfectly captures the attorney's combination of surface polish and base substance. The plate proclaims professional dignity while the office behind it houses conspiracy, perjury, and the near-starvation of a small servant girl.

## Description

Dickens describes the brass plate within the broader portrait of the [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]] office, with its bill in the parlour window advertising the "business of a scrivener, notary, and conveyancer" for sale [033-003]. The plate is not described in isolation but as part of the office's self-presentation — the street-facing exterior that promises legal respectability while concealing legal criminality.

The plate's material — brass — is significant. It is not gold, not silver, not even copper: it is brass, an alloy that mimics more valuable metals without possessing their worth. This material choice perfectly mirrors Brass himself, who mimics the form of an honest attorney without possessing the substance of one.

## Symbolic Meaning

The brass plate represents the gap between professional appearance and moral reality that is one of the novel's persistent concerns. Dickens was deeply suspicious of institutional authority — lawyers, in particular, come under sustained satirical attack in many of his novels — and the brass plate is a concrete emblem of that suspicion. It says "Brass, Attorney" but what it means is "here, legal forms are used to perpetrate injustice."

The plate also functions as a class marker. It announces that Brass belongs to the professional class — that he is "one of her Majesty's attorneys" [013-001] — and this claim to status is what enables his schemes. Without the brass plate's implicit guarantee of respectability, Brass would be just another London rogue. With it, he can frame an innocent man and nearly succeed.

> Daniel Quilp of Tower Hill, and Sampson Brass of Bevis Marks in the city of London, Gentleman, one of her Majesty's attorneys — and of which said Brass, the said Daniel Quilp was the principal creditor — they had been on terms of intimacy for some time.

## Associated Characters

- **[[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]**: The plate bears his name and represents his claim to professional legitimacy — a claim the novel systematically demolishes.

- **[[sally-brass|Sally Brass]]**: Though the plate names only "Brass," Sally is the real power behind the office, "of a strong and vigorous turn, having from her earliest youth devoted herself to the study of the law" [033-007].

- **[[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]]**: Swiveller's employment at [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]] — and his gradual discovery of what the brass plate conceals — is the vehicle for much of the novel's comedy and its eventual turn toward justice.

- **[[daniel-quilp|Daniel Quilp]]**: The plate marks the office where Quilp's schemes are executed through legal proxy, making the brass plate an unwitting accessory to his villainy.
