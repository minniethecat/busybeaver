# Codlin and Short

Codlin and Short are travelling Punch and Judy showmen — one of the novel's finest comic creations and a classic Dickensian double-act. Thomas Codlin is the grumbling misanthrope who carries the puppet theatre on his back and complains incessantly; Harris, known as Short or Trotters (for the small size of his legs), is the cheerful trumpeter and jester who keeps spirits high. Together they embody the precarious, hand-to-mouth world of Victorian itinerant entertainers.

## The Partnership

The division of labour between the two men is clear and comically asymmetrical. Short leads the way, blowing a brazen trumpet to announce their arrival in each town: "Short blew a blast upon the brazen trumpet and carolled a fragment of a song in that hilarious tone common to Punches and their consorts" [015-014]. Codlin follows behind, bearing the heavy puppet theatre and "cursing his fate, and all the hollow things of earth (but Punch especially)" [015-014].

Their contrasting temperaments are established from their first appearance. Codlin "had a surly, grumbling manner" and speaks "very slowly and eat very greedily, as is not uncommon with philosophers and misanthropes." Short, by contrast, is the optimist: he "beguiled the time with songs and jests, and made the best of everything that happened" [015-014].

Short's real name is Harris, but he acquired his nickname from the small size of his legs. "Short Trotters, however, being a compound name, inconvenient of use in friendly dialogue, the gentleman on whom it had been bestowed was known among his intimates either as 'Short,' or 'Trotters'" [015-013].

## Meeting Nell

Nell and her grandfather encounter Codlin and Short in a country churchyard where the showmen have set down their theatre to rest. The Grandfather is immediately captivated by the Punch puppets: "Are you going to show 'em tonight? are you?" he asks eagerly [015-010]. The showmen take the old man and child under their wing, and the party travels together toward the race town.

During the journey, Codlin attempts to win Nell's confidence, warning her darkly against Short and offering himself as her protector — a self-serving gesture that Nell, with her natural perceptiveness, sees through. Short, despite his cheerfulness, proves the more honest of the two.

## At the Jolly Sandboys

The showmen insist on stopping at [[the-jolly-sandboys|the Jolly Sandboys]] inn: "I won't go further than the mile and a half tonight. I put up at the Jolly Sandboys and nowhere else" [015-001]. There they join a colourful assembly of fellow entertainers — performing dogs, a giant, a limbless lady, and a card-sharp — in one of the novel's most vivid set-pieces.

## Connection to the Single Gentleman

Later in the novel, Codlin and Short are summoned to Bevis Marks by [[the-single-gentleman|the Single Gentleman]], who has an obsessive fascination with Punch and Judy shows. The Single Gentleman regales them with strong waters and holds long, mysterious conversations with them — in truth, employing them to help search for the missing Nell and her grandfather [011-029].

## Comic Significance

Codlin and Short represent the Dickensian gift for creating memorable minor characters. Their double-act — the grumbler and the jester — is a comic pattern as old as drama itself, but Dickens invests them with such specificity of speech and manner that they feel like real people met on the road. They also serve a narrative function: their travelling show provides cover for Nell and her grandfather's flight, and their connection to the entertainment world opens a window into a marginal Victorian subculture that fascinated Dickens throughout his career.
