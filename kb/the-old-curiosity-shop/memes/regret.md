Regret in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] is never merely nostalgic; it is always morally freighted. The grandfather's remorse over his gambling — the regret that never becomes reformation — haunts the novel from its earliest chapters to its closing pages. But regret also appears in gentler keys: the narrator's elegiac reflections on the passage of time, the schoolmaster's grief for his lost child, the quiet sorrow of characters who recognize, too late, the value of what they have lost. Dickens treats regret as the emotional price of moral awareness — the feeling that signals we have understood, however belatedly, what we ought to have done.

## In the Novel

The grandfather's regret is the novel's most sustained exploration of the emotion. Yet there is something hollow about his remorse: he regrets the consequences of his gambling but cannot stop gambling. His regret, like his pride, becomes another form of self-absorption — a wallowing in guilt that substitutes for genuine change. The narrator's haunting question — "why will they leave us some regret! why cannot we push them back" (040-002) — captures regret's essential torment: it always arrives too late.

[[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]'s "sentimental regret" (049-019) is the novel's satirical treatment of false remorse — the performative sorrow of a villain who regrets only being caught. The narrator's observation that his regret "struggled but weakly with a smile" (049-019) exposes the gap between genuine remorse and its theatrical imitation. For Nell, regret takes the form of sorrow for the life she and her grandfather have left behind — sorrow "and regret" (043-003) mingled with the relief of escape. Her regret is purer than her grandfather's because it acknowledges loss without being consumed by it.

## Key Examples

- **[[grandfather-trent|Grandfather Trent]]**: His consuming regret over his gambling losses is the novel's central case study in remorse without reformation. He feels the weight of his actions without the capacity to change them, making his regret a form of torment rather than a path to redemption.
- **[[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]**: His "sentimental regret" (049-019) is the novel's exposure of counterfeit remorse — the performative sorrow of a villain who regrets only exposure, not the evil of his acts.
- **[[the-schoolmaster|The Schoolmaster]]**: His quiet grief for his lost child represents regret in its most dignified form — sorrow that honors what was lost without being paralyzed by it. His regret is integrated into a life of active goodness, not wallowed in as self-punishment.
- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: Her "sorrow and regret" (043-003) for the life left behind is the regret of a child forced into premature adulthood. Unlike her grandfather's self-lacerating remorse, Nell's regret acknowledges loss without being defined by it.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens distinguishes carefully between regret that leads to moral growth and regret that substitutes for it. The grandfather's remorse is genuine in its suffering but sterile in its effects — he feels the full weight of his failures without ever altering his behavior. This is the novel's most penetrating psychological insight: that strong emotion, even morally appropriate emotion, does not automatically produce moral change. Regret, in Dickens's handling, is necessary but insufficient — a starting point for reformation that too often becomes a substitute for it. The schoolmaster's integrated grief and Nell's quiet acceptance offer the novel's alternative models of how regret can be honored without being indulged.

## Victorian Context

Regret and remorse were subjects of intense Victorian interest, from the religious tradition of the penitential confession to the emerging psychological discourse on guilt and conscience. The Oxford Movement's emphasis on moral seriousness and the Evangelical stress on conviction of sin both placed regret at the center of the spiritual life. Dickens, though suspicious of doctrinal Christianity, shared the broader Victorian conviction that remorse was a sign of moral health — the pain that proved the conscience was functioning. The grandfather's tragedy is not that he feels regret but that his regret produces no reformation, making him a case study in the gap between feeling and action that Victorian moralists increasingly recognized as the central problem of character.
