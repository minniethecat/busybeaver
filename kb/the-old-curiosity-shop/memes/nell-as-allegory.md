# Nell and the Allegorical Mode

## Thesis

Dickens frames Nell Trent within an explicitly allegorical register — the narrator describes her as existing "in a kind of allegory" (001-108) — but then complicates this framing by placing her in a world of vividly naturalistic characters. This tension between allegorical symbolism and psychological realism is central to the novel's moral architecture and to its controversial reception.

## Evidence

### The Explicit Allegorical Frame

The narrator establishes Nell's allegorical status early. The curiosity shop itself, with its "old and curious things," surrounds her with objects that seem to exist "in a kind of allegory" (001-108). This is not subtle — Dickens tells us directly that Nell functions symbolically. She is Innocence, she is Patient Suffering, she is the Child-Saviour. Her journey out of London mirrors the soul's journey toward salvation.

### Contrast with Naturalistic Figures

The allegorical mode is not uniformly applied. Quilp, Swiveller, the Brasses, and [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]] are rendered in a comic-naturalistic mode — their dialogue is earthy, their motivations are material (money, sex, power), and they are never described as allegorical figures. The narrator's treatment of Quilp in particular — "The Legal Gentleman" (033-003) — is ironic and specific, not symbolic and universal.

### The Waxworks as Meta-Commentary

[[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]'s waxworks provide a meta-commentary on the novel's dual mode. The waxworks are "so like life, that if waxwork only spoke and walked about, you'd hardly know the difference" (027-020). They are simultaneously allegorical (the waxwork of "a murderess" is a type, not an individual) and naturalistic (the details are grotesquely specific). Nell's sojourn among the waxworks places her between these two representational modes — she is both a real girl and a walking symbol.

### Narrative Consequences

The allegorical framing of Nell has narrative consequences. Her death scene is the most explicit — Nell's passing is rendered as a quasi-religious apotheosis rather than a medical event. The narrator describes her in the language of hagiography: a saint's death, not a child's. This is the allegorical mode at its most intense — and it is precisely what divided contemporary readers, with some finding it sublime and others finding it manipulative.

## Conclusion

The novel's power comes from the friction between its two modes. If Nell were entirely allegorical, she would be a cardboard saint; if she were entirely naturalistic, her death would be merely sad rather than transcendent. Dickens's achievement is to keep both modes in tension — Nell exists "in a kind of allegory," but the "kind of" qualifier is crucial. It is an allegory with dirt on its shoes, a saint who laughs, a symbol who bleeds.
