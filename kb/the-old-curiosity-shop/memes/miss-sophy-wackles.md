# Sophy Wackles

Sophy Wackles is the daughter of the widowed [[mrs-wackles|Mrs. Wackles]], who runs a day school in Chelsea. She becomes the romantic interest of [[dick-swiveller]], who idealises her from afar and invokes her name repeatedly as a kind of refrain: "Sophy Wackles," said Dick (007-049). Despite Swiveller's devotion, Sophy ultimately marries [[cheggs|Mr. Cheggs]], a market-gardener, in a subdued resolution that contrasts with the novel's more tragic love stories. Her role in the novel, though modest in page count, illuminates the romantic economy of Dickens's London — a world where charm competes with substance and rarely wins.

## Character Traits

Sophy is portrayed as cheerful, unpretentious, and kind-hearted. The narrator describes her as "a very nice girl, with a bright eye and a neat figure" (008-011), a description that is notably unadorned — Sophy is presented without the satire or grotesquerie that Dickens deploys for his more memorable characters. Her appeal lies precisely in her ordinariness.

She shows genuine warmth toward Swiveller without fully understanding the depth of his romantic attachment. When Swiveller departs, her "heart sunk within her" (008-047), suggesting a real if unexamined affection. She navigates the awkward quadrille of competing suitors — [[dick-swiveller]] on one side, [[cheggs]] on the other — with a grace that reflects her mother's practical training rather than any calculated design. Her blush at Cheggs's bashful attention (008-019) and her gentle protests to her sister Jane (008-027) convey a character of natural modesty.

## At the Chelsea School

Sophy resides with her mother and her eldest sister, [[miss-jane-wackles|Jane Wackles]], at the school establishment in Chelsea. The school is modest but respectable, its reputation resting on industry rather than gentility — "the art of needlework" and the "general fascination" exercised by Miss Sophy Wackles (008-013) being its principal attractions. The Chelsea atmosphere provides a contrast to the darker currents of the novel: a space of ordinary courtship and domestic arrangement rather than obsession or exploitation.

At the school's dance, Sophy becomes the center of romantic competition. The narrator notes that her mother privately wishes "that Sophy were for that occasion only far less lovely" (008-016), a wish driven by the complications her beauty creates among the competing suitors.

## Swiveller's Courtship

[[dick-swiveller]]'s infatuation with Sophy Wackles runs through the early chapters of the novel as a comic counterpoint to the main plot. He mentions her name at every opportunity, using it as a talisman against the disappointments of his daily life. The narrator notes his absorption in "meditations touching Miss Sophy Wackles" (008-010), a phrase that captures both the sincerity and the absurdity of his attachment.

At the dance hosted by the Wackleses, Swiveller secures Sophy's hand for the first quadrille — a triumph he savours with characteristic grandiosity (008-020). Yet his courtship is hampered by the presence of [[cheggs]], a rival whose material prospects far exceed Swiveller's own. Miss Cheggs's "vigorous and determined" advocacy (008-021) on her brother's behalf further erodes Swiveller's position, and Sophy herself is maneuvered into pressing Swiveller to dance with Miss Cheggs instead (008-022).

> "Are you going?" said Miss Sophy, whose heart sunk within her at the question.

## Marriage to Mr. Cheggs

Sophy's eventual marriage to [[cheggs]] represents one of the novel's practical outcomes — a match driven by circumstance rather than passion. Miss Cheggs announces that "Alick has been saying such things to Sophy" (008-042), and the courtship proceeds with a quiet inevitability. Swiveller accepts his defeat with theatrical resignation, and Sophy passes out of the narrative, "still fluttered and confused by all that had occurred" (008-045).

The marriage underscores a recurring theme in [[the-old-curiosity-shop]]: the gap between romantic aspiration and economic reality. Where [[nell-trent]]'s story arcs toward tragedy, Sophy's settles into ordinariness — neither a triumph nor a catastrophe, but simply the way of the world for those without the means to sustain grand passions.

## Key Relationships

- **[[dick-swiveller]]**: Her principal suitor throughout the early chapters. His romantic fixation on Sophy — expressed through repeated invocations of her name — is one of the novel's running comic motifs.
- **[[cheggs]]**: The market-gardener she ultimately marries. His bashful persistence and his sister's advocacy win him Sophy's hand over Swiveller's flashier appeal.
- **[[mrs-wackles]]**: Her widowed mother and the head of the Chelsea day school. Mrs. Wackles presides over the courtship with an eye toward practical advantage.
- **[[miss-jane-wackles]]**: Her elder sister, who assists in the delicate management of Sophy's suitors (008-027, 008-029).
