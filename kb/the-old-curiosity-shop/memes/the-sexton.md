# The Sexton

The Sexton is an old, deaf church officer who digs graves and maintains the churchyard in the rural hamlet where Nell Trent and her grandfather find rest near the end of their journey. His conversations with Nell about death and burial mark a somber turning point in the novel.

## Role in the Novel

The sexton appears during the later chapters of Nell's wandering, when she and her grandfather pause at a quiet country church. His occupation as gravedigger and his matter-of-fact talk of mortality serve as the novel's most direct meditation on death. Through the sexton's dialogues with Nell and the schoolmaster, Dickens explores themes of impermanence, memory, and the dignity of the dead (053-017 to 054-022).

## Character Traits

The sexton is marked by age and infirmity — he is "very deaf" (054-022) and speaks with a mixture of professional pride in his work and a touching concern for accuracy in his records of the dead. He keeps a spade that is "a well-used one" (053-017), and despite his physical limitations, he is "quite unconscious" of his own frailty (053-024). His petulant reply about his deafness (054-022) reveals a stubborn, human irritability beneath his grave-digging solemnity.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Nell Trent]]**: Nell encounters the sexton in the churchyard, and their conversations about graves and mortality deepen her contemplative state (053-017).
- **[[Grandfather Trent]]**: Nell's grandfather, who shares in the conversation at the churchyard (053-023).
- **[[The Schoolmaster]]**: The schoolmaster sits with the sexton and discusses the uncertainty of human life (052-019, 053-036).

## Significant Appearances

- First appearance: The sexton's spade is noticed by Nell in the churchyard (053-017)
- Major scene: The sexton discusses graves and Becky Morgan's burial with Nell (054-010 to 054-022)
- Later scene: The sexton recovers from illness while Nell visits (054-005)
- Key dialogue: Explaining the well and the grave to Nell (053-027 to 053-029)

## Critical Interpretation

The sexton functions as a memento mori figure — a living embodiment of death's proximity in a novel deeply concerned with mortality. His deafness and age position him as a liminal figure between the living and the dead, and his grave-side conversations with Nell prepare both character and reader for the novel's final tragedy.

> "What is it but a grave!" said the sexton. "What else! And which of us can tell how soon we may be brought here!"
>
> (053-029)
