# Mr. Witherden

Mr. Witherden is a notary with offices at Bevis Marks, identifiable by a brass plate reading "Witherden—Notary" (014-014). He is a minor but trusted figure in the legal framework of the novel, assisting the [[The Single Gentleman|single gentleman]], [[Abel Garland]], and others with their affairs. His presence provides a counterpoint to the corrupt legal practitioners [[Sampson Brass]] and [[Sally Brass]], demonstrating that professional integrity can exist even in a world of widespread legal abuse.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Witherden functions as a legal intermediary in the novel, handling the financial matters surrounding [[Nell Trent]]'s grandfather and serving as a witness to key events. He is first introduced when the old gentleman visits his office at [[Bevis Marks]] to discuss the grandfather's situation (014-016). His professional opinion carries weight — he states plainly, "I tell you ma'am, what I think as an honest man" (014-020), a declaration of integrity that distinguishes him from the Brass siblings.

He later appears during [[Kit Nubbles]]'s employment at the Bevis Marks office, welcoming Kit and providing him with honest work as an attendant to [[Mr. Abel]] (038-015). His office also becomes the setting for Abel Garland's clerkship, and he is consulted by [[Sally Brass]] when she appeals regarding Kit's case (060-038).

## Character Traits

Mr. Witherden is described physically as a "short, chubby, fresh-coloured" man (014-032). He projects an air of professional authority, with Mr. Abel describing him as having "an awful and venerable air" (020-028). He is also described as "a bustling gentleman" (020-028), suggesting an active and businesslike disposition.

His distinctive mannerism — sniffing a flower while conducting business — appears throughout his scenes. He is observed "smelling very hard at the nosegay" (020-026) and later "buried his nose in the flower" (020-036), a gesture that hints at an affected or self-conscious professionalism. Despite this theatricality, his legal judgments prove sound, and he remains a reliable figure whom even the antagonistic [[Sally Brass]] feels compelled to approach.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Mr. Abel]]**: His clerk and indispensable attendant (038-007), who works from the Bevis Marks office and handles much of the day-to-day business.
- **[[The Single Gentleman]]**: A client who brings the grandfather's case to Witherden's attention (014-016), initiating the legal thread that runs through the later chapters.
- **[[Kit Nubbles]]**: Witherden welcomes Kit into his office and employs him as an attendant to Mr. Abel, providing Kit with honest employment (038-015).
- **[[Abel Garland]]**: Witherden's practice serves as the setting for Abel Garland's clerk apprenticeship (014-025), and the notary provides a letter of recommendation for him (038-029).
- **[[Sally Brass]]**: She appeals to Witherden regarding Kit's case (060-038), a scene that demonstrates Witherden's authority even over those who oppose him.

## Significant Appearances

- **First appearance** (Chapter 14): The Bevis Marks office, where the old gentleman consults him about the grandfather's financial affairs (014-016).
- **Kit's employment** (Chapter 38): Witherden welcomes Christopher into the office, remarking "That's the lad. He fell in with my client" (038-017), establishing Kit's new position.
- **Private consultation** (Chapter 40): Witherden is closeted with the single gentleman, discussing the grandfather's affairs in confidence (040-038).
- **Sally Brass's appeal** (Chapter 60): Sally Brass approaches Witherden regarding Kit's case, a significant moment in the novel's legal subplot (060-038).

## Critical Interpretation

Mr. Witherden represents the role of law and professional integrity in a novel otherwise populated by corrupt legal figures like [[Sampson Brass]] and [[Sally Brass]]. His position at [[Bevis Marks]] places him in the geographical and moral center of the novel's commercial world. While not a fully developed character, Witherden serves an important structural function: his office is a site of resolution where financial mysteries are untangled and honest employment is secured. His flower-sniffing mannerism, described with Dickens's characteristic satirical touch, humanizes the figure of legal authority without undermining it.
