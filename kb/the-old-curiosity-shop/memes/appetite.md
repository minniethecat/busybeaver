## In the Novel

Appetite in *The Old Curiosity Shop* operates on multiple levels: literal hunger, the appetites of acquisitive capitalism, and the moral appetite for power and cruelty. Dickens uses the language of consumption to link bodily need to moral condition:

> "rather sharpened in wits and appetite, Mr. Swiveller forwarded" (008-001)

Dick Swiveller's perpetual state of hungry alertness establishes appetite as a comic motif, but the novel repeatedly pushes beyond comedy into darker territory. The body's demands become a register of moral health.

## The Hunger of Poverty

The novel's treatment of literal hunger is unflinching. Nell and her grandfather's journey is shadowed by the constant threat of starvation, and the food they encounter — or fail to encounter — measures their descent. The contrast between the well-fed and the hungry maps precisely onto the novel's moral geography of predators and prey.

## Quilp's Insatiable Appetite

Quilp represents appetite in its most monstrous form — a hunger that is never satisfied and that feeds on the suffering of others:

> "in the gratification of his appetites — have soothed his conscience" (023-061)

His appetites are explicitly linked to the suppression of conscience: consumption becomes a mechanism for moral evasion. Dickens's Quilp eats, drinks, and consumes with terrifying gusto — an appetite that stands for a total absence of moral restraint:

> "brought no bad effect upon his appetite" (026-042)

The remark that Quilp's cruelties never impair his digestion is Dickens at his most Swiftian: appetite as moral index, the body's indifference as a mark of the soul's corruption. The narrator's observation that "you always have your appetites too, and what a comfort that" (027-005) is layered with irony — appetite as the one constant in a world of loss.

## Appetite and Moral Economy

The novel contrasts Quilp's voracious appetite with Nell's abstemiousness, constructing a moral economy in which consumption and virtue are inversely related. To have fewer appetites is, in the novel's scheme, to be more fully human.
