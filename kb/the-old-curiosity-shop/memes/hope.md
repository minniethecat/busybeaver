# Hope

Hope is the tensile strength of *[[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]]* — the quiet force that keeps Nell walking, that sustains Kit through injustice, that draws the [[the-bachelor|Single Gentleman]] across England in search of his lost family. It is everywhere in the novel, but it is never cheap; Dickens refuses to offer hope that is not earned through suffering, and the novel's deepest affirmation is not that hope is always rewarded, but that hoping is itself a form of moral action.

## In the Novel

Hope manifests in the novel primarily through motion — through the act of continuing. Nell and her grandfather walk because they hope for something better ahead: "Let us be beggars," says Nell, passing an arm around his neck, "I have no fear but we shall have enough, I am sure we shall" [009-021]. This is not naive optimism but desperate resolve; Nell's hope is the courage to keep moving when stopping means despair.

The grandfather's gambling is the dark mirror of hope — hope perverted into compulsion. His conviction that the next game will restore their fortune is technically a form of hope, but Dickens shows it as destructive delusion, not virtue. The novel thus draws a crucial distinction between true hope (which accepts reality and persists through it) and false hope (which flees reality and destroys through it).

For Kit, hope is tied to loyalty and work. He hopes for justice not through passive waiting but through active goodness — caring for his mother, serving the Garlands, trusting in Providence. His hope is vindicated: "through all the struggles of their daily life, too mean and pitiful to tell, but dreadful to endure; she toiled on, in the deep devotion of her spirit" [069-048]. The language of endurance here is the language of hope properly understood — not as escape from suffering but as faithfulness through it.

[[single-gentleman|The Single Gentleman]] embodies the most active form of hope in the novel. His long search for Nell and her grandfather — tracing their steps across England, following fragmentary clues, never giving up — demonstrates hope as a practice, not just a feeling. When he finally learns their fate, he does not despair; he "went forth into the world, a lover of his kind" [073-023]. His hope survives its object's loss because it was never merely about getting what he wanted.

## Key Examples

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: Hope personified — a child who sustains her despairing grandfather through sheer force of will, whose hope is essentially maternal: she hopes *for* him more than for herself [009-021].

- **[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]**: Hope expressed as loyalty. Kit's hope is not verbalized but enacted — in his care for his family, his devotion to the Garlands, his refusal to become bitter even when wrongly accused.

- **[[single-gentleman|The Single Gentleman]]**: Hope as quest. His years-long search for his lost brother and his grandchild is the novel's most sustained image of hope in action [038-022][057-001].

- **[[the-grandfathers-gambling|The Grandfather's Gambling]]**: Hope's dark double. The grandfather's gambling represents hope severed from moral reality — wanting something for nothing, destroying the thing you hope to save.

- **[[suffering|Suffering]]**: Hope's necessary companion. In the novel's moral economy, hope that has not passed through suffering is untested and therefore unreliable. Nell's hope is credible precisely because she suffers so much.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens treats hope not as a feeling but as a discipline. The characters who hope well in the novel are those who hope *actively* — who walk, work, search, endure. The characters who hope badly — the grandfather at the gaming table, Quilp scheming for advantage — are those who try to short-circuit the connection between hope and effort.

This is a deeply Victorian conception, rooted in the era's emphasis on moral earnestness and the dignifying power of work. But Dickens complicates it through Nell's death. She hopes, she endures, she is good — and she dies. The novel does not flinch from this. What it offers instead is the suggestion that her hope was never about survival: it was about preserving her moral integrity through everything that happened to her. She dies whole, and that wholeness — not her physical survival — is the vindication of her hope.

> That friend — single gentleman, or younger brother, which you will — had at his heart a heavy sorrow; but it bred in him no misanthropy or monastic gloom. He went forth into the world, a lover of his kind. For a long, long time, it was his chief delight to travel in the steps of the old man and the child...
>
> [073-023]

## Victorian Context

The novel's treatment of hope reflects the broader Victorian preoccupation with the problem of undeserved suffering. In an era of industrialization, urban poverty, and high child mortality, the question of how to maintain hope in the face of real suffering was not merely literary but urgent. Dickens's answer — that hope is a moral practice rather than an emotional state, and that its value lies in what it enables us to become rather than what it enables us to obtain — is one of his most enduring contributions to nineteenth-century moral thought.
