## In the Novel

Hunger in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] is both a literal physical condition and a metaphor for deeper forms of longing and deprivation. Nell and her grandfather, driven from their home, encounter hunger as part of the cumulative suffering of their journey. Dickens catalogues their privations: "cold, wet, hunger, want of rest, and lack of any place to lay his head" (044-003). Hunger here is not an isolated hardship but one thread in a tapestry of deprivation that wears down both body and spirit.

The novel also presents hunger as a figure for desperation and desire. When Quilp's agent describes having "only enough to squench my hunger" (058-029), the phrase carries a note of animalistic need — hunger reduced to a raw physical urgency that strips away dignity. The grandfather's gambling addiction is itself a kind of hunger, a "sickly eagerness" (036-016) that consumes him as surely as starvation consumes the body.

## Key Examples

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: On the journey, Nell is described as having "no thought of hunger or cold, or thirst, or suffering" (012-036) — her selflessness erasing her own bodily needs as she focuses entirely on her grandfather's welfare.
- **Nell's grandfather**: His complaint of hunger on the road (045-017) marks a reversal of roles: the protector becomes the one who must be protected, his physical need exposing his dependence on the child he was meant to shelter.
- **The bachelor's account**: The bachelor recalls "a wretched priest who fainted of thirst and hunger at her door" (054-002), using hunger as a measure of sainthood — the woman who tends the starving is herself sanctified by the act.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens uses hunger as an instrument of social commentary. The hunger of Nell and her grandfather on the open road is not accidental — it is the direct consequence of a social order that offers no safety net for the displaced and the old. Even when food is found, "even hunger was forgotten in the strange and moving interest" (045-013) of kinder encounters, suggesting that human connection offers a sustenance that food alone cannot provide.

The novel's treatment of hunger aligns with Dickens's broader critique of the New Poor Law and the workhouse system. Hunger in *The Old Curiosity Shop* is never merely personal misfortune — it is systemic, a failure of a society that permits children and the elderly to go without food while others feast.

> It was very little, but even hunger was forgotten in the strange and moving interest of the scene.
>
> (045-013)
