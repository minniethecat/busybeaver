## In the Novel

Anxiety pervades *The Old Curiosity Shop* as both a psychological state and a narrative engine. From Nell's vigilance over her grandfather's nocturnal disappearances to the reader's anticipation of Quilp's next cruelty, anxiety structures the experience of the text:

> "a dreadful state of mind" (029-059)

The grandfather's anxiety about money, about exposure, about the future — these fears radiate outward, contaminating Nell's childhood with adult worries from which she should be protected. The novel presents anxiety as contagious: one character's fear becomes another's burden.

## The Geography of Unease

Dickens maps anxiety onto London itself. The streets through which the grandfather walks at night — the same streets Quilp prowls — become corridors of dread. The curiosity shop, supposedly a sanctuary, is permeated by the grandfather's hidden fear:

> "the same terror that had seized her on the previous night" (034-006)

The repetition of nocturnal terror — "the same" — suggests anxiety as a cyclical condition, returning each night with the regularity of a symptom. This is anxiety not as response to a specific threat but as a permanent condition of living.

## Quilp as Agent of Anxiety

Quilp functions as the novel's primary anxiety-generator — a figure whose very appearance in a scene raises the narrative temperature. His unpredictability is his power: characters (and readers) can never anticipate his next move, only that it will be cruel:

> "Mr. Quilp was certainly entertaining himself with vocal exercise" (023-061)

The juxtaposition of Quilp's casual self-amusement with the terror he inspires creates a specifically Dickensian anxiety — the dread produced by a world in which cruelty is merely someone's entertainment.

## Anxiety and the Child

Nell's experience of anxiety is particularly acute because she lacks the adult resources to contextualise or manage it. Her grandfather's gambling — the source of her anxiety — is a secret she must keep, a burden that isolates her. The novel makes clear that anxiety in childhood is not less serious than adult anxiety but more damaging, because the child has no framework for understanding that the world might be crueller than it should be.

## Narrative Anxiety

The novel's narrative structure itself generates anxiety. Dickens's use of parallel plots — cutting between Nell's journey and the London intrigue — creates a persistent tension in the reader, who knows more than any single character and is therefore in a position of helpless omniscience. The technique is particularly effective in the Quilp sequences, where the reader anticipates cruelty long before the characters on the page:

> "a kind of anxiety that was new to her" (015-031)

Nell's newfound anxiety — and the reader's — is the product of narrative knowledge: the gradual revelation that the world of the novel contains threats for which no prior experience has prepared her. Dickens exploits the gap between what the reader suspects and what the character knows to produce a sustained condition of narrative dread. This technique, which would become a hallmark of the Victorian serial novel, reaches an early perfection in _The Old Curiosity Shop_, where the journey structure allows anxiety to accumulate across chapters without resolution.

## Anxiety and Modernity

Dickens's treatment of anxiety anticipates modern psychological understanding in significant ways. The condition he describes — diffuse, objectless, resistant to reason — resembles what later centuries would diagnose as generalised anxiety disorder. The grandfather's anxiety about money, Nell's anxiety about the grandfather, the reader's anxiety about both: these nested anxieties create a system in which worry circulates without ever finding a resting point. The novel's refusal to resolve its anxieties except through Nell's death — a resolution that is also, paradoxically, the novel's greatest source of grief — suggests that anxiety is not a temporary condition to be cured but a permanent feature of life in the modern world.
