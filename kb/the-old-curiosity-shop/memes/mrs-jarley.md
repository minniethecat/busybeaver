# Mrs. Jarley

The proprietor of Jarley's Waxwork in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]]. Mrs. Jarley is one of Dickens's most vivid minor characters — the self-important, good-hearted proprietress of a travelling waxwork exhibition who gives Nell her most stable employment.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Jarley provides Nell with a brief period of security and ordinary life, representing the decent commercial world that stands between the extremes of the curiosity shop and Quilp's villainy.

## Character Traits

Proud, bustling, and fundamentally kind, Mrs. Jarley holds an unshakeable belief in the dignity and educational value of her waxwork exhibition.

## Key Relationships

- **[[nell-trent]]** — The child she employs and protects

## Significant Appearances

Mrs. Jarley appears in Chapter XXVII, when her waxwork caravan intersects with Nell's journey at a critical moment.

## Narrative Details

Mrs. Jarley announces herself with the directness of a woman who has built her own business: "That's me. I am Mrs. Jarley" (027-012). She is the proprietor of the travelling waxwork show whose billboard Nell reads aloud: "Jarley's Waxwork" (027-009), and the real Mrs. Jarley assures the child that "although she stood in the presence of the original Jarley, she must not allow herself to be utterly overwhelmed" (027-013).

Her promotional materials are a masterpiece of Dickensian comic detail — "compositions in prose, purporting to be dialogues between the Emperor of China and an oyster, or the Archbishop of Canterbury and a dissenter" (027-015), alongside the celebrated jingle:

> If I know'd a donkey wot wouldn't go
> To see Mrs. Jarley's waxwork show
> Do you think I'd acknowledge him? Oh no no!
> Then run to Jarley's—
> (027-014)

Mrs. Jarley is emphatic about the character of her exhibition. When Nell asks whether it is "funnier than Punch," she is scandalized: "It isn't funny at all. It's calm and — what's that word again — critical? — no — classical, that's it — it's calm and classical. No low beatings and knockings about" (027-020). She offers Nell employment helping to "dust the figures, and take the checks, and so forth" (027-044), providing the child with her first honest wage and a measure of dignity.
