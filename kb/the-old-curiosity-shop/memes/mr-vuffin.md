# Mr. Vuffin

**Mr. Vuffin** is a showman who appears in [[Charles Dickens]]'s novel
*[[The Old Curiosity Shop]]*. He is one of the travelling entertainers
gathered at [[the-jolly-sandboys|the Jolly Sandboys]] inn in Chapter 19,
where he discusses the business of exhibiting human curiosities with his
companion, [[jerry|Jerry]], and a silent performing gentleman (019-001).

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Vuffin is introduced as a newcomer to the inn, accompanied by his
colleague Sweet William, "probably as a pleasant satire upon his ugliness"
(019-001). He is associated with the travelling Punch-and-Judy show run by
[[codlin-and-short|Thomas Codlin and Short]], and his conversation reveals
the harsh economics of the early Victorian entertainment industry.

## The Economics of "Freaks"

> "Once get a giant shaky on his legs, and the public care no more about
> him than they do for a dead cabbage stalk." (019-005)

Throughout the evening, Vuffin holds forth on the commercial value of
different classes of performers. He argues that elderly dwarfs are the
most valuable attractions — "a grey-headed dwarf, well wrinkled, is
beyond all suspicion" (019-017) — while giants are a depreciating asset
who should be kept in caravans "where they get food and lodging for
nothing, all their lives" (019-013) rather than released to become common.
He cynically observes that advertising [[Shakespeare]] performed entirely
by wooden legs would "not draw a sixpence" (019-011), a commentary on the
primacy of novelty over art in popular entertainment.

## Character and Significance

Vuffin's pragmatic, unsentimental view of his trade offers Dickens's
readers a glimpse into a subculture that exploited physical difference
for profit. His matter-of-fact tone — discussing the life cycles of
giants and dwarfs as one might discuss livestock — serves as a satirical
exposure of an industry that reduced human beings to commodities.
