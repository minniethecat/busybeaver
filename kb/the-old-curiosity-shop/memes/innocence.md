# Innocence

A central thematic concern in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]], innocence is primarily embodied in the character of [[nell-trent|Nell Trent]], whose purity and goodness stand in stark contrast to the corrupting forces of greed, urban squalor, and moral decay that surround her throughout the novel.

## In the Novel

The theme of innocence manifests most powerfully through the figure of [[nell-trent|Nell Trent]], a young girl whose moral purity remains untainted despite the degrading circumstances of her life. The narrator reflects on this quality early in the novel, noting the innocence of the child as a foil to the old man's troubled conscience (001-104). Nell's innate goodness is presented not as naivety but as an active moral force — she responds with innocent directness even when faced with difficult questions (019-028), and her character moves others to sympathy precisely through her innocent nature (023-061).

The novel repeatedly contrasts Nell's innocence against the greed and corruption embodied by characters such as [[daniel-quilp|Daniel Quilp]] and the grasping adults who surround her. Her grandfather, though loving, tragically compromises her safety through his gambling addiction — Nell is described as the innocent cause of all this torture wrought by his compulsion (029-059).

The novel suggests a broader philosophical claim about childlike innocence: that innocent joys and the innocent dead are not forgotten. In a key passage near the novel's conclusion, a character affirms that nothing innocent or good, that dies, and is forgotten (054-074), articulating the novel's moral vision that innocence has enduring spiritual significance beyond individual mortality.

## Key Examples

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: The primary embodiment of innocence in the novel. Her purity survives poverty, displacement, and the moral failings of those around her. She is described as poor innocent Nell by the narrator, whose sympathies are clearly aligned with her virtue (023-061). Her interactions with her grandfather reveal her as simultaneously vulnerable and morally steadfast — bearing misfortunes that fall on her innocent head (003-052).

- **[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]**: Another figure of innocence whose wrongful accusation becomes a major plot driver. Kit is repeatedly described as innocent (061-001), and his ordeal — being falsely charged with theft — tests the novel's central thesis about whether innocence can prevail against corrupt social institutions. His eventual vindication affirms that innocence, though vulnerable, ultimately triumphs when his innocence is established and he is pardoned (068-002).

- **[[the-marchioness|The Marchioness]]**: The small servant girl represents a variation on the innocence theme — innocence degraded and nearly extinguished by cruel circumstance, yet ultimately resilient. Her case is described as one of innocence requiring proof and vindication (058-052).

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens treats innocence not merely as a passive quality of childhood but as an active moral force that exposes the corruption around it. The narrative voice is openly sentimental about innocent suffering, yet this sentimentality serves a polemical purpose: to indict a society that allows the innocent to be exploited.

> When Death strikes down the innocent and young, for every fragile form from which he lets the panting spirit free, a hundred virtues rise, in shapes of mercy, charity, and love, to walk the world, and bless it.
>
> (072-035)

This passage, coming near the novel's end, crystallizes Dickens's moral vision: innocence, even in death, is not extinguished but transfigured into a force for good that outlasts the individual. The novel's treatment of innocence is thus inseparable from its Victorian religious sensibility — innocent suffering, in Dickens's conception, has redemptive power that reverberates beyond the grave.
