## In the Novel

Sleep in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] carries a double valence: it is both the natural refuge of the innocent and the uncanny double of death. Little Nell's sleep is described with the language of angelic peace — "sleeping peacefully. So very young" (001-107) — yet Dickens repeatedly draws the reader's attention to the proximity between sleep and its darker twin. The narrator observes sardonically of death that "it is of sleep" (012-018), collapsing the distance between nightly rest and final oblivion.

The novel's moral economy is legible through who sleeps well and who cannot. The grandfather, burdened by his gambling secret, is described as having "no sleep or rest, but that which he takes in an old arm-chair" (006-069). Quilp, the novel's demonic figure, "took any sleep by snatches of a few winks at a time" (005-001) — his sleeplessness is a mark of his unnatural, restless malevolence. Sleep, or its absence, becomes a diagnostic of character: the good sleep soundly; the guilty do not.

## Key Examples

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: Nell's sleep is a recurring motif of her innocence. She is found "sleeping peacefully" (001-107) at the novel's opening, and her bedtime rituals structure the rhythm of her journey. Her sleep is frequently troubled, however — "often starting up again" (009-007) — as the anxieties of her waking life invade her rest.
- **[[quilp|Quilp]]**: Quilp's relationship to sleep is explicitly unnatural. He goes "to sleep with the ease of an old practitioner" (005-036) yet wakes after "a quarter of an hour" (005-037), his brief, predatory naps mirroring his vulturine character.
- **[[grandfather-trent|Grandfather Trent]]**: The grandfather's confession that he has "no sleep or rest" (006-069) marks his gambling addiction as a spiritual insomnia — he has forfeited the peace of the righteous.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens uses sleep as a moral barometer. The innocent sleep deeply and dream of better things; the wicked sleep fitfully or not at all. This is, in part, a conventional Victorian moral trope — the guilty conscience that "murders sleep" — but Dickens complicates it. Nell's sleep, though peaceful in its outward aspect, is haunted by dreams of her grandfather's fall. The narrator notes that "waking or in my sleep, the same thoughts recurred" (001-111), suggesting that even the purest characters cannot fully escape the novel's atmosphere of anxiety.

The novel also deploys sleep as a narrative device. Characters fall asleep at structurally significant moments — Mr. Swiveller "was fast asleep, dreaming that he had married" (008-056) — allowing Dickens to transition between scenes or to reveal character through the content of dreams. Sleep is never merely biological in The Old Curiosity Shop; it is always doing thematic work.

> Where, in the dull eyes of dying men, there is the mockery of it, that death is of sleep.
>
> (012-018)

## Victorian Context

Sleep in Victorian England was freighted with moral and medical significance. The Victorian obsession with separate bedrooms, the moral panic over "solitary sleeping," and the medical literature on insomnia all testify to sleep's status as a culturally charged act. Dickens, who himself suffered from insomnia and whose public readings were famously exhausting, was acutely sensitive to the economy of rest and its deprivation. The novel's treatment of sleep — as moral indicator, as death's rehearsal, as the refuge of innocence — participates in a broader Victorian discourse while remaining distinctively Dickensian in its fusion of sentiment and the macabre.
