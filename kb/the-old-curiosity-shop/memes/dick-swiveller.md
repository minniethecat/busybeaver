# Dick Swiveller

The charming, improvident clerk in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]]. Richard "Dick" Swiveller is one of Dickens's most delightful comic creations — a cheerful, feckless young man whose wit and good nature eventually lead him, almost despite himself, towards decency and heroism.

## Role in the Novel

Dick Swiveller provides comic relief and narrative counterpoint; his transformation from idle wastrel to inadvertent hero represents one of the novel's most satisfying arcs.

## Character Traits

A genial idler with a gift for quotation and an unshakeable conviction that fortune will favor him without any effort on his part. Beneath the affectation, genuine kindness.

## Key Relationships

- **[[fred-trent]]** — His friend, who draws him into the plot against Nell
- **[[sampson-brass]]** — His employer
- **[[the-marchioness]]** — The servant girl he befriends and protects
- **[[the-single-gentleman]]** — The lodger whose secret he helps uncover

## Significant Appearances

Swiveller appears both in the early London chapters and the later [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]] episodes, where his role shifts from comic observer to active participant in the plot's resolution.

## Narrative Details

Dick Swiveller is introduced in characteristic fashion — announced by a friend at the door: "There. It's Dick Swiveller. Sit down Swiveller" (002-016). His conversation is a stream of mock-philosophical pronouncements: "what is the odds so long as the fire of soul is kindled at the taper of conwiviality, and the wing of friendship never moults a feather!" (002-020).

His lodgings are "in the neighbourhood of Drury Lane... over a tobacconist's shop" (007-002), a suitably modest address for a young man who lives by his wits. When Fred Trent presses him about Nell, he observes with practiced detachment: "Fine girl of her age, but small" (007-025), yet his involvement deepens as the plot unfolds.

Swiveller's true character emerges when he becomes clerk to [[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]. Lodging in the [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]] establishment, he befriends the abused little servant girl, whom he christens "the Marchioness." It is through this relationship that Swiveller discovers his own decency — teaching the Marchioness to play cards, sharing his meagre meals, and ultimately helping to expose the conspiracy that frames [[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]] (057-044).

When Quilp introduces him at the office as "my intimate friend — a gentleman of good family and great expectations" (033-036), the irony is that Swiveller comes closer to deserving this description by the novel's end than anyone — including Quilp — could have predicted.
