# Employment

Employment in *The Old Curiosity Shop* is portrayed as precarious, scarce, and deeply tied to personal dignity. Characters' struggles to secure and maintain work drive key narrative developments and illuminate the economic fragility of urban life in the 1830s.

## In the Novel

The search for employment is a recurring motif. Kit Nubbles repeatedly tries to "procure employment" (020-001), his fortunes rising and falling with the whims of patrons. When the waxwork exhibition closes, its employees are "thrown out of employment" (048-001), illustrating the total dependence of workers on unstable commercial ventures. Mrs. Quilp, lacking any economic role, has "no better employment than to attack the common enemy" (004-005) — her domestic entrapment framed as idle hostility. Even Nell's grandfather, once a shopkeeper, is reduced to gambling in desperation after losing his livelihood.

## Key Examples

- **[[Kit Nubbles]]**: His employment arc — from shop boy to the Garlands' household to his wrongful arrest — structures much of the novel's middle section. Kit's work ethic and loyalty are repaid with the "least pleasant of Kit's employments" (040-006) transformed into pleasurable service through personal attachment.
- **The Waxwork Exhibition**: [[Mrs. Jarley]]'s waxworks functions as both a site of employment and a metaphor for the precarious entertainment economy. Its closing throws multiple characters into uncertainty (048-001).
- **[[the-single-gentleman]]**: As an anonymous benefactor, his ability to provide employment through patronage underscores the arbitrary, personality-driven nature of the labour market.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens treats employment not as an abstract economic condition but as a moral test of character. Kit's willingness to work — his "new effort to procure employment" (020-001) — is presented as proof of his virtue, contrasting with Daniel Quilp's exploitative idleness. The novel's interest in characters' "occupations" (001-002) reflects Dickens's broader commitment to documenting the varieties of urban labour, from the shopkeeper to the itinerant showman. Employment, in this framework, is both material necessity and moral identity.

## Victorian Context

The novel's publication in 1840–41 coincided with the economic depression of 1837–42, when unemployment in industrial England was widespread. The New Poor Law of 1834 had established the workhouse system, making the fear of destitution — and the loss of independent employment — an acute social anxiety. Dickens's portrayal of characters seeking work, losing work, and depending on patronage captures the unstructured, insecure nature of the pre-modern labour market, where personal connections often mattered more than skill or diligence.
