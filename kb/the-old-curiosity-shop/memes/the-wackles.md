# The Wackles

The Wackles are a Chelsea family consisting of the widowed [[mrs-wackles|Mrs. Wackles]], who runs a small day-school, and her daughters Jane and Sophy. The family appears chiefly in connection with [[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]]'s romantic life, providing one of the novel's comic subplots and a gentle satire on middle-class courtship rituals.

## Mrs. Wackles and the School

After the death of her husband, Mrs. Wackles turned to teaching as a means of supporting her family. She keeps a modest day-school in Chelsea — a respectable but unglamorous establishment that cannot compete with the more prestigious [[miss-monflathers|Miss Monflathers]]'s boarding school. The school is the family's anchor, providing both income and social standing within their small circle.

Mrs. Wackles is, by all accounts, a "very worthy" woman — though as Dickens wryly notes, her chief distinction is having a daughter who attracts the attention of a baronet's descendant. Her eldest daughter, [[miss-jane-wackles|Miss Jane Wackles]], assists with the school and is described as a capable, sensible young woman.

## Sophy Wackles

[[miss-sophy-wackles|Sophy Wackles]] is the younger daughter and the family's romantic centrepiece. Dick Swiveller waxes lyrical about her charms, declaring to his friend Fred Trent: "Between Miss Sophia Wackles and the humble individual who has now the honor to address you, warm and tender sentiments have been engendered" [007-053]. He compares her favourably to the Goddess Diana, noting that "the Goddess Diana, Sir, that calls aloud for the chase, is not more particular in her behaviour than Sophia Wackles" [007-053].

Swiveller's courtship, however, is complicated by the appearance of a rival: [[cheggs|Cheggs]], a market-gardener who also has designs on Sophy. The rivalry culminates in a spirited confrontation, and Sophy ultimately marries Cheggs, becoming [[miss-cheggs|Miss Cheggs]].

## The Family Dynamic

The Wackles embody a particular stratum of Dickensian society — the respectable poor, maintaining dignity through education and domestic industry. Mrs. Wackles's school is a world away from the grand establishments of Miss Monflathers, yet the family navigates their modest circumstances with a blend of practicality and social aspiration that Dickens treats with characteristic humour and a touch of sympathy.
