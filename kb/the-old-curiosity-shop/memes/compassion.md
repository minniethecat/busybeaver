# Compassion

Compassion runs through _The Old Curiosity Shop_ as a quiet moral force, most often
embodied in and inspired by Little Nell. Characters who encounter her are moved from
self-interest or indifference toward tenderness and pity, and this transformation
marks Dickens's clearest moral argument in the novel.

## In the Novel

Mrs. Quilp is introduced in mute appeal to "the compassion and clemency of her lord"
[005-002], establishing compassion's absence as a measure of domestic cruelty.
The single gentleman, speaking to the schoolmaster, adopts "a compassionate tone,
very different from his usual manner" when discussing Nell [031-018], showing how
her presence softens even detached characters. The schoolmaster himself is described
as "half-reproachful and half-compassionate" when he urges the single gentleman to
deal tenderly with Nell [046-005] [046-034].

The sexton speaks to Nell in "a half compassionate, half irritable tone" at the
churchyard [054-015], and even strangers bid her good day "compassionately" and
whisper as they pass [055-004]. The narrator observes "a tenderness towards her — a
compassionate regard for her, increasing with every day" [055-005], and the
schoolmaster reflects on compassion as the smile of good men who "compassionate
their own weakness or that of their fellow creatures" [056-078].

| Mrs. Jarley | Has "compassion on little Nell" | Takes her into the waxworks employment [031-059] |
| The Sexton | Half-compassionate toward Nell | Amidst his grim occupation [054-015] |

## Compassion and Cruelty

The novel contrasts compassion with its opposite — the deliberate cruelty of Quilp
and the indifference of a commercial society. Quilp's treatment of his wife is
measured precisely by the absence of compassion [005-002], while the city through
which Nell travels is largely indifferent to suffering. The waxworks, the races, the
industrial towns — all are backdrops against which small acts of compassion stand
out as Dickens's moral counterpoints.
