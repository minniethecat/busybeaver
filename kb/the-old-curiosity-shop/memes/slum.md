# Mr. Slum

Mr. Slum is a hack poet and writer who produces the descriptive verses and placards for [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]'s [[waxwork|waxworks]] in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]]. He is a satirical portrait of the literary grub street — the commercial writer who sells his pen to the highest bidder.

## In the Novel

Slum appears in Chapter 28 at Mrs. Jarley's waxworks exhibition. She greets him enthusiastically: "What, Mr. Slum!" [028-012], and he responds with theatrical grandiloquence: "'Pon my soul and honour, that's a good remark" [028-013]. He flatters Mrs. Jarley's business acumen while negotiating payment for his literary productions.

His commercial pragmatism is evident in his haggling. Asked to write descriptive verses for the wax figures, he first quotes a higher price and then concedes: "Five shillings. Come. Three-and-six" [028-025][028-027], using his pencil as a toothpick during the negotiation [028-025].

Slum's self-regard surfaces when he bristles at being undervalued: "A few smaller names than Slum" [028-023], he retorts, revealing a prickly pride beneath the commercial veneer. He enters the order in "a small notebook" [028-028], the professional writer transacting business.

His productions are described as "Mr. Slum's productions" displayed alongside the waxworks [028-035], blurring the line between art and commerce in a way typical of Dickens's satire of literary culture.

## Satirical Function

Slum represents the degradation of literary art by commerce. A poet who uses his pencil as a toothpick, who writes to order for three shillings and sixpence, and who flatters his patron while resenting his own dependence — he is both comic and pitiable. He is a minor sketch in Dickens's long-running critique of Grub Street hackery, precursor to figures like Mr. Micawber (who also lives by his pen) and the journalistic hacks of *Our Mutual Friend*.
