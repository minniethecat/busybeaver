# Glorious Apollos

The Glorious Apollos (also called the "Glorious Apollers") is a convivial fraternal lodge or drinking society in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]]. [[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]] is a member, and the society serves as a vehicle for Dickens's satirical treatment of masculine conviviality and its attendant pretensions.

## In the Novel

Swiveller is introduced as "one of that Lodge of Glorious Apollos whereof Mr. Swiveller was Perpetual Grand" [038-046]. The lodge is characterized by elaborate titles and rhetorical excess: Swiveller is referred to as "Perpetual Grand Master of the Glorious Apollers," elevating his hat in mock ceremony [013-052]. His speech adopts "an elevation of speech becoming a Glorious Apollo" [038-050], marked by grandiloquent declarations and mock-heroic posturing.

The lodge's rhetoric of brotherhood provides Swiveller with a social identity outside his precarious employment. "Enthusiasm were common among the Glorious Apollos," the narrator notes, "and were indeed the groundwork" of the society [056-014].

When Swiveller falls ill, his fellow Apollos abandon him. The narrator observes how he "hurried off and left the Glorious Apollos gazing at each other in silence" [056-043], exposing the hollowness of the society's professed fraternity.

## Satirical Function

The Glorious Apollos satirizes the culture of drinking clubs and mutual aid societies common in Victorian London. Its grandiose classical name ("Apollo" evoking the god of poetry and light) contrasts comically with the mundane reality of tavern drinking. The lodge's elaborate hierarchy and ceremonial language parody the pretensions of respectable society, while its members' failure to support Swiveller in crisis exposes the emptiness of its fraternal ideals.
