## Narrative Details

Quilp's relationship with the Thames is established early. He crosses the river routinely: "It was flood tide when [[daniel-quilp|Daniel Quilp]] sat himself down in the wherry to cross to the opposite shore" (005-024). His wharf is his domain — perfectly suited to a man who collects "the rents of whole colonies of filthy courts and alleys" (004-002).

The night of his death is enveloped in London fog. Pursued after the exposure of his conspiracy against Kit, Quilp flees through darkness. The streets are "filled with throngs of people — many in various stages of intoxication" (019-039), a hellish carnival through which the dwarf darts and stumbles. Cornered near his wharf, he loses his footing in the fog and plunges into the river and the thick Thames mud.

There is no rescue, no final speech — only the river closing over him, as indifferent to his evil as it was to his victims' suffering. The man who drowned others in misery is himself drowned, his body dragged from the mud that seems, in Dickens's rendering, to have been waiting for him all along.
