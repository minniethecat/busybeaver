## In the Novel

Temptation in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] is centered almost entirely on gambling — the "terrible temptations" (042-056) that undo the grandfather and set the novel's tragedy in motion. The grandfather's defense of his vice is couched in the language of benevolence: "All done to save money for tempting fortune, and make her triumph over the poverty of others" (009-060). This self-justifying rhetoric transforms temptation into a form of altruism, a charitable speculation that will enrich Nell. The irony is devastating: the grandfather's surrender to temptation is precisely what destroys Nell's security.

Dickens presents temptation as a spatial and temporal trap. The grandfather's gambling is enabled by his nocturnal excursions — the night becomes the time of temptation, the curiosity shop a place from which he slips away into moral danger. When Nell remembers "that the old man was with his tempters" (042-056), the plural form suggests a network of malign influence. Temptation is not merely internal weakness; it is a social phenomenon, mediated by the predatory figures who profit from others' susceptibility.

## Key Examples

- **[[grandfather-trent|Grandfather Trent]]**: The grandfather is the novel's central study in temptation. His gambling is not presented as casual vice but as consuming obsession — "the temptation before which he fell" (046-035). Dickens's language is deliberately biblical: temptation, fall, sin. The gambling table becomes an altar to false hope.
- **[[quilp|Quilp]]**: Quilp is the grandfather's tempter, though the novel complicates this role. "He was looked upon as his designing tempter when he was indeed nothing" (007-044) — Dickens suggests that Quilp is less the architect of the grandfather's ruin than its exploiter. Temptation originates within; the tempter merely profits from it.
- **Kit Nubbles**: Kit's resistance to temptation is shown through small moments. He refuses to be "tempted back again" (010-004) and maintains his integrity through minor trials, establishing the counterpoint to the grandfather's catastrophic failure.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens treats temptation as a moral test that reveals character rather than creates it. The grandfather's susceptibility to gambling is presented as a pre-existing weakness that temptation activates but does not originate. The narrative's sympathy for the grandfather — even as it condemns his actions — reflects Dickens's conviction that temptation is a universal human experience, and that moral judgment must account for circumstance and vulnerability.

The novel also registers temptation's comic dimension. Mr. Swiveller's "irresistible temptations" (066-002) to toast and ale are played for humor, establishing a spectrum from the trivial to the tragic. This tonal range — from Swiveller's gustatory weakness to the grandfather's soul-endangering gambling — is characteristic of Dickens's method: the same moral category operates across registers, binding high and low in a single ethical framework.

## Victorian Context

Victorian attitudes toward temptation were profoundly shaped by evangelical Christianity, which emphasized individual moral responsibility and the constant vigilance required to resist sin. Gambling, in particular, was a focus of moral reform movements in the 1830s and 1840s. Dickens's treatment of temptation is less theologically freighted than his evangelical contemporaries' but no less morally serious: he substitutes social observation for religious exhortation, showing temptation as a feature of a specific economic and social world rather than a universal spiritual condition.
