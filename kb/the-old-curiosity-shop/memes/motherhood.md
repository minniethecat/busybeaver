## In the Novel

Motherhood in *The Old Curiosity Shop* is defined largely by absence. Nell's mother died before the novel begins, and her loss haunts the narrative as the originating wound from which all subsequent suffering flows. The novel explores motherhood not through living maternal figures but through surrogates, substitutes, and the consequences of maternal failure.

> "Her mother was, and she was poor. I saved her from poverty" [001-044]

The grandfather's attempt to fill both paternal and maternal roles — to be, as he says, the one who "saved" Nell's mother — fails catastrophically. His gambling addiction undoes the protective maternal function he tries to perform, leaving Nell doubly orphaned.

## The Absent Mother

Nell's dead mother is the novel's structuring absence. The grandfather's narrative of the mother's death — "the miseries that brought her mother, my own dear child, to an early grave" [003-058] — establishes a causal chain: poverty caused the mother's death, and the mother's death caused the grandfather's desperate, self-destructive pursuit of wealth for Nell.

This absence defines Nell's character. Having never experienced maternal care, she becomes prematurely maternal herself — caring for her grandfather as a mother might care for a child. The inversion is both tender and tragic: the child who should be protected becomes the protector.

## Mrs. Jiniwin: The Present-but-Failed Mother

Mrs. Jiniwin, Mrs. Quilp's mother, is the novel's most prominent living maternal figure — and a study in maternal failure. She lives with the Quilps and witnesses her daughter's abuse, yet responds not with protection but with complicity:

> "Oh! he was a bad one from the beginning, you may depend upon it" [004-006]

Her maternal energy is redirected into resentment of Quilp rather than protection of her daughter. When she does confront Quilp, her defiance is performative rather than effective. She represents a motherhood that is physically present but functionally absent — a body in the house but no shelter for the child within it.

## Surrogate Mothers

The novel compensates for maternal absence through a series of surrogate figures. Mrs. Jarley, the waxwork proprietress, offers Nell the closest approximation to maternal care — providing food, shelter, and a form of employment that protects rather than exploits:

> "she had shown them so much homely kindness" [043-003]

The widow Jarley's maternal qualities — protectiveness, practical care, the desire to keep Nell safe — fill, however briefly, the void left by the dead mother. Other figures serve similar surrogate functions: the schoolmaster's care, the kindness of strangers along the road, even the elderly woman who tends Nell in her final illness.

## The Grandfather's Failed Maternity

The novel's most complex exploration of motherhood involves the grandfather, who attempts to fill a maternal role he is constitutionally unsuited to perform. His protective instinct is real — his love for Nell is not in question — but his execution is disastrous. He cannot provide the steady, self-sacrificial care that motherhood traditionally demands:

> "he told me about my mother, and how she once looked and spoke" [006-061]

When Nell says these words to Kit, she reveals that the grandfather's maternal performance consists largely of talking about the real mother, not replacing her. His gambling is not merely a financial failure but a maternal one — it betrays the trust that is motherhood's foundational element.
