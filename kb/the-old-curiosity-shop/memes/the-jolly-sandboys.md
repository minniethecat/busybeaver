# The Jolly Sandboys

The Jolly Sandboys is a small roadside inn of "pretty ancient date" that serves as a gathering place for travelling showpeople and a key setting in Nell and her grandfather's early wanderings. Its sign — "representing three Sandboys increasing their jollity with as many jugs of ale and bags of gold" — creaks and swings on its post opposite the road, a cheerful beacon for weary travellers [015-004].

## Description and Atmosphere

The inn embodies warmth and hospitality amid the novel's wandering narrative. The very clock has "a colour in its fat white face and looked a clock for Jolly Sandboys to consult" [015-005]. The landlord is obliging, the kitchen fire roars, and a "prospect of cold meat and beer" serves as "a piece of consolatory eloquence" to rain-soaked travellers [015-004].

The inn sits on the route to the race town, and as Nell and her grandfather approach it, they observe "gipsy camps, carts laden with gambling booths and their appurtenances, itinerant showmen of various kinds, and beggars and trampers of every degree" — all streaming toward the same destination [015-004].

## The Gathering of Showpeople

The Jolly Sandboys is most memorable for the extraordinary assembly of travelling entertainers who converge there. Among them are:

- **[[codlin-and-short|Codlin and Short]]**: The Punch and Judy men, who insist on stopping at the Sandboys — "I won't go further than the mile and a half tonight. I put up at the Jolly Sandboys and nowhere else" [015-001].
- **Jerry's performing dogs**: Four "very dismal dogs" in gaudy spangled coats, led by an "old bandy dog of particularly mournful aspect," who perform tricks on their hind legs [015-008].
- **Vuffin**: The proprietor of a giant and "a little lady without legs or arms."
- **Sweet William**: A silent gentleman who performs card tricks, so named "probably as a pleasant satire upon his ugliness" [015-010].

## Role in the Journey

The Jolly Sandboys represents a moment of relative comfort and community in Nell and her grandfather's otherwise harsh journey. Here, Nell encounters the travelling showpeople who will later employ them, and here the novel pauses to paint a vivid tableau of the strange, marginal world of Victorian itinerant entertainers — a world Dickens knew well and depicted with affectionate precision.

The morning after the storm, the travellers leave the inn into a world transformed: "The morning was fine and warm, the ground cool to the feet after the late rain, the hedges gayer and more green, the air clear, and everything fresh and healthful" [015-012].
