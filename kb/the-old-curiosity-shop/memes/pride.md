Pride in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] is a double-edged virtue: capable of sustaining dignity under conditions of poverty and humiliation, yet also capable of generating the novel's central tragedy. The grandfather's pride — his refusal to accept charity, his insistence on restoring their fortune through gambling — destroys his family as surely as Quilp's active malice. Against this destructive pride, Dickens sets the "becoming pride" (031-057) of the humble: [[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]'s pride in honest work, the schoolmaster's pride in his vocation, the quiet self-respect that enables characters to endure adversity without losing themselves.

## In the Novel

The novel's most consequential pride is the grandfather's. His determination to restore their fortune through gambling is driven not by greed but by a pride that cannot tolerate dependence: he will not accept what he cannot repay. The narrator explicitly identifies this as "the pride that libels our own old happy state" (012-018), linking the grandfather's personal failing to a broader human tendency to prefer self-destruction over the admission of need. This pride is the tragic flaw from which every subsequent catastrophe flows.

[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]] embodies the virtuous counterpoint. His mother feels "great joy and pride" (038-005) in his honest employment, and his own "pride in his appearance" (022-012) reflects not vanity but the dignity of the working poor who maintain themselves through labor. The narrator's distinction — between the pride that isolates and the pride that sustains — is central to the novel's moral vision. Even [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]], for all her commercial cynicism, takes "pride" in her waxwork exhibition (027-002, 054-001) that is presented as legitimate satisfaction in one's work rather than self-importance.

## Key Examples

- **[[grandfather-trent|Grandfather Trent]]**: His destructive pride drives the novel's tragedy. "Blush for the pride that libels our own old happy state" (012-018) — the narrator's direct moral commentary on the grandfather's inability to accept his reduced circumstances.
- **[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]**: Kit's pride in honest work — "a sufficient pride in his appearance" (022-012) — represents the novel's alternative model of self-respect. His pride is grounded in earning his way, not in refusing help.
- **[[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]**: Her pride in her waxworks — "its country's pride" (027-050) — is treated affectionately by Dickens as the legitimate satisfaction of an honest tradeswoman, distinct from the arrogance of wealth or status.
- **[[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]**: His pride is the professional vanity of the corrupt lawyer — pride in the manipulation of legal forms rather than in justice served. Dickens consistently exposes professional pride as a mask for moral failure.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of pride reflects a nuanced moral psychology. He recognizes pride as a legitimate response to poverty and social contempt — the working poor must have pride to survive a world that denies their dignity — while also diagnosing its destructive forms. The grandfather's pride is not condemned as simple arrogance but understood as a deformation of a legitimate impulse: the desire to provide, to be worthy of love. Dickens's innovation is to trace how a virtue, pushed to excess by desperate circumstances, becomes the engine of tragedy.

> "Blush for the pride that libels our own old happy state" — the narrator's indictment of a pride that would rather destroy itself than accept help.
>
> (012-018)

## Victorian Context

Pride was a charged concept in Victorian moral discourse, carrying the theological weight of the deadliest of the seven deadly sins while also being invoked as a necessary component of working-class respectability. The concept of "honest pride" — pride in one's work, one's family, one's independence — was central to the Victorian ideal of the deserving poor, distinguishing them from the "undeserving" who lacked self-respect. Dickens's treatment of pride in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] navigates this tension, condemning the pride that refuses help while honoring the pride that sustains dignity under conditions of deprivation. This nuanced treatment reflected Dickens's own ambivalent experience of social class: a man who had risen from the blacking factory to literary fame, whose pride in his achievement never fully erased the shame of his origins.
