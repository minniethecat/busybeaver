# Mr. Abel

Mr. Abel (full name Abel Garland) is a clerk articled to [[Mr. Witherden]]'s notary practice at [[Bevis Marks]]. Described as having a gentle and mild disposition, he serves as an employer and benefactor to [[Kit Nubbles]] and is closely associated with the Garland family at [[Abel Cottage]]. His surname gives the cottage its name, and his steady, reliable presence provides a moral counterpoint to the novel's more volatile and corrupt characters.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Abel functions as a link between the legal world of [[Bevis Marks]] and the domestic world of [[Abel Cottage]]. He is introduced during the signing of his articles of clerkship, where [[Mr. Witherden]] declares that "Mr. Abel's feelings did credit to his nature" (014-030). This formal endorsement establishes him early as a character of genuine worth rather than mere professional competence.

He later becomes [[Kit Nubbles]]'s employer when Kit is hired to drive him daily to the notary's office in a pony chaise (038-008). Through this arrangement, Abel provides Kit with honest employment and regular "small remittance[s]" (038-005), becoming one of the novel's quiet benefactors. His presence also connects the Garland household to the resolution of the grandfather's financial affairs.

## Character Traits

Mr. Abel is consistently characterized by his mildness and quiet dignity. He speaks "mildly" (038-019) and is noted for his "liberality" toward Kit (038-005), providing small financial support that makes a meaningful difference in Kit's life. He is described as an "indispensable attendant" to [[Mr. Witherden]] (038-007), suggesting diligence and professional competence.

Physically, Abel is depicted with "a quaint old-fashioned air" (014-032), and his travel arrangements — riding in "a little box behind" the chaise (014-033) — convey a modest, almost comic humility. When traveling with the old lady and Kit, he sits "looking very hard at Kit" and lifting "up his eyes by stealth" (021-002), observing the young man's character with characteristic quietness.

> "I'm much obliged to you, sir, I'm sure," replied Mr. Abel mildly.
>
> (038-019)

> Mr. Abel's feelings did credit to his nature.
>
> — Mr. Witherden, (014-030)

## Key Relationships

- **[[Mr. Witherden]]**: His employer and the notary whose articles he serves (014-025). Witherden provides a letter of recommendation for Abel, and their professional relationship is one of mutual respect.
- **[[Kit Nubbles]]**: Kit becomes Mr. Abel's driver and attendant, receiving daily kindness and small remittances (038-005, 038-007, 038-008). Abel's liberality provides Kit with a measure of financial security.
- **[[Mr. Garland]]** and **[[Mrs. Garland]]**: Close family friends at [[Abel Cottage]] (038-001, 038-006). Abel is a regular presence in their domestic circle.
- **[[Barbara]]**: A member of the Garland household with whom Mr. Abel shares domestic life (038-006), sitting together during evenings at the cottage.

## Significant Appearances

- **First appearance** (Chapter 14): Articles of clerkship at the Bevis Marks office, where his parents' pride in him is noted — "he had been a greater comfort to his parents than Abel Garland had been to his" (014-023).
- **Traveling with Kit** (Chapters 20–21): Abel rides with the old lady and observes Kit, lifting "up his eyes by stealth" (021-002), quietly assessing the young man's character.
- **Daily routine** (Chapter 38): Kit drives Abel to the Notary's office each morning in the pony chaise (038-008), and Abel provides Kit with small remittances through his "liberality" (038-005).
- **Domestic life** (Chapter 38): Present with the Garlands when Kit visits, sharing in the household's quiet contentment (038-001).

## Critical Interpretation

Mr. Abel belongs to the novel's network of benevolent minor characters whose quiet generosity sustains the protagonists. His mildness and liberality offer a moral contrast to the grasping legal figures of [[Sampson Brass]] and [[Sally Brass]]. The [[Bevis Marks]] office under Abel and [[Mr. Witherden]] represents a form of professional life governed by integrity rather than exploitation — a small but significant counterweight to the novel's broader critique of legal corruption.

Abel's role as Kit's employer is particularly significant: where [[Quilp]] exploits and torments, Abel supports and liberates. His quiet observation of Kit's character — conveyed through Dickens's understated physical description — shows how the novel's moral judgments are distributed across its minor characters as well as its protagonists.
