# Sally Brass

The formidable sister and legal partner of [[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]. Miss Sally Brass is one of Dickens's most memorably grotesque female characters — a gaunt, sharp-tongued woman who serves as her brother's clerk, housekeeper, and co-conspirator in the schemes orchestrated by Quilp.

## Role in the Novel

Sally Brass is an active participant in the conspiracy against [[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]], using her intelligence and ruthlessness in the service of Quilp's vendetta.

## Character Traits

"Gaunt and bony figure, and a resolute bearing" — a woman of formidable intelligence and zero warmth, who has devoted her life to the law and to the management of her weak-willed brother.

## Key Relationships

- **[[sampson-brass]]** — Her brother, whom she dominates
- **[[daniel-quilp]]** — The client whose schemes she facilitates
- **[[the-marchioness]]** — The servant girl she abuses
- **[[dick-swiveller]]** — Her brother's clerk

## Significant Appearances

Sally Brass appears throughout the [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]] chapters (XXXIII onwards), where she is a constant, intimidating presence in the Brass establishment.

## Narrative Details

Sally Brass is introduced with a description that brooks no argument: "Miss Sally Brass, then, was a lady of thirty-five or thereabouts, of a gaunt and bony figure, and a resolute bearing, which if it repressed the softer emotions of love, and kept admirers at a distance, certainly inspired a feeling akin to awe in the breasts of those male strangers who had the happiness to approach her" (033-006).

Her relationship with her brother is the defining dynamic of her life. Dickens catalogues her roles with devastating precision: she is Sampson's "clerk, assistant, housekeeper, secretary, confidential plotter, adviser, intriguer, and bill of costs increaser" (033-005). She has "from her earliest youth devoted herself with uncommon ardour to the study of the law" (033-007), and in her presence even her brother's name is softened — "in her mild and feminine lips, Sampson became Sammy, and all things were softened down" (033-009).

Her exchanges with her brother are a study in domestic tyranny. She takes pleasure in irritating him: "All I know is, that if every one of your clients is to force us to keep a clerk, you may as well make up your mind to get rid of your practice at once" (033-015). When Quilp declares that Sally is "the woman I ought to have married — there is the beautiful Sally Brass" (033-032), she responds with a "grim smile" and tells him to "hold your nonsense" (033-035).

Sally is central to the framing of Kit — she is the one who plants the evidence and maintains the fiction. When the conspiracy collapses, her fate mirrors her brother's: disgrace and flight.
