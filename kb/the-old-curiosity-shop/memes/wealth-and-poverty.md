## In the Novel

Wealth and poverty in *The Old Curiosity Shop* are not simply economic conditions but moral states. The curiosity shop itself — a repository of objects whose value has decayed — stands as a material metaphor for the instability of wealth. The grandfather's fixation on restoring the family fortune through gambling transforms the pursuit of wealth into the engine of tragedy:

> "the same delusion which had been the cause of all their misery" (029-059)

Dickens presents a world where money is simultaneously necessary for survival and spiritually corrosive — a tension that no character fully resolves.

## The Gambler's Paradox

The grandfather's relationship to wealth embodies a cruel paradox: he gambles to provide for Nell, yet each wager brings them closer to destitution. His dream of wealth is, from its inception, a source of poverty:

> "a man who, shrinking from the idea of involving her in their ruin, had recourse to the gaming-table" (034-006)

This framing makes the grandfather a figure of pathos rather than simple moral failure — his pursuit of wealth is inseparable from his love for his granddaughter, even as that pursuit destroys what it means to protect.

## Wealth and Character

The novel distributes wealth according to a moral logic. Quilp, the novel's most predatory figure, is also its most overtly commercial — a usurer and rentier whose wealth derives from the exploitation of others. By contrast, the Garlands, who possess modest but secure means, use their resources to extend hospitality and protection. The Marchioness, the novel's figure of absolute material deprivation, emerges as one of its most resilient characters, suggesting that poverty — while brutal — does not diminish human worth.

## Poverty as Moral Test

The journey of Nell and her grandfather through the industrial Midlands places poverty at the centre of the novel's moral vision. The beggars, furnace-workers, and roadside entertainers they encounter are not mere background but active participants in a social argument. Each encounter with poverty tests the travellers' — and the reader's — capacity for compassion:

> "many fell down before the fire, and were carried out" (044-027)

The industrial poor are shown as labouring bodies consumed by the same economic forces that drive the grandfather's gambling — a system that converts human beings into fuel.

## The Economic Uncanny

The curiosity shop itself represents what might be called the economic uncanny: objects that once held value but are now "old and curious" — caught between commodity and relic. The shop's dissolution mirrors the grandfather's financial collapse, suggesting that the line between wealth and poverty is not only thin but spectral, haunted by the objects that survive it.
