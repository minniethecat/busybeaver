# The Doctor

The Doctor is a red-nosed medical gentleman who appears in the later chapters of the novel, treating the Marchioness when she falls ill and later becoming a fixture in the household established by the bachelor and the single gentleman. His pragmatic bedside manner and professional gravity provide a note of comic relief amid the novel's domestic crises.

## Role in the Novel

The Doctor is summoned when the small servant known as the Marchioness falls dangerously ill after her ordeal in the Brasses' household. Through his medical interventions, she recovers and finds a new life. The Doctor later attends the bachelor in his later years, becoming part of the quiet domestic circle that concludes the novel. His presence signals the restoration of order and care after the chaos of the Quilp and Brass episodes (046-009, 073-015).

## Character Traits

The Doctor is first described as "a red-nosed gentleman with a great bunch of seals dangling below a waistcoat of ribbed black satin" (046-009). He speaks with deliberate, professional gravity — "I should give her a teaspoonful" (046-010) — and rises with ceremonial solemnity to deliver his prescriptions (046-014). His portentous manner ("Ay," said the doctor, in the tone of a man who had much experience) is gently satirized, but his medical advice proves sound, and the Marchioness's recovery vindicates his treatment.

## Key Relationships

- **[[The Marchioness]]**: His primary patient, whom he treats with a regimen of rest, nourishment, and "a teaspoonful" of medicine (046-009 to 046-014).
- **[[The Bachelor]]**: The Doctor becomes a regular visitor and later household member, attending the bachelor in his later years (073-015).
- **[[Dick Swiveller]]**: The Doctor treats the Marchioness while she is under Swiveller's care, and Swiveller reports on her progress.

## Significant Appearances

- First appearance: Called to treat the Marchioness during her illness (046-008)
- Major scene: Delivering his prescription with solemn professional authority (046-009 to 046-017)
- Later life: Living with the bachelor as a household companion (073-015)

## Critical Interpretation

The Doctor belongs to a Dickensian tradition of medical caricatures — figures whose professional gravity is undercut by physical comedy (the red nose, the dangling seals). Yet beneath the satire, he fulfills an essential narrative function: his successful treatment of the Marchioness marks the turning point from the novel's darkest passages toward its redemptive conclusion. His later role in the bachelor's household completes this arc, placing medicine alongside domestic affection as a force for healing.
