## In the Novel

Disguise in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] is both a narrative device and a thematic preoccupation. Characters don literal and figurative masks; truths are concealed and revealed; the world of the novel is one in which surface appearances are rarely to be trusted. The narrator observes early on that the old man "had preserved an undisguised surprise" (001-104), establishing a world in which frankness of emotion is the exception rather than the rule.

The most theatrical deployment of disguise occurs in the waxwork exhibition, where Mrs. Jarley's figures wear "various disguises, protesting aloud that the shew would shortly open" (032-021). This scene literalizes the novel's concern with the relationship between surface and substance — the waxworks are themselves a kind of disguise, standing in for absent living figures, just as many of the novel's characters present false faces to the world.

Quilp's mode of being is itself a kind of perpetual disguise. The narrator notes that his intentions were "by no means so scrupulously disguised as he had led the schoolmaster to suppose" (052-052), suggesting that even Quilp's deceptions are imperfect — his nature is so grotesque it resists concealment. The pony in the later chapters "had now thrown off all disguise, and without any mincing of the matter" (057-002) becomes a comic emblem of revelation — even animals, in this novel, participate in the drama of concealment and exposure.

## Key Examples

- **[[quilp|Quilp]]**: Quilp's surface politeness masks his predatory intentions — his deceptive nature is so thoroughgoing that even his honest moments are suspected of being disguised (052-052).
- **[[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]**: The waxwork exhibition literalizes the theme of disguise, with figures "under various disguises" (032-021) that mirror the performative identities of the living characters who surround them.
- **[[the-single-gentleman|The Single Gentleman]]**: The bachelor's concealed identity as Nell's great-uncle makes him the novel's most benign instance of disguise — a concealment that, when revealed, brings restoration rather than harm.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens treats disguise as symptomatic of a world in which social relations are mediated by performance. The Victorian marketplace, the legal system (embodied by the Brasses), and even the domestic sphere are spaces where people present false versions of themselves. Yet the novel insists that disguise is ultimately unsustainable — truth emerges, whether through the testimony of the Marchioness, the revelation of the bachelor's identity, or the self-destruction of Quilp.

The contrast between benign and malignant disguise is crucial to Dickens's moral scheme. The bachelor's concealment of his identity is protective — he watches over Nell from a distance, his disguise serving love rather than exploitation. Quilp's deceptions, by contrast, serve only domination. Dickens thus distinguishes not between disguise and honesty, but between the motives that drive concealment.

> They related to him without disguise or concealment, all that they knew.
>
> (047-047)
