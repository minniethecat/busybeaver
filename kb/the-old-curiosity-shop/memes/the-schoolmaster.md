# The Schoolmaster

A kindly village teacher who shelters Nell and her grandfather. The schoolmaster is one of the many figures of quiet goodness whom Nell and her grandfather encounter on their wanderings, a man whose simple kindness offers brief respite.

## Role in the Novel

The schoolmaster represents the provincial decency that stands in contrast to the urban corruption Nell has fled — a world of small communities, cricket on the green, and uncalculating hospitality.

## Character Traits

A kind face, a thoughtful manner, and the gentle authority of one who has spent his life among children.

- **[[nell-trent]]** — The child he shelters

## Significant Appearances

The schoolmaster appears in Chapter XXIV, providing Nell and her grandfather with their most peaceful interlude since fleeing London.

## Narrative Details

Nell and her grandfather encounter the schoolmaster in a small village where the men and boys were "playing at cricket on the green" (024-014). The schoolmaster sits "thoughtful and silent, in the little porch" and does not at first notice the weary travellers. The narrator notes that "he had a kind face. In his plain old suit of black, he looked like a gentleman" (024-017).

Nell is hesitant to approach him, perceiving "something in his manner which seemed to denote that he was uneasy or distressed" (024-018). When she finally speaks, "dropp[ing] a curtsey" and explaining that they were "poor travellers who sought a shelter for the night," the schoolmaster responds with quiet kindness (024-020).

The schoolmaster's own story is one of loss — he has recently buried a favourite pupil, and his melancholy is the sadness of a man who gave his heart to his work. His sympathy for Nell is immediate and genuine; he perceives in the child something of the innocence he has just mourned. The shelter he provides — simple food, a clean bed, and the peace of a village removed from the world's noise — represents one of the rare moments of genuine safety Nell experiences on her journey.
