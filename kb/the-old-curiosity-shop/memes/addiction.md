## In the Novel

Addiction in *The Old Curiosity Shop* is most powerfully embodied in the figure of Nell's grandfather, whose compulsive gambling becomes the engine of the novel's tragedy. His addiction is not presented as a mere character flaw but as a consuming force that transforms a loving guardian into an agent of destruction.

> "he, gambling with such a savage thirst for gain as the most insatiable gambler never felt" (029-059)

The grandfather's fixation on winning a fortune for his granddaughter — an apparently benevolent motive — is warped by addiction into a compulsion that destroys precisely what it seeks to protect. Nell becomes acutely aware that the addiction controls him:

> "losses and gains were to her... the sources of perpetual terror" (029-059)

The gambling booths and games that appear at the racecourse represent addiction's external, public face — a world of chance that seduces and ruins:

> "carts laden with gambling booths and their appurtenances" (018-001)

These scenes frame gambling not as an individual moral failing alone but as a systemic social ill, embedded in Victorian popular entertainment.

## The Corrupted Guardian

The grandfather's addiction inverts the natural order of the guardian–child relationship. Rather than protecting Nell, he becomes the source of her peril:

> "Many things he had said which led me to suppose that he was a wealthy man" (003-059)

The grandfather's secret life is built upon the fiction of wealth, sustained by the desperate hope that the next wager will restore what has been lost. This is the psychology of addiction in its purest form: the conviction that one more gamble will solve the problem created by gambling itself.

## Addiction as Thematic Engine

Beyond the grandfather, the novel presents addiction as a broader moral metaphor. Quilp's obsessive cruelty, Fred Trent's compulsive scheming, and the various characters who pursue wealth at any cost all exhibit the hallmarks of addiction: loss of control, disregard for consequences, and the corruption of initially legitimate desires.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens, writing in 1840–41, was acutely aware of gambling's destructive power in Victorian society. The Racing scenes and the gambling booths evoke a world familiar to his readers — a world of demobilised soldiers, travelling shows, and the precariousness of working-class entertainment that could easily tip into ruin. The grandfather's addiction is treated with a mixture of pity and moral clarity, characteristic of Dickens's approach to social ills.
