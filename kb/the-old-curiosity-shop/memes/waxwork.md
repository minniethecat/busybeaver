# Waxwork

Mrs. Jarley's Waxwork is a travelling exhibition of life-sized wax figures that appears in the novel as both a source of employment for [[nell-trent|Nell Trent]] and a richly symbolic element in Dickens's exploration of appearance, reality, and popular entertainment. The waxwork is a curious institution — part museum, part theatre, part fairground attraction — presided over by the formidable and self-important [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]].

## Nell's Introduction to the Waxwork

Nell first encounters the waxwork when she reads aloud "in enormous black letters, the inscription, 'Jarley's Waxwork'" painted on the side of a caravan [027-009]. Having never seen a waxwork before, she asks naively, "Is it funnier than Punch?" — to which Mrs. Jarley responds with considerable indignation, insisting that waxwork is a far superior form of entertainment: "No low beatings and knockings about, no jokings and squeakings like your precious Punches, but always the same, with a constantly unchanging air of coldness and gentility" [027-020].

Mrs. Jarley makes the striking claim that "if waxwork only spoke and walked about, you'd hardly know the difference from life" — but adds the pointed qualification: "I won't go so far as to say, that, as it is, I've seen waxwork quite like life, but I've certainly seen some life that was exactly like waxwork" [027-020]. This wry observation encapsulates the novel's recurring theme of the blurred boundary between the animate and the inanimate, the real and the artificial.

## The Exhibition

The waxwork exhibition includes a remarkable assortment of figures: historical celebrities, murderers, and allegorical representations. Mrs. Jarley herself is "the original Jarley" — a direct descendant, as she claims, of the creator of the original waxwork collection. Her caravan travels the country, setting up in town halls and assembly rooms, drawing crowds "of all ranks" who come to marvel at the lifeless likenesses.

The waxwork becomes a gathering point for the local community. "Grownup folks began to be interested in the bright-eyed girl, and some score of little boys fell desperately in love, and constantly left enclosures of nuts and apples, directed in small-text, at the waxwork door" [029-001].

## Nell as the Waxwork Child

Mrs. Jarley employs Nell as "the waxwork child" — a guide and attendant to the exhibition. Nell's gentle manner and bright eyes prove a valuable asset to the waxwork's appeal. However, her role also subjects her to the scorn of [[miss-monflathers|Miss Monflathers]], the formidable schoolmistress, who demands to know: "Don't you feel how naughty it is of you...to be a waxwork child, when you might have the proud consciousness of assisting, to the extent of your infant powers, the manufactures of your country?" [031-042].

## Symbolic Dimension

The waxwork serves as a rich metaphor within the novel. The figures, "so like life" yet utterly lifeless, mirror several of Dickens's living characters — figures like the waxwork-obsessed [[daniel-quilp|Quilp]], who in Nell's fevered dreams "was somehow connected with the waxwork, or was waxwork himself" [027-064]. The waxwork's "constantly unchanging air of coldness and gentility" becomes a commentary on the artificiality of social pretension, while Nell's lively presence among the wax figures underscores the contrast between genuine vitality and its lifeless imitation.
