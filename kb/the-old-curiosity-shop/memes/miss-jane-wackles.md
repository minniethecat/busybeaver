# Miss Jane Wackles

Miss Jane Wackles is the eldest daughter of [[Mrs. Wackles]], the widowed schoolmistress of a Chelsea day school. Proud, sharp-tongued, and socially conscious, Jane embodies the petty snobbery of the lower-middle-class establishment that Dickens satirises throughout his early fiction. Her role is primarily comic: she serves as an obstacle and a foil in the courtship plot between her younger sister [[Miss Sophy Wackles]] and [[Dick Swiveller]].

## Role in the Novel

Miss Jane appears in the Chelsea chapters (7–8), where Dick Swiveller, acting as an emissary for his friend [[Fred Trent]], attempts to deliver a letter to Sophy Wackles. Jane, having been "previously instructed for the purpose" (008-024), intercepts the messenger and delivers a prepared speech designed to rebuff him. The scene is one of Dickens's set-piece comic interludes — the pompous elder sister performing a scripted dismissal, while Dick responds with characteristic insouciance.

Dick's narration introduces Jane with a comic flourish, noting "the unwonted curls of Miss Jane Wackles who had kept her head during the whole of the preceding day screwed up tight in a yellow play-bill" (008-014) — a detail that captures her vanity and the threadbare gentility of the Wackles establishment.

## Character Traits

Jane's defining trait is her **pride** — a pride founded on the slenderest of social distinctions. As the eldest daughter of a day-school mistress, she occupies a precarious rung on the social ladder and defends it fiercely. Her prepared speech to Dick — "Take care, Sir, take care! What would the world say if they ever heard of such a thing as Miss Sophy Wackles receiving letters!" (008-026) — reveals a mind governed by propriety and the fear of scandal.

She also displays **sharpness of tongue**. When Sophy attempts to intervene with a gentle "Oh pray Jane—" (008-027), Jane overrides her, and Dickens notes that she was "one of those young ladies who are not to be prevailed upon" (008-029). She is both a guardian of her sister's reputation and, implicitly, a rival — the elder daughter who has not attracted a suitor of her own.

> Here Miss Jane (previously instructed for the purpose) rose and delivered a speech of some length, in which she stated that as the person who had the care of Miss Sophy Wackles she must be permitted to say, that she considered the receipt of letters from comparative strangers a proceeding of a most unjustifiable and unparalleled nature.
>
> (008-024)

## Key Relationships

- **[[Miss Sophy Wackles]]**: Jane's younger sister and rival. Jane simultaneously protects Sophy's reputation and envies her romantic prospects (008-026–029).
- **[[Mrs. Wackles]]**: Jane's widowed mother, the proprietress of the Chelsea day school. Jane assists in running the establishment. "Neither Mrs. Wackles nor her eldest daughter had any great affection for the younger sister" (008-015).
- **[[Dick Swiveller]]**: Jane delivers a formal rebuke to Dick when he attempts to deliver Fred Trent's letter to Sophy. Dick treats her with amused condescension (008-024–026).

## Significant Appearances

- First appearance: Chapter 7, described through Dick's narrative as he approaches the Wackles establishment (007-049–053)
- Major scene: Chapter 8, her prepared speech rebuking Dick Swiveller (008-024–029)
- The Wackles household dynamics: Chapter 8, revealing the family's internal rivalries (008-014–015)

## Critical Interpretation

Miss Jane Wackles belongs to a recognisable Dickensian type: the **proud elder sister** whose pretensions supply comedy while exposing the anxieties of a class-conscious society. Like Miss Monflathers — a more formidable version of the same type — Jane polices the boundaries of respectability, but her authority is undermined by Dickens's ironic narration. The detail of her curls "screwed up tight in a yellow play-bill" captures the pathos beneath the comedy: she is a young woman performing gentility with limited means.

In the novel's broader structure, the Wackles interlude — and Jane's role within it — provides comic relief before the darker chapters of Nell's journey. The courtship plot that Jane obstructs is a miniature of the marriage-market comedies that Dickens would develop more fully in later novels.
