## In the Novel

Revenge in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] operates as a subterranean current of motivation, driving character action and revealing the moral landscape of Dickens's world. Kit Nubbles, typically presented as the novel's embodiment of loyalty and good nature, is not immune to vengeful impulses: "the boy revenged himself by dancing on his head" (006-038). This comic, almost childish revenge serves to humanize Kit — even the novel's moral exemplar has moments of petty retaliation, and Dickens includes them without condemnation.

Quilp, by contrast, embodies revenge as a principle of being. His every interaction is charged with the threat of retaliation. When the old man speaks of Quilp giving him "his revenge to the last farthing he brought me back" (042-029), the language of revenge is entangled with economic exploitation — Quilp's financial power is inseparable from his capacity for punitive action. The narrator remarks that "more than half-disposed to revenge the fact upon him on the mere suspicion" (006-079), capturing the paranoid, hair-trigger quality of vengeful thinking that pervades Quilp's world.

## Key Examples

- **[[quilp|Quilp]]**: Quilp's relationship with the grandfather is explicitly framed in terms of revenge — the old man says "He gives me my revenge" (042-041), an inversion in which the victim claims agency through the language of retaliation even as he remains in Quilp's power.
- **[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]**: Kit's dance of revenge on Quilp's hat (006-038) is the novel's gentlest form of retaliation — comic, symbolic, and immediately forgiven — establishing revenge as a spectrum from the petty to the demonic.
- **[[the-marchioness|The Marchioness]]**: Though not named as "revenge," her eventual testimony against the Brasses carries the structure of long-deferred retaliation against those who starved and exploited her.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens treats revenge as a morally diagnostic emotion: the way a character handles (or resists) the impulse to retaliate reveals their ethical center. Kit's petty revenge is forgivable because it is proportionate and comic; Quilp's systematic vengeance is monstrous because it is boundless. This gradation reflects Dickens's broader moral framework, in which emotions are not condemned in themselves but judged by their intensity, duration, and consequences.

The novel also suggests that revenge can be transmuted into justice. The Marchioness's testimony, while motivated by personal grievance, serves the cause of truth and liberates Kit from false accusation. Dickens thus draws a distinction between private revenge (destructive, Quilp-like) and the public airing of wrongs that serves the communal good.
