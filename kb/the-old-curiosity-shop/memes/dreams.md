## In the Novel

Dreams in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] serve as portals to the inner lives of characters, revealing anxieties and desires that waking consciousness suppresses. The novel opens in a dreamlike register: the narrator describes "restless dreams, as if he were condemned to lie awake" (001-003), immediately establishing the permeable boundary between sleeping and waking that will characterize the entire narrative. The old curiosity shop itself is described as a place "designed in dreams" (001-028), its very architecture a product of the unconscious imagination.

Nell Trent is the novel's central dreamer, and her dreams map her psychological journey. As she and her grandfather flee London, her dreams are of protection and comfort: "she had slept so peacefully, and dreamed such pleasant dreams" (012-039). These peaceful dreams are contrasted with the nightmares that intrude later — "dreams of falling from high towers" (030-026) that register her growing fear and vulnerability. Quilp invades her sleeping consciousness: "throughout her uneasy dreams was somehow connected with the dwarf" (027-064), his monstrous presence crossing the boundary that should separate waking from sleeping.

The novel also deploys dreams as a figure for storytelling itself. When the narrator says of the old man "little dreaming of the story that lay hidden" (009-003), dreaming becomes a metaphor for narrative ignorance — the character, like the reader, does not know what story they are in until it has unfolded around them.

## Key Examples

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: Nell's dreams chart her psychological development — from peaceful visions of "light and sunny dreams" (001-111) to nightmares of falling and Quilp's intrusion, her dreaming life mirrors her waking journey.
- **[[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]]**: Swiveller "dreaming that he had married Nelly Trent" (008-056) reveals a submerged desire that his waking persona would never acknowledge — dreams as the unconscious speaking.
- **The little scholar**: Nell's dreams of the schoolmaster's dead pupil — "her dreams were of the little scholar" (026-003) — suggest that dreams are the novel's mechanism for processing grief and loss.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens uses dreams to expand the novel's psychological range. The waking world of *The Old Curiosity Shop* is governed by Victorian propriety and narrative convention; the dream world grants access to desires, fears, and impulses that cannot be directly stated. Swiveller's dream of marrying Nell, for instance, registers a romantic possibility that the novel's realist surface would never acknowledge.

The novel's dream aesthetics also connect it to the Gothic tradition. The recurring imagery of "wild grotesque companions" (001-109) in dreams evokes the nightmare visions of Gothic fiction, populated by "horribly grotesque and distorted" figures (005-021) that seem to step from the pages of a supernatural tale into the realist novel. Dickens thus uses dreams to smuggle Gothic intensity into a narrative that otherwise adheres to the conventions of domestic realism.
