# Nell's Flight from London

Nell and her grandfather flee London to escape Quilp and their creditors. This event marks a significant turning point in the narrative of [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]].

## Narrative Details

The flight is portrayed with intimate detail. Nell prepares through the night: "The old man had slept for some hours soundly in his bed, and she was yet busily engaged in preparing for their flight. There were a few articles of clothing for herself to carry, and a few for the old man" (012-037). The child, who should be protected, becomes the protector.

They slip out of London before the city wakes. Nell's resolve crystallizes in a moment of desperate clarity: "Let us be beggars, and be happy" (009-015). There is no sentimentality — only a child's determination to save what can be saved. Quilp, discovering their absence, is "both surprised and troubled by the flight" and notes that "indispensable articles of clothing were missing" (013-041).

The flight transforms both characters. The grandfather, stripped of his shop and illusions, depends entirely on Nell. She, forced into premature adulthood, discovers courage she did not know she possessed. The road ahead — hunger, exhaustion, predatory strangers — is also the only road away from Quilp, and any road away from Quilp is, for Nell, a road toward freedom.

> "What if we are," said the child boldly. "Let us be beggars, and be happy."
>
> (009-015)
