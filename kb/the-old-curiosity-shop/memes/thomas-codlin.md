# Thomas Codlin

A Punch-and-Judy showman. Thomas Codlin is a travelling entertainer who, along with his partner [[short-trotters|Short Trotters]], encounters Nell and her grandfather on the road and becomes part of their wandering narrative.

## Role in the Novel

Codlin and Short represent one of the many pockets of English life through which Nell and her grandfather pass, contributing to the novel's episodic, pilgrim-like structure.

## Character Traits

Defined by a surly, misanthropic temperament — a "discontented philosopher" who sees the worst in every situation.

## Key Relationships

- **[[short-trotters]]** — His partner in the Punch-and-Judy show
- **[[nell-trent]]** — The child they encounter on the road
- **[[the-old-curiosity-shop]]** — The world they briefly intersect

## Significant Appearances

Codlin and Short appear in the country episodes, providing a contrast between Codlin's grumbling pessimism and Short's more cheerful disposition.

## Narrative Details

Thomas Codlin is introduced as a Punch-and-Judy showman travelling with his partner Harris (known as Short or Trotters). The two men are "seated in easy attitudes upon the grass" when Nell and her grandfather first encounter them, with their puppet theatre box laid aside (016-004). On that first meeting, the merry man Short greets the wanderers with a nod and "observed that perhaps that was the first time he had ever seen a Punch off the stage" (016-007).

Codlin's defining trait is his "surly, grumbling manner" (016-015). "Never mind," he says with the air of "a discontented philosopher," "I know better now, and p'raps I'm sorry for it" (016-018). His partner Short describes him more directly: "Tommy Codlin is a calculating at this minute what we've lost through your coming upon us" (016-013).

The contrast between the two showmen sharpens as the journey continues. Codlin trudges heavily, "exchanging a word or two at intervals with Short, and stopping to rest and growl occasionally" (017-031). When Short is generous, Codlin rebukes him: "the principle's a dangerous one, and you're too free I tell you" (017-024). Yet for all his grumbling, Codlin is not wicked — merely a man hardened by a hard life, whose pessimism is more habit than conviction.

> "Never mind," said Mr. Codlin with the air of a discontented philosopher. "I know better now, and p'raps I'm sorry for it."
>
> (016-018)
