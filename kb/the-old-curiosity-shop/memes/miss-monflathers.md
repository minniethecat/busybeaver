Miss Monflathers presides over "Miss Monflathers's Boarding and Day Establishment" (031-033) with the self-importance of a minor deity, and her encounter with [[nell-trent|Nell Trent]] — whom she mistakes for a waxwork child employed by [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]] — generates one of the novel's most memorable satirical set-pieces. She is Dickens's portrait of institutional authority in its most petty form: the schoolmistress whose moral pronouncements are indistinguishable from egotism, whose "education" consists of indoctrination in social hierarchy, and whose cruelty to children is masked by the language of moral instruction.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Monflathers appears in a single extended scene that functions as a self-contained satire of Victorian education. Encountering Nell and [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]'s waxwork exhibition, she delivers a lecture on the wickedness of being "a waxwork child" (031-042) — a category she has invented for the occasion — that reveals more about her own character than about the child she berates. Her performance is witnessed by her "young ladies" (031-032), whom she has trained in the rituals of deference that sustain her authority.

The scene is structured as a comic humiliation that doubles as social criticism. Miss Monflathers's "vexed and irritated" state (031-058) is explained by the success of a rival establishment, Miss Edwards's, whose pupils have been "taking the air" in a location that upstages her own procession. The pettiness of her grievance — she is angry about the optics of a walk — exposes the vanity that underlies her moral posturing. When Nell quietly corrects her misapprehension, Miss Monflathers is momentarily disarmed, but the scene's satirical point has already landed: the woman who presumes to instruct children in morality cannot distinguish a waxwork from a living child.

## Character Traits

Miss Monflathers is defined by an authority that is simultaneously absolute and absurd. Her pronouncements — "it's very naughty to be a waxwork child" (031-040) — are delivered with the gravity of moral law, yet their content is nonsense. She represents the type of the petty tyrant that Dickens found everywhere in Victorian institutions: the person whose power exceeds their wisdom and whose certainty exceeds their knowledge. Her "rather uncertain temper" (031-038) suggests the instability beneath her performance of authority — she is perpetually "vexed and irritated" (031-058) because the world refuses to conform to the order she imagines herself to command.

## Key Relationships

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: Their single encounter is the scene's dramatic center. Nell's quiet dignity in the face of Miss Monflathers's tirade — her refusal to be diminished by a woman who does not understand her — constitutes a small, quiet victory that the scene does not need to announce.
- **Miss Edwards**: The rival schoolmistress whose success vexes Miss Monflathers (031-052, 031-060). She never appears in the novel, existing only as the shadow that darkens Miss Monflathers's mood — a reminder that even petty tyranny is haunted by its rivals.

## Significant Appearances

Miss Monflathers appears in a single extended scene (031-031 through 031-060), leading her pupils in a procession that encounters Nell and [[mrs-jarley|Mrs. Jarley]]'s waxworks. The scene is one of the novel's most sustained passages of pure satire, its comedy sharpened by the reader's knowledge that the "waxwork child" Miss Monflathers condescends to is the heroine whose moral worth dwarfs her own. She does not reappear, her function as a satirical target fulfilled in a single, devastating set-piece.
