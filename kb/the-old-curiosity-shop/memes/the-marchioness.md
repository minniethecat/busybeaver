# The Marchioness

The abused servant girl in the Brass household. The Marchioness — whose real name is never given — is the small, half-starved drudge of the Brass establishment, a figure whose suffering and eventual rescue form one of the novel's most affecting subplots.

## Role in the Novel

The Marchioness serves as both victim and witness: her intimate knowledge of the Brass household makes her the key to exposing the conspiracy against [[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]].

## Character Traits

Small, undernourished, and timid — but possessed of a quiet courage and a capacity for loyalty that outshines the adults who exploit her.

## Key Relationships

- **[[dick-swiveller]]** — Her protector and friend, who gives her her name
- **[[sally-brass]]** — Her abuser
- **[[sampson-brass]]** — Her master

## Significant Appearances

The Marchioness appears primarily in the [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]] chapters, particularly in her scenes with [[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]], where her character gradually emerges from terrified silence into self-possession.

## Narrative Details

The Marchioness is first introduced as "the small servant," a creature who lives in the subterranean regions of the Brass household. [[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]] notes "that the small servant always remained somewhere in the bowels of the earth under [[bevis-marks|Bevis Marks]], and never came to the surface unless the bell rang" (036-007). When summoned, she would scuttle up from the basement kitchen, perform her errand, and vanish again.

It is Swiveller who gives her the grand name "the Marchioness," in one of his characteristic flights of fancy. The two form an unlikely bond, sealed over games of cribbage played by candlelight. Swiveller puts down "two sixpences into a saucer" as stakes: "If you win, you get 'em all. If I win, I get 'em. To begin with, they're mine. Now, Marchioness, fire away!" (057-046). The girl, "holding her cards very tight in both hands, considered which to play" (057-047).

The Marchioness has observed the conspiracy at close quarters — she has overheard the plotting, seen the planted evidence, and understood more than anyone suspects. Her testimony is what finally breaks the case against Kit. When she flees the Brass household and appears at Swiveller's sickbed, her transformation from terrified drudge to active agent is complete.

In a novel filled with innocents who suffer passively, the Marchioness is a small but significant exception: she acts. Her rescue is not merely from the Brass household but into personhood — at the novel's close she has a name, a protector, and a future.
