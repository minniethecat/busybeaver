## Narrative Details

The shadow of Nell's death is cast early in the novel, in the narrator's premonitory reflections. Looking back on the first night in the curiosity shop, he pictures "the child in her bed: alone, unwatched, uncared for, (save by angels,) yet sleeping peacefully" (001-107). The grandfather's nightly blessing — "Sleep soundly Nell, and angels guard thy bed" (001-096) — acquires a terrible irony as the narrative progresses.

The journey from London is the slow unwinding of Nell's vitality. The narrator observes the paradox of parting: "Why is it that we can better bear to part in spirit than in body, and while we have the fortitude to act farewell have not the nerve to say it?" (015-002). Chapter by chapter, Nell's strength diminishes, her bright spirit dimming into a gentleness that is as much exhaustion as grace.

Her death is rendered with extraordinary restraint. Dickens does not dramatize the final moment; instead he describes the aftermath — the grandfather sitting by her bed, refusing to accept she is gone, talking to her as if she still breathed. The burial in the quiet churchyard, "so solemn and quiet, that every rustle" of leaves seems a prayer (046-049), provides the novel's most enduring image. Nell, who walked through a corrupt world untouched by its corruption, is laid to rest in the only place that ever truly suited her: a place of peace.
