# Short Trotters

A Punch-and-Judy showman and partner of [[thomas-codlin|Thomas Codlin]] in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]]. Short (or Trotters) is the more cheerful half of the travelling puppet-show duo, a man whose good humour offers a gentle counterpoint to Codlin's grumbling.

## Role in the Novel

As one half of the travelling showmen, Short provides moments of warmth and generosity on Nell and her grandfather's hard road.

## Character Traits

A merry, generous disposition — the optimist to Codlin's pessimist, quick with a kind word or a jocose reply.

## Key Relationships

- **[[thomas-codlin]]** — His partner in the Punch-and-Judy show
- **[[nell-trent]]** — The child they encounter and briefly protect

## Significant Appearances

Short and Codlin's episodes form part of the novel's rich tapestry of roadside characters, showing Dickens's gift for vivid minor figures.

## Narrative Details

The little man known as Short Trotters has a name that is itself a small piece of Dickensian comedy. His "real name of the little man was Harris, but it had gradually merged into the less euphonious one of Trotters, which, with the prefatory adjective, Short, had been conferred upon him" (017-027). The narrator addresses the reader directly: "Short, then, or Trotters, as the reader pleases" (017-028).

Unlike his glum partner Codlin, Short is the "merry man" who first "greet[s] the strangers with a nod" (016-007). When Codlin grumbles about the costs of hospitality, Short returns "a jocose answer calculated to turn aside his discontent" (017-028). He has a natural warmth that makes him sympathetic to Nell's plight, and he is more willing than Codlin to share their meagre provisions.

Yet Short is not merely a simpleton. He is a practical man who understands the economics of their wandering life — the flat box, the private luggage, the careful management of the few coins they earn. His kindliness is genuine, not naive; he simply chooses cheerfulness over complaint. In this he forms one half of a classic Dickensian double-act, the optimist and the pessimist on the road together.
