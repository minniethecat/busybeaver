# The Valiant Soldier

The Valiant Soldier is a country inn run by James Groves — "Jem Groves — honest Jem Groves" — a landlord of "unblemished moral character" who also boasts "a good dry skittle ground" [029-014]. Nell and her grandfather encounter the inn during their country wanderings, and it serves as a brief stopping place on their long and irregular journey.

## The Landlord

James Groves is proud of his establishment to the point of incredulity that anyone might not know of it. When Nell asks, "Is this house called the Valiant Soldier, sir?" the landlord responds with astonishment: "I thought everybody knew that. Where have you come from, if you don't know the Valiant Soldier as well as the church catechism?" [029-014].

The signboard announces: "The Valiant Soldier, by James Groves" [030-010]. Groves offers practical accommodation at straightforward prices: "Two good beds, one-and-sixpence; supper and beer one shilling; total, two shillings and sixpence" [030-012].

## Reputation

The Valiant Soldier is "pretty well known hereabouts" [029-012]. Its fame rests on Groves's reputation for honest dealing and the quality of his skittle ground — a detail that places the inn firmly in the tradition of English country public houses, where skittles (nine-pin bowling) was a popular pastime. The inn represents the stable, respectable face of country life, in contrast to the more colourful and disreputable [[the-jolly-sandboys|Jolly Sandboys]], which attracts a raucous assembly of travelling showpeople.

## Narrative Role

The Valiant Soldier is a minor stopping point in Nell's journey, but it contributes to the novel's rich geography of inns and way-stations — each with its own character and landlord — that mark the stages of her flight from London. Inns like the Valiant Soldier provide moments of rest and ordinary human connection amid the uncertainties of the road.
