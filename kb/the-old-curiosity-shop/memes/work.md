## In the Novel

Work in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] is the great denominator of character, revealing moral worth through the posture one adopts toward labor. The grandfather's anguished cry — "Leave Nell and I to toil and work" (002-040) — is ironic in context, for his gambling has precisely exempted them from honest toil and placed them in the usurer's grip. The word "work" in his mouth is a performative claim to virtue he has forfeited.

For the novel's virtuous poor, by contrast, work is an uncomplaining constant. Kit's mother is "still hard at work at an ironing-table" (010-010), laboring "without complaint since morning" (010-011). This silent, stoic labor is Dickens's signal of moral rectitude: those who work without resentment are good; those who avoid work, or who exploit the labor of others, are suspect. The narrator's observation that Nell's room is "worthily hung with poor patchwork than with purple and fine linen" (011-065) elevates humble domestic labor above the trappings of wealth.

Yet Dickens does not sentimentalize work entirely. The "vessels slowly working out of harbour" (005-024) and the laborers who populate the industrial margins suggest an economy of work that is grinding and impersonal. Work dignifies, but it also consumes.

## Key Examples

- **[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]**: Kit's trajectory is defined by work — from his initial employment at the curiosity shop to his eventual position with the Garlands. His willingness to work, even in humble capacities, marks him as the novel's moral exemplar. Quilp's command — "mind you're here my lad to work it out" (014-035) — frames work as both obligation and opportunity.
- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: Nell's needlework (003-050) is her quiet contribution to the household economy. Her prayer that "we may beg, or work in open roads or fields, to earn a scanty living" (009-017) elevates work to a sacred alternative to dependence.
- **The laboring poor**: The novel's crowd scenes register the working classes with respect — the washerwoman at the ironing table (010-010), the field workers (015-021), the needlework teachers at Miss Monflathers's school (008-013). Dickens grants these figures dignity through their labor.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of work reflects his own biography — the blacking-factory trauma that made him a lifelong advocate for labor reform. In The Old Curiosity Shop, work is a moral category before it is an economic one. The grandfather's gambling is sinful not merely because it leads to debt but because it represents a refusal of work's dignity. Quilp, who "went to work with surprising vigour" (013-053) in the pursuit of malice, demonstrates that industry without moral purpose is grotesque.

The novel's famous assertion that "man is the noblest work of God" (014-020), quoted approvingly, places work at the center of Dickens's humanism. To work is to participate in the divine order; to shirk work is to violate it. This is a profoundly Victorian conviction, and Dickens presses it with characteristic moral force.

> Man is the noblest work of God.

## Victorian Context

The 1840s marked the dawn of organized labor movements in Britain, with Chartism at its peak and the Condition of England question dominating public discourse. Dickens's treatment of work in The Old Curiosity Shop predates his more explicitly industrial novels (Hard Times, 1854) but already registers the tension between the dignity of labor and its degradation under industrial capitalism. The novel's nostalgia for artisanal and domestic work — needlework, ironing, field labor — reflects a broader Victorian anxiety about industrialization's transformation of the relationship between worker and work.
