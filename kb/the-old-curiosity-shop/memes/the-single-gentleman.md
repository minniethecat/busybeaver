# The Single Gentleman

The Single Gentleman is a mysterious lodger who takes rooms at the office of [[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]] on Bevis Marks, and whose arrival sets in motion a series of events that prove central to the novel's later plot. Muffled in winter garments despite summer heat, bald, and gruff, he is one of Dickens's most carefully drawn mystery figures — a man whose identity and purpose are revealed only gradually.

## Arrival at Bevis Marks

The Single Gentleman first appears when his enormous trunk is hauled up the narrow staircase of Sampson Brass's office, crushing and bumping against the walls. He is "closely muffled up in winter garments, though the thermometer had stood all day at eighty-one in the shade" [011-019]. Without ceremony, he demands the rooms, pays ten pounds down, and announces he will stay for two years [011-020].

His terms are simple and uncompromising: "If they disturb me, they lose a good tenant. If they know me to be that, they know enough. If they try to know more, it's a notice to quit" [011-024].

## Character and Habits

The Single Gentleman is defined by his peculiarities. He keeps early hours, makes no noise, pays for everything beforehand, and gives "very little trouble" [011-026]. He communicates almost exclusively through [[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]], addressing him with phrases Swiveller freely embellishes: "Swiveller, I know I can rely upon you" [011-026].

His most distinctive trait is his obsessive fascination with Punch and Judy shows. At the mere distant squeak of Punch's voice, he would — "though in bed and asleep" — start up, hurry on his clothes, and race to the spot, returning at the head of a procession of idlers [011-029]. He would set up the stage in front of Brass's house and regale the puppet showmen with strong waters afterward, to the consternation of the neighbourhood.

## Role in the Plot

The Single Gentleman's identity is one of the novel's central mysteries. His interest in the Punch and Judy men — [[codlin-and-short|Codlin and Short]] — is not idle curiosity but part of a deliberate search. He is, in fact, the brother of Nell's grandfather, searching tirelessly for Nell and her grandfather after they have fled London.

His choice of lodgings at Brass's office is strategic: Brass is [[daniel-quilp|Quilp]]'s lawyer, and by installing himself at the heart of Quilp's network, the Single Gentleman positions himself to gather information about the fugitives he seeks.

## Key Relationships

- **[[dick-swiveller|Dick Swiveller]]**: His sole channel of communication at Brass's; Swiveller's embellished accounts of their relationship elevate his own status in the household.
- **[[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]**: His landlord, whom he pointedly ignores, dealing only through Swiveller.
- **[[codlin-and-short|Codlin and Short]]**: The Punch and Judy showmen he hires in his search for Nell.
- **[[the-grandfather|Nell's Grandfather]]**: His brother, whose flight with Nell drives the Single Gentleman's entire course of action.
- **[[daniel-quilp|Daniel Quilp]]**: The antagonist whose lawyer's office he infiltrates.
