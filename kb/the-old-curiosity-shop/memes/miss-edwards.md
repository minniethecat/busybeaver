# Miss Edwards

Miss Edwards is a pupil at [[Miss Monflathers]]'s boarding school for young ladies. Her sole appearance in the novel — a scene of public humiliation for the crime of showing kindness to [[Nell Trent]] — serves as one of Dickens's most concentrated indictments of the institutional cruelty that passes for education and moral instruction.

## Role in the Novel

When Nell, accompanied by [[Mrs. Jarley]], visits Miss Monflathers's school to promote the waxworks exhibition, the governess delivers a blistering lecture on the dangers of waxworks as a moral influence. During this performance, Miss Edwards commits an unforgivable transgression: she is kind to Nell. Dickens reports that "it was Miss Edwards who did that, I know" (031-052) — the verb "did" referring to an act of sympathy that the narration deliberately leaves unspecified, as if kindness itself is too shameful to name in that establishment.

Miss Monflathers responds with a public shaming. She informs the entire school that "you will not take the air today, Miss Edwards" (031-060), punishing the girl by revoking a privilege that, in a boarding school regimen, constituted a significant deprivation. The governess further declares that "the gayest feather in Miss Monflathers's cap, and the brightest pupil of the establishment" has disgraced herself (031-059).

> It was Miss Edwards, and everybody said it was Miss Edwards who did it, and Miss Edwards, it was said, was much affected, and shed tears.
>
> (031-053)

## Character Traits

Miss Edwards is defined almost entirely by a single action — an act of **sympathy** in an environment that punishes it. Her tearful response to public humiliation marks her as sensitive, but Dickens withholds her speech: she has no dialogue in the scene. Her silence is the silence of a child who has learned that protest is futile.

What makes the character poignant is precisely her **ordinariness**. She is not the heroine of a subplot; she is not rescued or vindicated. She simply appears, is kind, is punished for it, and disappears. In this, she represents all the anonymous children who passed through such institutions — "boarded, clothed, and educated, all for the small sum of twenty guineas per annum" (031-033) — and left no trace but a bill.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Miss Monflathers]]**: The governess who publicly humiliates Miss Edwards for showing kindness to Nell. Miss Monflathers wields her authority with a cruelty disguised as moral instruction (031-052–060).
- **[[Nell Trent]]**: The unintended beneficiary of Miss Edwards's sympathy. Nell is the occasion for the girl's punishment — a visitor whose poverty and association with waxworks make her, in Miss Monflathers's eyes, a contaminating influence.

## Significant Appearances

- Sole appearance: Chapter 31, at Miss Monflathers's boarding school (031-052–060)
- Aftermath implied: Chapter 32, after the young ladies have gone home and Miss Monflathers is "reported to be from home" (032-010)

## Critical Interpretation

The Miss Edwards episode is one of Dickens's sharpest polemical miniatures. In a single page, he anatomises the moral bankruptcy of an institution that teaches deportment while crushing compassion. Miss Monflathers's speech — with its calculated public shaming and its weaponisation of "the world's" opinion — is a study in the rhetoric of power disguised as pedagogy.

Miss Edwards herself is a structural device as much as a character: the silent sufferer whose tears indict the system. Critics have noted that this episode echoes Dickens's own traumatic experience of being put to work in a blacking factory at age twelve — the shame of being singled out, the cruelty of adults, and the helplessness of a child who has done nothing wrong.
