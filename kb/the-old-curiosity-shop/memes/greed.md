# Greed

Greed functions as the primary engine of conflict in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]], manifesting most destructively through the grandfather's gambling addiction and [[daniel-quilp|Daniel Quilp]]'s predatory avarice. The narrator describes a world where watchers of strangers "made of it a greedy trade" (011-001), establishing greed as a systemic feature of the novel's social landscape rather than merely an individual failing.

## In the Novel

The grandfather's gambling is the most tragic expression of greed in the novel. His compulsion is not mere vice but a desperation born of love — he gambles to secure Nell's future, yet this very greed destroys the security he seeks to provide. The narrator describes a world in which characters are "greedily taking in all that passed" (009-023), treating human experience itself as something to be consumed.

[[daniel-quilp|Daniel Quilp]] embodies greed in its most monstrous form. His face is described as "pinched and sharpened by the greediness which made his eyes unnaturally bright" (030-038). Quilp's avarice is totalizing — it consumes his relationships, his business dealings, and ultimately himself. He represents the deformation of human nature when governed entirely by acquisitive desire. Even in small domestic moments, Quilp displays "extraordinary greediness" (005-023), drinking tea and eating with a voraciousness that mirrors his moral appetite.

The novel explores greed as a spectrum rather than a binary — from the grandfather's sympathetic but destructive compulsion to Quilp's sadistic avarice to the more mundane greed of characters who profit from others' misfortunes. The curiosity shop itself, filled with objects accumulated without purpose, becomes a monument to a form of material greed divorced from any meaningful use.

## Key Examples

- **Nell's grandfather**: His gambling addiction, though motivated by love for Nell, becomes a destructive force that drives the entire plot. His greed is the most tragic in the novel because it is inseparable from genuine affection.
- **[[daniel-quilp|Daniel Quilp]]**: The embodiment of voracious greed — consuming everything and everyone around him. His "greediness" is explicitly marked in his physical description (030-038) and expressed through his every action (005-023).
- **[[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]**: The lawyer whose legal and financial greed enables Quilp's schemes against Kit and others, representing institutionalized greed operating through the legal system.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens presents greed not as abstract moral failing but as a force that deforms both individuals and social relations. The novel's critique of greed extends beyond individual characters to indict a social system that rewards avarice and punishes generosity. The grandfather's gambling — a form of greed born of love — is particularly damning because it shows how even virtuous motives are corrupted by a society organized around acquisitive desire.

> ...the dwarf took from a cupboard a bottle and glass, and sitting down, began to help himself with great composure and extraordinary greediness.
>
> (005-023)
