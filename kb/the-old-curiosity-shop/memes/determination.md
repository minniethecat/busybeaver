## In the Novel

Determination appears in *The Old Curiosity Shop* as a double-edged force — it is the virtue that sustains Nell through her ordeals and the vice that locks her grandfather into his self-destructive course. The novel explores how the same quality, applied to different ends, produces radically different outcomes:

> "her arms as if in a resolute determination to do nothing" (005-017)

This early comic instance — Mrs. Jiniwin's obstinacy — establishes determination as a trait that cuts across the novel's full moral spectrum, from the trivial to the tragic.

## The Grandfather's Fixed Purpose

The grandfather illustrates determination in its most tragic form. His "determination that he would not go anywhere" (014-037) after an evening's gambling loss reveals the rigidity of purpose that characterises the addict:

> "having formed this Christian determination, he turned his thoughts" (022-005)

The irony of "Christian determination" is deliberate — the grandfather frames his compulsive gambling as a moral duty to provide for Nell, while the reader sees the destructive logic beneath. This is determination corrupted into rationalisation.

## Quilp's Malign Will

Quilp embodies determination in its most predatory form. His "determination" (048-044) to extract information and manipulate others is relentless — a will that acknowledges no limit and no moral boundary. Where the grandfather's determination destroys himself, Quilp's destroys others:

> "call on them to reconsider their determination" (066-018)

The legal and moral language of reconsideration surrounds Quilp's manipulations, but his own determination never wavers — making him the novel's most formidable agent of destruction.

## Nell's Quiet Resolve

In counterpoint to these destructive forms, Nell's determination — less often named as such — is the quiet resilience that carries her through hunger, exhaustion, and fear. It is determination stripped of rhetoric and self-deception, expressed in action rather than declaration.
