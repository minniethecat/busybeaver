# Nature

Nature in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] functions as a moral and spiritual counterforce to the corrupting influence of urban life. The narrator observes early on that impressions are continually produced by "external objects" of the natural world (001-108), suggesting that the physical environment actively shapes moral sensibility. Throughout the novel, proximity to nature serves as an index of virtue — characters who respond to natural beauty are portrayed as morally sound, while those indifferent to it are suspect.

## In the Novel

The countryside journey provides the novel's most sustained engagement with the natural world. As Nell and her grandfather travel through rural England, nature offers both physical sustenance and spiritual restoration. The freshness of the day and the beauty of waving grass become more than scenic detail — they are presented as healing forces that counteract the damage inflicted by urban poverty and greed. Abel's feelings are said to "do credit to his nature" (014-030), explicitly linking natural goodness to the natural world.

Dickens contrasts the organic rhythms of nature with the artificial, deforming environment of the city. The curiosity shop itself, "furthest removed from the sympathies of every natural heart" (001-108), represents the perversion of natural order into mere accumulation. The industrial landscapes through which Nell passes are described as antithetical to human flourishing — environments where nature has been extinguished and replaced by machinery, smoke, and the relentless pursuit of profit.

Nature also serves as a moral register for character. Those who respond to natural beauty — Nell, Kit, the schoolmaster — are aligned with goodness, while those indifferent or hostile to it — Quilp, the Brasses — are morally suspect. This Romantic sensibility, in which the natural world is both a source of truth and a measure of virtue, is central to Dickens's moral vision in the novel.

## Key Examples

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: Her deep affinity for the natural world reflects her innate purity. She finds solace in birdsong, flowers, and open landscapes throughout her journey.
- **The rural journey**: The pastoral landscapes through which Nell and her grandfather travel provide healing respite from urban corruption. The natural world is consistently described in language that emphasizes its restorative power.
- **[[daniel-quilp|Daniel Quilp]]**: His confinement to the wharf and urban spaces mirrors his moral deformity. He is associated with unnatural environments — damp wharves, cramped offices, and polluted waterways — that externalize his inner corruption.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens deploys nature as a Romantic counterweight to the dehumanizing forces of industrial capitalism. The natural world in the novel is not merely decorative but actively redemptive — a space where human goodness can be restored and recognized. This treatment reflects the influence of Romantic poetry on Dickens's imagination, filtered through his characteristic social conscience and moral urgency.

> We are so much in the habit of allowing impressions to be made upon us by external objects, which should be produced by reflection alone, and which are continually influencing us without our knowledge.
>
> (001-108)
