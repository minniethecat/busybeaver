# The Church

The Church is the quiet country church where [[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]'s journey ends — a "peaceful spot" marked by an ancient porch, moss-grown stones, and an atmosphere of tranquil sanctity. It serves as both her final resting place and the setting for the novel's closing scenes, symbolising the peace that eluded her in life.

## Nell's Connection to the Church

Nell's bond with the church is established soon after she and her grandfather arrive in the country village. Despite her delicate constitution, she shows no fear of entering the church alone at night, drawn to its quiet shadows: "she had never feared to enter the church alone at night, but had loved to linger there when all was quiet" [072-033]. The church becomes her sanctuary — a place of meditation and respite from the harshness of the world she has fled.

## Her Final Resting Place

After Nell's death, the churchyard becomes her grave. Under the porch she had so often sat, the old church "received her in its quiet shade" [072-030]. The village mourners gather around her grave, and the church clears slowly "in time of all but the sexton" — leaving only those who truly loved her [072-033].

## The Grandfather's End

The church is also the setting for the novel's final tragedy. After Nell's death, her grandfather lingers by her grave. A frightened schoolboy reports "having seen him, but a moment before, sitting in the church — upon her grave" [072-046]. Eventually, the old man dies beside her, and "in the church where they had often prayed and mused, and lingered hand in hand, the child and the old man slept together" [072-051].

## Symbolic Significance

The church represents the end of wandering. Throughout the novel, Nell and her grandfather are in flight — from [[daniel-quilp|Quilp]], from poverty, from the corrupting world of London. The church is where they finally stop. It embodies the spiritual peace that Nell has embodied throughout the narrative, and its "quiet shade" offers the only lasting home she ever finds.
