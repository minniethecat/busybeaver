# Mr. Cheggs

Mr. Cheggs is a market-gardener who appears in a single extended scene at the [[mrs-wackles|Wackleses']] Chelsea dance, yet his role as the successful rival to [[dick-swiveller]] for the hand of [[miss-sophy-wackles|Sophy Wackles]] makes him a meaningful figure in the novel's subplot of ordinary courtship. Where Swiveller offers sentiment and theatrical devotion, Cheggs offers substance — a steady trade, a secure income, and the prospect of a comfortable home. He is one of Dickens's representatives of the unglamorous but reliable suitor, a type that recurs throughout the author's treatment of marriage and social mobility.

## Character Traits

Cheggs is introduced as "bashful before ladies" (008-019), and his manner throughout the Chelsea dance alternates between awkwardness and determination. His jealousy, provoked by Swiveller's attentions to Sophy, is presented not as a flaw but as evidence of genuine feeling: as his sister declares, "Why shouldn't Mr. Cheggs be jealous if he likes? I like him for it" (008-028).

Physically, Cheggs is subject to Swiveller's satirical gaze — Swiveller examines his toes with a "supercilious smile" (008-031) and pointedly observes his face and figure (008-036). Yet Cheggs does not wilt under this scrutiny. He follows Swiveller into a corner and demands, "Do you speak to me Sir?" (008-030), and later presses "fiercely" for Swiveller to repeat himself (008-035). Beneath the bashfulness lies a stubborn pride.

## At the Chelsea Dance

Cheggs arrives at the Wackles establishment not alone but accompanied by his sister, [[miss-cheggs|Miss Cheggs]], who serves as both chaperone and advocate. The narrator introduces him with characteristic economy:

> a market-gardener, whose name was Cheggs. But Mr. Cheggs came not alone (008-016)

The presence of Miss Cheggs immediately establishes the family as a unit — a contrast to Swiveller, who stands alone, armed only with his wit and his wardrobe. Miss Cheggs proves a formidable ally:

> Miss Cheggs proved herself a vigorous and determined ally (008-021)

She manoeuvres Swiveller away from Sophy, pressing him to dance with her instead: "You must dance with Miss Cheggs," said Miss Sophy to Dick Swiveller (008-022). The episode is played for comedy, but the dynamics are clear: Cheggs has the advantages of family support, economic stability, and quiet persistence. Swiveller has only charm.

## Swiveller and the Rivalry

Swiveller's response to Cheggs reveals his own character as much as it defines his rival. He affects disdain, examining Cheggs's toes with a "supercilious smile" (008-031), then confronting him directly. But Cheggs is not intimidated. His jealousy, as Miss Cheggs points out, is evidence of genuine feeling.

The narrator observes Swiveller's predicament with characteristic irony, noting "how jealous Mr. Cheggs was" (008-024) even as the market-gardener's position grows steadily stronger. Swiveller eventually resigns his mistress to Cheggs, "conveying a defiance into his looks" (008-029) — a gesture that concedes defeat even as it pretends indifference. The rivalry concludes with Cheggs hastening "to tender his hand to Sophy" (008-040), and Miss Cheggs announcing that "Alick has been saying" (008-042), the use of his Christian name signaling the intimacy Swiveller has lost.

## Key Relationships

- **[[miss-sophy-wackles|Sophy Wackles]]**: The woman he courts and ultimately marries, displacing Dick Swiveller as her acknowledged suitor.
- **[[miss-cheggs|Miss Cheggs]]**: His sister, who serves as his "vigorous and determined ally" (008-021) at the Chelsea dance, actively campaigning on his behalf.
- **[[dick-swiveller]]**: His rival for Sophy's affections. The rivalry is conducted through glances, whispered remarks, and a brief verbal confrontation, with Cheggs emerging as the victor.
- **[[mrs-wackles]]**: Host of the Chelsea dance where the courtship plays out; Cheggs pays his court to her as the matriarch (008-045).

## A Representative of Practical Romance

Cheggs represents one of the novel's recurring types: the suitor who wins not through passion but through practicality. In the economic landscape of [[the-old-curiosity-shop]], where [[nell-trent|Nell]] and her grandfather are destroyed by financial desperation, Cheggs's modest prosperity constitutes a genuine virtue. He can provide; and in a world where love without means leads to the road and the grave, provision counts for much.

His victory over Swiveller is not presented as a moral triumph — Cheggs is too minor a figure for such weight — but as a reflection of how the world works. Sentiment, in Dickens's London, rarely pays the rent. The market-gardener's quiet substance wins over the law-writer's flashy charm, foreshadowing Swiveller's own eventual transformation into a character of genuine substance.
