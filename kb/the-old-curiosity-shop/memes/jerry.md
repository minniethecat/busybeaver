# Jerry

Jerry is the proprietor and manager of a company of dancing dogs who appears at the [[The Jolly Sandboys|Jolly Sandboys]] inn during [[Nell Trent]]'s journey with the travelling waxworks show. He represents the colourful, itinerant entertainment culture of early Victorian England — a world that Dickens knew intimately from his reporting days and depicted throughout his fiction with affectionate realism.

## Role in the Novel

Jerry appears in the chapters set at the Jolly Sandboys, where Nell and [[Mrs. Jarley]] encounter a gathering of travelling performers. His troupe of trained dogs performs tricks for the inn's patrons, and he is referred to familiarly by the other show-people — "these were Jerry's dogs" (018-030) — establishing him as a known figure in the itinerant entertainment circuit.

[[Short Trotters|Short]] mentions him in later chapters, referring to "a man of the name of Jerry — you know Jerry, Thomas?" (037-049), though [[Thomas Codlin|Codlin]] dismisses the reference with characteristic irritability: "Oh, don't talk to me of Jerrys" (037-050). This later mention suggests that Jerry is part of a wider network of travelling performers who cross paths and remember one another — a community bound by shared roads and shared audiences rather than fixed abodes.

## Character Traits

Jerry is introduced with a description that juxtaposes his profession against his demeanor: "Jerry, the manager of these dancing dogs, was a man of grave and solemn aspect" (018-031). The gravity of his manner contrasts comically with the absurdity of his trade — a trained dog act — yet Dickens presents this without mockery. Jerry's seriousness dignifies his work.

He takes his craft seriously, correcting onlookers with "no, it's not the custom" when they misunderstand dog-training practice (018-033). He exhibits pride in his animals, presenting them with showmanship: "I've got a animal here" (018-035) and "Here he is" as he produces a terrier (018-037). When a spectator disturbs the performance, Jerry walks "coolly" to address the situation (018-044), revealing a performer's composure under pressure. His command to the audience — "Now gentlemen" (018-046) — signals the resumption of the performance with professional authority.

## The Performing Dogs

Jerry's troupe includes a terrier and an old leader dog, the latter described with a mixture of professional pride and sentimental attachment. Jerry acknowledges the old dog's decline — "he has lost his appetite and doesn't take the air as he used to" — but insists on the animal's "considerable talent" and declares "I have no wish to part with him" (018-042). This loyalty to an aging performer mirrors the novel's broader concern with the fate of those whom the world has passed by.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Short Trotters]]**: Short knows Jerry by name, mentioning him familiarly in later chapters: "A man of the name of Jerry — you know Jerry, Thomas?" (037-049).
- **[[Thomas Codlin]]**: Codlin dismisses Short's reference to Jerry with characteristic gruffness (037-050), but his recognition is implicit.
- **[[Mr. Vuffin]]**: The giant exhibits mutual recognition with Jerry — "I know you do, Jerry," said Mr. Vuffin with profound gravity (019-015) — suggesting shared experience on the travelling-show circuit.
- **[[Mrs. Jarley]]**: Though they do not interact directly, Jerry and Mrs. Jarley belong to the same world of itinerant entertainment that shelters Nell during her journey.

## Critical Interpretation

Jerry belongs to the novel's gallery of itinerant performers — a world that Dickens knew well from his early reporting and depicted with affectionate realism throughout his fiction. The dancing-dog manager's gravity of aspect, his professional pride, and his loyalty to an aging animal echo the dignity that the novel extends to even its most marginal characters. The performances at the Jolly Sandboys serve as a moment of communal respite — a pocket of colour and noise — before the story descends into its darker passages of pursuit, illness, and death.
