## In the Novel

Sentiment functions in *The Old Curiosity Shop* as a moral language, guiding the reader's sympathy and shaping the ethical landscape of the narrative. Dickens employs sentimental appeal not as mere emotional manipulation but as a deliberate rhetorical strategy to engage the reader's moral imagination:

> "Inspired by this idea and the sentiments it awakened, sir" (003-031)

The narrator frequently invokes sentiment as a shared ground between author and reader, a space where fellow-feeling becomes the basis for moral judgment.

## Public and Private Feeling

Dickens draws a contrast between genuine private sentiment and its public performance. The novel's early chapters, set in the curiosity shop itself, establish the distinction between true feeling and its commodification. Characters who perform sentiment without feeling it are repeatedly exposed:

> "a little sentiment appropriate to the occasion" (007-006)

This scene at the Nubbles household demonstrates how authentic domestic feeling is cheapened when reduced to rhetorical display. The narrator, however, aligns himself with those "warm and tender sentiments" (007-053) that arise naturally from genuine affection.

## The Sentimental Traveller

The journey motif — Nell and her grandfather's flight from London — becomes a vehicle for sentimental education. The narrator remarks that his "heart dilated, and my sentiments of a corresponding description" (008-052) when recounting acts of kindness encountered on the road. This dilation of heart is the novel's sentimental ideal: feeling enlarged by sympathy for others.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens wrote in an age when literary sentimentalism was both a dominant mode and a target of critique. The novel acknowledges this tension — one character is "by no means of a sentimental turn, and perhaps had never" (014-003) experienced genuine fellow-feeling — but ultimately affirms sentiment as essential to moral life. For Dickens, the capacity to feel with others distinguishes the humane from the predatory.
