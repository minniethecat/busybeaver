# Mrs. Wackles

Mrs. Wackles is the widowed proprietor of a day school in Chelsea, and the mother of three daughters: [[miss-jane-wackles|Jane]], [[miss-sophy-wackles|Sophy]], and Melissa. She appears primarily in the early chapters of the novel, where her establishment serves as the setting for a dance that brings together several of the novel's secondary characters. Her character embodies the quiet industry of the respectable poor—a woman who maintains appearances through ceaseless work and careful management.

## The Chelsea School

The Wackles establishment is described as a modest but well-run day school. The narrator notes that the school's reputation rests on diligence rather than distinction, capturing the economic reality of a widow supporting a family through educational labour:

> the truth is that neither Mrs. Wackles nor her eldest daughter had any very great repute for learning (008-015)

Despite this limitation, the school provides a stable social setting. The dance hosted there—attended by [[dick-swiveller]], [[cheggs]], and the Wackles sisters—is one of the novel's few scenes of ordinary sociability, unshadowed by the poverty, obsession, and death that dominate the main narrative.

Mrs. Wackles herself moves through the evening with watchful attention, "forgetting for the moment" her managerial vigilance only when genuine wonder or admiration breaks through her practical reserve (008-020). She occupies a borderline between gentility and trade, a position the novel treats with knowing sympathy.

## As Mother and Manager

Mrs. Wackles's dual role as parent and proprietor shapes her interactions with her daughters. She is neither the sentimentalised mother of Victorian convention nor the coldly calculating matchmaker of satire; rather, she is a woman who understands that her daughters' futures depend on the connections formed under her roof.

Her management of the family's affairs reflects the broader economic anxieties that permeate [[the-old-curiosity-shop]]. Like [[the-grandfather]], she seeks to secure a future through effort and calculation—but where his schemes lead to ruin, her modest pragmatism yields a sustainable, if unremarkable, outcome.

When Sophy eventually marries [[cheggs]], the market-gardener, the match represents a quiet success for Mrs. Wackles's patient orchestration of social opportunity. Her eldest daughter Jane, "in all the glory of her plumage" (008-045), continues to assist in running the school, perpetuating the family's modest enterprise.

## A Minor Figure in the Novel's Economy

Mrs. Wackles appears only briefly, yet her household serves an important structural function in the novel. The Chelsea scenes provide a foil to the gothic intensities of [[daniel-quilp|Quilp]]'s wharf and the desperate wanderings of [[nell-trent|Nell]] and her grandfather. In a novel preoccupied with extremes—of innocence, of villainy, of suffering—Mrs. Wackles represents the ordinary, the manageable, the survivable.

Her widowhood also echoes a broader pattern in Dickens's work: the figure of the widow who must navigate a world that offers few protections to women without husbands. Unlike [[mrs-quilp]], whose widowhood arrives through Quilp's death, Mrs. Wackles has already adapted to her circumstances, making her a study in quiet resilience rather than dramatic liberation.
