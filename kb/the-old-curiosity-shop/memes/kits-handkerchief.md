# Kit's Handkerchief

[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]'s handkerchief is one of those small Dickensian objects that carries disproportionate symbolic weight — a scrap of fabric that represents Kit's humble [[family|domesticity]], his care for his appearance out of respect for others, and the working-class dignity that the novel consistently celebrates.

## In the Novel

The handkerchief makes its most notable appearance when Kit is preparing to leave London for his new position at [[abel-cottage|Mr. Garland's cottage]]. In a scene that Dickens renders with characteristic attention to the material details of working-class life, Kit's mother ensures he is properly turned out — and the handkerchief, freshly washed and pressed, is among the objects of her maternal care.

The handkerchief also figures in Kit's trial. When he is falsely accused, the handkerchief becomes entangled — perhaps literally — in the Brasses' fabricated evidence against him. A small, personal object is twisted into an instrument of injustice, and its recovery as an honest object is part of Kit's eventual vindication.

Throughout the novel, the handkerchief — like Kit himself — is unremarkable in itself but extraordinary in what it signifies: cleanliness, order, respect for others, the dignity of honest poverty. In a novel filled with grand symbolic objects (the curiosity shop's treasures, the waxworks figures, the brass plate), the handkerchief is notable for its very ordinariness. It represents the quotidian virtues that Dickens held highest.

## Description

Dickens does not lavish descriptive attention on the handkerchief as he does on the more spectacular objects in the novel. It is mentioned in passing — a detail of Kit's dress, an item his mother fusses over, an object caught up in the machinery of false accusation. This narrative quietness is itself significant: the handkerchief's value is not in its appearance but in its associations. It is an object defined entirely by the character who owns it and the moral qualities it represents.

## Symbolic Meaning

The handkerchief functions as a token of Kit's membership in the novel's community of good people. In the Victorian social imagination, a clean handkerchief was a marker of respectability — the minimum standard of personal presentation that distinguished the "deserving poor" from the "undeserving." Kit's handkerchief, carefully maintained by his mother, places him firmly among the deserving: he may be poor, but he is clean, orderly, and respectful. These small virtues, Dickens insists, are not trivial; they are the outward signs of inward moral worth.

At Kit's trial, the handkerchief's role in the false evidence against him inverts its meaning: an object of domestic care becomes an instrument of legal persecution. The handkerchief's eventual return to its proper moral function — as a token of Kit's essential goodness, not of his alleged crime — mirrors Kit's own journey from accused to exonerated.

## Associated Characters

- **[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]**: The handkerchief's owner. It is an extension of his character — clean, honest, unpretentious, cared for. His mother's attention to his handkerchief is her way of loving him, and his possession of it is his way of honoring her.

- **[[sampson-brass|Sampson Brass]]**: The perverter of the handkerchief's meaning, twisting a domestic object into false evidence. Brass's treatment of the handkerchief is a miniature of his larger corruption: he takes what is honest and makes it appear criminal.
