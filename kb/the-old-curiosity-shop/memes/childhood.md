# Childhood

Childhood in [[the-old-curiosity-shop|The Old Curiosity Shop]] is presented as a state of heightened moral perception and acute vulnerability. The narrator observes that 'the children of the poor' possess a wisdom born of early acquaintance with hardship — "the springs are too deep. Besides, the children of the poor know but few pleasures. Even the cheap delights of childhood must be bought and paid for" (001-042). [[nell-trent|Nell Trent]] is the supreme example — a child whose experiences of poverty and displacement have not coarsened but refined her, giving her a moral clarity that the adults around her conspicuously lack.

## In the Novel

The novel contrasts genuine childhood with its hollow imitations. In one of the novel's most rhetorically elaborate passages, the narrator reflects on "a state of childishness" that is "the same poor hollow mockery of it, that death is of sleep" (012-018). This meditation distinguishes between the natural innocence of real children — "the laughing light and life of childhood, the gaiety that has known no check, the frankness that has felt no chill, the hope that has never withered" (012-018) — and the artificial childishness affected by adults or imposed by circumstance. The passage culminates in an injunction: "Send forth the child and childish man together, and blush for the pride that libels our own old happy state, and gives its title to an ugly and distorted image" (012-018).

Nell's childhood is paradoxically both preserved and destroyed by her circumstances. She is forced into adult responsibilities — caring for her grandfather, managing their finances, navigating a hostile world — yet she never loses the essential quality of childlike goodness. Her early death can be read as the ultimate preservation of her childhood: dying before the corruptions of adulthood can claim her. The moonlight scene near the novel's close crystallizes this paradox: "the delicate face where thoughtful care already mingled with the winning grace and loveliness of youth" (039-012) — a face that is simultaneously childlike and aged by suffering.

The novel's treatment of childhood reflects Dickens's broader social concern with the exploitation of children in Victorian England. [[the-marchioness|The Marchioness]], Kit, and the anonymous street children who populate the novel's margins all testify to a society that fails its most vulnerable members. Nell's story, in this context, becomes both an indictment and an elegy. Dickens returns to this theme at the novel's end, imagining that the story of Nell might reach "some mother's pillow" as "faint dreams of childhood fading in its bloom, and resting in the sleep that knows no waking" (039-012).

The novel also distinguishes between different kinds of childhood experience. Kit Nubbles represents a working-class childhood that is hard but not tragic — he has employment, family, and purpose. The Marchioness represents childhood utterly consumed by servitude — unnamed, uneducated, invisible. Nell represents childhood under the peculiar pressure of love: she is burdened not by cruelty but by devotion, and it is this burden, as much as poverty, that exhausts her young life.

## Key Examples

- **[[nell-trent|Nell Trent]]**: The central figure of childhood in the novel — prematurely aged by responsibility yet preserved in essential innocence (001-042). Her childhood is "bought and paid for" by suffering, in the novel's economic metaphor for the cost of being young and poor.
- **[[kit-nubbles|Kit Nubbles]]**: Represents a different model of childhood — loyal, hardworking, and ultimately vindicated. His childhood survives intact because he has a family and community that protect it.
- **[[the-marchioness|The Marchioness]]**: The small servant girl whose childhood has been stolen by poverty and exploitation — the novel's most extreme example of childhood denied.
- **The grandfather**: His "second childhood" (012-018) represents the hollow mockery of genuine childhood — a regression that diminishes rather than ennobles.
