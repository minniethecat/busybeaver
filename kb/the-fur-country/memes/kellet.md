# Kellet

**Kellet** is a soldier of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] and one of [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]]'s carpenter's men in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. Like [[petersen|Petersen]] and the other rank-and-file colonists, Kellet exists largely in the background of the narrative — his name listed among the party, his labour absorbed into the collective effort of building [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]]. But in the novel's final crisis, a single act of memory — "Kellet remembered leaving his pickaxe in the kitchen" — proves decisive.

## In the Novel

Kellet is introduced in the roster of the expedition: "[[petersen|Petersen]], Belcher, [[rae|Rae]], Garry, Pond, Hope, and Kellet formed a body of clever, zealous carpenters, under the able superintendence of [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]]." He participates in the overland journey, the hunting expeditions, and the construction of [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] without ever stepping into the foreground.

> [013-004] "Petersen, Belcher, Rae, Garry, Pond, Hope, and Kellet formed a body of clever, zealous carpenters."

### The Seal Hunt at Walruses' Bay

Kellet's first moment of visibility comes during the expedition to Walruses' Bay, where he is chosen by [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] — alongside [[petersen|Petersen]] and Hope — to serve as a sentinel while Hobson, Mrs. Barnett, and Sergeant Long climb the cliffs to survey the coastline. Kellet takes up his position on the little promontory, and it is his signal that interrupts Hobson's geological musings, alerting the party to the presence of seals.

> [015-016] "Petersen, Hope, and Kellet, took up their position as sentinels on the little promontory, whilst Mrs. Barnett, Hobson, and Long, after promising not to lose sight of their comrades, and to be on the lookout for their signal, proceeded to climb the cliff."

When Hobson breaks off mid-discourse with the words "but Kellet is making signs to us," the sentinel's vigilance has already spotted the seals [015-029]. As the hunters draw near, Kellet joins Hobson in rushing upon the seals from one side while the Sergeant, Petersen, and Hope attack from the other, dispatching the animals with spears after lodging balls in their bodies [015-035].

### The Watcher in the Gloom

During the long Polar night, Kellet serves his turn as lookout. It is his vigilance that first detects approaching danger:

> [021-003] "On the 6th January, towards eleven o'clock in the morning, Kellet, whose turn it was to look out, suddenly called the Sergeant, and pointed to some moving masses indistinctly visible in the gloom."

The moving masses prove to be the first warning of the bears that will soon besiege the fort — a crisis in which Kellet's alertness gives the colonists crucial minutes to prepare.

### The Forty-Eight Hours

Later, Kellet and [[petersen|Petersen]] "once caused their companions grave anxiety by disappearing for forty-eight hours." Lost in the fog that frequently envelops the coast during the wet spring, they turn south when they believed they were near Walruses' Bay, returning "exhausted and half dead with hunger." The episode is recounted in a single paragraph but speaks volumes about the constant peril of Arctic travel, where even experienced men can be disoriented by fog and nearly perish within sight of the fort.

> [022-048] "Petersen and Kellet once caused their companions grave anxiety by disappearing for forty-eight hours."

### The Eastward Expedition

When Sergeant Long prepares to lead a perilous exploring expedition to the eastern coast, Kellet is among the four soldiers who step forward without hesitation. Alongside Belcher, Pond, and [[petersen|Petersen]], he "declared their readiness to start" [023-034]. The expedition ventures far beyond the known boundaries of the colony's surveys, placing Kellet among the small cadre of men entrusted with the most dangerous reconnaissance missions — a mark of the Lieutenant's confidence in his endurance.

> [023-034] "The four soldiers elected to accompany him were Belcher, Pond, Petersen, and Kellet, who declared their readiness to start."

### The Pickaxe that Saved the Colony

Kellet's most consequential moment comes at the novel's climax. When the colonists, adrift on the crumbling [[the-ice-island|ice island]], desperately need tools to break through the avalanche debris to reach their buried companions, it is Kellet who remembers:

> [041-034] "Some tools had been left in the house, and Kellet, one of the carpenter's men, remembered leaving his pickaxe in the kitchen."

That pickaxe — a tool the reader has never noticed, a detail the narrative has never flagged — becomes the instrument of survival. At three o'clock in the morning, Kellet's pickaxe "struck against something hard, which gave out a ringing sound" [041-059]. Kellet and Pond resumed their work, and in less than twenty minutes the hard body was uncovered — one of the rafters of the buried house's roof [041-063][041-064]. When the Lieutenant, feeling his way in the darkness, discovered a motionless body, "he consigned it to Pond and Kellet" [041-068] — the two soldiers now serving as the rescue party's receiving hands at the threshold between the living and the nearly dead.

### The Iceberg Insight

As the island drifts through the Aleutian Islands, Kellet demonstrates an unexpected grasp of the physical forces governing the colonists' fate. When the icebergs propelling the island begin to move ahead of it, the colonists are "oppressed with a presentiment of some new misfortune, and understood only too well what Kellet meant when he exclaimed—" [043-037]. Kellet perceives what the others sense but cannot articulate: the icebergs, with their immense submerged volume — "their volume being six or seven feet below the sea level for every one above" — impart the island its rapid motion through the submarine current. Once they detach, the island will be left "floating helplessly on the waves" like "a vessel with disabled masts and a broken screw" [043-039]. The narrative affirms: "Kellet was right" [043-040]. It is a fleeting moment of insight from a man otherwise portrayed as a simple soldier, and it deepens the tragedy of what follows.

### The Breaking Point

Kellet's arc takes a darker turn in the novel's final chapters. As the island drifts through the Aleutians and the colonists face new perils, the strain becomes too much. Kellet bursts into "a roar of hysterical laughter" and flings himself on the ground, "rolling over and over like a madman" [045-040].

> [045-040] "On this the soldier Kellet burst into a roar of hysterical laughter, and flinging himself on the ground, rolled over and over like a madman."

### The Promise

Kellet's madness deepens in Chapter XLVI. At about four o'clock in the afternoon, Kellet — "the same who had already given signs of insanity" — approaches [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] and speaks to her with an eerie calm [046-012]. He turns towards the sea, his unspoken intention unmistakable [046-018].

Mrs. Barnett addresses him gently, "with a half smile," reminding him of their long friendship [046-020]. "Yes, ma'am," Kellet replies calmly [046-021] — his tranquillity more disturbing than any outburst. She then makes him a remarkable promise: "Well, Kellet, if you like we will die together, but not today" [046-022]. The bargain works. Later, when survival seems possible, Kellet asks: "Yes, madam, and you will keep your promise to Kellet!" [046-038] — a plea carrying both childlike trust and the weight of a mind partially, but not wholly, returned. In the final pages, Kellet helps construct the escape raft, and the narrative notes he "had not yet reminded Mrs. Barnett of her promise" [046-046] — leaving the reader uncertain whether his silence signals recovery or merely a temporary reprieve.

## Character Arc

Kellet's trajectory through *The Fur Country* traces an arc that is understated and, in its final movement, devastating. He is introduced without fanfare — a name in a list — and for most of the novel remains a competent, unobtrusive soldier. His moments of visibility are defined by reliability: he watches, he signals, he volunteers.

Yet the narrative quietly invests him with attributes that distinguish him from the generic soldier. He is chosen repeatedly for missions requiring trust — the Walruses' Bay sentinel post, the eastward expedition with Sergeant Long. He spots the approaching bears in the January gloom. His forty-eight-hour disappearance with Petersen, while a near-tragedy, testifies to his willingness to venture beyond the perimeter. And in the novel's most critical hour, his memory of a pickaxe left in a kitchen saves lives.

The pivot comes with the iceberg insight of Chapter XLIII. Here Kellet is no longer following orders or shouting warnings; he is reasoning, explaining physical principles to his comrades. The man who once stood silently on a promontory watching for seals now diagnoses iceberg hydrodynamics. Verne gives him this moment of intellectual clarity, and then — with terrible swiftness — takes his mind away.

The madness that overtakes Kellet in Chapters XLV and XLVI is the Arctic's answer to prolonged fear. Every man in the colony has endured the same horrors — the earthquake, the bear siege, the avalanche, the slow disintegration of the island — but Kellet, alone among the soldiers, breaks. His hysterical laughter, his rolling on the ground, his calm approach to Mrs. Barnett with suicide in his eyes: these are the wages of cumulative trauma, rendered with clinical precision unusual in Verne's adventure fiction.

Mrs. Barnett's intervention — her promise to die with him "but not today" — buys Kellet not a cure but a stay. He returns to the collective labour of survival. But the final line about him — that he "had not yet reminded Mrs. Barnett of her promise" — is a masterstroke of ambiguity. It can be read as recovery, as exhaustion, or as a man who has set his despair aside for a more convenient hour. Verne does not tell us which, and Kellet's fate — like the Arctic itself — remains unresolved.
