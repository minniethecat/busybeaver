# Sergeant Loin

**Sergeant Loin** is a non-commissioned officer serving at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] under [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]'s command in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. His sole named appearance occupies a single chapter, yet the scene — the revival of a frozen man — encapsulates the communal ethos of the Hudson's Bay Company's far-flung outposts, where every available hand is pressed into service when a life is at stake.

## The Arrival of Thomas Black

Thomas Black's arrival at Fort Reliance is dramatic and communal. After the courier announces he has brought "the astronomer, Thomas Black" — to which someone responds bluntly "But he is frozen" and another replies "Well, he must be thawed" — Loin becomes one of the three men who carry the unconscious traveller into the fort:

> [003-025] "Thomas Black, carried by the Sergeant, the Corporal, and the courier, now made his entrance into the house of the fort, and was taken to a room on the first floor, the temperature of which was bearable."

The trio unwrap Black from his "wrappers and furred mantles, in which Thomas Black was rolled up like a parcel requiring care," revealing a short, stout man of about fifty, "literally frozen." The scene that follows is both urgent and faintly comic — Verne dwells on the homely practicality of Arctic emergency medicine.

## The Revival

When [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] determines that friction with snow is the necessary treatment — the standard remedy in polar countries for restoring circulation arrested by intense cold — Loin is dispatched to fetch snow from the fort courtyard:

> [003-031] "There was plenty of it in the court of Fort Reliance; and whilst the Sergeant went to fetch the snow, Joliffe removed all the astronomer's clothes."

Loin and [[corporal-joliffe|Corporal Joliffe]] together administer the vigorous treatment:

> [003-031] "Sergeant Loin soon returned, and he and Joliffe gave the new arrival such a rubbing as he had probably never before received."

The narrator describes the operation as "a vigorous shampooing most lustily performed, more like the scratching of a currycomb than the caresses of a human hand." The treatment, though rough, proves effective — Thomas Black eventually sighs and revives after half an hour of effort. The moment of resuscitation is a collective triumph: "He lives; he is coming to!" cries Hobson.

The revival scene, which occupies the better part of Chapter III, establishes the improvisational character of life at a Hudson's Bay Company fort. Rank offers no exemption from dirty work: a sergeant and a corporal rub a naked stranger with snow while a lieutenant supervises, because in the Arctic, protocol yields to necessity.

## Arctic Medicine and the "Snow Cure"

The treatment administered by Loin and Joliffe — vigorous friction with snow — was, as the narrator notes, "the means generally employed in the polar countries to set going afresh the circulation of the blood arrested by the intense cold, even as the rivers are arrested in their courses by the icy touch of winter." This medical practice, well-documented in nineteenth-century polar narratives, reflects the empirical, pre-clinical character of Arctic medicine: a treatment validated by experience rather than theory, transmitted orally among Company men rather than taught in medical schools.

The simile Verne chooses — "more like the scratching of a currycomb than the caresses of a human hand" — is characteristic of his style: it grounds an extraordinary situation (reviving a frozen man) in a homely domestic image (grooming a horse). The currycomb, a tool for the care of animals, becomes, by metaphorical extension, a tool for the care of a man who has been reduced by cold to something less than human. The friction is violent because the cold is violent; gentleness would be fatal.

The scene also demonstrates a recurring Vernean pattern: the transformation of practical, physical labour — rubbing, fetching, carrying — into a form of heroism. Loin and Joliffe are not heroic in the conventional sense of the word; they do not make speeches or take risks. But their willing, competent performance of an unpleasant task — rubbing a naked stranger back to life — is portrayed as a species of quiet courage, the everyday heroism of the fur trade.

## Character Note

Sergeant Loin is a background figure whose brief appearance carries disproportionate narrative weight. He and Corporal Joliffe function as a pair throughout the revival scene — the Sergeant fetching snow while the Corporal undresses the patient, then both rubbing in tandem — their combined efforts demonstrating the culture of mutual reliance that characterises the fort. Though Loin speaks no dialogue, his physical competence and unquestioning participation in the rescue embody the practical, unglamorous virtues that sustain life at the Company's far-flung outposts: readiness, resourcefulness, and the willingness to rub a frozen stranger back to life with snow and elbow grease.

Loin's name does not appear in the roster of the expedition that departs for [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], suggesting he remains behind at Fort Reliance — one of the garrison soldiers whose duty keeps him at the base while others venture north. His brief cameo is a reminder of the many men and women whose names flicker through Verne's pages for a single chapter and then vanish, having served their narrative purpose. The novel does not tell us what becomes of him after Chapter III, but his few minutes of page-time — fetching snow, rubbing a frozen astronomer, helping save a life — are enough to earn him his place in the story.
