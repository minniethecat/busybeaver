# Russian Fur Company

The Russian Fur Company represents the Russian Empire's fur-trading interests in North America during the nineteenth century. By the time of *The Fur Country*, these operations had been leased to the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]], giving the British enterprise access to vast new territories stretching to the Pacific Ocean and the [[bering-sea|Bering Strait]].

## The 1842 Lease

In 1842, the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] secured a landmark agreement with the Russian government:

> [002-023] Moreover, in 1842 it took a lease of all the Russian establishments in North America at an annual rent of £40,000, so that it is now working on its own account the vast tracts of country between the Mississippi and the Pacific Ocean.

This lease was a strategic masterstroke. For £40,000 per year — a substantial but manageable sum — [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]] eliminated a potential rival from the entire Pacific Northwest and gained access to some of the richest fur territories on the continent.

## Strategic Significance

The lease had profound implications for [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s operations:

| Before Lease | After Lease |
|-------------|-------------|
| Operations limited to British territory | Access to Russian American territory |
| Pacific coast out of reach | Pacific fur trade under Company control |
| [[bering-sea|Bering Strait]] region independent | [[bering-sea|Bering Strait]] under Company influence |
| Competition from Russian traders | Russian competition eliminated via lease |

The lease meant the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] was now, in effect, operating the vast tracts of country "between the Mississippi and the Pacific Ocean" on its own account.

## Geographical Reach

The Russian establishments in North America centred on what was then called **Russian America** — the territory that would later become Alaska. The region stretched along the Pacific coast from the [[bering-sea|Bering Strait]] southward, encompassing the Aleutian Islands, Kodiak Island, and the coastal settlements of what are now Sitka and other Southeast Alaskan communities.

The connection to the [[bering-sea|Bering Strait]] is particularly significant for the novel: the strait represents both the western boundary of [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s domain and the dangerous waters toward which [[victoria-island|Victoria Island]] was drifting.
