# Sir John Franklin

**Sir John Franklin** (1786–1847) is the celebrated — and lost — British Arctic explorer whose memory haunts Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. Though Franklin himself never appears in the novel (he had perished more than a decade before the story's 1859–1860 setting), his name recurs throughout as a shorthand for both the glory and the terror of Arctic exploration. To follow in Franklin's steps, as [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]]'s expedition does, is to court the same fate that consumed Franklin and his 129 men.

## In the Novel

Franklin's name enters the novel early, at [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]'s soirée, where the assembled Company men speak with mingled reverence and dread of the explorers who preceded them:

> [001-027] "To follow in the steps of Hearne, Mackenzie, Rae, Franklin, and others."

The litany of names — Hearne, Mackenzie, [[rae|Rae]], Franklin — functions as both inspiration and warning. These are the men who mapped the Arctic, but Franklin is the one who did not return.

### Franklin as Cautionary Figure

The narrator returns to Franklin repeatedly, most strikingly in Chapter V, where the parallel between Hobson's situation and Franklin's is made explicit:

> [005-032] "Granted that Jaspar Hobson did not think of going beyond the seventieth parallel, we must still remember that Franklin and his unfortunate companions died of cold and hunger before they had penetrated beyond 68° N. lat."

This passage underscores a central theme of the novel: the Arctic does not discriminate between levels of ambition. Franklin died well short of his goal, and Hobson — who also stays within modest latitudes — is no safer.

In Chapter VII, [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]] places Franklin in the pantheon of Arctic explorers who tried and failed to reach the highest latitudes:

> [007-013] "The most intrepid discoverers of the Arctic regions — Parry, Penny, Franklin, McClure, Dane, and Morton — did not get beyond 83° north latitude, seven degrees from the pole."

Later, in Chapter X, the novel moves from the tragedy of Franklin's death to the heroic scale of his achievements. The narrator explains how the Hudson's Bay Company's network of posts enabled exploration:

> [010-023] "It did, madam; and it was thanks to it that Captain Franklin was able to accomplish his voyage of 1819 to 1822 between the river discovered by Hearne and Cape Turnagain. This expedition endured great fatigue and hardships; provisions often completely failed, and two Canadians were assassinated and eaten by their comrades. But in spite of all his sufferings, Captain Franklin explored no less than five thousand five hundred and fifty miles of the hitherto unknown coast of North America!"

### The Search for Franklin

The novel also alludes to the massive search efforts launched after Franklin's disappearance — an episode that gripped the British public for over a decade:

> [010-029] "Was it not they who aided Captain Back in his voyage in 1834, when he discovered King William's Land, where Franklin met his fate?"

Franklin's fate was not known until 1859, when Francis McClintock's expedition found a written record on King William Island. The novel, set in 1859–1860, occupies the exact historical moment when the mystery was being resolved. In a later chapter, [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]] recalls another search method employed by John Ross:

> [026-021] "John Ross in 1848 tried similar means to acquaint the survivors of the Franklin expedition with the presence of his ships, the *Enterprise* and the *Investigator* in the Polar seas. He caught some hundreds of white foxes in traps, rivetted a copper collar round the neck of each with all the necessary information engraved upon it, and then set them free in every direction."

The image of collared foxes carrying messages across the ice — messages that were never received — captures the desperate hope and ultimate futility of the Franklin search.

### Franklin and the Northwest Passage

The quest for the Northwest Passage that consumed Franklin is discussed at length in Chapter X:

> [010-021] "Phipps in 1773, James Cook and Clerke in 1776 to 1779, Kotzebue in 1815 to 1818, Ross, Parry, Franklin, and others have attempted this difficult task; but it was reserved to McClure in our own day to pass from one ocean to the other across the Polar Sea."

This passage places Franklin in a three-century sequence of explorers seeking the northern route between the Atlantic and Pacific. Franklin's contribution — mapping 5,550 miles of coastline — was monumental, even though the ultimate prize of traversing the Passage was claimed by McClure.

Franklin's 1819–1822 expedition, during which he mapped 5,550 miles of the American coast and lost eleven of his twenty men to starvation and murder, is specifically referenced by [[captain-craventy|Craventy]]'s guests. His final 1845 expedition — the one from which no one returned — was still an open wound in the British imagination when Verne wrote *The Fur Country* in 1873.

## Franklin's Expeditions

| Expedition | Years | Outcome |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Coppermine River | 1819–1822 | Mapped 5,550 miles; 11 of 20 men died; cannibalism documented |
| Mackenzie River | 1825–1827 | Mapped the Arctic coast from the Mackenzie delta to the Coppermine |
| Northwest Passage (*Erebus* and *Terror*) | 1845–1847 | Ships trapped in ice off King William Island; all 129 men perished |

The 1819–1822 expedition, undertaken on behalf of the Hudson's Bay Company, was Franklin's first Arctic command. It established the pattern that would define his career: extraordinary geographical achievement purchased at terrible human cost. The expedition's descent into starvation and cannibalism — "two Canadians were assassinated and eaten by their comrades" — earned Franklin the grim nickname "the man who ate his boots," a detail that Verne's narrator suppresses but that nineteenth-century readers would have known well.

## Franklin in the Novel's Scientific Passages

Franklin also appears in the novel's scientific and observational passages. In Chapter XV, when [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]] and his companions debate the puzzling absence of tides at Cape Bathurst, Franklin's authority is invoked:

> [015-010] "Well, madam," replied the Lieutenant, "two conclusions are open to us, either of which I find it difficult to believe; such men as Franklin, Parry, Ross, and others, are mistaken, and there are no tides on this part of the American coast; or, as in the Mediterranean, to which the waters of the Atlantic have not free ingress, the straits are too narrow to be affected by the ocean currents."

And when discussing the scale of ice fields in Chapter XXVIII, Franklin's observations are cited alongside those of Parry and Penny:

> [028-017] "Parry, Penny, and Franklin had met with ice fields in the Polar seas one hundred miles long and fifty broad. Captain Kellet abandoned his boat on an ice field measuring at least three hundred square miles, and what was Victoria Island compared to it?"

These citations demonstrate how thoroughly Franklin's empirical observations had been absorbed into the store of Arctic knowledge — his measurements, rather than his tragedy, become the lasting reference point.

## Historical Note

Franklin's ships, HMS *Erebus* and *Terror*, were not found until 2014 and 2016 respectively — 140 years after Verne's novel was published. Verne's readers would have known Franklin only as a figure of tragic mystery, the man who had found the Northwest Passage but could not return to report it. His presence in *The Fur Country* is thus doubly freighted: he is both the heroic predecessor and the cautionary ghost. The novel's Franklin references span the full arc of the Franklin story — from the triumphant 1819–1822 survey to the desperate searches of the 1850s — making him the single most densely referenced historical figure in the book.
