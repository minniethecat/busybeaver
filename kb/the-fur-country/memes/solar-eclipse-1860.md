# Solar Eclipse of 1860

The total solar eclipse of **July 18, 1860** is the astronomical event that sets the entire northern expedition of *The Fur Country* in motion. The astronomer [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]] was sent to the extreme north of America by the scientific societies of Europe to observe this eclipse, attaching himself to [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]]'s expedition as the most practical means of reaching the zone of totality.  The eclipse serves as both a scientific spectacle and a narrative device, marking a moment of cosmic drama that briefly unites the colonists in shared wonder. Thomas Black, the expedition's astronomer, had specifically journeyed to the Arctic to observe this event, making it the culmination of his personal quest and a validation of astronomical prediction against the backdrop of the polar wilderness.

## Scientific Significance

### Overview

The eclipse represented a rare opportunity to solve one of the great unsolved questions of nineteenth-century astronomy: the origin of the **solar corona** — the luminous halo visible around the moon during a total eclipse:

> [003-054] We know that during a total eclipse of the sun the moon is surrounded by a luminous corona. But what is the origin of this corona? Is it a real substance? or is it only an effect of the diffraction of the sun's rays near the moon? This is a question which science has hitherto been unable to answer.

The corona had been scientifically described since 1706 and studied by astronomers across Europe during total eclipses in 1715, 1724, 1778, 1806, and 1842. Despite the efforts of "learned men of all nations — Airy, Arago, Keytal, Langier, Mauvais, Otto, Struve, Petit, Baily," the observations remained contradictory:

> [003-055] "The disagreement," says Arago, "of the observations taken in different places by skilful astronomers of one and the same eclipse, have involved the question in fresh obscurity, so that it is now impossible to come to any certain conclusion as to the cause of the phenomenon."

Additionally, the eclipse offered the chance to study the **red prominences** — mysterious flame-like projections appearing on the edge of the moon at the moment of totality.

## International Coordination

The eclipse of 1860 was to be total across a zone spanning the extreme north of America, Spain, and North Africa. Astronomers from multiple countries arranged simultaneous observations at various points along this zone:

> [003-056] It was arranged between the astronomers of different countries that simultaneous observations should be taken at the various points of the zone where the eclipse would be total. Thomas Black was chosen for the expedition to North America.

## Thomas Black's Mission

[[thomas-black|Thomas Black]] seized the opportunity with characteristic scientific avidity. His mission was twofold:

| Objective | Scientific Question |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Solar corona | Origin and nature of the luminous halo |
| Red prominences | Nature of the flame-like projections during totality |

A successful observation would entitle him to "the applause of the learned men of all Europe."

## Timeline

The eclipse created an unusual urgency in the expedition's timeline. Black arrived at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] a full year before the eclipse date, eager to reach the observation point as quickly as possible:
