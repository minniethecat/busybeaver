## Role at Fort Confidence

Sergeant Felton is the second officer at Fort Confidence, a Hudson's Bay Company post on the shores of Great Bear Lake. When Lieutenant [[jaspar-hobson|Jaspar Hobson]]'s expedition arrives at the fort during the captain's absence, Felton assumes the role of host and facilitator:

> [008-007] "In the absence of the Captain a Sergeant did the honours of the fort to Jaspar Hobson and his companions. This second officer, Felton by name was a brother-in-law of Sergeant Long. He showed the greatest readiness to assist the views of the Lieutenant, who being anxious to rest his party, decided on remaining two or three days at Fort Confidence."

The family connection to Long immediately establishes Felton within the expedition's social network, transforming what might have been a purely transactional stopover into a moment of personal warmth. That Verne introduces the kinship tie in the same sentence as Felton's official role — "This second officer, Felton by name was a brother-in-law of Sergeant Long" — suggests that, in the world of the fur trade, family relationships are as much a part of a man's identity as his military rank.

## Intelligence and Logistics

Hobson's first concern upon arrival is intelligence about the region ahead, and Felton proves immediately useful:

> [008-008] "Jaspar Hobson's first care was to ask Felton if any Indians from the north were then beating the shores of the Great Bear Lake."

Felton reports a nearby encampment of Hare Indians at the northern extremity of the lake, some thirty miles distant:

> [008-011] "About thirty miles," replied Sergeant Felton. "Do you wish to enter into communication with these Indians?"

He organises a boat and boatman for the crossing, enabling Hobson and [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] to visit the Indian camp directly — a crucial reconnaissance for the expedition's next stage.

When the party departs, Felton's practical assistance extends further:

> [010-004] "Sergeant Felton had done all in his power to make his guests comfortable and to aid their enterprise; some of the jaded dogs were replaced by fresh animals, and now the Lieutenant found all his sledges drawn up in good order at the door of the enceinte, and awaiting the travellers."

The departure scene is warm with gratitude:

> [010-005] "The adieux were soon over. Each one thanked Sergeant Felton for his hospitality, and Mrs. Paulina Barnett was most profuse in her expressions of gratitude. A hearty shake of the hand between the Sergeant and his brother-in-law, Long, completed the leave-taking."

When the captain is absent on the day of departure, Felton takes formal leave of his guests with characteristic courtesy:

> [008-037] "Sergeant Felton took leave of his guests with many apologies for being unable to accompany them in the absence of his chief."

## Guide to the Lakeshore

Felton leads the party on an excursion along the shores of Great Bear Lake:

> [008-019] "Mrs. Barnett, Hobson, two or three soldiers, Madge, Mrs. Mac-Nab, and Joliffe explored the shores of the lake under the guidance of Felton. The neighbourhood was by no means barren of vegetation; the hills, now free from snow, were crowned by resinous trees of the Scotch pine species."

He guides them to a natural harbour sheltered by granite rocks where the fort's fishing-boat is anchored, and identifies the region's flora:

> [008-019] "Sergeant Felton informed his guests that this plant was called the 'herb of incense' on account of the fragrance it emits when burnt."

The excursion's highlight is Felton's skilled tracking and killing of an otter — a specimen whose pelt he values at between fifty and sixty pounds:

> [008-024] "But," added Sergeant Felton, after the preceding explanation, "American inland otters are not to be despised, and those which frequent the Great Bear Lake are worth from £50 to £60 each."

> [008-027] "Yes, Lieutenant," replied Felton; "and if each day brought us such a skin as that, we should have nothing to complain of."

Felton also provides a rare glimpse into the practical craft of fur hunting, explaining the difficulties of the trade:

> [008-027] "But much time is wasted in watching these animals, who swim and dive with marvellous rapidity. We generally hunt them at night, as they very seldom venture from their homes in the trunks of trees or the holes of rocks in the daytime, and even expert hunters find it very difficult to discover their retreats."

This passage is unusual in the novel: a minor character articulating the granular, technical knowledge that underpins the fur trade. Felton's description of nocturnal hunting, the otter's diving speed, and the challenge of locating their dens transforms him from a mere plot functionary into a representative of a specific kind of expertise — the practical natural history accumulated by generations of Company hunters.

## The Economics of the Fur Trade

Felton's commentary on pelt prices — fifty to sixty pounds for a single otter skin — provides one of the novel's few explicit references to the commercial realities of the Hudson's Bay Company's operations. The figure is striking: in an era when a Company labourer might earn twenty pounds a year, a single otter pelt represented two to three years' wages. The declining otter population that Felton laments is thus not merely an ecological observation but an economic one: the fur trade was consuming its own resource base, driving hunters ever further north in pursuit of increasingly scarce animals.

This economic subtext — the tension between sustainable harvesting and commercial extraction — runs beneath the surface of *The Fur Country* without ever becoming an explicit theme. Felton's remarks, placed in the mouth of a minor character and delivered almost in passing, are Verne's way of acknowledging the material foundations of the exploratory enterprise without allowing them to overshadow the adventure narrative. The expedition that Hobson leads is, after all, fundamentally a commercial venture — a fur-trading expedition — and Felton's otter hunt is a reminder of what everyone in the novel, from Hobson to the lowliest trapper, is ultimately there to do.

## Family Connection

Felton is connected to the expedition's inner circle through his marriage to Sergeant Long's sister, making him a brother-in-law to one of Hobson's most trusted men. This kinship tie is more than incidental: it transforms Fort Confidence from an impersonal Company outpost into a node in the expedition's extended family network, and Felton's hospitality — the fresh dogs, the boat, the guided excursion — carries the warmth of personal obligation rather than mere institutional duty.

The leave-taking scene, with its "hearty shake of the hand between the Sergeant and his brother-in-law, Long," closes the episode on a note of familial intimacy unusual in Verne's Arctic fiction. In a novel whose characters are, for the most part, bound together by professional rather than personal ties, the Long-Felton connection is a rare instance of blood (or marriage) asserting itself within the institutional fabric of the Company.
