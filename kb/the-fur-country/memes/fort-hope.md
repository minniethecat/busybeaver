# Fort Hope

**Fort Hope** (also referred to as **Fort Good Hope**) is the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] trading post established by [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]] on the shores of the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]] in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. Built with painstaking care by Hobson's expedition party, the fort serves as the central setting for the novel's second half — and becomes the stage for one of Verne's most ingenious plot devices when its residents discover that their settlement rests not on solid ground, but on a vast ice island drifting southward toward the [[bering-sea|Bering Sea]]. The fort served as the primary setting for much of the novel's action, housing the expedition members through both the construction phase and the long Arctic winter. Its strategic position at the foot of Cape Bathurst made it both a defensible position and a natural observation point over the surrounding lagoon and coastline.

## Location and Setting

### Overview

Fort Hope was founded on what appeared to be a peninsula extending into the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]], north of the [[great-bear-lake|Great Bear Lake]] and beyond the [[arctic-circle|Arctic Circle]]. The site was carefully chosen by [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] for its strategic advantages.

> [012-026] "The site was indeed admirably suited for the foundation of a new settlement. The wooded heights surrounding the lagoon would supply plenty of pine, birch, and other woods for the construction of the fort, and for stocking it with fuel."

The immediate surroundings included:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Lagoon** | Sheltered anchorage for boats |
| **Wooded Heights** | Source of pine, birch, and poplar timber |
| **Hills (east)** | Well-covered with pine and fir trees |
| **Tacamahac Trees** | Species of poplar growing to great height |

## Construction

The fort was built by Hobson's party of ten soldiers, two hunters ([[marbre|Marbre]] and [[sabine|Sabine]]), and their officers. The construction reflected both the practical needs of an Arctic trading post and Hobson's characteristic prudence.

> [014-011] "Hobson determined with almost excessive prudence to have the Factory enclosed before the winter set in. A strong fence of pointed stakes, planted firmly in the ground, was set up as a protection against the inroads of wild animals or the hostilities of wandering Indians."

> [013-004] "Of the ten soldiers chosen by Captain Craventy, two—Marbre and Sabine—were skilful hunters; the other eight handled the hatchet with as much address as the musket. Like sailors, they could turn their hands to anything."

## Naming

> [013-028] "The colonists now considered themselves fairly installed in their new abode, and after due deliberation unanimously agreed to bestow upon the settlement the name of Fort Good Hope."

The name "Fort Hope" (or "Fort Good Hope") reflects the optimism of the colonists at the time of its founding — an optimism that would later prove tragically misplaced when the ice island was discovered.

## The Catastrophe

The fort's tragic irony — that the "solid ground" beneath it was in fact a drifting sheet of ice — is the novel's central dramatic revelation. The settlement, built with such care and hope, becomes a fragile vessel adrift on the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]], carrying its inhabitants southward toward an uncertain fate.
