# Coppermine River

The Coppermine River is one of the great rivers of Arctic North America, flowing from the south to the north and emptying into the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]] at [[coronation-gulf|Coronation Gulf]]. It is one of the two principal waterways of the region alongside the [[great-bear-lake|Mackenzie River]], and provided [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]]'s expedition with the shortest route from the [[great-bear-lake|Great Bear Lake]] to the [[arctic-ocean|Polar Sea]].

## Discovery by Samuel Hearne

The river was discovered by the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] agent **Samuel Hearne**, whose epic three-attempt journey was recounted by Hobson to [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Paulina Barnett]]:

- **1769**: First attempt — forced back by extreme cold and exhaustion of provisions
- **1770**: Second attempt — survived seven days on wild fruit, bits of old leather, and burnt bones before returning
- **1770–1772**: Third attempt — after nineteen months of struggle, Hearne reached the river on July 13, 1772, following its course to the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]]

Hearne's original mission was to locate a copper mine reported by native miners. "As always happens in scientific matters, in seeking one thing, another was found."

## Geography

The river and the Mackenzie form the two main arteries of the region:

> [007-004] The country through which the little troop was now passing was intersected by numerous streams, mostly tributaries of the two large rivers, the Mackenzie and Coppermine, which flow from the south to the north, and empty themselves into the Arctic Ocean.

## Hobson's Route

The Indian chief at [[great-bear-lake|Great Bear Lake]] advised Hobson to descend the Coppermine as the most direct path to the coast:

> [008-042] He advised the Lieutenant to go down the Coppermine river, from the northeast of the lake, which would take him to the coast by the shortest route. Once at the Arctic Ocean, it would be easy to skirt along its shores.

The valley widened as the expedition neared the ocean, with hills sinking lower and scattered resinous trees breaking the monotony of the landscape.
