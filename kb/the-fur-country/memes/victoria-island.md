# Victoria Island

Victoria Island is the central geographical discovery of *The Fur Country*. Initially mapped as **Victoria Land**, a peninsula of the North American mainland, it is later revealed to be a massive floating ice-island — a sheet of ice hundreds of square miles in extent, veneered with earth and vegetation, that broke away from the continent during a subterranean convulsion. The island's discovery—and the subsequent revelation that it was not a peninsula at all but a vast ice floe—transforms the novel from a conventional expedition story into a survival narrative. The colonists' gradual realization of their predicament, driven by inexplicable changes in latitude and the disappearance of the isthmus, constitutes one of Verne's most masterfully constructed plot revelations. The island serves as the stage for one of the most dramatic revelations in Verne's body of work, turning the colonists' world upside down in a single moment of scientific clarity.

## Discovery and Naming

### Overview

[[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]] gave the territory the name **Victoria Land** upon first surveying the region:

> [013-027] After scouring the country within a radius of several miles, the Lieutenant notified, as the result of his observations, that the territory on which they had established themselves, and to which he gave the name of Victoria Land, was a large peninsula about one hundred and fifty square miles in extent, with very clearly-defined boundaries, connected with the American continent by an isthmus, extending from the lower end of Washburn Bay on the east, as fair as the corresponding slope on the opposite coast.

The name was retained as "Victoria Island" after the true nature of the landmass was discovered.

## Geological Revelation

The most dramatic revelation of the novel comes when the colonists discover that what they believed to be solid continental ground is in fact a vast sheet of ice:

> [024-002] This peninsula was in fact nothing but an immense piece of ice, five hundred square miles in extent, converted by successive deposits of sand and earth into apparently solid ground well clothed with vegetation. Connected with the mainland for thousands of centuries, the earthquake of the 8th of January had dragged it away from its moorings, and it was now a floating island, at the mercy of the winds and waves, and had been carried along the Arctic Ocean by powerful currents for the last three months!

Hobson later explains the island's formation more fully:

> [024-027] "The peninsula of Victoria, now an island, which we thought firm ground with an immovable foundation, is nothing more than a vast sheet of ice welded for centuries to the American continent. Gradually the wind has strewn it with earth and sand, and scattered over them the seeds from which have sprung the trees and mosses with which it is clothed."

Key geological clues included the absence of stones or flints on the surface, perpendicular shores, and the discovery of ice ten feet below ground when digging — all explained by the ice-foundation theory.

## Physical Characteristics

The island measured approximately **forty miles in circumference** with a surface area of about **one hundred and forty square miles** — comparable to St. Helena or Paris within its fortifications. It supported a freshwater lake (Lake Barnett), a small river (Paulina River), forests of birch, pine, and fir, and abundant wildlife including reindeer, Polar hares, and waterfowl.

The island's southern side was the former isthmus that connected it to the mainland:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Northern point | [[cape-bathurst|Cape Bathurst]] |
| Eastern shore | [[washburn-bay|Washburn Bay]] |
| Western shore | Walruses' Bay |
| Southern boundary | Broken isthmus (former connection to continent) |
| Inland water | Lake Barnett, Paulina River |

## Drift and Fate

After breaking from the continent, Victoria Island drifted westward, carried by an uncharted current towards the [[bering-sea|Bering Strait]]:

> [025-056] The wandering island was moving in a westerly direction, borne along by a current unmarked on the chart, and unknown to hydrographers, which was evidently carrying it towards Bering Strait.

The colonists' survival depended on the island either touching the mainland again before winter, or freezing into the vast Arctic ice field that would allow them to cross to safety.
