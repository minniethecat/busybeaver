## Literary Connection

Captain Hatteras is not an original character of *The Fur Country* but a cross-reference to Jules Verne's earlier novel *The Adventures of Captain Hatteras* (1866). Published just seven years before *The Fur Country*, *The Adventures of Captain Hatteras* recounts a British expedition's obsessive drive toward the North Pole — a narrative that established many of the Arctic tropes Verne would draw upon in his later work. By invoking Hatteras in *The Fur Country*, Verne creates a shared fictional universe between his *Voyages Extraordinaires*, a technique he employed across several novels.

### The Original Novel

*The Adventures of Captain Hatteras* (original French title: *Voyages et Aventures du Capitaine Hatteras*) was Verne's second novel to be published in book form, though it was the fourth he wrote. The novel tells the story of a British polar expedition led by the monomaniacal Captain John Hatteras, whose driving obsession is to be the first human to stand at the North Pole. The narrative is divided into two parts: "The English at the North Pole" and "The Desert of Ice." Hatteras's crew, unaware of his true objective, mutinies when they learn he intends to push beyond all known limits — a plot that prefigures the tensions between ambition and survival that structure much of Verne's later polar fiction.

The novel is notable for its unflinching depiction of Arctic extremes: starvation, madness, and the physical and psychological toll of polar exploration. Hatteras himself — proud, secretive, and ultimately destroyed by the very obsession that drives him — is one of Verne's most complex protagonists. His fate at the novel's climax, standing at the North Pole but driven mad by the discovery that it sits atop an active volcano, is a cautionary tale about the limits of human ambition, and a thematic precursor to the more measured and collective ethos of survival in *The Fur Country*.

The reference functions as an Easter egg for Verne's regular readers, rewarding familiarity with his expanding literary cosmos while grounding the new narrative in the established geography of his imagined Arctic.

### Verne's Shared Universe

The cross-reference to Hatteras in *The Fur Country* is part of a broader pattern in Verne's work. Across the *Voyages Extraordinaires* — a series that eventually numbered fifty-four novels — Verne wove a web of intertextual references, creating what might be called the first modern literary shared universe. Characters from one novel appear or are mentioned in another; geographical discoveries from one narrative become background knowledge in the next. Captain Nemo's Nautilus resurfaces in *The Mysterious Island*; the explorers of *In Search of the Castaways* trace the 37th parallel that appears again in *Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea*; and Hatteras's fox, captured somewhere on the Arctic ice, turns up years later in [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]]'s conversation at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]].

This technique serves a dual purpose. For the casual reader, the Hatteras reference is a simple anecdote about animal messengers that requires no prior knowledge to understand. For the devoted Verne reader, it is a rewarding callback that deepens the fictional world by suggesting its events are part of a continuous history, not isolated adventures.

## In The Fur Country

Hatteras appears in a single passing reference during a tour of Victoria Island in Part II, Chapter III. Lieutenant [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]], discussing the possibility of sending messages via animals, recalls Hatteras's unusual discovery:

> [026-023] "Perhaps so," replied Hobson; "I know that an old fox was taken by Captain Hatteras during his voyage of discovery, wearing a collar half worn away and hidden beneath his thick white fur."

Hobson uses this anecdote to suggest an alternative to quadrupeds for carrying messages — birds:

> [026-023] "What we cannot do with the quadrupeds, we will do with the birds."

The reference is brief but layered: it places the events of *The Fur Country* in the same Arctic geography as Hatteras's adventures, it invokes the authority of a prior expedition (even a fictional one) to validate the plan under discussion, and it suggests that the two expeditions, though distinct in their personnel and objectives, share the same frozen world. The "half worn away" collar is itself a suggestive detail — a message that nearly arrived, a communication that time and the elements have almost erased, prefiguring the novel's concern with messages sent but never received.

### Chapter Context

The conversation occurs during a period of relative calm at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], before the discovery that Victoria Island is drifting. The colonists are debating practical means of communication with the outside world, unaware that their own situation is about to become far more precarious. The reference to Hatteras's fox thus carries dramatic irony: the characters discuss a message that almost failed to arrive, at a moment when their own messages — when they eventually attempt to send them — will face far greater obstacles than a worn collar.

## Animal Messengers in Arctic Exploration

The reference to Hatteras's collared fox serves a practical narrative function: it introduces the theme of animal-borne communication in the Arctic wilderness. The conversation in which the fox is mentioned unfolds as Fort Hope's residents debate how to send word of their situation to the outside world. [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] proposes using carrier birds, to which Hobson responds enthusiastically:

> [026-021] "A good idea, madam, a capital idea," said Hobson. "We might easily catch some hundreds of these birds, and tie a paper round their necks with our exact situation written upon it. John Ross in 1848 tried similar means to acquaint the survivors of the Franklin expedition with the presence of his ships."

Hobson's reference to the real historical precedent — John Ross's 1848 attempt to contact the lost [[franklin|Franklin]] expedition survivors via animal messengers — anchors Verne's fictional scenario in documented Arctic history. The collared fox anecdote thus links Hatteras's fictional voyage, the historical tragedy of Franklin, and the immediate predicament of Fort Hope's residents within a single thematic thread: the desperate ingenuity required for communication across the frozen void.

The chain of references is carefully layered: a fictional explorer (Hatteras) provides an anecdote that parallels a real historical attempt (Ross), both of which inform a fictional plan (the carrier birds) to address a fictional crisis (the drifting island). Verne uses this interleaving of fact and fiction to lend credibility to his narrative while maintaining its imaginative freedom.

## The Collared Fox as Symbol

The "half worn away" collar is more than a plot convenience — it is a miniature of the novel's central anxiety about communication and erasure. The fox carried its message for an unknown span of time, across an unknown distance, before being caught. The collar was "hidden beneath his thick white fur," meaning the message was always there, invisible to any observer who did not already know to look for it. This is the predicament of Fort Hope's residents: their situation is real, urgent, and invisible to the outside world. Like the fox, they move through a white expanse carrying a message no one is looking for.

The fox is also a creature of the Arctic itself — white-furred, adapted, indigenous. That the message was entrusted to such an animal, only to be "half worn away" by the elements, suggests the Arctic's indifference to human communication. The environment that the fox navigates effortlessly is the same environment that erases the human marks upon it. The collar, half-gone, is what remains of the human attempt to inscribe meaning onto the Arctic's blank surface — a theme that Verne explores with increasing intensity as Victoria Island drifts toward its dissolution.

## Historical Note

While Hatteras is fictional, his expedition's premise — a relentless push toward the North Pole — reflected the real ambitions of contemporary Arctic explorers. The period between 1845 and 1875 saw dozens of expeditions launched in pursuit of the Pole, many of them ending in disaster. The collared fox Hatteras captured symbolises the theme of lost messages, of communications attempted but never delivered, which runs through both *The Adventures of Captain Hatteras* and *The Fur Country*. In both novels, the Arctic emerges as a space where human signals — whether collars on foxes or notes tied to birds — are swallowed by the white expanse, and where the line between a message sent and a message received is measured in lives.
