# Madge

**Madge** is the faithful servant and devoted companion of [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. She accompanies the celebrated traveller on the Arctic expedition to [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], sharing every hardship and danger alongside her mistress. Though a secondary character, Madge represents the novel's quiet recognition that the achievements of celebrated figures depend on the loyalty and labour of those who serve them — and she emerges as a distinct personality in her own right, with a pragmatic wit and unexpected skills that enrich the expedition's daily life.

## In the Novel

Madge appears as [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]]'s constant shadow throughout the novel. She is introduced alongside Barnett at [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]'s soirée at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]], and remains with the expedition from its departure through the establishment of [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]].

> [001-028] "We must add that the celebrated traveller was accompanied by a servant named Madge. This faithful creature was not merely a servant, but a devoted and courageous friend, who lived only for her mistress."

Verne provides a rare physical description: Madge is "a Scotchwoman of the old type, whom a Caleb might have married without loss of dignity." She is about five years older than Mrs. Barnett, tall and strongly built:

> [001-028] "Madge was about five years older than Mrs. Barnett, and was tall and strongly built. The two were on the most intimate terms; Paulina looked upon Madge as an elder sister, and Madge treated Paulina as her daughter."

This familial framing is crucial — Madge is not a subordinate but a protector, and the maternal dimension of her devotion adds emotional weight to every hardship she endures.

### The Journey North

Madge participates actively in the overland journey, not as a passive passenger but as a capable traveller in her own right. She is provided with a sledge "rather more comfortable than the others" — a concession to her gender and status, but one she does not exploit. She drives her own team, "dexterously wielding the long Eskimo whip, terminating in a stiff thong":

> [005-009] "Jaspar Hobson and Sergeant Long went first; then Mrs. Paulina Barnett and Madge, the latter dexterously wielding the long Eskimo whip, terminating in a stiff thong."

The detail of Madge handling the Eskimo whip — a notoriously difficult implement requiring considerable skill — establishes her competence from the outset. She is not merely enduring the Arctic; she is navigating it with the same practical capability she brings to every other task.

### A Pragmatic Counterpoint

One of Madge's most memorable moments comes during the overland journey, when [[paulina-barnett|Barnett]] marvels at the Arctic landscape and contrasts it with the heat of their previous travels in Australia and India. Madge's response is a masterpiece of dry pragmatism:

> [005-024] "'No, Paulina,' replied Madge, wrapping her furs more closely round her, 'no, I remember nothing. How could I now recollect the sufferings to which you allude — the heat, the agonies of thirst — when we are surrounded on every side by ice?'"

She continues: "'You talk to me of heat, when we are freezing beneath our bearskins; you recall the broiling rays of the sun when its April beams cannot melt the icicles on our lips! No, child, no, don't try to persuade me it's hot anywhere else; don't tell me I ever complained of being too warm, for I shan't believe you!'"

This exchange reveals the fundamental difference between the two women: Barnett is an enthusiast who sees poetry and beauty in the frozen wilderness; Madge is a pragmatist who, as the narrator notes:

> [005-031] "Madge, more matter of fact than her mistress, disguised from herself neither the dangers of an expedition to the Arctic Ocean, nor the sufferings involved in wintering only thirty degrees at the most from the North Pole."

Madge's refusal to romanticise mirrors the novel's own ambivalence about the Arctic enterprise. Where Barnett sees sublimity, Madge sees frostbite; where Barnett recalls tropical heat with nostalgia, Madge — shivering in her bearskins — doubts such heat ever existed. The comedy of the exchange masks its wisdom: Madge's here-and-now pragmatism is a survival trait in a landscape where distraction can be fatal.

### The Angler

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Madge discovers an unexpected talent. She becomes a skilled angler, matching [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]] himself in the art:

> [016-008] "The faithful Madge, another worthy disciple of Isaak Walton, was perhaps his only equal. Day after day the two sallied forth together rod in hand, to spend the day in mute companionship by the riverside."

The image of Madge and Sergeant Long fishing together in silent companionship is one of the novel's quietest and most human moments — two practical, loyal people finding common ground in the simple act of catching fish for the colony. The reference to Isaak Walton, author of *The Compleat Angler* (1653), places Madge in a venerable English tradition of contemplative fishing, a cultural touchstone that Verne's readers would have recognised.

### Household Management

Madge's contributions to the colony extend beyond companionship and fishing. Together with [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]], she "was invaluable in providing for all the small wants, which would inevitably have escaped the notice of the men." This phrase — "the small wants" — encompasses the entire domestic infrastructure without which the fort could not function: the mending, the food preparation, the organisation of living spaces, the attention to comfort that transforms a barracks into a habitation.

She helps manage the household, shares a dormitory with her mistress looking out upon the lake, and assists in the manufacture of winter garments from the fort's abundant furs. She explores the shores of the Great Bear Lake alongside Barnett, Hobson, and the soldiers, and later accompanies Barnett into an abandoned Eskimo snow-hut — each excursion demonstrating that her role as "servant" carries no implication of passivity or helplessness.

### The Winter Work

As the polar night descends on [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Madge joins the other women of the colony — [[mrs-joliffe|Mrs. Joliffe]], Mrs. Mac-Nab, and Mrs. Rae — in the essential indoor labour that sustains the settlement. The manufacture of winter clothing from the furs brought in by [[marbre|Marbre]] and [[sabine|Sabine]] is a communal effort, and Madge's skill with needle and thread contributes to the stockpile of warm garments upon which survival depends. In an Arctic winter, a well-sewn coat is as vital as a well-aimed rifle.
