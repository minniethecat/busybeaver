# Cape Bathurst

**Cape Bathurst** is the prominent headland on the Arctic coast of North America above the seventieth parallel, and the destination of [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]]'s expedition in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. It is here, at the cape's extremity, that Hobson chooses to build [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] — a decision that appears strategically sound but conceals a geological secret that will determine the fate of the entire colony. The cape's distinctive geological features and its position at the threshold of the Arctic Ocean made it a natural landmark for navigators and explorers venturing into the polar regions. Its eastern face, covered with a thick layer of dry moss, provided essential materials for the colonists' construction efforts. The cape's strategic location made it an ideal site for the new settlement, offering natural advantages that Hobson and his men would soon put to use in constructing Fort Hope.

## In the Novel

### Overview

The expedition's journey to Cape Bathurst occupies the early chapters of the novel. From [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]], Hobson leads his party northwest across the [[great-bear-lake|Great Bear Lake]], through uncharted territory, and along the Arctic coast, with the cape as his fixed objective. The choice of location is described in careful geographical detail:

> [012-020] "It seemed that the extremity of Cape Bathurst would be a most favourable site for the new fort, as with this lagoon behind them, and the sea open for four or five months in the warm season, and giving access to vessels from Bering Strait."

This description — the lagoon, the open sea, the access to Pacific shipping — presents Cape Bathurst as an ideal site. The irony, which the reader only gradually apprehends, is that the cape's apparent advantages are built on a foundation that is not land at all.

> [011-005] "The coastline, running in a northeasterly direction, abruptly crosses the seventieth parallel at a sharp angle near the one hundred and thirtieth meridian."

The cape's geography is described with unusual precision for a Verne novel — a reflection of its narrative importance. The lagoon that lies behind it, the open sea stretching northward to the Pole, the "recent atlases" that "give no land beyond the north American coastline" — all these details become crucial when the true nature of the cape's foundation is revealed.

> [010-008] "The deep hollow formed by the gulf is bounded on the north by Cape Krusenstein, and from it the coast juts out towards the northwest, ending in Cape Bathurst, which is above the seventieth parallel."

## Historical Note

Cape Bathurst is a real geographical feature, named after Henry Bathurst, 3rd Earl Bathurst (1762–1834), who served as British Secretary of State for War and the Colonies. It was first charted during the Arctic expeditions of the early nineteenth century and remains a named cape on modern maps of the Northwest Territories.
