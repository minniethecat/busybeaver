# Rae

**Rae** is the blacksmith of [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. In an Arctic settlement where metal is the difference between survival and catastrophe, Rae's forge is one of the colony's most critical resources — and his courage, as [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] recognises, makes him one of "the bravest men in his party."

## Selection for the Expedition

Rae was among the guests assembled at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] when [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]] announced the formation of the expedition under [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]]. He is described as "John Rae, married to an Indian woman of the country" — one of the sixty soldiers and employees of the Hudson's Bay Company present at the festival. His selection reflected the expedition's need for a skilled smith who could fabricate and repair ironwork in a region where no manufactured replacements could be obtained.

## In the Novel

Rae arrives at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] as one of the picked men selected by [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]], listed among the carpenters who will build the new factory under [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]]'s supervision. He is one of the soldiers who, "like sailors, could turn their hands to anything," but Rae brings a specialised craft: his portable forge and his skill with iron distinguish him from the general labourers and hunters.

> [013-004] "Rae was most skilful at blacksmith's work, and with the aid of a little portable forge he was able to make all the pins, tenons, bolts, nails, screws, and nuts required."

This quiet description understates Rae's importance. In the Arctic, without access to manufactured goods, the ability to forge and repair metal tools is literally life-sustaining.

### Building Fort Hope

Rae's forge was central to the construction of [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]]. It was Rae who drove the strong iron bolts through the timber joints to secure the walls of the principal house. When even bolting proved insufficient to close the interstices hermetically, [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]] turned to calking — a process borrowed from shipbuilding — and Rae's ironwork remained the structural backbone of the entire edifice:

> [013-015] "To insure their being properly joined, Rae the blacksmith drove strong iron bolts through them at intervals; and when even this contrivance proved insufficient to close the interstices as hermetically as was necessary, Mac-Nab had recourse to calking, a process which seamen find invaluable in rendering vessels watertight; only as a substitute for tow he used a sort of dry moss, with which the eastern side of the cape was covered, driving it into the crevices with calking-irons and a hammer, filling up each hollow with layers of hot tar, obtained without difficulty from the pine-trees."

Rae's contributions extend beyond construction. As winter sets in at the fort, he is entrusted with setting up the condensers for collecting vapour suspended in the air, a critical task for managing the colony's water supply during the months of intense cold.

> [017-008] "About this time, Rae set up his condensers for collecting the vapour suspended in the air, which were to be emptied twice a week."

### The Bear Attack — Carnivorous Bay

Rae's character is most vividly displayed during the polar bear siege at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]]. When bears break through the loft of the principal house and threaten the entire colony, [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] calls together Long, [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]], and Rae as "the bravest men in his party." The three men volunteer for the desperate mission to retrieve fuel from the outer wood-house — a mission upon which the fort's survival depends, as without fire the colonists would freeze.

> [021-032] "He called together Long, Mac-Nab, and Rae the blacksmith, as the bravest men in his party."

Rae's courage manifests in action throughout the crisis. While [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]] volunteers to be the first to go outside, Rae climbs up to the trapdoor to reconnoitre:

> [021-043] "Rae climbed up to the trapdoor of the loft, and peeping through it, made sure that the bears were still on the roof."

He keeps watch over the bears' movements while Hobson and the others prepare. When Sergeant Long's mission appears to end in tragedy — the bears seeming to have devoured him — Rae's humanity breaks through his stoic exterior:

> [021-053] "A cry of grief and despair burst from the lips of Mac-Nab and Rae."

Yet moments later, when Hobson is struggling to close the inner door against a charging bear, Rae and Mac-Nab rush to their officer's assistance. Throughout the long hours of the siege, Rae remains in the thick of the action — crying "Fire!" to coordinate the defence with burning brands, and repeatedly offering himself for the fuel runs upon which the fort's survival depends:

> [021-059] "'Fire!' cried the blacksmith Rae."

> [021-065] "'I will go — I will go and fetch some wood!' cried the blacksmith Rae."

He and Hobson eventually venture out together into the darkness and cold to bring back the wood that saves the colony.

### The Married Man's Burden

Rae's married status becomes a recurring theme in Hobson's command decisions. When the Lieutenant must replace [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]] for a dangerous mission, the fact that Rae and [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]] are married men weighs heavily:

> [023-033] "It is true either Mac-Nab or Rae could have taken the Sergeant's place; but then they were married, and the one being a master carpenter, and the other the only blacksmith, the colonists could not well have dispensed with their services."

This dual consideration — Rae's family obligations and the irreplaceability of his craft — makes him simultaneously too valuable to risk and too valuable to lose. Hobson's calculus reflects the complex economy of human resources in an isolated Arctic colony.

### The Celestial Discovery

Rae is among the three men who independently deduce the truth about Victoria Island's drift. When [[marbre|Marbre]] first notices that the sun and stars are rising and setting on an altered horizon, he confides his suspicions to [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]] and Rae:

> [035-005] "Marbre, upon whom the truth had first dawned, confided his suspicions to Mac-Nab the carpenter and Rae the blacksmith."

Their shared knowledge becomes a quiet burden. As the celestial disorientation grows more pronounced, these three men — accustomed to reading the sky as labourers read the land — recognise what has happened before the officers confirm it:

> [033-042] "It was impossible that men like Mac-Nab, Rae, Marbre and others, accustomed to note the signs of the heavens, could fail to be struck by the change, and understand its meaning."

The three men face the revelation "calmly enough" and resolve to keep their discovery secret so as not to alarm their comrades and wives — a pact of stoic discretion that speaks to their character.

## Marriage and Family

Rae is married to an Indian woman of the country — one of the mixed-race unions that characterised life in the Hudson's Bay Company territories. His wife, Mrs. Rae, is part of the small community of married women at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] alongside [[mrs-joliffe|Mrs. Joliffe]] and Mrs. Mac-Nab.

> [013-016] "Mrs. Joliffe, Mrs. Mac-Nab, and Mrs. Rae were to lodge in the cabins of the fourth compartment. They would certainly be packed pretty closely; but it was only a temporary state of things."

The Raes occupy one of the dormitories in the principal house, where "these good people agreed so well together that it would have been a pity to separate them." During the long Arctic winter, Mrs. Rae joins the other women in their indefatigable labour:

> [028-047] "Mrs. Barnett, Madge, Mrs. Mac-Nab, Mrs. Rae, and Mrs. Joliffe — when she could leave her fires — were alike indefatigable."

She assists in the kitchen under [[mrs-joliffe|Mrs. Joliffe]]'s direction, forming part of the domestic economy that sustains the fort through the polar night.

## A Trusted Counsellor

By the later stages of the colony's ordeal, Rae has earned a place among [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]]'s inner circle of trusted advisers. When the Lieutenant must decide whether the colonists should abandon the disintegrating ice island, he turns to the same men who have proven their judgment and courage throughout the crisis:

> [043-030] "He consulted Sergeant Long, Mac-Nab, Rae, Marbre, and Sabine, in whom he had great confidence, and all agreed that it would be unwise to abandon the island before they were obliged."

This consultation confirms Rae's status — the blacksmith has become not merely a skilled labourer but a man whose opinion carries weight in the colony's most consequential decisions.

## The Saved Iron

Rae's foresight proves decisive at the very end of the colonists' ordeal. When the ice island begins its final disintegration and the survivors must build a raft to escape, it is Rae's hoard of iron that makes construction possible:

> [042-028] "The blacksmith, Rae, had fortunately found a large number of the iron bolts which had been brought from Fort Reliance, and they were invaluable for firmly fastening together the different portions of the framework of the raft."

These bolts, originally brought for construction and construction alone, become the literal fastenings upon which all the colonists' lives depend. It is a fitting coda to Rae's story: the blacksmith's iron, forged and saved through months of Arctic hardship, holds together the vessel that carries the survivors home.
