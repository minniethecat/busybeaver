# Thomas Black

**Thomas Black** is the eccentric astronomer who joins the Arctic expedition in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. His arrival at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] — frozen stiff in his sledge and requiring rescue by [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]] — is one of the novel's most memorable comic set-pieces. Throughout the story, Black's scientific obsessions, particularly his determination to observe a solar eclipse and the [[midnight-sun|midnight sun]], provide both comic relief and a vehicle for Verne's astronomical education of the reader.

## In the Novel

### Dramatic Arrival

Thomas Black is introduced dramatically in Chapter III, when his sledge bursts through the gates of [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] in the middle of the night. He has travelled from England to observe an eclipse of the sun predicted for 18th July 1860, and his monomaniacal pursuit of this goal has driven him into the Arctic with inadequate preparation.

> [003-022] "'The astronomer, Thomas Black.'"

The scene of his rescue is both tense and darkly comic. Frozen stiff, Black is carried into the fort by [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]], [[corporal-joliffe|Corporal Joliffe]], and a courier. His wrappings are peeled away to reveal a corpulent man of about fifty, barely alive.

> [003-025] "Thomas Black, carried by the Sergeant, the Corporal, and the courier, now made his entrance into the house of the fort, and was taken to a room on the first floor."

> [003-026] "The wrappers and furred mantles, in which Thomas Black was rolled up like a parcel requiring care, were removed, and revealed a man of about fifty."

[[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]]'s swift action saves Black's life. The astronomer is revived through the combined efforts of the fort's residents — snow-rubbing to restore circulation, followed by [[corporal-joliffe|Corporal Joliffe]]'s punch to warm him from within.

> [003-029] "Very happily for Thomas Black, however, Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson had another idea."

### The Journey North

Once recovered, Black joins the expedition's overland march to the Arctic coast. However, his participation is reluctant at best. Unlike [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]], who embraces the journey with curiosity, Black remains absorbed in his single scientific objective and shows little interest in the world around him.

> [004-025] "As for Thomas Black, he never opened his lips except when his own special mission was discussed."

His physical unsuitability for Arctic travel is a recurring motif. Corpulent and unaccustomed to exertion, he rarely leaves his sledge during the long marches.

> [007-030] "Thomas Black alone, absorbed in his one idea, never got out of his sledge, and indeed he was so corpulent that all exertion was disagreeable to him."

Yet when the landscape unexpectedly reveals its beauty, even Black is moved. Exploring the shores of a lagoon beyond the Arctic Circle, he expresses genuine surprise at the charm of the spot — a rare moment of engagement with his surroundings.

> [012-030] "'Well, Lieutenant,' said the astronomer when the examination was over, 'this is really a charming spot, such as I should not have imagined could have existed beyond the Arctic Circle.'"

### Life at Fort Hope

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Black settles into a routine of astronomical observation. He is assigned quarters in the principal house, sharing space with [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]], [[paulina-barnett|Barnett]], and [[madge|Madge]] (PN 013-008). His days and nights revolve around his instruments, and he frets impatiently whenever weather prevents his observations.

> [018-009] "Thomas Black screwed and unscrewed his instruments, and looked over his astronomical calculations, remaining almost always shut up in his cabin, fretting and fuming at the storm which prevented him from making nocturnal observations."

When the skies finally clear, however, his joy is unbounded. Verne gives him one of the novel's most rhapsodic moments of scientific wonder.

> [018-023] "Exclamations of admiration burst involuntarily from the lips of the delighted astronomer as he gazed into the cloudless heavens, once more undimmed by mists or vapours. Never had a more beautiful sky been spread out before the eyes of an astronomer."

Even the sublime spectacle of the northern lights leaves Black indifferent — he is too absorbed in his own calculations to notice.

> [017-012] "Thomas Black was the only one who remained indifferent to the sublime beauty of the scene. But what could one expect of an astronomer so wrapped up in his one idea, that he might be said to be present in the little colony in the body, but absent in spirit?"

### The Avalanche

Black's fate becomes entangled with [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]]'s when both are buried by the avalanche that strikes [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]]. For fifty-four hours, he remains trapped alongside her, [[madge|Madge]], and [[kalumah|Kalumah]]. When [[kalumah|Kalumah]] leads [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]] to the buried chamber, Black is the first to be found — alive, but barely.

> [041-068] "It was Thomas Black."

The entire colony had mobilised to rescue them, men wielding pickaxes and women tending fires, united by the desperate hope that the buried party might still be alive (PN 041-024). Black's survival, like Barnett's, testifies to the resilience that even the most unlikely Arctic traveller can discover when survival demands it.

## Character and Traits

Black is one of Verne's most successful comic characters — ridiculous without being contemptible, obsessive without being unsympathetic. His defining characteristics form a coherent portrait of the pure scientist:

- **Monomaniacal focus**: Black's entire being is consumed by his astronomical mission. He speaks only of the eclipse, thinks only of his observations, and views the entire Arctic expedition as merely the inconvenient conveyance to his observing site.
- **Physical comedy**: His corpulence and helplessness create a running contrast with the lean, capable soldiers around him. The image of Black "rolled up like a parcel" and requiring three men to carry him is one of the novel's most vivid comic images.
- **Genuine scientific passion**: Despite his absurdity, Black is no fraud. When the skies clear and he can finally observe, his joy is authentic and moving. Verne respects his science even as he mocks his person.
- **Accidental courage**: Black does not seek danger, but when the avalanche buries him, he endures fifty-four hours of entombment and survives — a resilience he likely never suspected in himself.

## Narrative Role

Black serves a dual function in the novel. As a scientist, he provides Verne's characteristic scientific exposition — explaining astronomical phenomena and lending authority to the novel's engagement with contemporary science. As a character, his physical awkwardness and monomaniacal focus on his observations create comic tension with the practical, action-oriented soldiers of the [[hudsons-bay-company|HBC]].

His presence also enriches the intellectual texture of the expedition. Where Hobson represents commercial and administrative competence, and [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] represents the spirit of exploration, Black embodies the pure scientific impulse — the drive to understand natural phenomena regardless of personal cost.

## Observations

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Black becomes absorbed in observing the [[midnight-sun|midnight sun]] and other Arctic astronomical phenomena. His instruments and calculations form a counterpoint to the physical labour of building and maintaining the settlement. He handles his equipment with genuine skill, and [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]] himself is capable of matching Black's precision with the instruments (PN 007-008).
