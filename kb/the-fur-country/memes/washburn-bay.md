# Washburn Bay

Washburn Bay is a deep indentation on the eastern coastline of [[victoria-island|Victoria Island]]. It served as a significant geographical marker for [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]]'s colonists, forming part of the original isthmus boundary and later becoming a crucial observation point during the island's drift.

## Discovery

The bay was first encountered by Hobson's expedition on the 4th of July during their exploration of the newly named [[victoria-island|Victoria Land]]:

> [012-019] On the 4th July the travellers skirted round another deep bay called Washburn Bay, and reached the furthest point of a little lake, until then imperfectly known, covering but a small extent of territory.

The bay was named by the colonists as part of their systematic mapping of the peninsula's coastline.

## Geographical Significance

Washburn Bay held a critical position in the original geography of [[victoria-island|Victoria Land]]. When Hobson first surveyed the territory as a peninsula, the isthmus connecting it to the American continent extended from the lower end of Washburn Bay on the east:

> [013-027] The territory on which they had established themselves, and to which he gave the name of Victoria Land, was a large peninsula about one hundred and fifty square miles in extent, with very clearly-defined boundaries, connected with the American continent by an isthmus, extending from the lower end of Washburn Bay on the east, as fair as the corresponding slope on the opposite coast.

After the earthquake broke the isthmus, this connection was severed, and Washburn Bay became part of the coastline of a drifting island.

## Exploration Circumnavigation

Hobson used Washburn Bay as a key waypoint during his circular exploration of the island's perimeter:

> [027-006] Hobson intended to encamp for the night at Washburn Bay. When there eighteen miles would have been traversed, and, if he were not mistaken, half his circular journey would be accomplished.

The exploration route ran from Walruses' Bay to Washburn Bay, then around the southern side of the island. At Washburn Bay, the explorers gained an extended southern view, though northern visibility was limited by the low sun on the horizon.
