# Hudson's Bay Company

The **Hudson's Bay Company** (HBC) is the historic British fur-trading corporation that serves as the institutional backbone of Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. Chartered in 1670 by King Charles II, the Company held a monopoly over the vast territory known as Rupert's Land — an area encompassing much of present-day Canada. In the novel, the HBC is not merely background but an active force that shapes every character's life and drives the expedition to the Arctic coast.

## In the Novel


> [001-002] "The Hudson's Bay Company, that powerful association of trappers, traders, and adventurers, had long ago stretched its network of forts and factories across the entire breadth of British North America."
### Overview

The Company's presence is pervasive throughout *The Fur Country*. [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]] is "a child of the Company" — his father served the HBC for forty years, and Jaspar himself was born into its service. The expedition to establish [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] is a Company enterprise, ordered by the directors in London and executed by [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]] at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]].

> [002-016] "The Captain then proceeded to give a brief sketch of the history of the famous Hudson's Bay Company."

The novel devotes an entire chapter (Chapter II) to the Company's history and operations, reflecting Verne's characteristic blend of fiction and documentary. The HBC's commercial logic — the search for new fur territories as older hunting grounds were depleted — provides the rational motivation for Hobson's expedition.

## The Fur Trade Imperative

The Company's decision to push north of the [[arctic-circle|Arctic Circle]] is driven by a simple economic calculus: the depletion of fur-bearing animals in more southerly territories. This commercial imperative, rather than scientific curiosity or national glory, is the engine that drives the narrative forward.

> [002-046] "Yes," said Mrs. Paulina Barnett, "the fact of the fur-bearing animals having taken refuge beyond the polar circle, is a sufficient explanation of the Company's motive in founding a factory on the borders of the Arctic Ocean."

## Motto

The Company's Latin motto — *Pro Pelle Cutem* ("a skin for a skin") — encapsulates its philosophy and the risks its employees undertake. The phrase suggests a transactional relationship with danger: one risks one's own skin in pursuit of animal skins.

## Historical Context

The HBC is one of the few elements of *The Fur Country* grounded firmly in historical reality. Founded in 1670, the Company still exists today as a Canadian retail business (The Bay). Verne's portrait is broadly accurate: the Company did maintain a network of forts across Rupert's Land, did employ a mix of European officers and Indigenous hunters, and did explore the Arctic in search of new fur territories.
