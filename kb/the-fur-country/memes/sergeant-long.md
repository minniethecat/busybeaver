# Sergeant Long

**Sergeant Long** is the loyal second-in-command to [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]] in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. A fifty-year-old veteran of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]], he represents the ideal of [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]] soldier: constitutionally brave, absolutely obedient, and utterly devoted to his commanding officer. His laconic responses and unshakeable composure under pressure provide both comic relief and a moral anchor throughout the expedition.

## In the Novel

Sergeant Long accompanies [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]]'s expedition to the Arctic coast from its departure at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]]. He is one of the first characters introduced in any detail, and Verne's physical description of him — with his beard "like coconut fibre" — is among the novel's most vivid character sketches.

Throughout the journey, Sergeant Long serves as Hobson's right hand. When the Lieutenant muses on the impossibility of their mission and asks hypothetically whether Long would return if ordered to go where no man could, Long's reply distills his character into a single word: "I should come back." This exchange encapsulates the blend of literal-minded obedience and quiet confidence that makes him a memorable figure.

> [001-025] "If the lieutenant was the type of a good officer, Sergeant Long was that of a good soldier. He was a man of fifty years of age, with a rough beard that looked as if it were made of coconut fibre. Constitutionally brave, and disposed to obey rather than to discuss an order, he was a true specimen of the disciplined soldier, and was sure to prove an invaluable auxiliary to Jaspar Hobson."

### The Brother-in-Law: Sergeant Felton

One of the few personal details Verne provides about Sergeant Long's life outside the expedition is his connection to [[felton|Sergeant Felton]], his brother-in-law who commands [[fort-confidence|Fort Confidence]] on the shores of the [[great-bear-lake|Great Bear Lake]] in the absence of its captain. When Hobson's party reaches Fort Confidence on May 30th, this relationship adds a rare layer of familial warmth to the otherwise stoic Sergeant [008-007].

Long's conduct at Fort Confidence reveals a different side of his character. During the party's three-day rest at the fort, "Sergeant Long did the honours of the table, and after a little pleasant conversation, all retired to rest to forget their fatigues in a healthy and refreshing sleep" [008-035]. Far from being merely a rigid disciplinarian, Long takes evident pride in his brother-in-law's post and hospitality — he personally escorts Hobson and Mrs. Barnett to the little harbour on the morning they set out across the lake [008-037].

When the expedition departs on June 2nd, the parting is marked by a simple but telling gesture: "A hearty shake of the hand between the Sergeant and his brother-in-law, Long, completed the leave-taking" [010-005]. The two Sergeant brothers-in-law — both career HBC men commanding remote outposts — mirror each other: competent, loyal, and uncomplaining servants of the Company at the extreme edge of the known world. After this farewell, Long rides in the sledge following Hobson and Mrs. Barnett, paired now with [[madge|Madge]] [010-006].

### The Arctic Circle Tempest

Sergeant Long's steadying presence proves vital during one of the expedition's first great trials. As the party attempts to cross into the Arctic Circle in late May, a ferocious tempest descends upon them. Hobson, though uneasy, "allowed none of his anxiety to appear, but had a long consultation with Sergeant Long" before deciding how to proceed [007-028].

When the storm reaches its full fury — with avalanches crashing through the pass and bears prowling nearby — Hobson "after consulting with Sergeant Long, gave the order to halt" [007-033]. The decision to shelter in snow-houses scooped from icebergs falls to Long as much as to Hobson. During the forty-eight hours that follow, Long shares a snow hut with [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]], the astronomer — a pairing that would test any man's patience [007-036]. The tempest, one of the most harrowing in the novel's early chapters, marks the first serious test of the expedition's endurance, and Long passes it with characteristic unflappability.

### Perilous Service: The Fuel Mission

One of Sergeant Long's most heroic moments occurs during the brutal winter at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], when the temperature plummets to seventy-two degrees below zero and the colonists face the choice between freezing to death or burning every stick of furniture [021-033]. With the wood-shed fifty paces across a bear-infested courtyard, a volunteer must brave the killing cold.

"Long, Mac-Nab, and Rae, all three volunteered for the perilous service; but the Sergeant reminded the other two that they were married, and insisted upon being the first to venture" [021-039]. When Hobson wished to go himself, Mrs. Barnett declared that the chief must not expose himself: "Let Sergeant Long go" [021-040].

Long swallowed half a glass of rum — "to insure a good load of wood" — and rushed out into the seventy-two-below night, dragging a cord behind him [021-042, 021-045]. The minutes stretched into agony. When Hobson's party hauled on the rope, they dragged back not a sledge of fuel, but the body of the Sergeant himself: "Poor Long had never reached the shed. He had fallen fainting to the ground, and after twenty minutes' exposure to such a temperature there was little hope that he would revive" [021-052]. The mission nearly cost him his life. A bear, drawn by the commotion, nearly forced its way into the fort before Mrs. Barnett shot it dead with Hobson's pistol, and the colonists were forced to burn the very walls of their house to restore Long's vital heat [021-056, 021-062].

Long's insistence on volunteering ahead of married men embodies his moral code: the unmarried soldier's life is his to risk. The near-fatal outcome makes it one of the novel's most dramatic episodes.

### Interactions and Relationships

#### With Hobson

The bond between Hobson and Long is the novel's central professional relationship. On the overland journey, Long sits beside the Lieutenant "as if rooted to his seat," and their dialogues are exercises in loyal shorthand [005-012]. When Hobson asks for Long's opinion, the Sergeant invariably agrees — "the Sergeant, who never differed from his chief" [005-013]. Yet his agreement carries weight precisely because it is not sycophancy but conviction: Long genuinely sees no gap between his own judgment and his commander's.

Throughout the disaster of the floating island, Hobson repeatedly turns to Long for counsel. Hobson, the Sergeant, and a few trusted soldiers regularly "put their heads together" — at one crucial juncture deciding it would be "unwise to abandon the island before they were obliged" [043-030]. During the November snowstorm crisis when the house's air became toxic, "the Lieutenant and Sergeant Long put their heads together, and it was decided" to cut through ten feet of ice to open a window [018-011].

When Hobson reveals to the colonists that Cape Bathurst has become a wandering ice island, he calls upon "Sergeant Long" to summon the men — and "Sergeant Long touched his cap, and turning on his heel left the room without a word" [034-046, 034-047]. The gesture, perfectly military, perfectly him.

#### With Mrs. Paulina Barnett

Long's relationship with [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] is marked by mutual respect. She travels beside him in sledges, accompanies him on hunting excursions, and during the fuel crisis it is she who declares "Let Sergeant Long go" — a statement of confidence from the one person whose opinion Hobson cannot overrule [021-040, 014-013].

In later chapters, Long increasingly confides in Mrs. Barnett, as when he observes optimistically during a brief favorable turn: "I think our bad fortune is at last at an end" [040-016]. His willingness to share his thoughts with her signals a trust that transcends military hierarchy and suggests she has earned a place in the expedition's inner circle.

#### With the Soldiers

Long is "rather hard upon his men, as he was upon himself" and "would not tolerate the slightest infraction of discipline" [001-025]. Yet the men respect him — when he calls them to assemble, they come without hesitation. His self-discipline earns their deference: a man who has "never been reprimanded" commands moral authority [001-025].

His interactions with specific soldiers reveal different registers. He joins Marbre and Sabine in hunting excursions, scouring the country with his "invaluable" experience [014-013]. He is a "first-rate angler" whose "skill and patience" in fishing provide food for the colony [014-019]. And at Fort Confidence, the soldiers and employees look to him as the natural authority figure in Hobson's absence.

### Long's Quiet Humour

For all his seriousness, Long possesses a dry wit that Verne deploys for comic effect. When the party discovers mysterious footprints near the Arctic Circle — clearly made by someone dancing — and speculates about Eskimos or Indians, it is Long who deadpans: "No, it was a Frenchman," and "all agreed that none but a Frenchman could have been capable of dancing on such a spot" [011-048, 011-049].

Later, when a tame Polar bear wanders into the fort enclosure and the soldiers seize their guns, "Sergeant Long began to fear he had let the joke go too far" — though the joke was Mrs. Barnett's, not his [037-043]. And when [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]] descends into feverish anxiety over whether clouds will obscure his eclipse, "Sergeant Long and the others could not understand how it was possible to be so unhappy for 'love of the moon'" [023-050]. His incomprehension of intellectual obsession is itself a punchline.

These moments of humour never undermine his dignity. They reveal, instead, a man whose simplicity is a form of wisdom: he knows what matters, and what does not.

Sergeant Long embodies the professional soldier's virtues in their purest form. He does not question, does not hesitate, and does not complain. His long service in the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] has forged a man whose entire identity is bound up in duty. Unlike the more complex and introspective Hobson, Long is refreshingly direct — a man who says exactly what he means and means exactly what he says.

Long was stationed at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] as early as 1845, when the temperature there fell to 70 degrees below zero — an experience he recalls with understated dread when discussing the severity of Arctic cold with Hobson and Mrs. Barnett during the Fort Hope winter [018-042, 018-045].

## Narrative Function

### Comic Relief

Sergeant Long functions as one of the novel's principal sources of comic relief, though his humour is almost entirely unintentional on his part. Verne mines comedy from the gap between Long's rigid literal-mindedness and the absurd situations into which Arctic life throws him. His deadpan one-liners — the Frenchman diagnosis, the bewilderment at Thomas Black's "love of the moon" — land precisely because Long himself is not trying to be funny. The humour belongs to the reader, not to the character.

He is at his funniest when taking an absurdly long time to open the postern gate while Thomas Black freezes outside, bellowing. Long's calm "I am opening" as the astronomer's cries grow desperate is the sort of exchange Verne deploys to leaven tense moments [003-005].

### The Moral Anchor

Beneath the comedy lies a deeper function. In a novel where Hobson wrestles with impossible decisions and Mrs. Barnett brings the curiosity of the outside world, Long provides the unshakeable moral centre. He is the character who reminds married men that they have obligations beyond the Company; who insists on being first into danger; who tells Hobson, in effect, that no order is too impossible to obey.

His self-annihilating devotion is not presented as weakness but as strength. Verne's mythological analogy — Briareus the hundred-armed giant versus the Hydra of a hundred heads — frames Long's type as the foundation of all effective action: "They are the arms of the service, obeying a single head. Is not this the only really powerful organisation?" [001-025].

### The Everyman Witness

Long also serves as the reader's proxy within the military world of the Company. Hobson is exceptional; Long is exemplary. Where Hobson must calculate, Long must simply endure. His presence grounds the expedition in the everyday realities of soldiering — the double pay beyond the seventieth parallel, the discipline of the ranks, the loyalty that needs no explaining [011-007]. When Long fears, the reader fears; when he is optimistic enough to say "our misfortunes are really over," the reader knows real peril is about to arrive.

In the novel's closing chapters, Long recedes somewhat from the foreground — his narrative work largely done. He has been the steady bass note beneath the expedition's rising and falling fortunes, and as the survivors reach safety, his quiet competence has been vindicated.
