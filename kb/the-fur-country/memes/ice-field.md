## Definition

An [[iceberg|ice field]], in the context of *The Fur Country*, refers to the sprawling sheets of sea ice and compressed pack ice that cover the [[arctic-ocean|Arctic Ocean]] and its marginal seas for most of the year. These frozen expanses are not static — they shift, crack, ridge, and break apart under the pressures of wind, current, and seasonal thaw.

## Seasonal Dynamics

The [[iceberg|ice field]] is subject to a relentless annual cycle. During the long polar winter it solidifies into an unbroken plain capable of supporting sledges and heavy loads. With the return of the midnight sun in spring, the ice begins to weaken, fissure, and ultimately break into floes — a process that, in the novel, carries catastrophic consequences for the inhabitants of [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]].

## Role in The Fur Country

The [[iceberg|ice field]] is both a highway and a trap. Throughout Part II, the characters depend on the frozen surface for dog-[[dog-sledging|sledging]], hunting expeditions, and communication between the fort and the mainland. Yet it is this same ice that, in Chapter XVI, undergoes a violent breakup that transforms [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s settlement from a continental outpost into a drifting island.

> [Ch-XVI] The breakup of the ice field is the pivotal geological event of the novel — the moment when the known geography of the Esquimaux coast is rewritten and the characters' survival becomes uncertain.

Once the ice has broken, the region is no longer traversable by sledge, isolating the party and forcing them to rely on the fort's dwindling stores until the breakup resolves.
