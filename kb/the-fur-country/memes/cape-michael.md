# Cape Michael

**Cape Michael** is the cape at the opposite end of the landmass from [[cape-bathurst|Cape Bathurst]], named by [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]]'s party in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. Its discovery is one of the first clues that the colonists' "mainland" is not what it appears to be — though the full significance of the geography will not become clear until much later.  The cape's geographical position and distinctive features make it a notable landmark in the novel's carefully mapped Arctic terrain. Its presence in the narrative contributes to the rich sense of place that Verne cultivates throughout the story, grounding the fictional expedition in a convincingly realized geographical framework.

## In the Novel

### Overview

Cape Michael is first encountered during the reconnaissance of the new territory. From [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], built at [[cape-bathurst|Cape Bathurst]], the land stretches away for approximately seven miles to this second cape, forming a roughly straight coastline. Hobson names it, like many features of the territory, for one of his companions: [[mac-nab|Michael Mac-Nab]], the expedition's master carpenter.

> [027-007] "It stretched away without a break for seven miles to the cape they had named Cape Michael."

The distance — seven miles — is recorded with Hobson's characteristic precision. But the cape's most significant appearance comes later, when the party needs to traverse from [[cape-bathurst|Cape Bathurst]] to Cape Michael in the midst of a terrible storm. It is during this journey that Hobson makes the discovery that changes everything.

> [030-015] "His idea was to cut across in a straight line from Cape Bathurst to Cape Michael, and he was provided with a pocket compass with which to ascertain his bearings."

## Geography

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Distance from [[cape-bathurst|Cape Bathurst]]** | Approximately seven miles |
| **Position** | Eastern end of the ice island |
| **Named for** | [[mac-nab|Michael Mac-Nab]], the expedition carpenter |
| **Significance** | One of the two capes defining the island's extent |

The coastline between the two capes runs "in an almost straight line from south to north for about eleven miles," a regularity that is itself suspicious — natural coastlines rarely exhibit such geometry. But it is only when Hobson tries to navigate from one cape to the other and finds the sea where he expected land that the truth begins to dawn.

> [027-051] "From Cape Michael to the former Port Barnett the coast ran in an almost straight line from south to north for about eleven miles."
