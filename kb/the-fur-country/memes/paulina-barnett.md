# Paulina Barnett

**Paulina Barnett** is a celebrated English traveller and the most distinguished civilian guest of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] expedition in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. A fellow of the [[royal-geographical-society|Royal Geographical Society]] and a seasoned explorer who has already visited Australia, India, and Brazil, she joins [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]]'s mission to the Arctic coast out of pure scientific curiosity — a motive that sets her apart from the commercial and military interests that otherwise drive the enterprise.

## In the Novel

### Arrival at Fort Reliance

Paulina Barnett arrives at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] in dramatic fashion, her sledge emerging from a snowstorm, and immediately becomes the intellectual centre of [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s social circle. Unlike the fur traders and soldiers around her, she asks questions that probe the larger purposes of Arctic exploration — above all, the search for the Northwest Passage.

> [002-014] "'That would indeed be an important result of the enterprise, if this Northwest passage can really be used,' replied Mrs. Paulina Barnett; 'but I think you spoke of a double motive.'"

Throughout the novel, Barnett serves as both participant and observer. She endures the same hardships as the colonists — the brutal overland journey, the Arctic winter, the breakup of [[the-ice-island|the ice island]] — but she also records and reflects upon the experience, bridging the world of action and the world of ideas.

> [018-010] "Mrs. Barnett shared everything with her companions, never holding herself aloof or remaining shut up in her cabin, but working zealously amongst the others, drawing out the most reticent by her intelligent questions and warm sympathy."

### The Overland Journey

During the expedition's march northward, Barnett proves herself an able and resilient traveller. She crosses the [[arctic-circle|Arctic Circle]] alongside the men, observing the natural world with a scientist's eye. When [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] points out the ingenious engineering of beaver dams along the route, she listens with keen attention.

> [016-008] "'The object of this dam,' said the Lieutenant to Mrs. Barnett, 'is to secure to the beavers a sufficient depth of water at all seasons of the year, and to enable the engineers of the tribe to build the round huts called houses or lodges, the tops of which you can just see.'"

She also participates directly in the expedition's practical life. During a hunting excursion, she joins a party that includes [[marbre|Marbre]], [[sabine|Sabine]], [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]], and [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]], helping to cut off the retreat of game animals among the rocks (PN 016-026).

### Winter at Fort Hope

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Barnett becomes the cultural heart of the small colony. During the long Arctic winter, she reads aloud to the assembled residents, her voice competing with the howling wind outside.

> [030-002] "The women were stitching away industriously, and Mrs. Paulina Barnett was reading aloud; but she was often interrupted not only by the noise of the wind, which shook the walls of the house like a battering-ram, but by the cries of the baby."

When [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]] and [[sergeant-long|Long]] undertake a dangerous night mission, Barnett insists on seeing them off personally, pressing their hands in a gesture of solidarity and concern.

> [030-004] "When everyone was asleep, Hobson and Long crept cautiously across the large room and gained the passage, where they found Mrs. Barnett, who wished to press their hands once more."

### Exploration and Discovery

Barnett does not confine herself to the fort. In early September, she resolves to explore the coast towards Cape Eskimo, setting out with only [[madge|Madge]] for company.

> [031-027] "On September 3rd Mrs. Barnett resolved to go a few miles along the coast towards Cape Eskimo."

During this excursion she witnesses the rich animal life surrounding the fort, observing furred creatures in their natural habitat (PN 031-031). Her willingness to venture out independently demonstrates the same spirit of curiosity that brought her to the Arctic in the first place.

### On the Ice Island

When [[the-ice-island|the ice island]] breaks free and begins drifting, Barnett faces the crisis with characteristic composure. She joins the reconnaissance parties, discussing their predicament with [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]] and weighing the possibilities of rescue.

> [029-009] "'We shall see well enough to recognise America, if we get anywhere near it,' said Mrs. Barnett. 'Whatever land we approach will be welcome.'"

When the ice pressure intensifies and threatens to crush their refuge, she fully grasps the magnitude of the danger.

> [038-014] "Mrs. Barnett, seeing the awful power of the pressure in the offing, and the violence with which the moving masses of ice crushed upon each other, realised the full magnitude of the peril they would all be in when the thaw commenced."

### The Avalanche and Rescue

Barnett's most perilous moment comes when she is buried alive by an avalanche. For fifty-four hours, she and her companions remain trapped beneath the snow and debris.

> [041-034] "They felt sure that with her usual courage, Mrs. Barnett would have tried to find some way out if her movements were free."

The entire colony mobilises to dig her out — men wielding pickaxes, women keeping fires burning — all united by a single purpose. When [[kalumah|Kalumah]] finally leads [[jaspar-hobson|Hobson]] to her, Barnett is found motionless and insensible, with her head scarcely above the water (PN 041-070). Her survival stands as a testament to both her own fortitude and the loyalty she has inspired in others.

## Character and Traits

Barnett is one of Verne's most interesting female characters — not a love interest or a damsel, but an intellectual equal to the men around her, whose authority derives from demonstrated competence and genuine scientific curiosity. Several distinctive traits define her:

- **Intellectual curiosity**: She joins the expedition not for pay or duty, but for the pure joy of discovery — a motivation unique among the party.
- **Physical resilience**: Despite being a civilian guest, she endures the same brutal conditions as the soldiers and traders, never asking for special treatment.
- **Social generosity**: She actively draws out the reticent members of the colony, sharing her knowledge and warmth with everyone from officers to servants.
- **Moral courage**: When others falter, Barnett remains steady — pressing hands in farewell, venturing out alone with Madge, and maintaining hope even when buried under an avalanche.

> [004-054] "She did not wish to be treated better than her travelling companions, but yielded to the urgent request of Captain Craventy, who was but carrying out the wishes of the Company."

That she is a woman in a male-dominated expedition is occasionally remarked upon but never treated as an obstacle; the narrative simply accepts her presence as a fact, and she earns respect through her conduct.

## Relationships

- **[[madge|Madge]]** — Her devoted servant and lifelong companion, who accompanies her on every journey and shares every danger without complaint.
- **[[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]]** — The expedition commander, who values Barnett's counsel and treats her as an intellectual peer throughout the mission.
- **[[kalumah|Kalumah]]** — The young Eskimo woman who becomes Barnett's friend and protégée, and who ultimately leads the rescue party to her after the avalanche.
- **[[thomas-black|Thomas Black]]** — The astronomer who travels alongside her; they share scientific interests and endure the avalanche together.
- **[[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]** — The Hudson's Bay Company captain who hosts her at Fort Reliance and briefs her on the expedition's objectives.
- **[[mrs-joliffe|Mrs. Joliffe]]** — A fellow woman at Fort Hope, with whom Barnett shares domestic duties and winter pastimes.
