# Arctic Ocean

The **Arctic Ocean** — called the Polar Sea throughout Jules Verne's *The Fur Country* — is the novel's defining geographical presence. It is both destination and antagonist: the body of water whose shores the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] seeks to colonise, and whose ice will ultimately swallow the colonists' settlement and carry it into the unknown. No other feature of the landscape exercises so complete a dominion over the narrative.  The Arctic Ocean serves as both setting and antagonist throughout Jules Verne's narrative, its frozen surface concealing the truth about Victoria Peninsula while its currents carry the drifting ice island toward an uncertain fate in the Bering Sea. The ocean's vast, trackless expanse underscores the colonists' isolation and the impossibility of outside rescue.

## In the Novel

### Overview

The Arctic Ocean enters the story as a commercial proposition. At [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]'s soirée, [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s plan to establish a fort on its shores is presented as a strategic response to the decline of the southern fur districts:

> [002-011] "'But what induces the Company to construct a fort on the shores of the Arctic Ocean?'"

[[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]]'s question — "what induces [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]?" — gets at the central tension of the novel. The Arctic Ocean is not, for [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s officers, a place of wonder or terror; it is a market. The beavers and foxes that have retreated northward must be pursued, and a factory on the Polar Sea is the logical next move. But for Barnett, and for the reader, the ocean represents something else entirely: the limit of the known world.

> [005-030] "'But wait until we reach the Arctic Ocean; wait until the winter shuts us in with its gigantic icebergs and thick covering of snow; wait till the northern storms break over us, and the glories of the Aurora Borealis flash across the heavens.'"
