# Michael Mac-Nab

**Michael Mac-Nab** is one of the ten soldiers selected by [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]] for [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]]'s Arctic expedition in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. A Scottish soldier from Stirling and a skilled carpenter in the service of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]], he travels to [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] with his wife [[mrs-mac-nab|Mrs. Mac-Nab]] and serves as the expedition's master builder, personally directing the construction of the factory, houses, fortifications, palisades, sentry-boxes, and all furniture at [[cape-bathurst|Cape Bathurst]].

## In the Novel

Mac-Nab is introduced at Captain Craventy's soirée at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]] in Chapter I, listed among the expedition's personnel alongside the other soldiers chosen for their energy and resolution. He is one of three married soldiers — Mac-Nab, [[corporal-joliffe|Corporal Joliffe]], and John [[rae|Rae]] — whose wives accompany the expedition, making [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]] not merely a military outpost but a small community of families enduring the Arctic together.

> [001-011] "Two only of the assembled company did not belong to the ordinary society of the neighbourhood, two women, whom we shall introduce when we have enumerated Captain Craventy's other guests: these were, Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson, Sergeant Long, Corporal Joliffe, and his bright active Canadian wife, a certain Mac-Nab and his wife, both Scotch, John Rae, married to an Indian woman of the country, and some sixty soldiers or employees of the Hudson's Bay Company."

Early in the expedition's journey, Mac-Nab demonstrated his value not only as a carpenter but as a potential hunter, tracking fur-bearing animals alongside [[sabine|Sabine]] during the reconnaissance of the districts surrounding the Great Bear Lake region.

> [013-004] "Sabine and Mac-Nab might many a time have shot a very valuable animal on this excursion, but the Lieutenant had wisely forbidden all hunting of the kind. He did not wish to alarm the animals before the approaching season — that is to say, before the winter months, when their furs become thicker and more beautiful."

## Master Carpenter of the Expedition

### Construction Supervisor

Mac-Nab's most valuable contribution to the expedition was his skill as a carpenter. A Scotchman from Stirling, he had considerable experience in the building both of houses and boats, and was placed in charge of all construction works at [[cape-bathurst|Cape Bathurst]]. He supervised a team of seven soldiers — Petersen, Belcher, Rae, Garry, Pond, Hope, and Kellet — all clever and zealous workmen.

> [013-004] "Petersen, Belcher, Rae, Garry, Pond, Hope, and Kellet formed a body of clever, zealous carpenters, under the able superintendence of Mac-Nab, a Scotchman from Stirling, who had had considerable experience in the building both of houses and boats."

Together with [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]], Mac-Nab personally selected the site for the principal house on the plateau at the foot of Cape Bathurst and laid out the outer walls. The design was a rectangle sixty feet by thirty, with a door and three windows on the promontory side and four windows overlooking the lagoon. The plan, agreed upon by Hobson and his master-carpenter, divided the house into four compartments: an antechamber with a double door, a kitchen, a large dining-hall, and a fourth compartment divided into cabins.

> [013-005] "In the afternoon of the same day, July 6th Hobson and Mac-Nab the carpenter went to choose the site of the principal house on the plateau at the foot of Cape Bathurst."

> [013-006] "The spot chosen, Hobson and Mac-Nab set out the outer walls of the house with the line."

Mac-Nab proceeded rapidly with the construction, employing shipbuilding techniques to make the structure weathertight. When the wooden walls could not be joined perfectly, he resorted to calking — a process used by seamen to render vessels watertight — using dry moss as a substitute for tow, driving it into the crevices with calking-irons and a hammer, and filling the hollows with layers of hot tar obtained from the pine-trees.

> [013-015] "To insure their being properly joined, Rae the blacksmith drove strong iron bolts through them at intervals; and when even this contrivance proved insufficient to close the interstices as hermetically as was necessary, Mac-Nab had recourse to calking, a process which seamen find invaluable in rendering vessels watertight; only as a substitute for tow he used a sort of dry moss, with which the eastern side of the cape was covered, driving it into the crevices with calking-irons and a hammer, filling up each hollow with layers of hot tar, obtained without difficulty from the pine-trees, and thus making the walls and boarding impervious to the rain and damp of the winter season."

He also constructed a substantial table with fixed benches for the mess hall, and erected wooden sentry-boxes at each extremity of the lagoon to command the coastline. When lime for mortar was needed, Mac-Nab and Hobson devised a plan to collect calcareous shells from the shore, grind and burn them into lime, and mould the lime into bricks.

> [014-002] "A camp-bed was set up in the hall, and the carpenter Mac-Nab constructed a most substantial table, around which were ranged fixed benches."

> [014-003] "Yes, Mac-Nab," replied Hobson; "we must collect the shells, grind them, burn them, and make them into lime, then mould the lime into bricks, and use them in the same way." "Let us try the shells, by all means," replied the carpenter.

> [014-011] "The factory was therefore entirely encircled, and at each extremity of the lagoon Mac-Nab undertook to erect a wooden sentry-box commanding the coastline, from which a watch could be kept without any danger."

## Family and Domestic Life

At [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Mac-Nab's household became central to the colony's domestic life. His wife, an Indian woman well qualified to care for animals, was entrusted with the reindeer kept in a paddock near the house. The couple shared one of the dormitories in the principal house alongside the Joliffes and the Raes.

On the 12th of November, during the first Arctic winter, Mrs. Mac-Nab gave birth to a son — a significant event for the isolated colony. [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Paulina Barnett]] stood as godmother, and the child was baptised Michael Hope, his middle name honouring the fort that his father had helped to build.

> [017-027] "On the 12th November a new member was born to the little colony. Mrs. Mac-Nab was safely confined of a fine healthy boy, of whom the head carpenter was extremely proud. Mrs. Barnett stood godmother to the child, which received the name of Michael Hope. The ceremony of baptism was performed with considerable solemnity, and a kind of fête was held in honour of the little creature which had just come into the world beyond the 70th degree N. Lat."

## Enduring the Arctic Winter

As September approached and the short Arctic building season neared its end, Mac-Nab and his workmen worked with urgent industry to complete the new buildings before winter set in. The doghouse was finished, the palisading was nearly complete, and the sentry-boxes were erected.

> [015-002] "September had now commenced, and as upon the most favourable calculation only three more weeks would intervene before the bad season set in and interrupted the labours of the explorers, the greatest haste was necessary in completing the new buildings, and Mac-Nab and his workmen surpassed themselves in industry."

During a violent winter storm, Mac-Nab's primary concern was for the chimneys he had built, which were liable to be blown over by the force of the wind. The house, however, held firm — a testament to the solidity of his construction.

> [018-005] "Fortunately the accumulation of snow round the walls broke the force of the squall, and Mac-Nab's only fear was for the chimneys, which were liable to be blown over."

His wife was part of the small community of women who shared the hardships and anxieties of life on the Arctic coast — and later, on [[the-ice-island|the drifting ice island]].

> [008-019] "Mrs. Barnett, Hobson, two or three soldiers, Madge, Mrs. Mac-Nab, and Joliffe explored the shores of the lake under the guidance of Felton."
