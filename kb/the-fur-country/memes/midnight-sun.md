# Midnight Sun

The **midnight sun** is the Arctic phenomenon of continuous daylight during the summer months, when the sun remains visible above the horizon for the full twenty-four hours. In Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*, the midnight sun is the subject of an entire chapter (Chapter XII) and represents one of the novel's most striking intersections of natural science and human experience.

## The Phenomenon

At latitudes above the [[arctic-circle|Arctic Circle]] (approximately 66°33′ N), the tilt of the Earth's axis means that for a period around the summer solstice, the sun never sets. The length of this period increases with latitude: at the [[arctic-circle|Arctic Circle]] it lasts for one day; at the North Pole it extends to six months.

For the colonists of [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], located well north of the [[arctic-circle|Arctic Circle]], the midnight sun was their first profound encounter with the strangeness of the polar world — a world in which the familiar rhythms of day and night no longer applied.

## In the Novel

The chapter titled "The Midnight Sun" captures the colonists' first experience of this phenomenon. For [[thomas-black|the astronomer]] [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]], it is a moment of professional fascination; for the soldiers and their wives, it is disorienting and slightly uncanny.

> [012-057] "For the first time they saw the shining of the midnight sun."

Verne's treatment of the midnight sun is characteristic of his method: he uses the phenomenon both as a piece of scientific education (explaining its astronomical cause) and as a narrative device (the disruption of normal temporal rhythms mirrors the colonists' growing sense of dislocation from the world they left behind).

## Contrast with Polar Night

The midnight sun is the summer counterpart to the [[polar-night|polar night]] — the winter period of continuous darkness. Together, these two phenomena define the polar year:
