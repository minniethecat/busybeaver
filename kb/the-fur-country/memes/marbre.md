# Marbre

**Marbre** is a skilled hunter, trapper, and soldier in the service of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]], and one of the two expert hunters (alongside [[sabine|Sabine]]) chosen for [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]]'s Arctic expedition in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. His unmatched tracking abilities, deep knowledge of animal behaviour, and expertise with nets and snares prove essential to the colonists' survival at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], where he and [[sabine|Sabine]] serve as the primary providers of fresh meat and valuable furs.

## In the Novel

Marbre is introduced in Chapter IV among the expedition's personnel list, designated alongside [[sabine|Sabine]] as one of the two soldiers specially selected for their hunting skills. The distinction is significant: while the other eight soldiers are builders and labourers under [[mac-nab|Mac-Nab]]'s supervision, Marbre and [[sabine|Sabine]] are the expedition's primary providers. [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]] had shown his wisdom in choosing two such intelligent men for the little troop.

> [013-004] "Of the ten soldiers chosen by Captain Craventy, two—Marbre and Sabine—were skilful hunters; the other eight handled the hatchet with as much address as the musket."

## Skill as a Hunter

### Hunting Expeditions

Marbre's expertise is demonstrated throughout the journey north. His ability to read tracks, identify species, and track game provides the expedition with a crucial food supply and protects them from surprise encounters with predators. His skill was so remarkable that even the cleverest Indians would not have surpassed him in keenness of sight, precision of aim, or manual address.

> [006-023] "The chief hunters of the expedition were the soldiers Marbre and Sabine, both very expert at their business. Their skill was wonderful; and the cleverest Indians would not have surpassed them in keenness of sight, precision of aim, or manual address. They were alike trappers and hunters, and were acquainted with all the nets and snares for taking sables, otters, wolves, foxes, bears, etc. No artifice was unknown to them, and Captain Craventy had shown his wisdom in choosing two such intelligent men to accompany the little troop."

> [006-026] "There could be no mistake about it; Marbre and Sabine were positive on that point, and could even have named the species to which the animals belonged."

Marbre's confidence in his fieldcraft is matched by his practical wisdom. When the party encountered the fighting wapiti, he advised sparing ammunition by letting the animals exhaust themselves rather than shooting immediately — a tactic reflecting years of experience in the wilderness.

> [006-041] "'Beg pardon, sir,' replied Marbre; 'but let us spare our powder and shot.'"

## The Wapiti Duel

Marbre's hunting prowess is most dramatically illustrated during the [[wapiti-duel|wapiti duel]] episode, where he and [[sabine|Sabine]] track and confront a pair of fighting wapiti (North American elk). Their confidence in their identification of the animals — against initial scepticism — demonstrates the depth of their wilderness knowledge.

> [006-031] "'No, no, sir,' cried Sabine; 'Marbre and I are not mistaken. These traces were left by deer, the deer we hunters call red deer, and the natives wapitis.'"

> [006-032] "'He is quite right,' added Marbre; 'old trappers like us are not to be taken in; besides, don't you hear that peculiar whistling sound?'"

The episode culminates with Marbre and Sabine dispatching the exhausted combatants with two well-placed shots.

> [006-057] "Two bullets put an end to the fight between the wapitis; and Marbre and Sabine taking immediate possession, carried off their skins to be subsequently prepared, leaving their bleeding carcasses to be devoured by wolves and bears."

## Provisioning the Colony

Once established at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Marbre and Sabine became the colony's daily providers. They scoured the country for miles around, sometimes accompanied by [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]], [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]], and even [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Paulina Barnett]], who shouldered her gun bravely and never allowed herself to be outstripped.

> [014-013] "Accordingly Marbre and Sabine, sometimes accompanied by the Lieutenant and Sergeant Long, whose experience was invaluable, scoured the country daily for miles round; and it was no uncommon sight to see Mrs. Paulina Barnett join them and step briskly along shouldering her gun bravely, and never allowing herself to be outstripped by her companions."

Their skill extended beyond big game to the abundant birdlife of the Arctic summer. During the migratory season, hundreds of wild geese, eider ducks, scoters, and other waterfowl fell victim to the unerring aim of the two hunters.

> [011-021] "White-headed eagles, huge birds with a harsh screeching cry; fishing hawks, which build their nests in dead trees and migrate to the Arctic regions in the summer; snow buntings with pure white plumage, wild geese, which afford the best food of all the Anseres tribe; ducks with red heads and black breasts; ash-coloured crows, a kind of mocking jay of extreme ugliness; eider ducks; scoters or black divers, etc. etc., whose mingled cries awake the echoes of the Arctic regions, fell victims by hundreds to the unerring aim of Marbre and Sabine."

Marbre and Sabine were skilled in all the artifices employed by sportsmen in stalking their prey, particularly the reindeer, which were exceedingly wary and required the utmost patience and fieldcraft to approach. They would patiently face the wind lest the creature's keen sense of smell should warn it of their approach, cunningly luring it on by displaying the magnificent antlers of some former victim above the birch-bushes.

> [014-014] "Marbre and Sabine were skilled in all the artifices which sportsmen employ in stalking their prey—particularly the reindeer, which are exceedingly wary. How patiently they would face the wind lest the creature's keen sense of smell should warn it of their approach!"

During the autumn, they ranged further afield in pursuit of valuable furs, hunting [[lynx|lynxes]] and wolverines with firearms, and tracking silver foxes along the coast at Walruses' Bay.

> [014-035] "In the pursuit of lynxes and wolverines or gluttons, firearms had to be used. The lynx has all the suppleness and agility of the feline tribe to which it belongs, and is formidable even to the reindeer; Marbre and Sabine were, however, well up to their work, and succeeded in killing more than sixty of them."

## Trapping and Winter Survival

Marbre's expertise as a trapper proved invaluable during the long Arctic winter. It was on his advice that a reindeer trap was constructed in the Eskimo style — a deep trench with a seesaw plank that would rebound when lowered, allowing the capture of multiple animals. The only difficulty Marbre had to contend with was the extreme hardness of the frozen ground, which revealed a surprising bed of snow beneath the surface.

> [017-035] "In accordance with the advice of Marbre the hunter, a reindeer trap was constructed in the Eskimo style. A trench was dug twelve feet deep, and of a uniform width of ten feet. A seesaw plank, which would rebound when lowered, was laid across it. A bait of herbs was placed at one end of the plank, and any animal venturing to take them, was inevitably flung to the bottom of the pit."

The trap soon proved its worth — though not with the quarry Marbre had expected. Approaching the trench with Sabine, he heard loud growls and immediately identified the creature: a bear had fallen into the pit instead of a reindeer.

> [017-038] "It's no reindeer making that noise," said Marbre, "I know well enough what creature has fallen into our pit." "A bear?" replied Sabine.

Marbre also looked forward to the winter trapping season for weasels and martens, whose supple bodies made them difficult to catch in summer but easy prey for traps in the cold months, when their valuable white winter coats would "ripen" — as Sabine expressed it — waiting for the cold to bleach them.

> [014-036] "Very few ermines or stoats were seen, and Jaspar Hobson ordered his men to spare any which happened to cross their path until the winter, when they should have assumed their beautiful snow-white coats with the one black spot at the tip of the tail. At present the upper fur was reddish-brown and the under yellowish white, so that, as Sabine expressed it, it was desirable to let them 'ripen,' or, in other words — to wait for the cold to bleach them."
