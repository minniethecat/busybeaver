# Aurora Borealis

The Aurora Borealis — the Northern Lights — is one of the most spectacular natural phenomena of the [[arctic-circle|Arctic region]]s and features prominently in the atmospheric backdrop of *The Fur Country*. For the colonists at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], the aurora provided one of the few sources of illumination and wonder during the long months of the polar night.

## Scientific Understanding

In the mid-nineteenth century, the aurora was a subject of active scientific investigation. Theories of the period linked the phenomenon to electrical discharges in the upper atmosphere, magnetic disturbances, and solar activity. The novel reflects this era of inquiry through the observations of the scientifically-minded members of the expedition, particularly [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]] and [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Paulina Barnett]].

## The Aurora in the Polar Night

For the colonists enduring their first Arctic winter, the aurora was both a practical aid and a psychological comfort. During the months when the sun never rose above the horizon, the aurora's shifting curtains of coloured light provided occasional illumination — enough to distinguish objects at a distance during what would otherwise be absolute darkness.

The phenomenon complemented the other extreme of Arctic illumination — the [[midnight-sun|Midnight Sun]], which dominated the summer months with its perpetual daylight. Between these two phenomena, the Arctic year was defined by extremes of light and darkness unknown in temperate latitudes.

## Cultural Significance

The aurora held particular significance for the native peoples of the region. The Chippeway and Hare Indians, and the Eskimo (Inuit) families encountered by the colonists, had their own traditions and beliefs about the lights. For European observers, the aurora represented one of the great unsolved mysteries of natural philosophy — a challenge to the explanatory power of nineteenth-century science.
