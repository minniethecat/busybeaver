# Royal Geographical Society

The **Royal Geographical Society** (RGS) is the London-based learned society whose endorsement and recognition establish [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]]'s standing as a celebrated traveller in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. Although the RGS appears only briefly in the novel's opening chapters, its prestige provides crucial narrative legitimacy to Barnett's presence on the Arctic expedition.

## Paulina Barnett's Affiliation

[[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] is introduced as a woman of considerable reputation in scientific circles, and her connection to the RGS is a key element of her characterization. She is not merely a tourist or adventurer, but a serious observer whose travels have been recognized by Britain's premier geographical institution.

> [001-027] "It is now time to say a few words of the two foreign women already alluded to more than once. They were both about forty years old, and one of them well deserved to take first rank amongst celebrated female travellers."

Her association with the RGS places her in the tradition of nineteenth-century scientific exploration — a world dominated by men, but in which a few exceptional women had begun to carve out a place. Verne uses the RGS as a marker of institutional credibility: Barnett is not an amateur but a professional explorer whose observations carry weight.

## Role in the Novel

The RGS serves as an unseen but influential presence. It has:

- Validated Barnett's previous expeditions and publications
- Provided her with the scientific credentials that make her presence on an [[hudsons-bay-company|HBC]] expedition plausible
- Functioned as a silent guarantor of the expedition's scientific value beyond its commercial purpose

## Historical Reality

The Royal Geographical Society was founded in London in 1830, making it a relatively young institution at the time of *The Fur Country*'s setting (circa 1859–1860). Historically, the RGS sponsored and endorsed numerous Arctic expeditions during the Victorian era, including the searches for the Northwest Passage and for [[franklin|Sir John Franklin]]'s lost expedition. Verne's reference to the Society is thus grounded in the real geography of Victorian exploration.
