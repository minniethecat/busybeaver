# Polar Night

The polar night is the period during the winter months when the sun remains continuously below the horizon in regions within the Arctic and Antarctic Circles. At the latitude of [[cape-bathurst|Cape Bathurst]], where [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] establishes [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], this total darkness lasted approximately two months. In Jules Verne's _The Fur Country_, the polar night is the subject of an entire chapter and represents one of the most visually and psychologically intense episodes in the novel — a trial by darkness that tests the endurance of the isolated colonists.

## The Onset of Darkness

The polar night descends with sudden drama, accompanied by a violent storm that compounds the transition from twilight to total darkness:

> A few days afterwards, on November 20th, the sun sunk below the horizon not to appear again for two months. The Polar night had commenced! (017-001)

The use of the exclamation mark in the original text signals the narrative weight of this moment. The long night is not a gradual fading but a decisive event, and its commencement is immediately followed by a storm so severe that the house, though well built, trembles to its foundations:

> The long night was ushered in by a violent storm. The cold was perhaps a little less severe, but the air was very damp, and, in spite of every precaution, the humidity penetrated into the house, and the condensers, which were emptied every morning, contained several pounds of ice (018-002).

## Imprisonment and Isolation

With the storm raging and the snow accumulating, the colonists are effectively buried alive. The doors become impassable, and the only contact with the outside world is through a window that must be laboriously cleared of ice:

> Outside drifts whirled past like waterspouts — the snow seemed no longer to fall horizontally but vertically. The Lieutenant was obliged to insist upon the door being kept shut, for had it been opened the passages would immediately have become blocked up. The explorers were literally prisoners (018-003).

The sense of confinement is heightened by the constant noise of the storm, which replaces the usual Arctic silence, and by the terrifying sound of icebergs collapsing in the distance:

> In the midst of the whistling of the wind, loud reports were heard, of which Mrs. Barnett could not conjecture the cause. It was the falling of icebergs in the offing. The echoes caught up the sounds, which were rolled along like the reverberations of thunder (018-006).

When the storm finally abates and the door can be opened, the party discovers the fort has been entirely buried beneath a frozen crust of snow ten feet thick. The palisade is useless, the traps are inaccessible, and the colonists must dig a moat simply to restore the boundary of their enclosure (018-026).

## Daily Life in Darkness

Set against this physical ordeal is the remarkable domestic resilience of the fort's inhabitants. Verne depicts a community that, though confined in a narrow space for days on end, maintains order and cheerfulness through routine, labour, and mutual support:

> The time was not wasted during this long imprisonment. All these good people agreed together perfectly, and neither ill-humour nor ennui marred the contentment of the little party shut up in such a narrow space (018-007).

Daily life during the polar night is structured around assigned tasks: garment-making and mending, weapons maintenance, boot manufacture, meteorological journal-keeping, and the constant vigilance required to prevent the accumulation of moisture and ice inside the house (018-009). In the evenings, the community gathers in the large room where [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] reads aloud from the Bible and travel narratives, her voice providing both entertainment and moral encouragement to the soldiers:

> Mrs. Barnett generally read aloud, and her audience listened with delight. The Bible and accounts of adventures received a fresh charm when read out in her clear earnest voice (018-010).

## Astronomical Splendours

The polar night is not entirely without visual compensation. When the sky clears after the storm, it offers [[thomas-black|Thomas Black]] — and the reader — some of the most spectacular celestial phenomena described in the novel. The moon appears haloed by a luminous corona, and the northern sky erupts in an [[aurora-borealis|aurora]] of extraordinary intensity:

> The lunar halo was a white corona with a pale red edge encircling the moon. This luminous meteor was about forty-five degrees in diameter, and was the result of the diffraction of the lunar rays through the small prismatic ice crystals floating in the atmosphere (018-053).

Fifteen hours later, the [[aurora-borealis|aurora]] borealis transforms the Arctic sky into a vast dome of fire:

> Fifteen hours later the heavens were lit up by a magnificent Aurora Borealis, the arch of which extended over more than a hundred geographical degrees. The vertex of this arch was situated in the magnetic meridian, and, as is often the case, the rays darted by the luminous meteor were of all the colours of the rainbow, red predominating (018-054).

These celestial displays, while scientifically explicable, carry an almost supernatural quality in the narrative. They function as moments of transcendence amid the oppressive darkness — reminders that even in the most extreme conditions, the natural world produces beauty as well as danger.

## The Psychological Dimension

Beyond the physical challenges, the polar night exerts a subtle psychological pressure on the colonists. The long confinement, the perpetual darkness, and the uncertainty about whether the coming spring will bring rescue or further catastrophe combine to create an atmosphere of latent anxiety beneath the surface of daily routine. The winter solstice marks the nadir of this experience:

> The winter solstice was now approaching, when the darkness of the Polar night would be most profound, as the sun would be at the lowest maximum point below the horizon of the northern hemisphere. At midnight the southern edges of the long white plains were touched with a faint glimmer of twilight, that was all, and it would be impossible to imagine anything more melancholy than the gloomy stillness and darkness of the vast expanse (018-055).

Verne does not belabour the psychological dimension, but it permeates the chapter: the relief when the storm ends, the eagerness with which everyone rushes outside after the week-long confinement, and the almost desperate gratitude for any sign of returning light all testify to the strain imposed by the polar night.
