# Sabine

**Sabine** is a skilled hunter and scout of the [[hudsons-bay-company|Hudson's Bay Company]] in Jules Verne's *The Fur Country*. Paired with his fellow hunter [[marbre|Marbre]], Sabine provides the expedition with fresh game, conducts reconnaissance, and represents the indispensable practical expertise upon which Arctic exploration depends. His steady marksmanship and knowledge of the northern wilderness are assets that [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] repeatedly relies upon throughout the novel.

## In the Novel

Sabine is introduced as one of the ten soldiers selected by [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]] for the expedition — two of whom, himself and [[marbre|Marbre]], were designated as the expedition's skilled hunters:

> [013-004] "Of the ten soldiers chosen by Captain Craventy, two — Marbre and Sabine — were skilful hunters; the other eight handled the hatchet with as much address as the musket."

He is consistently paired with [[marbre|Marbre]] — the two forming a hunting partnership that provides much of the fort's fresh meat and furs. Sabine is the more taciturn and businesslike of the pair, contrasting with [[marbre|Marbre]]'s more voluble and theatrical personality.

> [006-023] "The chief hunters of the expedition were the soldiers Marbre and Sabine, both very expert at their business. Their skill was wonderful; and the cleverest Indians would not have surpassed them in keenness of sight, precision of aim, or manual address."

Despite their differences in temperament, the two hunters operate with a practised efficiency that makes them one of the novel's most effective partnerships. They are alike trappers and hunters, acquainted with all the nets and snares for taking sables, otters, wolves, foxes, and bears — no artifice is unknown to them.

### The Wapiti Hunt

One of Sabine's earliest demonstrations of skill comes during the overland march, when he and [[marbre|Marbre]] identify the tracks of large deer — wapitis — and successfully track the animals to their location. Sabine's confidence is absolute; when [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] asks whether they should approach cautiously, his reply reflects the hunter's seasoned knowledge of animal behaviour:

> [006-051] "'There's plenty of time, ma'am,' said Sabine; 'they won't escape us now.'"

The hunters discover two males locked in a desperate duel, so engrossed in their struggle that they are oblivious to the approaching men:

> [006-035] "'Wapitis! wapitis! — there they are!' cried Sabine and Marbre at once, pointing to a group of animals distinctly visible about a quarter of a mile to the east."

Two well-placed bullets end the fight, and the hunters take possession of the valuable skins, leaving the bleeding carcasses to be devoured by wolves and bears. The episode establishes Sabine's defining qualities: calm assurance, unerring aim, and a hunter's pragmatic economy of effort.

### The Giant Ospreys

Sabine's marksmanship is further demonstrated when he brings down two gigantic ospreys — magnificent birds of prey with wingspans measuring nearly six feet from tip to tip:

> [008-021] "The hunter Sabine managed to bring down a couple of these gigantic ospreys, which measured nearly six feet from tip to tip of their wings, and were therefore magnificent specimens of these migratory birds, who feed entirely on fish, and take refuge on the shores of the Gulf of Mexico when winter sets in, only visiting the higher latitudes of North America during the short summer."

These ospreys, seasonal migrants to the Arctic, represent a different kind of quarry than the large mammals Sabine more commonly pursues — a demonstration of the versatility that makes him valuable to the expedition.

### Daily Life at Fort Hope

During the months at [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Sabine and [[marbre|Marbre]] scour the country daily for miles around, sometimes accompanied by [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] and [[sergeant-long|Sergeant Long]]. [[paulina-barnett|Mrs. Barnett]] often joins them:

> [014-013] "Marbre and Sabine, sometimes accompanied by the Lieutenant and Sergeant Long, whose experience was invaluable, scoured the country daily for miles round; and it was no uncommon sight to see Mrs. Paulina Barnett join them and step briskly along shouldering her gun bravely, and never allowing herself to be outstripped by her companions."

Throughout August the expeditions meet with great success. Sabine and Marbre's combined skill produces an astonishing tally: the store of provisions increases rapidly as game falls to their unerring aim. Their expertise is not limited to large game — they are equally adept at bringing down waterfowl:

> [011-021] "White-headed eagles, huge birds with a harsh screeching cry; fishing hawks, which build their nests in dead trees and migrate to the Arctic regions in the summer; snow buntings with pure white plumage, wild geese, which afford the best food of all the Anseres tribe; ducks with red heads and black breasts; ash-coloured crows, a kind of mocking jay of extreme ugliness; eider ducks; scoters or black divers, etc. etc., whose mingled cries awake the echoes of the Arctic regions, fell victims by hundreds to the unerring aim of Marbre and Sabine."

The catalogue of species — white-headed eagles, fishing hawks, snow buntings, wild geese, eider ducks, scoters — is itself a testament to the hunters' comprehensive knowledge of Arctic fauna. They know not only how to kill but what to kill: which birds provide the best food, which furs are worth the taking, and when each species can be hunted most profitably.

### The Wolverene Encounter

During an expedition from [[fort-hope|Fort Hope]], Sabine encounters a wolverene — a creature he describes with characteristic brevity. When Mrs. Barnett asks what the horrid creature is, Sabine replies in his "usual sententious manner":

> [014-026] "'A wolverene, ma'am,' returned Sabine, much delighted with the elegant way in which he had rounded his sentence."

This moment reveals Sabine's quiet pride in his craft — he is a man who speaks little but takes genuine satisfaction in knowing his business thoroughly. The adverb "sententious" — meaning given to pithy, moralising pronouncements — is used with evident affection by Verne: Sabine's concision is not rudeness but the economy of speech natural to a man whose primary language is the language of tracks, wind, and the behaviour of animals.

### The "Ripening" of Furs

Sabine's expertise extends beyond hunting to the judgment of pelts. When the expedition encounters fur-bearing animals whose coats are not yet in prime condition, Sabine articulates the hunter's patient aesthetic:

> [016-017] "At present the upper fur was reddish-brown and the under yellowish white, so that, as Sabine expressed it, it was desirable to let them 'ripen,' or, in other words — to wait for the cold to bleach them."

This notion of "ripening" — borrowed from agriculture and applied to the Arctic fur trade — reveals the depth of Sabine's craft knowledge. He understands fur not as a static commodity but as a seasonal product with an optimal harvest window. The cold, far from being merely an hardship to be endured, is the agent that transforms a mediocre pelt into a valuable one.

## The Ice Island Crisis

When the catastrophic drift of Victoria Island is discovered, Sabine's skills shift from provisioning to exploration. He is selected for the crucial scouting party that attempts to find a passage across the channel to the mainland:

> [038-019] "Hobson, Mrs. Barnett, Kalumah, Marbre, and Sabine were to go, and, if the route should be practicable, they would try and find a passage across the channel."

The composition of this party is telling: Hobson as commander, Mrs. Barnett as intrepid observer, Kalumah as native guide, and Marbre and Sabine as the expedition's most reliable hunters and scouts. Sabine's inclusion in this select group confirms his status — when the colony's survival hangs on finding a way across the ice, the taciturn hunter is one of the men Hobson trusts to bring them through.

Sabine's background before joining [[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]] is never elaborated — he exists in the novel as a type of the seasoned frontier hunter, a figure Verne's readers would have recognised from the literature of North American exploration. What matters narratively is his competence: when the expedition faces hunger or danger, Sabine can be counted on to find game or scout a route.
