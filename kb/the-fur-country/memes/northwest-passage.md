## Definition

The Northwest Passage is the direct sea communication between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans via the waterways of the Arctic Archipelago. In the mid-nineteenth century setting of *The Fur Country*, it remained one of the great unsolved geographical puzzles, its discovery carrying immense commercial and national prestige.

> [010-020] "But the Northwest Passage—that is to say, the direct communication by sea between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans—was not then discovered?"

## Historical Attempts

By 1859, when the novel opens at [[fort-reliance|Fort Reliance]], the Northwest Passage had been sought by generations of explorers. Key expeditions referenced in the novel's background include:

| Explorer | Expedition Focus | Period |
|----------|-----------------|--------|
| Samuel Hearne | [[coppermine-river|Coppermine River]] exploration | 1769–1772 |
| William Edward Parry | Northwest Passage attempts | 1819–1827 |
| [[franklin|Sir John Franklin]] | Lost Arctic expedition; discovered final link | 1845 |
| Robert McClure | First confirmed transit of the Passage | 1850–1854 |

Each of these explorers is invoked in the novel as part of the shared knowledge that [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]'s guests bring to their conversations.

## Role in The Fur Country

The Northwest Passage is not a direct plot element of *The Fur Country* ([[hudsons-bay-company|the Company]]'s expedition is overland, not maritime), but it functions as the novel's geographical and historical frame. [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Jaspar Hobson]] and his party operate in the same waters and on the same ice that the Passage-seekers had charted. The novel makes clear, especially in [[captain-craventy|Captain Craventy]]'s dinner-table discussion, that Arctic exploration is a continuum — each expedition builds on the work of its predecessors, and the Northwest Passage is the ultimate prize that haunts every northerly journey.

## Later Discovery

Robert McClure's 1850–1854 expedition achieved the first confirmed transit of the Northwest Passage (partly by sledge over frozen straits). [[franklin|Sir John Franklin]]'s lost 1845 expedition, whose ships HMS *Erebus* and *Terror* disappeared with all 129 men, had in fact located the final uncharted link of the Passage — though [[franklin|Franklin]] did not survive to report the discovery.

> [010-029] "Was it not they who aided Captain Back in his voyage in 1834, when he discovered King William's Land, where Franklin met his fate?"

[[franklin|Franklin]]'s ships would not be located until 2014 and 2016, a century and a half after Verne's novel was published. For Verne's contemporary readers, [[franklin|Franklin]] was a figure of tragic mystery — the man who had found the Northwest Passage but could not return.
