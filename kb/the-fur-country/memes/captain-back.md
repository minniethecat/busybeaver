## Historical Background

Captain (later Admiral Sir) George Back (1796–1878) was one of the most accomplished British Arctic explorers of the early nineteenth century. He served under [[franklin|Sir John Franklin]] on the Coppermine expedition of 1819–1822 and later led his own expeditions to the Canadian Arctic. His 1834 overland expedition to the Back River (then called the Great Fish River) discovered King William's Land — the very island where Franklin's lost 1845 expedition would later meet its tragic end.

Back's career spanned the heroic age of Arctic exploration. As a young midshipman he had been captured by the French during the Napoleonic Wars and spent five years as a prisoner. After his release he volunteered for Arctic service, joining Franklin's first overland expedition in 1819 — a harrowing journey during which eleven of the twenty-man party died of starvation and exposure, and Back himself walked 1,200 miles on snowshoes to secure supplies, an act of endurance that saved the surviving members. His own 1834 expedition, undertaken to search for the missing Captain John Ross, instead became a voyage of discovery when he mapped the Great Fish River and identified the northern coastline of King William's Land.

|------|-----------|------|---------|

| 1825–1827 | Mackenzie River Expedition | Lieutenant under Franklin | Explored Arctic coast from Mackenzie delta |

## In The Fur Country

Captain Back is invoked in Chapter X during a long retrospective conversation between [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] and [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] as they travel north along the Coppermine River valley. The conversation is a sweeping survey of Arctic exploration history: Hobson recounts how Samuel Hearne first mapped the Coppermine in 1769–1772, how the Hudson's Bay Company initially obstructed the search for the Northwest Passage, and how Parliament's offer of a £20,000 reward in 1746 failed to spur new efforts for thirty years. In this context, Back appears as the culminating example of HBC–explorer cooperation:

> [010-029] "I agree with you, madam; they are, as they have often proved, inured to the rigours of the Arctic climate. Was it not they who aided Captain Back in his voyage in 1834, when he discovered King William's Land, where Franklin met his fate? Was it not two of us, Dease and Simpson, who were sent by the Governor of Hudson's Bay to explore the shores of the Polar Sea in 1838, and whose courageous efforts first discovered Victoria Land? It is my opinion that the future reserves for the Hudson's Bay Company the final conquest of the Arctic regions."

Back is thus listed alongside Dease and Simpson — fellow HBC-backed explorers — in a roll-call of achievement that Hobson deploys to argue that the Company's agents, "living as they do close to the coast, are better fitted than anyone else" to complete the exploration of the polar regions. The passage also names [[franklin|Franklin]]'s fatal connection to King William's Land, a geographical link that foreshadows the novel's own themes of Arctic peril.

### Hobson's Rhetorical Purpose

Hobson's invocation of Back serves a specific rhetorical function within the conversation. [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]] has questioned whether the Hudson's Bay Company has been a help or a hindrance to Arctic exploration, noting that the Company had historically obstructed the search for the Northwest Passage for fear that discovery would disrupt its fur-trade monopoly. Hobson counters by citing Back's 1834 expedition as proof that the Company had changed its stance — actively aiding rather than impeding explorers. The name-drop is thus not merely historical colour but a strategic argument: Back represents the moment when Company policy pivoted from obstruction to support, and Hobson uses this precedent to justify his own expedition's legitimacy on Company-claimed territory.

## The Coppermine Context

The setting of this retrospective — the Coppermine River valley — is itself significant. The river, discovered by HBC agent Samuel Hearne in 1769, flows north into Coronation Gulf and the Arctic Ocean. Hobson recounts Hearne's harrowing journey to [[paulina-barnett|Paulina Barnett]]:

> [010-019] "Samuel Hearne, the agent, only went to reconnoitre the position of a copper-mine which native miners had reported. The fish and game on which Hearne had relied often failed him; and he had once nothing to eat for seven days but wild fruit, bits of old leather, and burnt bones. But he did not even yet despair, and started a third time, December 7th, 1770; and after a struggle of nineteen months, he discovered the Coppermine river, July 13th, 1772, the course of which he followed to its mouth."

As Hobson's sledges follow the Coppermine's course, the narrative explicitly maps the geography of past exploration onto the present journey: the valley widens as they approach the sea, the hills sink lower, and "scattered clumps of resinous trees" yield to tundra. Back's 1834 expedition had traced a different river — the Great Fish River (later renamed Back River in his honour) — farther east, but the two routes are spiritually linked as arteries of Arctic discovery running through Company territory. Both rivers drain northward into the Arctic Ocean across the barren grounds that formed the theatre of nineteenth-century polar exploration.

## The Franklin Connection

Back's discovery of King William's Land carries a tragic weight within the novel's historical framework. Hobson explicitly notes that this was the very place "where Franklin met his fate." The connection between Back's achievement and Franklin's disaster creates a narrative arc that spans decades of Arctic exploration. After Hearne's foundational discovery, Franklin's 1819–1822 expedition — on which Back himself had served — had extended the mapped coastline:

> [010-023] "It did, madam; and it was thanks to it that Captain Franklin was able to accomplish his voyage of 1819 to 1822 between the river discovered by Hearne and Cape Turnagain."

The chain of exploration — Hearne, Franklin, Back, Dease and Simpson — forms a relay of geographical knowledge, each expedition building on its predecessors' discoveries. Back's own 1834 voyage, supported by the Hudson's Bay Company, represents a pivotal link in this chain: the moment when an HBC-backed explorer mapped the very ground where the Company's most famous lost expedition would later perish. The irony is profound: Back's greatest discovery would become the site of Arctic exploration's greatest tragedy, and the survivor of Franklin's first expedition would map the grave of Franklin's last.

### The Back-Franklin Relationship

The personal dimension of the Franklin connection is worth noting. Back had served under Franklin as a junior officer on two expeditions (1819–1822 and 1825–1827), and the two men shared the bond of extreme hardship survived together. During the 1819 expedition, when the party faced starvation at Fort Enterprise, Back had trekked ahead to secure supplies from Yellowknife — a round trip of over a thousand miles in mid-winter that saved the lives of Franklin and the remaining survivors. This experience gave Back an intimate knowledge of the terrain where Franklin would later vanish, and his 1834 discovery of King William's Land added a melancholy dimension to the geography he mapped: he had unknowingly charted the destination of his former commander's final, fatal voyage.

## Legacy

Back's name endures in the geography of the Canadian Arctic. The Great Fish River — the waterway he followed in 1834 — was renamed the Back River in his honour, and it remains one of the principal rivers of Nunavut. His discovery of King William's Land (now King William Island) marked the last major geographical discovery in the central Canadian Arctic before the search for Franklin's lost expedition would fill in the remaining blanks on the map during the 1850s. In Verne's novel, Back's brief mention serves as a historical anchor — a reminder that the fictional expedition of [[jaspar-hobson|Lieutenant Hobson]] belongs to a real and continuing tradition of Arctic discovery, with real predecessors whose achievements and sufferings are inscribed in the geography through which the fictional characters travel.

## Significance

Back's mention serves a dual narrative purpose: it grounds the fictional expedition in the factual history of Arctic exploration, and it foreshadows the mortal stakes of the enterprise. That the Company's agents aided Back — and that Back's discovery became the site of Franklin's death — underscores the continuum of Arctic exploration as a relay of knowledge passed from one generation of explorers to the next, each building on the discoveries and disasters of their predecessors. Hobson's roll-call culminates in his declaration of faith in the Company's destiny:

> [010-029] "It is my opinion that the future reserves for the Hudson's Bay Company the final conquest of the Arctic regions."

The irony, for the reader who knows the novel's eventual outcome, is that Hobson's own expedition — like those of Hearne, Franklin, and Back before him — will discover that the Arctic reserves its conquests for no one. The ice island upon which he builds his fort will drift, rotating in the polar current, indifferent to the Company's ambitions and the explorer's claims alike.
